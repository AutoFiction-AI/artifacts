# Chapter 14

Back upstairs, the sixth-floor graduate office smelled of wet wool and the hot-dust cough the radiator gave off every time it woke. Outside the long window the lake looked skinned over with dirty pewter. June stood at the sill in her work boots, chewing nicotine gum hard enough that the muscle in her jaw kept ticking. Omar had taken off his coat and folded it over the back of the chair with the same care he used on shirts that cost more than any of them admitted. Leela sat at her desk with the grievance PDF open again, as if another pass through the thing might generate the evidence she had refused to include.

It was still the weakened version. No server logs. No clean line from Mohit's private clones to Ruderal. Their best chance had gone out at dawn in a file that smelled, even to her, of fear.

Ben texted, `Are you all upstairs? Need 5 min. Actually important.`

June looked over when Leela read it aloud. "He writes like a hostage trying to sound casual."

"Maybe he finally found a spine," Omar said.

Nobody believed that, but nobody said not to let him up.

Ben came in carrying cold with him. Sleet shone on the shoulders of his navy shell, and the lift-ticket band from some January ski weekend was still strangling the top loop of his backpack. He had twisted one strap twice around his wrist hard enough to blanch the knuckles. His cheeks were red from the wind. He shut the door with more care than the moment deserved and left his hand on the knob a second too long, as if the metal might still let him back out.

"What happened?" Leela asked.

Ben swallowed. He had a habit, when he was scared, of picking at the skin beside his thumb until it shined wet. He was doing it now.

Leela felt her pulse begin its stupid inventory. Who knew, what had moved, which version of the story would count as official by the end of the hour.

"So I need to say this before it becomes, like, even worse in a way I can't get in front of." He looked from Leela to June to Omar and back, already hunting for the face that might rescue him. "I sent Mohit the draft."

June stopped chewing. "What draft."

Ben gave a tiny helpless laugh. "Come on."

"What draft, Ben."

"The grievance. The PDF. Last night."

The room seemed to contract around the open file on her screen. She had spent months shaving the truth down to something survivable, and survivable had turned soft enough for Ben to pick up barehanded and pass along.

The radiator knocked once behind Leela, metal on metal, like a bad idea gaining confidence.

Omar sat back very slowly. "You forwarded the complaint to the person accused in the complaint."

"I know how that sounds."

"It sounds exact," June said.

Ben took two steps into the room and then seemed to realize that only brought him farther inside what he had done. "You were asking me to verify the thing about Omar's letter upload, and my name was in it, and some of the other stuff was about private meetings I wasn't even there for, and I just kept thinking, if this is going official and he gets it from somebody else first, then it looks like I was sitting on a document accusing my advisor of coercion and data theft and I didn't say anything. That's insane. That's not safe for me either."

"Safe," June repeated. She said it like she had found a dead insect in her coffee.

"I was trying not to get buried," Ben said. "I was trying not to be the freshman idiot who gets pulled into a thing and then everybody acts like obviously I should've known the rules."

"There were no rules," Leela said. Her voice came out flatter than she meant it to. "There was a private draft."

"Which named me. And the portal thing. And the part where the letter got uploaded under Mohit's signature, which, by the way, isn't a small detail, Leela."

There it was: the raw animal understanding that his own fingerprints were on something ugly. Fear had sent him straight to Mohit, the one person in the building who always volunteered to define safety for other people.

"When?" Omar asked.

"Like one fifteen. Maybe one twenty."

"With what note?" Omar said.

Ben blinked. "What?"

"Did you merely attach the file and ruin us passively, or did you add a little essay."

"I said I thought he should know there was a document circulating and that I wasn't sure everything in it was accurate as written." Ben heard himself, winced, and rushed on. "But I also said some of it looked serious. I wasn't trying to whitewash him. I wasn't. I just thought he should see it before it became a whole departmental thing."

June laughed once. It was a good laugh in another universe, quick and mean and almost musical. In this one it made the room smaller.

"You thought he should get first edit on his own indictment."

"That's not fair."

"Nothing about this is fair, you little shit."

Leela should have felt only fury. Instead there was fury and, beneath it, the smaller more humiliating recognition that Ben's logic was not foreign. He had done in twelve hours what she had been doing all year: feeding the worst thing to the person most capable of weaponizing it, because maybe if she moved first into the damage she could control how hard it landed.

June saw something move across her face and turned on her before she could hide it. "Don't."

"I didn't say anything."

"You got that exact expression you get when you're halfway to understanding him."

"I understand the mechanism," Leela said. "I don't forgive it."

"Can one of you just tell me how bad this is?" Ben asked. The need in his voice was naked now; he kept wiping his palm on the side of his quarter-zip and checking his phone screen as if disaster might have revised itself while he wasn't looking. "Procedurally, I mean. Or socially. I need the real score here."

Omar stared at him. "You are standing in the score."

All four laptops made noise at once. June's phone buzzed against the windowsill. Ben looked down first, as if he had been waiting for the gold star to arrive.

The subject line on Leela's screen was: `Concern re: circulation of confidential materials`.

Mohit had sent it to Gloria Fisk. He had cc'd Leela, June, Omar, and Ben.

For one stupid second Leela noticed the civility of the punctuation. No exclamation mark, no panic, no self-defense that could be screened to a lawyer later and called volatile. He had been awake at one in the morning, or awake by one-fifteen, and he had still given the subject line the quiet dignity of a donor update.

She opened it.

Dear Gloria,

I regret having to raise this before we have better facts, but last night I became aware of a draft memorandum circulating among current students that includes selectively excerpted advising conversations, unpublished work product, and a description of what appears to be an unauthorized revision/upload of visa-related funding documentation.

Because several of the underlying situations already involve sensitive compliance and mentoring matters, I am concerned that the circulation itself may now present a separate professional-misconduct issue. I am especially concerned that more junior students may have felt pressure to endorse claims or share private materials they are not in a position to evaluate independently.

Some of the draft's assertions appear to reflect misunderstandings of normal adversarial evaluation procedures inside the lab. Others may be better addressed through standard departmental process than through informal dossier-building that risks further confusion.

My priority is to protect the students, the integrity of their research records, and the department. I am happy to meet individually with anyone involved today so that people can clarify what they have directly observed and what they may have signed onto under stress.

Best,
Mohit

He had taken their night in the library, June's legal pad, Omar's visa panic, Ben's useless conscience, and Leela's own late confession about the ghostwritten letter, and ironed them flat into nouns. `Confidential materials.` `Compliance.` `Professional misconduct.` He had not denied anything important. He had simply rearranged where the danger sat.

June read faster than the others. Leela could tell from the way her mouth hardened, one side first. "Look at that. He found the phrase junior students."

Ben said, "He cc'd Gloria."

"Yes," Omar said. "That's what a counterattack looks like."

Leela kept reading. Ben's forwarded note was still below Mohit's email in the chain, visible if she scrolled. `I thought you should have this. Some of it seems serious and some may be missing context. I didn't want to mishandle it.`

Mishandle it.

Her stomach turned with the blunt physical certainty of bad cafeteria eggs. Mohit's knowledge itself barely registered; men like him always knew before the paperwork did. The violence was the conversion. Their fear had existed for one night in a document that still sounded like people. By breakfast he had turned it into a question of stewardship.

June looked at Ben over the top of her phone. "You didn't forward a memo. You forwarded the only room where anybody said what he did out loud."

Ben had gone pale under the wind burn. "I didn't know he was going to make it about misconduct."

"He didn't make it about misconduct," Omar said. "You handed him misconduct."

That landed. Omar rarely let his voice rise, which made the quiet in it more expensive. Ben opened his mouth, closed it, then said, "Should I reply and say I wasn't pressured?"

June's head snapped toward him. "Don't touch your keyboard."

"I mean it could matter how he framed me."

"Ben," Leela said, and heard how tired she sounded. "If you send another email right now, you're not clarifying anything. You're just giving him more sentences."

Ben nodded too fast. He wanted orders. That was the other sick joke of it. The lab had trained all of them to look for a parent-shaped voice at the exact moment judgment was required.

A second email came in before anybody moved. This one was to June alone, though Leela could see the subject when June's phone lit again on the sill.

`Ten minutes today?`

June picked up the phone. "Fuck."

"From him?" Leela asked.

June did not bother pretending otherwise. "Yes."

Omar said, "Don't go."

June slid the phone into her coat pocket. "I didn't say I was going."

But an hour later, when the wet snow outside had turned to sleet and then to a colorless kind of rain, Leela found June in the fifth-floor corridor outside Mohit's office anyway.

Keating always looked cheapest in February. The cinderblock sweated faintly under the paint. Salt had dried in fans around the building mats. Somebody had left a paper plate with two stale brownie corners on the radiator between faculty doors. June stood under Mohit's office placard with her hands jammed deep in her coat pockets, one heel lifting and dropping against the tile in a tiny metronome she would have denied.

"You really came," Leela said.

June did not turn. "I said I was thinking about it."

"That isn't the same thing."

"No." June finally looked over. Her hair needed trimming again; one side was beginning to hook against her jaw. "It isn't."

Leela stopped beside the dead ficus outside the copier room. "If you go in there alone, he's going to split hairs until you start agreeing with his nouns."

"He already sent the nouns."

"We can decide our own response first."

"We had our chance at that."

There it was again, the place June could always touch if she wanted the argument to bleed faster. Leela felt the old shame move through her like cold water under a door.

"That isn't fair either," she said.

June gave her a look that could have charred paper. "No? We sent a complaint with the middle cut out because you wanted justice that still let you graduate on time. Now he has the opening he needs, and you want me to perform solidarity like we're still in the clean version of this."

Leela started with precision because that was still her cheapest form of hope. "What I want is for him not to take us apart by identifying, correctly, where each person is most vulnerable and then offering the exact amount of rescue required to make that vulnerability private again."

June barked a laugh. "See, this is why I can't talk to you when you're scared. You start sounding like a grant abstract about your own life."

The office door opened behind them before Leela could answer. Mohit stood there with his reading glasses off in one hand and a glass jar of fennel seeds open on his desk behind him, the sharp sweet smell getting out into the hall. His shirtsleeves were immaculate. He wore the composure of a man mildly irritated that a day had become cluttered.

"June," he said, as if this were an ordinary advising check-in. Then to Leela: "Leela, if you need time with her after, I won't keep you long."

The generosity of that sentence was almost funny.

June pushed off the wall. "Ten minutes."

"Of course."

She went in. Mohit closed the door with the same quiet care Ben had used upstairs, but the old latch did not catch cleanly on the first pull. The door settled with a thin seam of light still showing at the frame.

Leela took three useless steps toward the stairwell and stopped. She did not leave. Mohit's door had the standard wired-glass slit, clouded with old tape ghosts, enough to flatten everyone inside into moving weather. She stood by the copier and watched the red digital clock over the faculty mailboxes turn from 2:11 to 2:12 while the machine beside her kept waking and sleeping with a throat-clear of rollers. At first the office gave her only a murmur, his voice pitched for reassurance, June's lower and harder. Then a chair leg scraped, and Mohit's words came through cleanly enough to bruise.

"I'm not asking you to defend me," he said. "I'm asking you to separate what you directly know from a grievance assembled out of everybody else's panic."

June answered too low for Leela to catch.

"What Gloria will see," Mohit said, "is a student coming off a bad seminar season and attaching herself to claims she can't independently document. That doesn't read as brave. It reads as unstable."

The copier clicked awake again. Through the wired glass Leela saw June go still, one hand braced on the back of the visitor chair. When the machine fell quiet, June was saying, "You really know how to make care sound filthy."

Mohit let the silence do a little work. "I know what institutions hear," he said. "And I know you need a future after this place. If you stop attaching your name to this draft, I can introduce you this week to a model-evaluation team in Brooklyn. Good infrastructure. Small group. No appetite for departmental theater. I can't make that call honestly while you're endorsing material you didn't witness."

June laughed once, flat enough that Leela felt it in her teeth. "So that's the price."

"That's me refusing to let one bad document close a door you'll want open in six months."

"A door you happen to own the key to."

Another scrape. June moved across the narrow strip of glass, and Leela saw her bite down on the inside of her lower lip so hard that when she pulled free there was already a bright wet line there. Mohit said something softer, lost to the copier's rollers, and June answered louder.

"Don't dress this up like you're saving me."

At 2:23 the door opened.

June came out with her face blank in the efficient way people blank it when they do not want to be seen deciding anything yet. The cut inside her lip had marked one corner of her mouth red. Mohit remained inside. He gave Leela a small nod, a colleague's nod, and disappeared back to his desk.

"Walk," June said.

They went down one flight into the concrete stairwell where the air was colder and smelled faintly of bleach. Someone had stubbed out a cigarette on the inside sill weeks ago and the ash still clung there because Keating's custodians did the visible rooms first.

"What did he say?" Leela asked.

June leaned against the railing and looked past Leela at the ash clinging to the stairwell sill. "You heard some of it."

"Not enough."

"Fine. He said the complaint as drafted is a fucking gift to him."

"Because it is."

"Right. Good. Glad we agree on reality."

Leela let that pass. "What else."

"He said if this goes any farther in exactly this form, Gloria's first question is going to be about the letter upload and the private notes, not Ruderal. He said he didn't want Omar exposed on the visa paperwork issue. He said he didn't want your existing compliance mess turned into a second record." June touched the split inside her lip with the tip of her tongue and winced. "'You're too smart to let yourself get cross-contaminated by other people's procedural mistakes. If your name comes off this, I can put you in front of a model-eval team in Brooklyn this week.'"

Leela closed her eyes for a second. Mohit loved a lab metaphor when it let him sound clean.

June wiped the blood from the corner of her mouth onto the heel of her hand. "He made it sound like he's doing me a favor by noticing what I'm actually good at."

"He's trying to buy you."

"He's pricing me," June said.

"No?"

June pressed her thumb into the split in the paper ID bracelet still looped around her key ring. "That's the problem. If he said, Here is cash, please betray your friends, I could spit in his face and leave. He said academia is already going to read me as unstable this year because of the seminar and the internal conflict, and he could put me in a room where none of that matters if I stop attaching my name to claims I can't document myself."

"You can document some of them."

"Some." June looked up at the stairwell window where rain glazed the dirty wire glass. "And some I got from you after you sat on them for months."

That one landed cleanly. Leela had no answer that did not sound like appeal.

June kept going because once she started talking plainly she tended to keep her hand on the bruise until both people had felt it. "He didn't ask me to defend him. He asked me to sort what I saw with my own eyes from what I got folded into because the rest of you wanted the coalition to look bigger than it was."

"That isn't what we wanted."

"What did we want, then?"

"We wanted enough corroboration that he couldn't call any one of us confused."

"He's calling us confused anyway."

Leela gripped the cold rail hard enough that the old wrist brace seam bit her skin through the sweater sleeve. The stairwell amplified every small sound: June's gum wrapper crackling in her pocket, the heavy door above opening and closing, water hitting the metal sill in erratic taps.

"If you take his introduction now," she said, "you're letting him write the story of why you took it."

June rubbed the heel of her hand over her mouth. For a moment the hardness drained out of her and left only a tired, compact cold. "He already writes the story, Lee. He wrote it before Ben ever hit send."

"Then why help him."

June looked at her for a long time. "Because I need a future too."

It was the simplest thing anybody had said all day, and it made argument feel obscene.

Leela tried anyway. "We all do."

"Yes, and some of us are worse placed than others. Omar needs paperwork. You need a defense date and a miracle. I need somebody to look at my CV and not see the girl who got gutted in seminar and then attached herself to a grievance with missing teeth." She pushed off the rail. "He knows exactly which hope to press with each person. That doesn't make the hope fake."

"So you're taking it."

"I'm taking the meeting," June said. "I already took the meeting. I'm taking the introduction if he sends it. I'm not promising him anything else today."

"Today."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Make me your clean loss. You don't get to look at me like I'm the first person here to choose my own skin."

That hurt because it was partly false and mostly true. Leela had been choosing her own skin in increments so small she had mistaken them for reason.

June dragged both hands through her coat pockets as if there might be a spare decision hidden there, came up with only a flattened receipt, and cursed under her breath. "Also, before you ask, no, I don't know if he forwarded the email to anybody else. He said Gloria would want to keep it contained. He said that like it was comfort."

"Are you going to tell Omar?"

"I don't want to tell anybody anything for maybe six hours."

She shouldered past the stairwell door and was gone before Leela decided whether to follow.

Omar was waiting in the basement lab when she got back, standing by the hummed-over whiteboard with his coat on again and an uncapped dry-erase marker in one hand, as if he had meant to write something and then lost the thread. The lab smelled of burnt coffee and old upholstery and the server closet's perpetual furnace breath. Someone had left a cup noodle fork on the couch arm. Ben was nowhere in sight.

"Well?" Omar asked.

"She met him."

"I assumed that from her disappearance."

Leela set her bag down on the desk that used to feel like hers before Research Integrity turned every login into somebody else's property. "He offered her an industry introduction if she disentangles herself."

Omar's expression barely shifted, but he set the marker down so carefully the plastic still clicked against the tray. "Of course."

"He framed it as protecting her from the weaker parts of the complaint."

"Which are weak because you removed the stronger parts."

She flinched. Omar saw it and looked away, not kind enough to spare her but not interested in theatrical cruelty either.

"That's not the whole situation," she said.

"No. It is only the part that matters today."

Leela sat. The desk edge was cold through her jeans. "She hasn't withdrawn. Not yet."

Omar let out a short, ugly breath that was nearly laughter. "What would exact withdrawal look like. A notarized statement."

"Can you not do this right now."

"I can do exactly this right now," he said. He had started snapping the marker cap on and off, on and off, until the sound became its own small alarm. "Because I'm the one whose visa paperwork is now sitting in an email thread labeled possible misconduct. I'm the one whose funding letter becomes an unauthorized upload the moment Mohit wants that language. I am very sorry if the ethical subtlety of June's job lead is emotionally complex for you. I'm trying to calculate whether I need my own counsel."

Leela opened her mouth, shut it, started again. "I know."

"No," Omar said. "You know in the way people know weather. I know in the way I will have to answer a question with dates and signatures if this expands."

His phone buzzed. He checked it and grimaced.

"Who is it?" she asked.

"International students office. Automated reminder about spring verification." He slid the phone back into his pocket with too much force. "Apparently the universe enjoys theme."

Leela wanted to say something useful and found only scraps. That had been the year in miniature: the desire to be exact colliding with the fact that exactness rarely arrived before damage.

"He's triaging," Omar said after a moment, quieter now. "He knows who can be offered prestige, who can be offered escape, who can be offered paperwork, and who can be offered blame."

"And Ben?"

Omar's mouth twisted. "Ben is offered the chance to be the good child."

As if summoned by the sentence, Ben appeared at the lab door ten minutes later, not crossing the threshold. He had lost his jacket somewhere and looked smaller without it, all quarter-zip and coffee stain and the red welt where his backpack strap had dug into one shoulder. He held his phone in both hands like an appliance he had not agreed to operate.

"Can I talk to you?" he asked Leela.

Omar said, "No."

Ben flinched. "I mean just like two minutes."

Leela was tired enough to be cruel, tired enough not to spend the energy. "Outside."

They went into the hall by the vending machines. One of them had been out of order since September and still displayed a cheerful digital `ENJOY`. The floor tile there always felt colder than elsewhere, probably because of the loading dock below.

Ben spoke before she had fully turned toward him. "He asked me to write down how the draft got to me."

Leela stared.

"Not in, like, a formal way. He said just so everybody is accurate about sequence and no one accidentally overstates pressure that wasn't there." Ben rubbed at his forehead. "He said because I'm junior, people may have assumed I were easy to lean on."

`I were.` Even now he was grabbing at older people's language and dropping pieces of it on the floor.

"And are you?" Leela asked.

"What?"

"Easy to lean on."

Ben looked offended for half a second, then miserable. "Apparently."

"You do understand he's making you a witness."

"He said he was trying to protect me."

"From what."

Ben laughed without humor. "From all of you, kind of."

The machine behind them buzzed and clicked, trying to dispense nothing. Leela could see the pulse in Ben's temple. He had not slept. His hair, usually arranged to look accidentally good, stood up crooked over one ear.

"I wasn't trying to betray you," he said. "That sounds pathetic now, but I wasn't. I just needed somebody senior to tell me what was safe."

There it was again, honest enough to be worse than apology.

Leela thought of Mohit's office door, of her own years translating his evasions into mentorship because the alternative cost too much, of Ben at twenty-four believing adulthood was a building with controlled entry and someone inside would eventually wave him through. It would have been easier to hate a snake. It was harder to deal with a boy who kept crawling toward the boot because the boot had a title.

"What do you want from me," she asked.

"Should I send the summary or not."

"To Mohit."

"Or Gloria. Or both. I don't know how this works."

"It works," Leela said, "by him getting more useful to him every time you panic."

Ben went still. The sentence hit. Good.

"But if I don't send anything, then it looks like I'm hiding."

"From him? Or from yourself."

He stared past her at the vending machine glass, at the dusty spiral of peanut-butter crackers nobody ever bought. "I don't know anymore."

Leela believed him. That was the worst part.

"Then stop writing until you do," she said. "Not because I'm asking for loyalty. That ship is gone. Because every sentence you give him becomes his sentence."

Ben nodded, once. "Okay."

He did not move. He wanted absolution still, or at least a tone of voice he could treat as the first plank back to shore.

He said, softer, "Are they going to hate me forever."

Leela was too tired to lie kindly. "I don't know."

He took that like a formal consequence, tucked the phone into his pocket, and went back down the hall toward Mohit's office anyway.

By evening June had not answered three texts.

`Can we talk in person?`

`Did he say anything else about Gloria or RI?`

`I'm not asking you to decide tonight. I just need to know where you are.`

The last one showed delivered, not read. Leela watched the small gray status line longer than the words deserved. Her radiator in graduate housing had started its nightly clang by the time Omar finally texted in the separate thread he and Leela had made after Ben became unsafe.

`I'm not putting anything sensitive in writing now.`

A minute later: `Do not send me more details unless necessary.`

He was right. It still felt like abandonment.

Leela called June once and listened to it ring under the mechanical banging of the radiator, the sound of old pipes making a case against sleep. No voicemail. June had killed that feature months ago after a conference organizer left three passive-aggressive reminders about a poster dimension error. Another private fact, stupidly tender in its uselessness, and Leela hated that even now her mind reached for the scraps of June that had nothing to do with survival.

The next day she saw June only in motion.

First at eleven, crossing the quad in the olive coat she wore when snow turned to rain, tote bag jammed under her arm, head down against the wind off the lake. Leela called her name. June looked up, raised a hand that might have meant wait or stop or simply I heard you, and kept walking.

Then again after three outside the machine room, where June was talking to somebody from systems staff about a broken queue and sounded almost normal, too normal, her voice dry and practical and untouched by the previous twenty-four hours. When Leela got close enough to interrupt, June said, without looking at her, "Don't use university channels for anything about this. Not email. Not Slack. Not text if you can help it."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the answer I have."

"Are you withdrawing."

The systems guy glanced between them, understood instantly that he was looking at a mess with tuition attached, and retreated through the machine-room door.

June finally faced her. She looked exhausted, eyes rubbed pink at the rims, hair clipped back with the cheap black claw clip she kept losing and rebuying. "I'm trying not to make one stupid decision after another at top speed."

"So that's a yes."

"That's me saying I need room."

"You had room. We made room."

Something tightened in June's face, slight as a paper cut and just as immediate. "Did we."

Leela felt the sting the moment the words left her. June did too. Neither of them apologized. They had long since exhausted apology as a solvent.

"Lee," June said, quieter, and the private register of it hurt more than a snapped line would have. "I can't be the place you come for faith right now."

Then she was gone into the machine room, door slamming shut behind her hard enough to rattle the posted safety goggles.

By Friday, the grievance had already changed shape in the air.

Nobody official said so directly. Gloria's assistant sent a calendared `climate check-in` for the following week. A faculty member Leela barely knew nodded to her in the hall with the careful over-kindness people used around a recent death or a person rumored to be difficult. Ben carried two boxes of sponsor folders into Mohit's office and would not meet her eyes. Omar stopped lingering anywhere public. The lab group chat, once a place for broken code, free food sightings, and late-night cruelty that counted as affection, became a strip of silence with one automated building notice about a steam shutdown on Saturday morning.

Leela texted June once more from the laundromat in the basement of her housing block, because the machines were loud enough to make desperation feel briefly practical.

`If you're out, just say you're out.`

The washers spun gray water behind thick glass. A child down the row was building a fort out of overturned plastic baskets while his mother counted quarters on top of a detergent box. Leela's own sheets were in the smallest machine because the next size up cost an extra dollar fifty she did not want to spend. Her phone sat on top of the warm lid, screen dark.

Silence.

She tried, against judgment, to picture June taking the introduction as logistics instead of betrayal. A train downstate. A clean office with standing desks and people who thought benchmark infrastructure was a product problem instead of a dissertation wound. Paychecks that arrived without being braided to one man's moods. It was easy to picture. That was what made it hurt. Mohit had offered something June could actually use.

By nine the dryers had eaten four dollars and left the fitted sheet still damp at the corners. Leela hauled the laundry upstairs anyway. In the hall outside her apartment someone had propped open a door with a statistics textbook. The radiator inside her unit was clanging again, furious with its own metal lungs.

She set the laundry basket down and checked her phone one last time.

There was a new message, but not from June.

From Ben: `He said not to worry if people seem weird. He said these things get clarified. I know you probably don't want updates. Sorry.`

No question mark. No request for forgiveness. Even shame had not broken his habit of reporting upward and sideways at once.

Leela stared at the text until the screen dimmed.

Then she opened the thread with June and saw only her own messages, stacked one over the other like notes slid under a locked door. The last thing in the conversation from June was three nights earlier, before Ben, before Mohit's email, before this new clean split in the air between people still speaking and people managing. It was about nothing. Eggs. Did Leela need eggs. June had been at the store.

Leela sat on the edge of the bed in her coat. The damp sheet in the basket cooled against her shin. Somewhere down the hall, somebody laughed at a television laugh track, huge and canned and oblivious. Her radiator kept striking the same two notes.

Across the room, the defense folder she'd left on the desk under the blinds waited where she'd dropped it, too orderly to belong to this week.

Mohit had moved first. That was the simple version. The truer one was worse: he had moved into openings they had made for him, and now the only coalition that had felt briefly larger than his office existed mostly as separate silences. Omar protecting paperwork. June protecting a future she could still imagine. Ben protecting the part of himself that still believed proximity to power could be mistaken for safety.

Leela put the phone face down on the mattress and listened to the pipes hammer through the wall like a building trying to warn itself too late.
