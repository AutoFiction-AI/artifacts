# The Register of Tides

**Narrative Engine**
Primary engine: literary fiction fused with character study, using a social-procedural scaffold rather than thriller mechanics. The book should move through identity, kinship, shame, and material survival: who can be named, who can be paid, who can remain attached to a household, and what it costs when a merciful lie must either harden into law or be broken in public.

Consequences should stay rooted in the premise's natural register:
- economic exclusion from compensation, land, and relocation housing
- emotional and relational fracture inside marriages, parent-child bonds, and long-held communal trust
- perceptual shifts in how characters understand their own origin stories
- bodily and environmental pressure from the river, monsoon mud, overcrowded boats, and document fragility
- moral consequence from choosing one truth over another, not action-movie escalation

**Core Arc**
For thirty years Halima Khatun has used her midwife's authority to improvise legal life where the island's real life would have gotten people sold, married off, or worked into debt. When Kadam Char is ordered evacuated, the district administration decides compensation will be paid only to households whose names can be matched across birth records, land slips, marriage papers, and national identity cards. Halima is asked to sit with the officials because she alone can reconcile the island's contradictory records. The novel tracks the conversion of private mercy into public evidence. Every chapter should force the distinction between legal truth and lived truth to become more expensive.

The ending is not about exposing a criminal plot. It is about whether a community built partly through fiction can survive being translated into state paperwork without becoming unrecognizable to itself.

**Structural Commitments**
- Keep Halima's body and labor central: she knows people through blood, milk, fever, cord-stump, scars, and shame before she knows them through paper.
- Let the river function as pressure and metaphor, not as spectacle. Erosion, ferry schedules, wet documents, and the smell of silt matter more than disaster-porn.
- Preserve tonal range. Chapters 3, 10, and 17 should carry genuine quiet. Chapters 5 and 13 should work through dread and preparation. Chapters 8 and 16 should be aftermath chapters. Chapters 4, 9, 12, and 14 should turn on information rather than direct confrontation.
- The novel must repeatedly show that competence is not enough for Halima. Her skill made the problem. Her skill cannot solve it cleanly.

**Chapter Plan**
1. **Notice on the School Wall**
   Focus: Halima. Texture: crowded, bodily, public.
   Halima delivers a baby in a tin-roof hut while the district launch circles the char broadcasting the evacuation order. By the time she reaches the schoolyard, the wall is already pasted with the notice: compensation only for verified households. Aminul Haque, the records officer sent from the mainland, quickly grasps that the island's paperwork is unusable without Halima's memory and asks her to help reconcile the lists. She understands at once that this is not clerical work. It is an invitation to reopen thirty years of lies. The chapter ends with her unwrapping the oilcloth ledger she has not meant to show anyone again.

2. **The First Table**
   Focus: Halima. Texture: dialogue-heavy, procedural, humiliatingly public.
   Verification begins in the primary school. Widows, fishermen, abandoned wives, and sons with laminated identity cards queue in the heat while Chairman Mofiz Uddin keeps trying to reorder the line. Halima attempts to save people case by case, smoothing a date here, a missing father there, but the state's digital records keep throwing up contradictions. When Kashem Sheikh's identity card links him to a father who cannot also be attached to the homestead he claims, Aminul asks Halima to confirm the original entry. Her hesitation is visible. By evening the island has learned that her book may outrank the government's records, and people begin to understand that she has been deciding who belongs to whom for decades.

3. **Nura's Certificate**
   Focus: Halima with strong Nura presence. Texture: domestic, reflective, low-scene-count.
   Halima tries to reestablish ordinary life with her granddaughter Nura: rice washed in a dented bowl, a torn mosquito net mended, gossip about a cousin's baby. The quiet breaks when Nura brings out her school certificates and says she wants her papers corrected in time to apply for nursing training at the relocation colony. The father listed on her birth record has no usable national identity trail. For the first time the crisis becomes personal in a way Halima cannot hold at arm's length. The lie she made to keep Nura in school has matured into the gate barring her from the future she wants.

4. **Cord-Cutting**
   Focus: Halima, with Kashem's crisis driving the turn. Texture: slow-burn discovery.
   Kashem comes looking for proof that the father on his card can anchor his compensation claim. Before Halima can answer, Rahima Bewa, who has lived for years as a marginal widow on the western edge of the char, begins speaking in the language of bodies rather than documents: who bled where, who nursed whom, who tied which black thread around which newborn ankle. Halima shuts her down, but too late. Kashem sees that the adults around him share a history he was denied. The chapter turns not on a revelation fully spoken but on the knowledge that his life story may be arranged rather than inherited.

5. **The Deadline List**
   Focus: Halima. Texture: preparation and dread, ensemble movement.
   Aminul posts the next requirement: every household must present papers, witnesses, and marriage proof before the district deadline, or the first list will become the last list. A storm is building. People drag trunks from under beds, spread moldy documents in thin sunlight, argue over spellings, and begin bargaining for testimony. Halima spends the chapter moving from house to house without resolution, seeing how panic sorts the island into those whose stories can be cleaned for the state and those whose very lives depend on contradiction. No single confrontation resolves the chapter. Its force comes from accumulation and impending failure.

6. **Jamila's Mother**
   Focus: Halima, Jamila, and Majed. Texture: intimate argument with social consequence.
   Jamila Khatun, whom Halima once moved on paper into a safer household to keep her from being traded into marriage for debt, now faces a second dispossession. If she stays the daughter of her dead aunt in the records, she can inherit that woman's homestead compensation. If she reenters her biological mother's line, the claim collapses but her mother will not be left alone in resettlement. Majed wants security for their children. Jamila wants not to surrender the life the lie gave her. Halima is forced to see that her interventions did not simply rescue people from danger; they produced whole worlds with their own loyalties and casualties.

7. **The Search**
   Focus: Halima. Texture: public violation, protagonist acted upon.
   Mofiz, worried that a fuller audit may expose his own manipulated land claims, spreads the story that Halima took money to alter births. During Friday prayers, young men and party loyalists search her house for a second ledger. Nura fights back and is shoved hard enough to split her lip. Halima cannot protect her, cannot protect the papers, and cannot control how the island now sees her: not as its midwife but as its forger. The chapter's consequence is a loss of privacy and moral leverage. Her secrecy becomes common knowledge even though the contents remain hidden.

8. **Mud Inventory**
   Focus: Halima. Texture: aftermath, sorting, damaged objects.
   A night storm floods the floor. Certificates curl, ink runs, and the house smells of wet tin, mildew, and lamp smoke. Halima and Nura spend the chapter laying documents out to dry and discovering what the search changed. Several copied pages are missing from the wrapped ledger, and the missing entries correspond to land on the char's higher eastern side, the side Mofiz has been quietly consolidating. Instead of a clean pivot into accusation, the chapter dwells in material aftermath: what can be saved, what smears beyond recovery, what the body can still remember after paper fails.

9. **The Upazila Office**
   Focus: Halima with Nura and Aminul. Texture: travel chapter, bureaucratic revelation.
   Halima, Nura, and Aminul cross to the mainland to compare the char's records against the upazila databases. The offices are fluorescent, indifferent, and overfull; the char residents are treated as people who arrived already incorrect. Yet the trip yields one narrow possibility. A magistrate can approve compensation by verified household occupancy when blood and land inheritance cannot be made internally consistent, provided enough witnesses testify in person. The consequence is not relief but a new, harsher kind of exposure. To save themselves, people will have to say out loud the things the island has managed for years by euphemism and mutual silence.

10. **At the Bus-Stand Lodge**
    Focus: Halima and Nura. Texture: quiet, confessional, memory-heavy.
    Stranded overnight near the bus stand, Halima and Nura finally lose the ability to postpone the question of fatherhood. Halima does not confess everything, but she remembers with painful specificity the death of her daughter Saleha after the marriage Halima failed to stop, and Nura hears enough to understand that her own papers were built as a shield against that same machinery. Aminul appears only briefly, tired enough to drop his procedural tone and admit that the state can accept only one coherent story per household. The chapter ends with Nura knowing she has inherited not a clean lineage but a strategy of survival.

11. **Blood List, House List**
    Focus: Halima in public assembly. Texture: argumentative, collective, politically charged.
    Back on Kadam Char, Halima presents the occupancy-affidavit route in the mosque courtyard. The room splits. Some residents prefer silence and would rather risk exclusion than testify to abandoned marriages, debt bondage, stepchildren, or daughters conceived outside legal wedlock. Others, led by Kashem and Rahima in very different ways, demand full truth. The debate matters because it determines what kind of future the island believes it deserves: a cleaned list of legal families or a messier recognition of actual dependency. By chapter's end the char is divided into factions, and Halima is no longer operating as a private moral authority but as a public partisan.

12. **The Neighboring Char**
    Focus: Nura. Texture: displacement chapter, discovery through absence.
    Acting outside Halima's control, Nura travels with a cousin to the neighboring char where the man named as her father once worked. She does not find him. She finds fragments: another woman who waited for him, a tea-stall owner who remembers his debts, men who say he boarded a dredging boat and vanished downriver years ago. The trip does not reveal a hidden patriarch ready to restore her identity. It reveals how cheaply men can disappear and how much administrative weight women must then carry. Nura returns with less illusion and more anger. She no longer believes the truth will save her, but she resents being denied the choice.

13. **Rehearsals**
    Focus: Halima, Aminul, ensemble. Texture: preparation and dread.
    Before the magistrate's visit, witness groups rehearse their testimony in the schoolroom. Aminul tries to reduce living households to statements that will survive scrutiny. Each rehearsal exposes fresh humiliations: a child born "too early" after marriage, a sister who has lived for years in a brother-in-law's house without status, an elderly aunt nobody wants to claim because she lowers the per-head compensation share. Halima watches her lifetime of expansive invention being inverted into administrative pruning. The chapter ends with several families quietly deciding to cut loose dependents in order to make their stories cleaner.

14. **Copies**
    Focus: Halima. Texture: informational turn, betrayal rather than spectacle.
    Sufia Begum, Halima's former apprentice, admits that years ago she made copies of selected ledger pages and passed them to Mofiz because she believed official backup was safer than one woman's hidden notebook. Those copies allowed Mofiz to thicken his own household and lay claim to land that was never legally his. The revelation stings not because a villain is unmasked but because Halima's methods and Mofiz's corruption are made from related materials: documentary improvisation, selective mercy, and the use of paper to harden advantage. Halima decides the only remaining leverage is radical exposure. She will stop protecting the ledger as private property and use it publicly.

15. **The Hearing**
    Focus: Halima, with ensemble rupture. Texture: longest, highest-pressure chapter.
    The magistrate arrives. Halima reads from the ledger in front of the whole char, and each entry alters the standing of a household. Kashem learns Rahima is his mother. Jamila's marriage and inheritance are argued over in front of neighbors who already know too much. Nura's own paternity becomes gossip, and Mofiz tries to collapse the proceedings by branding Halima a forger whose book cannot be trusted. Aminul, forced to choose between procedural tidiness and witnessed social fact, backs the household-affidavit route. The magistrate accepts a revised list for most residents but excludes the most disputed cases pending appeal. The island is largely saved. Halima's own household is not.

16. **Excluded Names**
    Focus: Halima with Kashem and Rahima in aftermath. Texture: aftermath, inventory of damage.
    The hearing's victory curdles immediately. Those left off the revised list curse Halima by name. Jamila keeps her household claim but suffers public contamination inside her marriage. Kashem rejects Rahima in fury, then returns when he learns she has collapsed with fever and exhaustion. He gives her a son's recognition too late to help her paperwork and almost too late to help her die. The chapter measures what success cost: compensation secured for many, trust broken with several, and no path by which Halima can emerge innocent in anyone's story, including her own.

17. **One Last Meal**
    Focus: Halima and Nura. Texture: quiet, communal, unsentimental tenderness.
    With evacuation boats already scheduled, the char spends a day dismantling itself. Doors come off hinges. Tin sheets are unhooked. Children play in the skeletons of houses that will not survive another season. A final meal is shared without strategy. Halima and Nura speak plainly about Saleha, about the difference between a father who exists and one who claims, and about whether a household can be chosen after so much coercion. They do not achieve absolution. They do achieve a workable tenderness that makes the ending emotionally credible rather than merely bleak.

18. **House Numbers**
    Focus: Halima. Texture: departure, arrival, transformed baseline.
    The last chapter begins with loading and ends with renaming. Some residents refuse to leave until the river has already bitten away the eastern edge; others board early, clutching compensation slips and cooking pots. Halima must decide what to do with the ledger now that it has served as both weapon and shelter. She leaves the book with Aminul as evidence for pending appeals and carries forward only what remains in memory. At the resettlement colony, new houses are marked by numbers instead of lineage. Even there, within hours, women begin asking Halima who belongs to whom. The novel closes on the recognition that bureaucracy can relocate bodies faster than it can settle kinship.
