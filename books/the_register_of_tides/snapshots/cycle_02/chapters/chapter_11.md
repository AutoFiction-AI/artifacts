# Chapter 11

The mosque courtyard still held the smell of wet foreheads and old jute when Halima came through the side gate. Men were shaking out the prayer mats and rolling them against the wall, slapping dust and damp from the reed weave. The noon sun had shifted far enough that half the yard lay under the mosque's shadow and half stayed hard with light. Women did not usually stand in the middle of that space unless somebody was in labor or dying. Today they stood under the low eaves and along the bamboo fence, saris drawn close under their chins, as if shade itself could count as modesty.

Halima had walked from the ghat without going home first. The mainland dust was still on the hem of her sari. Nura came beside her carrying the blue plastic folder Aminul had stuffed with photocopies and notes. The inside flap was still marked in pencil with the neighboring char's name Halima had finally given her at the lodge. She held the folder flat and high away from her hip as though the papers might bruise. The burn mark on her wrist flashed each time the folder shifted. Aminul followed two steps behind them in his pressed shirt, the collar already gone dark at the neck, his own file gripped away from his body to protect it from sweat. Mofiz was waiting on the low platform by the mosque wall in a white panjabi too clean for the char and sandals that had not touched mud all morning. The dyed edges of his beard had run a little in the humidity, leaving a faint tea-colored stain at his jaw.

The assembly had been his idea, which was one more reason Halima distrusted it. He had sent a boy to the ghat before their boat had even tied up, saying the men needed to hear what the mainland office had offered. The men, as if the women whose names would be pinched, stretched, erased, or dragged into public were only rain on the roof, not the roof itself.

She had nearly refused. Then she had pictured the school wall with the list still wet-pasted on it, names straight and official and wrong, and she had come.

Kashem was already in the yard, broad shoulders filling his cheap checked shirt, laminated card tucked into the breast pocket as if it were a talisman instead of a problem. He kept talking too loudly to the men beside him, laughing after things that were not funny. The chipped edge of his front tooth showed every time his mouth opened. Jamila stood farther back with Majed near the women's side, her sari pinned sharp and even, one hand closed around a packet of folded papers inside a clear polythene sleeve. Majed's nails were black with engine grease that never washed out completely. Sufia leaned near the fence post with her bad leg set sideways and a betel tin in her palm, watching everything the way she watched a ferry unload: not for spectacle, for inventory.

Rahima was not there yet. That did not surprise Halima. The western edge was a longer walk, and Rahima's cracked heels did not cover distance fast.

Mofiz spread one hand, generous already. "Since Halima-dai and Officer-saheb have brought word from the upazila office, let us hear it properly. We have enough panic on this char. What we need now is a path with some dignity left in it."

There were murmurs at that, men liking the word because it sounded as if nothing ugly would be asked of them.

Aminul cleared his throat. He had the careful face he wore when every syllable might later be quoted back at him. "There is a possible route for households whose records don't reconcile in the ordinary way. It isn't an amnesty, and it isn't guaranteed. A magistrate can accept household-occupancy affidavits where blood line, land record, or marriage proof can't be made internally consistent."

Blank looks met the phrase. Halima could almost feel the jargon drop into the courtyard like a metal bowl and sit there.

Nura said, before anyone else could pretend to understand, "He means the roof can count if the papers won't."

Several heads turned to her, annoyed at being helped by a girl. Mofiz did not look at her at all.

Halima stepped forward because if she let Aminul go on, the thing would sound harmless until it bit somebody. "It means this," she said. "If the father on the card is useless, the house may still count. If the land slip belongs to a man who's been in the grave ten years but the people under that roof are alive and have been eating from one pot, that may count. If a widow has been keeping a grandson, or a second wife's daughter, or an old aunt who can't stand without a wall, those people may count if witnesses say so in front of the magistrate."

For one long breath the courtyard only rustled. Women shifted their feet under wet hems. Somebody coughed. A child outside the fence started crying and was hushed.

Then a man Halima knew only as Babul the rice trader's younger brother called out, "May count for what?"

"For recognition as one household," Aminul said. "For compensation purposes. For relocation entitlement. Subject to review."

"Subject to review," Kashem repeated loudly, making the phrase sound like something spitty and rotten in his mouth. A few men laughed.

Mofiz raised a palm toward him. "Brother, let him finish."

But others had already started. Voices rose from different corners of the yard.

"Who gives witness?"

"In front of everybody?"

"Does the magistrate sit with the women too?"

"What if one witness lies?"

"What if three tell the truth and one man gets offended?"

"What if the house itself is disputed?"

The questions came fast, but they all bent the same way. Nobody wanted principle. They wanted to know where the knife would go in.

Aminul answered as carefully as a man stepping across rotten planks. "Live testimony would be required. The claimant, household witnesses, and supporting statements. The magistrate would need to hear enough factual consistency to recognize dependency and occupancy."

Jamila's voice came from the women's side, level enough to quiet two louder men. "Factual consistency about which facts?"

Jamila stayed in the shade. Only the muscles in her jaw moved, once, before she locked them still. A string of green bangles was wrapped in the end of her sari so they would not clink against each other. Halima had seen women muffle glass like that before funerals and before visits to moneylenders.

Mofiz smiled as if he welcomed the clarification. "Yes, let us keep the discussion practical."

Jamila did not look at him. She kept her eyes on Halima. "If a woman has lived in one house for fifteen years under one name, but there was another name before that, which facts do you want in a magistrate's ear? The name she survives under, or the one that can still spoil her daughters?"

The yard shifted around those words. Men who pretended not to know what she meant suddenly became intent on the ground. Women leaned closer.

Majed spoke without raising his voice. He never tried to fill a room he did not trust. "And how long does this route take? We have boats to load, debts to carry, work to find. A clean line moves. A disputed line sits in an office till the paper yellows."

His face gave away almost nothing, but Halima saw the strain in the way he kept one thumb rubbing the ridge of a callus on his other palm, like a man checking a stripped bolt. He was thinking in schedules and repair costs. Fear always reached him through things that could break.

"A clean line for whom?" Nura said.

Halima felt every head turn again. She wanted to tell the girl to let the old ones rot in their own caution for one minute more. She did not. Nura had the folder pressed against her ribs so tightly the plastic bowed.

Majed answered her, courteous because discourtesy to a young woman in front of everyone would look like weakness. "For children who need a roof at the colony. For a household that doesn't want to die standing in a queue."

"And the people who don't fit your clean line?" Nura asked. "They die quietly somewhere more respectable?"

Mofiz cut in before Majed had to answer. "We're not talking about letting anybody die. We're talking about whether this char needs a second humiliation when the first one has already arrived by government order. The officer has brought an option. An option is not a command."

The words dignity and option loosened two men near the platform. Along the fence the women tightened instead. One young wife worried the knot in her scarf until something hard showed under the cloth.

She said, "Silence is also a command if only one kind of household can survive it."

Mofiz turned to her with a patient tilt of the head he used on petitioners too poor to fight back. "Dai-ma, with respect, silence also protects women. It protects daughters. It protects whatever little standing a family has left when the river is already at the door."

Sufia spat red juice neatly into the dust outside the fence. "Standing," she muttered. "That and fifty taka buys tea."

Enough people heard that a dry ripple moved through the yard.

Mofiz pretended not to.

Men near the platform were nodding now. Along the fence, women kept their eyes lowered and counted on their fingers or on the knots in their saris.

"Listen properly," she said. Her voice cut sharper than she intended, and the courtyard answered with quiet. "This route is not clean. It means saying who actually slept where, who paid for whose rice, who kept whose child through fever, who moved into which yard after a husband ran off, who has been eating in a house without a paper right to it. It means saying in front of a magistrate what this char has managed for years with its mouth shut."

The words landed harder than Aminul's. She could see it on faces all across the yard. A young wife with henna still faded at the edges of her palms dropped her gaze so fast it looked like a flinch. An old man squinted at the wall as if hoping the plaster would open and swallow him.

The young wife spoke then, surprising even herself. "My elder girl was married with enough trouble already. If I stand up and start telling who was under my roof before the wedding month counted out, what happens to the younger one?" She was Hasina from the south lane, Halima remembered now, a woman who sewed torn school uniforms at night and always kept a brass thimble knotted into the corner of her scarf so she would not misplace it. The thimble bulged there now like a small hard fruit. "Will the magistrate marry her too?"

Laughter never came. The question had too much bone in it.

Jamila said, "That is not cowardice."

"No," Halima said. "It isn't."

Jamila's shoulders dropped a fraction. The young wife with the hidden thimble lifted her head. A man by the wall opened his mouth, found nothing useful there, and spat into the dust instead.

Aminul adjusted the file under his arm. "You aren't under an obligation to pursue the affidavit route," he said. "But if a household's documentary chain is already unstable, remaining silent does not make the instability disappear. It only leaves the existing record to decide the matter."

"The existing record is what people have," Mofiz said smoothly. "That is not nothing. Let those with clean papers pass cleanly. Let the more complicated cases be handled with care, one by one, not by dragging half the char into a witness pit."

Kashem laughed again, but now there was no play left in it. "Complicated cases," he said. "Hear that? That's me. That's her. That's whoever makes the neat men uncomfortable."

He came out from where he had been standing and slapped the laminated card against his palm. The plastic crack carried. "This card has a father's name. The school list has another spelling. The land people tell me the homestead line doesn't sit right. Everybody's been calling me complicated since the table at the school. What I want to know is this: how many more years am I supposed to stand under a father that works on paper and nowhere else?"

The men around him shifted, some away, some closer. Kashem always liked an audience until the audience grew teeth. Today he seemed to need them anyway.

Mofiz said, still patient, "Brother Kashem, one man's agitation should not force every household to undress in public."

"One man?" Kashem barked. "You think it's one man? Half this char is stitched from somebody else's cloth. You know it. We all know it. You just want the stitches hidden because once people start showing seams, the big houses split too."

The shot struck closer than Kashem maybe meant. Mofiz's expression did not change, but a thickness entered his voice. "Be careful where your mouth goes."

"You first."

Halima saw the line just before it snapped. She had watched that exact narrowing in laboring women when the pain came sideways and took reason with it. She said, "Kashem."

He looked at her so fast it was almost a jerk. Hurt was uglier on a grown man than anger. It made his face boyish in the wrong places. "No, you talk now," he said. "You went to the mainland. You brought back this house route. Fine. Then say it plain. If the blood's wrong, do we say the blood's wrong or not?"

The courtyard had become a throat waiting to swallow whatever came next.

Before Halima could answer, a stir moved along the fence. People made room with the reluctance reserved for someone poor enough to embarrass them by existing. Rahima had come in through the side gap by the ablution tap. Her smoke-dark shawl was thrown over one shoulder, damp with sweat down the spine. She had a length of goat rope looped around her wrist, as if she had tied the animal and walked straight over without going home to change. One sandal strap had rubbed a blister raw, and she had slipped that foot half out so the cracked heel rode the leather edge.

She took her time. People who had already pushed her to the edge of things usually granted her that much.

"Say the house first," she said.

Her voice was not loud, but the yard had strained itself so tight that it carried.

Kashem turned toward her with open irritation. "Nobody asked you first."

"I heard that," Rahima said. "You don't have to ask. Your shouting reached the west side before I did."

A few men snorted despite themselves. Kashem flushed darker.

Rahima went on. "Say the house first. Say who ate there. Say who was kept there. Say who has been sleeping on a mat by the grain tin for six years because there was nowhere else to put him. Say which widow's hands fed a boy when the father's people wanted his labor more than his bones. Say which old woman pisses through the night and still gets turned so her hips don't rot. Blood can come after."

Rahima kept her eyes on the women when she said it, not on Kashem. Sufia's betel-stained hand went still. Kashem shifted as if she had stepped past him and taken the ground with her.

Kashem heard something else. He heard challenge. "Easy for you to say blood comes after," he snapped. "You already know yours."

Rahima's face changed so little that only Halima noticed it: the brief tightening at the mouth, like someone bracing a pot lid against boilover. "Knowing and being allowed to speak are not the same thing."

That quieted him for one beat. Then pride dragged him forward again. "Fine. Then speak. Say it all. If this affidavit circus wants truth, let's have truth. I'm sick of half-stories handed to me like leftover fish."

His use of the full form landed as deliberate, heavy. Halima knew it was not grammar. It was accusation put into shoes.

Rahima looked at him and then past him, to the women at the fence, to the men pretending not to need any of them. "I did not come here to wash one man's face," she said. "I came because if this char chooses the clean list, the poor houses go first. The western edge goes first. The women who took in sisters' children, dead brothers' girls, second-wife sons, old mothers nobody wants on the colony count, we go first. Men with cards will still be men with cards. The rest of us will become weather."

Hasina nodded before she caught herself. Sufia stopped rolling betel leaf and just held it in her stained fingers. Jamila's grip tightened on her document sleeve.

Mofiz said, "Don't twist this into widows becoming weather."

Rahima turned her head toward him. "No. You only propose that we stay grateful while we disappear quietly."

The men nearest the platform stiffened at her tone. For a breath Halima thought one of them would tell Rahima to mind the mosque. Nobody did. Rahima was too old and too poor for easy scolding. To shame her would be to admit she had reached the level of a threat.

Aminul stepped in because he could feel the courtyard slipping past procedure. "Let us be precise," he said. "The affidavit route recognizes lived dependency where documentary proof fails. It's most useful in cases where existing legal documents either exclude actual household members or attach claimants to lines that can't be substantiated. That's the administrative value."

Nura muttered, "Listen to him wrapping a knife in cloth."

Halima almost told her not to be disrespectful. Instead she nearly smiled.

Majed looked from Rahima to Kashem to Halima and then back to the papers in Jamila's hand. He asked the question Halima had been expecting from him all along. "If we choose this route, can a witness story pull down a clean claim that's already standing?"

Jamila cut in before Aminul could answer. "Say it plain. If one house opens, do all the dirty winds start blowing through?"

The sound in the yard went thin. Jamila's polythene sleeve crackled in her fist. A woman by the fence tucked her papers deeper under her arm and took one step away from the women beside her.

Aminul waited a beat too long, and the answer showed its face before he gave it. "A competing statement can invite scrutiny," he said at last. "A magistrate would compare claims. Internal contradiction can affect credibility."

A hiss went through the yard. It was soft, mostly women, and far more frightened than any shout.

Jamila lifted her chin. She kept her voice composed because composure was the blade she trusted. "Then I say this route is a fire. You may call it truth if you like, but it's still a fire. Some of us built our children's rooms out of whatever scrap would hold. If you set flame to every old wall because the magistrate wants a better map, don't call that courage afterward."

The words lodged in Halima's ribs because Jamila was not wrong. She had helped build some of those rooms. She had hammered paper over holes and called it shelter. Now she was standing in a mosque courtyard inviting people to tear the paper back.

Kashem pointed at Jamila with the card still in his hand. "Easy to talk about walls when you already have them."

Jamila looked at him at last, cool enough to cut. "Easy to demand truth when you think it will make you taller."

The men around him made the sound men made when a woman said something too accurate in public.

Kashem stepped once toward her and stopped when Majed moved, not dramatic, just enough. Majed's body said what his mouth did not: not this.

The whole yard looked to Halima again. She hated that old feeling of authority when it came like this, after other people had already bled the room open and wanted her to make the wound lawful.

She thought of the bus-stand lodge, of Nura lying awake beside the wall, of Saleha's name finally said aloud between them after years of being stored like spoiled grain behind the teeth. She thought of how many lives on the char had been made by not saying the whole thing in the whole room. She thought too of the school wall and the notices and the office ink that would not care whether a silence had once been mercy.

When she spoke, she did not try to sound kind.

"Jamila's right about the tearing," she said. "Rahima's right about who gets shoved off first. Don't stand there polishing the clean list for me. It puts one child on the boat and leaves another in the mud, then calls the count settled."

The yard held still.

"If you want the clean list, say it straight," she went on. "Say whose mouth you can leave out. Say which widow can go knock on a brother's door with her plate. Say which girl keeps her marriage talk sweet and loses her rice. Say which boy watches a man board with the papers while he stays on the bank. Then say dignity if you've still got spit for it."

Her clouded eye watered in the heat, and she blinked it clear. "And if you want the house list, say that straight too. Old filth will come up. Girls will hear what was done to keep them fed. Men will have to answer for children they kept at the edge of the mat. Some marriages won't hold. Some talks already half-promised will rot in the mouth. I'm not hawking a clean cure. There isn't one."

She heard the scrape of somebody's sandal against the mosque step. She heard a crow squalling from the tin roof. She heard her own breath, rougher than she liked.

"But I know what counts inside a house," she said. "The roof. The mat. The hand that kept buying rice when the papers wouldn't feed anybody. If the office will hear where a body was kept alive, I'll say the house before I say any father's name."

Mofiz smiled without warmth. "So Halima-dai now asks the char to divide itself in the mosque yard."

Halima did not raise her voice. "The char was divided before I got here. Today you wanted names for the cut."

Kashem made a short, savage sound of agreement. "Then name it. Blood list or house list. Enough of this respectable coughing."

Someone laughed nervously. Someone else repeated it under his breath. Blood list. House list.

Majed said, almost to himself, "Two lists means two fights."

"One fight was already here," Nura said. "Two lists only let people admit which side of it they're standing on."

Mofiz turned to the crowd and lowered his voice until people leaned toward him, as if he were bringing the heat down instead of banking it for later. "This heat is no place for drunken oaths. Go home. Speak inside your households. Those with clean documentary standing should not throw themselves into unnecessary scandal. Those who think they need this other route may consult further. We are not cattle to be sorted by shouting."

Rahima said, "Private is how we got here."

Mofiz spread his hands again. "And public is how we lose whatever little face remains."

The crowd broke then, not with resolution but with noise. Men started talking over one another, all of them suddenly urgent to sound reasonable. Women peeled off in knots, not leaving so much as carrying the argument with them into shade. Kashem stayed in the middle another moment, chest moving hard, then shoved his card back into his pocket and stalked toward the gate without looking at anyone. As he passed Rahima he slowed, not enough to call it stopping.

"If you've got something to say," he said to her, low but not low enough, "say it before the river takes the bank and saves you the trouble."

Rahima looked at him with a fatigue so old it seemed older than either of them. "You still think truth comes to serve you first," she said.

He flinched as if she had slapped him. Then he was gone.

Halima felt Nura move beside her. The girl said nothing. That restraint, more than any outburst, told Halima how tightly she was wound.

Women began approaching in twos and threes, not to make speeches now that the main crowd had started to spill out, but to ask the questions they could not ask in front of husbands or brothers. Hasina came first, twisting the corner of her scarf until the hidden thimble dug a square mark into her thumb.

"If I say my sister's daughter stayed with me those two years," she asked, "will they ask why?"

"Yes," Halima said.

Hasina swallowed. "And if I don't say it?"

"Then the girl doesn't exist for the claim."

Hasina nodded once, hard, and backed away like a person measuring pain before agreeing to it.

Sufia limped over next, her betel tin shut now. "You've made yourself party leader, dai-ma."

"I didn't ask for a party."

"Nobody does. They still get one." Sufia jerked her chin toward the tea stalls by the ghat, though they were too far to see. "By evening they'll be saying blood-list people and house-list people like they invented the words. Men love naming a split after they've spent years feeding it."

She said it almost lightly, but her eyes were not light. They were fixed on the gate Kashem had used, then on the west-side path where Rahima stood alone, adjusting the goat rope on her wrist as though she had come merely to buy salt.

Jamila did not come to Halima. She gathered her sleeve of papers and turned to leave. Majed hesitated, looking once toward Halima and once toward Mofiz, who was already drawing three men from the eastern high ground into a tight courteous circle. Jamila spoke to her husband without turning her head. He went with her.

Aminul exhaled through his nose. "This was not consolidation."

"No," Halima said.

He lowered his voice further. "If witness groups come under open factions, every statement at the hearing will be attacked as camp testimony. That doesn't make the route impossible. It makes it political in a way the magistrate will notice."

She gave him a flat look. "You say that as if it wasn't political before."

"Before, it was administrative theater. Now people will arrive prepared to wound one another with categories I'll have to write down."

Nura gave a hard little laugh. "Welcome to the char."

Aminul accepted that without answering. Sweat had bled a gray map across the back of his shirt. For the first time since the mainland trip he looked his age instead of his job.

By the time Halima and Nura left the courtyard, the argument had outrun the walls. Men clustered in the path under the tamarind stump where no tree stood anymore, talking in low urgent bursts. Two boys repeated blood list, house list as if trying out the rhythm of a rhyme. At the tube-well a woman drawing water stopped mid-pump to ask another, "Will your husband's sister count then?" and the other woman said, "If she counts, my sons eat less," then drove the handle down hard enough to slop water over the pail.

They crossed the lane toward the school wall because Aminul wanted to collect himself before going back to his lodging space in the teachers' room. The posted list still hung there under a film of dust, names in straight official order. Someone had drawn a fingernail down one corner until the pasted paper curled away from the concrete like dead skin.

Nura set the folder on the school veranda ledge and opened it. The penciled char name on the inside flap showed for a moment before she pinned it under the exercise book. "If people are going to come," she said, "they need somewhere to come."

Her granddaughter's split lower lip darkened when she pressed it. She was angry, frightened, overprepared, all at once. On the mainland she had learned enough about her own life to make any public talk of roofs and fathers cut double. Still she pulled out a cheap exercise book and flattened it with both hands.

"Don't do it if you're doing it for me," Halima said.

"I'm not." Nura found the stub of a pencil tucked in the folder seam. "I'm doing it because if Mofiz gets the first line, he'll make this whole thing sound like decent people versus dirty people, and then we're finished."

She drew a line down the page and wrote two headings in her small careful hand: HOUSEHOLD AFFIDAVIT.

On the other side she paused, then wrote: CLEAN PAPERS / NO AFFIDAVIT.

"I hate that second one," she said.

"Write it anyway," Halima said. "People should get to name the lie they want."

Nura shot her a look at the word lie, but she did not argue. Aminul watched with the expression of a man observing something irregular that would nevertheless have to be managed because no cleaner instrument existed.

The first person to step onto the veranda was not who Halima expected. It was old Kuddus Ali's daughter-in-law, Rizia, a narrow woman with a silver nose pin too big for her face and a habit of cracking roasted gram between her molars when she was thinking. She had three small sons and an aunt by marriage who slept in their grain shed during storms because no one had ever bothered to make room for her on the formal household sheet. Rizia stood there chewing nothing now, jaw working from habit alone.

"Put my name on the house side," she said. "If they leave old Amena off, then her medicine comes from where? Air?"

Nura wrote.

The second was Rahima.

She climbed the two broken school steps slowly, as if testing whether they would hold, and stood close enough that Halima could smell goat hair and stale smoke in her shawl. Up close the raw place on her heel looked worse, the skin peeled white at the edge of red.

"You know what they'll say if I stand witness," Rahima said.

"Yes," Halima said.

"They'll say I'm an old hungry woman trying to climb into somebody else's claim."

"Yes."

Rahima nodded, satisfied by the absence of comfort. "Write me there anyway."

Nura's pencil stopped for half a second above the page before she set Rahima's name under the first heading. She did not look at Halima. She did not look at Rahima either. The stillness in her was so complete it was almost visible.

After Rahima came a boy Halima knew by face but not by name, sent by his mother from the west side, asking if a blind grandmother counted. After him came Hasina again, this time without speaking, only tapping the house-affidavit heading once with her forefinger and leaving before anyone could see. After her came no one for several minutes.

Across the schoolyard, near the path to the eastern ground, Mofiz had formed his own small queue without ever calling it one. Men stood with him holding document sleeves and tax receipts, their bodies arranged in a curve of caution around his white clothes. Jamila and Majed were there now. Majed held the papers. Jamila held herself.

Blood list, Halima thought. House list. The phrases would harden by dark.

Aminul said quietly, "If the magistrate asks later who encouraged which route, I will have to answer truthfully."

"Then answer it," Halima said.

"You understand what that means."

She looked past him. Mofiz had turned his little circle inward, yet every few breaths his eyes slid back to Nura's page. Jamila stood in that curve with Majed. Rahima stood at Halima's shoulder. Between the two groups, a strip of trampled schoolyard lay empty.

She put one broad palm flat on the exercise book so the breeze would not lift the page. The cheap paper was warm from the sun and rough under her skin.

"Let them say it," she said.

Down by the ghat, somebody shouted for more tea. A child took up the new words again, turning them into play before the adults could stop him.

"House list."

"Blood list."

Across the yard, one of Mofiz's men laughed. On Halima's side of the veranda, Rahima stood with her bad heel half out of the sandal and did not move. Another woman was already coming up the lane with a folded paper in one hand and a little girl in the other.
