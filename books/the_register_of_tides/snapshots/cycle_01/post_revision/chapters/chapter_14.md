# Chapter 14

Halima stayed on the mat after the last witness left, watching Aminul rub chalk off his fingers onto his trousers. The schoolroom still smelled of old sweat, damp jute, and chalk. All afternoon people had stood in front of the blackboard and practiced saying shame in ways that sounded administrative. A sister who had slept for eleven years in her brother-in-law's house had been taught to call herself a temporary guest. An aunt who had raised three children through two flood seasons had become, on Aminul's page, "not a regular dependent." Halima had sat through it with her bad eye watering from dust and rage, listening to lived years narrowed until they could pass under a magistrate's pen.

Sufia came in near the end with a kettle hooked in one cloth-thickened hand and six glasses trapped between the fingers of the other. She always sorted the glasses by cracks, tapping each rim with her thumbnail before she poured, as if she could hear which one would split under hot tea and which one would last another week. The boys at the ghat called her stingy for that. They did not know what six broken glasses cost a stall that already sold more on promise than on cash.

She poured for Aminul first, because he was the man with files. Then for the two men from the eastern side who had spent an hour deciding whether an old widowed aunt ate enough in their yard to count. Then for Nura, who had been copying names at the edge of the desk with the quick, angry neatness she used when she wanted not to speak. When Sufia reached Halima she tilted the glass wrong and the tea slopped over her own knuckles.

"Your hand's gone blind before your eye has," Halima said.

Sufia sucked tea off the base of her thumb and gave the little dry smile she used when she wanted a sentence to stay neighborly. "It's been a bad day for all body parts. The ferry boys broke one spoon. Your Nura wrote through two nibs. That old Karim uncle farted in God's own face and blamed the bench."

Nura snorted despite herself. Aminul looked up from his papers, not sure whether he was allowed to hear that. Halima kept her eyes on Sufia. The woman had missed her look twice already. That was unlike her. Sufia lived by noticing what other people wished she would miss.

Halima took the glass but did not drink. "Come to the house after evening prayer."

Sufia set the kettle down too hard. The glasses chimed against one another. "House? For what?"

"To answer something."

"Everything needs answering these days." Sufia bent to gather empty saucers that did not need gathering. "I've got a stall to run, Halima-dai. Men still drink tea while their households collapse. Maybe more because of it."

"Then answer here."

That made Sufia look up for a breath, but it was long enough.

The schoolroom had thinned. One of the eastern men was still arguing with Aminul about whether a daughter's marriage counted as household exit if she kept coming back to eat. Nura was stacking used papers and blowing grit off the top sheet. Outside, children were dragging a broken bench across the yard. The sound went through the wall like a saw.

Sufia lowered her voice. "Not in front of chalk and boys."

Halima set the tea untouched on the desk. "Then where?"

Sufia wiped her hands down the front of her sari, leaving two darker lines in the cloth. "When I close the stall. Behind it. After the last boat."

"What am I waiting to hear?"

Sufia gave a short laugh with no air in it. "If I knew how to say it fast, I'd have said it already."

That was when Halima understood the thing had shape. This was not rumor or some boy's theft or some party man's rummaging through other people's bedding. It belonged to someone whose face she knew.

She left Sufia to her kettle and stayed through the end of the rehearsal because leaving early would have started ten new stories. Aminul ran the final household again, trying to get a husband to say his stepdaughter had lived with him since age six instead of age eight because the school record would hold better. The man kept shaving the girl smaller with every sentence. Halima stopped him only when he got down to four.

"She wasn't born in your fishing basket," she said. "Stop cutting her to fit your mouth."

No one answered. Nura's pen stopped. Aminul made a mark in the margin and did not look at Halima.

By dusk the schoolyard had gone copper with dust and low sun. The air was drier now than it had been a week ago, but the char still held its smell of wet silt underneath everything, as if the mud had merely pulled a thin skin over itself and was waiting to be stepped through. Halima walked toward the ghat with the taste of unsipped tea and anger sitting together in her mouth.

Sufia's stall stood where it always did, just above the landing slope, half bamboo, half tin, its front bench listing because one leg had sunk farther into the earth than the others. She had folded an old election leaflet under that leg months ago and kept kicking it back into place whenever customers rocked the bench loose. She did it now without looking, heel finding paper by habit. Her limp was worse when she was tired. She hid it in company by moving faster.

Boatmen were taking their last tea before dark. A boy in a school shirt chewed betel and pretended he was old enough to spit red. Sufia worked the kettle, cut limes, counted coins, and did not once look toward Halima. She filled silence with business.

"The milk turned sour by noon," she said to no one and everyone. "Whoever brought that can must hate my father's soul. You, pay now or drink river water tomorrow. No, not tomorrow, tonight. Your debt already has grandchildren."

The men laughed. Sufia laughed with them. Her hands stayed quick. Only the skin at the base of her throat moved too fast.

Halima waited. She had delivered babies in smaller spaces than that stall, had stood between women's knees while mothers-in-law, goats, rain, and screaming children crowded the same room. Waiting did not tire her. It tired other people. At last the final boat pushed off and the last two men drained their glasses and went up the slope still arguing about rope prices. Sufia rinsed the kettle once, wiped the counter, closed the biscuit tin, and dropped the day's coins into a rusted tobacco box.

"Come then," she said without turning. "If I'm going to ruin my own evening, I might as well get on with it."

She led Halima behind the stall to the narrow strip where Sufia kept firewood under a bent sheet of corrugated tin. A torn fishing net hung there to dry. Beyond it the river took up the dark, flat and wide and never still. Water slapped the moored boats below in small patient blows. Somewhere farther down the bank a child was crying in the thin, affronted way of a child who had not yet understood that nobody was coming quickly.

Sufia lowered herself onto an upturned crate and rubbed once at the bad ankle through her sari. Halima stayed standing. The tin behind them still held the day's heat.

"Say it," Halima said.

Sufia looked at the river first, not at her. "You remember that flood year when the water took the goat sheds from the west side and everybody had to lift trunks onto charpoys? The year you went house to house telling women to sleep with the papers under their blouses if they had to?"

"I remember every year. Pick one."

"The year after Saleha died."

The name hit Halima low and hard, the way an elbow caught by accident in a crowd could hurt more than a planned blow. Sufia saw it land and did not look away this time.

"I copied them," she said.

The river kept slapping the boat hull. A rope strained and eased. Halima heard each sound separately, too clearly.

"What did you copy?"

"You know what."

"Make your mouth do the work."

Sufia swallowed. When she was frightened she usually packed the air with ordinary detail until the fear lost its edges. Halima could hear her trying now.

"The kettle cracked that season. Remember? I was boiling tea in that black pot with no handle. My boy had fever. Mofiz was bringing men from the union office around because of that survey on the eastern side. Everybody was saying land lines would be measured proper this time, not guessed from whoever shouted loudest. You had that fever after the twins. You were talking in your sleep. I sat there and thought if you died, half this char would wake up without names."

She stopped. Halima let the silence sit. Sufia had used the wrong road on purpose, walking around the thing until shame tired her out. Let it.

"I copied selected pages," Sufia said at last. "I couldn't manage the whole ledger. My hand cramped and my spelling was already ugly enough to insult the dead. I took the pages that touched the eastern high ground and the households always arguing over who belonged under which roof. I made copies at night when I helped you sort things. Sometimes when you were bathing. Once while you slept."

The last sentence came out small, though it cut deepest. It named the moment Sufia had crossed the mat while Halima slept within arm's reach.

Halima felt the old labor calluses in her palms as if she were gripping someone's knees. She could see Sufia at nineteen, a thin-armed girl with lamp smoke in her hair, holding hot water while Halima cut cords. She could see Sufia later, broadening, slowing in one leg, still good with frightened mothers and still quicker than most women with a clean cloth. She had let this woman into blood rooms, into the hour when a woman's insides were still partly outside her, into the little stupid jokes women made while waiting for pain. She had not let her into the ledger.

"Why those pages?" Halima asked.

"Because those were the ones Mofiz said would matter if the survey men came back. He said the higher ground would decide who got pushed west and who kept their bamboo. He said if there wasn't a second trace anywhere, one bad leak in your roof and whole households would disappear into mud."

"So you handed him a second trace."

"I handed him what I thought was a backup."

Halima laughed once, harsh enough to startle even herself. "To Mofiz."

"Yes. To Mofiz." Sufia's chin lifted, not in pride but in refusal to beg yet. "He had a steel trunk then. He had dealings with offices. Men listened when he said paper should be kept dry. You had one oilcloth bundle under a roof every gossiping fool on the char could point to."

"I kept it out of men's hands for a reason."

"I know why you kept it." Sufia's voice sharpened for the first time. "Don't talk to me like I found it under a bush. I was there when you wrote half those lines. I held the lamp. I know which father was made from mercy and which from fear and which from the need to keep a girl out of a house that would've eaten her. I know."

She breathed through her nose, once, twice. When she spoke again the sideways humor was gone.

"I also knew one woman was holding too much."

That hit differently. Halima had been ready for accusation, for apology, even for some miserable little confession that Sufia had taken money. This was worse because it had bone in it.

"You think I wanted it?" Halima said. "You think I sat up nights rubbing my hands because the whole char had to come through my door?"

Sufia shook her head. "Wanting isn't the only way to keep power."

The tin at Halima's back gave off stored heat. Sweat slid once down her spine and stopped under the waistband of her petticoat. She did not move.

Sufia went on, and now that she had stepped onto the straight road she stayed there.

"When women came to you, they came because they had to. A husband gone to debt work, a child born too soon after marriage, a girl who needed a father line that wouldn't get her sold, a widow who needed a brother's name to keep rice coming. You helped them. I'm not spitting on that. But it was still your door. Your decision. Your memory. If the book burned, all of it burned with you. That scared me. It scared me more because you acted like fear of that meant a person was weak."

Halima opened her mouth, then shut it. What came first was not a rebuttal but a row of bodies. Women crouched on her floor with wet hems. Men who could not meet her eye until they needed her to make a child belong somewhere. Grandmothers pressing coins into her hand to ask whether a date might move by two months if it saved a daughter. She had called it work because it was work. Blood work. Paper work. Women's after-midnight work done while officials slept. But every one of those people had still sat and waited while she decided what could be altered and what could not bear touch.

She had never taken bribes the way Mofiz did. She had never turned a widow out to enlarge a land strip. She had never made a line for profit. All of that remained true. Truth could sit beside another truth without canceling it. That was part of the misery.

"What else?" she said.

Sufia picked a splinter from the edge of the crate and rolled it between her fingers. "He used them."

Halima had known that was coming. Knowing did not soften it.

"Tell it properly."

"Whenever there was a quarrel over the eastern side, he had those copies in plastic. He never showed the whole page, only enough to feed whichever side needed feeding. This child was born under that roof, so the mother had entered that household before the old man died. This daughter had already married out, so she couldn't split a homestead later. This younger boy had no separate father line worth counting, so he stayed folded into the bigger house. Little lines. You know how much a little line can hold."

Halima knew.

"He started thickening his own people with them," Sufia said. "He didn't always do it by name. He did it by arrangement, by who had been attached to him long enough that he could sound inevitable. A widow here, a nephew there, a tenant turned kin when kin was useful. He made old improvisations sit under his hand like they were law. And because some of those lines came from your mercy, they looked old and respectable, already lived in."

The river smell thickened as the evening cooled, damp metal and fish oil and silt. Halima could almost see the copied pages, not as paper but as the births behind them. A baby rubbed blue to pink while rain hammered tin. A girl child registered under a safer uncle because the real father drank through every wage and had already promised one daughter to a creditor's son. A boy slid into a brother's household on paper because otherwise the dead man's debts would have reached him before his teeth were all in. Those were the kinds of bends she had made. Many had been necessary, though none of them had been clean.

And Mofiz had taken the bends and turned them into walls.

"He couldn't have done that without wanting it already," Halima said.

"No." Sufia looked at her then, direct enough to hurt. "But he couldn't have done it without me either."

That, finally, sounded like confession rather than argument.

Halima thought of the search at her house. The stomp of sandals, the young men's hands in bedding and rice tins, Nura's split lip. She had spent days thinking of theft as a sudden act, something taken in one afternoon by men who smelled blood. Sufia was showing her the slower theft underneath it, the one done years ago in lamplight by a woman with tired hands who thought she was preventing a future disaster and was, in fact, storing one.

"Did he pay you?" Halima asked.

Sufia flinched as if slapped, then recovered enough to answer straight. "Not like that."

"That means he paid you some way."

"He kept my stall where it was when the ghat got shifted. He talked to the ferry boys when they got drunk and started taking tea on credit they'd never settle. He made me feel like I wasn't just passing cups, like I had a place in matters bigger than milk and lime." Her mouth twisted. "There. That's uglier than money. Happy?"

Halima was not happy. The answer, though, made sense in the sour ordinary way human failure usually made sense. Fear had one pocket. Vanity had another. Need lived in both.

"Why tell me now?"

Sufia spat neatly into the mud away from the firewood, red betel darkening to brown almost at once. When she answered, her voice had gone flat.

"Because he's getting ready to use them at the hearing if the room turns against him. He showed me yesterday, not to confess but to frighten me. He had three copied sheets in a plastic sleeve with the corners taped. He said if you started preaching household truth in front of the magistrate, he'd remind the room what kind of truth you wrote with. He'll say your book shifts to suit whoever is in front of you. He'll stain the ledger enough to keep his own pieces useful."

Halima did not speak.

"He thinks because I gave them, I'll stand where he tells me if he needs witness. I won't." Sufia swallowed again. "I came to you instead. Late. Cowardly late. But not that far."

For a moment Halima wanted the easy thing: to call her rotten, call her sold, call her the kind of woman who could stand beside births for years and still miss what mercy cost. The words rose. None of them stayed true long enough to say.

"What did you think a backup was?" Halima asked quietly. "Paper doesn't stay innocent because you make a second copy."

Sufia put both hands flat on her knees. The tea-stall hands. Quick, scarred, strong at the thumb web from lifting kettles and pounding betel. Once those same hands had pressed a slippery newborn against a mother's breast while Halima stitched a tear. Once they had turned a breech baby a little lower when Halima told her to brace. There was nothing villainous about them. That was the worst of it.

"I thought a second copy meant the truth wouldn't drown with one old woman," Sufia said. "I didn't think hard enough about which truth, or whose hands. I thought if I tucked some of it near power, power might protect it. I was stupid. And I was frightened. Both are true."

Halima leaned one shoulder against the tin at last. It burned through the damp cloth of her blouse. Beyond Sufia the black water opened and shut around the anchored boats. Somewhere uphill somebody was pounding chili on stone. Each strike carried clean in the thinning air.

"I used to think hiding it was the clean part," Halima said, mostly to herself.

"Was it?" Sufia asked.

Halima did not answer. She saw her own yard instead: the low stool by the door, women crossing it with wet hems and old newspaper bundles tucked under an arm, babies burning against a shoulder. She had made them come one by one because paper bent differently under a single pair of hands; she needed to hear the breathing, see whose knuckles shook, judge whether a date could move or a father could. Still, one by one meant through her doorway, across her mat, under her eye. She had called that protection, and often it was. It was also a kind of power with no stamp on it except her own say.

She straightened from the tin.

"Who else knows you copied them?"

"No one I told. Maybe people guessed. Mofiz knows. One or two men he showed pieces to over the years, maybe. Not the whole thing."

"Does Nura know?"

Sufia shook her head hard. "No."

"Good."

The word came out harder than Halima intended. Nura's false father line was not some abstract household inconsistency. It was Saleha's death stretched forward through paper and into the girl's own mouth. Once the ledger went public, Nura's name would not stay clean at the edges. Halima knew that. She knew Jamila's would not. Kashem's would not. Rahima's would not. None of the careful silences would.

Sufia watched her thinking and seemed to understand enough not to interrupt.

At last Halima said, "If I keep it hidden now, he chooses which pieces of us get dragged out."

"He'll pick the ugliest bits and make them sound like the whole book."

"If I bring it out myself, he still drags."

"Then make him say it with the full pages in the room," Sufia said. "Not from three corners he's kept in plastic."

"Then there's no protected version left."

Sufia's face changed at that, not into relief but into the expression of a person hearing a sentence she had feared and expected both. "No," she said. "There isn't."

Halima pushed away from the tin. Her knees cracked. "You won't stand for him."

"I won't."

"You'll say what you told me if asked."

Sufia's chin went down, then up. "I will."

"And if you try to turn sideways again-"

"I know what your hand still weighs," Sufia said. There was almost humor in it, and the humor hurt too. "Go. Before I start crying like an idiot and make you hate me less."

"I don't hate you less."

"I didn't ask for less. I said before."

That, at least, sounded like Sufia. Halima left her there among the firewood and torn net and the stale tea smell sunk into bamboo.

The path home took her past the school first. The blackboard was still visible through the open door, a ghost rectangle in the darkening room. On it Aminul had written three witness headings in careful Bangla and rubbed out two of them so often the chalk dust had ground into the paint. Household head. Duration of residence. Supporting relation. All day they had been trying to shove lives under those three headings until people started trimming one another to fit. An aunt removed. A stepchild blurred. A married daughter pushed farther away from the cooking fire that had fed her half her life.

The rehearsals had felt cruel already. Sufia's confession made them look worse: the chalk headings on Aminul's board, the names shaved down to fit beneath them, the aunt moved off the cooking fire by a single word. Halima had thought she was deciding between hidden truth and public harm. That choice was gone now. The harm had already seeped out through copied sheets and plastic sleeves into Mofiz's hands. Keeping the ledger under cloth had not stopped him; it had only let him choose which scraps to wave in other people's faces.

By the time she reached the house, the cooking fire was lit. Nura was squatting by the stove with her dupatta tucked tight at the waist, blowing under the pot ring. The rice water hissed over and ran into the ash. She looked up once, quickly.

"You're late."

Halima set her sandals by the wall. "I had talk."

"With who?"

"With Sufia."

That made Nura stop. Not because Sufia mattered in herself, maybe, but because nobody sought Sufia privately unless news had gone poisonous.

"What now?" Nura asked.

Halima did not answer at once. The house smelled of rice starch, smoke, old paper, and the medicinal oil Nura had been rubbing into her wrist where the old burn tightened in damp weather. Above them, on the loft shelf, the oilcloth bundle sat where it had sat through storms, searches, and nights when Halima had sworn she would rather let it rot than let it rule the house again.

She climbed onto the low stool, reached up, and brought it down.

"What happened?" Nura said.

"Sufia made the copies."

"The missing pages?"

"Years ago."

Nura's face sharpened at once, every line in it going thin. "For Mofiz?"

"At first, she says, for backup. To him, yes."

Nura laughed once, bitter as neem. "A backup in his pocket. That's clever."

"She thought one hidden book was too much in one place."

The sentence had come out before Halima had decided whether to give Sufia that much fairness. Nura heard it anyway.

"Was she wrong?" Nura asked.

Halima laid the bundle on the mat and unwrapped the outer cloth. The ledger inside smelled faintly of mildew, lamp soot, and the old mustard oil from Halima's hands. Alongside it were the loose copied sheets still remaining, their edges softened from years of handling. Gaps showed where others had once sat.

"She was wrong about him," Halima said.

"That wasn't what I asked."

Nura's voice had changed in the last two weeks. It had not deepened, but it had sharpened into something more exact. She had stopped asking for comfort in ways that could be mistaken for obedience. Halima touched the ledger cover with two fingers. The board underneath was warped from damp.

"No," she said after a while. "She wasn't wrong about the weight of it."

Nura let out the breath she'd been holding. The sound carried no triumph or forgiveness, only recognition.

"What did he do with the copies?" she asked.

"Used lines from them to stiffen his own claims. Eastern side households. Land talk. Dependents folded where he wanted them. He plans to use them again if the hearing turns."

Nura sank back onto her heels. The fire snapped behind her. "Then he'll do it anyway. Whether you speak or not."

"He'll do it dirtier if I stay quiet."

"And if you speak, he'll say you forged it all."

"He's already decided what to call me. Silence just gives him the cleaner lie."

Nura looked at the open ledger, then at Halima. In the firelight the burn mark on her wrist shone pale and tight. "If you take that to the hearing, you're taking me too."

There was no theatricality in it. That was what made it hard. Nura was only stating a fact: a girl's father line, already rotten, was about to be handled in public by men with notebooks.

Halima sat down opposite her. The mat pressed the knobs of bamboo through her knees.

"I know."

Nura's mouth moved once as if she might say something cruel and useful. She did not. "Did you decide already," she asked, "or are you still pretending you haven't?"

Halima almost told her not to use that tone. Waste of breath. The girl was right.

"I decided walking home."

Nura nodded once. "Then call Aminul before he finishes cleaning everybody into lies."

Halima looked up sharply.

Nura shrugged, not insolent, just tired. "What? You think I didn't hear today? If one more man says his aunt only eats with him during festivals, I'll feed him his own pen."

A laugh tried to rise in Halima and failed halfway. It left ache behind.

She rewrapped the ledger, not tightly, not for hiding. She left the knot loose and set the bundle by the door instead of on the shelf. Nura watched that more closely than any spoken sentence. On this char, where walls were thin and hands quick and mud got into everything, placement was declaration. The book by the door was not a secret anymore. It was cargo.

Aminul was still at the school when Halima arrived. He had moved one bench under the hanging bulb and spread the witness sheets there in orderly stacks. Even at that hour he held his folder away from his body as if sweat were a form of contamination he could out-discipline. The room was quieter without the day's fear in it, but not kind. Chalk dust had settled into the grain of the desks. A gecko clicked from behind the calendar pinned to the wall. Outside, the river kept speaking to itself in the dark.

Aminul looked up when she came in. His eyes went immediately to the bundle in her hands.

"You brought it out," he said.

"Sit properly," Halima said. "I didn't come here to watch surprise crawl over your face."

He sat, not because she outranked him in any official way but because by now he knew when not to waste motion. "What happened?"

"Sufia copied selected pages years ago and gave them to Mofiz. He used them to strengthen his eastern-side claims. He means to use them again at the hearing if he starts losing ground."

Aminul's expression changed in stages: disbelief first, then calculation, then the tight carefulness he put on when a fact threatened his existing plan. "Are you certain?"

"Certain enough to bring the book here."

"Did she explain how much he has?"

"Enough."

"Can she testify to it?"

"Yes, if she doesn't crawl back into fear before morning."

Aminul rubbed his moustache once. "This complicates-"

Halima set the bundle down on top of his neat stacks hard enough that the pages shifted. "Don't say complicates to me tonight."

He did not finish the sentence.

For a moment none of them spoke: not Aminul, not Halima, not the gecko behind the calendar. The hanging bulb buzzed. Somewhere outside a bicycle bell rang twice and faded.

At last Aminul said, more carefully, "If Mofiz has excerpts, then he can challenge authenticity selectively. If we rely on witness summaries alone, he can use the copies to create doubt around any household you support."

"He already has doubt in his pocket," Halima said. "I'm done letting him spend it alone."

"If you produce the full ledger, he'll attack the whole document."

"Then he can come at the whole book in front of the room," she said. "Not keep cutting us with scraps."

Aminul's gaze stayed on the oilcloth. "And if the whole document is attacked, every altered line in it becomes potential ammunition. Including your own household."

He was not warning her about honor. He was warning her about consequence, which was his better quality. Halima respected that enough not to pretend otherwise.

"I know whose lines are in my house," she said.

He let that sit. "Then what are you asking me to do?"

Halima felt suddenly, vividly, the weight of the book when it was wet. The drag of paper that had taken on river air over years. The way it had grown heavier not because ink weighed much but because lives did. She had spent three decades deciding when that weight should remain under cloth and when it should be brought to light for one person, two people, never the room. That control was gone. Sufia had broken it years ago. Mofiz had been using the broken pieces while Halima still told herself the center held.

"No more rehearsals that trim people smaller," she said. "No more teaching men which old woman to forget and which child to half-claim. Tomorrow the magistrate gets the ledger with the households, the lies, and the reasons buried in them. If Mofiz wants dirt, he can stand in the full field, not pocket handfuls."

Aminul looked at her for a long moment. In the bulb light his shirt collar had finally wilted. He looked less like an official then and more like a tired man who had spent weeks trying to make an institution behave like it had ears.

"That's not a small decision," he said.

"Nothing small is left."

He almost corrected the wording, then thought better of it. "If you read openly, you lose control of what happens next."

Halima looked at the blackboard, at the three headings still ghosting there from the day's work. Household head. Duration of residence. Supporting relation. So clean. So hungry.

"I lost it the day those copied pages left my house," she said. "Tonight I just had to admit it."

Aminul exhaled through his nose. "If we do this, I need to change the order. I need Sufia held near the front if she's willing. I need the magistrate prepared for contested evidence rather than polished witness statements. And I need you to understand that once the ledger enters the record, there's no returning it to private status. Not legally. Not socially."

Halima almost said, It already left private status when men tore through my bedding. But that was only the loud version. The quieter leaving had happened when Sufia bent over copied sheets by lamplight and told herself she was making safety.

"Good," Halima said. "Then it can stop pretending."

Aminul gave one short nod. He was still afraid of the choice. So was she. Fear was not argument. He pulled the top witness sheet from under the bundle, read the first line, and then tore it once down the middle. He did it without theater and without looking at her for approval. The paper had become the wrong kind of paper.

He tore the next one too.

The sound was small but clean.

Halima laid a palm on the ledger. The cover board was rough where damp had raised the grain. Under her hand were shifted dates, borrowed fathers, rescued daughters, protected bastards, hidden mothers, children folded into households that had fed them if not birthed them. Under her hand were also openings for men like Mofiz, because paper did not keep its author's intentions once it entered the world. It only kept lines.

She had known that in pieces. Tonight she knew it whole.

When she left the school, the night had thickened. A strip of moon sat low over the river, too thin to light much. The ghat sounds had gone down to chain clink and water against wood. From somewhere west came a woman calling a child home by the full length of his name. The call crossed the char, struck tin, and came back smaller.

At her door Nura was waiting with the lamp turned low. She looked at Halima's empty hands first, then at her face, and nodded as if she had read the answer there already.

"Tomorrow?" she asked.

"Tomorrow."

Nura stepped aside. Halima went in. The shelf above the bedding stood bare where the bundle had sat for years, a rectangle of cleaner dust marking its old place. The sight of that empty patch hurt her more than she expected. She could still see the oilcloth there, damp at the corners in monsoon, smelling of mildew and her own hands. For years she had looked at that shelf and told herself the hidden bundle was keeping other people safe. The clean dust showed her something meaner: the book had also been keeping its power inside her room.

She lay down without undressing fully. The mat smelled of smoke and old reed. Through the wall the river kept moving its dark body past the char whether anybody's papers survived it or not. Sleep did not come quickly. When it did, it came with no kindness in it: only page edges, wet fingerprints, the sound of a kettle being set down too hard, and the knowledge that by morning she would carry the book into the schoolyard and it would no longer belong to her alone.
