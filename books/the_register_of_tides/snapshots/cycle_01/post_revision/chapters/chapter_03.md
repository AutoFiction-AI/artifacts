# Chapter 3

Halima washed the rice three times in the dented aluminum bowl until the water ran only faintly cloudy and the grains clicked cleanly against the metal. Grit always came up from char rice no matter what the seller promised. She tipped the bowl, let the last of the milky water slide into the yard, and watched it darken the packed earth by the threshold before the mud took it in. Under the tin roof the air had already gone wet and close. The schoolhouse heat from yesterday still sat in her bones as if she had carried its benches home on her back.

Behind her, Nura sat cross-legged with the torn mosquito net stretched over her lap. She had found a spool of blue thread from somewhere and was darning the rip in small mean stitches, the way she did anything that had to hold. Her narrow face stayed bent to the mesh. Every so often she licked the thread, pushed it through, and pulled hard enough to pucker the cloth. The little burn mark on her wrist flashed when the light caught it.

"You're making a knot there," Halima said without turning.

"I'm making it stay shut."

"A mosquito will still find that ridge and laugh at us."

"Then let it laugh. It won't bite through thread."

That was Nura, always ready to argue with an insect if the insect sounded certain of itself. Halima set the rice pot on the stove ring and crouched to feed the flame with a twist of dry jute and a chip of bamboo. Smoke licked her eyes. The clouded left one watered first.

Outside, a goat complained from somebody's yard. A child shouted for a missing sandal. Farther off, from the western side, came the thin metallic striking of someone beating mud off a cooking pot. Morning sounds. House sounds. The island had not yet remembered what the schoolroom had done to it.

Nura held the net up, checked her seam against the light, and said, "Sufia apa said Nazma's baby came out with a full head of hair. Thick as jute fiber."

Halima snorted. "Babies come out looking like old men and frogs. Hair doesn't improve them."

"You say that after you pull them out and hand them over."

"I say it because I've seen enough of them to know. Give the mother three days and she'll call the frog moon-faced and beautiful."

That got a quick breath of laughter from Nura. Not much, but enough to make the room feel inhabited instead of merely occupied. Halima took that as a good sign and reached for the onion. She peeled it with her thumbnail, split it with the small blackened knife, and scraped the pieces into mustard oil with two green chilies and last night's dried fish mashed fine. The smell rose sharp and honest. It was the kind of breakfast that made a house feel poorer and steadier at the same time.

Nura said, "Nazma's mother-in-law already wants to name the boy after her dead brother."

"Then the child is unlucky from the start."

"Nazma wants a film-name."

"Worse."

This time Nura laughed properly. She bent her head as though to hide it, but Halima heard the laugh go loose in her chest and took another small comfort from it. The girl had come back from the school yesterday with her mouth flat and watchful. She had eaten in silence, stacked the plates, and lain down without asking the questions that had been waiting in the rafters since Aminul had started calling fathers aloud in front of the whole char. Halima had been grateful for that silence in the way one is grateful for a storm that has not yet reached the house. She had no right to ask more from the morning than rice, patched netting, talk of somebody else's baby, and the illusion that ordinary work could still outrun what had already started.

She set two tin plates on the floor mat and ladled the rice when it swelled soft enough. Nura folded the mosquito net aside, washed her fingers from the pitcher, and came to eat. She always sat with one knee up when she was thinking about something, ready to get up before the meal had properly ended. Halima saw the posture and knew at once that the quiet had an edge under it.

Still, they ate first.

Nura mixed rice with the fish and onion, blew on the steam, and said, "Lutfa khala's son came by at dawn. The school wall has another paper under the first one."

Halima kept her eyes on her plate. "Every day they'll paste another paper until the wall falls down."

"This one lists what people should bring next."

"Then people will bring half of it and swear the other half washed away."

"Most of it did wash away."

"And the half that didn't, rats have eaten."

Nura took another bite. "You sound cheerful."

"I sound awake."

They finished without hurry. Halima carried the plates to the water jar, rinsed them, and set them upside down on the shelf. Nura shook out the mosquito net and spread it over the rope line that crossed the room. One corner still held the smell of their sleep, salt and damp cloth. Halima reached for the needle to mend a loose hem in her old blouse, and that was when Nura went to the tin trunk under the bed.

She did not make a performance of it. That was what tightened Halima's neck. If the girl had burst into complaint, there would have been something to push back against. Instead Nura opened the trunk, moved aside the folded kameez, lifted the biscuit tin where she kept her good things, and brought out a packet wrapped in clear plastic cut from an oil bottle. The certificates inside had curled a little from humidity but were still clean. Nura laid them on the mat with more care than Halima had ever seen her give a piece of jewelry.

"Eat some more if you're still hungry," Halima said.

"I'm not."

"Then hang the net higher. It'll catch the stove soot where it's hanging."

"Later."

Halima knew that word the instant it came back to her. Nura had used Halima's own delaying voice. She threaded the needle anyway.

Nura sat down beside the packet and flattened the top sheet with her palm. Secondary school certificate. Testimonial. Birth registration copy. Halima recognized them by color before she read a line. Papers had their own skins.

"Shirin madam sent word from the mainland," Nura said. "At Nabinpara they'll open the clinic with trainee posts once the first relocation blocks are settled. Not full nurses yet. Assistant work first. Dressings, vaccine days, keeping the ward book, helping with births if the family welfare people come short. If we go there and I get in early, they can forward my name for the district intake later."

She said it fast, as if the plan had been said over in her head so many times that the words had worn a groove.

"You never told me that," Halima said.

"I tried. You kept saying we'd talk after the school."

Halima put the blouse down. "How many posts?"

"Three at the clinic to start. Maybe four if the NGO women keep their promise." Nura's mouth bent at that, not trusting promises even while she reached for one. "Shirin madam says the first names will go from the relocation list. Girls who can read, write, keep records, and stay on night duty without fainting at blood."

"You don't faint at blood."

"No."

"You don't sleep enough for night duty either."

"I'll sleep when they pay me to."

That was the first place pride entered her voice, bright and quick. Halima saw, suddenly and cleanly, the whole private structure of the dream: Nura in a washed white apron that would never remain white, Nura writing doses in careful letters, Nura earning a wage that came from work instead of pity or marriage, Nura walking through rooms where men had to move because she carried work in her hands. The dream was small enough to carry: an apron, a ward book, money of her own. That made it more dangerous.

Halima said, "The colony isn't built. The clinic isn't open. Promises made before relocation are worth the spit used to say them."

"I know that." Nura tapped the certificates. "That's why I want the papers ready before the people with the real houses arrive and fill the places. If I wait until we're already shoved there with everyone else, they'll say come later, come next month, come after the list closes. Later is for people with fathers and land. I need to go in with the file already clean."

There it was, stripped to timing and paperwork, a plan laid against the grain of the world.

Halima reached for the birth copy because her hand needed something to do. Nura pulled it back first.

"Don't smudge it," she said. "The ink's already thinning at the seal."

"I know what paper does in damp."

"Then you know why I wrapped it."

Halima looked at the packet, not at Nura. "What needs cleaning?"

Nura let out one dry laugh. "You know that too."

She slid the birth copy free and placed it between them. Father's name: Monir Hossain. The letters sat there as steadily as they always had. Ink had no shame. The paper did not blush because the name could not walk into a room.

"For the training file they want the SSC certificate, birth registration, and either father's NID copy or a guardian line that matches across everything," Nura said. "For correction they want the same. I asked twice."

"Use my NID."

"I asked that too."

"And?"

"And they said a grandmother can stand as guardian for residence, stipend, and ration card if the household list allows it. For birth correction they still want the parent line. If the father is dead, they want the death paper. If the father is living, they want his card, or him."

She spoke the last word flatly, like a pot set down.

Halima reached for the needle again though the thread had slipped free. "People say many things at counters to move a line."

"This did not come from a counter. I checked it on the phone form too. Red box. Missing NID. Then mismatch." Nura pointed to the father's line with her thumbnail. "A name isn't enough now. They want the number behind the name, and the number behind that, and the father of that father if they think they can get it. Yesterday at school I watched them do it to everybody."

Yesterday at school. Halima had hoped the room's humiliations had not all lodged so sharply in the girl's mind. But Nura copied forms for half the char. Paper entered her faster than it did most people. She knew where the trapdoors were because she had spent years helping others step around them.

"The training isn't tomorrow," Halima said.

"No. Which is why I'm asking now."

"Relocation hasn't happened."

"No. Which is why I'm asking now."

Heat from the banked stove pressed against Halima's shins. On the mat, the birth copy's damp corners lifted and settled under Nura's hand.

Halima said, "Monir worked off-char. That much you know."

"I know what's written." Nura looked straight at her. "I know men at the ghat say he went with dredging crews. I know nobody here has seen him in years. I know whenever I ask a second question you start shifting pots or finding me a chore."

"A house gives a woman plenty to do."

"Not like that."

Halima stood and took the rice pot off the stove though it had already been moved aside. She set it on the floor, adjusted it, lifted it again, put it nearer the wall. Nura watched all of it.

"If he's alive," Nura said, slower now, "then help me find him. If he's dead, tell me where. If the name is wrong, then say the wrongness properly and let's see what can still be done."

Halima turned. "You think saying a thing properly makes an office kind?"

"No. I think not saying it keeps me exactly here."

The blue thread on the mosquito net had snagged in one place, pulling the mesh into a little puckered mouth. Halima stared at it because it was easier than staring at the girl she had raised. Nura was twenty-two. Old enough to know the cost of questions. Young enough to believe a clear answer might still open a road.

"You've got tutoring today," Halima said.

"At noon."

"Then go wash the lentils."

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Turn me into a chore because you don't want the sentence."

Halima's mouth went hard. "Mind your mouth."

"Mind mine?" Nura leaned forward. Her quick hands flattened over the certificates as if someone might snatch them. "I spent all yesterday listening while that officer asked every man on this island to prove the father above him and the land under him. I came home and thought, fine, ours is bad but at least you're here, at least you know. But you keep making me talk around a ghost as if I'll break if I say the name."

"Lower your voice."

"Why? There's nobody here but us."

"There's always people listening on a char."

"Then let them hear that I need a paper."

Halima's patience tore there, not into shouting but into the dry edge Nura hated most. "A paper. As if that's all. You think I can pull a father out of a trunk the way you pulled those certificates?"

Nura went still. Truly still. That was worse than anger.

"So there's nothing behind him," she said.

Halima heard the mistake she had made and could not take it back. She bent for the plate cloth she did not need and wiped at a clean place on the shelf.

"I didn't say that."

"You didn't have to."

"You're hearing what you came ready to hear."

"Then help me hear something else."

Halima threw the cloth down. "I put you in school. I kept you there when other girls were being dragged out to wash a husband's mother and spread their legs before they knew what a body could cost. That line on the paper did work. Don't stand here and talk as if it was nothing."

Nura's face changed at that. Hurt came first, then anger over it, the way shame always hardened too quickly in her.

"I never said it was nothing," she said. "I said it isn't enough anymore."

Halima looked away. Beyond the door the yard flashed with white heat. Somebody's hen scratched by the water jar. The ordinary world kept moving with obscene steadiness.

When Nura spoke again, her voice had gone lower and rougher. "Did my mother know?"

The question landed like a hand at the throat. Halima did not answer.

"Did she know his name was going on my paper?"

"Leave your mother out of this."

"How? She's in it before I am."

Halima could smell, with horrible clarity, the old sour iron smell of bedding that had held too much blood. It came and went so fast she almost thought the house had imagined it for her. She gripped the edge of the shelf until the wood bit her palm.

"Some things were done because there was no decent choice," she said.

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one I've got."

Nura made a sound in the back of her throat, half laugh and half disgust. "You always have exact answers for everybody else. Rahima's grandson needs an age shifted, you remember it. Majed's sister needs a witness name, you remember it. Half the island came to you before mobile forms and government seals and still you found a way. But for me you keep saying later, maybe, not now."

Each example struck because it was true. Halima had been exact for others. Exact enough to ruin herself. Exact enough to make people line up under yesterday's school veranda and wait for her mouth to place them in the world. She could not tell Nura that this case was harder because it was not a case. It was her daughter rotting backward through every paper.

"For you I'm thinking before I move," Halima said.

"Then think faster."

"Life isn't a form where you hit the next box and it opens."

"No. Life is you deciding for everybody and calling it weather."

That made Halima slap the mat with her open hand. Not Nura. Never Nura. But the sound cracked through the room all the same.

"Enough."

Nura flinched once and hated that she had done it. The hatred showed immediately.

"Fine," she said. "Then I'll ask without you."

"Ask who?"

"The ghat men. The dredging crews. Sufia apa's customers. Whoever remembers where Monir worked last."

"You won't."

"Watch me."

Halima took a step toward her. "You won't stand at tea stalls with men rolling betel in their cheeks and ask after a father they've never respected enough to keep track of. You won't hand your name to every loose mouth between here and the mainland."

"It's already on the paper."

"Paper isn't a body."

"It becomes one when they block a door with it."

Halima almost said more there. She almost said You were a body first, slick and furious in my hands while your mother bit through a cloth and then burned from the inside out. She almost said I used his name because a dead girl leaves a child too naked in this place. She almost said I would have written the river itself as your father if the river could have signed. But chapter and verse rose in her throat and stopped there, too jagged to pass.

What came out instead was, "You think men will tell you the truth because you're holding a school certificate? They'll smell need on you before you finish the question."

"Then come with me."

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because I said no."

Nura stared at her a long time. When she spoke again, the speed had gone. That frightened Halima more than the shouting had.

"You don't have to tell me the whole thing," Nura said. "I can see you won't. But give me one usable thing. An address. A union. Somebody's father's name who knew him. Tell me if the man on this line ever held me once. Tell me if he knew I existed. Tell me whether I'm supposed to wait for somebody who is dead, or vanished, or just written because you needed a blank filled."

Halima's hand slipped on the shelf edge. The tin spoon hanging by the stove knocked once against the wall, and the thin clang stayed in her teeth.

Halima said, very quietly, "Don't talk about blanks to me."

Nura's eyes flashed. "Then don't leave me in one."

Silence after that. It sat between them like a cut mouth holding blood.

Outside, a burst of schoolchildren's recitation traveled from a neighboring yard where somebody had started lessons early: the sing-song climb of multiplication tables. From farther off came the cough and grind of a boat engine turning over at the ghat. Life moved in counts and departures.

Nura gathered the certificates with fingers that had gone careful again. She slid them back into the plastic, then into the biscuit tin, but did not return the tin to the trunk. She kept it beside her knee.

"I need the correction before the relocation list closes," she said without looking up. "If I miss the first intake, they'll give it to somebody from the mainland side or somebody whose uncle already knows a clerk. You know that's how it goes."

Halima did know. A chance did not disappear cleanly. It got taken up by another hand and then spent as if it had always belonged there.

"I'll see what can be traced," she said.

Nura looked up at once. "When?"

"Soon."

Nura's mouth twisted. "That's not a date."

"This week."

"Before the next school day?"

"I said this week."

"That means you're still hoping the week will decide for you."

Halima had no answer that would not sound like more smoke. Nura stood, pushed the biscuit tin under her arm, and crossed to the line where the mosquito net hung. For one second Halima thought she meant to tear it down again. Instead she only pinched the new seam between finger and thumb, testing its hold.

"The knot will keep," Nura said. "Ugly, but it will keep."

Then she took the tin and went out into the yard.

Halima did not follow. She listened to the movement instead: the dipper lifted from the water jar, the splash of a rinse, the scrape of Nura's sandal against the threshold stone. After a minute came the smaller sounds that told more truth than tears would have done. The trunk lid opened outside under the eaves. Nura was rearranging her papers again. Straightening them. Counting the copies. Preparing for a road she had been told not to take yet and might take anyway.

Halima stood in the middle of the room with the needle still in her fingers until the thread cut a damp line into her skin.

By noon the heat had thickened enough to slow even flies. Nura had gone to tutor two girls near the mosque lane, carrying the biscuit tin in her cloth bag instead of leaving it in the house. That told Halima how much trust had been bruised. The house felt wrong without the girl's restlessness in it. Too still. Too much like a room that had been left to an old woman and her objects.

Halima shut the door against the glare and dragged the low stool under the loft shelf. Her back complained before she had one foot up. She ignored it, reached into the dark above the hanging pots, and found the oilcloth bundle by touch. Oil, dust, old paper, the faint medicinal ghost of the herbs she once kept near it to fool casual fingers. She brought it down and sat with it on the mat.

For a while she only held it.

Yesterday at school, people had looked at her as if the ledger inside could still save them. That was their stupidity and their hunger. A book could not turn a river or soften a magistrate. But it could do what it had always done: put a line where life had refused one. Sometimes that had been mercy. Sometimes it had been only a cleaner shape for damage.

She unwrapped the oilcloth.

The notebook's spine had cracked years ago and been sewn back with black thread. Its pages smelled of damp, smoke, and the fat of hands. Halima turned them carefully. Old births. Names that had married, drowned, left, or turned into men who now stood in school verandas demanding proof of themselves. Her own script changed across the years: tighter when Nura was small and she wrote after everyone slept, larger in her middle age when her fingers had thickened, now broken here and there by the drag of the clouded eye.

She found Saleha's page before she found Nura's. She always did.

Saleha, daughter of Halima. Fever after marriage noted in one margin from years before. Then the later entry below, cramped into space that had not been meant for grief. Halima's thumb rested beside the words and made them smaller.

She did not read the whole page at once. She took it as the body had come to her then, in pieces.

Her eye found the date first, the numbers still fixed to that lean wet season.

Her thumb slid down to the labor note and read what her own hand had written: girl child before dawn, cord loop once, loosened by hand.

Then she reached the line she had added later, after the bedding had been stripped and the women had gone home and Saleha's mouth had stopped needing water: Mother dead on seventh day. Child kept in Halima house.

Below that, she saw darker ink from months after, when school had first started to become imaginable instead of absurd:

For papers use Monir Hossain. Dredging line. No return to husband's people.

Halima shut her eyes.

The sentence had looked practical when she wrote it. Practicality was a powerful narcotic. It let a woman believe that if she used a steady hand on the page she had done something cleaner than desperation. Back then Nura had still slept with her fists tucked beside her face, mouth opening and closing for milk after the breast cloth had gone cold. Saleha's in-laws had already started asking what would happen to the child, who would keep her, what claim the dead girl's people imagined they had. There had been one cousin on the mainland whispering about a match for Halima's granddaughter as if the baby were a sack of rice to be promised forward. There had been one man, half-drunk on country liquor, laughing that a girl with no solid father could always be folded into some house later if the price was right. Halima had heard all of it. She had gone home, opened the ledger, and chosen a line that gave the child a father far enough away to be unreachable and respectable enough on paper to get her into class when the time came.

It had worked. That was the filthiest part. It had worked for years.

She turned to Nura's later school note, added in the margin when the first admission form had required consistency. Same name carried forward. Age adjusted by months. Everything neat enough to pass the headmaster's lazy eye. Halima remembered Nura at six, hair oiled flat, furious at the ribbon in it, holding a slate as if the slate had insulted her. She had walked into school because the form said she could.

Now the same line stood up like a bamboo pole across the road.

Monir Hossain. No NID known. No union certain. A man who had once been useful precisely because he could not be fetched. The world had changed around the old trick. Ghost fathers had worked when ink only needed another ink beside it. They failed when a machine demanded numbers and linked fathers to fathers to holdings to voter lists. Even if Halima started today, what would she bring back for correction? A tea-stall rumor? A dredging memory? A dead end on another char? Offices did not amend a girl into a future because an old midwife said please.

The pages blurred. The old cloudy ache had started again in her left eye.

She pressed the heel of her hand against it and sat listening to the roof tick in the heat. From outside came the faint cadence of Nura teaching. The girl was drilling spelling today, not arithmetic.

"Certificate," Nura's voice called from the lane.

Two small voices stumbled after her.

"Guardian."

Again the children echoed, wrong on the first try, then right.

Halima bowed over the book as if somebody had struck her between the shoulders. The child she had protected was out there teaching other girls the very words that were now barring her own way forward.

This week, she had said.

It was not enough. She knew it with the same bodily certainty with which she knew when a labor would turn bad hours before the mother understood. Delay always dressed itself as prudence first. On a char it called itself waiting for the rain to ease, waiting for the ferry to run, waiting for the bank to hold one more night. Then morning came and half the yard was in the river.

She looked again at the line she had written years ago: For papers use Monir Hossain.

Use. As if a man were a borrowed pot. As if the future would never ask for him back.

Halima closed the ledger and kept her palm on the cover. The house was hot, the mat rough under her legs, the smell of dried fish still caught in the air from breakfast. Nothing about the room suggested that a life could close inside it. But Nura's chance had already begun closing by inches: through dates, through lists, through a red box on a phone screen and a missing number no one could conjure by refusing to speak.

By the time the colony clinic called for names, waiting would already have chosen against the girl.
