# Chapter 17

The next morning, Majed levered the first roofing sheet free. Tin screamed against bamboo, and a square of pale sky widened above Halima where she stood in the middle of her front room with a coil of jute rope at her feet. Up and down the char, doors were already coming off hinges and going past on shoulders. Bed-frames leaned against courtyards waiting to become boat cargo or firewood, depending on how much river a family thought it still had left. The house smelled wrong already. Less smoke. More old dust shaken out of wall mats, rat droppings from the loft shelf, the stale sweetness of rice that had lived too long in one corner.

For five weeks the island had talked in the language of lists, fathers, witnesses, signatures. This morning people said nail, rope, pole, lift from that side, don't crack the pot. The school wall still held the revised names and the disputed names, chalk gone fuzzy from fingers and dew, but nobody could pack a house with chalk.

Nura had tied her orna tight at the waist and turned practical to the point of irritation, which was how fear sat on her. She had spread everything into separate zones on the packed floor: bedding rolls, cooking things, documents, clothes, things to leave. Her careful hands moved so fast the little burn mark on her wrist flashed in and out with the morning light.

"Three bedding rolls," she said, not looking up. "Two rice tins. The stove. One water lota. Your birth bag. The trunk. My certificates. The ledger."

Halima's head came around sharply. "Don't say that last thing aloud."

Nura snorted. "You think the whole char hasn't already married us to that notebook?"

"Thinking and hearing aren't the same."

Nura glanced up then, half apology and half annoyance, the expression she had worn since she was twelve and first learned how often adults wanted both obedience and usefulness from her at once. "Fine. The wrapped thing. Happy?"

"No."

That almost made Nura smile. Almost. She bent again to her sorting.

Above them Majed worked like a man repairing, not destroying. Grease always lived in the cuticles of his fingers no matter how much ash soap he used, and he tested each screw against his thumbnail before dropping it into Jamila's waiting bowl below. Jamila had hitched the end of her sari over one shoulder to keep it out of the dirt, and even while standing in rubble she was arranging the salvaged hardware by size, small with small, long with long, as if order might survive the move if she packed it properly enough.

"Catch this edge," Majed called down. "If it bends more, some mainland bastard will charge us double to flatten our own tin."

Jamila looked up without squinting. "He talks like he owns a hardware market. Yesterday he couldn't find his own hammer."

"You hid it."

"I hid it after you left it in the rain."

Their quarrel had no venom in it. Halima heard the shape of their old married bickering under the new strain and felt, for one moment, the small shock of ordinary life continuing by habit.

Outside, children had already claimed the first stripped rooms. Two boys ran in and out of Kashem's half-opened wall frame, batting a ball of wound cloth with a split bamboo lath. A smaller girl had climbed onto a removed door laid flat on the earth and declared it a launch, shrieking instructions at invisible passengers while another child rocked the plank from behind. Adults stepped around them with armloads of roofing and tied bundles. Nobody told them to stop for more than a breath at a time. The empty rooms made echo enough for play.

Halima dragged a low stool under the loft shelf and climbed up with her knees protesting. Birth cloths, the iron scissors, a chipped soap tin, old thread, the oilcloth-wrapped ledger: all of it had lived above her head for years in the strip of shadow that stayed driest through storms. She passed things down one by one. Nura took them without comment, except for the scissors.

"These are coming?" Nura asked.

Halima looked at the blunt silver blades in her hand. The screw at the hinge had rusted dark. She had cut more cords with those scissors than she could count. Children were walking around Kadam Char because of them, children already grown, already gone gray at the temples, children who would never know what their mothers tore on the way to bringing them out.

"Yes," she said. "If they have babies at the colony, they won't wait for permission."

Nura nodded as if that had always been obvious. Under pressure she got more exact, not less. Halima had spent weeks reading the sharpness as anger alone. This morning she saw the fear threaded through it. Nura was trying to build a crossing out of order because nobody had offered her anything sturdier.

At the back of the loft shelf her hand touched cloth that did not belong with the tools. She pulled it forward: a faded green shawl, thin at the fold lines, one corner darkened forever by old turmeric. Saleha's. Halima had wrapped it years ago because the rains had started and because putting it away felt easier than letting it hang where her eye could fall on it every day. Even now, after all the seasons in that loft heat, it held not a smell of the girl herself but camphor, mildew, old house.

Nura looked at the shawl and then away too quickly.

"Keep?" she asked.

Halima folded it once more before answering. "Keep."

She set it on top of the trunk. Nura did not touch it.

By midmorning the front wall was half bare. Sun found places on the floor where sun had not fallen in years. Halima was kneeling to tie cooking pots into a cloth sling when Kashem stepped into the yard carrying Rahima's door plank on one shoulder and cursing softly at the goat tugging behind him. The animal had her rope wound around one of Kashem's ankles and kept trying to eat the smoke-dark fringe of Rahima's old shawl, which he had knotted across its neck as a makeshift lead. He had never been a man who knew how to manage goats. The goat knew it too.

"Move, you spiteful thing." He jerked the rope, then looked up and saw Halima. Some of the noise went out of his face. "Dai-ma. You leaving that front door or taking it?"

The old ease of the title landed crookedly between them. It wasn't repair, but it wasn't nothing either.

"Taking it," Halima said. "It can be a bed-board if the colony gives us damp floor."

Kashem shifted the plank higher on his shoulder. His chipped tooth showed when he grimaced down at the goat. "Then I will stack it by the ghat side. Leave it here and boys will burn it for tea before dawn."

"Do that."

He nodded. The goat planted its feet and yanked again. Kashem muttered, "Your mother sold puffed rice, didn't she? Why is the damn beast acting like it grew up eating cloth?" Then he seemed to hear himself say your mother and went still for half a beat, eyes somewhere past Halima's shoulder.

Nura was the one who answered. "Because it did. Half the char's goats have eaten sari ends."

Kashem let out a breath that might have become a laugh if the week had been a different week. "Then it's better brought up than me." He tugged the rope and moved on.

Halima watched him go, broad back dark with sweat, Rahima's door riding his shoulder now as if it had always belonged there. Some truths arrived too late to become soft, but they still changed where a man's hands went.

Near noon Sufia limped into the yard with an aluminum kettle in one hand and a packet of salt in the other, betel red at the corners of her mouth. She had tied a man's checked lungi over her good sari to spare the hem while she worked, and the knot had slipped lopsided over her hip.

"Drink before it cools," she said. "The water tastes of tin if it sits."

Halima took the cup Sufia poured. The tea was overboiled and too sweet. Sufia made it that way when she was tired.

"Who's still cooking separately today?" Sufia asked. "Idiots, that's who. Everybody's bringing what will rot by tomorrow. We're doing one big pot after asr near the school. Pumpkin, fish, rice, whatever is left. I'm not carrying spoiled vegetables inland just to prove I once owned them."

"Who made you head cook?" Nura asked.

"Nobody. That's why it may succeed."

Nura's mouth twitched. Sufia saw it and pretended not to.

"Come when the fire is up," Sufia said. "Bring your own bowls if you can still find them under this disaster."

After she left, Halima sat on the threshold stump where the front door had been and drank the last of the tea. The room behind her no longer felt like a room. It felt like a place that had already started remembering itself as materials.

Nura came and stood beside her, looking at the split patches of light on the floor. "If we leave the broken winnowing tray, nobody will miss it."

"Use it for kindling."

"I already put it in the leave pile."

"Then the leave pile can burn first."

Nura folded her arms. "You keep pretending there will be space for every useless thing if I stop counting."

Halima set the empty cup down. "Not every thing."

Nura's gaze flicked to the trunk where the green shawl lay. She did not say another word. Instead she bent and hauled the next bundle toward the yard with both hands, as if silence itself were another load to move.

By afternoon the house stood open at the front and one side, its bamboo ribs showing. Children were still playing launch in the missing doorway. Adults still worked around them. The char had stopped sounding like argument and started sounding like salvage.

* * *

By the time the sun bent west the schoolyard smelled of cut pumpkin, fish scales, smoke, and the damp iron stink that came off old tin after a day in heat. Whatever could not travel well had come out of households and into common use. Bruised eggplants. Half sacks of rice too open to save. Small river fish that would sour by tomorrow if left ungutted. A chicken that had stopped laying and a handful of green chilies passed from palm to palm. Men fed the cooking fires with broken cot slats and cracked fence bamboo. Women brought knives, bowls, salt, and the authority to ignore men who tried to advise them.

Nobody called it a last meal. That would have made it ceremonial and sad too early. It was simply stupid to cook ten separate suppers on a day when half the stoves were already tied into bundles.

Jamila stood over the largest pot with her sleeves rolled above the elbow, scraping fish scales from the base of her thumb with a thumbnail between stirs because she could not bear the gritty feeling on her skin. Majed fanned the fire with a torn plastic folder someone had once used for land receipts, taking the same care with flame that he took with engines, as if air could be tuned by wrist angle.

"More water," he said.

Jamila did not turn. "You said that before the rice went in."

"That was before Kashem dropped half a pond in there."

From the other side of the fire Kashem lifted both hands in offended innocence. He had changed into a clean but fraying shirt for evening and still had Rahima's blackened metal bowl tucked under one arm as if he did not trust putting it down anywhere. "If the fish tastes of fish, you can complain to Allah."

"I'm complaining to the cook," Majed said.

"Then complain louder. Her ears are full of your bad advice already."

The line got a low, tired ripple of laughter, not much but enough.

Nura sat on her haunches beside Sufia, slicing chilies thin while Sufia crushed garlic under the flat of a knife. Sufia kept up a muttering side commentary for no audience in particular.

"Mark my words," she said, "inland water is dead water. It sits in ponds and thinks too highly of itself. River water knows how to move."

"You say that now," Jamila replied. "Wait until you see a tap."

"A tap is just a pipe pretending not to be a river."

"Everything off-char sounds fake to you."

"Most things are."

Nura laughed then, sudden and real enough that Halima's head turned before she meant it to. The sound was shorter than it had been before these weeks, more guarded. Still, it was laughter. Halima had not heard it from Nura's mouth without an edge under it in longer than she wanted to count.

Children ate first because they were already circling with bowls and because no adult had strength left to refuse them. The launch-game girl from earlier was now wearing one of her mother's bangles on her upper arm like a warrior band and announcing every second mouthful as if reading a passenger list. Two boys fought over a fish head until Kashem took it from both of them, sucked out one eye himself to their disgust, and handed the head back. That bought him ten full seconds of delighted shrieking.

Halima accepted her bowl from Nura and sat on an overturned trunk near the school wall. The revised list was only a few steps behind her. She did not turn to look. Rice steamed against her fingers. The fish had been fried first, so the little bones snapped clean between teeth. Someone had put enough chili in the pot to wake appetite where grief and work had flattened it. Halima had forgotten, these last days, that hunger could still arrive whole. She ate steadily and then, after a moment, held the bowl out for more. Nura raised her brows but said nothing, only ladled another scoop.

Around them conversation rose and dipped without fastening itself to the hearing. A boatman complained that inland roads would rot a man's knees faster than river work. Jamila said at least roads stayed where they were put. Majed answered that roads fooled people into believing tomorrow would resemble today, which was worse. Sufia announced that if the colony tea tasted weak she would open a stall by the gate and rescue the place herself. A woman from the western edge said she talked about tea more faithfully than she had ever talked about any man.

"Men leave," Sufia said. "Tea returns."

That got the biggest laugh of the evening, including from women who had no liking for Sufia on other days.

Kashem ate standing up at first, goat rope looped around his wrist. The animal had settled under a neem tree and was chewing one of Rahima's old straw mats in narrow patient bites. When Jamila noticed, she clicked her tongue.

"Look at that. It's eating your bedding before the river can."

Kashem glanced over. "Let it. My mother raised a more stubborn creature than any goat and still got no thanks."

The words were rough, tossed as if toward humor, but they landed and stayed a second in the air. Nobody hurried to smooth them. Halima saw Jamila's face change first, the quick private tightening around the mouth that meant a blade had brushed somewhere close. Then Kashem bent and finally sat on the edge of the school veranda, bowl in both hands, his shoulders slacker than she had seen them since the hearing.

Some hurts stopped asking for speeches once they had blood on them.

Nura carried a refill to him without being asked. Kashem looked up, surprised.

"What?" Nura said. "You think grief feeds a man?"

"No," he said, almost smiling. "But people do, if they can't help themselves."

"Eat before it cools."

She turned away before he could answer. Halima watched her go and thought of all the things Nura had learned that no school had put in front of her. It wasn't gentleness exactly. It was something harder to make and harder to fake.

When the bowls were nearly empty and the children had begun to droop against whichever adult knee sat nearest, Jamila lowered herself beside Halima with a grunt, wiping sweat from her neck with the end of her sari. Up close, tiny fish scales still shone silver along her forearm.

"If the allotment officer plays games at Nabinpara," she said quietly, not looking directly at Halima, "there will be floor space with us for a few days. For the girl too. Until your appeal moves."

There was no pity in the offer, which made it easier and worse.

Halima kept her eyes on the fire. "You have your own children and your own trouble."

"Yes. I still have floor."

Nura had come close enough to hear the tail of it. "I can hear, you know."

Jamila's mouth tipped. "Good. Then hear properly. I said floor space, not adoption."

For a second Halima expected the old snap from Nura, the quick bright anger that came whenever anyone tried to place her. Instead Nura stood with the empty serving pot in her hands and considered Jamila as if testing the weight of a real offer.

"I know what you said," she answered. "Thank you."

That was all. She didn't accept, refuse, or perform for anyone. Jamila nodded once and got up again to chase a child away from the embers.

The sky darkened by degrees. Somewhere down by the ghat a hammer kept tapping long after the meal should have ended, each blow carrying thin through the open evening. Families drifted back toward their half-made bundles and half-unmade homes. The schoolyard returned to ash, bowls, and the last smell of fish oil hanging in the air.

Halima rose slower than she used to. Her back had stiffened from the day's lifting, but her belly was warm with rice and heat and something close to relief. It came nowhere near absolution. It was only the fact that people had eaten, joked, cursed the future, and asked for seconds. The char had remembered, for one meal, that it was made of the living and not only of claims.

* * *

By the time they reached home the moon was up and the house looked like a mouth with several teeth missing. The front side lay open to the yard. One roofing sheet had been left over the back sleeping corner for the night, but the rest of the rafters showed black against the sky. Wind moved through too easily. Every sound from nearby houses came clearer than before: a baby coughing, someone tying rope around bamboo, one sharp marital quarrel extinguished before it could fully rise, the far river pushing at the dark.

Nura lit the kerosene lamp and set it on the trunk. In the new openness the light did not gather around them the way it used to. It spread out and thinned. She disliked that at once.

Stress always made her count. She checked the bundles again even though she had tied them herself: bedding, documents, pots, stove, rice, two sets of clothes each, Halima's birth bag, the door plank stacked outside, the wrapped ledger hidden at the bottom of the trunk beneath spare blouses and the green shawl. Her certificates sat inside an oilskin folder she had wrapped twice. She slid her fingertips over the edges, feeling for damp.

Behind her, Halima was folding and unfolding the same cloth without purpose. Nura had seen enough of her to know that for what it was: not indecision about fabric, but speech backing up in the throat.

"Say it," Nura said, still crouched by the trunk.

"I was folding."

"You were killing time."

The lamp hissed softly. Halima sat down on the rolled bedding opposite her. In the half-roofed room her broad palms looked darker, more cut up than usual, dust worked into the lines from the day's labor. The cloud in her left eye caught the lamp and gave nothing back.

She lifted the green shawl from the trunk and laid it across her knees. "This was Saleha's favorite one for one season," she said, using the name plain between them instead of saying your mother or that girl. "It was too hot for it most days. She wore it anyway because she thought the green made her look fairer."

Nura kept still.

Halima rubbed the cloth edge between finger and thumb. "It didn't. She was already dark as wet jute from her father's side. But she liked to argue with mirrors. She used to sit in the doorway with that broken comb and oil her hair till I wanted to slap the vanity out of her. Then she'd grin and ask if I was jealous."

Nura let out a breath she had not meant to. "Was she funny?"

"When it suited her." Halima's mouth moved, not quite smiling. "She stole tamarind before it was ripe and swore sour things made her eyes brighter. She hated washing blood from cloth. She would help me at births and then complain for a full hour after if anything splashed her feet. She laughed too openly with men and too rudely with women who deserved it. I spent half her life warning her and the other half missing the sound when she stopped making it."

The words did not come polished. They came as if Halima had been carrying them loose in her apron for years and finally set them down one by one.

Nura looked at the shawl, at Halima's hands on it. "You should've told me like that before."

"Yes."

That simple answer hit harder than defense would have. Nura shifted and sat back on her heels. Outside someone dragged a tin sheet across earth with a long metallic scrape.

"At the lodge you told me enough to wound me and not enough to use," she said.

"At the lodge I was still trying to hold the walls up."

"They're gone now."

Halima glanced at the missing front side of the house. "Yes."

For a while they listened to the night go on through other people's dismantling. Nura could smell the fish smoke still clinging to her hair from the meal. Her wrists hurt from tying rope. She thought, with a flicker of irritation at herself, that exhaustion made people too ready for mercy. She did not want mercy to do her thinking.

"Tell me straight," she said. "About her. About him. About why you wrote what you wrote."

Halima did not answer at once. When cornered, she usually reached for a task, a correction, some practical side road out of feeling. Nura watched her fingers start to refold the shawl and then stop.

"Saleha married because I let fear do the choosing," Halima said. "There were already too many eyes on her. Men look at a girl with no protection and begin measuring. A husband with a name, a roof, a family line, that can look like safety if you're tired enough and poor enough and stupid enough. I was all three."

Nura heard the flatness in it and knew Halima was closest to truth when she insulted herself instead of arranging a lesson.

"He was no safety," Halima went on. "He emptied her rice tin to pay a lender and slapped the inside of her mouth bloody when she asked what they were meant to eat. When she was carrying you high and heavy, he shoved her into the water stand over one late pot of rice. I picked clay shards out of her heel myself. Your mother kept thinking she could outlast his badness if she stayed useful. Women are taught that like it's scripture."

"Monir?"

Halima's face tightened, not in surprise but in recognition that the name was no longer avoidable. "Monir had a body and debts and a habit of slipping sideways when anyone asked him to stand still. You went and found that out for yourself."

"That's not an answer."

"It's part of one." Halima's voice roughened. "Monir could barely keep his own name standing. By the time you were born, two lenders were already at the lane mouth asking after him, and he was sleeping wherever they weren't. He never came to claim you, never brought lamp oil, never stood still long enough to get his thumb wet with ink and say, that's my girl. Any man can leave seed in a woman and vanish before the rice boils. That costs him nothing. Staying when a child burns with fever, when the jar shows bottom, when a clerk keeps tapping the father's box with his nail, that's the part he never paid."

Nura stared at her. "So he was nothing?"

"I didn't say nothing." Halima looked down at the shawl. "I said not enough."

The line lodged under Nura's ribs because it described too many men she had already seen.

Halima drew in a breath that sounded like work. "After Saleha died, I wrote what would keep your sleeping mat in my room and later your feet inside a school door. I wrote the dead husband's name so no man could stand in my yard counting mouths and asking whose bastard was eating my rice. I put a father's line on your school register so the headmaster stamped the form instead of sliding it back across the desk for everybody to stare at. Later the clerk copied that same line onto your exam papers, same as he'd copy a date or a village name. That's the truth of it. Dirty work. Ink under the nail. But it kept the door from shutting in your face."

Nura's anger stirred, old and familiar. "And you never thought maybe I would want the truth even if it was ugly?"

"I thought one more plain truth would strip you down the way it stripped your mother and every other woman who had to stand there and be measured."

That might have turned them back into the same fight if Halima had dressed it up one inch more. Because she did not, Nura could only look at her and see the tiredness under the statement, the old terror that had built a life and then kept defending it long after defense became another trap.

Still, anger was not gone merely because it had roots.

"You keep talking like there were only two doors," Nura said. "Lie or lose me. Fake father or no future. Maybe there were more."

"Maybe." Halima didn't fight the word. "I didn't see them then. I barely see them now."

Nura laughed once without humor. "That's the first useful thing you've said all week."

Halima accepted the insult like weather.

Wind moved through the missing wall and made the lamp tremble. Nura got up to shield the flame with one hand. When she sat again, Halima had placed the shawl beside the oilskin folder, close but not on top of it, as if refusing to pin one inheritance directly over the other.

"Jamila's offer was real," Halima said after a while.

Nura felt the conversation change shape and disliked that too. "I know."

"At the colony, if they hold my case aside again, if they put me with the disputed people near the edge or in some temporary shed, you don't have to come down into that with me. You could sleep under a verified roof. Keep your papers dry. Wait for the appeal there. You're young enough that people still move for your future if it's phrased right."

So there it was. It wasn't banishment. In a way it was worse: care offered in the form of separation.

Nura set the lamp chimney straighter though it did not need it. "You decided that after one bowl of rice?"

"I decided it while taking my roof apart."

"Convenient."

"Practical."

"For who?"

Halima met her eyes. "For you."

There had been a time, not long ago, when those two words would have driven Nura straight into louder anger. Now the force came differently. It came cold.

"No," she said.

Halima blinked once. "Think before you answer."

"I've been thinking for weeks. You only notice now because you finally asked out loud."

Halima's mouth flattened. "Nura."

"No." The word came cleaner the second time. Nura leaned forward, elbows on knees, speaking fast because if she slowed she might soften it. "I went looking for a man on another char so I could stop feeling like my life had been arranged in rooms I wasn't in. I came back knowing a vanished man is still just a vanished man. Then the hearing turned me into gossip. Now you want to shift me again, but gently this time, under somebody else's good number, for my own good. I'm not a sack of rice. I'm not one more line you can tuck where it fits cleaner."

Halima looked as if the words had struck and also as if she had expected no less. "I'm trying to spare you."

"From what? From being seen with you? Too late. From the disputed list? Also too late. From loving you and being angry with you at the same time?" Nura's voice cracked there and made her furious. She pressed on anyway. "I already live there."

The house was so open now that even her lower voice carried. Somewhere nearby a baby paused between cries, listening or not.

Halima did not answer. Nura saw her swallow and then fail to produce one of the tidy refusals she had spent a lifetime using in place of helplessness.

"Would you rather I went?" Nura asked, quieter now.

Halima's response came without delay, which made Nura believe it. "No."

That one word changed the air more than all the explanations before it.

Halima went on, slower. "No. But wanting hasn't stopped my hands from doing harm. I wanted Saleha breathing under my roof. I still pushed her back toward that house. I wanted you safe. I kept choosing over your head till you couldn't turn without hitting my hand. Those are both true. I know the difference now, only now."

Nura's throat hurt. She picked up the green shawl, more to keep her hands occupied than from sentiment, and felt how thin it had gone at the fold. "Did she know you like I know you?"

Halima gave a short, worn-out laugh. "Worse. She was my own child. She knew every mean trick in my mouth."

"That isn't what I meant."

Halima's expression changed. The bluffness fell off it. "She knew I loved her badly sometimes," she said. "She knew I could pick clay out of her heel and still tell her to go back by dusk because a married daughter with a baby had nowhere to stand without men naming a price. I thought if I kept the dates straight and put the ink in the right box, I could keep men and hunger and the office stamp from putting a foot on our mat. I was wrong. Your mother caught the first blow of that. You caught what was left."

Nura looked down at the shawl in her lap. The cloth was not beautiful. The green had gone dull, one edge hand-hemmed crookedly by someone who had rushed or refused to care about neatness. Saleha had chosen it anyway because she liked how it looked on her. The fact felt small and enormous.

"I'm still angry," Nura said.

"I know."

"I might stay angry in Nabinpara too."

"That seems likely."

In spite of herself, Nura huffed a laugh. Halima's answer had come dry as old wood, which was the closest she got to making room for another person's feeling without trying to run it.

Nura set the shawl carefully on top of the trunk. "I'm going with you."

Halima did not move.

"Hear the whole thing," Nura said. "I'm going with you because I choose it. Not because there's nobody else. Not because I owe you gratitude. Not because the char thinks I belong where you stand. I'm choosing my household with my own mouth this time. If later I leave for training, I'll say that too with my own mouth. But tomorrow I go with you."

The lamp flame leaned and straightened again.

Halima looked at her for a long moment, then nodded once, not like a woman granting permission but like a woman receiving terms she knew she had no right to improve.

"All right," she said.

Nura waited. "That's all?"

"What else do you want, fireworks? I said all right." A beat. "And thank you."

Coming from Halima, the words were ungarnished enough to matter.

Nura exhaled slowly. Some knot in her chest loosened just enough to let breath through. It wasn't forgiveness or even peace. It was something that could be carried.

Halima pushed herself up with one palm on the floor and went to the doorway gap. "If we're one household by your decree," she said, voice turned practical again, "then come help me drag the door plank inside. Dew will swell it if we leave it out."

Nura rolled her eyes, grateful for the task. "You can't leave one sentence alone without tying rope to it."

"That's why we're still alive."

"That's one reason."

They carried the plank together, one end each, angling it through the missing front wall. The wood was heavier than it looked. Halima's grip was steady. Nura adjusted hers rather than letting the weight pull her wrist. They laid it down beside the bedding rolls where it could be reached at dawn.

When the lamp was turned low and the night settled deeper around the half-open house, Nura lay with the oilskin folder under her folded clothes and the green shawl over the trunk so the metal would not sweat by morning. Halima stretched out on the other side of the room, not near enough to touch, not far enough to pretend distance.

Outside, another roof came down somewhere along the char with a long shriek of nails and then a thud. Neither of them spoke. The school wall could keep its disputed mark. At dawn Nura would take one handle of the trunk and Halima the other, and that was the household leaving Kadam Char.
