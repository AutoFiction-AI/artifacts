# Chapter 6

By late afternoon the sky had gone the hard white that came when the monsoon began loosening its grip without offering any coolness in return. Halima crossed the middle path of the char with mud drying in scales on her heels, passing laundry that smelled of half-rinsed river water and the sharp rot of fish laid out too long. Jamila had sent a boy to fetch her with one instruction: before maghrib, and not through the front if anyone was sitting there. That alone told Halima the matter was not one for the school wall or the queue at Aminul's table. On Kadam Char people whispered for only two reasons. Either a girl was in trouble, or paper was.

Jamila's yard held both dried fish and engine grease. Strips of loitta hung from a bamboo pole under netting, silver skin gone dull in the heat. Near the duck coop Majed had spread the opened belly of a small ferry engine over a jute sack, every bolt laid in a line as careful as a prayer mat. He looked up when Halima came through the side gap in the fence, wiped his hands on a rag that only moved the grease around, and gave her the brief nod of a man already ashamed of what he needed to ask.

"Come in," he said. "Before people start circling."

Halima glanced toward the lane. "People already circle. They call it walking."

He almost smiled, then did not. The chipped enamel mug by his foot held kerosene gone black with use. Even his tools looked anxious.

Inside, the room had been cleared in the way households only managed when worry outranked comfort. The sleeping mat was rolled and tied. The children's slates had been stacked under a bench. On the floor, over a faded floral sheet, Jamila had arranged documents in separate islands: marriage paper, tax receipts, a death certificate in a crackling plastic sleeve, a folded photocopy of an identity card, two vaccination cards pressed flat under a steel plate so the corners would stop lifting. She sat with her back straight and her ankles tucked under her, her sari pinned so neatly at the shoulder it looked like a refusal. There was always something disciplined in the way she occupied a room, as if mess, if allowed to touch her, would begin making claims of its own.

The children were missing. That was another sign. Jamila did not send them away unless she expected words that would stick to them.

Halima lowered herself beside the papers. Her knees complained against the hard floor. "Who has died now," she said, "that all this has been spread like a shroud?"

Jamila's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "Nobody has died. That's the difficulty."

Majed came in behind her and stayed by the door rather than sit. A tall man made himself look smaller by habit when he had bad news; Halima had noticed that about him years ago, long before he married Jamila. He kept one hand around the rag, twisting it the way other men twisted prayer beads.

Jamila touched the plastic sleeve with two fingers. "My mother came yesterday."

Halima looked up. "Your real mother?"

Jamila's eyes hardened a shade. "I only have real mothers, Khalamma. One is dead and made of paper. One is alive and hungry."

"Then say the alive one," Halima said. "Paper has ears."

Majed made a rough sound in his throat. "She's come three times in two days. This morning before sunrise. Again after noon. Karim has told her he won't take her into his line at relocation."

Jamila turned a receipt so Halima could read the aunt's name and the holding number beneath it. The plastic had a salt-stiff feel under Halima's fingers. "If I stay where you put me," Jamila said, quiet as if she were discussing the price of lentils, "I remain my aunt's daughter. This house stays tied to her homestead. When compensation comes, it comes through that line. Our children stay clean on the page. If I go back to my mother's line, I can take her with me, or at least stand beside her when they load the boats. But then this claim becomes a lie in the open. The land goes soft under us."

Halima heard, from the yard next door, a hen protesting the hand that had found her legs. Somewhere farther off a hammer struck tin. All over the char people were prying ordinary life apart and pretending it was only carpentry.

She lifted the death certificate. The dead aunt's name sat there in blue ink, formal and stubborn. Halima remembered the woman as a narrow-faced widow with a laugh too sudden for her thin body, a woman who never stopped oiling the hinges on her trunk even after the trunk had been emptied three floods in a row. The claim was not large. A strip of higher eastern ground, a house space, enough legal weight to keep a family from sliding into the category of loose occupants. Enough to matter now.

"And your mother," Halima said. "What line does she have if you do nothing?"

Jamila gave a small shrug that looked more tired than careless. "A widow's name. A western-edge shack that belonged to a man she never married and nursed through fever until he died. Karim says he can't shrink his share for her. His wife says the colony houses aren't courtyards where anybody can sleep at the edge. They said it nicely until they didn't."

Majed came off the doorframe at that. "Don't put it as if this is only Karim's meanness. The colony will be tin boxes in rows. They won't let ten half-attached people stack under one roof the way we do here."

Jamila did not turn toward him. "I know what rows are, Majed. I can see a queue when it's coming."

Halima laid the certificate down and reached for the land-tax receipt bundle. The paper had that old dry softness that came before it broke at the fold. She flattened it with the heel of her palm. "Why now? Why not last month? Why not last year, when there was still time to lie with some room around it?"

Jamila let out a breath through her nose. "Because last year nobody was counting households as if God would be checking against a register. Now my mother is looking at boat lists and house numbers. She says if I don't go back to her, she will be put wherever there is leftover room or no room. She says I know how that ends."

Majed said, too quickly, "And if she does go back, what happens here? This roof gets called stolen. The marriage paper gets pulled crooked. Every bastard at the school table starts asking whether my children were born inside a household that exists or one Halima invented while the ink was wet."

He stopped, as if he had heard his own voice rise and did not trust it. Majed was not a loud man by nature. When volume came to him it sounded less like aggression than like a plank splitting under weight.

Jamila folded her hands in her lap. Her nails were short and clean. She had prepared herself even for this. "That is why she sent for you," Majed said to Halima, quieter now. "Because you know where the first cut was made."

The first cut. Halima almost told him not to speak of it that way. Then she thought of the cord knife in her box, the little curved blade she still rubbed with oil against rust, and kept silent. A cut could save a mother. A cut could also decide which blood got recognized and which got wiped from cloth.

She had not thought of that night in months. The crisis with Nura, Kashem, the schoolroom, the notices on the wall, all of it had dragged older memories out by their ankles, but Jamila's case had stayed under the lid because it had once felt finished. The girl had been moved. The marriage-for-debt had failed. A decent enough husband had been found later. The papers held. Halima had let herself count that one among the usable mercies.

The night itself had smelled of wet jute and old fear. Jamila had been fifteen, maybe not quite, with a braid heavy from cheap coconut oil and henna still fading rust-red from a cousin's wedding. Her mother had brought her after midnight, not by the front path but along the back where the goat pens stood, because the men of the household were drunk on rice liquor and bargaining courage. The story had already reached Halima through two houses before they did: flood loss, borrowed money, interest growing teeth, a widower from downriver willing to cancel part of it if the girl was sent before the next market day. He was old enough to have a married daughter. Someone had said, laughing, that he wanted young hips because his first wife had gone dry and mean. Someone else had asked whether the girl was healthy, as if they were discussing poultry stock.

Jamila had stood in Halima's doorway with both hands pressed between her knees. There was a bruise above one elbow where somebody had tried to steer her toward agreement by force. Her mother had not wept. That was what Halima remembered most clearly. The woman had spoken in a voice scraped flat by terror, saying only, If she stays under their roof they will send her. Take her name away from us. Take it tonight.

The dead aunt had provided the hole in the wall. She had died six days earlier with no living child to carry her small homestead claim. The formal death had not yet traveled beyond the union desk. On paper there was still looseness, enough for Halima to push a girl through if she moved before dawn. She had sat Jamila down by the lamp and made her say the aunt's name until it came without hesitation. She had changed the mother's field, shifted the household line, walked a folded note to a clerk who owed her for two births and his own sister's obstructed labor, and by the time the debt men came sober with relatives in tow, Jamila belonged somewhere else.

Not safely. Never safely. But elsewhere enough to spoil the bargain.

Halima remembered Jamila's mother standing with both palms flat on the floor before leaving, not in gratitude exactly, more in bodily surrender to the fact that a child had just been cut loose from one danger into another nobody could yet see. That woman had paid for the lie too. She had lived thereafter as the mother who could not publicly mother her own daughter without threatening the daughter's shelter.

Jamila was watching Halima now, reading memory across her face the way other women read weather. "You remember," she said.

"I remember you smelling of wedding henna and wanting to be sick."

"I was sick," Jamila said. "I threw up behind your duck shed. You told me to wipe my mouth and learn a new mother before sunrise."

Majed looked from one woman to the other. Whatever pieces of the story he had been told over the years, not all of them had been told in front of him. His shoulders tightened.

Halima said, "If I had not done it, they would have married you off by market day."

Jamila's voice stayed polite. That was always when it cut deepest. "Yes. You didn't pick me out of thin air for sport."

The words landed harder because they refused drama. Halima nodded once. "Then what are you asking from me now? Blessing? Permission? Another trick?"

At that, Majed stepped fully into the room and squatted opposite them, his knees cracking. "If there was another trick, I'd take it," he said. "I wouldn't spit on it. But tell it straight. Is there one?"

There was a time, not even so many years back, when Halima might have found another path by instinct. A dead father's name left floating in one ledger. A cousin willing to call a widow his father's sister for a season. A birth date leaned three months one way or another until shame fit under marriage cloth. Kadam Char had always required looseness. The state had arrived now with laminated cards, cross-checked lists, men who typed a name once and expected it to behave forever. Every old soft place in the records had turned into a snag.

She looked at the papers again and hated how clean they seemed in Jamila's care. "Not one I can stand behind when Aminul starts matching households across the lists," she said. "If you return openly to your mother's line, this claim on the aunt's homestead becomes exposed. If you stay on this line, your mother's case weakens. I can move a lie between two people. I can't make the government forget it has three copies of the same question."

Majed dragged the rag over his palm though the grease there had already turned into a permanent dark under the skin. "Then say it plain. Keeping this line is the safer thing."

"It's the safer thing for this house," Halima said.

"For our children," he shot back. "For the roof. For the engine parts I still owe on. For the money her poultry collector comes for every week. For the fact that Nabinpara won't hand me work because I can speak nicely about fairness." He stopped again, then forced the rest out more quietly. "Do you think I want an old woman pushed aside? I know what gets done to people who arrive with no fixed place. I ferry enough of them. But fear is not greed, Khalamma. If this claim goes, I don't lose pride. I lose the floor."

There it was. Not the fat appetite of a man counting payout before grief, but the naked carpentry of his terror. Majed thought in things that could be repaired, replaced, bolted down. A line on paper that determined whether his children would sleep under their own tin or another man's pity was not abstract to him. Halima saw, as clearly as if he had spilled the contents of his pocket onto the mat, the parts debt waiting in the mainland market, the coming colony with its numbered boxes and unknown men, the way one broken claim could turn a mechanic into a day laborer begging for work beside boys younger than his own nieces. He was not simple enough to be a villain. That made the room worse.

Jamila rubbed one thumb across the seam of her sari border. Only that betrayed her. "And my mother?" she asked. "Where does she sleep inside your floor?"

Majed closed his eyes for a second. "Don't do that."

"What? Ask it where it lives?"

He opened them again and looked at Halima instead of at his wife. "She turns every word into a blade and leaves me to catch it."

Jamila's laugh was so small it hurt to hear. "You think you're catching them? I'm the one they get used on."

Halima had heard married people wound each other in worse rooms than this: in labor huts, in courtyards where wet rice stuck to ankles, in ferries at night when the whole river listened through plank seams. Still, something in Jamila's steadiness unnerved her. A woman who shouted could still be handled by time and exhaustion. A woman who made herself calm before cutting usually had already decided what part of herself she could afford to lose.

"Bring your mother in," Halima said.

Jamila's face changed, not much, just enough to show she had hoped to avoid that. "She's outside," she admitted.

Majed let out one sharp breath. "Of course she's outside."

Halima turned on him. "Did you think this was only a discussion between tidy people and clean documents? Bring her in."

He rose without another word. Through the doorway Halima heard his low murmur, then the scrape of a sandal against the threshold. Jamila's mother entered with her head bent not in submission but to clear the lintel. She was smaller than Halima remembered, or maybe poverty had pared her. The gray in her hair was not evenly spread; it clustered at the temples as if worry had settled there first. In the corner of her sari she had tied a brass key, rubbed bright by years of handling. Halima knew it before the woman sat down. It was the key to the dead aunt's old trunk, the trunk that had lost its lock to rust long ago. Some habits stayed alive longer than the objects they belonged to.

The woman did not come all the way onto the sheet. She sat on the bare floor at the edge of it and tucked one foot beneath her calf. "I heard enough through the wall," she said. Her voice had a dry grain to it. "No point pretending I didn't."

Jamila looked away.

Halima said, "Then speak with your mouth instead of your ear."

The older woman nodded. "I'm not asking to take her roof. If I had a roof anyone would honor, I'd sit under mine and die there. I'm asking not to be sent off as leftover. Karim's wife has already counted plates and sleeping bodies. She has counted me as too many."

Majed shifted his weight. "No one here called you leftover."

She turned her head toward him. "Men don't have to call a thing by its name before they do it."

The room stayed still after that. Outside, a goat knocked its horn against a post three times in a slow irritated rhythm.

Jamila's mother went on. "When my brothers fixed that marriage, I brought her to Halima-dai myself. Don't make me prettier than I was. I didn't fight them with a stick. I didn't run away carrying my daughter like a story heroine. I brought her here because they were measuring debt against her body, and I had nowhere else to put her. I'm asking now for the same reason. I've nowhere else to put myself."

Jamila flinched as if the sentence had physical weight. Halima noticed because Jamila was usually too careful to let the body answer before the face did.

Majed crouched by the door again, but the fight had gone out of his posture. "If she returns to your line," he said, speaking mostly to the floor, "this house comes under question. You know that."

"I know it better than you," the older woman said. She lifted the brass key and let it fall back against her knee. "My sister's land saved my daughter. Now it can save her children. I know the arithmetic. I also know what it's like to stand in a queue where no one wants your name attached to theirs."

Jamila looked at her then, finally. "If you know the arithmetic, why did you come?"

Her mother's mouth tightened. "Because knowing arithmetic is not the same as swallowing it."

Halima felt the old urge rise in her, the one that had built half the trouble now pressing on the char: the urge to reach into the gap between need and rule and stuff something living into it before the gap could close. A cousin. A false dependency. A widow attached by service instead of blood. But every possibility she touched already belonged to another household, another fragile line. To solve this one cleanly she would have had to dirty three others.

She placed both palms on the sheet. "Listen. The deadline list is coming. Once a line hardens there, any correction becomes public trouble. If Jamila goes back openly to her mother's line now, she will have to defend why the aunt's name held her all these years. That means her marriage papers get looked at. The homestead claim gets looked at. The children may get looked at. If Jamila stays on this line, you remain weak unless someone else takes you in and means it, not just for the count. There's no decent road here. There's only choosing which damage you can carry."

Majed said, "I can carry shame. I can't carry the state striking our household off."

His fingers had gone to the rag again, working it so hard the cloth twisted thin as rope. Halima imagined the parts seller on the mainland, the waiting account, the humiliation of turning up to relocation with tools but no recognized place to set them down. Majed was not counting future profit. He was counting the exact size of disaster.

Jamila's mother heard it too. "You think I want your children out under plastic?" she asked. "I've seen enough rain on babies."

Jamila closed her eyes. When she opened them, the calm in her had become something stricter. "Everybody stop talking as if I'm the road between two honest places," she said. "I'm not. I was shifted once because men wanted to settle debt with my body. Then I married under the name that kept me out of that bed. I made a household under it. I've sold eggs under it. I've stood in the market being called Majed's wife under it. My children know which grandmother is dead because I taught them from the story I was given. If I go back now, I don't return to some clean beginning. I tear up the only life that held."

Her mother said nothing.

Jamila kept going, and now the measured sentences began changing angle at the end, exactly where they hurt most. "If I step back into your line this week, I become that girl again for everyone watching. The one who was movable. The one who could be passed from debt to pity to duty depending on what solved a man's problem. I won't do that. Not for Karim's cruelty. Not for the government's list. Not even for you."

Majed looked at her with naked relief, then seemed ashamed of having looked that way in front of the older woman.

Her mother took the shame away from him by refusing to look at him at all. She watched only Jamila. "Then say the rest clean," she said. "Don't wrap it."

Jamila swallowed. The tin roof clicked once as the heat shifted overhead.

"I'll keep the line I'm living under," she said. "I'll defend the aunt's claim. I won't go back in the record."

There was no cry after it, no curse, no wailing fit for neighbors to gather around. The older woman only reached for the brass key at her sari corner and rubbed it between finger and thumb as if she might wear it through by the force of holding on. When she spoke, her voice stayed level.

"All right," she said. "Then I know what I'm carrying."

Majed rose halfway. "Aunty-"

She cut him off with a glance sharp enough to prove how much of Jamila had been her all along. "Don't call me aunty now because it makes your mouth easier."

The words struck him silent.

She got to her feet slowly, using one hand on the wall. Halima noticed the tremor not in her knees but in the hand that pushed against the mud plaster. Hunger did that before age did. The woman had always been slight. Now she had gone down to wire and intention.

Jamila stood too, but did not move forward. Halima could not tell whether that was cruelty or the only discipline keeping her from collapsing into a different answer. The older woman adjusted the end of her sari over her hair, a gesture too ordinary for the size of the room's change.

At the doorway she stopped. "You weren't ungrateful then," she said without turning. "Don't let anybody call you ungrateful now. Just don't ask me to call it mercy."

Then she went out into the yard.

Majed took a step as if to follow, then checked himself. Outside, Halima heard the soft thud of sandals being found and put on, then the gate string lifted, then dropped. No one shouted. Next door the hen had resumed complaining about her fate as if nothing in the world had shifted except the angle of a hand.

Jamila remained standing, her face gone pale beneath its composure. Halima pushed herself up with a grunt and regretted, at once, being old enough that every serious conversation had to end with the body reminding everyone it too had terms.

"For what it's worth," she began, "you chose the household you built. There is no filth in wanting your children housed."

Jamila turned on her so fast the movement made the papers stir.

"Please don't wash me," she said.

Halima stopped.

Jamila did not raise her voice. That made Majed shift back toward the wall as if noise would have been easier to bear. "Don't turn this into blessing because you can no longer turn it into rescue," she said. "You moved me, Khalamma. That was the truth of it. You moved me from one house into another line, and because it worked I grew there. I married there. I buried my aunt by the name you gave me. I bled my children onto that floor." She pointed, not dramatically, just once, at the packed earth beneath the sheet. "Now you want to tell me I've chosen family over blood, or future over duty, or some other clean shape. I've chosen who gets cut. That is all."

Halima felt the words travel through her with a steadier force than accusation. In labor rooms, women sometimes said the truest thing of the day while half-delirious with pain, then remembered none of it after. Jamila would remember this. She had been saving the sentence for years without knowing it.

Majed said, almost pleading, "She didn't mean harm when she did it."

Jamila rounded on him. "And I'm not trying to hurt her now. See how little that helps?"

He had no answer. His gaze dropped to the floor, to the marriage paper, to his own hands. Grease had worked so deep into the cracked lines of his fingers that no amount of scrubbing would bring the nails fully clean. Halima saw in that downward look the whole of his fear again: the parts debt at the market, the coming colony with its numbered boxes and unknown men, the way one broken claim could turn a mechanic into a day laborer begging for work beside boys younger than his own nieces. He was not simple enough to be a villain. That made the room worse.

Jamila lowered herself back to the floor and began, with maddening care, refolding the documents. She slid the land-tax receipt into plastic, smoothed the death certificate, set the marriage paper between two old calendar sheets so the crease would not deepen. A woman preparing evidence. A woman dressing a wound so it would stop leaking in public.

Halima said, quieter now, "I never thought it would come back like this."

Jamila did not look up. "That was your comfort, not mine."

The sentence had no ornament on it. That was why it stayed.

Halima looked around the room, and the room refused every category she had once used to survive her own decisions. The bamboo shelf with schoolbooks wrapped in old newspaper. The tin trunk with one handle mended by Majed out of braided wire. The basket of duck eggs under a net to keep the rats honest. The engine head cooling in the yard. The dead aunt's name holding the walls upright. The living mother outside the fence, reduced by hunger but not by confusion. None of it was false. None of it was clean either.

For years Halima had held on to a private arithmetic: girls rescued from one bed counted as saved even if the route was crooked; boys pulled from one father's debt line counted as protected even if another man's name had to be borrowed; a changed record could still be mercy if the breathing body beneath it got to keep breathing in relative safety. She had not been wrong exactly. Jamila was alive. Jamila's children had a roof. Majed had come to this marriage without beatings in his name. The intervention had built a real household, not a paper trick left flapping in a file.

But the household had not grown in empty air. It had grown where another claim thinned. It had eaten years from a mother who had agreed to disappear so her daughter could stay out of a market bargain. Halima could not look at Jamila now and sort the woman into victim on one side, beneficiary on the other. The categories slid apart in her hands like wet papers.

Majed finally spoke, his voice rough with all the things he was too ashamed to claim openly. "I'll go to Karim tonight," he said. "Or to the cousin near the ghat. Maybe someone will take her proper if I push."

Jamila stacked the papers and tied them with a strip torn from an old lungi. "Go," she said. "Push. Ask. Beg. Threaten. I'm not stopping you."

It was not hope. It was labor assigned after a wound.

Halima moved to the doorway. The air outside had thickened toward evening; cooking smoke drifted low, and somewhere on the western side a child was practicing the same two lines of a school lesson until they sounded like a curse. Jamila's mother was already at the bend in the path, smaller even than before, one hand pressed against the brass key at her hip as if it were a charm against being misplaced. She did not look back.

Behind Halima, Jamila said, "When the school asks, I will stand by the aunt's line."

Halima nodded, though Jamila could not see her face.

"And if later somebody tears it open in public," Jamila went on, "they tear it open. But I won't open it myself to prove I'm still somebody's daughter."

That, more than the decision itself, stripped the last ease out of Halima. Jamila would protect the life the lie had made, not because she mistook it for truth, not because she had forgotten the night of rescue, and not because duty had failed in her. She would protect it because a household built under false paper had become as bodily real as any child Halima had lifted wet and screaming into the world. To ask her to surrender it cleanly would be to ask her to pretend she had only ever been a girl being moved, never a woman who had since made meaning on the spot where she landed.

Halima stepped down into the yard. Majed, still inside, had taken up the rag again and was scrubbing grease from a wrench with a furious patience that would not fix anything but kept his hands from hanging empty. Jamila knelt over the papers, spine straight, binding the lie tighter because that was how a family survived the next week. Outside the fence, the older woman kept walking toward the western edge where the ground softened and the houses thinned.

Halima had once believed that if she cut a girl free quickly enough, the wound would close around the saving. In Jamila's house she saw what she had refused to count: cuts did not stay in the body that first received them. They traveled. They hardened into marriage lines, house posts, school names, resentments, debts, children, and old women with nowhere secure to stand when the boats came. She left the yard knowing that what she had called mercy all these years had grown roots of its own, and roots, once taken, were another way of making casualties.
