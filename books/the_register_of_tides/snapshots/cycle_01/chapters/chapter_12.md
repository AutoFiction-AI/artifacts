# Chapter 12

Nura left before Halima's first cough broke the dark.

The house held that thin hour when even the tin roof seemed to sleep. The lantern had gone out long ago. What stayed was the smell of dried mud from yesterday's sandals, lamp smoke in the cloth hanging by the doorway, and the sour paper smell that never fully left the room now that certificates and copies had been spread, lifted, spread again. Nura stood barefoot by the shelf and packed as if she were going to an exam she had not been told the subject of. She took the folded slip with Monir's name on it, the copy of her secondary-school certificate because she did not trust herself not to need proof of her own existence once she began asking strangers questions, a little money from tutoring tied into the corner of her orna, half a paratha wrapped in yesterday's newspaper, and a pencil. She checked each item twice, then once more, not because the list was long but because fear made her practical before it made her wise.

Halima slept on her side facing the wall, one arm flung out of the sheet, palm open as if she had let go of something in a dream and had not yet realized it was gone. In sleep her face looked older and less defended. The clouded eye sat half-open. Nura had spent enough years beside that face to know that if she waited for permission, she would get either silence or a command to wait until after the magistrate, after the hearing, after this next disaster, after one more thing that needed enduring first. Her whole life had been an after. She took the key from the nail, lifted the door latch slowly so it would not click, and stepped into the gray before dawn without leaving a note.

At the ghat, Shamima was already there, squatting on her heels beside a stack of jute sacks and twisting the loose end of her scarf into a cord around her forefinger. She was Nura's cousin in the elastic char way, older by three or four years, married across one lane and two family arguments, and always in the middle of some repair that was not quite hers. That morning she carried a sewing-machine belt in her cloth bag because her husband's widowed sister on the neighboring char had found a secondhand machine and needed the belt before market day. She had agreed to take Nura because Nura had once filled out a clinic form for her son's chest medicine and because women on chars learned early that one errand was rarely worth a boat fare but two might be.

"If your grandmother asks me, I'm telling her you bullied me," Shamima said when Nura came down the bank.

Her voice was low, but the grin was real. She had one tooth capped in silver that caught what little light there was.

"Tell her you had your own work," Nura said.

"I do have my own work. I don't like mixing it with yours. Yours has fire in it."

The boatman, Mannan, spat into the water and told them to get in or stay home. He was a narrow-hipped man with a knitted skullcap he kept adjusting even when there was no wind, steering with one bare foot while both hands fussed with the engine rope. Nura stepped down carefully. The plank was wet and slimed with old algae. Shamima caught her elbow before she slipped and gave her a look that said she was already regretting this.

The channel between chars was not wide, but it had the authority of something that could still decide to take a person. The engine started on the third pull, coughing diesel over the river's stale-sweet smell. Water hyacinth drifted by in broken mats. Dawn widened, and Kadam Char pulled back into low shapes of tin and bamboo, the school wall a pale smudge, the mosque dome barely catching light. Nura kept her eyes on it until the bend took it away. Only then did she unfold the paper in her hand and look once more at the name she had copied the night before from her birth record. The name on it was Monir, just that, with a father's line that might have been as false as the rest.

Between what Halima had let slip in the bus-stand lodge and what Sufia had muttered later while pouring tea as if she did not care whether Nura heard or not, Nura had gotten three usable things: the name, the neighboring char, and dredger work. That was more than Halima had intended to give. It was enough for a morning's rebellion and maybe enough to kill a fantasy cleanly, which was more mercy than Nura had been offered so far.

Shamima watched the folded slip in Nura's fingers. "You still haven't told me what you'll do if you find him."

"Ask him some questions."

"You're crossing a river for some questions. You could ask questions at home and get shouted at free."

Nura looked at the current where the engine chewed it white. "At home they answer around the thing. I want the thing."

Shamima snorted, then tucked the sewing-machine belt deeper into her bag. "Men are never the thing. They're only the hole after."

It was the kind of sentence Shamima said without posing it as wisdom. She said it while checking whether the newspaper around her belt had gotten wet, the same way she might have said the water would rise after noon. Nura almost smiled. Then she didn't. She touched the small burn mark on her wrist with her thumb and looked ahead.

The neighboring char woke noisier than Kadam Char. By the time their boat nudged into the mud, two boys were dragging a basket of duck feed up the bank, a woman was rinsing blackened tea glasses in a bucket gone gray with old sugar, and someone farther in was beating a rug against a bamboo rail with the rhythm of an argument. The ghat smelled of river slime, green chili frying in old oil, and the ammoniac edge of goat droppings warming under the first sun. A row of tea stalls had been made from tin, tarpaulin, and stubbornness. Men were already standing in lungis with their cups tilted from the saucers, talking about sand rates and fuel as if they had been at it all night.

Shamima jerked her chin toward the second stall. "Ask Momena. She knows who owes what before they know it themselves."

Momena was thick-wristed and quick, maybe forty, maybe older, with a faded orange shawl thrown over one shoulder and the top joint of her left ring finger missing as neatly as if a machine had bitten it off and then lost interest. She kept a carpenter's pencil tucked into the knot of her hair and wrote credit marks on the inside flap of a biscuit carton nailed beside the kettle. When Nura stepped up, Momena did not ask what she wanted. She looked once at Nura's face, once at the careful way she held her folded paper, and pushed a bench with her knee.

"Sit first. If you fall in this heat, I won't carry you."

Nura sat because refusal would have made her look younger.

"I'm looking for someone," she said.

"Everybody is. Tea?"

"No."

"Then you'll need a name that earns the trouble."

Nura unfolded the slip. "Monir. He worked here some years back. With dredger people, I heard."

Momena's hand stopped over the kettle handle, not with surprise but with the quick inventory of a woman deciding which Monir, which debt, which ruin.

"Tall?" she asked. "Not truly tall. Just thin enough to look taller. Hair always wanting oil and never getting it?"

Nura had no answer, and Momena saw that at once.

"You're not from his bed," Momena said.

The bench felt suddenly too narrow. "No."

"Then what are you from?"

Nura forced herself not to lower her voice. "He's on my birth paper."

Momena looked at her a long moment. Her eyes were not soft. They were not cruel either. They were working.

"Paper," she said at last. "All right." She lifted the kettle, poured tea through a cloth strainer into two glasses anyway, and set one in front of Nura. "Drink. You look like you'll faint from pride before hunger gets you. If it's that Monir, go to Noorjahan's place behind the dry pond. Third house with the blue drum outside. He slept there on and off one flood season and left owing me thirteen days of tea, four cigarettes he said he'd pay after wages, and one enamel cup he swore he'd only borrowed. If you want the part of him that stayed put, women kept it. Men only know which boat took him last."

Shamima took the second glass before Momena changed her mind. "Still writing everybody down?" she asked.

"What else would I do? Men think forgetting is a profession."

She said it while marking something on the biscuit carton with the carpenter's pencil. A little boy came up to the stall carrying a hen upside down. Momena wiped his nose with the end of her shawl without breaking her stare from Nura.

"Go before Noorjahan leaves for the fish yard," she said. "And if she's skinning koi already, don't stand in the doorway talking like a police clerk. Sit and ask clean."

The path to Noorjahan's house cut past a pond that had dried to cracked green slime and mosquito skin, then through a strip of low huts where fish were laid on mats to salt. Dogs slept in the shade under bamboo racks. Women stepped around one another with basins on their hips, speaking in that fast half-shared shorthand Nura always heard among people who had too much to do and no time to turn themselves into speeches. One of them pointed with her chin when Shamima asked for Noorjahan, then called after them, "Tell her I kept the chilies inside. Rain's coming later." Another answered from a doorway that the sky said otherwise. They argued without stopping their hands.

Noorjahan's house had a blue plastic water drum outside and a line of school shirts drying from the eaves, all of them patched at the collar. She was sitting on a low wooden stool in the yard with a basin between her knees, scraping silver skin off tiny fish with the back of a spoon. The spoon must once have belonged to a better set. Its handle had flowers worn down to almost nothing. Noorjahan herself was not old, perhaps not even thirty, but hard use had taken the soft guesses off her face. Her hair was braided and coiled tight. One heel pressed the stool rung; the other foot kept time against the earth as she worked. A little girl beside her was trying to braid red thread into a doll made from rolled cloth and muttering instructions to it under her breath.

When Nura said Monir's name, Noorjahan did not drop the spoon or freeze or perform the shock of memory. She scraped one more fish clean, flicked the scales from her fingers, and only then looked up.

"Which Monir?" she asked.

Shamima, who had clearly decided she would rather finish this once than ferry Nura back half-satisfied, answered before Nura could trip over herself. "The dredger one. Thin. Used to drink tea at Momena's and sleep here sometimes."

"Sleep everywhere sometimes," Noorjahan said.

The little girl looked up. "Ma, is this about the man who stole the mug?"

"Go inside, Juthi."

"I'm braiding."

"Take the doll and braid inside."

Juthi scooped up the limp cloth doll and went, but slowly enough to hear more if she could. Noorjahan wiped her fish-slick hands on the edge of her sari and nodded to the overturned bucket by the wall. "Sit. If this is going to be about papers, I don't want you fainting and making extra work."

Nura sat. The bucket flexed under her weight. Heat from the yard had already climbed into the plastic. She could smell fish blood, dish soap, and the cumin someone nearby had just thrown into hot oil.

"I'm from Kadam Char," she said. "His name is on my birth record. I needed to know if he's alive. If he has any papers. If..." She stopped. The sentence had run out into something she did not want to say in front of a stranger.

Noorjahan rescued her from finishing it, not kindly but efficiently. "If he can stand up and call himself your father for the government. That kind of thing."

Nura hated how quickly her face warmed. "Something like that."

Noorjahan leaned back and let out a short breath through her nose. "Then you've spent your boat fare badly."

There was no meanness in it, only the flatness of a verdict long familiar to its speaker.

"He stayed here one wet season," Noorjahan said. "Not every night. He worked near the dredger cut when there was work and vanished when there wasn't. He said he had people upriver. He said he didn't. He said he'd bring sugar after payday. He said a lot. When fever took him, I boiled him coriander water and Momena sent tea on credit. When his wages came late, he borrowed from me, from Momena, from Ruksana next door for kerosene. He left a lungi and one rusted shaving blade and took the money. That's the shape of him I knew."

Ruksana herself appeared in the doorway next door as if summoned by the sound of her name. She was broad across the hips, with a sunflower-printed housecoat under her sari and henna still clinging in orange ghosts to two fingernails. She had a baby balanced on one side, not her own by the look of how carelessly familiar the child's weight sat on her.

"He borrowed from me for kerosene and never returned the bottle," she said. "Say that properly if you're listing him."

Noorjahan clicked her tongue. "See? Women remember the real estate of a man. Bottle, blade, cup, debt."

Ruksana shifted the baby higher and studied Nura. "Who's she to him?"

Nura made herself say it cleanly. "He's on my papers."

Ruksana's face changed, but only around the mouth. The change was not pity or surprise. It was something like recognition of a kind she wished there were less of.

"Come in out of the yard, then," she said. "If you're going to hear ugly things, hear them with water in your hand."

Inside Noorjahan's room it was darker and cooler. The packed floor smelled faintly of damp rice sack. A shelf held a steel plate, two schoolbooks with warped corners, a jar of cumin, and a cracked bottle of cough syrup with no cap. Juthi was on the bed platform, still braiding the doll's red threads. Ruksana set the baby down beside her and poured water from a jug into an aluminum tumbler. Nobody asked Nura if she wanted it. They handed it to her the way women handed one another necessary things.

Noorjahan did not sit this time. She stood by the doorway, one hand on the frame.

"You're not the first girl to come asking after a man's name," she said. "You're only the first one this week."

Shamima barked a laugh before she could stop herself. "Only this week."

"I don't mean him," Noorjahan said. "I mean men. They spread trouble wide. We do the walking."

She nodded toward the house across the lane, where an elderly woman was shaking water from a sari with both arms. "When Monir got fever, that one watched my girl while I went for tablets. When he went missing after taking money, Ruksana told Momena before Momena sent her nephew to ask the dredger boys. When my roof tore in the storm after, Jharna loaned me plastic until my brother came. Nobody asked whose wife I was before helping. They asked if the water was already on my bedding. That's the only useful question most days."

Nura drank the water. It was warm and metallic. She did not know why that simple line undid something in her more than the debt list had. Perhaps because it made Halima smaller without making her lesser. Halima had always seemed to carry the whole logic of women's survival alone inside her body, as if every compromise began and ended in that house. Here the work was spread through cups of tea, watched children, borrowed kerosene, a neighbor knowing which roof leaked first. It was not noble or pretty. It was simply built.

"Did he have papers?" Nura asked.

Noorjahan's mouth bent. "He had a voter slip once. He kept it in his shirt pocket until the shirt mildewed. After that I don't know. When labor men came counting hands for the dredger contractor, he gave his name fast and his father's name faster. Could have been true. Could have been Tuesday."

Ruksana said, "Ask Babul at the pump yard. He was on that cut then. If anyone saw which boat took Monir, Babul did. He sees everything and keeps it because nobody else wants it."

Juthi, from the bed platform, said, "He whistles with his nose when he sleeps."

Noorjahan pointed at her daughter without turning. "That is not useful."

"It is if she's going to find him."

"She's not going to find him."

The words landed in the room with a rough certainty that did not feel theatrical because nobody there had arranged themselves around Nura's feelings before speaking. The child returned to braiding. Ruksana lifted the baby and bounced him once on her hip until he hiccupped quiet. Shamima adjusted her scarf and did not look at Nura.

Nura put the tumbler down carefully. "Did he say where he came from? Anything true?"

Noorjahan scratched at a fish scale stuck to her wrist. "He said once that the river had taken his land. He said once that his brothers had. He said once there was a wife who wouldn't take him back. I never met the land, the brothers, or the wife. What I met was a man who could make a face like a hungry boy and borrow the price of tea with it."

"And you waited for him?" Nura asked before she could stop herself.

It was a rude question. She knew it as soon as it left.

But Noorjahan only shrugged. "For a while. Not because he was worth waiting for. Because when a man says he's coming back with wages and you already spent your own on his fever tablets, you wait long enough to find out whether you're stupid or robbed. Turns out you can be both and still need to cook rice."

Ruksana laughed, open and unashamed. Even Noorjahan smiled for half a second.

"Go to Babul," Noorjahan said. "Then eat something before you fall over. If you go home empty, at least go home standing."

The pump yard lay near the cut where the channel widened and the bank had been hardened with sandbags that were already splitting at the seams. Two engines sat open under a shade roof, each ribbed with hoses, bolts, and rags black with grease. Men moved around them in the detached hurry of workers on someone else's schedule. The dredger itself was farther out, a rusted hulk moored in brown water, its pipe arcing to the bank like a stiff neck.

Babul was easy to pick out. He was short and square, with one ear folded in on itself from some old infection and a pair of cracked eyeglasses he kept pushing back up with the inside of his wrist because both hands were slick with oil. He listened to Shamima's question while tightening a nut with a wrench so worn it no longer had any paint on it.

"Monir," he said, and spat to the side. "Which Monir?"

Shamima gave up first. "The useless one."

Babul barked out a laugh. A younger worker behind him, all elbows and beard fluff, said, "That narrows nothing."

Nura stepped forward. "Thin. Used to work this cut years ago. Stayed on the char behind the dry pond. Maybe went downriver with a dredger crew."

Babul's laugh faded. He squinted at her over the top of his glasses. "You're kin?"

"He's on my papers," Nura said again, and hated the phrase more each time.

Babul wiped his hands on a rag already too dirty to help. "Ah."

That one sound held more understanding than sympathy. He jerked his chin toward an upturned drum. "Sit if you want. Or don't. He worked here some, yes. Not steady. Nobody steady stayed on that cut. Contractor paid advances to men who looked like they had no one to chase after them, then changed crews as soon as the bank moved. Monir took an advance and got on a southbound dredger one season when the sand shifted. Might have gone as far as Sirajganj side. Might have jumped off at the next market. Might have been thrown off for drinking. There was talk of an accident near a propeller, but there is always talk. River work eats men and rumor spits out bones."

The younger worker, whose name turned out to be Selim when somebody shouted for him, leaned against the engine frame. He had rolled his lungi up to one thigh and was using a blade to scrape mud from under his toenails with complete concentration.

"He had a blue bag," Selim said. "Small one. That's all I remember."

"Everybody had a blue bag," Babul said.

"Then he had one too."

"Did he send money back anywhere?" Nura asked.

Babul gave her the kind of look older men gave when they thought a question had been asked from too far inside paper. "Back where?"

She had no answer. The word itself shamed her. Back required an origin. A place that claimed a person even while he was gone. Everything she had come for was tied up in that missing word.

Selim scratched one last line free of mud and flicked it away. "Men like that sent money only if a woman stood at the ghat the same day wages landed. Even then half of it turned into tea and cards before the boat left."

Babul said, "If you're needing him for some government thing, don't. Even alive, he'd be no use. Those floating crews change names the way they change landing points. Papers get wet. Cards go missing. Half the fathers in those registers could be one man wearing three stories."

The sentence should have angered her more. Instead it settled into her like the hard edge of an object finally found under muddy water. Even alive, he'd be no use.

That was what she had crossed for. It was not romance or reunion. It was use.

She felt suddenly embarrassed by the careful fold of her certificate copy in her bag, by the neatness of her handwriting around the name Monir, by the private little corner of hope she had protected from Halima's contempt and her own intelligence. She had thought perhaps she could arrive, ask the right questions, and walk back onto Kadam Char with something solid: a workplace, an ID number, a witness, a man gone old enough to be sorry. Instead she had found thirteen days of tea, a kerosene bottle, fever water, a lungi, a blue bag that might not have been his, and a southbound boat that could have taken anybody.

"There was a woman before Noorjahan too," Selim said, looking not at Nura but out toward the dredger pipe. "At another landing. I don't remember her name."

"Then don't start a useless story," Babul said.

"It's not useless. It means he repeated."

Babul looked back at Nura. "That part matters. Not because it tells you who he was. Because it tells you what he wasn't. He wasn't a man whose name could hold a roof up."

There it was again, not pretty enough to be wisdom, too exact to ignore.

Shamima thanked them because Nura had gone quiet. On the way back from the yard she bought two guavas from a boy under a tarp and cut them with her thumbnail, salting the slices from a twist of newspaper she kept in her bag.

"Eat," she said, handing one to Nura.

"I'm not hungry."

"That's not what I asked."

Nura bit into the guava because speaking would have taken more effort. It was hard and almost sour. Salt stung the split place inside her lip where old tenderness still lived from the day men had shoved her in Halima's doorway. The sky had brightened to the bleached white that came before noon heat turned mean. Nearby, Momena's stall clattered with cups. Across the lane, Noorjahan had already gone back to her fish basin. Life had sealed itself over the conversation without pretending it had not happened. Nura respected that more than comfort would have deserved.

Before they headed to the ghat, Ruksana came hurrying after them with a twist of puffed rice in a page torn from a school copybook. "For the ride," she said, pressing it into Nura's hand. The baby was asleep on her shoulder now, drooling onto the printed sunflowers of her housecoat. "You'll get a headache on empty stomach. And don't go back asking old people for fairy tales. Ask what they wrote and who will swear to it."

Nura stared at her.

Ruksana shrugged, suddenly almost shy under the force of her own bluntness. "What? You think you're the first girl to learn that a man can be both fact and waste?"

She turned away before Nura could answer, calling to Juthi to come inside before the crows got the fish eyes. Shamima watched her go and said, "Well. That saved us one speech."

The return boat left later than Mannan had promised because Mannan had found an extra passenger with two sacks of duck feed and was not a man who respected anybody's urgency except his own. By the time they pushed off, the sun sat overhead like a hand holding the whole river down. The channel looked flatter now, crueler for being so bright. Diesel fumes thickened in the boat well. One duck-feed sack leaked a sweet dusty smell that made Nura think of school storerooms and damp grain.

Shamima sat with her knees up and the sewing-machine belt looped around her wrist like a black bracelet. She waited until the boat cleared the bank before speaking.

"You got what you came for?"

Nura looked at the water shredding off the bow. "I got enough."

"That's not the same thing."

"No."

Shamima let the answer sit. She knew when not to nag a wound. After a while she said, "You know your grandmother will say she was trying to save you from this."

Nura laughed once, and it came out ugly. "From what? Boat fare? Heat? Hearing that men lie in several districts at once?"

"From wanting a clean answer where there isn't one."

Nura unfolded the slip with Monir's name and smoothed it on her thigh. Sweat had dampened the edges. The pencil line was still dark. That, more than anything, enraged her. The name held better than the man ever had. Paper survived by being carried carefully, dried, recopied, hidden from rain. Men like Monir survived by stepping out of one landing and onto another until nobody could prove where they belonged. Halima had spent years teaching paper to do the work people failed to do. Maybe that was mercy. Maybe it was another way of cornering a life before the person inside it could choose.

She thought of Noorjahan's doorway, Ruksana's water tumbler, Momena's biscuit carton covered in credit marks. None of those women had waited for authority before building a way to last. They had not been freer than Halima. They had simply been distributed. The work of keeping one another legible was spread across neighbors, cups, children, borrowed plastic, a memory for debt. Nura had grown up inside Halima's house believing the old woman's ledger was a singular dangerous thing. Maybe it was dangerous because it was singular. Too much of one person's choosing pressed onto too many other lives.

The boat slammed down from a chop of current and spray hit her calves. Mannan cursed the engine, then patted the skullcap back into place as if decorum could steady the river. The extra passenger fell asleep sitting up with his cheek against the duck-feed sack. Shamima chewed guava seeds and watched Nura from the corner of her eye.

"So what now?" she asked.

Nura folded the slip once, then again. She did not tear it. That surprised her.

"Now I stop asking for a father," she said.

Shamima raised her brows. "That easy?"

"No. Not easy. Just useless."

She said it and felt the truth of it scrape through her. There would still be nights when she would want a face to put under the name, a body to blame, some man she could hate without having to divide the hatred among the dead, the missing, and the women who had chosen for her. There would still be the childish stab of seeing other people produce fathers like proof of purchase. But a useless want was still useless. She had seen the scale of Monir now: tea debts, borrowed kerosene, and a blue bag maybe. That was all the factual fatherhood on offer.

What remained was not cleaner. It was only hers to ask for.

She did not need Halima to tell her who he was. She needed Halima to tell her what she had written, who could witness it, and what kind of lie would still let Nura get on with her life.

The river widened. Kadam Char came back into view in pieces: a run of tin roofs, the school wall with its pasted notices lifting at the corners, the mosque courtyard gone pale in noon glare, children no bigger than fingers on the bank. It was home only if that word still meant the place where a person had to argue for room. Nura felt no relief at the sight of it. She felt only readiness, and under that a harder anger than the one that had sent her out in the dark. Before, she had been angry because Halima had denied her choice. Now she was angry because Halima had made the choice first and then kept the method hidden, as if gratitude should cover the theft.

Mannan nosed the boat into the mud. Shamima stood, slung her bag over one shoulder, and said, "If she starts shouting, come get me after. Not during. I don't fight old women before lunch."

For the first time that day, Nura smiled properly.

"Coward."

"Practitioner," Shamima said, stepping off.

Nura followed her into the sucking mud at the bank. The puffed rice crackled in the copybook paper under her arm. The folded slip with Monir's name sat hot inside her palm. By the time she had climbed the path toward the house, the question that had brought her across the channel was already dead. The one she carried back was harder and would not wait: not where is Monir, but what did Halima put into my name, and what will it take to make a life stand on it without asking any vanished man to claim me.
