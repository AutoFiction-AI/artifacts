# Chapter 5

By the time Halima reached the school wall, Aminul had already driven two rusted tacks through the new notice and was flattening its corners with both palms so the wind would not peel it loose. The sheet was official-white, thick enough to survive one rain if God was inattentive, and the Bengali on it came in fat government lines that made even the people who could read lean in suspiciously. Behind Halima, sandals sucked at the schoolyard mud. Ahead of her, the concrete wall sweated heat left over from morning. The storm had not arrived yet, but the air had closed up in the way it did before bad weather, as if the char were holding its breath and already regretting it.

Nura stood to one side of the crowd with her hair pinned back anyhow, though the damp kept pulling thin curls loose around her temples. She was reading silently before Aminul spoke, her lips moving over the lines faster than the others could track them. Mofiz had stationed himself near the doorway in a fresh white panjabi, beard edges dyed too evenly for a man who claimed not to care about appearances. He kept one hand lifted in a patient-chairman way, as if the notice were his idea and the fear on the faces below it a small inconvenience he would manage for them.

Aminul cleared his throat. He did not shout. He never shouted unless the launch engine forced him to. He held a folder away from his shirt, careful even now about sweat, and said, "Please listen once before you start asking. Every household has until Friday noon to present whatever supporting papers it has. National identity cards, land-tax receipts, birth entries, school certificates, ration cards if that's all you've got. If a husband and wife are claiming one household, bring the marriage paper. If the paper is missing, bring two adult witnesses who can stand for the marriage and the household together. Names must agree across the file as far as possible. If you wait past the deadline, the first list may become the last list for anyone missing from it."

The yard broke at once.

"What if the card's with my brother on the mainland?"

"My husband never made an NID, what then?"

"The kazi died."

"The kazi ran off with the second wife."

"My marriage paper went in the flood three years ago."

Aminul raised his hand, then lowered it when he saw the hand did nothing. "Bring what exists," he said. "Bring witnesses who can be defended. Do not wait for the last day."

Mofiz turned half toward the women at the back. "Why's everyone panicking? We're not strangers to paperwork. Bring your files to me, and I'll see what can be arranged in order."

Halima felt the yard shift at that, bodies angling not toward the notice but toward whoever looked likeliest to own a way around it. She had seen that movement in labor rooms too, when the pain stopped being bearable and every woman searched the faces around her for the one person who might still know a trick.

She said, loud enough to cut the yard but not loud enough to sound like Mofiz, "Go home and open your trunks. Anything that smells of mildew comes out now. Anything wrapped in wet cloth gets unwrapped now. If your paper is under the bed, drag it out before the rain beats you to it."

Several heads turned. An old man near the front, Abdul Karim with the cataract milk in both eyes and the habit of nodding before he had heard the end of a sentence, called, "Dai-ma, what if the name is wrong in one place?"

Halima answered him as if she were scolding a nephew. "Then you find the place where it's less wrong and you don't stand here talking while the sky darkens."

The crowd laughed because she had been sharp, and then they stopped because she had been right. That was the moment the fear changed shape. Before, the deadline had been a thing posted on a wall. After that, it became an afternoon's worth of reachable work and unreachable work. People began leaving while Aminul was still speaking, first one man, then two women, then a cluster of boys sent running toward houses with orders. Nura stayed long enough to read the notice aloud to the women who could not make the letters hold still, then came away from the wall with her jaw set.

"What did they write under the list heading?" Halima asked quietly.

Nura wiped sweat off her upper lip with the back of her wrist, leaving a streak of newsprint. "Preliminary verification for compensation eligibility."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning if you're not alive on their paper by Friday, they won't look hard for you after."

That was not what Aminul had said. It was what he had meant.

By noon the char no longer looked like a place preparing to evacuate. It looked like a place preparing to sit an examination set by men who had never had to keep paper dry in a tin-roof room. Courtyards that usually held fish baskets and sleeping children were crowded with documents weighted under enamel plates, brass tumblers, cracked stones, the wooden pestles used for chili. Women shook out old sari ends and spread land slips on them as if they were sick infants needing sun. Boys were sent under beds with broom handles to hook out rusted trunks. Men who ordinarily let their identity cards live folded in lungi knots or wallet scraps tucked them into shirt pockets and kept patting the pockets as though the cards might breed legs.

Halima turned first toward the eastern side, where the ground held a little longer before surrendering to rain. Jamila's house stood there, its yard swept even in monsoon, with two ducks complaining under an upturned basket and Majed's disemboweled engine parts arranged on a mat as neatly as prayer offerings. Today the parts had been pushed aside. Majed had laid documents over an overturned fish crate and was staring at them as if enough looking might make them multiply.

Grease sat black in the half-moons of his nails. One small screw was tucked behind his ear out of habit even though there was nothing mechanical in front of him worth tightening. Jamila knelt beside a square biscuit tin wrapped in old blouse cloth. When she opened it, a faint sweet smell came up, cloves and stale sugar fighting with dried fish. She must have stored the papers with spice to keep insects off. The kabinnama lay inside, folded around their marriage date like something too embarrassed to be seen at midday.

"You came quickly," Jamila said. Her voice was even, composed to the point of insult. When she was frightened, she grew more polite, not less. "Sit, Khalamma. Or don't sit. The stool is damp."

Halima remained standing. "Let me see what you've got."

Majed slid the receipts toward her. "Tax slips from three years. The newest one's half-eaten at the corner. My NID. Her card. Two children's birth cards. The marriage paper. My father's old occupancy slip, though the ink's running."

Jamila tapped the kabinnama. "And my name is one spelling here and another on the NID. The clerk wrote an extra letter. He said it didn't matter at the time."

"It mattered the second he took the pen off the page," Halima said.

Majed scratched his cheek with a knuckle. "Can they throw a whole household for one letter?"

"They can do whatever the file lets them pretend is reasonable."

Jamila gave a short breath that was almost laughter. "That means yes."

Halima went through the packet in order. Jamila had done better than most. Each paper had its own wrapping. The children's cards were tucked between school notebook covers so they would not crease. Majed had even found a length of plastic fertilizer sack to line the tin. This was a household that understood logistics. Fear had turned them efficient before it turned them theatrical.

"Who can witness?" Halima asked.

Majed answered immediately. "The imam. My mother's cousin. Both know the marriage."

Jamila said, "The imam knows the ceremony. My mother's cousin knows the house."

Halima looked up. The correction had come too fast to be casual. Jamila's face remained smooth, but one of her thumbnails kept rubbing the edge of the biscuit tin until the metal sang lightly under the nail.

"And if somebody remembers more than the ceremony?" Jamila asked.

There it was, quiet as a knife laid on a plate. Not spoken plainly, but plain enough. Halima could have told her that most people remembered less than they claimed and more than they admitted. She could have told her that the char had lived for years on selective memory. Instead she said the drier thing, the thing that sounded like work.

"Keep the packet separate from the children's papers. Find cloth that isn't damp. If the rain starts before evening, move the tin off the floor."

Jamila's mouth tightened. She had not asked about cloth. She had asked whether a life built under one name could survive being carried under another. Halima had answered her with storage instructions because storage was the only honest help she had on hand.

Majed, who always sounded most like himself when there was a tool or a schedule in reach, seized on the practical. "I've got a plastic drum top. I'll set the tin on that. If water comes in, it won't wick up."

"Do that," Halima said.

When she left, Jamila was already repacking the papers by category, and Majed had gone to find the drum top. They were the sort of household the state preferred: a man who knew how to arrange objects, a woman who knew how to keep a face steady, children folded inside both their names. Even so, one wrong witness could unpick them.

The path west turned soft underfoot. By the time Halima reached the poorer huts the mud had begun taking sandals whole, belching when she pulled free. Everywhere she looked, paper had come into daylight that had not touched daylight in years. A girl with a runny nose held open three school certificates while her mother waved a hand fan over them. A man had spread yellow land receipts across a fishing net and was blowing on them through a gap in his front teeth. At another yard, two sisters were arguing over whether a dead husband's nickname counted as a legal alias if everyone on the char had used it longer than his formal name. Nobody was speaking about the river. Nobody was asking whether the administration could be trusted. The whole island had dropped from politics to inventory.

Rahima's hut crouched lower than the others, as if the ground under it had already made peace with submergence. One of her goats had dragged its rope around the threshold peg until the knot sat hard with mud. Rahima herself stood in the doorway with her smoke-dark shawl thrown back and a tin of puffed rice under one arm. She had opened the tin for the papers and forgotten to shut it; half the rice had gone damp and clung to the inside in a pale paste.

She said, not greeting Halima, "Look what passes for a file in my house."

Inside the tin lay two land-tax receipts turned tea-colored with age, a ration card with her dead husband's name half rubbed off, and a folded scrap on which someone had once written a date so faintly it looked like breath on glass. The papers smelled of old kerosene, rat droppings, and sweet stale grain. Halima touched the top receipt and felt the fibers rise under her fingertip. Another rain like the last and it would come apart in layers.

"Where was this kept?" she asked.

"Under the bedding. Where else? You think I've got a wooden chest with brass lock like Jamila bibi?"

On the threshold sat Noorjahan from two huts over, a narrow-hipped woman who never removed the cheap man's wristwatch she wore loose over her bangles, though the watch had been stopped at 6:12 for years. Every morning she set the hands back there by habit and wore the dead time all day. Her younger son had a wet cough that dragged from him nights like a saw through green bamboo. Halima knew the sound because it traveled through the lane. Noorjahan was shelling dried peas into her lap and pretending not to listen.

"I told her," Noorjahan said, "if she needs me to stand up at the school, I'll stand. But a witness loses a day too. My boy needs syrup. The shop won't take blessings."

Rahima did not look at her. "She wants two hundred taka."

"One fifty," Noorjahan corrected. "And I didn't say cash only. Rice counts."

Halima felt something inside her go flat and hard, like wet cloth slapped onto a stone. "So that's the price now."

Noorjahan lifted one shoulder. "The price of anything is what the frightened person will pay."

Rahima barked one humorless laugh. "Hear that, Dai-ma? Even my marriage needs rent."

"Do you have anybody else?" Halima asked.

Rahima's eyes moved toward the west, toward the lane where people preferred not to be seen going if they had other options. "Two men saw the burial when my husband died. One's half deaf, the other drinks. A woman from my old para remembers the wedding, maybe. If I go ask her, her daughter-in-law will make a face first."

"And you?"

Rahima's mouth shifted. Stress made her more exact, never less. "I lived with the man fifteen years. I bled in that house. I buried two babies from that house. I kept his mother wiped when she lost her bladder. But none of that prints itself."

Noorjahan resumed shelling peas. "Printing costs extra," she said.

Halima wanted to snap at her. Instead she crouched, because the receipt edges were curling and needed flattening before they cracked. The packed-earth floor was cool and damp through her knees. "Find dry cloth," she said. "Not from under your bedding. Dry. Wrap each paper alone. And raise this tin. If water comes, it comes low first."

Rahima watched her hands. "You'll speak for me?"

The question carried more than widowhood inside it. Kashem's name lay between them unsaid, raw from the last chapter of daylight. Halima kept her eyes on the paper. "I'll speak to what I can defend."

Rahima gave one slow nod. "You have defended more dangerous things than a widow's roof."

The words landed cleanly because they were true. Halima repacked the tin and left before either of them could open the rest.

At Sufia's tea stall the whole char had narrowed into a queue of temporary bargains. The stall stood just above the ghat on bamboo posts gone black with spilled tea and river damp. Sufia moved behind the kettle with her usual slight limp, quick hands lifting glasses by the rims. Betel had stained the corners of her mouth a deep brick red. She kept the cash folded inside the sleeve of an old sweater no matter the heat, because years ago a customer had snatched coins off the counter and she had never trusted open surfaces since.

Today money was not the only thing changing hands.

"I'll stand for your sister if you stand for my wife," one man said.

"I've never been in your courtyard."

"You've eaten there on Eid."

"Eating isn't the same as witnessing."

A woman Halima knew only as Shila, who braided her daughters' hair so tight the girls looked startled all morning, held a damp envelope to her chest and asked Sufia, "If the kazi's nephew swears he saw the register, is that enough?"

Sufia poured tea without looking up. "Enough for whom? For God, for the officer, or for the man who'll challenge you after?"

The woman did not answer.

Halima stood just outside the awning, listening. A boy too young for a beard and too old for innocence was offering to take papers across the water for lamination if paid in advance. Another man had already begun rehearsing dates aloud, not because he remembered them but because he meant to remember them by Friday. Testimony had stopped being memory. It had become labor for hire, favor, leverage, revenge. A mouth that would say the right thing on a school bench had acquired market value by lunchtime.

Sufia finally looked at Halima. Her public voice stayed dry even when the world got ugly. "You'll want tea."

"I'll want a second sky before I want tea."

"Can't sell that."

"You'd try if it came in glass."

One corner of Sufia's mouth went up. Then she nodded toward the lane. "Kashem's been prowling since the notice went up. He asked three men to stand for him. Two said maybe. One said yes too fast, which means he'll ask for something rotten later."

Halima did not thank her. She went on.

Kashem was by the boat sheds, exactly where Sufia had said he would be, moving too quickly for a man with nowhere useful to go. He had tucked his laminated identity card into the band of his lungi and kept pulling it out to check it, then shoving it back, then pulling it out again. The card itself was safe. The card was not the problem. The problem was that a single laminated lie could sit dry and shining in a man's hand while every path connecting it to a household dissolved around him.

He saw Halima and barked a laugh that drew two other men closer just to hear what would follow.

"Dai-ma," he called. "Come bless the file. Maybe it'll start telling the truth if you breathe on it."

His voice had the loud, mocking looseness he used when he felt himself sliding lower in a room and meant to kick dirt upward before anybody noticed. The chipped place in his front tooth flashed when he grinned. His palms were rope-burned and wet with sweat. He must have been working the card edges hard because the plastic was cloudy where his thumb kept rubbing.

"Show me the rest," Halima said.

"The rest is the joke." He slapped the card against his other hand. "This says one thing. Land says another. Old men say wait. Officer says bring witnesses. Everybody wants two names and a paper bed for them to lie in."

One of the listening men drifted away when Halima looked at him directly. The other pretended to adjust a rope.

"Who'll stand for you?" she asked.

Kashem shrugged with theatrical carelessness. "Depends what day they want honesty. For one day I can probably buy enough of it."

"With what money?"

"With fish, with labor, with the shame I've still got left. Same as anybody."

He leaned closer. His breath smelled of stale bidi and river water. Under the mockery, hurt had made him suddenly plain. "Tell me one thing true, then. If I bring the card, the tax slip, two men who'll say they know the house, will they take me? Or am I supposed to stand there and let my life get laughed at again?"

Halima could not answer the question he meant. The answer to the narrow question was maybe. The answer to the wider one was no, not without cost, not ever again. She said, "Bring everything. Let Aminul see it together. Don't wait till Friday."

Kashem's face shut by increments. Pride came back into it like shutters closing. "That's your medicine for all of us now. Bring everything. Wait nowhere. Very good." He tucked the card back into his waistband. "Don't touch my papers, Dai-ma. They bite."

He said it lightly enough that the men behind him could pretend it was joking. It was not joking. Halima left him with the river smell and the tarred hulls and the knowledge that a man she had once carried on her hip as a feverish child now treated her like contamination he had to manage in public.

When she reached home, Nura had turned the front room into a sorting shed. Mats were rolled up to keep them from damp. The low table had been taken over by stacks of other people's paper bound with thread. One pile held school certificates. One held ration cards. Another held copied name lists in Nura's neat hand. She had even chalked small headings on broken pieces of cardboard and propped them against clay bowls as if she were running an office out of a room that still smelled of lentils.

A little girl from the next lane slept by the wall with her cheek on an atlas primer. Two teenage brothers waited in the yard, shifting from foot to foot because Nura had told them not to come in with muddy hems. She was not sitting. She was moving from stack to stack, burn-marked wrist flashing each time she reached.

"What's this?" Halima asked.

"What it looked like it would become." Nura did not stop moving. "They're bringing me the papers they can't read. I'm writing household lists so they stop shoving everything loose into one bag."

She held up a tax receipt rolled tight as a bidi. "If one more grown man hands me paper like this, I'll push it back down his throat."

Despite herself, Halima felt the edge of a smile. It did not stay long. "How many households?"

"Twelve already. More coming after Asr. Jamila apa sent word asking if a different spelling counts as a different woman. Noorjahan wants to know if she can witness two marriages on the same day. Somebody asked me to copy a date cleaner than it was written the first time. I told him to get out."

"Who?"

Nura glanced toward the yard where the brothers waited and lowered her voice. "Not one of ours. A man from the western lane. I didn't even bother keeping the name."

That, too, was the island reorganizing. Nura's literacy had always brought people to the house for forms, school letters, phone numbers. Now they were bringing her the boundary between salvage and fraud and asking her to redraw it with better penmanship.

"Did you eat?" Halima asked.

Nura made a face. "Did you?"

Neither had. Halima washed her hands at the pitcher and the water ran gray. She could smell old paper on her skin already, that mold-sour smell that sank into the creases beside the nails. Outside, the sky had turned the color of tin scrubbed with ash. The thin sun people had trusted all afternoon was failing.

She worked beside Nura for the next hour, not sitting either. They untied packets, aired cards, separated school documents from land slips so the cleaner paper would not stain from the older rot. Halima found one birth entry stuck to the back of a political leaflet and peeled them apart with the patience of a woman lifting gauze off a healing wound. She sent boys home with instructions shouted from the door: off the floor, out of wet trunks, each paper alone, each witness told clearly what name belonged to which house. It was impossible work. It was still work.

When the first thunder rolled, it sounded not from the sky but from the school roof, a great sheet-metal boom traveling across the char. Everyone froze for that one breath in which hope sometimes still survives. Then the wind came sideways through the yard and scattered two loose receipts off Nura's table. The teenage brothers lunged after them. One page slapped against the wet threshold and darkened instantly where the water touched.

"Inside," Halima snapped. "All of it inside now."

The rain arrived as a wall.

Tin roofs across the char broke into noise so total it seemed to erase distance. Houses vanished a few yards beyond their doorways. The yard turned from dirt to moving brown skin in seconds, water pushing around the brick edges and carrying chili stems, goat pellets, a child's lost pencil. Nura and Halima lifted the paper stacks to the loft shelf and then started receiving whoever reached the door first.

A woman came with three school certificates wrapped in her blouse and weeping because the ink had already begun to feather. Halima laid them between two aluminum plates and told her to stop crying and fetch dry cloth. Majed sent one of his sons with the biscuit tin high under the boy's shirt. The plastic lining had held, but the old occupancy slip inside was damp along one edge and would need air by morning. Noorjahan arrived not with Rahima's witness promise but with Rahima's kerosene tin, because water had come under the west wall and the goat had kicked the bedding loose. The tin leaked from one corner. Half the puffed rice had turned to paste against the paper.

Halima took one look and went herself.

The western lane in storm light was barely a lane. Water shoved at her calves where the ground dipped. The wind snatched her shawl back so hard it thumped against her spine. At Rahima's hut the threshold had vanished under the runoff. Rahima was inside on her knees, both hands pressed over the lid of the bedding chest as if holding down an animal. The goat screamed from the corner where its rope had tangled in a stool leg.

"Leave the chest," Halima shouted. "The papers first."

Rahima lifted her face. Rain had found its way through the roof seam and was running down her temple into the collar of her blouse. "The chest has the blanket."

"The blanket can get wet."

"So can I."

Still she let go of the chest. Together they hauled the kerosene tin onto the string cot. Halima opened it and saw at once what the water had done. The oldest tax receipt had wicked damp halfway up. The ration card had fused at one fold. The scrap with the faint date was bleeding into nothing, numbers losing their edges like bank under current.

Rahima watched Halima's hands with a concentration usually reserved for surgeons and money counters. "Can you save it?"

"Maybe."

"Maybe is a rich person's answer."

"It's the only one I've got."

They laid the papers on the cot slats one by one. There was nowhere truly dry. Rain rattled through the tin roof and dripped from two nailed holes near the wall. The room smelled of goat dung, wet ash, and paper coming apart. Rahima stood with her hands hanging useless because there were no more containers left to lift.

Then, over the rain, Noorjahan's voice came from the doorway. "I've brought my rice tin. It's higher than yours."

She stepped in without asking, dead watch flashing at her wrist. In her other hand she held not only the tin but a square of blue polythene cut from a fertilizer sack. Her son's cough medicine had not yet been bought. Halima could see the calculation still working behind her eyes. Yet she had come anyway.

"Put the good paper in this," Noorjahan said. "The worst one can stay out and curse the sky."

Rahima looked at her for a long second, suspicious even inside gratitude. "You'll still ask your one fifty tomorrow."

"I'll ask my one fifty whether you drown tonight or not. Take the polythene."

The exchange was so exact it almost steadied Halima. Even mercy had invoices now. At least that made the terms visible.

They got the driest papers into the new wrapping, raised the tin on two bricks, cut the goat loose and moved it to the doorway where the water ran less deep. By the time Halima left, her kameez clung cold to her back and her fingers were pruned from wet. The storm had not weakened. It had only spread.

All over Kadam Char, lights stayed on later than oil should have allowed. Through rain and tin and wind, Halima could still hear the altered life of the island. Not the usual evening sounds of quarrel, radio, spoon against pot. People were reciting names. They were drilling dates. They were calling across courtyards for elder cousins and former neighbors. They were deciding who could stand up in a schoolroom and make a household sound simple enough to survive the trip to the mainland file.

At home, the loft shelf bowed under the added weight of packets drying in borrowed safety. Nura sat cross-legged under it with a notebook in her lap, writing by lantern because two more families had sent names after dark. Her eyes were red from smoke. She did not look up when Halima entered. She said only, "Kashem bhai sent a boy. He wants to know if a man can witness a father he never met."

"What did you tell him?"

"That men do worse things every day."

Halima hung her wet shawl on the door peg. Water dripped off it onto the packed earth in a line as straight as writing. She listened to the rain hit the roof hard enough to shake dust from the rafters. On the shelf above her head lay Nura's certificates, Rahima's rescued tin, Jamila's carefully wrapped packet, three school cards from another lane, and a marriage deed so damp the seal had begun to smell of glue again. Other houses would have their own piles tonight, their own little altars to the state, each guarded from children, goats, in-laws, and weather.

But paper was only half the hoard. The rest of the island was storing human mouths. Men who had not visited certain courtyards in years would go tomorrow and drink tea there. Women who hated one another would sit close and rehearse each other's marriages. Old quarrels would be priced, delayed, or sharpened depending on what a witness could earn by remembering wrong. In one house a family would have enough receipts, enough names, enough neighbors willing to say yes. In another house they would have only the life itself, which had never once learned how to look official.

Halima stood in the middle of her room with the smell of wet silt and mold rising around her and understood that the char had already begun to sort itself. Not into the innocent and the guilty. Not into the truthful and the false. Into the households whose damage could be arranged into something the state might call consistent, and the households whose only honest shape was contradiction. The rain kept beating both kinds of paper alike.
