# Chapter 15

By nine the schoolyard smelled of old mud baked back into dust, goat dung, dried fish from somebody's breakfast basket, and the hot iron smell tin roofs made when the sun started climbing. Men had dragged the school benches out under the veranda because the two rooms inside could not hold the magistrate, the clerks, the disputed households, the watchers at the windows, and the bodies that had come simply because shame was worth seeing when it belonged to somebody else. Halima stood with the oilcloth ledger under her arm and felt the notebook's corners through the cloth, hard as the shoulder blades of starved children.

Mofiz had arrived early enough to arrange hospitality. A bamboo table stood under a white sheet. Three glasses of tea sweated rings onto a plastic tray. He had found an electric pedestal fan from somewhere on the eastern side and placed it so it turned first toward the magistrate's chair, then toward his own, then away from the crowd entirely. His beard edges were freshly dyed, too black against the gray in his cheeks, and every few minutes he patted the front of his panjabi as though the cloth itself might testify for him.

Aminul stood beside the table with his folders held off his chest so the papers would not drink his sweat. He had changed into a clean pale shirt and still looked tired enough for the cuffs to seem like punishment. Nura was a little behind him, papers squared in both hands, a pencil tucked over one ear. She had tied her hair tighter than usual. Whenever she was frightened she made herself look prepared enough to shame the world.

The magistrate came up from the ghat ten minutes later in cream cotton and dust-filmed sandals, with a rubbed sore at the back of one heel that made her shift her weight when she stopped. She was perhaps thirty-five, perhaps younger, with a small ink stain on the side of her thumb and a habit of pinching the bridge of her nose whenever two men began speaking at once. She took one look at the crowd crushed against the veranda, one look at the tin roof already snapping in the heat, and pushed the sweet tea away untouched.

"I won't rehear every quarrel Kadam Char has had since God made silt," she said. "You asked for disputed-household review. I'll hear disputed households. I need a list I can sign today. The removal schedule isn't waiting for all of you to cleanse your blood."

A nervous laugh went through the back rows and died there.

Mofiz put a hand over his heart, all courtesy. "Madam Magistrate, with respect, clarity requires first deciding whether this so-called ledger is evidence at all. The woman standing there has been accused, by many residents, of altering births, fathers, household lines. Public funds can't rest on a notebook of private inventions."

He did not point at her. That was his smoothness. He made accusation sound like manners.

Halima stepped forward before the words could spread and root. "Then don't pay by fathers," she said. "Pay by who has lived in which house, who eats from which pot, who carries which old body to the latrine when that body can't walk."

Mofiz smiled without looking at her. "The state is not a kitchen, Halima-dai."

"No," she said. "A kitchen keeps people alive."

That brought a different sound from the crowd, sharper this time. Not laughter. Agreement from people too tired to bother hiding it.

Aminul cleared his throat. In public he always sounded as if he were placing each word on a shelf and checking whether it would bear weight. "Madam, the occupancy-affidavit route was already discussed with the upazila office. For these disputed cases, if live testimony establishes durable household residence and dependency, the list can be revised without resolving every blood inconsistency."

"Can be," Mofiz said. "If the testimony is credible."

The magistrate looked at Halima, at the ledger under her arm, at the blackboard behind the table where one clerk had already chalked two columns: ACCEPTED and DISPUTED. The chalk dust streaked the board like fish scales.

"Fine," she said. "We hear testimony. We don't turn this into a village opera. Every case must end with one of those two words." She tapped the board with the capped end of her pen. "Accepted. Disputed. If anybody forgets that, I'll remember it for you."

Halima untied the oilcloth.

The notebook had been wrapped and rewrapped so many times the cloth had taken the smell of her loft shelf: lamp smoke, old paper, dried neem leaves, the faint medicinal bitterness of castor oil that had leaked once years ago and never entirely gone. When she opened it, the spine crackled. She knew that sound better than some voices in the crowd. It was the sound of girls not being sent away too young, of boys not being handed to men who would work them flat, of widows not losing their last patch of roof. It was also the sound of what she had done to make those things possible. Both truths lived in the same paper. Today there was no room to keep them apart.

They began with the cases nobody had come to savor, because those were easiest to strip. A bent old aunt whom her nephew had quietly left off his form because she no longer earned and took twice as long to shit. A sister's abandoned boy who had been sleeping under one eave for six years and whom the head of household now wanted to describe as temporary because one more mouth meant one smaller share. A half-blind brother-in-law whose cot had not moved from the north wall in three monsoons and who still risked being called a visitor because his name spoiled the neatness of the paper line.

The nephew, a man with turmeric packed into the cracks of his fingernails from hauling sacks at the market, tried first. "Benu khala only stays with us sometimes."

Halima did not even look at the ledger for that one. "Sometimes?" she said. "Then tell madam who turns her on the mat so the sores on her hip don't open. Tell her who boiled rice soft enough for her gums when she lost the last bottom tooth. Tell her why your wife washed Benu's shroud cloth three weeks ago and hung it behind the house in case she died before Friday."

The man's ears went dark. His wife, from somewhere in the crowd, said, "He's talking nonsense. She hasn't left our yard since last winter."

The magistrate made one small mark with her pen. "Accepted."

The nephew began to protest about shares, about how many people were being tied to one roof, about arithmetic. The clerk had already moved on.

That was how the morning went. Every name Halima read made somebody stiffer, somebody softer, somebody poorer, somebody safer. The hearing did not uncover secrets the island had never guessed. It took what had always been managed by glances and courtyards and women's low voices and pinned it to a public board. A child born too soon after marriage became an official question. A man who had called his brother's deserted wife "sister" for five years had to say in front of everybody that she cooked, slept, and bled inside his house and could not be waved off now because the compensation line had narrowed. A middle-aged woman with a cough like wet cloth had to hear her sons argue, under a government pen, about whether her married daughter counted because she returned so often with bruises and children.

Each time Mofiz tried to pull the room back toward the cleaner cruelty. "Where is the document?" "Where is the father's trace?" "Where is the marriage proof?" He asked it with the generous patience of a man who wanted blood on the floor but did not want it on his own hands.

Each time Halima dragged the room back to what could be touched. Which bamboo post had the child measured against year after year. Which roof leaked over which couple's sleeping place. Who had sold a brass plate for fever medicine. Who had remained when the river took the outer yard. She was drier than usual, more cutting. Pressure always stripped her voice down to bone.

By the time Mofiz said, "Let us take Sheikh Kashem, then," the crowd had pushed so close to the veranda that the school windows were no longer windows at all, only faces framed in concrete.

Kashem came up with his laminated identity card already in his hand. There was a peeling cricket sticker on the back, put there by some child or by Kashem himself in a moment of foolish tenderness he would deny. His palms were rope-burned and shiny with sweat. He had the broad shoulders of a man who needed other men to notice that he still had them. He smiled when he reached the table, but it was the kind of smile men use when a net has torn and they are still pretending the fish will not go through.

"You keep calling me like I'm a show goat," he said, loud enough for the back rows. "Say the fee first, Chairman sahib."

A few men laughed because he had invited them to. Halima heard the extra force under it. He was already bracing.

Mofiz spread his fingers. "No fee. Only facts. Your card gives one father. Your homestead trace links elsewhere. The ledger, I'm told, may offer us a third miracle."

Kashem's smile thinned. "Then hear the miracle and let me go fish."

Aminul read from the file, careful, neutral. "Recorded father line and claimed occupancy chain don't reconcile. Witness rehearsal flagged maternal dispute. Household determination pending."

"Maternal dispute," Kashem repeated, with a short laugh. "Hear that. I've become a maternal dispute."

Halima knew the page before she turned to it. The paper there had gone thin at the edge from years of her fingers finding it in the dark. She saw her old writing, tighter then, less tremor in it, the ink browned almost to rust. Outside, a crow screamed from the school wall. Inside, nobody moved.

"Read it," said the magistrate.

Halima did.

She read the date first, because dates gave a room one more second before truth landed. She read the storm night, the hut on the western edge, the delivery complication. Then she read the line she had written for herself and never meant for any official mouth: male child born to Rahima, placed into Sheikh household on third night.

Kashem stared at her as though he had misheard a number and could fix it if he listened harder. "What did you say?"

Halima kept her eyes on the page. If she looked at him too soon she might remember the first time she had held him, slippery and furious, and then she would speak like an old woman instead of a witness. "I said the mother was Rahima."

No one gasped. It was not that kind of room. The silence was worse. It had weight.

Rahima did not wait to be called. She was already coming through the bodies, small and weather-crooked, the rope of her white goat still looped around one wrist because she had not trusted the animal to anybody else's post. Puffed-rice crumbs clung to the fold of her smoke-dark shawl. She stopped beside the table, breathing through her mouth, and looked at Kashem the way hungry people looked at food they had no right to touch.

"You came feet first," she said.

Kashem turned toward her as if somebody had shoved him. "Don't."

"You came feet first," Rahima said again, exact as a knife. "Your left ear was folded over. Halima had to push you back and turn you. The cord was round your neck once. When you finally came, you did not cry till she slapped the sole of your foot three times. Your little toe on the right had that extra nail bit. You still have it."

Against his will Kashem looked down, as though the toe might have vanished in the last minute and save him.

Rahima went on. Under stress she did not grow vague. She got cruelly precise. "My nipples split feeding you because you bit before your gums filled out. You had the hot rash behind both knees that first winter. I wrapped burnt jute ash in mustard oil and rubbed it there. When you were seven months old, you would only sleep with your fist knotted in the edge of my sari."

"Enough," Kashem said, but the word cracked in the middle.

Mofiz lifted a palm. "Madam, anyone on the char could have heard these stories."

Rahima turned on him with a contempt so old it seemed to come from before his beard had gone black at the edges. "Could they hear me bleed too?"

The crowd stirred.

She did not lower her voice. "My brother had already signed his hand to debt. Not money for rice. Debt for nets and boat timber, debt that breeds men who come back for labor when they don't get cash. They had counted ahead to the first boy. Halima put him in the Sheikh line before those bastards could fasten a rope round his future. That's what was done. If you want the polite version, choke on it yourself."

The magistrate's pen stopped moving.

Kashem's face had gone flat in a way Halima had never seen on him, stripped of swagger, stripped even of anger for a second. Child-plain, exactly as the style of hurt she had feared. "So all these years," he said, and now he was not talking to Rahima alone, not even to Halima. He was speaking to the air that had held one name around him his whole life. "All these years I said the wrong man when they asked me who I was."

Rahima's mouth tightened. "You said the man that kept you out of the net sheds."

"You could've told me."

"Could I?" she said. "When? Which year was safe? Pick one."

That landed harder than any wail would have. There had not been a safe year. They all knew it. Debt, shame, male pride, marriage talk, school forms, NID enrollment, land slips. Every door truth might have entered had already been barred by some other necessity. That did not make the lie smaller in his mouth now.

Kashem slapped his laminated card down on the table so hard the magistrate's tea glass jumped. "Then what am I standing on today? If that father is false and this mother is true and everybody here knew enough to keep their mouths shut, what am I supposed to claim? Air?"

Halima answered because nobody else could say it without sounding either kind or official, and kindness would insult him while official language would bury him. "You claim the house you built and slept in. You claim the nets you mended there. You claim the yard where your wife dried fish if she had a yard and the mat where you fevered if you fevered there. Blood is one kind of line. Life is another."

He swung toward her. "Easy for you. You wrote both."

It struck where it was meant to. She felt it all along the old bend of her back.

Mofiz seized the moment. "This is exactly the problem, madam. One woman writes both, as she pleases, and now we're asked to distribute compensation on the basis of whichever truth hurts least today."

"No," Aminul said.

He had not raised his voice until then. The single syllable cut the room cleaner than shouting would have.

He set Kashem's card beside the file instead of on top of it, aligning the corners as if order could still be made from the day. "What this establishes is that the paternal chain is compromised. We already knew several paternal chains were compromised. The question before this hearing is whether Sheikh Kashem is a verifiable resident with a durable household claim under the occupancy route. On that question we have his residence, western-edge witnesses, and now the explanation for why the blood record broke."

"Explanation," Mofiz said. "A sentimental one."

Aminul looked at him with something very near disgust, which for him was almost a public loss of control. "A practical one. There's a difference."

The magistrate asked Kashem three short questions. Where was his current sleeping place. How long had he occupied it. Who would swear to it. He answered like a man chewing sand, each word slower than the last. Rahima gave names. Two fishermen from the western edge raised their hands without waiting to be called. One had salt-crusted cuffs on his lungi; the other kept worrying a fishhook bent flat in his thumb and forefinger as though that tiny piece of metal could carry him through public speech.

The magistrate wrote for a long moment. Finally she said, "Maternal identity noted. Paternal line unreliable. Occupancy witness sufficient pending signatures. Accepted under household-affidavit review."

Kashem did not look relieved. That was not the right verb for what passed through his face. He had stayed on the list and lost the name that had carried him there. He snatched up his card and stepped away as if the table might burn him. Rahima took one half-step after him, then stopped because reaching in public was another risk and she had already taken the larger one.

Halima called the next case before the silence could thicken into spectacle.

The heat moved from high morning into the brutal flatness before noon. Children cried in the back until their mothers pinched their thighs quiet. The clerk's chalk wore down to a stub. Ink from the thumbpad got on Aminul's cuff and then on the side of his jaw when he wiped sweat there without thinking. Mofiz drank one of the teas at last, though it had gone lukewarm and formed a skin. The fan kept turning toward people who already had power.

Halima read and read. A deserted second wife whose marriage had never been registered but whose cooking pots, hens, and labor were embedded in her husband's brother's yard. An old man who had hidden his widowed daughter from the list because he did not want the magistrate knowing she had come back pregnant. A boy everyone called nephew until compensation math made his exact parentage interesting. The ledger redistributed not only money but the right to be looked at. Faces turned toward some people as if they had never seen them properly before and wished they had not now.

Then Jamila came.

She had put on the green sari with the thin gold border she kept for Eid and funerals, though one edge had been mended so neatly most people would miss it. The tin key to her trunk was knotted into the end of her anchal; she never let anyone else keep it because small things vanished too easily in a crowded house. Her face was composed to the point of discipline. Beside her stood Majed, tall and slightly stooped, a stripped brass screw riding in and out of his fingers from one pocket to the other. Grease sat under his nails despite whatever scrubbing he had done before coming.

Mofiz's politeness returned in full. That meant he smelled blood. "Madam, here we have another example of benevolent improvisation. Jamila Khatun draws claim through an aunt-line identity that, according to our respected dai's own recent explanations, was created after the fact. If we reward such paper substitution, then what principle remains?"

Jamila answered before Halima could. Under pressure she got quieter, not louder. "The principle that children shouldn't be traded like tin plates."

There was a murmur at that. Not because anyone doubted it. Because she had put the thing into the open without decoration.

Mofiz opened both hands, gracious, inviting more rope. "Please. Tell madam what you mean."

Jamila looked at the magistrate, not at him. "My mother's brother had debt. Not charity debt. The kind that comes with counting your daughters before they even bleed. When I was thirteen and had only just started staining cloth, a widower from the mainland came to see me. He held my chin and looked at my teeth. He squeezed my upper arm to see if I could carry water. He asked my uncle whether my breasts had risen enough yet. Halima moved me into my dead aunt's line on paper before they could settle me away against that debt."

The schoolyard seemed to tilt around the sentence. Men in the back went very still with the stillness of people trying not to be implicated by recognizing something familiar. Several women did not look at Jamila at all. That was the form of respect available to them.

Mofiz clicked his tongue softly, as if pitying history. "A sad story. But the question before the state is not what nearly happened to you as a girl. It is whether the compensation linked to that aunt-line should go to your present household."

Majed's screw stopped moving in his fingers. "It should."

Mofiz gave him a courteous nod. "And on what legal basis, Majed bhai?"

Majed swallowed once. When he was frightened, he came back to things that could be repaired. Numbers, tools, engines. People were harder. "On the basis that it's our house now. She keeps it. She sells eggs. She dries fish. The collector comes for the poultry payments and she's the one who faces him, not you. My engine parts debt sits where we sleep. The children know her house as theirs. If you want to know which household is hers, it's the one she's been keeping from breaking."

"That is not a lineage answer."

"No," Majed said. "It's a house answer."

For the first time all day Halima saw something like admiration flicker across Nura's face toward a man older than her. It vanished immediately, but it had been there.

Mofiz turned back to Jamila. "And your biological mother? If this claim survives, what happens to her?"

The question was meant to force nobility or guilt. Either would weaken her. Jamila refused both.

"What has already happened," she said. "She lives with what I couldn't carry back to her without dropping my own children into the same hole."

"So you admit self-interest."

Now Jamila looked at him, and the room cooled by a degree because everybody could feel the blade under the calm. "Chairman sahib, if self-interest disqualified household claims, we'd be home by now."

A few people barked laughter before catching themselves.

Mofiz tried once more. "The girl was moved on paper. The aunt is dead. The blood mother remains elsewhere. This is exactly the kind of sentimental corruption that ruins a record."

Halima felt the ledger's rough page edge under her thumb and remembered Jamila at thirteen, skinny knees, the sour smell of fear sweat, the way she'd kept wiping her palms on a frock too small for her because strange men were in the yard pretending to discuss debt while looking at her chest. Mercy had created this problem. Mercy had also created the woman standing here with the key to her own trunk tied into her sari. Both truths again. Same paper.

She was about to speak when Aminul did it first.

"Madam," he said, and now the balanced caution in him thinned enough for the human underneath to show, "I want this stated clearly. I support the household-occupancy affidavit route for Kadam Char."

The crowd shifted. Even people who had not understood half the morning understood the importance of an official saying I support.

Aminul continued, his voice still courteous, but sharper than before. "If we insist on clean blood and inheritance chains in every disputed case, we will exclude actual resident households and certify a list that is neat on paper and false on the ground. We have live witnesses. We have durable occupancy. We have dependency that is socially visible even where formal records failed or were manipulated for survival. The route exists for exactly this situation."

Mofiz gave a soft, unbelieving laugh. "For survival. That is a broad excuse."

"It's not an excuse," Aminul said. "It's the administrative fact in front of us."

He turned toward the magistrate rather than continuing to spar. "Jamila Khatun's aunt-line documents are part of the existing paper chain. Her current household occupancy with Majed Ali is uncontested in practice. The contradiction does not disappear by striking her off. It only punishes the people who have actually lived in that house."

The magistrate looked at Jamila. "Will you sign an affidavit acknowledging the discrepancy and attesting to current household occupancy?"

"Yes."

"Will your husband?"

"Yes," Majed said.

"Will two neighbors?"

Before Mofiz could arrange the room, two women spoke from the crowd at once. One sold eggs beside Jamila during the dry months and still had chicken feed dust in the hem of her sari. The other was a widow who could never resist correcting anybody's prices and who had spent half the morning muttering about everyone's arithmetic. Both stepped forward because they knew exactly what house Jamila belonged to, and because once the room had heard what had nearly been done to her as a girl, letting her drop now would have been another way of doing it.

The magistrate wrote. "Accepted under affidavit."

Jamila closed her eyes only for the length of one breath. Relief was too soft a word for it. It was more like surviving a public beating with one useful bone still unbroken.

Majed touched her elbow as if to guide her away, then seemed to remember where they were and let his hand fall before anyone could read too much into tenderness. The dropped hand hurt almost as much to see as the rest of it.

Mofiz was losing the room and knew it. The more households moved from DISPUTED to ACCEPTED, the less his talk of principle sounded like morality and the more it sounded like appetite. His smoothness grew ceremonial. He began calling the magistrate "Madam Magistrate" every second sentence and Aminul "Officer Haque" as though titles themselves could rebuild the wall crumbling under him.

"With deepest respect," he said after the next two cases also went the wrong way for him, "we're on the edge of making forgery into custom. At what point does this hearing stop being verification and become mass pardon?"

The magistrate pinched the bridge of her nose. "At the point where you stop repeating yourself."

That earned open laughter, and Mofiz hated open laughter more than insult.

It went on into late afternoon. The sun shifted enough that the veranda shade no longer protected the front bench, and people in the first row had to squint through a stripe of light. Halima's throat tasted of chalk and old paper. Her left eye watered in the glare and made the crowd on that side seem to ripple. She kept reading. Not because she believed reading redeemed anything. Because stopping now would hand the day back to men who thought neatness and justice had ever been twins.

By the time the clerk ran out of room in the ACCEPTED column and had to start writing smaller, the whole yard knew where the hearing was going. Most of the island would stay legible enough to be moved. That knowledge brought no peace with it. It brought only the next question. Who was paying for the legibility.

Mofiz saved his last knife for where he knew the softest flesh lay.

"Before signature," he said, almost gently, "there remains one household we have somehow treated as though it were above ordinary scrutiny. The witness's own."

Halima had known it would come. Still, the body prepared differently from the mind. Her hand closed on the ledger so hard the skin over her knuckles shone.

Nura stepped closer before anyone called her. The burn scar on her wrist flashed pale as she moved a bundle of certificates onto the table and lined their edges by habit. Fear always made her tidier.

Mofiz inclined his head toward her with poisonous courtesy. "Halima-dai's granddaughter. Or ward. Or whatever relation the paperwork requires this week."

Something hot went through Halima's chest, not metaphorically, not as mere anger. Actual heat, like a ladle handle forgotten in flame and taken up bare-handed.

"Mind your mouth," she said.

"I'm minding process," he replied.

The magistrate did not rebuke either of them. She looked at the file Aminul pushed forward. It was thinner than the others. Thinness had become its own kind of omen.

Aminul spoke first, careful again because the ground here was rotten. "Current household consists of Halima Khatun and Nura Begum. Long-term co-residence undisputed. Documentary issue concerns Nura's paternal line and the authority by which that line was created."

"Created," Mofiz said softly. "A useful verb."

Nura's chin lifted. "The man on my papers never raised me."

Mofiz turned to her at once. "Then perhaps you'd like to name the one who did."

The crowd's hunger changed shape. It had been feeding all day on other people. Now it smelled the one house everybody had wanted access to for years and had rarely gotten.

Nura did not look away. "Halima raised me."

"Not grandmother. Father."

"I heard you."

"Then answer."

Halima could feel the day trying to split. If Nura answered from anger, if the room got what it wanted in the wrong order, everything accepted so far might start to wobble. Administrative hearings were like bamboo platforms in flood season. One panic in the wrong place and the whole structure swayed.

"Madam," Aminul said, "the occupancy itself is clear."

Mofiz cut across him. "Occupancy by what lawful chain? A dead daughter, a missing man, a witness who writes fathers as she pleases and then asks the state to reward her for it. If this house is accepted on her own word, why should any excluded claimant not arrive tomorrow with a notebook and a story?"

The word story spread through the yard like spit. Everybody understood the insult in it. Women's memory, reduced to story. Men's paperwork, called evidence even when it had been bought, threatened, or lazily guessed into existence by clerks on the mainland who would never set foot in Kadam Char.

Halima should have answered with strategy. Instead what came out was the dry blade of her stress voice. "Because notebooks don't keep a child fed through pox, Chairman. Houses do."

He smiled. "Then whose house fed this one? Name him."

Nura answered before Halima could stop her. "No man has brought rice to our door in years. Not in any way that matters."

That would have been enough for people with sense. Sense was not the mood of the yard anymore.

Somebody in the back said Saleha's name. Another voice, male, half-hidden, said Monir. Another asked which Monir. A woman hissed for silence. The silence did not come. It frayed at the edges, full of other people's ownership over any girl whose origin had ever gone soft in the records.

Halima saw Nura hear it all. Not as words. As the old mechanism. The turning of a female life into a question other people could pass around and inspect.

The magistrate rapped the table with her pen. "Quiet."

The yard obeyed in layers.

Nura's face had not changed much, which was how Halima knew she was near breaking. Her granddaughter's hurt rarely arrived wet. It arrived dry and bright and cutting. "My mother's dead," she said. "The man on the paper is useless. That's true. What's also true is that I've slept in Halima's house, I've studied in Halima's house, I've eaten in Halima's house, and if you throw me off this list because no man kept good records around me, then write that clearly too. Don't dress it up."

For one second Halima could not breathe properly. Pride and fear crowded the same small place under her ribs and did not fit together.

Aminul spoke toward the magistrate, but some part of it was for Nura. "Household residence is not in question. The complication is evidentiary. The principal witness is also the party whose prior undocumented interventions created the contradiction. That makes same-day acceptance vulnerable on appeal."

Mofiz nodded, satisfied to hear harm in official grammar. "Exactly."

"Don't enjoy yourself too much," Aminul said without looking at him.

It was the closest thing to open contempt Halima had heard from him yet.

The magistrate sat back. Outside the veranda, somebody's baby had begun fussing again, the thin irritated cry of a child who had been held too long in heat. Chalk dust floated through the shaft of sunlight at the table's edge. A clerk flexed his cramping fingers. The whole day had narrowed to one decision and everybody knew it.

"Halima Khatun's household exists," the magistrate said at last. "That is not my difficulty. My difficulty is that the same hidden record being used to clarify other households also directly benefits the record-keeper's own case, in a file where paternity and authority are both already challenged. If I clear it today on the basis of her testimony, I create a ready-made appeal point against the entire revised list."

Nura started to speak. Halima put a hand on her forearm. The skin there was hot and fine-boned and alive. She felt the little ridge of the burn scar under her palm. Years ago she had held that same arm when it was no thicker than a green banana stem. Today she used the touch to silence her.

"Then what?" Nura said anyway, low and furious.

The magistrate looked at the blackboard. "Disputed pending appeal."

The words were ordinary. That was their violence. No curse, no slap, no official disgust. Just a category, and a life moved by it.

Mofiz exhaled through his nose like a man receiving a private blessing.

Halima did not look at him. If she had looked, she might have ruined more than her own day.

Aminul tried once more. "Madam, if supporting affidavits are attached from noninterested witnesses -"

"Then attach them," the magistrate said. "For appeal. I'm not holding the island here until every wound becomes administratively elegant."

That was the end of it. Not because it was just. Because the day was over and the launch would not wait and the char could only be rearranged so many times under one sun.

The clerk took fresh chalk and drew a shorter third space beneath DISPUTED for the names that would carry forward unresolved. Halima watched his hand write as though it belonged to some species of insect: quick, clean, indifferent. Their household went there. Two others joined it, one a widow whose witnesses had failed to appear, another a land-edge claim tangled with missing receipts and Mofiz's eastern-side manipulations that nobody had time to pull fully apart. Not many names. That was the point. Most of the island had been squeezed into legibility. The remainder looked smaller on the board than the pain of exclusion felt in the body.

Then came the signatures and thumbprints.

That part was nearly worse. Relief made people clumsy. Men who had shouted all morning now bent carefully over the inkpad as if their own thumbs had become legal instruments too delicate to trust. Women wiped sweat off upper lips and pressed blue-black prints beside names that had cost them daughters' shame, brothers' failures, old hidden births. Rahima stood in line with the goat rope still around her wrist, waiting to mark witness for Kashem. He had not come back beside her, but neither had he left the yard. He stood under the school neem tree staring at nothing, his card folded and unfolded in his fingers until the lamination clicked. When the clerk called his name as accepted, and called Rahima's with it as witness, he flinched as though acceptance itself had struck him.

Jamila signed with steady hand. Majed signed after her, slower, like a man tightening a bolt without knowing whether the thread beneath it has already stripped. He did not look at her, but when they stepped away he adjusted the key knot at the end of her sari because it had come loose in the crush. A tiny domestic motion. More intimate than an embrace would have been there. More hopeless too, because everybody saw it.

Aminul's collar had collapsed by then. The neatness had gone out of him. He moved from paper to paper with the defeated energy of a man who had won his argument and hated what winning required. Once, while the clerks sorted the accepted bundles, he put Halima's ledger back inside the oilcloth with both hands, almost respectfully, and did not meet her eyes.

The revised list was pinned to the school wall before evening. Two nails, one crooked, through the top corners. People surged toward it at once, the press of hips and shoulders hard enough to buckle the paper against the plaster. Bodies that had spent weeks fearing nonexistence now needed to see their names in ink. Children were lifted to read for elders. One woman laughed and cried together when she found her household under ACCEPTED and then slapped her own mouth shut as if joy in public might still be taxed away.

Nura did not go to the wall immediately. She stood beside Halima, breathing fast through her nose, fury held together so tightly it made her look almost calm.

"You should look," Halima said.

"I know what column we're in."

"Look anyway."

Nura went because anger had not yet made her disobedient in that particular way. She pushed into the crowd with her shoulders, not asking space, only taking enough. Halima stayed where she was under the veranda. Her knees ached. The ledger weighed strangely little now that it had been used. Or perhaps that was the opposite of what she felt. Perhaps her arms had simply gone numb.

From where she stood she could see only the upper half of the paper. ACCEPTED took most of the sheet. DISPUTED crouched below in meaner lettering. She did not need to read it. She knew the shape of their absence already.

Nura came back after a moment. Her face was white around the mouth.

"Kashem's there," she said. "Jamila's there. Benu khala. That widow with the cough. Even the boy Hamid tried to shake loose. Almost everybody."

Halima nodded.

Nura swallowed. "And us."

Halima looked past her to the wall where the paper lifted and slapped once in the late wind. Behind it, beyond the school and the houses and the cooking smoke beginning to rise, the river kept moving with the same patient appetite it had shown before any of them were born.

She had done what she came to do. That was the hardest part to forgive.

Almost the whole island was on the paper. Her house wasn't.
