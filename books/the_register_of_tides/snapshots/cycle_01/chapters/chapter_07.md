# Chapter 7

By late Friday morning the schoolhouse roof had stored enough heat to soften every paper edge in the room. Aminul kept lifting his folder clear of the table before sweat from his wrist could creep into the forms, then setting it down again in the same careful square, as if neatness might spread by contact. The wall behind him was already patched with old tape and newer notices. Outside, goats nosed the damp steps. Inside, the queue had thinned for prayers, but the air had not. It held body salt, wet jute, mildew from opened document bundles, and the sharp iron smell of people waiting to hear whether their names still belonged to them.

Halima had worked since dawn with a dryness in her mouth she could not wash away. She kept her thumb on one corner of each sheet while Aminul read the lines aloud, because the papers wanted to curl back into themselves and because her hands wanted work. When her hands were still, she heard too much.

People had begun lowering their voices when she looked up. That was new. The gossip itself was not. Kadam Char had always talked like a shared courtyard, one mouth picking up where another left off. But until this week the talk around her had still carried some ordinary need in it: a fever, a labor pain, a question about whether a baby ought to be nursing so little in this heat. Now the lowered voices carried appetite.

Mofiz Uddin came to the table with a widow from the eastern rise and two boys scrubbed harder than boys liked to be scrubbed. The widow kept pinching the corner of her sari over and over at the same place, worrying a tear she had already stitched once with blue thread. One of the boys had river mud still dried inside his left ear. Mofiz, in a cream panjabi that had somehow stayed clean where everybody else's cuffs had gone gray, rested two fingers on the receipt bundle as if he had brought not papers but calm itself.

"This one should be simple," he said. "Farhad Jalil's house. Wife, two sons, occupancy uninterrupted. If we move cleanly, Officer sahib, we can finish three more before the khutba."

Aminul adjusted the sheet in front of him. "The tax receipts are under Jalil, then Farhad. The younger boy is fine. The older one is not tracing to Farhad in the vaccine list. He is attached elsewhere."

Mofiz smiled without changing the set of his mouth. "Attached elsewhere by mistake, maybe. That happens on a char."

The widow's eyes flicked to Halima and away. She knew whose memory had been summoned into the room before anyone said it.

Aminul turned, polite as always when he needed something ugly from her. "Halima-dai, can you confirm the older boy's father line?"

Halima looked at the child. He was eight, maybe nine, with thin wrists and a scab on one knee that he had worried open again with his nails. She had seen him enter the world in a different house, on a night when the wind kept flattening the lamp flame and his mother bit a strip of old gamchha so hard she bled onto it. The boy had not come from Farhad Jalil's line. Everybody at this table knew why he had been brought under it afterward. He needed stability, food, and a roof on the higher side when the western edge started breaking off. But stability was not the same thing as truth, and Mofiz had been stacking that eastern ground with useful people for years, thickening one household into three and three into five so the better plots would land where his hand already rested.

If Aminul ever ran a real audit through births instead of tax receipts and public boasting, some of Mofiz's branches would fall clean off the tree.

Halima said, "The younger one was Farhad's. The older boy came before that marriage."

The widow shut her eyes. One of the boys started picking the skin beside his thumb until she slapped his hand down without looking.

Aminul made a note. "Then this claim needs witness support before inclusion."

Mofiz let out a soft breath that might have passed for amusement if Halima had not known how carefully he spent every expression. "Witness support," he said. "Yes. These days we're rich in witnesses. Poor in certainty, rich in witnesses."

He shifted his attention to the people standing closest behind the widow, letting them feel included. That was his real talent. He did not speak loudly. He spoke so that a crowd began to hear itself inside him.

"And of course," he went on, "some records are public, some are private. Some memories sit on the table where everyone can see them. Some stay wrapped away and come out only when the mood is right."

A few faces changed, though not much. Mouths tightened. One person leaned quickly toward another. The room did not need more than that. It had been waiting.

Aminul said, "If there is another document relevant to a claimant, it can be submitted through the same process."

"Submitted," Mofiz repeated pleasantly. "Yes, exactly. Through process. Not through favor. Not through gifts. We all want the same thing."

Halima turned her head. "Then say it with your full tongue. Don't lick around it."

The widow drew in breath through her nose. Aminul's pen stopped moving.

Mofiz gave Halima a small bow of the head, almost respectful. "People are nervous, Halima-dai. That's not slander. It's weather. They hear that a hidden book can make one child belong here, another there. They hear men have carried fish, cash, a duck, whatever they had, and walked away with a cleaner father than the one God gave them. Fear asks questions. I don't stop fear from asking questions."

Halima felt the old schooling in her body before she answered: she must not rise to theater, and she must not feed a crowd a shape it could take hold of. But she had spent too many years in birth rooms where a woman would die if nobody named the thing plainly. She had no gift left for varnish.

"Fear?" she said. "This is not fear. This is you seeing your own roof leak and pointing at mine."

That landed. It was not hard enough to cut him, but it was hard enough to let the people nearest the table feel the contact. Mofiz's beard edges, dyed too dark and always a little rusty at the roots by the second week, twitched once at the jaw.

"Be careful," he said softly. "A respected woman should protect her own respect first."

"Then step away from it."

A boy laughed under his breath and stopped when Mofiz's head tilted half an inch. The room held still. From outside came the flat clank of a hand pump and, farther off, the first testing burst from the mosque loudspeaker, somebody tapping the microphone before the call.

That was when Tuli came running in from the lane, braid half-open, slate-pencil dust still gray on the side of her hand from the sums Nura had made her copy that morning. She slowed only long enough to look frightened of the room she had entered, then pushed toward Halima anyway.

"Khalamma," she said, breath tearing in and out of her. "They're at your house."

The heat in the schoolhouse changed shape. Halima stood before she asked.

"Who?"

"Rasel-bhai and Biplob and more. Chairman sahib's boys. They're saying there's another book. Nura apa is shouting. They pushed the door."

Aminul came halfway out of his chair. "Who's conducting a search?"

Mofiz spread one hand, hurt by the word. "Search is a heavy term, Officer sahib. Some excited young men have gone to ask for reassurance. The island is strained. Let's not criminalize worry."

"You don't ask reassurance with hands," Halima said.

She had already moved clear of the bench. Her knees protested. She ignored them. The room parted for her the way rooms once had when she came carrying hot water and old knowledge, except there was a difference now. The parting was not trust. It was space given to trouble.

Aminul said, "If private property is being interfered with, that is outside the verification procedure."

"Then write that down," Halima said, and did not wait to see whether he would.

Tuli turned and ran again. Halima followed her into the white glare. Behind her, Mofiz's voice floated out in its smooth village-chairman patience.

"Please, everyone. We will settle this with dignity."

He did not sound like a man hurrying to stop anything.

The lane to her house took seven minutes in decent weather. In half-dried mud, with Friday gathering the men toward the mosque and all eyes already leaning ahead of her, it felt longer than the crossing to the mainland. Her sandals dragged. Mud climbed her hems and cooled, then warmed again against her shins. She passed the pond where ducks worried at algae. She passed Jamila's yard, where a row of washed egg trays had been propped in the sun and left. She passed two boys wrestling over a bicycle rim until they saw her face and fell apart from each other at once.

From the mosque came the full call at last, long and practiced, the muezzin's voice sailing over the tin roofs and the fish racks and the wet clotheslines. It would have sounded beautiful on another day. Today it only sharpened the obscenity of what waited ahead: men rinsed for prayer putting their hands into women's trunks.

Halfway there she saw the first cluster of watchers. It was not a crowd, not formally. Women stood at their thresholds. Two old men pretended to discuss a net weight by the lane turn. A girl with a baby on one hip stood without bouncing it, just holding still and looking. Nobody blocked her. Nobody spoke. Thin walls made privacy a lie on the char; public silence finished what the walls started.

Her gate rope hung loose.

Nura was in the courtyard, hair out of its braid, one side of her kameez yanked low where somebody had grabbed cloth and missed flesh by an inch. Her school certificate packet lay split open near the water jar, pages fanned in the dirt like pale fish bellies. Rasel stood at the doorway with a bamboo pole in his hand. He had tied his new white skullcap too high, so both ears stuck out red beneath it. Biplob was on the veranda, breathing through his mouth, his upper lip shiny with the effort of trying to grow a beard that only came in at the chin. A third boy Halima knew only as Selim-from-the-ghat had both hands inside a bedding roll, shaking it as if papers might fall out with the dust.

"Don't touch that," Nura shouted, and went for the certificate packet first.

Rasel swung the bamboo pole toward the loft shelf instead, jabbing at the stacked quilts, the old birth basin, the bundles of washed cloth Halima kept above rat height. A rain of small things came down: string, a cracked feeding cup, a packet of dried neem leaves, a rolled mosquito net. Something rusted hit the floor with a chime.

Halima crossed the courtyard and said, in the voice that used to stop girls from pushing too early and boys from fainting at the sight of blood, "Out."

No one moved.

Mofiz was not inside the house. Of course he was not. He stood just beyond the threshold shade, still in clean cream, damp at the temples from ablution, one hand resting lightly on the doorpost he had not technically crossed. He had arranged himself where he could oversee without touching. Behind him two men from the mosque lane slowed and stayed.

"Please," he said. "Nobody wants insult."

Halima did not look at him. "Then call them out."

"If there is no hidden ledger, this ends quickly. If there is one, people deserve clarity."

Nura laughed once, a sound too sharp to be laughter. "Clarity? Your pigs are in my clothes."

Selim let the bedding roll drop and went to the tin trunk instead. He was the one who always chewed fennel because he wanted to quit cigarettes and never quite managed it. Halima recognized the sweet stale smell when he lifted the lid and plunged both hands in among folded blouses, Nura's bras, the packet of washed cloths for bleeding days, the wrapped comb and mirror, the old yellow frock Halima had kept because Nura had once been small enough to disappear inside it. He held up a bundle and shook it. Menstrual cloths, sun-stiff and square-folded, opened in the air like slapped birds.

The women at the lane edge saw. Halima knew they saw because nobody made a sound.

Nura flew at him. "You bastard."

Biplob caught her by the forearm. She twisted hard enough to leave her nails across the soft part of his wrist. He swore and let go, but Rasel had already come off the step, bamboo pole dropped, both hands on the next obstruction in front of him, which was Halima's granddaughter.

He shoved her once, impatiently, the way a man shoved a door that ought to have opened faster.

Nura hit the iron trunk's hasp with her mouth. The sound was small and mean. Then blood came, bright on her lower lip, then darker when she sucked air through it and split it wider.

For one half second Halima did not see the room. She saw only the blood crossing the brown dust on Nura's chin and the stupid looseness of one hand where Nura had tried to catch herself. It was the same hand that wrote forms cleanly, with the little burn scar at the wrist from tipping a kettle as a child. Halima had spent her whole life being first to blood. It had never once felt ordinary.

"You touched her," she said.

Rasel took one step back. "She came at us."

Nura wiped blood with the heel of her hand and stared at what the hand had become. "Try it again."

The call to prayer still hung in the air outside, not finished, the last notes dragging over the courtyard like wire. Mofiz said, more sharply now because the scene had developed edges he had not planned, "Enough. Conduct yourselves properly."

But nobody left. Properly, on that day, meant the boys kept searching and he kept his hands clean.

Rasel went back to the loft shelf, angrier now. He struck upward with the bamboo pole, once, twice. The second jab tore through the old reed mat Halima had pinned across the back beam. Something heavy shifted behind it.

Halima knew what before it fell. She had taken the ledger out two nights running and hidden it badly each time because exhaustion made fools of older bodies. She had meant to push it deeper after Fajr. Nura had asked for tea. Somebody had come with a witness slip. The morning had eaten the intention whole.

The oilcloth bundle dropped from the shelf and hit the floor between them all with a dense, wet sound.

Nobody in the courtyard moved at first. The cloth was old blue once, then gray, then the color of river tin. One corner had been rewaxed so many times it held a different shine from the rest. It did not look official. It looked worse than official. It looked used.

Nura lunged.

So did Rasel.

His fingers caught one end just as Nura got the middle. The old wrapping gave with a ripping noise that Halima felt in her own teeth. A black notebook corner slid into view. Behind it came three folded sheets in Halima's own hand, copy pages she had kept tucked inside the wrap for quick checking, the names and dates crammed smaller than her eyes liked now.

Then everybody moved at once.

Halima was across the room before she remembered her knee. She drove her shoulder between Rasel and Nura, not to knock him down, only to make a wall where there had not been one. The notebook hit her palm. One of the folded sheets fell open just enough to show columns. Another skated under somebody's heel. Nura, blood on her mouth, bit down on a curse and clutched the torn oilcloth.

From behind Halima, Mofiz's voice came smooth again, because now the thing existed and smoothness could afford to return.

"Careful," he said. "If there is nothing improper, there is no need to panic."

Halima gathered the notebook and what sheets her hands found, then shoved the whole torn mass inside the front of her blouse, under the crossed sari edge, against the hot fold of skin below her breasts. She was sixty-three years old and broad-palmed and sweating. She had delivered half the island. If one of them reached there in front of the lane and the mosque call and the staring women, nobody on the char would ever pretend again that this was only a search.

Rasel stopped. So did Biplob. Even Selim, with a pair of Nura's underwear hanging from two fingers because he had forgotten to drop them, froze and looked away first.

That was the only boundary left to her, and she hated them for leaving her nothing else.

"Come take it, then," she said.

Nobody answered.

Nura spat a clot of blood onto Rasel's foot.

"Cowards."

Mofiz did not step inside. He folded his hands in front of him, chairman again, grievance settled into community language. "You see the problem," he said to nobody and everybody. "A private register affecting public claims. Hidden from process. Hidden from scrutiny. This is exactly why people are worried."

Halima's breath came hard enough to lift the notebook against her ribs. She wanted to go for his throat with words. She wanted, more stupidly, to go for it with her hands. What came out was flatter than either.

"You sent boys into a woman's room on Friday and found an old book. If God was measuring shame by weight, you'd sink through the char."

Somebody in the lane murmured. It was not agreement. It might have been shock, or admiration too thin to stand on.

Mofiz inclined his head as if receiving public insult was itself a public service. "No one is accusing you yet," he said. "But this will have to be explained properly."

The word yet did its work and sat down.

He turned to the boys. "Enough. Leave the room."

Now they listened. Rasel set the bamboo pole aside. Selim dropped the underwear back into the trunk with two fingers, suddenly delicate. Biplob looked at the blood on his wrist where Nura had scratched him and seemed uncertain whether he was proud of it or ashamed. One by one they backed out into the courtyard, taking their muddy footprints with them only in the sense that feet always did. The prints stayed.

Halima stood until the last of them had cleared the threshold. Only then did she let her hand leave the front of her blouse and go to Nura's face.

"Sit."

"I'm fine."

"Sit before I put you there."

The command still worked on one person in the house. Nura dropped onto the edge of the trunk, wincing when the movement jarred her hip. Blood kept welling from the split in the lower lip, not fast, but steadily enough to stain the front of her kameez.

Outside, Mofiz was speaking to the watchers in his lowered public voice, the one that implied sorrow and administration in equal measure. Halima could not catch every word. She caught enough words to hear the shape of it: regrettable, necessary, community trust, hidden entries, proper review.

By the time the courtyard thinned, the story had already learned how to walk without him.

Inside the room, the heat felt fouled. Male sweat, damp prayer cloth, the fennel stink from Selim's mouth, mud ground into the mat where Nura slept. Halima fetched the brass lota with shaking fingers, rinsed a clean cloth, and came back to her granddaughter.

"Hold still."

Nura did, but only from the neck down. The rest of her was all insult.

"Don't dab," she said thickly. "Press it."

"If I press harder you'll bite me."

"I'm already biting."

Halima folded the cloth smaller and put steady pressure to the lip. Nura hissed and caught Halima's wrist. The burn scar flashed white against her own blood.

"Teeth apart," Halima said.

Nura obeyed. Pink water ran over Halima's fingers and down into the hollow between Nura's collarbones. Under the blood smell lay the dry-paper smell of the certificate packet and the older smell of a house opened too roughly: rice dust, kerosene, stored cloth, things that should only know women's hands.

"He touched my trunk," Nura said after a moment. She named the trunk before the injury or the blood. "That fennel-breathed bastard put his hands in my trunk."

"I saw."

"And that one with the cap. He pushed me like I was a goat in the lane."

Halima looked at the swelling starting along Nura's mouth and at the darkening patch on the side of her hip where the cloth had tightened. Under the girl-angry, there was a tremor she would deny if named.

"Lie down after this."

"No."

"You may want a different answer. That one stays the same."

Nura laughed and immediately regretted it. "You should've let me take his eye."

"And then what? Let them drag you through the yard by your hair while the whole lane watched?"

Nura pulled her hand free and wiped the side of her face with her own palm. "They already had my bras in their hands. What's left to save?"

That went through Halima cleaner than a shout would have. She turned away long enough to wring out the cloth over the basin. The water pinked, then clouded.

She surveyed the room properly for the first time. The trunk stood open. Bedding lay torn loose. Birth cloth bundles were split. Nura's certificates were creased in the dirt near the water jar. The old basin lay on its side. The loft shelf hung open at the back where the reed mat had been ripped down. The house looked less robbed than translated into the wrong language.

Her hand went instinctively to the notebook hidden under her blouse. It was still there, though the oilcloth was torn and the copy pages sat uneven against it. She did not dare unwrap it yet, not with Nura bleeding and the lane still full of ears.

"We'll wash the papers after your mouth settles," she said.

"My mouth's settled. The whole char is what's not settled."

Halima gave her a look. Nura gave one back, lip swelling around the contempt.

Neither of them said what sat between them: the boys had found the book because the book existed. Mofiz had not created that part from rumor. He had only chosen the moment and the witnesses.

The first person to come after the courtyard emptied was not Aminul and not a neighbor bearing sympathy. It was Jamila.

She paused at the threshold as if measuring the distance from her sandals to the room's disorder. Jamila was dressed as carefully as if she were going to market instead of stepping into a violated house: sari pleats straight, hair oiled and parted cleanly, blouse pinned near one shoulder where the hook had loosened. She carried no basket. That told Halima more than if she had come empty-handed in tears. People brought baskets when they meant comfort. They brought bare hands when they meant retrieval.

Majed waited just outside the gate with a screwdriver tucked behind one ear and grease still dark in the cuticle of his thumb. He did not come in. He kept one hand on the gate rope as though testing whether it would hold if the char got any softer underfoot.

Jamila's gaze flicked once to Nura's swollen mouth, once to the open trunk, then settled carefully on Halima's face.

"I heard there was a disturbance," she said.

The extra politeness made the sentence colder than accusation.

Halima said, "You heard right."

Jamila nodded. "I left my papers here after the other night."

There it was.

Nura gave a short laugh that broke into pain halfway through. "Of course."

Jamila did not look at her. "I thought it was safer. Now people are saying anything kept in this house will be looked at. I've got children. I can't let their file get mixed into another story."

Halima could have answered with anger. She had earned some. Jamila had sat in this very room two nights ago and told the truth of her own life in a voice so controlled it had felt like cut bamboo under Halima's palm. Halima had watched her choose one loyalty over another because the state had turned kinship into arithmetic. But the arithmetic did not stop because the numbers hurt.

"Top shelf of the smaller trunk," Halima said.

Jamila crossed the room without touching anything else. She bent, lifted the packet wrapped in old calendar paper, checked the twine knot, and held it to her stomach as if the papers could feel exposed too.

Majed spoke from the yard, not entering, not quite absent either. "Are they saying the officer saw the book?"

Halima said, "The officer saw nothing. The chairman's boys saw enough."

Majed rubbed the side of his nose with the back of his wrist, leaving a gray streak there. "That'll do."

Jamila still did not accuse her. That was not how Jamila cut when angry. She cut by sounding reasonable.

"Khalamma," she said, "if they ask, I didn't leave our packet here overnight."

Halima felt the shape of the request and all the filth packed inside it. Jamila was not only asking her to protect them. She was asking to be protected from being known as needing her.

"Take your papers," she said. "Tell whatever story lets your children eat."

Jamila's chin moved once, almost a flinch. For a second Halima thought she might answer from the softer place, the place that still remembered being fifteen and moved like contraband from one house to another to escape a debt marriage. But Majed shifted outside. The screwdriver behind his ear glinted in the light. Household came first. Household always.

"I didn't come to fight," Jamila said quietly.

"No," Halima said. "You came in time."

Jamila absorbed that without showing where it landed. She touched Nura's shoulder once, lightly, because she was not all caution yet. "Put alum on it," she said. "Not just water."

"We know."

"I know you know."

She left. Majed stepped aside to let her pass, then followed without meeting Halima's eyes. The gate rope rasped shut behind them.

Nura stared at the doorway a moment longer than the absence required.

"You going to tell me she was only being practical?" she asked.

Halima folded the bloody cloth and set it in the basin. "She was being exactly herself."

"That's a pretty way to say coward."

"No. Cowards are the boys who needed a chairman to lend them courage."

Nura touched her lip and winced. "Then what is she?"

Halima looked at the empty threshold where Jamila had stood holding her packet to her own stomach as if papers could be injured there and nowhere else. "Fed by the same fire as the rest of us."

By late afternoon the house had been put back into enough shape to prove it could still be lived in, which was not the same as repair. Halima re-folded cloths with hands that wanted to shake. She brushed dirt from the certificates and laid them under the least warped part of the trunk lid. She pinned the torn reed mat aside and told herself she would mend it at dusk, knowing she would not. The notebook remained hidden against her body until the heat of it became intolerable. Then she rewrapped it in another cloth, tied it tight, and shoved it under the rice bin for the moment. She still did not count the copy pages. Her head felt full of metal filings. Counting required a steadier mind.

When the cloth in the basin dried stiff with Nura's blood, she took the small brass bowl and went to Sufia's stall for alum.

The path to the ghat had not changed. That was what hurt about it. The same duck droppings dried in the lane. The same fish scales glittered near the racks. The same boys slapped cards onto an upturned crate. But the people in it had adjusted themselves around her by some quick communal intelligence that needed no meeting and no notice on the school wall. Conversations narrowed as she approached. Eyes moved, then moved back too late to pretend they had not moved.

Sufia was at the stall cutting lime with the little black-handled knife she used for everything from guava to betel nut. By afternoon her bad knee always stiffened, and she had the habit of leaning her hip against the counter between customers, one foot angled out to spare the joint. Her lips were red from betel, but not freshly. She had been talking before Halima came within earshot. Now she was not.

Kashem sat on the bench with one knee up, heel planted, chipped tooth showing where his lip had caught on it as he worried the inside of his mouth. He looked like a man who had come for tea and stayed for injury. Beside him were two others from the river, cups in hand, bodies turned the lazy way men turned when they wanted a thing to look accidental.

Sufia said, too evenly, "Tea's gone bitter. Want some anyway?"

"Alum," Halima said. "A lump if you've got it."

Sufia reached under the counter. Glass jars clicked. Nobody else spoke. The silence was worse than open insult for the length of one breath.

Then Kashem said, not to Halima and very much to her, "I used to think the old stories were mercy."

One of the men beside him muttered, "Leave it."

Kashem ignored him. He had the fisherman habit of talking in circles until the hook set. "You know. A name bent here, a date slid there. We all said it. Dai-ma knows how people survive. Dai-ma knows where a child belongs if the paper doesn't. Fine. Fine. That was what I told myself."

Sufia put the alum on the counter. She did not slide it toward Halima.

Kashem laughed once. It carried no pleasure in it. "Now look. A hidden book under the blouse like smuggled money. Boys pulling it out in daylight. So what was it, then? Mercy? Or business?"

Halima put coins on the counter one by one because her fingers needed something smaller than his face.

"Mind your tongue," Sufia said, but the words came late and weak, like a lid set on a boiling pot after the milk had already run over.

Kashem turned then. His eyes were bloodshot, whether from the river wind or shame or lack of sleep Halima could not tell. "No, let me ask plain. How much did a father cost in your house, Halima-dai? A fish basket? Two witnesses? A little cash tucked in the ledger?"

The bench men did not laugh. That made it worse. Laughter still left room for embarrassment. Their stillness meant the question had already become thinkable.

Halima picked up the alum.

"If money were what made fathers," she said, "half the men on this char would have bought themselves better children."

One of the bench men let out air through his nose. Kashem's face tightened, not because he had been defeated but because she had denied him the full public fight he wanted. His pride needed volume. She gave him a smaller wound.

"There," he said. "Hear that? Still talking like she's above the rest of us. Not a midwife now. A writer for hire."

The phrase sat between the tea glasses. Writer for hire was not even clever enough to be cruel. Cruelty on the char usually came plainer. The words paid forger came from the man beside him a second later, almost thoughtful, as if he were testing how they balanced together.

"That's the trouble with a paid forger," he said. "You don't know when she was helping and when she was selling."

Nobody corrected him.

Sufia finally slid the alum closer. Her fingers were quick as ever, tea-stall fingers, good at handling heat without drama. "Take it," she said to Halima. "It melts if you leave it open."

Ordinary detail came first. That was always her way.

Halima took the lump and the change due back to her and left without tea.

The sun dropped redder toward the river. Smoke from cooking fires thickened low across the lane. Somewhere a baby started its hungry evening cry, stopped, started again. She walked home carrying the alum and the new title with equal care because both would sting on contact.

At her own gate she paused. From two houses down came the slow animal sound of a young woman trying not to moan through the start of real pain. It was Nasima, on her first baby, narrow-hipped enough that Halima had been watching her belly ride low for a week. Usually the first strong pains brought a child running before the second wave finished. That was not because Halima was miraculous. It was because she was close, and because people trusted the distance between her hand and disaster.

She waited without meaning to wait.

Inside, Nura shifted on the mat and called, "Did you get it?"

Halima said, "Yes."

The moan came again from Nasima's yard. Then the slap of bare feet on packed earth, quick and small, heading down the lane.

The footsteps passed Halima's door without slowing.

At the lane turn a boy's voice rose, carrying clearly in the evening thinness.

"Go get Rokeya-khala. Not Halima. Ma said not her."

Halima stood with the alum in her palm until the damp from it started eating into her skin.
