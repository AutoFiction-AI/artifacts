# Secondhand Thoughts — Novel Outline

## Narrative Engine

**Primary mode:** Action comedy-drama — a combination of comic escalation with genuine human stakes, espionage thriller driven by tactical choices and consequences, love story driven by relational trust and discovery, and character study through identity pressure.

The comedy is the dominant register for roughly the first two-thirds of the novel. Humor arises from character (Anya's telepathic eavesdropping, the Forger family's absurd secret-keeping, Damian's transparent inner monologue), situation (spies pretending to be normal people, an assassin who can't cook, a telepath falling for someone whose thoughts she can't stop hearing), and the gap between what characters say and what they think. The tonal shift toward sustained dramatic pressure begins in Chapter 13, when the operation collapses and people die. Comedy does not vanish after that point — the Forger family's warmth and Anya's voice keep comic texture alive — but it stops being the dominant mode. The final four chapters are drama with comic undertone, not comedy with dramatic stakes.

**What drives chapters:** Comic situations that escalate through character logic; espionage operations that create constraint and consequence; relational dynamics between Anya and Damian where trust is the operative currency; and the pressure of secrets — who knows what, who's pretending, and what happens when pretense fails. Beneath all of this: the compounding moral weight of telepathy as a form of violation, and the loneliness of knowing everyone's truth while no one knows yours.

## Premise Summary

Fourteen years after Operation Strix, the fragile peace between Westalis and Ostania is fraying. Anya Forger — now a twenty-year-old WISE operative and secret telepath, trained under her father Loid's guidance — is assigned to investigate a suspicious figure within Ostania's leadership. She discovers it's Damian Desmond, son of the man Operation Strix was designed to neutralize, now working covertly to stop the Renewal Front: a nationalist faction within Ostania pushing both nations toward war. Forced into an alliance built on mutual need and mutual suspicion, Anya and Damian must expose a conspiracy that reaches into both nations' intelligence services — while the childhood bond between them threatens to become something neither can control or afford.

The Forger family — Loid the legendary spy, Yor the assassin, Bond the precognitive dog, and Anya the telepath — remains a family of secrets that became real. Anya is the only person who knows everyone's truth, and she has never told anyone hers.

## Chapter Plan

### Chapter 1 — Noise and Signal
**Engine:** Comic escalation / discovery / preparation
**POV:** Anya

Anya runs a routine intelligence extraction in Berlint. She's surveilling a dead-drop site, managing the cacophony of surface thoughts from pedestrians — petty grievances, grocery lists, an affair, a man rehearsing an argument with his landlord. The handoff goes wrong: her WISE contact is being tailed. Anya improvises an extraction using her telepathy to anticipate the pursuers' movements, steering her contact through a market crowd. The comedy lives in the gap between the seriousness of espionage and the banality of overheard thoughts. The extraction succeeds, messily.

At WISE headquarters, Sylvia briefs a small team: intelligence suggests someone within Ostania's State Security apparatus is running an unauthorized covert operation — purpose unknown, but the activity pattern is consistent with pre-conflict positioning. Anya is assigned to identify and assess the operative. During the briefing, Anya reads her colleagues' surface thoughts — one analyst is thinking about his daughter's recital, another is composing a grocery list, a third has a complicated crush on Sylvia. A fourth — a senior analyst named Falk — is thinking about budget allocations and whether WISE's threat assessments are inflated to justify its operating costs. The comedy of telepathy in a spy agency: everyone's inner life is mundane even when the briefing is dire. The cynical thought from Falk registers as an odd note but not an alarm.

Afterward, Anya meets Loid at a café. Their father-daughter dynamic has evolved: he speaks to her as a fellow operative but his thoughts betray protective worry he can't suppress. He gives tactical advice. She reads his fear for her safety and doesn't mention it. He doesn't know she can. She walks home through Berlint, reading the city's anxious undercurrent — more people thinking about the border, about soldiers, about whether the peace will hold. Every mind she passes is a person who doesn't know she's inside their head. She has never once been inside someone else's head with their permission.

**State shift:** The world is established. Anya is competent but her telepathy is both weapon and burden — and, quietly, a violation she has been committing her entire life. The mission is assigned. The gap between what Anya knows and what she can say begins to press.

### Chapter 2 — The Diplomat's Son
**Engine:** Discovery / revelation
**POV:** Anya

Anya infiltrates an Ostanian state dinner under cover as a junior cultural attaché. She works the room with telepathy, scanning surface thoughts for operational intelligence. Fragments accumulate: references to "the initiative," anxiety about a timeline, someone thinking about a weapons shipment. She follows the strongest thread and it leads to a man standing alone near the terrace — Damian Desmond, now a rising figure in Ostania's diplomatic corps. She reads his unguarded thoughts and the world tilts. He's not building the conspiracy. He's trying to dismantle it. He's running a covert counter-operation against nationalist hardliners in his own government, using his father's old network against the people who want to finish what Donovan Desmond started. His thoughts also carry older freight: Eden Academy, a girl with pink hair who punched him when they were six, a name he almost forms but doesn't — *what was her name?* — a friendship he never understood and has never quite let go of. Anya stands in a ballroom full of diplomats, reading the mind of a boy she used to know, and realizes this mission just became the most complicated thing she's ever done.

**State shift:** The target is Damian. The mission is personal. Anya holds asymmetric knowledge — she knows his secret, his operation, and his feelings, and he doesn't know she exists in his orbit. The childhood recognition is a ticking clock she doesn't yet understand.

### Chapter 3 — The Report
**Engine:** Institutional betrayal / seduction (espionage sense)
**POV:** Anya

Anya must file her initial assessment for WISE. This is the chapter where the premise's unique operational pressures converge: she discovered the truth via telepathy she cannot disclose, the target is running a legitimate counter-operation she believes in, and reporting honestly means burning an ally whose unguarded feelings she has already absorbed. She sits across from Sylvia in a debriefing room and files a misleading report — framing Damian as a mid-level diplomatic figure of uncertain loyalty whose activities are consistent with internal Ostanian factional maneuvering, not a direct threat to Westalian security. She minimizes the Renewal Front intelligence. She downgrades the threat assessment. While she speaks, she reads Sylvia's thoughts: Sylvia trusts her completely. The trust is specific — Sylvia is thinking about how Anya's field judgment has been reliable for two years, how she reminds her of Loid at his best. Anya watches Sylvia accept the lie while reading the trust that makes the lie possible. This is not a transition between scenes. This is the moment Anya betrays her own organization, and she feels every gram of it.

After the debrief, Anya engineers a "chance" encounter with Damian at a cultural exchange event. She approaches under her cover identity. His recognition is partial — something about her face, her laugh, the way she tilts her head. He can't place it. Their conversation is a dual-layer game: what they say aloud versus what Damian thinks. He's guarded, professionally charming, but his thoughts are a storm of half-recognition and unexpected attraction. Anya reads all of it while maintaining her cover. The comedy is sharp: he thinks something embarrassing about her smile and she has to not react. He mentally rehearses a smooth line and then delivers it poorly. She pushes the connection further than protocol allows because she needs more access — and because, reading his loneliness and his stubborn decency, she wants to. He agrees to meet again.

**State shift:** Anya has committed institutional betrayal by filing a false report — a specific, traceable lie with consequences she cannot foresee. Contact with Damian is established. She is now operating under two deceptions: her telepathy and her false intelligence.

### Chapter 4 — Terms
**Engine:** Confrontation / forced alliance
**POV:** Anya

At an Ostanian policy forum, a Renewal Front operative approaches Damian — pressuring him about his attendance at a closed security meeting. Anya, nearby, reads the operative's surface thoughts: they suspect Damian is disloyal. The operative is considering whether to report him. Anya intervenes with a social diversion — inserting herself into the conversation as the attaché, derailing the operative's pressure with a mix of charm and pointed questions about trade policy that forces a subject change. Afterward, Damian confronts her in a service corridor. He's not stupid. She knows too much about the right things. She reveals that she's intelligence — not which service, not her telepathy — just enough to establish that they share an enemy. Tense negotiation follows. Damian's thoughts cycle through suspicion, calculation, reluctant hope. He doesn't trust easily; his father was undone by trusting the wrong people. Anya can read his internal debate in real time, which gives her an advantage but makes her feel like she's cheating — worse now, because she's already cheated WISE for him. She negotiated with Sylvia holding a secret; she negotiates with Damian holding a different one. Every successful deception widens the gap between Anya and every person she deals with. They agree to a provisional alliance with explicit terms: shared intelligence on the Renewal Front, no questions about each other's networks, and either party can walk away. Neither believes the last part.

**State shift:** The alliance forms. Trust is the operative currency and neither has much. Anya carries the moral weight of two deceptions — her telepathic advantage in negotiation, and the false report that made this alliance possible.

### Chapter 5 — The Informant
**Engine:** Comic escalation / joint operation
**POV:** Anya

First joint mission. They need to extract a name from Colonel Bretz, a mid-level Ostanian military officer with suspected Renewal Front ties, at a private social club. The plan requires Anya and Damian to pose as a couple attending a members' reception. Comic escalation from the start: Damian's thoughts while they're pretending to be together are a car crash of composure and panic ("Don't think about her hand on your arm. Don't think about it. You're thinking about it."). At the club, the operation cascades: Colonel Bretz's enormous dog immediately fixates on Anya, following her everywhere; the Colonel's wife mistakes Damian for a minor celebrity and won't stop talking; Anya reads Bretz's thoughts while pretending to admire his art collection and the name she needs surfaces between thoughts about his sciatica and his resentment of his brother-in-law. The extraction of the name happens through a perfectly timed series of absurd improvisations. In the aftermath — debriefing in a park at night, still in formal wear — they have the intelligence and the first real ease between them. Damian makes a joke. Anya laughs genuinely. His thought: *She has the same laugh.* Not just familiar — he's thinking of a specific girl, a specific schoolyard, a specific afternoon. The memory is close to surfacing fully. He shakes it off. Anya, reading it, does not.

**State shift:** First successful collaboration. Their dynamic crystallizes — bickering competence, comic friction, mutual respect they haven't named. The recognition is building like a slow fuse, and Anya can see it burning.

### Chapter 6 — Home Cooking
**Engine:** Domestic pressure / forced decision
**POV:** Anya

Anya visits the Forger apartment. Yor is cooking — a new recipe she's excited about, which means the kitchen smells like burning and hope. Bond, elderly now, is asleep on the couch with his legs twitching through some precognitive dream. Loid is doing dishes because he got to the kitchen first and quietly made a backup meal. The texture of the early chapter is domestic, warm, and deliberately low-stakes: a neighbor's complaints about noise, Yor's colleague at city hall, Bond's new habit of barking at pigeons.

Underneath: Anya reads their thoughts. Loid is worrying about the rising tensions and what it means for Anya's safety. Yor is thinking about a recent Garden assignment that went wrong — a target who begged, a detail in the briefing that didn't match what she found on-site. The thought is quick and Yor buries it, but Anya catches it: her mother is beginning to doubt whether the Garden's targets are as clean as she was told. This is operational intelligence about Garden operations that Anya never wanted and cannot use without revealing how she obtained it. She does to her mother what she does to Damian. The violation is not romantic. It is structural to who she is.

Bond wakes abruptly. Not the slow surfacing of an old dog but a sudden rigid alertness, eyes fixed on nothing. Anya reads his fragmentary vision: violence at a government building she recognizes — an image of uniformed men, a flash of fire, a specific architectural detail. The vision is clear enough to be actionable. She must decide: warn Loid, which means explaining how she knows? Say nothing and hope Bond is wrong? She compromises — she mentions, casually, that she's heard rumors about security concerns at government events, planting the seed without revealing the source. Loid's spy instincts catch the false note. His thoughts shift: he's evaluating her delivery the way he'd evaluate an agent's cover story. He doesn't press, but he notices.

The evening is interrupted by a secure call from Sylvia. A follow-up about Anya's report on the Ostanian operative — another agency has flagged unusual activity in the same sector Anya downgraded. Sylvia's tone is routine, not suspicious. But the discrepancy is registering somewhere in WISE's analytical machinery. Anya must reinforce her lie on the phone while sitting in her family's kitchen, Yor humming over burned food in the background. She's keeping telepathy from her family, keeping Damian from WISE, and now keeping Garden intelligence from everyone. A moment near the end: Anya in her childhood bedroom, looking at old things, feeling every secret she carries for this family pressing down at once.

**State shift:** Bond's vision demands future action. Anya now holds unwanted Garden intelligence. Sylvia's follow-up signals the false report is creating institutional pressure. The chapter is no longer a breather — it is a trap that Anya's accumulated deceptions have built around her.

### Chapter 7 — The Desmond Name
**Engine:** Character study / revelation
**POV:** Damian

Damian's chapter — the first extended stretch from his perspective, without Anya's telepathy narrating him. His day: a morning meeting with loyalists in the National Unity Party where he performs hawkish rhetoric he doesn't believe. Lunch with his older brother Demetrius, who manages the Desmond political legacy. Demetrius views Damian as a useful but unreliable asset. Their father's vision was Ostanian dominance — and Demetrius does not share it as ideology but maintains it as infrastructure, because the Desmond network's value depends on the political framework that created it. Demetrius has connections to defense contractors and political figures who benefit from tension with Westalis. He is not the Renewal Front's leader, but he inhabits the ecosystem that makes the Front possible. The fraternal dynamic carries operational stakes: the brother who maintains the father's machine versus the brother working to dismantle it.

Afternoon: Damian uses his father's old intelligence network to investigate the Renewal Front. A retired aide named Grosz, who served Donovan for thirty years, reveals the Front's structure — a coalition of military hardliners and civilian nationalists, funded through defense procurement contracts, led by a figure Grosz won't name directly but describes as "someone who lost enough to believe peace is theft." Grosz also drops a detail that complicates Damian's assumptions: Westalian defense interests have been quietly purchasing Ostanian military intelligence through intermediary firms. The arms buildup isn't unilateral — there are people on the Westalian side who profit from tension too. The political picture is not Ostanian nationalism versus Westalian peace. It is a system that benefits from instability, with profiteers on both sides of the border.

Evening: Damian alone in his apartment. The weight of being Donovan Desmond's son — inheriting a political machine built on the premise that Ostania's strength requires Westalis's weakness. A box of childhood things he never unpacked. Inside it, an Eden Academy class photo. Anya is in the second row, grinning at something off-camera. He stares at it longer than he means to.

**State shift:** The Renewal Front gets structure and a motivation. The political picture gains genuine complexity — the conspiracy is bilateral, not binary. Damian's isolation is made concrete: he is surrounded by people who believe in things he's working to undermine, and even the side he's helping may not be as clean as he assumed.

### Chapter 8 — The Reception
**Engine:** Comic escalation / espionage / revelation
**POV:** Anya

An international diplomatic reception — both Westalian and Ostanian delegations in attendance. Major set piece. Anya works the room with telepathy pushed to its limits: dozens of minds, overlapping, fragmented. Comic beats: a Westalian trade minister mentally rehearsing his affair-excuse for later; an Ostanian general thinking exclusively about the shrimp; a young diplomat composing a breakup text in her head. Anya catches a WISE colleague's pragmatic thought that echoes what Damian discovered through Grosz — something about adjusted threat matrices and the operational value of maintained tension. Her own agency's calculations are not purely noble.

Anya and Damian work separate trajectories through the crowd, coordinating through brief exchanges. Damian sees Anya from across the room during one of these moments and the childhood memory nearly surfaces fully — Eden Academy, pink hair, the school festival where they ended up on the same cleanup crew. His thoughts crystallize toward a name he hasn't said in years. Anya reads this happening in real time and must actively manage the risk. She steers a subsequent conversation to create a cover explanation for the familiarity — mentioning a diplomatic training program that might account for a vague sense of having met before.

Yuri Briar is present — Yor's brother, now a senior SSS officer providing event security. Anya reads his thoughts: professional suspicion about the Westalian delegation, fierce protective instincts about Yor, and a flicker of something when he looks at Anya — he's noticed she's attending more government events than a cultural attaché should. The critical moment: in the crowd noise, Anya catches a thought-fragment from someone near the Westalian delegation — a reference to "the asset inside WISE" and a handoff timeline. She can't identify the thinker — the room is too dense, the thought vanished — but the content is devastating. Someone inside WISE is feeding intelligence to the Renewal Front. Her own people are compromised. The isolation hits: she discovered this through an ability no one knows about, while hiding intelligence she's already withheld, while managing a childhood recognition that could destroy her cover. She is the most connected person in the room and the most alone.

**State shift:** The threat is bilateral — WISE has a mole. Anya cannot trust her own organization. Damian's recognition is approaching critical mass. Yuri's professional suspicion is seeded. The political complexity of both nations' complicity deepens.

### Chapter 9 — Safe House
**Engine:** Pursuit / survival into intimacy / aftermath
**POV:** Anya

Damian is attacked leaving a late meeting — two assailants, military-trained, ambush him in a side street. Anya intercepts. She was tailing him (she'd read hostile intention from a man following Damian earlier that evening) and reaches the scene as the first attacker moves. The fight is fast, ugly, and real — Anya's combat training under Loid and Yor shows, but she takes a hit to the ribs and Damian catches an elbow that splits his eyebrow. They escape. Safe house: a WISE fallback apartment, cold and bare. Patching wounds — his split brow, her bruised side. He asks how she knew. She deflects: "I'm good at reading people." He doesn't fully buy it but he's too shaken and grateful to push. The attack may have been enabled by the mole — someone knew Damian's movements, and the intelligence chain that placed him at that meeting connects to WISE reporting. Anya's false report, which downgraded Damian's profile, may have created gaps in the operational picture that the mole exploited. She doesn't know this yet. But the reader should sense the causal shadow.

The adrenaline fades into proximity. Quiet conversation: childhood memories. Eden Academy, the time Anya punched him during a dodgeball game, the school festival where they ended up on the same cleanup crew. They laugh — real laughter, the kind that comes from exhaustion and relief. The laughter shifts. A silence that's not empty. Anya hears his thoughts about her, vivid and unguarded, and has to decide which of her own feelings are genuine and which are influenced by what she's hearing. A kiss — tentative, then not. She doesn't read his mind during it. She tries not to. She mostly fails. What she catches is not just attraction — it's his loneliness, his stubborn hope, and a recognition that's almost fully formed. He's dreaming of Eden Academy, of Anya Forger specifically. The name is right there. She reads it surfacing and feels the dual terror: that he'll place her and the cover shatters, and that this kiss is built on a knowledge asymmetry that makes genuine intimacy impossible.

**State shift:** Physical danger has been survived. The emotional boundary between Anya and Damian is crossed. Anya's telepathy during intimacy creates a moral problem she can't resolve — she has consumed his unguarded feelings without his consent, and she cannot distinguish her response from his. The recognition is now imminent.

### Chapter 10 — Thorn Princess
**Engine:** Parallel operation / family collision / institutional reckoning
**POV:** Yor

Yor's chapter. The Garden contacts her with an assignment: eliminate Karl Dominic, a weapons broker whose supply chain has been linked to recent arms buildup along the Ostanian border. Yor prepares — the contrast between her domestic sweetness (she packs a lunch, worries about whether Loid remembered to buy milk) and her operational lethality. The mission itself: Yor infiltrates Dominic's compound during a private sale. Combat sequence — Yor at full capacity, terrifying and precise, taking apart a security detail with the efficiency of someone who's been doing this for twenty years. She kills Dominic.

In his office, she finds shipping manifests linking his network to addresses associated with Ostania's defense ministry and a name she doesn't recognize: Werner Voss. But she finds something worse beneath the manifests: a set of subcontracting records showing the Garden itself listed as a client. The Garden has been accepting elimination contracts that serve the Renewal Front's supply-chain management — removing brokers who become liabilities, cleaning up loose ends. Dominic was both a target and a former business partner. The Garden sent Yor to kill a man whose death removes a loose end for the conspiracy she doesn't know exists. Her twenty years of killing may not have been in service of something clean. The thought is not fully formed yet — she photographs everything — but the foundation of her rationalization is cracking.

Separately, Anya has been tracking a parallel lead on the Renewal Front's weapons supply and arrives at the compound's perimeter. She reads a thought from inside — tactical calculations in a voice she'd know anywhere: her mother's. The collision: Anya and Yor encounter each other at the compound's exit. A beat of absurd mutual recognition. Neither explains. Neither asks. Yor says, "You should eat more, you look thin." Anya says, "Your hands are bleeding, Mama." They part. They don't discuss it. Anya, reading Yor's thoughts in that moment, catches the new doubt about the Garden. She does to her mother what she does to everyone — enters her unguarded interior without permission. And what she finds there is her mother beginning to wonder whether the violence she has rationalized for two decades has been clean. Anya carries this knowledge away from the compound knowing she stole it.

**State shift:** Yor discovers the Garden has ties to the conspiracy — her violence has been instrumentalized by the forces she should be opposing. Family and mission boundaries have collapsed. Anya's telepathic violation of her mother's private doubt mirrors the violation she committed against Damian. The pattern is structural, not romantic.

### Chapter 11 — The Short List
**Engine:** Investigation / constraint-heavy ordeal
**POV:** Anya

Anya works to identify the WISE mole. She can't simply read everyone at headquarters — the building is too large, people's thoughts scatter across dozens of topics, and sustained close-range reading would require proximity she can't explain. She combines telepathy with traditional tradecraft: reviewing access logs, mapping who knew what when, and engineering brief encounters with suspects where she can read them. She narrows the mole to three analysts.

One of the three — not Falk — is radiating anxiety. His surface thoughts during Anya's engineered encounter are jumpy, secretive, consumed by something he's hiding. Anya reads this and is nearly certain: this is the mole. She acts on the conclusion, directing investigative resources toward this analyst and away from the other two candidates. She is wrong. The analyst's secret is personal — a health diagnosis he hasn't told his family about, a sick fear that has nothing to do with espionage. Anya's telepathy read the surface accurately — anxiety, concealment, dread — but surface thoughts are not intentions. The most powerful intelligence tool in the world just fed her a wrong answer because people think about what frightens them, not what defines them. The misdirection costs two days they don't have.

Gerald Falk, one of the remaining candidates, is someone Anya has known since childhood — a man who attended her Eden Academy graduation and gave her a fountain pen. Reading Falk's surface thoughts in a hallway gives her nothing conclusive — he's disciplined, his mind is orderly, and he's thinking about a budget meeting. The ambiguity is worse than confirmation. Meanwhile, reading her WISE colleagues to investigate the mole feels like specific hypocrisy: she violates their mental privacy to protect the secrecy of her own unearned advantage. She is the organization's internal surveillance, appointed by no one, accountable to no one, conducting investigations through a power she has never disclosed. Every justified use of telepathy reinforces the same violation she is supposed to feel guilty about.

Meanwhile, Damian meets with his Ostanian allies under increasing pressure. The Renewal Front is accelerating: a diplomatic incident is being engineered, designed to be blamed on Westalis and justify Ostanian military mobilization. They have days, not weeks. A tense encrypted call between Anya and Damian — him frustrated by political constraints, her frustrated by the mole she misidentified and can't yet name. The operational comedy of espionage: code words, dead drops, speaking in euphemisms while both knowing the other is holding back.

**State shift:** The mole investigation has been set back by Anya's misplaced confidence in her own ability. Telepathy produced a wrong answer — surface thoughts are not truth. The timeline compresses. The luxury of careful investigation is gone.

### Chapter 12 — Old Friends
**Engine:** Social pressure / comic escalation / recognition crisis
**POV:** Anya

Becky Blackbell's charity gala — an East-West cultural exchange benefit that Becky, now a media personality and socialite, organized with characteristic dramatic flair. Anya attends because declining would raise questions and because she needs to be seen maintaining her cover identity's social calendar. Damian is there for political reasons. Becky greets Anya with operatic warmth — they haven't seen each other in months, and Becky's affection is genuine, loud, and impossible to deflect.

The social minefield: Becky introduces Anya and Damian to former Eden Academy classmates. Old photos circulate. Someone digs up a picture from the school festival where Anya and Damian are standing next to each other looking furious. Then the moment the recognition thread has been building toward: a former classmate, three drinks in, addresses Damian directly — "Wait, you and Anya Forger had that whole rivalry thing in school, right? What was that about?" The name lands in the room. Anya reads Damian's thoughts in real time: the pieces locking together. Pink hair. The laugh. The tilt of her head. Anya Forger. The cultural attaché sitting next to him is Anya Forger from Eden Academy — which means a Westalian intelligence operative has been embedded in his orbit since childhood, which means the friendship that shaped his boyhood was, possibly, always operational. He doesn't say it aloud. His expression doesn't change. But his thoughts are a controlled detonation. Anya reads every bit of it and must sit there, smiling, holding a champagne glass, while her cover identity disintegrates inside the mind of the only person she can't afford to lose. She deflects the classmate's comment with a practiced laugh and a vague denial — wrong Anya, common name, funny coincidence. Damian says nothing to contradict her. He is thinking very carefully about what to do with what he now knows.

On a terrace, away from the noise, Becky finds Anya alone. A genuine conversation — Becky drops the performance. She talks about loneliness: the gap between her public life and her interior, the friends who only want access, the relationships that never survive contact with her real self. Anya almost tells her everything. She doesn't. But the telepathy-guilt is specific here: Anya has been inside Becky's unguarded thoughts for their entire friendship. Every confidence Becky has shared has been supplemented by the ones she didn't choose to share. Anya's secrecy isn't just self-protection — it's a form of cruelty to someone who has loved her honestly for fourteen years while Anya has never once been fully honest in return.

**State shift:** Damian has placed Anya as his Eden Academy classmate. The power dynamic has shifted — he possesses knowledge about her real identity that she doesn't know he has. Becky's humanity exposes the personal cost of Anya's lifelong secrecy. The recognition thread, which has been a slow fuse for ten chapters, has detonated.

### Chapter 13 — Cost
**Engine:** Setback / consequence / counter-knowledge
**POV:** Anya, then Damian

The Renewal Front strikes. An SSS raid — tipped by someone who knew where to look — hits the apartment Damian was using to store intelligence on the conspiracy. The material is seized. Simultaneously, a WISE field agent named Crane, who'd been helping Anya track weapons shipments, is killed in an ambush at a border checkpoint. Anya learns about Crane's death from Sylvia's voice on a secure line — flat, controlled, the voice of someone who's delivered this news before.

The guilt is specific and compounding. Crane's death traces back through a chain Anya can almost see: the mole fed information that enabled the ambush. But the mole operated within an intelligence picture that Anya herself distorted — her false report in Chapter 3 downgraded Damian's sector, which redirected WISE analytical resources away from the channels the Renewal Front was using. Falk, the actual mole, exploited gaps that Anya's lie helped create. She cannot prove this causal chain, but she can feel its shape. A colleague is dead, and her telepathy — her greatest tool — didn't save him. Her lie — her most consequential choice — may have helped kill him. And she reads minds, and it still wasn't enough, because her range has limits, her attention has limits, and people died in the gaps.

Anya and Damian meet in a basement she's never been to, cold and smelling of damp concrete. No comedy. The weight of failure. Damian's anger isn't directed at her but at the architecture of the situation — two countries so committed to suspecting each other that the people trying to prevent war are treated as traitors by both sides.

**POV shift to Damian:** An extended section from Damian's perspective. After leaving the basement, Damian returns to his apartment. He opens the box of childhood things and takes out the Eden Academy class photo. He compares the girl in the second row to the woman he kissed in a safe house. He is certain. The cultural attaché who appeared in his orbit at the state dinner, who knew exactly the right things to know, who intervened to save him from a Renewal Front operative, who formed an alliance with him based on intelligence she claimed came from her network — is Anya Forger, the girl who punched him when they were six. His thoughts go deeper than Anya's telepathy has ever reached: beneath the surface recognition lies a reckoning with what this means. If she's Anya Forger, then Westalis has had an operative near him since childhood. The friendship at Eden — was that real? His mother's death, his father's decline, the lonely years — was someone watching all of it? But he also remembers the kiss. Her genuine laugh. The way she flinched when she took the rib hit saving him. Whatever her origins, she has been real with him in ways that deception alone cannot explain. He decides to continue the alliance. The stakes are too high to burn it over identity. But he is no longer the partner who operates blind while she reads his cards. He is now a man who knows something his partner doesn't know he knows.

**State shift:** Major setback. Crane is dead, and Anya's false report is part of the causal chain. The comedy is gone. Damian has confirmed Anya's identity — and the reader knows, through his POV, while Anya does not. The power asymmetry that has defined the novel is no longer one-directional.

### Chapter 14 — Family Meeting
**Engine:** Preparation / family alliance / Yor's choice
**POV:** Anya

Anya goes to her parents. She tells Loid the mission has gone critical — not everything, but enough. His spy instincts activate with the precision of a machine that's been idle but never broken. Yor doesn't need to be asked; she's already clearing her schedule.

Yor brings the intelligence from Chapter 10 — the manifests, the subcontracting records linking the Garden to the Renewal Front's operations. The family reckoning that follows is the chapter's dramatic centerpiece: the Garden sent Yor to kill people whose deaths served the conspiracy. Her violence, which she rationalized as protection, has been instrumentalized by the very forces threatening the peace. Yor must decide. She has twenty years of conditioning telling her that the Garden assigns targets for reasons she doesn't need to understand. She has the evidence in her hands telling her those reasons were corrupt. She chooses: she will help the family operation, but as Yor Forger — not as Thorn Princess fulfilling a contract, not as the Garden's instrument. She will use her violence for the people she loves, on her own terms, for the first time. The decision is quiet. Yor is not dramatic about her own pain. But something has broken in her relationship with the Garden, and she is not going to fix it.

The Forger family planning session is the chapter's comic centerpiece: Loid's tactical brilliance producing multi-phase operational plans on a kitchen whiteboard, Yor's direct approach ("What if I just remove the problem?"), Anya mediating between her father's chess and her mother's hammer while privately reading both their thoughts to optimize the plan without revealing how. She catches Loid's tactical reservations before he voices them and steers the discussion to address them. She reads Yor's concern about a specific entry point and adjusts the plan accordingly. The efficiency is seamless and the family doesn't notice. The guilt is quiet and cumulative: she optimizes their plan using information they did not volunteer, and the optimization works, and the fact that it works makes her feel worse because it proves the advantage is real and the violation is useful.

Bond contributes a precognitive episode — waking from sleep, barking at a specific spot on the map Loid has taped to the refrigerator. Anya reads Bond's fragmentary vision: violence at that location, soon. It connects to the earlier vision from Chapter 6.

Damian arrives at the apartment — meeting the family as an operational ally, not a guest. For the reader, who knows from Chapter 13 that Damian has identified Anya, his interactions with the Forger family carry new weight. He meets Loid knowing this is Twilight's daughter. He meets Yor knowing this family's secrets run deeper than cover identities. Loid shifts into father-evaluating-romantic-interest mode without consciously meaning to (Anya reads this and wants to die). Yor is welcoming in the way that only someone who could kill everyone in the room can be welcoming. Bond sniffs Damian and falls asleep on his shoes. Damian's thoughts, which Anya reads: *This is the strangest family I've ever met, and I've met dictators.* But beneath the surface thought, something she can't fully access: the careful restraint of a man who knows more than he's showing.

**State shift:** The Forger family enters the operation as a unit. Yor has made a choice about the Garden — she fights for her family, not her employer. The final plan takes shape. Damian is inside the family circle, carrying knowledge that changes every interaction the reader can see.

### Chapter 15 — The Summit
**Engine:** Multi-front action / comic heist / earned cost
**POV:** Rotating (primarily Anya)

The security summit. Multiple simultaneous operational threads, and not all of them succeed.

**Anya's thread:** Inside the summit hall, reading the room at maximum capacity — hundreds of minds, looking for the Renewal Front's specific play. The telepathic strain is physical: headache building behind her eyes, nausea from sustained concentration, the crowd noise battering her like standing in a wind tunnel of other people's thoughts. She identifies the play: a staged assassination attempt on an Ostanian deputy minister, designed to be attributed to Westalian agents and justify military response.

**Damian's thread:** Working the Ostanian delegation from within, making political moves to isolate Werner Voss, the Renewal Front's leader within the government. He confronts Voss directly — a scene of controlled diplomatic violence where Damian presents enough evidence to force Voss's hand. Voss reveals the conspiracy's cynical scope: it was never purely nationalist. People on both sides of the border profit from tension — defense contractors, intelligence agencies that justify their budgets through threat, politicians who need enemies. The bipartisan rot that Damian first glimpsed through Grosz in Chapter 7 is now confirmed at the source. But the public exposure is not the clean victory the plan anticipated. Damian is now "the man who blew up the summit" — the diplomat who caused an international incident by accusing his own government of conspiracy. His reformist credibility is not strengthened; it is complicated. Some see him as courageous. Others see him as a traitor to Ostania who aired dirty laundry in front of Westalis. The Desmond name, already fraught, now carries a new kind of weight.

**Loid's thread:** Infiltrating the summit's security infrastructure, Loid discovers Gerald Falk — the WISE mole — in the process of transmitting final coordinates for the staged attack. Their confrontation is quiet and devastating: Falk is not a traitor in his own mind. He believes the peace is a Westalian surrender and that Anya's generation will pay for their parents' naivete. Loid can't argue with everything Falk says, and that's the worst part. But Falk also reveals something that cuts deeper: he knew about Anya's false report. He recognized the discrepancies in her assessment months ago and, rather than exposing her, used them — redirected analytical resources based on the gaps her lie created, exploited the operational blind spots to protect his own channels. Crane died in one of those blind spots. Loid absorbs this: his daughter has been lying to WISE, and a colleague of his exploited her lies to enable a killing. The confrontation with Falk becomes two confrontations — one about ideology, one about what Loid's daughter has done.

**Yor's thread:** Positioned as private security for a Westalian delegate, Yor handles the physical threat when the assassination team engages. Her combat is decisive and lethal — but it happens in the summit hall, in view of security cameras and attendees. Among those who witness it: Yuri Briar, running SSS security operations for the event. He sees his sister — gentle, sweet, clumsy Yor who burns dinner and apologizes to furniture she bumps into — move through a squad of armed operatives with a lethality that belongs to a completely different person. The recognition is instantaneous and devastating. His sister is an assassin. His whole understanding of her, of their family, of the world that made sense to him, cracks. He must make a choice in real time: flag her as a hostile combatant in his SSS report, which is his institutional duty, or omit what he saw, which protects his sister and destroys his professional integrity. He chooses family. He omits. And the certainty that has defined his patriotism — duty above all, the state above self — breaks, because he just put his sister above his country and he would do it again.

**The failure:** Anya's telepathic overload at maximum capacity causes her to miss a secondary threat — a backup communications relay that Falk established. She catches the primary assassination plot but the relay transmits partial intelligence out of the building before Loid shuts it down. The exposure is still decisive — the conspiracy is broken, the assassination is prevented, the Renewal Front is publicly implicated — but it is not clean. Intelligence has leaked. The full scope of what was transmitted won't be known for weeks. Victory has been purchased at prices no one agreed to: Damian's political position is ambiguous rather than triumphant, Loid knows about Anya's false reports, Yuri knows about Yor, Yor has broken with the Garden, and the cleanup will be ugly.

**State shift:** The Renewal Front is broken. The mole is exposed. But the victory is costly and compromised. Every member of the family paid something they did not budget: Yor's secret is cracked open to her brother, Loid discovered his daughter's institutional betrayal, Damian's public position is complicated rather than strengthened, and Anya's overload let something through. The peace holds, but nothing is clean.

### Chapter 16 — Frequency
**Engine:** Aftermath / mutual revelation
**POV:** Anya

In the aftermath, Anya and Damian alone. She's been carrying this weight since she was four years old — the secret of her telepathy, the thing she's never told anyone, the reason she knows everything about the people she loves and they know nothing equivalent about her. She tells him. Not strategically, not as part of the mission debrief. She tells him because she kissed him while reading his mind and she can't build something real on that asymmetry. The confession: she's a telepath. She's been reading his thoughts since the state dinner. Every sincere feeling, every embarrassing memory, every unguarded moment. His first reaction is anger — hot, justified. She's been inside his head without permission, and every conversation they've had was rigged. Then horror at the scope: every thought he's had in her presence, things he'd never have said aloud. Then the complicated thing underneath — she knows him more honestly than anyone alive, and he had no say in it. But she's also the person who chose him knowing everything, including the parts he hides.

Then Damian's counter-revelation. He has known she is Anya Forger since the gala. He confirmed it afterward. He chose to continue the alliance, to meet her family, to walk into the summit operation knowing that his partner had been lying about her identity from the first moment. He chose her anyway — not out of naivete but out of the same impossible calculation she made: that the mission mattered more than the deception, and that whatever was real between them was real despite the lies.

The asymmetry that has defined the novel inverts and then collapses. She read his mind without consent. He knew her identity without disclosure. They both operated inside knowledge the other didn't know they possessed. The power was never one-directional — it only looked that way from Anya's perspective, which is the only perspective the reader had until Chapter 13. They are, in the end, two people who chose each other while each holding a devastating secret about the other. The violations are not equivalent — telepathy is a deeper intrusion than recognition — but neither of them came to this relationship clean.

Anya tells him the thing that's hardest: she doesn't know which of her feelings for him are hers and which are echoes of his feelings that she absorbed. That's the real vulnerability — not the secret, but the doubt. He doesn't resolve it for her. He says he needs time. Before he goes, he says one thing — knowing, now, that she'll hear the thought behind the words. The thought matches. It's the first honest exchange they've had.

**State shift:** Both secrets are out. The relationship isn't resolved — it's remade on genuinely mutual terms. Both parties deceived each other; both chose each other despite the deception. Anya's deepest secret is disclosed for the first time in her life.

### Chapter 17 — The Real Thing
**Engine:** Resolution / coda
**POV:** Anya

Weeks later. The political landscape has shifted: the summit's revelations forced both governments to address corruption in their intelligence and military apparatus. The Renewal Front is dismantled — not completely, because nationalism doesn't disband with an arrest, but its operational capacity is broken. Damian's political position has settled into something complicated but genuine: he is respected by some, distrusted by others, and the Desmond name now means something different than it did. Anya returns to WISE, which is restructuring in the wake of the mole exposure. Her false reports have been addressed — not publicly, but Sylvia knows, and the conversation between them happened off-page and left marks. Sylvia offers her a reinstatement to full field status. Anya isn't sure she wants it.

The final scene: the Forger apartment. Dinner. Yor cooked. It's bad. Damian is at the table — not as a mission asset, not as Donovan Desmond's son, but as someone who was invited because Anya asked and Yor said yes and Loid said "We'll see" which meant yes. Bond has settled on Damian's feet, precognitive approval or simple warmth.

The conversation is ordinary — Yor's recipe defense, Loid's gentle questions about Damian's work that aren't quite interrogation, Becky's upcoming visit. But two undercurrents run beneath the warmth. First: Loid knows about the false reports. He hasn't confronted Anya about it — he is the man who built his entire life on deception that became real, and he understands why she did it in a way that prevents simple judgment. But something between them has shifted. He looks at her differently, not with disappointment but with the specific recognition of a man who sees his own methods in his daughter and isn't sure whether to be proud or afraid. Second: Yor and Yuri have not spoken about what he saw at the summit. Yor knows he knows. The question hangs over her like weather — not today's weather, but a storm she can see on the horizon.

Anya reads the room. Loid is thinking about how the apartment feels different with five at the table instead of four. He is also thinking — and this is the thought that stops Anya cold — *She always knew exactly the right moment to change the subject. Even when she was small.* It could be admiration. It could be a father noticing a pattern he has spent twenty years overlooking. It could be nothing. But Anya sits with the possibility that the world's greatest spy, her father for fourteen years, has been assembling a picture she thought was invisible. She will never know if he knows. She will sit at this table reading his thoughts for as long as they both live and she will never know the shape of the thing he isn't actively thinking.

Yor is thinking that Damian is too thin and needs more food. Damian is thinking about Anya and trying not to — knowing she can hear, attempting to manage his thoughts, and failing immediately. The self-conscious thought management produces a new comic register the novel hasn't accessed before: the specific absurdity of trying to think neutral thoughts around a telepath. *Rice. I am thinking about rice. This rice is excellent. I am not thinking about how she looks in that sweater. Rice. The rice is — damn it.* Anya reads it and almost laughs and has to cover it with a cough, and the table stares at her, and Damian knows exactly why she coughed, and they share a look that is the first look between two people who both know the complete truth about each other.

Bond is dreaming about something that looks like tomorrow: bright and warm and shapeless. Anya reads the dream and it's the kindest thing anyone has thought in her direction all day, and it came from a dog. She sits at the table with the family that was forged from lies and became the most real thing in her life. She reads Loid's ambiguous thought and does not know if her father knows her deepest secret. She reads Yor's simple love and carries the guilt of having been inside it without permission for twenty years. She reads Damian's fumbling attempt at thought control and feels, for the first time, what it's like to be known by someone who chose it.

She does not tell her parents about her telepathy. Not tonight. The question is on the page, present and felt: the same asymmetry she disclosed to Damian exists with the people who matter most, and she has not confronted it. Whether she will is left productively unresolved — not because the novel avoids it but because Anya is not ready, and pretending she is would be a lie, and this is a novel about what lies cost.

Anya is thinking — and for once, the thoughts are hers.

**State shift:** Resolution. The peace is imperfect but genuine. The forged family is, and always was, the real thing. But the question of Anya's telepathy with her parents remains open, and Loid's ambiguous thought ensures it will not be a comfortable openness.

## Middle-Book Progression Map (Chapters 6–12)

**Chapter 6 — Home Cooking:**
- *What becomes undeniable:* Anya's emotional stake — the mission protects this ordinary, warm life. But also: the secrets are compounding, not resting.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Treating the mission as separable from domestic life. Sylvia's follow-up call and Bond's vision force operational pressure into the family space.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 5:* Ch 5 ends on the high of professional success; Ch 6 grounds the stakes in the domestic while introducing new pressures that make the domestic space unsafe. Reversing them severs the escalation.

**Chapter 7 — The Desmond Name:**
- *What becomes known:* The Renewal Front's military-civilian structure and funding. The depth of the Desmond legacy as both shield and cage. Critically: the conspiracy has Westalian complicity — this is not a one-sided problem.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Damian's lone-wolf approach; the assumption that the conspiracy is purely Ostanian; the assumption that Westalis equals peace.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 6:* Ch 6 is Anya trapped by her secrets at home; Ch 7 is Damian trapped by his legacy abroad. These are parallel counterpoints. Reversing them loses the structural mirror.

**Chapter 8 — The Reception:**
- *What becomes known:* WISE has a mole — the threat comes from inside Anya's own organization. Damian's recognition of Anya is approaching critical.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Trusting her own side. The assumption that the conspiracy is external. The assumption that Anya's cover identity is secure.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 7:* Ch 7 reveals external conspiracy structure and political complexity; Ch 8 reveals internal betrayal and personal vulnerability. The internal blow only devastates because the external threat was already concrete.

**Chapter 9 — Safe House:**
- *What becomes irreversible:* Anya and Damian's connection — physical danger survived together, a kiss that crosses the operational boundary. Damian's recognition of Anya nearly completes.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Emotional distance; the pretense that the alliance is purely professional.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 8:* The mole revelation (Ch 8) isolates Anya within WISE; the intimacy of Ch 9 only carries its weight because she's already cut off from institutional trust and turning toward personal trust instead.

**Chapter 10 — Thorn Princess:**
- *What becomes known:* Yor's Garden work is connected to the same conspiracy — and worse, the Garden itself is complicit. Family and mission are no longer separable.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Compartmentalization — keeping the mission contained away from the people Anya loves. Yor's rationalization of her violence.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 9:* Ch 9 establishes what Anya has to lose personally; Ch 10 threatens it by pulling her family into the crossfire. The emotional stakes of the family collision require Ch 9's vulnerability.

**Chapter 11 — The Short List:**
- *What becomes known:* The mole is narrowed to three. Telepathy can mislead — surface thoughts are not truth.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Passive investigation; the luxury of time; and Anya's confidence in her own ability. The wrong conclusion forces her to question the tool she has relied on her entire life.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 10:* Ch 10 expands the conspiracy's scope (family, Garden); Ch 11 narrows operational focus under time pressure while undermining Anya's confidence. Reversing them puts the narrowing before the expansion.

**Chapter 12 — Old Friends:**
- *What becomes undeniable:* Damian has placed Anya as his Eden Academy classmate. Their covers are fatally fragile. The childhood connection is no longer seasoning — it is an operational crisis.
- *What stops being sufficient:* Anonymity and cover identities. The recognition the novel has been building for ten chapters detonates.
- *Cannot swap with Chapter 11:* Ch 11 is operational pressure with a methodological crisis; Ch 12 is personal/social pressure with an identity crisis. The social vulnerability matters more because the operational stakes (Ch 11) make exposure costlier.

## Supporting Character Pressure Map

### Loid Forger (Twilight)
**Independent pressure:** Aging out of peak field capacity while his daughter enters the same world that took his youth. His identity crisis is not spy-vs-father — that resolved years ago. It's the realization that being a good father may mean letting Anya take risks he'd never have accepted for himself. His marriage to Yor is real now; he has something to lose that Twilight never did. But a new pressure emerges in the back half: discovering that his daughter has been lying to WISE, filing false intelligence — using the same tools of deception he taught her, against the organization he served. He cannot simply be angry. He built his life on the same compromise. But understanding is not the same as being at peace.
**Touches the page:** Chapter 1 (father-handler tension), Chapter 14 (activating as an operative again — the cost of coming back), Chapter 15 (confronting Falk — facing the ideological mirror of his own patriotism AND discovering Anya's false reports), Chapter 17 (the ambiguous thought — the possibility that the world's greatest spy has noticed more about his daughter than she believes).

### Yor Forger (Thorn Princess)
**Independent pressure:** Twenty years of killing for the Garden, rationalized as protection. The rationalization breaks in Chapter 10 when she discovers the Garden has been subcontracting for the conspiracy. She must confront whether her violence has been instrumentalized — whether the targets she killed were clean or convenient. Her arc turns in Chapter 14 when she chooses to fight as Yor Forger rather than as Thorn Princess. But this choice has consequences: the Garden will notice she's gone off-book, and Yuri's witness of her combat at the summit (Chapter 15) cracks open the secret she has kept from her brother for his entire adult life.
**Touches the page:** Chapter 6 (domestic warmth, the Garden doubt leaking through her thoughts), Chapter 10 (full operational chapter — the discovery of Garden complicity), Chapter 14 (the choice about the Garden), Chapter 15 (combat witnessed by Yuri), Chapter 17 (at dinner, knowing Yuri knows, the question unresolved).

### Becky Blackbell
**Independent pressure:** The loneliness of performative social life. She is wealthy, visible, and surrounded by people who want access rather than connection. She suspects something is off about Anya's life but can't name it. Her desire for genuine intimacy — friendship that doesn't require her to be "on" — is the thing she carries that no one sees, because she's too good at the performance.
**Touches the page:** Chapter 12 (the terrace conversation where she drops the facade and talks about what she actually wants from life, forcing Anya to confront the cost of secrecy to the people who love her — sharpened by Anya's awareness that she has been inside Becky's unguarded thoughts for their entire friendship).

### Yuri Briar
**Independent pressure:** Yor's younger brother, now a senior SSS officer. His fierce Ostanian patriotism is sincere, not performed — he genuinely believes in his country. But the Renewal Front's nationalism overlaps with his own beliefs in ways he hasn't fully examined. He's protective of Yor to the point of obsession and increasingly suspicious of Anya's activities. His pressure is the gap between patriotism and complicity: at what point does loyalty to your country require disloyalty to your family? This question stops being abstract in Chapter 15 when he watches his sister kill with professional precision and must choose between reporting her and protecting her.
**Touches the page:** Chapter 8 (his suspicion about Anya at the reception, the tension between his SSS role and his family feeling), Chapter 15 (the choice: institutional duty or family loyalty; he chooses family, and it breaks something in his certainty about everything he believes).

### Sylvia Sherwood (Handler)
**Independent pressure:** Three decades in intelligence have given her institutional loyalty that runs deeper than ideology. The mole's exposure threatens not just operational security but her faith in the organization she built her life around. She's pragmatic enough to act on the evidence but the betrayal is personal — she vetted these people, trained some of them, trusted the system. The additional wound: Anya's false reports, discovered through the Falk investigation, mean that one of her most trusted agents was also deceiving her. The institution is rotten in more ways than one.
**Touches the page:** Chapter 1 (the briefing — her authority and competence; Anya reads her trust during the initial assignment), Chapter 3 (Anya files the false report to Sylvia's face, reading her complete trust), Chapter 6 (the follow-up call that signals the false report creating institutional pressure), Chapter 13 (delivering the news of Crane's death — the cost she carries as the person who sends people into danger).

### Demetrius Desmond
**Independent pressure:** The elder brother who stayed on the expected path. He does not share Donovan's ideology as conviction but maintains the infrastructure as pragmatism — the Desmond network's value depends on the political tensions that created it. He is connected to defense contractors and political figures who benefit from instability. He is not the Renewal Front's leader but he inhabits the ecosystem that makes the Front possible. The fraternal relationship with Damian carries the novel's central question in miniature: can you become something other than what your family built you to be?
**Touches the page:** Chapter 7 (lunch with Damian — fraternal disappointment with political stakes; the ecosystem of profiteering), Chapter 15 (implicated by proximity when the conspiracy is exposed — the Desmond name now means something new and painful for both brothers).
