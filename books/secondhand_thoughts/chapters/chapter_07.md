# Chapter 7

The conference room on the third floor of the National Unity Party headquarters smelled like old carpet and fresh coffee, and Damian Desmond was lying.

Not badly. He'd been lying in rooms like this since he was fifteen, since his father first brought him to a donors' reception and said, "Smile at the man in the grey suit. He funds our eastern district campaigns." Donovan Desmond hadn't needed to explain what kind of smile. Damian had understood already. You learned to lie early in this family, the way other children learned to ride bicycles.

"The Westalian delegation's posture at the trade summit was instructive," Damian said, standing at the front of a table of eight party loyalists. He kept his hands at his sides. "Their willingness to revisit border tariffs suggests they're testing our resolve. If we concede on agricultural inspection protocols, we signal flexibility. If we're perceived as flexible, we're perceived as weak."

Nods around the table. Deputy Chairman Holtz, who had known Donovan for thirty years and still considered himself a kingmaker, made notes in a leather notebook. Beside him, Petra Lang from the defense policy committee watched Damian with the fixed attention she gave people she hadn't decided whether to use or sideline.

"I'd recommend we draft a counter-position paper before the next session," Damian continued. "Something that reframes the tariff question as a security concern rather than an economic one. The language matters."

"Your father would have just told them no," Holtz said, not looking up.

"My father had leverage I don't have yet," Damian said. He kept his voice warm, a little rueful. "But the principle is the same. You don't negotiate from a posture of accommodation."

Holtz looked up. "You sound like him."

Damian accepted the compliment the way it was intended, as a leash lightly fitted. "I had a good teacher."

None of it was what he believed. The tariff question was routine economic maneuvering, and framing it as a security concern was the sort of escalatory language the Renewal Front fed on. Every time a trade dispute became a sovereignty dispute, the men who wanted war got to accuse everyone else of yielding ground. Damian had drafted the counter-position paper already, in his head. It disgusted him. He would write it this afternoon and it would be good, precise, persuasive, exactly the kind of thing a hawkish young Desmond would produce. Every sentence would be a small betrayal of the work he was actually trying to do.

The meeting went another forty minutes. Budget allocations for the eastern district campaigns. A proposed media response to the Westalian cultural exchange program, which Lang wanted to frame as soft-power infiltration. Damian contributed where expected, deflected where he could, and watched the room the way his father had taught him: not for what people said but for who looked at whom when someone else was talking. Holtz deferred to Lang on media strategy but resented her for it. Lang wanted a committee chairmanship and was building alliances Holtz hadn't noticed yet. A younger member named Fitz, who Damian had met twice, kept checking his watch, bored or anxious, hard to tell.

None of them were Renewal Front, as far as Damian could determine. That would have been simpler. They had organized their whole professional lives around Ostanian strength and Westalian threat, and they would lose status, funding, and purpose if those assumptions were dismantled. The Front didn't need to recruit people like Holtz and Lang. It only had to speak a little hotter than they already did.

Damian smiled at Fitz on the way out. "Good meeting," he said. Fitz looked relieved that someone had spoken to him.

He had almost reached the corridor before Lang's voice caught him.

"Desmond. A minute."

Holtz kept walking. Fitz disappeared around the corner with the speed of a junior man who had decided not to be visible.

Lang tucked her notebook under one arm. Up close, she smelled faintly of cigarette smoke under expensive soap. "Thursday. Eight o'clock. Small conference room off the defense committee offices."

Demetrius had said Thursday as if the invitation were still theoretical. Damian kept his face neutral. "I was planning to come."

"I know. Your brother made sure of that." She watched him for a beat. "What I need from you is not another trade memo in a different jacket. I need language that makes infrastructure sound like defense and defense sound like common sense."

"That's usually possible."

"Of course it is. The question is whether you're willing to be useful in the less polite rooms." Her gaze dropped to the folder in his hand, then came back to his face. "There will be contractors there. Procurement staff. Men who think every customs dispute should end with uniforms. If somebody says something ugly, don't look at them like it's new. People notice."

Damian let the pause sit just long enough to count as courtesy. "I'll try to conceal my delicate nature."

Lang's mouth moved a fraction. "Bring the tariff draft. Holtz wants to see whether you can make a routine disagreement sound like national peril."

"He'll be glad to learn I can."

"He'll be glad to learn you're trainable." She stepped aside. "Thursday."

---

Demetrius had chosen the restaurant, which meant the Kaiserhof and the window table overlooking the ministry gardens, where half the room could see who had chosen it. Lunch with Demetrius was never just lunch. It was another way of putting the family in order.

He was already seated when Damian arrived, ten minutes early because Demetrius was always fifteen minutes early and would note the difference. His older brother wore a dark suit that fit too cleanly to ignore. His hair was combed in the same severe style their father had worn in ministry photographs. At twenty-four, Demetrius looked older than that. He had for years. The resemblance to Donovan was the kind of thing people mentioned in front of him as if it were praise. Demetrius treated it as praise because, for him, it was.

"You're early," Demetrius said.

"You're earlier."

"A call ran short. Kessler at Vanguard Defense. Procurement's being rearranged." He set down his water glass. "Sit."

The waiter brought menus. The Kaiserhof had taken every solid Ostanian staple and pushed it up into ministerial prices: soup clarified into consommé, sausages pressed into terrines, bread arriving with explanation. Damian ordered the schnitzel because it was the plainest thing on the menu, which only made it feel like a statement.

"Holtz says the meeting went well," Demetrius said.

"Holtz says that whenever I manage not to set the room on fire."

"He said you sounded like Father."

"He says that too."

"Because that comparison keeps you useful to him." Demetrius picked up the menu once more, though he was going to order the duck. He always ordered the duck. "Look, the party's fine with you at the moment. You show up, you do clean work, you carry the name. That's the minimum and you're meeting it. But Holtz told me you've been selective."

"Selective."

"Don't do that." Demetrius looked up. "Lang's working group. The security liaison committee. The rooms that matter once the speeches end. You keep finding reasons not to be in them."

"I've been focused on trade."

"Trade is a waiting room and you know it."

The waiter returned. Demetrius ordered the duck. Damian's schnitzel came with a scatter of microgreens that looked embarrassed to be there.

"I'll go to Lang's meeting on Thursday," Damian said.

"Go on Thursday. Go after Thursday. Stop making people ask if you're solid."

Damian set down his fork. "Kessler asked that?"

"Kessler asked whether your position on national defense was real. I said yes."

"So that's it. I sit in Lang's little war room and smile while Kessler counts his percentages—"

"Don't." Demetrius's knife stopped halfway through the duck. "If you want to moralize, do it somewhere without waiters."

Damian looked past him, out at the gardens. "I wasn't moralizing."

"Then what were you doing?"

"Thinking out loud."

"Try not to." Demetrius cut a piece of duck and chewed. "The defense portfolio is where this family still has weight. That means contractors, committee chairs, people in uniform, people adjacent to people in uniform. You don't have to like that. You do have to understand it."

"I understand it."

"No. You understand how it's supposed to work." He set down the fork. "I'm talking about the actual — which deputy minister returns whose call. Which briefing paper gets read because a Desmond handed it over. Those are errands, Damian. Father did them. I do them. You keep acting like there's a version of this where you get the results without doing the errands."

"Maybe I'm being careful."

"Then be careful where nobody can see it." Demetrius took a sip of water. "Nobody in that room needs to know what you think, Damian. They need to see you in the chair when it matters."

"And if I haven't decided?"

Demetrius gave him a flat look. "Then decide. Before somebody decides for you."

The duck was lacquered with a reduction Damian couldn't identify. His schnitzel was fine. The acid in his throat annoyed him.

"You sound like Father," he said.

"For God's sake." Demetrius exhaled through his nose. "Not everything is Father. Though yes, he'd agree with me." He leaned back a fraction. "He asked about you."

"What did you tell him?"

"That you're doing well. That the party's pleased enough. That the hesitation is temporary."

Damian set his water glass down. "You told him I was hesitating."

"I told him you were thinking too hard. He considers that a form of hesitation." Demetrius picked up his fork again. "I'm trying to keep people from deciding you're unreliable before you've even — look, there's a difference."

"You always did like speaking for me."

"Yeah. Someone had to."

Demetrius did not look away. Neither did Damian.

"I'm not — look, I'm not going to embarrass the family," Damian said.

"No," Demetrius said after a beat. "I don't think you will."

They spoke about smaller things after that. A cousin's wedding. The ministry gardens, on which Demetrius had opinions. Their father's view that modern Ostanian literature had mistaken confusion for depth. Damian listened to his brother's clipped certainty and tried, not for the first time, to find the point where the work had stopped being a means and become the thing Demetrius actually cared about: the calls returned, the names remembered, the right man seated at the right table. He couldn't find it.

They split the bill because Demetrius insisted on splitting bills, a gesture toward equality that convinced no one. Outside the Kaiserhof, with the autumn air sharp on his face, Demetrius shook his hand as if they had just concluded business.

"Thursday," Demetrius said. "Lang's working group. I'll tell her you're coming."

"Fine."

"And call Father. He listens to your voice longer than mine."

To Damian, that was as close to vulnerability as Demetrius got: the sideways admission that Donovan preferred the son who questioned to the son who complied. Damian had never known whether Demetrius resented that or tucked it into the same mental drawer as vote counts, donor names, and every other fact that might matter later.

"I'll call him tonight," Damian said.

Demetrius nodded and walked toward the car already waiting for him, its engine running, its driver opening the door before he reached it. He moved with the grim efficiency that always made Damian feel as if the day had been arranged in advance. Damian stood on the pavement in his cheaper suit and watched him go, thinking of Kessler, Thursday, and how every attempt at independence seemed to end in another waiting room where somebody already knew whose son he was.

---

Grosz lived in a third-floor apartment in the Schiller district, east of the government quarter and far enough out that a retired civil servant could afford it without explaining himself. The stairwell smelled like boiled vegetables and floor wax. A cat watched Damian from the second-floor landing as if he were already late.

He knocked twice, then once, then twice again, the old pattern from his father's network, unchanged in thirty years because the people who used it kept old habits long after they stopped being sensible. The door opened on a chain. One eye, watery and sharp, examined him through the gap.

"Herr Grosz. It's Damian."

"I can see that." The chain rattled. "You look like your mother. Come in."

The apartment was small and meticulously kept. Damian took in the folded newspaper, magnifying glass, toast crumbs, and the bookshelves lined with Ostanian political history, military memoirs, and more novels than he would have expected. On the mantel stood three framed photographs: Grosz in a ministerial corridor, younger and straighter; Grosz shaking hands with a man Damian did not recognize; Grosz behind Donovan Desmond at what looked like a state function, both of them wearing the expression Damian had seen in old ministry photographs when men still believed they were building a permanent order.

Grosz himself was seventy-three, thin in the way of men who had once been vigorous and were now just wiry, with liver-spotted hands and a collar buttoned to the throat despite the warmth of the room. He moved carefully but not slowly. A cane leaned beside the armchair, though he did not touch it while Damian was looking.

"Sit. I'll make coffee. Or I have schnapps, if you want to disappoint yourself."

"Coffee."

"Your father would have taken the schnapps."

"My father was better at pretending not to be drunk."

That got the corner of Grosz's mouth to move. "Better at pretending, yes."

He disappeared into the kitchen. Water ran. A kettle clicked. Damian sat in the armchair that did not have the cane beside it and waited. The room had the stale quiet of a place used to only one voice. Traffic filtered up from the street, a bus, a horn, metal dragged over concrete. Inside the apartment, each sound seemed to hang around too long.

Grosz returned with two cups on saucers, the porcelain heavy and old-fashioned, blue flowers worn thin at the rim. He lowered himself into the other armchair.

"You said you had questions about the current situation." He set his cup down. "I assumed you didn't mean tariffs."

"Not tariffs. The Renewal Front. And Vanguard Defense."

Grosz went still so fast Damian saw the fear before the old man had time to arrange his face.

"Who said Vanguard?"

"Demetrius. At lunch. Kessler's name came with it."

Grosz looked toward the hall, then toward the door, listening with his whole head. "Keep your voice down."

"You know them?"

"I knew paperwork that led in their direction. That's not the same thing." He picked up his cup, put it back down untouched. "What else do you have?"

Damian held his gaze. "Holtz wants me drafting trade language as a security issue. Lang wants me in the defense working group on Thursday. Kessler is leaning on Demetrius about my reliability. Somewhere in that I started hearing the words Renewal Front from people who used to know better than to say things in front of me." He paused. "That's what I have."

Grosz studied him for a long moment. "All right," he said. "That's enough to talk."

He stood with a suppressed wince, crossed to a narrow sideboard, and opened the bottom drawer. From the back he took a thin file jacket that looked older than Damian. Inside was a single onion-skin sheet, yellowed, the carbon copy faint but legible.

"Don't touch the middle. It smears."

Damian leaned forward. Across the top of the page, in faded typed capitals, was MAINTENANCE CONTINUITY ANNEX. Below that: a ministry seal, a contract number, and the name VANGUARD DEFENSE in pale, stubborn ink. Four line items sat under the header. The third and fourth were for the same northern depot, the same month, and almost the same wording. The amounts matched exactly: 31,400 dinars each.

"Read the bottom two lines," Grosz said.

Damian did. The third billed for relay-seal replacement on a border communications site. The fourth billed a continuity retention reserve for the same site, routed through Klee Civic Advisory.

He looked up. "They charged for the same work twice."

"Close." Grosz lowered himself back into the chair. "They charged for the parts once and the men twice. Klee had a brass plaque, one rented office, no mechanics, no tools. It existed so the second payment could leave the ministry books wearing a different hat."

Damian looked back down. Same depot. Same month. Same amount. The fraud was lazy enough to feel practiced. "So the annex proves a skim."

"It proves money was being moved off the clean ledger." Grosz tapped the page with one finger. "What that money bought is where I start inferring instead of knowing. I want you clear on that."

"Fine. What do you know?"

"I know more annexes like that kept showing up. And I know officers, contractors, old party men who had no business being in the same building started — I'd see them at the same side tables. Same receptions. People who shouldn't have been in the same room." He rubbed his mouth. "What I think — and I'm calling it what I think, not what I can prove — is that somebody needed those people available to each other. No minutes, no audits."

"The Renewal Front."

Grosz made a face. "That's what people call it. Sure. But some of them wanted a war. Some of them wanted the budget that comes with promising a war. And some of them were just skimming and calling it service." His shoulders shifted under his shirt. "It isn't one thing. I need you to understand that."

"How many annexes did you see?"

"I stopped — enough. More than a few." He picked at the saucer rim. "Enough that it wasn't one lazy accountant."

"That's not a number."

"No." A pause. "No, it isn't."

The apartment buzzer rasped hard enough to make both cups jump in their saucers.

Grosz muttered, "Damn it," folded the onion-skin page in half, and shoved it back into the jacket. "Quiet."

He crossed to the door without the cane, which was how Damian knew he was frightened. He put one eye to the peephole and held up two fingers without looking back. Wait.

Someone's shoes stopped outside the apartment. The shadow under the door thickened, then shifted. A woman downstairs started complaining about post that had been left in the wrong box. Another apartment buzzed in answer. The shoes moved on.

Grosz stayed at the door long enough to count as habit, not paranoia, then returned and slid the file jacket under a cushion instead of back onto the table.

"This is why you don't come here twice in one week," he said.

Damian kept his own voice low. "You think that was for you?"

"I think old men who keep papers should assume every hallway is for them." Grosz sat again, slower this time. "Now ask one useful question and then leave."

"All right. Thursday. Lang's room. What am I looking for besides duplicate charges?"

"Supplement numbers, if they come up. Emergency retention codes — those are the budget lines where the money moves." He thought for a moment. "Watch who talks to the contractors directly. Who skips the staff. And who leaves the room when numbers get specific." He exhaled through his nose. "And don't — look, not everyone in that room is part of it. Lang might just like being important. Half the damage in this country comes from people who aren't even conspiring, they're just comfortable."

"And Voss?"

Grosz's mouth tightened. "Voss is not on that page. I want to be clear about that."

Damian waited.

"His name kept — I kept hearing it. Around the same people, in the same rooms. Enough times that I stopped telling myself it was coincidence." He shifted in the chair. "I'm not saying he ran anything. I'm saying that when a conversation turned from budgets to grievance, when somebody started talking about how peace had made the country weak — Voss was there, or he'd just left, or somebody had just come from talking to him."

"The phrase peace is theft."

"Yes." Grosz's eyes flicked toward the door again. "His, maybe. Or maybe somebody said it and it stuck to him. I never heard it from his mouth directly."

"This is —" Damian stopped. Started over. "Why tell me this now?"

Grosz was quiet for a moment. "Because you came here with Kessler and Vanguard in the same sentence. That's different from the other times." His voice roughened. "And because Kessler's name being in your brother's mouth the same day it's in mine — I don't like that. I've probably said too much already."

"You think this conversation puts you at risk?"

"I think I'm seventy-three and I live alone in a building with one stairwell." He looked at Damian without blinking. "So, yes."

Damian nodded.

"Don't come back here to tell me what you find," Grosz said. "Don't come back at all. Not next week, not the week after. Not unless you have proof in your hand that changes everything." He leaned forward. "Thursday you go to Lang's meeting. You sit, you listen. If annex numbers come up, write them down afterward, not in the room. Watch who talks to whom when procurement gets specific. Then you leave looking like a Desmond son who showed up to do what Desmond sons do."

"And if they notice what I'm doing?"

Grosz looked at him for a moment. "Then you'd better hope they noticed too late." His eyes lifted to the photograph of Donovan on the mantel. "Your father never did any of this alone. He had people in hallways, cars downstairs, staff who knew which file to lose if somebody came asking. You don't have any of that. Keep that in mind."

Damian let the paper sit where it was. "You were loyal to him."

"I was loyal to your father, yes." Grosz leaned back carefully. "I'm still — I suppose I'm still loyal to what I thought we were building. You give your career to a ministry and you stay loyal because otherwise what were the years for?" He rubbed his knee. "But that's not the same as wanting them to use you up the way they used me."

The steps in the stairwell were gone. The apartment had gone quiet again. Damian stood.

"I'll take the contract number," he said.

"You'll memorize it." Grosz slid the page back into the file jacket. "Nothing leaves this apartment."

Damian read the number twice, then a third time.

He was at the door before he turned back. "One more thing."

Grosz closed his eyes for a moment. "You people always say that like it won't be the costly part."

"Does this stay inside Ostania?" Damian asked. "The money. The buyers. Any of it."

For the first time since opening the file, Grosz looked genuinely tired.

"I saw one routing sheet before I retired," he said. "One. Threat assessments moving through a broker to a Westalian-owned procurement firm. Not stolen. Sold."

Damian stared at him. "By whom?"

"If I knew that, I wouldn't be sitting in this apartment with a bad radiator." Grosz's voice had gone flat. "I don't know if that channel still exists. I don't know if Vanguard was part of it. I don't know if the same people handled both sides. What I know is one sheet crossed my desk and I never found a clean reason for it."

Westalian money. Ostanian grievance. The same old appetite on both sides of the border.

"Good," Grosz said. "Now go before I decide I've lost my mind."

---

The walk home took twelve minutes, east along Friedrichstrasse and then north toward his building. The streetlights were coming on. Leaves had pasted themselves into the gutters. The cold got through the cloth of his coat faster than it had last week and stayed there.

He kept hearing four things. Kessler. Voss. Westalian money. Maintenance continuity annex.

He would have to write the tariff paper before evening and then attend Lang's working group on Thursday pretending Demetrius had won. He would have to sit through defense men talking budget and posture and readiness while trying to catch contract numbers without looking like he was catching contract numbers. If he did it badly, the suspicion would not stop with him. It would travel backward to an old man in a third-floor apartment with a cane by the chair and no one else in the room.

Damian's apartment was on the fourth floor of a building that had once been elegant and was now merely respectable, which suited the story he told about himself. Modest enough to signal independence. Well-located enough to remind anyone paying attention that the independence was elective.

He let himself in, hung up his coat, and turned on the kitchen light. He should have drafted the counter-position paper. He should have written down the contract number while it was still clean in his head, then burned the page. Instead he stood at the counter for a full ten seconds, looking at the telephone, and picked it up.

Not calling would make it back to Demetrius by breakfast anyway.

The house line rang twice. A secretary answered on the third ring and put him through after a pause just long enough to remind him that even in his father's home there were gates inside gates.

"Damian."

His father's voice had not softened with age. It was still dry and level, and Damian still found himself straightening under it before he could stop.

"Father."

"Demetrius said you were calling."

"Then at least one branch of the family communicates efficiently."

A breath crossed the line, close enough to amusement that Damian resented it. "How did Holtz take the room?"

"He left believing I think tariffs are a security emergency."

"Good."

Damian stared at the black window over the sink. His own reflection looked thin in the glass. "Lang wants me in her working group on Thursday."

"She should have wanted you months ago."

"Trade kept me occupied."

"Trade keeps young men occupied. Security decides who matters." Paper shifted on the other end, another conversation maybe waiting just beyond the receiver. "Will you be there?"

"Yes."

"Then be there properly. Don't stand in a corner looking civilized while other men do the ugly part."

Kessler's name rose in Damian's throat and stopped there. Grosz had said keep your voice down, and somehow the warning had followed him into a different building. "And if the ugly part is the point?"

His father was quiet long enough for Damian to picture him weighing whether the question deserved an answer.

"Then learn who is paying for it," Donovan said. "Don't confuse disgust with strategy."

There it was, the familiar weight of the family method, interested in usefulness before anything else. He could hear how much of it had shaped him before he'd been old enough to refuse it.

"I'll keep that in mind."

"Do. And write the tariff paper before midnight. Holtz likes quick sons." Another beat. Softer, brief enough to doubt. "You sound tired."

"It's been a long day."

"They all are."

The line went dead.

Damian stood with the receiver in his hand until the dial tone flattened out. Then he set it down, opened the hall closet, and pulled the cardboard box from the top shelf.

It was the only box he had brought from the family house. Everything else in the apartment had been bought new, a deliberate break from the curated weight of the Desmond household. This box he had never unpacked and never thrown away.

Inside were school things. An Eden Academy report card with marks that were good but not exceptional, which had been a particular kind of failure in his father's house. A certificate for the inter-class spelling competition, second place. A ribbon from athletics day. He had run the four-hundred-meter badly and finished fourth, and the ribbon said PARTICIPANT in silver letters, the kind of consolation Eden preferred to dress up as dignity. A folded program from the school festival, his name listed as part of the gymnasium cleanup crew. A pen that had run dry years ago. At the bottom, under tissue paper that his mother had probably put there before she got sick, a photograph.

Class photograph. Eden Academy, third year. Four rows of students on the gymnasium steps, faces arranged into the tense stillness children gave a camera after one warning too many. Damian found himself in the third row, second from the right, looking furious.

In the second row, between Becky Blackbell and a boy he couldn't place, a girl with bright pink hair and a crooked tie grinned at someone just outside the frame.

He looked at her longer than he meant to, then turned the photograph over. On the back, in his mother's tidy blue hand, the rows had been labeled years ago. A damp brown ring had eaten through the middle of the second row. Becky Blackbell's name was still clear at the edge of the stain. The next name had washed into one capital A and a tail of blue.
