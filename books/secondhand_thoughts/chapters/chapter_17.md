# Chapter 17

On the tram to WISE, Anya could tell Berlint had started thinking about other things.

The air still wanted a coat and gloves, but the thoughts didn't. All winter the city had been thinking about arrests, investigations, borders, who would take the fall and who would get paid to bury the embarrassing parts. Now the noise had thinned into ordinary stuff: paint samples, grocery lists, a man worrying about his mother's hip surgery, somebody trying to remember whether the butcher closed at six. The Renewal Front had gone where scandals went after the shouting, into courtrooms and committees and procedural language. Both governments were doing accountability in public and burial work in private. Damian still showed up in the papers, but in the tired middle now, in consultations and threats and reform panels. He was not a hero and not a disgrace. He was inconveniently real.

Inside WISE, the restructuring showed. Falk's desk in the analyst pool had been cleared within days of his arrest, but the gap still held its shape. There were new faces and new security checks. One junior officer at the checkpoint was comparing signatures as if ink itself had turned treasonous. Sylvia's review board had gone through the place hard enough that even the coffee tasted more cautious.

Sylvia had a file open on her desk when Anya came in. She closed it before Anya could read the label. The thought on the surface was quick and sour: *record. gaps. should've seen it sooner.*

"Sit down."

Anya sat.

"The review board has completed its assessment of your record. Your field status is reinstated, effective immediately. The cover identity remains intact. The summit exposure stayed in political channels, not intelligence ones."

She said it like a weather report. It wasn't cold or kind. It was final.

Anya let out a breath she had not wanted Sylvia to hear. "That's it?"

"No." Sylvia opened the file again, not enough for Anya to read, enough to make the paper sound deliberate. "That is the part you needed. This is the part I need. Your reports will be complete. Your improvisation will stop where institutional damage starts. And if you ever decide on your own what this service can survive not knowing, I will take your field status back so fast you will think this morning was a clerical error."

"Understood."

Sylvia looked at her for a long second over the rim of her glasses. *Too young for this. old enough to know better. both can be true.*

"You are very good at your job," she said. "That is not the same thing as being harmless."

Anya swallowed. "I know."

"Good. Then I don't have to waste time making you say it twice." Sylvia slid a badge wallet across the desk. "You're on rotation next week. Desk brief this afternoon. If I change my mind before then, you'll hear about it."

The leather felt familiar in her hand and not familiar at all. It held the old access card, her name, and her cover face. It was the little institutional rectangle that meant doors would open again.

"Thank you," she said, because she had already said the wrong thing once.

"Do not thank me. Do the work."

Outside, the sky over the diplomatic quarter had that late-March brightness that looked warmer than it was. She tucked the badge wallet into her coat and started walking.

The easy part, now, was calling Damian and knowing he would answer.

That had not been true in January.

The first call had come after a week. She was halfway back from a miserable briefing and thinking about dumplings when an unknown number lit up her phone.

"It's me," Damian said, and before she could say anything else he added, "I'm outside your building. Across the street. In the car. I know that sounded terrible."

He was standing beside the sedan when she got there, hands in his pockets, wind dragging at his coat. They didn't do the whole conversation from the top. He said the silence had started lying for him. He said if he needed a thought to stay his, he'd say so out loud. If he meant her to have it, he'd leave it where she could hear.

At the corner he looked at her on purpose and let one sit between them.

*I missed you.*

That had been enough to move the winter forward. After that there had been calls, bad tea in paper cups, one museum afternoon where neither of them cared about the paintings, and a week in February when Damian had to disappear into committee hearings and sent three-word messages at midnight like somebody learning not to make absence do all the talking for him. It had not become simple. It had become real enough to use.

By March, she could call him on the walk home and know he would answer.

"Six o'clock," she said when he picked up.

"Six. I'll be there."

"Don't bring wine. Papa will judge the label."

A pause. "I wasn't going to bring wine."

"You were absolutely going to bring wine."

Another pause, more wounded this time. "What kind does he drink?"

Anya laughed. It came easy now, easier than it used to, because the laugh no longer had to drag a private audit behind it. Damian knew about the telepathy. He knew and had stayed, not cleanly, not magically, but on purpose.

"Red," she said. "Nothing with a year on it he might actually recognize."

"That's an impossible category."

"I believe in you."

"You shouldn't."

She hung up smiling and walked the rest of the way home through streets full of people thinking about dogs and trams and paint and dinner, and for once none of it felt like weight she had to carry alone.

---

The Forger apartment smelled like onions pushed a little too far and hot oil full of cumin.

"Mama made curry," Anya said as she hung up her coat.

She could tell from the smell. She could also tell from Loid's thought: *The potatoes are raw in the middle. I checked one when she wasn't looking. The rice is ready. The rice will save us all.*

"Your mother worked very hard on it," Loid said from the kitchen.

He was setting out bowls, the ceramic set they used when somebody was coming over. There were four bowls and four place settings. He had angled Bond's water dish away from the table and left enough open floor near Damian's chair for Bond to appoint himself part of the evening without pretending to be a diner.

He glanced up at her once. *How did Sylvia put it. Don't ask too fast.*

"How'd it go?"

"I'm back in."

A small pause. "Good."

He put down the last spoon. "Did she make it sound like a favor."

"No. More like a threat with paperwork."

"That sounds right."

Anya stepped into the kitchen proper. The stove was doing several things at once. Rice steamed in one pot. Curry thickened in another. Yor's handwriting on the recipe card had acquired new oil stains and one frantic underline under the word *stir*. On the counter beside the sink sat a stack of clean plates on a dish towel.

Loid had his reading glasses on and was slicing a cucumber into exact clean coins.

"Help me with the tea things," he said.

She took down the tray because it was easier than saying no.

"I read the board abstract," he said after a minute.

There it was.

"Of course you did."

"It was on my desk."

"That feels worse somehow."

He did not answer that. He set the knife down in a straight line against the cutting board and looked at the cups instead of her.

"They confirmed enough," he said. "About Falk. About the blind channel. About the report."

"Yeah."

"Do you understand what I mean when I say enough."

Anya lined the cups up on the tray one by one. "I know what my report opened."

"That's not what I asked."

The answer wanted to come out defensive. She bit it back because that would have turned him into her handler and she was tired of that being the easiest version of him to fight.

"Yes," she said. "I understand."

Loid was quiet for a beat. *Good. She should.* Then, under that: *Too young. Not an excuse. Still true.*

"The board kept calling it improvisation," he said.

"It wasn't."

"No."

He rinsed the knife though it did not need rinsing yet. "It was a choice."

She leaned a hip against the counter. "I know that too."

"Do you."

"Stop asking like I don't."

His mouth tightened, not angry exactly. He looked more tired than that.

"I'm asking because dead people don't care whether the mistake was deliberate," he said. "Crane doesn't. Falk certainly doesn't."

The name hit like a bruise you had forgotten until somebody pressed it.

"I wasn't trying to get anybody killed."

"I know." He turned off the tap. Water kept ticking down the drain. "That is part of why this is hard to be angry about cleanly."

She looked at him.

He reached up, took off his glasses, and set them beside the sink. "The worst part isn't that you lied into the system. It's that I know exactly how a person gets to that lie."

Her face must have changed because he looked away first.

"You thought you could hold the cost yourself," he said. "You thought if you managed it well enough, the damage would stay local."

"You don't get to sound like you weren't there," Anya said.

"I'm not."

"You trained me."

"I know."

"You taught me how to read rooms and move people before they noticed I was moving them."

"I know that too."

The words landed between them with no way around them.

From the stove, curry burped ominously. Neither of them moved.

Loid set his hand on the edge of the counter. "This isn't me pretending I'm innocent because I'm older. It isn't me pretending I don't recognize my own methods in you. I do. That's the problem."

Anya swallowed hard enough it hurt.

"So what," she said. "Now what."

He looked down at the counter again, more carefully than he needed to.

"Now you don't do the next one alone."

She frowned. "The next what."

"The next impossible call where somebody you care about and the work stop fitting in the same sentence." He met her eyes then, direct enough to make it uncomfortable. "If you decide protecting somebody is worth lying into the system again, you tell me before I find out from the wreckage."

"What if you try to stop me."

"I probably will."

"That doesn't make the sales pitch stronger."

"I'm not selling it. I'm telling you the new terms."

She looked down at the tea tray because the porcelain gave her somewhere to put her eyes.

"Are you saying this as my father or my handler."

"I'm saying it as the person who got very tired of discovering how much those jobs have been the same job."

That one hit harder than she wanted, not because it was cruel but because it wasn't.

The kitchen door bumped open with Yor's hip. She came in holding a wooden spoon like she had forgotten where she'd meant to take it.

"Oh," she said, looking between them. "Did I interrupt something."

"No," Loid said at once.

"Yes," Anya said at the same time.

Yor blinked. *They're doing the quiet sharp thing. Please not tonight.*

"I wanted to ask if we have enough tea," Yor said. "And also if the curry smells normal or if I should start over. Not all the way. Not from the beginning. Maybe from the point where it became... this."

Loid crossed the kitchen in two steps and kissed her temple in passing on his way to check the pot.

"It smells good," he said.

"You are a very loving liar."

"I'm a very loving husband."

That got the corner of her mouth up, which had obviously been the point.

Anya carried the tray out to the table while Yor followed with the spoon and the thought-noise of somebody trying not to have a ruined evening by sheer force. Halfway to the sideboard Yor stopped, head turning toward the apartment door at a sound in the hall. Footsteps passed. Two voices moved by. There was no knock.

Her shoulders stayed high until the sound moved on.

*Not him. Or maybe good if not him. I don't know anymore.*

Anya pretended to fuss with the napkins so she would not have to look directly at her mother while hearing that.

Loid came out with the cucumber plate and set it down.

"Not before six," he said quietly to Anya as he passed.

"That was already more than before six."

"Yes."

"Was that the whole thing."

"No."

Before she could answer, the buzzer went at six-oh-three.

Yor's thoughts came in a rush: *He's here. Is there curry on my face. Thank god it's him. Why am I relieved it's him.*

Loid opened the door.

Damian stood in the hall in a dark coat with his hair combed and a bottle of wine held in both hands like evidence.

*Don't think about the wine. You brought the wine. She told you not to bring wine. Don't think about it.*

"Damian," Loid said, holding out his hand.

The handshake was exact. Loid's were always exact. Under the politeness, his assessment clicked into place: *Firm grip. Nervous. New coat. He dressed for this anyway.*

"Thank you for having me, sir. Loid. Mr. Forger." Damian's face tried to recover and did not quite get there. *Pick one. You practiced this. You practiced this on the walk over.*

"Come in," Loid said.

Bond got there before Yor did, lumbering up from the rug to inspect Damian's cuffs with grave household seriousness.

"Oh," Yor said, appearing from the kitchen with her sleeves shoved up past her elbows and a streak of curry on her cheek. "Damian! You came. And Bond likes you already. That's a good sign. Oh, and you brought wine. That's so thoughtful."

Loid took the bottle and looked at the label.

Damian's mind went white-hot with panic. *He's reading it. She told me he'd read it. Think about anything else. The couch. Dog hair. Very normal family. Extremely normal family dog hair.*

"It's a nice bottle," Loid said.

Whether that was true or diplomatic was impossible to tell.

They sat. There were four human plates and four cups. Bond stationed himself near Damian's chair with the patience of an animal who had been in this house long enough to know food eventually found a floor.

Yor served the curry with the hopeful pride of somebody who had spent all afternoon on a thing and needed it to count. The sauce was thick and dark. The potatoes were hard in the center. The spice hit fast enough that Anya's eyes watered on the first bite. She reached for the rice. Loid had made it perfectly, of course.

Damian took one bite and his thoughts turned into a full-body emergency. *Oh. Oh god. My mouth. Okay. Smile. This is Anya's mother's cooking. You are going to eat this and you are going to survive it.* He reached for his water. Then panic doubled back on itself. *Don't reach too fast. People drink water. Normal dinner water. Why am I thinking about how to drink water. She can hear me thinking about water.*

He caught her catching it.

For a second his face blanked like he meant to force every thought back down where she could not get at it. Instead he looked at her over the rim of the glass.

*It's bad. Don't laugh. I'm still glad I came.*

It landed differently. The water thoughts had spilled out of him. This one was placed. She had not taken it. He had offered it awkwardly, like he had set a fragile thing on the table between them and was pretending not to watch whether she would pick it up.

Anya bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to keep her face steady.

"This is really good, Mrs. Forger," Damian said.

His voice held. His eyes did not.

"Oh! Really? You don't think it's too... Loid thought maybe the chili was a bit much..."

"It's got character," Loid said.

He kept eating with the durable calm of a man who had been married to Yor for fourteen years and understood that this was one of the forms love took in his house. *Better than last month. Potatoes are a problem. Sauce is better.*

"I used less chili this time," Yor said brightly.

Damian took another bite with the grim focus of a diplomat entering hostile territory. *I am finishing this plate. I am not disgracing myself in front of her parents. If my tongue dies, it dies in service.*

Anya looked down at her rice before the laugh could get out.

"You're too thin," Yor told him.

"I..."

"She says that to everyone," Anya said.

"I don't say it to everyone. I said it to Becky once and she was very pleased."

"That's different. Becky hears it as a compliment."

"Well, with Damian I mean it nutritionally." Yor pushed the curry closer to him. "Have more."

Damian did not look at the serving spoon like it was a weapon. He deserved credit for that.

"Thank you," he said, with the bravery of a condemned man.

The conversation settled into small things. Yor asked about work. Damian gave the polished version, careful and qualified. Loid asked about the new oversight committee in the same tone he might have used to ask someone to pass the salt. Damian answered. Loid lined up another question in his head. *Who actually chairs the subcommittee. See which name he gives.* At the exact same time Damian thought, *If he asks for committee names I'm dead,* and Yor thought, *Nobody is eating enough rice.*

"Nobody is talking about committee chairs at dinner," Anya said.

Three faces turned toward her.

Loid set down his fork. "I hadn't asked about committee chairs."

Heat climbed her neck too fast. "You were about to. You get that face."

"What face?" Yor asked.

"The interview face," Anya said. "The one where he acts normal and then suddenly you're explaining procurement law for half an hour."

Damian made a noise into his water glass that might have been relief trying not to become laughter.

Loid looked at her for a beat. His thought flashed first. *One evening. Keep the promise.* Then, aloud, he said to Damian, "I'm interested in your friend's work."

"He's not my friend."

"Your colleague."

"He's not my colleague either."

Loid took a sip of Damian's wine. "What term would you prefer."

Anya opened her mouth and immediately regretted it. Damian's thoughts were no help at all for a second. *We do not have a term. We read each other's minds and kissed in a safe house and I have no term for that. There is probably not a term in any language.*

Then, quieter and very deliberately, he gave her the only answer he could get away with.

*Please don't let me answer that in front of your father.*

She nearly choked on rice.

"Damian's fine," she said, coughing once into her hand. "Just Damian."

Loid inclined his head. He did not ask about committee chairs.

Yor brightened at the successful redirect as if she had personally arranged it. "Good. Then Damian, can you tell me if young men still wear those wide ties on purpose or if Becky was right and they are a warning sign."

"I made one mistake," Damian said.

"A neck-level mistake," Anya said.

"An internationally witnessed neck-level mistake," Yor agreed.

"Thank you both."

"She called him herself," Anya said to Loid. "Becky phoned and told him his summit tie was a sartorial war crime."

Yor laughed hard enough to put a hand over her mouth. Loid's expression stayed nearly straight and still somehow became visibly amused.

"Did she," he said.

"Twice," Damian said. "Then she sent color options."

"Did you listen."

"I am not an idiot."

"Debatable," Anya said.

His face stayed politely resigned. *You're enjoying this too much.*

She was, and she knew it.

Bond wandered under the table halfway through the meal, followed the warmth to Damian's chair, and folded himself onto Damian's feet with all his weight on Damian's good shoes.

Damian looked down. His face went soft at once. *The dog is on my feet. The spy family's precognitive dog is on my feet. This feels important.*

He did not move.

"He likes you," Yor said.

"Bond doesn't settle on anyone," Loid added.

Damian's ears went pink again.

"You can move him if you need to," Yor said.

"No," Damian said, too quickly. Then, more carefully, "No, this is fine."

Bond sighed in a way that suggested he agreed.

Yor cleared the curry and brought out fruit and tea. The apartment shifted with the meal the way it always did. Plates stacked up. Steam changed shape. Chairs eased back a little from the edge of whatever formality had kept them upright through first servings.

At the sideboard Yor paused with the teapot halfway to the tray. Someone had stopped outside the apartment door. It was only weight on the floorboards and a breath of hallway quiet, but every line of her body changed at once. Stillness came first. Then the social smile went back on a second too late when the footsteps moved on and kept going.

She poured the tea too full into her own cup.

*If it was Yuri he'd knock. If he wanted to knock he'd have done it by now. Not tonight. Not tonight.*

Damian was talking about a deputy minister who had tried to pronounce Becky's surname three different ways on a panel and failed all three. Nobody at the table but Anya heard the catch in Yor's breathing when she sat back down.

She set the fruit plate in the middle. "Sorry. I spilled a little."

"It's fine, Mama."

Yor nodded and touched the saucer back into place though it was already straight.

The talk turned lighter on purpose, circling Becky's spring fundraiser, the fact that one of the auction items was apparently a weekend at a hunting lodge nobody actually wanted to hunt at, Loid's quiet refusal to be drawn into any conversation about charitable galas, and Yor's instant willingness to be drawn into all of them.

"Becky's very good at organizing," Yor said. "She planned Anya's twenty-first birthday party. The one with the... what was it, the ice sculpture?"

"It was a swan," Anya said. "It melted all over the buffet table."

"It was beautiful before it melted," Yor said, loyal to the end.

Damian's thoughts snagged on the word *birthday.* *At her birthday three months ago. I knew her name. I didn't know the rest of her yet. I still don't know all of it.* He looked across the table at her and tried, badly, to think about tea. *Tea. Tea is hot. Tea is normal.*

Then his gaze caught on her hair in the kitchen light and the thought dissolved completely. *Oh, come on.*

Anya looked down at her cup because her hand had gone unsteady again.

They were talking about windows and neighbors and whether the tram schedule was actually changing when Loid's next question started building. *When does Sylvia put her back in the field. How much did they make her say.* Anya cut across it before it reached his mouth.

"Mama, weren't you saying you wanted to see that movie with the train crash."

Yor brightened at once. "Yes. The one where the conductor has a secret child and also maybe steals diamonds."

"That sounds improbable," Damian said.

"That's what makes it fun," Yor said.

Loid reached for the teapot. His face had gone very still in a way nobody at the table but Anya ever noticed quickly enough.

Then the sentence was there, plain enough to stop her hand on the cup.

*She always knew exactly the right moment to change the subject. Even when she was small.*

Anya went still with the teacup in her hand.

He did not think the rest out where she could hear it. He poured Yor more tea and looked at Anya with that focused face he got when pieces almost fit and he had not decided yet whether he wanted them to. The next thought never came. In the silence after it, Anya's own memories started kicking up like startled birds: birthday presents in Yor's closet, a school meeting she had steered sideways before anybody said too much, a WISE training exercise where she had answered before the briefing was done. Maybe those were his moments. Maybe they were only hers. She couldn't tell. If he had a real conclusion, it stayed down where her telepathy couldn't reach.

"Anya?" Yor asked. "Do you want more tea?"

"I'm good."

The tea had gone cold. She drank it anyway.

Loid reached to top off his own cup. His thoughts had already moved somewhere gentler. *Four at the table, dog under it. Different. I don't mind it.*

Under that, under all the dinner noise, Anya kept catching the same buried thing in Yor. They weren't full sentences, only scraps rising and sinking with the steam from her cup. *Yuri.* *No knock at the door.* *He stopped it.* *Not tonight.*

Anya did not get the whole shape from Yor's head. She got the rest from the silence that had stretched for weeks and from the fact that nobody from the SSS had come. If the summit camera review had gone up the chain clean, it would have been over by now. Yuri had buried the footage or stalled the review or locked it in a drawer only he could open. But the footage and Yuri were not the same problem. A camera could show a woman moving too fast. Yuri could say who she was. Yuri could say what he had seen with his own eyes.

Anya looked at her mother. There was curry on her wrist, her sleeves were shoved up, and old pale scars showed on her hands. She was a woman who could kill four men in under a minute and spend the afternoon making dinner because she wanted tonight to be nice.

She could tell them. *I hear thoughts. I hear yours. I've always heard them.*

The thought turned her stomach cold. Not because they would stop loving her. Because it would hand twenty years back to them changed.

*Not tonight.*

Damian was watching her. For once his thoughts were not trying to hide from her.

*Heavy. Can't fix it. Can stay.*

She looked at him. He held the look and, because he meant her to have it, left the rest where she could hear it too.

*I'm here. Your dad's a spy, your mom nearly killed me with curry, your dog has my feet, and I'm still here.*

Her chest squeezed hard and warm.

The evening wound down in pieces. Coats were found. Shoes were rescued from under Bond. Yor packed leftover curry into a container Damian failed to refuse. Loid shook Damian's hand at the door, and one quick surface thought slipped past before it vanished: *He'll do.*

Anya sat on the couch. Bond had taken back the rug and pressed his side against her bare feet. The apartment was quiet in the good way, with pipes in the wall, a cupboard door closing in the kitchen, and one floorboard in the hall giving the usual soft complaint.

She let the quiet stay what it was.
