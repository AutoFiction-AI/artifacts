# Chapter 15

The International Conference Center smelled of floor polish and burned coffee from the press mezzanine. Anya cleared the east-side screening with Damian's credential clipped high on her lapel and a reporter's notebook under one arm. The screener barely looked at her. Good. It left her free to notice the two details that mattered from the East Gallery: the south wall was all glass, and Deputy Minister Gerhardt would have to stand with it at his back.

Four hundred minds hit her at once.

She sat, braced one hand on the armrest, and let the noise come through.

*if this runs past four my daughter will kill me...*

*translator three is missing paragraph six again...*

*coffee's burned, of course it's burned...*

*south service stairs at thirteen fifty, don't look at the doors again...*

Anya opened her eyes.

That could have been security. It could have been catering. In a room this crowded every guilty thought arrived looking important until the next one shoved it aside.

She kept listening.

*Voss is here. Great. So the old men brought their knives after all...*

*if the Westalians say inspections we slow-walk it to committee...*

*first report has to sound Westalian or the whole thing turns stupid...*

That one came from below and to the right, not the press row beside her. It was a male voice, flat with concentration, not panic or fantasy.

On the platform the moderator shuffled his remarks. Gerhardt sat with one hand on the note card beside his water glass. The printed program inside Anya's notebook put his response at fourteen hundred exactly, second speaker after the Westalian deputy minister. He would have to stand to reach the microphone.

*when he stands, center mass. Glass won't matter if the angle stays clean...*

Anya's fingers bit into the notebook.

She looked down as if checking her notes and found a man in a charcoal suit under the gallery with a press lanyard he wore too still. Everyone else in that row did normal bored summit things. He kept mapping doors, railings, glass, podium.

Her headache started there, a blunt pulse behind her left eye.

She listened harder.

*thirteen fifty they're in place, fourteen hundred the deputy stands, if the first noise comes from Westalian side security writes the rest for us...*

It was not one clean thought. Three different ones, caught in pieces and stitched together around the same geometry: south service stairs, south glass, Gerhardt standing at fourteen hundred, Westalian blame. Bond's red-smeared building vision overlaid the terrace for one sick second. Anya swallowed against it and kept breathing.

It was a staged assassination attempt. Gerhardt dead on camera. Westalis blamed before the blood dried. The summit over with him and peace going with it.

The headache sharpened when she opened herself wider, trying to hold onto the source.

*don't look at the camera, don't look at the...*

*if the line's compromised, south first, comms after...*

*why is the shrimp cold in the middle...*

The last one punched a laugh into her throat. It also wrecked the signal. By the time she cut through the absurdity and reached for the operative mind again, it had sunk back into four hundred other thoughts, some frightened, some petty, most useless.

She scanned the hall anyway. Gerhardt's water glass flashed at the podium. The south doors sat under a discreet security camera. A clean shot from the terrace would not need much time. It would only need him upright.

She stood.

She had no earpiece and no radio. The summit's countermeasures would flag any unauthorized transmission before she got half a sentence out. Their plan had dead drops for exactly this reason. She tucked the notebook under her arm and took the side stairs down to the service corridor, forcing herself not to hurry until the gallery doors shut behind her.

The corridor smelled of bleach, old wiring, and government coffee. Staff and security moved through it in practiced silence. Anya walked fast and read what she could as each mind slid past.

*get the interpreter backup to room five...*

*my God, if this blazer button goes now I'm resigning...*

*Falk's in east comms. If the main board drops the cold line on rack three buys twelve...*

She stopped so hard the heel of one shoe skidded.

It was Falk.

Gerald Falk in east comms, knotted up with venue traffic he should never have touched. And the rest of the thought was worse: main board, rack three, buys twelve.

It was not enough yet. It could be building contingency chatter. It could be the one foggy thread that stole minutes from the live shooter on the terrace if she chased it now.

She hated decisions that only worked if she was right the first time.

At the east-side fire exit she crouched behind a red extinguisher cabinet, tore a page from her notebook, and wrote fast enough to make the letters lean.

*South terrace 1400. Gerhardt target. Falk in comms room east corridor.*

She added *rack 3? cold line?* and stared at it.

Question marks killed field notes. So did bad facts.

She struck the line out hard enough to tear the paper, folded the sheet twice, and slid it behind the extinguisher bracket where Loid would check. Fifteen minutes was their agreement. If everything held, he would find it before 13:45. If it did not, paper was already the smallest broken part of the plan.

On the way back she kept listening.

*if the deputy from Westalis cuts the second paragraph the old bastard will still stand, fine...*

*don't scratch your neck, only guilty people scratch their necks...*

*east rack stays dark unless the board dies, remember that, remember...*

That one vanished before she could sort whether it came from Falk, a technician, or someone who only knew one narrow job and nothing bigger. She carried it with her anyway.

By the time she retook her seat the hall had thickened with the specific silence that came right before microphones went live. Her temples pulsed in time with it. She put both feet flat on the floor and reopened herself to the room.

The moderator gave introductions. The Westalian deputy minister rose. Gerhardt sat with one hand on his note card, waiting for his turn.

Below her, the man in the charcoal suit still did not look at the south doors.

*keep the line clean, wait for the stand...*

Anya counted breaths. She tracked Gerhardt's hand, the south glass, the press row's boredom, and the flickering security thoughts already beginning to bend west because Damian had started moving before anyone had invited him to.

The first stir came from the West Gallery while the deputy minister was still on his opening paragraph.

Damian crossed toward Werner Voss with one hand in his pocket and his face arranged too flat to pass for casual. Half the security detail thought *Desmond* before they even turned their heads. His own surface thoughts hit her sharper than the rest because she knew the shape of them by now.

*Railing. Papers. Make him look at the second signature.*

Voss turned before Damian reached him.

*Not here, you stupid boy.*

Anya could not hear the first exchange through the translation feed and the room's collective throat-clearing, but she did not need every word. Damian set the papers on the gallery rail instead of handing them over. Voss touched the top page. His thumb tightened when he hit the second sheet.

*He knows it,* Damian thought, hot and immediate.

Voss's mind moved faster and meaner.

*Who else saw this. How many copies. Desmond's child, Desmond's posture, same disease.*

The microphones caught a pocket of quiet. This time the words carried.

"You say my name out loud," Voss said, voice low but distinct across the hush, "and your own side calls you unstable before they call me guilty."

Damian did not move. "That sounds like an admission."

"That sounds like advice." Voss's gaze flicked to the floor, the cameras, the delegates already leaning their way. "You think you've cornered one man. Pull my thread and see how fast both sides start burning paper. They'll bury you before they let me stand here alone."

That was enough. Not a lecture. A threat. And the threat told her what she needed anyway: the rot crossed the room and both flags were already standing on it.

*Terrace team hears the floor break and moves. Good,* Voss thought.

Anya lurched half out of her seat.

Damian caught it in Voss's face or his own nerve ran out at the exact right time. He gathered the papers before Voss could decide to take them and turned toward the floor.

The moderator looked annoyed when Damian requested a procedural point. The Ostanian delegation chair looked alarmed before the microphone even went live. Alarm, Anya thought, not surprise.

"State your point," the moderator said.

Anya did not hear Damian's first words so much as watch the room receive them.

Heads turned before he finished the sentence. The Westalian deputy minister stalled mid-breath. Two photographers near the side aisle lifted cameras in the same motion, grateful and vicious.

"I am entering documentary evidence," Damian said, his voice steadier now that he had already set fire to the thing, "that Councillor Werner Voss and connected procurement entities inside our own defense apparatus routed funds and materiel to the Renewal Front. I am also stating for the record that those entities overlap with commercial intermediaries tied to Westalian defense firms represented in this building."

Silence took the hall for half a beat.

Then minds burst open everywhere.

*Desmond, sit down.*

*Moderator, cut his mic.*

*Jesus Christ.*

*He's right.*

*He's dead after this.*

*No, he just saved the minister. Unless he didn't. Unless we're already late.*

Damian kept going because stopping would have turned the moment into a plea.

"If you want shell names, I have them. If you want signatures, I have those too. If anyone in this room has been using defense procurement to fund the same people this summit was called to address, the documentation is here. It runs in both directions. It has for years."

The Ostanian chair grabbed for Damian's sleeve and missed. Damian's thoughts came through in hard clipped flashes now, no polish left.

*Too late to be careful. Too late to be anyone's son about it.*

Across the gallery, Voss did not leave. He stayed broad and still and began calculating the wreckage.

*Which desk moves first. Which counsel panics. Which reporter gets the first clean name.*

Anya pushed farther into the room and regretted it instantly. Four hundred minds under pressure did not get louder. They got sharper. Security. Press. Delegates. Terrace team. East comms. Every thread came barbed.

From somewhere east and below, concrete and wiring dragged another channel against the inside of her skull.

*It's Falk.*

It was Loid, not spoken but thought, cold with recognition.

Then Falk, thinner through the building but unmistakable once it hit.

*No abort code. There was never going to be an abort code.*

Anya's hand went to the side of her head.

The moderator hammered for order. Nobody gave it to him. People were already sorting themselves into camps visible only in posture and surface thought: furious that the allegation existed, furious it had been said in public, furious because they had just understood what would happen to them if it stuck.

And under all of it, from the south side, the shooter's mind tightened like a wire.

*wait for the noise. wait for the stand. now if the room breaks first, now...*

The shot cracked from the terrace doors before Gerhardt ever got to his feet.

Glass burst white at the south side of the hall. For one stupid beat the room tried to misname it as a camera pop, an equipment failure, somebody dropping a tray.

Then the shooter thought at her in one clean spike.

*Missed.*

Anya saw Yor through the burst-glass glare a fraction late, dark blazer, cream blouse, already inside the distance where aim stopped mattering.

After that the south side stopped looking like procedure and started looking like Bond's vision with the timing pulled tight.

One man near the doors dropped with both hands at his throat and made a sound the hall could hear even through the alarm. Another turned too slowly and went to his knees with blood punching through his fingers at the side of his neck. A third hit the railing hard enough to ring it, a knife sunk above his collarbone. On the stairs the last body jerked once under a twist that looked wrong even from the gallery.

Four minds flared and went out in ugly fast sequence.

*what is she...*

*knife knife knife...*

*radio, move, move...*

Nothing.

The live minds around them hit harder.

*South side, shots fired.*

*Who engaged?*

*No, don't let press see the blood, Jesus...*

And over the top of all of it, bright enough to cut through everything else for one impossible second:

*Sis.*

Yuri.

The word hit Anya so hard she stood without meaning to. Her chair legs scraped. The photographer beside her cursed and swung toward the glass.

Somewhere beyond the broken south doors, radios erupted. One voice demanded identification. Yuri's thoughts came back rigid with effort.

*Don't say her name. Don't say sister. Don't say anything you can't live inside.*

"Unknown Westalian security asset," he said.

The lie cost enough that she felt it through two floors of concrete and noise.

Gerhardt had vanished under security bodies. The moderator was still yelling into a microphone nobody was listening to. Damian stood half turned between the floor and the south side, exposed in every direction now, and the room had no idea whether to drag him down or point cameras closer.

The thoughts from east comms kept sawing at her.

*Twenty years.*

*You used my child?*

*Your building called it good work.*

*Crane.*

That last one hit her in the knees.

It came tangled with Falk's stubborn conviction and Loid's blank controlled fury. She could not pull the whole exchange through concrete and panic, only pieces, but the pieces were enough to make her stomach pitch. Crane. Her report. Good work. She did not need the full sentence to know the direction it pointed.

The hall disintegrated into managed panic. Press already had handsets up. Security minds flooded the room in blunt urgent bursts.

*camera six locked south side, hold that feed...*

*Desmond just detonated his own career...*

*No, he just saved the minister, don't be stupid...*

*green light. good. lower east is still moving. booster's hiding it...*

Anya froze.

Not because the thought was complete. It was one surface flare from a man three rows behind her already thinking about exits and blame. But it hit the earlier corridor fragment and locked into place: rack three bought twelve if the board dropped, and the lower east channel was still moving with the booster hiding it.

She got up so fast the seat snapped back against her legs.

The headache that followed had teeth. The room tipped left, then right. She grabbed the press rail until the wave passed and shoved toward the side stairs with one hand hard against her temple.

If she stayed, she could keep reading the room and maybe learn what the relay was carrying. If she left, Damian's thread vanished to her and the summit became four hundred muffled radios she could not sort. The decision still was not clean. She made it anyway.

The east corridor lights stabbed at her eyes. Every footfall jarred her teeth. She passed the fire exit where she had left the note. She passed two security officers running the other way and caught *where the hell is east comms support* from one of them before she hit the Communications Room door hard enough to send it banging into the wall.

Loid was inside, shoulder near the doorway. Falk sat on the floor against the far wall with his wrists cinched behind him by Loid's belt. The main screens were dead.

"Lower east rack," Anya said. Her voice came out thin and wrong. "A backup relay's still live. He kept thinking green light and the booster."

Loid did not ask who *he* was. He moved at once.

The room felt smaller with the main board dead, all dark screens, residual heat from the electronics, and the smell of hot dust and cable insulation crowding the air. Falk turned his head to watch them. Bound did not make him harmless. It barely made him quiet.

Loid dropped to one knee in front of the lower rack. Anya went down beside him because standing had become unreliable and because sight alone would not find the thing fast enough.

Metal faceplates and bundled cables crowded the rack. Low on it sat a dull gray signal booster, rectangular and innocent-looking, with more dead space behind it than the casing should have allowed.

*good. green light. just keep it moving.*

Her hand shook when she pointed. "There. Behind it."

Loid tore the mounting screws free with two hard turns and wrenched the booster housing sideways. A smaller device sat recessed in the dark behind it, no larger than a paperback, one green indicator burning steadily on its face.

Falk closed his eyes.

Loid ripped the power lead free.

The green light died.

For one second nobody spoke. The silence after a correct guess never felt like relief. It felt like standing exactly where the next bad fact would land.

Then Loid found the tiny status strip along the relay edge and tipped it toward the ceiling light.

`BUFFER REMAINING 10:48`

Twelve minutes total. Just over a minute spent since the main board died.

The arithmetic held. That made it worse, not better.

"What was it carrying?" Loid asked.

Falk opened his eyes again. "Enough."

Loid set the dead relay on the desk with such care that the restraint became its own violence.

Anya was still on the floor. The relay's green light had burned itself onto the back of her eyes. She kept seeing it every time she blinked.

Falk's thoughts would not stay out, even with her skull trying to close around the noise.

*too neat. should've known she'd hear the rest sooner or later.*

*the girl writes like him.*

That one hit harder than the green light.

Loid must have read the change in her face. He turned from the dead relay to Falk.

"Say it again," he said.

Falk looked from Loid to Anya and back. His breathing was rough from being thrown down, but conviction still sat under it, dry and ugly and upright.

"Her first assessment," he said. "The one that told your desk to stop looking where it should've kept looking. It cut a clean lane through review, and I used it. Traffic the building stopped prioritizing moved through that lane. The packet that put Crane at that checkpoint was one of mine."

Anya did not feel herself breathe.

Loid's face emptied out the way it did when whatever he actually felt had become too expensive to show in front of witnesses.

"You used my child to clean your channel."

"I used work your building stamped excellent." Falk swallowed and kept going because ruining other people had turned into his idea of honesty years ago. "That was the whole sickness. Everyone wanted the numbers filed smooth enough to call surrender peace."

Crane.

She had spent months refusing to think his name all the way through. Border operation. Checkpoint compromise. The memorial Loid said she did not need to attend. Now Falk had shoved the name into the room and there it was, whole and ugly and impossible to misfile again.

Boots pounded in the corridor.

Loid crossed to the door, checked the hall, then came back long enough to haul Falk upright by the arm.

"Not another word in here," he said.

He marched Falk out ahead of the response team and handed him over with only the rank and minimum explanation the corridor required. Security flooded the room around the dead consoles. Nobody asked Anya anything direct yet. That felt temporary in the worst possible way.

When she finally got clear of the room and the building's bright official interior, the cold outside hit her like a second concussion.

She made it to the stone wall beside the service exit and braced one hand against it.

The summit was still emptying by the front steps. Sirens climbed the street. Camera crews crowded the barricades in hard bright knots. Every mind within range was doing the same frantic sorting: shots fired, summit blown open, Desmond, Voss, minister safe, who gets blamed, who gets buried, who gets quoted.

Crane.

She had written the report because Damian was useful and frightened and not what WISE thought he was. She had written it because she wanted room to move. She had written it because the first lie had worked and competence made the second lie easier. After that the reasons had piled up until they looked like structure.

None of them put Crane back anywhere.

Loid came out a few minutes later with his belt still missing and her folded note in one hand. He stopped beside her, not close enough to touch.

"Can you walk," he asked.

She nodded.

He studied her for one long second, the way he studied locked doors before deciding whether they were safe to open.

"Good," he said. "You're with me until we're home."

"Dad, I..."

"Not here." His voice stayed level. "And not in pieces. All of it."

That was worse than shouting would have been.

Out by the barricades, three separate minds were already trying Damian's name on different verdicts.

*traitor.*

*hero.*

*useful.*

Loid turned toward the street. Anya went with him.

He kept her close enough to stop if she drifted and far enough that their sleeves never touched.
