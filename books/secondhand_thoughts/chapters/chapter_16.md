# Chapter 16

By the time Loid got her back to the apartment, the summit smoke had dried into her coat and the kitchen clock said 2:11.

He turned on the stove light, not the ceiling one. It left the room in stripes: sink, table, the edge of the whiteboard still scarred with summit routes. His shirt was clean in the places he'd had time to clean. The cuff wasn't.

"Start with the part Desmond already knew," he said.

Anya stood at the table because sitting felt like permission to shake. Crane's name kept surfacing out of the summit noise with the same hard stop at the end. Falk was in cuffs. Yuri was still on the terrace in her head. Her father's hand had failed to land on her shoulder.

"He knew my real name before yesterday," she said. "Since the gala. He kept it quiet. At the summit he moved me off the main screening line because he knew somebody there could connect the cover to Eden."

Loid went still in the disciplined way that meant his mind had started moving too fast.

"How much does he know."

"My name. School. My family." She swallowed. "Not the rest."

The pause stretched.

"I'm due at Station in forty minutes," he said. "Sylvia will want Falk's access chain before dawn, and I'm not taking your explanation in fragments while half of WISE is still on fire." He looked at her then, direct and cold. "You get one stop. Desmond. You tell him whatever he needs before other people start filling in the blanks for him. Then you come back here and tell me all of it."

"Dad..."

"All of it," he repeated. "No disappearing."

The words landed with the same cold force as the street outside. Then, quieter and uglier for it:

"Crane is still dead. I'm not spending today chasing another surprise."

He left five minutes later with his coat half-buttoned and the kitchen light still on. Anya stood in it until the building door shut downstairs.

By the time she reached Damian's floor, Loid was on his way to WISE, Yuri's face on the terrace kept flashing through her whether she wanted it or not, and Crane was not getting any less dead.

She stood outside his door for four minutes before she knocked.

Four minutes gave her three neighbors through plaster and pipes: the woman downstairs arguing with herself about whether to call her sister, the man two doors down rewriting a complaint about the hallway lights until the wording got nasty, somebody above coughing wetly into a sink. It was normal apartment stuff from normal-size brains. Anya put her palm on Damian's door, caught the low hum of him on the other side, and jerked back before the thought shaped itself.

*Not like this. Not before the knock.*

She knocked.

The lock turned after one exact pause. Surprise hadn't done that. Decision had.

When the door opened, Damian was wearing a dark sweater with the sleeves shoved up. He looked like he'd been sitting still too long.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

*She's here. Okay.*

That one came through simple and flat, like he'd set it on the table between them.

He moved aside. She went in.

His apartment was neat in the boring, lived-in way. Books were lined up straight. One glass was drying beside the sink. Policy journals were stacked on the side table with a pen clipped to the top one. The couch still had the dent from his usual spot. One shoe lay half under it and the other had been left by the door. Last time she'd been here she read the place like a floor plan, exits and corners and signal strength. Tonight it just looked like where he took off his shoes and forgot about them, and where he'd spent an hour failing to do anything that counted as work.

The childhood box sat open on the coffee table.

The class photo had been pulled out and left beside it. Beside the photo lay a ministry notepad with one sentence started and then gouged out so hard the paper had torn.

Anya stopped.

It wasn't because of the photo. That part wasn't new. Since Becky's gala she'd heard the schoolyard flashing through him in pieces: pink hair, Becky crying, a six-year-old punch, the almost-name catching and slipping away. The bad part was the box open right now. He had been in there tonight.

He didn't offer a drink. Neither of them sat.

"I need to tell you this," she said.

*Yeah. I figured.*

His thoughts were already crowding around the wrong guesses: that the alliance was over, that there was new fallout from the summit, that she was leaving the city. None of it was close.

"Okay."

He leaned against the counter and crossed his arms. It might have passed for casual if you didn't look at the shoulders.

"It's not about the operation," Anya said. "It's about me. About what I can do."

She had rehearsed this in the bathroom mirror that morning with toothpaste on her lip. She'd rehearsed it on the walk over too. Every version sounded stupid. There was a clinical one, a dramatic one, and a tiny one. *I can kind of hear people.* That one made her want to throw herself into traffic.

"I'm a telepath," she said. "I read minds. I've been doing it since I was little. Four, maybe five, I don't know. I've never told anybody. Not my parents, not Becky, not WISE. Nobody."

Silence hit the room hard enough to feel.

*What.*

Then, a beat later:

*She said she reads minds.*

He drew one careful breath that barely moved his shoulders. Thoughts started firing past in broken pieces: the state dinner, the service corridor, Bretz, the safe house, every time she'd answered too fast, every time she'd known facts she shouldn't.

*She read my thoughts. All this time.*

"At the state dinner," he said. "That first night. You were reading my thoughts then too."

"Yes."

"Everything in that room. About the operation. About..."

The rest flashed before he said it: her smile, Eden Academy, a pink-haired girl.

"Yes," Anya said again.

His face changed, not because of the confession but because she'd answered the thought.

"Stop doing that." Sharper now. "Can you stop?"

"I can stop reacting. I can't stop hearing it. It's like sound."

He pushed off the counter and crossed to the sink, then back again, as if distance might blunt it. It didn't. The thoughts kept arriving from the same radius, only sharper now because he was listening to himself listening.

*Chair. Faucet. Window latch. Don't think about the safe house. Don't think about anything with a face.*

Anya looked at the floor.

"You can hear me trying not to think about it."

"Yes."

He squeezed the bridge of his nose until the skin blanched. "Jesus Christ."

*Every thought. God. The negotiations. The club. The park. The safe house.*

He caught the edge of the counter hard enough to click the glass beside the sink. Then he let it go and scrubbed a hand over the back of his neck. He had nowhere to put his hands. He had nowhere to put any of it.

*The kiss.*

That thought came up jagged and hot.

*The safe house. She was in my head when I kissed her.*

"Yes," Anya said quietly.

Anger arrived all at once. It wasn't loud, which made it worse. Every word came out too clear, clipped into the politeness he used when he was closest to fury.

"So every conversation we've had. Every time I thought I was telling you anything because I chose to tell you. You already had it."

"Yes."

"You used it on purpose."

The answer had to come fast or it would sound like she was shopping for one.

"Yes."

His mouth flattened.

"Say it properly."

Anya swallowed.

"I answered questions before you finished asking them. I changed what I was going to say because I already knew the answer sitting on top of you. Sometimes I stayed because I heard you wanted me there. Sometimes I left because I heard you were about to ask for more than I could take cleanly."

He stared at her.

"How many times is sometimes."

"Enough that I can't make it small."

His gaze jerked away again, like the sight of her made the fact worse.

"The safe house. My father. My mother. Things I said because I was trying to be honest."

"I already knew."

He looked away fast, like looking at her hurt. Two steps took him to the window. Both hands braced on the sill.

"My mother," he said to the glass. "At the reception."

"The marigolds on the terrace," Anya said. "Before she got sick."

His breath hitched out of him.

*I never said that out loud.*

Outside, lights kept turning on across Berlint like nothing in this room mattered to anybody else.

Anya let him have the anger. There was no excuse to offer, no speech about not choosing the ability, no little pile of true things that would still be useless here.

"How far does it go?" he asked.

"Surface thoughts. What's on top. If a memory is live, I get that too because you're thinking it right then. But I can't go digging. I can't pull up things you aren't having."

"Just the surface."

*As if the surface isn't the part that counts.*

He wasn't wrong.

"And it's not truth exactly," Anya said. "That's part of why it's such a mess. Surface thoughts lie all the time. People rehearse things, panic about things, imagine things. I get access. I don't get God."

He turned from the window enough to look at her.

"That's not better."

"I know."

"It's worse in a different way."

"Yeah."

His hand dragged down over his mouth.

"Does anybody else know?"

"No."

"Nobody."

"Nobody."

That landed differently. It didn't ease anything. It made the room stranger.

*I'm the first.*

Anya kept her face still. She had expected fury, revulsion, even a door in her face. She had not expected to hand him the ugliest thing she owned and realize he was the first person who had ever actually touched it.

"I'm sorry," she said. "And I'm not going to do the thing where I tell you not to be angry. Be angry."

"Don't manage it for me."

"Okay."

*Good.* A beat. *No. Not good. I don't know what I want.*

The next wave came through messier. It wasn't clean anger now. It was sick realization: every ugly little private thought handed over without consent, every soft one too.

Then, under all of it:

*She knows the bad parts and she still came here.*

Anya kept her face still. For once she let the thought stay where it belonged, even though she'd heard it.

"I don't know what to do with this," Damian said. His eyes were red around the edges. "I genuinely don't know what to do with the idea that every private thought I've had in front of you was..."

He stopped.

His gaze had dropped to the coffee table, to the box, to the photo lying beside it.

*Tell her.*

The thought came through clean as a bell.

He crossed the room, picked up the class photo, and held it up.

"Second row," he said. "Third from the left. Pink hair. That's you."

Anya looked.

She was six years old, mouth open in a grin at the photographer, one hand blurred from waving. Damian was in the third row, second from the right, already looking annoyed in exactly the way he'd been born to be.

"I know who you are," he said. "Anya Forger. Eden Academy, Class Three. You punched me when we were six because I called Becky a crybaby. I've known since the gala."

The shock wasn't *who*. It was *when*.

It was November, this apartment, the photo under his lamp, months stacked behind his voice.

"The gala," she said. "I knew you were close then. I heard that part. I didn't know you went home and made it certain."

"A classmate said your name. I came back here, found this, checked. It was you."

*She caught the circling. Not this room. Not the part where it became a fact.*

That hit harder than the school photo: his desk lamp, this table, his hands on the picture. She hadn't been here.

"At my parents' apartment," Anya said. "When you met my dad. At the summit yesterday. All that time."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

Damian took longer with the next breath.

"Because the operation still mattered. Because saying it in November would've blown everything sideways. And because I kept taking one more day instead. Then another."

He looked back at the photo, not at her.

"That's not noble. It's just what I did."

The notepad with the torn line sat between them. One more day sat there too, written into the paper until it failed.

"And the summit," Anya said. "The badge. East Gallery."

His head came up.

"What about it."

"You moved me there before plenary. Better sight lines, separate entrance, all that. I knew you were keeping me off the main line. I never knew why."

The pause this time was answer enough before he gave the words.

"The main press intake was under ministry screening after Kreuzfeld. Security was cross-checking credentials for anyone near the central galleries. One of my father's aides was sitting in that room, and he knew Eden rosters well enough to be dangerous. If you'd gone through the main line wearing borrowed papers, somebody had a real chance of putting the cultural attaché and Anya Forger in the same file before the afternoon session. East Gallery used the foreign overflow desk and a side stair. Less scrutiny. No Desmond staff. I kept you off that screening line."

The room tipped a little.

"Because you knew."

"Because I knew."

"You were protecting my cover."

"I was trying to keep it from getting worse."

There was no clean version of that. He knew it. She knew it.

"Why not tell me then," Anya asked, "if we were already standing in the same fire."

"Because if I said it there, in the middle of the summit, we would've had this conversation with half the ministry still watching the doors. And because..." He stopped, the correction visible before it happened. His eyes stayed on the photo, and his thumb worked harder over the paper edge. "Because I didn't want to lose you."

*Both. But mostly that second thing.*

Air went out of Anya in one rough push. Her knees gave up before the rest of her did. She sat on the floor beside the table because that was where her body had decided she belonged.

"So we both did it," she said. "Hidden information. Kept going."

Damian lowered himself onto the couch instead of staying over her. That was good. The distance helped.

"Not the same thing," he said.

"No. What I did is worse."

"Maybe."

"No. It is."

He didn't answer. The photo stayed in his hands. Two children stared up from glossy paper while the adults who used to be them failed to say anything useful.

"When you came to my parents' apartment," Anya said after a while, "what did you think you were doing."

His laugh was short and dead on arrival.

"Trying not to stare at your family photos. Trying not to act like I'd shown up at someone's dinner table carrying a second conversation nobody else could hear."

That made her chest hurt in a new place.

"You could've walked."

"Yes."

"You didn't."

"No."

His thumb moved over the edge of the photo without touching either of the children on it.

"I kept waiting for the girl from school and the woman from the ministry to separate into two different people. They didn't. That did not make any of this easier."

Anya folded her arms around her knees.

"No."

That was the nearest they got to agreement before the room ran out of first-hour words.

---

The second hour went quieter.

At some point Damian had put the kettle on and forgotten it. One mug of tea went cold beside the sink. Her water glass sat untouched and sweating on the table. Damian was back in the dent on the couch. Anya had climbed up to the far cushion with her knees tucked under her chin. The box stayed open between them.

The anger was still there. So was the part underneath it.

"When you say it's like sound," he said at last, "what does that actually mean."

"If it's one person and they're close, it's clear enough to hurt," Anya said. "Crowds smear. Strong feelings punch through walls. Distance helps. Not enough."

"And there's no way to shut it off."

"No."

He nodded once at the window, like the dark outside had just offered him a new category of bad.

"Does anybody else know?"

"No."

That landed differently.

"I'm the first."

"Yeah."

He looked back at her.

"And with people you care about."

She pressed her thumbnail into the seam of the cushion.

"I stay far enough back that it doesn't matter."

"Did that work."

"Until you."

The room held that without helping either of them.

"Then why now."

"Because Loid's waiting at home for the full account," she said. "Because Crane's still dead. Because after the gala you knew my name and still didn't say it, and yesterday I was still taking from you. I was done doing that."

His jaw moved once.

"Can I ask the ugly part now."

"Yeah."

"When we kissed in the safe house. Was that you, or was that me hitting you hard enough that you couldn't tell the difference."

Anya let out one thin breath.

"I don't know how to split it clean," she said. "In the safe house, when we kissed, I was reading you. I was trying not to, but you were too close and feeling too much. I could feel what you wanted. My body answered before I'd figured out whether I was answering you or me. That's the part I still can't sort."

He sat very still.

"Meaning maybe you wanted me because I wanted you badly enough for both of us."

"Meaning maybe the signal hit first. Not all of it. Not none of it. I don't know."

He looked down at his hands.

"Did you ever hear anything from me and leave it alone."

"Yes."

"What."

"You don't want that one."

"No," he said. "I probably don't."

But he stayed where he was, waiting. She fixed her eyes on the rim of the water glass.

"In the safe house bathroom, before you came back out, you were thinking that if you put your hands on me again you wouldn't stop where you should. You hated yourself for it and still wanted it. I didn't use that either."

His eyes closed.

*I asked. Christ. I asked for that one.*

He set the tea down too hard. A little spilled over his knuckles. Then he was moving.

"Anya."

She didn't answer. Her throat had gone useless.

"I'm not saying this makes sense."

"I know."

His hand came up, hovered once at her jaw, then settled there warm and unsteady. Her pulse kicked hard against his thumb. Thought and touch hit at the same time, rough enough to make her breath catch.

*Still want you. Still. Even now.*

He kissed her.

It went wrong before it went right. Her teeth clicked lightly against his. Wool scratched her palm when she grabbed his sleeve. His breath was hot and uneven against her cheek, and the want coming off him was so bright it hurt, tangled with shame and anger and the same stubborn refusal to let go.

*Don't make this clean. Don't make it mercy.*

When he pulled away, he stayed there a second with his forehead against hers, both of them breathing like they'd run for it.

"I can go," Anya said.

He looked at her, eyes wrecked open now.

"I can leave right now. I won't stay in the hall listening. If you want me back in the room after that, you ask."

For a long moment he said nothing. Then he sat down beside her instead of going for the door.

The couch dipped under the added weight. Their shoulders didn't touch. The class photo lay face-up on the table. In the kitchen, the kettle clicked as the metal finished cooling.

After a while he scrubbed a hand over his mouth.

"I need time," he said. "Not because I'm done. Because I need to think about this somewhere you can't hear me doing it."

"Okay."

"And when you leave, don't stay outside trying to measure it."

"I won't."

"I'll call you."

He believed the promise maybe halfway. It was enough for tonight.

Anya got one arm into her coat. He walked her to the door and stopped with his hand on the knob.

"Anya, wait."

She looked up.

"I'm glad you told me."

*I'm glad you told me.*

Her hand stalled on the other sleeve.

"Goodnight," she said.

"Goodnight."

The hallway light flattened everything. The place where his thumb had pressed under her ear kept beating under the cold. She took the stairs toward Loid's kitchen light without leaning back toward the door.
