# Chapter 6

By the time she reached their landing, Anya knew her mother was cooking. Garlic, sugar, butter, and heat had pushed past partnership into open hostility, a scorched sweetness that stuck to the stairwell walls and would probably still be there tomorrow.

Anya let herself in with the spare key she'd carried since she was nine. The apartment hit her the way it always did: warm, cluttered, faintly of Bond's fur beneath whatever Yor had done to the kitchen. Loid's shoes were lined up by the door, toes to the wall, exactly parallel. Yor's were kicked off at different angles, one on its side. Bond was asleep on the couch with his head hanging off the armrest, white fur covering the cushions in the thin permanent layer no amount of vacuuming ever fully beat.

"Anya!" Yor appeared from the kitchen doorway, flushed and holding a wooden spoon like a weapon she'd forgotten to put down. "You're early! I was going to have everything ready. The stew isn't quite, well, it's almost, it's getting there, I think."

*She used too much heat again. The bottom layer is definitely burned. But the top might be. No. The top is also burned. Oh no.*

"Smells great, Mama."

"Does it? Really?" Yor's face brightened with a hope that made Anya's chest hurt. "Loid said it smelled 'interesting,' which I think means he was being polite."

"It means it smells interesting," Loid said from inside the kitchen, where he was wiping down the counter with the unhurried efficiency of a man who had already finished his own preparations and was now managing the aftermath of someone else's. He came out drying his hands on a dish towel. "Welcome home."

He hugged her. Brief and warm, his arms tightening once before letting go. His thoughts were louder than the embrace. *She's thinner. The circles under her eyes are worse than last month. She's not sleeping enough. Is she running double shifts or is this something else? I should ask. I shouldn't ask. She'll tell me if it's work. She won't if it's about her. She never does.*

Anya pulled back and smiled at him. "I'm fine, Papa."

"I didn't say anything."

"You were about to."

He gave her the look, half amused and half something harder, the spy's assessment running beneath the father's warmth. It lasted less than a second. Then it softened and he was just her father again. "I cleaned up the leftover chicken — there's a plate on the counter."

"The stew is fine!" Yor called from the kitchen. There was a clatter, then a smaller clatter, then a pause. "Sorry. Fine. Everything's fine."

Anya dropped her bag by the door and crossed to the couch. Bond opened one eye, registered her, and closed it again. His tail moved once against the cushion, a single thump of acknowledgment that counted as his whole greeting now that his joints were what they were. Her fingers found the warm place behind his ears and his thoughts drifted through her like bathwater. *girl. home. floor cold. sleep.* A dog's mind was still the kindest thing in the world. No layers. No second meaning. Just the body and the moment.

Anya sat on the arm of the couch and let the apartment settle around her. The television murmured low, a news broadcast about ministerial trade talks that nobody was watching. Loid's reading glasses sat folded on a stack of newspapers he had already annotated with small pencil marks in the margins. They looked like idle habit. They were an indexing system he kept by reflex, cross-referencing sources and flagging discrepancies, and Anya had known that since she was seven and had read the system in his thoughts while he taught her long division. Yor's cardigan was draped over a chair. Two of Bond's rubber toys sat by the bookshelf in a row too orderly for a dog to have arranged. Loid's hand, tidying without noticing.

Loid settled into his chair and picked up the newspaper. His thoughts moved beneath the newsprint like current under ice. *The border patrol deployments are increasing again. Sylvia mentioned adjusted threat levels for the southeastern corridor last week. If Anya's sector is affected, no. She'd tell me. Or she wouldn't. She's twenty. She knows her job and I have to trust that even when I don't.*

The worry wasn't new. He carried it the way he carried everything else: quietly, efficiently, knowing full well that part of it was care and part of it was surveillance and not stopping anyway. Reading that knot in him had never gotten easier.

"The news said something about the trade negotiations stalling," she said, because it was the kind of thing a daughter would say.

"They'll restart. They always do." He turned a page. *She's asking about geopolitics on a family visit. That's either casual or it's not.*

Dinner was the stew. Yor served it with a hopefulness that bordered on prayer, ladling it into bowls with extra care as if gentleness could retroactively improve the cooking. Anya ate it. The stew was thick and combative and tasted like three different recipes had argued over the same pot and nobody had won. Her mother had spent the afternoon on it, though, and love was the only ingredient that had survived the heat. The chicken plate sat on the counter for anyone who needed it.

"Mrs. Kaplan complained about the pipes again," Yor said, blowing on her spoon. "She said the water pressure on the fourth floor was, what was her word, 'insulting.' She said the water pressure was insulting to her personally."

"It was low last week," Loid said. "I adjusted the valve in the basement."

"She didn't seem to know that. She was telling Bond about it in the hallway for about ten minutes. I think she believes he's the building superintendent."

"Bond was in the hallway?"

"He got out when I was bringing in the groceries. He just lay down in front of her door and she started talking. I didn't want to interrupt."

Anya laughed. It came out real and easy and for a moment she wasn't carrying anything. The sound of it filled the kitchen, too loud, probably, for a joke about water pressure and an old dog, but she had not laughed in days and the release was involuntary.

*She should laugh more. When did she stop laughing like that? She used to do it all the time. Stop. She's here. Don't catalog what's wrong. She's here.*

Loid's thought pressed against her and she looked at her plate.

"How's work, Mama? Besides the pipes."

"Oh, fine, fine." Yor waved her spoon. "Busy, but you know. They reorganized the whole records system at city hall, which was, um. An experience. I don't think Mr. Henning has recovered. He kept trying to file things in the old cabinets and they'd already been removed, so he was just standing in front of an empty wall holding folders. I felt terrible for him."

"Did you help?"

"I helped him find a chair and made tea. That seemed like the right amount."

Yor smiled, and the smile was real. Then the thought crossed under it, quick and bright enough to sting.

*The man on his knees. Begging. His hands weren't like the briefing said. The briefing said armed, dangerous, but his hands were empty and he was saying please, I have a, and the layout was wrong too, the door was on the wall where they'd said the window would be, the whole floor plan was*

Yor's fingers tightened on the spoon. "Anyway. The new system is fine. Just takes getting used to."

The smile came back a beat too fast. Anya lowered her spoon. Anya had not reached for that thought or tugged at it. It had just broken the surface and vanished again.

A man on his knees. Empty hands. A room that did not match the file. That was enough to tell her something had gone wrong on a Garden job and Yor did not want to look straight at it yet. Maybe the doubt was new. Maybe it had been there a while and this was just the first time it rose high enough for Anya to catch. There was no way to know. Yor had already pushed it back under and asked the table to stay dinner.

Anya knew even that much because she had stolen it.

Her hand went to the bread because it needed somewhere to go. Using any of this would mean admitting how she had gotten it. The only decent thing left was to act like she had heard nothing.

"This bread's good," she said.

"The place on Kirchner Street," Loid said. "The woman with the, what do you call it, firm customer service approach."

"She's mean," Yor said.

"She's efficient."

"She's efficiently mean. She told me I was buying too much rye."

"Were you?"

"That's not the point, Loid."

---

Bond woke up.

Not the slow surfacing of an old dog working himself out of sleep, the groaning, leg-cycling, dream-chasing kind where his nose twitched at phantom smells and gravity gradually hauled him back to the couch. This was different. His head came up rigid, eyes fixed on nothing, ears flat to his skull. For a second the years fell off him.

"Bond?" Yor half rose from her chair.

Anya was already inside it. Bond's mind cracked open and the images came hard, not thoughts or words but raw visual data hitting faster than she could organize it.

A building. Through Bond it came as a wide stone facade with columns, pale government stone, ceremonial steps. Then the angle changed. An upper terrace. Iron railing. Carved balustrade overlooking grounds torn up in ugly strips, grass and ornamental trees displaced. Men in stiff grey-blue formal SSS dress moved fast across the terrace. One was running. Behind them, below them, fire climbed with the hard orange color of accelerant. Stone cracked. Heat shoved at the image. Bond couldn't name any of it. His body only knew wrong.

Bond whined. The image shredded. Another one flashed, briefer this time: the entrance from ground level, people spilling through the portico, pressure in the air like a blow before sound.

Then it collapsed. Bond blinked twice, confused by his own alertness, and let his head drop back to the couch arm. His tail gave one uncertain wag. He sighed.

The whole episode had lasted maybe eight seconds.

"Just a dream," Loid said, settling back in his chair. He was watching Bond with the casual attentiveness he gave to everything nearby, the kind that looked like ease and was surveillance. *Precognitive episode. He hasn't had one that intense in months. Usually the signals are vague. Shapes. Feelings. This had edges. His eyes were tracking something. What did he see?*

Anya's fingers had dug into the couch cushion. Bond's visions blurred fast once they passed, but she had the bones of it: a government building, a terrace, SSS uniforms, fire that did not look accidental. When Bond's visions came this sharp, they were never nothing.

On the television, the trade coverage had shifted to an anchor listing upcoming diplomatic events. The ticker was too far away to read from the couch.

Telling Loid would blow open the oldest secret she had. Keeping her mouth shut meant hoping Bond had finally gotten one wrong. Neither felt possible.

"Papa." The casualness in her voice was something she had learned from him, that flat register that made important information sound incidental. "I heard something at work. Probably nothing, but there's been chatter about security concerns at government venues lately. Some kind of threat assessment floating around. Have you heard anything?"

Loid looked at her over the top of his newspaper.

For one second she was his daughter asking a normal question. Then something behind his eyes recalibrated and she felt the shift in his thoughts like a temperature drop.

*Interesting. She led with a disclaimer, 'probably nothing,' like she's trying to cushion the hit. She wouldn't do that unless it mattered more than she's letting on. 'Chatter at work.' From where? She didn't say. 'Threat assessment floating around.' That's not half-listening language. Either she heard it somewhere she can't name, or she's hiding where it came from.*

He set the newspaper down. "I haven't heard anything specific. Which venues are we talking about?"

"I don't know the details. Just a general thing about security posture at government events. Heightened levels or something. I wasn't really listening at the time." Too many extra words. Anya could hear them piling up and couldn't stop.

*She's not lying about the threat itself. She wouldn't make up a security scare just to fill silence. But it didn't come from work chatter. She got it somewhere else, or in some way she can't explain. She tightened the second I asked for details. Remember that.*

"I'll mention it to a few people," Loid said. "See if anything matches."

He picked the newspaper back up. The thought that came with it landed clean and cold. *Something is off about her sourcing and she knows I caught it. Revisit.*

Anya took another bite of stew. He had shelved it, not believed it, and that was bad enough.

Yor was already telling a story about a colleague at city hall whose birthday cake had arrived with the wrong name on it. Yor laughed at the part where Heinrich took the whole cake home before anyone else could touch it. Loid half smiled behind the paper. Bond had gone back to dreaming about pigeons in the yard. The room still looked ordinary. It just wasn't.

---

The phone rang at half past eight. Not the kitchen landline. The other one, in the drawer by the hallway bookshelf, with the ringtone her family politely treated as normal.

"Excuse me," Anya said, and stepped into the hall. Through the doorway she could still see the kitchen: Yor washing dishes, humming something off-key, the back of her head moving a little with the tune. Loid was drying a pot. He did not look up as she left, but his thought reached her anyway. *That phone again. She gets calls on it more than she used to. More since her sector changed.*

The line connected and Sylvia's voice was there, flat and stripped to function.

"This is a routine follow-up on your assessment of the Ostanian operative in the diplomatic corps sector."

"Sure." Anya leaned against the hallway wall. Water ran in the kitchen. Yor said something about the dish rack being full again.

"Another agency, I will not specify which, has flagged activity in the same sector you assessed in your initial report. They are identifying communication and movement patterns consistent with a coordinated operation. Their analysis does not match your conclusions."

The stew, which had already been sitting badly, felt like it was trying to leave her body by force.

"What kind of patterns?"

"Movement intercepts, financial flows, communication timing. Their analysts are reading the sector as high-activity and potentially high-threat. Your assessment characterized it as mid-level factional maneuvering with limited strategic significance."

"That was my read," Anya said. Through the doorway she watched her mother hold a plate to the light, checking for spots, turning it carefully. The thoroughness Yor gave to a white dinner plate.

"I am not questioning your read." The absence of accusation in Sylvia's voice had its own pressure. "I am informing you that the picture is moving in a direction your assessment did not anticipate. If your evaluation has changed since filing, now is the appropriate time to update it."

"It hasn't changed. The sector is factional noise. Internal Ostanian positioning. It looks coordinated because factions coordinate. That's what they do."

Silence. Anya could picture Sylvia at her desk: reading glasses on the chain, coffee long cold, fluorescent light flattening everything in sight. She couldn't read thoughts through a phone line. Without that access, Sylvia's pauses felt enormous.

"Noted." Precise, neutral, and therefore worse. "I will include your reaffirmation in the file. If your assessment changes, contact me directly. Not through the regular reporting chain."

"Understood."

"How is your family?"

The question arrived with no transition, the way Sylvia asked personal questions, as if they belonged on the same checklist as movement intercepts.

"They're good. My mom made stew."

"Edible?"

"Interesting."

Something that might have been amusement, or might have been Sylvia breathing out. "Good night, Anya."

The line went dead.

Another agency had found the seam she'd left in the file. The lie she'd pushed into WISE was coming back with teeth. Sylvia's voice had stayed flat, but the instruction to call direct sat hot in her palm long after the receiver went quiet.

Anya put the phone back in the drawer and returned to the kitchen. Yor had finished the dishes and was wiping down the counter. Loid was reading again, or acting like it.

"Everything okay?" he asked without looking up.

"Fine. Work stuff."

*Work stuff. On that particular phone. At eight-thirty on a family visit. She's carrying something. Multiple things. I can see it in the way she's standing in the doorway instead of sitting down. I did the same thing at her age. I was older before it started eating me.*

"I'm going to lie down for a bit," Anya said. "Long week."

"Of course." Yor appeared with a cup of tea, steaming, already placed in Anya's hands before the offer had really been made. "Chamomile. For sleeping. If you want it. You don't have to drink it. I just thought it might help."

"Thanks, Mama."

Tea in hand, Anya went down the hall to her old bedroom.

The room was smaller than she remembered, which meant she was bigger, which meant time had done that mean trick it always did in old rooms: left everything where it was and made her too big for it. The bed was the same narrow frame from when she was six. Her desk still stood by the window. The drawers held pencil stubs, a protractor, a notebook from her last year at Eden Academy with handwriting that looked like a stranger's because it was a child's. Two glow-in-the-dark stars still clung to the headboard. In the dark they kept their faint dirty green.

Her old headband sat on the shelf. Black, horned, a little ridiculous, retired years ago for simpler clips except on nights she was back here and reached for it without thinking. The plastic was scuffed and slightly bent from daily use. Anya put it on and sat on the bed with the tea cooling on the desk.

Through the wall, her parents' voices moved low and warm, a conversation between people who had been together long enough to imply half of what they meant. Anya did not reach for the words. Anya had taken enough from this apartment tonight.

Still, the night would not leave her alone. Sylvia's measured follow-up. Loid's quiet *Revisit.* Yor's flash of empty hands and the wrong room. Bond's terrace on fire. All of it wrapped around the same lie she had told for Damian and kept telling.

Anya set the tea down, pulled the old school notebook from the drawer, and flipped to a blank page in the back. Before Bond's vision could rot any further, she drew what she had: columns, steps, terrace, iron railing, fire breaking stone from below. The lines came out rough and mean. By the time she finished, the edges were already starting to blur again.

She folded the page once and shoved it under her pillow. The crease made a hard line beneath the pillowcase.

Anya lay down with the folded page under her cheek and watched the two stars over the headboard glow their dirty green. When sleep finally came, morning was already waiting.
