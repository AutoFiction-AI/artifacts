# Chapter 17

By the third week of March, Berlint had started thinking about other things. Anya noticed it on the tram to WISE. The air still wanted a coat and gloves. The thoughts didn't. All winter the city had been thinking about arrests, investigations, borders, who would take the fall and who would get paid to bury the embarrassing parts. Now the noise had thinned into ordinary stuff. Paint samples. Grocery lists. A man worrying about his mother's hip surgery. A teenager wondering if his jacket made him look stupid. The Renewal Front had gone where scandals went after the shouting. Courtrooms. Committees. Procedural language. Both governments were doing accountability in public and burial work in private. Damian still showed up in the papers, but in the tired middle now. Consultations. Threats. Reform panels. He was not a hero and not a disgrace. Just inconveniently real.

Inside WISE, the restructuring showed. Falk's desk in the analyst pool had been cleared within days of his arrest, but the gap still held its shape. New faces. New security checks. Sylvia's review board had gone through the place hard enough that even the coffee tasted more cautious.

Sylvia had a file open on her desk when Anya came in. She closed it before Anya could read the label. The thought on the surface was quick and sour: *record. gaps. should've seen it sooner.*

"Sit down."

Anya sat.

"The review board has completed its assessment of your record. Your field status is reinstated, effective immediately. The cover identity remains intact. The summit exposure stayed in political channels, not intelligence ones."

The words were right. The tone under them wasn't the old one. Sylvia used to sound like this with steel under the words. Anya could improvise and come back alive and still be trusted in the ugly middle. Now there was a seam in it. Their talk two weeks ago sat in the room with them even if neither of them named it. The false report. Falk. Crane. Sylvia had listened, kept her, and not given back the part that broke. Anya could still hear the trimmed-down surface of it: *good agent. bad choice. don't make me do this twice.*

"Thank you," Anya said.

"Do not thank me. Do the work."

By the time she left the building, she had just enough time to get home before Damian came to dinner. Yor had sent: *I'm trying a new recipe! Don't eat before you come home!* Loid had sent, twelve minutes later: *I've prepared side dishes. The rice turned out well.*

She called Damian on the walk.

"Six o'clock," she said.

"Six. I'll be there."

"Don't bring wine. Papa will judge the label."

A pause. "I wasn't going to bring wine."

"You were absolutely going to bring wine."

Another pause. "What kind does he drink?"

Anya laughed. It came easy now, easier than it used to. Damian knew about the telepathy. He knew and had stayed, and it meant she did not have to spend every good feeling asking where it came from.

"Red," she said. "Nothing with a year on it he might actually recognize."

She hung up and walked the rest of the way home through streets full of people thinking about dogs and trams and paint and dinner, and for once none of it felt like weight she had to carry.

---

The Forger apartment smelled like burning onions and something sharp and warm that had probably come from Yor reaching for the spice jar with confidence instead of measurement.

"Mama made curry," Anya said as she hung up her coat.

She could tell from the smell. She could also tell from Loid's thought: *The potatoes are raw in the middle. I checked one when she wasn't looking. The rice is ready.*

"Your mother worked very hard on it," Loid said from the kitchen.

He was setting out bowls, the ceramic set they used when somebody was coming over. Five bowls. Five place settings. The extra chair made the table look both crowded and right.

He glanced up at her. Just once. *How did Sylvia put it. Don't ask too fast.*

"How'd it go?"

"I'm back in."

A small pause. "Good."

He set the last bowl down. "We still need to talk."

"I know."

"Not before six."

Since the summit they'd been living inside that kind of sentence. No shouting. No fake normal. Just the thing sitting there between them, named enough that neither of them could pretend it was gone.

It wasn't a fix. It was just clear. Lately clear felt rare enough to count.

Yor came out of the kitchen with her sleeves shoved up past her elbows and a streak of curry on her cheek. "Anya! You're early. Oh, that's good. Is six too early? I told Damian six. Should I have said six-thirty? Your father said six was fine but I keep thinking..."

"Six is fine, Mama."

"Okay. Okay, good. The curry is almost ready. I'm not sure if the—" She looked toward the stove and visibly changed course. "It's going to be really good."

On the surface: *Please don't let it be too spicy. I couldn't feel my tongue for a minute. Thank god Loid made rice.*

Anya kissed the cheek without curry on it and went to find Bond. He was on the rug by the heater, huge and white and old enough now that getting up took planning. He lifted his head when she knelt beside him. His tail gave two careful thumps.

She scratched behind his ears. Bond's thoughts stayed what they always were, warm images more than words. Rug. Food. Heat. Home. Then a brighter flicker slid through, a quick gold shape like sunlight on a floor she didn't know. Tomorrow. Next week. Maybe nothing that clean. It felt kind.

"Good boy," she said.

The buzzer went at six-oh-three. Yor's thoughts came in a rush: *He's here. Is there curry on my face. Why am I nervous. This is dinner. This is normal dinner.*

Loid opened the door.

Damian stood in the hall in a dark coat with his hair combed and a bottle of wine held in both hands like evidence. His thought hit Anya before his voice did: *Don't think about the wine. You brought the wine. She told you not to bring wine. Don't think about it.*

"Damian," Loid said, holding out his hand.

The handshake was exact. Loid's were always exact. His thought ran under it: *Firm grip. Nervous. New coat. He dressed for this.*

"Thank you for having me, sir. Loid. Mr. Forger." Damian's face tried to recover and didn't quite get there. *Pick one. You practiced this. You practiced this on the walk over.*

"Come in," Loid said.

Yor appeared from the kitchen. "Damian! You came. Oh, you brought wine. That's so thoughtful."

Loid took the bottle and looked at the label.

Damian's mind went white-hot with panic. *He's reading it. She told me he'd read it. Think about something else. The couch. Dog hair. Normal family. Very normal family dog hair.*

"It's a nice bottle," Loid said.

Whether that was true or diplomatic was impossible to tell.

They sat. Yor served the curry with the hopeful pride of somebody who had spent all afternoon on a thing and needed it to count. The sauce was thick and dark. The potatoes were hard in the center. The spice hit fast enough that Anya's eyes watered on the first bite. She reached for the rice. Loid had made it perfectly, of course.

Damian took one bite and his thoughts turned into a full-body emergency. *Oh. Oh god. My mouth. Okay. Smile. This is Anya's mother's cooking. You are going to eat this and you are going to survive it.* He reached for his water. Then panic doubled back on itself. *Don't reach too fast. People drink water. Normal dinner water. Why am I thinking about how to drink water. She can hear me thinking about water.*

"This is really good, Mrs. Forger," he said.

His voice held steady. His eyes did not.

"Oh! Really? You don't think it's too... Loid thought maybe the chili was a bit much..."

"It's got character," Loid said.

He kept eating with the durable calm of a man who had been married to Yor for fourteen years and understood that this was one of the forms love took in his house. *Better than last month. Potatoes are a problem. Sauce is better.*

"I used less chili this time," Yor said brightly.

Damian took another bite with the grim focus of a diplomat entering hostile territory. *I am finishing this plate. I am probably having seconds. My tongue is on fire. This is fine.*

"You're too thin," Yor told him.

"I..."

"She says that to everyone," Anya said.

"I don't say it to everyone. I said it to Becky once and she was very pleased."

"That's different. Becky hears it as a compliment."

"Well, with Damian I mean it nutritionally." Yor pushed the curry closer to him. "Have more."

Damian had more.

The conversation settled into small things. Yor asked about work. Damian gave the polished version, careful and qualified. Loid asked about the new oversight committee in the same tone he might have used to ask someone to pass the salt. Damian answered. Loid lined up another question in his head. *Who actually chairs the subcommittee. See which name he gives.* At the exact same time Damian thought, *If he asks for committee names I'm dead,* and Yor thought, *Nobody is eating enough rice.*

Anya said, "Nobody is talking about committee chairs at dinner."

Three faces turned toward her.

Loid set down his fork. "I hadn't asked about committee chairs."

Heat climbed her neck. Too fast. "You were about to. You get that face."

"What face?" Yor asked.

"The interview face," Anya said. "The one where he acts normal and then suddenly you're explaining procurement law for half an hour."

Damian made a noise into his water glass that might have been relief trying not to become laughter.

Loid looked at her for a beat, then back at Damian. His surface thought flickered, quick and unreadable after the first word: *Protective.* Aloud he said, "I'm interested in your friend's work."

"He's not my friend."

"Your colleague."

"He's not my colleague either."

Loid took a sip of Damian's wine. "What term would you prefer?"

Anya opened her mouth and immediately regretted it. Damian's thoughts were no help at all. *We do not have a term. We read each other's minds and kissed in a safe house and I have no term for that. There is probably not a term in any language.*

"Damian's fine," she said. "Just Damian."

Loid inclined his head. He did not ask about committee chairs.

Bond wandered in halfway through the meal with the slow certainty of an old dog who knew where the warmth was. He folded himself onto Damian's feet under the table, all his weight on Damian's good shoes.

Damian looked down. His thought went soft at once. *The dog is on my feet. The spy family's precognitive dog is on my feet. This feels important.*

He did not move.

"He likes you," Yor said.

"Bond doesn't settle on just anyone," Loid added.

Damian's ears went pink again.

Yor cleared the curry and brought out fruit and tea. The talk turned lighter. Becky's spring fundraiser. The fact that she had apparently called Damian herself and informed him that his tie from the summit aftermath had been, in her words, a sartorial war crime.

"She said that?" Yor asked, delighted.

"She said it twice. Then she told me what color to wear."

"Becky's very good at organizing," Yor said. "She planned Anya's twentieth birthday party. The one with the... what was it, the ice sculpture?"

"It was a swan," Anya said. "It melted on the buffet table."

"It was beautiful before it melted," Yor said, loyal to the end.

Damian's thoughts snagged on the word *birthday.* *Three months ago. I didn't know her then. Or I did and didn't know it.* He looked across the table at her and tried, badly, to think about anything else. *Tea. Think about tea. Tea is hot. Tea is a normal thought.*

Then his gaze caught on her hair in the kitchen light and the thought dissolved completely. *Oh, come on.*

Anya looked down at her cup because her hand had gone unsteady again.

They were talking about windows and neighbors and whether the tram schedule was actually changing when Loid's next question started building. *When does Sylvia put her back in the field. How much did they make her say.* Anya cut across it before it reached his mouth.

"Mama, weren't you saying you wanted to see that movie with the train crash?"

Yor brightened at once and the conversation turned.

Nobody seemed to notice except Loid.

His thought came through clean and quiet.

*She always knew exactly the right moment to change the subject. Even when she was small.*

Anya went still with the teacup in her hand.

He did not think the rest out where she could hear it. He just looked at her with that focused face he got when pieces almost fit and he had not decided yet whether he wanted them to. The next thought never came. In the silence after it, Anya's own memories started kicking up like startled birds. Birthday presents in Yor's closet. A school meeting she had steered sideways before anybody said too much. A WISE training exercise where she'd answered before the briefing was done. Maybe those were his moments. Maybe they were only hers. She couldn't tell. If he had a real conclusion, it stayed down where her telepathy couldn't reach.

"Anya?" Yor asked. "Do you want more tea?"

"I'm good."

The tea had gone cold. She drank it anyway.

Loid reached to top off Yor's cup. His thoughts had already moved somewhere gentler. *Five at the table. Different. I don't mind it.*

Under that, under all the dinner noise, Yor kept catching on the same buried thing. Not full sentences. Just scraps rising and sinking with the steam from her cup. *Yuri.* *No knock at the door.* *He stopped it.* *Not tonight.*

Anya did not get the whole shape from Yor's head. She got the rest from the silence that had stretched for weeks and from the fact that nobody from the SSS had come. If the summit camera review had gone up the chain clean, it would have been over by now. Yuri had buried something or stalled it or locked it in a drawer only he could open. But the footage and Yuri were not the same problem. A camera could show a woman moving too fast. Yuri could say who she was. Yuri could say what he had seen with his own eyes. That was the part still sitting at the table with them, even in his absence.

Anya looked at her mother. Curry on her wrist. Sleeves shoved up. Old pale scars on her hands. A woman who could kill three men in under a minute and had spent the afternoon making dinner because she wanted tonight to be nice.

She could tell them.

The thought hit hard and plain. She could just say it. *I hear thoughts. I hear yours. I've always heard them.*

Yor would freeze with the cup in both hands. Loid would go very still. Then the questions would start. Then all the old moments would come back wrong. Every time she'd answered too fast. Every time she'd known the thing nobody had said yet. The good memories too. Birthday surprises. Bad days. Quiet panic they had thought belonged only to them.

That was the part that turned her stomach cold. Not that they'd stop loving her. That was not it. It was making them look back at twenty years and see how often she'd been standing inside the private part of their heads without asking.

Not tonight.

Not with Bond asleep on Damian's shoes. Not with Loid drying plates before there were plates to dry, already preparing to help Yor clean up. Not with the apartment warm and fogged from food and five people breathing in it.

Damian was watching her. For once his thoughts were not trying to hide from her. *Something heavy. Can't fix it. Can stay.*

She looked at him. He held the look and let her hear the rest. *I'm here. Your dad's a spy, your mom nearly killed me with curry, your dog has my feet, and I'm still here.*

Something in her chest squeezed hard and warm.

The evening wound down. Coats found. Shoes rescued from under Bond. Yor packing leftover curry into a container Damian failed to refuse. Loid shaking Damian's hand at the door with one quick surface thought that slipped past before it vanished: *He'll do.*

Damian left. Yor went to bed. Loid stayed in the kitchen long enough to finish the last of the dishes Yor had missed.

Anya sat on the couch. Bond had taken back the rug and pressed his side against her bare feet. The apartment was quiet in the good way. Pipes in the wall. A cupboard door closing in the kitchen. One floorboard in the hall giving the usual soft complaint.

Someday she would tell them. Not because Loid might already be circling it. Not because Yuri's silence sat over Yor like bad weather. Just because they were her parents and the truth was still there, waiting.

But someday was not tonight.

Tonight there was no tram full of strangers in range. No crowd noise. No borrowed panic. Just the faint smell of burned curry, Bond's slow breathing, and her own mind finally loud enough to hear.

Anya Forger. Twenty years old. Telepath. Daughter. Operative.

Her thoughts were small and ordinary and warm.

They were hers.

They were enough.
