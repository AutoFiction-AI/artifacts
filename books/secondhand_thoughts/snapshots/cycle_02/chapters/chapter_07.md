# Chapter 7

The conference room on the third floor of the National Unity Party headquarters smelled like old carpet and fresh coffee, and Damian Desmond was lying.

Not badly. He'd been lying in rooms like this since he was fifteen, since his father first brought him to a donors' reception and said, "Smile at the man in the grey suit. He funds our eastern district campaigns." Donovan Desmond hadn't needed to explain what kind of smile. Damian had understood already. You learned to lie early in this family, the way other children learned to ride bicycles.

"The Westalian delegation's posture at the trade summit was instructive," Damian said, standing at the front of a table of eight party loyalists. He kept his hands at his sides. "Their willingness to revisit border tariffs suggests they're testing our resolve. If we concede on agricultural inspection protocols, we signal flexibility. If we're perceived as flexible, we're perceived as weak."

Nods around the table. Deputy Chairman Holtz, who had known Donovan for thirty years and still considered himself a kingmaker, was writing something in a leather notebook. Beside him, Petra Lang from the defense policy committee watched Damian with the alert stillness of someone waiting to see whether he would be useful or disappointing.

"I'd recommend we draft a counter-position paper before the next session," Damian continued. "Something that reframes the tariff question as a security concern rather than an economic one. The language matters."

"Your father would have just told them no," Holtz said, not looking up.

"My father had leverage I don't have yet," Damian said. He kept his voice warm, a little rueful. "But the principle is the same. You don't negotiate from a posture of accommodation."

Holtz looked up. "You sound like him."

Damian accepted the compliment the way it was intended, as a leash lightly fitted. "I had a good teacher."

None of it was what he believed. The tariff question was routine economic maneuvering, and framing it as a security concern was the sort of escalatory language the Renewal Front fed on. Every time a trade dispute became a sovereignty dispute, the people who wanted war got to say the people who wanted peace were surrendering something. Damian had drafted the counter-position paper already, in his head. It disgusted him. He would write it this afternoon and it would be good, precise, persuasive, exactly the kind of thing a hawkish young Desmond would produce. Every sentence would be a small betrayal of the thing he was actually trying to do.

The meeting went another forty minutes. Budget allocations for the eastern district campaigns. A proposed media response to the Westalian cultural exchange program, which Lang wanted to frame as soft-power infiltration. Damian contributed where expected, deflected where he could, and watched the room the way his father had taught him: not for what people said but for who looked at whom when someone else was talking. Holtz deferred to Lang on media strategy but resented her for it. Lang wanted a committee chairmanship and was building alliances Holtz hadn't noticed yet. A younger member named Fitz, who Damian had met twice, kept checking his watch, bored or anxious, hard to tell.

None of them were Renewal Front, as far as Damian could determine. They were something more ordinary and harder to uproot: people who had organized their whole professional lives around Ostanian strength and Westalian threat, and who would lose status, funding, and purpose if those assumptions were dismantled. The Front didn't need to recruit people like Holtz and Lang. It only needed the weather they kept warm.

Damian smiled at Fitz on the way out. "Good meeting," he said. Fitz looked relieved that someone had spoken to him.

---

Demetrius had chosen the restaurant, which meant the Kaiserhof, the window table overlooking the ministry gardens, and a lunch that was not lunch so much as demonstration. Demetrius did not do casual. He did legible. He did useful.

He was already seated when Damian arrived, ten minutes early because Demetrius was always fifteen minutes early and would note the difference. His older brother wore a dark suit that fit like it had been measured under military supervision. His hair was combed in the same severe style their father had worn in ministry photographs. At twenty-four, Demetrius looked older than that. He had for years. The resemblance to Donovan was the kind of thing people mentioned in front of him as if it were praise. Demetrius treated it as praise because, for him, it was.

"You're early," Demetrius said.

"You're earlier."

"A call ran short. Kessler at Vanguard Defense. Procurement's being rearranged." He set down his water glass. "Sit."

The waiter brought menus. The Kaiserhof served the kind of Ostanian food that existed to prove Ostanian food could be expensive: thick soups refined into consommés, sausages turned into terrines, bread that arrived with explanation. Damian ordered the schnitzel because it was the plainest thing on the menu, which was probably a statement and annoyed him for being one.

"Holtz says the meeting went well," Demetrius said.

"Holtz says that whenever I manage not to set the room on fire."

"He said you sounded like Father."

"He says that too."

"Because he wants it to stay true." Demetrius glanced down at the menu once more, though he was going to order the duck. He always ordered the duck. "The party's satisfied. For now. You show up, you turn in clean work, you carry the name. Fine. But Holtz also says you've been selective."

"Selective."

"Don't do that." Demetrius looked up. "Lang's working group. The security liaison committee. The rooms that matter once the speeches end. You keep finding reasons not to be in them."

"I've been focused on trade."

"Trade is a waiting room and you know it."

The waiter returned. Demetrius ordered the duck. Damian's schnitzel came with a scatter of microgreens that looked embarrassed to be there.

"I'll go to Lang's meeting on Thursday," Damian said.

"Go on Thursday. Go after Thursday. Stop making people ask if you're solid."

Damian set down his fork. "Kessler asked that?"

"Kessler asked whether your position on national defense was real. I said yes."

"So that's it. I sit in Lang's little war room and smile while Kessler counts his"

"Don't." Demetrius's knife stopped halfway through the duck. "If you want to moralize, do it somewhere without waiters."

Damian looked past him, out at the gardens. "I wasn't moralizing."

"Then what were you doing?"

"Thinking out loud."

"Try not to." Demetrius cut a piece of duck and chewed. "The defense portfolio is where this family still has weight. That means contractors, committee chairs, people in uniform, people adjacent to people in uniform. You don't have to like that. You do have to understand it."

"I understand it."

"No. You understand the theory." He set down the fork. "I'm talking about rooms. Favors. Which deputy minister takes whose call. Which briefing paper gets read because it came from the right nephew. That's the work. Father knew it. I know it. You keep acting like if you stay one step away from it, your hands stay clean."

"Maybe I'm being careful."

"Be careful in private. In public, be clear." Demetrius took a sip of water. "People don't need your soul, Damian. They need to know where you'll be standing when the door closes."

"And if I haven't decided?"

Demetrius gave him a flat look. "Then decide before they notice."

The duck was glossy with some reduction Damian couldn't identify. His schnitzel was fine. He hated that the lunch was giving him acid.

"You sound like Father," he said.

"For God's sake." Demetrius exhaled through his nose. "Not everything is Father. Though yes, he'd agree with me." He leaned back a fraction. "He asked about you."

"What did you tell him?"

"That you're doing well. That the party's pleased enough. That the hesitation is temporary."

Damian's jaw tightened. "You told him I was hesitating."

"I told him you were thinking too hard. He considers that a form of hesitation." Demetrius picked up his fork again. "I'm trying to keep people from deciding you're unreliable. There is a difference."

"You always did like speaking for me."

"Someone had to."

The line landed between them and stayed there. Demetrius did not look away. Neither did Damian.

"I'm not going to embarrass the family," Damian said.

"No," Demetrius said after a beat. "I don't think you will."

They spoke about smaller things after that. A cousin's wedding. The ministry gardens, on which Demetrius had opinions. Their father's view that modern Ostanian literature had mistaken confusion for depth. Damian listened to his brother's clipped certainty and tried, not for the first time, to figure out when the work itself had become enough for him: the calls returned, the names remembered, the right man seated at the right table. He couldn't find the seam.

They split the bill because Demetrius insisted on splitting bills, a gesture toward equality that convinced no one. Outside the Kaiserhof, with the autumn air sharp on his face, Demetrius shook his hand as if they had just concluded business.

"Thursday," Demetrius said. "Lang's working group. I'll tell her you're coming."

"Fine."

"And call Father. He listens to your voice longer than mine."

That was as close to vulnerability as Demetrius got: the sideways admission that Donovan preferred the son who questioned to the son who complied. Damian had never known whether Demetrius resented that or tucked it into the same mental drawer as vote counts, donor names, and every other fact that might matter later.

"I'll call him tonight," Damian said.

Demetrius nodded and walked toward the car already waiting for him, its engine running, its driver opening the door before he reached it. He moved through the world like the world had been briefed on his schedule. Damian stood on the pavement in his cheaper suit and watched him go, thinking of Kessler, Thursday, and how every attempt at independence seemed to end in another waiting room where somebody already knew whose son he was.

Grosz lived in a third-floor apartment in the Schiller district, east of the government quarter but far enough out to be affordable, the kind of building where retired civil servants went to get smaller and quieter. The stairwell smelled like boiled vegetables and floor wax. A cat watched Damian from the second-floor landing with the calm contempt of an animal that had seen everything worth seeing.

He knocked twice, then once, then twice again, the old pattern from his father's network, unchanged in thirty years because the people who used it no longer changed easily. The door opened on a chain. One eye, watery and sharp, examined him through the gap.

"Herr Grosz. It's Damian."

"I can see that." The chain rattled. "You look like your mother. Come in."

The apartment was small and meticulously kept. A sitting room with a settee and two armchairs around a coffee table that held a folded newspaper, a magnifying glass, and a plate with toast crumbs. Bookshelves lined one wall: Ostanian political history, military memoirs, a surprising number of novels. On the mantel stood three framed photographs. Grosz in a ministerial corridor, younger and straighter. Grosz shaking hands with a man Damian did not recognize. Grosz behind Donovan Desmond at what looked like a state function, both of them wearing the expression of men who believed they were building something permanent.

Grosz himself was seventy-three, thin in the way of men who had once been vigorous and were now just wiry, with liver-spotted hands and a collar buttoned to the throat despite the warmth of the room. He moved carefully but not slowly. A cane leaned beside the armchair, though he did not touch it while Damian was looking.

"Sit. I'll make coffee. Or I have schnapps, if you want to disappoint yourself."

"Coffee."

"Your father would have taken the schnapps."

"My father was better at pretending not to be drunk."

That got the corner of Grosz's mouth to move. "Better at pretending, yes."

He disappeared into the kitchen. Water ran. A kettle clicked. Damian sat in the armchair that did not have the cane beside it and waited. The apartment had the stillness of a room used to only one voice. Traffic filtered up from the street, a bus, a horn, somebody dragging something metal over concrete. Inside, the quiet held.

Grosz returned with two cups on saucers, the porcelain heavy and old-fashioned, blue flowers worn thin at the rim. He lowered himself into the other armchair.

"You said you had questions about the current situation." He set his cup down. "I assumed you didn't mean tariffs."

"Not tariffs. The Renewal Front. And Vanguard Defense."

Grosz went still. Not theatrically. The stillness was small and immediate, the body deciding before the face had time.

"Who said Vanguard?"

"Demetrius. At lunch. Kessler's name came with it."

Grosz looked toward the hall, then toward the door, listening with his whole head. "Keep your voice down."

"You know them?"

"I knew paperwork that led in their direction. That's not the same thing." He picked up his cup, put it back down untouched. "What else do you have?"

Damian held his gaze. "Holtz wants me drafting trade language as a security issue. Lang wants me in the defense working group on Thursday. Kessler is leaning on Demetrius about my reliability. Somewhere in that I started hearing the words Renewal Front from people who used to know better than to say things in front of me." He paused. "That's what I have."

Grosz studied him for a long moment. "All right," he said. "That's enough to talk."

He stood with a suppressed wince, crossed to a narrow sideboard, and opened the bottom drawer. From the back he took a thin file jacket that looked older than Damian. Inside was a single onion-skin sheet, yellowed, the carbon copy faint but legible.

"Don't touch the middle. It smears."

Damian leaned forward. Across the top of the page, in faded typed capitals, was MAINTENANCE CONTINUITY ANNEX. Below that: a ministry seal, a contract number, and the name VANGUARD DEFENSE in pale, stubborn ink.

"Twelve years old," Grosz said. "Maybe older than that. The skim was never in the base contract. It lived in addenda like this. Continuity, emergency retention, systems preservation. New names every year, same idea. You pad the maintenance estimate, route the excess through an intermediary, and suddenly meetings, travel, extra communications, all the things nobody wants in a party ledger, pay for themselves."

"The Renewal Front."

"Partly." Grosz sat again. "The Front isn't a party. It isn't even cleanly one organization. It's officers whose budgets shrank when the border quieted, old-guard nationalists who never accepted the quiet in the first place, procurement people, hangers-on, men who have been calling their appetites patriotism for so long they don't hear the difference anymore."

"How high does it go?"

"High enough." Grosz rubbed a thumb along the saucer's rim. "Maybe higher than I knew when I was active. Maybe lower than people imagine when they want a tidy villain. I can't give you a chart with names and arrows. If that's what you came for, you came to the wrong apartment."

"I'm not asking for tidy."

"Good, because nothing about this is tidy." He pointed at the page. "If Lang's working group circulates annex numbers or maintenance supplements, write them down. Don't steal the papers. Don't photograph anything. Just the numbers. Then compare them to the ministry budget supplements. If the same contract swells twice, once in the open ledger and once in a continuity annex, you've found where they're hiding the money."

Damian looked up from the paper. "You want me to audit procurement?"

"I want you to stop asking me what the Front is as if it were a debating society. Follow the money first. Labels can wait."

"And the leadership?"

Grosz's mouth tightened. "There is a man whose name keeps turning up near the old guard, near the defense ministry, near every conversation where somebody starts saying peace has made the country soft. Werner Voss."

Damian said nothing.

"I'm not telling you Voss is the center," Grosz said. "I'm telling you what his name does to a room. Men lower their voices. Somebody looks at the door. Big man, military carriage, old grief from the border years. The phrase that keeps following him is peace is theft. Maybe he says it himself. Maybe his admirers say it for him. I never heard that part cleanly."

"You think he's building toward something?"

"I think men like that do not fund themselves just to enjoy committee work."

The radiator clicked. Someone moved in the stairwell outside, a slow tread, then a pause. Grosz went silent. He waited until the steps kept going upward.

"There's more," he said, quieter now. "And this is where your patriotism stops being useful. I saw one routing sheet before I retired. One. Ostanian threat assessments moving through a broker to a Westalian-owned procurement firm. Not theft. Purchase."

Damian stared at him. "Westalian firms buying Ostanian military intelligence."

"At least one firm did. At least once. Enough to tell me those threat assessments were leaving the country for cash." Grosz folded his hands together, then unfolded them. "Do I know how current that channel is? No. Do I know whether Vanguard was part of that side too? No. Do I know whether the men cashing those payments cared which flag stood over the bank? I very much doubt it."

Damian looked back at the onion-skin page. A maintenance annex. One faded contract number. One name from lunch that now refused to stay ordinary.

"Why tell me this now?"

"Because you brought me something specific, which means you're finally old enough to ask the right question." Grosz's voice roughened. "And because if Kessler's name is in your brother's mouth and in mine on the same day, then I have already said more than is good for me."

"You think this conversation puts you at risk?"

"I think I live alone." Grosz gave him a dry look. "You understand the difference."

Damian did.

"You do not come back here next week to discuss what you find," Grosz said. "You do not come back here at all unless the building is on fire and you are carrying proof worth dying over. Thursday you go to Lang's meeting. You listen. You write down the annex numbers. You watch who talks to Voss when procurement comes up. Then you leave like a good Desmond son and let everyone think you were there to become your father."

"And if they notice what I'm doing?"

"Then they'll notice too late if you're good, and too early if you're not." Grosz's eyes lifted to the photograph of Donovan on the mantel. "Your father never walked into a room like this alone. He had men in hallways, cars downstairs, secretaries who knew which paper to lose. Remember that when you decide how brave you feel."

Damian let the paper sit where it was. "You were loyal to him."

"I was. I am, in the way old men stay loyal to ministries that stamped their papers and used up their years because otherwise the years were just used up." Grosz leaned back carefully. "That is not the same thing as wanting them to use you up too."

The steps in the stairwell were gone. The apartment had gone quiet again. Damian stood.

"I'll take the contract number," he said.

"You'll memorize it." Grosz slid the page back into the file jacket. "Nothing leaves this apartment."

Damian read the number twice, then a third time.

"Good," Grosz said. "Now go before I decide I've lost my mind."

---

The walk home took twelve minutes, east along Friedrichstrasse and then north toward his building. The streetlights were coming on. Autumn was doing its patient grey work on Berlint: leaves in the gutters, darkness arriving earlier every week, cold settling into the cloth of his coat and staying there.

He kept hearing four things. Kessler. Voss. Westalian money. Maintenance continuity annex.

He would have to write the tariff paper before evening and then attend Lang's working group on Thursday pretending Demetrius had won. He would have to sit through defense men talking budget and posture and readiness while trying to catch contract numbers without looking like he was catching contract numbers. If he did it badly, the suspicion would not stop with him. It would travel backward to an old man in a third-floor apartment with a cane by the chair and no one else in the room.

Damian's apartment was on the fourth floor of a building that had once been elegant and was now merely respectable, which suited the story he told about himself. Modest enough to signal independence. Well-located enough to signal the independence was elective.

He let himself in, hung up his coat, and turned on the kitchen light. He should have called his father. He should have drafted the counter-position paper. He should have written down the contract number while it was still clean in his head, then burned the page. Instead he went to the hall closet and pulled down the cardboard box from the top shelf.

It was the only box he had brought from the family house. Everything else in the apartment had been bought new, a deliberate break from the curated weight of the Desmond household. This box he had never unpacked and never thrown away.

Inside were school things. An Eden Academy report card with marks that were good but not exceptional, which had been a particular kind of failure in his father's house. A certificate for the inter-class spelling competition, second place. A ribbon from athletics day. He had run the four-hundred-meter badly and finished fourth, and the ribbon said PARTICIPANT in silver letters because institutions liked euphemism when children were involved. A folded program from the school festival, his name listed as part of the gymnasium cleanup crew. A pen that had run dry years ago. At the bottom, under tissue paper that his mother had probably put there before she got sick, a photograph.

Class photograph. Eden Academy, third year. Four rows of students on the gymnasium steps, faces arranged into the tense stillness children gave a camera when an adult had just told them, for the last time, not to move. Frau Henderson stood at the edge with the expression of a woman who liked children and expected trouble from all of them.

Damian found himself in the third row, second from the right. He looked furious. At ten, his face had specialized in indignation and thwarted pride.

His gaze moved to the second row.

A girl with bright pink hair stood between Becky Blackbell and a boy he couldn't place. Her tie was crooked. She wasn't looking straight at the camera. She seemed to be grinning at something just outside the frame, as if the order to behave had reached everyone but her.

He knew he should remember her immediately. Becky anchored the row. The school festival program in the box anchored the year. The girl's face still snagged in him somewhere familiar, irritatingly familiar, and the name would not come.

He thought, for a second and without meaning to, of the cultural attaché from the state dinner. Not her whole face. Something lighter and harder to pin down than that. The angle of attention. The sense that the rules in any room were only provisional. The impression thinned before he could catch it.

He sat with the photograph longer than he meant to. The apartment had gone dark around the edges. Streetlight reached through the window and laid a dim amber wash across the hallway floor. The girl in the photograph was grinning at something that had been funny ten years ago, and Damian sat there with the box open beside him, a contract number in his head, Thursday waiting for him, and a childhood name still just out of reach.

At last he put the photograph back in the box, though he left the box on the floor. Then he went to the kitchen, pulled a clean page toward him, and wrote the annex number in the top margin before he began the counter-position paper about tariffs he did not believe in, language that would be in other people's mouths by the next reception.

Outside, Berlint settled into its autumn dark. By the time he reached the paragraph about resolve, the side of his hand was smudged blue.
