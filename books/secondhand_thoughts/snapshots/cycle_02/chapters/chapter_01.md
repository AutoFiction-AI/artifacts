# Chapter 1

The man at the turnip stall was going to leave his wife.

Not today. Probably not this year. But Anya Forger could hear him building up to it while she stood three stalls away pretending to examine pears, and the level of rehearsal was impressive in a miserable kind of way. He'd run through the speech four times since she'd come within range. Each version was worse. The current draft included the phrase "I deserve to feel alive," which, no. Nobody delivered that line out loud and left the conversation with any dignity. She almost wanted to help him edit.

*Listen, Gerda, we've had a good run, but I think we both know things have changed.*

She didn't help. She was working.

Berlint Central Market spread through three city blocks between the diplomatic quarter and the government district, a maze of canvas canopies and wooden stalls that thinned in autumn but never fully closed. The afternoon crowd was thick enough to be useful: tourists consulting pocket maps, pensioners comparing vegetable prices with the exhaustive seriousness of arms negotiators, mothers with children who ran at shin height and thought about nothing except wanting things. Anya held her position by the pear display and waited for her contact.

Turnip Man's rehearsal continued in the background, layered under a woman in a brown coat who was composing an indignant letter to her landlord about the mold in the bathroom. *It's been four months, Herr Dietrich. I don't think it's unreasonable to ask.* Two stalls over, a teenager was replaying an argument with his mother and losing, even inside his own head. Behind Anya, a man was thinking about bratwurst with a focus that bordered on worship.

This was the noise. It was always the noise. Sixty, maybe seventy minds within range, roughly twenty meters in every direction, give or take, and every one of them talking at once, none of them aware anyone could hear. Anya couldn't turn it off. Hadn't been able to since she was four, when the first thought she'd ever registered was a nursery teacher's exhausted prayer for nap time. Whatever anyone within range was actively thinking arrived in her head, in their voice, at their volume, whether she wanted it or not.

She mostly didn't want it.

The contact was late. His codename was Kinglet, a mid-level courier, nothing remarkable, just a man who picked things up and left things behind and worried about it the entire time. Anya had been watching the dead-drop bench near the fountain for seventeen minutes, and in that time she'd learned that the pretzel vendor across the way had a sciatica problem he was managing with anger and a philosophy about the proper ratio of coarse salt to fine salt that she would never fully forget.

Somewhere in the crowd, faint and easily missed, a woman was thinking about her son. *If his unit gets redeployed to the eastern sector again, I'll drag him home myself.* Anya let it pass. Background static. Everyone was worrying about something.

Then Kinglet's thoughts entered range from the south. *Should've worn the grey jacket. The grey doesn't wrinkle. Why do I never think about that before I leave? Okay. Focus. The newspaper is on the bench. Walk naturally. Am I walking naturally? This doesn't feel natural.*

Standard courier anxiety. He was a nervous man doing his nervous job. Anya had worked with worse.

She scanned outward. The crowd noise was a wall of competing voices, but she'd been doing this long enough to know what she was listening for. Trained minds sounded different from the usual churn. They were organized, repetitive, and they stayed on target.

There. East side of the market, near the cheese stall.

*Subject approaching the bench. Hold position. Wait for visual confirmation of the pickup.*

The thought was crisp and male and utterly wrong for the setting. Nobody in a market thought in tactical present tense unless they were working.

She found the second one nine seconds later, threading through the crowd from the south. *East approach set. I'll take the exit route once he commits west. Awaiting signal.*

Two tails. Professional. They were positioned around the dead-drop bench, waiting for Kinglet to pick up the newspaper with WISE microfilm and then follow him back to wherever he was going, which was the diplomatic quarter, which was WISE, which was everything.

The pears blurred in front of her. Okay. Okay, think.

Kinglet was ten meters from the bench. He was still thinking about the grey jacket. He had absolutely no idea he was walking into a trap. He was going to sit down, pick up a newspaper full of intelligence, and lead two hostile surveillance professionals straight back to the heart of Westalian intelligence operations in Berlint.

Anya put the pear down. She'd been holding it for seventeen minutes and hadn't paid for it, but she also hadn't bitten it. So that was probably fine. It didn't matter. She started walking.

The first problem was communication. She had no earpiece to Kinglet. This was a passive surveillance assignment. She was there to observe the dead-drop and confirm the exchange, not to manage the handoff. She didn't have a signal protocol with him because she wasn't supposed to need one. The second problem was that she couldn't explain to anyone how she knew the contact was tailed, because the answer was *I read their minds* and that was a sentence she had never said out loud to anyone in her life.

So she'd improvise. She was bad at plans. She was genuinely good at the part where the plan was already ruined.

She cut between stalls, moving fast, angling toward the fountain. Behind her, Turnip Man's resolve was collapsing. *Maybe I won't mention the car. The car is a whole thing. If I bring up the car she'll throw something.*

*Subject reaching for the newspaper.* The first tail, repositioning.

Anya moved faster.

She reached Kinglet just as he was bending toward the bench. She caught his elbow, laughed too loudly, and said, "Oh my God, I'm so sorry. I thought you were someone else. You look exactly like my friend's cousin. Wow, that's embarrassing." She gripped his arm hard enough to make the point.

His thoughts were a siren of confusion. *What? Who is she? Her hand is on my arm. This is not protocol. There is no contact protocol for this.*

"Don't sit down," she said through her smile. "Walk with me right now. You've got two on you."

He went white. Not a little pale. Actually white, the blood dropping out of his face like water through a drain. He was a courier. He filed paperwork and left newspapers on benches and occasionally met someone in a parking structure. This was not his job. His thoughts collapsed into a high, thin whine of panic that Anya had to push through like wading in mud.

She kept the smile, kept the grip, and steered him north into the densest part of the market, where the stalls pressed close and the crowd moved slow.

*She's intercepting the subject. Is she an asset? Move to secondary approach. North side.* The first tail, sharp with alarm.

*I've lost visual. The crowd is useless. Damn it.* The second tail, already behind.

Anya read them both and steered. The first was going to loop around the spice stalls to cut them off from the north. She turned left, pulling Kinglet into a narrow gap between a bakery stand and a man selling secondhand books who was thinking, with great bitterness, about a woman named Elke.

"Keep up," she murmured.

"What is happening?" His voice shook. He was twenty years older than her and his hands were trembling and hers were steady and she didn't have time to feel anything about that asymmetry right now.

*There. North side, between the canopies. I've got a partial. Pursue through the stalls? Too narrow. Circle to the west exit and intercept.* The first tail had caught a glimpse.

West exit. Anya went east.

"Why are we going this way?"

"Because they're expecting west."

"How do you know that?"

"I'm good at reading situations."

She pulled him through a row of vegetable crates. Behind them, the first tail was committing to the west exit, already calculating arrival time, line of sight, and where to plant himself for interception. He was good. He was also going to be standing at the wrong exit in about forty seconds.

The second tail was trying to close from the south, but the crowd had thickened. A knot of tourists had stopped to photograph a flower stall, blocking the aisle like a clot in an artery. Anya used the gap. She steered Kinglet past a display of jarred honey, through a service lane between stalls that smelled like overripe apples and cardboard, and out through a slit in the canvas onto the market's east-side service street.

Kinglet stumbled after her. "You said west."

"I said they expected west."

The service street ran behind the outer line of stalls, narrow and half blocked by crates and delivery carts. Anya turned north first, keeping the bulk of the market between them and the main street. At the next cross-lane she cut west behind a row of produce sheds, the tarps and stacked boxes hiding them from anyone waiting at the obvious exit.

A fruit cart sat crooked in the cut-through, wooden and heavy, loaded with late-season plums. A boy of about sixteen was leaning against it, thinking about a girl named Sofie with a level of romantic anguish that was almost operationally distracting.

Anya bumped the cart with her hip as she passed. Not hard. Just enough to shift it, send a cascade of plums tumbling across the cobblestones behind them, and turn the whole lane into a mess that anyone following would have to fight through.

The boy's thought exploded. *The plums. Oh shit, oh shit, the plums, my uncle is going to kill me.*

The second tail hit the spill and lost them for good. *Obstruction. Completely lost. They could be anywhere back here.*

By the time the first tail reached the west exit, he was already too late. His thought caught her just as she and Kinglet came around the market's north-west corner and cut toward the street that fed into the diplomatic quarter. *She went east first. She's looping. How did she read that?*

Once they crossed into the Westalian mission's orbit, pursuit became a political problem no professional tail wanted to create. The men peeled off. They always did at that line.

Anya let go of Kinglet's arm.

Her head was throbbing. She'd been reading forty-plus minds at full intensity for almost twenty minutes, and the noise had been stacking up behind her eyes like pressure from a fever. Fatigue loaded her arms and legs with that wrong, boneless drag she only got from too much listening. She'd need to stop soon. Sit somewhere quiet. Let the noise thin.

But not yet.

Kinglet was leaning against a lamppost, breathing through his mouth. His jacket was wrinkled, the wrong jacket, and his forehead was slick. His thoughts were a wreck of relief and leftover terror. Under both sat one clean fact: he was never doing this again.

"You're fine," Anya told him. She pushed her hair back from her face, fingers brushing the familiar spot near her ears, and said it again. "You're fine. We're clear."

The microfilm was still on the bench. The newspaper was unrecovered. That was a loss, but a recoverable one. The dead-drop site was burned now, but the intelligence could be rerouted through another channel.

Kinglet looked at her with an expression she'd seen before on people she'd pulled out of bad situations: gratitude layered over something less comfortable, the awareness that a woman twenty years his junior had just handled a crisis he'd frozen inside.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't use that dead-drop site again."

"I won't."

"And next time, wear the grey jacket. It doesn't wrinkle."

His thought flashed once, startled and small. *How does she know that?*

But she was already walking away, and he was already out of range, and she was very, very tired.

---

Forty minutes later, in a room that smelled like institutional carpet and coffee that had outlived any reasonable definition of freshness, Anya sat in the third row of a WISE briefing and tried not to read anyone's mind for five consecutive minutes.

She failed before she finished the thought.

*If this runs past six I'll miss the opening number. She's been practicing the Chopin for three weeks. She's got the dynamics down now. If I'm not there in the front row she'll look for me first.* That was Reinhardt, two seats to her left. Analyst, mid-forties, reliable, entirely consumed with the fact that his seven-year-old daughter Marta had a piano recital tonight and he had promised to be there in the front row, which at seven was law.

*Eggs. Bread. Something for dinner. Did she say laundry soap? Actually, do we have soap?* Analyst Brewer, assembling a grocery list with the uncertainty of someone who lived alone and had not opened her own cupboards with conviction in some time.

*The line of her jaw when she's making a point. She doesn't know she does that thing with her glasses, does she?* Berg, sitting directly behind Anya and, for the sixth consecutive month, failing to conceal a crush on their handler that radiated from his mind like a space heater. Anya had endured this since spring. She knew more about what Berg found attractive about Sylvia Sherwood than any person alive, including Berg, and she would have paid real money to stop knowing.

The briefing room was on the third floor of a building in the Westalian diplomatic quarter that, from the outside, looked like a modest commercial property. Inside, it was fluorescent lights and ventilation hum and the particular carpet that government offices shared across every nation and era, beige, resilient, hostile to personality. The coffee on the credenza had been brewed that morning by Sylvia, who did not replace a pot before 1600 hours because wasting coffee offended a principle she had never articulated and that no one in the department had ever been brave enough to question.

Sylvia stood at the front of the room in a grey blazer and a collared shirt that together said *I am here to work and you should be too*. Reading glasses on the chain around her neck. Short grey-blonde hair that she cut, Anya suspected, at the same place on the same schedule every six weeks, because Sylvia did not adjust things that were functioning. Her face showed thirty years of difficult decisions made in rooms like this one. Whatever habit of apology she'd once had was long gone.

"The pattern is consistent with covert operational activity, not standard political maneuvering," Sylvia said. Her diction was precise. She did not use contractions when she briefed, and her sentences arrived pre-structured, situation, assessment, directive, as if she had formatted them before opening her mouth. "We are looking at a structured campaign inside Ostania's security apparatus. The scope suggests an organized effort with specific objectives and an active timeline."

She touched the map pinned to the briefing wall. Red markers clustered near the Ostanian government district, east of center.

"The operative or operatives are unknown. The purpose is unknown. What we do know is this: the activity pattern is consistent with pre-conflict positioning. Weapons procurement channels that had been dormant for several years have been reactivated. Military intelligence sharing between Ostanian departments has been restricted in a manner that suggests compartmentalization for a specific project." She paused, letting it breathe for exactly long enough to register as serious without tipping into theater. "We do not yet have a clear picture of the objective. But the indicators are serious enough to warrant dedicated investigation."

*I wonder if there'll be parking near the recital hall.* Reinhardt, already drifting.

*And the way she holds a pen. Just like that.* Berg. Hopeless.

From the back row, quieter, Gerald Falk was thinking.

*Threat assessments calibrated to budget cycles. Same as last quarter, same as the one before. Inflate the numbers to justify the operational allocation, deflate them when the appropriations review calls for discipline. Sherwood presents it as intelligence, and it is intelligence, but the framing keeps shifting to whatever posture keeps the lights on. Everyone in this room knows that. Nobody says it.*

Falk sat near the wall in a cardigan that could have been brown or grey depending on the light. He was a senior analyst, easy to miss in a room like this if you weren't listening. Anya had heard variations of that complaint from older staff before. She let it pass and turned back to Sylvia.

"Forger." Sylvia's gaze found her with the directness of someone who did not scan a room but targeted a specific person. "You will take point on identification and assessment. Your diplomatic cover provides access to Ostanian state functions. Use it. I want a preliminary assessment within ten days."

*She always picks Forger for the exposed ones.* That was Berg again, equal parts admiration and resentment and entirely not Anya's concern.

"Understood," she said.

*Should I bring flowers to the recital? Is that too much for a seven-year-old? No. She'd love flowers. Marta would love flowers.* Reinhardt, fully inhabiting the evening ahead of him.

Anya bit the inside of her cheek and did not smile.

The briefing ended with Sylvia collecting her notes in the precise order she always collected them, map last, folded into thirds. People stood, gathered their files, began the small post-meeting exchanges that were half professional and half procrastination. Sylvia left the room without lingering, because Sylvia did not linger.

Anya had caught Sylvia's thoughts about her during the briefing, as she always did: *Solid under pressure. Her field judgment has been reliable for two years. She reminds me of Loid at his sharpest, the instinct for the right read in the wrong moment.* The assessment was warm in a contained, institutional way that was as close to affection as Sylvia's professional register permitted.

Anya stood and felt the headache from the market settle more firmly behind her right eye. The fluorescent lights were needles. She wanted, suddenly and badly, to be in a room with no one else in it. But her father was waiting for her at Cafe Linden, and there was nowhere within twenty meters of anywhere in Berlint that didn't have someone thinking in it.

---

Cafe Linden was a narrow room with dark wood tables and a window that hadn't been cleaned since the previous season. It occupied a side street between the diplomatic quarter and the residential neighborhoods south of center, close enough to WISE to be convenient, far enough to feel like somewhere else. Anya and Loid had been meeting here for two years. The waiter knew their order and had long ago stopped pretending he hadn't noticed they were always slightly too alert for a father and daughter having coffee.

Loid was already seated when she arrived. Coffee untouched, a cherry strudel on the plate across from him, her favorite since she was eight. He never ordered one for himself. She'd asked about this once and he'd said he wasn't hungry, which had been a lie, because his thought at the time was *She runs too hard and she doesn't eat enough*, and the lie was so small and so fatherly that she'd let it sit.

He looked up when she came in. Forty-four. Blond hair with grey at the temples that had accelerated over the past two years in a way neither of them mentioned. Reading glasses folded beside his coffee cup. He needed them for menus now, and for small print, and the first time she'd seen him put them on something had shifted her understanding of time in a way she hadn't been ready for. His face was kind in a way that was both genuine and the residue of a lifetime of cover identities, though by now the distinction between the real warmth and the performed warmth had dissolved so thoroughly that even Anya, reading him, couldn't always find the seam.

"How was the market?" he asked.

"Eventful." She sat down and pulled the strudel toward her. "Dead-drop was burned. Two tails on Kinglet, coordinated, somebody professional. I took him out through the east service lane and looped him around to the diplomatic zone."

His face didn't change. His thoughts did.

*Two tails. She was alone. She handled two professional tails in a crowd-density environment without backup.* The analytical thread caught on something beneath it, and the assessment wavered. *She was alone. Twenty meters of range and forty civilians between her and hostile surveillance.* He caught himself. Redirected. *East service lane was the right play. Use the market bulk as cover, reappear at the mission boundary, let the politics do the rest.* The spy's evaluation, clean and precise, laid over the father's alarm like fresh paint.

"Smart route," he said. "Once you hit the diplomatic line, they were done."

"That was the idea." She took a bite of strudel. It was good. It was always good here.

"We'll reroute the recovery through an alternate channel. The microfilm isn't time-sensitive." He took a sip of coffee with the unhurried precision of a man who measured his gestures the way other people measured their words. "What about the new assignment?"

"Sylvia wants me in Ostanian rooms for the next ten days," Anya said. "Enough of them that if Yuri works security on the bigger ones, he's going to start seeing the same face too often."

He set his coffee down. The gesture was careful, which meant he was interested.

*Repeated exposure. SSS guest logs. Yuri Briar notices patterns for a living.* His thought went fast, mapping event security, ministry access, which rooms drew which personnel, how often a cultural attaché could plausibly show up before that plausibility turned brittle. And under all of it: *She's twenty. She's going to be in rooms where one bad glance from the wrong officer could close the door on her for good.*

"You'll need sustained access to Ostanian government functions," he said. "Your attaché cover is solid, but repeated presence at high-level events draws pattern recognition. Vary the attendance. Some cultural, some diplomatic, some social. Give them a profile that looks enthusiastic rather than strategic."

"Boring but visible."

"Exactly." A small smile, and then the thought behind it: *She's sharp. She's sharp and she's young and she's going into places I can't follow.* He cut himself off there, hard enough that the silence of it landed in her.

She looked down at the plate and busied herself with her fork. This was the arrangement, though only one of them knew it existed. He worried about her in the privacy of his own mind, and she let him believe it was private, because telling him otherwise would mean explaining everything, and explaining everything was the one thing she had never been able to do.

"I'll be careful," she said. She was answering a thought he hadn't spoken, and she'd gotten very good at making that look like normal conversation.

They talked for another twenty minutes. He suggested specific diplomatic events that would give her access to the Ostanian apparatus without overexposing her. He mentioned security protocols at state functions. He said Yor was trying a new stew recipe and that she should come home for dinner soon, and the sentence was simultaneously a father's invitation and an operative's wellness check and she couldn't untangle the two, and she didn't want to, because the tangled version was the true one.

They sat for a while after that. The operational conversation wound down and something quieter replaced it, the version of them that existed in the gaps between work, where he was just her father and she was just his daughter and neither of them was particularly good at knowing what to do with that.

"How's the apartment?" he asked.

"Fine. The radiator makes a noise."

"What kind of noise?"

"A sort of... ticking? When it comes on."

"That's the pipe expanding. It's normal." He said this with the absolute certainty of a man who had once maintained six separate cover identities across two countries and who now, in semi-retirement, channeled the same operational precision into home repair. "If it starts banging, that's a different issue. Call the building manager."

"Okay."

"Or call me. I can look at it."

"You'd drive forty minutes to look at my radiator."

"I'd drive forty minutes to make sure your radiator isn't going to rupture a pipe in the middle of the night, yes." His face was straight. His thought was not. *She's living alone. She insists on living alone. This is the correct developmental step for a twenty-year-old. I know this.* The thought blurred after that into something less structured, less analytical, just a father's awareness that his daughter was out in the world and he couldn't follow her there.

"I'll call if it bangs," she said.

"Good." He picked up his coffee cup, found it empty, and set it down again. "Your mother's making stew tonight. New recipe. You should come by sometime this week."

The way he said *new recipe* carried a warning she didn't need telepathy to decode. Yor's culinary experiments were acts of love that frequently crossed the line into acts of violence against ingredients. But the stew was real and the invitation was real and the kitchen would smell like burning and hope, and she missed it.

"Maybe Thursday," she said.

She paid for the coffee because he always forgot to bring cash. He didn't forget. It was part of the routine, one of a hundred small rituals that had accumulated over fourteen years of a cover family that stopped being cover somewhere along the way.

Outside, Berlint was going amber. Early autumn evening, the sun low enough to turn the west-facing windows into sheets of dull gold. The air had a crisp edge, summer's warmth gone, winter still distant, the city held in the kind of cool that made you walk faster without deciding to. Leaves on the cobblestones. A tram rattling down Gartenstraße, its bell sounding twice as it rounded the corner.

Anya walked south toward home.

The thoughts came without effort. She was tired, and tired meant the filter she'd spent twenty years building was thinner. The range didn't shrink, but the noise got louder, less organized, a layered hum of voices she hadn't asked to hear.

A woman closing her shop, pulling down the metal shutters with practiced rhythm. Her thoughts matched the repetition: *Elsa hasn't called. She always calls on Tuesdays and it's Wednesday now and she hasn't called and the news said there were delays at the eastern checkpoints and if something happened they'd tell me, wouldn't they? Someone would call?* The sister lived near the border. Anya walked past and the thought faded behind her.

A student coming the other direction, headphones in but the music wasn't working. His thoughts were louder than whatever he was listening to. *If tensions get worse and they reinstate conscription, do university exemptions still hold? They used to. Lehmann said they did but Lehmann failed civics.* He was nineteen, maybe twenty. He was thinking about a draft that didn't exist yet as if the notice had already come in the mail. He'd grown up hearing about the last one.

A shopkeeper locking up early, his thoughts running numbers that wouldn't add up. *If the eastern trade routes stay restricted another month, the cost on imported stock alone. Frau Dittmar wants her order by Friday. I don't have enough. Maybe Korsch can source it from the south.* He was solving a logistics problem that was really a political problem that was really a fear he couldn't name.

An old man on a bench. His mind was moving slowly, settling, the way water calmed after a stone dropped through it. He was thinking about pigeons and the cold and a woman named Ilse who had been dead for a long time. The grief was so worn it was almost texture now, not sharp anymore, just there, like a knee that ached in weather. Anya didn't want to hear it. She heard it.

A couple walking ahead of her, close together. He was thinking about whether the restaurant on Müllerstraße would be crowded. She was thinking about her father's medication and the pharmacy closing early this week. Underneath both thoughts, unspoken between them but legible to Anya in tandem, the same pressure as everywhere else: the border, the news about the border, the delays, the rumors about troop movements that might be rumors or might not be.

Neither of them said any of it aloud. They talked about dinner and parking and whether Thursday worked for the dentist, and below the ordinary logistics of a shared life they were both quietly afraid in a way they hadn't been six months ago.

Anya read it all. She'd been reading it all day, since she'd woken up in her apartment and caught her neighbor's thoughts about a leaking pipe before she'd finished brushing her teeth. More people thinking about the border this month than last. More people with a cousin in the military, a sister near the checkpoints, a headline they couldn't stop carrying around. Berlint was getting nervous before it admitted it was nervous.

She turned onto her own street. Quieter here, a residential block with three or four minds within range. A neighbor watching television, his thoughts a muddy echo of whatever the announcer was saying. A child on an upper floor, drawing, thinking the colors: *Green. No, the darker green. The one that looks like trees.*

Somewhere nearby, a dog was dreaming in short sensory pulses. Warmth. A hand. Food.

None of them knew she'd already heard them.

Anya stood on the sidewalk with her key in her hand. From the next building came a tired thought, thin and private. *After tea. I take the pills after tea.* Upstairs, the child settled on the darker green. The key teeth bit into her palm. She crossed to her building, got the door open, and went inside before the next stranger could say anything else in her head.
