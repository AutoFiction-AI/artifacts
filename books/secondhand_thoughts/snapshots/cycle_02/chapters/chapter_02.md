# Chapter 2

The car left her two blocks from the Foreign Affairs Ministry. Standard practice. A private sedan vanished into the district's evening traffic. A diplomatic car with Westalian plates lingered in memory.

Anya paid the driver, stepped onto the sidewalk, and took the city in for one second before the gates.

Berlint at dusk looked almost forgiving. Streetlights came on under a flat grey sky. Leaves scraped along the pavement in thin circles. The air carried wet stone, car exhaust, and the sweeter diesel stink from the government lot behind the Ministry. Somewhere back the way she'd come, a woman argued with a child about ice cream in October. *He'll get sick. Fine. Maybe just one scoop. Damn it.* The thought hit first, then the voices.

Maybe later for normal people. Now for fake-diplomat people.

Her credentials sat in a slim wallet inside the clutch: Marta Kramer, junior cultural attache, Westalian mission. The cover was deep enough to have paperwork, a salary, an office, and a supervisor who thought Marta Kramer did decent work when she remembered deadlines. There was even a plant on the desk. Anya kept forgetting to water it and then feeling guilty at lunch.

Two SSS officers stood at the entrance. The taller one checked her papers against the guest list. The shorter one watched her face with professional boredom. The taller man's thoughts skimmed across her skin as lightly as the cold.

*Division with remainders again. Mina's going to cry and I won't get home before bedtime and Nora will be furious.*

"Attache Kramer." He handed the papers back. "Main gallery. Second doors on the left."

"Thank you."

Heat met her in the foyer. So did the noise.

Not the quartet. Not the talk. Minds. Too many, all at once, pressing at the inside of her head until the bones around her ears felt thin. Snatches. Lists. Petty grievances. Lust. Grocery worries. People rehearsing lines they had already said badly. A crowd always felt like thirty radios left on in a locked room. Tonight the volume was turned up.

Breathe. Don't grab every voice. Don't be stupid.

She smoothed the front of her dress, dark blue and respectable, the sort of dress a junior cultural attache wore when she wanted to look serious and forgettable, and walked into the ballroom.

The Ministry did state dinners the way it did everything else: with marble, chandeliers, and the steady suggestion that everyone present ought to feel honored. Warm gold light sat on the floor. White linens glowed under crystal. A quartet near the windows played a Baroque piece nobody cared about. Waiters moved through the room with champagne, wine, and a pale nonalcoholic drink nobody wanted. Ministry catering meant the food would look official and taste tired.

Warm rooms made loose thoughts. So did alcohol. So did people standing too close to one another and pretending not to notice. Pressure gathered behind her left eye almost at once.

Rule one: keep moving.
Rule two: let the useful thought trip over you by accident.
Rule three: do not puke on foreign policy.

A waiter passed. Anya took a glass of champagne and went hunting.

Deputy Minister Werfel saw her first and brightened in a way that had nothing to do with diplomacy. "Attache Kramer. Good to see you again."

"Deputy Minister Werfel. How was the recital?"

His face opened. *She remembered. Elena will be ridiculous about this.*

"Wonderful. One missed note in the Handel, but she recovered beautifully."

"I missed six notes the last time I touched a piano, so that sounds heroic."

Werfel laughed. Good. Easy. He kept talking about his daughter's bow hold and the National Gallery exhibit and whether the ministry had overfunded a restoration nobody would admit liking. Anya nodded in the right places and let the room keep muttering around him.

*Schiller can sign the budget himself if he wants it that badly.*

*That is the Trade Minister's wife's dress. No, worse, it is almost the same dress.*

*The pork is dry. How is pork dry in gravy?*

Then one line caught and held.

*If Defense doesn't push the review through this week, the southern checkpoint won't clear in time.*

Charcoal suit. Mid-fifties. Back to the windows. White wine in her left hand. Anya did not look at her yet. Looking was how you told on yourself.

Werfel was still talking. "I always think modernists improve when they're slightly underfunded. Desperation helps."

"That's comforting. I've been underfunded for years."

Another polite laugh. Another sip of wine. Another slice of thought from the woman near the window.

*Three weeks. Four at most. Then the whole initiative dies on the floor.*

Checkpoint. Timeline. Defense. Fine. That was a real piece.

Anya drifted on with apologies about finding her table card and let the crowd take Werfel away. The first clue sat in her head and refused to move.

Near the center of the room, General Brennan from border infrastructure command stood with three men who all looked as if they enjoyed using the word strategic. Numbers rolled through his mind in hard clean blocks.

*Four hundred thousand plus transport. Move it through the liaison allocation or Muller starts asking why concrete costs as much as rifles.*

There. Money. Wrong budget. Same edge of panic as the woman by the window.

Brennan's sciatica barged in right after it. *If this chair is as bad as the hotel chair, I'm burning the place down.* Apparently the state ran on bad backs and fake invoices.

Not the center, then. Just another man carrying one box in from the truck.

A tray came by. Bubbles slid dead and flat along the inside of her glass. Her own hand had tightened hard enough around the stem to make her knuckles ache. The headache sharpened. Behind Brennan, an elderly official worried about his grandson's conscription placement. Two steps later a younger diplomat was writing an explicit apology text in his head to a woman he had definitely lied to. Anya jerked away from that line of thought so fast she nearly hit a waiter.

God. No. Not that one.

The room would not stop being full of people just because she needed it to. That was the trick nobody ever put in training notes. Useful thought or useless thought, it all came in through the same door.

By the time the quartet shifted to a quicker piece, checkpoint and money were enough. She needed less noise, not more, and the quieter edge of the room sat beyond the French doors.

Terrace.

Anya turned toward the French doors at the far end. The crush eased with every step. Good. Better. A minister's aide near the wall was composing the resignation letter he had been writing in his head for three years and would never send. An SSS officer by the terrace entrance was on shift change. A woman in green had gotten sauce on her sleeve and wanted to die. Then, under all of it, a cleaner line began to pull.

Focused. Ordered. Not dinner chatter.

Names. Dates. A meeting. A second meeting in case the first one failed.

A tall man cut across her path before she could get closer. Grey hair. Military back even in retirement.

"You're with the Westalian mission, yes?"

"Cultural affairs." Her smile held. "Marta Kramer."

"Colonel Dreher. Retired." He gave her a practiced look that had probably unsettled younger women for decades. *Westalian. Young. Alone. Who sent you?* "Enjoying the evening?"

"Very much."

"Good."

His next thought turned toward seating patterns and ministerial rivalries. Boring again. Anya escaped the moment he decided she was not worth lingering over.

Cold hit the minute she stepped through the doors. Iron sconces threw weak light across the terrace stones. A couple argued in hushed vicious voices near the balustrade. An older man checked his watch with naked hatred. A woman smoked like she was stealing time from God.

And near the far railing, alone, stood the mind she'd been looking for.

Dark suit. Straight back. Hands resting on stone as if he had taught them calm by force. The signal sharpened so fast it made her teeth hurt.

*Griesler won't hold till the end of the month. If he goes to the Committee first, the checkpoint story turns into a deployment and then it's blood everywhere.*

Anya slowed without meaning to.

*Tuesday with Kleist. Wednesday if his office keeps stalling. Father's old files have to be good for one useful thing.*

Another fragment, hard and quick.

*The Front's meeting is on the fourteenth. Need the attendance list. Need the civilian names before they bury them behind procurement language again.*

Not a man planning the fire. A man trying to get in front of it.

Her breath snagged. No. Wait. Check it. Three scraps were not a whole case.

He shifted, reaching for the drink on the railing, and the light caught his face.

Damian Desmond.

Older, obviously. Sharper through the jaw, darker under the eyes, the soft roundness of Eden sanded out by adulthood and bad sleep. But Damian. Same age as her. Same face under the years. WISE kept a file on him because WISE kept a file on every Desmond worth breathing near. Junior diplomat. Moderate potential. Uncertain loyalties. That was what the paperwork said.

The thoughts on the terrace pointed the same way: trouble, and a man trying to stop it before anyone noticed which way he was moving.

*If the retired bastards still trust the family name, maybe one of them will talk. One name. One contract. That's enough to blow a hole in it.*

A pulse of fear came with that, sharp enough to taste.

*If they see me before I'm ready, everyone I leaned on burns.*

Not fear of exposure because the plan might fail. Fear of exposure because the people around the plan might die first.

Anya stayed where she was, arms crossed against the cold because it looked natural and because the cold had finally reached her skin. The sensible move was obvious: mark him, go back inside, grab one more confirming scrap from the room, leave. Actual answer: keep listening. Damian Desmond on a government terrace, thinking about border blood and old Desmond files, was not a clue you walked away from.

More pieces came, never neat long explanations, just the top layer of a mind running too hot.

*No more delays. No more waiting for them to hand me a clean version.*

*The southern route, the defense budget, the same names twice. Idiots. Greedy idiots.*

*If the list matches the forum guest log, I can prove it.*

Enough. More than enough. Ballroom whispers had pointed one direction. Damian's thoughts flipped the arrow around. He was tracing the same money and meetings the room kept leaking. It looked less and less like he was moving them. It looked like he was trying to tear the cover off them before the checkpoint became bodies.

Her stomach dropped anyway. Relief would've been the tidy response. Instead the whole night lurched sideways. Tomorrow she had to sit in front of Sylvia and make this sound like ordinary tradecraft: a face in the crowd, a few overheard bits, good instincts. It couldn't sound like what she'd actually done.

He hadn't chosen to tell her any of this. He'd stepped outside for air, and she'd reached straight through his skull for what she needed.

That thought landed hard enough that for one second she almost backed away. Almost. The mission still sat there. So did Damian.

Inside, the quartet changed pieces again. The sound slipped through the glass doors, thin and high. Damian's concentration wavered with it.

*Eden courtyard in spring. Bell late again.*

Her fingers dug into her own sleeves.

Pink hair in sun. A flash of motion. Pain and humiliation arriving together.

*You hit me right in the face. God.*

Not spoken, only remembered. He was thirteen again, nose bleeding on the front of his school shirt, furious because it hurt and because half the yard had seen it. Then the next piece followed.

A wet paper towel shoved at him.
A girl's face gone wide with horror.
That same girl's mouth twitching because the hit had been an accident and also, unfortunately, impressive.

Anya stopped breathing for one beat.

He kept turning the memory over in scraps.

Green eyes. Head tilted left. An apology that had not sounded sorry enough. Pink hair again, impossible to mistake.

And then the start of a name. Just the first sound. *An.*

The terrace door opened.

"Desmond," a man in a grey suit called from inside. "The deputy minister wants you."

Damian straightened at once. The schoolyard vanished. His mind snapped back into neat political lines so fast it felt violent.

"Of course."

He picked up his glass and turned toward the doors. As he crossed past her, six feet away at most, his thoughts were already on appropriations, border posture, who hated whom at the deputy minister's table. Public face back on. Diplomat again.

One brief glance brushed over her. Automatic, polite, forgettable.

*Westalian. Freezing. Why don't any of them ever bring coats?*

Then he was through the doors and gone into the big hot noise of the ballroom.

He hadn't recognized her fully. All he had was pink hair, a punch, the front edge of her name.

Anya stayed on the terrace until the cold bit through the dress and into muscle. Her fingers ached around the champagne glass. Tomorrow she'd hand Sylvia a report built out of scraps and timing and leave out the part where she'd gone rifling through Damian Desmond's head. The lie was already sitting on her tongue, flat as the champagne.
