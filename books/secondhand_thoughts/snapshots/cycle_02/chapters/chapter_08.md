# Chapter 8

The noise started in the elevator.

Not from the machinery. The Concordia's elevators glided without a sound, part of what people were paying for. The man beside Anya said nothing. Leather portfolio, neutral face, expensive tie. His thoughts, unfortunately, were having a full panic spiral.

*Left the gas on. No. Did I? I turned the knob.*
*I'm almost sure I turned the knob.*
*If the building burns they'll find the Ackermann notes on my counter and think I wrote a suicide note. Christ.*

Anya adjusted her earring and watched the floor numbers climb. She'd been hearing other people's thoughts since she was four. It never became normal. The startle had mostly burned off over the years. What stayed was the drag of it, the low constant pull, like carrying a radio under her ribs that nobody else could hear and nobody else could switch off.

Tonight was going to be loud.

The elevator opened onto the third-floor lobby. Beyond the double doors, the Westalian trade delegation's autumn reception was already underway. Quartet through the wood. Glassware. Shoes on polished floor. Under all that, the other layer.

*If he says grain tariff one more time I'm leaving.*
*Nice shoes. Who is she?*
*Where is the shrimp. They said seven.*
*Maria's going to assume the worst. Again.*

Two hundred minds, maybe more, all stacked on top of each other. The gas man was right behind her, still thinking about the stove. Her fingers found the spot below her right ear before she caught herself. Old habit. Bad habit. Anya dropped her hand and went in.

Warm amber light hit first. Then crystal. Then bodies. Westalians had claimed the bar. Ostanians had claimed the food. The quartet was playing hard for people who did not care. The ballroom had two exits and a fully stocked bar. Spies liked the exits. Everybody liked the bar.

A glass of white wine gave her hands a job. She started moving.

In the first twenty minutes she got this:

A Westalian trade minister near the terrace doors was building an alibi for his wife in careful little pieces. *Traffic. Then Kessler wanted the export figures. Receptions always run late. If I leave at ten I can say ten-thirty.* Too detailed. Too prepared. Definitely not about export figures.

At the buffet, an Ostanian general with enough medals to plate a second jacket was thinking about shrimp with the focus of a man inspecting artillery. *Tail came off clean. Good kitchen. Sauce is wrong. Too much lemon. Not enough bite.* He lined four shrimp on his plate like he expected them to hold formation.

By the window, a young diplomat kept rewriting the same breakup text in her head. *We should take a break. No. That's awful. I need space. No. Also awful. I just don't want to feel like this every Sunday anymore.* Her thumbnail had gone white against the stem of her champagne flute.

Near the service entrance, a Westalian aide in a bad jacket was thinking about his boss's speech, his mother's test results, and Ackermann's title in one miserable braid. *I wrote it phonetically. He still won't look at the card. Why do I make the cards. Why do I even own pens.*

An older Ostanian deputy minister, moving through the room with quiet gravitational pull, was thinking about natural gas imports and whether her daughter had remembered the cat's wet food. The cat was winning.

That was crowded-room telepathy most nights. Cat food. Affairs. Zippers. A policy fragment if she got lucky. The headache behind her right eye had already started its steady drilling, which meant the room was earning its keep.

WISE called this observation. Later she'd turn the stolen parts into clean report language. Assessment. Body language. General temperature. Nobody got to write, I was inside his head and here is the useful part. Two years in, she could do the translation fast. Two years in, it still felt like cheating.

Yuri Briar stood watch at the east side of the ballroom.

Dark suit. Earpiece almost invisible. Anybody who'd never worked security would have called his posture relaxed. Anybody who had would have seen the readiness in it. Weight balanced. Hands loose. Eyes making the same sweep every eight seconds. Anya counted once to be sure.

His thoughts cut through the room with familiar sharpness.

*West entrance clear. Kraus has kitchen corridor.*
*Three extra Westalian staff not on the advance sheet. Logged it. Don't like it.*
*Three is planning, not sloppiness.*

He'd always been good. Anya still remembered him in the apartment years ago, glaring at Loid's miso soup like it might leap out of the bowl and confess.

Then the work track slipped and Yor rushed in.

*Need to call Sis. Thursday? Friday?*
*If I missed it she'll have cooked anyway.*
*It'll be terrible. I'll tell her it's perfect.*

That hit with the usual stupid tenderness. Yuri loved his sister so hard it hurt to overhear. He wasn't built for half-measures in any direction.

Then his focus landed on her.

*There. Cultural attaché. Third government room this month.*
*That isn't culture. That's circuit work.*
*Sis would tell me to stop being suspicious. Sis is not on security detail.*

Cold moved through her stomach. His eyes were on her now, not warmly, not as family by marriage. As pattern recognition.

Anya slipped into the nearest conversation knot, all bilateral arts funding and grant language. Ceramics residencies. Exchange fellows. She kept her face interested and her shoulders loose. Nobody in that circle cared whether the Ostenheim program was oversubscribed, which helped.

Four minutes later she peeled off toward the bar and passed within range of Strauss.

Strauss worked economic analysis at WISE. Gin and tonic. Good suit. Tired face. He looked like the sort of man who alphabetized his worries for efficiency.

*Halverson cut the ugly columns again.*
*Not cut. Shaped. That's the polite word.*
*We kept the numbers that bought us money and buried the ones that cooled the room.*
*Everybody at the desk knows it. Nobody says it where minutes get kept.*

Cold bubbles bit the back of her teeth as she lifted a sparkling water from a tray. Great. So WISE did the same trick in better tailoring. Not a grand revelation. Just a man with a drink and a budget line helping a chart lie by standing beside it. She did her own version every time she filed *behavioral indicators* instead of *he thought it so loudly I nearly winced.* Same trick. Smaller font.

The room didn't change. She just felt stupider in it.

Water finished, she kept moving.

* * *

Damian was near the terrace doors, listening to a woman from the Foreign Affairs Ministry explain port logistics with real interest. That should not have been attractive in anybody. On him it was weirdly effective.

*If they fix the northern bottleneck the border looks calmer. If the border looks calmer the ministry loses its excuse for the defense allocation.*

Even his thoughts sounded like briefing notes that had gone to school.

Anya waited until the woman excused herself.

"The shrimp's apparently safe," she said.

"High praise."

"An Ostanian general has been auditing it for forty minutes."

That got a glance toward the buffet. Damian's mouth almost moved. Under the social layer, his attention was already on her read of the room.

"Anything useful from the Westalian side?"

"Budget shaping. I'll explain later."

"Right." Fingers went to his cuffs, not nervous, just controlled motion while his interior rearranged itself. *Twenty minutes in a room and she has usable material. Observation only gets you so far. She's doing more than that. Or she's simply better than everyone else here, which is not impossible and annoys me.* He cut the thought there and put his diplomatic smile back on. "I'll take the south end. The defense liaison is finally cornerable."

"Don't antagonize him."

"I don't antagonize people."

"You absolutely do."

"I create conditions for candor."

"That is the same thing."

A tiny crease appeared beside his mouth. Then he was gone into the crowd and she was back in it too, two colleagues who had just discussed seafood.

Quadrants helped. Upper left, upper right, lower left, lower right. A room this big could drown her if she let it turn into one blur. Read. Sort. Move. Keep moving. Cultural attachés were allowed to mingle. They were not allowed to haunt.

The next half hour paid out in fragments.

A military aide was consumed by the question of whether he'd parked in a tow zone.

One junior diplomat within range was thinking with total spiritual commitment about whether his fly was down.

It was.

There was no professional way to solve that. Anya left him to history and kept walking.

Useful threads surfaced now and then.

*Werner doesn't go quiet unless a move is queued.*
*Ackermann says the checkpoint schedule holds if the review doesn't interfere.*
*Do not discuss procurement numbers at functions, Dietrich, for God's sake.*

Those were worth keeping. The zipper was not. The room kept handing her both anyway.

Then Damian looked up from the far side of the ballroom.

Not the polite sweep he gave everybody. This hit different. Old memory, not present calculation.

*There. By the bar. Elbow tucked in. Glass almost against her ribs.*
*I've seen that before. Where.*
*Eden. Festival cleanup. Henderson's shoes full of soap. Pink hair everywhere and that laugh.*

The laugh. That was the problem. The thought snagged there and went hunting for the rest.

*No. Wait. She used to tilt her head before saying the worst possible thing.*
*This woman just did it.*
*How many people in Berlint have that exact shade. No. Stop.*

Her name hovered at the edge of his mind, not in the thought yet, close enough to burn.

A hand landed on his arm.

The defense liaison had stopped avoiding him. Damian's focus snapped away hard enough that Anya felt the memory sink under the professional layer in real time. Name. File. Border posture. Handshake. Tone. The childhood image dropped straight through the floorboards.

Glass down. Palm flat on polished wood. Cool surface. Good. Her pulse was hitting hard enough to make the stem rattle against her fingers.

Too close. Three more heartbeats and school-Anya and attaché-Anya would have clicked together in his head. After that came the questions. Why was an Eden girl wearing Westalian credentials. Why did she keep appearing anywhere his covert life got dangerous. Why did she keep landing on the right answer half a beat before the rest of the room even saw the question.

Hesse was talking educational exchange with an Ostanian academic near the west pillar. Anya slid into the gap and waited through a long, stupid twenty-count for an opening.

"The preparatory courses help with that," she said when the academic mentioned networking. "I did the embassy exchange three years ago and I still keep almost recognizing people from it. Whole week of name tags, forced smiles, and bad coffee. Faces blur after that."

Hesse agreed. The academic agreed. Nobody cared. Perfect.

A few minutes later, blessedly, a cultural program officer named Lentz started talking to her about wood-firing kilns and glaze chemistry. For three whole minutes Anya got to stand in front of a mind that matched its mouth. No second layer. No concealed knife. Just ceramics.

Then Damian appeared at her shoulder with a fresh drink and the bland smile he used when he was absolutely running a probe.

"You did the preparatory exchange?" he asked.

Anya looked at him. "Spring intake."

"At the embassy annex?"

There it was. Not casual. A probe.

Hesse was still nearby, thinking about the annex's leaky roof and the classroom coffee that could strip paint. Good. Enough to grab.

"The annex for the seminars, the old ministry rooms for language drills," she said. "Hesse kept pretending the coffee wasn't terrible."

Damian's face did not change. His thoughts did.

*Fine. The annex. The bad coffee. Then why did I go chasing the annex off one stupid school memory. Why Henderson. Why that laugh.*

"I was trying to place you," he said aloud.

"Everybody tries at these things," Anya said. "Do enough receptions and every face starts stealing pieces from the others."

"Fair."

Not convinced. Not yet. But he'd tested her. That was new, and it cost more than the planted anecdote had.

He moved on. Lentz resumed explaining kiln temperatures with patient enthusiasm. Anya nodded in the right places and heard none of it.

From the east side of the room, Yuri tracked her again.

*Bar. Hesse. Ceramics officer. South window cluster.*
*She's not mingling. She's mapping.*
*Sis would hate that I notice the difference. I still notice it.*

No good answer existed for that. Anya did the only thing she had. She crossed back to the bar and stayed put long enough to look boring.

* * *

By nine o'clock the ballroom had gone thick.

Three hundred people, maybe. Enough that the thought-noise stopped sounding like separate minds and turned into pressure. The ache behind her right eye had deepened. Jaw tight, shoulders tight, fingers flexing around the water glass without permission. A waiter went past thinking in furious detail about three days of unwashed dishes and a pan of burned rice his roommate claimed was "soaking." Keep moving. Keep sorting.

A woman near the ambassador was dying inside because she'd realized too late that her dress matched the ambassador's wife's.

Brandt was still stalling the November deployment signature.

A man by the far curtain was thinking about his sick kid's fever and pretending to follow an energy conversation.

Then the clean one hit.

*Asset inside WISE is still holding. Move the handoff before review closes the window.*

Anya stopped.

Not dramatically. Just enough that the server behind her had to swerve.

The voice came again, sharp as a knife on tile.

*No delay. Coverage shifts after the review. Pull it forward if you have to.*

Close. Very close. Somebody inside this cluster of Westalian staffers, Ostanian counterparts, servers, security, trailing spouses. Somebody within range. Somebody thinking about a mole in her organization like it was a timetable problem.

Anya turned her head once, slow. Twelve faces. Fourteen if the passing server counted. A trade official. A mission analyst. Green dress. Bald man by the window. Two men talking fishing rights and actually thinking about fishing rights. Nobody lighting up with WISE. Nobody thinking the words again.

She cut left through the knot and let a tray nearly clip her shoulder. Hotel booking. Knee pain. Affair. Nothing.

Back toward the window. Another pass. Still nothing.

One more. Faster this time.

*Handoff.*

Thin, already breaking apart.

Then gone.

Whoever had thought it had shut the door or moved or let some other trivial human concern flood the surface back over it. The room rushed in to fill the space. Parking meter. Divorce. Border review. Sore feet. The signal was buried again and the only solid thing Anya had was the phrase sitting under her ribs like swallowed glass.

Inside WISE.

Not rumor. Not Damian's guess. Not one of Grosz's poisoned stories. Inside WISE.

The glass in her hand started to bend her fingers around it. She set it on a passing tray before she snapped the stem.

A marble column near the east windows gave her cold stone to lean against. Music on one side. Voices on the other. Too much perfume in the air. Somebody laughing near the bar. Normal room. Normal party. Normal if you ignored the traitor somewhere inside it.

Sylvia would ask how. Sylvia had already swallowed one clean lie from her three weeks ago. Going back now with *There's a mole in WISE* and no source would survive the first question.

Damian would ask how too. Damian asked cleaner questions than Sylvia, but he kept them sharper.

The truthful answer was the one answer she'd never given anyone in her life.

Fine.

Then it was hers.

Start with the review cycle. Start with who had access, who touched the files, who suddenly needed odd hours, who went too quiet at the wrong moment. Quiet work. Ugly work. Work she could do because Loid had taught her how to build a pattern and because telepathy, filthy unfair telepathy, had dropped the first thread in her lap.

Across the room, Damian found her. Concern first. Then restraint.

*She's off. What happened. Later. Not in here.*

Anya gave him the smallest nod she could manage.

Then she scanned the ballroom again, and the room did not feel warm anymore. It felt searchable.

The general at the buffet was still thinking about shrimp. Tail, sauce, lemon, bite. Not him.

The young diplomat by the window had deleted the breakup text and was now drafting tomorrow's apology. Not her.

Yuri checked the cracked watch Yor had given him years ago and thought, with deep private irritation, that he still couldn't remember whether he'd promised Thursday or Friday dinner. Not him.

A server worried about the tram schedule. A trade official worried about gout. Lentz worried about whether hotel water would ruin a glaze test she'd left soaking at home.

Ordinary thoughts kept surfacing. Ordinary thoughts kept clearing people by accident. It should have made the room feel safer. It didn't. It only proved how many covers ordinary life could wear.

The quartet brightened. Glasses clinked. Somebody laughed too loudly near the terrace doors. Somewhere in the middle of all that, a traitor had gone silent and only Anya knew the hunt had started.

She drank the water down, set the empty glass aside, and went to find her coat with the phrase still lodged under her ribs.
