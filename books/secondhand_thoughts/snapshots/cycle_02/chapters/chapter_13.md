# Chapter 13

The phone rang at 6:14 in the morning.

Anya was already awake. Maybe she had slept an hour. Maybe less. Most of the night had gone to encrypted border traffic on her WISE terminal and the neighbor in 4B thinking, *please please please just sleep, I'm begging you, just one hour.* The baby on the other side of the wall kept crying. The mother's thoughts were louder.

She picked up on the second ring. Sylvia got straight to it.

"At approximately 0340, border control at Kreuzfeld was breached. Crane was moving on forged papers when a three-man team intercepted him. They had his route, his timing, and his cover. He died on site."

Anya sat on the edge of the bed with the phone hard against her ear. The baby cried again. The neighbor thought, *please, please, this one time,* and then nothing but blank tired static.

"His body has been recovered," Sylvia said. "The documents have not. I have informed his wife."

Crane. David Crane. Parking space, three months ago. "Chief," every single time. Forty-one. Wife, no kids. Six weeks running eastern routes Anya had built out of scraps she stole from other people's heads and then cut down before she handed them over. The fact of that hit behind her eyes, right where every other voice hit first.

"There is another development," Sylvia said. "At approximately 0300, an SSS tactical unit executed a search warrant on a residential address in eastern Berlint. The location matches an address from our analytical review last week. The warrant was specific. They knew what they were looking for."

Damian's cache. The apartment. The shelves arranged by region. The coffee stain near the kitchen that he had left in the carpet because replacing it meant letting the landlord inside. She had laughed when he said it. He had not.

"The material was seized intact," Sylvia said. "Analysis is pending. The eastern sector is compromised."

Compromised. Broken. Burned open. Gone.

"I have questions about your reporting timeline," Sylvia said. "We will address them at 0800. Attend in person."

"Yes."

Sylvia went quiet for half a second. Not uncertainty. Just the tiny stop between the work and the next piece of work.

"Crane was a good officer," she said. "We will debrief in the morning."

The line went dead.

Anya stayed where she was. The neighbor in 4B adjusted the baby and thought, *okay, okay, maybe like this,* and the baby screamed harder. The thought blew apart into exhausted misery.

She called Damian.

He picked up at once. "I'm aware," he said. His voice was controlled, too even, and she could hear how hard he was working at it. "My contact in the SSS reached me twenty minutes ago. They took everything."

"Crane is dead."

A pause. "Who?"

"WISE field officer. He was running border routes I was coordinating."

"I'm sorry."

He meant it. That was the problem. He did not know what he was apologizing for. He did not know those routes were built from intelligence she had sanded down before it ever reached Sylvia. He did not know her lie had opened a pocket of bad information wide enough for Falk, or whoever it was, to work inside. She could not prove a neat line from her false report to Crane bleeding out at a checkpoint at 3:40 in the morning. She could still see the space. She had made it. People were dying inside it.

"There's something else," Damian said. "Reinhardt called an hour ago."

The name hit and brought the rest with it. A ministry official. Careful. Nervous. Two daughters. Saturday football. Photocopies in a gym bag, left in the back seat of his car in the ministry garage because that was the blind spot nobody bothered to search.

"He's out," Damian said. "Told me not to call again. He thinks the SSS got the address through his department's records. He thinks they will come for him next."

"Will they?"

"I don't know. Maybe. If the files were taken intact, they will find his name soon enough. I tried to tell him I could arrange something. He hung up before I got there."

Anya went to the kitchen window without knowing why. November had turned the glass the color of dishwater. She thought of Crane's parking space. Reinhardt in a ministry garage with a gym bag. The report she had put in Sylvia's hands. She had drawn a line around Damian and called it strategy. Everything outside that line had paid for it.

*Please just sleep,* the neighbor thought. *Please.*

"We need to meet," Anya said. "Not the usual place."

"I know somewhere."

The basement was underneath a printing shop on a quiet block in western Berlint, reached through a service door beside a dumpster that smelled like wet ink and cardboard. Damian had a key. He did not explain how. The stairs were steep concrete. By the halfway mark, the railing had leached enough heat from her palm that she shoved both hands into her coat pockets.

The room below was maybe fifteen feet by twenty, all concrete except for a steel folding table, four mismatched chairs, and one fluorescent tube on the ceiling. Damp had browned the far wall in vertical streaks. Damian was already there with his elbows on the table and his hands folded loose, coat still on, as if he had been holding the same position for an hour. The burn mark near his left cuff stood out darker than the rest of the wool.

She pulled out the chair and sat. Neither of them spoke.

Above them, someone in the shop dragged something heavy across the floor and let it drop. The thud came down through the ceiling, dull and blunt. Damian kept looking at the table.

*Too static. Stupid. Should've moved it. Reinhardt's name is in there. God.*

There was a pale coffee ring on the table, half rubbed away. Anya put two fingers beside it and kept them there.

"They knew exactly where to go," Damian said at last. "The warrant was not broad. Someone gave them the address."

"The mole."

"Your mole."

He did not put heat on the word. He did not need to.

"I know."

His thoughts kept tripping over the same things. *Three months. Contact maps. Shell companies. Reinhardt. Eight and ten. Football on Saturdays.* His eyes dropped back to the coffee ring.

"Reinhardt's going to be okay," Anya said.

He looked up.

She heard how bad it sounded too late. "No. I don't know that. I just... I know he mattered to you."

A drip hit the floor somewhere behind her. Slow. Then another.

"He has two daughters," Damian said. "That's the part I can't stop thinking about."

Anya nodded because anything else would have been a lie.

"Tell me about Crane."

The question landed hard enough that she almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because a man was dead and Damian still wanted him to be a person, not a line item. That almost broke something.

"He called everybody chief," she said. "Even Sylvia, once. He used too much powdered creamer in bad coffee and then pretended he liked it. He could spot a bad stamp on forged papers faster than anyone I know. He was forty-one." She swallowed. "He was helping me on the eastern routes."

Damian said nothing. The room held it.

She had been reading the wrong minds. That was the ugly part. Hundreds of scraps, thousands maybe, and not the one that mattered. Not the person passing a route. Not the person clearing the ambush. Not the person deciding Crane should die at a checkpoint before dawn.

"The thing I keep thinking," she said, and stopped.

If she finished it, she would have to tell him why the thought belonged to her.

"What?"

She rubbed at the heel of her palm with her thumb, hard enough to feel the old training scar pull. "That I missed it. That's all."

His surface thought came quick and clear. *Not all. Not even close.*

He was right. He was annoyingly, relentlessly right about her in all the places that had nothing to do with telepathy.

Silence again. Upstairs, somebody laughed at a joke she could not hear and thought about a football score. The ordinary world kept moving over their heads.

"We're burned," Damian said. "My network is compromised. Your border routes are compromised. The mole is still inside WISE, and now they know we mattered enough to hit."

"Yes."

"What do we do?"

She looked at the coffee ring. At her own hand beside it. At his hand on the other side of the table, close enough to touch if either of them had been different people.

"I don't know."

*Neither do I,* he thought, immediate and bare.

That, more than the words, made her look up.

He pulled off his coat and held it out across the table. "Take this."

"I'm fine."

"You're shaking."

"I'm cold."

"Yes," he said. "That's why."

She did not take it. After a second he laid it on the table between them, the sleeve falling over part of the coffee ring. The wool smelled faintly of outside air and old smoke.

"I should've moved faster," she said. "At the reception I caught a fragment about the mole and I let it slide because I couldn't place it. Then I burned two days on the wrong analyst."

"You missed a clue," Damian said. "That's not the same thing as killing a man."

She looked down at the coat. "Maybe not."

He said her name, the cover name, but his mind supplied the real one underneath it. *Anya Forger.*

She kept her face still. She did not look at him. She did not look away either. The coffee ring sat under his sleeve like a target nobody had hit cleanly.

"The Renewal Front is moving faster," Damian said after a moment. His voice had gone flat again, diplomatic and usable. "The raid and the ambush were coordinated. That means the provocation is close. Days, not weeks."

"I know."

"Then we work with what we have left."

She made herself ask it. "Which is what?"

He watched her for a second, then looked at the wall behind her, at the water stains, at anything but her face.

"Us," he said.

It was not comfort. It was not forgiveness. It was barely even hope. But it was a line that still existed.

Anya nodded once. The city kept going without asking permission. She thought of Crane's wife answering a call that could not be unsaid. She thought of Sylvia already moving to the next failure, because the work did not stop for grief. When they finally stood, the coat was still on the table.

* * *

&nbsp;

Damian took the long way home.

He walked east through Berlint's commercial district past shuttered storefronts, delivery trucks, cafes rolling up awnings for a day that had no idea what had happened. The air bit harder without his coat. He had left it in the basement. He did not go back.

He kept moving because sitting still meant thinking in circles, and he was already doing that.

Reinhardt had daughters. Eight and ten. Damian had never asked their names. That had felt prudent. Now it felt cheap. He knew about the football instead. He knew about the gym bag. He knew Reinhardt took the long ramp into the ministry garage because the short stairs hurt his knee in winter. Useless information, except it was not. It was the part that kept exposure from staying abstract.

By the time he reached his building, rain had started. He let himself in, locked the door, and stood in the entryway listening to the refrigerator hum and the pipes settle. Wet footprints spread on the tile. The apartment looked half inhabited, as it always did when he was too tired to pretend otherwise. Books on the floor. One lamp on. Cold air leaking out of the bedroom because he had left the window cracked again.

The box was in the closet on the top shelf, behind a stack of diplomatic histories he kept meaning to finish and never did. He had opened it once since moving in, late at night after a dinner where he had smiled through opinions that made him want to break the glass in his own hand. He had looked at the photo then and put it back. Tonight he took the box down.

It held the usual things. A school blazer he had outgrown at thirteen, folded badly. A note from his mother, the ink almost gone. A runner-up certificate from a math competition. Three pens his father had given him for the entrance exams. Second place had always been the safe result in his house. Not good enough to display. Too good to throw away.

And the class photo.

He sat on the edge of the bed with it in both hands. Standard group portrait. Teacher at the side, already tired. Second row, third from the left. Pink hair. Wide grin. Uniform rumpled. Looking somewhere off camera like whatever was there was more interesting than the photographer. Small enough that somebody had probably told her where to stand twice. Maybe seven.

Anya Forger.

He had known it at Becky's gala, really. The spoken name had only finished the job.

The woman at the state dinner had been Anya Forger. The cultural attache who found him too quickly. The woman who stepped into an ambush at exactly the right second. The woman who always seemed to know where pressure was before it showed on anybody's face.

Did that mean Westalis had put someone near him that early? No. Start over. That did not hold. She had been a child. He had been a child. She had punched him during dodgeball because he had called her a freak and deserved what came after. Nobody spent years cultivating the second son of a family not yet worth the trouble.

Maybe the cover had come later. Maybe the family had always been a cover. Maybe neither was true. He could not prove any of that from one school photo and a handful of memories that refused to behave like tradecraft.

He remembered stupid, ordinary things. Half a peanut butter sandwich shoved at him after Emile stole his lunch. Anya telling off a teacher so fiercely the whole classroom went quiet. Her laugh. The way she never looked embarrassed for more than two seconds. None of that felt arranged. It felt like childhood. Messy, mean, brief, real.

He remembered the safe house too, in ugly detail. Antiseptic on the cut above his eyebrow. Her fingers cold from rinsing out a washcloth. The way she said sorry like she meant the rib and not everything else. The kiss. There was no point pretending he had forgotten the kiss.

Was any of it real?

The girl in the photo was. The woman in the basement had been too. When she said she should've moved faster, there had been no performance in it. He knew performance. He had been raised on it.

He had been trained from childhood to see attachment as exposure. That training was not helping him now. He lied for a living too. Not the same lies. Close enough to matter.

He did not forgive her. The word was too neat, and too early. He also was not walking away. The raid and the ambush had happened on the same night. Reinhardt was exposed. WISE still had a mole. If he cut the alliance here, all he would be doing was finishing somebody else's work.

He would keep working with her. He would keep what he knew. Those were separate decisions, and he made both.

At the next meeting he would not say her real name. He would listen for the places she stopped herself. He would note what she chose to tell him and what she could not. He was done being blind, even if he was still very far from understanding her.

He slid the photo back into the box and put the box on the shelf behind the unread histories. Then he crossed the room, shut the bedroom window, and stood with his hand on the latch while the rain tapped once or twice against the glass. The apartment was still cold. It was only quieter.
