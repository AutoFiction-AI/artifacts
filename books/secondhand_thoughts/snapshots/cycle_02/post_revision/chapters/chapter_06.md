# Chapter 6

The next evening, twenty-four hours after the Bretz operation had run long and cold and left Hartig's name stuck to the back of her teeth, Anya hit the landing outside her parents' apartment and paused with the spare key between her fingers. Yor was cooking. Garlic, sugar, butter, and heat had pushed past partnership into open hostility, a scorched sweetness that stuck to the stairwell walls and would probably still be there tomorrow.

She'd spent the day at her cover office moving paper from one pile to another and pretending she wasn't still hearing Damian on the park bench, still feeling the shape of his hand from the walk back to the car, still running Hartig through every corridor of her head until the name stopped sounding like a person and started sounding like a door. None of it had worked. By then she was tired enough for home to feel less like a comfort than a place where she had to keep too many parts of herself from spilling.

Anya let herself in with the spare key she'd carried since she was nine. The apartment hit her the way it always did: warm, cluttered, faintly of Bond's fur beneath whatever Yor had done to the kitchen. Loid's shoes were lined up by the door, toes to the wall, exactly parallel. Yor's were kicked off at different angles, one on its side. Bond was asleep on the couch with his head hanging off the armrest, white fur covering the cushions in the thin permanent layer no amount of vacuuming ever fully beat. A grocery sack sat by the kitchen entrance with leeks sticking out of the top. Someone had meant well. Someone else had added heat and optimism.

"Anya!" Yor appeared from the kitchen doorway, flushed and holding a wooden spoon like a weapon she'd forgotten to put down. "You're early. I was going to have everything ready. The stew isn't quite, well, it's almost, it's getting there, I think."

*Too much heat again. Bottom's gone. Maybe the middle survived. No, that is also gone. Oh no.*

"Smells great, Mama."

"Does it? Really?" Yor's face brightened with a hope that made Anya's chest hurt. "Loid said it smelled 'interesting,' which I think means he was being polite."

"It means it smells interesting," Loid said from inside the kitchen, where he was wiping down the counter with the unhurried efficiency of a man who had already finished his own preparations and was now managing the aftermath of someone else's. He came out drying his hands on a dish towel. "Welcome home."

He hugged her. Brief and warm, his arms tightening once before letting go. The thought under it hit in pieces. *Too light. Eyes bruised. Not sleeping. Ask later. Don't start at the door.*

Anya pulled back and smiled at him. "I'm fine, Papa."

"I didn't say anything."

"You were about to."

That got a real corner of a smile out of him, though the assessment stayed in his face a beat longer than the smile. Then it softened and he was her father again. "I cleaned up the leftover chicken. There's a plate on the counter. I made rice too."

Yor made a small sound of affront. "The stew is fine."

There was a clatter, then a smaller clatter, then a pause.

"Sorry," Yor called. "Fine. Everything's fine."

Anya dropped her bag by the door and crossed to the couch. Bond opened one eye, registered her, and closed it again. His tail moved once against the cushion, a single thump of acknowledgment that counted as his whole greeting now that his joints were what they were. Her fingers found the warm place behind his ears and his thoughts drifted through her like bathwater. *girl. home. floor cold. sleep.* His head tipped harder into her palm.

She sat on the arm of the couch and let the apartment settle around her. The television murmured low, a news broadcast about trade negotiations that nobody was admitting to watch. Loid's reading glasses sat folded on a stack of newspapers he had already annotated with small pencil marks in the margins. They looked like idle habit. They were an indexing system he kept by reflex, cross-referencing sources and flagging discrepancies, and Anya had known that since she was seven and had read the system in his thoughts while he taught her long division. Yor's cardigan was draped over a chair. Two of Bond's rubber toys sat by the bookshelf in a row too orderly for a dog to have arranged. Loid's hand, tidying without noticing.

Loid settled into his chair and picked up the newspaper. The thought beneath the rustle came broken and quick. *Border patrol again. Southeastern corridor. If her sector... no. Not at dinner.*

The worry wasn't new. He carried it the way he carried everything else: quietly, efficiently, pretending it was only vigilance when some part of it had become love years ago and he no longer knew where the line sat.

"The news said the trade talks were stalling," she said, because it was the kind of thing a daughter could say without meaning anything by it.

"They'll restart. They always do." He turned a page. *Geopolitics at dinner. Casual, or not casual.*

Dinner was the stew, plus the rice Loid had made without announcing himself as backup, plus the chicken plate on the counter under foil like a diplomatic alternative nobody needed to mention unless absolutely necessary. Yor served the stew with a hopefulness that bordered on prayer, ladling it into bowls with extra care as if gentleness could retroactively improve the cooking. Anya ate it. The stew was thick and combative and tasted like three different recipes had argued over the same pot and nobody had won. Her mother had spent the afternoon on it, though, and love was the only ingredient that had survived the heat.

"Mrs. Kaplan complained about the pipes again," Yor said, blowing on her spoon. "She said the water pressure on the fourth floor was, what was her word, 'insulting.' She said the water pressure was insulting to her personally."

"It was low last week," Loid said. "I adjusted the valve in the basement."

"She didn't seem to know that. She was telling Bond about it in the hallway for about ten minutes. I think she believes he's the building superintendent."

"Bond was in the hallway?"

"He got out when I was bringing in the groceries. He lay down in front of her door and she started talking. I didn't want to interrupt."

Anya laughed. It came out real and easy and louder than the joke deserved, but she had not laughed in days and the release was involuntary.

Loid's thought brushed past her, rough as static. *There. Good. When did she stop doing that so often. Stop.*

She looked down at her bowl.

"How's work, Mama? Besides the pipes."

"Oh, fine, fine." Yor waved her spoon. "Busy, but you know. They reorganized the whole records system at city hall, which was, um. An experience. I don't think Mr. Henning has recovered. He kept trying to file things in the old cabinets and they'd already been removed, so he stood in front of an empty wall holding folders. I felt terrible for him."

"Did you help?"

"I helped him find a chair and made tea. That seemed like the right amount."

Yor smiled, and the smile was real. Then the thought crossed under it, quick and bright enough to sting.

*On his knees. Wrong room. Hands empty. Please, I have a... briefing said armed. Window there, not door. Wrong.*

Yor's fingers tightened on the spoon. "Anyway. The new system is fine. It takes getting used to."

A man on his knees. Empty hands. Wrong room.

Her hand went to the bread because it needed somewhere to go. If she said anything now, she'd have to say how she knew.

"This bread's good," she said.

"The place on Kirchner Street," Loid said. "The woman with the, what do you call it, firm customer service approach."

"She's mean," Yor said.

"She's efficient."

"She's efficiently mean. She told me I was buying too much rye."

"Were you?"

"That's not the point, Loid."

Yor stood to get more bread and nearly knocked over the salt. Loid caught it without looking. The movement was so automatic it would have been invisible to anyone who had not spent a childhood watching him do six things at once and call it normal.

"You should take food back with you tonight," Yor said. "Not the stew unless you want to, although if you want to I can pack it, but maybe the chicken is safer. Or the rice. I made too much rice. Well. Loid made too much rice."

"I didn't make too much rice," Loid said. "I made the correct amount of insurance."

Anya snorted.

"I eat, you know."

"I know," Yor said quickly. "I mean when you get home late and don't feel like cooking and then you decide crackers are a meal, which they aren't."

Loid glanced at her bowl, then at her face, not even trying to hide the parental arithmetic. "Crackers are not a meal."

"They're a category."

"They are not."

"Sometimes they are."

Yor made a helpless little gesture with the spoon. *She looks tired enough to fall asleep sitting up. Don't say that. Feed her first.*

"You both do realize I'm an adult," Anya said.

"We do," Loid said. "It's inconvenient."

That got Yor laughing. Bond opened his eyes again at the sound and this time pushed himself upright with the offended groan of an old dog having to negotiate with his own hips. His nails clicked across the floor in an uneven rhythm as he made his way toward the kitchen. Loid got up and uncovered the backup plate. Yor immediately apologized to Bond for the stew, as if he'd been counting on it and been personally let down.

"You didn't want this," she told him. "That's all right. I understand."

Bond accepted two pieces of chicken with solemn gratitude. *chicken. good. knees bad. girl here. good.* He left one shred behind, either full or making a point.

The anchor on television had moved from stalled trade talks to a list of upcoming diplomatic events in the same calm voice people used to read weather. A transport delegation. A party conference. Renovation delays at some state venue the camera showed only in passing before moving on. Loid muted it with the remote without looking away from Bond.

"How's your place?" Yor asked. "Is the upstairs radiator still making that knocking sound?"

"Only when it wants attention."

"That sounds expensive."

"It sounds haunted."

"Haunted is cheaper," Loid said.

"That is not how home repair works."

"It's how landlords work."

Anya ate more stew because it gave her a job.

Yor was already telling a story about a colleague at city hall whose birthday cake had arrived with the wrong name on it. Heinrich, apparently, had taken the whole cake home before anyone else could touch it because the icing error made it "administratively contaminated." Loid asked if that was an official city policy. Yor said it was now. Anya laughed in the right places and let the rhythm of the table carry her a little longer than she deserved.

When the bowls were empty, Loid stood to clear them. Anya stood too because sitting still had started to feel suspicious, even to herself.

"Leave it," Yor said. "You're visiting."

"I'm visiting the sink," Anya said, taking the towel Loid handed her.

"Dry, then," he said. "No heroic interventions."

That was how the next ten minutes went: plates passed hand to hand, Yor talking about Mrs. Kaplan and the building committee and a woman at city hall who had declared the new filing labels anti-clerical, Loid making small corrective noises that were either agreement or factual amendment, Anya slotting forks into the drawer by muscle memory. Bond resumed his place by the couch after one hopeful detour back toward the chicken. If someone had photographed the kitchen then, it would have looked like the safest room in Berlint.

Then Bond woke up.

Not the slow surfacing of an old dog working himself out of sleep, not the stiff-legged resettling she'd watched. This was different. His head came up rigid, eyes fixed on nothing, ears flat to his skull. For a second the years fell off him.

"Bond?" Yor half turned from the sink, hands wet.

Anya was already inside it. Bond's mind cracked open and the images came hard, not thoughts or words but raw visual data hitting faster than she could organize it.

A building. Through Bond it came as a wide stone facade with columns, pale government stone, ceremonial steps. Then the angle changed. An upper terrace. Iron railing. Carved balustrade overlooking grounds torn up in ugly strips, grass and ornamental trees displaced. Men in stiff grey-blue formal SSS dress moved fast across the terrace. One was running. Behind them, below them, fire climbed with the hard orange color of accelerant. Stone cracked. Heat shoved at the image. Under the fuel stink came a sweeter rot, cut flowers left too long in water and then baked under lights. Bond couldn't name any of it. His body only knew wrong.

Bond whined. The image shredded. Another one flashed, briefer this time: the entrance from ground level, people spilling through the portico, pressure in the air like a blow before sound.

Then it collapsed. Bond blinked twice, confused by his own alertness, and let his head drop back to the couch arm. His tail gave one uncertain wag. He sighed.

The whole episode had lasted maybe eight seconds.

"Just a dream," Loid said, settling back in his chair. He was watching Bond with the casual attentiveness he gave to everything nearby, the kind that looked like ease and was surveillance. The thought under it came clipped. *Precog. Strong one. Too sharp. What did you get?*

Anya's fingers had dug into the couch cushion. Bond's visions blurred fast once they passed, but she had the bones of it: a government building, a terrace, SSS uniforms, fire that did not look accidental, and that stray sweet stink of flowers going hot. When Bond's visions came this sharp, they were never nothing.

On the television, the muted anchor had moved to a shot of suited men shaking hands under flags. The ticker was too far away to read from the couch.

Telling Loid would blow open the oldest secret she had. Keeping her mouth shut meant hoping Bond had finally gotten one wrong. Neither felt possible.

"Papa." The casualness in her voice was borrowed from him, that flat register that made important information sound incidental. "At work somebody mentioned extra security at some government events. Maybe nothing. I thought you might've heard."

Loid looked at her over the top of his newspaper.

For one second she was his daughter making dinner conversation. Then his gaze recalibrated and she felt the shift like a draft through a door.

*Disclaimer first. Too careful. Government events, vague on purpose. From where?*

He set the newspaper down. "I haven't heard anything specific. Which events?"

"I don't know. It was general. Security posture, heightened levels, that kind of thing. I wasn't really listening." Too many extra words. Anya could hear them stacking up and couldn't stop.

*Threat might be real. Source isn't what she said. She knows I heard that.*

"I'll mention it to a few people," Loid said. "See if anything matches."

He picked the newspaper back up, but he folded the top corner first, marking the page before he hid behind it again.

*Off-source. Revisit later.*

Anya took another bite of stew and tasted nothing.

Yor asked if anyone wanted tea. She cut pears that weren't ripe enough to matter. She resumed the birthday-cake story from the point where Heinrich had carried the contaminated cake out like evidence. Loid half smiled behind the paper. Bond had gone back to dreaming about pigeons in the yard. Anya bit into a pear and hit grit.

Anya dried the last plate and put it in the rack. Her hands were steady. That felt insulting.

---

The phone rang at half past eight. Not the kitchen landline. The other one, in the drawer by the hallway bookshelf, with the ringtone her family politely treated as normal.

"Excuse me," Anya said, and stepped into the hall. Through the doorway she could still see the kitchen: Yor wiping down the counter now that the dishes were done, humming off-key, the back of her head moving a little with the tune. Loid was stacking the rice containers in exact descending size order because he could not help himself. He did not look up as she left, but his thought reached her anyway. *Secure line. Again. More often lately. Since the sector change.*

The line connected and Sylvia's voice was there, flat and stripped to function.

"This is a routine follow-up on your assessment of the Ostanian operative in the diplomatic corps sector."

"Sure." Anya leaned against the hallway wall. Dish soap and burned sugar hung in the air behind her. "Go ahead."

"Another agency, I will not specify which, has flagged activity in the same sector you assessed in your initial report. They are identifying communication and movement patterns consistent with a coordinated operation. Their analysis does not match your conclusions."

The stew, which had already been sitting badly, felt like it was trying to leave her body by force.

"What kind of patterns?"

"Movement intercepts, financial flows, communication timing. Their analysts are reading the sector as high-activity and potentially high-threat. Your assessment characterized it as mid-level factional maneuvering with limited strategic significance."

"That was my read," Anya said. Through the doorway she watched her mother hold a plate to the light, checking for spots, turning it carefully. The thoroughness Yor gave to a white dinner plate.

"I am not questioning your read." The absence of accusation in Sylvia's voice had its own pressure. "I am informing you that the picture is moving in a direction your assessment did not anticipate. If your evaluation has changed since filing, now is the appropriate time to update it."

It hadn't changed. That was the problem. Damian's operation had gotten more dangerous, not less, and she still wasn't going to hand him to WISE because the alternative was watching them crush the only man inside that machine who had said no to it with his whole body. She pressed her shoulder harder into the wall.

"It hasn't changed. The sector is factional noise. Internal Ostanian positioning. It looks coordinated because factions coordinate. That's what they do."

Silence. Anya could picture Sylvia at her desk: reading glasses on the chain, coffee long cold, fluorescent light flattening everything in sight. She couldn't read thoughts through a phone line. Without that access, Sylvia's pauses felt enormous.

"Noted." Precise, neutral, and therefore worse. "I will include your reaffirmation in the file. If your assessment changes, contact me directly. Not through the regular reporting chain."

"Understood."

"How is your family?"

The question arrived with no transition, the way Sylvia asked personal questions, as if they belonged on the same checklist as movement intercepts.

"They're good. My mom made stew."

"Edible?"

"Interesting."

A breath that might have been amusement, or might have been Sylvia breathing out. "Good night, Anya."

The line went dead.

Another agency had found the seam she'd left in the file. The lie she'd pushed into WISE was coming back with teeth. Sylvia's voice had stayed flat, but the instruction to call direct sat hot in her palm long after the receiver went quiet.

Anya put the phone back in the drawer and returned to the kitchen. Yor had finished the dishes and was packing the chicken into a glass container with the stubborn optimism of a woman who refused to let anyone she loved leave empty-handed. Loid was reading again, or acting like it.

"Everything okay?" he asked without looking up.

"Fine. Work stuff."

The thought under the page came in hard little strikes. *Work stuff. Secure line. Eight-thirty. Doorway instead of table. Too much weight. I know that stance.*

"I'm going to lie down for a bit," Anya said. "Long week."

"Of course." Yor turned immediately, already reaching for the kettle she'd put back on without anybody noticing. "Chamomile. For sleeping. If you want it. You don't have to drink it. I thought it might help."

"You can stay the night," Loid said, still on the page, which was how she knew he'd been paying attention to every line of her body since she came back from the hall. "The bed's made."

"Thanks." The word caught more than she wanted it to. "Maybe I will."

Yor pressed the mug into her hands a minute later. "And take the chicken when you leave tomorrow. Or tonight. Whenever. It's already packed."

"Thanks, Mama."

Tea in hand, Anya went down the hall to her old bedroom.

The room was smaller than she remembered, which meant she was bigger, which meant time had done that mean trick it always did in old rooms: left everything where it was and made her too big for it. The bed was the same narrow frame from when she was six. Her desk still stood by the window. The drawers held pencil stubs, a protractor, a notebook from her last year at Eden Academy with handwriting that looked like a stranger's because it was a child's. Two glow-in-the-dark stars still clung to the headboard. In the dark they kept their faint dirty green.

Her old headband sat on the shelf. Black, horned, a little ridiculous, retired years ago for simpler clips except on nights she was back here and reached for it without thinking. The plastic was scuffed and slightly bent from daily use. Anya put it on and sat on the bed with the tea cooling on the desk.

Through the wall, her parents' voices moved low and warm, a conversation between people who had been together long enough to imply half of what they meant. Anya did not reach for the words. She had taken enough from this apartment tonight.

Still, the night would not leave her alone. Sylvia's measured follow-up. Loid's quiet *Revisit later.* Yor's flash of empty hands and the wrong room. Bond's terrace on fire. All of it wrapped around the same lie she had told for Damian and kept telling.

Anya set the tea down, pulled the old school notebook from the drawer, and flipped to a blank page in the back. Before Bond's vision could rot any further, she drew what she had: columns, steps, terrace, iron railing, fire breaking stone from below. Then she went back and added the other thing, the detail that made less sense than the flames. She wrote it in the margin because she did not know how to draw a smell: flowers in bad water. Hot stone. The lines came out rough and mean. By the time she finished, the edges were already starting to blur again.

She folded the page once and slid it under her pillow.

That lasted maybe half a minute.

Anya sat back up. From the hall came the small ordinary sounds of the apartment settling for night: cupboard door, bathroom tap, Yor laughing once at whatever Loid said too low for the words to carry. If she lay down now, she would not sleep. She would listen to Sylvia saying not through the regular reporting chain and Loid thinking off-source and Bond pushing heat and flowers and stone into her skull again.

The paper had already warmed under the pillow.

She pulled the paper back out, tucked it inside the notebook, and stepped into the hall in her socks.

The living room lights were off. The kitchen light was down to one lamp over the stove. Her parents' bedroom door had closed. On the coffee table Loid had left the evening papers folded with his pencil still clipped to one section. He would call it careless if anyone asked. It wasn't. He left things that way when some part of him wanted to come back to them before morning.

Anya sat on the edge of the couch beside Bond's sleeping bulk and opened the section he'd marked.

There it was halfway down the second page, small enough to miss if you weren't already looking for trouble: a story about summit preparations at the International Conference Center running behind schedule because the front grounds were still under renovation. Beneath the article sat a grainy photograph. Pale stone frontage. Ceremonial steps. Upper terrace behind iron railing. The grounds below torn up in ugly strips where new security barriers and landscaping were still unfinished.

Anya's stomach dropped so hard Bond twitched in his sleep beside her.

It wasn't proof. Bond's visions never came with labels. The angle in the photograph was wrong. The fire wasn't there. The men weren't running yet. But the bones matched. Enough of them did. Enough for her hands to start shaking after the recognition instead of before it.

She looked down the hall toward her parents' room.

If she took the paper to Loid right now, he would ask how she got from a dog freezing on the couch to a marked article in the Metro section. He would ask gently. That would somehow be worse. If she called Sylvia back tonight, direct line like instructed, she would be reporting an impossible source the same hour another agency was already checking her file for rot. If she did nothing, she was back to hoping Bond had seen the wrong building, the wrong terrace, the wrong dead flowers cooking under summit lights.

Bond let out a soft sleep-noise and pressed his nose harder into the cushion. *warm. pack. safe.*

Anya swallowed. "Yeah," she whispered, because there was nobody awake to hear it and because sometimes agreeing with a dog was the most honest thing available.

She took the pencil from the paper, carried the notebook back to her room, and wrote on the back of the drawing in small block letters:

International Conference Center?
Check venue files.
Do not tell Papa unless you can explain it.

She stared at the last line until it felt stupid and true at the same time.

Then she folded the page smaller than before and slid it into the passport sleeve of her work satchel, behind her cover identification and in front of the old receipt she kept there to make the pocket look used. Not under the pillow this time. Not in the drawer. In the bag she would take with her in the morning.

That was the real choice, and her body knew it before her head caught up. Under the pillow the sketch could still pretend to be a nightmare. In the satchel it was a task. Tomorrow she would go in early, pull whatever public-event and venue files she could justify, and find out whether Bond's burning terrace belonged to the summit. If it did, then the warning she'd thrown at Loid over dinner would not be enough, and neither would silence.

Anya set the satchel by the bedroom door so she could not conveniently forget it. Then she lay down under the old blanket and watched the two stars over the headboard glow their dirty green.

The folded page was no longer under her cheek. It was in the bag waiting by the door, and that was worse, because bags meant morning and morning meant choices that stayed made.

When sleep finally came, it came late and thin.
