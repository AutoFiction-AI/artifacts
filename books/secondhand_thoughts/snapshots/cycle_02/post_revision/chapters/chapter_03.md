# Chapter 3

Anya put the folder on the table and kept her hand on it a second too long.

Burned coffee. Paper. The metal taste in the back of her mouth that showed up when she was about to do fieldwork or lie or both. Sylvia sat across from her with a legal pad and the calm face she wore when she already expected competence.

*On time. Prepared. Good.*

Three versions of the report had existed last night. The first had been true and unusable: Damian Desmond running a quiet war inside Foreign Affairs, Renewal Front chatter sharper than WISE knew, too many lines pointing toward a real operation. The second had cut the telepathy out and still pointed too close to him. She had deleted that one too.

The folder in front of her held the third version. Clean formatting. Correct stamps. It was a lie.

"Your initial assessment of the Ostanian operative," Sylvia said. "Walk me through it."

"The operative is a mid-level diplomat. Damian Desmond, yes, Donovan's son, but he's kept visible distance from the family's political apparatus." Anya opened the folder, though she knew every line already. "His activity looks like internal party positioning. I don't read him as a direct security threat to Westalian interests."

*Matches the secondary indicators.*

No suspicion. Not even friction. Sylvia just made a note.

"The Renewal Front intelligence."

"Fragmentary." Anya turned a page she did not need. The paper nicked the pad of her thumb. Good. Pain was useful. "Mentions of an initiative. Some nerves around timing. It tracks with the usual nationalist noise. I'd log it as elevated chatter, not active preparation."

Sylvia's pen kept moving.

*Sound judgment. She doesn't inflate.*

Anya kept her eyes on the folder because looking up would make it worse. Sylvia trusted her enough to write quickly. Trusted her enough not to stop and ask the next question. Two years of clean work bought that kind of silence.

"The Desmond name usually gets escalated," Sylvia said.

"I know." Anya heard herself answer too fast and slowed down. "I know. But the son isn't the father. At dinner he came off careful, ambitious, politically housebroken. Not dangerous in a way that needs immediate weight."

That was not even the clean lie. It carried too much truth inside it. Damian was careful. He was ambitious. He was also hiding an operation big enough to tear the sector open if WISE leaned on it.

Sylvia turned to the last page and rotated the folder back toward her.

"If you stand by the assessment," she said, "sign it."

There was a blue line waiting at the bottom of the page. Her name typed underneath. Her thumb still stung where the paper had cut it.

Anya picked up Sylvia's pen.

*No hesitation. Good.*

The lie became a signature in her own hand.

Sylvia took the folder back at once, capped the pen, and stamped the corner with practiced force.

"Watch and report, then. Passive observation through your cover. Monthly unless his profile changes."

"Understood."

That should have been it. File closed. Morning gone. Sylvia looked up anyway.

"Your work at the dinner was clean," she said. "Positioning was good. You kept the read despite the crowd. I noticed."

*Tell her clearly. Loid doesn't.*

The praise hit low and ugly, right under Anya's ribs. She felt her grip tighten on her own knee under the table.

"Thanks."

"You've earned trust here," Sylvia said, matter-of-fact. "Don't start doubting your instincts because the target's name is loud."

Anya nodded because her throat had gone tight in a way that would not help anything.

At the door Sylvia said, "There is loose-leaf in the break room. I keep it behind the mugs. Henderson drinks anything that sits still."

*He will find it. He always does.*

"Yes, Handler."

The small joke almost got a smile out of her. That made it worse.

In the corridor she kept walking until she hit the women's bathroom. Empty. She locked herself in a stall and sat with the folder on her knees.

Her hands were shaking. Not dramatically. Just enough to rattle the paper.

The top sheet still showed the header and time stamp.

FORGER, ANYA  
08:14  
Sector assessment: low priority monitoring.

Her signature sat at the bottom in dark blue, ordinary and obedient.

If another desk laid it beside the real sector traffic later, the hole would show. Not today. Maybe not soon. But it was there now, stamped and filed.

She flattened the page with both palms until the tremor eased.

Nobody trained you for the paperwork. The ordinary font. The clean stamps. Her stomach turned at how steady the letters of her name looked.

She flushed the unused toilet, washed her hands too long, and stared at her face in the mirror until it looked like her again.

Then she made tea in the break room because Sylvia had told her to.

The break room smelled like dish soap, scorched kettle metal, and the stale sweetness of sugar spilled days ago and never fully wiped up. Sylvia had been right. The loose-leaf sat behind the mugs in an unmarked tin, hidden with the optimism of a woman who believed at least one man in the building could be trained.

Anya spooned too much into a strainer and let the kettle shriek itself empty while thoughts passed the half-open door in fragments.

*...if Henderson took my yogurt again I swear to God...*

*...meeting at eleven, why is everything always at eleven...*

*...good tea? no, impossible...*

That last one was almost certainly Henderson. She nearly laughed. The sound came up wrong in her throat and turned into a cough.

She drank half the mug standing at the counter, because sitting still felt dangerous, then carried the rest into the corridor and took the long way back to her cover desk. The long way cut through records and analysis. She knew that. That was why she took it.

The concealed side of the station looked especially ugly in daylight. Fluorescents. Grey cabinets. Coat hooks bowed under rain jackets. Someone had pinned a cartoon of a sinking ship beside the routing board weeks ago and no one had taken it down. WISE liked to think of itself as invisible. Most days it looked overworked instead.

Her folder was already open on the records counter.

Anya stopped without meaning to. The top page had been turned just enough that her signature showed at the bottom. Above it, Sylvia's routing stamp had dried into blunt black certainty.

LOW PRIORITY MONITORING.

No one around the counter treated it like anything. A records clerk in sleeve garters fed carbon copies through a scanner one page at a time, thinking about train delays and whether her son had remembered his gym shoes. A junior analyst clipped cable summaries into distribution bundles and wondered if the cafeteria soup had crossed the line from old to dangerous.

The folder sat open between the scanner and the distribution bundles, her signature drying in plain view.

"Morning, Forger."

Gerald Falk had a stack of briefs under one arm and a mug in the other hand that smelled like burnt chicory and surrender. He was reading as he walked, tie slightly off center, not even pretending the day had improved him.

"Morning."

He nodded at the open folder. "Sherwood cleared this already?"

"She did."

He set his mug down, skimmed the first page, and made a face that meant numbers were happening somewhere in his head.

"Good," he said. "If I had to watch another desk escalate a Desmond just because of the surname, I'd start billing for theater."

*One less aristocrat burning bandwidth.*

His thought was cynical, ordinary, not malicious enough to warn anybody about anything. That almost made it worse.

Anya said, "Try billing the aristocrats directly. Maybe we all retire."

Falk snorted. "Now there's a funding model."

He initialed the routing sheet and dropped the file into the blue tray. The tray already held six other assessments, all thinner than hers. The motion took less than a second. That was all it took to move Damian from suspicion to queue.

Across the lane, a young analyst in shirtsleeves uncapped a grease pencil, leaned over the glass tracking board, and erased DESMOND from the red column marked PENDING REVIEW. He rewrote it in blue under PASSIVE / MONTHLY.

The pencil squeaked on the glass.

Anya took a sip of tea she could no longer taste.

The analyst caught sight of her and gave a quick, tired smile. "If it's your read, I can stop losing sleep over that file."

*She's usually right. Thank God.*

"You shouldn't lose sleep over anyone before lunch," Anya said.

"That's not how this place works."

"No," she said. "It really isn't."

He went back to the board. Falk picked his mug up and drifted away toward the inner offices, already thinking about a procurement cable and whether the minister attached to it was stupid or merely greedy. Around Anya the station kept doing what it always did: sorting, stamping, ranking. The scanner took another page. Someone laughed two desks over. Her tea hit the back of her tongue bitter as pennies.

She wanted to hit something.

She had lied to Sylvia. She had lied to the analysts who would work downstream from the file. She had lied while reading their surface trust as casually as other people read clock faces. The asymmetry of it sat ugly under her sternum. Everyone else in the room was blind in the normal human way. She was the one walking around with a knife nobody could see.

On the legitimate side of the building, her overt desk looked almost cheerful by comparison. Mission letterhead. Cultural calendars. A framed print of a Westalian museum no one in the office had ever visited. Someone had left a cream envelope on top of her keyboard with the Ostanian Academy of Arts seal stamped in gold.

Attached to it was a note from the deputy chief's secretary.

Need mission representation tonight. Academy opening reception. I am still dying of whatever plague Kessler brought back from the trade office. Please attend in my place and look diplomatic. Guest list enclosed.

Anya put the tea down and opened the envelope.

The guest list was thick paper and black ink and too many titles. She skimmed ambassadors, patrons, ministry deputies, donors, composers, one television critic with an inflated self-assessment, and then found the line she had been looking for.

DESMOND, DAMIAN  
Ministry of Foreign Affairs

Not keynote. Not host. Just present.

Which somehow felt more dangerous.

She read the list again, slower. The reception was for a reconstruction retrospective funded by a joint cultural grant, which meant speeches about memory, photographs in front of damaged-looking canvases, and at least one man pretending a budget line counted as healing. The chamber quartet would play in the main hall. There was a side gallery with the larger pieces. Foreign Affairs had sent Damian as part of the Ostanian delegation because somebody in his office had decided culture could still be useful if properly photographed.

Passive observation through your cover.

Sylvia had said it like routine. Routine had become permission the second Anya signed the report.

She pulled the program closer and started marking the margins with a pencil. Names of curators. Donors. The title of the lead painting. The exact phrasing from the grant summary so she could recognize it later when someone repeated it in a speech as if they'd invented it. She circled three safe opening lines and crossed out two of them immediately.

The first sounded too eager.

The second sounded like a woman trying to flirt on purpose, which was somehow worse.

The third was about the quartet and at least had the advantage of being true if they were bad.

At noon she ate half a sandwich over her desk and read the program again. At one she drafted a short mission note about attending in the deputy chief's place and filed it through the correct cover channel. At two she told herself she would keep the contact clean: twenty minutes, public room, no volunteering of off-book details, no letting him set the pace. At two-fifteen she rewrote the plan in her head and admitted the real problem was not protocol.

The real problem was that she wanted to go.

Strategic need, yes. He was a live line into Foreign Affairs and whatever he was doing against the Renewal Front. She needed contact. She needed something more than stolen thoughts and formal smiles across big rooms.

When she pictured the terrace again, heat climbed her neck before she could call it tradecraft.

He had looked at her on the terrace like a memory trying to fight through fog. He had thought her face, her laugh, the front edge of her name. She should have wanted distance from that. What she wanted instead was another chance to stand close enough to hear him think.

It would have been cleaner if she had simply wanted the intel.

By late afternoon the station had settled into the dull roar of paper moving and phones being answered with institutional patience. Her file on Damian was no longer visible from her desk. That should have helped. It did not. Somewhere behind two secure doors and one scanner queue, her signature was drying into archive.

At six she left the mission, stopped by her apartment long enough to change out of office grey and into something the cultural desk could plausibly have chosen on purpose, and stood in front of her bathroom mirror trying out the three marked opening lines.

The first still sounded false.

The second sounded thirsty.

The third sounded like her if she was being careful.

She went with careful.

On the tram into the government district she kept the folded guest list in her coat pocket and watched Berlint slide by in streaks of rain-dim glass and lit shop fronts. The carriage was crowded enough to hum, not crowded enough to drown her. She got surface noise in bursts: grocery lists, sex, a blister on someone's heel, a woman silently workshopping a resignation she would never send. Human spill. Easy. Familiar.

Every time the tram lurched she felt the paper in her pocket tap against her hip like a reminder.

Her lie had bought her room. Tonight she was going to spend it.


The Ostanian Academy of Arts was bright, crowded, and blessedly smaller than the state dinner. Thoughts moved through the hall in manageable bands instead of storm fronts: complaints about funding, annoyance about the quartet, one man's desperate hope that the salmon puffs had not run out yet.

Anya stood near the refreshments table with a glass of white wine she did not want and found Damian by the far wall.

He was talking to a woman in a grey suit. He looked lighter here, one cuff unbuttoned, his wine glass loose in his hand. When the woman said something and he laughed, the sound came out before the diplomatic version of it could catch up.

*The attaché from dinner. Don't stare. Listen.*

His eyes came back to her anyway.

Anya moved on him in stages. Brief stop with a Westalian press officer. Nod to an Ostanian academic. One useless canapé for camouflage. By the time the woman in grey stepped away, Anya was already in the opening.

"The quartet's ambitious tonight," she said. "I think that's Schumann."

"It is," Damian said, turning toward her. "Though ambitious is fair."

Up close he looked less like the boy in the old memory and more like a man who had come straight from work and stayed anyway. Slight dampness in his palm when they shook hands.

"Anya," she said. "Westalian mission."

"Damian Desmond. Foreign Affairs. We met at the dinner. Sort of."

"Sort of is generous. I was mostly trying to find the bar."

That got the small laugh again.

*She's funny. Be normal.*

Normal did not last. His gaze caught on her face a beat too long, moved to her hair, came back. The pause was tiny. Anya felt every second of it.

*Her face. Where...*

She smiled like she had not heard that and kept the conversation moving.

"So you've been in..." She stopped. Berlint was wrong. Schools were wrong. Anything with a timeline was wrong. "Sorry. Foreign Affairs. Is it as glamorous as people say?"

The redirect was visible. Damian paused half a beat before answering.

"It depends who says it." He loosened a little. "Mostly it's position papers and meetings about meetings."

Then, because he could not help himself, "The interesting work lands higher up the chain. For now."

His mouth tightened almost immediately. He looked into his wine like it had betrayed him.

*Too much.*

"You don't have to..." Anya heard herself start and caught it. He had not said anything that needed saving. "That's honest," she said instead.

He glanced at her, held there half a beat, then looked down into his wine. "That wasn't supposed to be. I mean, it was. Just not that soon."

Still awkward. Still better. His ears had gone slightly pink. Anya looked at her wine so he would not catch what her face was doing.

Music shifted at their backs. Someone near the donor wall started talking too loudly about regional healing. Damian turned his head toward the sound and then back to her with a look that was not quite apology and not quite conspiracy.

"You're attached to the exhibition?" she asked.

"Only in the sense that Foreign Affairs gets blamed for any evening involving flags."

"That does explain the string quartet."

"Does it?"

"Everything bad has a committee behind it."

His mouth moved before he caught it. "That may be the most professional thing anyone's said to me all week."

She tipped her glass toward the nearest wall card. "'Resilience Through Shared Tenderness,'" she read. "That sounds like a committee wrote it while one of them held the others hostage."

Damian followed her gaze and then laughed outright. Not the dinner laugh. This one was less careful.

"Three deputy directors and a translator lost that fight," he said.

"You know this for a fact?"

"I sat through part of the process by accident."

"How do you sit through that by accident?"

"My brother's office wanted me visible. Culture was considered harmless."

"Was it?"

"Not to the translator."

He stepped closer to the wall card and lowered his voice like he was sharing classified material. "It originally said 'mutual care in reconstruction.' Then someone decided care sounded soft. Then someone else decided reconstruction sounded punitive. This is where we landed."

"Shared tenderness."

"An atrocity."

"I kind of love it now."

"That reflects badly on you."

"It reflects accurately on me."

He looked at her for a second too long again, but this time the look had something warmer in it.

*Don't grin. You are grinning. Stop.*

Anya had to look away before her own face did something unhelpful. She used the movement to inspect the donor board, which was almost as tall as the painting beside it.

"That plaque is bigger than the art."

"The plaque paid for the art."

"That doesn't mean it has to win."

"That's not how donors think."

Anya snorted. "Westalia had a visiting pianist last spring. We printed six hundred brochures promising selections from our nation's mournful digestive tradition."

Damian blinked. "What was it supposed to be?"

"Folk heritage."

He put the side of his glass against his mouth and failed to hide the laugh. "That's worse. I respect it, but it's worse."

"People showed up on purpose."

"For the music?"

"For the digestive tradition. The hall sold out."

His shoulders loosened another inch. He didn't look back for the woman in grey or scan the room for rescue. He stayed with her.

The quartet hit a phrase badly enough that three people near the front flinched in sequence.

Damian murmured, "Our cellist is trying to escape the violinist."

"I knew it."

"You looked pleased with yourself before I said anything."

"I was."

"That's unnerving."

"Then don't volunteer supporting evidence."

He laughed again. Two guests squeezed past behind him, and he shifted with them without breaking off. Bureaucratic fraud. Bad translation. Committee language bad enough to count as a crime. Stupid material. Enough to keep him there.

Then Damian's attention snagged.

Not on her face this time. On her hands.

She had started talking with both of them without noticing, sketching the shape of the donor plaque in the air. His eyes dropped, tracked the motion, and slowed. Something moved behind them.

*Courtyard bench. Paint on cuffs. Both hands when she argues.*

"Did you..." he started, then stopped. His eyes came back to her face. "Sorry. Did you study in Ber..."

Too close.

Anya cut across him so fast it bordered on rude. "Hold on. Is that plaque actually blocking the signature?"

She pointed at the bottom corner of the painting. A passing donor in pearls was already thinking furiously about the placement.

*If my name is under that awful man from customs I want the whole board redone.*

Damian blinked, question still hanging in the air behind his eyes, and followed Anya's finger.

"It is," he said.

"That's criminal."

"It's donor relations."

"Same thing."

He turned back to her slowly. Not fooled. Not pushing yet.

*That was a dodge.*

Anya lifted one shoulder. "You were about to ask a geography question at a reception. I panicked on your behalf."

His mouth twitched. "I might have made it interesting."

"Regional vowels are never interesting."

"That's a very strong position."

"It's a correct one."

He looked at her another beat, then let the question slide. "You sound local sometimes," he said. "And then not."

Tangential honesty. Bad evasion. The only kind she was any good at.

"I had tutors who cared too much," she said. "Everyone wanted my language corrected into their version of civilized."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It was boring first. Then exhausting."

Something in his face eased. Not because he fully bought it. Because it was enough for now.

They drifted away from the donor board toward a cluster of smaller canvases, following the space that opened naturally between guests. A cultural secretary from the Ostanian side nodded to Damian and then glanced at Anya's mouth before remembering manners. A critic with an expensive scarf thought, *He's in a better mood than he was at the ministry,* and kept moving.

"Do you actually do much of the arts portfolio?" Anya asked.

"Enough to complain about it convincingly."

"That wasn't the question."

"No," he said. "It wasn't."

He took a sip of wine he clearly disliked. "I get pulled in when the ministry wants someone young enough to make funding cuts look like modernization."

"That's bleak."

"It's also true."

"Westalia sends me when they want someone who can smile at a sculpture and make it sound bilateral."

"Can you?"

"Depends on the sculpture."

He glanced at the nearest canvas, which looked like wet ash with aspirations. "And this one?"

"This one wants me dead."

"Finally. An honest critic."

He should have sounded smoother than he did. Public Damian probably did. This version kept letting little honest parts break through and then watching them like they might need to be recalled.

"You hate these things," Anya said.

"I hate what people do in rooms like this."

That came out too fast. He stopped, jaw tightening around the mistake.

*Careful.*

Anya waited.

He corrected, but not all the way. "Everyone talks around the art. Over it. They say culture when they mean image, or continuity, or budget camouflage. By the end of the night you learn more about procurement than painting."

That was closer to the bone than his public face ought to have allowed. She kept hers neutral.

"That sounds useful."

"Sometimes."

"Sometimes sounds like yes."

He looked at her sidelong. "Do you always interrogate people this politely?"

"Only if they start talking like they regret their job."

"I don't regret it."

"No?"

"I regret large parts of the institution attached to it."

He said institution like it had edges.

Anya let herself smile. "That's more specific than most diplomats get on first meeting."

"Most diplomats don't compare grant language to hostage situations."

"That's because most diplomats are weak."

"That's a terrible thing to say at an arts reception."

"Then it's the right venue for it."

His next laugh came with a shake of his head. For a second he looked young in a way the dinner had not allowed.

*She's impossible. Keep talking to her.*

There it was. Not just recognition danger anymore. Not just that old schoolyard static pressing at the edges. He wanted the conversation. He was stepping into it on purpose.

That changed the temperature under her skin.

They stopped in front of a bronze sculpture with an explanatory placard so vague it could have described weather.

"Do you know what's funniest?" Damian asked.

"I'm worried already."

"This grant came out of a policy fight over industrial repair funds."

"That's not funny."

"Wait." He lowered his voice again. "The arts lobby won because one committee member thought public restoration sounded less expensive than infrastructure."

Anya stared at him. "You are joking."

"I wish I was."

"So this whole exhibition exists because someone heard the word restoration and pictured a mural instead of a bridge."

"Essentially."

"That is the dumbest thing I've heard this month."

"It's only the twenty-ninth."

"Exactly. Strong competition."

He looked like he wanted to say more and should not. Surface thoughts broke against the leash of his public training in brief clean flashes.

*Budget committee.*

*Trade office.*

*If she'd seen the numbers... no.*

Anya kept her expression easy and did not chase the scraps harder than she already was just by standing there. Surface thoughts were not consent. They were just open doors she walked through anyway. The guilt came and went in quick ugly bursts, never strong enough to stop her.

"So why are you really here?" she asked.

He didn't answer immediately.

Good. He kept watching the spill being cleaned instead of reaching for the easy answer.

Around them the room thickened and thinned. Someone somewhere dropped a glass and then spent six full seconds thinking *Don't turn around* while everyone turned around. The quartet staggered into recovery. Damian watched the spill being cleaned and said, without looking at her, "Because people treat rooms like this as off-stage."

Anya waited again.

"They aren't," he said.

That was all.

She rolled the stem of the glass against her palm. No names. No intel. Just him showing her where he stood in the room, and that got under her skin worse.

"Fair," she said.

He turned back to her. "And you?"

Too direct. Too soon.

Her first answer was the true one: because she had lied to her own side this morning and wanted to know if it had bought her something worth the damage. Her second answer was also true: because his thoughts felt different from everyone else's and she kept wanting to hear the next one. Neither was usable.

She took a sip of wine she still hated. "I go where people underestimate the portfolio," she said. "They get lazy when they think the room doesn't matter."

His gaze sharpened. Not alarm. Interest.

*Not just sculpture and canapes. I knew it.*

He said, "That sounds less like culture and more like work."

"Those aren't opposites."

"No," he said quietly. "They aren't."

For a second neither of them spoke. The nearest thought in the room belonged to a donor trying not to stare at Damian's mouth. The second-nearest belonged to a waiter thinking about cigarette break timing. Normal noise. Harmless noise. Between it, the silence held.

Damian glanced past her shoulder toward the smaller gallery and then back at her.

"Have you seen the next room?"

"No."

"There's a painting in there that's supposed to justify the whole exhibition." He shifted his glass from one hand to the other, then back again. "Or embarrass it. I honestly can't tell."

*Too eager.*

Protocol said not to let the target set the pace on first contact. Protocol was already losing.

"Show me."

The gallery was cooler and quiet enough that Damian's surface thoughts rose cleanly. Fifteen people, maybe fewer. Soft shoes on polished floor. One guard thinking about cigarettes. A woman near the back wondering if she could leave without greeting the deputy minister's wife.

They stopped in front of a huge dark canvas that looked like a wall after a fire.

"This one?" Anya said.

Damian folded his free hand behind his back and put on the face of a man preparing to say an intelligent thing about art.

*Negative space. Say negative space.*

Anya bit back a grin. "Go on. Skip the gallery answer. What do you actually think?"

Damian looked at her. "The gallery answer?"

"The polite one. Everyone has one."

He studied her a beat longer than the moment needed, then turned back to the painting.

"The use of negative space is..." He stopped hard enough that she saw the decision happen in his jaw. "Honestly, I hate it. It looks like the painter got furious at a door."

The laugh got out of her before the attaché version could catch it. It bounced off the gallery wall, too loud, too familiar, entirely hers.

Damian went still.

*That laugh.*

His eyes narrowed. The flirtation went out of his face. Search took its place.

"Sorry," Anya said, still smiling. "That was mean."

"No." He kept looking at her. "No, it was accurate."

He kept hold of it.

"This is a strange question," he said. "Have we met before?"

There it was. The half-memory from the terrace had crossed into the open and turned into a question.

Anya felt the old school courtyard flare up in her own head so fast it was almost comic. Mud on shoes. Becky yelling. Damian with a red mark on his cheek and righteous fury. She shoved it down.

"I don't think so," she said.

Too quick.

She added, "Unless you've been trapped at a lot of exchange receptions. They blur after a while."

He watched her for another second. Long enough that she had to work not to fill the silence.

*That isn't it.*

"Right," he said finally. "Sorry. You just seem familiar."

"That can happen when everyone in diplomacy starts wearing the same face after eight p.m."

That got a breath of a laugh out of him, but not full relief. Good. Or bad. Hard to tell.

They moved on from the painting, but not back to where they had been. The field had changed. He was still listening to her words. He was also checking them against the question he had finally said aloud.

So she stopped pretending the conversation was harmless.

"That wasn't the gallery answer either," she said.

"What wasn't?"

"The question."

He looked at the next canvas and said, "No."

"Should I answer it again?"

"Would I get a different one?"

"Probably not."

"Then we can spare ourselves."

He sounded dry. Not offended. Not convinced.

Anya said, "You notice people in inconvenient patterns, don't you?"

"Occupational hazard."

"Mine too."

That made him turn back to her fully.

"And what exactly is your occupation hazard?" he asked.

There was the dangerous version again. Not where are you from. What do you do when the room isn't looking.

She should have smoothed it over. She heard the proper, polished line and let it die.

"Listening past what people mean to show me," she said.

His eyes held hers.

*Jesus.*

Then, almost in the same breath, *Too much again. Stop doing that.*

Anya glanced at the next placard because if she looked at him another second she was going to make the wrong face. "You really are bad at harmless conversation."

"So are you."

"I know."

He huffed a laugh. The tension didn't break, exactly. It resettled into something thinner and stranger.

"Most people in these rooms want the easy version," he said.

"Of what?"

"Of everyone." He took a breath and let it out through his nose. "The harmless attaché. The grateful donor. The minister who cares deeply about access to the arts and not at all about the front page. The version where no one is using anything for leverage."

"And you're tired of performing yours."

"Tonight?" He considered that. "A little."

"That's honest again."

"You keep rewarding it."

"Maybe stop offering it."

"That doesn't seem to be happening."

They had reached the far end of the gallery where the windows looked out on black courtyard glass. Their reflections floated faintly over the city lights: her pink hair toned down by the dark, his suit turned almost charcoal. For one ugly second the reflected shapes looked young enough to belong to the wrong decade.

Damian saw it too. Not the same thing, probably, but something. His shoulders drew tight and then eased by force.

"Did I make this odd?" he asked.

"It was already odd."

"That's not reassuring."

"I wasn't aiming for reassuring."

He smiled despite himself. "No. I noticed."

A server came through with fresh drinks. Anya took sparkling water and Damian, after a visible argument with himself, did the same.

"Wise choice," she said.

"I need a clearer head."

*Around you, apparently.*

That one came through with enough heat behind it that she nearly inhaled her water wrong.

"You okay?" he asked at once.

"Fine. Bubbles."

He waited one beat, like he could tell there was more and had decided not to embarrass her by asking for it.

The pause caught under her ribs.

He said, "For what it's worth, I'm not trying to interrogate you."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"If you were interrogating me, you'd be smoother."

"That's offensive."

"It's accurate."

"You really enjoy telling me accurate things that sound insulting."

"You keep setting them up."

He shook his head and looked down into the water like he expected it to improve him. "I don't usually do this badly."

"Do what?"

"Meet people."

Her pulse stumbled once anyway.

"You're not doing badly," she said.

"I asked if we'd met before."

"We might have met before. That's objectively possible."

"You answered that too quickly."

"You noticed that too."

"Yes."

"Then maybe you aren't bad at this. Maybe you're just annoying."

That made him laugh properly again. The sound bounced softer here than her own had. Less dangerous. More dangerous. Hard to tell.

"That's unfair," he said.

"No, that's accurate."

"Right. Your favorite category."

"One of them."

He leaned one shoulder against the wall, closer to relaxed than she'd seen him yet. "All right. Since we're apparently done pretending to be normal diplomats for five minutes. What do you actually think this exhibition is doing?"

The question was serious enough that she answered it seriously.

"Trying to make austerity look humane," she said. "Trying to tell everybody that if the state funds damaged-looking paintings it still has a soul."

He stared at her.

*Exactly.*

"That's a grim read."

"It's not wrong."

"No," he said. "It isn't."

He looked back toward the big room beyond the gallery threshold, where speeches and donors and strings were still grinding away. "The ministry loves culture when culture can stand in for repair. It's cheaper. Cleaner. No one has to admit what isn't getting fixed."

He said it while looking out at the donors, not at her, like the room could take the blame for him.

Anya said, "You really shouldn't say things like that to foreign attachés."

"You really shouldn't say things like that to diplomats either, but here we are."

"Fair."

He rubbed a thumb once against the rim of the glass. "There's a forum at the ministry on Thursday afternoon. Trade and cultural appropriations."

Anya looked at him.

He kept going before embarrassment could stop him. "Public enough to justify foreign attendance. Miserable enough that no one bothers performing for long. The deputy minister says tariff reform at least three times before anyone stops him."

"That is a terrible invitation."

"It's an honest one."

"You already established that's your problem."

"Apparently." He exhaled. "If you're actually covering East-West exchange work, it's more useful than tonight. People start telling the truth by accident somewhere around the third repetition."

She stopped turning the empty glass in her hand. Fluorescent ministry room. Tariff reform. People bored enough to tell the truth by accident. Not a polite brush-off.

Anya heard the correct professional answer and stepped around it.

"Yeah," she said. "Send me the details."

His surprise was tiny but real. He took out his phone at once and then made himself slow down.

"I mean tomorrow," he said. "I'll send them tomorrow."

*Not tonight.*

"Tomorrow's fine."

She gave him the official cultural attaché address, the one WISE monitored, on a mission card. He read it once, entered it carefully, then slid the card into his inner pocket as if he did not trust himself to remember it later.

"That really is your work address," he said.

"You sound disappointed."

"I sound cautious."

"That too."

He put the phone away. "For the record, the forum may still be terrible."

"I believe in consistency."

"Good."

"Do you go to all of these?"

"No."

"Then why this one?"

He looked toward the doorway again, where the sound from the quartet came in thin and overcommitted. "Because this is where the budget language gets cleaned up before it starts doing damage somewhere else."

That was closer to the wire than anything he'd said yet. He seemed to hear it after it left his mouth. His jaw tightened. The old diplomatic reflex came down a second late.

"Sorry," he said. "That was vague and bleak."

"It was specific enough."

"Was it?"

"Yeah."

He studied her face the way he had at the start of the night, but this time the question in it was not only memory. It was whether she understood more than she was supposed to. She let him sit with that without helping him.

Eventually he said, "You don't sound much like a cultural attaché."

"You don't sound much like a man enjoying his reception."

"I didn't say I was enjoying it."

"Exactly."

That won another small, unwilling smile.

When they finally circled back toward the main hall, the quartet was packing up and half the room had already defected to coats and exits. A donor with lacquered hair caught Damian's sleeve and thanked him for his ministry's commitment to national healing. He delivered the required answer with flawless public warmth, and the whiplash of it almost made Anya laugh. By the time he got free again, he looked faintly embarrassed to have been overheard being competent.

"I assume that's the version you're better at," she said.

"Unfortunately, yes."

"Shame."

"On what grounds?"

"I liked the other one."

He went very still for half a second.

*Don't react to that. Absolutely react to that.*

Anya looked away fast enough to make it look like she was watching someone fetch a coat.

At the entrance he offered his hand again.

"It was good to meet you, Anya."

"You too."

He held on half a beat longer than protocol required. Then, quieter, "If I remember where from, I'll tell you."

Anya kept her smile in place by force. "Please do."

He nodded and headed out into the cold with the look of a man carrying home a question he did not intend to drop.

Outside, the boulevard had emptied into the usual government-district dark. Streetlights on. Trees half-turned. Her wine buzz had never arrived. All that was left was the cold and the day.

She had blown past the twenty-minute mark, let the recognition question live, and accepted a second meeting in a ministry room because he offered it and because she wanted it. The lie to Sylvia had bought her room. She had used all of it and already started spending tomorrow's.

City thoughts brushed past as she walked toward the diplomatic quarter. Dinner. Rent. Wet shoes. A child reciting spelling words to herself. A man rehearsing an apology he was never going to make. The usual human spill, unasked for and easy.

At a red light she pulled one hand from her pocket and held it under the signal glow.

Steady.

She curled it back into a fist and waited for the light to change, with Sylvia's quiet trust still in one ear and Damian's not-quite-confession about off-stage rooms in the other.

The report was in WISE's system now. Her cover address was in Damian's pocket. By tomorrow there would be a ministry forum in her inbox, and her own signature was already sliding deeper into archive behind two secure doors.

When the signal changed, she crossed with everyone else, hand steady in her pocket, feeling the file disappear into one system while the line she'd opened to Damian pulled taut in the other direction.
