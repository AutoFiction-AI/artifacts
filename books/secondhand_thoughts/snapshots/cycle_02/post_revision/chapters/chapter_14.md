# Chapter 14

Three days after David Crane died at the Kreuzfeld border checkpoint, Anya stood outside the apartment door with the key in her hand and thought, not for the first time, that he had no business being in this hallway on a Tuesday night. He was there anyway. The apartment smelled of sesame oil and sugar catching at the bottom of a pan. Sylvia's voice: *We lost Crane at the checkpoint.* The checkpoint lights. The gap in the coverage pattern Anya had helped cut open with a lie.

Through the door she heard Bond's nails on hardwood and the low murmur of the radio Loid always kept on in the evenings. Yor moved in the kitchen. Water ran at the sink. Home sounded normal enough to make her throat close.

She should've called. There was a protocol for this. When an operation went bad and you needed a handler outside the chain, you filed a contact request and waited for a secure window. You did not disappear for three days and then show up at your parents' apartment at nine on a Tuesday with your hands shaking and a dead man's name lodged behind your teeth.

She let herself in.

Loid was at the sink in an apron, sleeves pushed to the elbows. Yor had the refrigerator door open and a bottle of sauce in one hand. Bond thumped once from the living room. For a second nobody had turned yet, and Anya saw the evening as it had been before she came in: dinner half finished, radio low, the kind of ordinary hour Crane would never get back.

"Anya!" Yor shut the refrigerator too fast. "You should've called. I would've made more rice. Have you eaten? Sit down. Wait, no, you look cold. Sit down first and then tell me if you've eaten."

Bond lumbered in from the living room and shoved his head against Anya's hip hard enough to make her stumble. The thought from him knocked into her in three blunt beats. *Home. Her. Good.* She bent to scratch behind his ears because it gave her one extra second before she had to speak.

Loid turned. His face stayed easy. The quick check behind it did not.

*She came alone. No call. Her hands. How bad.*

His gaze dropped to the white grip of her fingers around the strap of her bag.

"How's work?" he asked.

It was the kind of question a father asked at a kitchen table. It was also cover for *tell me what room we're in*.

"Papa." Her voice came out rough. She cleared it. "I need to talk to you."

The reading glasses came off his head. That was the only large movement. The rest happened in smaller places: the change in his stance, the way his attention locked, the quiet that settled over him without touching his face.

Yor set the sauce bottle down. "Anya?"

"They killed Crane," Anya said.

The kitchen went still.

Loid crossed to the radio and switched it off. The weather report died in the middle of a sentence. Yor pulled out a chair without taking her eyes off Anya.

"Sit," Loid said.

Anya sat because her knees had already started to shake. Bond pressed against her leg. Yor filled a glass from the sink and put it in front of her. Anya wrapped both hands around it and did not drink.

"When?" Loid asked.

"Three mornings ago. Before dawn. Kreuzfeld checkpoint."

"You were there?"

"No." The word scraped. "I got the call after."

Yor sat too, hands folded tight in her lap. "Anya, what happened?"

She had spent the train ride over telling herself she would say it cleanly. Just facts. Just what they needed. The facts came out anyway, but not cleanly.

"Crane was working a courier line tied to summit prep. Not official prep. Off-book movement, procurement chatter, access schedules that shouldn't have been on the same route. Somebody burned the checkpoint before first light. He didn't make it through. The line went dark after."

Loid's eyes did not leave her face. "You think the checkpoint hit and the summit are one operation."

"I know they touch." She swallowed. The water in the glass trembled against her fingers. "Renewal Front isn't waiting anymore. They've got help inside both governments and they're tightening the timetable. The summit's not a pressure tactic now. It's the stage."

"What kind of hit?" he asked.

"Public. Fast. Something that reads Westalian before anybody can get in front of it."

Yor made a small sound and looked down at the table as if she could see the conference center there in the wood grain.

Loid said, "How much of this does WISE know?"

"Pieces. Border breach. Dead courier. Noise in the procurement channels." She forced herself to meet his eyes. "Not the whole shape. Not from me."

He waited a beat.

"Why not from you?"

Because she had lied once already. Because there were forms with her name on them and one false line still spreading rot through rooms she could not see. Because Sylvia had trusted her and Crane was dead and every hour since the call had sounded like blame.

What came out was smaller and truer in a different direction.

"Because there's a mole in WISE and I still can't name them. If I push the wrong packet through the wrong desk, we lose whatever's left."

Loid did not nod. He did not reassure her either. He just held the line of the question.

"And that is the only reason?"

Anya looked at the water.

"No."

Yor leaned forward a little. "Honey."

"I kept thinking I could get one more clean piece," Anya said. "One thing nobody could argue with. One thing that would make it look less like panic and more like a case file. I kept thinking if I came to you with enough in my hand I could do this right, and every time I had something new it still wasn't enough."

The kitchen stayed quiet.

Loid's thoughts came through in jagged, disciplined fragments.

*No sleep. Guilt first, then tradecraft. Not useful. Fix that. Summit schedule. Venue ingress. Who else has seen her working this line.*

"You should've come sooner," he said.

"I know."

It did not feel like absolution. It was not meant to.

Yor reached across the table and touched Anya's wrist, just once. "Did you eat anything today?"

Anya shook her head.

"That wasn't the question," Loid said, still looking at her. "What else are you not saying?"

Her stomach dropped hard enough to hurt.

"Some of the sourcing."

"Whose?"

"Mine."

His expression did not change, but he heard the dodge for what it was. Anybody would have. Anya could feel the lie-shaped absence sitting on the table between the glasses and the soy sauce and the half-made dinner.

"Back channel?" he asked.

"Yes."

"Is it compromised?"

"Not yet."

"Can you prove that?"

"No."

Yor's fingers tightened on her wrist, then loosened. "Is that the part that's dangerous to say here?"

Anya let out a breath that shook on the way out. "Partly. Partly it's just..." She looked away. "If I start on every wrong thing in the right order, we're going to be here all night and the summit's tomorrow."

Loid watched her for one more second.

"Is what you're holding back the difference between stopping this and not stopping it?"

"No."

"Is it the difference between whether I can trust the summit threat?"

"No."

"Then I can leave it alone for tonight."

He said it flat, as a temporary operational choice and nothing kinder than that. The mercy in it made her throat burn worse.

"Tell me the solid part," he said. "Just the part you can stand behind."

So she did.

She told them about the opening session being the obvious window because the cameras would already be live and the delegations trapped in place. She told them there had to be an internal relay point inside the building because no exterior team could keep a line clean through the first minutes of panic without help from the floor. She told them the target was still missing, that she could not yet tie the shooters to one named unit, that the operation depended on making the blame legible before the facts were.

She did not tell them about telepathy. She did not tell them about the false report sitting in the WISE system like a planted fault line. She did not tell them that every useful thread she'd pulled in the last three days had started with a thought she'd stolen off somebody else's mind.

Loid interrupted only to narrow things down.

"Opening session, not lunch."

"Yes."

"Gunfire or explosive?"

"Gunfire first, I think. Something directional. Something camera-readable."

"Do you know who the inside relay serves?"

"No. Just that they're feeding somebody on the floor."

"Do you know the route from relay to shooters?"

"No."

"Do you know where Voss will position himself?"

"Not yet."

That got his eyes up.

"You think Voss is close enough to the action to matter."

"I think he wouldn't build this and then miss the first five minutes."

Yor had gone pale in a very steady way. Not shock anymore. Recognition moving into place.

*Dominic's office. The manifests. The watermark.*

She stood up so abruptly her chair legs scraped the floor.

"I have something," she said.

Loid turned to her. "Yor?"

She was already moving into the hall. Anya heard the closet door, the quick shift of boxes, paper against paper. Yor came back carrying a thick manila envelope and set it on the table between the soy sauce and the stack of plates.

"I should've shown you sooner too," she said. "I kept waiting until I understood exactly what I was looking at, and I don't. Not all of it. But I know enough."

She opened the envelope. Grainy photographs slid across the wood. Shipping manifests. Procurement records. A photographed purchase order with the Garden watermark at the top and three completed eliminations listed below it.

Loid picked up the manifests first. His eyes moved fast.

*Voss. Civilian intermediaries. Defense procurement. This is the supply chain.*

"Where did you get these?" he asked.

"A job last month. Karl Dominic. The broker who handled some of the off-books movement." Yor kept her voice even by effort, not ease. "He had them in his office safe and on his desk. Not locked separately. Like he'd gotten sloppy or like he thought nobody around him knew what they were."

Loid's hand stopped on the Garden sheet. He looked at her. She met his eyes before he could ask.

"The Garden took contracts for them," she said. "Not officially. Through cutouts. They marked people as liabilities in the chain and sent me after them."

Behind the words came the sentence she was not saying aloud.

*I killed them and never asked who was paying.*

Anya looked at the list again. Three dates. Three names. Three neat entries sitting on the kitchen table while Crane's death still burned at the edge of every thought she had.

Yor touched the page with one finger.

"I know this watermark. Not from city hall paper, not from anything ordinary. This is ours. Internal. The same mark that's on assignment sheets when they think nobody outside the shop will ever see them." Her mouth tightened. "I copied it before I left Dominic's office. He was on the floor bleeding through the carpet and I stood there with his camera in my hand taking photographs because I knew if I came home with nothing but a feeling I'd talk myself out of it by morning."

Loid did not move.

"How many?" he asked.

"Three on this sheet. More in the manifests. I don't know the full count."

"How long have you known?"

"Since Dominic. Since I saw the records." She folded her hands together to stop them from shaking. "Long enough to know I'm not going back."

He did not rush her.

"Say that again."

Yor blinked once. Not because she had not heard him. Because she understood exactly why he was making her do it.

"I'm not going back," she said. "If they send a handler, I won't answer. If they leave instructions, I won't take them. I'll help with the summit, but not as Thorn Princess. Not as their asset." She looked at Anya first, then at Loid. "As Yor Forger. As your family."

That was all. She didn't make it a speech, and she didn't apologize. The break sat in the room with its full weight.

Loid reached across the table and covered her hand with his.

"All right," he said.

Yor turned her hand under his and held on.

Anya looked down at Bond's head against her knee. His fur smelled like dust and soap and the apartment itself. Safe things. Crane had had safe things too. Everybody did, right up until the moment they did not.

Loid let the silence sit for a breath, then another, then shifted back to the table because that was what he did when the wall was already on top of them.

"Your documents corroborate the procurement chain," he said to Yor. Then to Anya: "Your summit read gives it a clock. We can work with that."

He pulled one photograph free and flattened it with his palm. "We still need floor placement, leadership seating, and whatever Voss thinks his deniability looks like tomorrow."

Anya lifted her head. "Damian can get that."

The kitchen changed again. Not as sharply as it had on Crane's name, but enough.

Yor looked between them. "Damian."

"The ministry diplomat?" Loid asked.

"Yes."

He leaned back a fraction. "Why him."

Not who is he. Not what do you feel. Just the operational knife straight through the middle.

"Because he was hit too," Anya said. "The same morning Crane died, somebody raided one of his caches. One of his people went to ground. He has his own line into this and whoever's building the summit attack wants him blind almost as badly as they want us blind."

Loid said nothing.

She kept going because stopping now would sound weaker than the truth was.

"He can get close to Voss. He can get party communications and interior movement faster than I can from my side. He already knows enough about the operation to matter and shutting him out doesn't make him safer. It just means he does tomorrow alone and we do it without half the floor."

Yor asked quietly, "Do you trust him?"

Anya stared at the edge of the Garden sheet.

"Not cleanly," she said.

That got Loid's attention in a way a prettier answer would not have.

"But enough?"

"Enough that when he had chances to burn me, he didn't. Enough that if he says Voss is in one gallery instead of another, I believe him." She looked up. "He doesn't know about you two. He knows me and the summit. That's all."

Loid's face gave away nothing. His thoughts did not.

*Useful. Risky. Young. Attached to her in some fashion. Possibly vice versa. Separate problem. Later.*

Anya wanted the floor to open.

"If he comes here," Loid said, "he comes because we need what he knows, not because you're asking me to take him on faith."

"That's what I'm saying."

Yor looked at the family photos on the hall wall and then back at Anya. "If he helps stop this tomorrow, I don't need faith. I need to know what we're asking him to walk into."

Anya looked at her phone.

"A summit attack," she said. "And probably the end of whatever cover he's still got left."

Yor nodded once, accepting the shape of it.

Loid picked up the Garden sheet again, then set it down. Decision made.

"Text him," he said. "If he's coming into this room, I want him here before we lock assignments."

Anya sent the message under the table because it made her feel less exposed even though both of them obviously knew what she was doing.

`Need you at my parents'. Tonight. Summit.`

The reply came back before she could lower the phone.

`On my way.`

Loid saw the light in her hand. "Fast."

"He was awake."

"Of course he was," Loid said.

Yor stood and went automatically toward the stove. "Everybody should eat before we start moving maps around. Thinking is worse on an empty stomach. I know that's not the biggest problem in the room, but it's still true."

Nobody argued hard enough to stop her.

She spooned rice into bowls with the same steady hands she had used to lay out proof that she'd been murdering the wrong men for the wrong people. Loid moved aside the papers just long enough to make room. Bond followed the smell and parked himself under the table with the solemn patience of a dog who believed all crises ended in dropped food.

Anya got three bites down because Yor was watching and because Loid said, "Two more," in the exact tone that meant he had already decided this counted as medicine.

The rice tasted like sesame and old metal.

No one filled the silence with anything fake. Yor apologized once for overcooking the vegetables, realized what room she was in, and stopped. Loid ate standing up, reading the manifest photographs between bites. Anya watched steam go thin over the bowls and tried not to listen to the room harder than the room was speaking.

It did not work.

*Whiteboard. Venue plan. No outside calls yet. Need clean paper.*

*Knife case in the hall closet. Flats, not heels. If they call from the shop, don't answer. Don't answer.*

*Eat. Keep chewing. Don't think about the checkpoint. Don't think about Sylvia saying his name. Don't think about Crane in the cold.*

She put the chopsticks down before she snapped them.

Loid noticed at once. "Enough?"

She nodded.

He took the bowls to the sink, ran water over them, and transformed the kitchen by stages. Plates stacked. Sauce bottle moved. Whiteboard lifted off the side wall and leaned beside the refrigerator. Venue map up with two magnets shaped like oranges and one shaped like a pear. Yor's photographs aligned on the table. Pens uncapped. The ordinary planning board Bond occasionally stared at while waiting for dinner turned into a war map in under a minute.

By the time the rice went cold on the counter, the kitchen looked less like dinner and more like evidence.

"Opening session is still the obvious window," Loid said. He marked the south terrace doors, then the east service corridor, then the west leadership row. "They need visibility and clean attribution. If the first footage reads Westalian, the correction loses the first hour and maybe the day after."

"The south side is theatrical," Yor said. She had not touched the tea in front of her. "Good sight lines. Room for panic. Bad if you want to keep the shooters alive."

Loid gave her a look.

She returned it, unoffended. "I'm only saying the plan values drama."

He tapped the map with the red pen. "Relay point is here or near here. Interior enough to push coordinates, close enough to the floor to watch timing, not obvious enough for venue staff to notice the same face twice."

Anya did not mean to read ahead. She just caught it because it was there, because Loid thought in route lines and risk blocks, because the room kept dropping usable things into her lap like knives.

*If Yor starts on the terrace and the team comes through the corridor first, she loses the angle.*

"Foyer junction," Anya said. "If Mama starts there, she can cover the west approach and still get south."

Both of them looked at her.

Too fast. Again.

Loid asked, "Why."

"Because the terrace is too open from the start."

"That isn't an answer."

She gripped the back of the chair. "Because from the junction she gets both lines until contact."

Loid stared at the map. "And loses the terrace for the first stretch of panic."

"Not all of it."

"Enough of it."

Yor leaned forward and measured distance with two fingers. *Forty seconds if the heels are wrong. Less without them.* "If I start inside and move on the first break, I can make it. Thirty-five, maybe forty."

Anya heard the unspoken part and said it before she could stop herself.

"Go in flats first."

Yor's head came up.

Anya wished very sincerely to stop doing this in front of other people forever.

"I was only thinking," she said too quickly. "Because running."

Yor still looked at her for one beat too long, then back at the map. "Yes. Flats first."

Loid's pen hovered.

*How is she getting there before I say it. Pattern. Later.*

He tapped the south side again. "Even with flats, forty seconds is too long if the first burst comes from outside."

The room tightened. Anya had solved nothing. She had just dragged an unspoken route into the open and proven it had a hole in it.

Good. Great. Excellent use of telepathy. Steal the thought, say it too early, still be wrong.

They reworked the lines once. Twice. Loid moved Yor back toward the terrace. Yor pushed herself toward the junction again. The map gathered arrows without getting clearer. The more useful Anya tried to be, the more she heard how much she was taking.

Bond had been asleep through most of it. Then he stood so abruptly the nails of his front paws scraped the floor.

Yor half rose. "Bond?"

He crossed the living room fast, faster than an old dog with stiff hips had any right to move, and planted himself in front of the refrigerator. He barked once and shoved his nose against the lower edge of the map.

Anya was already reading him. She could not help it.

Low wall. Wet stone. Grass crushed under hard shoes. Men in dark clothes coming fast along the edge. Light flashing on glass. Heat. Noise. Not words, never words, but a future with teeth.

Bond barked again, sharper this time.

Loid followed the line of his muzzle. "South perimeter."

The place Bond had pinned was outside the south terrace, where the landscaped grounds narrowed near the vehicle approach.

"That's not random," Anya said.

Loid looked at her, then at Bond, then back to the map. A different memory moved through his thoughts.

*Government event. Weeks ago. She pushed me away from it without telling me why.*

He did not ask the question. He only circled the south perimeter in red.

"Then the exterior route is real," he said. "Staging or extraction. Either way it matters."

Yor stood and came to the refrigerator. She measured from the foyer junction to the south terrace with two fingers again, then from the junction to the west side.

"If they're coming along the low wall, I shouldn't start on the terrace," she said. "I start inside, stay out of sight, and move south on contact. I can take the first man at the bend before the terrace line sees me."

Loid nodded once. "Better."

It had taken a failed suggestion, Bond's panic, and a future full of wet stone to get there. The map looked cleaner now, but not because Anya had plucked the perfect answer whole out of someone's head and handed it back. They had missed, adjusted, and paid for the adjustment with time and nerves. The knot under her ribs loosened by almost nothing.

Bond pressed closer to the refrigerator, the vision fading now into the simpler distress of a dog who knew only that the place on the map was bad.

Anya crouched and put her arms around his neck. "Good boy," she said into his fur. "I know."

Loid set the red marker down and recapped it with a click.

"You're not on the south side," he said without looking at her.

Anya straightened. "I know that."

"You're not on first contact at all."

"Papa."

Now he looked at her.

"No," he said. "We've got enough unknowns. I need one variable in this apartment that doesn't get shot at."

Yor looked from one to the other and did not intervene yet.

Anya felt the heat come up her neck.

"You need somebody on the floor who can tell when it turns," she said. "Not after. Before."

"Somebody," Loid said, "does not have to be you."

The sentence landed harder because he said it like a handler and a father at the same time and did not bother separating the two.

"I know the players better than anybody you're going to place in a corridor," she said.

"That still doesn't make you expendable."

Yor set her mug down very carefully. "She's not saying expendable."

Loid's jaw tightened.

"Then what is she saying."

Yor answered before Anya could. "That if the room shifts before the shooting starts, she'll see it faster than I will from a junction and faster than you will from a relay."

He looked at Yor.

She met it calmly. Not social-flustered Yor, not Thorn Princess exactly either. The third voice. The one that came out only when family and danger sat in the same chair.

"I don't like it," she said. "I still think she's right."

Loid's thoughts flickered in hard, precise lines.

*No open floor. Not after Crane. Not with the line already burned. Find another way.*

The doorbell had not rung yet, but Anya could already feel the shape of the answer coming up the stairwell in a damp coat with its thoughts pushed into neat stacks.

She texted Damian a second line.

`Need seating and Voss placement. Also press if possible.`

His reply took longer this time.

`Understood. Five minutes.`

At twenty past ten, his thoughts reached the stairwell before the bell did.

*You can walk into a kitchen. You can brief a family. Crane is dead, Reinhardt is in hiding, and you can still say hello like a person. Do not look at the photographs. Do not think Eden. West Gallery. South perimeter. Keep it on the summit.*

The bell rang.

Yor got there first. "Hello. You must be Damian. Come in. It's awful out there. Do you want tea? We have tea. And food. Please ignore the maps."

"Thank you, Mrs. Forger."

"Yor," she said at once. "Mrs. Forger sounds like somebody who sends complaint letters."

That got a surprised breath of laughter out of him. Not much, but enough for Anya to hear how tired he really was.

He stepped into the kitchen damp from the rain, coat dark at the shoulders, hair flattened at the temples. He looked like he had been sleeping badly in clean shirts. When his eyes found Anya, the next thought came too fast and too polished.

*Tea. Maps. Hallway photos. Don't. West Gallery. Keep it on the summit.*

There it was again. It wasn't a second layer or a hidden room, just a clean, deliberate choice to put one thought in front of another and lock the door behind it.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

Loid came out of the hall wiping his hands on the apron. "Damian. I'm Loid."

The handshake lasted the exact right length. Loid's smile stayed easy. Behind it, Anya caught the fast inventory click into place.

*Good suit. No aide. Diplomat's manners, no sleep. He looked at her first. Personal, then.*

Anya wanted to die on the spot.

"Thank you for having me, Mr. Forger," Damian said.

"Loid is fine."

Yor pressed a mug into Damian's hand before anybody else could choose a seat. "Anywhere except that chair. Bond claims it."

Bond, who apparently claimed everyone now, approached Damian with solemn attention, inspected his shoes, inspected his coat hem, then turned once and settled on Damian's feet with a noise of total surrender.

Damian went still.

*The dog has me pinned. I have negotiated with ministers under less pressure than this.*

It hit Anya wrong and almost made her laugh. Crane was dead. The kitchen table was covered in conspiracy paperwork. Her mother had just quit an assassination organization. And still there was Bond, flattening a diplomat by the ankles.

"He likes you," Yor said warmly.

Damian looked down at the dog and then around the kitchen. He left the top layer sitting right out in the open.

*This is real. The radio. The dog hair. Fruit magnets on a war map. She comes from this. No wonder she fights like someone protecting a house, not a flag.*

Loid did not move back to the refrigerator yet.

"Before we go any further," he said, "tell me why you're here."

Damian looked at him over the rim of the tea mug.

"Because the same people who killed Crane hit my side before lunch," he said. No diplomatic padding. None of his usual preamble. "One of my caches is gone. Reinhardt's underground. If I keep pretending tomorrow is still a political event with a security problem, more people die."

Loid said nothing.

Damian set the mug down.

"And because Anya asked."

There. Terrible. Mortifying. Fully on the table.

Loid's thoughts sharpened.

*Too honest on the second point. Good. Or dangerous. Same skillset.*

"If I think you're protecting your own position at her expense," Loid said, "this conversation gets short."

Damian nodded once. "Fair."

Yor, apparently satisfied that the men had done whatever strange animal test that counted for them, set a plate of reheated vegetables on the table.

"Please eat something," she said. "Operations improve when people chew. I don't know if that's an official intelligence principle, but it should be."

"Thank you," Damian said, and because he was either too polite or too well trained to refuse her, he sat down and took the plate.

Then Yor, trying to be kind, said, "Anya barely touched dinner when she was small unless I told her there was dessert waiting. She used to bargain like a union organizer."

Damian's gaze flicked once toward the hallway where framed family photos hung. Then the next four thoughts came up in a hard, neat stack.

*West Gallery. Voss. South perimeter. Ask about seating.*

He smiled at Yor with perfect politeness. "Can we go over the summit first? I brought the delegation update."

The swerve was so clean Anya almost missed it. But she saw the glance at the hall. She heard the swap. Harmless thoughts lifted into place where a riskier one had almost gone.

She could read the dodge. She could not read what he had dodged. He knew a piece of her life he was refusing to hold still long enough for her to catch. That was all she had. The rest was inference.

Loid was already back at the refrigerator. Father mode had retreated without disappearing. "Walk us through it."

Damian set the fork down untouched and stepped closer to the map. The whole room shifted with him. He stopped being the wet young man in the doorway and became what he had always actually been underneath the diplomatic polish: a trained political operator with nowhere safe to put the truth except work.

"Security tightened after the border hit," he said. "Party leadership is frightened enough to overcorrect. Voss will stay close to the floor, but not on it. West Gallery. That's where he can watch the hall and still deny operational responsibility if things go badly."

"Can you get near him?" Loid asked.

"Yes. If I look like I'm there to warn him, not expose him."

"Will he believe that?"

"He wants to." Damian's mouth flattened. "That helps."

Loid nodded once. "Good. That gives us the public channel."

Yor tapped the south perimeter circle Bond had chosen. "If the team uses the exterior approach, I take the foyer junction and move south on contact."

"Agreed," Damian said. "If the first shots come from outside, the panic drives inward. That buys the relay seconds."

Loid uncapped a black marker. "Communications room still my problem."

"Not the main room," Damian said. "Too watched. There's a secondary technical station off the east service corridor. Half venue staff, half ministry volunteers. If somebody's feeding the shooters live updates, I'd start there."

Anya caught the next thought before he voiced it.

*And if I wait until speeches start, Voss boxes himself with security and I lose the approach.*

"Do it during seating," she said.

The room paused.

Damian looked at her.

Too fast again. She was going to claw her own face off.

Loid asked, "Why seating."

"Because once the remarks start, everybody locks forward," she said. "If Voss is pretending to be informal, he'll only do it while people are still shuffling."

Damian's shoulders dropped a fraction before he hid it.

"Yes," he said. "That's better."

Another arrow stayed where she put it. Useful. Stolen. The old nausea turned over again.

They moved faster now, but not smoothly. Loid wanted a relay line nobody else liked. Yor rejected one corridor because it trapped her between glass and crowd control tape. Damian pushed back on a timing window because the party aides would notice him disappearing twice. Anya kept her mouth shut until she couldn't, then said too much too early, then shut it again. Every improvement cost somebody something visible.

Loid recapped one marker and reached for another.

"Anya stays in the diplomatic annex," he said. "Westalian side, back row. Minimal movement."

"No," Damian said.

The word came out too fast. He corrected at once. "No. Not the annex."

Everybody looked at him. Even Bond raised his head.

Damian kept his eyes on the map. What he let float up had already been cleaned for company.

*Too boxed in. Too visible to the wrong staff list. Press is cleaner. East Gallery.*

Loid's voice stayed mild. "Why not."

"Because annex seating now runs through ministry roster review," Damian said. "They tightened it this afternoon after the border breach. She gets boxed behind staff, searched by people I don't control, and if somebody on that line decides her face deserves a second look, the day gets worse before lunch."

Anya stared at him. "My face."

He did not look at her.

"Any extra face," he said. "They're jumpy. Jumpy people improvise badly."

It was not an answer. It was not enough. It was also the only version of it he was going to hold still.

Loid said, "And your alternative."

"Press seating in East Gallery. Better sight lines. Full-room view of the floor, the terrace access, and the west leadership row. Separate entrance. If she has to move, she moves with credential cover instead of waiting on a ministry usher."

Loid folded his arms. "You can get her press."

"Yes."

That answer was too quick too. Damian knew it, and Anya knew he knew it. The next thought he let up was drier.

*Petrov will hate this. Good.*

Loid's eyes narrowed a fraction. "At what cost."

"It burns a favor in the party communications office." Damian finally looked at him. "If the accreditation gets audited after the summit, the trail lands on me. I can live with that."

Anya heard the safe thought and the hitch beneath it, where he stopped himself before the real reason showed. He wasn't just finding her a better angle. He was keeping her off some other list and out of some other line of sight. He wouldn't think the reason clearly enough for her to catch.

Loid did not say yes.

"Why so fast for her," he asked.

There was father in it now, not hidden well enough to miss.

Damian held his gaze.

"Because she's already on too many lines in this operation," he said. "I can move one of them."

That was not the whole answer either. It was honest enough to leave a mark.

Yor, who had apparently decided subtlety was for other households, said, "That sounds expensive."

"It is," Damian said.

"Can you afford it?"

He gave a short, tired laugh with no humor in it. "No. But I can spend it anyway."

Loid studied him for one beat too long, then nodded.

"Do it."

Damian took out his phone and stepped into the hall. They could hear only his side of it.

"Petrov. It's Damian. I need one media credential for tomorrow. East Gallery, full-room access if you can manage it. Yes, I know what hour it is. I also know whose office covered for your nephew in November, so let's not waste each other's time."

Yor blinked. "Oh."

Anya pressed her mouth shut.

Loid made one note on the edge of the whiteboard while Damian was in the hall. He wrote `EAST PRESS` in block capitals, then underlined it once.

*He spends favors for her fast. Noted. Deal with summit first.*

Anya considered climbing into Bond's crate and staying there until winter.

Damian came back in with the same phone in his hand and more rain on his coat cuff where he must have leaned against the wall.

"Badge by seven," he said. "Press entrance on the east side. Use that, not the diplomatic queue. They tightened the ministry list this afternoon."

There it was again: the recommendation, with the reason missing behind it.

Loid rewrote the assignments on the whiteboard. "Yor, foyer junction to south perimeter. Me, east corridor relay and secondary technical station. Damian, West Gallery and Voss during seating. Anya, East Gallery press row."

He turned back to her before capping the marker.

"You are not chasing shooters."

"I know."

"You are not leaving the gallery unless the room is already broken."

"I know."

"If it breaks before that," Damian said quietly, "she'll see it."

Loid's jaw moved once. He hated the sentence because he agreed with it.

"Then she calls it," he said. "Nothing heroic."

Anya nodded. She did not trust her voice.

Yor stood at the map with her arms folded, measuring it not as lines on paper now but as bodies through space. "If south goes loud, I cut the first man at the bend and push the rest back toward the low wall. If there are more than three, somebody else has to make sure nobody on the floor mistakes me for part of the attack."

"I can do that," Damian said. "If Voss still has half a conscience left, that's where I use it against him."

Loid added, "And if he doesn't."

"Then I use the other half of the room."

The answer sat there, ugly and practical.

They checked the plan again from the top. Not because it had become elegant. Because tomorrow was going to happen whether it was elegant or not.

Loid built the relay timing from the opening applause and the first probable panic lane. Yor walked the junction route twice in the kitchen, silent and exact, once in socks and once after disappearing into the hall long enough to come back in the shoes she would not actually wear. Damian marked where Voss's aides usually clustered and where party photographers liked to stand when they wanted leadership in the frame. Anya said less now. When she did speak, everybody looked at her for the half beat before the sentence and the half beat after, as if they were all quietly admitting the same thing: her intuition had started arriving with teeth.

Once, Loid shifted the relay thirty seconds later and Damian said, "Too late." They stared each other down until Anya heard the overlap in both their heads and said, "First applause, then count twenty, not thirty."

Loid exhaled through his nose. Damian nodded. No one thanked her.

That was almost worse.

Yor caught her rubbing the side of her head and slid the tea closer without comment. The mug had gone lukewarm. Anya drank it anyway.

By eleven the board was crowded but legible. Red around the south perimeter. Black through the east corridor. Blue at West Gallery. `EAST PRESS` underlined once. Fruit magnets holding down the corners as if this were a grocery list and not a map of tomorrow's violence.

Bond had gone back to sleep with his nose wedged against Damian's shoe. He twitched once, chasing something in a dream that did not look as bad as the one he'd already given them.

Loid finally set the marker down.

"If any one thread slips," he said, "the others keep moving. Nobody waits for the perfect version after the first break."

Yor nodded.

Damian nodded.

Anya looked at the board, at the red circle around the south perimeter, at Bond's nose pressed into Damian's trouser cuff, at the line that put her in East Gallery instead of the diplomatic annex. East Gallery meant a separate entrance, better sight lines, and one less ministry checkpoint between her and the room.

Tomorrow she'd take the east press entrance with Damian's badge and put the whole hall under her. If the room turned, she had to hear it before the first shot made the pattern obvious.
