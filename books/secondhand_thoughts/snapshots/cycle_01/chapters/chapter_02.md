# Chapter 2

The car dropped her two blocks from the Foreign Affairs Ministry, which was standard practice — arriving by private car at an Ostanian state dinner drew less attention than arriving by diplomatic vehicle with Westalian plates. Anya paid the driver, stepped out onto the sidewalk, and stood for a moment in the autumn evening.

Berlint at dusk. The streetlights were coming on, warm against a grey sky that had been threatening rain since noon and hadn't delivered. Leaves skittered along the pavement in front of the Ministry's iron gates, and the air smelled like wet cobblestone and the faint diesel sweetness of the government car park beyond. Two blocks back, in the direction she'd come from, a woman was arguing with a child about whether they could get ice cream in October. The woman's thoughts — exasperated, guilty, already capitulating — reached Anya in the brief quiet before the gates.

She walked toward the entrance. Her credentials were in a slim wallet in her clutch: Marta Kramer, junior cultural attaché, Westalian diplomatic mission. The invitation had been arranged through legitimate channels — WISE had embedded her cover deep enough that an actual cultural attaché position existed in the mission's records, complete with a desk she occasionally sat at and a supervisor who thought she was competent but disorganized. The desk had a plant on it. She kept forgetting to water it.

Two SSS officers flanked the Ministry's main entrance. The taller one checked her credentials against a printed guest list while the shorter one watched her face with the specific blankness of someone trained to observe without appearing to. She read the taller one without trying: his thoughts were about his daughter. Math homework. Division. She kept getting the remainders wrong, and he was going to miss dinner again because of this posting, and his wife would be the one to explain it, and he resented that more than the cold.

"Attaché Kramer." He returned her papers. "Welcome. Through the main gallery, second doors on your left."

"Thanks so much."

She stepped through the entrance into the heated foyer, and the noise hit.

Not the audible kind — that was still muffled behind the gallery doors, a low hum of conversation and string music. The other kind. Two hundred minds, give or take, pressing against the limits of her skull like a headache that hadn't decided where to settle. Surface thoughts: fragments, loops, half-formed sentences. The quality of crowd telepathy was less like eavesdropping on individual conversations and more like standing in a train station where every announcement played simultaneously in a different language. You could tune toward one signal, but the background never disappeared. It just became a texture — a constant neural static that she'd been living with since she was old enough to understand that other people didn't hear it.

She adjusted her posture, smoothed the front of her dress — dark blue, appropriate for a junior cultural attaché who wanted to look serious but not ambitious — and walked through the gallery doors into the ballroom.

The Ostanian Foreign Affairs Ministry did state dinners the way it did everything else: heavy curtains, too many chandeliers, and an atmosphere that suggested you should be grateful to be here. The room was long and high-ceilinged, lit in that particular warm gold that made marble floors look like honey. Round tables with white linens. A string quartet near the windows playing something vaguely Baroque that nobody was listening to. Waitstaff circulating with silver trays — champagne, wine, something non-alcoholic in a frosted pitcher that no one was touching. The Ministry's own catering, which meant the food would be technically correct and entirely forgettable.

The heat was welcome after the autumn air outside, but it did something unpleasant to the telepathic noise — warmth and alcohol and proximity made people's thoughts looser, louder, less contained. She could feel the room as a pressure against the back of her skull, a low-grade throb that would become a proper headache within two hours if she didn't manage it. She'd learned management. You couldn't stop the noise, but you could change your relationship to it — let it become ambient, like traffic sounds, filtering for anomalies rather than trying to process every signal. The alternative was drowning, and she'd drowned in crowds before, in her first year at WISE, standing in a Berlint market with her hands over her ears and tears running down her face because she couldn't make it stop. Loid had found her in a doorway. He'd thought she'd had a panic attack. She'd let him think that.

She took a champagne glass from a passing tray and began to work.

Working a room, for Anya, meant running two operations simultaneously in the same body. Track one: social navigation. Move through clusters, introduce yourself, smile, ask questions about cultural programming and bilateral arts funding that were boring enough to be invisible but specific enough to seem informed. Track two: listening. Not to what people said. To what they thought.

She approached a circle of mid-level officials near the bar, and the deputy minister who'd met her at a reception two weeks ago — Werfel, a soft-faced man with kind eyes and a permanent crease of worry between his brows — recognized her and turned.

"Attaché Kramer, isn't it? Good to see you again."

"Deputy Minister Werfel. How's your daughter? The cello recital was this week, wasn't it?"

His face opened. He was pleased. His thoughts confirmed it — *she remembered, that's — Elena would love that someone asked* — and for a moment he was just a father at a work event, happy that a near-stranger had bothered to recall a detail about his child.

"She was wonderful. Well, she missed a note in the Handel, but she covered it beautifully. She's got her mother's composure." He paused, swirling the wine in his glass. "Are you finding the cultural attaché role interesting? I know Berlint's offerings can be... idiosyncratic."

"I'm learning," she said, which was true in ways he couldn't imagine. "The National Gallery exhibition last month was actually fascinating. I didn't expect the Ostanian modernists to be that political."

"Art is always political here," he said, and his thought underneath — *everything is political here, that's the whole problem* — carried a weariness she recognized from dozens of officials she'd read over the past two years. People who served the state because they believed in something and stayed because the alternative felt worse.

She lingered with his group for a few minutes, letting the conversation carry her while she scanned the surrounding minds. Most of what she caught was dinner-party noise:

*—told him the budget was finalized but Schiller's office hasn't signed off and if he thinks I'm going to beg—*

*—she's wearing the same dress as the Trade Minister's wife, someone should tell her, except absolutely nobody is going to be the one to—*

*—pork's undercooked. I can tell from here. The caterer cut corners again—*

*—flight leaves at six, which means the airport by four, which means leaving by three, which means this dinner's already too long and we haven't even sat down—*

She let the noise wash past her without grabbing. The trick — something Loid had taught her before she'd understood she needed it — was not to hunt. You didn't chase a signal through a crowd. You let the crowd talk, and you waited for a signal to distinguish itself. Operational thoughts had a different texture than social ones. Tighter. More controlled. People thinking about dinner plans or marital anxiety radiated outward, undisciplined; people thinking about covert timelines or unauthorized meetings held their thoughts closer, which paradoxically made them louder once you caught the edge, because the effort of containment produced a specific cognitive pressure she'd learned to recognize.

She moved to a new cluster. A woman in a charcoal suit, mid-fifties, standing with her back to the window. Her thoughts ran along institutional lines:

*—the initiative won't survive the next review cycle unless someone from Defense makes a case directly to the Committee. Three weeks. Maybe four. The southern checkpoint has to clear before—*

A waiter refilled the woman's glass and the thought fractured. *Oh. The white, not the — well, fine. Thank you.*

Gone. But Anya had tagged it. "The initiative." A timeline measured in weeks. A checkpoint. She filed the woman's face and position and moved on.

Near the center of the room, a general she recognized from WISE files — Brennan, border infrastructure command — was thinking in numbers. Procurement figures. Unit costs for something she couldn't quite identify, except the numbers were large and the anxiety attached to them was the kind that came from moving money through channels that wouldn't survive scrutiny. He was thinking about arms shipments in aggregate — quantities, routing, a southern depot that needed to be ready before a date he kept circling back to — and also, simultaneously, about his sciatica and whether the hotel bed tonight would be any better than last week's.

*—four hundred thousand, plus the logistics surcharge, which Müller is going to flag unless I can route it through the cultural liaison budget where nobody looks—*

She caught that and let it go. Not the operative. A person connected to something, maybe. But not the center.

The headache was settling in behind her left eye. She shifted her champagne glass to the other hand and pressed her thumb briefly against her temple — a gesture that might have looked like tiredness to anyone watching, which it was. Sustained scanning in a room this size was like reading a book printed in six languages simultaneously: you could do it, but the processing cost accumulated, and by the end your brain felt like it had been wrung out. She had maybe another hour of sharp reception before the resolution started to degrade.

An elderly official passed close enough that his thoughts grazed hers — a vivid, specific worry about his grandson's military service placement and whether he could pull strings without it looking like he was pulling strings. She caught the grandson's name, his age, his posting. She hadn't wanted any of it. That was the thing about telepathy that the operational briefings never covered: you didn't get to choose what you learned. You heard what people thought, and people thought about everything, and most of it was none of your business and all of it was in your head now, permanent, alongside every other private truth you'd ever accidentally consumed.

A younger diplomat — male, early thirties, handsome in the way that was useful in his profession — was composing a message to someone he'd rather be with tonight. The message was detailed and intimate and Anya shifted her attention away, studying the string quartet instead. It didn't fully help. You couldn't close your ears to thoughts the way you could close your eyes to a page.

The fragments accumulated across the first hour. "The initiative." Timelines measured in weeks. Arms procurement with numbers too large for routine budgets. A southern checkpoint. Money being routed through back channels. Anxiety about exposure. None of it coming from a single source — multiple people carrying pieces of something larger, the way workers on a construction site each hauled their own materials without seeing the building's shape. The thoughts were second-order: echoes, not origins. People who'd been told about a plan, not the person making it.

She was looking for the person who could see the whole thing.

***

An hour in, and the champagne in her glass had gone flat and warm. She hadn't drunk any of it. The glass was a prop, and props were useful — a woman holding an empty hand at a dinner party looked like she was looking for something, while a woman holding a glass looked like she'd found it.

She'd worked two-thirds of the room. Most of the thoughts she'd caught were institutional white noise: career anxiety, personal resentment, schedule management, the one diplomat still composing his love letter in his head. The operational fragments — the initiative, the timeline, the procurement — were all reflections. People thinking about something they'd heard or been briefed on, not something they were driving. She needed the source, not the echo.

The string quartet shifted to something faster, and a few couples moved toward the cleared space near the windows. Anya repositioned toward the far end of the room, where the crowd thinned near the terrace doors. Fewer people meant less noise and sharper individual signals, though it also meant less cover — you couldn't disappear into a crowd of six.

She paused at a display case of framed photographs documenting forty years of Ostanian cultural policy — handshakes, ribbon cuttings, a prime minister squinting into stage lights — and pretended to study them while she scanned.

An SSS officer by the terrace door was thinking about shift changes. A woman in a green dress was worrying about a spot of sauce on her sleeve. A minister's aide was mentally composing a resignation letter he'd been writing in his head for three years and would never send.

And then she caught the thread.

It was different from the others. Not anxious, not fragmented, not the loose and leaking quality of dinner-party cognition. Someone was thinking with focus — sustained, organized, moving from consideration to consideration with a discipline that most people in a social setting couldn't maintain. Like a musician tuning an instrument while a party played around them. The thoughts had structure: a sequence of names considered and evaluated, each one filed with a note about reliability, risk, timing. A meeting that needed to happen before a specific event, through a specific channel, with a person whose cooperation was uncertain.

She turned slowly, scanning. Telepathy wasn't directional the way hearing was — she couldn't point at a signal like a compass needle. But proximity sharpened reception, and as she moved, the signal either gained or lost resolution, and the difference told her roughly where to go.

Toward the terrace.

She moved through the thinning crowd. A brief detour around a conversation she didn't want to get caught in — the Trade Minister's wife, whose distress about the dress situation had curdled into something genuinely miserable — and she angled toward the French doors.

An official she didn't know stepped into her path. Tall, grey-haired, the alert bearing of someone from military intelligence. "Excuse me — you're with the Westalian mission?"

"Cultural affairs," Anya said, keeping her voice light. "Marta Kramer."

"Colonel Dreher. Retired." He didn't look retired. His thoughts were sharp and appraising: *Westalian. Young. Alone. Interesting.* "Enjoying the evening?"

"Very much. The Ministry always puts on a beautiful event."

"Mm." His thoughts shifted to something about attendance patterns, and she smiled and excused herself before he could decide she was worth a longer conversation.

Through the French doors, the terrace was lit by iron sconces and the reflected glow from the ballroom behind. The autumn cold was immediate — it pressed against her bare arms and the back of her neck. A few guests had stepped out: a couple arguing in low voices near the balustrade, a woman smoking a cigarette with the furtive speed of someone breaking a rule she didn't respect, an older man checking his watch with the expression of a person who'd already decided to leave.

And apart from all of them, near the far railing, a man standing alone.

His posture was formal without being rigid — the learned ease of someone who'd grown up in rooms like this and knew how to occupy them without appearing to try. Dark suit, well-fitted. Dark hair, kept short. His hands rested on the stone railing, not gripping, just resting — a studied casualness that was so practiced it might have actually become natural. She couldn't see his face clearly from this angle, but she didn't need to. The signal was unmistakable. He was the source.

She stepped onto the terrace, moving along the railing to a position about fifteen feet away. Not close enough to seem deliberate. Close enough for clear reception. The cold bit at her arms and she crossed them, which looked like a woman who was chilly and was not an operative taking position.

His thoughts came into focus like a radio dial clicking into station.

*—Griesler won't hold past the end of the month. If he goes to the Committee before I can establish an alternative channel through Kleist, the whole sequence falls apart. I need to talk to the old man's people. Tuesday. Maybe Wednesday if Kleist's office confirms.*

Names she didn't recognize. But the operational grammar was clear — this was someone managing a network, maintaining contacts, running parallel timelines. Not a person who'd heard about the initiative. The person running his own.

*—the Front's meeting on the fourteenth. If I can get attendance confirmed by Thursday, we'll have enough to map the full civilian side. The military connections are already documented through Father's files—*

Father's files.

The man turned slightly, reaching for a glass he'd set on the railing, and she saw his face.

She knew it.

Not from WISE briefing files, though he was in those too — a low-priority line item in the Desmond family monitoring file that listed him as a junior diplomat of moderate potential and uncertain loyalties. She knew the face the way you knew a face you'd seen every day for years and then not at all — changed, older, the soft lines of childhood carved into someone adult, but the same underneath. The jaw was sharper. The mouth more set. The expression of someone carrying something heavier than anyone around him guessed.

Damian Desmond.

Twenty years old. Her age. Son of Donovan Desmond, whose political machine had driven two nations to the brink of war fourteen years ago, whose shadow still shaped everything in this city. She'd known Damian was in Ostania's diplomatic corps — WISE tracked the Desmond family as a matter of routine. But the monitoring file said junior diplomat, factional player, possible asset for the national unity caucus. Not this. Not what she was reading in his head right now.

His thoughts continued, and she listened, standing in the cold, her hands going numb against her own arms. She should have gone back inside. A professional would have tagged him, noted his identity and position, and returned to work the room for corroboration. Instead she stood on the terrace and listened to Damian Desmond think, because his thoughts had the quality of something she recognized — not the content but the shape. A person carrying too much alone. She knew what that felt like. She'd known what it felt like since she was four years old and realized she could hear things nobody else could hear and had nobody to tell.

That wasn't a useful thought. She filed it away with the other ones she didn't examine.

***

He was thinking about the Renewal Front.

The name surfaced in his mind with the familiarity of something he'd been carrying for months — not new information but a constant, the way a person with chronic pain thinks about their body. The Renewal Front: a coalition of military hardliners and civilian nationalists organized around the conviction that the peace with Westalis was a controlled surrender, a slow erosion of everything Ostania had been. They wanted an incident. Something that would make diplomacy untenable. Something that would justify mobilization, force the engine of conflict back to life, finish what the last generation started and this generation was supposed to have buried.

Damian's thoughts laid the Front out the way someone maps a problem they've been living inside alone for too long. Not organized for an audience. No neat categories. Just the constant background processing of a mind that had been tracing a maze in the dark — each new corridor confirmed, the exits still unclear. He thought about funding channels: defense procurement contracts, money routed through legitimate military budgets into operations that had nothing to do with defense. He thought about personnel: officers attending unauthorized meetings, civilians whose businesses existed to clean political violence into economic activity. He thought about the person at the center, whose name he knew but whom Anya hadn't caught yet — someone he associated with grief that had hardened into conviction, loss that had calcified into ideology.

There was more. Specific worries, cycling. A contact in the military — Griesler, the name she'd caught earlier — was getting nervous and might pull out. A retired aide from his father's era who had information about the Front's civilian funding but was afraid to share it. A meeting at a policy forum next week where a Renewal Front operative would be present and Damian planned to observe without being observed, which he knew was riskier than it sounded because his last name made him visible in any political room. And underneath all of this, a thought he kept returning to without finishing, the way you keep touching a bruise: *If they find out what I'm doing before I'm ready —* The sentence never completed. It didn't need to. The consequences were clear enough in the way his thoughts tightened around the possibility and then deliberately released it, like someone practicing not flinching.

And he was trying to take it apart.

She stood on the terrace and felt the understanding arrive the way weather arrives — not all at once but as a change in pressure, a shift in what the air could hold.

His father's network. The intelligence contacts, the retired operatives, the institutional relationships that Donovan Desmond had built over decades of political maneuvering — Damian was using all of it. Not continuing the work. Inverting it. The same relationships that had constructed Ostanian nationalism's infrastructure were being repurposed to map and dismantle the people who wanted to finish what Donovan started. He'd been identifying Renewal Front members, documenting connections, tracing funding. Running a covert counter-operation against extremists inside his own government, using the only tools available to a twenty-year-old diplomat with a famous last name and no institutional backing: his father's leftovers and his own position in the Ministry.

Anya read this and understood, with the particular clarity that came from hearing someone's unfiltered truth, that Sylvia's briefing had been right about the unauthorized operation and wrong about everything else. The activity pattern wasn't pre-conflict positioning. It was pre-conflict prevention. One person, working alone, trying to stop a war before it started.

He was doing what his father never would have.

She should have felt relief. The mysterious operative wasn't a threat — he was an ally she didn't know she had, and his intelligence on the Renewal Front was exactly what WISE needed.

She didn't feel relief. What she felt was closer to the sensation of missing a step on a staircase — the lurch, the recalibration, the ground suddenly a different distance than your body expected. She felt the specific vertigo of realizing that every assumption she'd built this evening on had just inverted, and that what she'd learned — all of it, the Front's structure, the counter-operation, the names and timelines — she'd acquired through an ability she could never disclose. She couldn't walk into Sylvia's office tomorrow and say: *I read his mind and he's on our side.* She'd have to construct a version of tonight that made sense without telepathy. Observation. Social engineering. Pattern analysis. A plausible intelligence pathway built on methods that would survive institutional scrutiny.

She'd have to lie.

But that was the next problem. That was tomorrow. Right now she was standing on a cold terrace reading Damian Desmond's unguarded mind, and his thoughts were shifting.

It happened the way memories happen — not as narrative, not as story, but as sensory association. Something about the evening — the cooling air, the faint sound of the quartet through the glass doors, some neural pathway she couldn't trace and he probably couldn't either — pulled him away from operational thinking and into something older.

*Eden.*

The word surfaced like a coin turning over in shallow water. Eden Academy. He didn't think the full name. Just the shape of it — the way a place you'd lived in for years compressed into a single image. For him it was the courtyard. The specific green of the grass in spring. The bell that always rang two seconds late.

*The girl with the pink hair.*

Anya's fingers pressed harder against her own arms where they were crossed.

His memory wasn't a story he was telling himself. It was fragments, arriving in the order that association dictated. A color first — her hair, that specific pink that nobody else had. Not dyed. Just hers. He'd thought it was strange, and then he'd stopped thinking it was strange, and then he'd stopped thinking about it at all because it was just her, just what she looked like. A sound next — laughing. She was always laughing, or about to laugh, or still recovering from the last one. She'd hit him during a game — dodgeball, he was almost sure — and his nose had bled, and he'd been furious, standing there with blood on his shirt and his pride hurting worse than his face, and she'd come back with a wet paper towel from the bathroom and she'd looked sorry. Genuinely sorry. But also a little like she was trying not to smile, because the hit had been an accident but it had also been, objectively, impressive, and she knew it, and he'd known she knew it.

She tilted her head. In the memory, the girl tilted her head the way she always did — to the left, chin down, deciding whether to say the honest thing or the careful thing. And her name was—

*An—*

The terrace door opened behind him. Warm air and noise flooded out. A man in a grey suit leaned through the doorway.

"Desmond, the deputy minister's asking for you."

Damian straightened. The memory dissolved — not gradually, but like a hand wiping condensation off glass. His face composed itself. The shift from private to public was so fast that if Anya hadn't been reading his thoughts she'd have missed it entirely. One moment he was a boy remembering a girl from a schoolyard; the next he was a diplomat being summoned.

"Of course. Tell him I'll be right there."

He picked up his glass and turned. His stride was measured, unhurried. As he passed within six feet of Anya, his thoughts were already reorganizing around the deputy minister's known positions on border-security appropriations — a series of arguments mentally rehearsed, concessions calculated, the smooth machinery of a mind trained to operate in political rooms. He glanced at her as he passed. A brief, automatic social acknowledgment — the kind you gave someone sharing a terrace at a party.

*Westalian, probably. Cold without a coat.*

Then he was through the doors and his thoughts folded into the noise of two hundred other minds and she couldn't distinguish his signal anymore.

He hadn't recognized her.

Anya stayed on the terrace. The cold had gotten into her hands and the exposed skin above her neckline, and she didn't move. Inside, the dinner continued under its warm light. The chandeliers. The white tablecloths. The whole careful theater of two nations performing cordiality while their intelligence services worked the margins. Somewhere in there, Damian Desmond was shaking the deputy minister's hand, wearing the diplomatic composure that was both a performance and a genuine part of who he was, carrying a counter-operation that nobody in that room knew about.

Nobody except her.

She knew his operation. His network. His timeline. His fear that the Front would move before he was ready to expose them. She knew names he hadn't shared with a single other person. She knew the structure of a conspiracy that WISE had barely begun to sketch. She knew all of this because she had stood fifteen feet away and entered his mind without his knowledge or consent, the same way she'd entered every mind she'd ever been near, since she was small, since before she understood what she was doing or what it cost anyone.

And she knew he'd almost remembered her.

Pink hair. A punch during a game. A wet paper towel. A name that started to form and dissolved when someone opened a door.

He'd been carrying that memory for fourteen years. Not prominently — it lived in the place where old, unresolved things settle, surfacing when something triggered it and submerging when the present took over. But it was there. She had mattered to him in some way he'd never examined and never quite released, and she was standing right here on this terrace, with a different name and a different life and the same face he'd looked at without placing.

The distance between what he almost knew and what she completely knew was going to shape everything that came next. She was certain of this the way she was certain of the cold against her skin — as a fact, physical and present and not going anywhere.

She was going to have to go back inside. She was going to have to file a report. She was going to have to sit across from Sylvia and construct a version of tonight that would satisfy WISE — careful, plausible, stripped of telepathy and childhood memory and the ache of hearing someone almost say your name.

She set the warm champagne on the stone railing. The glass made a small sound in the quiet. Her breath was visible. Below, the Ministry gardens were dark shapes — hedgerows, gravel paths, the outline of a fountain that wasn't running. The remaining leaves on the trees moved in a wind she could feel on her arms.

Then she smoothed her dress, uncrossed her arms, and walked back into the warm noise of the party.
