# Chapter 6

The stairwell smelled like garlic and something that had crossed over. Sugar, butter, and heat pushed past the point of partnership into open hostility — a scorched sweetness that stuck to the walls and would probably still be there tomorrow. Her mother was cooking.

Anya let herself in with the spare key she'd carried since she was nine. The apartment hit her the way it always did: warm and cluttered and smelling faintly of Bond's fur beneath whatever Yor had done to the kitchen. Loid's shoes were lined up by the door, toes to the wall, precisely parallel. Yor's were kicked off at different angles, one on its side. Bond was asleep on the couch with his head hanging off the armrest, his white fur covering the cushions in a thin permanent layer that no amount of vacuuming had ever fully addressed.

"Anya!" Yor appeared from the kitchen doorway, flushed and holding a wooden spoon like a weapon she'd forgotten to put down. "You're early! I was going to — the stew isn't quite — well, it's almost — it's getting there, I think."

*She used too much heat again. The bottom layer is definitely burned. But the top might be — no. The top is also burned. Oh no.*

"Smells great, Mama."

"Does it? Really?" Yor's face brightened with a hope that made Anya's chest hurt. "Loid said it smelled 'interesting,' which I think means—"

"It means it smells interesting," Loid said from inside the kitchen, where he was wiping down the counter with the unhurried efficiency of a man who'd already completed his own preparations and was now managing the aftermath of someone else's. He came out drying his hands on a dish towel. "Welcome home."

He hugged her. Brief and warm, his arms tightening once before letting go. His thoughts were louder than the embrace: *She's thinner. The circles under her eyes are worse than last month. She's not sleeping enough — is she running double shifts or is this something else? I should ask. I shouldn't ask. She'll tell me if it's operational. She won't tell me if it's personal. She never does.*

Anya pulled back and smiled at him. "I'm fine, Papa."

"I didn't say anything."

"You were about to."

He gave her the look — the one that was half-amused and half-something harder, the spy's assessment running beneath the father's warmth. It lasted less than a second. Then it softened and he was just her father again, and he said, "There's rice in the cooker. And I made a small thing with the leftover chicken, in case you want options."

"The stew is fine!" Yor called from the kitchen. There was a clatter, then a smaller clatter, then a pause. "Sorry! Fine. Everything's fine."

Anya dropped her bag by the door and crossed to the couch. Bond opened one eye, registered her, and closed it again. His tail moved once against the cushion — a single thump of acknowledgment that constituted his entire greeting now that his joints were what they were. She scratched behind his ears and felt his thoughts drift through her like warm water: *girl. home. floor cold. sleep.* The simplicity of a dog's interior had always been a relief. No layers. No second meanings. Just the fact of the moment and the body that inhabited it.

She sat on the arm of the couch and let the apartment settle around her. The television was on low — a news broadcast about ministerial trade talks that nobody was watching. Loid's reading glasses sat folded on a stack of newspapers he'd already annotated with small pencil marks in the margins. They looked like idle habit. They were an indexing system he maintained by reflex, cross-referencing sources and flagging discrepancies, and Anya had known this since she was seven and had read the system in his thoughts while he taught her long division. Yor's cardigan was draped over a chair. Two of Bond's rubber toys were lined up near the bookshelf in a row too orderly for a dog to have arranged — Loid's hand, tidying without noticing.

Loid settled into his chair and picked up the newspaper. His thoughts moved beneath the newsprint like current under ice. *The border patrol deployments are increasing again. Sylvia mentioned adjusted threat levels for the southeastern corridor last week. If Anya's sector is affected — no. She'd tell me. Or she wouldn't. She's twenty. She has her own operational judgment and I have to trust it even when I don't.*

The worry wasn't new. He carried it like he carried everything — efficiently, without display, and with the specific self-awareness of a man who knew that his protectiveness was partly surveillance and couldn't make himself stop. Anya had been reading this particular strain of his thoughts for years. It never got easier.

"The news said something about the trade negotiations stalling," she said, because it was the kind of thing a daughter would say.

"They'll restart. They always do." He turned a page. *She's asking about geopolitics on a family visit. That's either casual or it's not.*

Dinner was the stew. Yor served it with a hopefulness that bordered on prayer, ladling it into bowls with extra care as if gentleness could retroactively improve the cooking. Anya ate it. The stew was thick and combative and tasted like three different recipes had argued over the same pot and nobody had won. But her mother had spent the afternoon on it, and love was the only ingredient that had survived the heat, and the backup rice was sitting discreetly on the stove for anyone who needed rescue.

"Mrs. Kaplan complained about the pipes again," Yor said, blowing on her spoon. "She said the water pressure on the fourth floor is — what was her word — 'insulting.' She said the water pressure was insulting to her personally."

"It was low last week," Loid said. "I adjusted the valve in the basement."

"She didn't seem to know that. She was telling Bond about it in the hallway for about ten minutes. I think she believes he's the building superintendent."

"Bond was in the hallway?"

"He got out when I was bringing in the groceries. He just lay down in front of her door and she started talking. I didn't want to interrupt."

Anya laughed. It came out real and easy and for a moment she wasn't carrying anything. The sound of it filled the kitchen — too loud, probably, for a joke about water pressure and an old dog, but she hadn't laughed in days and the release was involuntary.

*She should laugh more. When did she stop laughing like that? She used to — stop. She's here. Don't catalog what's wrong. She's here.*

Loid's thought pressed against her and she looked at her plate.

"How's work, Mama? Besides the pipes."

"Oh, fine, fine." Yor waved her spoon. "Busy, but — you know. They reorganized the whole records system at city hall, which was, um. An experience. I don't think Mr. Henning has recovered. He kept trying to file things in the old cabinets and they'd already been removed, so he was just standing in front of an empty wall holding folders. I felt terrible for him."

"Did you help?"

"I helped him find a chair and made tea. That seemed like the right amount."

Yor smiled, and the smile was real, and then beneath it — not deep, just passing through — the way a cloud passes across the sun and is gone before you can look up:

*— the man on his knees. Begging. His hands weren't like the briefing said. The briefing said armed, dangerous, but his hands were empty and he was saying please, I have a — and the layout was wrong too, the door was on the wall where they'd said the window would be, the whole floor plan was*

Yor picked up her spoon. "Anyway. The new system is fine. Just takes getting used to."

She was smiling again. The thought was gone, pushed back down to wherever Yor kept the things she couldn't hold and couldn't put down. She'd buried it the way she handled every dark thing — by being present and kind and not looking at the shape of what she'd just remembered.

Anya's hand had stopped over her bowl. The stew had gone cold on the spoon she was holding midair. She lowered it. She had not reached for that. She had not pressed, had not focused, had not done any of the things she did when she was working. Her mother's private horror had simply been there, close to the surface for a second, and Anya had caught it the way she caught everything — involuntarily, completely, without consent from either of them.

A man on his knees. Hands that didn't match the file. A floor plan that was wrong. Her mother was beginning to wonder whether the people the Garden told her to kill were who they said they were. It was a new thought — raw and small and unfinished — and Yor wasn't ready to follow it yet. She was tucking it away the same way she tucked away every contradiction in her life: by choosing the warm thing over the true thing and hoping the warm thing would hold.

Anya knew all of this because she had stolen it.

She reached for the bread. She could not use this knowledge. She could not mention it, reference it, act on it. She possessed operational intelligence about a covert assassination organization, pulled from her mother's unguarded mind while her mother hummed and served stew. The only decent thing she could do was pretend she'd never heard it.

"This bread's good," she said.

"The place on Kirchner Street," Loid said. "The woman with the — what do you call it — firm customer service approach."

"She's mean," Yor said.

"She's efficient."

"She's efficiently mean. She told me I was buying too much rye."

"Were you?"

"That's not the point, Loid."

Bond woke up.

Not the slow surfacing of an old dog working his way out of sleep — the groaning, leg-cycling, dream-chasing kind where his nose twitched at phantom smells and gravity gradually hauled him back to the couch. This was different. His head came up rigid, eyes open and locked on nothing, ears pressed flat against his skull. His whole body went taut, the loose-limbed mass of him transformed into something that looked like the animal he'd been before the grey muzzle and the stiff joints and the years.

"Bond?" Yor half-rose from her chair.

Anya was already in it. Bond's mind had cracked open like a door kicked in and the images were flooding through — not thoughts, not words, but raw visual data pouring in faster than she could organize it.

A building. She saw it through Bond's processing — a wide stone facade with columns, not decorative but structural, the heavy pale stone of old government architecture. Wide ceremonial steps leading up to the entrance. She tried to hold the image but Bond's attention was already swinging — the same building from a different angle, a terrace extending from the upper level with an iron railing, a balustrade of carved stone overlooking landscaped grounds that were wrong, torn up, the grass and ornamental trees displaced. And then people — uniformed figures, four or five of them, wearing the stiff grey-blue formal dress of Ostanian state security. They were moving fast. One was running. And behind them, behind the terrace, a blooming brightness — not daylight but fire, the specific aggressive orange that meant accelerant, that meant deliberate, rising from below the terrace and cracking the balustrade stone. The heat of it. The feeling of wrongness, which was Bond's way of processing what his precognition couldn't put into words — not a prediction but a certainty, held in the body, that this was coming and it was bad.

Bond whined. The image shredded. Another came, briefer — the building's entrance from ground level, people streaming out through the columned portico, and a concussive vibration Bond couldn't hear but felt as pressure, as disturbance, as the air being wrong.

Then it collapsed. Bond blinked twice, confused by his own alertness, and his head dropped back down to the couch arm. His tail gave one uncertain wag. He sighed.

The whole episode had lasted maybe eight seconds.

"Just a dream," Loid said, resettling in his chair. He was watching Bond with the casual attentiveness he gave to everything in his vicinity — the attention that looked like ease and was surveillance. *Precognitive episode. He hasn't had one that intense in months. Usually the signals are vague — shapes, feelings. This looked specific. His eyes were tracking something. What did he see?*

Anya was gripping the edge of the couch cushion. The images were already losing resolution in her memory the way Bond's visions always did — the specific details softening into impressions, the architectural features blurring. But she'd seen enough to hold the shape of it. A government building with a columned stone facade. A formal terrace with iron railings and carved balustrades. Men in state security dress. Fire that was not an accident. An attack on a government venue, clear enough to be actionable, and Bond's visions, when they arrived this sharp, were not wrong.

On the television, the trade negotiation coverage had shifted to an anchor summarizing upcoming diplomatic events. Anya watched the ticker scroll. She couldn't read it from the couch.

She could tell Loid. She could say: Bond just showed me a precognitive vision of a government building being attacked, with specific architectural details — a columned entrance, a terrace where fire was set deliberately — and men in SSS formal dress running.

She could say all of that, if she wanted to explain how she'd read a dog's mind. If she wanted to watch her father's face change as he absorbed the fact that his daughter had been a telepath since she was four years old and had sat at this table a thousand times and never said a word.

She could also say nothing. Hope Bond was wrong for the first time. Go back to the bread and the stew and pretend the evening was still intact.

She did neither.

"Papa." The casualness in her voice was something she'd learned from him — the flat register that delivered information without signaling urgency, the spy's trick of making an important thing sound incidental. "I heard something at work. Probably nothing, but — there's been some chatter about security concerns at government venues lately. Some kind of credible threat assessment floating around. Have you heard anything?"

Loid looked at her over the top of his newspaper.

For one second she was his daughter asking a normal question. Then something behind his eyes recalibrated and she felt the shift in his thoughts like a temperature change.

*Interesting. She led with a disclaimer — 'probably nothing' — which is a softener an amateur uses to manage the listener's reaction. She wouldn't need to manage me unless the information matters more than she's presenting. 'Chatter at work' — from which section? She didn't say. 'Credible threat assessment floating around' — that's specific language for something she claims not to have paid attention to. Either this is lazy sourcing or she's being deliberately vague about where she actually learned it.*

He set the newspaper down. "I haven't heard anything specific. Which venues are we talking about?"

"I don't know the details. Just a general thing about security posture at government events. Heightened levels or — I don't know. I wasn't really listening at the time." She was adding words now, each one a small mistake, and she could hear herself doing it — the overcorrection of someone who'd underplayed the setup and was trying to recover by being more casual, which only made the initial delivery more conspicuous. She picked up her spoon.

*She's not lying about the threat itself — she wouldn't fabricate a security concern to fill conversation. But the source. The source isn't work chatter. She learned this somewhere else, or in a way she can't account for. Her delivery shifted when I asked which venues. She doesn't want to be more specific because specificity would reveal the origin. File this.*

"I'll mention it to a few people," Loid said. "See if anything matches."

He let it go. He picked up his newspaper and turned a page and his posture eased back into the shape of a man reading the evening edition. But Anya caught the thought he had as he settled, clean and filed away like a document placed in a drawer he knew he'd open again later: *Something is off about her sourcing and she knows I caught it. Revisit.*

He would not forget. Loid Forger — Twilight — did not forget things he'd flagged, and he did not lose track of discrepancies, and the specific discrepancy he'd just identified was his daughter's credibility on one very small point, and the erosion of trust happened in exactly this way: not a collapse but a sequence of small wrong notes that accumulated until the melody was unrecognizable.

Anya ate a spoonful of stew. It had gone cold and it tasted like nothing.

Yor was telling a story about a colleague at city hall whose birthday cake had been delivered with the wrong name — "They spelled it 'Hendrick' and his name is 'Heinrich' and he got so upset he took the cake home before anyone could have a piece, which I thought was, um, a strong reaction? But also I understand, because your name is important." She was laughing as she told it, and Loid was half-smiling behind his newspaper, and Bond was asleep again, dreaming about the yard behind the building where pigeons gathered in the afternoon, and the evening had the shape of ordinary warmth and the weight of everything Anya had just done wrong sitting inside it where nobody could see.

---

The phone rang at half past eight. Not the kitchen landline but the other one — the phone in the drawer by the hallway bookshelf, the one with the specific ringtone that Anya's family politely pretended was a normal household phone and never discussed.

"Excuse me," Anya said, and stepped into the hallway. Through the doorway she could still see the kitchen: Yor washing dishes, humming something off-key, the back of her head moving slightly with the tune. Loid was drying a pot. He did not look up as she left, but his thought reached her clearly: *That phone again. She gets calls on it more than she used to. The frequency has increased since her sector assignment changed.*

The line connected and Sylvia's voice was there — flat, unadorned, the voice of someone who had spent thirty years delivering information on secure lines and had long ago removed anything from her speech that did not serve the transmission.

"This is a routine follow-up on your assessment of the Ostanian operative in the diplomatic corps sector."

"Sure." Anya leaned against the hallway wall. She could hear water running in the kitchen. Yor saying something to Loid about the dish rack being full again.

"Another agency — I will not specify which — has flagged activity in the same sector you assessed in your initial report. They are identifying communication and movement patterns consistent with a coordinated operation. Their analysis does not match your conclusions."

The stew, which had already been sitting uneasily, felt like it was reconsidering its relationship with her stomach entirely.

"What kind of patterns?"

"Movement intercepts, financial flows, communication timing. Their analytical team is reading the sector as high-activity and potentially high-threat. Your assessment characterized it as mid-level factional maneuvering with limited strategic significance."

"That was my read," Anya said. Through the doorway she watched her mother hold a plate up to the light, checking for spots, turning it carefully. The thoroughness Yor gave to a white dinner plate.

"I am not questioning your read." The absence of accusation in Sylvia's voice was its own pressure — a negative space shaped like doubt, bordered by professionalism. "I am informing you that the analytical picture is developing in a direction that your assessment did not anticipate. If your evaluation has evolved since filing, now is the appropriate time to update it."

"It hasn't changed. The sector is factional noise. Internal Ostanian positioning. It looks coordinated because factions coordinate — that's what they do."

Silence. Anya could picture Sylvia at her desk: reading glasses on the chain, coffee long cold, the fluorescent overheads of WISE station casting their flat institutional light over everything. She couldn't read thoughts through a phone line. Telepathy needed proximity, needed bodies in the same room, and the absence of access made Sylvia's pauses enormous — empty spaces Anya usually filled with stolen information and now could only sit inside.

"Noted." The word was precise and carried no particular warmth or chill. "I will include your reaffirmation in the file. If your assessment changes, contact me directly. Not through the regular reporting chain."

"Understood."

"How is your family?"

The question arrived without transition, the way Sylvia asked personal questions — as if they were simply the next item on the same operational checklist.

"They're good. My mom made stew."

"Edible?"

"Interesting."

Something that might have been amusement, or might have been Sylvia exhaling. "Good night, Anya."

The line went dead.

Anya stood in the hallway with the phone in her hand. Another agency had flagged the sector she'd deliberately downgraded. The analytical machinery of allied intelligence was doing what it was built to do — cross-referencing, comparing, catching the places where one analyst's picture didn't match another's. Her lie was inside that machinery now, a misaligned gear, and the system was starting to grind around it. Sylvia wasn't suspicious yet. Her tone had been procedural, not interrogative. But the gap between Anya's assessment and the other agency's findings would produce questions, and questions would generate reviews, and reviews would open the original report, and the original report contained a lie that Anya had told while reading the trust in Sylvia's thoughts and knowing exactly how much that trust was worth.

She put the phone back in the drawer and returned to the kitchen. Yor had finished the dishes and was wiping down the counter. Loid was reading again, or performing reading — which was, for him, functionally the same thing.

"Everything okay?" he asked without looking up.

"Fine. Work stuff."

*Work stuff. On that particular phone. At eight-thirty on a family visit. She's carrying something. Multiple things — I can see it in her posture, the way she's standing in the doorway instead of sitting down. I did the same thing at her age. I was older before it started eating me.*

"I'm going to lie down for a bit," Anya said. "Long week."

"Of course." Yor appeared, as Yor always appeared, with a thing she hadn't been asked for — a cup of tea, steaming, placed in Anya's hands with the gentle certainty of someone whose love expressed itself in warm drinks and unanswerable kindness. "Chamomile. For sleeping. If you want it. You don't have to drink it. I just thought—"

"Thanks, Mama."

She took the cup and walked down the hall to her old bedroom.

The room was smaller than she remembered, which meant she was bigger, which meant time had passed in the specific way it does when you notice a childhood space can no longer contain you. The bed was the same narrow frame from when she was six, pushed against the wall with a headboard she'd once covered in glow-in-the-dark stars. Most had fallen off over the years. Two remained, clinging to the wood grain near the top, and in the dark they still glowed their faint greenish light — a fact she verified every time she slept here, the way you check on a thing you love to make sure it's still there. Her desk was against the window. The drawers held pencil stubs, a protractor, a notebook from her last year at Eden Academy with notes in handwriting that looked like a stranger's because it was a child's.

Her headband was on the shelf. The old one — black, with the horn-shaped decorations she'd worn every day until she was fourteen and decided they were too much. She picked it up. The plastic was scuffed and slightly bent from years of daily use. She put it on. It sat differently now — not tighter, exactly, but positioned on a head that had done things the girl who'd worn it couldn't have imagined.

She sat on the bed with her tea going cold on the desk and the headband pressing against her temples and the weight of the evening settling over her like a second blanket.

Three secrets. She counted them the way you count bruises after a fall — touching each one to find the edges.

The first: her family. She had been reading her father's thoughts and her mother's thoughts and her dog's visions since before she could write her own name, and none of them had ever been asked. Tonight she had caught her father's worry about her safety, her mother's buried doubt about the Garden's honesty, and her dog's premonition of violence at a government building, and she had carried all three pieces of intelligence out of their private minds and into her own, where they sat alongside everything else she'd taken without permission over the course of twenty years.

The second: Damian. She had lied to WISE about him. Twice now — once in the debriefing room with Sylvia, once on the phone ten minutes ago. Another agency's analysis was pressing against her false assessment, and the institutional machinery was starting to notice the discrepancy, and Sylvia still trusted her, which was the specific part that made Anya's stomach clench around the stew she wished she hadn't eaten.

The third: the Garden. She knew something about her mother's employer that her mother herself was barely ready to think — that the targets might not be clean, that the briefings might not be truthful, that twenty years of violence might not have served what Yor needed it to serve. And she could not share this with anyone. Not Loid, who would act on it. Not Yor, who wasn't ready. Not WISE, who would want to know how she'd obtained intelligence about a covert assassination organization. The knowledge sat in her like something swallowed wrong — too big to digest, impossible to bring back up.

Through the wall, she heard her parents' voices. Low, murmured, the frequency of a conversation between people who had been together long enough that half the words were implied and the other half were warmth. She couldn't make out specifics at this distance. She didn't reach for them. She had taken enough from this apartment tonight.

She pulled off the headband and set it back on the shelf, next to a small ceramic cat she'd won at a school fair when she was eight. Her tea had gone lukewarm and bitter from oversteeping. She drank it anyway. It tasted like her mother's care and her own neglect, which felt like the right combination for the evening.

From the living room, faintly, Bond's dreaming thoughts reached her through the walls. Not the vision this time — just the ordinary drift of an old dog in a warm room. *Soft. Floor. Girl in the back room. Good. Sleep.*

The girl in the back room set down her tea and pulled the covers to her chest and lay looking at the two remaining glow-in-the-dark stars on the headboard. They glowed. They had always glowed. She had been looking at them since before she could read minds, which was as close to before as she could remember, and they still glowed, and she did not sleep for a long time.
