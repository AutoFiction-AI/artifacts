# Chapter 16

She stood outside his door for four minutes before she knocked.

Four minutes was long enough to read three of his neighbors through the walls — a woman on the floor below deciding whether to return her sister's call, a man two doors down drafting a complaint about the hallway lights in his head, getting angrier with each revision. Normal people with normal-sized interiors. Anya pressed her palm flat against Damian's door and felt the wood under her hand and, underneath, the hum of his thoughts. He was sitting. Not moving much. Thinking about — she pulled her hand back. Not yet. She was going to do this one thing without cheating first.

She knocked.

The pause before the door opened was precise. Not the pause of someone who hadn't expected a visitor — more the pause of someone deciding whether to answer. When he did, he was wearing a sweater she hadn't seen before, dark, rolled at the sleeves. His split eyebrow from the ambush had healed to a thin pink line above his left eye. He looked tired in a way that was specific to him: the tiredness of someone who had been thinking too hard, not someone who hadn't slept enough.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

*She's here. Okay. She's here.*

The thought was his, arriving a beat before he stepped aside to let her in. Simple recognition. Not hostile, not warm. The mental equivalent of bracing for something.

Damian's apartment was the kind of neat that came from habit rather than effort. Books shelved with their spines aligned. A single glass rinsed and drying on a cloth beside the sink. The couch had the permanent indent of someone who sat in the same spot every evening. She'd been here once before, briefly, during the planning phase — she'd catalogued it with operational attention then, exits and sight lines and signal quality. Now she looked at it the way you looked at the place where a person actually lived. A framed certificate from the Foreign Ministry on the wall near the kitchen. A stack of policy journals on the side table with a pen clipped to the top one. One of his shoes was under the couch and the other was by the door, as if he'd kicked them off in two different directions.

The box of childhood things was on the coffee table. Open.

A pair of old school shoes sat on top, too small for the person who'd kept them. Below them, its edges visible, the class photo from Eden Academy.

Anya's hands went still at her sides. Not at the photo itself — she'd known about it since the gala, when she'd read the recognition crystallizing in Damian's thoughts around a second-row girl with pink hair and an off-camera grin. What stopped her was that the box was open, which meant he'd been looking through it before she arrived. He'd been in there, in the past, in the version of them that existed before any of this.

He didn't offer her a drink. She didn't sit down.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

*Yeah. I figured.*

His surface thought was dry, almost resigned. Underneath, less organized: a scramble of possibilities — she was ending the alliance, she had new intelligence about the summit fallout, she was leaving Berlint. None of them were close.

"Okay," he said. He leaned against the kitchen counter and crossed his arms. The posture was defensive without meaning to be. She read his intention — trying to look casual, giving her the floor — and the gap between the intention and the tight set of his shoulders.

"It's not about the operation," she said. "It's about me. About how I — what I can do."

She had rehearsed this. In the bathroom mirror that morning, standing in her underwear with toothpaste on her lip. On the walk across the city, mouthing words into her scarf. Standing outside his door for four minutes while the neighbor composed his angry letter and the woman downstairs thought about her sister. Every version she'd practiced sounded wrong. The clinical one: *I possess a telepathic ability that allows me to read surface-level thoughts within a limited range.* The dramatic one: *I've been hearing your thoughts since the night we met.* The small one: *I can kind of... hear people.*

What came out was none of them.

"I'm a telepath," she said. "I can read minds. I've been able to since I was a kid — since I was little, four or five, I don't know exactly when it started. I've never told anyone. Not my parents, not Becky, not WISE. Nobody." She paused. The pause felt enormous. "That's — yeah. That's the thing."

The silence that followed had a texture she could feel against her skin. His thoughts slammed to a halt the way a crowded room goes quiet when something breaks — not gradually but all at once, every thread of mental activity suspending.

*What.*

Then, slower, as if he were reading the words off a screen: *She said she's a telepath. She said she can read minds.*

He was processing it the way he processed diplomatic shocks — lining the information up against what he already knew, testing it for coherence, looking for the structural logic. She watched the sequence unfold inside his head like watching someone sort a deck of cards face-up. The state dinner. The Renewal Front operative she'd intercepted before he could approach Damian. The alliance negotiations where she'd addressed his objections before he voiced them. The safe house, when he'd asked how she'd known about the ambush and she'd said *I'm good at reading people.* Colonel Bretz's name emerging as if by luck. Every impossible thing she'd known, every too-perfect read, every conversation where she'd been precisely one step ahead of the spoken word.

*She read my thoughts. She's been reading my thoughts this entire time.*

"At the state dinner," he said. His voice was careful, each word placed. "That first night. You were reading my—"

"Yes."

"Every thought I had in that room. About the operation, about—" He stopped. She could feel the next part forming in his mind — *about you, about how you smiled, about Eden Academy and a girl with pink hair* — and the moment he realized that she was hearing him think it right now.

"All of it," Anya said. She was doing it again — responding to the thought rather than the words, answering the thing he hadn't said — and she watched his face change as the demonstration landed harder than the confession had.

"Stop." The word was sharp, louder than his usual register. "Stop reading my — can you stop?"

"I can try not to react to it. I can't turn it off. It's not — it's like hearing. I can't close my ears."

*She can't turn it off. Every thought. The negotiations, the service corridor, the club with Bretz. The park afterward when I was thinking about her laugh. She heard me think about her laugh.*

His arms uncrossed. His hands went to his pockets, came out, went to the back of his neck, came down again. She watched him cycle through the physical vocabulary of a man who didn't know what to do with his body while his mind caught fire. His thoughts were louder now, the disciplined structure cracking under the weight of retroactive exposure, memory after memory surfacing and being recontextualized — not just the operational moments but the personal ones, the private ones, the thoughts he'd had in the late hours that he'd believed were his alone.

*The kiss. Oh god. She read my mind during the kiss. She was inside my head when I — when we were in the safe house and I thought about—*

"Yes," Anya said. Very quietly.

He looked at her and the anger arrived. Not as heat — as a kind of precision. His jaw set. His eyes narrowed. The diplomatic composure she'd watched him maintain through state dinners and policy forums and conversations with people who wanted to destroy him reassembled itself around a new, colder center.

"So every conversation we've had," he said. "Every moment I thought I was trusting you with something. Every time I chose to tell you how I felt or what I thought or what I—" His voice caught. He reset. "You already had it. All of it."

"Yes."

"The safe house. When I told you about my father. About what the Desmond name means to me, about what it's like to carry—"

"I already knew. I'd read it before you said it."

*She watched me decide to be honest. She sat there and watched me struggle to say something real, and she already had everything I was going to say. My honesty was a play she'd already read the script for.*

He turned away from her. Walked two steps toward the window and stopped with his hands on the sill. The evening light caught the scar above his eyebrow — the one she'd earned with him in a side street, fighting people who wanted him dead. She had read the attackers' intended routes and steered him clear. Telepathy had saved his life. Telepathy had also meant she'd been sitting inside his unguarded interior for months while he believed he was choosing what to share.

"My mother," he said. He was speaking toward the glass. His voice had changed — lower, less precise, the formality stripped out of it. "At the reception, when I was thinking about her. You read that."

"You thought about the flowers she grew on the terrace. Before she got sick. The marigolds."

He made a sound. Not a word. Just air leaving him, the specific exhalation of someone who has learned that a private room had a window.

*She knows about the marigolds. She knows the thing I never told anyone because I never said it out loud, I only thought it while she was standing next to me, and I didn't know I was handing it to her.*

He pressed his forehead against the window glass. Outside, Berlint was conducting its evening business — lights switching on across the government district, traffic thinning on the boulevard below, two students walking with their arms linked, a taxi waiting at the curb with its engine running. The city did not know that two people in an apartment above it were taking everything apart.

Anya stood very still and let his anger hit her. She had earned this. Not as penance, not as performance — she had earned it because she had done the thing, the lifelong thing, the thing she had done to every person she'd ever been close to, and the person she'd done it to most had the right to feel the full weight of the violation. She did not look away. She did not flinch. She did not explain or qualify or contextualize.

When he spoke again, his voice had a roughness she'd never heard from him, not even in the basement after Crane died. "How far does it go?"

"Surface thoughts. What you're actively thinking. Not memories unless you're remembering them in the moment. Not deep motivations, not — I can't reach down into you. Just what's on top."

"Just." A short sound that wasn't a laugh. "Just the surface."

*The surface. As if the surface thoughts aren't the real ones. As if the thing you're actively thinking isn't more honest than anything you'd choose to say.*

He was right. Surface thoughts were more honest. That was the entire problem.

"I want to—" Anya started, and caught herself. The instinct was to explain, to contextualize, to say *I didn't choose this ability* or *I've tried to use it responsibly* or any of the justifications she'd assembled over sixteen years of knowing she was violating everyone she met. She bit down on all of it. "Sorry. I'm not going to — you should be angry. You're right to be."

"Don't tell me what I should be."

She took that. Fair.

*She flinched. Good.* A beat. *No. Not good. I don't want — I don't know what I want.*

His thoughts were cycling now, anger layering over something less organized, less safely categorized. Horror at the scope of it — not theoretical horror but the specific, nauseating horror of a person realizing that every private thought, every unguarded impulse, every embarrassing or tender or ugly thing that had crossed their mind in the presence of another person had been received. It was the horror of discovering that a room you believed was locked had a window, and someone had been standing at it for months, watching you when you thought you were alone.

Then, underneath the horror, barely legible, the third thing. The complicated thing.

*She knows everything about me. Everything. The worst things I've thought, the petty things, the things I'd never say out loud. She knows all of it and she's still here. She came here to tell me.*

Anya felt that thought arrive and did not react to it. That was the hardest piece of discipline she'd ever performed — harder than holding position in a crowd of two hundred minds, harder than filing a false report to Sylvia's trusting face. She felt the thought and she kept her expression still and she gave him the space to have it without her responding to it, because he hadn't offered it. It was his. It was private. She was trying, for once, to let a private thought stay private even though she'd heard it.

"I don't know what to do with this," Damian said. He turned from the window. His eyes were red but dry — the redness of sustained internal pressure, not tears. "I genuinely don't know what to do with the information that every private thought I've had in your—"

He stopped. His gaze had dropped to the coffee table. To the box. To the class photo with its edge visible beneath the school shoes.

*Tell her. Tell her now.*

The thought was so clear, so deliberate, that Anya almost didn't recognize it as internal. It had the quality of a decision already made. Then he crossed the room, pulled the photo from the box, and held it up, and everything in the apartment shifted register.

"Second row," he said. "Third from the left. Pink hair. That's you."

Anya looked at the photo. She had been six. Her hair was the exact shade it was now — genetics didn't outgrow that particular pink — and she was grinning at something off-camera, her mouth open, one hand blurred because she'd been waving. Damian, in the front row, was scowling at the photographer with the dignity of a boy who took himself extremely seriously. Between them, twelve other children she hadn't thought about in years.

"I know who you are," he said. "Anya Forger. Eden Academy, Class Three. You punched me when we were six because I called Becky a crybaby. I've known since the gala."

The floor stayed where it was. The walls didn't move. But something inside Anya's processing went silent — a deep, total pause, as if every channel she'd been receiving on for twenty years had gone to static at once. For a span she couldn't measure, she wasn't hearing anyone's thoughts. Not his, not the neighbors', not the city's. Just the roar of her own shock flooding every frequency she had.

"The gala," she repeated. Her voice sounded thin and far away from her. "Becky's gala. You — that was months ago."

"The classmate said your name. I went home that night, found this—" He held up the photo. "Compared. It's you. It's been you since the state dinner, and I've known since November."

*She didn't know. She didn't know I knew. She's been reading my thoughts for months and she missed the biggest thing in my head because it happened when she wasn't in the room.*

His thought broke through her static, and the irony was so precise it felt like a physical object — something with edges, something she could cut herself on. She was a telepath who had missed the most important secret her target was carrying. She had read the recognition building for months, the near-misses and almost-names and schoolyard memories surfacing and submerging. She had watched it crystallize at the gala, felt the pieces lock into place inside his head in real time. But she hadn't known what happened after. The confirmation — the photograph held up to the light of his desk lamp, the comparison, the long still reckoning with what it meant about Eden and the friendship and the kiss and everything — had happened in this apartment, at this coffee table, while she was across the city with no signal to read. The deepest part of his processing had occurred beyond her range.

He had kept a secret from a telepath. Not through discipline or deception. Simply by thinking his most consequential thoughts when she wasn't there.

"You knew," she said. "At my parents' apartment. When you met my dad. When Mama made dinner and Bond fell asleep on your shoes. You knew everything."

"Everything except this." He meant the telepathy. "I knew your name, your school, your family. I didn't know about — this."

"And you didn't say anything."

"No."

"You came to the summit. You walked into an operation with someone who'd been lying to you about her identity from the first second, and you didn't—"

"I made a decision." His voice was steady now, the roughness smoothed into something more deliberate. "The stakes were too high. The operation mattered more than the lie. And—" He stopped. She could feel the sentence heading somewhere more honest than he'd planned, and she watched him decide — visibly, the effort showing in his jaw — not to redirect. "And I didn't want to lose you. Even knowing who you were. Even knowing the identity was fake. I didn't want to burn it."

*That's true. That's the load-bearing part. The operational calculus was real but it was the scaffolding. Underneath it was just — I didn't want to lose her.*

The room held still around them.

"So we both," Anya said. She sat down — not on the couch, on the floor, beside the coffee table, because her legs had made a decision her mind hadn't cleared. The childhood box was at her eye level. The school shoes were scuffed on the left toe, as if their owner had dragged that foot. "We both had a secret about each other. And neither of us knew the other one—"

"Knew. Yeah." He sat too, not on the floor — on the couch, in the permanent indent, the cushion receiving him the way habits do. The distance between them was about four feet. "You read my thoughts without my permission. I knew your real name without telling you. We both operated on hidden information and we both chose to keep going."

"They're not the same," she said. "What I did is worse."

"Maybe."

"It is. You knowing my name — that's recognition. That's just knowing a fact. What I did is — I was inside you. Without your consent. For months."

He didn't argue. She hadn't expected him to. His thoughts moved through the comparison without settling on a verdict, and she let them move.

---

The second hour was quieter.

They had rearranged — Anya on the couch now, one cushion of distance between them, her knees pulled up, her shoes on the floor where she'd slipped them off without thinking about it. Damian still in his indent. He'd gotten water at some point, two glasses, and hers was sweating a ring onto the coffee table next to the open box of childhood things.

The initial charge had dissipated into something less urgent but harder to hold. The anger was still present in his thoughts — she could feel it the way you felt a bruise when you weren't pressing on it, a constant low awareness of damage — but it had been joined by other things. Curiosity. A kind of vertigo at the scale of what he was recalibrating. And underneath, moving slow like water under ice, something that felt close to relief.

*She told me. She actually told me. Nobody made her. She could have kept this for the rest of her life and I'd never have known.*

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"Yeah."

"When you read someone — when you read me — do you get the feeling? Or just the words?"

Anya considered this. The truthful answer was complicated and she was tired of simpler alternatives. "Both. Thoughts come through mostly as words. Or phrases, fragments — sometimes just a word repeating, like when you're stuck on something. But feelings are different. Feelings come through as — it's more like weather? Like if you're angry, it's not that I hear 'I am angry.' It's that the pressure changes. The temperature. And sometimes the feeling gets in." She paused. "Gets confused with mine."

"Confused how?"

This was the part. The real part. The thing she had been circling since before the telepathy disclosure, the thing the telepathy disclosure was the preamble to, the thing she had never said to another person because she had never been close enough to anyone for it to matter in exactly this way.

"I don't always know which feelings are mine," she said. "When I'm around someone — when I'm around you — and you're feeling something, and I feel it too, I can't always tell if I'm feeling it because it's mine or because I absorbed it from you. Like an echo I can't trace back to the original sound."

*She doesn't know if her feelings for me are hers.*

His thought arrived clean and immediate, and it was so precisely the thing she was trying to say that she had to physically stop herself from nodding. The instinct to confirm — to respond to his understanding of her problem as though it were part of the conversation — was exactly the behavior she was describing as the problem.

"At the safe house," she said. "When we kissed. I was reading your thoughts. I was trying not to but I couldn't stop — the signal was too strong, you were feeling too much, too close, and I was feeling it at the same time, and I don't know—" She ran out of sentence. Started a new one. "I don't know if I kissed you because I wanted to or because I was feeling what you wanted and it got inside me and I couldn't tell the difference. That's — do you understand what I'm saying? I might have wanted you because you wanted me and the signal was loud enough to drown out whatever I actually—" She stopped again, because the sentence was going to keep going and the going wasn't going to help.

The apartment was very quiet. The neighbor's television had stopped. Even the traffic on the boulevard below seemed to have thinned to nothing, though that was probably just Anya's attention narrowing to this room, this couch, this four-foot gap.

*That's the worst thing she's said tonight. Not the telepathy. Not the months of reading me. This. She doesn't know if she loves me or if she's reflecting me back at myself.*

"Is that possible?" His voice was rough in a way she'd never heard from him, not even in the cold basement. "That you'd feel something just because I'm feeling it? That none of it would be — yours?"

"I don't know. Maybe. I've never been this close to someone while I was reading them. Not like this, not — I've never let anyone get near enough for it to matter, because I knew this would happen. That I wouldn't be able to tell."

"Have you always—" He paused, reconsidering the question. "Since you were four?"

"Since before I can remember being anything else."

"And you've never told anyone."

"No."

"Not your parents."

"No."

He was quiet. His thoughts moved through the implications of that — a child hearing every thought in every room she entered, a teenager navigating school with the entire social architecture audible, a young woman building a career in espionage on an ability she could never disclose to the organization she served. The loneliness of it settled into him as a physical weight, and Anya could feel him feeling it, which meant she was doing the thing, the thing where his emotions bled into hers, and she couldn't tell if the sadness pressing behind her eyes was for herself or was his sadness about her reflected back.

"I'm sorry," she said. "Not for telling you. I'm sorry for — the months. For every thought I took that you didn't give me."

"I know."

"That's not enough."

"I know that too."

He rubbed his eyes with the heels of his hands. His thoughts were tired now, the cycling slower, each revolution covering less new ground. She could feel him approaching a decision not as a single thought but as a convergence — multiple threads drawing toward the same point.

"I think I need time," he said.

"Okay."

"I'm not — I don't mean I'm done. I mean I need to think about this without—" He gestured at the space between them. At her. At the air that carried his thoughts to her whether he wanted it to or not. "Without you hearing me think about it."

"Yeah. I understand."

"Is that—" He started to ask if it was okay, then caught himself, because asking a telepath's permission to think in private was absurd in a way that almost cracked the solemnity of the evening, and she saw the near-smile cross his face before he pressed it away. "Right. I'll call you. When I'm ready."

Anya stood up. Her glass of water was still full; she'd never touched it. The box of childhood things remained open on the table. The class photo had ended up face-up between them, and both of them — six years old, one scowling at the camera, one grinning at something invisible — stared up at the ceiling of an apartment that neither of them could have imagined from the vantage point of that schoolyard.

She picked up her coat from the arm of the couch. The movement was ordinary and the ordinariness felt wrong — the way ordinary things always felt wrong when the inside of your life had just rearranged itself. She got one arm through a sleeve and stopped, because she needed to say one more thing and if she finished putting the coat on the momentum of leaving would carry her out the door.

"The reason I told you," she said. "It wasn't to fix anything. It might not fix anything. I told you because I can't build something with you where I'm holding all your cards and you don't know I'm holding them. That's not — that's not the thing I want. Even if you decide you don't want me. I needed you to decide that knowing the real situation."

*She told me so I could have a real choice. One that wasn't rigged.*

He stood up. They were about four feet apart, the same distance as on the couch but vertical now, and the room had a different quality standing. She could see his pulse in his throat. She could hear his thoughts moving — not the frantic cycling from before but something steadier, more deliberate, as if he were choosing what to think with the new awareness that thinking had an audience.

He was trying not to perform his thoughts. He was trying not to think strategically. He was trying to find the thing that was just true and let it be the thing he thought, knowing she would hear it.

"Anya."

"Yeah."

He said it simply, the way you said a thing when you'd stopped performing. "I'm glad you told me."

And the thought behind it: *I'm glad she told me. I'm glad it was her. I'm glad she chose this and nobody caught her or cornered her into it. I'm glad she told me.*

The words and the thought were the same.

Anya had spent her entire life listening to the gap between what people said and what they meant. The gap was the territory she'd lived in since she was old enough to understand that thoughts and speech were different countries. She had heard her father say "I'm fine" while thinking about mission contingencies that terrified him. She had heard her mother say "Work was good today" while a Garden assignment sat heavy in the quiet of her mind. She had heard Sylvia say "I trust your judgment" while genuinely meaning it, and the weight of that match had been the thing that made the lie unbearable.

Now she heard Damian say "I'm glad you told me" and think *I'm glad she told me* and the gap between the two was nothing. Zero. The same sentence, carrying the same weight, in both registers. He meant exactly what he said, and he was saying exactly what he meant, and he knew she could verify that, and the knowing didn't change it because it was already true.

It was the first fully honest exchange she had ever had with another person. Not because the words were extraordinary — they weren't, they were simple and unremarkable and you could say them about being told the time — but because for the first time in her life, the person speaking to her knew she could hear both channels, and chose to let them match.

She finished putting on her coat. Her eyes were wet and she didn't wipe them. He walked her to the door and opened it, and the hallway outside was lit with the flat fluorescent light of an apartment building that had no idea it was a significant hallway, and the woman on the floor below had decided not to call her sister.

They didn't touch. She wanted to, and she could feel that he wanted to, and she let both wants sit there in the hallway without acting on them. Time. He'd asked for time.

"Goodnight," she said.

"Goodnight."

She walked out into Berlint. The city's collective thoughts pressed against her the way they always had — pedestrians and shopkeepers and students and taxi drivers, all broadcasting their interiors to an audience of one who had never asked for the frequency. A man on the corner was thinking about dinner. A woman across the street was worried about money. Two teenagers were arguing about something that felt, from the shape of their thoughts, desperately important and very small.

Anya had been hearing them since before she could remember. She had never told anyone until tonight.

The air was cold. The streets were lit in the patchy orange of Berlint's older neighborhoods, and her breath made small clouds that disappeared before they reached the streetlamps. Somewhere behind her, in an apartment with a permanent dent in the couch and a box of childhood things open on the coffee table, a man she might love — for her own reasons, or for his reflected back through her, or for some blend of both she might never be able to separate — was thinking about her. She could feel it at the edge of her range, thinning as she walked. Not the specific words anymore. Just the warmth of it. A signal without content, like a light left on in a window.

She didn't try to hear what the thoughts were. She let them fade. For the first time in her life, she didn't need to know what someone was thinking about her. He'd said the true thing and thought the true thing and they'd matched, and that was enough to walk home on.
