# Chapter 12

The Blackbell estate glowed against the October dark with the restraint of someone who didn't know the word. White lanterns lined the drive at meticulous intervals. The topiary garden had been strung with lights that made it look like a magazine spread, which it would be — Becky had confirmed photographers from three publications, all carefully vetted to make the event look effortlessly elegant rather than the three weeks of controlled panic it had actually been.

Anya stepped out of the car and the thoughts hit before the cold air did.

*—if the valet scratches my paint I'm going to lose it, I just had it detailed, I'm literally watching him—*

*—she's wearing the Moreau. She's wearing the Moreau. I was told I was the only one with the Moreau—*

*—seven more handshakes. I can do seven. Maybe eight if I count the wife—*

Two hundred minds. She let them blur to a murmur and picked through it for signals: security detail — two private, one off-duty SSS officer she recognized from the diplomatic reception — exit routes, and the running calculation of how long she could stay before Becky noticed she'd left.

The answer, from a thought she'd caught three weeks ago during a phone call: *If she cancels on me one more time I'm killing her. I'm not joking. I will actually kill her.* Becky would not actually kill her. Becky did not know how to kill anyone. But the sentiment was unmistakable.

Damian came around the car. Dark suit, precise posture, the diplomatic composure that was both trained and innate. The thin scar through his eyebrow was the only thing that didn't match the packaging. He hadn't said much on the drive over. The operational background pressed on both of them — the mole still unidentified, the Renewal Front's timeline compressing, days not weeks, and the particular luxury of attending a charity gala while all of that hung in the air. But covers needed maintenance. Anya's social calendar needed to look like a real person's social calendar.

"Ready?" she said.

"No."

*The things I do for East-West cultural exchange. I'm going to end up cornered by Kirchner again, and he's going to talk about tariffs for forty minutes, and I'm going to smile and want to throw myself into the garden—*

"It'll be fine," Anya said. "Becky throws a good party."

"You keep saying that."

"The food'll be amazing."

"That's what you said about the reception."

"And was I wrong?"

He paused. "The shrimp was decent."

*She wore that color before. The state dinner. Don't— stop it. Trade policy. Think about trade policy.*

Anya walked toward the entrance and did not think about what she could hear him thinking about not thinking about.

Becky intercepted them in the foyer with the precision of a guided missile in a cocktail dress.

"You came!" She grabbed Anya by the shoulders, held her at arm's length, and subjected her to the kind of visual inspection that suggested either deep affection or quality control. "You actually came. I was going to be so mad if you bailed."

*She looks tired. She always looks tired now. Why doesn't she eat? I'm going to make her eat something if I have to physically—*

"I said I'd come," Anya said.

"You said that about the gallery opening in August and then you texted me from a parking lot at eleven saying something came up." Becky released her shoulders and turned her attention to Damian with the sudden focus of someone spotting something interesting at a market. "And you brought a guest."

"He's not—"

"Damian Desmond." She extended her hand. "Eden Academy. You cried at the eighth-grade science fair because your volcano didn't erupt."

*Oh no. She remembers the volcano. Of course she remembers the—*

"There was a structural issue with the—" Damian started.

"It didn't erupt."

"The sodium bicarbonate ratio was—"

"Damian. It didn't erupt. I was there. You cried."

His diplomatic composure held, barely. "It was a frustrating afternoon."

*Kill me. Just kill me now.*

Anya bit the inside of her cheek.

Becky smiled — the real one, not the camera version. Her dark curls were swept up in something elaborate, and she was wearing a dress that probably cost more than Anya's monthly cover salary, but underneath the production she was the same Becky who'd traded her lunch for Anya's dessert every day for a year at Eden and thought she was getting the better deal.

"I'm glad you're here," Becky said, and the thought underneath was the same shape as the words: *I'm glad. I'm glad she came.* "Both of you. Go eat, talk to people, enjoy yourselves. I have to deal with a crisis — they brought Riesling instead of Gruner Veltliner and I'm going to start a war."

She was already moving, heels sharp on the marble, thoughts scattering between logistics and the genuine pleasure of having her oldest friend in the room.

*Maybe tonight. Maybe tonight will feel like something real.*

Anya watched her go and felt the first specific pang of the evening settle behind her ribs, in the place where the bruise from the ambush had mostly healed but not entirely forgotten.

The gala's main room was the kind of ballroom that only existed in estates like this — gilt frames, a chandelier that could have doubled as a weapon, and the particular acoustics of a space designed to make conversation both possible and overheard. Tables with white cloths ran along one side. A buffet ran along the other. The bar occupied the far wall with the gravity of a small nation.

Anya worked the room the way she'd been trained: peripheral scanning, threat assessment, social mapping. But tonight was maintenance, not operations. She needed her cover identity to be a person who attended parties and talked to people and had a friend who threw galas. Normal. Unremarkable. A cultural attache with a social life.

The problem was that Eden Academy alumni were everywhere.

Becky had been pulling from the old school network for years — the class of kids who'd grown up into lawyers, diplomats, media producers, and at least one person who Anya was fairly certain had become a professional horse trainer, though she decided not to confirm this telepathically. They populated the room like a reunion nobody had officially called: clusters of people recognizing each other across tables, rediscovering childhood hierarchies and discovering they no longer applied, or discovering that they still did.

A woman in green intercepted Anya near the buffet. "Anya! From Miss Henderson's class! Do you remember me?"

*Please say yes, please say yes, I rehearsed this in the car—*

"Of course. You sat next to the window. Third row."

"Yes! Oh my God." Relief flooded the woman's thoughts like warm water. *She remembers.* "I'm Petra. Petra Vogt. I'm in publishing now."

"That's great."

"What about you? Someone said you're with the diplomatic service?"

"Cultural attache. Mostly paperwork and events."

"That sounds amazing."

It did not sound amazing. Petra's thoughts confirmed this: *Sounds boring, honestly, but she looks good. Is that dress—*

"We should catch up properly sometime," Anya said, which they both knew meant they would not.

She extracted herself and found the buffet, where the food was as extraordinary as Becky's three weeks of controlled panic deserved. She loaded a small plate and drifted toward a table near the terrace doors. Damian had been absorbed into a group near the bar — dark suits, policy talk, the particular energy of young diplomats who weren't yet important enough to be interesting but were important enough to be invited. His thoughts surfaced occasionally in the crowd noise: *—bilateral trade framework, that's safe, say that— Kirchner still parts his hair the same way, hasn't the man seen a mirror in—*

She ate something with goat cheese and allowed herself a small grin.

A passing waiter offered champagne. She took a glass and didn't drink from it — the glass was a prop, something to hold so her hands had a purpose. Across the room, a retired diplomat was telling a story about a customs mishap in Frigis that everyone had heard before and would pretend to laugh at again. An elderly woman near the terrace doors was thinking, with great vividness, about the argument she intended to have with her husband when they got home. *And I'm going to tell him exactly what I think about the way he talked to Martin, and if he says one word about my mother I'm going to—* Anya tuned away. Some people's inner lives were soap operas you didn't have a ticket to.

They were careful with each other, in public. They had been careful since the safe house — since the bruised ribs and the split eyebrow and the quiet afterward, since the line they'd crossed and then, by mutual unstated agreement, stepped back to the edge of without discussing which side they were standing on. Here, in a room full of people who knew one or both of them, careful was the only option. But careful had a weight to it, and the weight was worse in a place where everyone else was being careless and happy and slightly drunk.

Once, near the buffet, their paths crossed. Damian was reaching for a napkin. Anya was reaching for a second helping of the goat cheese thing that she'd decided she was going to eat until it was gone or she was asked to leave.

"The cheese is good," she said.

"I noticed you're eating all of it."

"It's strategically important cheese."

He almost smiled. *Don't smile. Don't— you're smiling.* "I'll leave you to your strategy."

He moved away. The almost-smile stayed in her chest for longer than it should have, and she hated that, because she could feel the operational clock ticking under the party's surface and she should have been thinking about the mole, about the accelerating timeline, about anything other than the specific way his mouth moved when he was trying not to be amused.

Then someone got out the photos.

It started with a woman named Lila — one of Becky's media contacts, also an Eden alumna — scrolling through her phone at the bar. She'd found a scanned yearbook photo: thirty children in Eden Academy uniforms, squinting at the photographer with expressions ranging from dutiful to mutinous.

"Oh my God." She held up the phone. "Look at us."

The nostalgia spread the way it always did at parties with open bars: fast, loud, and with decreasing accuracy. More phones appeared. Someone had a folder of digitized class pictures. Someone else had photos from sports day, the end-of-year ceremony, the school festival. People crowded around screens, pointing, laughing, producing the specific joyful noise of adults confronting evidence that they had once been very small and very ridiculous.

Anya watched from the edge of the cluster and felt something shift in her stomach.

A man named Erich Kessler materialized at the table with his third beer and the comfortable posture of someone who'd found his equilibrium several drinks ago. Erich ran logistics for his family's distribution company — she caught that from his surface thoughts, along with a complicated knot of feeling about his older brother's recent promotion and the awareness, carried lightly but carried everywhere, that Eden Academy had been the last time anyone treated him as remarkable for something other than his family name.

*Good to be here. Good party. I miss this. I miss when we were all just in the same building and nobody'd figured out yet who was going to matter and who wasn't.*

"Anya!" He sat down, grinning. "From Eden, right? Becky's friend?"

"That's me."

"You look exactly the same." He pointed at his own head. "The hair. I'd recognize it anywhere. Nobody else had pink hair. You were like the one spot of color in the whole school."

*Pink hair. Class photo. Second row. She sat behind me in Mr. Kraft's—*

"I've been told it's distinctive," Anya said.

"Distinctive? It's a landmark. I used to navigate the hallways by your head."

She laughed, and the laugh was real enough, and Erich was harmless enough, and for a moment it was just a party. Old classmates and bad memories and wine that Becky would insist was worth the price.

Then someone passed Erich a phone.

The photo on the screen was from the school festival cleanup day — a row of twelve-year-olds holding brooms and dustpans with the expressions of prisoners assigned to yard work. Anya was in the frame. So was Damian. They were standing next to each other with the body language of two people who would rather be standing next to anyone else in the world.

She recognized the photo. She'd been furious that day — about the assignment, the heat, the way Damian had said something about her sweeping technique that she'd found intolerable. She'd caught his thought that afternoon, fourteen years ago, clear as a bell: *She's the worst person on this committee. Why does she look like she's having fun? Nobody should be having fun.*

"Oh, this is great," Erich said. He was grinning at the screen with the delight of someone discovering buried treasure. "Hey — Desmond!"

He called it across the cluster, loud enough to turn heads. Damian looked up from his conversation.

"Erich." Smile. Measured. "Good to see you."

"Come look at this. School festival, cleanup crew. Remember this?"

Damian walked over. His thoughts shifted as he moved — *old photos, this happens at these things, smile at the photo, say something about how young we all—*

He looked at the screen.

*The festival. The brooms. The girl standing next to me who—*

His thoughts slowed. Not stopped — slowed, the way a mind slows when it's approaching something it can't take back.

Erich was still talking, beer-warm and easy, flipping between the photo and the present with the speed of someone for whom the gap between past and now was a source of amusement rather than anything sharper. He looked at the photo. He looked at Anya. He looked at the photo again.

"Wait." His face did the thing that faces do when alcohol removes the filter between recognition and speech. "Wait, wait. You and Anya Forger — you had that whole thing in school, right?" He was talking to Damian now, jabbing his beer toward the phone screen. "The rivalry, or whatever. She punched you once, didn't she? During dodgeball or something?"

The name landed in the room like a glass hitting a tile floor.

Anya Forger.

Said aloud, at a party, by a man on his third beer who was thinking about the fact that his best years were behind him and had no idea what he'd just done.

And Anya felt Damian's mind open like a lock.

It wasn't an explosion. It was worse — it was mechanical, precise, the tumblers of a pattern falling into place one after another in the mind of someone who had been building toward this for months without knowing he was building anything at all.

*Forger.*

*Pink hair.*

*The state dinner — she tilted her head the same way, exactly the same—*

An image: a class photo in a box in his apartment. A girl in the second row, grinning at something off-camera. He'd found it weeks ago and held it and stared at it and put it back in the box without understanding why his hands didn't feel steady.

*The laugh. In the park after Bretz. She had the same laugh.*

*The safe house. Her face. The way she flinched when she took the hit to her ribs.*

*The cultural attache who appeared at the state dinner. Who knew exactly the right things to know. Who intervened when the operative had me cornered. Who kissed me in a cold apartment while I was bleeding.*

Then, clear and final, like a door closing:

*She's Anya Forger.*

His expression didn't change. Twenty years of political upbringing, the Desmond discipline — you didn't show what you knew in a room full of people who were always watching. His face produced a half-smile. His hand stayed steady around his glass.

"That was a long time ago," he said, and his voice was perfect, and inside him a controlled fire was burning through everything.

*Anya Forger. Eden Academy. Westalian intelligence. She's been — since the beginning? Was the friendship at Eden — was that—*

*The alliance. The shared intelligence. The terms in the service corridor. She already knew. She knew everything before I opened my mouth.*

*The kiss.*

A silence inside the noise. A held breath in his thoughts.

*What do I do with this.*

Erich was still going. "—and she punched him, right? Like, full-on? During a game? That's legendary, honestly—"

"I think you're mixing me up with someone," Anya said.

She heard her own voice from a distance — bright, casual, the precise inflection of deflection. "Anya's a pretty common name. And I don't think I've ever punched anyone during dodgeball." She laughed. It sounded right — easy, unbothered. She'd spent years learning to produce exactly this kind of laugh, and it had never cost her this much.

"Huh." Erich squinted at her. *She does kind of look like— nah. Lots of people have pink hair now. Wait, do they?* "Yeah, must be." He shrugged and took a drink. "Common name. My cousin's called Anya, actually. Well, Anna. Close enough."

He moved on. The moment passed. The cluster of classmates shifted back to their photos and memories and the specific pleasure of discovering that the tallest kid in class was now the shortest adult.

But Damian hadn't moved on.

He stood where he'd been, glass untouched, and his thoughts moved with the careful deliberation of a man deciding what to do with something he hadn't known he'd been handed.

*Don't say it here. Not yet. Think about what this means. Think about what it changes.*

*Everything.*

His eyes found hers across the table. Half a second. Less.

Something passed through his expression that wasn't anger, wasn't accusation. Something older and stranger. The look of a person recognizing another person for the first time and the last time simultaneously.

*I see you.*

Then it was gone. He turned to answer a question about wine that someone beside him had asked, and Anya put her champagne glass down on the table because her fingers had gone numb.

The party was still happening. The string quartet had started something new. Erich was showing the phone to someone else. Two hundred people were eating and drinking and thinking their small, private, unguarded thoughts, and none of them knew that the woman by the terrace doors was standing in the wreckage of a life she'd spent two years building.

She pressed her palms flat on the white tablecloth and breathed.

He knew. And he wasn't going to say it tonight. He was going to carry it out of this room and decide, privately, what to do — and she was going to stand here reading the process of that decision and be unable to influence it, because that was what her power was. She could see everything and fix nothing.

Somewhere behind her, Erich was laughing about the dodgeball story with someone new. The name — her name, her real name — bounced around the conversation like a ball no one thought was important. *Anya Forger, yeah, she was wild, remember the time she—* Bits and fragments. Nobody connecting it. Nobody except the one person who mattered.

Damian had drifted back toward the bar. His posture was unchanged. His conversation was unchanged. He was saying something to a trade minister's aide about economic cooperation, and it was perfectly appropriate, and the woman he was talking to was charmed, and underneath all of it his mind was running the same loop: *Anya Forger. Anya Forger. What does it mean. What does it change. What do I—*

The crowd noise pressed in — thoughts layering on thoughts, the party's cheerful oblivious hum. She needed to not be in this room.

* * *

The terrace was colder than she'd expected. The party burned warm behind the glass doors, but out here the October air had the bite of a season that had decided it was done being polite. Wide flagstones, wrought-iron railing, a view of the grounds that disappeared into black about twenty feet out. A garden that probably looked impressive in daylight. Right now it was just dark, and Anya was grateful for the dark, because it didn't ask anything of her.

She gripped the railing. The cold metal helped — something solid, something that wasn't a thought or a thought about a thought. Her hands steadied.

Damian was still inside, at the edge of her range. The muffled rhythm of careful thinking, dampened by walls and distance. *What does it mean. What does it change. Think about this.*

She couldn't read what he'd decide. She could read that he was deciding, and the deciding was worse than knowing, because the outcome wasn't fixed and she had no way to tilt it except by doing the thing she'd already been doing — taking his private thoughts and using the knowledge to stay ahead of him.

The thought sat in her like something spoiled.

"You look like you're about to jump."

Becky appeared in the doorway, shoes in one hand and wine in the other. She'd taken her hair down at some point during the evening. The elaborate arrangement had surrendered to gravity and the evening's humidity, and her curls hung around her face in a way that made her look less like a media personality and more like the girl Anya had known since kindergarten.

"I'm fine."

"Mm-hmm." Becky padded barefoot across the flagstones and leaned on the railing beside her. "You're doing the thing."

"I'm not doing a thing."

"You're doing the thing where you go quiet and stare at nothing and say you're fine in a voice that means the opposite. You've been doing it since we were six. I know the thing, Anya."

*She's not fine. Something happened in there. Did someone say something? Did Damian do something? If he did something I'll—*

Anya said nothing.

They stood like that for a while. The party murmured on the other side of the glass. Below them, somewhere in the garden, an irrigation system ticked on and started its cycle, spraying arcs of water across dark grass.

"Good event," Anya offered.

"It's fine." Becky sipped her wine. "The Riesling situation has been resolved. The shellfish thing has been contained. The Brahms problem is ongoing."

"The Brahms problem?"

"The quartet keeps playing that one piece — you know the one — the slow sad one that makes everyone stare at their plates. I asked for uptempo. I specifically said uptempo."

"Did you give them a playlist?"

"I gave them a mood board."

"A mood board. For a string quartet."

"It had very clear visual cues."

Anya almost smiled. Almost. The comfort of this — the easy, particular rhythm of talking to the one person who'd known her longest — was exactly the thing that made what came next unbearable.

Becky's thoughts shifted. The surface chatter about caterers and wine and string quartets fell away, and underneath it something heavier came through. Not sudden. More like something she'd been carrying all evening, held carefully below the performance, waiting for a moment when the performance could stop.

*I should say it. She's the only one I can say it to. If I can't say it to Anya—*

"Can I tell you something?" Becky said.

"Yeah."

"Something real."

"Always."

Becky looked at her wine. She was quiet long enough that the irrigation system completed a full cycle below them and started over.

"I broke up with Marc."

"I'm sorry—"

"Don't be. He was fine. That was the whole problem." She turned the glass between her palms, a slow rotation. "We dated for five months. He came to every event. He stood where I put him. He smiled for photos. He said the right things. And in five months he never once asked me a question he didn't already know the answer to."

*He didn't want me. He wanted the press-release version. The one that gets you into rooms and onto lists.*

"He asked me about my work once," Becky said. "Early on. I got excited — I started telling him about this documentary project, a series about artists in the eastern districts, people nobody covers. And he listened for about thirty seconds and then said 'That's great, babe' and changed the subject to the party we were going to that weekend." She took a sip of wine. "That's when I knew. He didn't actually want the answer. He wanted to have asked."

The irrigation system clicked through its rotation below. Inside, through the glass, the party continued — someone laughed too loudly, a waiter navigated between clusters with a tray of something Anya couldn't see.

"And the thing is, Marc's not special. Not in a bad way — he's a perfectly fine person. But everyone's like that." Becky's voice had lost its performance quality. It was flatter, quieter, the voice she used when no one was recording and no one was watching. "Everyone who gets close. They want the Blackbell, not the Becky. They want the introductions and the guest lists and the table at the right restaurant. Nobody's ever once asked me what I do on a Tuesday when there's nothing on the calendar."

*I watch cooking shows. That's what I do. I watch cooking shows alone in my apartment and eat cereal for dinner and talk to my cat and I don't tell anyone because it doesn't match the brand.*

"People think being visible is the same as being known," Becky said. "It's not. It's the opposite. The more people look at you, the less they actually see."

She turned to Anya. Her face was open in a way that Anya had seen only a handful of times in fourteen years — unguarded, stripped of the charm and the performance and the social armor that Becky wore so well that most people forgot it was armor. Underneath, her face was younger and plainer and more tired than anyone at the gala tonight would have guessed.

"You're my best friend," Becky said. "You know that, right? You're the only person who actually listens. Not the networking version. Not the I'll-pretend-so-you-invite-me-next-time version. You hear me. The real way."

*She's the only one who sees me. The real me. The Tuesday-night-cereal me. I don't know what I'd do if I didn't have her.*

And there it was.

The thing that was worse than the recognition, worse than Damian's thoughts locking her name into place, worse than the cover dying — because this one had no operational excuse, no mission to hide behind, no greater good to justify it.

She had been inside Becky's thoughts for their entire friendship.

Since they were six years old. Since the first day Becky sat next to her at Eden Academy and thought, *I like her hair. I want to sit with her every day.* Anya had heard that thought, and she'd been hearing them ever since. Fourteen years of Becky's private inner life — the fears she spoke aloud and the ones she swallowed, the insecurities she showed and the ones she kept buried, the silent three-AM assessments of herself that she thought belonged to no one. Every confidence Becky had ever shared with Anya had been accompanied by the ones she hadn't chosen to share. And Anya had heard them all.

Becky thought Anya was a good listener. She thought Anya heard her, the real way.

She did hear her. She heard everything. She heard the things that weren't meant for anyone, the things that were supposed to belong only to Becky, and she'd been hearing them so long she could no longer tell which parts of their friendship were built on what Becky had given and which parts were built on what she'd taken.

The thought rose in her like something physical — tell her, right now, say it: *I'm a telepath. I've heard your thoughts since we were children. Every private fear, every silent thing you thought was only yours. I took it. I took all of it. And I used what I stole to be exactly the friend you needed, and I can't tell anymore whether what I feel for you is love or something I built out of fourteen years of stolen access.*

She didn't say it.

She didn't say it because Becky needed the friend she believed Anya was. Because the truth would destroy the one relationship Becky trusted — the one person she believed saw her clearly. And the destruction would be worse because the betrayal wasn't a single act. It wasn't a lie that could be confessed and forgiven. It was a condition. Every day of their friendship, every conversation, every phone call, every silence — all of it, retroactively poisoned. There was no version of the truth that left the friendship standing.

"You're mine too," Anya said. "My best friend. You know that."

It was true. And it was the most dishonest thing she'd said all night. Becky's thoughts warmed with relief and gratitude, and Anya stood in the warmth of it and felt the particular self-loathing of someone who had taken something precious from someone who loved her and could never give it back.

Becky leaned her head against Anya's shoulder. Her thoughts went quiet — not empty, but settled. The stillness of a person who had said what she needed to say and been met with the only thing she wanted.

*Thank God for her.*

Anya stared at the dark garden. The October air stung her eyes. Inside, through the glass, the party continued — lights, laughter, the string quartet finally playing something uptempo. She could hear Damian at the far edge of her range, still there, still careful, still turning it over.

*What to do. What to do with this.*

And Becky's head on her shoulder, warm and trusting and entirely undefended.

And the cold, which was useful, because it meant she didn't have to explain why her eyes were wet.
