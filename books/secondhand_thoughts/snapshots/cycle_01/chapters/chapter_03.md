# Chapter 3

The debriefing room at WISE headquarters smelled like burned coffee and carpet glue. The fluorescent tubes overhead hummed at a frequency that settled behind Anya's teeth, a vibration she'd stopped noticing consciously three years ago but which was there now, in her jaw, because she was noticing everything. The water pitcher on the table had a chip in the handle that maintenance had never replaced. The chairs were the same beige plastic as every other room in the building. Someone had left a pen cap on the floor under the far chair, blue, the kind that came in packs of twelve from the supply closet.

Sylvia sat across from her. Reading glasses on the chain around her neck, not on her face — which meant receiving mode, not review mode. Her hands were folded on the table, her posture the precise uprightness of a woman who had spent thirty years in rooms like this and had long ago stopped distinguishing between comfort and duty. She looked at Anya the way she always looked at agents during debrief: attentive, evaluative, and underneath both of those, something that wasn't quite warmth but that Anya could hear if she listened.

*Good. She is punctual. Loid's influence, or her own — hard to say now. She has become her own operative.*

The thought arrived in Sylvia's voice: clipped, structured, the mental equivalent of a briefing memo. Sylvia's surface thoughts ran like institutional prose — orderly, sequential, formatted. Even her private impressions came with headings.

"Your initial assessment of the Ostanian operative." Sylvia uncapped a pen. "I want the full picture. Walk me through what you observed."

Anya opened the folder she'd prepared. She'd typed the report at her apartment the night before, sitting on the kitchen counter with her shoes off because the desk chair squeaked and the squeak was annoying at two in the morning. She had written three drafts. The first was true: Damian Desmond, son of Donovan, running a counter-operation against the Renewal Front from inside the Foreign Affairs Ministry, using his father's old intelligence network against the people who wanted to finish what his father had started. She'd read his sincerity. She'd read his isolation. She'd read the memories of a girl with pink hair that kept surfacing in his unguarded thoughts. She'd deleted that draft because filing it would trigger a full WISE operational response — analysts swarming the sector, surveillance assets redirected, Damian identified and assessed and ultimately burned or turned or used until the mission consumed him the way missions consumed people.

The second draft was close to true, with the telepathic intelligence stripped out. It still identified Damian too clearly. It still named the Renewal Front as the primary threat in the sector. Filing it would have been honest. Filing it would have been correct. She'd deleted it too.

The third draft was a lie.

"The operative is a mid-level diplomat. Damian Desmond — yes, son of Donovan, but he's kept visible distance from the family's political infrastructure." Anya kept her voice level. Conversational. The register you used to report something that was worth mentioning but not worth prioritizing. She'd learned that register from Loid, who'd taught her how to modulate information delivery without ever calling it that. "His activity profile is consistent with internal Ostanian factional positioning. Party maneuvering. I don't think he represents a direct security concern for Westalian interests."

*Consistent with the eastern sector pattern we have been tracking. Her assessment aligns with the secondary indicators.*

Sylvia's thought was approving. Procedural. Not a flicker of suspicion. Two years of reliable field work were a bank account, and Anya was emptying it in a single transaction.

"The Renewal Front intelligence," Sylvia said. Not a question. A prompt.

"Fragments." Anya turned a page in the folder — a prop, something to do with her hands. "References to 'the initiative,' some anxiety about a timeline. It could be the standard nationalist rhetoric we've been hearing since spring. I'd classify it as elevated chatter, not operational activity."

The fluorescent hum was louder now, or she was more aware of it. She was minimizing intelligence about a conspiracy that was engineering a diplomatic incident designed to push two nations toward war. She was doing it in a building full of people whose careers existed to prevent exactly that. She was doing it while the woman across from her thought —

*Her field judgment has been consistently sound. Two years without a significant analytical error. She has Loid's instinct for threat assessment — the ability to distinguish signal from noise under pressure. That is not a common quality.*

Anya's hand rested on the table. She focused on the grain of the laminate under her fingertips. Fake wood. Fake assessment. Fake everything in this room except Sylvia's trust, which was the realest thing Anya had ever felt someone direct at her, and which she was currently using as the surface her lie slid across.

"The Desmond name alone would normally trigger an escalation protocol," Sylvia said. Institutional process, not challenge. She was making a note in the margin of the printed report — small, precise handwriting that Anya couldn't read upside down.

"I know. But the son isn't the father." True. "What I observed at the dinner didn't suggest operational sophistication or hostile intent." False. "He struck me as someone navigating his family's political inheritance, not weaponizing it." True and false simultaneously, which was the worst kind of lie because the truth in it made the falseness almost invisible.

*She is correct. We have been over-indexing on the Desmond name for a decade. It is time to recalibrate.*

Sylvia was already recalibrating. Anya could hear it happening — the institutional machinery shifting, resource calculations adjusting, two analysts who would have been assigned to Damian's sector now redirected to the border situation because Anya Forger had told her handler that the threat was manageable.

"I am classifying this as watch-and-report." Sylvia capped her pen. "You will maintain passive observation on Desmond through your cover function. Monthly reporting unless something changes."

"Understood."

"Anya." Sylvia looked at her properly — without the glasses, without the pen, with the full weight of her attention, which was considerable even without telepathy to verify it. "Your work at the dinner was clean. The approach, the positioning, the extraction of observations under crowd pressure. I noticed, and I want you to know that."

*She does not hear this enough. Loid is the same way — he assumes competence is its own reward. But she is young, and the work is isolating, and it costs nothing to tell someone they did well when they did well.*

The trust was not generalized institutional confidence. It was specific. It was aimed at Anya by name, informed by specific field reports and specific conversations with Loid and a specific memory of an extraction six months ago that Anya had handled under pressure. Sylvia was thinking about that extraction now — about how Anya had improvised a route change when the original plan fell apart, and how she'd remained calm, and how the contact had gotten out clean.

Anya sat there and received all of it. The specific, professional, earned trust of a woman who had given her career to this organization. The trust that made the lie possible. Without it — without the two years of clean work, without the accumulated credibility, without Sylvia's assessment that Anya's judgment could be relied on — the false report would have been questioned. Scrutinized. Flagged. The trust was the infrastructure her betrayal rode on, and she was reading it in real time, consuming it like oxygen.

"Thanks," Anya said. Her voice held. Her father had taught her how to maintain composure under operational stress, and she was grateful for the training in the specific, nauseating way you could be grateful for something that was currently enabling you to do something terrible.

She gathered the folder. Stood. The chair scraped against the carpet — the same institutional scrape it always made, the sound of every debriefing she'd ever been in, except this was the one where she'd lied.

"Anya."

She turned at the door.

"Coffee is fresh. Left pot. Do not drink from the right pot — Henderson left it there on Wednesday and I believe it has become a research project."

*I should throw it out. I keep meaning to throw it out. Tomorrow.*

Anya almost smiled, and the almost was where the real pain lived. Sylvia was a person. Not a system or an institution or an abstract concept Anya was betraying — a fifty-eight-year-old woman who made dry jokes about old coffee and remembered which of her agents needed encouragement and wore her glasses on a chain because she kept losing them on her desk. Anya had just lied to her face. And Sylvia's surface thoughts had contained nothing but Monday-morning logistics and a genuine, unsolicited thought about making sure a young operative heard she was doing good work.

"I'll grab some. Thanks, Handler."

She walked out. The corridor was empty — WISE at eight in the morning, most analysts not yet in, the overnight team cycling out, the building hollow and echoing the way schools echo before first period. Anya's shoes were quiet on the institutional carpet. She passed two closed doors, a water fountain with a persistent drip, and a notice board where someone had taped a cartoon of a cat wearing a security badge.

She went into the women's bathroom. Locked the stall. Sat on the toilet lid with the folder in her lap.

Her hands were shaking. Not much. Enough that the folder's pages rustled against each other, a tiny paper sound in the tiled room. The fluorescent hum was different in here — higher, or maybe she was just more aware of everything now that she didn't have Sylvia's thoughts to focus on.

The false assessment was in the system. A traceable document with her name and her classification and her professional judgment committed to WISE's institutional memory. If someone cross-referenced her report with other intelligence — with whatever the Renewal Front was actually doing in the sectors she'd downgraded — the discrepancy would be there. Not today. Maybe not next month. But the lie had a half-life she couldn't calculate, and somewhere in the building's analytical machinery it was already being absorbed as reliable baseline data, shaping resource allocation and threat models and the operational picture that other agents would trust with their decisions.

She pressed her palms against her knees and breathed.

She'd done it. She'd actually done it. Not the dramatic kind of betrayal — no stolen files, no dead drops, no midnight meetings in parking garages. The bureaucratic kind. The kind that looked like routine assessment and slid into the system and sat there like a slow-release poison because nobody suspected it was anything other than a competent young agent's honest evaluation.

For Damian Desmond. For a man whose thoughts she'd read without his knowledge at a state dinner, whose counter-operation she believed in because she'd seen his sincerity from the inside, whose sincerity she'd accessed through a power he didn't know existed.

She'd lied to her handler. Using information stolen from her target. Using an ability no one knew she had.

Two deceptions now. The telepathy — lifelong, involuntary, the condition of her existence. And the false report — chosen, deliberate, four minutes old.

Anya flushed the toilet she hadn't used, washed her hands she didn't need to wash, and went to get coffee from the left pot.


The Ostanian Academy of Arts occupied a limestone building in the government district, its columns either original eighteenth-century or very good fakes — Anya couldn't tell and didn't much care. The cultural exchange reception filled the main hall: eighty people, wine and finger food, a string quartet in the corner playing something she was fairly sure was Schumann. Diplomatic staff from both delegations, some academics, a few journalists, and the particular species of government-adjacent socialite who appeared at every event with wine and a guest list.

The telepathic noise was manageable. Not the overwhelming density of the state dinner, where hundreds of overlapping thoughts had battered her like standing in a wind tunnel. This was more like a cocktail party playing on multiple radios at moderate volume — she could let the murmur wash over her or tune toward individual signals with a focused effort.

*—absolute waste of funding, this exhibition, I could have—*
*—need to confirm the flight before—*
*—are those the salmon ones? Where are the salmon—*

She stood near the refreshments table with a glass of Ostanian white — a little sweet, not her preference, but it was the prop that made her look like a person at a party instead of an intelligence operative surveilling the room. The cover was easy tonight: a young Westalian cultural attaché attending an official East-West exchange event. She'd worn her hair down, the pink falling past her shoulders, and a dress that was professional without trying hard enough to look like it was trying. Loid had once told her that the best cover identity was one where you didn't have to remember what you were pretending to be, because it was close enough to what you were. Good advice from a man who had built an entire real life on that principle.

Damian was near the far wall, talking to a woman in a grey suit. Anya picked up the woman's surface thoughts — flight schedules, a mild resentment about the catering, a vague thought about whether the cellist was attractive — and dismissed them. She focused on Damian.

He looked different here than at the state dinner. The diplomatic armor was lighter. His posture was still trained — the Desmond spine, the product of a childhood where every room was a performance space — but his face was less composed, and when the woman in grey said something mildly funny, his laugh was a real one.

*—her hair. The attaché from the dinner. She's here. Don't — you're mid-conversation. Focus. Ms. Jansen is talking about the trade delegation. The trade delegation. Focus on trade.*

He was trying not to look at her. He was failing. His thought had the quality of someone slamming a drawer shut on their own fingers — the discipline and the impulse and the pain of catching himself all happening at once.

Anya took a sip of wine and began to move.

Three intermediate stops. A brief exchange with a Westalian press officer about the new cultural exhibition schedule — thirty seconds, enough to be seen, not enough to anchor. A polite nod to an Ostanian academic she'd met at a previous function. A pause at the hors d'oeuvres table where she selected something involving goat cheese that she had no intention of eating. Each stop moved her closer to Damian. When the woman in grey excused herself to greet a colleague, Anya stepped into the gap with the timing of someone who'd been trained to enter rooms at exactly the right moment, which she had.

"The quartet's ambitious tonight," she said. "I think that's supposed to be Brahms."

He turned. Up close his face was sharper than she remembered from school — the boyish softness gone, the jaw angular, the eyes the same dark brown but guarded now in a way they hadn't been when he was six years old and furious that a girl with pink hair had punched him during a dodgeball game. He was taller than her by enough that she had to angle her head slightly to meet his eyes.

"It's Schumann, actually," he said. Polite. Measuring. The diplomatic register engaged. "The Piano Quintet in E-flat."

*Westalian delegation. She was at the state dinner. Something about her face. Her — what is it about her face?*

"Oh. Well, now I'm embarrassed." She wasn't, but the admission of error was operational — Loid again, always Loid: the fastest way to make someone comfortable is to be slightly less competent than they expect. "I'm Anya. Cultural attaché with the Westalian mission."

"Damian Desmond. Foreign Affairs." He shook her hand. Firm grip, warm palm, a little damp in a way that told her he was more nervous than his face suggested. "I thought I noticed you at the state dinner last week."

"Probably. I was the one trying to find the bar without looking like I was trying to find the bar."

A small sound that was almost a laugh. His thought: *She's funny. That's — stop cataloguing her personality. You met her literally ten seconds ago. This is professional contact. Professional.*

"The bar was well-hidden," he said. "I think they do that deliberately. Keep the diplomats sober."

"Hasn't worked in my experience."

This time the laugh was real, and Anya felt it arrive with a small, warm concussion in her chest that had nothing to do with her telepathy and everything to do with the specific sound of Damian Desmond laughing without performing.

*Her face when she said that. She scrunches — her nose. Someone used to — not the dinner. Before. Years. Why does she—*

The recognition was there at the edges. Not a clear signal — more like the peripheral awareness of something moving just outside your line of sight. He was looking at her hair, at her face, at the way she tilted her head when she waited for him to speak, and his brain was trying to match the pattern to a memory filed away in whatever storage system twenty-year-old Ostanian diplomats kept their childhoods in.

"So you're in Foreign Affairs," Anya said, steering onto safe ground. She knew exactly where he worked, what he did there, what he was secretly trying to accomplish, and the name of his childhood dog. "Is that as exciting as it sounds?"

"It sounds exciting?"

"To someone who reads about cultural funding all day, yeah."

He relaxed a degree. His shoulders dropped maybe a centimeter — invisible to anyone not trained by Loid Forger to read posture as language. "Mostly it's paperwork. Position papers. Meetings about meetings. The exciting parts are someone else's pay grade." A beat. "For now."

*The 'for now' was too much. That sounded ambitious. She's going to think you're one of those Desmond people who talks about destiny. Pull it back.*

"Although pay grade implies there's a ladder, and I'm not sure there is." The correction was clumsy — he'd heard himself sound too eager and was trying to sand it down into something modest, but the sanding was visible and the modesty was worse than the ambition because it was so obviously performed. His thought while he said it: *That was worse. That was definitely worse. Stop talking about your career. Ask her something. Anything.*

"What about the cultural attaché work? Is it—" He searched for the right question and she watched him search, his diplomatic training offering several options and his actual interest discarding all of them. "Is it what you expected?"

"Nobody expects cultural policy," Anya said. "You sort of fall into it and then realize you care about exhibition catalogues."

"That's — yeah. That's how it works in Foreign Affairs too. You arrive thinking you'll change the world and then you spend three years learning the filing system."

He meant it. His thought didn't contradict the words — it reinforced them, which was rare for Damian, whose private and public registers usually ran on separate tracks. He was being honest, and the honesty made his voice sound younger, less curated, closer to the boy he'd been before the training and the name and the weight of his father's legacy taught him to sound like something else.

Anya noticed she was staring at him and picked up her wine glass.

*She's looking at — was she looking at me? She picked up her glass very fast. Either she's nervous or she doesn't like the wine. The wine is pretty bad. It's probably the wine.*

"The wine's not great," she said, because she couldn't help herself, and his expression of surprised agreement was so transparent that she had to press the glass against her lips to hide whatever her face was doing.

They talked. The conversation moved through the safe corridors of diplomatic small talk — the exhibition, East-West cultural funding, a translated speech that had gone wrong in a way that was funny in retrospect and apparently a minor international incident at the time. Damian was good at this. His public register was smooth, each sentence doing social work while appearing casual, the product of a lifetime of rooms like this one.

But his thoughts ran alongside his words like a second track.

When she mentioned growing up in Berlint: *Berlint. Eden Academy was in Berlint. A girl — there was a girl with — no. Has to be a different person. Berlint is a big city. Lots of people grew up here.*

When she gestured with her hand to make a point about funding: *Her hands. She uses them when she talks. She — someone used to — stop it. You're projecting. You met her at a reception and now you're inventing connections because she's pretty and you're lonely. That's not a connection. That's a Tuesday.*

When she tilted her head to listen to his explanation of the eastern cultural exchange program: *There. That exact tilt. Who tilts their head like that? I've seen — when I was—*

She was managing two conversations and the gap between them was getting harder to hold. Every time his thoughts edged toward recognition, she had to steer the spoken exchange away from anything that might deepen the association — away from schools, from childhood, from anything that might give the half-formed memory enough material to crystallize. Every time his thoughts edged toward attraction, she had to not react. This was the high-wire act of being Anya Forger: the person most qualified to have this conversation and the person least able to have it honestly.

He was thinking about her smile. Specifically, he was thinking about the slight asymmetry — the left side of her mouth pulling up first — and about how he found it—

She turned to look at a painting on the wall. Abruptly. Transparently. He noticed.

"Sorry," she said. "Thought I recognized someone over there."

*She keeps looking away when I'm talking. Am I boring her? I might be boring her. I was about to talk about the funding reconciliation framework. That would bore anyone. Change the subject. Something interesting. Something—*

"Have you seen the exhibition in the next gallery?" he asked. The question came out slightly too fast, the pitch slightly too high, and his internal reaction was immediate: *That sounded eager. That sounded like I'm trying to get her alone in a gallery. I'm not trying to — I just think the art is — it doesn't matter what I think the art is. She's going to say no. She should say no. This is a professional reception and I—*

"I haven't," Anya said. "Show me?"

Protocol said: maintain a light touch at first contact. Don't extend the encounter past twenty minutes. Don't let the target set the location or pace. She was already forty minutes in and now she was following him into a quieter room.

His thought as she said yes: a small, startled burst of pleasure that he immediately tried to discipline into professional composure, like a man trying to stuff a firework back into its tube.


The gallery was cooler than the main hall, and quieter. Maybe fifteen people drifting between paintings. The crowd's telepathic noise dropped to a murmur — distant enough that individual thoughts surfaced cleanly, like voices carrying across water. Damian's thoughts were easier to isolate here, and she was aware that this was part of why she'd said yes. More access. Better signal. She was an intelligence operative doing intelligence work, and the fact that she was also a person who wanted to keep talking to him was a complication she wasn't ready to name.

They stopped in front of an abstract piece — large, aggressive, the canvas worked over in layers of dark paint that looked like someone had fought with the material and neither side had won.

"This one's interesting," Anya said, because it was there and she needed to say something.

Damian studied it. His public face arranged itself into the expression of a man considering art.

His thought: *I don't understand abstract art. I've never understood abstract art. I nodded through six years of Eden Academy art history and I still don't understand it. If she asks me what I think, I'm going to say something about the use of negative space because that's what Demetrius always said and it worked for him. Negative space. The interplay of form and—*

"What do you think?" Anya asked. She was, at her core, a person who couldn't resist watching someone try to bluff when she could see their cards.

"The artist's use of negative space is—" He stopped. Something in his face shifted — the diplomatic polish cracking, a flash of the stubbornness underneath. He started over. "Honestly? I don't get it. It looks like someone had a bad day and took it out on a canvas."

Anya laughed. Not the professional laugh — the one she used at receptions, controlled and warm and calibrated for social function. The real one. It came from her chest and carried the slightly too-loud quality that Loid had spent years trying to train out of her, the laugh she'd had since she was a child, the one that echoed.

It echoed in the gallery.

*That laugh.*

The thought was louder than anything she'd read from him all night. Not analytical. Not self-correcting. A raw, unfiltered sensory response — the sound of her laughter hitting something deep and unexamined in him, a memory he couldn't locate, a recognition that was almost but not quite—

*A girl at school. She laughed like that. Exactly like that. In a courtyard — no, a hallway — no, outside somewhere. After something happened. Something funny. She was laughing and I was standing there and I thought—*

The memory reached for completion. Anya could feel it stretching, trying to close the circuit — pink hair, a courtyard, a specific afternoon — and she watched it not quite get there. The sediment of a childhood stored away and rarely disturbed shifted but didn't fully surface. He blinked. Twice. His hands went into his pockets — the stress tell, the one that meant he'd felt something he couldn't categorize.

"That funny?" he said. His voice was lighter than his thoughts.

"The bad-day-canvas thing." She was still smiling and couldn't entirely stop. "Sorry. I just — you're right, though. It does look like that."

"You know, most of what I say at these events is rehearsed." He was half-turning toward her, the gallery light catching the angle of his jaw. "The honest answers always come out worse."

*I had a line prepared. About the interplay of form and intention. It was actually a decent line. I rehearsed it in the car. And then she looked at me and I panicked and said the real thing and the real thing made her laugh and now I don't know if that's good or—*

"It came out better than rehearsed," Anya said. "Honest stuff usually does."

He looked at her. Not the diplomatic look. The other one — the one where the polish pulled back and the earnestness underneath was visible, stubborn and slightly bewildered, the expression of someone unaccustomed to being seen clearly and not sure whether to lean into it or away.

"I — there's a lecture series next month," he said. He pulled one hand from his pocket, then put it back. "Comparative cultural policy. It sounds dry. It is dry. But the last one was actually not bad, and if you're interested in the East-West exchange space—"

*Don't ask her. You met her ninety minutes ago. This is not professional networking. You're asking a woman you find attractive to attend a lecture about cultural policy because you can't think of a normal way to see her again. That's — that is the most Damian Desmond thing you have ever done.*

"—if that's the kind of thing that falls under your work, it might be useful. No pressure. I can send you the details."

Anya should have said she'd think about it. She should have maintained the light touch. She should have been the cover identity — the pleasant Westalian attaché who took a polite interest and promised nothing.

"I'd like that," she said. "Send me the details?"

She gave him her official contact information — the cultural attaché email that routed through WISE-monitored channels. He saved it in his phone. His thoughts while he typed were a collision of professional justification and something warmer that he was working very hard to label as networking.

*This is standard diplomatic contact. Completely standard. I am networking with a cultural attaché from the Westalian mission. The fact that she has — that I find her — this is networking. I'll send the details tomorrow. Not tonight. Tonight looks eager. Tomorrow morning. No, mid-morning. Mid-morning is professional. Mid-morning says 'I had this on my calendar and happened to remember.'*

He was going to send it at six in the morning. Anya was certain.

They walked back through the main hall toward the exit. The reception was winding down — the quartet packing up, the catering staff clearing plates, the room's telepathic noise thinning as guests left in twos and threes. At the door he shook her hand again. Same firm grip. Drier now.

"It was nice to meet you, Anya."

"You too, Damian."

*She said my name like she's said it before. Like it was already — no. That's the thing your therapist talks about. You assign significance to—*

He had a therapist. The information arrived and she absorbed it and immediately wished she hadn't, because it was another piece of his private life stolen without his permission, another item in a growing inventory of things she knew about Damian Desmond that he'd never chosen to share with her.

"Have a good night," he said. Formal. The diplomatic register resettling, like a coat being pulled back on against the cold.

"Yeah. You too."

She watched him go. He walked well — straight-backed, unhurried, the trained gait of someone who'd been taught that exits matter. But his thoughts as he crossed the lobby were not about the exit.

*The laugh. Something about her laugh. And the way she tilted her head when she listened. I've — someone — a specific girl. Pink hair. At a school. She used to—*

*What was her name?*

He pushed through the doors into the autumn night. His thoughts faded past the edge of her range, dissolving like a radio signal losing its station, the half-formed recognition thinning to static and then to silence.

Anya stood in the lobby of the Ostanian Academy of Arts with her coat over her arm. Outside, the government district was going dark. The streetlights had come on along the boulevard, and the trees lining the pavement were turning — orange and brown, the leaves curling at the edges, autumn moving through Berlint the way autumn moved through any city, indifferent to the people worrying under it.

She'd pushed past protocol. Forty minutes past the recommended first-contact window. She'd let him choose the gallery. She'd laughed like herself instead of her cover. She'd agreed to see him again. Every operational guideline she'd been trained to follow was screaming at her, and the screaming sounded a lot like her father's voice, measured and precise, explaining the principles of contact management while she sat at the kitchen table as a teenager eating cereal and absorbing lessons she'd never expected to use like this.

But she'd read his thoughts all evening. She knew what he carried — the Desmond name and the counter-operation and the loneliness of a life built on a performance he couldn't drop. She knew his sincerity was real because she'd been inside his head while he produced it, and that was the problem, wasn't it. She knew things about Damian that Damian had never chosen to tell her. His nervousness. His stubborn decency. His memory of a girl he couldn't quite name. His therapist. His rehearsed line about negative space. The specific quality of his attraction to her, which she'd consumed without asking, the way she'd consumed Sylvia's trust that morning — taking what wasn't offered and using it to make her own choices easier.

She put on her coat. Her hands were steady now. The shaking from this morning had passed, replaced by something quieter and worse: the recognition that she'd done two things today, and she'd chosen both of them, and both of them had worked exactly as she'd intended.

The false report was in WISE's system. The contact was established. Two deceptions — one aimed at the institution that trusted her, one woven through every word she'd spoken to the man she was supposed to be assessing — and both of them were hers. Not compelled by the mission. Not forced by circumstance. Chosen, because Anya Forger had decided she knew better than everyone around her. Better than WISE's analytical machinery. Better than Loid's training. Better than Sylvia's thirty years of experience. Better than Damian's right to meet someone without that someone already knowing his thoughts.

She walked out into Berlint. The evening air was cold enough to feel on her face, the first real bite of autumn, and the city's thoughts washed over her as she headed toward the diplomatic quarter. Commuters thinking about dinner. A couple arguing about whose turn it was to cook. A child thinking about a cartoon. Someone rehearsing what they'd say to their boss tomorrow. Someone else not thinking about anything at all, just walking, just tired, their mind a low hum of fatigue and the particular emptiness that came at the end of a long day.

None of them knew she was there. Inside their heads, taking what they hadn't offered. She'd been doing this her entire life. Every person she'd ever passed on a street, every classmate, every neighbor, every stranger on a tram — she'd been inside them, uninvited, for as long as she could remember.

Today that lifelong fact had become a choice. Two choices. And both of them had cost something that she could feel in her body — not in her throat or her chest, not the stock vocabulary of guilt, but in her hands, which had held the folder steady while she lied to Sylvia, and which had shaken afterward in a bathroom stall, and which had been steady again by the time she shook Damian's hand in a gallery. The steadiness was the worst part. It meant she was getting used to it.

Anya walked home through the autumn dark, reading the city all the way, carrying the specific weight of the day she'd become a person who betrayed her handler's trust and used a man's unguarded thoughts to build a connection he believed was honest. Two deceptions. One she'd been born with. One she'd made this morning at a table in a room that smelled like burned coffee.

She was good at this. That was the thing she couldn't stop thinking about. She was really, really good at this.
