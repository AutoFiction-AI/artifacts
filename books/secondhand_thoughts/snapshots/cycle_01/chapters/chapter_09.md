# Chapter 9

She caught the thought from across the street — sharp, focused, nothing like the ambient noise of Berlint's evening commuters. *Left side, low approach, maintain visual on target.*

Anya had been following Damian for twenty minutes, keeping a block and a half between them since he left the Foreign Affairs Ministry. A late meeting, the building mostly dark behind him. She'd told herself this was standard protocol — monitoring an operational partner in an unsecured environment. Which was partly true. The rest of the truth was that three days after catching a thought-fragment about *the asset inside WISE* at the diplomatic reception, she couldn't leave Damian's movements unchecked.

The schedule was not clean.

She stopped at a newsstand, tilting a copy of the *Berlinter Abendblatt* toward the streetlight, and opened her range.

Two minds. Both disciplined — surface thoughts running operational, not personal. The first: *Distance closing. He'll turn at the next block. Move to intercept position.* The second, further back: *Package is unaware. Clean approach.*

Package. Not target, not subject. Package. Military vocabulary, not SSS, not police.

The newsstand vendor said something about exact change. Anya dropped coins on the counter without counting them and moved.

Damian was a hundred meters ahead, walking at the unhurried pace of someone thinking about something else. She caught the edge of his thoughts — budget figures from the meeting, a line he wished he'd delivered differently to an undersecretary, the particular irritation of a man who'd sat through three hours of committee procedure. Oblivious.

The first attacker was thirty meters behind him, closing the gap. The second had crossed the street and was angling toward the mouth of a side street — Schultzgasse, narrow, poor lighting, residential doors locked by nine. If Damian turned left at the corner, the way he always did, they'd catch him in the gap between the streetlights.

She couldn't shout his name. Not her cover name for him, not his real one. Any name she used in earshot of two assailants became operational intelligence.

She ran.

The cobblestones were slick with fallen leaves — autumn in Berlint made every sidewalk a liability — and her flats had no grip. She cut across the street between a parked delivery truck and a taxi, reading both attackers' intent as she moved. The first had his hand inside his jacket. The second was reaching for something in a pocket — not a gun, smaller, a collapsible baton or a blade.

Damian turned left onto Schultzgasse.

*Now,* the first attacker thought. *Go.*

They moved in from both ends. Anya came around the corner and saw it: Damian walking between two lamp gaps, the first man accelerating behind him, the second stepping out from a recessed doorway ahead. Coordinated. Professional. They'd rehearsed this.

"Damian!"

He turned. That saved him — the first man's grab caught his shoulder instead of his neck, and Damian stumbled sideways against a building wall. The second man pulled a telescoping baton and closed fast.

Anya hit the second man from the side, driving her elbow into his kidney the way Yor had shown her — find the floating rib, commit your weight, follow through. He grunted and bent, and she grabbed his baton arm with both hands and twisted. Yor would've taken the arm out of the socket. Anya wasn't Yor. The man wrenched free and swung the baton backhanded, and it connected with her left side — ribs — a white shock that made her vision pinch at the edges and stole the breath from her chest.

She read his next thought — *step back, reset, take the throat* — and ducked under the follow-up swing, putting her shoulder into his diaphragm and driving forward. They went down together onto the wet cobblestones. He outweighed her by forty kilos at least. He was already rolling, pinning her leg with his hip, one hand reaching for her neck—

Damian hit the first attacker with his briefcase, a wild overhead swing that caught the man across the ear. The man staggered. Damian hit him again. The briefcase burst open and papers scattered across the wet street in a slow white flutter, and the man caught Damian's arm and drove an elbow into his brow, splitting the skin above his left eye. Blood sheeted down his face immediately.

*Knee,* Anya thought — not a plan, just an instinct — and she brought her heel down hard on the second attacker's kneecap. She felt something give under her shoe, a crunch that transmitted up through her leg, and he screamed and rolled off her. She scrambled to her feet. Her ribs pulsed with a deep, sick ache, and she couldn't fully straighten on the left side.

"Get up," she said. "Move. *Now.*"

The first attacker had recovered. He was circling back toward them, his gait steady, professional — the kind of person who took a briefcase to the head and recalculated rather than panicked. She read his intent: *neutralize the woman first, she's the trained one, the man is secondary.* He'd identified her as the threat inside six seconds of combat. That told her something about the caliber of what they were dealing with.

Damian was on his knees, one hand pressed to his bleeding eyebrow, the other still gripping the handle of his destroyed briefcase. She grabbed his arm and hauled him upright. Behind them, the first attacker was recovering, shaking off the briefcase hit. The second was trying to stand on a knee that wouldn't hold. She caught the first man's thought — *abort, regroup, secondary extraction* — and pulled Damian the other way, toward the main street, running.

He kept pace. Blood ran into his left eye and he wiped it with his sleeve and kept running. Neither of them spoke until they'd crossed two blocks and turned onto a residential avenue where the streetlights were bright and a few late pedestrians still walked dogs and carried groceries.

Anya pressed her back against a building wall and tried to breathe. A woman walking a dachshund gave them a wide berth on the sidewalk, her thoughts a startled loop of *blood — that man is bleeding — should I call someone — no, keep walking, this isn't your business.* Her left side protested each inhale — not broken, she thought, probably not, but badly bruised. She could feel the shape of the baton's impact like a stripe painted across her ribs.

"What—" Damian started, and then had to stop because he was breathing too hard. Blood was still running from his eyebrow, tracking down the side of his face, dripping off his jaw onto his coat collar. "What the hell just happened."

She was already thinking about the precision of it. They'd known which meeting. They'd known what time he'd leave, which route he'd take. The meeting hadn't been publicly scheduled — it was a closed session with ministry staff. Someone with access to Damian's calendar, or access to the intelligence networks that tracked it.

*The asset inside WISE.*

"I need to get you somewhere safe," she said.

---

They walked west for fifteen minutes. Damian held a handkerchief to his eyebrow — he'd produced it from his coat pocket with the automatic propriety of someone whose upbringing included pressed linen for every occasion, even being ambushed on a Tuesday — and Anya kept her arm pressed to her left side and her range open, scanning every approaching mind for hostile intent. She found nothing. A woman thinking about her cat. A man composing a text message to someone who'd annoyed him at work. A teenager listening to music so loud Anya could hear the bass line bleeding from his headphones *and* catch the lyrics repeating in his thoughts. Normal Berlint. Normal evening. Except for the two of them, walking too fast, bleeding.

The WISE fallback apartment was in a residential block in the west district, far from both the diplomatic quarter and the government buildings. Anya had memorized its location during her first week in Berlint and had never used it. The building's front door opened with a six-digit code she had to enter twice because her hands were shaking. The apartment was on the third floor, no elevator, and by the time they reached it she was holding her left side with her arm and breathing through her teeth.

Inside: bare walls, a couch frame with a thin mattress where cushions should have been, a kitchen counter with a sink and nothing on it. A bathroom. A closet that held a first-aid kit, two changes of generic clothes, a prepaid phone, and a handgun she didn't look at. The heat wasn't on. The air smelled like dust and the staleness of a room no one had opened in months. A window overlooked the back of another apartment building, its curtainless glass showing the blue-grey flicker of someone else's television.

Damian sat on the edge of the mattress-couch and pressed the heel of his hand to his eyebrow. Blood seeped between his fingers. His thoughts were still cycling at combat speed — *who were they, how did they know, the Ministry calendar is internal, was I followed earlier, is she hurt worse than she's showing, her hands are shaking, she's trying to hold them still and they're shaking.*

They were. She noticed when he noticed.

Anya opened the first-aid kit. Gauze pads, antiseptic wipes, butterfly strips, ibuprofen in a foil packet, a small bottle of saline solution. She washed her hands at the kitchen sink with dish soap that had been sitting on the counter since the apartment was stocked, and brought the kit to the couch.

"Let me see."

He moved his hand. The cut was about two centimeters long, just above his left eyebrow, deep enough to still be bleeding freely but not down to bone. A clean split — the elbow had landed precisely.

She cleaned it with saline, pressing gauze to the wound while he winced. His jaw tightened but he didn't pull away. She applied two butterfly strips, pressing the edges of the cut together. Blood welled at the margin and she blotted it. His skin was warm under her fingers, even in the cold apartment. She was aware of this — the warmth, the closeness, his breath catching when the antiseptic stung — in a way she didn't want to examine.

"Your turn," he said.

"I'm fine."

"You're holding your side like something's broken."

"It's not broken." She sat on the other end of the couch and, because there was no way to check bruised ribs that didn't involve lifting her shirt, she lifted her shirt. The bruise was already forming — a dark bar across her left side, following the line of the baton strike, the red deepening toward a color that would be purple by morning. She pressed her fingers along the ribs and winced but nothing shifted under the skin. Bruised, not fractured.

Damian was looking at the bruise with an expression she didn't need telepathy to read. His thoughts confirmed it: *That's from getting to me in time. She took that hit because she was protecting me and I was—* The thought cut itself off. Self-recrimination, then. He ran his hand through his hair and looked at the floor.

"Ibuprofen," she said, and he handed her the foil packet from the kit. She swallowed three with water from the kitchen tap that ran rust-colored for the first few seconds, then clear and cold. She sat back down.

A silence settled. The apartment ticked and creaked — pipes, settling building, the small sounds that empty spaces produce at night. The television next door was audible through the wall, a sportscaster narrating something with practiced enthusiasm. Anya could hear the neighbor's thoughts, too — faint, domestic, something about a bet on the match. Normal life, on the other side of the plaster.

"How'd you know," Damian said. Not quite a question. He was looking at his hands, which had blood on them — his own, mostly. *She was already there. Before it started. She was already behind me. How is that possible unless—*

"I'm good at reading situations."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one I've got."

He looked at her. His thoughts were running a cost-benefit: push the question and risk the alliance, or accept the non-answer and keep what they had. She watched the calculation happen, the way she always did — like reading a document someone was drafting in real time. He chose the non-answer.

"Okay," he said. And then, quieter: "Thank you. For—" He gestured vaguely at the room, the night, the bruise on her side. "Yeah."

"Yeah."

More silence. The sportscaster was getting excited about something. Anya could feel the adrenaline leaving her body like water running out of a bath — not all at once but steadily, and what it left behind was heavy and cold and unsteady. She wanted to sleep. She wanted to call Loid. She wanted to go home and eat Yor's terrible cooking and not think about the precision of an ambush that should have been impossible.

"They knew your schedule," she said.

"I noticed."

"That meeting wasn't public."

"No." *Ministry coordination only. Internal calendar. If someone had access, it had to be — but who would —* He stopped the thought. Rubbed his face. "Can we do this tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow."

He leaned his head back against the wall. The butterfly strips on his brow were already darkening with blood soaking through at the margins. He'd have a scar there. Small, but permanent. She'd given him that by being three seconds too slow.

They sat without talking for a while. The silence wasn't uncomfortable — it was the silence of two people whose bodies had just spent all their available panic and were now subsiding into the heavy, stupid calm that follows. Anya could feel a tremor starting in her hands and folded them together in her lap. The bruise on her side was stiffening, the tissue tightening as it swelled, and when she shifted on the mattress a bright line of pain ran from her ribs to her hip.

Damian was looking around the apartment — the bare walls, the naked bulb, the sink with its rust-edged faucet. *This is where spies go when something goes wrong. This is what safe means for people like her. A closet with a gun in it and a mattress with no sheets.* The thought had a quality she recognized. Not pity. Something closer to understanding.

"Eden Academy had this thing," he said.

The shift was so abrupt she almost asked him to repeat it.

"After the dodgeball tournament," he went on. His eyes were still closed, head against the wall. "The losing team had to clean the gym. Mop the floors, stack the chairs, the whole ordeal." He touched the butterfly strips absently. "My team lost because this girl punched me during the game. Not with a ball. With her actual fist. Because I said something about her hair."

"You got punched during dodgeball."

"I got punched during dodgeball. I was eight. She was — I don't remember how old. Same age, maybe. I dropped the ball I was holding and her team got us out and we had to mop for an hour." His mouth twitched. "And the thing is, I can't even remember what I said about her hair. But I remember the punch."

Anya pressed her lips together. She remembered the punch too. She remembered the exact words he'd said about her hair — something about it looking like bubblegum, delivered with the full confidence of a boy who thought he was being clever — and she remembered the satisfaction of her small fist connecting with his shoulder. She'd gotten a detention. It was worth it.

"And then the school festival cleanup," he said. "Same year, maybe? The paint crew. Someone knocked over a whole shelf of poster paints and the volunteers had to stay until nine scrubbing the floor." *She was there too. The pink-haired girl. She was on the cleanup crew and she was laughing about the paint — she had blue paint on her shoes and she was sliding around on the wet floor — and her laugh—*

He opened his eyes and looked at her.

"You remind me of someone," he said.

"You've told me that."

"I know." *Anya Forger. Her name was Anya. She had pink hair and she punched me during dodgeball and her laugh was—* The thought was clear and specific and right there, a name on the tip of his mind, not yet spoken but forming. He rubbed his eyes with the back of his hand. "Sorry. Getting hit in the head makes me nostalgic, apparently."

She laughed. Not the controlled, cover-appropriate laugh she deployed at diplomatic functions. A real laugh, too loud for the small apartment, the kind that comes from being exhausted and hurt and alive and sitting next to someone who just described the worst dodgeball game in Eden Academy history while bleeding from his eyebrow.

He laughed too. Startled by her laugh, and then caught by it — his laughter came out rough and surprised, and the butterfly strips pulled as his brow moved and he pressed gauze to his face while still laughing, which made them both laugh harder. The sportscaster next door hit a crescendo. Anya's left side screamed with every convulsion and she didn't care.

It wound down the way real laughter does — in diminishing waves, each one quieter, until they were both just breathing hard and the apartment was still around them again.

"I haven't laughed like that in a while," he said.

"Me either."

His thoughts were settling. The operational noise was fading — the fear, the tactical cycling, the who-knew-what — and underneath, something quieter and less guarded was surfacing. He was thinking about the apartment, the thin mattress, the cold. About the gauze in his hand and the woman sitting a cushion's width away from him. Not the intelligence officer. Not the alliance partner. Her. The way she'd fought — not graceful, not by-the-book, but fierce and willing to take the hit. *She got between them and me. Didn't even hesitate.*

And then, softer, almost not a thought at all: *I don't want to go home.*

---

She should have moved to the other side of the room. Should have checked the building's perimeter, or called in a report to the WISE emergency line, or started cataloguing the operational security failures that had put two military-trained killers on Damian's walking route. She knew this. She listed the things she should have done even as she didn't do any of them.

The silence between them had changed. It was not the silence of people who'd run out of things to say. It was the silence of two people who both knew what was about to happen and were deciding whether to let it.

His thoughts were open. Not deliberately — he was too tired and too hurt and too relieved to maintain the usual diplomatic surface. She was reading him the way she'd read a hundred rooms at a hundred receptions, except this wasn't a room full of strangers and the thoughts she was catching were not state secrets or diplomatic gossip. They were: *she's right there. She's close enough to touch. If I reached out —*

She should have stopped listening.

"Damian."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry about your eyebrow."

"It's fine." He touched the butterfly strips. "I'll look rugged."

"You'll look like you got elbowed in the face."

"Ruggedly elbowed." He was almost smiling, and his thoughts had gone quiet — not empty, but still, the way attention narrows to a single point when everything else has been stripped away. *Just look at her. Just look at her and don't think about the rest of it.*

She was looking at him. The overhead light in the safe house was a single bare bulb, and it cast everything in flat, unforgiving brightness — the cut on his brow, the blood that had dried along his jaw, the dark hair falling forward because he'd been running his hands through it for an hour. He looked like exactly what he was: a twenty-year-old who'd nearly been killed and was sitting in an empty apartment with a woman he was trying not to think too much about.

He leaned forward. She didn't move away.

The kiss started carefully — his mouth on hers, tentative, a question in the pressure of it. His hand found the side of her face. His fingers were cold from the unheated room. She could taste copper where he'd wiped blood from his lip earlier, and underneath that, nothing — just him, just the warmth of his mouth and the slight roughness of his jaw and the fact that he was kissing her and she was letting him and nothing about this was operational.

She kissed him back. And the diplomatic caution, the alliance terms, the negotiated parameters of their professional arrangement — all of it became distant and abstract. His other hand settled on her hip, light, giving her the chance to pull away. She didn't pull away. She put her hand on the back of his neck and felt his pulse under her fingers, fast, and his thought was just *oh* — and then his arm tightened around her and the caution in the kiss burned away.

She was trying not to read his mind. She was actively, consciously trying to close the part of herself that had been open since she was a child — the involuntary reception that operated whether she wanted it or not, the thing that made her what she was. She tried to close it the way you try not to hear music bleeding through a wall. The way you try to ignore a voice calling your name.

She mostly failed.

What she caught came in fragments, unstructured, nothing like the organized sentences of his diplomatic thinking: *this is real — she's — her mouth — I want —* And then, below the fragments, the layer he wasn't controlling at all. Loneliness. Not the curated loneliness of a man making sympathetic conversation about his difficult life, but the structural kind — the kind that had been there so long it had become architecture. Years of it. Dinners eaten alone in the apartment with the unpacked boxes. Meetings where he performed beliefs he despised. The weight of a surname that meant something to everyone in the country except the person carrying it. And beneath the loneliness, stubborn, almost defiant: hope. That this woman who'd appeared in his life and fought for him and laughed at his terrible stories and sat with him bleeding in a cold apartment — that she was real. That this meant something.

Anya felt it all. She felt it the way she'd felt Sylvia's trust when she'd sat across from her handler and filed the false report — involuntarily, completely, and with the specific nausea of receiving something she hadn't earned and couldn't return honestly.

He kissed her harder and his hand moved from her hip to the small of her back, pulling her closer, and his thoughts dissolved. Not words anymore. Want, and warmth, and the half-formed image of her face and a physical awareness that was just his body against hers — the thin mattress shifting under their combined weight, her fingers in his hair, the ache in his brow that he was ignoring.

And then, like something breaking the surface of deep water, a thought rose through the rest of it with terrible clarity.

*Anya Forger.*

Not a question. Not a speculation. A name attached to a face attached to a memory: a girl in the second row of a class photo, eight years old, grinning at something off-camera. Pink hair pulled back with clips shaped like horns. The girl who punched him during dodgeball because he said something about her hair. The girl who laughed on the wet floor of the school festival with blue paint on her shoes.

*It's her. It's Anya Forger. It's—*

The recognition didn't complete. It hovered — achingly specific, close enough to name, but not yet committed to. His conscious mind was too overwhelmed to finish the work. Too much was happening in his hands and his chest and his mouth for the analytical part of him to lock it down. The name floated and the kiss continued and he thought *later, think about it later, right now she's here and she's—*

Anya heard every word.

She kept kissing him. She kept her hands in his hair and her mouth on his and she did not pull away, because pulling away would be the correct operational response and she was not, in this moment, capable of the correct operational response. She was capable of nothing except the knowledge that she was stealing from him. Every second of this was a theft. His most private feelings, laid open to her not by his choice but by the fact of what she was — the thing she was born with, the ability she'd never asked for, the same ability that had let her betray Sylvia and deceive Loid and read her mother's buried doubts about the Garden. And now, here, this: the unguarded tenderness of a man who was kissing her because he believed they were meeting each other equally.

They weren't. They had never been.

He pulled back eventually. Not far — his forehead against hers, both of them breathing hard. His thoughts were still warm and undefended and she could feel them like standing too close to an open fire.

"I don't know what this means for the operation," he said.

"I don't either."

"Probably nothing good."

"Probably not."

He laughed — quiet, close, just breath against her face. *I don't care. Whatever it costs. I don't care.*

She cared. She cared in a way he couldn't see and she couldn't explain and would never be able to justify. Because the feelings moving through her right now — the want, the tenderness, the specific ache of being close to someone who was close to her — she couldn't separate them from his. She'd been inside his mind for the most unguarded moment of his life, and her emotional responses were tangled with his emotional output, and she didn't know where his feelings ended and hers began.

That was the thing about telepathy that no one would ever understand, because no one knew. It didn't just give you information. It gave you feelings that weren't yours and made them indistinguishable from the ones that were. She had felt his loneliness, and now she felt lonely. She had felt his hope, and now she was hopeful. Was that connection? Or was it just contamination she'd mistaken for something real?

She thought about Loid. About the way he could compartmentalize — separate what he felt from what he did, keep the operation clean of personal debris. She'd trained under him for years, and she'd never learned that trick. Maybe you couldn't learn it when the personal debris arrived in other people's voices, uninvited, indistinguishable from your own thoughts.

She thought about Yor. About the stillness her mother carried after an assignment, the careful way she washed her hands, the gap between the sweetness and the thing underneath. Yor managed by not looking. Anya couldn't not look. That was her whole problem.

He settled back against the wall, his shoulder touching hers. The apartment was cold and neither of them moved to find a blanket or turn on a heater that probably didn't work anyway. His thoughts were drifting toward sleep — the exhaustion of adrenaline and injury finally catching up — and as they drifted, the name surfaced again. *Anya Forger.* Quiet this time. Almost fond. Not a deduction anymore, just a feeling. Like a song you used to know the words to.

She sat very still beside him and listened to his thoughts slow and thin toward sleep. The name came and went. Came and went. Each time, she waited for it to crystallize into certainty, for him to open his eyes and say *I know who you are.*

He didn't. He fell asleep with his head tilted sideways against the wall and his hand resting against hers on the mattress, the dried blood on his jaw cracking at the edges where his skin moved. Anya could hear the neighbor's television still going — the sportscaster had given way to a late-night talk show, murmured voices she couldn't make out.

She stayed awake. Her ribs ached when she breathed. His blood was on her fingers from the butterfly strips, dried now, brown at the margins of her nails. The bare bulb overhead buzzed at a frequency that had become part of the room's silence.

She had kissed him. She had read his mind while she kissed him. She had heard him think her real name and she had kept kissing him anyway. These were facts. She arranged them in order and they did not become less true.

Tomorrow she'd think about the ambush — about who had access to his schedule, about the chain of intelligence that had placed two military-trained killers on exactly the right block at exactly the right moment. Tomorrow she'd think about the mole inside WISE, and about the gaps in the operational picture that someone was exploiting, and about whether those gaps connected to a false report she'd filed in a debriefing room months ago while reading her handler's trust. Tomorrow she would be the operative again.

Tonight she sat in a cold apartment beside a man who was dreaming of a girl he used to know, and she let the name *Anya Forger* move through his sleeping thoughts like a current she couldn't stop, and she did not move, and she could not tell whether what she felt was love or its echo.
