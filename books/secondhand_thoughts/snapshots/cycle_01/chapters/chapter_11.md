# Chapter 11

Anya had spent three days inside WISE headquarters, and her head hurt in a way that was becoming structural.

Not the sharp spike of telepathic overload — a crowded reception, hundreds of minds pressing in at once. That was acute and temporary, like staring into bright light. This was different. Three days of reading her own colleagues one at a time, in engineered encounters she manufactured throughout the building, each requiring sustained close-range attention while pretending she was doing something ordinary. Waiting seven minutes by the third-floor elevator for a specific person to leave their desk. Timing her coffee run to coincide with someone else's. Standing near a conversation pretending to look at her phone, except the conversation was inside someone's skull and the phone was a prop and the hovering was the most invasive thing she had ever done on purpose.

She sat in her cubicle on the fourth floor with her coffee going cold and an access log spreadsheet open on her screen and rubbed her left temple with two fingers. The fluorescents buzzed. Someone's reheated fish from the break room was staging an olfactory assault on the entire floor. In the corridor outside, two analysts were arguing about sourcing methodology, and underneath their spoken words she could hear one of them thinking about a dog he'd had to put down last week and the other one worrying about a credit card bill that was overdue.

This was the thing about WISE headquarters. You could read the building all day and get nothing operational. People at spy agencies spent most of their mental energy on the same things everyone else did — errands, grievances, relationships, the small accounting of daily survival. The intelligence work happened in bursts, and between the bursts: lunch plans, parking validation, whether anyone had replaced the toilet paper on the third floor.

The thought-fragment from the diplomatic reception — *the asset inside WISE* — was what had started this. A reference to a mole, from an unidentifiable thinker in a crowd too dense to triangulate. She couldn't go back to that moment and isolate the source. She'd tried, lying in bed the night of the reception, rerunning the memory, but telepathy didn't work like a recording. It was experienced in real time and retained imperfectly, the way you couldn't reliably reconstruct which voice in a crowd had said a particular word.

So she'd started the other way. Paper first, the way Loid had taught her since she was sixteen: before you use the cheat, do the work. Access logs. Communication intercepts. Briefing attendance records cross-referenced against the timeline of specific intelligence that had reached the Renewal Front's operational pipeline. Which WISE operations had the Front anticipated, and who had been briefed on those operations beforehand? Grosz's intelligence — the picture of the Front's military-civilian structure and defense-contract funding channels that Damian had relayed to her — narrowed the question further. If the mole was passing intelligence that served the Front's specific procurement and positioning needs, the access pattern should overlap with those channels.

Eleven analysts had both the clearance and the operational access. She'd spent two weeks on the paperwork before she started reading anyone. Eleven became seven on access timing alone — two had been on postings outside Berlint during key leak windows, two more lacked compartment access to the operations the Front had anticipated. From seven she brought it to five, and then to three, through brief telepathic reads of colleagues whose surface thoughts were transparent and ordinary: professional tedium, private worries, the unremarkable inner weather of people whose worst secret was a parking ticket or an unfinished report.

Anna-Lise Berger she'd read in a shared elevator. Anya had timed her departure to coincide with Berger leaving the fifth-floor meeting room, pressed the button for the wrong floor so she'd have extra time, and spent forty seconds standing next to a woman she'd eaten lunch with maybe a dozen times, reading her most private thoughts without so much as asking. Berger was thinking about a posting she'd applied for in Vienna — the rejection still fresh, her resentment at the hiring committee specific and detailed, names she was mentally ranking by how much she disliked them. Underneath that: a worry about whether her mother's memory was getting worse or just her imagination, a dentist appointment she'd been postponing for three months, and a faint persistent hum of professional dissatisfaction that had nothing to do with espionage and everything to do with being forty-three and feeling overlooked.

Anya had stepped out of the elevator knowing that Anna-Lise Berger was not a spy. She also stepped out knowing things about Berger that Berger had chosen not to share with anyone — her mother's decline, her professional bitterness, the small humiliations she replayed privately. Berger had smiled at her when the doors opened. A normal smile. A colleague's smile. She didn't know that Anya had just been inside the part of her mind she showed no one.

That left two. Martin Dreher and Gerald Falk.

---

Dreher was fifty-one and worked in signals analysis on the third floor. Anya knew him the way you know office colleagues — assembled from years of incidental detail rather than deliberate attention. His coffee order was black with two sugars in the styrofoam cups that made everyone wince. He had a photo on his desk: his wife, two teenage sons, and a Golden Retriever whose expression in the picture suggested professional disappointment in the photographer. He hummed when he was concentrating, a tuneless repetitive drone that his third-floor neighbors tolerated because he was good at his job and brought in pastries from a bakery near his apartment on Fridays. He'd been at WISE for fifteen years. He was steady, competent, and the kind of person you'd describe by listing his habits rather than his qualities, because his qualities were the quiet kind that didn't announce themselves.

She found him in the third-floor break room on a Tuesday morning. He was standing by the coffee machine with the particular stillness of someone who'd walked in for a reason and gotten lost somewhere between the door and the mug rack. The mug in his hand was empty. He was looking at the motivational poster above the microwave — an eagle soaring over a mountain with the word PERSPECTIVE beneath it in bold sans-serif — but his eyes had the flat distance of someone who was seeing something else entirely.

Anya poured herself a cup of coffee. She stood close enough.

His thoughts hit her like stepping into cold water.

*—told me Tuesday and it's Tuesday and I still haven't called because what if the second panel is worse than the— three weeks maybe four before it becomes obvious. Kessler said three to four weeks before I can't— Eva sat with her mother for nine months, nine months watching her disappear, and I won't— I can't put her through—*

The fragment broke apart and reconstituted into something controlled:

*Budget meeting at two. Need the Baltic assessment. Focus.*

But the controlled thought was thin, like paper over a hole, and beneath it:

*—the referral. I need the second referral before I can start and the referral means paperwork and the paperwork means the WISE medical plan and the plan flags claims to HR and if HR flags it I'm pulled from active analysis and I can't— I've been paying cash. The cash is running— Kessler's office is at four. I'll tell Eva I'm staying late for the Wilkinson debrief. She'll believe that. She always—*

The thoughts dissolved into something that wasn't language anymore. Fear. Not the word, the thing itself — formless and enormous, the kind that lived underneath every other thought and made the surface tremble.

What Anya was reading was concealment. Active, deliberate concealment. He was hiding something from his wife. He was scheduling covert meetings. He was tracking a timeline — *three to four weeks.* He was paying cash to avoid a paper trail. His anxiety wasn't diffuse or situational; it was specific and structural, organized around a single secret he was spending most of his waking energy protecting.

Her fingers pressed into the styrofoam cup hard enough that it creaked.

"Hey, Martin."

He startled. The mug jerked and the focus came back to his face in layers, like someone surfacing from deep water. "Anya. Hi. Morning."

"You okay? You look a little..."

"Fine." Too fast. "Just a long week. You know how it is."

*Please don't look at me right now. Don't ask. I can't do this in the break room. I can't—*

"Sure," Anya said. "Get some rest."

She took her coffee back upstairs and sat with the certainty building in her chest like a stone settling into mud.

---

She was nearly sure.

Dreher had the access. Signals analysis put him squarely in the intelligence streams the Renewal Front needed — Ostanian military positioning, border activity, procurement channels. His schedule had changed recently; she checked the third-floor log-in records and found irregular hours, late departures, two-hour gaps on afternoons that corresponded to nothing in his meeting calendar. The concealment she'd read wasn't ambient personality. It was active, sustained, and consuming the majority of his mental bandwidth. He was running a double life — secret meetings, cash payments, an information timeline he was managing with the careful precision of someone trained to manage operational timelines.

It fit. It all fit. Surface thoughts of anxiety, concealment, covert meetings, a declining timeline — the pattern was what you'd expect from a compromised asset under increasing operational pressure.

She wrote a memo. Careful, stripped of anything that would reveal her source. She cited behavioral irregularities, schedule anomalies, the access profile overlap. She noted that Dreher's demeanor had shifted over the preceding three weeks in ways consistent with acute operational stress. She framed everything as analytical observation — the kind that any attentive colleague might produce — and hand-delivered it to the internal security liaison, a woman named Kurtz whose office was on the second floor and whose face had the practiced neutrality of someone whose job was to suspect the people around her.

Two analysts from counterintelligence were pulled from the Ostanian desk the next morning to run a focused investigation on Dreher. An IT specialist was reassigned to audit his terminal access logs and communication metadata. The third-floor signals team had a gap in their rotation while Dreher was placed under enhanced monitoring he didn't know about, surrounded by colleagues who smiled at him the way they always did while someone examined his entire professional life under a light he hadn't consented to.

Two days. The investigation ran for two days.

On the evening of the second day, Kurtz's report appeared in Anya's secure inbox. She opened it at her desk, read the first three lines, and walked to the fourth-floor bathroom. She locked herself in the end stall and sat on the closed toilet lid and read the rest under the hard white light that buzzed overhead.

Martin Dreher was not selling intelligence to the Renewal Front.

Martin Dreher had been diagnosed with pancreatic cancer three weeks earlier. Stage III. His oncologist — Dr. Kessler, at a private clinic on the other side of Berlint — had given him a treatment timeline that began with additional diagnostic imaging and ended with a conversation he had not yet been able to have with his wife. He'd been paying for consultations in cash to avoid triggering the WISE medical plan's automated notifications to HR, because a flagged claim would pull him from active analysis and he wasn't ready for that — wasn't ready for any of it, for the offices and the sympathy and the way people would start looking at him as someone who was leaving. The covert meetings were oncology appointments. The irregular schedule was treatment planning. The concealment was a fifty-one-year-old man trying to hold his ordinary life together for a few more weeks before he had to sit his wife down and tell her it was happening again, the thing she'd watched destroy her mother, except this time it was him.

The timeline — *three to four weeks* — was not an operational window for intelligence delivery. It was how long his doctor estimated before the symptoms would become physically apparent and the secret would keep itself no longer.

Anya sat in the stall with the folder in her lap. The ventilation pushed warm stale air through the ceiling vent. Someone walked into the bathroom, used the sink, left. In the corridor outside, two people discussed something about the Denmark file.

She had read him perfectly. Every fragment of his surface thoughts was accurate. The fear, the concealment, the secret meetings, the cash payments, the timeline — all real. All true. All pointing toward the thing he was hiding. Her telepathy hadn't given her a wrong answer. It had given her exactly what Martin Dreher was thinking about, which was his own death, and she had looked at a man drowning in the most private terror of his life and diagnosed treason.

Surface thoughts were not truth. They were not intentions, not identity, not the reason underneath the feeling. They were what a person was afraid of at any given moment, and people were afraid of what haunted them, not what defined them. A dying man's anxiety and a traitor's anxiety wore the same face. Her telepathy couldn't tell the difference because there was no difference — not on the surface, and the surface was all she had ever been able to read.

Two days of investigative resources. Two counterintelligence analysts pulled from the Ostanian desk, where they'd been tracking the intelligence channels the Renewal Front was actually using. An IT specialist diverted to audit the terminal logs of a man who was spending his lunch breaks researching survival rates. Two days in which the actual mole — whoever and wherever they were — had operated with two fewer people watching for them.

She thought of the photo on his desk. His wife. The two boys, one of whom had a football tournament on Saturday that his father had been privately bargaining with the universe to attend. The Golden Retriever with the judgmental expression. She had read the most guarded thoughts of a man whose private world was collapsing and turned them into a referral to counterintelligence, and the referral had been processed by colleagues who didn't know Dreher the way she did — who didn't know the Friday pastries or the tuneless humming or the way he said "long week" while holding his empty mug like an anchor.

She flushed the toilet she hadn't used. Washed her hands. The face in the mirror looked like hers but belonged to someone she trusted less than she had a week ago.

---

Gerald Falk was drinking tea in the far corner of the fourth-floor bullpen the next morning, reading a cable digest, when Anya found a reason to stand near him.

The reason was a printout she didn't need from the shared printer, which sat eight feet from Falk's preferred corner desk. She collected the pages slowly, aligning them, and stood in his radius.

He was the most familiar presence in the building. Twenty-three years at WISE — the kind of institutional tenure that turned a person into part of the architecture. People referenced his assessments the way they referenced standing policy, as things that had always existed and probably always would. He had thinning hair, reading glasses he wore low on his nose, and the posture of a man who'd spent the better part of three decades at desks and had made a kind of peace with what that did to the spine. He wore the same grey cardigan most days, the one with a pulled thread at the left cuff that nobody mentioned and he never fixed.

Anya had known him since she was twelve. He'd been at her Eden Academy graduation ceremony — not in any official capacity, just a colleague of Loid's who came because that's what you did for the families of people you'd worked alongside for years. Afterward, while other parents crowded the courtyard for photos, he'd found her near the refreshments table and given her a fountain pen. Silver barrel, heavier than she expected, her initials engraved in small letters on the clip. "For signing important things," he'd said, in that mild way of his, and she'd thought it was the most adult gift anyone had ever given her. She'd used it to sign her WISE intake forms two years later, sitting at a desk in this same building while her hand shook with something she'd mistaken for excitement.

"You look tired," Falk said now, not looking up from his digest. "You should eat breakfast. You always skip."

*Budget allocation for Q3 is overstated by twelve percent. Same pattern as last year. Sylvia won't challenge the escalation projections because they serve the funding cycle, same as always. Pension committee at noon — the actuarial adjustment needs review. Is it raining? Looks like it might rain.*

Anya stood near the printer, pages in hand, reading.

"I'm fine," she said. "Long week."

"Isn't there always."

*The Wilkinson assessment was sloppy. Geller sourced it from a single intercept without corroboration. Someone ought to redo the whole thing. I'll raise it at the committee meeting.*

She stayed. Thirty seconds. Forty-five. A minute. His thoughts moved through budget irritation, a colleague's substandard work, the pension committee, the weather, whether the cafeteria had changed its soup rotation on Tuesdays. There was no turbulence. No concealment. No secret currents running beneath the bureaucratic surface, no doors she could sense that were closed against her. Just a man thinking the thoughts of a man who had spent twenty-three years in an intelligence bureaucracy and had settled into its rhythms the way furniture settles into carpet — the impression so deep it was invisible.

*Anya still doesn't eat enough. Loid was the same way at her age — all work, no meals. Some things pass down whether you mean them to or not.*

The thought was warm. Avuncular. She almost flinched from it.

With Dreher, the surface had been a storm. Everything pointed inward, toward a hidden thing. The sheer volume of anxiety had made her certain, and the certainty had been wrong, and now she was standing near a man whose mind was a quiet room and couldn't determine whether quiet meant innocent or whether quiet was what disciplined concealment looked like after twenty-three years of practice.

The ambiguity was worse than confirmation would have been. Confirmation she could act on. Silence was just silence, and it told her nothing about what might or might not exist beneath it.

She walked back to her desk.

Falk's initials were on a memo she'd noticed earlier that week — a reallocation of analytical coverage from the Ostanian diplomatic sector. The memo cited her own assessment, the one she'd filed to protect Damian, as justification: since the field report had reduced the sector's threat profile, resources could be redirected to higher-priority channels. It was standard procedure. It was exactly what any senior analyst would recommend when a field operative's report deprioritized an area. The two analysts reassigned from those channels had been covering intelligence streams that, Anya knew from her work with Damian, the Renewal Front was actively using.

She'd seen the memo days ago and felt the guilt then — her false report was becoming institutional fact, redirecting real analysts from real intelligence work. Her lie had created a gap in coverage, and Falk had formalized the gap with a bureaucratically correct memo that made her dishonesty into standing policy. She looked at his initials and felt what she had felt before: the sick, specific weight of watching her own deception harden into something other people acted on. He was following available intelligence. That the intelligence was a lie she'd told was her failure, not evidence of his.

She sat at her desk in the bullpen's overhead hum and understood, with a clarity that felt more like nausea than insight, what she had been doing for the past week. She had been reading her colleagues' minds — their fears, their frustrations, their private medical crises, their small grievances and buried shames — without their knowledge or permission, to identify a person who was betraying WISE's institutional trust through an undisclosed advantage. And she was conducting this investigation as someone who had been betraying that same institutional trust since her first day: through a power she had never disclosed, accountable to no one, appointed by no one, performing surveillance that no one had sanctioned or could review. She was the organization's secret internal eye, and she had been wrong about Dreher, and she couldn't confirm Falk, and the only certainty the week had produced was that she had violated the privacy of four people who trusted her and gotten nothing usable in return.

She opened her laptop and started rereading the access logs, because the alternative to working was thinking, and thinking was not available to her right now.

---

The encrypted call came at nine that evening. One-time cipher, routed through a frequency they'd agreed on — Anya had to dig through the small notebook in her bag to find the current key because she'd mixed up the rotation order and entered the wrong sequence twice before the connection cleared. She sat on the kitchen floor of her apartment with her back against the cabinet and the phone pressed to her ear and waited through three seconds of static.

Damian's voice was careful, stripped of the diplomatic warmth that was his default register. He sounded like someone talking from a room he'd swept for devices and still didn't fully trust.

"The weather's changed faster than the forecast."

"How fast?"

"Our friend at the garden center has moved up the planting schedule. Significantly. There's a neighborhood event being organized. Something visible. Something designed to make the people across the street very unhappy."

The Renewal Front was engineering a diplomatic incident. Something public, something built to look like Westalian provocation. *Neighborhood event* was the operation. *People across the street* was Westalis. The code words strained against what they were holding, like a bag packed too full.

"When?"

A pause. She could hear him breathing. She couldn't read his thoughts over the phone — telepathy needed proximity, line of sight or close to it — and working without it felt like trying to see in a room where someone had taken half the lights. She was left with his voice, his pauses, the choices he made about which words came first. The way everyone else navigated conversation all the time.

"The forecast says this weekend. Midweek at the latest. It's..."

"Days."

"Days. Not weeks. The planting season moved."

The word sat between them in the connection static.

"I had a setback on the personnel issue," she said. She hadn't planned to tell him. But his voice on the other end of the line — careful, tired, not performing — made her want to be honest about the one thing she could be honest about without revealing everything else.

"What kind?"

"I spent two days looking at someone and I was sure. And it wasn't — what he was hiding was personal. Private. I shouldn't have been looking."

"That's two days out of a very short schedule."

"Yeah."

"Do you still have candidates?"

"One I can't rule out. One I can't confirm either. His... garden's very tidy."

"Tidy gardens aren't always what they look like."

She almost laughed. "That's really not helpful."

"I know it's not."

A silence. The kitchen faucet dripped — a problem she'd been meaning to fix for two weeks and kept not fixing, which was the kind of domestic neglect that would have made her laugh in a different month. A spy running a cross-border intelligence operation who couldn't manage a leaking tap.

"If the neighborhood event goes off before we—"

"If it goes off," Damian said, "our friend at the garden center gets enough neighborhood support to change the whole community board. Permanently. And they'll have the backing to do it."

If the diplomatic incident succeeded, the Renewal Front would have political cover for military posturing. Both governments pushed closer to a confrontation that neither population wanted but that the people who profited from tension had been engineering for months. The gardening metaphor was buckling under the weight.

"I need to identify the personnel issue first," Anya said.

"Can you? In this window?"

"I don't know." She said it flat, without the operational confidence her training told her to project. Under the code words and the cipher and the carefully maintained distance, this was what their alliance actually sounded like: two people who had been honest enough with each other to hear the gap when the honesty ran out.

"Be careful." The diplomatic smoothness dropped from his voice. What remained sounded younger, less curated. Just a man on the other end of a phone who was running out of room.

"You too."

She ended the call. The phone was warm against her palm. Outside, a tram passed somewhere below, its bell thin and distant in the autumn dark. Upstairs, her neighbor dropped something heavy and swore, a muffled thud followed by a word she didn't need telepathy to understand.

Three days reading people's private thoughts. Two of those days spent on a man whose secret was his own death. A counterintelligence investigation that had produced nothing except Anya's knowledge of Martin Dreher's pancreatic cancer and Anna-Lise Berger's resentment about Vienna and the specific depth of professional frustration that a forty-three-year-old woman carried privately. And against these: Falk's quiet thoughts about pensions and budgets and whether she was eating enough, orderly and warm and completely unreadable, and a reallocation memo bearing his initials that cited her own lie as institutional justification for moving analysts off the channels the enemy was using.

She didn't know if Falk was the mole. She didn't know if the nothing she'd read in his mind was innocence or whether twenty-three years of institutional routine could make a performance invisible even to a telepath. She knew that her telepathy — the ability she had relied on her entire life, the thing that made her valuable, the secret advantage that justified every other secret — had just spent a week producing a wrong answer, no answer, and a catalog of her colleagues' private suffering, and she couldn't tell which of those outcomes frightened her most.

Days. Not weeks.

She turned off the kitchen light. The apartment was dark and quiet and for once the silence was external — pipes, a distant radio, the neighbor settling down — all of it belonging to people whose thoughts she could not reach from here.

Her head still hurt. But it was the normal kind now, the kind that came from staring at screens and not eating and being twenty years old and tired. She'd take it.

She went to bed and lay in the dark and did not sleep for a long time.
