# Chapter 17

The first real thaw came in the third week of March. Anya noticed it on the walk to the diplomatic quarter — not in the temperature, which still needed a coat and gloves, but in the thoughts. All winter Berlint had thought in a tight, circling register: the arrests, the investigations, the question of what the governments would actually do versus what they said they would. Now the city's collective mood had loosened into something scattered and ordinary. A woman on the tram was thinking about paint samples for her hallway. The man standing next to Anya was worried about his mother's hip surgery — the specific worry of a son who lived forty minutes away and should visit more often. A teenager near the doors was thinking about a girl and also about whether his jacket looked stupid, both thoughts running at equal intensity, and Anya had to turn her face toward the window so he wouldn't see her almost smile.

Nobody was thinking about the Renewal Front. Two months ago, the name had surfaced in every crowd she walked through — in anxious fragments, in half-formed opinions about the border, in the particular fear of people who remembered what the last escalation had felt like before Operation Strix had paused it. Now the arrests had moved into courtrooms and committees. Werner Voss's name appeared in the papers less often, and when it appeared it was attached to procedural language — indictments, discovery motions, jurisdictional disputes between Ostanian and Westalian legal authorities who couldn't agree on who had been defrauded worse. Both governments had committed to the appearance of accountability. Both governments had also quietly buried the parts of the investigation that touched their own defense procurement budgets, because the bilateral rot didn't make for good headlines on either side. Anya knew this because she read intelligence briefs, and she also knew it because she rode the tram and the people on the tram had stopped caring, which meant the governments could afford to stop performing.

Damian's name still appeared, but the texture of the coverage had changed. In the first weeks after the summit, the stories had been sharp — the Desmond heir who exposed the conspiracy, or the Desmond heir who betrayed his own country's leadership, depending on the paper and the border it was published behind. Now the stories were softer, more procedural. He'd been invited to three separate ministerial consultations about intelligence reform. He'd also received two death threats. He'd told her about both categories in the same sentence, in the same flat tone, like items on a list he was tired of reading. His political position had settled into something that wasn't heroism and wasn't disgrace — it was the complicated middle, the space where people respected what you did but couldn't forgive you for making them look at what they'd ignored. The Desmond name meant something different now. Damian hadn't decided yet what he wanted it to mean.

The WISE building looked the same from outside. Unremarkable commercial facade on a street of unremarkable commercial facades. Inside, the restructuring showed. Falk's desk in the analyst pool had been cleared within days of his arrest, but the absence still had a shape — a gap in the row that nobody had filled, as if the furniture itself was evidence. The analyst pool had been reshuffled. New faces. New security protocols. A review board Sylvia had assembled with the grim efficiency of a woman who had discovered her house was full of rot and intended to find every board that had gone soft. The coffee in the break room was the same. Everything else felt different enough to notice.

Sylvia was in her office. Reading glasses on the chain around her neck, a file open on the desk that she closed when Anya entered. The file was thick. Anya didn't look at the label, but she caught Sylvia's thought — *operational history, the gaps, the pattern I should have caught two years ago* — before Sylvia shifted, deliberately, to the business at hand.

"Sit down."

Anya sat.

"The review board has completed its assessment of your record. Your field status is reinstated, effective immediately. The cover identity remains intact — the summit exposure was contained to political channels, not intelligence ones." Sylvia's voice was measured, institutional, precise. The same as it had always been. But something in the delivery had changed. Not coldness — Sylvia had never been warm in any conventional sense. What was gone was the ease. The automatic certainty that had once lived underneath the professional tone, the part that said *I trust your judgment* without ever needing to say it aloud.

That certainty had been addressed in a conversation two weeks ago, in this same office. Forty minutes. Sylvia had not raised her voice. She had laid out the false report, the discrepancies another agency had flagged, Falk's exploitation of the gaps Anya created, and the causal chain that linked those gaps to operational failures — to Agent Crane's death. She had asked Anya to explain. Anya had explained. She'd sat across from Sylvia and told the truth about Damian, about the alliance, about why she'd filed the misleading assessment — everything except the telepathy, which she'd hidden by attributing her intelligence access to tradecraft and intuition. Sylvia had listened. She'd asked three precise questions. And then she'd sat in silence, and Anya's telepathy had made that silence worse — because she could hear Sylvia thinking about trust, about the sixteen-year-old girl she'd watched develop into a field agent, about whether the instincts she'd praised for two years had been real or had been something she'd never be allowed to understand. *She's good. She made a choice that cost people their lives. She's also twenty years old and I have been twenty years old making choices I believed in and I was wrong too, and I didn't have anyone's blood on my wrong.*

Sylvia had not forgiven her. That wasn't the right word. Sylvia had assessed the situation, decided Anya was an asset worth retaining, and filed the personal injury in the same locked drawer where she kept every other cost of thirty years in intelligence. The injury was still there. Anya could hear it whenever she sat across from Sylvia now — a slight wariness in the texture of her thoughts, the way you might watch a bridge that you'd been told was safe but that you'd once seen crack.

"Thank you," Anya said.

"Do not thank me. Do the work."

Anya left the building and walked south toward home. The air was cold but the sky had the particular pale quality of late winter starting to remember what light looked like — not spring yet, not in Berlint, where spring arrived late and grudging, but the suggestion of it, the way a room gets brighter before someone opens the curtain. She passed through the diplomatic quarter's polished streets and into the residential blocks where the buildings were older and shorter and the sidewalks had cracks that the city never quite got around to fixing.

She checked her phone. A message from Yor: *I'm trying a new recipe! Don't eat before you come home!* A message from Loid, sent twelve minutes later: *I've prepared side dishes. In case you want options.*

She called Damian on the walk. He picked up on the second ring.

"Six o'clock," she said.

"Six. I'll be there."

"Don't bring wine. Papa will judge the label."

A pause. "I wasn't going to bring wine."

"You were absolutely going to bring wine."

Another pause, longer. "What kind does he drink?"

Anya laughed. It came out easily, without the awareness of who might hear or what it might reveal. She'd been laughing more easily since the conversation with Damian — the real one, the one that changed the shape of everything. He knew about the telepathy. He knew, and he'd chosen to stay, and now when she laughed she didn't have to account for whether the laugh was something she'd absorbed from his feelings or something that was hers. It was hers. She was almost sure.

"Red," she said. "Nothing with a year on the label he might actually recognize."

She hung up and walked the rest of the way home through streets full of people she could hear thinking about paint and surgery and dogs and whether the tram schedule had changed again, and for once none of it pressed on her like something she was responsible for carrying.

---

The Forger apartment smelled like burning onions and something else — ginger, maybe, or a spice Yor had found at the market and added in an amount the recipe didn't recommend and Yor's hands had overruled.

"Mama made curry," Anya said from the hallway as she hung up her coat. She could tell from the smell. She could also tell from Loid's thought, clear as a headline: *The potatoes are raw in the middle. I checked one when she wasn't looking. The rice is ready.*

"Your mother worked very hard on it," Loid said from the kitchen. He was setting out bowls — the ceramic set they used when someone was coming for dinner. Five bowls. Five place settings. The table looked different with an extra chair wedged between Anya's usual seat and the wall.

Yor came out of the kitchen with her sleeves pushed up past her elbows and a streak of curry on her cheek. "Anya! You're early. Oh, that's good. Is six too early? I told Damian six. Should I have said six-thirty? Your father said six was fine but I keep thinking—"

"Six is fine, Mama."

"Okay. Okay, good. The curry is almost ready. It might be a little..." She looked at something in the middle distance, reconsidered, and redirected. "It's going to be really good."

Her thought: *Please don't let it be too spicy. I tasted it and I couldn't feel my tongue for a minute but maybe that's just me. Loid made rice. Thank god for the rice.*

Anya kissed her mother's cheek — the one without the curry — and went to find Bond. He was in the living room on the rug near the heater, his large white body occupying most of the space between the couch and the coffee table. He was old now. Slower in everything, thicker around the middle, his legs stiff in the mornings in a way that made Anya's chest hurt to watch. He raised his head when she knelt beside him and his tail moved once, twice, with the careful economy of a dog who had learned that enthusiasm cost more than it used to.

She scratched behind his ears. His thoughts were soft, warm, unhurried — images more than words, which was how Bond had always thought. The shape of the apartment. The smell of food. The specific warmth of the rug beneath him. A flicker of something brighter surfaced: a precognitive fragment, shapeless and golden, like sun through a window that hadn't been opened yet. Tomorrow, or next week, or a feeling that wasn't attached to time at all. Anya couldn't tell what it meant. It felt kind.

"Good boy," she said. Bond closed his eyes and set his head on her knee.

The buzzer went at six-oh-three. Yor's thought arrived in a rush: *He's here. Do I look okay? Is there curry on my — why am I nervous? I've met him before. At the planning session. But that was different, that was operational. This is dinner. Normal dinner. Normal dinner with Anya's... person.*

Loid opened the door.

Damian stood in the hallway in a dark coat with his hair combed and a bottle of wine held in both hands like a man presenting evidence to a court. His thought hit Anya before his greeting hit the room: *Don't think about the wine. You brought the wine. She told you not to bring wine. You brought it anyway. Don't think about it.*

"Damian," Loid said. He extended his hand. The handshake was exactly the right duration and exactly the right pressure — Loid's handshakes always were, even the ones that weren't operational. His thought: *Firm grip. Nervous, which is appropriate. The coat is new. He dressed for this.*

"Thank you for having me, sir. Loid. Mr. Forger." Damian's face did something that wanted to be a recovery but landed closer to a flinch. His thought: *Pick one. You rehearsed this. You literally practiced on the walk over. 'Thank you, Loid.' See? Easy. The world's greatest spy is looking at you and you said three different names.*

"Come in," Loid said, and something in his voice — a warmth that was hard to distinguish from professional hospitality but wasn't, Anya was almost sure it wasn't — gave Damian room to enter without having to solve the name problem.

Yor appeared from the kitchen. "Damian! You came! Oh, you brought — that's so thoughtful. Loid, look, he brought wine."

Loid took the bottle. Looked at the label. His expression didn't change.

Damian's thought: *He's reading the label. She told me he'd read the label. She told me and I still brought wine and he's reading the label. Think about something else. Think about the apartment. The apartment is nice. It's warm. There's dog hair on the couch. That's normal. Normal families have dog hair on—*

"It's a nice bottle," Loid said, setting it on the counter. Whether this was true or diplomatic, the tone made impossible to determine.

They sat down. Yor served the curry with the careful pride of someone who had spent the afternoon on a project and needed it to have been worthwhile. The curry was dark and thick and the potatoes, as Loid's preliminary intelligence had indicated, were hard in their centers. The spice level was substantial. Anya took her first bite and her eyes watered immediately. She reached for the rice — plain, white, flawlessly cooked, set out in a separate bowl as if it had always been part of the plan and not Loid's quiet act of marital devotion.

Damian took a bite. His thought was a controlled scream: *Oh. Oh god. My mouth. My entire mouth is — okay. Okay. Smile. This is Anya's mother's cooking. Anya's mother who could kill you with this serving spoon. You are going to eat this and you are going to appreciate it and you are going to—* He reached for the water glass. Then: *Don't reach for the water, it looks like you can't handle it. Everyone drinks water at dinner. Just drink the water like a normal person. Why am I thinking about how to drink water? She can hear me thinking about how to drink water.*

"This is really good, Mrs. Forger," Damian said. His voice was steady. His eyes were not.

"Oh! Really? You don't think it's too — Loid said it might be a little—"

"It's got character," Loid said. He was eating with the methodical endurance of a man who had been consuming his wife's cooking for fourteen years and understood it as a form of love he was not permitted to refuse. His thought was mild, fond: *The curry is better than last month's attempt. Measurable improvement. The potatoes are a setback but the sauce is thicker.*

"I used less chili this time," Yor said, brightly.

Damian's ears were faintly pink. He took another bite. His thought, with the grim resolve of a man walking into hostile territory: *I am going to finish this plate. I am going to have a second serving. I am a diplomat. I have eaten worse at state functions. This is fine. My tongue is on fire and this is fine.*

"You're too thin," Yor said to him.

"I—"

"She says that to everyone," Anya said.

"I don't say it to everyone. I said it to Becky once and she was very pleased."

"That's different. Becky treats it as a compliment."

"Well, with Damian I mean it nutritionally." Yor pushed the curry pot closer to his side of the table. "Have more."

Damian had more.

The conversation found its rhythm the way dinners do — not through any organizing topic but through the current of small ones that carried people through the act of eating together. Yor asked about Damian's work; he gave the diplomatic version, measured and qualified and slightly too polished, and Yor nodded along with the genuine interest of someone who had never worked in politics and found it fascinating in the way she found cooking shows fascinating, which was completely and without irony. Loid asked a follow-up about the restructured ministerial oversight committee. The question sounded casual. It was not casual. Damian answered it well — specific enough to show competence, vague enough to protect operational details — and Loid asked another question, and another, and the questions built on each other with a precision that was invisible unless you could hear the thoughts behind them.

Loid's thought, threading through each exchange: *He understands institutional architecture. He knows which details to withhold, which means he's been trained or he's naturally cautious — probably both. She chose someone who thinks before he talks.* Then, quieter: *She chose someone. My daughter chose someone and brought him to this table.*

"Papa," Anya said.

"Hm?"

"You're doing the thing."

"I'm having a conversation."

"You're having a conversation with sub-questions and evaluation criteria."

Loid's expression was perfectly neutral, which was how you knew it was performed. "I'm interested in your friend's work."

"He's not my friend."

"Your colleague."

"He's not my colleague either."

Loid took a sip of wine. Damian's wine. "What term would you prefer?"

Anya opened her mouth and closed it. Damian's thought, unhelpfully: *She doesn't have a term. I don't have a term. We read each other's minds and saved the country and kissed in a safe house and I know she's a telepath and she knows I know she's a telepath and we don't have a term for this. Is there a word in Ostanian? There is not a word in Ostanian.*

"Damian's fine," Anya said. "Just Damian."

Loid inclined his head slightly and went back to his curry and did not ask any more sub-questions for the rest of the main course, which was its own form of mercy and also, Anya suspected from the faint amusement in his surface thoughts, its own form of victory.

Bond had migrated from the living room during the first course. He crossed the short hallway with the slow deliberation of an old dog navigating familiar terrain and settled himself on Damian's feet under the table — all of his considerable weight pressing down on Damian's dress shoes. Damian looked down. His thought went soft: *The dog is on my feet. The spy family's precognitive dog is sitting on my feet. This feels more significant than the ministerial consultations.* He didn't move. Bond sighed the deep, structural sigh of a large animal who had found warmth and intended to keep it, and Damian left his feet exactly where they were.

"He likes you," Yor said.

"Bond doesn't settle on just anyone," Loid added, and the comment landed with the specific weight of a statement that was both about the dog and not about the dog, and Damian's ears went pink again.

Yor cleared the curry plates and brought out fruit and tea. The conversation shifted to lighter things — Becky's spring fundraiser, which Anya had agreed to attend and which Damian had been invited to. Becky had apparently called him directly and told him she expected him to show up and that his tie from the summit aftermath event had been, in her words, a sartorial war crime.

"She said that?" Yor asked, delighted.

"She said it twice. And then she told me what color to wear."

"Becky's very good at organizing," Yor said. "She planned Anya's twentieth birthday party. The one with the — what was it, the ice sculpture?"

"It was a swan," Anya said. "It was enormous. It melted on the buffet table."

"It was beautiful before it melted," Yor said, loyally.

Damian's thought: *Anya's twentieth birthday. Three months ago. I didn't know her then. Or — I knew her, I just didn't know I knew her. She was reading minds at her birthday party with an ice swan. She's been reading minds her entire life.* He looked at her across the table and his managed thoughts fell apart in the way they kept falling apart — not catastrophically, just softly, like a stack of papers in a draft. *She's reading me right now. She knows I'm thinking about her birthday. I should think about something neutral. The tea. The tea is very hot. I am having a thought about tea temperature and that is all I am—*

His gaze snagged on her hair, the way the kitchen light caught the pink of it, and the neutral thought dissolved into: *— she's so — god damn it.*

Anya looked at her teacup. The surface of the tea trembled very slightly because her hand was not as steady as the rest of her, and she took a sip to cover it.

It was during tea that Loid's thought arrived.

They were talking about nothing in particular — the window that needed recaulking, a neighbor's renovation that had been going on for months, whether the tram schedule was actually changing or whether that was a rumor. The conversation drifted toward Anya's work, and Loid asked a question about her upcoming schedule that was headed somewhere she didn't want it to go — toward the specifics of her reinstatement, toward Sylvia, toward the bruised institutional territory she wasn't ready to discuss at the dinner table. She heard the question building in his thoughts before it reached his mouth, and she redirected. Smoothly. The way she'd been doing her entire life — catching where a conversation was heading and steering it somewhere safer before it arrived. She asked Yor about the film she wanted to see, which Anya already knew about because she'd read it in Yor's thoughts two minutes ago, and the conversation turned and nobody noticed.

Nobody except Loid.

His thought came through clearly, without preamble, the way Loid's thoughts always arrived — organized, precise, the product of a mind that had spent decades sorting information into patterns:

*She always knew exactly the right moment to change the subject. Even when she was small.*

Anya's hand stopped on her teacup.

The thought didn't elaborate. It sat there in Loid's mind the way a stone sits in a river — present, solid, worn smooth by years of current flowing over it. He was looking at her, but not with suspicion. Or not only with suspicion. His face was the one he wore when he was assembling something from incomplete information, and the process of assembly was so habitual he might not know he was doing it. He was a spy. He'd been a spy for longer than she'd been alive. He watched people for a living and he watched his daughter because he loved her, and the watching was the same skill applied to two different rooms, and he couldn't turn it off any more than she could turn off what she did.

*Even when she was small.*

She could hear him thinking about her. At six, ducking a question about how she'd known where Yor hid the birthday presents. At twelve, deflecting a conversation about a classmate's home trouble with uncanny timing. At sixteen, in a WISE training exercise, anticipating a scenario three moves ahead of the brief. The moments didn't assemble into a theory in his mind — not on the surface, not where she could read them. They assembled into a texture. A quality. The particular impression of a daughter who always knew things slightly before she should have, who always responded to the thing that hadn't quite been said yet, who always changed the subject at exactly the moment when not changing it would have revealed something she couldn't explain.

He wasn't thinking the word *telepath*. He wasn't thinking any word for it. He was thinking about the shape of a twenty-year pattern, and the pattern was detailed enough to be meaningful and vague enough to be coincidence, and whether it led somewhere definite or just lived in his mind as one of those things a father notices and files and never presses — Anya couldn't tell. Because the conclusion, if there was a conclusion, was not a surface thought. It was deep. It was in the place where Loid kept the things he had chosen not to examine fully, the locked room in his operational mind where the spy's pattern recognition and the father's willful blindness had reached an arrangement she was not permitted to see. Her telepathy read what people were thinking. It did not read what they had decided not to think.

She would sit at this table and read his thoughts for as long as they both lived, and she might never know.

"Anya?" Yor said. "Do you want more tea?"

"I'm good." She looked at her cup. The tea was cold. She drank it anyway, because it gave her something to do with her hands and her face while Loid's thought moved on — to the dishes, to the window caulking, to the specific temperature at which Yor preferred her tea and whether the kettle was still warm enough.

His thought, as he reached to refill Yor's cup with the absent precision of a man who had memorized this particular domestic measurement: *Five at the table. It's different. I don't mind it.*

Yor was thinking about Yuri.

Not actively — not in the focused, articulate way that surfaced thoughts as readable sentences. It was underneath the evening, the way weather was underneath a conversation about weather. You could talk about films and tram schedules, but the sky was still doing what it was doing. She hadn't called Yuri since the summit. He hadn't called her. The longest silence between them since he was a teenager, and the silence said more than a phone call could have. He saw what she was. She knew he saw. Whether he would report what he'd witnessed — his sister killing three men with the specific, practiced efficiency of someone who had been doing it for twenty years — or whether he would omit it from his file and carry it instead, the way the Briar family had always carried things, in the body rather than in the record — that was a question Yor had decided to hold rather than force.

Anya read it in the way Yor held her teacup: both hands wrapped around the ceramic, firm, grounding herself in something warm and present and hers. *He'll call when he's ready. Or he won't. I won't make him. He's my brother. That doesn't change because of what he saw.*

Anya looked at her mother. The streak of curry still on her wrist that she'd missed. The sleeves still pushed up past her elbows, showing the small pale scars on her hands that she explained as cooking accidents, which was not entirely untrue. The particular gentleness of a woman who could kill with precision that bordered on art and chose instead to spend three hours making curry for her daughter's person because she wanted tonight to be nice.

Anya could tell them.

The thought arrived not as a decision but as a door she'd been walking past for twenty years and was only now stopping to look at. She could sit at this table and say: *I can hear your thoughts. I've always been able to hear your thoughts. Every kind thing you thought about me when you believed you were thinking it privately — I heard it. Every worry, every doubt, every moment you thought you were alone inside your own head in this apartment — I was there. I have been inside the most private part of you for my entire life and I never asked permission and I never told you.*

Yor would say *Oh.* And then she'd be quiet, and then she'd ask questions, and she'd be confused and possibly hurt in a way she wouldn't know how to name, and then she'd make more tea because that was what Yor did when the world cracked. Loid would go still. Not the stillness of anger — the stillness of a man rapidly reclassifying twenty years of data, reprocessing every interaction, every hunch, every time he'd thought his daughter was unusually perceptive and filed it under *gifted* rather than *impossible.* And both of them would understand, eventually — because this family was built from secrets that had become true, and the architecture had held under worse.

But she wasn't ready.

Not because she was afraid of their reaction. She was afraid of what the telling would do to every moment that came before it. Twenty years of reading her parents' thoughts without their knowledge. Twenty years of knowing Loid's fear and Yor's doubt and Bond's warm, formless dreams. Every time she'd said the right thing at the right time, every time she'd known what they needed before they asked — all of it would change in the retelling. It wouldn't be love anymore, or not only love. It would also be a violation they'd have to find a way to live inside, the way she had been living inside it her entire life.

That wasn't a conversation for tonight. Not with the curry still on Yor's wrist and Bond asleep on Damian's feet and the apartment fogged with warmth and cooking and the particular domestic gravity of five people in a room that had held four for fourteen years and was learning the weight of one more. She'd have the conversation. She knew she would. But not tonight, and the honesty of that — the refusal to pretend she was ready when she wasn't — felt like the most truthful thing she'd done in a long time.

Damian was watching her. His thought, for once, was not managed or redirected or policed. It was just there, open, in the way his thoughts had started to be open around her since he'd learned the truth — not because he'd stopped caring what she heard, but because he'd decided that being heard was the price of being with her, and he'd decided to pay it: *She's thinking about something heavy. I can't help with it. I can be here, though.*

Anya looked at him. He looked back. His managed thoughts didn't fall apart this time because they hadn't been managed to begin with. What she read was simple and warm and his: *I'm here. Her dad is a spy and her mom tried to kill me with curry and the dog won't get off my feet and I chose this. I'd choose it again.*

Something in her chest did a thing she didn't have a clinical word for and didn't want one. She held his gaze for a moment and then she looked down at her cold tea, and when she looked up again Yor was washing dishes and Loid was drying them with the synchronized choreography of two people who had been cleaning this kitchen together for so long that the movements had worn grooves in the air. Yor washed. Loid dried. Yor handed. Loid stacked. Neither of them spoke. They didn't need to, and not because of telepathy — because of fourteen years of practice at being beside each other in a small room.

Bond dreamed. His large body twitched once on Damian's feet — a precognitive ripple, or just an old dog chasing something in his sleep. Anya read the dream: bright, warm, shapeless, the color of morning in a room she didn't recognize. It wasn't tomorrow. It might have been next week, or next year, or just the general optimism of a dog who had been warm and fed and was now asleep in a house full of people who loved him. It was the kindest thought she'd heard all day, and it came from someone who didn't know he was thinking it.

Damian had finished his cold tea. He was holding the empty cup in both hands the way Yor held hers — both palms around the ceramic, grounding himself. She hadn't told him Yor did that. He'd just picked it up, the way people pick things up from the rooms they sit in, and the fact that he was already absorbing the Forger household's habits without knowing it made Anya feel something she couldn't attribute to telepathy or to anyone else's emotions leaking into hers. It was hers. She was sure.

The evening wound down the way evenings do — coats found, shoes retrieved from beneath a reluctant dog, Yor pressing leftover curry into a container Damian tried to refuse and could not, Loid shaking his hand at the door with the same measured grip and a thought that Anya almost didn't catch because it was quiet and quick and already moving on: *He'll do. He's not what I expected. He'll do.*

Damian left. Yor went to bed. Loid stayed up to finish the dishes Yor had missed, because Loid always finished the dishes Yor missed, and the consistency of this was as legible as any thought he'd ever had.

Anya sat on the couch. Bond had reclaimed the rug and was asleep, breathing slowly, his body warm against her bare feet where she'd tucked them under his side. The apartment was quiet. The city outside was dark and cold and full of people she could not hear from here — the paint-sample woman, the worried son, the boy who wanted a dog — all of them thinking their private thoughts in their private rooms, and none of them hers to carry tonight.

She thought about the walk to the diplomatic quarter that morning. The thaw. The tram. The way the city's thoughts had loosened like a fist unclenching.

She thought about Sylvia's careful new distance and what it would take to rebuild, and whether the rebuilding would make something different from what had been there before, and whether different was worse or just different.

She thought about the conversation she would have with her parents. Someday. Not as a plan, not as a schedule, but as a fact — a thing she owed them that she would find the nerve for when the nerve was real and not performed. The conversation would change everything. It would also, she thought, be a relief so large she couldn't quite imagine the shape of it. To be known. By the people who made her, in the house where she grew up, with the full and specific truth of what she was. To stop carrying it.

Someday.

She thought about Damian walking home through Berlint with a container of Yor's curry and his thoughts full of her, and she couldn't hear them from here, and that was fine. She'd hear them next time. He'd let her.

She thought about Bond's dream — the bright, warm, shapeless thing — and about Loid's ambiguous thought, which she would never resolve and would never stop turning over, and about Yor's hands around the teacup and the silence between Yor and Yuri that would break when it was ready to break or would hold if holding was what they needed, and either way it was not hers to fix.

She thought about herself.

Not about what anyone else was thinking. Not the second layer, the frequency she'd lived on her entire life, the constant uninvited access to every private thought in range. Just her. Anya Forger. Twenty years old. A telepath who had told one person the truth about herself and was sitting with the possibility of telling two more. A daughter. An operative. A person who had spent her whole life hearing other people's thoughts and was only now, in a quiet apartment with a sleeping dog and fogged windows and the faint smell of burned curry, learning what it sounded like to listen to her own.

Her thoughts were small and ordinary and warm, and they were hers, and they were enough.
