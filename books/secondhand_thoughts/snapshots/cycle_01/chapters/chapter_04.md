# Chapter 4

The Ostanian Forum on Economic Realignment was held in a conference room on the third floor of the Foreign Affairs Ministry, and it was exactly as tedious as its name suggested. Anya stood near the back wall with a glass of sparkling water she hadn't touched, wearing her cultural attaché smile, while a deputy minister explained his vision for bilateral tariff reform to an audience that was mostly thinking about lunch.

*...the salmon looked good at the buffet earlier, wonder if there's any left...*

*...if Dreher mentions the import quota one more time I'm going to lose my mind...*

*...call Marta, tell her I'll be late, no, text her, if I call she'll want to talk about the bathroom renovation again...*

The usual hum. Thirty-odd bureaucrats and party functionaries in a room meant for twenty, the radiators working too hard, condensation on the windows turning the grey October sky into a smear. Anya let the thoughts wash through her and tried to pick out operational texture from the noise. Mostly there was none. These forums existed so mid-level officials could be seen attending them, and the thoughts in the room reflected that: career anxiety, social positioning, one woman silently ranking the men in the room by attractiveness with a specificity that made Anya want to take notes.

Then she found Damian.

He was standing near the front, to the left of the podium, in conversation with two men she didn't recognize. Dark suit, the posture he wore in government spaces — squared shoulders, the practiced ease of someone who'd grown up performing comfort in rooms like this. His surface thoughts were professional, half-listening to whoever was speaking to him while composing a mental draft of talking points for a meeting later that afternoon.

*...need to frame the border checkpoint issue as procedural, not political, Grosz was right about the framing...*

Normal. Controlled. Damian's mind in public settings was a disciplined thing — not blank, but organized, the thoughts of someone who'd learned to manage his own interior. Anya had noticed this at the cultural exchange event. He wasn't naturally closed; he just didn't let himself wander. Even now, the only stray thought she caught was a brief flicker about whether his dry cleaning would be ready before Friday.

She was about to let her attention drift back to the broader room when the man on Damian's left thought something that made her fingers tighten around her glass.

*...brought up the security meeting again, deflected, but Dreher told me he wasn't on the original invite list. Someone got him in. Why would a mid-level diplomat need access to defense procurement briefings?*

The man was tall, mid-forties, thinning blond hair combed carefully over a scalp that was losing the battle. His suit was better than his rank warranted — Anya could tell because it fit properly, and government-salary suits in Ostania never fit properly. He had a name tag she couldn't read from this distance, but his thoughts were loud and structured, the kind of organized suspicion that came from training rather than paranoia.

*...the Desmond name opens doors, always has, but this one's been opening different doors lately. The meeting with the naval attaché. The questions about the supply depot audit. Pattern.*

Anya set her glass down on the windowsill behind her. Her hand was steady. She'd been trained for this. She'd been trained by the best spy in the world, and the best assassin, and she could handle a policy forum with one suspicious functionary.

But the man's next thought scraped through her with the particular nausea of watching something dangerous unfold in real time:

*...report to Gessler tonight. Either Desmond's angling for a committee seat, which is fine, or he's running something off-book. If it's the second one, Gessler will want to know. Gessler always wants to know about the Desmond boys.*

Gessler. She didn't know the name. But the man's mental association was clear: Gessler was above him, connected, someone whose attention you directed toward people you wanted watched. The Renewal Front's internal structure was still opaque to Anya, but this had the shape of a chain — an informant flagging a suspected dissident for someone higher up.

She needed the man's name. She needed to see his badge.

Anya picked up her glass again and began a slow circuit of the room, the kind of aimless drift that cultural attachés were expected to perform at these events. Smile at a cluster of trade officials. Nod at a panel moderator she'd been introduced to once. Accept a canape from a tray and not eat it. Each step brought her closer to Damian's group, and with proximity the man's thoughts sharpened.

*...Desmond's doing the thing his father used to do — the charm offensive, the one where he agrees with everyone and commits to nothing. But Donovan was better at it. This one's got tells. He gets too specific when he's lying. Mentioned the Velden procurement audit by name, and that audit isn't public knowledge. Who told him about Velden?*

Close enough now. The badge read: BRANDT, K. — Ministry of the Interior, Policy Liaison.

Brandt, K., with his ill-hidden scalp and his well-fitted suit, was methodically building a case in his own head that Damian Desmond was a security risk. And he was going to report it tonight.

Anya's stomach turned. Not the dramatic plunge of a crisis, but the specific low-grade nausea of watching a trap close in real time while the person inside it smiled and shook hands and thought about dry cleaning. She had maybe hours before Brandt's report reached this Gessler. Maybe less, if Brandt decided to call instead of meeting in person.

She looked at Damian across twelve feet of overheated conference room. He was nodding at something one of the other men had said, his public face perfectly calibrated — interested, engaged, mildly amused. He didn't know Brandt was watching him. He didn't know Brandt was counting the doors he'd opened and the questions he'd asked and assembling them into a pattern that pointed at exactly what Damian was actually doing.

*...I'll tell Gessler to pull Desmond's access logs from the procurement office. If the pattern holds, we'll know by the weekend.*

Anya moved.

---

She came at the group from the side, arriving with the breezy momentum of someone who'd just spotted an acquaintance across a crowded room. Which, technically, she had.

"Mr. Desmond, hello again — I didn't know you'd be at this one." She extended her hand and gave him the attaché smile, the one that was warm without being memorable. "We met at the cultural exchange last week? Anna Kessler, Westalian mission."

Damian's surface thoughts registered recognition and something more complicated beneath it — a quick pulse of the half-familiarity that had been dogging him since the exchange event. *Her again. That laugh. Something about...*

He took her hand. "Of course. Ms. Kessler. The cultural attaché." His public voice was smooth, diplomatic, and his grip was brief and correct. But his thoughts added: *Why is she at an economic forum? Cultural attachés don't usually show up for tariff reform panels.*

Good. He was suspicious of her. That was fine. That was manageable. What wasn't manageable was Brandt, who was thinking:

*...Westalian diplomatic staff. Why is she approaching Desmond? Log that.*

Anya turned to Brandt with the full force of her professional friendliness. "I don't think we've met. Anna Kessler — I'm with the cultural liaison office at the Westalian mission. And you are...?"

"Brandt." He didn't offer a first name. His handshake was perfunctory, and his thoughts were already filing her: *Westalian. Cultural attaché. Approached Desmond specifically. Could be routine. Could be a contact protocol.*

She needed to break his focus on Damian. Not by being subtle — subtlety would just give him more data to analyze. She needed to make herself the more interesting object in the room.

"I actually came over because I overheard the tail end of your conversation about the cross-border trade metrics," Anya said, which was a lie; she'd heard nothing except Brandt's internal surveillance report on Damian. "Our office has been looking at the discrepancy between Ostanian and Westalian figures on the Velden corridor trade volume. Do you work on that at all?"

She'd chosen the word Velden deliberately. Brandt's own thoughts had flagged the Velden procurement audit as sensitive — by introducing it as a mundane trade topic, she was muddying the signal. If Velden came up in casual cross-border conversation, it was less significant that Damian had mentioned it.

Brandt's expression didn't change, but his thoughts flickered: *She mentioned Velden. Coincidence? The trade corridor stats are public, even if the procurement audit isn't. Different Velden issue.*

"The trade metrics are handled by the Commerce Ministry," Brandt said. His voice was flat, the kind of careful neutrality that intelligence-adjacent functionaries used when they weren't sure if they were being worked. "My office deals with policy coordination."

"Oh, of course, sorry. It's just that the bilateral figures have been a bit of a headache for us." Anya let herself fumble slightly — a half-step too eager, the enthusiastic junior diplomat who doesn't quite know where the boundaries are. "We keep getting asked to reconcile the numbers and nobody can agree on the baseline methodology. Do you know if there's been any standardization effort on the Ostanian side?"

The question was boring enough to be real. That was the trick — not to be interesting, but to be tediously plausible. Brandt's suspicion was built for pattern recognition, and what she was giving him was noise. A young Westalian attaché cornering people at forums to ask about trade methodology was annoying, not operational.

*...probably just doing her job. Westalian cultural staff are always wandering into the wrong meetings. Not a contact protocol. Too clumsy.*

The word *clumsy* stung, but it meant the performance was working.

Damian, meanwhile, was watching her with an expression of polite engagement and a mind that was working much faster. *She's deflecting him. That question about Velden — she introduced it to dilute the significance. She did that on purpose. How does she know about Velden?*

Anya kept her attention on Brandt, forcing the conversation deeper into the weeds of bilateral trade statistics while the operative's focus gradually loosened from Damian and settled into the irritated patience of a man being trapped in a conversation he hadn't asked for. She asked about import quotas. She asked about harmonization protocols. She asked about a statistical methodology paper she'd supposedly read, which she'd invented thirty seconds ago but described with enough specificity that Brandt had to take it seriously.

*...Jesus, she's still talking. I need to get to Dreher before the break ends...*

Three minutes. That was all she needed. Three minutes of Brandt's evening report not being about Damian Desmond but about the irritating Westalian attaché who'd wasted his time with questions about trade methodology. Three minutes of diluted focus, of the suspicion losing its sharp edges as the man's thoughts turned to logistics and scheduling and the salmon that might or might not still be available at the buffet.

Brandt excused himself with a politeness that barely qualified. Anya watched him go, tracking his thoughts as he crossed the room: *...forget about the Westalian. Talk to Dreher. The Desmond thing can wait until I've got the access logs.*

Can wait. Not abandoned, not forgotten. But delayed. She'd bought Damian time, not safety. The distinction sat in her like something she'd swallowed wrong.

She turned back to Damian, and the smile she found on his face was not the diplomatic one.

"Ms. Kessler," he said. "That was quite a detailed question about trade methodology."

"I'm thorough."

*She's not a cultural attaché. She's not even close to being a cultural attaché. Nobody who works in cultural liaison knows the word 'harmonization protocol.'*

"Clearly." His voice was even but his thoughts were racing, reassembling the last five minutes from a new angle: her arrival at the exact moment Brandt's questions were getting pointed, the Velden reference that muddied intelligence water she shouldn't have known was muddy, the performance of ditzy enthusiasm that had been precisely calibrated to bore an intelligence-adjacent functionary into disengagement.

"I should let you get back to—" she started.

"There's a service corridor behind the east stairwell," Damian said quietly, cutting her off. His public face hadn't changed. He was still smiling, still holding his posture, still looking for all the world like a diplomat wrapping up a pleasant conversation. But his voice had dropped and his thoughts had gone very still. "Five minutes."

He walked away. Anya stood there holding her untouched sparkling water and watched him rejoin a cluster of officials near the podium, and his surface thoughts were already back to talking points and procedural framing, smooth as a door closing.

Five minutes.

She put the glass down and headed for the east stairwell.

---

The service corridor was narrow, fluorescent-lit, and smelled like cleaning solution and the metallic tang of the building's ventilation system. Pipes ran along the ceiling in grey conduit. A mop bucket sat against the far wall, half full of brown water that nobody had emptied.

Damian was leaning against the wall with his hands in his pockets. His tie was loosened — the first time she'd seen any crack in his public presentation — and his jaw was tight. When Anya came through the door, his thoughts were:

*Don't be angry. Be smart. Figure out what she is, then be angry.*

"So," he said.

"So."

A silence. The fluorescent tube above them buzzed with a faint, irregular pulse. Somewhere in the walls, a pipe clanked.

"You're not a cultural attaché," Damian said. Not a question.

"I am, actually. It's just not the only thing I am."

*Intelligence. She's intelligence. Which service? Westalian — she's posted at the mission. WISE? BND? One of the smaller shops?* His thoughts were quick and organized, running through possibilities the way someone trained in political analysis would — not panicked, but rapid, systematic.

"The man you just rescued me from," he said. "Brandt. How did you know he was a problem?"

"I'm good at reading people."

His jaw tightened further. *That's not an answer. She knew about Velden — that's not public. She knew Brandt's angle of approach before he finished asking his questions. She anticipated his reporting chain. That's not 'reading people.' That's operational intelligence.*

"He was going to report you tonight," Anya said. Because this was the part where she had to give him something real, and the clock was running, and she couldn't protect him again from outside the conversation. "Someone named Gessler. Brandt's been watching your access patterns — the procurement briefings, the naval attaché meeting, the questions you've been asking about supply depots. He was putting together a case."

Damian's face went very still. The stillness wasn't calm. It was the specific rigidity of someone absorbing bad news while maintaining composure by force of will.

*Gessler. He knows about Gessler. That means either she's been investigating the same network I have, or she's been investigating me.*

"I'm not investigating you," Anya said, and then immediately wanted to bite her own tongue off because she'd responded to the thought, not the conversation, and now she had to cover the gap. "I mean — I know how this looks. Westalian operative shows up, knows your business, stages a rescue. I'd be suspicious too."

"You should know," he said, "that your cover has been suspicion for most of this conversation."

Fair.

"I work in intelligence," she said. "I can't tell you which service. I can tell you that we've been tracking the same threat you've been tracking, and the man who just walked out of that room is part of it."

*The Renewal Front. She knows about the Renewal Front.*

His thoughts went through a rapid sequence: surprise, then calculation, then something that looked almost like relief before he locked it down. The relief was what got her. He'd been doing this alone. Running his counter-operation against his own government's hardliners, using his dead father's network, and every ally he'd found so far was either old and frightened like Grosz or too compromised to trust fully. The idea that someone else knew — that someone with resources and institutional backing knew — produced a crack in his composure that he sealed almost instantly.

Almost.

"I don't know what you're talking about," he said. Diplomatic reflex. The words were automatic and he knew it and she knew it, and his thoughts said: *Stupid. She already knows. She just derailed Brandt in real time. The denial buys nothing.*

Anya waited. She had learned this from Loid, actually — the tactical value of silence. Let the other person close the distance. Don't fill the gap.

Damian looked at the mop bucket. Looked at the pipes. Looked at the fluorescent tube that was still buzzing like it had a personal grievance against the building's electrical system. Then he looked at her.

"My father built something," he said carefully. "An infrastructure. People, relationships, channels. It's still running. Some of the people who inherited it want to use it for things I don't agree with."

"The Renewal Front."

A muscle moved in his jaw. "I don't use that name."

"They do."

Another silence. His thoughts were louder now, the disciplined order fraying at the edges as the reality of the conversation sank in: *She knows the name. She knows about Brandt's chain. She may know about Grosz, about the network, about the whole operation. If she's hostile, I'm already burned. If she's not...*

"What do you want?" he asked.

"Same thing you want. To stop it before it gets worse."

*The phrasing is too vague to be operational. She's testing whether I'll specify. She wants me to say what I'm doing so she doesn't have to tell me what she already knows.* He almost smiled at that — the grim, tight-lipped recognition of someone who'd been raised in a household where conversational maneuvering was a survival skill.

"I'm running a counter-operation," Damian said. Each word landed with the weight of a decision being made. "Against elements within the Ostanian government who are using nationalist sentiment and defense procurement infrastructure to push both nations toward a conflict that benefits a small number of people on both sides of the border."

Both sides. She'd read that in his thoughts at the state dinner — the counter-operation wasn't just anti-Ostanian. It was anti-corruption on a bilateral scale. But hearing him say it aloud, committing to it as a stated position, was different. It cost him something. She could see the cost in his thoughts: *My father would call this treason. Maybe it is.*

"I can help," she said.

"In exchange for what?"

"Shared intelligence on the Renewal Front. Your network's operational picture, my network's analytical picture. We cover more ground together than apart."

*She's making it sound simple. It's not simple. My father was undone by trusting the wrong people. My brother would say this is exactly how foreign intelligence services compromise domestic assets. You let them in, you show them the architecture, and then you belong to them.*

"Terms," Damian said. His voice had gone quieter, which she was learning meant he was getting more serious, not less. The diplomatic polish was thinning and what was underneath was younger, more stubborn, more afraid than he'd have wanted her to know.

He crossed his arms. Not a power move — she could see that in his thoughts. It was self-containment. Something to do with his hands while the rest of him decided whether to walk out of this corridor or stay.

*...Father trusted the Westalian intermediary in '08. Gave him the channel architecture. Six months later the network was compromised and three people were dead. Trust is how they get you. Trust is how they always get you.*

The memory was vivid — not his own, she realized, but inherited. A story told enough times that it lived in him like experience. Donovan Desmond's cautionary tale, passed down to his sons like an heirloom: this is what happens when you let someone inside.

"Terms," she agreed. She kept her voice steady and tried not to think about the fact that she was reading his father's ghost in his thoughts while negotiating her way in. "Shared intelligence on the Front. No questions about each other's networks — I don't need to know your sources and you don't need to know mine. And either of us can walk away. No conditions, no penalties. If this stops working, it stops."

He stared at her. His thoughts moved through the terms like a lawyer examining a contract: *Reasonable. Too reasonable. The exit clause is the tell — nobody offers an exit clause unless they're confident you won't use it. Or unless they don't mean it.*

He was right. She didn't mean it. Not fully. Walking away from him now would mean walking away from the best intelligence source on the Renewal Front she'd ever found, and she'd already committed institutional treason to protect him. The exit clause was a courtesy, not a genuine offer.

And she was reading his doubts about it in real time, which meant she was negotiating with the cards face-up while he played blind. She'd done the same thing with Sylvia two days ago — sat across from her handler and constructed a lie while reading the trust that made the lie possible. Now she sat in a service corridor with a man she was supposed to be helping and read his fears and hesitations and used them, not to exploit, but to calibrate. To say the right things at the right moments. To know when to push and when to wait.

The difference between exploiting and calibrating was a line she'd drawn herself, and she wasn't sure it existed outside her own head.

*...she seems sincere. But seeming sincere is literally what intelligence operatives do. I can't verify anything she's told me. I can only verify what she did — she saw Brandt targeting me and she intervened, and the intervention was precise and effective, and she burned her own cover story to do it. That's not nothing.*

"How did you know about Brandt?" he asked again. "The truth, this time."

And here it was. The question she couldn't answer honestly. I read his mind. I heard him building a case against you inside his own thoughts. I knew he was going to report you because I listened to him decide to do it.

"My service has been monitoring Renewal Front activity channels," she said, which was true in the broadest sense and false in every specific one. "Brandt's name came up. When I saw him working you at the forum, I recognized the approach."

Damian held her gaze for a long moment. His thoughts: *She's lying. Not about all of it — the operational picture is probably real. But the specifics don't add up. She reacted too fast, too precisely. She didn't recognize an approach pattern. She knew, in real time, what he was about to do. That's not analysis. That's something else.*

He was sharper than she'd expected. Sharper than most people she'd worked. And the thing about sharp people was that they filed their doubts. They didn't press them in the moment — they stored them for later, and later always came.

"Okay," he said. Not *I believe you.* Just *okay.* The word sat in the fluorescent corridor like a provisional thing, a handshake that both parties knew was temporary.

"Okay," she said.

*...she's intelligence. She's good. She just saved my operation, maybe my life if Gessler had escalated. And I don't trust her. But I don't have to trust her. I just have to work with her. And working with someone is not the same as trusting them, and the fact that I want to trust her is exactly the reason I shouldn't.*

He pushed off the wall, straightened his tie, and became the diplomat again with a speed that was almost physical — the posture shifting, the jaw unclenching, the eyes going from wary to pleasant. The transformation was good. It was the kind of thing you learned growing up in a house where the dinner table was a stage.

"I'll be in touch," he said. "Through regular channels. Cultural exchange calendar."

"I check it every morning."

A ghost of something — not quite a smile. "You really aren't a cultural attaché."

"I really am. It's the other thing that's the problem."

He left first. She waited the standard two minutes, leaning against the wall opposite the mop bucket, listening to the pipes clank and the fluorescent tube buzz and the distant muffled noise of the forum continuing without them.

Her hands were shaking. She noticed this the way she noticed most things about her own body during operational work — late, and with faint surprise, as if the body was a peripheral system she'd neglected to monitor. The shaking wasn't fear. It was the specific tremor of sustained performance, the adrenaline afterburn of having managed three conversations at once for twenty minutes — Brandt's thoughts, Damian's thoughts, and the words coming out of her own mouth, each track running on a different frequency, each demanding a different version of herself.

She'd done it. Alliance formed. Terms set. Damian's operation preserved, for now. Brandt's report delayed, diluted, redirected. From the outside, it looked like a clean win.

From the inside, she'd just negotiated an alliance by reading her partner's mind without his knowledge or consent. She'd heard his doubts, his fears, his grudging hope, and she'd used all of it — not cruelly, not to harm, but to optimize. To say the right thing at the right time. To know when he needed reassurance and when he needed space. She'd been inside his negotiating position the entire time, and he'd been on the other side of the table playing blind, and he'd still chosen to work with her.

She didn't know if that made the alliance stronger or if it just made her the worst kind of partner. The kind who cheats before the game starts and then pretends the result was fair.

Two days ago, she'd sat across from Sylvia and filed a false report while reading the trust that enabled it. Today, she'd stood across from Damian and negotiated an alliance while reading the doubts that shaped it. Every successful deception widened the gap between Anya and every person she dealt with. Sylvia trusted her. Damian was starting to trust her. And both forms of trust rested on information she'd stolen and lies she'd told, and the lies worked because she was good at this, and being good at this was the thing she couldn't forgive.

The corridor was empty. The mop bucket sat in its corner, the dirty water dark and still. Anya pressed the heels of her hands against her eyes until she saw colors, and then she straightened up and went back to the forum and smiled at people and held her sparkling water and did not drink it.
