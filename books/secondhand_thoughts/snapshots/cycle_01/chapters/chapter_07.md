# Chapter 7

The conference room on the third floor of the National Unity Party headquarters smelled like old carpet and fresh coffee, and Damian Desmond was lying.

Not badly. He'd been lying in rooms like this since he was fifteen, since his father first brought him to a donors' reception and said, "Smile at the man in the grey suit, he funds our eastern district campaigns." Donovan Desmond hadn't needed to explain what the smile should look like. Damian had understood already. You learned to lie early in this family, the way other children learned to ride bicycles.

"The Westalian delegation's posture at the trade summit was instructive," Damian said, standing at the front of a table of eight party loyalists. He kept his hands at his sides, resisting the pocket. "Their willingness to revisit border tariffs suggests they're testing our resolve. If we concede on agricultural inspection protocols, we signal flexibility. If we're perceived as flexible, we're perceived as weak."

Nods around the table. Deputy Chairman Holtz, who had known Donovan for thirty years and considered himself a kingmaker, was writing something in a leather notebook. Beside him, Petra Lang from the defense policy committee watched Damian with the particular alertness of someone waiting for him to be useful or to fail.

"I'd recommend we draft a counter-position paper before the next session," Damian continued. "Something that reframes the tariff question as a security concern rather than an economic one. The language matters."

"Your father would have just told them no," Holtz said, not looking up from his notebook.

"My father had leverage I don't have yet," Damian said. He kept his voice warm, a little rueful. The trick was to acknowledge the shadow without seeming diminished by it. "But the principle is the same. You don't negotiate from a posture of accommodation."

Holtz looked up. "You sound like him."

Damian accepted the compliment the way it was intended — as a leash, gently applied. "I had a good teacher."

None of it was what he believed. The tariff question was routine economic maneuvering, and framing it as a security concern was the kind of escalatory rhetoric that the Renewal Front depended on. Every time a trade negotiation became a sovereignty dispute, the people who wanted war got to claim the people who wanted peace were surrendering something. Damian had drafted that counter-position paper already, in his head, and it disgusted him. He would write it this afternoon and it would be good — precise, persuasive, exactly the kind of thing a hawkish young Desmond would produce — and every sentence would be a small betrayal of the thing he was actually trying to do.

The meeting went another forty minutes. Budget allocations for the eastern district campaigns. A proposed media response to the Westalian cultural exchange program, which Lang wanted to frame as soft-power infiltration. Damian contributed where expected, deflected where he could, and watched the room the way his father had taught him: not for what people said but for who looked at whom when someone else was talking. Holtz deferred to Lang on media strategy but resented her for it. Lang wanted a committee chairmanship and was building alliances Holtz hadn't noticed yet. A younger member named Fitz, who Damian had met twice, kept checking his watch — bored or anxious, hard to tell.

None of them were Renewal Front, as far as Damian could determine. They were something more ordinary and in some ways more durable: people who had organized their entire professional lives around a set of assumptions about Ostanian strength and Westalian threat, and who would lose status, funding, and purpose if those assumptions were dismantled. The Front didn't need to recruit people like Holtz and Lang. It just needed the atmosphere they maintained.

Damian smiled at Fitz on the way out. "Good meeting," he said. Fitz looked relieved that someone had spoken to him.

---

Demetrius had chosen the restaurant, which meant the Kaiserhof, which meant the table by the window overlooking the ministry gardens, which meant this was not a casual lunch. Demetrius did not do casual. He did appropriate, calculated, and precisely legible to anyone in the room who might be watching. Every meal with his brother was a performance for an audience Damian couldn't always identify.

He was already seated when Damian arrived, ten minutes early because Demetrius was always fifteen minutes early and would note the gap. His older brother wore a dark suit that fit like it had been born on his body. His hair was combed in the same style their father had worn in photographs from the ministry — parted severely, held in place by some force stronger than product, possibly will. At twenty-four, Demetrius looked forty. He had looked forty since he was eighteen. The resemblance to Donovan was the kind of thing people commented on in his presence as though it were a compliment, and Demetrius accepted it as one, because for him it was.

"You're early," Demetrius said.

"You're earlier."

"I had a call that ran short. Kessler at Vanguard Defense. They're restructuring their procurement division." He said this the way other people would mention a dentist appointment — incidentally, but with the expectation that the information was being filed. "Sit down."

Damian sat. The waiter brought menus. The Kaiserhof served the kind of Ostanian cuisine that existed to prove Ostanian cuisine could be expensive: thick soups elevated to consommes, sausages deconstructed into terrines, bread that arrived with its own explanation. Damian ordered the schnitzel because it was the simplest thing on the menu, which was also a kind of statement, though he wished it weren't.

"I heard the party meeting went well," Demetrius said, scanning the menu without appearing to read it. He would order the duck. He always ordered the duck.

"Who told you that?"

"Holtz. He called to say you sounded like Father." Demetrius set the menu down. "He says that every time you speak. I think he means it as both praise and instruction."

"I know what he means by it."

"Do you." The question was flat, not inflected upward. Demetrius looked at him across the white tablecloth with the expression that had been his default since they were children: appraisal, faintly impatient. "The tariff counter-position. You'll draft it?"

"This afternoon."

"Good. Kessler mentioned that the defense ministry is watching the trade negotiations closely. A strong Ostanian position helps their budget projections."

"Kessler at Vanguard Defense is watching trade negotiations."

"Kessler's projections align with the ministry's. Defense procurement is downstream of trade policy, Damian. This isn't complicated."

It wasn't complicated. That was the problem. The flow was so clean it looked like gravity: trade tensions justified defense spending, defense spending funded procurement contracts, procurement contracts enriched firms like Vanguard, and Vanguard's profits funded the political infrastructure that ensured trade tensions remained tense. Demetrius didn't engineer this system. He just knew where the pipes ran and made sure they didn't leak.

"How's Father?" Damian asked, because that was a thing he could ask, and because it was simpler than anything else in this conversation.

"The same. He reads the papers. He has opinions about the eastern district that I've stopped relaying because they're twenty years out of date." Demetrius paused. "He asked about you."

"What did you tell him?"

"That you're performing well. That the party leadership is pleased with your engagement."

"Are they?"

"They're satisfied. You attend, you contribute, you produce competent policy work, and you carry the name. Satisfaction is the appropriate response." He picked up his water glass. "But Holtz mentioned that your engagement has been... selective. You attend the meetings that require your presence and decline the ones that would benefit from it. The defense policy working group. The security liaison committee. Petra Lang noticed."

"I've been focused on the trade portfolio."

"The trade portfolio is a holding position and you know it. The real work in this party — the work that justifies the Desmond name's weight — is in defense and security. That's where the relationships are. That's where the money moves."

The waiter arrived. Demetrius ordered the duck. Damian's schnitzel came with a microgreen garnish that looked apologetic about being on the plate.

"I'll attend the next defense working group," Damian said.

"Attend all of them." Demetrius cut his duck with the precision of someone who considered carving a social skill. "You are either inside the room where the decisions are made or you are irrelevant to the decisions. There is no middle position."

"That sounds like Father."

"It sounds like common sense. Father just happened to articulate it well." He chewed, swallowed, set down his knife and fork at the precise angle that indicated a pause, not completion. "Damian. I understand that you have... reservations about the direction of party policy. You've always been the thoughtful one. Father said that about you once — 'Damian thinks before he speaks, which is admirable until it becomes hesitation.' I manage the infrastructure. You provide the vision. But the vision must be legible, and lately yours has been difficult to read."

"I'm being careful."

"Careful is fine. Ambiguous is not. The people who fund this party and the people who benefit from its positions need to know which side of the table you're sitting on. Kessler asked me, directly, whether your commitment to national defense is genuine. I told him yes. Don't make me a liar."

Damian looked at his brother — the sharp jaw, the controlled posture, the suit that cost more than a schoolteacher's monthly salary. Demetrius wasn't cruel. He wasn't even cynical, exactly. He was something harder to argue with: practical. He saw the Desmond political machine as infrastructure that required maintenance, not ideology that required belief. The machine produced outcomes. The outcomes benefited people who had organized their lives around those outcomes. Disrupting the machine wasn't justice; it was vandalism. Demetrius genuinely believed this. Not because he wanted the world to be this way, but because this was the world as he found it, and he considered it childish to wish for a different one.

"I'm not going to embarrass the family," Damian said.

Demetrius studied him for a moment that lasted slightly too long. "No," he said. "I don't think you will." He returned to the duck. The statement had the shape of confidence and the weight of a warning.

They talked about smaller things. A cousin's wedding. The state of the ministry gardens, which Demetrius apparently had opinions about. Their father's deteriorating opinion of modern Ostanian literature. Damian listened to his brother's voice — clipped, sure, shaped by the same household that had shaped his own — and tried to identify the moment when Demetrius had decided that maintaining the system was the same as believing in it. He couldn't find it. It was possible there hadn't been a moment. It was possible Demetrius had simply never experienced the gap.

They split the bill because Demetrius insisted on splitting bills, a gesture toward equality that was also a reminder that equality was a gesture. On the pavement outside the Kaiserhof, autumn air sharp against the warmth of the dining room, Demetrius shook his hand — formal, as if they hadn't shared a womb's worth of childhood — and said, "The defense working group meets Thursday. I'll let Lang know you're coming."

"Fine."

"And call Father. He responds better to your voice than mine."

This was the closest Demetrius came to vulnerability: acknowledging, sideways, that their father preferred the son who questioned to the son who complied. Damian had never been sure whether Demetrius resented this or simply logged it as another data point about infrastructure maintenance.

"I'll call him tonight," Damian said.

Demetrius nodded and walked toward a car that was already waiting, its engine running, its driver opening the door before Demetrius reached it. He moved through the world like the world had been briefed on his schedule. Damian stood on the pavement in his simpler suit and watched him go and thought about the word *infrastructure* and what it meant when the thing being maintained was the architecture of a conflict that killed people.

---

Grosz lived in a third-floor apartment in the Schiller district, east of the government quarter but far enough to be affordable — the kind of building where retired civil servants went to grow quiet. The stairwell smelled like boiled vegetables and floor wax. A cat watched Damian from the second-floor landing with the unblinking contempt of an animal that had seen better tenants.

He knocked twice, then once, then twice again — the old pattern from his father's network, unchanged in thirty years because the people who used it were too old to learn a new one. The door opened on a chain. One eye, watery and sharp, examined him through the gap.

"Herr Grosz. It's Damian."

"I can see who it is." The chain rattled. The door opened. "You look like your mother. Come in."

The apartment was small and meticulously kept. A sitting room with a settee and two armchairs arranged around a coffee table that held a folded newspaper, a magnifying glass, and a plate with crumbs that suggested toast, recently eaten. Bookshelves lined one wall — Ostanian political history, military memoirs, a surprising number of novels. On the mantle, framed photographs: Grosz as a younger man in a ministerial corridor, Grosz shaking hands with someone whose face Damian didn't recognize, and, in the center, Grosz standing behind Donovan Desmond at what appeared to be a state function, both of them wearing the particular expression of men who believed they were building something permanent.

Grosz himself was seventy-three, thin in the way of men who had been vigorous and were now just wiry, with liver-spotted hands and a collar buttoned to the neck despite the warmth of the apartment. He moved carefully but not slowly — the distinction mattered to him, Damian suspected. A cane leaned against the armchair but Grosz did not use it while Damian was watching.

"Sit. I'll make coffee. Or I have schnapps, if you prefer."

"Coffee, please."

"Your father would have taken the schnapps."

"My father could hold it better than I can."

Grosz almost smiled. "He couldn't. He just never let on." He disappeared into the kitchen. Water ran. A kettle clicked. Damian sat in the armchair that didn't have the cane next to it and waited.

The apartment was very still. Traffic noise filtered up from the street — a bus, someone's car horn — but inside, the quiet was the kind that accumulated in rooms where one person lived alone. Damian recognized it. His own apartment had the same quality, though he was fifty years younger and would have denied the comparison.

Grosz returned with two cups on saucers, the porcelain heavy and old-fashioned, a pattern of blue flowers that had probably been a wedding set. He sat in the opposite armchair with the deliberate care of someone protecting his knees.

"You said you had questions about the current situation." He set his cup down without drinking. "I assumed you didn't mean the tariff negotiations."

"No."

"Then ask."

Damian had rehearsed this, but rehearsal was useless with Grosz. The old man had spent three decades managing Donovan Desmond's intelligence network — not the public face of it, not the policy arm, but the operational infrastructure that gathered information, assessed threats, and occasionally acted on both. Grosz had seen every technique for extracting information that a politician's son might attempt, because he'd taught several of them to the politician himself.

"The Renewal Front," Damian said. "I've been hearing the name in channels that used to be reliable. I need to know what it is."

Grosz's expression didn't change. He picked up his coffee, took a sip, and set it back down with the saucer precisely aligned to the table's edge. "Where did you hear it?"

"That's not something I can share."

"I served your father for thirty years. I'm not asking out of curiosity."

"I know. But the people who used the name don't know I'm here, and I'd like to keep it that way."

Grosz studied him. The watery eyes were sharper than they looked — assessing, the way a retired doctor might assess a symptom he'd seen before. "You're careful. Good. Your father was careful too, until he wasn't."

"Tell me about the Front."

Grosz was quiet for a moment. The radiator ticked. Somewhere below, the cat yowled at something.

"It's not a party," he said finally. "Not a faction, not a caucus. It's a coalition. Military officers — mid-level, mostly, the ones who built their careers during the last escalation and saw their budgets cut when the peace held. Civilian nationalists from the old guard, people who consider the current detente a temporary humiliation. And a funding structure that runs through defense procurement — contracts, subcontracts, maintenance agreements, the whole apparatus of military spending that doesn't appear in political budgets because it's classified as operational infrastructure."

He said the word *infrastructure* and Damian thought of Demetrius and felt something cold settle in his chest.

"How large?" Damian asked.

"I can't give you numbers. But the funding is substantial. Defense contracts generate revenue that's difficult to trace because the contracts themselves are classified. A procurement firm inflates a maintenance estimate by fifteen percent. The excess is routed through an intermediary. The intermediary funds activities that are... off-book. Meetings, travel, communications, and the operational capacity to act when the moment comes. No one signs their name to anything. The money just moves."

"And the leadership?"

Grosz set his cup down again. His hands, Damian noticed, were not entirely steady. "The Front isn't led the way a party is led. There's no chairman, no public face. But there is a figure who organizes, who connects the military and civilian elements, who articulates the purpose." He paused. "Someone who lost enough to believe that peace is theft. That's the phrase he uses. Peace as theft. As if the absence of war took something from him that he's owed."

"You won't name him."

"I won't name him to you in this apartment, no. I don't know who might come through that door after you leave, and I'm an old man who lives alone." He looked at the photograph on the mantle — the one with Donovan. "Your father knew some of these people. Not the Front — it didn't exist in its current form during his time. But the network of military and civilian figures who believe Ostanian strength requires Westalian weakness. He worked with them when it suited him. He managed them when it didn't. The difference between your father and these people is that Donovan understood leverage as a tool. They've made it an ideology."

"My father's ideology was Ostanian dominance."

"Your father's ideology was his own authority. Ostanian dominance was the frame he used. The distinction matters, because a man who uses a frame can change it when circumstances require. A man who believes the frame is the point — that man can't stop. That man will build a provocation, a crisis, an incident, whatever it takes to make the world match the shape in his head." He paused. "That is what the Renewal Front is for. Not policy. Not governance. An incident."

The radiator ticked. Damian let the word sit in the room.

"There's something else," Grosz said. His voice dropped — not for drama, Damian thought, but because the walls were thin and this was the part that frightened him. "The funding channels aren't one-directional. I've seen — not recently, but when I was still active, before your father withdrew — I saw documentation that defense procurement firms with Westalian ownership were purchasing Ostanian military intelligence assessments. Not stealing them. *Purchasing* them, through intermediary firms that brokered the transaction."

Damian stared at him. "Westalian firms buying Ostanian military intelligence."

"Buying it. And both sides knew. The Ostanian sellers understood who was buying. The Westalian buyers understood what they were purchasing. The transaction served both: the Westalian defense establishment got intelligence that justified their own spending projections, and the Ostanian military got revenue that funded further capability, which justified further Westalian spending, which funded further purchases. A circuit."

"That's — " Damian stopped. He had been about to say *insane,* but it wasn't. It was the opposite. It was the sanest possible arrangement for people whose livelihoods depended on tension between two nations. A self-sustaining market in mutual threat. "How current is this?"

"The arrangements I knew about were from eight, ten years ago. But the infrastructure doesn't dismantle itself. If anything, the current political temperature would have made the transactions more valuable. Higher tension means higher defense budgets on both sides. Higher budgets mean more procurement. More procurement means more opportunities for the kind of brokering I described." He picked up his cup again, held it without drinking. "You assumed the Renewal Front was an Ostanian problem. A faction of nationalists pushing your country toward war. It is that. But it exists within an ecosystem that profits from the *possibility* of war, and that ecosystem has shareholders on both sides of the border. The Westalian peace establishment is real. But so is the Westalian defense establishment, and its interests are not identical."

Damian sat with this. The coffee was going cold. He didn't care. What Grosz was describing wasn't a conspiracy in the way Damian had been thinking — a plot, a group of men in a room, a plan with a timeline. It was a system. A system of aligned incentives that produced outcomes no single actor needed to orchestrate because the architecture produced them automatically. The Renewal Front was the visible edge of something structural, and the structure crossed the border he'd assumed divided the problem into two sides.

"Why are you telling me this?" Damian asked.

Grosz looked at him with something that was not quite affection and not quite pity. "Because you're Donovan's son. And because you're trying to dismantle something your father helped build, and you deserve to know what it actually is before you break yourself against it." He set the cup down. "I was loyal to your father. I'm loyal to his name. That means I'm loyal to you, even when — especially when — what you're doing puts the name at risk. But I am frightened, Damian. Not of the Front. Of what happens when the people who profit from this system realize that someone is trying to interrupt it."

"You think they'll come after me."

"I think they won't need to. They'll come after the alliances you've built. The people who trust you. The channels you've opened. They'll isolate you, and then the isolation will do the rest." He folded his hands in his lap — the liver-spotted fingers interlaced, a gesture that was both composed and protective, as if he were holding something fragile between his palms. "Your father was never alone. He had infrastructure. People, resources, leverage. You have a name and a counter-position paper and whatever allies you've found outside this room, which I will not ask about because I don't want to know."

"That's probably wise."

"It's certainly wise. I'm too old to be brave." He almost smiled again, and this time it reached his eyes, briefly, before settling back into the watchful stillness that was his default. "Be careful with the name, Damian. The Desmond name opens doors. It also tells everyone exactly where to find you."

---

The walk home took twelve minutes, east along the Friedrichstrasse and then north toward his building. The streetlights were coming on. Autumn was doing its slow, grey work on Berlint — leaves in the gutters, darkness arriving earlier each week, the kind of chill that got into your coat and sat against your ribs. Damian walked with his hands in his pockets and his collar turned up and thought about systems.

A system that profits from tension. Westalian firms purchasing Ostanian intelligence through intermediaries. Ostanian military budgets swelling on Westalian threat assessments. The Renewal Front funded through procurement contracts that nobody signed their name to. And somewhere in the middle of it, a man who lost enough to believe peace is theft, organizing a coalition of soldiers and nationalists toward an incident that would crack the détente and let the whole machine run at full capacity again.

Damian's apartment was on the fourth floor of a building that had been elegant fifty years ago and was now respectable — which was the appropriate register for a junior diplomat who happened to be Donovan Desmond's son. Modest enough to signal independence. Well-located enough to signal that the independence was a choice, not a circumstance.

He let himself in. Hung his coat. Put the kettle on out of habit and then turned it off because he'd had enough coffee and the alternative was schnapps and Grosz was right, he couldn't hold it. The apartment was quiet in the particular way that single people's apartments were quiet in the evening — not the quiet of peace but the quiet of absence, every surface waiting for a second voice that wasn't coming.

He should call his father. He should draft the counter-position paper. He should eat something. He should review the notes he'd made after the Grosz meeting, which were coded in a shorthand he'd developed himself — nothing as sophisticated as a cipher, just a personal notation that would look like shopping lists to anyone who found them.

He didn't do any of those things. Instead, he opened the closet in the hallway and pulled down the cardboard box from the top shelf. It was the only box he'd brought from the family house — everything else in this apartment had been purchased new, a deliberate severance from the curated aesthetics of the Desmond household. But this box had come with him. He'd never unpacked it. He'd never thrown it out, either.

Inside: school things. An Eden Academy report card with marks that were good but not exceptional, which had been a source of quiet disappointment his father never voiced and Damian never stopped hearing. A certificate for the inter-class spelling competition, second place. A ribbon from the athletics day — he'd run the four-hundred-metre, badly, and finished fourth, and the ribbon said PARTICIPANT in silver text, which was the institutional word for failure. A folded programme from the school festival, his name listed as part of the cleanup crew for the gymnasium. A pen that had run out of ink years ago. And, at the bottom, beneath a layer of tissue paper that someone — his mother, probably, before she got sick — had placed there to protect it, a photograph.

Class photograph. Eden Academy, third year. Four rows of students on the gymnasium steps, in uniform, faces arranged in the specific configuration of children told to hold still and smile simultaneously. The teacher, Frau Henderson, stood to the left, her posture communicating both warmth and the absolute certainty that someone was about to misbehave.

Damian found himself in the third row, second from the right. He looked furious about something. He usually had. At ten, his face had been a rotating exhibition of indignation, wounded pride, and the stubborn refusal to admit he wanted anyone's approval while desperately wanting everyone's.

He moved his gaze to the second row. He didn't decide to look there. His eyes just went.

She was standing between a boy Damian didn't remember and Becky Blackbell, who he did. Pink hair — bright, ridiculous, unmistakable even in a fading photograph. She was grinning at something off-camera, not at the photographer, which meant she'd been told to look forward and hadn't. Her uniform was slightly crooked. Her eyes were screwed up with whatever had made her laugh. She looked like trouble in the specific way that certain children look like trouble — not malicious but incapable of leaving the world as they found it.

Anya Forger. The girl who'd punched him during a dodgeball game in year one because he'd said something about her hair and she'd decided the appropriate response was violence. The girl who'd ended up on his cleanup crew at the school festival and spent the entire afternoon arguing about the correct way to stack chairs. The girl who'd been the first person at Eden Academy to treat him like a person instead of a surname, which at the time had enraged him because he'd built his entire identity around being a surname and she'd refused to notice.

He thought about the cultural attache he'd met at a state dinner. The woman with the quick smile and the laugh that caught him somewhere he hadn't expected. The way she'd tilted her head when she was listening — not politely but with a kind of total attention that made him feel like the only frequency in a crowded room.

He looked at the photograph. He looked at the girl in the second row.

He held the photograph for a long time. Longer than he meant to. The kettle was cold. The apartment was dark — he hadn't turned on the overhead light and the only illumination came from the streetlight outside the window, casting the room in the amber half-tones of a Berlint evening. The girl in the photograph grinned at whatever had been funny sixteen years ago, and Damian sat on the floor of his hallway with a box of childhood things and stared at a face he was only beginning to let himself recognize.

He put the photograph back in the box. He didn't put the box back on the shelf. He left it on the floor, open, and went to the kitchen to draft a counter-position paper about tariffs that he didn't believe in, for a party that was part of the machine he was trying to dismantle, carrying a name that opened every door and told everyone exactly where to find him.

The apartment was quiet. Outside, Berlint settled into its autumn dark, and somewhere in the system Grosz had described — the circuit of money and threat and mutual profit — the gears turned without anyone needing to touch them.
