# Chapter 14

The apartment smelled like scorched sugar and sesame oil, which meant Yor was trying the Korean recipe again. Anya stood in the hallway outside the door, key in her hand, and let the smell settle around her like something she'd been missing without knowing it. Through the wall she could already hear them. Not words — just the texture of home. A pan hissing. Bond's nails clicking on hardwood. The low hum of the radio Loid kept tuned to the news even when he wasn't listening.

She hadn't called ahead. She should have. There was a protocol for this — for when a mission went wrong and an operative needed to loop in a handler outside the normal chain. You filed a contact request. You waited for a secure window. You didn't show up at your parents' apartment at nine on a Tuesday evening with shaking hands and a dead colleague's name sitting behind your teeth like something she couldn't swallow.

She let herself in.

The living room was the same managed disaster it had always been — Loid's bookshelves organized by size, Yor's throw pillows organized by whatever instinct governed Yor's relationship to throw pillows, Bond asleep on the couch with his enormous head hanging off the edge and one paw twitching through some dream she chose not to read. The kitchen light was on. She could see Loid's back as he stood at the sink, washing something — his posture relaxed, his sleeves rolled up, the grey at his temples catching the overhead light.

*—should've used less sugar, but she's enjoying herself, and the smoke alarm hasn't—*

Loid's thoughts, warm and mundane and exactly the shape of a man who was thinking about his wife's cooking and nothing else. Anya felt the stupid, childish impulse to turn around and leave. To not ruin this. To let them have the evening.

"Anya!" Yor appeared from behind the refrigerator door holding a bottle of something dark. Her face lit up. "Oh, you should've told me you were coming, I would've — there's plenty, I made too much, I always make too much, Loid says I cook for eight people and honestly he might be right but—"

"It's fine, Mama."

"Are you hungry? Sit down. Sit. Let me get you a plate."

Anya sat at the kitchen table. Bond had woken up — she heard the couch creak, the heavy thud of him landing on the floor — and a moment later he pushed his massive head into her lap, tail wagging slowly, his thoughts a warm blur of *her, her, she's here, good*. She scratched behind his ears and tried to figure out where to begin.

Loid turned from the sink. He was wearing an apron that said WORLD'S BEST DAD in letters Anya had painted when she was nine, faded now to a ghost of the original pink. His reading glasses were perched on top of his head. He looked at her and his expression didn't change, but his thoughts did.

*Something's wrong. She came alone. No call. Her hands.*

His eyes went to her hands. She realized she was gripping the edge of the table.

"How's work?" Loid asked, drying his hands on the apron. Casual. Easy. A man asking his daughter about her day.

"It's—" She stopped. Started again. "Papa, I need to talk to you."

The shift was instant. Not in his face — his face stayed warm, stayed fatherly. But his posture changed in a way that was nearly invisible unless you knew what to look for, and Anya had been watching her father change modes her entire life. The reading glasses came off his head and into his hand. His weight redistributed. He was standing the same way but he was standing differently, and the man in the apron was suddenly the man who had spent thirty years moving through the world in somebody else's name.

*Operational. She's bringing me something operational. How bad.*

"Sit down," Anya said.

He sat. Yor was still at the stove, but she'd stopped stirring. Her spoon hung in the air over the pan, dripping sauce onto the burner where it sizzled and smoked.

Anya told him. Not everything — not the telepathy, not the false report, not the specific shape of the guilt that had kept her from sleeping for three days. But enough. The Renewal Front. The conspiracy inside both governments. The mole inside WISE she couldn't yet name. The assassination plot accelerating toward the security summit. Agent Crane dead at the border. The timeline: days.

Loid listened without interrupting. His thoughts were something she'd never quite heard before — not the father's warmth and not the spy's calculation but both at once, running in parallel like two songs in different keys.

*How long has she been carrying this. Who's her support structure. Entry points — the summit security will be SSS-run, that's Ostanian jurisdiction, three layers minimum. She looks like she hasn't eaten. Timeline — if the Front is moving on the summit, the provocation needs to be staged inside the venue for maximum attribution impact. She's twenty. She's twenty years old and she's doing this alone.*

"How's your intelligence on the summit venue?" he asked.

"Partial. We know the date. We know it's an assassination — staged, meant to look Westalian."

"Target?"

"We don't have it yet."

He nodded. His mind was already building — she could feel the architecture of it, the way tactical planning organized itself in his head like rooms being furnished in real time. Entry points, exit routes, sight lines, personnel placement. A machine that had been idle, never broken.

"I'm in," he said.

"Papa—"

"Anya." His voice was quiet. "I'm in."

Behind them, the sound of Yor's spoon being set down very carefully on the counter. The burner hissed where the sauce was burning. Yor turned the flame off. Her movements were steady, unhurried, the way she moved when she was thinking about something that required her full attention. She wiped her hands on her apron — hers said KISS THE COOK in letters that, unlike Anya's paint, had not faded at all.

*Clearing my schedule. The Tuesday morning thing at city hall, the garden assessment on Thursday — no. Not the Garden. Not for this.*

The thought hit Anya sideways. Something in it she hadn't expected — not the Garden as an organization but the way Yor's mind flinched from the word. As if it had developed an edge since the last time Anya had been here.

Yor disappeared into the hallway. She came back carrying a manila envelope — not the kind you buy at an office supply store but the kind that gets handed to you by a man in a parking lot, thick and slightly warped from being stored in a place that wasn't temperature-controlled. She set it on the kitchen table between the soy sauce and a plate of burned scallion pancakes.

"I've got something too," Yor said.

She opened the envelope and spread the contents across the table. Printed photographs — enlarged from phone captures, slightly grainy. Shipping manifests. Subcontracting records with columns of names and figures and dates, annotated in a hand Anya didn't recognize. And a separate sheet, photographed at an angle as if the person taking the picture had been in a hurry or in the dark: the Garden's organizational watermark at the top of a purchase order that listed three names, three dates, three completed eliminations.

Loid picked up the manifest first. His eyes moved over it the way they moved over intelligence — fast, systematic, missing nothing.

*Werner Voss. These supply chains — military procurement, defense contracts, routed through civilian intermediaries. This is the Renewal Front's logistics.*

"Where did you get these?" Loid asked.

Yor folded her hands in front of her. "A job. Last month. A weapons broker named Karl Dominic."

Loid looked at her. Then he looked at the subcontracting records with the Garden watermark. Anya watched the connection form in his thoughts — the same way she'd watched hundreds of connections form in hundreds of minds — except this one was happening inside her father's head and the conclusion he was reaching was about her mother, and the silence in the kitchen was the loudest thing she'd ever heard in this apartment.

*Yor has been doing Garden work. The Garden is subcontracting for the Renewal Front. The eliminations on this sheet — these are people who were killed to serve the conspiracy.*

He didn't say it. He looked at Yor, and Yor looked back at him, and whatever passed between them was not something Anya could read because it was older and deeper than surface thought.

"How long," Loid said. Not a question. An opening.

"The Garden's been taking these contracts for at least two years." Yor's voice was even. Her thoughts were not. *I killed them. Dominic, Reiter in March, the man in the warehouse whose name I never knew. The Garden said they were threats. I didn't ask to whom.* "The people I was sent to — some of them were broker targets. People who'd become liabilities for the supply chain. The Garden cleared them because someone paid the Garden to clear them, and the money came through channels connected to the Renewal Front."

She said this the way she said everything that mattered — simply, without performance, as if the information weighed no more than a recipe correction. But her hands on the table were very still, and Anya noticed for the first time that her mother's knuckles were white.

"Mama—"

"I'm not going back," Yor said.

Loid waited.

"They'll send a handler. Tomorrow, or the next day. There's a procedure when an operative goes silent." She smoothed the edge of the envelope. "I won't answer. I won't take the assignment. I'm done."

*Twenty years. I believed them for twenty years. That the targets were dangerous, that the eliminations were necessary, that I didn't need to understand the reasons because the reasons were above me. And the reasons were money. The reasons were someone else's war.*

The thought was quiet, and Yor's face was quiet, and the kitchen was quiet except for Bond's breathing and the tick of the clock above the stove. Anya sat with the thought the way she'd sat with a thousand stolen thoughts before — inside it without permission, holding something that belonged to her mother and not to her.

"I'll help," Yor said. "Whatever you need for the summit. But I'm not — I won't be operating as a Garden asset. I'll be here. As myself." She paused. "As me."

She didn't say *as Yor Forger*. She didn't need to. The name was in the space between what she said and what she meant, the way it had always been — a cover name that had stopped being a cover so gradually that no one in this family could have said exactly when it became real.

Loid reached across the table and put his hand over hers. He didn't say anything. His thoughts said it: *You're safe. You're here. We'll figure out the Garden. We'll figure out all of it.*

Yor's hand turned over under his, and her fingers closed around his, and the white in her knuckles eased.

Anya looked away. Bond was watching from the couch with his chin on the armrest, his eyes moving slowly between each of them, and his thoughts were a shapeless warmth that didn't need translating.

* * *

Forty minutes later, the kitchen had become a tactical operations center. The scallion pancakes had been pushed to the counter. In their place: a security map of the summit venue that Loid had produced from a locked filing cabinet Anya hadn't known existed, taped to the refrigerator with magnets shaped like fruit. A second map of the surrounding streets, annotated in Loid's precise handwriting with sight lines, access points, and estimated SSS patrol routes. A legal pad filled with operational notes. And Yor's photographs from the Dominic compound, arranged by relevance along the table's edge.

Loid was standing at the refrigerator with a dry-erase marker he'd taken from a kitchen drawer — also previously unknown to Anya — drawing entry vectors on the laminated map with the focus of a man who had spent his entire adult life turning chaos into something you could walk through alive.

"The summit runs three days. The provocation — the staged assassination — would need to happen on the opening session. That's when delegations are fully assembled and media presence peaks." He drew a line from the main foyer to the south terrace. "If it's designed to look Westalian, the team needs proximity to the Westalian delegation area, which puts them here" — he circled the east gallery — "or here." The west service corridor. "SSS controls the perimeter and internal security. The venue has three public entrances and two service corridors."

"How many people on their team?" Yor asked. She was sitting at the table with her legs crossed and a cup of tea she hadn't touched, studying the map with the particular focus she reserved for situations where things might need to die.

"Unknown. If they're serious about attribution, they'd use a small team. Three, maybe four. Enough for the hit and immediate extraction."

*The south terrace is exposed. If they want a public kill — a spectacle, something cameras catch — that's the staging ground. But the terrace connects to the exterior grounds through a low wall, which means the extraction route runs south.*

Anya absorbed her father's tactical thoughts as they formed, watching the architecture of his plan assemble itself. She could see where the gaps were before he saw them — not because she was smarter, but because she was reading the reservations he hadn't voiced yet. The east service corridor: he was thinking it was a choke point, that if the mole transmitted coordinates from inside the venue, a single corridor was both the best intercept point and the worst place to be trapped.

"What about the east corridor?" Anya said. "If the mole's inside, he'd need a communications point. Something off the main halls."

Loid turned from the map. His thoughts flickered: *Good instinct. She's right — the communications room off the east corridor is the only secure relay point that isn't monitored by SSS. How did she—*

He didn't finish the thought. Just: "That's a good catch."

Yor leaned forward. "So we know where they'll try to do it and where the traitor will be sending signals from." She set down her tea. "Why don't I handle the assassination team and Loid handles the communications room?"

*Straightforward. Efficient. She's absolutely going to suggest killing them.*

"We could also," Yor added, "remove the threat entirely. Before the summit."

"Remove," Loid said carefully.

"The team. If we know where they'll stage, we can intercept before—"

"We don't know the team composition. And preemptive elimination of foreign nationals on Ostanian soil—"

"They're planning to murder a diplomat. I think we can adjust our standards."

*She's not wrong. She's also proposing assassination as a first option, which is — that's just who she is. Focus.*

Anya pressed her lips together to keep from laughing. It wasn't funny. None of this was funny. A man was dead and a conspiracy was trying to start a war and her mother was offering to kill people over the kitchen table where Anya had done her Eden Academy homework. But the gap between the conversation's content and its setting — Loid's WORLD'S BEST DAD apron and the fruit magnets and Bond's snoring from the living room — was so enormous that something inside her kept threatening to crack.

"We need to coordinate four things," Loid said, turning back to the map with the marker. "One: identify and intercept the assassination team at the venue. Two: locate and neutralize the communications relay — the mole will be transmitting from inside. Three: Damian exposes the conspiracy through political channels — the evidence needs to be public in a way that can't be suppressed. Four: Anya works the crowd. Your—" He paused. "Your read on the room has always been good. You'll be our primary sensor for real-time threat identification."

*What I mean is: your instincts are uncanny and I've never understood how. You read people better than any operative I've ever trained, including myself, and someday I'm going to ask you how.*

Anya swallowed. "Yeah. I can do that."

"The teams are exposed during transit — the corridors, the foyer, the terrace approach." Yor was looking at the map now with the focus that meant she was counting bodies and calculating angles. "If I'm positioned near the Westalian delegation, I can move to the south terrace through the west corridor in under a minute. That gives me access to the assassination staging area and a sight line on the foyer." Her thoughts were clipped, tactical: *Three exits, two choke points, the terrace wall is waist-height, they'll come over it or through the corridor, either way I meet them in open ground.*

"You'd be in full view of security cameras," Loid said. "And SSS personnel."

"I know."

*Yuri might be there. Yuri runs event security. If he sees me—*

The thought cut off. Yor picked up her tea and drank.

Anya read the silence in her mother's mind — the shape of a brother who didn't know what his sister was, and the possibility that after the summit, he would. Yor had considered this. She'd considered it and she was doing it anyway.

The plan had a gap. Anya could feel it — not in the logistics, which Loid was covering with his usual exhaustive precision, but in the intelligence flow. Loid was building the operational plan around what they knew, but what they knew had holes. He hadn't said it yet, but she could hear it: *The timing on the delegation movements is estimated, not confirmed. If the south terrace staging is wrong, Yor is out of position. The east corridor intercept depends on the mole following the predicted comms protocol. Too many conditionals.*

"Papa," Anya said. "What if we stage Yor closer to the foyer instead of the delegation area? If the team's extraction route runs through the exterior grounds, she'd cut them off at the corridor junction rather than meeting them at the terrace. That way she covers both the west approach and the foyer."

Loid looked at the map. His marker hovered over the foyer junction.

*That's better. How does she — never mind. She's right.*

"That repositions the intercept point." He drew a new arc on the map. "She'd have the corridor junction and the foyer exit. Better coverage."

"And I can still reach the terrace in forty seconds from the junction," Yor said, measuring the distance on the map with her fingers.

Anya's stomach turned. It had worked again. She'd read Loid's reservation — *too many conditionals on the south terrace positioning* — before he'd said it, caught Yor's mental calculation about corridor distances, and steered the plan toward the better configuration without revealing how she knew. The plan was tighter now. More efficient. Because she'd cheated.

She'd been doing this for twenty years and it still made her feel like she was stealing from people who'd given her everything.

Bond had been asleep through the entire planning session, his enormous body curled on the couch like a white boulder with legs. Now, without warning, he stood up.

Not the slow unfolding of an elderly dog. A snap to vertical — rigid, alert, his head swiveling toward the kitchen with the same sudden intensity Anya had seen once before, in this apartment, months ago. His eyes were fixed. His body was trembling.

"Bond?" Yor said.

He crossed the living room in four strides — fast for a dog his age, fast enough that Loid moved to intercept him — and planted himself in front of the refrigerator. In front of the map. He barked once, sharp and loud, and his nose pressed against a specific spot on the laminated surface.

Anya's breath stopped.

She was reading him. She couldn't help it — Bond's mind was always open, always broadcasting, and when he was like this, when the precognition kicked in, his thoughts weren't words but images, raw and fragmentary, with the texture of a dream being had by someone who knew it was real.

*A wall. Low. Stone. Grass on the other side. Men in dark clothes moving fast along the wall. Something bright — fire, or light, bright like fire. The building behind them, the same building on the map, the glass facade catching the light. A sound like—*

The image fractured. Bond barked again, his nose jabbing at the map.

He was pointing at the exterior grounds. The south perimeter of the summit venue — the landscaped area outside the south terrace, where the low wall met the vehicle approach.

"That's the same—" Anya stopped herself. She'd almost said *the same vision from before*. From the night Bond had woken up rigid and staring at nothing and she'd read the fire in his mind and tried to warn Loid without explaining how she knew.

Loid was watching Bond. His expression was the one he wore when information didn't fit the model — attentive, careful, not dismissing it.

*He's done this before. The same intensity. Anya mentioned security concerns at government events, weeks ago, and I caught the false note in her delivery and didn't press it. And now the dog is pointing at the summit perimeter.*

"The exterior grounds," Loid said slowly. "That's the extraction route I just marked."

Bond barked a third time and sat down, pressing his body against the refrigerator with his eyes still locked on the map. His mind was settling — the vision fading, replaced by the simpler feelings of an old dog who was agitated and didn't understand why. *Bad place. Bad. Don't go there.*

Anya crouched down and put her arms around his neck. His fur smelled like the couch and like the apartment and like every safe thing she'd ever known. "Good boy," she said quietly. "I hear you."

Loid circled the exterior grounds on the map with a red marker. He didn't ask how Bond knew. He didn't ask why Anya had mentioned security concerns at government events weeks ago. He added the location to the operational plan as if a precognitive dog was a perfectly standard intelligence source, which — in the Forger family — it was.

* * *

Damian arrived at twenty past ten. Anya heard him before the doorbell rang — his thoughts pushing ahead of him up the stairwell like a man rehearsing lines he knew he'd forget.

*Okay. You're meeting her parents. Her father is a diplomat or something and her mother works at city hall. Normal people. You've met heads of state. You've met your father's contacts. You can handle a family dinner. It's not a dinner. It's an operational briefing. Same thing. Different cutlery.*

The doorbell rang. Yor got there first.

"Oh! Hello! Come in, come in — you must be Damian. Anya's told us — well, she's told us some things, I'm sure there's more, there usually is — take off your shoes if you — or don't, that's fine too, Loid is particular about the floors but I think in this case—"

"Thank you, Mrs. Forger. It's very kind of you to—"

"Yor! Please. Call me Yor. Mrs. Forger sounds so — I don't know, it sounds like someone else. Can I get you tea? Or there are scallion pancakes, they're a little — they're fine, they're on the counter."

*She's... very warm. This is the woman who — Anya said her mother is a city hall clerk. She's a city hall clerk who smiles like that. Okay. This is fine.*

Anya stood up from the table where she'd been pretending to review the operational notes and not dying of anticipatory humiliation. Damian stepped into the kitchen. He was wearing the dark coat she'd seen him in a dozen times, and his hair was slightly damp from the rain she hadn't noticed starting, and the scar above his eyebrow — the one he'd gotten the night they were ambushed, the night she'd fought for him and bled for him and kissed him in a cold safe house — caught the kitchen light.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi." He looked at her. *Don't think about the kiss. Don't think about the safe house. Don't think about the fact that you know she's Anya Forger and she doesn't know you know and her parents are right there and —*

He smiled. It was a good smile. A little stiff at the edges, a little too controlled, but real underneath the polish.

"Damian." Loid materialized from somewhere — the hallway, maybe, or the other dimension fathers occupied when they were assessing young men who were interested in their daughters. He extended his hand. "I'm Loid. Anya's father."

The handshake lasted exactly long enough and not a fraction longer. Loid's posture had shifted again — not into the spy's readiness from earlier but into something else, something Anya had never seen before. His smile was warm. His eyes were doing something that wasn't warm at all.

*Firm handshake. Good. He's making eye contact — that's trained, political-family composure. Split eyebrow scar — recent, healed but recent. What happened to his face. He's taller than I expected. Anya said he was a diplomatic ally. Is he. How old is he. Is he Anya's age. He's looking at my daughter the way—*

Anya wanted to sink through the kitchen floor.

"Thank you for having me, Mr. Forger. Anya's told me about your work — the operational experience you bring is—"

"Loid is fine." Another warm smile. The eyes hadn't changed. *His tie is good quality. Diplomatic family money. The Desmond name carries political weight in Ostania, which means either he's an asset or a liability and the distinction matters. He's looking at Anya again. He keeps looking at Anya.*

"Papa," Anya said through her teeth.

"What? I'm being welcoming." *He seems decent. I need more information. Is his interest in my daughter operational or personal. Both. It's both. I can see it in the way he stands near her. I stood near Yor like that, at the beginning, before I knew—*

Yor appeared with tea she hadn't been asked to make, pressing a mug into Damian's hands with the serene efficiency of a woman who solved problems by feeding people. "Sit anywhere. Oh, not that chair — Bond sleeps there. Actually, Bond sleeps everywhere. He's very old."

As if summoned, Bond padded into the kitchen. He was moving slowly again — the precognitive urgency had passed, and he was just a large, elderly dog with stiff hips and white fur that got on everything. He approached Damian, sniffed his shoes extensively, sniffed his hand, sniffed the hem of his coat. Then he sighed — a deep, whole-body exhalation — and lay down on Damian's feet.

*The dog is on my feet. A very large dog is sleeping on my feet. I can't move. This is the strangest — I've been in rooms with defense ministers and intelligence chiefs and I've never felt this outmatched by a domestic environment.*

"He likes you," Yor said, beaming. "He doesn't do that with everyone. Just people he trusts."

This was not true. Bond did this with delivery drivers, mail carriers, and a neighbor's cat he was afraid of. But Anya didn't correct it.

Damian looked down at Bond, then up at Anya. His surface thoughts were warm — genuinely warm, the kind of warmth that came from someone who hadn't been in a family kitchen in a long time, who'd been eating alone in a government-district apartment and attending political functions where every smile was a calculation.

*This is her family. The spy and the city hall clerk and the precognitive dog and the — and Anya. This is where she grew up. That's the couch she probably fell asleep on as a kid. That's the kitchen where she learned to — where she became whoever she is. God, this is real. These people are real.*

But beneath it — and Anya could feel this, could sense the edges of it the way you could sense a door closed behind a wall — something held back. A layer of thought that was controlled, disciplined, kept below the surface with the specific care of someone who knew what he was doing. She couldn't read it clearly. It was there and it wasn't. Like a room she could see the outline of but couldn't enter.

He knew something. She could feel that much. The restraint wasn't casual — it was structural, the kind of deliberate thought management she'd encountered in trained intelligence operatives, in people who'd learned to keep their real thoughts beneath the ones you were allowed to see.

But he wasn't trained. He was a diplomat, not a spy. So why—

"Let me walk you through what we have." Loid was at the refrigerator map, marker in hand, and the warmth in his voice had been replaced by operational precision. The father-evaluating-boyfriend had been shelved — or, more accurately, filed for later review. "The summit runs three days. The Renewal Front has engineered a provocation — a staged assassination attempt designed to be attributed to Westalis."

Damian shifted into his own operational mode — straighter posture, his expression sharpening, the diplomatic composure settling over him like a coat he'd worn his entire life. He listened as Loid outlined the plan. He asked questions — precise ones, politically informed in ways Loid's military-intelligence training didn't cover.

"The Ostanian delegation seating is determined by ministry rank," Damian said, studying the map. "If you want me to isolate Voss, I'll need to be in the West Gallery. That's where the party leadership watches the floor. I can force a confrontation there — I have enough evidence from Grosz and from Mrs. Forger's materials to make it public."

"Yor," Yor said from the counter.

"Yor," Damian corrected, and something about the way he said it — the careful respect, the slight fumble of using a first name he'd been given permission for thirty seconds ago — made Yor smile and Loid's thoughts darken fractionally.

*He said her name like he meant it. I don't like how comfortable he is here. No — I do like it. That's worse.*

Anya pressed her fingers to her temple.

They worked. For the next hour, the Forger kitchen was a war room. Loid built the operational timeline on the refrigerator map with the methodical precision of a man who'd been running field operations since before any of them were born. Yor contributed tactical assessments of physical engagement scenarios with the calm specificity of someone who'd spent two decades professionally killing people. Damian mapped the political terrain — who'd be at the summit, who could be leveraged, where the institutional fractures could be widened. And Anya moved between all of them, reading what they thought and steering what they said, adjusting the plan's seams before anyone noticed the seams existed.

She caught Loid worrying about the communications room intercept timing and suggested a delayed approach that matched the concern he hadn't voiced. She read Yor's instinct about the foyer junction's blind spot and mentioned it before Yor found the words. She felt Damian's hesitation about the West Gallery confrontation's political exposure and proposed a staging sequence that gave him cover to approach Voss without declaring intent.

Each time, the plan improved. Each time, her family said something like *good point* or *I hadn't thought of that* or, from Loid, a look that meant he was filing the observation away in the part of his mind where he kept things that didn't quite add up.

And each time, Anya felt the old guilt settle a little deeper. The optimization was real. The intelligence was stolen. She was making the plan better by reading the minds of the people she loved most in the world, taking their private reservations and tactical instincts and feeding them back as her own insights, and it worked — and the fact that it worked was the worst part, because it meant the violation was useful, and useful violations were the hardest kind to stop.

Bond was still asleep on Damian's feet. Yor had made a second round of tea without being asked. Loid's map was covered in red and blue lines — approach routes, intercept points, extraction contingencies — and the plan, improbable and insane and built in a kitchen by a spy and an assassin and a telepath and a diplomat and a dog, was taking shape.

Damian looked at the map, then at Anya. His surface thoughts were clear and warm: *This could work. This family is insane and this could actually work.*

And beneath that — the door she couldn't open, the thought she couldn't reach: the careful, deliberate restraint of a man carrying knowledge he wasn't ready to share.

She held his gaze. She didn't push. She didn't read deeper.

The kitchen was warm. The tea was getting cold. Yor was asking Damian if he'd eaten — *he's too thin, he needs to eat, I'm going to reheat the pancakes* — and Loid was annotating the summit schedule with a precision that bordered on compulsive, and Bond was dreaming about something Anya chose to believe was good.

Her family was going to war. All of them, together, for the first time. A spy who'd built a life on lies that became real. An assassin who'd just chosen the life over the lies. A diplomat who knew more than he was showing. A dog who'd seen the future and barked at it. And Anya — who could hear every thought in the room and still couldn't tell anyone the truth about herself, who'd stolen the intelligence that made the plan possible from the people who trusted her most, who was twenty years old and had been lying since she could talk.

This was her family. The forged thing that had become real. And tomorrow they were going to try to stop a war.

Bond whimpered in his sleep. Yor reheated the scallion pancakes. Loid uncapped a fresh marker.

"Okay," Anya said. "Let's go over it one more time."
