# Chapter 15

The International Conference Center smelled like floor polish and new carpet, the kind of institutional freshness that meant someone important was expected. Anya took her seat in the East Gallery, press credentials clipped to her lapel, and opened herself to the room.

Four hundred minds hit her at once.

She'd done crowd reads before — the diplomatic reception had been bad, the market extraction in her first week had been worse — but the summit was something else entirely. Delegates from both nations packed the Main Assembly Hall below, and their collective anxiety produced a pressure behind her eyes like standing at the bottom of a swimming pool. She gripped the arm of her chair until the wood grain pressed ridges into her palm and let the noise wash through her.

*—should never have agreed to this format, the Westalians will use it to—*

*—my daughter's recital is at four, if this runs over I swear—*

*—Voss is here, which means the hardliners are treating this as—*

*—did I lock the car—*

*—coordinates confirmed, south terrace, fourteen hundred—*

Anya caught the last thought like a fishhook snagging fabric. She held still and tried to isolate the thinker. The mental voice was male, controlled, thinking in clipped operational shorthand — but four hundred people were between her and the source, and the signal dissolved back into the noise before she could triangulate.

South terrace. Fourteen hundred. That was ninety minutes from now.

She scanned the assembly floor. The Ostanian delegation sat in curved rows on the west side, dark suits and rigid posture. The Westalian delegation mirrored them from the east. Between them, a raised platform where the deputy ministers would deliver their addresses. Deputy Minister Gerhardt was already seated — Ostanian, mid-fifties, the kind of bureaucrat who'd survived three administrations by being useful to all of them. His thoughts were about his prepared remarks and whether his translator had gotten the idiom in paragraph four right.

*—the minister is exposed from the south approach, confirmed sightlines from the terrace through the gallery window—*

That one was closer. Anya turned her head a fraction, pretending to adjust her press lanyard, and narrowed her focus to the Westalian overflow section. Twenty people. She sifted through grocery lists, a hangover, someone composing a text to their ex, genuine policy anxiety — and there. A man in a charcoal suit, second row, whose surface thoughts were running tactical calculations under a veneer of diplomatic boredom.

*—team in position by thirteen-fifty, window opens when Gerhardt takes the podium, attributed to Westalian—*

The plan assembled itself in Anya's head with sickening clarity. A staged assassination attempt on Deputy Minister Gerhardt, timed to his address, executed from the south terrace, designed to be attributed to Westalian operatives. The summit's security would interpret it as proof that Westalis had sent assassins to their own peace conference. The Ostanian response would be immediate and devastating. Fourteen years of fragile peace, shattered by a provocation manufactured by the people who were supposed to want it preserved.

Bond's vision. The government building. The uniformed men. The fire. This was the place he'd seen.

Anya's headache was building — a dull throbbing that started behind her left eye and spread across her temples. She had maybe two hours of sustained reading at this intensity before something gave. She stood, tucked her notebook under her arm, and moved toward the stairs that connected the East Gallery to the service corridor below.

She needed to reach Loid. Their plan had designated specific communication drops inside the venue — a particular men's room, a particular fire exit, a particular janitor's closet where messages could be left in a pre-agreed code. She couldn't use her earpiece for this; the summit's electronic countermeasures would flag any unauthorized transmission. So she walked, and while she walked she kept reading, catching fragments from every mind she passed like a radio tuner dragged across stations.

*—the shrimp is excellent, whoever catered this should—*

*—if the Westalians propose mutual inspections we need to—*

*—I should call my wife—*

*—Falk's drop point is the comms room, east corridor—*

Anya stopped walking.

Falk. The name snagged her like a door she'd walked into. Gerald Falk, who had attended her Eden Academy graduation. Gerald Falk, whose surface thoughts in the WISE hallway had been orderly and unremarkable. Gerald Falk, who was one of the three mole candidates she'd narrowed to — and who she'd deprioritized after the wrong analyst's anxiety had eaten two days.

Falk was here. Falk had a drop point. And the thought had come from someone who knew it, someone near the East Gallery staircase — she spun and scanned, but the corridor was emptying as the opening session began, and whoever had been thinking about Falk's location was already inside the hall.

She changed direction. The janitor's closet could wait. She needed to tell Loid about the assassination timeline and Falk's position — both, now, because they were running out of the luxury of doing things one at a time.

She found the fire exit on the east side of the building and left a folded note behind the extinguisher: *South terrace 1400 — Gerhardt target — Falk in comms room east corridor.* Loid's signal. He would check within fifteen minutes. That was the plan.

Then she went back to the assembly hall, sat down, and reopened herself to four hundred minds.

The headache behind her eyes had graduated to a steady pulse.

Damian watched Werner Voss from across the West Gallery and thought about the precise weight of what he was about to do.

Voss stood near the railing, heavyset and contained in a dark suit that didn't quite disguise the military posture beneath it. He was talking to a National Unity Party delegate — a woman Damian recognized from a fundraiser, someone connected to defense procurement — and his expression carried the settled anger of a man who had been furious for so long the fury had become architecture. Voss had lost a brother in the last border conflict. The loss hadn't made him wise. It had made him certain.

Damian had the evidence in his breast pocket. Photocopies — old-fashioned, because electronic documents could be denied as fabricated but handwritten marginalia in Voss's own script on defense ministry stationery was harder to disavow. The shipping manifests Yor had found at Dominic's compound, cross-referenced with the contract records from his father's old network that Grosz had helped him decode. A paper trail connecting the Renewal Front's funding to specific defense procurement channels, with Voss's name at three critical junctions.

The opening session was underway. The Westalian deputy minister was delivering prepared remarks about security cooperation, and most of the gallery's attention had turned downward to the assembly floor. Damian moved along the railing toward Voss.

"Councillor Voss," he said. "A word."

Voss turned. He recognized Damian — everyone in the Ostanian political world recognized the Desmond name, even when they wished they didn't — and his expression arranged itself into the careful neutrality of a man who knew he was being approached by someone he couldn't openly dismiss.

"Young Desmond." Voss's voice was deep, unhurried. "I didn't expect to see you in the gallery. Your father preferred the floor."

"My father preferred a lot of things I don't." Damian kept his voice low. The gallery was not private, but the opening remarks had drawn attention downward, and the acoustics favored discretion. "I'd like to talk about your relationship with Kruger Defense Systems, their Ostanian procurement subsidiary, and the specific contract modifications you approved in the last eight months that redirected roughly four million marks toward entities that don't seem to exist outside of the Renewal Front's operational accounts."

Voss's face didn't change. But something behind his eyes recalculated.

"That's a significant accusation, Desmond. I hope you understand what you're putting at risk."

"I understand what I'm ending."

Damian set the documents on the gallery railing between them. Voss looked at them and did not pick them up. He looked at Damian instead — a long, measuring look that carried no fear and no surprise, which was worse than either.

"You think you're the first person to try this." Voss's voice dropped. Not threatening — tired, in a way that was more unsettling than anger. "Your father tried. He thought he could control the machine. He couldn't. He became the machine. And you — you think dismantling it makes you the hero."

"I'm not trying to be a hero. I'm trying to stop you from starting a war."

Voss picked up the documents. Scanned the first page. His mouth thinned. "These are from Dominic's files."

"Among other sources."

"Dominic is dead."

"I know."

Voss turned the page. Read. Turned another. His face had gone very still, and Damian could see him calculating — not how to deny it, but how to reframe it. That was the political instinct: not *whether* the truth, but *whose* truth.

"You want to know what your father understood that you don't?" Voss set the papers down. "This isn't Ostanian nationalism. It never was. The people who fund us — and I mean *us*, Desmond, including the people on the other side of this room — they don't care about Ostania's sovereignty. They care about the procurement cycle. Instability is an industry. Westalian defense firms buy our military intelligence through intermediaries. Our defense ministry buys their surveillance technology through shell companies. The same people who attend peace summits invest in the factories that build the missiles. Peace is a negotiating position, not a destination. Your father knew that. He made his peace with it. You haven't, and that's why you're standing here with papers you don't understand the implications of."

Damian had expected ideology. He had not expected this — the cynical, almost weary clarity of a man who had stopped believing in his own cause and continued anyway because the machine required operators, not believers.

"You're telling me the conspiracy is bilateral."

"I'm telling you there is no conspiracy. There is an economy. And you can burn me, Desmond. You can take those papers to the Westalian delegation and watch the diplomats perform their outrage. But the economy will survive. Because the people who profit from tension sit in *both* delegations, and they will rebuild whatever you tear down. They always do."

Damian's mouth was dry. He had prepared for defiance, for ideology, for the righteousness of a man who believed Ostania deserved more than the peace it had settled for. Voss was giving him something harder to fight: the truth, stripped of principle, offered freely because it cost Voss nothing. The machine would survive the operator.

But the assassination was still ninety minutes away. And the machine needed to be stopped today, even if it rebuilt itself tomorrow.

"Then I'll make it expensive," Damian said. He picked up the documents. "I'm going to the floor."

Voss's expression finally shifted — not fear, but a kind of bitter recognition. "Then you're the Desmond who dismantled it. Good luck living with that. In Ostania, the man who tore the house down doesn't get to build the new one. He's just the man with a hammer."

Damian went down the stairs. His hands were steady. The rest of him was not.

On the landing between floors, he passed a young delegate he recognized from the Foreign Affairs Ministry — a woman named Hartig who had organized his first briefing book, who had told him once over coffee that she thought the Desmond name could mean something different if the right Desmond carried it. She would hear about this in the next hour. She would have to decide whether she'd meant what she said.

He stepped onto the assembly floor during the Westalian deputy minister's closing remarks, walked to the Ostanian delegation's section, and asked for a point of procedure. The moderator — confused, consulting rules — granted it. Damian stood in front of both delegations, in front of the press gallery where Anya was sitting, in front of the security cameras and the SSS detail and the diplomats who had known his father, and he laid out what he had. Not the full intelligence picture — there were operational details that would compromise Anya and the family. But enough. The defense procurement channels. The Renewal Front's funding structure. The bipartisan rot.

The hall went very quiet. Then it went very loud.

The Ostanian delegation chair called for a recess. The Westalian deputy minister's translator whispered urgently. Press cameras swung toward Damian like compass needles finding north. The SSS security detail — including, somewhere in the Main Foyer, Yuri Briar — began moving.

Damian stood at the center of it all, and he could already feel the interpretation splitting. Half the room saw courage. The other half saw a young man who had just betrayed his country's secrets in front of its enemies. The Desmond name, already haunted, had acquired a new ghost.

He'd done what was necessary. He had no idea yet what it would cost.

Loid found the note behind the fire extinguisher at 13:42. Eighteen minutes to the assassination window. The handwriting was Anya's — he'd know it anywhere, the slightly impatient letterforms she'd had since she was a child, the way she still crossed her sevens like he'd taught her.

*South terrace 1400 — Gerhardt target — Falk in comms room east corridor.*

Falk.

The name sat in Loid's chest like a stone dropped into still water. Gerald Falk, who had been at WISE for twenty-three years. Who had vetted three generations of field operatives. Who had brought a bottle of wine to Anya's fifteenth birthday and stayed to help Yor with the dishes, which was an act of either extraordinary politeness or extraordinary bravery given Yor's approach to washing crystal.

Loid folded the note and moved. The East Service Corridor was a staff-access hallway running the length of the building's east side — fluorescent lights, concrete floor, the smell of cleaning solution and old coffee. He passed two security guards and a caterer carrying a tray of empty glasses. His credentials — Westalian diplomatic support staff, a cover he'd held dormant for three years — got him through the first checkpoint. The second he bypassed by timing the guard rotation, which hadn't changed since the security briefing he'd memorized the night before.

The Communications Room was at the end of the corridor. Single door, no window. Loid could hear the hum of radio equipment from outside. He tried the handle. Unlocked.

Gerald Falk was sitting at a console, headset around his neck, transmitting.

For a moment, Loid watched. Falk's fingers moved across the keyboard with the calm precision of a man performing routine work. His posture was the same posture Loid had seen in a thousand WISE briefing rooms — slightly hunched, the curve of someone who'd spent decades at desks, unremarkable in every particular. The ordinariness was its own cover. You didn't suspect Gerald Falk because Gerald Falk looked exactly like someone who would never be suspected.

"Gerald."

Falk's fingers stopped. He didn't turn around immediately. His back straightened, just slightly, the way a man adjusts when he understands that the last period of his life has just ended and the next has begun.

"Loid." Falk turned. His face was the same face it had always been — thinning hair, reading glasses pushed up on his forehead, the unremarkable features of a man who had moved through institutional spaces like furniture. "How much time do I have?"

"Not much. The coordinates you just sent — is that for the terrace team?"

Falk didn't deny it. "Thirteen-fifty deployment, fourteen hundred engagement. They'll be in position in eight minutes."

"And you're going to give me the abort code."

"I don't have one. That's not how it works. Voss's people operate independently once they're deployed."

Loid stepped into the room and closed the door. The radio equipment hummed between them. A green status light blinked on the console.

"Twenty-three years, Gerald."

"Twenty-four." Falk took off his glasses. Without them, he looked older and smaller, which was also a kind of honesty. "I know what you're going to say. Betrayal. Trust. All the words we've used on other people. But I'd like you to hear something first, because you're the only person in this building who might actually understand it."

Loid didn't respond. He was already cataloguing the room — one exit, the console, a secondary workstation, cable runs along the walls, a backup transmitter on a rack to the left. But he let Falk speak.

"The peace is a Westalian surrender, Loid. We gave up leverage for stability, and the stability is a fiction maintained by people who profit from the friction on both sides. Your generation signed an agreement that mortgaged this country's strategic position for the appearance of coexistence, and my generation is supposed to smile and accept it. I watched the analytical models we built get adjusted to fit political conclusions instead of the other way around. Threat assessments curved to match budgets. Intelligence product shaped to justify the peace rather than question it." Falk's voice was even, controlled, the voice of a man who had rehearsed this argument with himself for years. "I didn't sell secrets for money. I'm not a mercenary. I gave the Front what they needed because someone had to stop pretending that this arrangement serves anyone except the people at the top of both governments."

"And Crane?" Loid asked. "Did he serve the people at the top?"

Falk was quiet for a beat. "Crane was an operational cost."

"Crane was twenty-eight. He had a dog."

"I know how old he was. I vetted him."

The silence that followed had a specific weight — the weight of two men who had done the same work for the same institution and arrived at different conclusions about what it meant. Loid could feel the argument's gravity, the uncomfortable proximity of Falk's logic to positions he'd argued against inside his own head on bad nights.

"There's something else," Falk said. "And you're going to want to sit down for it, but there's no chair and there's no time, so I'll just say it. Your daughter filed a false report. Two months ago, maybe three — the initial assessment of the Ostanian operative she was assigned to track. She downgraded the threat profile, minimized the Renewal Front intelligence, redirected WISE analytical resources away from the sectors the Front was using. I don't know why she did it. I know what I did with it."

Loid's hands were in his pockets. They did not move.

"I found the discrepancies three weeks after she filed. Her analysis didn't match the cross-reference patterns from the Westalian intercepts. It was a good lie — careful, specific, the kind of report that passes first review — but the gaps were there if you knew what to look for. I'm an analyst. I've been reading these assessments for twenty-four years. I know what careful omission looks like."

"What did you do with it."

"I used it." Falk's voice carried no apology. "The gaps her report created — the sectors it deprioritized, the channels it left unmonitored — I redirected my own transmissions through those blind spots. She built me a corridor without knowing it. The intelligence that led to the ambush on Crane's checkpoint passed through a channel that your daughter's false report had taken off WISE's active watch list."

The room hummed. The green light blinked.

Loid stood very still. He was processing two things simultaneously, the way he'd been trained to process multiple intelligence streams, and the fact that he was processing them at all — analytically, operationally — when what he was actually hearing was that his daughter had lied to the organization he'd spent his life serving, and that her lie had been exploited to kill a man. The spy in him was already mapping the implications. The father in him had run into a wall and was standing in front of it unable to move.

*She files reports like I taught her. Careful. Specific. The kind that pass first review.*

"I'm going to stop the terrace team," Loid said. His voice was the same voice it always was — calm, precise, revealing nothing. "And then I'm going to shut down your transmitter. And then we're going to have a very different conversation with Sylvia."

"I know." Falk put his glasses back on. "For what it's worth, I didn't do this to hurt her. The girl, I mean. Anya. I watched her grow up. I gave her a pen at her graduation."

"I know what you gave her."

Loid reached past Falk and pulled the main transmission cable from the console. The green light went dark. Then he took the backup transmitter's power cord.

He did not look at Falk again until the SSS security detail arrived eleven minutes later. He spent those eleven minutes standing in the doorway, blocking the exit, the corridor's fluorescent lights humming behind him and the muffled sound of the assembly hall's chaos filtering through the walls. The radio equipment was silent now — both consoles dark, both transmission lines dead. The room smelled like warm electronics and the particular staleness of a space with no windows.

Eleven minutes. Loid used them to think about a twenty-year-old woman who had inherited his gift for careful lies and used it to build a corridor that a man she'd known since childhood had walked a killing through. He thought about the report he'd read three months ago — Anya's initial assessment of the Ostanian operative — and how he'd noted its precision without suspecting its dishonesty, because the craft was good. Because he'd taught her. Because the craft was his.

Yor heard the first gunshot from the West Service Corridor.

She'd been posted near the Main Foyer, watching the crowd filter back from a refreshment break, when Loid's signal came — a specific pattern of two coughs, audible through the open space, the same signal system they'd used for fourteen years of marriage because some things didn't need updating. She'd been moving toward the South Terrace ever since, heels clicking on the corridor tile in a rhythm she kept steady because uneven footsteps attracted attention. The signal meant *hostile contact, south.* It meant the terrace team that Bond had seen — the violence at the government building, the uniformed men in his fragmented vision — was here and real and about to begin.

She was wearing a dark blazer over a cream blouse, the kind of outfit that read as private security staff for the Westalian delegation. She had a blade in a thigh holster and another in her left boot. She had not brought the weapons the Garden would have supplied, because she was not here for the Garden. She was here for her family.

The South Terrace opened through a set of glass double doors at the end of the corridor. Through them, she could see the landscaped exterior grounds — flagstones, trimmed hedges, a line of vehicles at the checkpoint. And four men, moving fast, in civilian clothes that didn't hide the way they carried their bodies. Military. Two had sidearms already drawn. One was moving toward the terrace's sight line on the assembly hall windows. The fourth was positioning himself at the base of the exterior stairs — the escape route.

Deputy Minister Gerhardt was scheduled to take the podium in nine minutes. The terrace provided a clear line of fire through the gallery windows into the assembly floor. These men were here to kill a man and make it look like Westalis had done it.

Yor pushed through the glass doors.

The first shooter saw her and adjusted his aim. He was fast — trained, economical, the kind of shooter who didn't waste motion. He put a round into the doorframe six inches from her head, and the glass behind her shattered inward.

Yor was already moving. She closed the distance to the first shooter in three steps — she was faster than people expected, always had been, and the gap between expectation and reality was where she did her work. She caught his gun hand at the wrist, redirected the weapon upward, and drove the heel of her other hand into his throat. He went down choking. She took the sidearm from his hand and threw it over the terrace railing.

The second shooter fired. The round hit the flagstone where Yor had been standing a half-second before. She was already moving laterally, boot blade drawn, closing on him at an angle that forced him to choose between tracking her and covering his partner. He chose wrong. She sliced the tendons in his gun arm below the elbow — a precise cut, Garden-trained, the kind of wound that disabled without killing immediately. He dropped the weapon and she kicked it into the hedgerow.

The third man — the one who'd been positioning for the shot on Gerhardt — turned from the sight line and raised his weapon. Yor threw the boot blade. It caught him in the right shoulder, above the collarbone, and he went sideways into the railing. She closed on him before he could recover and took him down with a strike to the temple that left him unconscious on the flagstones.

Three down. The fourth — the one at the base of the stairs — was running. Not toward her. Away. Escape route. He had a radio and he was shouting into it.

Yor caught him at the bottom of the stairs. She hit him from behind, drove him face-first into the gravel path, put her knee between his shoulder blades, and pulled the radio from his hand. He was still trying to transmit when she put him out with a choke hold. Seven seconds from engagement to silence. The gravel bit into her knee through her trousers.

She stood up. Her blouse had blood on it — the second shooter's, from the wrist cut. One knee was wet from the gravel. She could feel the cold air on her face and the specific post-violence silence that always followed the last body going down, a silence that was different from ordinary quiet because it contained the echo of the sounds that had just stopped.

Her hands were shaking, just slightly, the fine tremor that always followed violence no matter how many times she'd done it. She wiped them on her blazer and checked the perimeter — flagstones, hedgerow, vehicles at the distant checkpoint, a gardener's utility shed twenty meters south. No secondary team. No backup. The four on the ground were all of it.

Someone was watching her from the West Service Corridor entrance.

Yuri stood in the doorway, SSS uniform crisp, sidearm half-drawn, frozen. He had come running at the first gunshot — standard protocol, his sector, his responsibility — and he had arrived in time to see the last forty seconds.

Yor looked at her brother.

Yuri looked at his sister.

She was standing over four incapacitated men on a government terrace, blood on her clothes, a blade in her boot, breathing evenly. Her face — gentle, sweet, the face that burned dinner and apologized to furniture — was calm in a way that belonged to a completely different woman. Yuri had watched her move through the four-man team with the precision and lethality of someone who had been doing this for a very long time. His sister. His gentle, clumsy, warmhearted sister. His sister who packed lunches and worried about him wearing a scarf.

"Sis." His voice cracked on the word. Not loud — Yuri, who got louder under pressure, who filled uncertainty with volume — barely audible.

Yor's hands stopped shaking. Something else took over. Not combat focus. Something older, from before she'd ever picked up a weapon. The thing that had made her take Garden assignments in the first place: the need to protect Yuri from the world that would hurt him.

"Yuri." Her voice was steady. "I need you to call medical for these men. The one with the shoulder wound is losing blood."

"How did you—" He stopped. He knew how. He could see how. The question he was asking wasn't about technique. "How long?"

Yor didn't answer. The answer was *since before you were old enough to remember,* and she couldn't say it on a terrace with four bleeding men and security cameras recording everything.

Yuri holstered his sidearm. He looked at the four men. He looked at his sister. He looked at the security camera mounted on the corner of the building, its red recording light steady and unblinking.

"I'm going to call medical," he said. His voice was professional now — SSS-officer professional, the kind of control that was a wall built in real time. "And I'm going to file my report on the terrace engagement. The report will note that four unidentified hostiles were neutralized by a member of the Westalian security delegation whose identity has not yet been confirmed."

The lie was clumsy. It wouldn't survive a detailed review. The camera footage alone would identify Yor within twenty-four hours. But Yuri wasn't thinking about twenty-four hours. He was thinking about right now, this second, the gap between what he was supposed to do and what he was going to do.

He was supposed to flag her. Report her. He was SSS, and a foreign national — his own sister, who was apparently not the person he thought she was — had just committed lethal violence at a diplomatic summit. His institutional duty was absolute and unambiguous.

He looked at Yor. She was picking up her boot blade, wiping it on the grass, sliding it back into the hidden holster with the practiced ease of someone who had done this motion ten thousand times.

"Go inside," Yuri said. "Before the response team gets here."

Yor hesitated. She wanted to say something — he could see it in the set of her mouth, the way her hands moved toward him and then stopped. She wanted to explain. She wanted to apologize. She wanted to say *I did it to protect you, everything I've ever done was to protect you,* but the words were inadequate and the terrace was wrong and the men on the ground were groaning and there was no version of this conversation that could happen here.

"Thank you," she said. It was completely insufficient and absolutely sincere.

She went inside.

Yuri stood on the South Terrace and called for medical. His hands were steady. His voice was steady. The thing inside his chest that had been certain about duty and country and the clean separation between right and wrong had cracked straight down the middle, and the crack felt permanent.

He had just put his sister above his country. He would do it again. He would do it every time. And the part of him that had built his entire adult identity on the principle that the state came first had nothing to say about that, because it turned out that principle was load-bearing only until it was tested.

The medical team arrived. Two paramedics, one stretcher. Yuri directed them to the shoulder wound first — the man Yor had hit with the boot blade was losing color, his shirt darkening. Yuri watched the paramedics work and he kept his hands behind his back because he didn't trust them not to shake. The camera was still recording. His report was already filed. The hole in it was already there, shaped like Yor, shaped like every meal she'd ever cooked him, every scarf she'd insisted he wear, every time she'd said *be careful* when he left for work, and he'd thought she was worrying about traffic.

Anya felt the secondary relay go active at 14:07.

She was in the East Gallery, managing four hundred minds through a headache that had progressed from pulse to pressure to something that felt like her skull was being squeezed in a vise. She'd tracked the terrace engagement through fragmented thoughts — *gunshots south, security response, someone's down* — and she'd caught Yor's combat in a flash of tactical precision from a mind she'd have known in a crowd of a thousand. She'd caught the moment Damian reached the assembly floor and started talking, the room's collective shock arriving as a wave of surface thoughts that hit her like a physical blow — *Desmond, what is he doing, is this authorized, the Renewal Front, is he saying what I think he's—*

And then, underneath all of it, a signal she almost missed.

A quiet electronic hum from the back of her awareness. Not a thought — a presence. Equipment. Specifically, a secondary transmitter that she'd felt earlier as background static and dismissed because the summit's communications infrastructure was full of radio noise. But this one had changed. It had gone active. And the mind nearest to it was thinking: *—backup relay online, Falk's primary went dark but the secondary has a twelve-minute buffer, transmitting cached coordinates and delegation positions—*

Anya surged to her feet. The movement was too fast and the headache hit her like a wall — the gallery tilted, her vision went grey at the edges, and she grabbed the seat back in front of her. The delegate sitting there turned and frowned. She muttered an apology and pushed toward the stairs.

The Communications Room. That was where the backup relay was. Loid had shut down Falk's primary console, but Falk had established a secondary — a contingency, the kind of redundancy that a twenty-four-year intelligence analyst would build into any operation he cared about surviving his own capture. The backup was transmitting. It had been transmitting since the moment Loid pulled the main cable. It was sending cached intelligence out of the building — delegation positions, security configurations, the summit's internal communications architecture — to whoever the Renewal Front had waiting to receive it.

She ran. Down the gallery stairs, into the East Service Corridor. The fluorescent lights were too bright and every footstep sent a jolt through her temples. She passed the fire exit where she'd left the note, passed two security guards who didn't stop her because her press credentials were still clipped to her blazer, and reached the Communications Room door.

Loid was still inside. Falk was sitting on the floor, hands behind his back — Loid had secured him with his own belt, which was resourceful and also, Anya noted distantly, the kind of improvisation that her father had taught her to do when she was twelve. An SSS detail was on its way. Loid looked up when she entered and his face did something she hadn't seen before — a complicated thing, relief layered over something harder that she couldn't read because her telepathy was giving her everything and nothing at the same time, the room's thoughts crashing into her like surf.

"The backup relay," she said. "It's been transmitting since you cut the primary. Twelve-minute buffer."

Loid's expression shifted — the spy replacing the father, clean and immediate. He moved to the secondary workstation, the one on the rack to the left. "Here?"

"Yes. It's—" She pointed. The backup transmitter was a small unit, barely larger than a radio, tucked into the equipment rack between a signal booster and a cable junction. Its status light was solid green. Loid reached it in two steps and pulled the power.

The green light went dark.

Anya leaned against the wall. The headache had peaked. She could still read the room — Falk on the floor, thinking *it's done, it doesn't matter, the buffer already—*; Loid standing over the dead transmitter, thinking — she tried to read him and the signal swam, his surface thoughts wrapped in that same hard thing she couldn't name.

"How long was it active?" Loid asked.

She checked the console display. "Eleven minutes, forty seconds."

Nearly the full buffer. The relay had transmitted almost everything it cached before they'd shut it down. Delegation positions. Security layouts. Communication protocols. Intelligence that the Renewal Front's external network could use to rebuild, to regroup, to complicate the cleanup in ways that wouldn't be clear for weeks.

They'd won. They'd stopped the assassination. They'd caught Falk. Damian had exposed the conspiracy on the summit floor. Yor had stopped the terrace team. The Renewal Front was broken.

And Anya had missed the backup relay because four hundred minds at maximum capacity for two hours had turned her brain into a blunt instrument, and the secondary signal had been hiding under the noise like a splinter she couldn't feel until it was too late.

*—she filed a false report—*

The thought came from Falk, on the floor, looking at her. His surface mind was churning with the aftermath of capture and the particular resentment of a man whose operation had been stopped, and in that churn she caught the thing he'd told Loid.

She looked at her father.

Loid was looking at her. His face was calm — it was always calm, he was the greatest spy in the world and his face was always exactly what he chose it to be. But his eyes. She'd been reading her father's thoughts for twenty years and she had never needed the telepathy to know when something was wrong. The way he was looking at her now was new. Not angry. Not disappointed. Something worse: the look of a man who recognized himself in his daughter's worst decision.

*I trained her,* he was thinking. *I taught her how to do this.*

Anya opened her mouth. She wanted to explain — the false report, why she'd done it, Damian's counter-operation, the impossible convergence of her cover and her feelings and her institutional obligations. She wanted to say it all, right now, in this room with Falk on the floor and the dead transmitter and the fluorescent lights.

"Not here," Loid said. Quiet. The spy's economy. "Later."

She nodded.

Outside, the summit was in controlled chaos. Damian's exposure had sent both delegations into emergency session. The terrace team was being processed by SSS and Westalian security. Press were running. Phones were ringing. Someone had pulled a fire alarm, either as a diversion or by genuine accident, and the alarm's shriek cut through the building like a blade.

Anya walked through the Main Foyer toward the exit. The four hundred minds she'd been reading had thinned — delegates evacuating, security corralling press, the building half-emptied — but the remaining thoughts were louder, sharper, the way voices get louder when a room goes quiet.

*—did the Desmond kid just tank the summit—*

*—Gerhardt is fine, he's in the secure room, the attempt failed—*

*—who is the woman from the terrace, the footage is—*

*—Briar filed his report, but something in the redactions doesn't—*

*—this changes nothing, Voss was right, the economy survives the operator—*

Anya kept walking. Her head was splitting. She stepped out into the cold air of the exterior grounds — early winter, grey, the kind of cold that cut through a blazer — and she leaned against the building's stone wall and closed her eyes.

They'd won. The Renewal Front was broken. Gerhardt was alive. The mole was caught. The conspiracy was public. The peace, fragile and flawed and maintained by people who were not as noble as they pretended, would hold.

And her father knew she'd been lying to WISE. And Yuri Briar had watched his sister kill four men with the efficiency of a career assassin. And Damian was standing in the assembly hall being called either a patriot or a traitor depending on which delegation you asked. And the Garden would learn that Yor had gone off-book, acted independently, used her training for people who hadn't authorized it. And eleven minutes and forty seconds of intelligence had leaked through a relay Anya had missed because her power had limits and those limits had consequences.

Victory, she thought, should feel different from this.

The building's stone was cold against her back. Above her, through the gallery windows, she could see the assembly hall's lights still burning. Somewhere inside, Damian was becoming "the man who blew up the summit." Somewhere inside, Loid was standing over Gerald Falk, carrying the knowledge that his daughter had inherited his talent for careful lies. Somewhere inside, Yuri Briar was filing a report with a hole in it the exact shape of his sister.

Anya read the thoughts of the security guards at the perimeter — tired, confused, wondering if they'd get overtime — and the thoughts of a janitor pushing a cart through the foyer — *this is going to take all night to clean up* — and the thoughts of a delegate's driver dozing in a parked car — *dreaming about a beach, warm sand, his wife's voice calling from the water.*

She read them all because she couldn't stop. She had never been able to stop. She'd been reading people since she was four years old and she had never once been inside someone's head with their permission, and today that power had done exactly what it was supposed to do — found the threat, saved the minister, broken the conspiracy — and it still wasn't enough, because four hundred minds at maximum volume for two hours had turned the most powerful tool in the world into a blunt instrument that missed the one thing it needed to catch.

The cold pressed through her blazer. Her hands were in her pockets. She stood there until Loid came to find her, which he did, because he was her father, and that was what fathers did, even when they had learned something about their daughters that changed the shape of everything they thought they knew.

He didn't say anything when he reached her. He stood next to her against the cold stone wall, and they watched the summit's aftermath unfold through the glass doors — security vehicles, shouting, the controlled disorder of institutions responding to a crisis they had almost failed to prevent.

"You did good work today," Loid said, after a while.

"Dad—"

"Not here." Still quiet. Still the spy's economy. But his shoulder was touching hers against the wall, and he didn't move it.

They stood there. The cold got worse. Neither of them went inside.
