# Chapter 13

The phone rang at 6:14 in the morning.

Anya was already awake. She'd been awake for most of the night, cycling between the encrypted traffic on her WISE terminal and the anxious surface thoughts of the neighbor in 4B, who was up with a colicky infant and thinking, *please please please just sleep, I'm begging you, just sleep for one hour.* The baby's wailing came through the wall as a thin, persistent complaint. The neighbor's thoughts came through louder.

She answered the secure line on the second ring. Sylvia's voice was flat. Not angry, not shaken — flat in the particular way that meant the news was final.

"Agent Crane is dead. The border checkpoint at Kreuzfeld was compromised at approximately 0340. He was engaged by a three-person team while crossing on forged documents. The team had his route, his timing, and his cover identity. He died at the scene."

Anya sat on the edge of her bed with the phone pressed to her ear and the neighbor's desperate thoughts running through her mind — *please sleep, please just this once* — and she could not speak. The baby wailed. The neighbor's exhaustion was a gray static that filled her apartment like weather.

"His body has been recovered," Sylvia continued. "The documents were not. I have informed his wife."

Crane. David Crane. He'd given Anya his parking space three months ago when she was hauling equipment from a field op, said it with the particular casualness of someone who wanted to be helpful without making a production of it. He called everyone "chief." He was forty-one. He had a wife and no children and he'd been helping her track weapons shipments through Ostania's eastern border crossings for six weeks, running routes she'd flagged based on fragments she'd pulled from a dozen minds at a dozen different receptions. Fragments she had not reported accurately. That fact pressed against the inside of her skull — not her chest, not her throat, but the place behind her eyes where the telepathic noise lived, as if the knowledge were another person's thought she was being forced to receive.

"There is an additional development," Sylvia said. "An SSS tactical unit conducted a search-and-seizure operation at a residential address in eastern Berlint at approximately 0300. The address corresponds to a location flagged in our analytical review last week. The unit was operating on a specific warrant. They knew what to look for and where to find it."

Damian's cache. The apartment where he'd stored the intelligence files on the Renewal Front — the contact maps, the financial trails, the careful documentation of a man dismantling something from the inside. She'd been in that apartment once. The living room had a shelf of diplomatic histories organized by region, and there was a stain on the carpet near the kitchen where he'd dropped a coffee mug and never replaced the carpet because, he'd said, that would require calling his landlord and he didn't want anyone looking closely at the unit. The stain was shaped like a small country.

"The material was seized intact," Sylvia said. "Analysis is pending. The operational picture in the eastern sector is compromised."

*Compromised* was Sylvia's word for destroyed. The eastern sector was where Damian operated. The material that was seized included everything he'd spent months building — the network maps, the funding trails, the names of people who had trusted him enough to share information about the Renewal Front's structure. People who were now exposed.

"I have questions about reporting timelines for your sector that I will raise in the morning briefing," Sylvia said. "Attend at 0800."

Anya said, "Yes."

There was a pause. On the other end of the line, Sylvia was too far away to read — just a voice doing the work that voices did when someone was dead and the institution continued. But the pause itself carried something. Sylvia had done this before. She would do it again. The delivery was not cold — it was structural, the way a building's foundation was structural. The flatness was load-bearing.

"Crane was a good officer," Sylvia said. It sat in the same register as everything else she'd said, but it was the only sentence in the call that carried weight it hadn't been assigned. "We will debrief in the morning."

The line disconnected.

Anya sat. The neighbor in 4B was thinking *okay, maybe if I just hold him differently, maybe if I just —* and the baby screamed and the thought dissolved into raw, bone-tired misery that leaked through the wall and through Anya's mind and sat down next to her own.

She called Damian.

He answered immediately, which meant he already knew. His voice was controlled — the diplomatic register, the one that kept everything at arm's length — but she could hear the strain underneath it. "I'm aware," he said. "My contact in the SSS reached me twenty minutes ago. They took everything."

"Crane is dead," Anya said.

A pause. Then: "Who?"

"WISE field officer. He was running border routes I — routes I was coordinating."

"I'm sorry," Damian said, and it sounded like he meant it, which made it worse, because he didn't know the shape of what he was offering condolence for. He didn't know that the routes Crane had been running were built on intelligence Anya had filtered — that her false report, the one she'd filed sitting across from Sylvia, had redirected WISE's analytical attention away from the channels the Renewal Front was actually using, and that Falk, or whoever the mole was, had operated more freely in the blind spots her lie had opened. She couldn't draw the line directly from her report to Crane's body at a border checkpoint at 3:40 in the morning. But the line was there. The gaps she'd created in the operational picture had made space for the mole to move, and people had died in the space.

"There's something else," Damian said. "Reinhardt called me an hour ago."

Reinhardt. The name landed and Anya searched her memory — Damian had mentioned him twice, both times carefully, the way you mention someone whose trust you can't afford to lose. A mid-level official in the administrative ministry, not a Renewal Front sympathizer but not a crusader either. A man who'd fed Damian scheduling information and department memos because he'd believed, quietly, that the government's hardline elements were dangerous. Reinhardt had two daughters and coached their football team on Saturdays. He brought Damian photocopies in a gym bag and left them in the back seat of his car in the ministry garage. Anya had read his name in Damian's thoughts weeks ago, alongside a particular gratitude that Damian never expressed aloud.

"He's done," Damian said. "He told me not to call again. He thinks the SSS pulled the address from his department's records. He thinks they'll come for him next."

"Will they?"

"I don't know. Maybe. If they're working from the seized material, they'll find his name eventually. I told him I could arrange — " He stopped. "He hung up."

Anya stood in her apartment, in the gray November light that came through the kitchen window and turned everything the color of used water, and she thought about David Crane's parking space and Reinhardt's Saturday football and the file she'd handed Sylvia with the assessment she'd designed to protect the man on the other end of this phone call. She had protected Damian. She had protected his operation. She had drawn a line around the things she cared about and everything outside the line had paid for it.

*Please just sleep,* the neighbor thought. *Please.*

"We should meet," Anya said. "Not at the usual place."

"I know somewhere," Damian said.

The basement was underneath a printing shop in a quiet block of western Berlint, accessible through a service door next to a dumpster that smelled like wet ink and cardboard. Damian had a key. He didn't explain how. The stairs were steep concrete and the railing was cold enough that Anya released it immediately and put her hands in her coat pockets.

The room at the bottom was maybe fifteen feet by twenty. Concrete floor, concrete walls, a steel folding table and four chairs that didn't match. A single fluorescent tube on the ceiling that buzzed at a frequency she could feel in her teeth. The air was cold and damp and sat on the skin like a compress. There were water stains on the far wall in long vertical streaks, as if the wall had been weeping slowly for years.

Damian was already there, sitting in one of the chairs with his elbows on the table and his hands loosely clasped. He looked like he'd been there a while. He was wearing the same coat he'd worn the night she'd saved him from the ambush — she recognized the burn mark near the left cuff where something hot had grazed the fabric. His split eyebrow had healed into a thin pale line that pulled his expression slightly upward on the left side, a permanent look of mild skepticism that he probably didn't know about.

She sat down. The chair was metal and the cold came through her jeans immediately. The fluorescent tube buzzed.

*I should have seen this coming,* his surface thought ran, and beneath it a rolling current of anger and guilt and calculation. *The cache was too static. I kept it in one place too long. That's basic. That's a mistake a first-year would make. And Reinhardt — I put his name in that file. I wrote it down. His name is in a file that the SSS is reading right now.*

She didn't respond to his thoughts. She sat in the bad light and felt the cold metal of the chair through her clothes and let his self-recrimination wash through her alongside her own. There was a coffee stain on the folding table, old and pale, someone else's meeting. Someone else had sat here before them with their own failure, and the stain was all that was left of it.

"They knew exactly where to look," Damian said aloud. "The warrant wasn't a fishing expedition. Someone gave them the address."

"The mole," Anya said.

"Your mole."

The emphasis was not an accusation. But it wasn't nothing. The mole was inside WISE. WISE was Anya's organization. The intelligence that enabled the raid had traveled from the mole through the Renewal Front to the SSS, and the mole operated inside the same institution that Anya had lied to about Damian's significance.

"I know," she said.

His thoughts churned. *Three months of work. All of it. The contact maps — Reinhardt, Grosz's second-level names, the financial routing through the shell companies — all of it. They have all of it now.* And underneath, quieter: *Reinhardt's girls are eight and ten. Football on Saturdays. He coaches because he wanted them to have something that wasn't politics.*

"Reinhardt's going to be okay," Anya said, and immediately hated herself for saying it because she didn't know that and the words had come from reading his worry, not from any intelligence. "I mean — I think he'll be okay. His involvement was peripheral enough that the seized material might not — "

"You don't know that."

"No."

The fluorescent tube buzzed. A pipe somewhere in the wall behind them made a knocking sound — water hammer, the building's plumbing protesting some pressure change in the system above. One knock, two, three, and then silence.

"Tell me about Crane," Damian said.

"He was a field officer. Forty-one. He ran border crossings — document verification, courier routes, the physical side of intelligence movement." She paused. The words were operational and she could hear them being operational and she wanted to stop talking that way but didn't have another way to talk about someone who was dead because of work she'd directed. "He was helping me. He was running routes I'd mapped from — from my analysis."

*From the fragments.* From reading strangers' surface thoughts at receptions and dinners and briefings, assembling a picture no one had authorized her to assemble, and then filing a version of that picture that was incomplete because she'd left Damian's sector out. And the incomplete picture had left gaps. And the mole had moved in those gaps. And Crane had died in one.

Her telepathy hadn't saved him. She read minds — hundreds of them, thousands across the months of this operation — and she hadn't read the one that mattered, at the time it mattered, in the place it mattered. Her range had limits. Her attention had limits. She'd been reading a drunken classmate at a charity gala while someone, somewhere, was finalizing the intelligence that would put a three-person kill team at Crane's checkpoint.

"The thing I keep — " she started, and then stopped, because the sentence she'd been about to say was *The thing I keep thinking is that I could have prevented this*, and the sentence underneath that was *I helped cause this*, and neither of them was something she could say to Damian without explaining how. Without telling him that she'd lied to WISE about him. Without telling him that her lie had created the operational architecture in which a mole could operate undetected. Without telling him things that would make him look at her differently, and she needed him to not look at her differently, because he was the only person left.

"What?" Damian said.

"Nothing. I was — nothing."

*She's doing that thing,* his surface thought read, *where she starts and then pulls back. She does that when what she was about to say would cost her.*

He was right. He was usually right about her, in the ways that didn't require telepathy. He saw more than she wanted him to see, even without the advantage she carried and he didn't know about.

They sat in the cold for a while. The fluorescent tube buzzed. Anya's telepathy picked up faint thoughts from above — the printing shop employees arriving for the morning shift, someone thinking about a football match, someone cataloguing a paper delivery. The mundane world continuing above them while they sat in a hole in the ground with a dead colleague and a destroyed operation.

"We're burned," Damian said. "My Ostanian network is compromised. Your border routes are compromised. The mole is still inside your organization and now they know — whoever's running this — that we're enough of a threat to warrant a coordinated strike."

"Yes."

"So what do we do?"

Anya's hands were cold. She'd put them in her pockets and they were still cold. The concrete bled warmth out of everything it touched — the table, the chairs, the air itself. She thought about the question and didn't have an answer.

"I don't know," she said.

His thought, quiet: *Neither do I.*

The honesty of it — his internal admission matching what she'd said aloud — felt like the most intimate thing that had passed between them since the safe house. Not the kiss. Not the laughter or the wound care or the shared adrenaline. This. Two people sitting in a cold room without answers.

"We're still here," Anya said. "That's — I know that's not much."

"It's not nothing."

*She looks exhausted,* he thought. *Her hands are shaking — no, she's cold. We're both cold. This basement is freezing. I should —*

He took off his coat — the one with the burn mark — and held it out across the table. She stared at it.

"I'm fine," she said.

"You're shaking."

"I'm fine. I don't — " She stopped. She didn't want his coat because she didn't want comfort, and she didn't want comfort because comfort implied that this was a problem that could be made smaller by warmth. A man was dead. "I'm fine."

He set the coat on the table between them. He didn't insist. His thought was small and resigned: *Okay.*

The pipe knocked again. Once, twice. Silence.

"I should've been better," Anya said, and the words came out smaller than she intended. "At the reception — I caught a fragment. About the mole. A thought I couldn't source. And then the investigation — I was wrong about the analyst and it cost us two days and in those two days — "

"You can't calculate like that."

"I can, though."

"Anya."

He said her name — her cover name, the one he'd been calling her since the state dinner — and underneath it, in the part of his mind she was always listening to whether she wanted to or not, the thought was clear and certain: *Anya Forger.* She heard it. She'd been hearing it since the gala, the recognition hardening in his mind like concrete setting. He hadn't said it aloud. He was holding it, the way she held things — information as weight, knowledge as a thing you carried.

She didn't address it. She couldn't. Not here, not now, not sitting in this room where a dead man's logistics occupied the same air as the slow collapse of her cover. She touched the edge of the folding table. The metal was cold. Everything in this room was cold, including the conversation they were about to have about what was left.

"The Renewal Front is accelerating," Damian said, switching to operational register, the diplomatic precision reasserting itself over the rawer thing that had been showing through. "The raid and the ambush were coordinated. That means the provocation is close. Days."

"I know."

"Then we don't have time to be burned." He paused. *Rebuild from nothing.* "We have to work with what's left."

"Which is what?"

He looked at her across the folding table in the buzzing fluorescent light. His thought was simple: *Each other. That's what's left.*

He said, "We'll figure it out."

She didn't believe him. She didn't disbelieve him either. She sat with the uncertainty and the cold and the guilt and the faint thoughts leaking down from the printing shop — paper stock, a football score, someone's aching knee — and she thought about David Crane's wife, who had been told by Sylvia's flat, practiced voice at some terrible hour. She thought about the parking space and the word "chief" and the routes she'd mapped and the gaps she'd created, and the thing she could not say to the man sitting across from her, which was that she had drawn those gaps herself, in a room with a woman who trusted her, and a colleague had died in one.

Damian took the long way home.

He walked east through Berlint's commercial district, past shuttered storefronts and early-morning delivery trucks and the cafes rolling up their awnings for a day that didn't know yet what had happened. The November air was sharp and he'd left his coat in the basement — set it on the table for her and she hadn't taken it and he hadn't taken it back — and the wind cut through his dress shirt at every cross street. He didn't go back for it.

He walked because movement was better than sitting, and because his apartment was only ten minutes from the government district by the direct route, and he needed more than ten minutes before he did what he was about to do. A woman passed him carrying a bag of groceries and looking at her phone, and she didn't look up, and the ordinariness of her not looking up was the kind of thing that could break you if you let it.

Reinhardt had daughters. Eight and ten. Damian didn't know their names. He'd been careful not to learn their names because names created attachment and attachment created vulnerability. He'd learned their ages anyway. He'd learned about the football. He'd learned that Reinhardt brought the photocopies in a gym bag because the gym bag was the only thing he carried in and out of the ministry garage that nobody searched. These were details a person trusted you with by accident, over time, in the gaps between useful exchanges. These were the details that made it impossible to think about compromise and exposure and SSS warrants as abstractions.

His apartment was on the fourth floor of a building that had been elegant once and was now maintained just enough to justify its rent. The lobby had marble flooring with three cracked tiles that the management company had covered with a rug. The elevator groaned between floors. His unit was small by Desmond standards, which meant it was ordinary by everyone else's — a living room, a bedroom, a kitchen where the light above the sink flickered when the neighbor ran the shower. He'd chosen it because it was unremarkable. Nothing about it invited attention.

He locked the door behind him and stood in the entryway. His shoes left wet prints on the tile from the rain that had started while he was walking, a fine drizzle that he hadn't bothered to avoid. The apartment was cold. He'd left the bedroom window cracked last night and the November air had settled over everything like a thin, patient guest that had no plans to leave. The kitchen light above the sink was off, which meant the neighbor hadn't showered yet. The refrigerator hummed. The sound was the apartment's only sign of life.

The box was in the bedroom closet, on the top shelf, behind a stack of diplomatic histories he'd never finished. He'd put it there when he moved in three years ago. He'd opened it once before, months ago. Late at night, after a dinner where he'd performed beliefs he didn't hold for people who genuinely held them. He'd opened the box and found the class photo and looked at the girl in the second row and felt something he couldn't name and put the box back and gone to sleep and dreamed about a schoolyard.

He took the box down now.

It was a cardboard filing box, the kind sold at office supply stores, slightly crushed on one corner from an old move. Inside: a school blazer he'd outgrown at thirteen, folded badly. A handwritten note from his mother, the ink fading toward illegibility. A runner-up certificate from a math competition, which he'd kept because second place in a Desmond household was a specific kind of achievement — not good enough to display, too good to discard, the certificate equivalent of Damian himself. Three pens his father had given him for the entrance exams. "I expect you to return them blunted," Donovan had said, which was the closest he'd come to wishing luck. Damian had returned them blunted. His father had not mentioned it again.

And the class photo.

He sat on the edge of the bed and held the photo in both hands. Standard group portrait — three rows, the back row standing on a bench, the teacher at the side with a smile that looked mandated. The second row, seated. Third from the left: a girl with pink hair and a grin so wide it was nearly aggressive, aimed at something off-camera. She was small — the smallest in the row. Her uniform was slightly rumpled in a way that suggested she'd been doing something physical right before the photo was taken. She was six, maybe seven. She was unmistakable.

Anya Forger.

He set the photo on his knee and looked at the girl and then looked away and then looked at her again because looking away didn't change anything.

The woman at the state dinner — the cultural attache who'd appeared in his orbit with impeccable timing and an ease that felt earned rather than constructed — was Anya Forger. The woman who'd read a room full of diplomats and located him within minutes. Who'd intervened to save him from a Renewal Front operative with a social diversion so precisely targeted it could only have come from someone who understood the operative's mind with perfect clarity. Who'd formed an alliance with him based on intelligence she attributed to her network. Who'd sat in a park with him after the Bretz operation and laughed at his joke with a laugh he'd recognized without knowing why.

The classmate at Becky's gala had said the name and the room hadn't changed because the room didn't know what the name meant. But Damian had known. He'd felt it land with the specific weight of a thing he'd already been carrying — recognition that had been forming since the first night, crystal growing in solution, and the name was the nucleation point. He'd spent the rest of the gala not saying it. He'd spent the days since not saying it. He'd sat across from her in a freezing basement this morning and thought her real name and watched her not react.

She was good at not reacting. She was intelligence. Not reacting was the craft.

He tried to think about what it meant. His father had taught him, through example and absence, that emotion was a vulnerability other people exploited, and the analytical mind was the only reliable defense. His brother had taught him the same thing less nobly: maintain your position, manage your exposures. Damian had spent twenty years absorbing these lessons and he was good at them — good enough to run a covert operation against his own government, good enough to perform loyalty he didn't feel in rooms full of people who felt it genuinely.

But the analytical mind kept breaking against the same set of questions.

If she was Anya Forger, then Westalis had placed an operative near him since childhood. But that didn't hold. She'd been a child. Whatever WISE had done with her afterward, the girl in the second row of this photo had been seven years old and she'd punched him during dodgeball because he'd called her a freak and he'd deserved it. That wasn't positioning. That was being seven. No intelligence agency recruits a seven-year-old to cultivate the second son of a politician who wasn't yet important enough to cultivate. Whatever brought her back into his orbit at the state dinner, it wasn't a continuation of something that started in a schoolyard.

But it was connected. It had to be connected. Eden Academy was where the Westalian diplomatic community sent their children, and the Forgers were — what? He'd never thought about it at the time. Anya's father was a doctor, or something administrative, the kind of profession that nobody questioned. Her mother worked at city hall. They were normal. Conspicuously normal, maybe, in retrospect. The kind of normal that a skilled operative might construct.

The friendship at Eden — was that real? He could not resolve this from the outside. He could only look at the photo and remember specific things. She'd shared her lunch with him once, after his was stolen by Emile — half a peanut butter sandwich, thrust at him with the particular ferocity of a child embarrassed to be caught being kind. She'd defended him, once, fiercely and without being asked, against a teacher who'd called him his father's son in a tone that made it an indictment. She'd said something — he couldn't quite remember the words — about people and their fathers, and the teacher had looked startled, and the class had gone quiet, and Damian had felt for the first time that someone was on his side who didn't want anything from him for it.

The safe house. Her hands shaking as she cleaned the cut above his eyebrow. Her fingers were cold and precise. She'd flinched when she laughed and the bruised rib protested. The antiseptic she'd used stung and she'd said sorry and he'd said it was fine and they were both lying about different things. The kiss — he remembered it with a clarity that surprised him, because under stress his memory typically compressed events into summary. But this one was high-resolution. Her mouth, the cold room, the rough blanket underneath them on the narrow cot. The way she'd pulled back once, as if catching herself, and then hadn't pulled back the second time. The feeling — he was twenty and still clumsy with feeling, still not sure which emotions were permitted and which were tactical liabilities — that she was holding something she couldn't give him, and he was holding something he couldn't ask for.

Was any of it real.

He looked at the photo. The girl was seven. She was grinning. She was real.

And the woman in the basement this morning, who'd said *I should've been better* with a voice that was small and hurt and completely without performance — that was real too. He didn't need intelligence training to know it. He'd grown up in a house of performances. He knew the difference between a person in genuine pain and a person performing pain for an audience. She hadn't been performing. She'd been sitting with a dead colleague and a failed operation and she'd been smaller than he'd ever seen her, and whatever else she was — Westalian operative, childhood friend who'd reappeared under cover, someone who knew things she couldn't possibly know through the channels she claimed — she'd been real.

He set the photo down on the bed. He looked at the ceiling above him — water damage from the roof, a stain shaped like an archipelago. He'd looked at it on nights when sleep wouldn't come and the weight of what he was doing pressed hardest, and the stain had never offered anything, which was its virtue. It was just damage. It didn't mean anything. It didn't require anything from him.

The decision wasn't hard. That was the part that surprised him. He'd expected it to be hard — the betrayal of discovering his partner had been lying about her identity since their first meeting, that Westalis might have had eyes on the Desmond family since he was a child. He'd expected sustained anger. He'd felt anger, at the gala, when the name locked into place. A controlled detonation. But the anger had been brief, and what replaced it was the recognition that he was not innocent of deception himself. He had hidden his counter-operation from colleagues who trusted him. He had performed loyalty to a faction he despised. He had maintained relationships with people whose beliefs he found dangerous because those people were useful. He had lied, daily, about who he was and what he wanted. Anya had done the same. And the fact that her lies happened to include lying to him specifically did not make them categorically different from his own.

He would continue the alliance. The Renewal Front was accelerating — a provocation days away, the conspiracy reaching into both governments — and the stakes were too high to collapse over identity. Burning the partnership meant burning the last operational link either of them had. It meant Reinhardt's daughters growing up with their father arrested, or worse, and no one left to work the angles that might protect him. It meant the mole operating unchecked inside WISE. It meant the Renewal Front achieving its provocation and the fragile peace between Westalis and Ostania cracking in a way that couldn't be repaired by the next generation, or the one after that.

He would continue. But the terms had changed, and the change was not something he intended to announce.

He was no longer the partner who operated blind while she held cards he didn't know existed. He didn't understand how she knew the things she knew — her intelligence sourcing was opaque in ways he hadn't solved and might not solve, and that uncertainty was its own kind of weight. But he knew who she was. He knew the girl in the second row was the woman in the basement, and that knowledge — held, unspoken, carried into every future exchange like a stone in the pocket of a coat he no longer had — meant the asymmetry between them was no longer running in one direction.

She thought she was the one who saw clearly. She thought she held the advantage. She didn't know that the boy she'd punched during dodgeball was sitting in his apartment with a class photo on his knee, seeing her.

He put the photo back in the box. He put the box on the shelf, behind the diplomatic histories he would never read. He closed the closet door, and the small click of the latch was the loudest sound the apartment had made since he'd come home.

The apartment was still cold. The bedroom window was still cracked. He closed it and the November air stopped coming in and the room was quiet, and he stood there for a while in the quiet, not looking at anything in particular, which was the only rest he was going to get.
