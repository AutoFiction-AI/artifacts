# Chapter 5

Damian's car smelled like leather conditioner and something underneath it — cedar, maybe, or whatever cologne he'd put on and then apparently tried to remove, because the faintest chemical sweetness of soap clung to his collar.

*She can probably smell that. Can she smell that? It doesn't matter. Focus. The operational parameters—*

Anya focused on the Gesellschaft Club's membership roster instead. Damian had pulled it from somewhere — she didn't ask where, because their terms included not questioning each other's networks, and the less she knew about how a twenty-year-old diplomat obtained a private club's internal documents, the cleaner her operational conscience stayed. Not that her operational conscience was in great shape these days.

"Bretz arrives around eight," Damian said, turning the folder's pages with the controlled precision of someone handling classified material rather than a seating chart. "He's a creature of habit. Cognac in the salon, dinner at nine, never stays past eleven because his driver has an early rotation. His wife is—"

"Margarethe. I read the file."

"Right." A beat. *She always does that. Finishes the sentence before I— never mind.* "The point is, we have approximately three hours to get close enough for a sustained conversation, and Bretz doesn't like new people."

"He likes dogs."

"He brings the dog."

"He brings the dog to the club?"

"He brings the dog everywhere. He's a colonel. People accommodate colonels."

Anya looked at the club through the windshield. It sat on the corner of Lindenstrasse like an architectural rebuke — stone facade, wrought-iron balconies, the kind of building that had survived two wars and three governments because the people inside it were always useful to whoever was in charge. Light pooled behind tall curtains from windows on the first floor. A man in a dark overcoat was being admitted through the front door with a handshake and a nod.

"I still think we should approach him separately," Anya said. "You work the room, I find Bretz, we converge when I've got a read on his—"

"No. Bretz is old guard. He respects couples. He respects men who bring their partners to social functions because it means they've got nothing to hide." Damian's voice had the measured quality of someone who'd thought this through several times. "If you approach him alone, you're a young woman asking questions. If we approach together, we're a couple making small talk."

He wasn't wrong. She didn't like that he wasn't wrong, because it meant she had to do this his way, and his way involved—

"So," Damian said. He was looking straight ahead. "The cover."

"The cover."

"We're — they'll expect us to be. Together."

"That's what 'posing as a couple' means, yes."

His jaw tightened. Not anger — something worse. His thoughts were a controlled demolition failing to stay controlled: *Don't think about her arm. She's going to take your arm when you walk in. That's normal. Couples do that. Don't — professional. This is a professional arrangement. Your hand goes on the small of her back to guide her through a doorway and that is a NORMAL SOCIAL GESTURE that NORMAL PEOPLE—*

"Damian."

He looked at her.

"You're grinding your teeth."

"I'm not." He was. He stopped. *Was I? Damn it.* "I'm reviewing the operational parameters."

"You're reviewing them very loudly."

That came out wrong. She meant his visible tension — the rigid posture, the white-knuckle grip on the steering wheel of a parked car. She did not mean that she could hear his thoughts. That would have been — she couldn't have meant that.

He exhaled. A deliberate exhalation, the kind she imagined he'd learned at some diplomatic training seminar where they taught you to breathe through international incidents. "I'm fine. Let's go over the approach one more time."

"We enter as a couple. You introduce me to people. I steer us toward Bretz. You keep him talking about his military career — he's vain about it — while I read the room." She caught herself. "Read the room. Socially. See who's friendly, who's watching."

*Read the room.* He nodded. He didn't suspect anything. He was too busy thinking about how her dress — deep green, borrowed from a WISE wardrobe she'd rather not have known existed — sat against her collarbone, and then he was furiously not thinking about it, which produced a thought somehow louder than the original.

"The name," Anya said, because one of them had to stay operational. "Bretz has been linked to defense procurement irregularities. If the Renewal Front is funding operations through military contracts, Bretz knows who's coordinating the money. We need the name of his contact in the chain."

"And your assessment — from the file — is that he'll be relaxed enough here to let something slip?"

"People let things slip when they're comfortable," she said. Which was true. She just couldn't explain the mechanism.

"Fine." Damian straightened his tie — dark blue, knotted with the care of someone who'd been taught that a tie was a form of armor. "If this goes wrong—"

"It won't go wrong."

"—we leave separately. You through the west exit, me through the front. We don't acknowledge each other."

"It's not going to go wrong."

"First rule of operations," he said. "Plan for what goes wrong."

*His father probably told him that,* Anya thought, and then wished she hadn't, because Damian's thoughts flickered — brief, involuntary — toward something dark and heavy that she wasn't going to follow. She looked out the window instead.

He gathered the folder, straightened his cuffs, and opened his door. The autumn air came in cold, carrying the smell of damp leaves and distant frying oil from a street vendor somewhere south.

"All right," he said. He walked around to open her door, which she hadn't expected. His thoughts: *This is what a boyfriend would do. I am performing the role of a boyfriend. I am not actually—*

She took his arm.

*Oh,* he thought.

They walked toward the club.

***

The Gesellschaft Club's interior matched its exterior: old money whispering through old wood. Dark panels lined the walls of the entrance hall, punctuated by oil paintings of men in uniforms who all shared the same expression of institutional self-regard. A crystal chandelier threw complicated light across a marble floor where a doorman in a coat that had its own dry-cleaning schedule was checking names against a leather-bound ledger.

Damian handled the door. His public mode engaged like a switch — the grinding tension vanished and a smooth, warm, slightly self-deprecating version of himself appeared in its place. "Desmond," he said to the doorman. "And guest."

The doorman found his name, nodded, and waved them through with the practiced disinterest of someone who had been paid not to be curious.

The reception occupied a long salon with high ceilings and too many candles. Thirty or forty people clustered around a bar and several cocktail tables, their conversation producing the specific acoustic texture of polite society — measured laughter, clinking glass, the rustle of expensive fabric. The crowd thought: a trade agreement, a bad hip, a new mistress, a parking fine, the price of heating oil, whether it was too early to leave without seeming rude, an argument about an inheritance that had been going on since June.

Anya breathed.

The noise was manageable. This wasn't a state dinner — fewer minds, less pressure, and most of these people were thinking about nothing more threatening than their investments. She tuned down the static and looked for Bretz.

She found him.

Colonel Bretz stood near a display of watercolors — harbor scenes, boats, competent but uninspired — with a glass of cognac in one hand and, at his side, the largest dog Anya had ever seen outside of a veterinary textbook.

The dog was the color of wet straw and roughly the size of a small couch. Its head came up to Bretz's hip, and Bretz was not a short man. It sat with the dignified patience of an animal that knew it was the most important creature in any room and saw no reason to prove it.

*OK,* Anya thought. *That's a very big dog.*

"That's a very big dog," Damian murmured beside her.

Bretz himself was sixty or so, with the thickened build of a man who'd been fit once and had since negotiated a truce with good meals. His face was ruddy and creased in the pattern of someone who spent a lot of time outdoors squinting at things he disapproved of. He was thinking about his sciatica. Specifically, he was thinking that the chair he'd been sitting in earlier had set the nerve on fire in his left hip, and that if his wife made him stand for another hour he was going to sit down whether she liked it or not, and that he didn't care if she thought he was embarrassing because he was a colonel and he had earned the right to sit down when his back was killing him.

He was the most human person in the room.

"Colonel Bretz," Damian said, stopping at a polite distance. "What a pleasure. Damian Desmond — we met briefly at the Eastbridge reception last spring."

Bretz turned. His thoughts registered: *Desmond. The younger one. Diplomatic corps. His father was—* and then a careful blankness, the kind of disciplined non-thought that military men produced around political names. "Desmond. Yes, I remember. You're the one who didn't eat the fish."

"The fish was ambitious," Damian said.

Bretz laughed — a single, gruff syllable. His sciatica sent a complaint up his hip and he shifted his weight. "Who's your friend?"

"My girlfriend," Damian said, and the word landed in his thoughts like a glass dropped on tile: *Girlfriend. I said girlfriend. That's the cover. I said it correctly. Why does it sound like—*

Anya smiled. "Elena," she said. "I've heard wonderful things about the club."

"It's all right," Bretz said. "The cognac's decent. The company varies." He glanced at Damian with the appraising look of an older man sizing up a younger one through the woman he'd brought. Whatever he found was satisfactory enough. He drank his cognac.

The dog chose this moment to notice Anya.

It happened in stages. First, the massive head swiveled toward her. Then the nostrils flared — an audible intake of air, like a bellows engaging. Then the dog stood up, which was a process that involved considerable vertical displacement, and walked directly to Anya with the unhurried certainty of a glacier.

It pressed its nose into her palm.

*Oh. Oh no. OK. Hello.*

"Kaiser likes you," Bretz said, and it was the first genuine warmth in his voice. "He doesn't like most people."

Kaiser was now leaning against Anya's leg with his full weight, which was substantial. She shifted her stance to keep from toppling. The dog's thoughts were simple and clear: *Good. Warm. Smells good. Stay.* And then, with uncomplicated animal conviction: *Mine now.*

"He's beautiful," Anya said, because saying *your dog has just claimed me as personal property* seemed operationally ill-advised. She scratched behind Kaiser's ears. The dog's eyes closed in slow bliss. His tail, which was approximately the size and force of a rolled magazine, began to thump against a side table.

"Careful with the tail," Bretz said. "He knocked over a Reinhardt last month. The club committee wasn't — they weren't happy about it."

"Damian!" A voice cut across the salon like a ceremonial horn. A woman in emerald silk was bearing down on them with the velocity of someone who had spotted exactly the person she'd been looking for. "Damian Desmond, I KNEW it was you!"

Damian's thoughts: *No. No no no. Who is — I don't—*

"Margarethe Bretz," Anya murmured, just before the woman arrived. Bretz's wife. She'd seen the photos in the file, but the file hadn't captured the sheer propulsive force of Margarethe Bretz's social energy. Mid-fifties, handsome, with the bearing of someone who organized things — committees, parties, other people's seating arrangements, other people's lives.

"Liebling, you didn't tell me you'd invited the young Desmond," Margarethe said to her husband, who had not invited anyone. Then she turned the full beam of her attention on Damian. "You look exactly like that actor. What's his name — the one from that film about the diplomat who falls in love with the — oh, you know the one."

"I don't think—" Damian started.

"Friedrich Engel! That's it. You're the spitting image. Has anyone told you? I'm sure people tell you all the time."

*Nobody has ever told me this. I have no idea who Friedrich Engel is.* "It's come up," Damian said, because he was, despite everything, still a diplomat.

Margarethe seized his arm with the practiced authority of someone accustomed to seizing things. "You must come meet the Hasslers. Ernst was just saying the other day that the diplomatic corps needs more young men, and you have SUCH a presence, doesn't he, Heinrich?"

"His presence is fine," Bretz said, without looking up from his cognac.

"Come, come." Margarethe was already towing Damian away from the watercolors with a force that suggested she could have had a career in maritime salvage. He threw Anya a look over his shoulder — the controlled helplessness of a man being extracted by a superior force.

*Sorry,* he thought. *I'll get back as soon as — she's very — I'll try—*

Anya watched him go. Then she looked at Bretz. Then she looked at Kaiser, who was still leaning against her leg with the structural commitment of a flying buttress.

"Your wife is... energetic," Anya said.

Bretz drank his cognac. "That's a word for it." He was thinking: *Margarethe and her celebrities. Last month it was that opera singer. The month before that she cornered some painter at the Langsdorf benefit. She'll have the boy pinned for an hour at least.* Then, with weary affection: *She's happier at these things than I am. Always has been.*

An hour. That was either a problem or an opportunity. Without Damian to steer the conversation toward military matters, Anya was working alone with a colonel who didn't know her and a dog she couldn't shake. But Bretz was relaxed here — this was his club, his cognac, his watercolors. And his wife had just removed the social obligation that might have kept his guard up.

"Do you collect?" Anya asked, nodding at the harbor scenes on the wall.

"God, no. These are terrible. Koenig — he's a member — he paints them. We all pretend they're acceptable because his wife left him last year and he needs the encouragement." Bretz shifted his weight again, his sciatica flaring. *Damn nerve. If I sit down Margarethe will tell me later I was being antisocial. If I don't sit down I'm going to be in bed for two days.* "I've got a few things at home. Nothing serious. Some landscapes. A Feldmann."

"I love Feldmann." Anya did not love Feldmann. Anya had looked up Feldmann twenty minutes ago in the car and could recall exactly one painting, which featured a cow in unconvincing light. "The pastoral work especially."

"Mm." Bretz was not interested in discussing Feldmann. He was thinking about his brother-in-law. *That bastard Gerhard borrowed another three thousand marks last Tuesday. Told Margarethe it was for the car. It's never for the car. It's for the card games at Oschmann's. Everybody knows except Margarethe, or she knows and pretends not to, which is worse.*

This was the texture of Bretz's mind. Not a military intelligence problem, not a Renewal Front contact — a man with a bad back and a wife who spent more than he'd like and a brother-in-law who gambled and an enormous dog who was, at this moment, attempting to nose his way into Anya's handbag where it hung against her hip.

She redirected Kaiser with her knee. "Down," she whispered.

Kaiser looked at her with wounded dignity. *But bag. Bag smells interesting.* He sat, but maintained nasal surveillance.

"Your dog is very determined," Anya said.

"He gets it from his mother. She was a military service dog — used to pull equipment carts at the border garrison. Kaiser thinks he's still on duty." Bretz's expression softened when he talked about the dog. He scratched Kaiser's flank absently, and the dog leaned into it. *Good dog. Only honest thing in this building.*

*Military,* his thoughts drifted. *The procurement review is Tuesday. Hartig wants the numbers before the committee even looks at the allocation. Hartig always wants the numbers early. Says it's efficient. Efficient for who—*

There. The edge of something.

Anya leaned against the wall, casual, letting Kaiser's bulk serve as cover for her proximity to Bretz. "It must be exhausting, though. The military life. My father was career military and he said the bureaucracy was worse than the enemy."

Bretz grunted. "Your father was right. I spend more time filling out procurement forms than I ever spent on a firing range." His thoughts ran: *The new defense contracts. Hartig's been pushing the Krueger-Schiff allocation through the secondary channel before the committee reviews it. Said it was standard procedure but nobody routes four hundred thousand through a secondary authorization without a reason.*

She needed to keep him in this track.

"My father always said the contract people were the real power in the military," she offered.

"They are." Bretz's face darkened. "You want to know who runs things? It's not the generals. It's the procurement officials who decide which contracts go where. And some of them — " He stopped himself. Looked at her. *Why am I talking about this with a girl I just met? She's Desmond's girlfriend. She doesn't need to hear about—*

Kaiser chose this precise moment to lunge for the handbag.

It happened fast. The massive head shot forward, jaws closing around something — her compact, it turned out — and then thirty-plus kilograms of dog was backing away from her with the clumsy triumph of an animal who had finally completed his mission. His tail hammered a cocktail table. A glass rattled. A woman nearby flinched.

"Kaiser! No!" Anya grabbed for the compact. Kaiser dodged — surprisingly nimble for his size — and trotted three paces toward the bar, tail wagging, the compact clamped between his jaws like a prize duck.

"Kaiser, drop it," Bretz said, with the resignation of a man who had been saying this sentence for seven years.

The dog did not drop it. A bartender was staring. Two women at a nearby table had turned to watch with the delighted horror of people witnessing someone else's social catastrophe.

Bretz bent to retrieve it and his sciatica fired like a flare: *Christ. My hip. My goddamn hip.* He straightened slowly, one hand on his lower back, and managed to extract the compact from Kaiser's mouth with the other. It came free with a strand of drool connecting it to the dog's lower lip like a suspension bridge.

"I apologize," Bretz said, holding the compact out to her at arm's length, slightly mortified. "He's usually — well, he's never well-behaved, but he's not usually this committed."

Anya took the compact back. It was warm and damp. She wiped it on the inside of her coat pocket without looking at it. "It's fine. He's got good taste."

Bretz laughed — a real laugh this time, not the diplomatic kind. Two syllables, rough and surprised. His guard dropped. *She's all right. Desmond's girl. Better than the usual crowd in this place. At least she doesn't pretend to like Koenig's paintings.*

Kaiser circled back and pressed himself against Anya's leg again, unrepentant. His thought was serene: *Good. She's still here.*

And then, like water following a familiar channel, Bretz's mind returned to the thing that had been sitting under the surface all evening: *Hartig. The meeting Tuesday. Four hundred thousand through the secondary channel. "Strategic national interest" — that's the phrase he uses. Same phrase every time. And the authorization came from the defense ministry's discretionary fund, which means no parliamentary oversight, which means—*

The name was there. Not a vague reference — a specific person, a specific position, a specific pattern.

*Erich Hartig. Undersecretary for Defense Procurement. He's been routing contract allocations outside the standard review process for at least six months. Says the committee is too slow, says national security can't wait for bureaucratic timelines. And maybe he's right. But nobody routes that much money that quietly unless someone told them to.*

Bretz took a long pull of his cognac. His sciatica throbbed. His thoughts continued, more tired than angry: *And I keep signing the forms because what am I going to do? Go to the Inspector General? With what? I've got a feeling, not evidence. Hartig's got authorization stamps from the ministry itself. I'm a colonel with a bad hip and six years to retirement. I'm not the man who makes the complaint. I'm the man who signs the form.*

He wasn't corrupt. He wasn't even complicit, not in the way that mattered. He was a tired man inside a system he could see was wrong but couldn't afford to challenge, doing his job because the alternative was everything he'd built for thirty years burning down around his pension. He thought about Hartig the way a clerk thinks about the executive three floors up who makes decisions that shape the clerk's life without knowing his name.

Anya stood very still while Kaiser nuzzled her knee and Bretz complained about his hip and the salon hummed with forty minds thinking about wine and weather and money, and she held the name like a key she'd found in a coat pocket — small, specific, waiting for a lock.

Across the room, Damian was trapped. Margarethe had him cornered between a potted fern and a heavyset man who was explaining something with great enthusiasm and broad hand gestures. Damian's thoughts reached her across the crowd noise: *I am going to die here. I am going to die next to this fern listening to a man describe his horse's digestive requirements and nobody will find my body until they water the plant—*

Anya bit the inside of her cheek.

"You all right?" Bretz asked.

"Fine. Sorry — your dog is standing on my foot."

Kaiser was standing on her foot. His full weight bore down on her left instep through the thin sole of a borrowed formal shoe. She shifted. He shifted with her, maintaining contact like a satellite tracking its ground station.

They talked for another fifteen minutes. Bretz told her about the club's new menu, which he disapproved of. He told her about the charity dinner his wife had committed him to next month. He told her about his brother-in-law's latest request for money, which he was going to refuse this time, which he had said the last four times. His thoughts confirmed what his words offered: he was a man in the last stretch of a long career, managing a body that was failing him and a family that was costing him and a conscience that occasionally kept him up at night but not long enough to make him do anything about it.

He was not a villain. He was not even particularly useful to whoever was using those procurement channels, except as the man who signed the forms and didn't ask why. He bore the specific human weight of someone who had chosen compliance over confrontation at every juncture and was living with the accumulated residue of those choices, and his dog loved him, and his wife drove him crazy but he couldn't imagine dinner without her, and his back hurt.

Damian materialized at her elbow. His tie was subtly looser. He looked like someone who had survived a natural disaster and was trying to pretend it hadn't happened.

*The horse man,* his thoughts said, with the shell-shocked flatness of a veteran. *Forty-five minutes about equine digestion. Forty-five.*

Kaiser sniffed Damian once, thoroughly, then turned back to Anya. Dismissed.

"We should go," she murmured, leaning into his arm — the cover, and also the only thing keeping Kaiser from following her to the bar. "I've got what we need."

Damian's thoughts shifted immediately. The diplomatic exhaustion vanished and something sharper took its place. *She got it. Already?* "You're sure?"

"I'm sure."

He looked at her for a beat longer than operational necessity required. *How does she— every time. She was alone with him for twenty minutes and she—* He shook it off. "All right. We leave together. Normal pace."

They made their exit. Damian produced a gracious goodbye — thanking Bretz for the conversation, complimenting the club's watercolors with exactly enough sincerity to be polite and not enough to be believed, apologizing for the early departure with the seamless regret of someone who had been trained since childhood to leave rooms well. Bretz shook his hand and told him the fish was better now, if he ever came back.

Kaiser watched Anya go. He stood at Bretz's side and his massive head tracked her all the way to the door, and his thought — clear and simple and devastating in its sincerity — was: *Come back.*

Anya did not look at the dog again because she was a professional, and also because if she looked at the dog she was going to feel something about it and she couldn't afford to feel things about a colonel's dog right now when she was holding the name of a man who was funneling defense money outside parliamentary oversight.

The night air hit them on the steps. Cold — real cold, autumn with its teeth in, not the decorative chill of early October. Anya exhaled and watched her breath vanish.

"Erich Hartig," she said quietly. "Undersecretary for Defense Procurement. He's routing contract allocations through a secondary channel — four hundred thousand marks that Bretz knows about, probably more he doesn't. The authorization comes from the defense ministry's discretionary fund. No parliamentary oversight. Bretz has been signing the forms for months."

Damian stopped walking. His thoughts went very still. *Hartig. Defense procurement. The discretionary fund. That's — if the Renewal Front is using military contracts to fund its operations, this is the pipe. This is how the money moves.* He looked at her. "Bretz told you this?"

"Bretz told me he collects Feldmann landscapes and his brother-in-law owes him three thousand marks. The rest I— pieced together. From context." She met his eyes. "He doesn't know he gave us anything."

Damian's expression — the real one, underneath the diplomatic polish — was something she hadn't seen before. Not quite admiration, not quite wariness. Something between. *She scares me,* he thought, and then immediately: *Not like that. She's just — she's very good at this. Unnervingly good.* And then: *Stop thinking about how good she is. Focus.*

"Hartig reports to someone," he said. "Nobody runs a shadow procurement channel on their own authority."

"Bretz thinks so too. He doesn't know who."

"I might." Damian started walking again. His thoughts were working — connecting the Hartig name to things Anya couldn't see, intelligence from his father's old network, patterns he'd been tracking. *The defense ministry restructuring two years ago. The new discretionary authorities. If Hartig is the mechanism, then the question is who built him.* "This gives us something to trace."

"That's why we came."

They crossed the street toward his car.

***

The park was two blocks from the club. They sat on a bench under a linden tree whose leaves had gone gold and were falling in the windless air, drifting down in slow spirals that caught the light from a distant streetlamp and turned briefly bright before landing dark on the gravel. Anya's shoes were off — the borrowed heels had been punishing her feet for three hours and one hour of being used as a dog's landing pad — and her stockinged toes curled against the cold. Damian had loosened his tie and undone his top button, which was, for him, the sartorial equivalent of complete collapse.

It was quiet. The park was empty except for them and the linden and the sound of a tram somewhere east, its bell carrying across the rooftops.

"Hartig," Damian said again, turning the name over. He'd been doing this since they left the club — repeating it, connecting it to things, building the architecture of what it meant. *If Hartig controls the procurement pipe, then the Renewal Front isn't operating on ideology alone. It has infrastructure. Funded infrastructure. That changes what we're dealing with.*

"It changes the timeline too," Anya said. "If they've been running money through the discretionary fund for six months, they're not planning something. They're building something."

"Yeah." He looked at the ground between his shoes. "It's always further along than you think."

They sat with that. A leaf landed on the bench between them. Neither moved it.

"The horse man," Damian said.

Anya snorted.

"His name is Ernst Hassler. He has three horses named after Ostanian generals, two ex-wives, and — I want you to understand I'm not exaggerating — a forty-five-minute theory about the relationship between oat quality and equine temperament." A pause. His mouth twitched. "Margarethe also wanted me to come to dinner next Thursday. To meet her niece. She said her niece would love my bone structure."

"Did you say yes?"

"I said I'd check my schedule." *Which is diplomatic for I would rather eat the horse.* He almost said it aloud but caught himself. What he actually said was: "Margarethe told me Friedrich Engel is forty-seven and balding. So the resemblance is apparently spiritual rather than physical."

The laugh came out of Anya before she could manage it — not her polished social laugh or her operational that's-amusing-keep-talking laugh but the real one, the one that started somewhere below her ribs and came out slightly too loud and had a catch at the end that she hated because it sounded like a hiccup and she could never control it. She pressed her hand over her mouth but it was already out, loose in the cold air.

Damian was looking at her.

His thoughts stopped.

Not slowed — stopped. Like a record player lifting its needle. For a half-second there was nothing, just a blankness that Anya had learned to recognize as the instant before something surfaced from deep water.

Then:

*She has the same laugh.*

Not the vague familiarity from the cultural exchange event, the half-recognition at the policy forum. This was specific. A memory rising through murky water, gaining shape and color as it climbed.

*A schoolyard. The big linden tree near the east wall of the campus — the one everybody climbed, the one with the flat branch halfway up where you could sit and see the whole field. An afternoon. Autumn, like this. A girl sitting on that branch. She was — six? Seven? She'd beaten him at something. The relay race. The one where the teams were picked by the teachers and she ended up on the opposing side and she was FAST, impossibly fast for someone that small, and he'd been so sure he was going to win because he'd been training all week in the garden and—*

*She beat him. She beat him by four steps and then she climbed the tree to watch the next race and she sat up there laughing. Not at him. Not cruelly. Just this enormous, delighted, graceless laugh because she'd won and it was a beautiful afternoon and she was six years old and everything was exactly as good as it was supposed to be.*

*And her hair was—*

*Pink,* Damian thought. *Her hair was pink. And she laughed just like—*

He blinked. The memory wavered at the surface, almost whole. Anya could see it in his mind like watching someone reach for a glass on the edge of a table — the fingers stretching, brushing the rim. The name was close. Closer than it had ever been. A girl. A specific girl. Anya—

The glass didn't fall. It didn't settle back either. It just hung there, trembling, at the edge.

*That's strange,* he thought. He shook his head — a tiny movement, barely visible. *That was a long time ago. Why did I — that girl. What was her—*

The thought dissolved. Not all the way. It sank back down to where it lived, still there, still reaching, but no longer breaking the surface.

"What?" Anya said. Her voice sounded normal. She was almost sure her voice sounded normal.

"Nothing." He almost smiled. "You just reminded me of someone. For a second."

"Good someone or bad someone?"

He considered it. *Good,* he thought, and the thought carried warmth and an ache and the specific golden quality of autumn light through the branches of a tree he hadn't seen in fourteen years. What he said was: "Haven't decided yet."

Anya pulled her coat tighter. The cold had deepened while they sat here, and her feet were going numb in the damp air. She should say something operational. She should say they should go.

She didn't, for a moment. She sat on the bench with the linden dropping its leaves around them and the name Erich Hartig sitting between them like a grenade with a slow fuse, and she looked at Damian looking at the dark shapes of the trees against the city's reflected glow, and she thought about a schoolyard she hadn't thought about in years.

The relay race. She remembered it differently, of course. She remembered reading his thoughts during the race — the fierce, absolute determination to win, the fury when he realized she was ahead of him, and then, when she crossed the line first, a feeling she hadn't expected: grudging admiration, buried so deep under wounded pride that she almost missed it. She'd climbed the tree because she'd wanted to be somewhere nobody was thinking at her. And she'd laughed because she was six and it was autumn and the leaves were gold and she didn't yet know what it cost to be inside everyone's head all the time.

She didn't know, sitting on this bench, whether the warmth she felt right now was hers or an echo of his. The warmth was indistinguishable from its source. It usually was.

"Good operation," Damian said.

"Chaotic operation."

"Those are the same thing, in my experience."

"That's a terrible philosophy of operations."

"It's gotten me this far."

"You're twenty. That's not far."

He laughed. Not a big laugh — a short exhale through his nose, the beginning of something he decided not to finish. But his thoughts loosened, and for a moment he wasn't the diplomat or the counter-operative or Donovan Desmond's son. He was just a tired person on a bench, glad to be sitting next to someone instead of sitting alone.

*I could tell him,* Anya thought. Not about the telepathy. Not about the false report. But about Eden. About the tree and the relay race and the name he was reaching for.

She didn't.

"We should go," she said. "It's cold."

"Yeah." He stood. Offered his hand to help her up — the cover, and also just the thing he did. She took it. His fingers were warm. His thought, unbidden: *Her hand is freezing.*

She pulled her shoes back on, grimacing at the cold leather. They walked to the car without talking, their breath making brief clouds that dissolved in the lamplight, and Anya carried the name of a man who was moving money for people who wanted a war, and the sound of her own laugh living inside someone else's memory of a golden afternoon, and the knowledge that the boy walking beside her was one clear moment away from remembering everything.

The fuse was shorter than it had been this morning. She could see it burning, and she couldn't put it out, and she wasn't sure anymore that she wanted to.
