# Chapter 10

The milk was going to be a problem.

Yor held the carton at an angle under the kitchen light, reading the date stamp. Tomorrow. She put it back in the refrigerator, behind the leftover stew, where Loid was less likely to discover it. He had opinions about expiration dates that she considered disproportionate to the actual science of dairy spoilage, but she'd never found a way to say that without sounding like she was defending spoiled milk, which she wasn't. She was defending the principle that milk didn't become poison at midnight on the printed date. She was fairly sure about this.

Bond was on the living room rug, occupying approximately one-third of the available floor space with the unselfconscious entitlement of a very large, very old dog who had decided that this particular section of rug was his and always had been. His breathing was slow and even, and his legs twitched with whatever passed through a precognitive dog's sleeping mind. Yor sometimes caught Anya watching Bond with an expression that suggested she was listening to something Yor couldn't hear, but the habit was so old and so gentle that Yor had filed it years ago under the broad category of things about her daughter that she loved without understanding.

She was making a bento box. This was not strictly necessary — Loid was at the university for a consulting session, one of the occasional appointments that maintained a cover identity that had long since stopped feeling like one — and he wouldn't be back until evening. But Yor liked making them. The routine was grounding. Rice balls, three, with pickled plum. Tamagoyaki, two slices, trimmed where the browning had become something closer to charring. A thermos of mushroom miso soup from a recipe she'd clipped from a magazine in the city hall break room.

She tasted the soup. Too salty. Not catastrophically, but enough that she could hear Anya's polite silence if she ever served it — the careful way Anya said "It's good, Mama" when what she meant was *This is mostly salt*.

The bento went into the refrigerator with a note: *The soup is maybe a little salty. Sorry! Please don't feel like you have to drink it. But I think it's okay? — Y*

She was dotting the question mark when her phone buzzed.

Not a ring. A single pulse against the countertop, the kind that came from numbers that didn't exist in any directory. She set down the pen and picked up the phone.

An address on the northern outskirts. A name: Karl Dominic. A timeframe: tonight. And the six-digit code at the bottom that confirmed it was real.

She read it twice. The message would erase itself in ninety seconds. She set the phone face-down on the counter and went back to the bento box, adjusting the thermos so it wouldn't leak during refrigeration. She closed the lid. She washed the knife she'd used for the tamagoyaki, dried it, and returned it to the block.

Bond opened one eye from the rug. Closed it.

"I'm going out tonight," Yor told him. "Don't eat Papa's dinner. It's in the fridge."

She showered. In the bedroom, she dressed — fitted dark jacket, pants that didn't restrict movement at the hip, flat shoes with grip soles she'd ordered from a work-supply catalogue under the reasoning that icy sidewalks were dangerous and practical footwear was just common sense. Which was true. The shoes were also excellent for moving quietly across concrete and gravel, which was the actual reason she'd bought them.

The closet. Behind the shoe rack, behind a box of seasonal clothes she rotated every April and October with a conscientiousness that Loid found endearing and Anya found baffling. A locked case. The key lived on a chain around her neck alongside a small enamel peanut charm — a prize from an Eden Academy festival booth, won by a six-year-old Anya and presented with the solemnity of a military decoration. The charm's paint was chipping at the edges. She'd never replaced it.

Inside the case: two blades, a garrote wire, a compact medical kit, and lockpicks that had been Garden standard issue since before Anya was born. She selected the longer blade and the picks. The garrote she left. Single target, compound setting — the blade was cleaner, faster, something she could sheathe when the job was done. The garrote required sustained proximity she didn't enjoy. Not out of squeamishness. Because it was slow, and slow meant time spent close enough to hear the sound the body made, and that was a sound she preferred to keep brief.

She strapped the blade to the inside of her left forearm, under the jacket sleeve, and checked the draw. Smooth. The lockpicks went into her inner pocket.

In the hallway mirror, she looked like a woman heading out for the evening. A bit severe. She tried a small smile — the kind she gave the reception desk at city hall when she arrived for her shift. It softened things.

On her way out, she stopped at the hall table where Loid had left a shopping list in his careful handwriting: rice, laundry soap, Bond's joint medication, milk. She'd meant to remind him. He would buy a new carton, find the old one hiding behind the stew, pour it out, and give her the expression — not disapproval, exactly, more the gentle bewilderment of a man who had spent decades in intelligence operations and could not fathom his wife's relationship with dairy.

She added *milk — please check fridge first!* in her rounder, larger handwriting, and beneath it: *Back late. Don't wait up? There's stew.*

She locked the door behind her. The landing smelled of the neighbor's floor wax and the building's old radiators kicking against the October chill.

Then she went to kill Karl Dominic.

---

The compound was forty minutes north of Berlint proper, where the city's residential blocks thinned into industrial lots and gated estates belonging to people who valued privacy over proximity. Yor took the tram to the northern terminal and walked the remaining two kilometers along a service road paralleling a drainage canal. The air smelled of wet concrete, diesel from a freight depot she'd passed a kilometer back, and the faintly vegetal rot of standing water in the ditch.

The landscape changed as she walked. Berlint's streetlights gave way to intermittent industrial security lamps, and the sounds of the city — tram bells, traffic, a bar's music leaking from somewhere — faded into the low drone of freight machinery and then, past the last depot, into nothing much at all. The service road was unpaved beyond the terminal's reach: packed dirt and gravel that crunched under her soles. She passed a shuttered machine shop, a fenced lot full of construction aggregate, a drainage pump station where someone had left a folding chair and an empty thermos. The only other living thing she'd encountered in the last kilometer was a cat that crossed the road, evaluated her with the professional indifference of an animal that knew it couldn't be caught, and vanished into a culvert pipe.

She'd read the briefing on the tram — a file downloaded to her phone and programmed to delete after thirty minutes. Karl Dominic. Fifty-three. Arms broker. His supply chain had been linked to the recent military buildup along the Ostanian border: weapons transfers routed through shell companies, the kind of infrastructure that made wars possible by making weapons available. The Garden's assessment classified him as a destabilizing agent. Eliminate. Standard contract.

Standard. For twenty years, that word had been a foundation she could stand on. The Garden assigned targets. She carried out the assignment. The reasons existed at a level above her operational role, and she'd trusted them because the alternative — questioning every target, demanding justification for every life she ended — would have made the work impossible. Trust was the price of function. She'd paid it willingly.

She'd paid it for the shipping clerk, too. Three months ago. A man in the port district whose briefing profile listed arms connections that turned out, when she'd checked afterward, to be invoices for industrial machine parts. Legitimate equipment for a legitimate factory. The man had begged — not for himself, which she could manage, but for his daughter, who was waiting at home. They sometimes begged. She'd trained herself to close that channel years ago. But the invoices. The gap between the profile and the evidence. She'd reported the discrepancy to her handler. The handler had said: *Thank you, Yor. Don't worry about it. These things are reviewed.* And she'd stopped worrying.

She'd mostly stopped worrying.

She reached the compound at half past ten. The perimeter was a three-meter concrete wall topped with razor wire and motion sensors at the corners. Beyond it: the roofline of the main building, interior lights, the muted geometry of an estate that had been a manufacturing facility before Dominic repurposed it. Sealed loading docks on the east side. The main gate faced south, connected to the highway by a paved access road.

She settled into surveillance. Three men visible: one stationed at the gate, shifting his weight foot to foot with the restlessness of a man assigned a post he found boring; one near a cargo van parked in the loading area, its rear doors open; one walking the interior perimeter on a patrol route. She timed the patrol. Four minutes per circuit. Consistent rhythm. The pace of routine rather than alertness.

The north wall had a vulnerability. A motion sensor housing mounted at an angle that covered the airspace above the wall rather than the wall face itself — a factory installation, never corrected. The kind of error that accumulated in security systems maintained by people who checked boxes rather than tested coverage.

She waited for the patrol to round the south wall, went up the north wall at the sensor gap, and dropped into the compound's northeast corner behind a maintenance shed. The shed smelled of machine oil and dried grass clippings. She held still for thirty seconds. The patrol guard's boots crunched past on the other side — steady, unhurried, the steps of a man completing a task he'd completed a thousand times without incident.

The crunch faded toward the south. She moved.

The man at the cargo van was leaning against the rear bumper, smoking. His sidearm sat on his right hip, holstered, the retention snap undone. Structural carelessness — he'd never encountered trouble here, and the boredom of guard work had eroded whatever discipline he'd once maintained. Yor came around the van's blind side. The last three meters took less than a second. She put the blade through the base of his skull at the junction where the brainstem lived, and he dropped. She caught his weight against the van's bumper and lowered him to the pavement without noise. His cigarette rolled under the tire.

Inside the van: long wooden crates, unmarked, secured with military-grade packaging straps. The chemical smell of preservative oil seeped through the wood grain. Rifles. She counted twelve crates. Enough for a company.

She moved toward the main building.

The loading dock's sealed entrance had a secondary door — service access, a standard pin-tumbler lock that the picks handled in nine seconds. A corridor inside, fluorescent tubes overhead, half of them dead and the survivors flickering with a buzz that set her teeth on edge. An office door on the left, closed. Voices from deeper in the building — male, three or four of them, the measured rhythm of negotiation. The private sale. Buyers in the building.

She adjusted. Buyers meant witnesses and potentially more armed men. She needed the principal and the exit, nothing else.

Two guards in the corridor, stationed outside a conference room. The first noticed her at six meters and reached for his radio. Wrong decision. She closed the gap in a stride and drove the heel of her palm into his trachea. The sound he made was the sound of someone trying to breathe through a crushed straw. He buckled. The second guard was faster — he'd drawn his sidearm by the time she reached him — but his partner was between them, and the half-second he spent calculating a clear firing line was a half-second he didn't have. Her blade opened the inside of his forearm from wrist to elbow, deep enough to sever the flexor tendons, and the gun clattered to the floor tiles. She followed with her elbow below his ear, where the mastoid process made a target the size of a coin, and he folded.

She collected the first guard's radio. Quiet channel. No alarm.

The corridor branched. Left toward the offices. Right toward the voices. She went left. The buyers were not her contract.

Karl Dominic was alone in a corner office at the corridor's end, behind a desk buried in paperwork, speaking into a phone. He was shorter than his file photo suggested — stocky, balding, in a dress shirt with the sleeves pushed past his elbows. The office smelled of cigar smoke layered over cheap instant coffee, the kind sold in bulk at freight depot commissaries. A framed photograph on the desk: Dominic with a woman on a boat, somewhere sunny, both of them squinting at the camera with the particular resignation of people who'd been told to smile.

He looked up when the door opened. His face processed: confusion, then recognition of what she was, then fear. Three expressions in under a second, which was more than most people managed.

He dropped the phone and lunged for the desk drawer.

She was faster. She had been faster than every person she'd ever been sent to kill, and twenty years had not changed that — the speed lived below thought, below decision, in the architecture of muscle and nerve that the Garden had trained and she had maintained through thousands of hours of practice she told Loid was yoga. Her blade caught his reaching wrist before his hand found the drawer, and the cut was deep enough to part tendons. He screamed — a short, bitten-off sound, the kind that comes from someone whose body has overridden the preference for silence.

He tried to stand. She put the blade through the left side of his neck, angled for the carotid, and held it for the two seconds it took. His legs buckled. He went down against the desk, scattering papers, and slid to the floor trailing a red line down the laminate front.

The phone was still connected. A tinny voice on the other end: "Karl? Karl, what—"

Yor disconnected the call.

She cleaned the blade on the hem of Dominic's shirt and sheathed it. Her hands were steady. They were always steady during the work — the part of her body she trusted most, the part that didn't shake or hesitate or feel the weight of what it had done until afterward, when the adrenaline metabolized and the steadiness left and she was just Yor again, standing in a kitchen, cutting vegetables, and her hands would tremble for a moment over the cutting board and she'd tell herself it was the cold.

The papers on the desk.

She'd been trained not to investigate targets beyond the scope of the assignment. The Garden provided the briefing; the operative executed the contract. Investigation was a different role, different people, a different level of the organization. For twenty years, that division had been enough.

But the shipping clerk. The invoices that hadn't matched. The handler's voice: *Don't worry about it.* And the daughter, waiting at home.

Yor looked at the papers scattered across Dominic's desk and the ones that had fallen to the floor, some of them soaking up the blood pooling from his neck, and she didn't leave.

Shipping manifests. Transfer orders for military hardware — rifles, communications equipment, explosive ordnance — routed through holding companies whose names meant nothing but whose addresses she could parse: locations in Berlint's government district, near the defense ministry. The shipments were coded, but the routing told a story — military materiel flowing from international suppliers through Dominic's brokerage into channels that fed the Ostanian armed forces outside any official procurement process.

A name recurred on three separate manifests: Werner Voss. She didn't recognize it. She photographed the pages — every manifest, both sides, the routing numbers and destination codes.

One set of orders held her attention longer than the rest. Encrypted field communications equipment — military grade, the kind used for coordinating ground operations — shipped six weeks ago to a receiving address she recognized. Not the defense ministry directly but a warehouse in the adjacent district, the kind of anonymous drop point that existed to keep deliveries off official books. The quantities suggested equipping a field unit, not filling a depot. An active operation, being assembled through Karl Dominic's desk.

She checked the shipping dates against her own recent assignments. Three months ago, the Garden had sent her to eliminate a logistics coordinator in the eastern industrial district — a man whose file said he'd been diverting weapons shipments for personal profit. She'd completed the contract in his office, at his desk, not unlike the way she'd completed this one. His name didn't appear in Dominic's manifests. But the shipping routes overlapped. The same holding companies. The same coded destination addresses. The same pipeline.

She'd killed a man inside that pipeline. She might have been cleaning it up for the people who built it.

She photographed everything remaining on the desk.

Then she found the drawer.

It was the one Dominic had been reaching for. Not a weapon inside — a binder. Heavy paper, neatly organized, held together with a metal clip. Administrative documentation maintained with care: not the hurried paperwork of a desk but the careful records of a man who understood that documentation was insurance.

Subcontracting records. A ledger of completed assignments. Dates, payments received, and two columns: the party that had contracted the work, and the organization that carried it out.

The contracting party was coded. She didn't recognize the designator.

The executing organization, for nine entries spanning four years, was listed by a numerical code she knew as well as her own address. She'd seen it on her payment records for twenty years — the deposits that arrived on the fifteenth of each month, the brief confirmations after each completed assignment, the secure documents she signed when she accepted new contracts.

The Garden.

Her hands stopped being steady.

She read the entries again. Nine assignments. Four years. Targets eliminated by Garden operatives — maybe by her, maybe by people she'd trained alongside, people whose faces she saw at the biannual operational reviews the Garden held in rented banquet halls where they ate canapes and discussed logistics the way accountants discussed quarterly reports. These were their assignments. At the request of whatever entity ran Dominic's weapons pipeline.

The targets were listed by code, not by name, but the pattern was legible: former associates who'd become liabilities. Witnesses. Competitors whose continued existence created risk for the supply chain. The Garden hadn't been eliminating threats to national stability. The Garden had been removing loose ends for an arms-trafficking network that fed the very military buildup the Garden cited as justification for Dominic's own elimination.

Karl Dominic was a problem. And the Garden had spent four years solving problems for the people who created him.

She photographed every page. Her phone screen was bright in the dim office. Dominic's blood had reached her shoes — a dark tongue extending from under the desk — and she stepped back the way she'd step away from a puddle in the street. Automatic. Her body doing the practical thing while her mind tried to hold a shape it couldn't yet see the edges of.

Twenty years. The measurement kept arriving — not as an argument but as a fact that demanded reckoning. Twenty years of trust. Twenty years of the Garden saying *this person is dangerous* and Yor believing it, because believing was how she lived with what her hands did. She'd told herself a story about protection — that she killed so that people like Anya and Loid and Bond could live in a world with fewer dangerous men in it. The story had been good enough. It had been the thing she held at night when the steadiness left her hands and the faces arrived in the dark behind her closed eyes: the story that said their deaths served something, that her violence had a shape and the shape was shelter.

What if some of them hadn't been dangerous. Not all — she wasn't ready for all. But some. The shipping clerk with the machine-parts invoices and the daughter waiting at home. The nine entries in a binder in a dead man's drawer.

The thought wasn't fully formed. It was a fracture in a foundation — visible, running from somewhere she couldn't see to somewhere she couldn't reach. She could still stand on it. She could still function. She'd photographed the evidence and she would bring it home and she would —

She didn't know what she would do. That was the part that frightened her. She had always known.

She pocketed the phone, checked the corridor — clear — and moved back through the loading dock. The guards in the hallway were still unconscious. The gate guard was at his post, facing south, out of sightline. The buyers' muffled negotiation still carried through the building's interior walls, the deal proceeding as if nothing had changed inside the corner office at the end of the corridor.

She went back over the north wall at the same sensor gap, the same route — a mirror of her entry. Her left hand caught the razor wire on the descent. She felt the resistance and the tug but not the pain. The pain arrived thirty seconds later on the service road: a line of hot sting across her palm that resolved into a shallow cut running along the old scar — the one from when Anya was eight and learning to cut carrots, and the knife had slipped from her small grip and caught Yor's hand instead. Anya had cried for twenty minutes. Yor hadn't minded the cut. She'd held her bleeding palm behind her back so Anya wouldn't feel bad and said, "See? It's nothing. Let's try again."

She wrapped the cut in her jacket sleeve and kept walking.

---

She was fifty meters from the compound wall, where the service road curved toward the canal, when she saw a figure behind the industrial waste containers that lined the drainage ditch.

The figure stood.

It was Anya.

Yor stopped. Her daughter was in dark clothes — not the diplomatic wardrobe, not the outfits of a cultural attache at Westalian embassy events, but the kind of clothes Yor herself was wearing. Practical. Unreflective. The kind that existed to not be noticed. Anya's hair was pulled back under a dark knit cap. She had a small pack over one shoulder. She looked like someone who had no legitimate reason to be standing outside a secured compound on the northern outskirts of Berlint at eleven o'clock at night.

She also looked thin. She always looked thin. Yor worried about this the way she worried about the big things — not the milk or the soup, which were small worries that had solutions, but the kind of worry that lived in her chest and didn't go away because Anya was twenty and an adult and you couldn't make an adult eat.

Neither of them spoke for a moment. The canal water moved in the ditch. The compound's lights threw a pale halo against the low October clouds.

"You should eat more," Yor said. "You look thin."

Anya's mouth opened. Closed. Her eyes dropped to Yor's left hand — the jacket sleeve dark and stiff with blood, the makeshift wrapping already soaking through.

"Your hands are bleeding, Mama."

"Oh." Yor looked at her hand as if the blood were a social inconvenience, like noticing a stain on her shirt at a work dinner. "It's — it's not bad. I scraped it on a, um. A fence. It's really not deep. I just didn't have a — I'll clean it when I get home."

Anya didn't ask what fence. She didn't ask why Yor was here, what was inside the compound, what she'd done. Yor didn't ask any of the equivalent questions — why her daughter was crouched behind waste containers at an arms broker's estate, what intelligence she was following, what professional capacity took her so far past cultural attache that the cover had become its own quiet joke. These questions existed in the space between them like furniture they'd both learned to walk around without touching.

"I need to get back," Yor said, gesturing toward the road to the tram station. "It's, um. It's late."

"Yeah." Anya nodded. "I'm going the other way."

"Right. Of course."

A silence. Not empty — weighted, like a breath someone was holding. The canal gurgled. Something shifted behind the compound wall: a light switching on, a distant door.

"Be careful getting home," Yor said. "It's cold tonight. You have a proper coat? Not just that—"

"I'm fine, Mama."

"There's stew. In the fridge. From yesterday. If you want to stop by. You don't have to. But it's there."

"Okay."

"And the soup. The mushroom one. It's new. It might be — I think I used too much miso. But it's, you know. It's fine. It's an experiment."

"I'll try it."

Yor wanted to close the distance between them. She wanted to hold her daughter against her the way she had when Anya was small and the world was something Yor could physically stand between her and. But her hand was bleeding and she'd just killed five men in a dead broker's compound, and the evidence of something she couldn't yet name was on her phone pressing against her hip, and putting her bleeding palm against Anya's back would leave a mark. And it would mean standing in the open center of a question they both refused to ask.

"Goodnight, Anya."

"Night, Mama."

Yor turned and walked. She didn't look back. Looking back had always been the harder thing, and tonight every hard thing was harder.

---

Anya watched her mother's figure diminish along the service road until it became a shape against the distant freight depot lights, and then just the idea of a shape, and then gone.

She'd come here following a thread — a weapons broker on the northern outskirts whose name had appeared in three separate intelligence streams from Damian's network. The plan had been reconnaissance. Scout the security perimeter, photograph the compound layout, report back. The kind of careful preliminary work Loid had drilled into her since she was old enough to hold a camera: *You look before you touch. Always.*

She hadn't expected the compound to already be breached.

She'd known it was Yor before she'd seen her face.

Two hundred meters out, approaching along the canal, the thoughts had reached her through the thin ambient noise of the industrial outskirts — her mother's mind in a register Anya had only ever caught in fragments, quick glimpses that surfaced and vanished before she could process them. But tonight the compound's population was sparse enough and her proximity close enough that the signal came through clearly:

*Two in the corridor. Closing. Right turn. Office at the end.*

Yor's tactical mind was nothing like her domestic one. At home, her mother's thoughts moved in warm, looping patterns — had everyone eaten, was the apartment too cold, had she added too much salt, should she apologize to the neighbor for the noise. The loops circled and reassured and doubled back on themselves. In the compound, the loops were gone. What replaced them was clean and geometric, stripped of everything except function. Distances measured in steps. Threat levels ranked by proximity and weapon access. Approach angles calculated with the fluency of a woman who had been solving this kind of problem longer than Anya had been alive.

It was like hearing a different person speak. Except it wasn't. It was the same person with a wall removed — a wall Yor maintained at all times, in every room, around everyone who loved her. The wall that let the woman who burned eggs and apologized to furniture she bumped into continue to exist alongside the woman who could kill five armed men in twelve minutes without raising her pulse.

She'd stayed frozen behind the containers while the operation played out inside the compound, barely breathing, the pack clutched against her chest. Her mother's thoughts had come in clean waves — tactical processing, then briefer flashes of the private self surfacing between tasks. A quick involuntary image of the bento box in the refrigerator. A flicker of annoyance at a buzzing fluorescent tube. The detached observation that one of the corridor guards had favored his right knee and drawn too slowly because of it. Anya had read her father's operational mind many times — Loid's thoughts carried the weight of consequence, the careful balancing of options and costs. Yor's tactical mind didn't balance. It identified and acted with a directness that left no space for doubt, and the absence of doubt was the thing Anya found hardest to sit with, because she understood where it came from. The total conviction that the work was necessary. That each person removed was a threat subtracted from a world that contained the people she loved. The math was simple. The simplicity was what made it bearable, and what would make the breaking of it — the evidence Anya could feel her mother beginning to process in the office at the corridor's end — so much worse.

And then, as Yor came back over the perimeter wall and started down the service road, the geometry had come apart. The operational architecture collapsed, and underneath it, the thoughts the tactical mind had been holding back while there was work to do:

*The code. Our code. The Garden — on his records. Nine contracts. Four years.*

And beneath the words, in the layer where thought became feeling and feeling became the thing Anya had been reading in people her entire life: the slow structural crack of a woman realizing that the foundation she'd built twenty years of killing upon might not hold her weight anymore. Not the shock of new information but the nauseating click of a pattern half-glimpsed before — the shipping clerk three months ago, the invoices that didn't match, the handler's too-quick reassurance — assembling into a picture she'd been refusing to see.

Anya had received all of it. Standing behind the waste containers in the dark, close enough to hear her mother's boots grinding the gravel, she had taken in her mother's most private reckoning the way she took in everything: without permission, without announcement, without the possibility of being asked to stop.

She did this to everyone. She'd done it to Damian in a safe house two nights ago, reading the unguarded center of him while he kissed her. She'd done it to Sylvia, absorbing trust while delivering a lie. She did it to her father every time his worry leaked past his careful composure. And now she'd done it to her mother — who was walking alone down a dark road with blood drying on her hands and a crack spreading through the story she'd been telling herself for two decades — and Anya had caught every tremor of that crack because that was what she was. Not what she chose to be. What she was.

The violation wasn't romantic this time. It wasn't operational. It was the same violation it had always been, the one that didn't change shape depending on who she committed it against: structural, constant, built into the fact of her existence, committed against everyone she loved by a power she hadn't asked for and had never once been given consent to use. Damian's unguarded feelings in a safe house and Yor's private grief on a service road. The scale differed. The mechanism was identical.

She picked up her pack and started south, toward the tram station on the other side of the canal, the opposite direction from her mother.

The night was cold. Her ribs, still bruised from the ambush two days ago, sent a dull pulse through her side with every step. She could still feel the fading edge of Yor's thoughts — weakening as the distance between them widened — the tactical mind fully gone now, replaced by the domestic frequency reasserting itself like something Yor's brain did to keep functioning:

*I hope she eats the stew. She looked thin. She looked so thin.*

Anya walked. She didn't look back either.
