# Chapter 8

The noise started in the elevator.

Not the audible kind — the Concordia Hotel's elevators were smooth and quiet, the kind of quiet that expensive places maintained to justify the room rates. The man standing next to Anya in the elevator car was silent too, his hands folded over a leather portfolio, his expression set to the pleasant neutral that everyone in the diplomatic quarter wore after hours. But his thoughts were loud.

*— definitely left the gas on. I turned the knob before I left but did I check? I didn't check. If the building catches fire they'll find my notes about the Ackermann meeting on the kitchen counter and they'll think it's a suicide note. That's — no. I turned the knob. I'm almost sure I turned the knob.*

Anya adjusted her earring and watched the floor numbers climb. She'd been reading other people's thoughts since she was four years old. It never got less strange, but it had gotten quieter in certain ways — the surprise was gone, replaced by a low-grade fatigue she'd learned to manage the way some people managed bad knees. It was always there. She could work around it. Most days she barely noticed.

Tonight was not going to be one of those days.

The elevator opened onto the third-floor lobby of the Concordia, where the Westalian trade delegation's autumn reception was already underway behind a set of double doors. She could hear the string quartet and the glassware through the wood. She could hear everything else through the walls.

*— if he brings up the grain tariff one more time I swear I'll —*
*— nice shoes, who is she, haven't seen her at —*
*— the shrimp is late, they promised shrimp by seven and it's seven-fifteen and I'm not —*
*— didn't tell Maria I'd be out tonight, she's going to assume the worst, she always —*

Two hundred minds behind a set of doors. All going at once, all at different volumes, overlapping like radio stations bleeding into each other when you drove between cities. The gasman was right behind her, still agonizing over the knob. Anya rubbed the spot below her right ear — an old gesture, childhood, she was supposed to have stopped doing that years ago — and pushed through the doors.

The ballroom opened warm and bright, all crystal and amber light, both delegations already circulating. Westalian trade officials clustered near the bar with the loose posture of people who'd staked their territory early. Ostanian diplomatic staff had colonized the appetizer stations with the quiet efficiency of a border maneuver. A string quartet was playing something tasteful and completely ignored in the far corner. The Concordia's ballroom had two exits — one at each end — which appealed to intelligence professionals, and a well-stocked bar, which appealed to everyone else.

Anya picked up a glass of white wine she wouldn't drink and started moving.

Within the first twenty minutes she caught:

A Westalian trade minister near the terrace doors, mentally rehearsing an explanation for his wife about why he'd be home late. *— traffic, obviously, and then Kessler insisted on reviewing the export figures, these receptions always run long, if I leave at ten I can say ten-thirty because the taxi queue, and then —* The explanation was too detailed and too carefully constructed. He was building scaffolding around a lie, and the lie had nothing to do with export figures. None of her business. She kept moving.

An Ostanian general at the buffet — the one whose medals ran from his shoulder to his collar in a decorative fence — thinking about shrimp with an analytical rigor that would have been impressive in a briefing room. *— the tail came off clean. That's the mark of quality. If you have to twist, the preparation was rushed. This kitchen knows what it's doing. The cocktail sauce is adequate. Not exceptional. The horseradish ratio is off — too much lemon, not enough bite —* He selected four shrimp from the platter with the deliberation of a man making a strategic decision, held them in a brief visual inspection, and placed them on his plate in a row. His thoughts about the shrimp were more analytically rigorous than most of what Anya heard at WISE briefings.

A young Ostanian diplomat by the window, holding a champagne flute with both hands and composing a text message in her head, word by word, revising as she went. *— I think we should take a break. No. I think we need to talk about where this is going. No, that's worse. I think I need some space to figure out what I want. But I know what I want. I want to stop feeling like this every Sunday. Okay: maybe we should take some — god. I'll figure it out later.* She hadn't sipped her champagne. Her thumbnail was pressed white against the flute's stem.

A Westalian aide near the service entrance, holding a clipboard and thinking with the controlled despair of someone whose boss was about to give a speech. *— he's going to mispronounce Ackermann's title again, he did it at the Ostenheim reception and nobody corrected him, I wrote it phonetically on the note card but he won't look at the card, he never looks at the card, why do I make the cards —* The aide's jacket didn't fit. He kept tugging the sleeves down and the sleeves kept riding up, and his thoughts ran underneath the speech anxiety to a quiet, persistent worry about his mother's health that he'd been carrying all evening like a stone in his shoe.

An older Ostanian woman — some kind of deputy minister, based on the deference people showed when she passed — thinking about whether her daughter had remembered to feed the cat. *— she forgets the wet food. The dry is in the automatic feeder but the vet said he needs wet food twice a day for his kidneys, and she's twenty-eight years old and she can't remember to feed a cat —* The deputy minister was also, in a secondary track, reviewing talking points about natural gas imports. The cat took priority.

These people ran the foreign policy apparatus of two nations. Their inner lives were grocery lists and affair logistics and social anxiety and petty resentments and, occasionally, in the gaps between all of that, thoughts about actual governance. This was what telepathy in a crowded room felt like: standing in a river of other people's weather, most of it unremarkable, all of it uninvited, scanning for the handful of operationally useful fragments that justified the headache building behind her eyes.

She was here to work. The reception was an intelligence target — any gathering where both delegations occupied the same room offered proximity scanning that no surveillance equipment could replicate and no other operative in the world could perform. WISE didn't know that, of course. WISE thought she was here to observe, make contacts, and file a report on the diplomatic temperature. Which she would do. She'd translate her stolen intelligence into phrases like *my assessment* and *body language analysis suggests* and *observed behavioral indicators*, because that was how you cited a source that was someone else's private thoughts.

Two years of this. Two years of cheating and calling it fieldwork.

She drifted toward the east end of the ballroom and caught sight of Yuri Briar.

He was in a dark suit — SSS security detail for the evening, positioned where he could cover the main entrance, the corridor to the service stairwell, and most of the room. His earpiece was nearly invisible. His posture was the particular kind of alert stillness that people who'd never stood a security post would mistake for relaxation: weight balanced, hands free, eyes tracking the crowd in a steady sweep. Anya counted the interval. Every eight seconds.

She read him from across the room, his signal cutting through the noise with the clarity of a voice she knew.

*— west entrance covered. Kraus has the kitchen corridor. The Westalian delegation brought three extra staff that weren't on the advance sheet — logged it, but I don't like it. Three is an operational number. Three means someone planned for contingency.*

Sharp. He'd always been sharp. She remembered him at the Forger apartment when he was twenty, furious and adoring and so protective of Yor that he'd inspected Loid's cooking as if looking for poison in the miso soup. He'd mellowed about the cooking. A little.

*— need to call Sis. Told her dinner this week. Thursday or Friday. Can't remember which one I said. If it was Thursday I've already missed it and she'll have cooked anyway, she always makes too much food, and it'll be —* A flash of affection so bright it cut through the crowd noise like sunlight through a window. *— terrible. It'll taste terrible. I'll eat all of it and tell her it was great. That turmeric phase is not helping.*

Anya looked away before her expression could betray her. Yuri's love for his sister was so simple and so fierce that catching it always felt like stepping on something delicate. She shouldn't have been reading it. She was always reading it.

Then his attention shifted, and the warmth in his thoughts hardened into something professional.

*— there she is. The cultural attaché. Forger — Sis's family. Third function this month. Cultural attachés don't work this circuit. They cover arts events, memorial lectures, the gallery openings. They don't show up at trade receptions and mixed-delegation briefings unless they're doing something beyond culture.*

A beat. His gaze tracked across the room, and Anya realized with a cold little lurch that he was looking directly at her.

*— Sis would say I'm overthinking. But three events, three different kinds. A cultural attaché doesn't circulate like that. Not unless she's got a reason nobody filed with my office.*

Anya turned smoothly toward the nearest conversation cluster and inserted herself into a discussion about bilateral arts funding that three Westalian attachés were conducting with the enthusiasm of people fulfilling a professional obligation. She was careful not to look at Yuri. She was careful to look like someone who had strong opinions about ceramics residency grants.

"The Ostenheim program is actually oversubscribed this year," one of the attachés said, and Anya nodded like this was the most interesting thing she'd heard all evening.

She asked an intelligent question about studio access and extracted herself after four minutes. She moved toward the bar, where a cluster of Westalian staff had gathered, and passed within range of Strauss.

Strauss worked the economic intelligence desk at WISE. He was nursing a gin and tonic, watching the room with half-attention, thinking in the slightly cynical register of someone who'd been doing analytical work long enough to see the framework's seams. Anya didn't seek him out deliberately — his thoughts were just there, the way all thoughts were, crowding in uninvited.

*— the current Ostanian defense posture is consistent with escalation, but if we're honest — and we're never honest about this — the threat assessment we filed last quarter was shaped. Halverson shaped it. I helped. We didn't invent the threat. We selected the data that made the budget request look urgent and left out the numbers that complicated it. If the tension drops, WISE's operating budget drops. Everybody at the desk knows this. Nobody says it because saying it out loud makes us the same kind of people we're supposed to be watching.*

Anya reached past him for a glass of sparkling water and said nothing. She took the water and drifted away and let his thought sit inside her like a stone she'd swallowed.

Both sides. She'd suspected it — you didn't work in intelligence without developing a sense for where institutional incentive bent the truth. But suspecting was abstract, a thing you could live alongside without looking at directly. Reading it from a colleague's unguarded thoughts was concrete. Strauss, with his gin and tonic and his decent shoes and his two kids in the Berlint school district, shaping threat assessments to justify his agency's budget. Not lying, exactly. Selecting. Framing. The same thing diplomats did, the same thing she did every time she filed a report crediting "behavioral indicators" instead of "I heard his thoughts." Everyone in this room was adjusting the picture for their own reasons, and the picture they produced — the geopolitical landscape that actual people would live and die inside — was the sum of all those adjustments, not the truth underneath.

She'd been naive to believe WISE was different from the machinery on the other side. She'd been naive for exactly as long as it had taken to read one honest thought from a man who didn't know she was listening.

Anya finished her water and kept moving.

* * *

She found Damian near the terrace doors, talking to a woman from the Ostanian Foreign Affairs Ministry about port logistics. He was listening with the genuine interest of someone who actually cared about container-handling regulations, which was both a diplomatic skill and, Anya suspected, a genuine condition. He cared about infrastructure. It was one of his more alarming qualities.

*— the tariff revision would solve the northern port bottleneck, but the ministry won't move on it because reducing friction at the border reduces the appearance of tension, and the appearance of tension is what keeps the defense allocation from —*

Even his thoughts ran through policy architecture. Anya waited for the port-logistics woman to excuse herself, which she did after two more sentences about dredging schedules.

"The shrimp's worth trying," Anya said, low enough to sound personal.

"Yeah?"

"A general out there has been analyzing it for forty minutes. His assessment is favorable."

Damian glanced toward the buffet. He was thinking about the general and also, underneath, about whether Anya had picked up anything useful from the Westalian side. His mind organized itself neatly — the social register on top, the operational calculations beneath, and below those, something warmer and less controlled that he was working hard to keep out of his active thoughts.

"Anything from the trade people?" he said. His voice was casual. His thoughts were not.

"Budget shaping. I'll explain later."

"Right." He straightened his cuffs — not nervous, deliberate, something to do with his hands while his interior reorganized. *— she does this. She walks through a room for twenty minutes and comes back with more actionable material than I get in a full evening. How does she — observation, sure, but it's more than that, there's something about the way she —* He stopped the thought, filed it somewhere she couldn't reach, and produced the diplomatic smile. "I'll take the south end. The defense liaison's here. He's been avoiding me, which means he's got something worth hearing."

"Don't antagonize him."

"I don't antagonize."

"You absolutely do."

"I create conditions for candid dialogue."

"Sure."

They separated. Two colleagues at a party, exchanging small talk about seafood — nobody watching would have caught anything. Anya moved back toward the bar and Damian crossed toward the south windows, and for a moment the room was just a room: people eating, drinking, rehearsing lies for their spouses, thinking about their shoes, performing the elaborate social labor of pretending to enjoy standing upright for three hours in formal clothing.

Through the terrace doors, the Concordia's garden was dark and cold, the last of the autumn light long gone. A few smokers stood on the terrace in their overcoats, their thoughts drifting outward into the night — a financial dispute, a half-written resignation letter, the particular melancholy of standing outside at a party where you knew almost everyone and felt close to almost no one. Anya caught these as background radiation and moved on. The headache was settling in behind her right eye now, the low, steady ache that came after an hour of sustained scanning. She'd learned to work through it. She'd never learned to like it.

She scanned the room in quadrants, the way Loid had taught her — not his teaching method but the spatial discipline she'd adapted from watching him work rooms, years ago, before she was old enough to understand what he was doing and long after she'd read it from his thoughts. Upper left, upper right, lower left, lower right. Read, assess, move. The pattern kept her from circling back to signals she'd already caught, and it kept her moving, which was important — a cultural attaché who stood still too long at a trade reception was a cultural attaché who looked like she was waiting for something.

Anya worked the next half hour with the steady patience of someone who'd been doing this since her first field assignment. She passed within range of six Ostanian officials, two defense attachés, a military aide whose thoughts were exclusively about whether he'd parked in a tow zone, and a very nervous junior diplomat who was thinking about whether his fly was down.

It was.

She couldn't tell him. She could not, under any circumstance, approach a stranger at a diplomatic reception and inform him that his fly was down based on intelligence she had obtained by reading his mind. She moved on. The junior diplomat continued to radiate anxiety about his zipper, and she had to actively steer around his signal because it was loud enough to interfere with her operational scanning, and the whole thing was a perfect miniature of her entire life: she knew the truth, she couldn't say it, and the person it concerned would never know she'd been there.

She caught fragments. The useful kind, the ones she'd compress into cover phrasing for her report:

*— Voss has been quiet this week. Too quiet. Werner doesn't go quiet unless something's ready to move, and if the timeline is —*

*— northern checkpoint reinforcements are ahead of schedule, told Ackermann the timeline holds, but if the budget review interferes with —*

*— we're not supposed to discuss the procurement contracts at functions, somebody tell Dietrich, if the Westalians overhear the contract numbers —*

Pieces. Threads. None of it formed a picture on its own, but each thought was a data point she'd cross-reference later against Damian's network. The work was patient and cumulative — intelligence as data entry, one stolen thought at a time — and the tedium was broken only by the occasional comic fragment that reminded her these powerful officials were also regular people with regular problems and regular digestive concerns.

Then Damian looked at her from across the room.

She was standing near the eastern bar, turning her water glass in her hand, when she felt his attention swing toward her. Not his diplomatic attention — the kind he pointed at everyone. Something older. Something that came from a different part of his memory. She read it as it happened:

*— there she is. By the bar. The way she holds things — close to her body, like she's protecting them. I've noticed that. When did I start noticing that?*

A hesitation. His thoughts rearranged.

*— she looks like someone. Not just familiar. She looks like — Eden Academy. The school festival. Second year. We got assigned to the same cleanup crew and she knocked over an entire bucket of soapy water on Henderson's shoes and when she laughed she —*

The memory was nearly formed. Anya could feel it surfacing through his thoughts like something rising from deep water: a girl with pink hair, younger, smaller, laughing with her whole body about the ruined shoes. The specific quality of afternoon light through Eden's courtyard trees. Her laugh. The way she tilted her head when she was about to say something terrible.

*— pink hair. She had pink hair. The attaché has pink hair. That's not — how many people have pink hair? The girl at Eden used to tilt her head exactly like — exactly like she's doing right now, she's doing it right now —*

He was one synapse away from the name. Anya could feel it sitting behind the image like a word on the tip of a tongue — *Forger* — not yet spoken in his thoughts, not yet locked to the face of the woman he'd been working with for weeks, but close. Three seconds. Maybe two.

Someone touched his arm.

The Ostanian defense liaison — the one who'd been avoiding him all evening — had apparently decided to stop avoiding him, and the man's greeting broke through Damian's concentration like a hand shattering a water's surface. His thoughts scattered into handshake protocol — the liaison's name, his file, his known positions on border security, the correct register for professional warmth. The childhood memory submerged. Sank back below the professional surface, unfinished. The name still unspoken.

Anya put her glass down on the bar because her hand was shaking. She pressed her palm flat against the bar's polished surface, the wood cool and solid under her fingers, and breathed. In through the nose, out through the mouth, the way Loid had taught her for tactical stress and the way Yor had taught her separately for the kind of stress that came from wanting to hit something but not being allowed to. Different teachings. Same daughter. She waited until her pulse stopped hammering against the inside of her wrist and then she picked up the glass again because a person standing at a bar without a drink looked like a person who'd just had bad news.

That was too close. Three seconds from her cover identity disintegrating inside his mind, from Damian connecting the girl at Eden Academy to the Westalian attaché to the intelligence operative who'd been working alongside him for weeks. And when he got there — when, not if, because the memory was closer each time and it was never going to stop surfacing — the questions that followed would be the kind she couldn't answer without burning everything. Why was a girl he'd known at school now working as a Westalian cultural attaché? Why had she appeared in his orbit at the state dinner? Why did she always know the right things to say and the right moments to intervene?

She needed cover. Now.

Anya found Hesse — a Westalian diplomat who ran training programs for junior staff — and inserted herself into a conversation Hesse was having with an Ostanian academic about educational exchange programs. She waited for an opening that took twenty seconds to arrive.

"The diplomatic preparatory courses are actually great for that," Anya said, when the academic mentioned networking challenges. "I went through the training exchange three years ago and I still keep running into people at these receptions who I think I recognize from the program. You spend a week in intensive sessions and everybody's face sort of blurs together afterward, you know?"

Hesse agreed. The academic agreed. Nobody cared about this anecdote. That was the point — a verbal footnote, planted in a conversation near enough to Damian's end of the room that the idea might settle into his uncertainty later. A false explanation for why the attaché looked familiar. A trail laid over the real one.

She was getting better at this. At the covering, the deflecting, the small deceptions that kept the larger ones intact. She wasn't sure that was something to feel good about.

She drifted through the crowd and spent a few minutes with a woman near the south windows who was genuinely, uncomplicatedly passionate about ceramics — a cultural program officer from the Ostanian side, actually doing the job Anya was pretending to do, thinking about glaze chemistry and firing temperatures and the specific satisfaction of pulling a bowl from a kiln and finding it had turned out right. The woman's name was Lentz. She wore a silver ring shaped like a coiled wire and her thoughts matched her words perfectly. No second layer. No concealed agenda. Just ceramics.

Anya asked about the Ostenheim residency program and received a detailed, enthusiastic answer about studio access and traditional wood-firing. It was the most restful three minutes of her evening, and she told Lentz the exhibition sounded wonderful and meant every word.

Near the east entrance, Yuri's attention tracked over Anya again. Not scanning for her, exactly — but noting her position the way he noted every position, adding it to the map he was building of the room's movements.

*— she went from the bar to the Hesse group to the ceramics woman to the south-window cluster. Four groups in twenty minutes. Cultural attachés park in one cluster and work outward. She's moving through the room. She's covering ground.*

A pause. His thoughts shifted register, and the professional sharpness softened at the edges.

*— Sis would be upset if she knew I was watching her like this. But watching is what I do. And wanting to trust someone isn't the same as having a reason.*

Anya moved away from the south windows and made herself visible at the bar for a few minutes, standing still, holding a glass of sparkling water, performing the specific kind of social stillness that said *I'm resting between conversations* and nothing more suspicious than that. It was the best she could do.

* * *

By nine o'clock the room was at peak density.

Both delegations had arrived in full. The second wave of guests had merged with the first, aides and plus-ones and late arrivals filling the spaces between clusters until the ballroom held something close to three hundred people, and the noise — her kind of noise — had become a physical pressure behind Anya's eyes.

She'd been scanning for two hours. The fatigue was specific — not sleepiness but a flattening, as if the resolution of her reception was degrading the way a radio signal degrades in a tunnel. Individual thoughts were harder to isolate. She had to focus longer to lock on, and the effort cost more each time, and the headache behind her right eye had deepened into something that pulsed when she turned her head too quickly. Her jaw was tight. She kept unclenching it and then finding it clenched again a minute later, the muscles working on their own while her attention was somewhere else. The crowd noise had layered into something atmospheric, a weather system sitting on her awareness, dense and formless, punctuated only occasionally by a thought clear enough to grab.

*— she's wearing the same dress as the ambassador's wife, this is going to be a problem —*
*— my feet, god, I knew these shoes were a mistake —*
*— the November deployment schedule still hasn't been signed because Brandt is stalling, and if he stalls past the review date —*

She kept the Brandt fragment. Discarded the shoes and the dress. A waiter passed close and his thoughts were entirely about a fight with his roommate over dishes in the sink, and the specificity of his grievance — three days, three sets of dishes, a pan with burned rice that his roommate claimed was "soaking" — was so vivid and so minor that Anya almost laughed, and the almost-laugh hurt her head.

She was near the Westalian delegation now, passing through a cluster of staffers and officials discussing energy policy in the circular, patient way that diplomatic conversations moved when nobody wanted to commit to a position. She caught their thoughts as she drifted through — energy markets, energy tariffs, someone thinking about a sick child at home and whether the fever had broken, energy projections, someone attracted to the speaker and trying not to think about it, energy subsidies.

And then:

*— the asset inside WISE has been reliable, but the handoff timeline needs to move forward before —*

The thought was clear. Surgically clear, the kind of clarity that came from concentrated focus — someone working a specific problem in their mind with real attention. It cut through the crowd noise like a signal on a clean frequency.

Anya stopped walking.

Her fingers tightened on the stem of her glass. She turned, scanning the people around her — faces, positions, who was close enough, who was thinking that —

*— need to accelerate the reporting window, the asset's coverage shifts after the review cycle and if the handoff isn't —*

Close. Someone within her range — fifteen meters, maybe less. Someone in this cluster of Westalian staffers and Ostanian counterparts and servers passing with trays and security personnel stationed at the walls. Someone who knew about an asset inside WISE. Someone who was thinking about a handoff timeline and a reporting schedule, thinking about it here, at a reception, standing within reach of the very people the intelligence was being stolen from.

Anya turned her head. Twelve people within range. Fourteen. She couldn't fix on it — the thoughts were overlapping, someone was pushing through the crowd to her left, the signal shifted.

*— the trade surplus numbers are going to —*
*— left my phone in the car, I need to —*
*— asset inside WISE — the extraction protocol —*

She moved two steps to her left. The signal didn't sharpen. She turned toward the window and caught a fragment — *handoff* — thin, dissolving — and then it was gone, swallowed by the roar of three hundred minds thinking about dinner plans and office politics and their children and the border and tomorrow's meeting agenda and whether they'd said something stupid to the wrong person earlier.

Gone. The thinker had moved, or shifted focus, or been interrupted by a conversation, and the thought had sunk back below the surface like a coin dropped into dark water. The crowd noise rushed back in to fill the gap — someone's knee pain, someone's parking meter, someone's husband, someone's budget meeting tomorrow — and the signal was buried, irretrievable, and she was standing in a room full of people and she had no idea which one of them had been thinking about betraying the agency she worked for.

She stayed where she was. She scanned the nearby faces — a Westalian analyst she recognized from the mission, an Ostanian trade official she'd passed earlier, a woman in a green dress whose thoughts were about a hotel booking, two men discussing something about fishing rights whose thoughts were actually about fishing rights. Nobody thinking about WISE. Nobody thinking about assets or handoffs or extraction protocols. The thought had been there and now it wasn't, and she couldn't chase a signal that no longer existed any more than she could chase a word someone had spoken ten minutes ago into a wind.

Anya stood in the middle of the ballroom holding her glass hard enough to feel the stem flex.

*The asset inside WISE.*

Someone in her organization was compromised. Someone inside WISE — the agency she'd trained for, built her cover around, given two years of active fieldwork to, the agency her father had given decades of his life to — was feeding intelligence. To the Renewal Front, or to someone connected to the Renewal Front, or to someone whose operational interests aligned with the forces Anya and Damian had spent weeks trying to map. She didn't know which. She didn't know who. She'd caught a fragment — clean, specific, devastating — and then lost the thinker in the crowd, and there was no way to recover the signal because she couldn't explain to anyone why she'd need to scan the room again, because she couldn't explain how she'd found it in the first place.

She put the glass down on a passing server's tray. Her hand was steady because she'd made it steady.

She found a marble column between the bar and the east windows and leaned her back against it. The stone was cool through the fabric of her dress. She closed her eyes for one second and let the noise of the room wash over her without trying to sort it.

*— hope the car's still there, meter ran out at —*
*— she said "me too" instead of "I love you too" and those aren't the same, are they? Are they the —*
*— if the eastern corridor isn't reinforced by November the whole defensive posture —*
*— I'm so tired of this, tired of smiling and being charming and pretending I care about trade routes, I just want to go home and take off these shoes and sit somewhere nobody wants anything from me —*

She opened her eyes. The room was the same. The string quartet had started something new. Near the bar, someone laughed — a big, genuine, uncomplicated laugh — and Anya wanted to find whoever it was and stand near them, because their thoughts would be warm and unconcerned and thinking about something that didn't matter, and right now she wanted very badly to be near a thought that didn't matter.

She couldn't do that. She was working. She was always working.

Here was what she knew: someone inside WISE was passing intelligence, and she'd discovered it by reading a thought she wasn't supposed to hear, using an ability she'd never told anyone she possessed, while standing at a reception she'd attended under a cover identity that was itself a deception wrapped inside a deception. She couldn't report this through channels. She couldn't explain how she knew without explaining what she was. She couldn't tell Sylvia — who trusted her, who she'd already lied to with a false assessment three weeks ago, whose trust she'd read in real time while the lie was being accepted. She couldn't go to Sylvia and say *There's a mole in WISE* because Sylvia would ask *How do you know?* and the answer was *I heard someone thinking about it*, and the conversation that followed would end Anya's career and her cover and every relationship she'd built inside the agency.

She couldn't tell Damian. Their alliance ran on the premise that she was a competent operative with a network and good instincts, not a girl who could hear people's private thoughts and had been doing it to everyone she'd ever known since she was four years old.

She couldn't tell anyone. That was the joke, if it was a joke. The most connected person in the room — the one who could hear every mind, every secret, every unguarded fear and private tenderness — was also the most alone. Because every piece of intelligence she gathered was stolen property, and the one honest thing she could say about herself was the one thing she could never, under any circumstance, say to anyone.

Anya straightened up from the column. She smoothed the front of her dress. She picked up a fresh glass of water from a tray and held it like a prop, because that was what people did at parties — they held things so their hands had a purpose.

She would need to find the mole herself. Quietly. Without telling WISE what she was looking for or why she was looking. She would investigate her own agency using tradecraft her father had taught her and an ability she'd never disclosed to him, conducting an unauthorized investigation inside an organization she'd already lied to, on the basis of information she'd obtained through a method that no one in the world knew existed.

She'd add it to the list. Below the false report. Below the concealed alliance with Damian. Below the childhood recognition that was three seconds from shattering her cover. Below the nightly, ordinary reality of being inside every person she cared about's head without once having been invited.

Across the room, Damian caught her eye and gave a small nod — the signal that meant he was wrapping up, that they'd debrief afterward. His thoughts, which she caught in the moment of eye contact, were professional and tired and, underneath, gently concerned.

*— she looks off. Something happened. I'll ask later. Not in here.*

She nodded back. The nod meant *I'm fine*, which was a lie, but she'd had a lot of practice with those tonight.

The string quartet started something brighter. The general made another pass at the shrimp platter and took four without hesitation this time, the etiquette debate apparently settled. The young diplomat by the window had deleted her mental breakup text and decided she'd try again tomorrow. Yuri Briar checked his watch — a battered analog thing with a cracked crystal that Anya knew was a gift from Yor, years ago, because she'd been in the room when Yor wrapped it — and thought about whether he'd told his sister Thursday or Friday.

Anya stood in the middle of a room full of people whose inner lives she could hear, who didn't know she was listening, who would never know, and felt the specific loneliness of knowing everything about everyone and being fully known by no one.

She finished her water and went to find her coat.
