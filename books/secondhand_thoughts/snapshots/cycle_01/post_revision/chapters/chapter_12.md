# Chapter 12

The Blackbell estate glowed against the October dark with the restraint of someone who didn't know the word. White lanterns lined the drive at meticulous intervals. The topiary garden had been strung with lights that made it look like a magazine spread, which it would be. Becky had confirmed photographers from three publications, all carefully vetted to make the event look effortless instead of like the three weeks of controlled panic it had actually been.

Anya stepped out of the car and the thoughts hit before the cold air did.

*If the valet scratches my paint, I'm going to lose it. I just had it detailed. I'm literally watching him.*

*She's wearing the Moreau. She's wearing the Moreau. I was told I was the only one with the Moreau.*

*Seven more handshakes. I can do seven. Maybe eight if I count the wife.*

Two hundred minds. She let them blur to a murmur and sorted for signals: security detail, two private guards, one off-duty SSS officer she recognized from the diplomatic reception, exit routes, and the running estimate of how long she could stay before Becky noticed if she slipped out early.

The answer, from a thought she'd caught three weeks ago during a phone call: *If she cancels on me one more time, I'm killing her. I'm not joking. I will actually kill her.* Becky would not actually kill her. Becky did not know how to kill anyone. But the sentiment had been clear.

Damian came around the car. Dark suit, precise posture. The thin scar through his eyebrow was the only thing that didn't match the packaging. He hadn't said much on the drive over. The mole was still unidentified. The Renewal Front's clock had shrunk to days. Attending a charity gala while all of that pressed at their backs felt obscene, but covers needed maintenance. Anya's social calendar had to look like a real person's social calendar.

"Ready?" she said.

"No."

*The things I do for East-West cultural exchange. Kirchner is going to corner me and talk about tariffs for forty minutes, and I'm going to smile until my face detaches.*

"It'll be fine," Anya said. "Becky throws a good party."

"You keep saying that."

"The food'll be amazing."

"That's what you said about the reception."

"And was I wrong?"

He paused. "You weren't entirely wrong."

*She wore that color before. The state dinner. Stop it. Trade policy. Think about trade policy.*

Anya walked toward the entrance and tried not to listen to what he was trying not to think.

Becky intercepted them in the foyer with the precision of a guided missile in a cocktail dress.

"You came!" She grabbed Anya by the shoulders, held her at arm's length, and ran a brisk head-to-heels check that could've meant deep affection or quality control. "You actually came. I was going to be so mad if you bailed."

*She looks tired. She always looks tired now. Why doesn't she eat? I'm going to make her eat if I have to physically drag her to the buffet.*

"I said I'd come," Anya said.

"You said that about the gallery opening in August and then you texted me from a parking lot at eleven saying work blew up." Becky released her and turned to Damian with sudden, predatory interest. "And you brought a guest."

"Damian Desmond." She extended her hand. "Eden Academy. You cried at the eighth-grade science fair because your volcano didn't erupt."

*Oh no. She remembers the volcano. Of course she remembers the volcano.*

"There was a structural issue with the display," Damian started.

"It didn't erupt."

"The sodium bicarbonate ratio was off."

"Damian. It didn't erupt. I was there. You cried."

His public smile held, barely. "It was a frustrating afternoon."

*Kill me. Just kill me now.*

Anya bit the inside of her cheek.

Becky smiled, the real one, not the camera version. Her dark curls were pinned into an arrangement complicated enough to need blueprints, and she was wearing a dress that probably cost more than Anya's monthly cover salary, but underneath the production she was still the girl who'd traded her lunch for Anya's dessert every day for a year at Eden and thought she was getting the better deal.

"I'm glad you're here," Becky said. The thought under it matched the words: *I'm glad. I'm glad she came.* "Both of you. Go eat, talk to people, enjoy yourselves. I have to deal with a crisis. They brought Riesling instead of Gruner Veltliner and I'm going to start a war."

She was already moving, heels sharp on the marble, thoughts scattering between logistics and the plain relief of having her oldest friend in the room.

*Maybe tonight. Maybe tonight will feel real.*

Anya watched her go and felt the first pull of the evening settle behind her ribs, right where the bruise from the ambush had mostly healed and not quite vanished.

The ballroom had gilt frames, a chandelier that could have doubled as a weapon, and acoustics that carried every low conversation farther than anyone admitted. Tables with white cloths ran along one wall. The buffet ran along the other. The bar held the far end of the room with the gravity of a small nation.

Anya worked the room the way she'd been trained: peripheral scanning, threat assessment, social mapping. Tonight was supposed to be maintenance, not operations. She needed to be seen doing ordinary things: making small talk, circling the buffet, existing in public beside a friend who threw galas. Normal. Unremarkable. A cultural attache with a social life.

The problem was that Eden Academy alumni were everywhere.

Becky had been mining the old school network for years. Lawyers, diplomats, media producers, one person Anya was fairly sure had become a professional horse trainer. They filled the room like a reunion nobody had officially scheduled, people rediscovering childhood hierarchies and discovering, with varying levels of pain, whether those hierarchies had survived adulthood.

A woman in green intercepted Anya near the buffet. "Anya. From Miss Henderson's class. Do you remember me?"

*Please say yes. Please say yes. I rehearsed this in the car.*

"Of course. You sat next to the window. Third row."

"Yes. Oh my God." Relief flooded the woman's mind like warm water. *She remembers.* "I'm Petra. Petra Vogt. I'm in publishing now."

"That's great."

"What about you? Someone said you're with the diplomatic service?"

"Cultural attache. Mostly paperwork and events."

"That sounds amazing."

Petra's thoughts corrected the sentence for her. *Sounds boring, honestly, but she looks incredible. Is that silk?* Anya smiled, said they should catch up properly sometime, and escaped before either of them had to pretend they meant it.

Near the bar, the off-duty SSS officer from the drive accepted a refill and laughed at a joke he wasn't listening to. Under the laugh his mind ran on a separate track. *Kreuzfeld moved to before dawn. Good. Let them hit wire instead of fog for once.*

The name cut through the room. Kreuzfeld was a border checkpoint. Moving a roster on a Thursday night meant somebody expected movement, not paperwork.

She found the buffet. Becky's money had bought the panic menu: gorgeous, expensive, too much of it. Anya loaded a small plate and drifted toward a table near the terrace doors. Damian had been absorbed into a group near the bar, dark suits and policy talk and the restless self-importance of young diplomats who weren't yet important enough to be interesting but were important enough to be invited. His thoughts surfaced through the crowd noise at intervals. *Bilateral trade framework, that's safe. Say that. Kirchner still parts his hair the same way. And if Kreuzfeld is tightening tonight, the usual route is dead.*

That last line caught like a hook. She didn't look at him. She took a bite of goat cheese on toast and chewed slowly while the operational clock, which had been background pressure all evening, moved up a notch.

A waiter offered champagne. She took a glass and didn't drink from it. The glass was a prop, an excuse for her hands. Across the room, a retired diplomat was telling the same customs story he told at every event. Two tables over, an elderly woman was planning the argument she intended to have with her husband when they got home. *If he says one word about my mother, I swear to God...* Anya tuned away. Some minds were soap operas you hadn't bought tickets for.

They were careful with each other in public. They had been careful since the safe house, since the bruised ribs and split eyebrow and the quiet after, since the line they'd crossed and then, by mutual unstated agreement, backed away from without deciding which side they meant to live on. Here, in a room full of people who knew one or both of them, careful was the only option. It also weighed a ton.

Once, near the buffet, their paths crossed. Damian reached for a napkin. Anya reached for a second goat cheese toast.

"The cheese is good," she said.

"I noticed you're eating all of it."

"It's strategically important cheese."

He almost smiled. *Don't smile. You're smiling.* "I'll leave you to your strategy."

He moved away. The almost-smile stayed in her chest longer than it should have, and she hated that, because her brain should have been on the mole, on Kreuzfeld, on the clock, on anything except the exact way his mouth moved when he was trying not to be amused.

Then somebody found Becky's nostalgia trap.

She'd set a side table near the bar with stacked Eden yearbooks, a leather binder of archival photo reprints, and a rented carousel projector clicking school pictures onto the cream wall between the terrace doors and the quartet. Lila, one of Becky's media contacts and an Eden alumna, had apparently decided this counted as public service.

The wall filled with a yearbook class photo first: thirty children in Eden blazers, squinting at the photographer with expressions ranging from dutiful to mutinous.

"Oh my God," Lila said. "Look at us."

Nostalgia spread fast and got less accurate by the minute. People abandoned drinks to crowd around the table and wall. Somebody flipped through yearbooks. Somebody else dug through the binder. The projector clicked from sports day to the end-of-year ceremony to a class picnic where half the boys looked seasick. Adults laughed at the evidence that they had once been tiny and ridiculous.

Anya watched from the edge of the cluster and felt her stomach tighten.

Erich Kessler materialized at her table with his third beer and the comfortable posture of a man who had found his equilibrium several drinks ago. Erich ran logistics for his family's distribution company. His surface thoughts gave her the rest: resentment over his older brother's recent promotion, and a homesick gratitude for the one place where people had talked to him before they learned his last name mattered.

*Good to be here. Good party. I miss this. I miss when we were all in the same building and nobody'd figured out who was going to matter and who wasn't.*

"Anya." He dropped into the chair across from her, grinning. "From Eden, right? Becky's friend?"

"That's me."

"You look exactly the same." He pointed at his own head. "The hair. I'd recognize it anywhere. Nobody else had pink hair. You were like the one bright flag in the whole school."

*Pink hair. Class photo. Second row. She sat behind me in Mr. Kraft's class.*

"I've been told it's distinctive."

"Distinctive? It's a landmark. I used to navigate the hallways by your head."

She laughed. The laugh was real enough. Erich was harmless enough. For half a minute it was just a party.

Then he leaned over the nostalgia table, pawed through the clear sleeves in the binder, and let out a delighted noise. "No. No, this is criminal. Lila, put this one up."

The projector clacked. The wall filled with school festival cleanup day: a row of twelve-year-olds holding brooms and dustpans with the expressions of prisoners assigned to yard work. Anya was in the frame. So was Damian. They stood shoulder to shoulder with the body language of two people who would rather have been anywhere else.

She knew the exact afternoon. The heat. The dust. Damian making a remark about her sweeping technique in a tone she had found intolerable. His thought from that day came back clean as a bell. *She's the worst person on this committee. Why does she look like she's having fun? Nobody should be having fun.*

"Oh, this is great," Erich said. He pointed his beer at the wall. "Desmond!"

He called it loudly enough to turn heads. Damian looked over from his conversation.

"Erich." Smile. Measured. "Good to see you."

"Come look at this. Cleanup crew. We look like a penal colony."

Damian crossed the room. His thoughts shifted as he moved. *Old photos, of course. Smile at the wall. Say we all look young.*

Then he saw it.

*The festival. The brooms. The girl next to me.*

His thoughts slowed. Not stopped. Slowed, the way a mind slows when it's walking toward a conclusion it can't undo.

Erich kept talking, beer-warm and easy, flipping between the wall and the present with the delight of a man who had found the exact story he wanted to tell. He looked at the projection. He looked at Anya. He looked back at the projection.

"Wait." His whole face lit with the loose clarity alcohol stripped him down to. "Wait, wait. You and Anya Forger had that whole thing in school, right?" He was talking to Damian now, jabbing the beer toward the projected photo. "The rivalry or whatever. She punched you once, didn't she? During dodgeball?"

The name landed in the room like a glass hitting tile.

Anya Forger.

A man on his third beer had said it out loud at a party while grieving his own vanished importance and having no idea what he'd just done.

And then something in Damian's mind clicked hard enough she felt it in her teeth.

Not an explosion. A sequence. Clean, brutal, one catch after another.

*Forger.*

*Pink hair.*

*The state dinner. She tilted her head the same way.*

An image: the class photo in a box in his apartment. A girl in the second row, grinning at whoever stood outside the frame. He'd found it weeks ago, stared at it too long, put it back without understanding why his hands felt wrong afterward.

*The laugh. In the park after Bretz. Same laugh.*

*The safe house. Her face. The way she flinched when she took the hit to her ribs.*

*The cultural attache who appeared at the state dinner. Who knew the right things to know. Who intervened when the operative had me cornered. Who kissed me in a cold apartment while I was bleeding.*

Then, clear and final:

*She's Anya Forger.*

His expression didn't change. Twenty years of political upbringing and Desmond discipline held the line. His face produced a half-smile. His hand stayed steady around his glass.

"That was a long time ago," he said, voice perfectly even.

*Anya Forger. Eden Academy. Westalian intelligence. Has this been there since the beginning? Was Eden real? Was any of it?*

*The alliance. The service corridor. The terms. She knew before I opened my mouth.*

*The kiss.*

A pause inside the noise.

*What do I do with this.*

Erich was still going. "And she punched him, right? Like full-on? That's legendary, honestly."

"I think you're mixing me up with somebody else," Anya said.

She heard her own voice from a distance, bright and easy and built for exactly this kind of deflection. "Anya's a pretty common name. And I have never punched anyone during dodgeball."

"Huh." Erich squinted at her. *She really does look like... no. Lots of people have pink hair now. Do they? Maybe not.* He took a drink. "Yeah, must be. My cousin's called Anya, actually. Well, Anna. Close enough."

He moved on. The cluster shifted back toward the wall, the binder, the yearbooks, the harmless pleasure of finding out the tallest boy in class had become the shortest man in the room.

Damian did not move on.

He stood where he'd been, glass untouched, and his thoughts turned with the careful deliberation of a man sorting evidence under pressure.

*Don't say it here. Not yet. Think about what it means. Think about what it changes.*

*Everything.*

His eyes found hers across the room. Half a second. Less.

A flicker went through his face. Not anger. Not accusation. Recognition stripped of every childish layer that had kept it harmless.

*I see you.*

Then it was gone. Somebody beside him asked a question about wine. He answered without missing a beat.

*Not here.*

*Think.*

Anya set her champagne glass down before she dropped it. The quartet slid into a waltz. Erich turned another page in the binder. Around them the party kept moving.

She pressed her palms flat to the tablecloth and breathed.

Her pager vibrated inside her evening bag.

Two short pulses. One long.

Border channel.

She turned just enough to shield the tiny screen with her hand.

`KZF 2300 MISSED. RETURN SECURE ASAP.`

Kreuzfeld.

The cold that went through her had nothing to do with October. She'd caught the checkpoint name once from the SSS officer. Once from Damian. Now WISE was paging her out of Becky's gala because a border check-in had gone missing and the room was still full of people laughing at their twelve-year-old haircuts.

The crowd noise pressed in. She needed to not be in this room.

* * *

The terrace was colder than she'd expected. The party burned warm behind the glass doors, but out here the October air had finally given up pretending to be kind. Wide flagstones, wrought-iron railing, a view of the grounds that disappeared into black after about twenty feet. In daylight the garden probably looked expensive. Tonight it looked like a place you could vanish into.

She gripped the railing. The cold metal helped. It was an object, a boundary, a fact.

Damian was still inside, at the edge of her range. The careful rhythm of his thoughts came through thinned by glass and distance. They were no longer one-track. *Anya Forger. Don't say it here. Kreuzfeld's too early. If they didn't move the papers already...*

She couldn't read what he'd decide about her. She could read that he was deciding, and that was worse. The border alert sat under it like a second blade. By morning, whatever was moving toward Kreuzfeld would either make it through or bleed out on a checkpoint road, and there was no world in which she could do both jobs at once.

The guilt sat in her like spoiled fruit.

"You look like you're about to jump."

Becky appeared in the doorway, shoes in one hand, wine in the other. At some point during the evening she'd taken her hair down. The engineered updo had surrendered to gravity and humidity, and the loose curls made her look less like a magazine spread and more like the girl Anya had known since kindergarten.

"I'm fine."

"Mm-hmm." Becky came out barefoot and leaned on the railing beside her. "You're doing the thing."

"I'm not doing a thing."

"You're doing the thing where you go quiet and stare at nothing and say you're fine in a voice that means the opposite. You've been doing it since we were six. I know the thing, Anya."

*She's not fine. Did someone say anything in there? Did Damian say anything? If he did, I'll throw him into a hedge.*

Anya said nothing.

They stood for a while. The party murmured on the other side of the glass. Below them, somewhere in the garden, an irrigation system clicked on and started spraying arcs of water across the dark lawn.

"Good event," Anya said at last.

"It's fine." Becky sipped her wine. "The Riesling situation has been resolved. The shellfish issue has been contained. The Brahms problem is ongoing."

"The Brahms problem?"

"The quartet keeps playing that one piece, the slow sad one that makes everybody stare at their plates like they regret their lives. I asked for uptempo. I specifically said uptempo."

"Did you give them a playlist?"

"I gave them a mood board."

"A mood board. For a string quartet."

"It had clear visual cues."

Anya almost smiled. The ease of it, the old groove of talking to the person who had known her longest, made the next turn worse.

Becky's thoughts shifted. The surface traffic about caterers and wine and quartets dropped away. Under it was the heavier thing she'd been carrying all evening, tucked under performance and timing and hostess energy, waiting for a moment when she could stop being watched.

*Say it. She's the only one I can say it to. If I can't say it to Anya, then who.*

"Can I tell you one real thing?" Becky asked.

"Yeah."

Becky looked down into her wine. The pause lasted long enough for the irrigation system to complete one cycle and begin again.

"I broke up with Marc."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be. He was fine. That was the whole problem." She turned the glass slowly between her palms. "Five months. He came to every event. He stood where I put him. He smiled for photos. He said the right things. And in five months he never once asked me a question he didn't already know the answer to."

*He didn't want me. He wanted the version that gets you into rooms.*

"He asked me about my work once," Becky said. "Early on. I got excited — I started telling him about this documentary series I want to make, artists in the eastern districts, people nobody covers. And he listened for about thirty seconds and then said 'That's great, babe' and changed the subject to the party we were going to that weekend." She drank. "And I just — sat there thinking, oh. Okay. That's what this is."

The sprinkler clicked through another rotation below. Inside, somebody laughed too loudly. A waiter crossed behind the glass with a tray of tiny pastries.

"And Marc's not—" Becky stopped. Started again. "He's not special. That's the disgusting part. Everyone does that. Everyone who gets close." Becky's voice had lost the performance register. It was quieter now, flatter, the voice she used when nobody was filming and nobody needed to be charmed. "They want the guest list. They want the introductions. The table at the right restaurant. Nobody has ever once asked me what I do on a Tuesday when there's nothing on the calendar."

*I watch cooking shows. That's what I do. I eat cereal for dinner and talk to my cat and I don't tell anyone because it ruins the brand.*

She looked down at her wine. "I'm just—" She shook her head. Didn't finish.

She turned toward Anya. Her face was open in a way Anya had seen maybe a dozen times in fourteen years, stripped of charm, performance, and the social armor Becky wore so well most people forgot it was armor at all.

"You're my best friend," Becky said. "You know that, right? You're the only person who actually listens. Not the networking version. Not the I'll-pretend-so-you-invite-me-next-time version. You hear me. The real way."

*She's the only one who sees me. The Tuesday-night-cereal me. I don't know what I'd do without her.*

That landed harder than Damian's recognition.

This one was just Becky.

Since they were six, Becky had been talking to her twice: out loud and in the thoughts under the words. *I like her hair. I want to sit with her every day.* Since then Anya had taken the swallowed panic, the ugly little midnight inventories, the private parts Becky never chose to hand anyone.

Becky thought that was listening.

The truth pushed at Anya's teeth. *I've been hearing the rest since kindergarten.*

She swallowed it. Tell Becky now and every year of the friendship would go bad at once.

"You're mine too," Anya said. "My best friend. You know that."

It was true. It was also the most dishonest sentence she'd said all night.

Relief moved through Becky so plainly Anya could have touched it. Becky leaned her head against Anya's shoulder. Her thoughts quieted, not empty, just settled. A person who had finally said the hard part and been met where she needed to be met.

*Thank God for her.*

Anya stared out at the dark garden. The October air stung her eyes. Inside, the quartet had finally found an upbeat piece. Damian still hovered at the far edge of her range, careful and distant and impossible to unread.

Her pager vibrated again inside the bag at her wrist.

Border channel.

Becky didn't move.

In another minute Anya would have to go back inside, make an excuse, and disappear into secure traffic that would run the rest of the night. For now she kept her shoulder where it was and looked out into the dark until the garden lights blurred.
