# Chapter 16

Damian had to come first. The summit had ended yesterday. Falk was in custody. Loid's unfinished questions were waiting back at the apartment, and Sylvia had not called her in yet, which meant she would. Damian had gone into the summit already knowing her real name, and if Anya left the telepathy hidden one more day then she was just adding fresh rot to old rot.

She stood outside his door for four minutes before she knocked.

Four minutes gave her three neighbors through plaster and pipes: the woman downstairs arguing with herself about whether to call her sister, the man two doors down rewriting a complaint about the hallway lights until the wording got nasty, somebody above coughing wetly into a sink. It was normal apartment stuff. Normal-size brains. Anya put her palm on Damian's door, caught the low hum of him on the other side, and jerked back before the thought shaped itself.

*Not like this. Not before the knock.*

She knocked.

The lock turned after one exact pause. It wasn't surprise. It was a decision.

When the door opened, Damian was wearing a dark sweater with the sleeves shoved up. He looked like he'd been sitting still too long.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

*She's here. Okay.*

That one came through simple and flat, like he'd set it on the table between them.

He moved aside. She went in.

His apartment was neat in the boring, lived-in way. Books lined up straight. One glass drying beside the sink. Policy journals stacked on the side table with a pen clipped to the top one. The couch still had the dent from his usual spot. One shoe lay half under it and the other had been left by the door. Last time she'd been here she read the place like a floor plan, exits and corners and signal strength. Tonight it just looked like where he took off his shoes and forgot about them.

The childhood box sat open on the coffee table.

Old school shoes on top. A class photo underneath.

Anya stopped.

It wasn't because of the photo. That part wasn't new. Since Becky's gala she'd heard the schoolyard flashing through him in pieces: pink hair, Becky crying, a six-year-old punch, the almost-name catching and slipping away. The bad part was the box open right now. He had been in there tonight.

He didn't offer a drink. Neither of them sat.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

*Yeah. I figured.*

His thoughts were already crowding around the wrong guesses. Alliance over. New fallout from the summit. She was leaving the city. None of it close.

"Okay."

He leaned against the counter and crossed his arms. It might have passed for casual if you didn't look at the shoulders.

"It's not about the operation," Anya said. "It's about me. About what I can do."

She had rehearsed this in the bathroom mirror that morning with toothpaste on her lip. On the walk over too. Every version sounded stupid. There was a clinical one, a dramatic one, and a tiny one. *I can kind of hear people.* That one made her want to throw herself into traffic.

"I'm a telepath," she said. "I read minds. I've been doing it since I was little. Four, maybe five, I don't know. I've never told anybody. Not my parents, not Becky, not WISE. Nobody."

Silence hit the room hard enough to feel.

*What.*

Then, a beat later:

*She said she reads minds.*

He went still in that scary tidy way he had when bad news landed. Thoughts started firing past in broken pieces. State dinner. Service corridor. Bretz. The safe house. Every time she'd answered too fast. Every time she'd known something she shouldn't.

*She read my thoughts. All this time.*

"At the state dinner," he said. "That first night. You were reading my thoughts then too."

"Yes."

"Everything in that room. About the operation. About..."

The rest flashed before he said it. Her smile. Eden Academy. A pink-haired girl.

"Yes," Anya said again.

His face changed. Not because of the confession. Because she'd answered the thought.

"Stop doing that." Sharper now. "Can you stop?"

"I can stop reacting. I can't stop hearing it. It's like sound."

*Every thought. God. The negotiations. The club. The park. The safe house.*

His arms uncrossed. Hands went to his pockets, came back out, landed at the back of his neck, dropped again. No place to put them. No place to put any of it.

*The kiss.*

That thought came up jagged and hot.

*The safe house. She was in my head when I kissed her.*

"Yes," Anya said quietly.

Anger arrived all at once. It wasn't loud. That was worse. It locked his jaw and made every word come out too clear.

"So every conversation we've had. Every time I thought I was telling you something because I chose to tell you. You already had it."

"Yes."

"The safe house. My father. My mother. Things I said because I was trying to be honest."

"I already knew."

He looked away fast, like looking at her hurt. Two steps took him to the window. Both hands braced on the sill.

"My mother," he said to the glass. "At the reception."

"The marigolds on the terrace," Anya said. "Before she got sick."

His breath hitched out of him.

*I never said that out loud.*

Outside, lights kept turning on across Berlint like nothing in this room mattered to anybody else.

Anya let him have the anger. There was no excuse to offer, no speech about not choosing the ability, no little pile of true things that would still be useless here.

"How far does it go?" he asked.

"Surface thoughts. What's on top. If you're remembering something, I get that too because you're thinking it right then. But I can't go digging. I can't pull up things you aren't having."

"Just the surface."

*As if the surface isn't the part that counts.*

He wasn't wrong.

"I'm sorry," she said. "And I'm not going to do the thing where I tell you not to be angry. Be angry."

"Don't manage it for me."

"Okay."

*Good.* A beat. *No. Not good. I don't know what I want.*

The next wave came through messier. It wasn't clean anger now. It was sick realization. Every ugly little private thought handed over without consent. Every soft one too.

Then, under all of it:

*She knows the bad parts and she still came here.*

Anya kept her face still. For once she let the thought stay where it belonged, even though she'd heard it.

"I don't know what to do with this," Damian said. His eyes were red around the edges. "I genuinely don't know what to do with the idea that every private thought I've had in front of you was..."

He stopped.

His gaze had dropped to the coffee table. To the box. To the photo half hidden under the shoes.

*Tell her.*

The thought came through clean as a bell.

He crossed the room, pulled out the class photo, and held it up.

"Second row," he said. "Third from the left. Pink hair. That's you."

Anya looked.

Six years old. Mouth open in a grin at something off camera. One hand blurred from waving. Damian in the front row already looking annoyed at the photographer, tiny and severe in exactly the way he'd been born to be.

"I know who you are," he said. "Anya Forger. Eden Academy, Class Three. You punched me when we were six because I called Becky a crybaby. I've known since the gala."

The shock wasn't *who*. It was *when*.

It was November, this apartment, the photo under his lamp, months stacked behind his voice.

"The gala," she said. "I knew you were close then. I heard that part. I didn't know you went home and made it certain."

"A classmate said your name. I came back here, found this, checked. It was you."

*She caught the circling. Not this room. Not the part where it became a fact.*

That hit harder than the school photo. His desk lamp. This table. His hands on the picture. She hadn't been here.

"At my parents' apartment," Anya said. "When you met my dad. At the summit yesterday. All that time."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"Because the operation still mattered. And because..." He stopped, the correction visible before it happened. Jaw tight. Eyes on the photo instead of her. "Because I didn't want to lose you."

*Both. But mostly that second thing.*

Air went out of Anya in one rough push. Her knees gave up before the rest of her did. She sat on the floor beside the table because that was where her body had decided she belonged.

"So we both did it," she said. "Hidden information. Kept going."

Damian lowered himself onto the couch instead of staying over her. That was good. The distance helped.

"Not the same thing," he said.

"No. What I did is worse."

"Maybe."

"No. It is."

He didn't answer. The photo stayed in his hands. Two children stared up from glossy paper while the adults who used to be them failed to say anything useful.

---

The second hour went quieter.

Water had appeared at some point. Her glass sat untouched and sweating on the table. Damian was back in the dent on the couch. Anya had climbed up to the far cushion with her knees tucked under her chin. The box stayed open between them.

The anger was still there. She could feel it the way you feel a bruise through clothes. Not every second, but enough.

*She told me. Nobody dragged it out of her.*

"Can I ask something?" he said.

"Yeah."

"When you read someone, do you just get words?"

"Not just words. Mostly words, or scraps of words. But feelings come with them when they're strong. Not sentences. More like pressure. Weather. If somebody's furious, the room goes sharp. If somebody's scared, it's loud."

"And mine?"

There it was.

Anya pressed her thumbnail into the seam of the couch cushion.

"This is the part I came here to say and I still don't know how to say it right." A breath. "In the safe house. When we kissed. I was reading you — I was trying not to but you were too close, you were feeling so much, and I just." She stopped. "I could feel what you wanted. Not guess. Feel it. And my body answered before I'd figured out whether I was answering you or me."

He didn't move.

"Afterward you went to wash the blood off your hands. I stayed on the floor and kept shaking even after you left the room. That part's real. The part after is where I don't — where it gets." She pressed her thumbnail harder. "I don't know how much of what I feel for you started as yours. Your feelings hitting me so hard I just — carried them home."

"Meaning what."

"Meaning maybe I wanted you because you wanted me and the signal was loud enough that I couldn't tell which one lit first." Her mouth had gone dry. "That's not — I'm not saying none of it's mine. I'm saying I can't sort it. And I'm scared of that."

Nothing in his face changed, but his thoughts tightened.

*Not the telepathy. This.*

"You think you might have kissed me because I wanted it badly enough for both of us."

"Maybe. Or maybe I wanted it and hearing you at full volume made it impossible to tell which part lit first. I don't know. That's the problem."

Silence came back, thicker now.

"So you don't know," he said. "If any of it was yours."

"Yeah."

"And if you can't tell, I definitely can't."

"Yeah."

That hurt him more than some of the earlier things had. Anya could hear it. Also see it, in the way his hand closed over the glass and then let go before it cracked.

"I've never had this with anybody before," she said. "I've never let anybody get close enough for it to matter like this. Strong feelings leak through. That's always been true. Crowds are hell because of it. But this..." She shook her head. "This is me guessing from one concrete mess and being afraid of the rest."

He looked at her then. Not forgiving. Not anything that easy.

"Do you think any of it is yours?"

"I think some of it is." The answer came out too fast. She made herself do the harder part. "I also think wanting that to be true is not the same as knowing."

*God.*

He looked down again.

"Since you were a kid," he said after a while. "You've been living like that since you were a kid."

"Yeah."

"And nobody knew."

"No."

Thoughts moved through him slowly then. Four years old. A classroom. A train car. No quiet anywhere. He wasn't seeing her memories. He was building bad little pictures from the facts she'd given him, and the loneliness in them came through anyway.

Sadness pressed behind Anya's eyes. Maybe it was hers. Maybe it was his. Maybe that was the whole stupid point.

"I'm sorry," she said. "Not for telling you. For the months before."

"I know."

"That's not enough."

"I know that too."

His hands scrubbed over his face. When they came down he looked older and more tired and much more like a twenty-year-old who had run out of good answers.

"I need time," he said.

"Okay."

"I don't mean I'm done. I mean I need to think about this somewhere you can't hear me doing it."

That should have been absurd enough to break the room. It almost did. A tiny near-smile touched his mouth and vanished.

"Yeah," Anya said. "I get it."

"I'll call you when I'm ready."

Anya stood. Her water glass was still full. The class photo lay face-up on the table now, both of them trapped forever at six.

She got one arm into her coat and stopped. There was one more thing. She had to say it before the hallway took over and turned everything flat.

"The reason I told you — it wasn't to fix this, or make you stay, that's not." She stopped. Tried again, plainer. "I couldn't keep knowing everything you think while you didn't know I could hear it. You deserved to decide this with the real — with all of it out. Even if you decide no."

*A real choice.*

Damian stood too. Four feet between them again. Same room, different air.

He went very still, like he was trying not to pack the thought in anything diplomatic before letting it out.

"Anya, wait."

"Yeah."

"I'm glad you told me."

*I'm glad you told me.*

Her hand stalled on the other sleeve. She had to look at the chain lock instead of his face.

He opened the door.

The hallway was flat fluorescent light. Somebody downstairs had finally called her sister after all. The building kept being a building.

They did not touch.

Anya wanted to. Damian wanted to. Both wants hung there and stayed wants.

"Goodnight," she said.

"Goodnight."

Outside, the city came back all at once: dinner plans, sore feet, rent, a teenager worrying about his jacket, a cab driver thinking about soup. Damian stayed at the far edge of her range for half a block, warm and blurred and then less, then gone.

She could have leaned back toward it and listened harder.

She didn't.

For once, the first thing was enough.
