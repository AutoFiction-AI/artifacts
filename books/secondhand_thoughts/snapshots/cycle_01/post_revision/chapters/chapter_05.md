# Chapter 5

Anya kept the Gesellschaft Club roster open across her knees as Damian brought the car in along the curb. Soap still clung to his collar over whatever cedar thing he'd put on and then tried to wash off before picking her up.

*She can probably smell that. It doesn't matter. Focus.*

She kept her eyes on the roster. Getting it had cost him something. She could tell from the way his thoughts kept catching and restarting. *Bretz? Over the phone? Absolutely not.* *Why do you need that list, Desmond?* *Forget it.* He had cut the calls off before curiosity turned into a report. He had not wanted to bring her. He had wanted that even less than he wanted to burn another favor.

Which was why she was here.

If the lead was real, it was moving too fast to hand off. If she was lying to him about something fundamental, better to keep her where he could see her.

"Bretz arrives around eight," Damian said, turning a page with the care of someone handling something more serious than a seating chart. "Cognac in the salon, dinner at nine, never stays past eleven. Tuesday is his procurement review, so if he's wound up about anything, it'll be tonight."

"And this couldn't wait until you found someone you actually trust."

His jaw tightened. "No."

That was at least honest.

"His wife is Margarethe," he went on. "She likes arranging people. If she decides you're decorative, you'll spend the night getting handed from one conversation to another. If she decides you're attached, you stay where she put you."

"So we go in attached."

"We go in as a couple because that's the only version of this room that gets close to Bretz without setting off alarms." He kept his eyes on the windshield. "A young man alone looks ambitious. A young woman alone looks suspicious. A couple looks boring. I want boring."

Anya glanced at the club. Stone facade. Iron balconies. Tall curtained windows lit from inside. The place looked older than every government that had used it.

"I still think we should approach separately," she said. "You work the room. I find Bretz. We converge when I've got a read on his mood."

"No." He said it quietly enough that she almost missed the edge in it. "If this goes wrong, I need to know where you are."

She looked at him.

He adjusted his cuffs, too precise about it. "Operationally."

*Mostly operationally.*

Not flattering. Fair.

"Fine," she said. "Then let's say the ugly part out loud."

"The cover."

"The cover."

"We're together."

"That is what a couple is, yes."

His thoughts lost formation immediately. *Normal. This is normal. People do this every day. They hold hands. They sit close. It isn't a procedure.*

"Damian."

He looked at her.

"You're doing the thing with your teeth again."

"I'm not."

He was. He stopped.

"You don't trust me," Anya said. "You trust the clock less. That's what this is."

He let out a breath. "Yes."

There it was. Clean enough to stand on.

"Good," she said. "I hate mystery in cars."

That got the smallest flicker at the corner of his mouth.

"One more time," he said. "We enter together. I make the introductions. We stay near Bretz, not on him. If Margarethe peels me away, you keep him talking. If he shuts down, we walk. We do not push hard enough that he remembers us tomorrow."

"I won't push." She turned another page. "If he has anything, it'll be on the surface. Men like Bretz don't hide irritation well."

His thoughts snagged on that sentence. *How would you know that with this much certainty?*

She kept her eyes on the roster.

"If it goes bad," he said, "west exit for you, front for me. No acknowledgment."

"It won't go bad."

"That isn't a plan."

"It is if you believe in me."

"I don't."

"Wow."

"I believe you're competent," he said. "That's different."

That, annoyingly, was probably the nicest true thing he could have said.

He gathered the folder, opened his door, then came around the car to open hers. Cold air swept in with the smell of damp leaves and frying oil from somewhere down the block.

*Open the door. Offer your arm. Normal. Don't make it strange.*

She took his arm.

*Oh, for God's sake.*

They walked toward the club.

***

The Gesellschaft Club's entrance hall was polished wood, marble, and portraits of dead men in uniforms, all wearing the same satisfied expression. A chandelier threw hard white light over the ledger on the doorman's stand.

Damian changed the second they crossed the threshold. The tension in him sealed up and the public version stepped forward, warm, easy, faintly amused at the world and perfectly calibrated for it.

"Desmond," he said. "And guest."

The doorman found the name, lifted his eyes once toward Anya, then waved them through.

The reception occupied a long salon crowded with candles and bad art. Thirty or forty guests stood in clusters around cocktail tables and the bar. Their thoughts slid over her in scraps: heating oil, a bad hip, a parking ticket, an affair, dinner running late, the fish last month, whether anyone had noticed the inheritance fight was back on.

Manageable. No state-dinner pressure. No wall of noise. Just a roomful of self-important strangers and one target.

She found Bretz near a display of harbor scenes. He had cognac in one hand and a dog at his side big enough to block a doorway.

"That's a very big dog," Damian murmured.

"That's a military installation."

Bretz himself looked like he'd been hard once and now lived with pain too long to hide it. His thoughts were not about politics. *Hip again. Sit down now and she'll say I told you so all the way home.*

He was, immediately, the most comprehensible person in the room.

"Colonel Bretz," Damian said, stopping at the exact distance that read respectful rather than eager. "Damian Desmond. We met at Eastbridge last spring."

Bretz turned. *Desmond. Younger one. Foreign Affairs. Father was... no.* "You didn't eat the fish."

"The fish was ambitious."

Bretz gave a single rough laugh and shifted his weight to save his hip. "Who's your friend?"

"My girlfriend," Damian said.

The word scrambled his thoughts at once. *Girlfriend. Fine. Cover. Nobody died.*

Anya smiled. "Elena."

"Club's decent," Bretz said. "Cognac's better than the conversation."

The dog noticed Anya then. His head turned. A second later the rest of him followed.

It pressed its nose into her palm.

*Oh. Hello.*

"Kaiser likes you," Bretz said, and it was the first unguarded warmth in his voice. "He doesn't like most people."

Kaiser leaned into her leg. His thoughts came through simple and pleased. *Warm. Good. Stay here.*

"He's beautiful," Anya said, because saying *your dog has adopted me* felt unwise.

She scratched behind his ears. His eyes closed. His tail thumped the side table.

"Careful," Bretz said. "He broke a lamp in the card room last month and the committee still acts like it was sabotage."

"Damian!" a woman called from across the salon.

Anya did not need the file to know who that had to be. Margarethe Bretz arrived in emerald silk, moving like refusal had never once slowed her.

"Damian Desmond, I knew that was you." She seized his arm before he had agreed to any of this. "You look exactly like that actor. What is his name. The one from the picture about the diplomat and the actress."

*No. Who is this. Smile. Leave politely.*

"Friedrich Engel," Margarethe said to herself a second later. "That's it."

"It's an honor to resemble someone with that much hair," Damian said smoothly.

Margarethe laughed, delighted. "Come meet the Hasslers. Ernst has been saying the ministry needs more young men with shoulders."

"His shoulders are fine," Bretz said into his cognac.

"Come along."

She had Damian moving before he found a polite excuse. He looked back once.

*Sorry. I'll get loose. Give me a minute.*

Anya watched him disappear into a knot of well-dressed strangers. Then she looked at Bretz. Then at Kaiser, who had planted himself against her leg with devotional certainty.

"Your wife is a lot," she said.

Bretz drank. "That's the charitable version."

His surface thoughts followed the same line. *Opera singer in September. Painter in October. Boy'll be stuck there till she's done with him.* Then, softer under it: *She likes this part. Always did.*

Anya let herself lean against the wall beside him, casual, almost sheltered by the dog.

"Do you collect these?" she asked, nodding at the watercolors.

"God, no. Koenig paints them. We all pretend they're respectable because his wife left him." Bretz shifted again, pain flashing hot and mean through his hip. "I've got a few decent things at home. Feldmann. Some landscapes."

"I love Feldmann."

This was not true. She had looked him up in the car and remembered one cow.

Bretz gave her a flat look that suggested he did not believe her for a second. His thoughts moved right past art and landed on something else. *Tuesday. Procurement. Hartig wants the numbers before committee.*

There.

"My father used to say the army wasn't run by generals," Anya said. "It was run by the men with clean cuffs and rubber stamps."

"Your father was right." Bretz scratched Kaiser's shoulder. "At this point I spend more time on procurement paperwork than I ever spent near a firing range."

His mind skipped, not in a speech, just in irritated fragments. *Krueger-Schiff. Four hundred thousand. Secondary channel. Again.*

Anya kept her voice light. "That sounds worse than war."

"War at least has the decency to be honest."

Kaiser chose that moment to dive for her handbag.

It happened fast enough that she only got one hand on the strap before the compact was gone. The dog backed away with it in his jaws, pleased with himself.

"Kaiser. No."

Anya lunged. Kaiser dodged and trotted toward the bar with the compact.

Two women turned to watch. A bartender froze mid-polish.

Bretz bent to intercept the dog and pain tore through him so sharply she felt it before she heard the thought. *Christ. Hip.*

He caught Kaiser by the collar, pried the compact loose, and straightened with one hand braced at his lower back. A folded cream memorandum slipped halfway out of his inner pocket with the handkerchief he dragged free.

Defense Ministry crest.

A typed header from the visible page beneath it: Office of the Undersecretary.

And lower down, before he shoved it back into place: Strategic National Interest.

"I apologize," Bretz said, offering the compact back at arm's length. "He's usually difficult on a smaller scale."

"It's fine," Anya said. The compact was warm and damp. "He's got taste."

Bretz barked out a real laugh. His guard loosened with it. *All right. Desmond's girl isn't useless.*

Kaiser pressed himself against her again, serene and shameless.

Bretz tucked the memo deeper into his jacket with visible annoyance. *Hartig and his slogans. Strategic national interest. Sign here. Don't ask.*

Anya did not look at the pocket. "Bad phrase?"

He snorted. "It's the kind of phrase bureaucrats use when they want you to do something ugly and feel patriotic about it."

His thoughts flashed another broken set of pieces at her. *Tuesday. Secondary authorization. Four hundred thousand. Discretionary.*

She kept him there.

"And you still have to sign."

Bretz looked at her, sharper now.

For a second she thought she'd stepped too close. Then his face settled into the expression of a man too tired to bother lying well.

"At my age," he said, "I sign a lot of things I don't respect."

His hand flattened once over his breast pocket. *Six more years. Sign it. Keep the pension. Get home before the leg locks.*

That was the truth of him. Not villainy. Not courage either. Just a man who had practiced endurance until it turned into obedience.

Kaiser put a paw on Anya's shoe.

"You're standing on me," she told him.

The dog looked up at her. *Correct.*

Across the room Damian's thoughts cut through the crowd. *Horse digestion. Still horse digestion. This is punishment.*

Anya bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to keep her face straight.

"You all right?" Bretz asked.

"Fine. Your dog thinks I'm furniture."

"He thinks that about very few people. You should take it as an honor."

Margarethe's voice rose again somewhere near the fern. Damian made his escape three minutes later and appeared at Anya's elbow looking subtly looser around the tie and slightly stunned behind the eyes.

*Forty-five minutes. Oats. Temperament. He had charts.*

Kaiser sniffed him once, dismissed him, and leaned harder into Anya.

"We should go," she murmured. "I've got enough."

His attention sharpened at once. "You do."

"I do."

He searched her face a second longer than necessary. *How. Every time, how.* Then he nodded. "All right. Normal pace."

They said their goodbyes. Damian thanked Bretz, lied politely about the watercolors, and let Margarethe invite them back as if it might happen. Bretz shook his hand. Kaiser watched Anya all the way to the door.

*Come back,* the dog thought, simple and devastating.

She did not look back because she had already stolen enough from one household for a night.

The cold hit them on the club steps.

"What did you get?" Damian asked, keeping his voice low as they crossed toward the street.

"Tuesday review. Krueger-Schiff allocation. Four hundred thousand." She kept walking. "Bretz is worried about Hartig pushing it through a secondary channel before committee review. He had a Defense Ministry memo in his pocket from the undersecretary's office. Same phrase kept looping. Strategic national interest."

Damian stopped for half a beat, then started again.

"Hartig," he said. "Erich Hartig?"

"That's the surname I saw. Bretz never thought it cleanly enough for more than that."

Damian's thoughts locked onto the pieces and started arranging them. *Erich Hartig. Procurement. Secondary authorization. Discretionary money. Not sloppy. Built.*

"Erich Hartig," he said out loud now. "Undersecretary for Defense Procurement."

So there it was. Not handed to her whole. Built between them in the cold.

"Bretz also hates Feldmann and resents his brother-in-law," Anya said. "In case that helps."

It startled a breath out of him that almost counted as a laugh.

"Did Bretz tell you any of this," he asked, "or did you just stand there while his internal filing cabinet fell open."

"He told me his dog has excellent instincts." She tucked her hands into her coat pockets. "The rest I had to work for."

That answer did not satisfy him. It was not supposed to. But it was true enough to leave standing.

"Hartig reports to someone," he said.

"Bretz thinks so."

"Yeah." Damian was already elsewhere, mentally, tracing lines she could not see. "Nobody builds a pipe like that for personal amusement."

"This gives us a place to start."

"It does."

They crossed the street toward the park instead of the car without discussing it.

***

The bench sat under a linden tree gone gold with autumn. The gravel below it was slick with leaves. Anya had taken off the borrowed heels and tucked her feet up under the bench for a minute of relief. Damian had loosened his tie and undone the top button of his shirt, which on him looked almost reckless.

For a while they just sat there with the name between them.

"If Hartig is the mechanism," Damian said at last, "then the timing is worse than I thought."

"Because of the money."

"Because of the speed." He rubbed a hand over his mouth. "Discretionary funds mean no one waits for committee unless they feel like being polite."

"People planning something small don't build extra channels."

"No."

A leaf landed on the bench between them and stayed there.

Then Damian said, "The horse man had charts."

Anya turned to look at him.

"Charts," he repeated. "On oats. He had brought them in his pocket. I think he thought this might happen and prepared."

She snorted.

"Margarethe also invited me to dinner next Thursday so her niece could assess my bone structure."

"Did you accept?"

"I said I'd check my schedule. Which I think, in diplomatic language, means I plan to die before then."

That one got her. The laugh came out before she could manage it, loose and too loud in the cold, the real one with the hitch at the end.

Damian went still.

Not abstractly still. Not privately. He turned toward her with the look of someone who had just heard a floorboard move in an empty house.

His thoughts came in flashes instead of a scene. *Linden tree. East wall. Chalk on his socks. Pink hair. No. That relay.*

And then, before he could stop himself, he said, "Did you go to Eden?"

The question hit her low and hard.

"What?"

He blinked like he had surprised himself first. "When you laughed. I remembered somebody."

Not somebody. A girl in a schoolyard. A branch over the field. Chalk on his socks. Four steps. The fury first, then the spark underneath it.

She could feel the shape of the memory in him without getting the whole thing. That was almost worse.

"No," she said too quickly. Then had to correct. "Not Eden. Why?"

Bad evasion. She knew it while saying it.

His gaze stayed on her a moment longer. *That sounded rehearsed. Or scared. Or both.*

"There was a girl," he said slowly. "At school. She used to laugh like that."

"A lot of girls laugh."

"Not like that."

She looked away toward the path.

"She beat me in a relay race once," he said, and now the memory was working on him from the outside as much as the inside, reshaping his face as he spoke. "I was furious about it for, honestly, an unreasonable amount of time."

Anya could see it because she had been there. The chalk on his socks. The righteous outrage. The way he'd spent the rest of the afternoon pretending not to care while caring with his entire small body.

"Sounds like she was right to beat you," she said.

That finally made him laugh, a short breath that loosened something in both of them.

"Probably."

He looked down at her shoes by the bench. "You're lying about something."

There it was. Not accusation exactly. Just quiet fact.

She could have asked which thing. She did not.

"You already knew that," she said.

"Yeah."

Neither of them moved.

The city hummed beyond the trees. A tram bell carried across the dark.

He should have pressed. She could feel the question he wanted and did not trust himself to ask. *Were you there. Was it you. Why does this keep happening.*

Instead he said, "Still a good operation."

"Chaotic operation."

"Those can be the same thing."

"That's a terrible philosophy."

"It's gotten me this far."

"You're twenty."

"Cruel."

She smiled despite herself.

For one brief stretch the pressure eased. Not gone. Just set down beside them with the shoes and the leaves and the name of the man moving money through the ministry.

*I could tell him,* she thought. Not everything. Not the impossible part. Just the tree. The relay race. The fact that he had not imagined that girl.

She did not.

"We should go," she said. "My feet are freezing."

"Yeah."

He stood and held out his hand. She took it. His fingers were warm.

*Her hand is ice cold.*

She pulled her shoes back on and they walked to the car without talking. They had Hartig's name now. Her laughter was still in the air between them, or maybe that was only the shape of his memory making a place for itself. Either way, the fuse was shorter now.

And this time it had spoken out loud.
