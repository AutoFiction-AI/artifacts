# Chapter 4

The deputy minister was on tariff reform for the third time when Anya stopped pretending the forum might improve. The conference room on the third floor of the Foreign Affairs Ministry was too hot, the windows were sweating grey, and most of the audience had already given up on listening.

*...salmon looked better before they put the dill on it...*

*...if Dreher says "framework" again I'm leaving...*

*...Marta will kill me if I don't text before six...*

Then one thought cut straight through the mush.

*...security meeting again. Not on the invite list. Who cleared Desmond for that?*

Damian was near the front, just left of the podium, speaking to two ministry men in the careful public tone he used in government rooms. Dark suit. Straight back. One hand loose at his side, the other around a folder he probably did not need. His thoughts, when she brushed them, were neat and annoyingly disciplined.

*...border checkpoint issue, keep it procedural...*

*...pick up dry cleaning before Friday...*

The suspicious thought was not his. It belonged to the blond man beside him, tall and thinning and dressed one salary bracket above what his badge suggested he should manage.

*...naval attaché, depot questions, procurement office... not random. Pattern.*

Anya shifted a step to the right until she could read the badge when he turned.

BRANDT, K. Ministry of the Interior, Policy Liaison.

Brandt was still looking at Damian when the next fragment hit.

*...Gessler tonight. He'll want the Desmond boy looked at.*

Gessler meant nothing to her, but Brandt had laid the name right beside depot questions, procurement offices, and Damian's face. He was not gossiping in his own head. He was stacking details for somebody above him. Maybe Renewal Front. Maybe one floor out from it. Either way, if that report went upstairs tonight, Damian's life got smaller.

She set her conference packet on the sill and started across the room. No extra minute to think. If anybody ever lined up the badges she had worn for Damian over the last two weeks, it would look absurd. Tonight's said ANNA KESSLER. Last week she had let him get away with just Anya. The state dinner had been a different card for a different room. That was the job. It still made her skin itch.

If Brandt checked the guest list later, Anna Kessler would be on it, and Anna Kessler had every right to be annoying in public.

She reached them just as Damian finished saying something bland about bilateral coordination.

"Mr. Desmond, hello again." She offered the bright mission smile. "Anna Kessler, formally. I think we only did first names last week."

Damian took her hand. "Ms. Kessler. Right. We were less formal last time."

*Anna now. Last week Anya. Fine. Public room.*

Brandt looked from one to the other.

"I don't think we've met," Anya said, turning before he could ask the useful question. "Anna Kessler. Westalian mission."

"Brandt."

He gave her a thin handshake and kept studying Damian over her shoulder.

*...Westalian staff. Approached him directly. Maybe routine. Maybe not.*

"I was actually hoping to steal someone from the Interior Ministry for thirty seconds," Anya said. "Our office keeps getting tangled up in Velden freight numbers. The trade-corridor ones, not anything exciting. Is that your mess or somebody else's?"

She used Velden because Brandt had just thought it, and because if the word sounded boring enough in her mouth maybe it stopped sounding dangerous in Damian's.

Brandt's expression barely moved.

*...Velden. Coincidence? Public freight data, private audit. Could still be coincidence.*

"Commerce handles the numbers," he said.

"Of course. Sorry. We got bounced three times already and now everyone keeps saying methodology at me like that fixes anything. I just need to know whose office hates me."

Damian was looking at her with perfect diplomatic patience and a mind that had gone sharper by the second.

*...she's doing this on purpose.*

Anya kept going before Brandt could leave. Freight counts. Committee delays. Whether rerouted cargo got counted twice if it crossed the corridor and came back. None of it was clever, just sticky and dull. She kept feeding him freight loops because every answer pinned him there another few seconds.

Brandt tried twice to step away. She caught him with one more question each time.

*...Jesus. Still talking.*

When he finally escaped, it was with the strained courtesy of a man leaving a conversation he already planned to complain about later.

*...Dreher first. Desmond after. Pull the access logs when I'm back upstairs.*

Can wait. Not gone. Just pushed.

She turned back to Damian. His public smile had vanished.

"Ms. Kessler," he said. "You seem to have developed very strong opinions about freight counts."

"I'm adaptable."

*...not just a cultural attaché. Not even close.*

"There is a service corridor behind the east stairwell," he said softly. "Five minutes."

He walked off without waiting for her answer. By the time she reached the door, he was already back by the podium, one shoulder angled toward a deputy minister, face composed again.

Five minutes.

---

The corridor behind the east stairwell was narrow, lit by one buzzing fluorescent strip, and smelled like cleanser and old metal. Pipes ran along the ceiling. A mop bucket sat against the far wall with brown water in it that should have been emptied hours ago.

Damian was already there. His tie was loose at the throat. His hands were in his pockets, which with him meant he wanted them somewhere else.

*...don't start angry. Figure her out first.*

"So," he said.

"So."

The strip hummed overhead. Somewhere in the wall, a pipe knocked twice.

"You're not just a cultural attaché."

"No."

His expression sharpened.

"I am a cultural attaché," she said. "That's just not all."

His eyes narrowed.

*...Westalian service. WISE? Some other desk using the mission as cover?*

"Brandt," he said. "How did you clock him?"

"I got close enough."

"To what?"

"To him. To the room. I don't know, I..." She cut herself off, hard. "He was building a file on you."

"That isn't an answer."

"No," she said. "It's the useful part."

She pushed off the wall.

"He was taking your meetings apart in his head. Naval attaché. Depot questions. Procurement access. He was going to take it to someone named Gessler tonight."

Damian went still enough to make the pipes sound loud.

*...Gessler.*

At Gessler, one shoulder dropped a fraction before he locked it back in place. Bad name. Real name.

"You're being watched," she said. "Maybe you've known that for a while. Fine. Brandt has enough to make it worse."

"I'm not under discussion with you," Damian said automatically.

Then, after a beat: "No. That's not true."

His jaw tightened.

*...either she's on the same trail or she's running me.*

"I'm not running you," Anya said.

His head came up fast.

There. Too quick again.

"I mean I'm not doing what Brandt was doing," she said. "I came over because he was about to make your week much worse."

"You have a remarkable amount of confidence for someone who still hasn't answered the actual question."

"I work in intelligence," she said. "I can't give you more than that right now. But I know Brandt wasn't worried about trade policy."

Damian looked at the mop bucket, then the pipe beside it, then back at her.

"I don't use the name you're about to say," he said.

"Renewal Front."

His mouth thinned.

"Fine," he said. "Them."

The next part cost him. She could see that before she heard anything at all.

"My father left people behind," he said. "Not loyalists, exactly. Habits. Favors. Old channels nobody bothered to shut down because the name still opened the right doors. Some of the people inside that mess want a border incident badly enough to build one."

"And you're trying to stop them."

"I'm trying to stop a business model," Damian said. "Nationalism is just the sales pitch. Contracts. Procurement. Panic. People here make money off it. People across the border do too. If the temperature rises, everybody involved gets fed."

No ministry gloss this time. Just contracts, procurement, panic, and the people eating well off all three.

"So yes," he said. "I'm trying to stop them."

"Okay."

He let out a breath that was almost a laugh and not friendly.

"That is not the part that should be easy for you."

"It isn't." She touched two fingers to the side of her head, then dropped her hand before it turned into a tell she could feel happening. "The easy part is knowing Brandt is a problem. The hard part is standing here without saying too much."

He watched that closely.

*...too much how. Too much from where.*

"What do you want?" he asked.

"To keep Brandt from landing on you again. To stop them. Same as you."

"You keep saying that as if we're already on the same side."

"We're in the same corridor," she said. "That's not the same thing."

For the first time since she walked in, the set of his mouth loosened. He still didn't trust her. He'd at least heard the offer in the right language.

*...Father let a Westalian inside once. Three dead. Don't be stupid.*

The warning hit her like something he'd heard so many times it had sunk into the bone.

"If this is recruitment," he said, "say so."

"It isn't." She paused. "Or it is, badly. I don't know. I'm trying to make a limited offer."

"Limited how."

"Shared intelligence on the Front. Names. Dates. Meetings. Movement if we have it. Not sources."

"My people stay mine."

"Mine too."

"No questions about networks."

"Fine."

"And if one of us decides the other is lying?"

"Then we stop." She held his eyes. "No penalties. No dragging each other down on the way out. It stops."

He said nothing.

The ugly part was how easy it was to keep pace with him. His doubt would harden, and she would wait. It would ease a fraction, and she would step forward again. She knew exactly how much pressure the moment would bear because she could hear it moving under his face.

*...recruitment. Leverage. Maybe both.*

"Regular channels only," Damian said at last. "If this happens."

"Meaning?"

"The cultural exchange calendar. Invitations. RSVPs. Little useless diplomatic notes. If Brandt is already looking, we do not invent a new trail for him."

"Okay."

"Shared intelligence on the Front," he said. "No digging into each other's people. Either one of us can end it."

"Yes."

He was quiet again, reading her the old-fashioned way now.

"How did you know about Brandt?" he asked. "The truth this time."

There it was.

"My side has been hearing his name around the same people you're watching," she said. The edges were true. The middle was a lie. "When he started leaning on you, I moved."

Damian held her gaze.

*...too fast. Still doesn't account for Gessler.*

He knew she was lying. Enough seams showed for him to keep the answer at arm's length.

"Okay," he said.

He stacked that answer with the rest of his doubts.

*...work with her. Don't trust her.*

He straightened his tie with two quick motions and put the public version of himself back on.

"I'll contact you through the exchange calendar."

"I check it."

That almost got a real smile out of him.

"You really do work for the cultural office, don't you."

"I do," she said. "That's part of the problem."

He left first.

Anya stayed where she was and counted to one hundred and twenty with her eyes on the mop bucket. Around sixty, a fine stupid tremble started in her fingers, the one that always showed up after she had held too many lies in place.

Brandt. Damian. Her own mouth. One face for the room, another for the corridor, and a third for the thing she still could not say out loud.

Damian had just agreed to work with her without knowing the worst part of the deal.

That was bad enough. The other bad thing was simpler. The alliance already had a clock on it.

When she went back into the forum, the room was somehow even hotter. Damian was near the podium again, listening with his usual blank diplomatic attention. He did not look at her. Good.

Brandt did.

He was three rows back now, bent over a notebook.

*...Kessler. Westalian mission. Check the calendar. Check Desmond.*

WISE had no idea she had just made herself part of Damian's file.

Anya picked up a warm glass of sparkling water from a passing tray and took a swallow because her mouth had gone dry. Across the room Brandt wrote one more line. Damian kept his face turned toward the podium. When the deputy minister started in on tariff schedules again, Brandt had a calendar to check and Gessler had a second name to pull.
