# Chapter 10

The milk was going to be a problem.

Yor held the carton at an angle under the kitchen light and read the date stamp. Tomorrow. Back into the refrigerator it went, behind the leftover stew, where Loid was less likely to discover it. He had opinions about expiration dates that she considered disproportionate to the actual science of dairy spoilage, but she'd never found a way to say that without sounding like she was defending spoiled milk, which she wasn't. What she was defending was the principle that milk didn't become poison at midnight on the printed date. She was fairly sure about this.

Bond was on the living room rug, occupying approximately one-third of the available floor space with the unselfconscious entitlement of a very large, very old dog who had decided that this particular section of rug was his and always had been. His breathing was slow and even, and his legs twitched with whatever passed through a precognitive dog's sleeping mind. Yor sometimes caught Anya watching Bond with an expression that suggested she was listening to something Yor couldn't hear, but the habit was so old and so gentle that Yor had filed it years ago under the broad category of things about her daughter that she loved without understanding.

A bento box sat open on the counter. This was not strictly necessary. Loid was at the university for a consulting session, one of the occasional appointments that maintained a cover identity that had long since stopped feeling like one, and he wouldn't be back until evening. But Yor liked making them. The routine was grounding. Rice balls, three, with pickled plum. Tamagoyaki, two slices, trimmed where the browning had become something closer to charring. A thermos of mushroom miso soup from a recipe she'd clipped from a magazine in the city hall break room.

The soup tasted too salty. Not catastrophically, but enough that she could hear Anya's polite silence if she ever served it, the careful way Anya said "It's good, Mama" when what she meant was *This is mostly salt*.

The bento went into the refrigerator with a note: *The soup is maybe a little salty. Sorry! Please don't feel like you have to drink it. But I think it's okay? Yor*

As she dotted the question mark, her phone buzzed.

Not a ring. A single pulse against the countertop, the kind that came from numbers that didn't exist in any directory. She set down the pen and picked up the phone.

An address on the northern outskirts. A name: Karl Dominic. A timeframe: tonight. And the six-digit code at the bottom that confirmed it was real.

Twice was enough. The message would erase itself in ninety seconds. She set the phone face-down on the counter and went back to the bento box, adjusting the thermos so it wouldn't leak during refrigeration. Once the lid was closed, the knife she'd used for the tamagoyaki got washed, dried, and returned to the block.

Bond opened one eye from the rug, then closed it.

"I'm going out tonight," Yor told him. "Don't eat Papa's dinner. It's in the fridge."

After a shower, she dressed in a fitted dark jacket, pants that didn't restrict movement at the hip, and flat shoes with grip soles she'd ordered from a work-supply catalogue under the reasoning that icy sidewalks were dangerous and practical footwear was just common sense. Which was true. The shoes were also excellent for moving quietly across concrete and gravel, which was the actual reason she'd bought them.

In the closet, behind the shoe rack and a box of seasonal clothes she rotated every April and October with a conscientiousness that Loid found endearing and Anya found baffling, sat a locked case. The key lived on a chain around her neck alongside a small enamel peanut charm, a prize from an Eden Academy festival booth, won by a six-year-old Anya and presented with the solemnity of a military decoration. The charm's paint was chipping at the edges, and she'd never replaced it.

Inside the case: two blades, a garrote wire, a compact medical kit, and lockpicks that had been Garden standard issue since before Anya was born. From it she selected the longer blade and the picks. The garrote she left. It was a single target in a compound setting. The blade was cleaner, faster, something she could sheathe when the job was done. The garrote required sustained proximity she didn't enjoy. That wasn't out of squeamishness. It was because the garrote was slow, and slow meant time spent close enough to hear the sound the body made, and that was a sound she preferred to keep brief.

With the blade strapped to the inside of her left forearm, under the jacket sleeve, Yor checked the draw. The motion was smooth. The lockpicks went into her inner pocket.

In the hallway mirror, she looked like a woman heading out for the evening. It was a bit severe. She tried a small smile, the kind she gave the reception desk at city hall when she arrived for her shift. It softened things.

On her way out, she stopped at the hall table where Loid had left a shopping list in his careful handwriting: rice, laundry soap, Bond's joint medication, milk. That reminder had been on her list all day. He would buy a new carton, find the old one hiding behind the stew, pour it out, and give her the expression. Not disapproval exactly. More the gentle bewilderment of a man who had spent decades in intelligence operations and could not fathom his wife's relationship with dairy.

At the bottom she added *milk, please check fridge first!* in her rounder, larger handwriting, and beneath it: *Back late. Don't wait up? There's stew.*

The door locked behind her. The landing smelled of the neighbor's floor wax and the building's old radiators kicking against the October chill.

Then she went to kill Karl Dominic.

---

The compound was forty minutes north of Berlint proper, where the city's residential blocks thinned into industrial lots and gated estates belonging to people who valued privacy over proximity. Yor took the tram to the northern terminal and walked the remaining two kilometers along a service road paralleling a drainage canal. The air smelled of wet concrete, diesel from a freight depot she'd passed a kilometer back, and the faintly vegetal rot of standing water in the ditch.

The landscape changed as she walked. Berlint's streetlights gave way to intermittent industrial security lamps, and the sounds of the city, tram bells, traffic, a bar's music leaking from somewhere, faded into the low drone of freight machinery and then, past the last depot, into nothing much at all. The service road was unpaved beyond the terminal's reach: packed dirt and gravel that crunched under her soles. Past a shuttered machine shop came a fenced lot full of construction aggregate, then a drainage pump station where someone had left a folding chair and an empty thermos. The only other living thing she'd encountered in the last kilometer was a cat that crossed the road, evaluated her with the professional indifference of an animal that knew it couldn't be caught, and vanished into a culvert pipe.

On the tram she'd read the briefing, a file downloaded to her phone and programmed to delete after thirty minutes. Karl Dominic. Fifty-three. Arms broker. His supply chain had been linked to the recent military buildup along the Ostanian border: weapons transfers routed through shell companies, the kind of infrastructure that made wars possible by making weapons available. The Garden's assessment classified him as a destabilizing agent. Eliminate. Standard contract.

Standard. For twenty years, that word had been a foundation she could stand on. The Garden assigned targets. The assignment was hers to carry out. The reasons existed at a level above her operational role, and she'd trusted them because the alternative, questioning every target, demanding justification for every life she ended, would have made the work impossible. Trust was the price of function. She'd paid it willingly.

So had the shipping clerk. Three months ago. A man in the port district whose briefing profile listed arms connections that turned out, when she'd checked afterward, to be invoices for industrial machine parts. Legitimate equipment for a legitimate factory. The man had begged, not for himself, which she could manage, but for his daughter, who was waiting at home. They sometimes begged. She'd trained herself to close that channel years ago. But the invoices. The gap between the profile and the evidence. She'd reported the discrepancy to her handler. The handler had said: *Thank you, Yor. Don't worry about it. These things are reviewed.* And she'd stopped worrying.

Mostly, she'd stopped worrying.

Half past ten found her at the compound. The perimeter was a three-meter concrete wall topped with razor wire and motion sensors at the corners. Beyond it: the roofline of the main building, interior lights, the muted geometry of an estate that had been a manufacturing facility before Dominic repurposed it. Sealed loading docks on the east side. The main gate faced south, connected to the highway by a paved access road.

Surveillance settled into place. Three men visible: one stationed at the gate, shifting his weight foot to foot with the restlessness of a man assigned a post he found boring; one near a cargo van parked in the loading area, its rear doors open; one walking the interior perimeter on a patrol route. She timed the patrol. Four minutes per circuit. Consistent rhythm. The pace of routine rather than alertness.

The north wall had a vulnerability. A motion sensor housing mounted at an angle that covered the airspace above the wall rather than the wall face itself, a factory installation never corrected. The kind of error that accumulated in security systems maintained by people who checked boxes rather than tested coverage.

When the patrol rounded the south wall, she went up the north wall at the sensor gap and dropped into the compound's northeast corner behind a maintenance shed. The shed smelled of machine oil and dried grass clippings. Thirty still seconds passed while the patrol guard's boots crunched by on the other side, steady and unhurried, the steps of a man completing a task he'd completed a thousand times without incident.

The crunch faded toward the south. She moved.

The man at the cargo van was leaning against the rear bumper, smoking. His sidearm sat on his right hip, holstered, the retention snap undone. It was structural carelessness. He'd never encountered trouble here, and the boredom of guard work had eroded whatever discipline he'd once maintained. Yor came around the van's blind side. The last three meters took less than a second. She put the blade through the base of his skull at the junction where the brainstem lived, and he dropped. She caught his weight against the van's bumper and lowered him to the pavement without noise. His cigarette rolled under the tire.

Inside the van: long wooden crates, unmarked, secured with military-grade packaging straps. The chemical smell of preservative oil seeped through the wood grain. Rifles. She counted twelve crates. Enough for a company.

From there she moved toward the main building.

The loading dock's sealed entrance had a secondary door, service access, a standard pin-tumbler lock that the picks handled in nine seconds. A corridor inside, fluorescent tubes overhead, half of them dead and the survivors flickering with a buzz that set her teeth on edge. An office door on the left, closed. Voices from deeper in the building, male, three or four of them, the measured rhythm of negotiation. The private sale. Buyers in the building.

That changed the approach. Buyers meant witnesses and potentially more armed men. She needed the principal and the exit, nothing else.

Two guards in the corridor, stationed outside a conference room. The first noticed her at six meters and reached for his radio. Wrong decision. Yor closed the gap in a stride and drove the heel of her palm into his trachea. He folded with a wet, shocked gasp. The second guard was faster. He'd drawn his sidearm by the time she reached him. But his partner was between them, and the half-second he spent calculating a clear firing line was a half-second he didn't have. Her blade opened the inside of his forearm from wrist to elbow, deep enough to ruin the arm, and the gun clattered to the floor tiles. An elbow below his ear dropped him after that.

The first guard's radio came with her. The channel stayed quiet. No alarm followed.

The corridor branched. Left toward the offices. Right toward the voices. She went left. The buyers were not her contract.

Karl Dominic was alone in a corner office at the corridor's end, behind a desk buried in working stacks of paperwork, speaking into a phone. He was shorter than his file photo suggested, stocky, balding, in a dress shirt with the sleeves pushed past his elbows. The office smelled of cigar smoke layered over cheap instant coffee, the kind sold in bulk at freight depot commissaries. A framed photograph on the desk showed Dominic with a woman on a boat, somewhere sunny, both of them squinting at the camera with the particular resignation of people who'd been told to smile.

He looked up when the door opened.

"No," he said into the phone, already irritated, already mid-argument. "I want the confirmations in front of me before they touch the second shipment. If Voss wants deniability, he can pay for it twice."

Then his face processed confusion, recognition, fear.

He dropped the phone and lunged for the desk drawer.

Yor was faster. She had been faster than every person she'd ever been sent to kill, and twenty years had not changed that. The speed lived below thought, below decision, in the architecture of muscle and nerve that the Garden had trained and she had maintained through thousands of hours of practice she told Loid was yoga. Her blade caught his reaching wrist before his hand found the drawer, and the cut was deep enough to part tendons. He screamed, a short, bitten-off sound, the kind that comes from someone whose body has overridden the preference for silence.

He tried to stand. She put the blade through the left side of his neck, angled for the carotid, and held it for the two seconds it took. His legs buckled. He went down against the desk, scattering papers, and slid to the floor trailing a red line down the laminate front.

The phone was still connected. A tinny voice on the other end asked, "Karl? Karl, what happened?"

Yor disconnected the call.

Wiping the blade on the hem of Dominic's shirt, she sheathed it. Her hands were steady. They were always steady during the work, the part of her body she trusted most, the part that didn't shake or hesitate or feel the weight of what it had done until afterward, when the adrenaline metabolized and the steadiness left and she was just Yor again, standing in a kitchen, cutting vegetables, and her hands would tremble for a moment over the cutting board and she'd tell herself it was the cold.

The papers on the desk were organized in working stacks, not abandoned clutter. Tonight's crates. Tonight's buyers. Tonight's payments.

Garden training had been explicit on this point. The Garden provided the briefing; the operative executed the contract. Investigation was a different role, different people, a different level of the organization. For twenty years, that division had been enough.

But the shipping clerk. The invoices that hadn't matched. The handler's voice: *Don't worry about it.* And the daughter, waiting at home.

Yor looked at the papers scattered across Dominic's desk and the ones that had fallen to the floor, some of them soaking up the blood pooling from his neck, and she didn't leave.

The top stack held shipping manifests and transfer orders for military hardware: rifles, communications equipment, explosive ordnance, all routed through holding companies whose names meant nothing but whose addresses landed in Berlint's government district, near the defense ministry. The shipment codes repeated. So did a name. Werner Voss.

Every page went into her phone.

One order held her attention longer than the rest. Encrypted field communications equipment, military grade, shipped six weeks ago to a receiving address she recognized: not the defense ministry directly but a warehouse in the adjacent district, the kind of anonymous drop point that existed to keep deliveries off official books. The quantities suggested equipping a field unit, not filling a depot. An active operation, being assembled through Karl Dominic's desk.

Under the manifests sat a second packet, clipped to the first on heavier cream stock. Those were Dominic's confirmations.

The radio in her hand hissed. Someone at the gate asked when Karl was ready for signatures. A chair scraped in the conference room down the hall. Yor lifted the second packet anyway.

These were not freight receipts or customs clearances. They were completion records, the sort of back-office paper that existed so a man like Dominic could prove a liability had been handled and demand payment for it. One look told her why they felt familiar. The forms used the same sequence as the Garden's own closeout notices: assignment number, compensation release, executing party.

The contracting entity was coded. She didn't recognize the designator. The executing entity, on nine separate jobs over four years, appeared under a numerical code Yor knew as well as her own address. She'd seen it on her payment records for twenty years, on the deposits that arrived on the fifteenth of each month, on the brief confirmations after each completed assignment, on the secure documents she signed when she accepted new contracts.

The Garden.

Her hands stopped being steady.

Faster now, she read. Nine assignments. Four years. Targets identified by code, not name, but the function of the work was legible from the notes attached to each completion: witness management, channel protection, logistics interference, liability removal. Loose ends inside a weapons pipeline. Not threats to national stability. Problems for Karl Dominic and whoever stood above him.

One entry carried a date from three months ago and a district marker from the port. She knew that one. She remembered the small office, the machine-parts invoices, the man who had started begging only when he understood she wasn't there to threaten him. He had said his daughter was waiting at home.

The shipping clerk had not been an exception. Or if he had been, this dead man had eight others filed beside him.

Karl Dominic had not hidden a confession in a lucky drawer. He had been working through tonight's sale with the paperwork laid out exactly as he needed it, shipment on top and cleanup underneath, because men like him believed paper was insurance.

Yor photographed every page in the second packet. Her phone screen was bright in the dim office. Dominic's blood had reached her shoes, a dark tongue extending from under the desk, and she stepped back the way she'd step away from a puddle in the street. Automatic. Her body doing the practical thing while her mind tried to hold a shape it couldn't yet see the edges of.

Twenty years. The number kept arriving without rhetoric. For twenty years the Garden had said *this person is dangerous* and Yor had arranged her hands around the sentence until it became something she could live inside. Protection. Shelter. The people she loved sleeping safely in rooms paid for with other people's blood.

Now an arms broker had kept proof that the Garden did cleanup for his supply chain in the same neat stacks as freight paperwork.

What if the shipping clerk had not been a mistake. What if the mistake was believing he had been the only one.

No clear next step followed that thought. That was the part that frightened her. She had always known what came next.

The phone went back into her pocket. After a glance at the corridor, she moved back through the loading dock. The guards in the hallway were still down. The gate guard remained at his post, facing south, out of sightline. The buyers' muffled negotiation still carried through the building's interior walls, the deal proceeding as if nothing had changed inside the corner office at the end of the corridor.

Back over the north wall she went, using the same sensor gap, the same route. Her left hand caught the razor wire on the descent. She felt the resistance and the tug but not the pain. The pain arrived thirty seconds later on the service road, a line of hot sting across her palm that resolved into a shallow cut running along the old scar from when Anya was eight and learning to cut carrots and the knife had slipped from her small grip and caught Yor's hand instead. Anya had cried for twenty minutes. Yor hadn't minded the cut. She'd held her bleeding palm behind her back so Anya wouldn't feel bad and said, "See? It's nothing. Let's try again."

With the cut wrapped in her jacket sleeve, she kept walking.

---

Twelve meters from the compound wall, where the service road curved toward the canal, movement broke beside the industrial waste containers lining the drainage ditch.

A figure rose. One shoulder lower under a light pack. Weight on the back foot. Small enough to be underestimated.

Yor's mind measured the distance, the angle, the quickest path to the throat.

Then the figure stepped into the spill of security light and it was Anya.

Yor stopped. Her daughter was in dark clothes, not the diplomatic wardrobe, not the outfits of a cultural attaché at Westalian embassy events, but the kind of clothes Yor herself was wearing. Practical. Unreflective. Made not to be noticed. Anya's hair was pulled back under a dark knit cap. Six paces separated them.

Anya looked too thin. She always looked thin. Yor worried about this the way she worried about the big things, not the milk or the soup, which were small worries that had solutions, but the kind of worry that lived in her chest and didn't go away because Anya was twenty and an adult and you couldn't make an adult eat.

Neither of them spoke for a moment. The canal water moved in the ditch. The compound's lights threw a pale halo against the low October clouds.

"You should eat more," Yor said. "You look thin."

Anya's mouth opened. Closed. Her eyes dropped to Yor's left hand, the jacket sleeve dark and stiff with blood, the makeshift wrapping already soaking through.

"Your hands are bleeding, Mama."

"Oh." Yor looked at her hand as if the blood were a social inconvenience, like noticing a stain on her shirt at a work dinner. "It's, um. It's not bad. I scraped it on a fence. It's really not deep. I just didn't have a, um. I'll clean it when I get home."

Anya didn't ask what fence. She didn't ask why Yor was here, what was inside the compound, what she'd done. Yor didn't ask any of the equivalent questions, why her daughter was crouched behind waste containers at an arms broker's estate, what intelligence she was following, what professional capacity took her so far past cultural attaché that the cover had become its own quiet joke. Those questions stayed where they always stayed, between them, large and untouched.

"I need to get back," Yor said, gesturing toward the road to the tram station. "It's late."

"Yeah." Anya nodded. "I'm going the other way."

"Right. Of course."

A silence sat down between them. The canal gurgled. Something shifted behind the compound wall, a light switching on, a distant door.

"Be careful getting home," Yor said. "It's cold tonight. You have a proper coat? Not just that one?"

"I'm fine, Mama."

"There's stew in the fridge. From yesterday. If you want to stop by. You don't have to. But it's there."

"Okay."

"And the soup. The mushroom one. It's new. It might be... I think I used too much miso. But it's fine. Probably. It's an experiment."

"I'll try it."

Yor wanted to cross the six paces between them. She wanted to put a hand on the back of Anya's head and say something ordinary enough to get them safely through the night. Instead she kept both hands to herself. One was bleeding. The other had held the phone with the photographs on it.

"Goodnight, Anya."

"Night, Mama."

Yor turned toward the tram road. Looking back felt impossible.

By the time she reached the bend, the domestic thoughts were trying to reassemble themselves: stew in the fridge, soup too salty, whether Anya had lied about the coat. Under them sat the other thing, flat and impossible. Nine contracts. Four years. The Garden's code on Dominic's papers.

Toward home she kept walking because it was the only next step she had.
