# Chapter 9

Across the street, the thought hit hard and clean, nothing like the smear of evening commuter noise. *Left side, low approach, maintain visual on target.*

For twenty minutes, Anya had kept Damian a block and a half ahead after he left the Foreign Affairs Ministry. The building behind him was mostly dark, late-meeting dark, with only a few office windows still lit. She'd told herself the tail was protocol, just an operational partner in an unsecured environment. Partly true. The rest was simpler and worse: three days after catching *the asset inside WISE* at the reception, she couldn't leave his movements alone.

The schedule was not clean.

At a newsstand on the corner, she lifted a copy of the *Berlinter Abendblatt* toward the streetlight and opened her range.

Two minds. Disciplined. Surface thoughts stayed operational, not personal. The first: *Distance closing. He'll turn at the next block. Move to intercept position.* The second, farther back: *Package is unaware. Clean approach.*

Package. Not target. Not subject. Package. Military language, not SSS, not police.

The vendor grumbled about exact change. Coins hit the counter without being counted, and Anya was already moving.

A hundred meters ahead, Damian walked with the unhurried drag of somebody still half inside a budget meeting. The edges of his thoughts came through anyway: figures from the session, a line he wished he'd pushed harder on an undersecretary, the headache of three hours spent with committee procedure. He had no idea.

Thirty meters behind him, the first attacker closed the gap. The second had crossed the street and was angling toward Schultzgasse, a narrow side street with bad lighting and locked residential doors. If Damian turned left the way he usually did, they'd take him in the dark stretch between the lamps.

No name. Not her cover name for him, not the real one. Anything she shouted within earshot of two assailants became information they could use.

She ran.

Wet leaves made the cobblestones slick. Her flats skidded once and caught. Between a parked delivery truck and a taxi, she cut into the street and kept reading as she moved. One hand was already inside the first man's jacket. The second man had a metal tube half out of his pocket. Baton, most likely. Maybe a blade.

Damian turned left onto Schultzgasse.

*Now,* the first attacker thought. *Go.*

They came in from both ends. Anya hit the corner in time to see the shape of it: Damian in the middle, one man accelerating behind him, the other stepping out of a recessed doorway ahead. Coordinated. Professional. Rehearsed.

"Damian!"

He turned. The grab caught his shoulder instead of his neck, and that difference probably kept him alive. Damian stumbled into the wall. The second man snapped a telescoping baton open and came in fast.

Her elbow drove into his kidney, exactly where Yor had taught her to aim. Find the floating rib. Commit. Follow through. Yor would've taken the arm out of the socket after that. Anya wasn't Yor. He wrenched free, swung backhanded, and the baton cracked into her left side. White pain flashed across her vision and emptied her lungs in one violent rush.

His next thought came bright as a flare. *Step back. Reset. Take the throat.* She ducked the follow-up swing, drove her shoulder into his diaphragm, and took him down onto the wet stones with her. Forty kilos on her, maybe more. In half a second he was rolling, pinning her leg with his hip, hand reaching for her neck.

Damian smashed the other attacker across the head with his briefcase. It was a wild swing, no technique at all, but it landed. The man staggered. Damian hit him again. Leather split. Papers burst into the street and lifted in the cold air like startled birds before rain slapped them flat. Then the attacker caught Damian's arm and buried an elbow above his left eye. Skin opened. Blood sheeted down immediately.

*Knee.* Not a plan. Just the part of her brain that wanted him off. Her heel came down hard on the second attacker's kneecap. Cartilage or ligament gave with a wet crunch she felt through her whole leg. He screamed, rolled clear, and Anya scrambled up with one arm locked over her ribs. Standing all the way straight was out of the question.

"Get up," she said. "Move. Now."

The first man was already recalculating, circling back with that steady professional gait that meant the briefcase hit had only delayed him. His intent came through in one cold strip: *woman first. trained. man second.* Six seconds and he'd tagged her as the threat. That said enough about who they were dealing with.

Damian knelt in his own blood, one hand at his brow, the broken briefcase handle still clenched in the other. Anya hauled him upright. Behind them, one attacker was shaking his head clear. The other kept trying to stand on a leg that wouldn't take weight. Then the first thought flashed again. *Abort. Regroup. Secondary extraction.* She didn't wait to parse the rest. She dragged Damian toward the main street and ran.

He kept up. Blood ran into his left eye, and he wiped it away with his sleeve without breaking stride. Speech didn't arrive until two blocks later, when bright residential lamps replaced the bad dark of Schultzgasse and late pedestrians were still out with dogs and grocery bags.

A building wall caught Anya between the shoulder blades. She leaned on it and tried to breathe past the grinding pain under her ribs. A woman with a dachshund crossed the street rather than pass them, her thoughts spinning on repeat. *Blood. He's bleeding. Call someone. No, keep walking. Not your business.* Every inhale hurt in the same stripe. Not broken, probably. Badly bruised, definitely.

"What the hell," Damian said, then broke off to breathe. Blood kept tracking down from the split above his eye and onto his collar. "What the hell just happened?"

Precision. That was the first ugly part. They had known which meeting, what time he would leave, which route he'd take. The session had been closed, ministry staff only. No public calendar. No open schedule.

*The asset inside WISE.*

"I need to get you somewhere safe," she said.

---

They walked west for fifteen minutes.

A handkerchief appeared from Damian's coat pocket with the kind of automatic propriety that came from being raised in a house where pressed linen existed for every possible contingency, including ambush on a Tuesday. Anya kept one arm locked over her left side and her range open. Every mind coming toward them got checked. A woman thinking about her cat. A man drafting an angry text to a supervisor and deleting each version before he reached the next corner. A teenager listening to music loud enough that the bass leaked from his headphones and into his thoughts at the same time. Normal Berlint. Normal evening. Except for the two of them, moving too fast and leaving blood behind them in small, invisible increments.

The fallback apartment sat in a residential block in the west district, far from the diplomatic quarter and farther from government buildings. Anya had memorized the address during her first week in Berlint and had never used it. The site belonged to an old emergency tier that field agents learned on paper and analysts never saw unless someone activated it. Until that happened, it stayed dark.

The front door opened with a six-digit code she had to enter twice because her fingers wouldn't stop shaking. Third floor. No elevator. By the time they reached the apartment, each step sent a hot pulse from her ribs down into her hip.

Bare walls. Couch frame with a thin mattress instead of cushions. Kitchen counter, sink, dust. Bathroom. Closet. In the closet: a first-aid kit, two changes of anonymous clothes, a prepaid phone, a handgun she left where it was. No heat. No smell except stale air and old plaster. Through the uncurtained window, another building's television flickered blue-grey across the back courtyard.

Damian sat on the edge of the mattress and pressed the heel of his hand to his eyebrow. Blood seeped between his fingers. Combat-speed thoughts still ran under his skin. *Who were they. How did they know. Ministry calendar was internal. Was I watched earlier. Is she hurt worse than she's showing. Her hands are shaking. She's trying to hold them still and they are shaking.*

They were. She noticed when he did.

The first-aid kit had what it always had: gauze, saline, antiseptic wipes, butterfly strips, ibuprofen in foil. At the sink, her hands went under dish-soap-thin water that ran rust for two seconds and then cleared. The kit came back to the mattress with her.

"Let me see."

He moved his hand.

Two centimeters, maybe a little less. Deep enough to keep bleeding, not deep enough for stitches if she could get the edges to hold. Clean split. Precise hit.

Saline first. Gauze. He flinched at the antiseptic and stayed still anyway. By the time the second butterfly strip went on, the cut had stopped bleeding hard and settled into an angry seep at the margins. Warm skin under her fingers. Warmth in the cold apartment. His breath catching when it stung. None of that needed further analysis and none of it stayed out of her head.

"Your turn," he said.

"I'm fine."

"No, you're not."

"It's bruised."

"You're holding yourself like breathing is a bad idea."

That almost made her laugh. "Breathing is a bad idea."

There was no clean way to inspect bruised ribs without lifting her shirt, so she lifted it. A dark bar had already started to bloom across the left side of her torso, red at the edges, going purple by morning. Fingertips moved along the line of it. Nothing shifted. No grinding. No sharp instability. Just pain.

Damian looked once and then looked away too late. His thought got there before his face did. *That hit was meant for me.* Hair pushed back from his forehead with a blood-smeared hand. The rest of the thought broke itself off before it turned into apology.

"Ibuprofen," Anya said.

He passed the foil packet over. Three pills went down with tap water, rust first, then cold. By the time she sat again, the room had found its after-midnight noises. Pipes. Building settle. Next door, a sportscaster calling a football match with too much feeling. Under that, the neighbor's thoughts: table money on the second half, regret already forming.

"How did you know?" Damian asked.

Not quite a question. More like a problem set down between them. Blood dried on his fingers while he stared at them. *She was there before it started. Already in position. How does anyone do that unless--*

"I'm good at reading a bad street."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one you're getting."

He looked up. The calculation ran in real time behind his face: press and risk the alliance, or let the lie stand and keep tonight intact. She watched the decision happen. He let it go.

"Okay," he said. A beat later he added, quieter, "Thank you. For all of it."

"Yeah."

Silence settled again, but not empty this time. Adrenaline was leaving in stages, like drainwater taking forever to clear a clogged sink. Cold came in behind it. So did exhaustion. So did the sudden vicious desire for home, for Yor's catastrophic cooking, for Bond on the rug, for Loid asking the wrong question in exactly the right tone.

"They knew your schedule," Anya said.

"I know."

"Walk me through it."

His mouth tightened. "The route wasn't even settled until the end of the meeting. There was a traffic protest on Lindenplatz, so security pushed us out the west side instead. Revised note went out at eighteen twelve."

"To who."

"My office. Ministry security. Driver pool." He pressed the clean side of the handkerchief to his brow and thought past the pain. "And the Westalian liaison mailbox we use for movement alerts."

Anya sat up straighter despite what it did to her ribs. "The standard one?"

"WS-seven. Why."

Not all of WISE, then. Not every analyst with access to a database. Whoever had put men on Schultzgasse needed either the ministry revision or the liaison traffic that mirrored it. A corridor. Still too wide. Smaller than everything. Her false report had already taught WISE to read Damian through the wrong lens. If the ambush had moved through a mailbox tied to his movements, then her lie was somewhere in that chain whether she wanted it there or not.

"Pen," she said.

He blinked. "What."

"Do you have one."

A diplomat who left home without a pen probably got disowned. Damian reached into his inside coat pocket and handed one over without comment. Anya pulled the folded instruction sheet out of the first-aid kit and wrote on the blank side: `18:12 / west exit revision / WS-7`. The letters looked jagged. Good enough.

"What is that?" he asked.

"The first useful thing we've got."

A crease formed between his brows. "You think it came through that mailbox."

"I think somebody with access to your revision moved fast enough to put two trained men in the dark before you got there. That narrows the field."

"To what."

"Not enough." The paper got folded twice and pushed into her coat pocket. "But it's not nothing."

His eyes tracked to the closet. To the prepaid phone sitting on the shelf inside. "Can't you call this in?"

Anya got up, crossed to the closet, and picked the phone up anyway. Cheap plastic. Still wrapped in the kind of institutional dust that settled on objects nobody used. The battery was clipped in but not seated.

"One call on this activates the apartment," she said. "Duty officer logs the address, dispatch logs the time, somebody opens a file, and everybody with emergency access can see that this location is hot."

"And if your leak is inside emergency access."

"Then I hand them you alive and tell them where I hid you."

That landed. Damian sat very still for a second, handkerchief pressed to his brow, blood drying brown at the edge of it.

"Then don't call," he said.

The battery stayed in her hand. Practical. Immediate. Infuriating, because he was right and because she had known it before he said it.

"If I keep this place dark," Anya said, "it stays outside the active grid. No medic. No extraction team. No report until I can move you somewhere else and send it from a line I trust."

"I said don't call."

"You don't get to order me around in my own safe house."

"Fine," he said, with the politeness he used when he was too tired to hide irritation. "I'm bleeding on your mattress and respectfully suggesting you don't notify the mole."

That almost did make her laugh.

The phone went back on the shelf. Battery beside it, not in it.

"First light," she said. "I move you first. Then I report."

He nodded once. Choice made. Cost attached.

For a while neither of them said much. The room felt different after the decision, tighter but clearer, as if refusing the call had given the danger edges instead of letting it spread everywhere at once. In the closet, the dead phone sat useless. In her pocket, the note pressed against her thigh.

Damian looked around the apartment again. Bare bulb. Rust-edged faucet. Mattress without sheets. A whole emergency life reduced to the minimum number of objects. *This is what safe means for her.* No pity in it. No disgust. Just the plain, startled recognition that the cold room was not an abstraction.

"Eden Academy had this thing," he said.

The turn was abrupt enough that Anya almost asked him to repeat it.

"After the dodgeball tournament. Losing team had to clean the gym. Floors, chairs, all of it." One finger touched the butterfly strips as if checking whether the wound was still there. "My team lost because a girl punched me."

Anya stared at him. "During dodgeball."

"During dodgeball."

"With her fist."

"With her actual fist. I was eight. It was humiliating."

A laugh threatened. She held it back. Barely.

"I'd said something about her hair," Damian went on. "I still can't remember the line, which is probably good for me, but I remember the punch exactly. Shoulder, not face. Very considerate."

Bubblegum. He had called it bubblegum. Full confidence, zero survival instinct. Her knuckles had hurt for an hour afterward and the detention had been worth every second.

"And then there was the school festival cleanup." His eyes stayed half-closed, voice drifting with exhaustion. "Poster paints everywhere. Blue all over the floor. There was this girl on the cleanup crew laughing so hard she kept sliding when she tried to mop." The thought underneath arrived less neatly. *Pink hair. Blue shoes. Same laugh. Same tilt of the head when--*

He opened his eyes fully and looked at her.

"You ever go to Eden?" he asked.

It came out quiet, almost casual. The thought behind it wasn't casual at all. *Say no. Say yes. Tell me why I know that laugh.*

Anya's stomach dropped. "No."

Too fast. He heard it. So did she.

*That's not right. Or I've got a concussion and I'm building a whole childhood ghost out of a laugh and a bad light.*

"You remind me of someone," he said.

"You've mentioned that."

"I know." Pain flared behind his eyes and broke the memory apart again before it could settle. He rubbed at his brow with the heel of his hand and exhaled through his nose. "Sorry. Getting hit in the head has apparently made me nostalgic."

That did it. The laugh got out.

Not the diplomatic one. Not the polished, cover-friendly version she used at receptions. The real one came up too loud for the room, sharp with exhaustion and relief and the simple fact that she was sitting in a freezing safe house beside a bleeding diplomat who had just confessed to losing a dodgeball match because he insulted a girl's hair.

Damian blinked at her and then started laughing too. Surprise first. Then surrender. Gauze went back to his eyebrow mid-laugh because the strips pulled when his face moved, which only made it worse. Next door, the sportscaster went into a goal call at exactly the wrong time and their laughter broke open again. Pain stabbed through Anya's ribs with every breath and she didn't care enough to stop.

Laughter wound down the way it actually did, in smaller and smaller aftershocks until all that was left was hard breathing and the cold apartment around them.

"I haven't done that in a while," Damian said.

"Same."

The tactical churn in his thoughts had finally burned itself out. What came through now was quieter and much more dangerous. The thin mattress. The cold room. The handkerchief in his lap. The woman sitting close enough that if he moved one hand-width, he could touch her. Not the operative. Not the ally. Her. The way she'd gone between him and the baton without waiting to think.

*I don't want to go home.*

---

Nobody was coming.

No medic. No handler. No second pair of hands. She'd chosen that with the battery in her palm and the phone still dark in the closet. The apartment held only the bare bulb, the distant sportscaster, their blood, their breathing, and the note in her pocket that meant the mole now had a shape even if it didn't have a face.

The silence between them changed anyway.

It wasn't the silence of people who had run out of language. It was the one that arrived when both people knew exactly what was about to happen and were still giving each other time to stop it.

Damian's surface was wide open. Exhaustion and pain had stripped him down to almost nothing. At a reception he could keep half a dozen thoughts moving in parallel and never let one show. Here the current ran clear. *She's right there. If I touch her and she moves away, fine. If she doesn't--*

Anya should have shut her range down. Same as she should have made the call. Same as she should have told him the truth when he asked about Eden. That entire list of correct decisions stood off to one side and watched her ignore it.

"Damian."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry about your eyebrow."

"It's fine." His fingertips found the strips. "I'll look rugged."

"You'll look like you got elbowed in the face."

A crooked mouth, almost a smile. "Ruggedly elbowed."

That was enough.

The safe house light did nobody any favors. It showed the split in his brow, the blood dried at his jaw, the hair he'd shoved back too many times because he needed his hands to do anything except shake. It showed the same thing in reverse, probably: bruised ribs, shirt tugged down in a hurry, mouth still swollen from laughing and breathing too hard. Twenty years old. Nearly killed. Alone together in a room too cold to mistake for comfort.

He leaned in. Anya stayed where she was.

Careful at first. Mouth to mouth. Question in the pressure of it. His fingers touched the side of her face, cold from the room. Copper lingered where blood had gotten to his lip earlier. Under that there was only him, no diplomatic polish, no operational language, no cover story strong enough to stand between them and what was happening.

She kissed him back.

Everything professional went vague at once. Alliance terms. Risk assessments. The negotiated fence around whatever this had been until now. His other hand came to her hip and stayed light for half a second, giving her room to pull away. She didn't. Her hand found the back of his neck. Pulse, fast. A single startled *oh* from him, and then the hesitation was gone.

Trying not to read his mind worked exactly as well as trying not to hear a radio in the next room.

Fragments hit first. *Real. Her mouth. Don't stop. Want--* Under that, the layer he wasn't steering at all. Loneliness, not curated, not social, not the version a man could package into a self-deprecating anecdote. The structural kind. Dinner for one in an apartment still half boxed up. Meetings spent performing beliefs he hated. A surname heavy enough to bend a life around it. Under the loneliness came another thing, smaller and more dangerous. Hope. Stubborn. Defensive. The hope that she was real, that this was real, that he hadn't built the whole connection out of bad timing and a need he didn't like examining.

All of it went through her in one rush. Not earned. Not given. Taken because she was built wrong for fairness.

He pulled her closer. The mattress shifted. Her fingers tightened in his hair. His thoughts lost syntax and broke down into pure sensation, heat and want and the raw relief of being answered.

Then another image surged up from under the rest of it.

A class photo. Second row. A girl grinning at somebody off-camera instead of the lens. Pink hair clipped back. Soap water on a gym floor. Blue paint on canvas shoes. The memory sharpened hard enough to hurt. Language reached for it and missed.

*That girl. Eden. That--*

Pain spiked behind his wounded brow and the thought broke apart before it could lock.

Anya heard even that much. Heard how close it was. Heard the recognition pushing at the edge of form and failing. Kept kissing him anyway.

That was the worst part. Not the telepathy itself. Not the old guilt, though that was in the room too, as sour and familiar as stomach acid. The worst part was that she knew the exchange wasn't even and wanted it badly enough to keep going. His private hope, his private hunger, the unguarded trust of a person kissing her because he thought he was arriving at her cleanly. Every second of it sat in her hands and none of it had been freely offered.

He drew back just far enough to breathe. His forehead rested against hers. One hand stayed spread between her shoulder blades, warm even through the fabric of her shirt. The other still held her at the hip.

"I don't know what this does to the operation," he said.

"I don't either."

A breath of laughter touched her mouth. "Probably nothing good."

"Probably not."

He didn't move away. Neither did she. For a few seconds the only thing that existed was the fact of his hand between her shoulders and the way both of them were still breathing like they'd run to get here.

Eventually he leaned back against the wall. Contact thinned but didn't break. Their knees still touched. Her shoulder stayed against his. His fingers, without seeming to decide it, caught in the fabric at her sleeve and remained there.

Exhaustion hit him fast after that. Adrenaline had been doing impossible work and now it was done. The eyelids got heavy. The line of his mouth softened. Thought slowed, then drifted.

What came back as he slid toward sleep wasn't a name. It was the same cluster of memory, over and over. Pink hair in the gym lights. Wet floor. A laugh too loud for the room. A girl turning her head before the class photo snapped. Each pass came closer to language and then slid off again.

Anya stayed very still.

The bare bulb buzzed. His blood had dried brown at the edges of her nails from the butterfly strips. In her pocket, the folded note pressed against her thigh: `18:12 / west exit revision / WS-7`. The prepaid phone sat in the closet with its battery beside it, both of them waiting for morning. Dawn would be for moving him, reporting from somewhere cleaner, and starting with the one corridor she'd managed to narrow.

For now he slept in brief, uneven drifts with his shoulder against hers and his fingers still slack in her sleeve. The missing name kept rising in him and disappearing before it formed. The ache under her ribs might have been only the baton bruise. It might not. She had no clean way to separate what belonged to her from what she'd taken. She stayed anyway.
