# Chapter 11

After the canal, Anya spent three days inside WISE headquarters, and by now the fluorescent buzz felt lodged behind her eyes.

Not from the reception itself. Crowds gave her the bright-light headache and then let go. This was three days of precision work: engineered elevator rides, timed coffee runs, standing beside colleagues with a dead phone in her hand while their thoughts scraped past hers in fragments. The most invasive thing she had ever done on purpose.

Yor kept breaking through it anyway. Dominic's records. Garden's code in Dominic's paperwork. The crack that had opened in her mother's head on the service road. But Anya had nothing she could carry into a briefing folder, no way to ask Yor what she'd found without admitting she'd stolen it out of her mind, and no reason to think Yor would tell anyone before she tried to think her way back into obedience. That was the bet Anya had made when she turned away instead of following Yor home. Maybe Yor would go quiet for a few days. Maybe, in those same few days, Garden would clean the trail and Yor would let them. Damian's warning about the WISE leak felt shorter than that. So Anya came to headquarters and stayed.

In her fourth-floor cubicle, coffee went cold beside an access-log spreadsheet. The fluorescents buzzed. Reheated fish from the break room was making the whole floor smell like punishment. Outside, two analysts argued about sourcing methodology while one of them worried about his dead dog and the other about an overdue credit-card bill.

Reading WISE all day got her the same junk everybody else carried around: errands, resentment, money, lunch, whether anyone had replaced the toilet paper on the third floor. Intelligence came in bursts. Between bursts, life kept being stupid and ordinary.

It had started with a single fragment from the diplomatic reception: *the asset inside WISE*. A mole, somewhere in a crowd too dense to pin to one skull. She had tried replaying the room afterward, flat on her bed, but telepathy wasn't a recording. It happened once and then it was memory like any other, full of holes.

So she'd started the other way. Paper first, the way Loid had taught her since she was sixteen: before you use the cheat, do the work. Access logs. Intercepts. Briefing attendance cross-referenced against the specific intelligence the Renewal Front kept anticipating. Grosz's information, the Front's military-civilian structure and defense-contract channels, narrowed it further. If somebody inside WISE was feeding their procurement machine, the access pattern should touch those lanes.

Eleven analysts had the clearance and the opportunity. Two weeks of paperwork got that down to seven. Two had been out of Berlint during key leak windows. Two more never touched the right compartments. Brief reads of colleagues whose surface thoughts were banal and obvious dropped the list again. By Monday morning she had three names.

Anna-Lise Berger had been one of them. An elevator ride killed that. Berger's head was full of Vienna resentment, her mother's slipping memory, a dentist appointment she'd been avoiding for three months, the dull ache of being forty-three and professionally overlooked. When the doors opened, Berger smiled at her and stepped out still innocent, and Anya stepped out carrying things Berger had never offered her.

That left two. Martin Dreher and Gerald Falk.

---

Dreher worked signals analysis on the third floor. He was fifty-one. He drank black coffee with two sugars in the cheap styrofoam cups everybody hated. A desk photo showed his wife, two sons, and a Golden Retriever who looked disappointed to be there. He had been in the building fifteen years and brought Friday pastries from a bakery near his apartment. He was the kind of colleague people described by habit because the habits were what you noticed first.

Tuesday morning put him alone in the break room with an empty mug in his hand, staring at the motivational poster above the microwave as if the eagle might tell him what to do next. Anya filled her own cup and stopped within range.

His thoughts hit like cold water.

*Tuesday. He said Tuesday. If the second panel is worse than the first one, if Kessler was wrong, if I've got three weeks, maybe four before it shows, before Eva looks at me and knows. Nine months with her mother and I can't do that to her. I can't.*

The panic broke, re-formed, and tried to pass as work.

*Budget meeting at two. Baltic assessment. Focus.*

Then it slipped again.

*Second referral first. Referral means paperwork. Paperwork means the WISE medical plan. HR sees the claim and I'm done. Cash for one more visit. Kessler at four. Tell Eva it's Wilkinson. Tell her I'm staying late. Please just believe that one more time.*

By the time the thought-stream collapsed into raw fear, Anya's hand had tightened hard enough on the cup to make the styrofoam creak.

"Hey, Martin."

He startled. "Anya. Hi. Morning."

"You okay? You look a little..."

"Fine." Too fast. "Just a long week. You know how it is."

*Don't ask. Please don't ask. Not here.*

"Sure," she said. "Get some rest."

Upstairs, she pulled Dreher's access history, printer releases, and leave calendar into the same window and stared until the pattern stopped looking accidental. Three leak windows matched three unexplained afternoon absences, each between ninety minutes and two hours. Each absence landed after he'd pulled procurement annexes or border-positioning summaries that the Renewal Front had later anticipated almost exactly. Nothing on his calendar explained those gaps. Nothing in the system accounted for the hard-copy releases. Today's schedule had another blank block beginning at 3:40.

The pattern said four o'clock with Kessler, cash only, no paper trail.

If that was a handoff and she spent the afternoon trying to get a cleaner read on Falk, she'd be waiting through another leak to avoid making herself uncomfortable. Falk was still there. Dreher had a clock on him.

---

By 3:12 she had written the memo.

The wording was careful. Nothing in it exposed how she knew. She cited access overlap, irregular hours, unscheduled absences tied to prior leak windows, sudden changes in demeanor consistent with acute operational pressure. Kurtz, in internal security, took it without reaction and said she'd look into it.

Counterintelligence pulled two analysts off the Ostanian desk the next morning. IT started auditing Dreher's terminal traffic and communication metadata. By lunchtime half the third floor was compensating for holes nobody could name out loud while Dreher moved through it all under surveillance he hadn't agreed to and couldn't yet feel.

The investigation ate two days.

Late on the second evening, Kurtz's report hit Anya's secure inbox. Three lines in, she got up from her desk and took it into the bathroom.

Martin Dreher was not selling intelligence to the Renewal Front.

Three weeks earlier, Martin Dreher had been diagnosed with Stage III pancreatic cancer. Dr. Kessler was an oncologist at a private clinic across the city. The referrals were medical. The cash was medical. The blank blocks in his afternoons were appointments, imaging, treatment planning, and time parked two streets over because he couldn't yet drive straight home and tell his wife it was happening to him now, not someday. He'd kept it off the WISE plan because a flagged claim would pull him from active work before he was ready to be looked at like a man leaving.

The line about three to four weeks was not an operational window. It was when his doctor thought the symptoms might stop staying hidden.

In the stall, the folder sat open on her lap while warm air pushed through the vent overhead. Somebody came in, used the sink, left again.

Every piece she'd read had been true: Tuesday, cash, the second referral, the terror. None of it had been about treason. A man trying to keep chemo off an HR system had sounded, at the surface, exactly like a man preparing a covert handoff, and she had handed that mistake to counterintelligence in a neat memo.

Two analysts lost two days on him. An IT specialist burned hours on the terminal of a man who had been reading survival rates on his lunch break. Somewhere in the building, the actual mole had all that room.

His desk photo came back to her, along with the Friday pastries and the low tuneless humming from three floors down. She had turned the worst private fear of his life into a security referral.

Cold metal bit into her palm while she held the stall latch and waited for the nausea to level out. At the sink, the face in the mirror looked the same. Trusting it felt harder.

---

Gerald Falk was in his usual corner of the fourth-floor bullpen the next morning with tea and a cable digest. Seeing him there felt less like spotting a colleague than realizing a familiar fixture in the room had shifted half an inch overnight. He had once shown up at her Eden graduation with a silver fountain pen that still lived in her desk drawer.

The shared printer gave her an excuse to stand near him. Pages fed out. She straightened them one by one and stayed inside his radius.

"You look tired," Falk said without lifting his eyes from the digest. "Eat breakfast."

His mind moved under the words in tidy little rows.

*Budget allocation for Q3 is twelve percent high again. Same trick as last year. Sylvia won't fight the escalation numbers because they're useful. Pension committee at noon. Actuarial adjustment still wrong. Might rain.*

"I'm fine," Anya said. "Long week."

"Isn't there always."

*Wilkinson routing note is sloppy. Geller used one intercept and called it a pattern. Move the extra eyes. Fix it later.*

Forty seconds passed. Then a minute. His thoughts kept moving through budget irritation, pension math, weather, soup rotation. Then, warm and unguarded in the middle of it:

*She doesn't eat enough. Loid used to do the same thing.*

That was all. There was no spike, no concealed channel, no buried panic broadcasting through the walls. After Dreher, the quiet was almost worse. Noise had made her wrong. Silence gave her nothing to be wrong about.

Back at her desk, the Wilkinson reference pulled at her until she opened the file. Falk had annotated it at 8:12 that morning and signed off on a further coverage shift starting the next day at 0600. Two more analysts off live review of the Ostanian diplomatic procurement traffic. Temporary on paper. Forty-eight hours. Bureaucratically clean. The exact desk Damian had spent the last week warning her the Renewal Front still used to move material and instructions.

It wasn't proof. A senior analyst could justify the move with her old false assessment and the thin sourcing on Geller's note. Anyone above him would probably sign it too. But the blind spot her lie had opened was getting wider, and Falk's name was on the paperwork making it happen.

She wrote down the channel numbers, the shift time, and Falk's initials. Tomorrow's hunt stopped being abstract. If somebody inside WISE wanted that lane dark before the weekend, 0600 was the hour to watch.

---

The encrypted call came at nine that night, one-time cipher and frequency rotation. Anya sat on her kitchen floor with her back against the cabinet and had to try the key sequence twice before the line cleared through static.

Damian's voice came on stripped of its diplomatic polish.

"The weather turned."

"How bad?"

"Our friend at the garden center moved up the planting schedule. Public event. Meant to look like the people across the street started it."

It was a diplomatic incident, something visible and meant to read as Westalian provocation.

"When?"

"This weekend. Midweek at the latest. Days, not weeks."

The kitchen faucet dripped once into the sink. Then it dripped again.

"I lost two days on the personnel issue," she said.

"What happened?"

"I was sure about one of them. I was wrong. He was hiding that he's sick. Bad sick."

Silence held on the line. Then, quieter, "I'm sorry."

The words hit harder than they should have.

"I've still got Falk," she said. "Not proof. Just paperwork. He signed a forty-eight-hour coverage cut on one of the diplomatic procurement desks. Starts at six tomorrow."

"Which desk?"

She gave him the channel designation.

Another pause followed. Papers shifted on his end. "Don't watch speeches, then. Watch deliveries. If the event is this weekend, they'll move parts first. Your person is clearing air for that or somebody is using his paperwork to do it."

"That's more useful."

"I know."

Her shoulder eased a fraction against the cabinet. It wasn't enough to matter.

"If I'm right about him, I let him widen the hole."

"If you're wrong again, you burn another innocent person." Damian exhaled. "So don't guess. Sit on the lane and see who uses it."

That was the first clean instruction anybody had given her all week.

"Okay."

"And Anya."

"Yeah?"

"Be careful."

"You too."

The line clicked dead.

For a moment she stayed on the floor listening to the pipes and the neighbor upstairs moving around in shoes that should have been illegal after nine. Then she pulled an electricity bill off the counter, turned it over, and wrote:

0600  
diplomatic procurement desk  
Falk

Underneath that she added, after a second:

Yor one more night.

It was ugly and it was still the calculation. Her mother was somewhere in the city with Dominic's photographs and whatever was left of her faith in Garden. Anya was betting Yor would stay quiet until tomorrow because the other clock had reached six in the morning and a desk inside WISE was about to go blind.

The apartment went dark when she hit the switch. Her head still hurt, but now it was the ordinary version, the screen-stare, skipped-meal kind. She set the note beside the phone, got into bed, and stayed awake long enough to count the hours until six between the faucet drips.
