# Chapter 15

The International Conference Center smelled like floor polish and new carpet, the kind of institutional freshness that meant someone important was expected. Anya took her seat in the East Gallery, Damian's borrowed press credential clipped to her lapel, and opened herself to the room. The borrowed badge had given her better sight lines, a separate entrance, and still no answer about what list he'd kept her off.

Four hundred minds hit her at once.

She'd done crowd reads before. The diplomatic reception had been bad. The market extraction in her first week had been worse. The summit was something else entirely. Delegates from both nations packed the Main Assembly Hall below, and their collective anxiety pressed behind her eyes like pool water. She gripped the arm of her chair until the wood grain marked her palm and let the noise come through.

*should never have agreed to this format, the Westalians will use it to...*

*my daughter's recital is at four, if this runs over I swear...*

*Voss is here, which means the hardliners are treating this as...*

*did I lock the car...*

*coordinates confirmed, south terrace, fourteen hundred...*

Anya caught the last thought like a hook in cloth. She held still and tried to isolate the thinker. Male. Controlled. Operational shorthand. Four hundred people stood between her and the source, and the signal dissolved back into the murk before she could pin a face to it.

South terrace. Fourteen hundred. Ninety minutes.

She scanned the assembly floor. The Ostanian delegation sat in curved rows on the west side, dark suits and rigid posture. The Westalian delegation mirrored them from the east. Between them stood the raised platform where the deputy ministers would speak. Deputy Minister Gerhardt was already in his seat, mid-fifties, tidy, useful, and currently thinking about whether the translator had mangled the idiom in paragraph four.

*the minister is exposed from the south approach, clear line through the gallery glass...*

That one was closer. Anya turned her head a fraction, as if checking her lanyard, and narrowed her focus toward the Westalian overflow seats. Twenty people. Grocery lists. A hangover. Someone rehearsing an apology text. Genuine policy fear. And there, a man in a charcoal suit whose surface thoughts kept arranging people into sightlines.

*team in place by thirteen fifty, window opens when Gerhardt stands, Westalian attribution holds if the first shot lands...*

The plan assembled itself in her head all at once. A staged assassination attempt on Deputy Minister Gerhardt, launched from the south terrace and pinned on Westalian operatives. The summit security apparatus would read it as proof that Westalis had sent killers into a peace conference. The Ostanian response would be immediate. Fourteen years of ugly, fragile peace would go up in one burst of broken glass.

Bond's vision. Government building. Uniformed men. Fire. This was it.

The headache had already started, a blunt throb behind her left eye. She had maybe two hours at this intensity before her range got sloppy and the noise won. She stood, tucked her notebook under one arm, and moved toward the stairs that connected the East Gallery to the service corridor.

She needed Loid. Their plan had designated dead drops inside the venue, a men's room, a fire exit, a janitor's closet. She could not use her earpiece. The summit's countermeasures would flag any unauthorized transmission. So she walked, and while she walked she kept reading, catching fragments from every mind she passed like stations sliding across a bad radio.

*the shrimp is excellent, whoever catered this should...*

*if the Westalians propose inspections we need to push the date...*

*I should call my wife...*

*Falk's in the comms room, east corridor... if the main board dies the cold relay on rack three carries twelve...*

Anya stopped so hard her heel skidded on the polished floor.

Falk.

Gerald Falk, who had stood in WISE hallways for years looking exactly as suspicious as wallpaper. Gerald Falk, who had attended her Eden graduation. Gerald Falk, one of the three mole candidates she'd narrowed to, then shoved down the list after the wrong analyst's panic ate two days of her life.

And the rest of the thought. Cold relay. Rack three. Twelve minutes.

She closed her eyes for one beat and weighed it. She could chase a half heard contingency through a building full of people, or she could get Loid the one thing that mattered right now, the fact that Gerhardt was about to die unless somebody moved. If she guessed wrong about the relay and lost the shooter, the minister was dead. If she guessed wrong about Falk, the mole walked. One clear alarm beat three foggy ones.

She found the east side fire exit and left a folded note behind the extinguisher.

*South terrace 1400. Gerhardt target. Falk in comms room east corridor.*

She almost added the relay fragment. Her pen hovered. Then she crossed out the half written word and folded the paper instead. Rack three was not enough. Not yet.

Loid would check within fifteen minutes. That was the plan.

She went back to the assembly hall, sat down, and reopened herself to four hundred minds.

The pulse behind her eyes deepened.

Damian watched Werner Voss from across the West Gallery and thought about the exact weight of paper in his inside pocket.

Voss stood near the railing talking to a National Unity Party delegate Damian recognized from a fundraiser, someone connected to defense procurement, someone who smiled like the smile was a tax write off. Voss himself looked as he always did, heavyset, disciplined, carrying military posture inside an expensive civilian suit. His brother had died in the last border conflict. The grief had not made him generous. It had made him certain.

Damian had photocopies. Old-fashioned on purpose. Electronic files could be called fabrication. Handwritten marginalia on ministry stationery was harder to wave away. The manifests Yor had recovered from Dominic's compound sat cross referenced with contract records from his father's old network, the notes Grosz had helped him decode, and Voss's name at three places where money stopped pretending to be public.

The Westalian deputy minister was still speaking about security cooperation when Damian crossed the gallery.

"Councillor Voss. I need a word."

Voss turned. Recognition came first, then calculation.

"Young Desmond. This seems like poor timing."

"You've been financing a paramilitary faction through Kruger Defense subsidiaries and shell procurement accounts. I'd call the timing overdue."

Voss glanced at the delegate beside him. "Would you excuse us."

She moved off two steps, not far enough to be polite, close enough to hear if the conversation went bad. Damian respected that.

Voss held out a hand. "Let's see what you've brought."

Damian put the copies on the railing between them. Voss did not touch them yet.

"If this is theater," Voss said, "pick a smaller room."

"If it were theater I'd have brought a microphone."

That got the documents into Voss's hand. He scanned the first page. His face did not change much, but Damian saw the tiny tightening at the mouth and knew the hit had landed.

"Dominic's files," Voss said.

"Partly."

"Dominic is dead."

"I know."

Voss turned a page. "And what exactly do you think this proves."

"That you moved ministry money into Renewal Front channels. That the channels stayed open because people above you wanted them open."

"Above me." Voss gave a short breath that was not a laugh. "Listen to yourself. You still think this is vertical. Good men, bad men, a tidy ladder."

"Then untidy it for me."

Voss laid the pages flat. "Your father tried that once."

"Don't bring him into this."

"Why not. You brought his network."

Damian felt his jaw lock. Voss saw it and pressed.

"He understood before you did. This was never about pure Ostanian blood and old speeches, no matter what our louder idiots say into cameras. It is contracts. It is shipping. It is the same ten men investing in missiles on one side of the border and surveillance arrays on the other."

"Names."

"You have some of them."

"Not enough."

"There are never enough." Voss tapped the page with one thick finger. "Westalian firms buy our military reporting through intermediaries. Our defense ministry buys their hardware through shells that exist for six months at a time. Everybody at this summit knows the temperature is profitable. That's why peace is sold in installments."

Damian stared at him. He had expected ideology or denial. What Voss offered instead was a bored accountant's version of betrayal.

"So that's the argument," Damian said. "No conspiracy. Just market conditions."

"Call it what you want."

"And the terrace team."

That finally moved something across Voss's face. Not fear. Annoyance.

"Careful."

"I am being careful."

"No, you're being young. Those are not the same thing." Voss gathered the papers, then stopped himself and let them go. "Take this to the floor if you like. Say my name into the microphones. Watch how many people rush to condemn me and how many rush to contain the blast radius. The second number is the real one. Your own delegation will help with it."

"Maybe."

"Not maybe. Certainly." Voss leaned in, voice lower now, rougher. "You think this makes you clean. It doesn't. It makes you the Desmond who said it out loud first."

Damian picked the papers back up.

"Good," he said. "Somebody has to."

He was already moving before Voss answered.

The moderator on the floor looked confused when Damian asked for a point of procedure. The Ostanian delegation chair looked furious even before the microphone came on.

"State your point," the moderator said.

Damian did.

"I am entering documentary evidence that Councillor Werner Voss and connected procurement entities inside our own defense apparatus routed funds and materiel to the Renewal Front. I am also stating for the record that those entities overlap with commercial intermediaries tied to Westalian defense firms represented in this building."

The hall went silent for half a second.

Then the Ostanian delegation chair was on his feet. "Desmond, sit down."

Damian kept going.

"If you want the shell names, I have them. If you want the signatures, I have those too. If anyone in this room intends to keep using patriotism as a receipt printer for private violence, this is the part where you stop pretending the money moves in one direction."

The Westalian deputy minister rose partway from his seat. "Moderator, this is an internal allegation and a reckless one."

"It stopped being internal when your contractors were billing our crisis," Damian said.

Somewhere to his left, an Ostanian delegate hissed, "Are you out of your mind."

Another said it louder. "You've just handed state procurement to foreign press."

"No," Damian said. His voice was shaking now, but it carried. "I just handed the room its own paperwork."

Press cameras swung toward him in a bright mechanical flock. Security started moving at the perimeter. On the raised platform the moderator called for order twice and got less of it each time.

Damian could feel the interpretation splitting in real time. One part of the room was shocked that anyone had said it. Another part was shocked that it had been him. Courage in one row. Treason in the next. By the time the moderator called recess, Damian already knew what people would call him if this held.

The man who blew up the summit.

He had no idea yet whether the name would save anything.

Loid found the note behind the fire extinguisher at 13:42.

The handwriting was Anya's. He would have known it anywhere, the impatient angle of the letters, the way she still crossed her sevens the way he'd shown her when she was ten and furious about numbers that all looked the same.

*South terrace 1400. Gerhardt target. Falk in comms room east corridor.*

Falk.

The name dropped through him hard and clean. Gerald Falk. Twenty-four years in WISE. Three generations of analysts and field operatives vetted by his desk. A bottle of wine at Anya's fifteenth birthday. A man who had once stayed late in the Forger kitchen drying crystal for Yor because she was too embarrassed to admit she kept chipping it.

Loid folded the note and moved.

The east service corridor ran the length of the building, fluorescent lit, concrete underfoot, old coffee under the sharp smell of cleaning solution. His cover credentials got him through the first checkpoint. The second he cleared on timing alone, stepping through during the guard change he'd memorized the night before.

The Communications Room sat at the far end. Single door. No window. He could hear radio static through the metal.

He tried the handle.

Unlocked.

Gerald Falk sat at the main console with a headset around his neck and both hands on the keyboard.

For one second Loid only watched. Falk's movements were quiet and practiced, the hands of a man doing office work he had done for most of his life. That ordinariness had protected him for years.

"Gerald."

Falk stopped typing. He did not turn right away.

"Loid," he said. "You found me fast."

"Terrace team?"

Falk checked the clock on the console. "In position in eight minutes."

Loid opened the door again without taking his eyes off him and gave two sharp coughs into the corridor.

Yor was covering the Main Foyer because it gave her the fastest line to the south approach. She would hear it. If Loid ran for the terrace himself, Falk kept transmitting or vanished and the summit lost its only live line into the operation. He shut the door.

"Abort code," Loid said.

"There isn't one."

"Then names."

Falk looked at him properly now. "You still think this breaks down into names."

"It breaks down into whether Gerhardt leaves this building alive."

"Gerhardt is one function."

"Don't."

Falk's jaw shifted. "You know what I watched happen in those rooms, Loid. Every assessment bent to fit a peace nobody was allowed to question. Every ugly number sanded smooth because stability photographed better."

"I questioned plenty."

"And still served it."

"I did not feed target coordinates to men with guns."

"No. You kept the machine polite."

Loid crossed the room, caught the main transmission cable under the desk, and ripped it free. The console screens went black.

A small box mounted low on the rack stayed dark. No light, no sound. He gave it half a glance and turned back to Falk.

"Now we're done talking around it," Loid said.

Falk did not look at the dead console. He looked at Loid.

"Are we. Crane was dead the minute people like you decided a bad peace was good enough."

"Crane was forty-one," Loid said. "He had a wife. No kids. He still sent the office photos of that idiot dog like the rest of us hadn't seen it already. Don't turn him into an abstraction because it's easier on you."

Silence sat between them for a beat. Then Falk said, quieter, "You want easy. Start at home."

Loid felt the room narrow.

"Leave my family out of this."

"I can't. Your daughter wrote the blind spot."

The words landed hard enough that Loid's body registered them before his thoughts did.

Falk kept talking. Maybe because he wanted to hurt him. Maybe because he was finally past concealment. Maybe because the years had built up too much pressure to stop now.

"Two months ago. Her first assessment on the Ostanian operative she was tracking. Too clean. Too careful. She cut the threat profile, shifted analytical attention away from the sectors we were using, and wrote it so neatly it got praised for judgment."

Loid's hands stayed at his sides. "What did you do."

"I read it."

"I know you read it."

"I used it." Falk swallowed. "I rerouted transmissions through the blind channels she created. Traffic the building stopped watching because her report told it not to look there. The packet that put Crane at that checkpoint went through one of those channels."

Loid did not move.

He knew exactly how a lie like that passed review. Strip out one dangerous fact. Leave the supporting structure intact. Sound certain where uncertainty would attract a second look. He had spent years teaching Anya how to write reports that held under pressure. Falk was telling him she had used the same workmanship against WISE.

From somewhere beyond the wall came the first ripple of assembly floor chaos. Raised voices. More feet in the corridor. Damian, then.

"You don't know why she did it," Loid said.

"No."

"And you still used it."

"Of course I used it. I'm an analyst, not a priest."

Loid stepped back to the doorway and stayed there, body angled to block the only exit.

He could not go south now. Yor was already moving. If he left the room, Falk ran or talked or both. If he dragged Falk out himself, he lost the chance to hold the mole at the source and whatever else the room still contained. So he stayed and listened to the summit start breaking on the other side of the wall.

By the fourth minute a fire alarm started somewhere in the building. By the seventh, people were running in the corridor. By the ninth, SSS voices were close.

Loid used the time badly. He stood in a room that smelled of hot electronics and thought about a report he had read three months ago, the part of it he had admired for its precision, and the fact that admiration was apparently how the lie had gotten past him.

Yor was near the Main Foyer when Loid's signal cut across the open space.

Two sharp coughs from the mouth of the east corridor. Deliberate, not a throat tick or nerves.

Loid.

She changed direction at once.

If he was signaling from the east corridor instead of coming himself, then he had found Falk and could not leave him. That meant the south approach was hers.

She moved through the foyer with measured steps, dark blazer neat over a cream blouse, the sort of outfit that passed for private security if nobody looked too hard. One blade rode her thigh holster. Another sat in her left boot. She had left the Garden's preferred tools at home. She was not here for the Garden.

Through the glass doors of the South Terrace she saw flagstone, clipped hedges, a line of official cars beyond the checkpoint, and four men in civilian clothes moving like soldiers pretending not to be soldiers. Two already had sidearms out. One was setting himself at the sightline to the assembly hall windows. The fourth was falling back toward the exterior stairs with a radio in hand.

Gerhardt was due up in minutes.

Yor pushed through the doors.

The first shooter's round broke the frame beside her head. Glass burst inward across the tile.

She crossed the distance before he could correct his aim, caught his wrist, forced the muzzle high, and drove the heel of her hand into the front of his throat. Cartilage gave. He dropped backward clutching at the ruined airway, making a wet animal sound that cut off before his head struck the stone.

The second man swung toward her. Yor went under the barrel, drew the blade from her boot, and opened the side of his neck in one short pull. Blood came out in a dark sheet over his collar and hand. He stared at it once, sank to both knees, and folded sideways without firing again.

The third man turned from the window line with his weapon half raised. Yor threw the blade. It went in above the collarbone and buried deep enough that he hit the railing already drowning. His gun clattered from his hand. By the time he slid down the metal and crumpled on the flagstones, he was still.

Three.

The fourth broke for the stairs, shouting into his radio. Yor caught him halfway down, drove him face first into the stone lip hard enough to break teeth, then seized his head and twisted. The neck went with a sharp, dry sound that stayed in the air a moment longer than the rest of him did.

The terrace went quiet.

Not ordinary quiet. Post-violence quiet. Four bodies. Blood on stone. One gun in the hedge, another under the railing, another at the dead man's feet, the fourth still trapped under the stairs where his hand had opened and let go of the radio.

Yor's hands shook once. Fine tremor. Then stopped.

Someone stood at the entrance to the West Service Corridor.

Yuri.

He had his sidearm half drawn. His gaze moved over the terrace, the gun in the hedge, the knife buried to the hilt, the blood on Yor's blouse sleeve, and then stopped at her face.

"Sis," he said.

Yor swallowed. "Yuri. Call it in."

He looked up at the security camera mounted on the corner of the building, the red recording light steady and indifferent.

"How long."

Glass grated under Yor's shoe when she shifted her weight. She didn't answer.

Yuri holstered his weapon and lifted the radio at his shoulder.

"South Terrace. Four hostiles down. Send medical and response."

The dispatcher answered at once, thin and urgent through the speaker. Yuri kept his eyes on the camera.

"The shooter was a member of the Westalian security delegation," he said. "Identity not yet confirmed."

Yor stared at him. Her mouth moved, stopped, moved again.

"Go inside," Yuri said. "Before the team gets here."

"Yuri..."

"Go."

She nodded once and went back through the broken glass.

Boots hit the stairs less than twenty seconds later. Yuri did not move from the middle of the terrace. When the first response officer asked who had engaged the team, he said, "Unknown Westalian security asset," and heard the lie come out in his own voice.

At 14:07 Anya caught the thought she should have treated like fire the first time.

She was still in the East Gallery, trying to hold four hundred minds apart with a headache that had gone from pulse to pressure to something like a clamp around her skull. She had tracked Damian's floor speech through the room's shock and the terrace engagement through splinters of fear, broken glass, blood, Yuri, security, south side, get medical. She was still sorting it when a thought from three rows behind her snapped cleanly out of the noise.

*good, green light is up, east rack bought the full twelve after Falk's board died...*

Green light.

East rack.

The half heard fragment from the corridor slammed back into place. Not a second room. Not another operator. A cold relay in the same communications rack, dark until the main board went down. She had heard it earlier. She had weighed it against Gerhardt's live danger and let it go.

If she stayed in the gallery, she could keep reading the floor and maybe catch whatever came next. If she left now, Damian's thread went dark to her and the summit became four hundred muffled radios she was no longer even trying to separate. The relay was already live. Every second mattered.

She got up.

The headache hit so hard the room tilted. She grabbed the seatback in front of her, muttered an apology to the delegate who turned around, and pushed toward the stairs with one hand pressed hard to the side of her head.

She ran east.

The fluorescent corridor light stabbed. Every footfall jarred her teeth. She passed the fire exit where she had left the note. She passed two security guards who clocked her credentials and let her through. She reached the Communications Room door and shoved it open without knocking.

Loid was still there, one shoulder against the doorway, Falk on the floor with his wrists cinched behind him by Loid's belt.

"Lower rack," Anya said. Her voice came out rough. "Behind the booster."

Loid moved at once.

The relay sat bracketed behind a signal booster so small it disappeared until he crouched. Now it had a solid green indicator burning on its face.

He pulled the power cable.

The light died.

For a second nobody in the room said anything. Then Loid leaned across the rack, found the tiny status screen set into the relay casing, and read it.

"How long."

Anya looked too.

"Eleven minutes, forty seconds."

Almost the full buffer.

Cached delegation positions. Internal security notes. Pieces of the summit that did not matter until somebody hostile had them, then mattered all at once. Enough to leak consequences past the day of the operation. Enough to prove that missing one hidden thing in a room still cost you even after you stopped the shooting.

Falk sat against the wall and looked at her.

His thoughts were bitter and busy and impossible to shut out.

*too clean, too careful, should've known the girl would hear about it this way...*

She looked at Loid.

He was already looking at her.

His face was composed because his face was almost always composed. But his eyes had changed. Not anger. Not even disappointment yet. Recognition. The sort that arrives when scattered facts stop scattering and lock into one shape.

He knew how her report had survived review. He knew because he had taught her what certainty looked like on the page, how omission hid best inside competence, how a dangerous judgment could wear the exact clothes of a good one.

Anya opened her mouth. She wanted to say all of it at once, Damian, the first lie, the reason for it, the way every choice after that had gotten harder and smaller and worse.

"Not here," Loid said.

Quiet. Precise. Final.

She nodded.

Outside, the summit was breaking open. Press were already at the barricades. A cameraman near the front steps was thinking *Desmond's finished* while the producer beside him thought *No, Desmond just saved the minister,* and kept saying Damian's surname into a handset like he was trying out headlines. On a security radio, somebody asked for camera six again, the one with the woman on the south doors, and got told to wait for clearance.

Anya kept walking until the cold hit her. She put one hand on the stone wall and bent her head. The relay's green indicator stayed on the back of her eyes even with them shut. Eleven minutes and forty seconds. Long enough to turn one missed fragment into something that would keep moving after today.

Loid found her there a few minutes later. He came out with his belt missing and her folded note in one hand. He stopped beside her and looked through the glass with her.

"You did good work today."

"Dad..."

"Later."

He folded the note once more and put it in his pocket. His shoulder touched hers through both jackets and stayed there.

Sirens climbed the street. The conference center kept spilling people into the cold. By then Falk was in custody, and Damian's name was already out in the street wearing arguments he hadn't chosen. Neither of them went back inside.
