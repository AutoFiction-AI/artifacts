# Chapter 14

Anya stood outside the apartment door with the key in her hand and thought, not for the first time, that David Crane had no business being in this hallway. He was there anyway. The apartment smelled of sesame oil and sugar catching at the bottom of a pan. Sylvia's voice saying *Agent Crane is dead.* The border checkpoint. The gap in the coverage pattern that Anya had helped cut open with a lie.

Through the door she heard Bond's nails on hardwood and the low murmur of the radio Loid always kept on in the evenings. Yor moved in the kitchen. Water ran at the sink. Home sounded normal enough to make her throat close.

She should have called. There was a protocol for this. When an operation went bad and you needed a handler outside the chain, you filed a contact request and waited for a secure window. You did not show up at your parents' apartment at nine on a Tuesday with your hands shaking and a dead man's name lodged behind your teeth.

She let herself in.

Loid was at the sink in an apron, sleeves pushed to the elbows. Yor had the refrigerator door open and a bottle of sauce in one hand. Bond thumped once from the living room. For a second nobody had turned yet, and Anya saw the evening as it had been before she came in: dinner half finished, radio low, the kind of ordinary hour Crane would never get back.

"Anya!" Yor shut the refrigerator too fast. "You should've called. I would've made more rice. Have you eaten? Sit down. Wait, no, you look cold. Sit down first and then tell me if you've eaten."

Bond lumbered in from the living room and shoved his head against Anya's hip hard enough to make her stumble. The thought from him knocked into her in three blunt beats. *Home. Her. Good.* She bent to scratch behind his ears because it gave her one extra second before she had to speak.

Loid turned. His face stayed easy. The quick check behind it did not.

*She came alone. No call. Her hands. How bad.*

His gaze dropped to the white grip of her fingers around the strap of her bag.

"How's work?" he asked.

It was the kind of question a father asked at a kitchen table. It was also cover for *tell me what room we're in*.

"Papa." Her voice came out rough. She cleared it. "I need to talk to you."

The reading glasses came off his head. That was the only large movement. The rest happened in smaller places: the change in his stance, the way his attention locked, the quiet that settled over him without touching his face.

Yor set the sauce bottle down. "Anya?"

"Crane is dead," Anya said.

The kitchen went still.

She told them enough. Not the telepathy. Not the false report. Not the full shape of the guilt, which lived in her like swallowed metal and had not moved in three days. But enough. The Renewal Front. The conspiracy inside both governments. The mole inside WISE she still could not name. The summit. The fact that the provocation had narrowed to days, not weeks.

Loid did not interrupt. The read hit her in overlapping bursts, numbers and worry stepping on each other.

*Summit venue. Ostanian jurisdiction. They want spectacle, so the attack point has to read clean on camera. She has not slept. Crane is dead. She's carrying this alone. No, not alone anymore.*

"How solid is your venue intelligence?" he asked.

"Partial. Opening session is still the best window. We don't have the target yet."

"Do you know the relay point?"

"No. Just that there has to be one inside."

He nodded once. The plan was already assembling behind his eyes.

"I'm in," he said.

Yor had not moved since Crane's name. Now Anya caught the flicker in her mother's mind, not panic but recognition.

*Karl Dominic's office. The manifests. The watermark.*

Yor turned off the burner, wiped her hands on her apron, and went into the hall. She came back with a thick manila envelope and set it on the table between the soy sauce and the stack of plates.

"I've got intelligence too," she said.

She opened the envelope. Grainy photographs slid across the wood. Shipping manifests. Procurement records. A photographed purchase order with the Garden watermark at the top and three completed eliminations listed below it.

Loid picked up the manifests first. His eyes moved fast.

*Voss. Civilian intermediaries. Defense procurement. This is the supply chain.*

"Where did you get these?"

"A job last month. The broker had them in his office."

Loid's hand stopped on the Garden sheet. He looked at Yor. She met his eyes before he could ask.

"The Garden took contracts for them," she said. "Not officially. Through cutouts. They marked people as liabilities in the chain and sent me after them."

Behind the words came the sentence she wasn't saying out loud.

*I killed them and never asked who was paying.*

Anya looked at the list again. Three dates. Three names. Three neat entries sitting on the kitchen table while Crane's death still burned at the edge of every thought she had.

Yor kept going, because once she had started she was not going to protect herself with softness.

"I thought I was removing dangerous people. Brokers. Fixers. Men moving weapons and money. Some of them were that. Some of them were just in the way of whatever this network needed next." She touched the corner of the page with one finger. "The Garden was clearing paths for the Renewal Front. I was one of the tools."

Loid set the paper down very carefully. His jaw tightened once.

"How long have you known?"

"Since Dominic. Since I saw the records." Yor folded her hands together to stop them from shaking. "Long enough to know I'm not going back."

He did not rush her.

"If they send a handler, I won't answer. If they leave instructions, I won't take them. I'll help with the summit, but not as Thorn Princess. Not as their asset." She looked at Anya first, then at Loid. "As Yor Forger. As your family."

That was all. No speech. No apology. The break sat in the room with its full weight.

Loid reached across the table and covered her hand with his.

"We'll deal with the Garden after we get through tomorrow," he said.

Yor turned her hand under his and held on.

Anya looked down at Bond's head against her knee. His fur smelled like dust and soap and the apartment itself. Safe things. Crane had had safe things too. Everybody did, right up until the moment they did not.

Forty minutes later the kitchen looked less like dinner and more like evidence. Loid had pulled a venue map onto the refrigerator beside his whiteboard and covered both with times, arrows, and access points. Yor's photographs sat in a neat row on the table. Anya stood near the counter with a pen she was not using.

"The opening session is still the obvious window," Loid said. He marked the south terrace doors, then the service corridors. "They need visibility and clean attribution. If the shooting reads Westalian, the first minutes matter more than the escape."

"The south side is theatrical," Yor said. She had not touched the tea in front of her. "Good sight lines. Room for panic. Bad if you want to keep the shooters alive."

Loid gave her a look.

She returned it, unoffended. "I'm only saying the plan values drama."

He tapped the east service corridor. "The relay point is here or near here. Only interior spot with enough privacy to push coordinates without public line of sight."

He was already thinking it. Anya heard the unspoken part of it anyway.

*And if I put Yor on the terrace and the team comes through the corridor first, she loses the angle.*

"What about the foyer junction instead?" Anya said. "If Mama starts there, she can cover the west approach and still reach the terrace."

Loid glanced at the map, then at her.

*Better.*

"Yes," he said. He redrew the line. "That gives us both exits."

Yor leaned forward and measured distance with two fingers. *Forty seconds if the heels are wrong. Less without them.* "I can do that."

The plan tightened. It always did when Anya stole the part nobody had said yet and handed it back as intuition.

Her stomach turned.

They kept going. Loid built routes. Yor asked practical questions about choke points and camera coverage. Anya said less than she usually did because every useful sentence felt like theft now. Crane stayed in the room even when nobody named him. He was in the speed of Loid's corrections, in the flatness of Yor's voice when she talked about kill zones, in the fact that none of them pretended this was theoretical.

Bond had been asleep through most of it. Then he stood so abruptly the nails of his front paws scraped the floor.

Yor half rose. "Bond?"

He crossed the living room fast, faster than an old dog with stiff hips had any right to move, and planted himself in front of the refrigerator. He barked once and shoved his nose against the lower edge of the map.

Anya was already reading him. She could not help it.

Low wall. Wet stone. Grass. Men in dark clothes coming hard along the edge. Light flashing on glass. Heat. Noise. Not words, never words, but a future with teeth.

Bond barked again, sharper this time.

Loid followed the line of his muzzle. "South perimeter."

The place Bond had pinned was outside the south terrace, where the landscaped grounds narrowed near the vehicle approach.

"That's not random," Anya said.

Loid looked at her, then at Bond, then back to the map. A different memory moved through his thoughts.

*Government event. Weeks ago. She pushed me away from it without telling me why.*

He did not ask the question. He only circled the south perimeter in red.

"Then the extraction route is real," he said. "Or the staging route. Either way we hold that ground."

Bond pressed closer to the refrigerator, the vision fading now into the simpler distress of an old dog who knew only that the place on the map was bad.

Anya crouched and put her arms around his neck. "Good boy," she said into his fur. "I know."

She texted Damian while Loid copied the south-side access times onto a second sheet. He answered in under a minute. *On my way.*

At twenty past ten, his thoughts reached the stairwell before the doorbell did.

*You can walk into a kitchen. You can brief a family. Crane is dead, Reinhardt is in hiding, and you can still say hello like a person. Do not think about the class photo.*

The bell rang.

Yor got there first. "Hello. You must be Damian. Come in. It's awful out there. Do you want tea? We have tea. And food. Please ignore the maps."

"Thank you, Mrs. Forger."

"Yor," she said at once. "Mrs. Forger sounds like somebody who sends complaint letters."

That got a surprised breath of laughter out of him. Not much, but enough for Anya to hear how tired he really was.

He stepped into the kitchen damp from the rain, coat dark at the shoulders, hair flattened at the temples. He looked like he had been sleeping badly in clean shirts. When his eyes found Anya, the next thought came too fast and too polished.

*Tea. Maps. West Gallery. Keep it on the summit.*

There it was again. Not a second layer. Not a hidden room. Just a choice, clean and deliberate, to put one thought in front of another.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

Loid came out of the hall wiping his hands on the apron. "Damian. I'm Loid."

The handshake lasted the exact right length. Loid's smile stayed easy. Behind it, Anya caught the fast inventory click into place.

*Good suit. No aide. Diplomat's manners, no sleep. He keeps checking Anya before he answers. Personal, then.*

Anya wanted to die on the spot.

"Thank you for having me, Mr. Forger," Damian said.

"Loid is fine."

Yor pressed a mug into Damian's hand before anybody else could choose a seat. "Anywhere except that chair. Bond claims it."

Bond, who apparently claimed everyone now, approached Damian with solemn attention, inspected his shoes, inspected his coat hem, then turned once and settled on Damian's feet with a noise of total surrender.

Damian went still. *The dog has me pinned. I have negotiated with ministers under less pressure than this.*

It hit Anya wrong and almost made her laugh. Crane was dead. The kitchen table was covered in conspiracy paperwork. Her mother had just quit an assassination organization. And still there was Bond, flattening a diplomat by the ankles.

"He likes you," Yor said warmly.

Damian looked down at the dog and then around the kitchen. He left the top layer sitting right out in the open.

*This is real. The radio. The dog hair. Fruit magnets on a war map. She comes from this. No wonder she fights like someone protecting a house, not a flag.*

Then Yor, trying to be kind, said, "Anya barely touched dinner when she was small unless I told her there was dessert waiting. She used to bargain like a union organizer."

Damian's gaze flicked to the hallway where framed family photos hung. Then he stacked the next four thoughts like agenda items.

*West Gallery. Voss. South perimeter. Ask about seating.*

He smiled at Yor with perfect politeness. "Can we go over the summit first? I brought the delegation update."

The swerve was so clean Anya almost missed it. But she saw the glance at the hallway. She heard the swap. Harmless thoughts lifted into place where a riskier one had almost gone.

She could read the dodge. She could not read what he had dodged. He knew a piece of her life he was refusing to hold still long enough for her to catch. That was all she had. The rest was inference.

Loid was already back at the refrigerator. Father mode had retreated without disappearing. "Walk us through it."

Damian set his tea down untouched and stepped closer to the map. The whole room shifted with him. He stopped being the wet young man in the doorway and became what he had always actually been underneath the diplomatic polish: a trained political operator with nowhere safe to put the truth except work.

"Security tightened after the border hit," he said. "Party leadership is frightened enough to overcorrect. Voss will stay close to the floor, but not on it. West Gallery. That is where he can watch the hall and still deny operational responsibility if things go badly."

"Can you get near him?" Loid asked.

"Yes. If I look like I'm there to warn him, not expose him."

Loid nodded. "Good. That gives us the public channel."

Yor tapped the south perimeter circle Bond had chosen. "If the team uses the exterior approach, I take the foyer junction and move south on contact."

"I agree," Damian said. "If the first shots come from outside, the panic pushes inward. That buys Loid seconds at the relay."

They moved quickly after that. Loid handled the communications room and internal intercept. Yor took the south-side response and the junction. Damian took Voss and the political blast radius. Anya listened, read, and kept stealing small improvements where she could.

Loid worried about the relay timing before he voiced it. She asked whether the first intercept should lag the opening applause by thirty seconds. Yor measured the blind angle at the junction with two fingers, and Anya named it before she had to. Damian stalled for half a breath over how close he could get to Voss without burning his whole network. Anya told him to force the approach during seating instead of the speech window, and his shoulders dropped before he said thanks.

Another arrow stayed where she put it. Loid quit reworking the relay timing. Yor stopped touching the junction line. The board got cleaner and the knot under Anya's ribs pulled tighter. She kept taking the first thing in everybody's head and laying it on the table like they had offered it.

Loid capped one marker and reached for another. "Anya can work the room from the diplomatic annex."

"No," Damian said.

The word came out too fast. He corrected at once. "No. Not the annex."

Everybody looked at him. Even Bond raised his head.

Damian kept his eyes on the map. What he let float up had already been cleaned for company.

*Too boxed in. Too visible to the wrong staff list. Press is cleaner. East Gallery.*

"From the annex," he said more evenly, "she gets boxed behind ministry staff and misses the south doors. Press seating in East Gallery gives her a full view of the floor, the terrace access, and the west leadership row. Better lines. Better movement if she has to get out."

Loid considered it. "Can you get her press?"

"Yes."

That answer was too quick too. Damian knew it, and Anya knew he knew it. The next thought he let up was drier.

*Petrov will hate this. Good.*

Loid's eyes narrowed a fraction. "At what cost?"

"It burns a favor in the party communications office." Damian finally looked at him. "If the accreditation gets audited after the summit, the trail lands on me. I can live with that."

Anya heard the safe thought and the hitch beneath it, where he stopped himself before the real reason showed. He wasn't just finding her a better angle. He was keeping her off some other list and out of some other line of sight. He wouldn't think the reason clearly enough for her to catch.

It wasn't enough, but it got her out of the annex.

Loid studied him for one beat too long, then nodded. "Do it."

Damian took out his phone and stepped into the hall. They could hear only his side of it.

"Petrov. It's Damian. I need one media credential for tomorrow. East Gallery, full-room access if you can manage it. Yes, I know what hour it is. I also know whose office covered for your nephew in November, so let's not waste each other's time."

Yor blinked. "Oh."

Anya pressed her mouth shut.

Damian came back in with the same phone in his hand and more rain on his coat cuff where he must have leaned against the wall. "Badge by seven," he said. "Press entrance on the east side. Use that, not the diplomatic queue. They tightened the ministry list this afternoon."

There it was again: the recommendation, with the reason missing behind it.

Loid rewrote the final assignments on the whiteboard.

"Yor, foyer junction to south perimeter. Me, east corridor relay. Damian, West Gallery and Voss. Anya, East Gallery press row." He set the marker down. "If any one thread slips, the others keep moving."

Bond huffed in his sleep and shoved his nose deeper against Damian's shoe.

Anya looked at the board, at the red circle around the south perimeter, at Bond's nose wedged against Damian's shoe. East Gallery meant a separate entrance, better sight lines, and no place on the tightened ministry list. Damian had shifted her from one line on Loid's board to another, and the second move was the one she couldn't see.

Tomorrow she'd walk into the conference center wearing his press badge and still not know what list he'd kept her off.
