# Chapter 1

The man at the turnip stall was going to leave his wife.

Not today. Probably not this year. But Anya Forger could hear him building up to it while she stood three stalls away pretending to examine pears, and the level of rehearsal was impressive in a miserable kind of way. He'd run through the speech four times since she'd come within range. Each version was worse. The current draft included the phrase "I deserve to feel alive," which, no. Nobody delivered that line out loud and left the conversation with any dignity. She almost wanted to help him edit.

*Listen, Gerda, we've had a good run, but I think we both know things have changed.*

She didn't help. She was working.

Berlint Central Market spread through three city blocks between the diplomatic quarter and the government district, a maze of canvas canopies and wooden stalls that thinned in autumn but never fully closed. The afternoon crowd was thick enough to be useful: tourists consulting pocket maps, pensioners comparing vegetable prices with the exhaustive seriousness of arms negotiators, mothers with children who ran at shin height and thought about nothing except wanting things. Anya held her position by the pear display and waited for her contact.

Turnip Man's rehearsal continued in the background, layered under a woman in a brown coat who was composing an indignant letter to her landlord about the mold in the bathroom. *It's been four months, Herr Dietrich. I don't think it's unreasonable to ask.* Two stalls over, a teenager was replaying an argument with his mother and losing, even inside his own head. Behind Anya, a man was thinking about bratwurst with a focus that bordered on worship.

This was the noise. It was always the noise. Sixty, maybe seventy minds within range, roughly twenty meters in every direction, give or take, and every one of them talking at once, none of them aware anyone could hear. Anya couldn't turn it off. Hadn't been able to since she was four, when the first thought she'd ever registered was a nursery teacher's exhausted prayer for nap time. Whatever anyone within range was actively thinking arrived in her head, in their voice, at their volume, whether she wanted it or not.

She mostly didn't want it.

The contact was late. His codename was Kinglet, a mid-level courier, nothing remarkable, just a man who picked things up and left things behind and worried about it the entire time. Anya had been watching the dead-drop bench near the fountain for seventeen minutes, and in that time she'd learned that the pretzel vendor across the way had a sciatica problem he was managing with anger and a philosophy about the proper ratio of coarse salt to fine salt that she would never fully forget.

Somewhere in the crowd, faint and easily missed, a woman was thinking about her son. *If his unit gets redeployed to the eastern sector again, I'll drag him home myself.* Anya let it pass. Background static. Everyone was worrying about rent or dinner or the border or a child they couldn't keep safe.

*Should've worn the grey jacket. The grey doesn't wrinkle. Why do I never think about that before I leave? Okay. Focus. The newspaper is on the bench. Walk naturally. Am I walking naturally? This doesn't feel natural.*

Kinglet, coming up from the south before she even spotted him. Standard courier anxiety. He was a nervous man doing his nervous job. Anya had worked with worse.

She scanned outward. The crowd noise was a wall of competing voices, but she'd been doing this long enough to know what she was listening for. Trained minds sounded different from the usual churn. Not calmer. Tighter. They snagged on the same object and kept circling back.

There. East side of the market, near the cheese stall. *Bench in sight. Don't look at the bench. Wait for hands. If he rubs his jaw again, look away.* Nobody buying cheese thought in tactical fragments.

A second thread cut in from the south before she finished locating the first. *South lane clear. Let him drift west. Once he moves, once he commits, then go.*

Two of them. One holding the bench, one shaping the exit. Professional enough to think in routes instead of groceries. If Kinglet touched the paper, he would not be taking the walk back alone.

The pears blurred in front of her. Okay. Okay, think.

Kinglet was ten meters from the bench. He was still on the grey jacket. Still nowhere near the right problem. He was going to sit down, take the newspaper, and step straight into the route they were building for him.

Anya put the pear down. She'd been holding it for seventeen minutes and hadn't paid for it, but she also hadn't bitten it. So that was probably fine. It didn't matter. She started walking.

The first problem was communication. She had no earpiece to Kinglet. This was a passive surveillance assignment. She was there to observe the dead-drop and confirm the exchange, not to manage the handoff. She didn't have a signal protocol with him because she wasn't supposed to need one. The second problem was that she couldn't explain to anyone how she knew the contact was tailed, because the answer was *I read their minds* and that was a sentence she had never said out loud to anyone in her life.

So she'd improvise. She was bad at plans. She was genuinely good at the part where the plan was already ruined.

She cut between stalls, moving fast, angling toward the fountain. Behind her, Turnip Man's resolve was collapsing. *Maybe I won't mention the car. The car is a whole thing. If I bring up the car she'll throw the keys at my head.*

*Hands on the paper. Move.*

That one hit hard enough to kick her into a run.

She reached Kinglet just as he was bending toward the bench. She caught his elbow, laughed too loudly, and said, "Oh my God, I'm so sorry. I thought you were someone else. You look exactly like my friend's cousin. Wow, that's embarrassing." She gripped his arm hard enough to make the point.

His arm locked under her hand. *What? Who is she? Her hand is on my arm. This is not protocol. There is no contact protocol for this.*

"Don't sit down," she said through her smile. "Walk with me right now. You've got two on you."

He made the mistake of glancing toward the bench. Anya pinched the inside of his elbow hard enough to make him flinch and kept laughing like they'd just shared a private joke.

"No, really," she said at full social volume. "I cannot believe I did that."

He went white. Not a little pale. Actually white, the blood dropping out of his face like water through a drain. *This is not my job. This is not my job.* He was a courier. He filed paperwork and left newspapers on benches and occasionally met someone in a parking structure. The panic coming off him was high and thin enough that Anya had to push through it like wading in mud.

She kept the smile, kept the grip, and steered him north into the densest part of the market, where the stalls pressed close and the crowd moved slow.

*Who is she with him? Asset? North side. Go.* So they'd clocked her. Somewhere behind them another mind slammed into the flower stall and lost visual hard.

Anya did not need the full plan. North first, then a wide cut west if they lost visual. She turned left, pulling Kinglet into a narrow gap between a bakery stand and a man selling secondhand books who was thinking, with great bitterness, about a woman named Elke.

"Keep up," she murmured.

"What is happening?" His voice shook. He was twenty years older than her and his hands were trembling and hers were steady and she didn't have time to feel anything about that asymmetry right now.

*Blue canopy. Small woman, courier, north lane. Too tight in here. Take the west mouth. Catch them there.* The thought skimmed across her from somewhere back and left.

West exit. Anya went east.

"Why are we going this way?"

"Because they're expecting west."

"How do you know that?"

"I'm good at reading situations."

She pulled him through a row of vegetable crates. Behind them, the first tail had committed to the west exit, moving on the only clean guess he had left. He was good. He was also going to be standing at the wrong exit in about forty seconds.

The second tail was trying to close from the south, but the crowd had thickened. A knot of tourists had stopped to photograph a flower stall, blocking the aisle like a clot in an artery. Anya used the gap. She steered Kinglet past a display of jarred honey, through a service lane between stalls that smelled like overripe apples and cardboard, and out through a slit in the canvas onto the market's east-side service street.

Kinglet stumbled after her. "You said west."

"I said they expected west."

The service street ran behind the outer line of stalls, narrow and half blocked by crates and delivery carts. Anya turned north first, keeping the bulk of the market between them and the main street. At the next cross-lane she cut west behind a row of produce sheds, the tarps and stacked boxes hiding them from anyone waiting at the obvious exit.

A fruit cart sat crooked in the cut-through, wooden and heavy, loaded with late-season plums. A boy of about sixteen was leaning against it, thinking about a girl named Sofie with a level of romantic anguish that was almost operationally distracting.

Anya bumped the cart with her hip as she passed. Not hard. Just enough to shift it, send a cascade of plums tumbling across the cobblestones behind them, and turn the whole lane into a mess that anyone following would have to fight through.

The boy's panic detonated. *The plums. Oh shit, oh shit, the plums, my uncle is going to kill me.*

Behind them a second mind snagged on the mess. *Obstruction. Lost them. Could be anywhere back here.*

By the time the first tail reached the west exit, he was already too late. *She went east first. Looping. How did she know that?* The thought clipped her just as she and Kinglet came around the market's north-west corner and cut toward the street that fed into the diplomatic quarter.

Once they crossed into the Westalian mission's orbit, pursuit became a political problem no professional tail wanted to create. The men peeled off. They always did at that line.

Anya let go of Kinglet's arm.

Her head was throbbing. She'd been reading forty-plus minds at full intensity for almost twenty minutes, and the noise had been stacking up behind her eyes like pressure from a fever. Fatigue loaded her arms and legs with that wrong, boneless drag she only got from too much listening. She'd need to stop soon. Sit somewhere quiet. Let the noise thin.

But not yet.

Kinglet was leaning against a lamppost, breathing through his mouth. His jacket was wrinkled, the wrong jacket, and his forehead was slick. His thoughts were a wreck of relief and leftover terror. Under both sat one clean fact: he was never doing this again.

"You're fine," Anya told him. She pushed her hair back from her face, fingers brushing the familiar spot near her ears, and said it again. "You're fine. We're clear."

Kinglet dragged air into his lungs like he had to remember how. "The paper."

"Stays there."

"The film-"

"Is gone if you go back for it and worse than gone if you get seen trying." She kept her voice level because his panic was still loud enough to make her teeth hurt. "Walk north two blocks. Buy a newspaper you won't read. Circle once. Report the compromise from a clean phone."

He stared at her. *Who gave her authority to-*

"Move," she said.

He moved. He went north fast, shoulders up, trying to look like a man hurrying home instead of one who had nearly taken a surveillance team back to WISE.

The microfilm was still on the bench. The newspaper was unrecovered. That was a loss, but a recoverable one. The dead-drop site was burned now, but the intelligence could be rerouted through another channel.

Kinglet looked at her with an expression she'd seen before on people she'd pulled out of bad situations: gratitude layered over humiliation, the awareness that a woman twenty years his junior had just handled a crisis he'd frozen inside.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't use that dead-drop site again."

"I won't."

"And next time, wear the grey jacket. It doesn't wrinkle."

His thought flashed once, startled and small. *How does she know that?*

But she was already walking away, and he was already out of range, and she was very, very tired.

---

Forty minutes later, in a room that smelled like institutional carpet and a pot that had outlived any reasonable definition of freshness, Anya sat in the third row of a WISE briefing and tried not to read anyone's mind for five consecutive minutes.

She failed before she finished the thought.

*If this runs past six I'll miss the opening number. She's been practicing the Chopin for three weeks. She's got the dynamics down now. If I'm not there in the front row she'll look for me first.* Two seats to her left, Reinhardt was already halfway to the recital hall.

Somewhere else Brewer was trying to build dinner out of eggs, bread, and whether they were out of laundry soap. Directly behind Anya, because the universe kept making choices, Berg had reached the part of his crush where Sylvia adjusting her glasses counted as a private event. *The line of her jaw when she's making a point.* Anya had endured this since spring. She knew more about what Berg found attractive about Sylvia Sherwood than any person alive, including Berg, and she would have paid real money to stop knowing.

The briefing room was on the third floor of a building in the Westalian diplomatic quarter that, from the outside, looked like a modest commercial property. Inside, it was fluorescent lights and ventilation hum and the particular carpet that government offices shared across every nation and era, beige, resilient, hostile to personality. The pot on the credenza had been brewed that morning by Sylvia, who did not replace it before 1600 hours because wasting coffee offended a principle she had never articulated and that no one in the department had ever been brave enough to question.

Sylvia stood at the front of the room in a grey blazer and a collared shirt that together said *I am here to work and you should be too*. Reading glasses on the chain around her neck. Short grey-blonde hair that she cut, Anya suspected, at the same place on the same schedule every six weeks, because Sylvia did not adjust things that were functioning. Her face showed thirty years of difficult decisions made in rooms like this one. Whatever habit of apology she'd once had was long gone.

"The pattern is consistent with covert operational activity, not standard political maneuvering," Sylvia said. Her diction was precise. She did not use contractions when she briefed, and her sentences arrived pre-structured, situation, assessment, directive, as if she had formatted them before opening her mouth. "We are looking at a structured campaign inside Ostania's security apparatus. The scope suggests an organized effort with specific objectives and an active timeline."

She touched the map pinned to the briefing wall. Red markers clustered near the Ostanian government district, east of center.

"The operative or operatives are unknown. The purpose is unknown. What we do know is this: the activity pattern is consistent with pre-conflict positioning. Weapons procurement channels that had been dormant for several years have been reactivated. Military intelligence sharing between Ostanian departments has been restricted in a manner that suggests compartmentalization for a specific project." She paused, letting it breathe for exactly long enough to register as serious without tipping into theater. "We do not yet have a clear picture of the objective. But the indicators are serious enough to warrant dedicated investigation."

*I wonder if there'll be parking near the recital hall.* Reinhardt again, drifting on schedule.

Behind Anya, Berg had moved on to the way Sylvia held a pen.

From the back row came Falk, quieter than either of them and harder to shake once he got in. *Red pins on a wall, same quarterly panic. Sherwood means it, probably. The numbers still bend around whatever keeps the lights on.*

Falk sat near the wall in a cardigan that could have been brown or grey depending on the light. He was a senior analyst, easy to miss in a room like this if you weren't listening. Anya had heard variations of that complaint from older staff before. She let it pass and turned back to Sylvia.

"Forger." Sylvia's gaze found her with the directness of someone who did not scan a room but targeted a specific person. "You will take point on identification and assessment. Your diplomatic cover provides access to Ostanian state functions. Use it. I want a preliminary assessment within ten days."

Berg was still stuck on the fact that Sylvia always picked Forger for the exposed ones, equal parts admiration and resentment and entirely not Anya's concern.

"Understood," she said.

By then Reinhardt had moved on from parking to flowers and was fully inhabiting the evening ahead of him.

Anya bit the inside of her cheek and did not smile.

The briefing ended with Sylvia collecting her notes in the precise order she always collected them, map last, folded into thirds. People stood, gathered their files, began the small post-meeting exchanges that were half professional and half procrastination. Sylvia left the room without lingering, because Sylvia did not linger.

"Forger. Stay a moment."

Chairs scraped. Berg's attention snagged once more on Sylvia's hands as she stacked her notes; Reinhardt was already calculating recital traffic. When the room emptied, Sylvia set the folded map on the table and slid a thin packet across to Anya.

"Event calendars. Guest lists. Start with the rooms big enough to hide you," she said. "Smaller committees remember faces. State functions remember titles. I want patterns before I want conclusions."

Anya took the packet. "That's your way of making a room sound boring."

"It is my way of keeping you employed." Sylvia hooked her glasses onto the chain at her neck. "Foreign Affairs is hosting a state dinner later this week. Your cover gets you in without strain. Start there."

She tapped the packet with two fingers. "I want who circles whom, who leaves the room together, who stops talking when the wrong person arrives. If the room gives you gossip, bring me gossip. Gossip becomes structure faster than analysts like to admit."

"High floor to open on."

"High noise floor," Sylvia corrected. "If Briar is on security, you will be forgettable."

"And if the room offers a real lead?"

"You remember it," Sylvia said. "You do not improve it. I do not need improvisation at a diplomatic dinner."

"That seems hostile."

"It is practical."

During the briefing she'd caught the private line under Sylvia's public one: *Forger again. Untidy. Fast. Right. Loid had the same bad habit of moving before the forms caught up.* It was as close to affection as Sylvia's professional register permitted.

Anya stood and felt the headache from the market settle more firmly behind her right eye. The fluorescent lights were needles. She wanted, suddenly and badly, to be in a room with no one else in it. But her father was waiting for her at Cafe Linden, and there was nowhere within twenty meters of anywhere in Berlint that didn't have someone thinking in it.

---

Cafe Linden was a narrow room with dark wood tables and a window that hadn't been cleaned since the previous season. It occupied a side street between the diplomatic quarter and the residential neighborhoods south of center, close enough to WISE to be convenient, far enough to feel like somewhere else. Anya and Loid had been meeting here for two years. The waiter knew their order and had long ago stopped pretending he hadn't noticed they were always slightly too alert for a father and daughter meeting in public.

Loid was already seated when she arrived. His coffee sat untouched, a cherry strudel on the plate across from him, her favorite since she was eight. He never ordered one for himself. She'd asked about this once and he'd said he wasn't hungry, which had been a lie, because his thought at the time was *She runs too hard and she doesn't eat enough*, and the lie was so small and so fatherly that she'd let it sit.

He looked up when she came in. Forty-four. Blond hair with grey at the temples that had accelerated over the past two years in a way neither of them mentioned. Reading glasses folded beside his cup. He needed them for menus now, and for small print, and the first time she'd seen him put them on to read a menu had shifted her understanding of time in a way she hadn't been ready for. His face was kind in a way that was both genuine and the residue of a lifetime of cover identities, though by now the distinction between the real warmth and the performed warmth had dissolved so thoroughly that even Anya, reading him, couldn't always find the seam.

"How was the market?" he asked.

"Eventful." She sat down and pulled the strudel toward her. "Dead-drop was burned. Two tails on Kinglet, coordinated, somebody professional. I took him out through the east service lane and looped him around to the diplomatic zone."

His face didn't change. The hit of him did. *Two tails in a market. Alone?* Then the rest of him snapped into place around it: east lane was right, use the crowd, use the mission boundary, but alone? The spy's evaluation laid itself over the father's fear, not hiding it so much as giving it somewhere to stand.

"Smart route," he said. "Once you hit the diplomatic line, they were done."

"That was the idea." She took a bite of strudel. It was good. It was always good here.

"We'll reroute the recovery through an alternate channel. The microfilm isn't time-sensitive." He took a careful sip with the unhurried precision of a man who measured his gestures the way other people measured their words. "What about the new assignment?"

"Sylvia wants me in Ostanian rooms for the next ten days," Anya said. "Enough of them that if Yuri works security on the bigger ones, he's going to start seeing the same face too often."

He set the cup down. The gesture was careful, which meant he was interested.

*Yuri logs faces. Guest books. The same attaché twice and someone pulls the file. Start her big. Keep it social. She's twenty.* His thought moved fast through ministry floors, security rotations, reception lines, which rooms were crowded enough to hide her and which would make her stick.

"You'll need sustained access to Ostanian government functions," he said. "Your attaché cover is solid, but repeated presence at high-level events draws pattern recognition. Vary the attendance. Some cultural, some diplomatic, some social. Give them a profile that looks enthusiastic rather than strategic."

"Boring but visible."

"Exactly." A small smile, and then the thought behind it: *Sharp kid. Too young for those rooms. Can't follow her into any of them now.* He cut himself off there, hard enough that the silence of it landed in her.

She looked down at the plate and busied herself with her fork. This was the arrangement, though only one of them knew it existed. He worried about her in the privacy of his own mind, and she let him believe it was private, because telling him otherwise would mean explaining everything, and explaining everything was the one thing she had never been able to do.

"I'll be careful," she said. She was answering a thought he hadn't spoken, and she'd gotten very good at making that look like normal conversation.

He reached for the sugar dispenser, thought better of it, and pulled a paper napkin into the space between them instead. With the pen from his coat pocket he drew three small boxes in a row, then crossed out the first.

"Not the museum lunch," he said.

"Too small?"

"Too still. Ten people in a room that size remember everybody." He tapped the middle box. "Foreign Affairs dinner later this week. That is where you start. Ministers pull attention. Staff drift. A junior cultural attaché can move through a room like that without anyone building a file unless she gives them a reason."

"That's Sylvia's first choice too."

"Then Sylvia is right." He drew an arrow to the third box. "After that, one harmless room. A school grant reception. A museum board thing. Break the pattern before you go back into a government room."

Anya tore off a piece of strudel. "So Marta Kramer becomes deeply interested in exchange programs and chamber music."

"In public, yes." He slid the napkin a little closer to her. "Ask boring questions. Arts funding. Youth initiatives. Which orchestra toured where. People dismiss what they think is decorative."

"I hate using that."

"I know. Use it anyway."

"Also eat before you go."

She blinked. "That's operational advice?"

"It's advice." He glanced at the plate between them. "If you're reading half a ballroom, you do not want to do it on sugar and an empty stomach."

"I resent how specific that is."

"Then stop making it accurate."

She looked down at the boxes. "And if Yuri's on the door?"

"Give him one neat answer and no second puzzle." His tone stayed mild. "Invisible is wrong with officers like him. Invisible makes them curious. Boring is safer."

"That's depressing."

"It's useful."

He tapped the third box. "And do not wear the same thing twice in a row to the same cluster of officials. Pattern recognition does not need perfect memory. It needs one clerk with a stubborn face."

"Now you're doing fashion."

"I'm doing surveillance from the other end."

He turned the napkin and wrote three more words under the middle box: arrive late enough, leave early enough.

"Crowded rooms forgive timing if your reason is dull," he said. "Traffic. A prior reception. A headache. Do not be the last person still there when staff starts noticing who remains."

She nodded. "No lurking near ministers like someone obviously lurking near ministers."

"That would help." The corner of his mouth moved. "You get one real read per event. Do not chase a second because it feels available."

"That sounds personal."

"It sounds like a pattern in your work." He recapped the pen, then pulled it free again. "You do not have to win the room in one night."

He slid the napkin back toward her. "Try the door."

She looked at him. "Right now?"

"Right now." He tapped one of the boxes. "You are late to the Foreign Affairs dinner. Why?"

"Traffic."

"Everyone says traffic."

She exhaled through her nose. "The west lot was jammed and one deputy minister held up the car line because he didn't want to walk in dress shoes."

"Better. Reason for attendance?"

"Marta Kramer. Cultural desk. My office got an invitation and my supervisor hates these things."

"Keep the second half. Lose the first. Nobody at a door wants your biography unless you make them want it."

She tore off another piece of strudel. "You make bureaucrats sound like weather."

"That is because they are weather." He watched her chew. "Again."

She swallowed. "The cultural office got seats for the dinner. My supervisor sent me because she hates staying past dessert."

"Good. What do you do all day?"

"Paperwork nobody respects. School partnerships, visiting orchestras, translation grants, smiling at people who say culture like it's decorative trim."

He nodded once. "People stop listening halfway through that answer. Use it."

"That feels insulting to my fake job."

"Your fake job is keeping you alive."

She looked down at the napkin. "And if Briar asks a second question?"

"He might."

She opened her mouth with three answers already colliding in her head, then shut it again. Because men like Yuri did ask second questions. Because men like Yuri remembered faces. Because the wrong answer at the wrong door could turn one week of room-reading into an SSS pattern file before she'd even found what WISE was sending her in to find. None of that was sayable here. None of it was even fully provable yet.

"I give him one answer that's boring enough to write down," she said at last. "Then I stop helping."

Loid's thought came sharp and private. *Good. Do exactly that.*

"Fine," she muttered, because she was tired and because the right advice always irritated her most when it arrived carrying fear.

"That was not an order."

"That made it worse."

After that they worked through smaller details with the same stubborn precision: which coat looked diplomatic instead of memorable, how long to stay before an early exit became suspicious, which habits of Marta Kramer could safely read as ordinary incompetence instead of fabrication. Eventually the operational talk thinned. He mentioned security protocols at state functions, then Yor trying a new stew recipe and how she should come home for dinner soon, and the sentence was simultaneously a father's invitation and an operative's wellness check and she couldn't untangle the two, and she didn't want to, because the tangled version was the true one.

They sat for a while after that. The operational conversation wound down and a quieter version of them replaced it, the one that existed in the gaps between work, where he was just her father and she was just his daughter and neither of them was particularly good at knowing what to do with that.

"How's the apartment?" he asked.

"Fine. The radiator makes a noise."

"What kind of noise?"

"A sort of... ticking? When it comes on."

"That's the pipe expanding. It's normal." He said this with the absolute certainty of a man who had once maintained six separate cover identities across two countries and who now, in semi-retirement, channeled the same operational precision into home repair. "If it starts banging, that's a different issue. Call the building manager."

"Okay."

"Or call me. I can look at it."

"You'd drive forty minutes to look at my radiator."

"I'd drive forty minutes to make sure your radiator isn't going to rupture a pipe in the middle of the night, yes." His face was straight. His thought was not. *Living alone. Fine. Normal. She's twenty. People do this every day.* The thought blurred after that into a father's awareness that his daughter was out in the world and he couldn't follow her there.

"I'll call if it bangs," she said.

"Good." He picked up the cup, found it empty, and set it down again. "Your mother's making stew tonight. New recipe. You should come by sometime this week."

The way he said *new recipe* carried a warning she didn't need telepathy to decode. Yor's culinary experiments were acts of love that frequently crossed the line into acts of violence against ingredients. But the stew was real and the invitation was real and the kitchen would smell like burning and hope, and she missed it.

"Maybe Thursday," she said.

She paid because he always forgot to bring cash. He didn't forget. It was part of the routine, one of a hundred small rituals that had accumulated over fourteen years of a cover family that stopped being cover somewhere along the way.

Outside, Berlint was going amber. Early autumn evening, the sun low enough to turn the west-facing windows into sheets of dull gold. The air had a crisp edge, summer's warmth gone, winter still distant, the city held in the kind of cool that made you walk faster without deciding to. Leaves on the cobblestones. A tram rattling down Gartenstraße, its bell sounding twice as it rounded the corner.

Anya walked south toward home.

The thoughts came without effort. She was tired, and tired meant the filter she'd spent twenty years building was thinner. The range didn't shrink, but the noise got louder, less organized, a layered hum of voices she hadn't asked to hear.

A woman closing her shop pulled down the metal shutters in the same rhythm as *Elsa hasn't called. Tuesday, always Tuesday. The news said delays at the eastern checkpoints. If he got stopped or hurt they would call. Somebody would call.* Anya walked past and the worry thinned behind her.

A student with dead headphones came the other direction carrying conscription math like a fever. *If they bring conscription back, do university exemptions still count? Lehmann said yes, but Lehmann failed civics.* He was nineteen, maybe twenty, and already arguing with a letter that did not exist.

The shopkeeper two doors down from him was already in next month. *Another month like this and imported stock is gone. Frau Dittmar wants the order Friday. Korsch from the south maybe. If he can get through.* He rattled the handle twice after he locked it, as if the door could solve any part of it.

An old man on a bench gave her pigeons, cold, and Ilse in no order she would have chosen. Anya did not want that one either. She got it anyway.

She turned onto her own street. Quieter here, a residential block with three or four minds within range. A neighbor with the television too loud. A child on an upper floor, drawing, thinking the colors: *Green. No, the darker green. The one that looks like trees.*

Somewhere nearby, a dog was dreaming in short sensory pulses. Warmth. A hand. Food.

None of them knew she had already been inside their heads.

Anya stood on the sidewalk with her key in her hand. From the next building came a tired, careful thought. *After tea. Pills after tea. Not on an empty stomach this time.* Upstairs, the child settled on the darker green. The key teeth bit into her palm. She crossed to her building, got the door open, and went inside before the next stranger could say anything else in her head.
