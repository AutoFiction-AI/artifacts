# Chapter 12

At 05:58 the Communications Room sounded like three clocks losing patience at different speeds.

Fluorescent buzz. Receiver hiss. Someone two desks over turning a page too hard. Anya sat with the headset pressed to one ear and Falk's diplomatic procurement lane open in front of her, a thin green trace that had spent the last hour pretending to be ordinary. The note she'd written on the back of the electricity bill lay folded beside the keyboard, softened at the corners from how many times she'd touched it.

*watch, don't report*

Damian had said it like an operational correction, not advice. If Falk needed the desk blind for a reason, the useful thing was not to warn WISE first and let the reason disappear. Sit on the lane. See who used it.

At 06:00 the trace vanished.

Not degraded. Not noisy. Gone.

Anya straightened. "Run that back."

The duty analyst at the next terminal frowned at his board. "There's nothing to run back. Feed's dead."

"How dead?"

"Switched off, not dropped. That's clean."

Clean. Deliberate. Exactly on the minute. She kept the headset on as if the channel might decide to grow a voice out of static. Nothing crossed. No courier. No pickup. No useful mistake. Just the blind lane sitting there like somebody had lifted a floorboard and shown her the hole underneath.

The analyst glanced over. "Do you want me to flag it upstairs?"

Every instinct she'd been trained into said yes. Damian's voice, six hours old and still annoyingly solid, said no.

"Not yet," she said. "Keep the room on it."

He hesitated, then nodded.

By 06:11 the null had hardened into fact. Whatever was supposed to move through Falk's desk had not used the window because Falk had already done whatever he needed to do. Waiting longer would not give her proof. It would just make the salvage smaller.

She picked up the secure line. Sylvia answered on the second ring.

"Report."

"The lane went blind on schedule and stayed blind," Anya said. "Nobody touched it while we had eyes. If there's still anything clean in the packet, we need copied papers moving north before the search pattern tightens."

"Route."

"Kreuzfeld. Forged customs sleeve. West-side check-in by 2300 if the border's still lazy."

"If it is not lazy?"

"Then we find out tonight instead of tomorrow."

Paper rustled on Sylvia's end. "I can release the copy set and put Crane on it. You will return to your cover engagement this evening."

Of course she would. "Understood."

When she put the phone down, the dead trace was still dead. The room smelled like burnt coffee and hot wiring. Outside the narrow basement window the sky had turned the dull color of old tin.

She stayed long enough to watch the rest of the room wake up around the blind spot. Printers coughed to life. A junior analyst swore at a paper jam. Someone unwrapped a cheese sandwich that made the whole basement smell like onion. At 07:03 border confirmed Crane had the copy set and was moving north on forged customs papers. At 07:17 Sylvia sent a three-word follow-up over secure internal: HOLD COVER TONIGHT. By the time Anya finally stood up, the channel was still dead and the ordinary noise around it somehow made the silence worse.

* * *

The Blackbell estate glowed against the October dark. White lanterns lined the drive in a row so straight Anya could picture Becky stopping the car to fix one if it leaned. The topiary garden was lit from every flattering angle. Becky had confirmed photographers from three publications, which meant the effortless look on display had been built out of seating charts, florist threats, and at least one hang-up.

Anya stepped out of the car and the thoughts hit before the cold air did.

*If the valet scratches my paint, I'm going to lose it. I just had it detailed. I'm literally watching him.*

*She's wearing the Moreau. She's wearing the Moreau. I was told I was the only one with the Moreau.*

*Seven more handshakes. I can do seven. Maybe eight if I count the wife.*

Two hundred minds. She let them blur to a murmur and sorted for signals: security detail, two private guards, one off-duty SSS officer she recognized from the diplomatic reception, exit routes, and the running estimate of how long she could stay before Becky noticed if she slipped out early.

The answer, from a thought she'd caught three weeks ago during a phone call: *If she cancels on me one more time, I'm killing her. I'm not joking. I will actually kill her.* Becky had probably meant socially. The sentiment had been clear.

Damian came around the car. Dark suit, precise posture. The thin scar through his eyebrow was the only thing that didn't match the packaging. He hadn't said much on the drive over. The only useful thing he'd offered was a complaint about road checks north of the city, and the thought under it had translated cleanly: the SSS had pushed one Kreuzfeld inspection roster earlier than expected. If their courier was already on the road, earlier mattered. The mole was still unidentified. The Renewal Front's clock had shrunk to days. Attending a charity gala while all of that pressed at their backs felt obscene, but covers needed maintenance. Anya's social calendar had to look like a real person's social calendar.

"Ready?" she said.

"No."

"Still nothing from six?" he asked quietly.

"Nothing that helps."

His mouth tightened. "That's a terrible answer to get before a party."

*The things I do for East-West cultural exchange. Kirchner is going to corner me and talk about tariffs for forty minutes, and I'm going to smile until my face detaches.*

"It'll be fine," Anya said. "Becky throws a good party."

"You keep saying that."

"The food'll be amazing."

"That's what you said about the reception."

"And was I wrong?"

He paused. "You weren't entirely wrong."

*She wore that color before. The state dinner. Stop it. Trade policy. Think about trade policy.*

Anya walked toward the entrance and tried not to listen to what he was trying not to think.

Becky got to them in the foyer before the door had fully shut.

"You came!" She grabbed Anya by the shoulders, held her at arm's length, and frowned. *Mascara under one eye. Left earring fastened crooked. And she's doing the hair thing again. Something happened.* Becky swiped a thumb gently beneath Anya's eye, then straightened the earring with two quick fingers. "You actually came. I was going to be so mad if you bailed."

"I said I'd come," Anya said.

"You did your face in a moving car," Becky said under her breath. "That's never a good sign."

"Traffic."

"Same category."

"You said that about the gallery opening in August and then you texted me from a parking lot at eleven saying work blew up." Becky released her and turned to Damian with sudden, predatory interest. "And you brought a guest."

"Damian Desmond." She extended her hand. "Eden Academy. You cried at the eighth-grade science fair because your volcano didn't erupt."

*Oh no. She remembers the volcano. Of course she remembers the volcano.*

"There was a structural issue with the display," Damian started.

"It didn't erupt."

"The sodium bicarbonate ratio was off."

"Damian. It didn't erupt. I was there. You cried."

His public smile held, barely. "It was a frustrating afternoon."

*Kill me. Just kill me now.*

Anya bit the inside of her cheek.

Becky smiled, the real one, not the camera version. Her dark curls had been pinned into something that had probably required a second pair of hands and a prayer, and she was wearing a dress that probably cost more than Anya's monthly cover salary, but underneath all that she was still the girl who'd traded her lunch for Anya's dessert every day for a year at Eden and thought she was getting the better deal.

"I'm glad you're here," Becky said. The thought under it matched the words: *I'm glad. I'm glad she came.* "Both of you. Go eat, talk to people, enjoy yourselves. I have to deal with a crisis. They brought Riesling instead of Gruner Veltliner and I'm going to start a war."

She was already moving, heels sharp on the marble, thoughts skipping ahead of her.

*Check the wine table. Find Lila. She's here. Okay. She's actually here. Maybe tonight. Maybe tonight will feel real.*

Anya watched her go and felt the first pull of the evening settle behind her ribs, right where the bruise from the ambush had mostly healed and not quite vanished.

Inside, Becky had managed the impossible trick of making a donor gala feel like an Eden reunion with better tailoring. Old classmates kept spotting each other across the room and reacting with the same faces they'd worn at thirteen. Board members hovered near the logo wall pretending not to monitor who had been photographed with whom. Even the waitstaff moved like they'd been briefed that disappointing Becky Blackbell personally was a career-ending event. She'd even found a way to make nostalgia obey a seating chart. Place cards carried old house colors. The photo wall had been angled so nobody looked tired. Every part of the room said the same thing: Becky had built this on purpose, and everybody in it was cooperating whether they meant to or not.

Anya worked the room the way she'd been trained: peripheral scanning, threat assessment, social mapping. Tonight was supposed to be maintenance, not operations. She needed to be seen doing ordinary things: making small talk, circling the buffet, existing in public beside a friend who threw galas. Normal. Unremarkable. A cultural attache with a social life.

The problem was that Eden Academy alumni were everywhere.

Becky had been mining the old school network for years. Lawyers, diplomats, media producers, one person Anya was fairly sure had become a professional horse trainer. Every few feet another face clicked into place with a surface thought attached to it. *She still looks like that?* *Don't bring up the debate trophy.* *If Kessler says merger one more time, I'm leaving.* Childhood hadn't loosened its grip on anybody.

A woman in green intercepted Anya near the buffet. "Anya. From Miss Henderson's class. Do you remember me?"

*Please say yes. Please say yes. I rehearsed this in the car.*

"Of course. You sat next to the window. Third row."

"Yes. Oh my God." The relief in her head was immediate. *She remembers.* "I'm Petra. Petra Vogt. I'm in publishing now."

"That's great."

"What about you? Someone said you're with the diplomatic service?"

"Cultural attache. Mostly paperwork and events."

"That sounds amazing."

Petra's thoughts corrected the sentence for her. *Sounds boring, honestly, but she looks incredible. Is that silk?* Anya smiled, said they should catch up properly sometime, and escaped before either of them had to pretend they meant it.

Before she could make it to the terrace doors, Becky reappeared with the expression she wore when an event had slipped half an inch off center and everybody around her had become useless.

"No disappearing yet," Becky said. "I need one respectable photograph with the youth foundation board, because apparently the room only believes in charity if enough attractive alumni stand near the logo."

"You have photographers from three publications," Anya said.

"And not one of them can manufacture legitimacy out of thin air. Move."

She herded Anya and Damian toward a step-and-repeat set up by the musicians, where a photographer and three board members were arranging themselves into something that would read as spontaneous generosity in print. Otto Kirchner, silver-haired and pleased with himself, shook Damian's hand and launched directly into complaint.

"If anyone in Foreign Affairs asks," Kirchner said, "my logistics people lost half a day to customs because Kreuzfeld decided every truck needed to be turned inside out. Children's medicines do not improve in storage."

Damian's expression didn't change. "I'm sure the relevant offices have their reasons."

*Or somebody upstream decided the road mattered tonight.*

The photographer lifted his camera. Becky adjusted Anya by the elbow, adjusted Damian by the sleeve, then stood back and frowned the way she did when people were making her work harder than necessary.

"Closer," she said. "You look like you were assigned alphabetically."

They stepped half an inch toward each other. Flash. Another flash. Kirchner kept talking about manifests, delays, a refrigerated shipment stalled north of the city. Half of it was donor theater. Half wasn't. Kreuzfeld kept coming up, in Kirchner's complaint, in Damian's earlier thought, in the officer by the bar. The word had started following her around the room. WISE's courier was supposed to cross there before the tighter search rotation fully bit, papers hidden inside a customs sleeve that would look ordinary under a lazy inspection. If the inspection was no longer lazy, eleven o'clock would not be a routine signal. It would be the moment she found out whether the whole salvage plan had just died in line.

Becky examined the preview monitor, satisfied enough to stop tormenting them. "Great. You both look expensive and vaguely public-minded. Go circulate."

Near the bar, the off-duty SSS officer from the drive accepted a refill and laughed at a joke he wasn't listening to. Under the laugh his mind ran on a separate track. *Kreuzfeld moved to before dawn. Good. Let them hit wire instead of fog for once.*

The name cut through the room. Kreuzfeld was the northbound salvage, the copy set Sylvia had pushed through when the desk went blind. A courier should have cleared on forged customs papers, crossed to the west side, and signaled by eleven. Moving a roster on a Thursday night meant somebody expected movement, not paperwork, and "before dawn" meant whoever had planned that route might already be walking into brighter light than promised.

She found the buffet. Becky's money had bought the panic menu: gorgeous, expensive, too much of it. Anya loaded a small plate and drifted toward a table near the terrace doors. Damian had been absorbed into a group near the bar, dark suits and policy talk and a fog of young-diplomat thoughts trying to sound older than their owners. *Regional framework. Don't say tariff twice. If he asks about my posting, smile.* His thoughts surfaced through the crowd noise at intervals. *Bilateral trade framework, that's safe. Say that. Kirchner still parts his hair the same way. And if Kreuzfeld is tightening tonight, the courier with the forged papers is walking into a search line.*

That last line caught like a hook. She didn't look at him. She took a bite of goat cheese on toast and chewed slowly while the operational clock, which had been background pressure all evening, moved up a notch.

10:17. Forty-three minutes until the check-in that was supposed to tell her the salvage route had worked. Forty-three minutes of donor smiles, alumni chatter, and pretending she wasn't listening for the first sign the night had gone bad.

A waiter offered champagne. She took a glass and didn't drink from it. The glass was a prop, an excuse for her hands. Across the room, a retired diplomat was telling the same customs story he told at every event. Two tables over, an elderly woman was planning the argument she intended to have with her husband when they got home. *If he says one word about my mother, I swear to God...* Anya tuned away before she got the rest.

They were careful with each other in public. They had been careful since the safe house, since the bruised ribs and split eyebrow and the quiet after, since the line they'd crossed and then, by mutual unstated agreement, backed away from without deciding which side they meant to live on. Here, in a room full of people who knew one or both of them, careful was the only option. It also weighed a ton.

Once, near the buffet, their paths crossed. Damian reached for a napkin. Anya reached for a second goat cheese toast.

"The cheese is good," she said.

"I noticed you're eating all of it."

"It's strategically important cheese."

He almost smiled. *Don't smile. You're smiling.* "I'll leave you to your strategy."

He moved away. The almost-smile stayed in her chest longer than it should have, and she hated that, because her brain should have been on the mole, on Kreuzfeld, on the clock, on anything except the exact way his mouth moved when he was trying not to be amused.

Then somebody found Becky's nostalgia trap.

She'd set a side table near the bar with stacked Eden yearbooks, a leather binder of archival photo reprints, and a rented carousel projector clicking school pictures onto the cream wall by the terrace doors. Lila, one of Becky's media contacts and an Eden alumna, had apparently decided this counted as public service.

The wall filled with a yearbook class photo first: thirty children in Eden blazers, squinting at the photographer with expressions ranging from dutiful to mutinous.

"Oh my God," Lila said. "Look at us."

Nostalgia spread fast and got less accurate by the minute. People abandoned drinks to crowd around the table and wall. Somebody flipped through yearbooks. Somebody else dug through the binder. The projector clicked from sports day to the end-of-year ceremony to a class picnic where half the boys looked seasick. Adults laughed at the evidence that they had once been tiny and ridiculous.

Anya watched from the edge of the cluster and felt her stomach tighten.

Erich Kessler materialized at her table with his third beer and the comfortable posture of a man who had found his equilibrium several drinks ago. Erich ran logistics for his family's distribution company. His surface thoughts gave her the rest: resentment over his older brother's recent promotion, and a homesick gratitude for the one place where people had talked to him before they learned his last name mattered.

*Good to be here. Good party. I miss this. I miss when we were all in the same building and nobody'd figured out who was going to matter and who wasn't.*

"Anya." He dropped into the chair across from her, grinning. "From Eden, right? Becky's friend?"

"That's me."

"You look exactly the same." He pointed at his own head. "The hair. I'd recognize it anywhere. Nobody else had pink hair. You were like the one bright flag in the whole school."

*Pink hair. Class photo. Second row. She sat behind me in Mr. Kraft's class.*

"I've been told it's distinctive."

"Distinctive? It's a landmark. I used to navigate the hallways by your head."

She laughed. The laugh was real enough. Erich was harmless enough. For half a minute it was just a party.

Then he leaned over the nostalgia table, pawed through the clear sleeves in the binder, and let out a delighted noise. "No. Becky is evil for this. Lila, put this one up."

The projector clacked. The wall filled with an Eden charity bazaar planning committee from the year Becky had decided twelve-year-olds should save a pediatric ward through sheer force of personality. A folding table vanished under ribbon spools, raffle books, paper flowers, and a dented cash box. Becky stood in the middle pointing at somebody outside the frame like a tiny dictator. Damian was at one end with a clipboard. Anya was at the other with scissors in one hand and a face that clearly objected to the concept of volunteer labor.

She remembered glue drying on her fingers. Becky assigning jobs in a voice even the teachers had obeyed. Damian getting handed the tally sheet because Becky said his handwriting looked trustworthy enough for money.

"Oh, this is incredible," Erich said. He slapped a yearbook open beside the projector and pointed his beer at the wall. "Desmond!"

He called it loudly enough to turn heads. Damian looked over from his conversation.

"Erich." Smile. Measured. "Good to see you."

"Come look at this. Charity committee. She had us doing unpaid labor before puberty."

Damian crossed the room. His thoughts shifted as he moved. *Of course Becky kept this. Of course Erich found it now.*

Erich kept talking, beer-warm and easy, one hand on the yearbook, one on the beer. He looked at the projection. He looked at Anya. He looked down at the caption block under his thumb.

"Wait." His whole face lit with the loose clarity alcohol stripped him down to. "This is the funniest possible thing. Becky has seriously been throwing the same event her whole life." He jabbed the page at Damian. "Look. That's you, that's me, that's Becky, and that's Anya Forger pretending not to murder us over raffle tickets. Same face and everything."

The name landed in the room like a glass hitting tile.

Anya Forger.

A man on his third beer had said it out loud at a party while grieving his own vanished importance and having no idea what he'd just done.

And then something in Damian's mind locked hard enough she felt it in her teeth.

*Forger.*

The yearbook page under Erich's hand. The girl in the photo box in his apartment. The woman six feet away in silk with a champagne glass she hadn't touched.

*Anya Forger.*

Not resemblance. Not the same laugh, the same tilt of the head, the same almost-memory. Her.

Anya's stomach dropped hard enough to feel like a missed step. The photo in Damian's apartment, the one he'd kept tucked away in that childhood box, flashed through her head with humiliating clarity. Becky had done this without knowing she'd done it, built a whole room out of the past and invited them both to stand in it.

His expression didn't change. Twenty years of political upbringing and Desmond discipline held the line. His face produced a half-smile. His hand stayed steady around his glass.

"That was a long time ago," he said, voice perfectly even.

*Don't do this here. Not in Becky's house. Not with cameras.*

Erich was still going. "And Becky still has the same staffing model, apparently. Child labor and threats."

"I think you're reading a yearbook with beer in your eyes," Anya said.

She heard her own voice from a distance, bright and easy and built for exactly this kind of deflection. "This is why archivists shouldn't serve alcohol."

"Maybe," Erich said, squinting down at the caption. *Tiny print. Why is the print this small?* He took a drink. "Or maybe Becky is committing typographic crimes. Same difference."

He moved on. The cluster shifted back toward the wall, the binder, the yearbooks, the harmless pleasure of finding out the tallest boy in class had become the shortest man in the room.

Damian did not move on.

He stood where he'd been, glass untouched.

*Not here. Think first.*

His eyes found hers across the room. Half a second. Less.

Then somebody beside him asked a question about wine. He answered without missing a beat.

*Later.*

Anya set her champagne glass down before she dropped it. The music slid into a waltz. Erich turned another page in the binder. Around them the party kept moving.

She pressed her fingertips into the tablecloth until the weave marked her skin, then headed for the terrace doors. Not fast. Fast would've looked like panic.

Crossing the ballroom took forever. Kirchner had Damian by the cuff again, still talking about customs exemptions with the damp intensity of a man who mistook access for friendship. Becky was laughing at a joke she clearly hated because a camera was pointed at her. Erich had already found another audience for his nostalgia. Everybody else was fully occupied with the ordinary business of a rich party, which made the single locked thought in Damian's head feel even louder.

He looked up once as she slipped toward the doors. His face stayed perfectly diplomatic. The thought under it did not.

*Later. Not here. Think first.*

She kept walking because looking back would count as an answer.

* * *

The terrace was colder than she'd expected. The party burned warm behind the glass doors, but out here the October air had finally given up pretending to be kind. Wide flagstones, wrought-iron railing, a view of the grounds that disappeared into black after about twenty feet. In daylight the garden probably looked expensive. Tonight it looked like a place you could vanish into.

She gripped the railing. The cold metal helped. It was an object, a boundary, a fact.

Damian was still inside, at the edge of her range. His mind kept striking the same points and sliding off them. *Forger.* *Not here.* *If Kreuzfeld tightened before dawn, the courier is already late.*

If border traffic hit now, she'd need a secure line, a reason to vanish, and more than thirty seconds before somebody came looking for her. The bag at her wrist felt heavier than it was.

The pager had not moved yet, but she kept feeling the outline of it against her skin anyway, as if her body already knew the night was about to get worse.

"You look like you're about to climb over."

Becky appeared in the doorway, shoes in one hand, wine in the other. At some point during the evening she'd taken her hair down. The engineered updo had surrendered to gravity and humidity, and the loose curls made her look less like a magazine spread and more like the girl Anya had known since kindergarten.

"I'm fine."

"That's not even convincing fake-fine." Becky came out barefoot and leaned on the railing beside her. "You're doing the thing."

"I'm not doing a thing."

"You're doing the thing where you go quiet and stare at the middle distance like it insulted you personally. You've been doing it since we were six. I know the thing."

*She's not fine. Did someone say something in there? Did Damian say something? If he did, I'll throw him into a hedge.*

Anya said nothing.

They stood for a while. The party murmured on the other side of the glass. Below them, somewhere in the garden, an irrigation system clicked on and started spraying arcs of water across the dark lawn.

"Good event," Anya said at last.

"It's fine." Becky sipped her wine. "The Riesling disaster is over. The shellfish disaster is over. The Brahms disaster is not."

"The Brahms disaster?"

"The quartet keeps playing that one miserable piece that makes donors remember mortality. I asked for upbeat."

"Did you give them sheet music?"

"I gave them a mood board."

"A mood board. For a string quartet."

"It was a very clear mood board."

Inside, the quartet finally gave up on the funeral music and found something quicker.

Becky's thoughts shifted. The traffic about caterers and donors and musicians dropped away. Under it was the heavier thing she'd been carrying all evening, tucked under timing and hostess energy, waiting for a moment when she could stop being watched.

*Just say it. Say it wrong if you have to.*

"Can I tell you one real thing?" Becky asked.

"Yeah."

"You're my best friend." Becky winced before Anya could answer. "No, that's not, I mean obviously that's true, but I didn't come out here just to announce friendship like I'm drunk at prom. I broke up with Marc."

Anya turned toward her. "When?"

"Six days ago. Five. I don't know. Tuesday was in there somewhere and now I can't stop thinking about Tuesdays, which is insane." Becky rubbed at one eye with the heel of her hand. "You know what I do on Tuesdays? When nothing's on the calendar? I eat cereal for dinner. I watch cooking shows. I talk to my cat. That's the real humiliation, by the way. The cat knows everything."

Despite herself, Anya almost smiled. "That is pretty bad."

"I told Marc that once and he did this face like I'd said something adorable on purpose." Becky stared out into the garden. "Then I started talking about that documentary I want to do, the eastern-district one, with the artists nobody covers unless there are famous people in the room, and he said, 'That's great, babe,' and asked what we were doing Saturday."

She took a drink, swallowed, then shook her head.

"No, that's not fair. It is fair. I don't know." Her laugh came out thin and wrong. "He wasn't mean. That's the part making me crazy. He sent orchids on my birthday. He said he was proud of everything I'm building. Building what? A guest list?"

She rubbed the stem of the glass between two fingers and looked annoyed at herself for even needing to explain it. "I kept meaning to call you, and every time I picked up the phone I could hear myself about to do the polished version. The funny version. Like, surprise, rich girl problem, man admired the brand and missed the person. I couldn't stand hearing it come out that way."

The sprinkler clicked through another rotation below them.

"We'd been sleeping together for three months and he still talked about me like he'd skimmed a profile." Becky dragged a hand through her loose curls and stopped there, gripping the back of her neck. "He didn't want me. He wanted the version that gets you into rooms."

The sentence sat between them.

Becky looked down at her wine. "And I almost did it to you just now. Came out here and almost gave you the room version because I don't know how to stop doing it."

The truth pushed at Anya's teeth. She had been hearing the private version since kindergarten.

Her pager vibrated against the inside of Anya's bag, hard enough to tap Becky's bare wrist. Two short pulses. One long. Border channel.

Anya didn't move. Becky went on, quieter now. "You're the only person I don't have to do that for."

She slid her hand into the bag and shielded the tiny screen with her palm. `KZF 2300 MISSED. RETURN SECURE ASAP.`

The railing bit into the scar in her left palm. Becky was still leaned into her shoulder.
