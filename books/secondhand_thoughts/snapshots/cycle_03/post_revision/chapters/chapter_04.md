# Chapter 4

Eight days after Damian walked out of the arts reception with the Westalian mission address in his pocket, Anya was back in the Foreign Affairs Ministry under another name. His promised forum details had reached her the next morning in one dry official note, scheduled for the following Thursday, and there had been no contact at all between that note and this room. This was that forum at last. Tonight the badge on her jacket said ANNA KESSLER, because this room liked full names and department lines and last names that matched the guest list. At the gallery he'd gotten Anya, easy and informal, because the cultural exchange people treated first names like a policy instrument. The state dinner had been a third card for a third room. If anybody from either government ever laid the badges side by side, they'd stop laughing at the bureaucracy and start asking harder questions.

Not tonight. Later was how damage liked to arrive.

The deputy minister had been murdering tariff reform for forty straight minutes when Brandt slid into the empty seat beside Damian and bent his head in a way that looked polite from a distance and predatory from where Anya sat. Most of the audience had the dead-eyed patience of people being held hostage by their own careers, and the abandoned translation headsets on the back table had already knotted themselves into a little plastic nest of surrender. Damian still had his diplomatic posture on, one ankle hooked back under the chair, folder balanced on one knee, but the line of his shoulders had gone half an inch stiffer the second Brandt sat down.

*...if Dreher says framework again I'm going to bite somebody...*

*...don't forget potatoes. He'll complain all through dinner if there aren't potatoes...*

*...who put dill on salmon in October...*

Then Brandt's surface thoughts came through hard enough to lift gooseflesh on Anya's arms.

*...security meeting again. Not on the invite list. Naval attaché Monday, depot office Wednesday, now this. Not random.*

So that was how the night was going.

She kept her eyes on the podium and listened sideways. Brandt was tall, blond, thinning up top, one of those men who dressed as if every room were a chance to be taken for slightly more important than he was. His badge read BRANDT, K. Ministry of the Interior, Policy Liaison. She already knew that from the seating packet. Knowing it from his own head felt worse.

Damian didn't turn fully toward him. Publicly he could make indifference look courteous.

"If this is about the revised border brief," he murmured, "it can wait until the minister finishes."

*...make him say it twice. Make him commit to the lie in public.*

Brandt smiled with only one side of his mouth. "This isn't about the brief. I was curious about the smaller meeting upstairs. The one that didn't make it onto the calendar."

Damian let half a beat pass. "A lot of meetings don't make it onto public calendars."

"Not all of them include a naval attaché."

Two rows ahead, somebody coughed into a handkerchief. The deputy minister droned on about harmonized customs language. Nobody was listening. The room had thinned into private miseries and dinner plans and one man trying very hard to remember whether he had actually signed a school permission slip or merely thought about signing it. Anya caught all of it in smears. Brandt cut through anyway.

*...say procurement. See if he flinches.*

"Then perhaps I should be more direct," Brandt said softly. "You seem interested in procurement questions that sit a little outside your remit."

Damian moved one thumb along the edge of his folder. That was all. His face stayed easy.

"I work in Foreign Affairs. Everything is outside my remit until it isn't."

A couple of people near the aisle laughed, because they thought he was making a joke about ministries. Brandt did not.

*...there. Smooth little bastard.*

Anya looked down at her notes as if tariff codes deserved the full devotion of her pen. Her pulse had already climbed. She could let Damian handle it. He had handled worse. He was better at this than she was. He had grown up in rooms full of people trying to peel information off him with their teeth.

Then Brandt thought Gessler.

*...take it upstairs tonight. Gessler will want the access log and every room Desmond's touched this week.*

That made the problem bigger.

If Gessler pulled access logs, he'd get Damian's closed-door meetings. That alone was bad. Stack enough odd rooms into one week and anybody who loved paper more than people started seeing pattern. Anya didn't know the full shape of what Damian was doing. She knew it got harder fast once somebody upstairs started drawing lines between doors.

Anya closed her notebook.

A ministry aide was moving down the side wall with a tray of sweating water glasses and the blank expression of a man dissociating professionally. The minister finally stopped speaking and invited questions. Chairs shifted. Brandt put one hand on the back of Damian's seat as if he meant to keep him there through the break.

That was enough of that.

Anya stood, gathered her packet, and crossed the room before common sense could stage an intervention. If she took two minutes to think about it, she might take five. Five was Brandt walking Damian into a smaller conversation with fewer witnesses.

She stopped beside them with her mission smile already in place.

"Mr. Desmond. Hello again."

Brandt's hand came off the chair at once. Damian looked up. For a second the surprise on his face was unhidden enough to be human before the public version came back over it.

"Ms." He glanced at her badge. "Kessler."

"Anna Kessler, formally." She gave a little apology to the air. "Last week was much more civilised."

*Anna now. Last week it was Anya. Fine. Different room.*

Good. At least he was tracking the same problem.

Brandt's eyes moved over her face, badge, folder, and back again.

*...Westalian mission. Convenient.*

"I don't think we've met," Anya said, turning to him before he could ask the question that would hurt. "Anna Kessler. Cultural office at the Westalian mission."

"Brandt." His handshake was dry and brief. "Interior."

"I'm so glad you're interior and not commerce." She shifted her packet into her left arm and made herself sound relieved enough to be annoying. "I've been trying to find the person responsible for the Velden freight corrections and Commerce keeps acting like the corridor numbers are folklore. Do you know who handles the cross-posted manifests when exhibition cargo gets counted once at the border and again when customs bounces it for documentation?"

The trick was not cleverness. Cleverness got remembered. The trick was paperwork dense enough that people wanted out.

Brandt blinked.

Damian looked at her like he realized she was either saving him or digging a second trap under the first one.

"Commerce handles trade numbers," Brandt said.

"I know, but then they said methodology and sent me to transport, and transport said if the trucks originated under cultural waiver then foreign liaison had to sign off, and foreign liaison is apparently you people tonight? Or someone adjacent to you?" She opened the packet as if it contained proof of injury. "We've got a touring exhibition sitting on three separate inventories and every office says the duplicate line is somebody else's duplicate line."

That part was almost true. Some poor exhibit probably was sitting in a warehouse somewhere because governments loved paper more than paintings.

Brandt did not take the bait so much as get webbed by it.

*...Velden. Public problem. Could still be coincidence. Could still be cover.*

"I don't handle inventories," he said.

"Right, but you know who does."

"No."

"You don't know or you don't want to tell me?"

He gave her the look bureaucrats reserved for women they considered mild inconveniences. Anya had worn worse looks to worse rooms.

Behind him Damian got slowly, beautifully blank.

*...she's doing this on purpose.*

Good. Keep up.

A cluster of ministry staff started filing toward the coffee urns at the back. People rose around them. Brandt could have walked away. Anya moved half a step to follow before he made up his mind.

"Everyone keeps saying the word audit at me like that's a magic spell," she said. "I don't need magic. I need one person with a stamp who'll admit the numbers are wrong."

Brandt tried to angle his shoulder past her. She pivoted with him.

"Was it transport or procurement that changed the routing code last spring?" she asked. "Because if it was procurement then I need a different complaint entirely."

There it was: procurement.

His expression barely changed, but the thought landed hard.

*...Jesus Christ. Even Westalian arts girls are saying procurement now.*

He did not like that. Good.

Damian took pity on him then, or on himself. It was hard to tell.

"Ms. Kessler," he said, smooth as polished brass, "surely this can wait until the break is over."

"No, because if I don't pin down the office tonight then tomorrow I'll get three new answers and one of them will be wrong in a more official font."

That got a real noise out of a man by the coffee urn. Not a laugh exactly. Close enough.

Brandt's patience frayed a little at the edges.

*...Desmond comfortable with her. Not good. Noted.*

He looked back at Damian. "You're unusually popular."

"With cultural administration?" Damian said. "Always."

"If only." Anya offered Brandt a page from the packet before he could leave. It was the printed agenda. "Would you mind circling whoever I should annoy next?"

He did not take the paper.

"Commerce," he said again.

"Commerce is hiding from me."

"Then perhaps that's instructive."

"It isn't, though. It's rude."

Brandt actually closed his eyes for one brief second. When he opened them, his smile had teeth in it.

"I have another meeting."

"Of course you do," Anya said brightly. "Would tomorrow be better if I sent a note?"

"No."

"Great. Then I'll keep guessing."

He walked off with the stiff-backed speed of a man who knew that if he stayed another five seconds he'd say something professionally regrettable.

Anya let him go three steps before she brushed his thoughts again.

*...Dreher first. Gessler after. Pull the access log. Check Kessler against the calendar. Then Desmond. Tuesday should be enough.*

Enough for what on Tuesday?

There wasn't enough room to grab it cleanly. There was too much other noise. He was already threading into the coffee crowd and the room crashed back over him in salt, boredom, bad coffee, school pickups, a blister on someone's heel, three separate resentments about the deputy minister, and one woman privately wondering if divorce papers counted as formal wear if they were still in her handbag. Anya's temple pulsed.

It could wait. It wasn't gone. It had only been shoved half a step down the line.

She turned back. Damian was on his feet now. Up close he looked composed in the usual way and furious in the smaller ones. One hand had gone to his folder hard enough to crease the paper inside.

"Ms. Kessler," he said. "You have very strong feelings about freight accounting."

"I'm developing them."

"At speed."

"That's one of my gifts."

His mouth almost moved. Not a smile. The outline of one aborted on principle.

*...not only cultural office. Not even close.*

He angled himself so the room couldn't read the conversation off his face.

"There's a service corridor behind the east stairwell," he said softly. "Five minutes."

"That sounds ominous."

"It isn't a suggestion."

Then he walked away before she could answer, threading back toward the front with the easy, trained indifference of a man who had not been publicly rescued by a woman using exhibition inventories as a weapon. By the time he reached the podium, he had already tilted himself toward a deputy minister. Public Damian had zipped back up.

Anya stood where he left her long enough to look like somebody deciding between water and bad coffee. Her fingers had started the tiny post-adrenaline shake they always threatened after a move she had not fully thought through.

Tuesday should be enough.

Whatever enough was, Brandt thought he could prove it by Tuesday.

She took a glass off the passing tray and drank because her mouth had gone dry. The water was warm.

*...Marta will kill me if I don't text before six...*

*...if Desmond asks for another annex I am transferring departments...*

*...that girl's badge says Kessler. Wasn't it Keller on the seating chart? No, Kessler. Read properly, idiot.*

Anya set the glass down and waited out the five minutes without looking at Damian once.

---

The corridor behind the east stairwell smelled like cleanser, old metal, and the wet rubber lip of the mop bucket abandoned against the far wall. One strip light buzzed overhead. Pipes ran along the ceiling with brown paint flaking off at the joints. Somewhere deeper in the building a door banged and footsteps faded.

Damian was already there.

His tie was loosened. His jacket was still on. His hands were in his pockets, which with him meant he wanted them anywhere else.

*...don't start angry. Figure her out first. Then be angry.*

Too late for the second part.

"So," he said.

"So."

The strip hissed.

He looked at her badge. "Is it always a different name?"

"Only when the room demands it."

"That's not an answer."

"It is. It's not the whole answer."

His gaze stayed on her face a fraction too long.

*...same eyes. Stop that.*

That hit weirdly low in her stomach and she shoved it aside. That was not the emergency currently in progress.

"Try again," he said. "Who are you really?"

That was the dangerous version of the question because the truthful answer had too many parts. WISE operative. Cultural attaché. Liar. Telepath. Girl from Eden he almost remembered. None of them fit cleanly beside a mop bucket.

"I am Anna Kessler in rooms that need Anna Kessler," she said. "I'm Anya in rooms that don't. And I work for more than the cultural office."

His mouth changed shape and went still.

"Intelligence."

"Yes."

"WISE?"

There was no point pretending not to understand. "Westalian intelligence," she said. "That's as specific as I'm going."

He absorbed that with the stillness he used when he wanted someone else to fill the silence for him. She did not. Barely.

*...WISE. Or she wants me to think WISE. Why step in. Why now.*

"Brandt," he said at last. "How did you clock him?"

"I got close enough."

"To what?"

"To him. To the conversation. To the room."

"That is three different evasions."

"Yes."

His stare sharpened. "You're not very good at this part, are you."

"Evasion?"

He waited.

"No," she said. "I'm not."

That slowed him for half a beat. Good. True answers in the wrong places made people reset.

He took one step closer, not aggressive exactly, more like he was closing the corridor down to the part they occupied.

"He mentioned a meeting upstairs that wasn't on the calendar," Damian said. "He mentioned procurement without saying the word until you said it for him. He has a superior named Gessler who apparently gets access logs. You knew all of that fast. Too fast."

"I heard enough."

"How."

Her mouth almost outran her. She could feel it line up wrong.

"Because he was practically shouting it in his head." The sentence was out before she could stop it.

She bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to get copper. That was a terrible answer.

"Because he was obvious," she said instead, the swerve ugly and late. "His face. His tone. The questions. Come on."

Damian's expression changed in the smallest possible way. Not because he believed her. Because he had watched the correction happen.

He didn't say that. He didn't need to.

"You approached me last week under one name," he said. "Tonight you appear under another. You knew enough to keep Brandt pinned. You also knew the name Gessler mattered." His voice stayed quiet, which made it worse. "So let's save time. Did your people put you on me after the gallery?"

That was almost the truth. The actual truth was she had put herself on him, and then falsified the report that should have done the opposite.

"My people were already watching some of the same mess," she said. True at the edges. Not enough in the middle. "When Brandt started leaning, I moved."

Damian looked at her for a long moment.

*...too fast. Still doesn't account for Gessler.*

No, it didn't.

He breathed out through his nose. "All right. Then let's work from the part I can use."

That was not trust. It was triage.

He leaned one shoulder back against the pipe behind him, as if he needed the cold, ugly thing there.

"Gessler is deputy director on the internal review side," he said. "He isn't Front leadership. He's worse for me than that. He likes paper. If Brandt takes him a pattern and Gessler starts checking my room entries against closed committee minutes, every quiet conversation I've had in this building gets louder."

That was specific enough to hurt.

"So you were right to interrupt."

A beat.

"I don't enjoy saying that."

"You're welcome."

His eyes flicked up. That might have been the wrong level of lightness. Might have been fine. Hard to tell with him.

"What exactly do you think you saved me from?" he asked.

"Tonight?" Anya folded her arms because if she didn't, her hands would start doing too much. "Brandt wanted your meetings lined up in a neat row for Gessler. He was already halfway to thinking you weren't only curious. He was thinking pattern. Once bureaucrats get to pattern, they never stop until they've built a shrine to it."

Damian's gaze dropped briefly to her left hand where it was tucked under her right elbow. His own hands stayed in his pockets.

*...she talks like field, not desk.*

Good. Fine. Whatever kept him from asking the one question she couldn't survive.

"I don't use the name you're about to say," he said.

She tipped her head. "Renewal Front."

His mouth flattened.

"There it is."

"If we're pretending that's not what this is, then we both wasted the walk."

Something tightened in his face and then gave a little, like she had finally hit the right locked door instead of all the decorative ones around it.

"My father left—" He stopped. Started again from a different angle. "There's a standing procurement authorization that runs through the Defense Liaison Office. My father's signature is on the original. He hasn't touched it in years and it's still disbursing because nobody in this building wants to be the person who admits the contract was never about border security." His hand came out of his pocket, made a vague gesture at the pipes, went back. "The Front didn't build anything new. They moved into what was already running. That authorization. The committee seats at the Gesellschaft Club. Procurement desks that only exist because my family's name was on the appointment memo."

"Brandt?" she asked.

"I don't know yet." His eyes cut past her shoulder and came back. "Useful to somebody. Gessler definitely knows which doors still open with the old keys." He looked past her shoulder at nothing. "The point is it's not—" He caught himself, started over. "It's not ideology. Or it is, on the surface, but underneath that it's invoices. Checkpoint staffing contracts. Emergency appropriations where somebody has to supply the emergency first. People who got comfortable when the border was hot and need it hot again because cold borders don't pay."

"And you're—"

"Trying to shut it down," he said, cutting across her before she finished. "Or slow it down. I don't know." He scrubbed a hand over his mouth and caught himself doing it. "Anything."

The pipe behind him knocked once, sharp enough to make both of them glance at it. A secretary passed the far end of the corridor without looking in. Her thoughts brushed Anya for a second and vanished into grocery lists.

Damian waited until the footsteps were gone.

"So yeah," he said, quieter. "That's what I'm doing here."

"Okay."

His eyebrows went up. "That's it?"

"What do you want me to say? I got between you and a ministry liaison. I'm not going to pretend I don't get it."

He breathed out through his nose. Not quite a laugh.

"That isn't the part that should be easy for you."

No. It wasn't. The easy part was him. The impossible part was her side of the wall.

She touched two fingers to the side of her head, then dropped her hand when she realized what she was doing.

"The easy part is knowing Brandt is a problem," she said. "The hard part is being in this building at all."

"Because?"

Because Sylvia had believed her. Because the report was in the system. Because she was now in a mop-smelling corridor cutting a deal with the man she'd told WISE not to treat as urgent. Because every correct move she made from here deepened the wrong one already on paper.

"Because the room has ears," she said instead. "Because yours does. Because mine does. Pick one."

He watched her closely enough that she could feel the weight of it on her skin.

*...too much how. Too much from where. God, what are you actually hiding.*

Everything.

"What do you want?" he asked.

"Right now? Brandt not walking upstairs with your week in his pocket." She uncrossed her arms. The rest came out too fast once it started. "After that, I want a lane. Not a partnership. Not trust. Not whatever face you're making. I mean if I hear a name, a room, a date, a move that puts you in front of him again, I pass it. If you hear I'm about to walk into the same trouble twice, you pass it. Shared intelligence, limited, boring, ugly, and small enough to kill if it turns rotten."

He stared at her. "You just packed three arguments into one breath."

"I didn't say any of it well."

"You also skipped the part where I agree."

"Yes," she said. "I noticed that while I was saying it."

"You say lane and shared intelligence like we're already on the same side."

"No. I said Brandt." She grimaced. "That came out wrong. I mean we're in the same mess for the next few days whether we like it or not."

He went quiet. In the silence his surface thoughts sharpened instead of disappearing.

*...recruitment. Leverage. Maybe bait.*

*...Father let one Westalian inside. Three dead. Don't be stupid.*

There it was again. It wasn't only reflex. It was a wound.

Anya kept her face still.

"If this is recruitment," he said, "say it plainly."

"It isn't." She heard how thin that sounded. "Or if it is, I'm doing a terrible job of it. I'm not here with a folder and a speech. I'm trying to keep Brandt from handing your week to Gessler before either of us can do anything with it."

"Limited how."

"Names. Dates. Meetings. Movement if either of us gets it. Warnings. No sources."

"My people stay mine."

"Mine too."

"No questions about networks."

"Fine."

"If you think you've bought yourself access to every quiet conversation I have with anyone who ever served my father, you haven't."

"I didn't think that."

"Good. Because you'd be disappointed."

The corridor hummed. Anya kept guessing off the scraps she caught and the parts of his face he couldn't control, which was not the same thing as certainty no matter how much she wanted it to be. It still helped more than it should, and she hated that too.

He seemed to be pulling back now, testing the width of the offer.

"Regular channels only," Damian said.

"Meaning?"

He tipped his chin toward the building around them. "The cultural exchange calendar. RSVPs. Invitations. Notes about panel seating and youth orchestras and whatever other useless things your office can plausibly send my office without lighting up anybody's desk."

"So the boring stuff."

"The boring stuff keeps people alive."

Fair.

"If Brandt is already looking," he went on, "we don't invent some dramatic secret trail for him to trip over. If you need to contact me, it goes through paper that can survive daylight."

Their first alliance term was paperwork.

"Okay," she said. "Calendar. RSVPs. I can do boring."

He looked dubious. Justified.

"And if one of us decides the other is lying," he said, "it stops."

"No penalties," Anya said. "No revenge tour. No dragging each other down on the way out. We stop."

His attention sharpened on that phrase.

*...she cares about the exit.*

Because she already lived there.

"I mean it," she said before he could press. "If this goes bad, it ends clean."

"Nothing like this ends clean."

"Cleaner, then."

He considered her. The white light made the shadows under his eyes look older than the rest of him.

"My side of this isn't abstract," he said. "If Gessler gets enough paper to justify it, he'll pull my staff records, my scheduling history, everything. He'll sit on it until he can make a loyalty question out of any name that repeats too often. One wrong inference and people who never asked for any of this get questioned because they happened to have coffee with me twice in one month."

The admission cost him. She could see the cost more than hear it. His voice lost structure when it got close to people who mattered.

"Okay," she said, softer now. "Same on mine."

He frowned. "You said your people were already on the same mess."

"They are."

"Then why does helping me sound like self-harm."

Because it was.

She looked at the mop bucket instead of him. Dirty water. One drowned paper label floating near the rim. It was an easy object. Easier than his face.

"Because I've already put skin in it," she said.

His silence changed.

"How."

Anya licked the copper taste off her teeth. Too close.

"I can't answer that."

"Convenient."

"Yeah." She looked back at him. "You think I don't know that?"

He held her gaze. Something in his expression nearly softened and didn't.

"What happens if Brandt checks the calendar tonight?" she asked, because leaving his last question open was safer than trying to fill it. "Not in general. Specifically."

He let her get away with it for the moment, probably because he wanted the answer himself.

"He sees you approached me in public at a ministry forum," Damian said. "He sees whether the Westalian mission requested extra seating near Foreign Affairs. He sees whether my office approved anything unusual around your attendance. If he's thorough, he checks whether your name showed up near me anywhere else this week."

"This week only?"

"If Gessler tells him to go back, farther."

And if somebody on her side ever decided to do the same, they'd find the gallery address, the lie in her report, now this.

"Good," she said, not because it was good but because naming the shape helped.

"It isn't."

"I know."

A sound from the stairwell made both of them turn. Two junior staffers came through arguing about a translation memo. They barely glanced down the corridor. Damian waited until the door shut behind them again.

"Say I believe you enough for tonight," he said. "What then."

"Then we start with something small enough to kill if it stinks."

He folded his arms now, mirroring her earlier without noticing.

"Such as."

"Such as if I hear a name tied to Brandt's side of this, I pass the name. If you hear movement that would put me or my office in front of the same people twice, you warn me before I walk into it."

"You expect me to protect your cover."

"I expect you not to let the Front use me to get to you if we're doing this."

He stared at her.

"That's almost the same thing."

"Almost."

Another beat. Another tiny shift.

"All right," he said. "One warning each. No more than that until something proves useful."

"That's fair."

He looked away first, which surprised her. His gaze went to the pipe above her shoulder, fixed there while he made himself ask the next question.

"Why were you at the gallery last week before I spoke to you?"

It wasn't a trap exactly. It was worse. It was personal.

Because she had known his name before he knew hers. Because she'd been sent to identify an unknown operative and found a boy from school with his father's face and a mind she couldn't stop listening to. Because she had used his own thoughts to walk herself into his orbit and kept going when she should have gone back to Sylvia with the full truth.

"Work," she said.

"That's the answer your badge would give."

"It's still true."

His jaw tightened. "Do you always answer like this when you're cornered?"

"No. Sometimes I panic."

That pulled his eyes back to her. Honest again. Wrong place again.

He huffed a little laugh in spite of himself.

"I'll make note of that."

"Please don't."

He pushed off the wall. Decision time.

"Shared intelligence on the Front," he said. "No questions about networks. No meetings off ordinary channels unless it becomes unavoidable. Either of us can end it."

"Yes."

"And if Brandt or Gessler touches your office because of tonight, you tell me."

That wasn't about protecting her. It was about knowing how much damage the evening had already done. Still.

"I tell you," she said.

He nodded once. Almost formal again. The public shell climbing back over the private one because the conversation had reached the part where he needed armor.

Then he stopped.

"How did you know about Brandt?" he asked. "The truth this time."

There it was. The corridor had finally found the one loose wire she had been stepping around.

She could lie cleaner. She should lie cleaner. She heard that and did it badly anyway.

"My side has been hearing his name around the same people you're watching," she said. "When he started leaning on you, I moved."

Damian didn't blink. Didn't help her. Didn't call it false either.

The silence held long enough for the buzz overhead to turn mean.

"That's what you're giving me," he said.

"For now."

He considered that, then straightened his tie with two quick, irritated motions.

"All right," he said. "Then here's what I'm giving you. If your first note through that calendar smells wrong, I burn it and we're done."

Fair.

"If yours smells wrong, same."

His mouth pulled tight. "You really aren't afraid of sounding rude."

"I'm afraid of lots of things. That's not in the top ten."

That did it. A real smile almost surfaced and got strangled on the way up.

"Good night, Ms. Kessler."

"Good night, Mr. Desmond."

He took two steps, then turned back once more.

"If Tuesday comes up tonight," he said, voice low, "pay attention."

Then he left.

That was new.

Anya stayed where she was until the stairwell door shut behind him and the corridor felt empty again. Tuesday. So he had heard it too, or guessed the same pressure point. Either way she had a concrete point to look for besides her own pulse.

The tremble started in her hands around second twenty.

She shoved them under her arms and counted backward from one hundred because it gave panic numbers to chew on.

Seventy-three.

Her teeth hurt. She had been grinding them without noticing.

Fifty-nine.

The sequence kept arranging itself the same way in her head: the report in the system, Damian in the corridor, Brandt upstairs with Gessler's name in his head. One face for the forum, another for the hall, a third for the truth she still had not said out loud once, not even alone.

Thirty-eight.

She had made an alliance with a man who did not know the ugliest thing about it.

Not the telepathy. That was its own rot, and older. The other thing was the report.

She had lied about Damian to WISE after the gallery and bought herself breathing room with the lie. Now she was spending that room on him. If anybody ever laid tonight's attendance beside the assessment she had filed, the contradiction would light up hard enough to hurt.

Sylvia would not miss a contradiction like that for long.

Her stomach clenched hard enough to hurt.

No moral speech arrived after it. No clean verdict. There was only consequence.

When she could trust her legs again, she went back into the forum.

---

Somebody had found coffee while she was gone. That made the air smell burnt and punitive. The deputy minister was back at the podium grinding through a new page of notes with the glazed persistence of a man who believed data could become charisma if forced long enough.

Damian stood near the front beside a vice minister, all public edges restored. If she had not been in a mop corridor with him she might have believed the reset. He did not look at her.

Brandt did.

He was three rows back now, notebook open on one knee, writing with short annoyed strokes.

*...Kessler. Westalian mission. Check whether she was at the gallery. Check whether she was at dinner. Desmond doesn't get three coincidences.*

Anya kept walking as if she had only come back from the toilets. No flinch. No glance. She crossed to the back wall, took another warm sparkling water off a tray, and stopped where she could watch the room through the glass.

Brandt kept writing.

*...Tuesday. Bretz. If Desmond turns up there too, Gessler gets the whole packet.*

There it was.

Bretz.

Something small and vicious skipped through her pulse. It wasn't relief. It was relief's uglier cousin. A usable name.

Colonel Bretz. Procurement review Tuesday. Enough for Gessler if Damian showed up. Enough for Damian to get there first if he moved fast.

Brandt flipped a page.

*...and Kessler. Why was Kessler near him twice.*

Twice. So he already had the gallery in mind or some other room. Good to know. Bad to know.

Anya kept the water glass steady by gripping it harder than necessary. Across the room Damian turned a page in his folder and did not once let his gaze drag toward her. Smart. If anybody had been timing how long it took people to betray themselves, they were currently failing the test in the boring direction.

The minister called on a man from trade compliance. Somebody near Anya muttered, *kill me now*. Another mind supplied an itemized dinner plan involving sausage, mustard, and a fight with a brother-in-law about football. Telepathy never respected tone.

Brandt stood. Notebook in hand.

He moved toward the side door that led deeper into the ministry offices. Gessler. Or Dreher first, the way he had thought before. Either way, his paperwork night had started.

Anya put the water glass down untouched.

The new channel Damian had named came back to her in practical pieces: the cultural exchange calendar, invitations, RSVPs, idiotic official notes nobody could flag without admitting they had read the note too carefully.

She could wait until morning. Morning would be safer on the face of it. More office-like. Less suspicious than a night note.

Morning also gave Brandt the whole night with his access logs.

No.

She left by the diplomatic side exit instead of the main hall, signing her name on the ministry clipboard in the round careful hand she used for cover work. ANNA KESSLER. Time out: 18:14. The clipboard already held five other neat lies about where the people on it had gone and why.

Outside, Berlint had gone to iron. The sky over the government district was the color of dishwater and the sidewalks shone with a damp sheen that made every streetlamp look doubled. Cars hissed past the curb. Somebody down the block was selling roasted chestnuts from a cart and the sweet scorched smell hit her all at once, hard enough to make her realize she had forgotten to eat.

She bought a paper cone because standing still without purpose made her look wrong. The vendor's thoughts were a practical flood of small concerns.

*...charcoal low. Need change. Girl looks too thin. Charge extra for diplomats. No, too obvious...*

Anya gave him exact coins and walked toward the mission with the chestnuts burning her fingertips through the paper.

Every few steps she caught stray thought-shards in the crowd. Wet socks. Rent. Call mother back. Idiot in the blue coat. She let them pass without trying to hold them. Too full already.

By the time she reached the Westalian mission annex, night staff had settled into their preferred rhythm of paperwork, quiet resentment, and tea gone cold. Her badge got her past the front desk without comment. The guard on rotation was thinking about a television detective and whether shaving in the evening counted as self-respect. Safe enough.

Her office on the third floor smelled faintly of radiator heat, toner, and the lemon soap the cleaner used on Fridays whether the carpet wanted it or not. One lamp was on. Filing trays were stacked. The cultural calendar binder lay open where she'd left it that morning on a page about youth orchestra exchange dates.

It was perfect, ridiculous, and useful.

Anya shut the door halfway, not enough to look secretive, enough to keep hallway chatter out. Then she sat, pulled the exchange calendar toward her, and stared at the ministry contacts sheet until the names stopped blurring.

Bretz, Colonel Emil. Gesellschaft Club patron committee. Tuesday dinner rotation.

It was there, printed in the kind of directory that passed for innocent because culture liked rich men in uniform if they donated to the right concerts.

So Brandt's Tuesday was real. Bretz had a reason to exist in the same social ecosystem as Damian's headache.

Anya took out the mission note cards.

Her first instinct was to write too much: Tuesday, Gesellschaft, Brandt's watching, Gessler pulling logs, don't be seen twice. That was not a cultural invitation. That was a confession with letterhead.

She tore the first card in half.

On the second she wrote, in the dead banal mission language she hated most:

The Westalian Mission Cultural Office is finalizing guest coordination for Tuesday's Gesellschaft Club dinner. We understand Colonel Bretz remains on the patron committee. Please advise whether your office requires updated seating notes for any ministry attendees with corridor trade interests.

Hideous, and perfect.

Anyone skimming it would see bureaucratic static. Damian would see Tuesday, Gesellschaft, Bretz, corridor trade interests. If he wanted the lead, he had it. If he didn't, he could ignore it and nothing on the card explicitly begged to be burned.

She read it twice.

Then a third time, because now came the other decision. Official channel meant record. Record meant trace.

The WISE report sat in another building under her name and Sylvia's trust. The new note would sit in this one under her cover and the Foreign Affairs ministry's letter stamp. Put together, they did not say alliance in neat language. They said she had buried a live line and then built one anyway.

Her right hand had started to shake. Not much. Enough to make the pen tap the desk in a stupid bright rhythm.

She put the pen down.

This was the actual point, not the corridor or the terms or even Brandt. This was the moment where the thing became paper.

If she sent nothing, Damian lost time and could lose the Tuesday lead with it. If she sent it, there was now a document trail between them that both Brandt and WISE could eventually find. It wasn't romance or abstract guilt. It was a trail. A clerk could retrieve it. A supervisor could initial it. A hostile audit could staple it to her false assessment and ask why.

She thought of Damian in the corridor saying people who never asked for any of this got pulled in because they had coffee with him twice. That was true on both sides. Once the records started talking to one another, nobody stayed bystander.

Anya pressed her thumbnail into the scar on her left palm until the pain steadied the rest of her.

There was no more waiting for a cleaner version of bad.

She fed the card into the mission envelope, marked it for Foreign Affairs cultural liaison, and added the tiny routing notation that would put it on the next internal pouch instead of tomorrow's public stack. A little abuse of office routine. Not enough to ring bells. Enough to buy hours.

Before she sealed it, she copied the text into the outgoing log.

Name of correspondent: D. Desmond, Foreign Affairs.  
Subject: Tuesday guest coordination.  
Action: advisory / no response required.

The logbook line looked harmless. That was almost the worst part. Harm should look like harm. Instead it looked like stationery.

She sanded the ink, closed the book, and sealed the envelope.

When she carried it downstairs to the dispatch tray, the night clerk barely glanced up.

"Late addition?" he asked.

"Club dinner nonsense," Anya said.

He grimaced with instant bureaucratic solidarity. "Tragic."

"If only culture were less important."

"One can dream."

He stamped the corner of the envelope and dropped it into the ministry pouch without another thought.

That was it. There went the paper.

Anya watched the pouch swallow it and had the sudden animal urge to grab it back, laugh, say wrong envelope, make a joke, anything. She kept her hands at her sides until the urge passed or turned into plain nausea.

It was too late now. Good.

Back upstairs, her office felt smaller. She opened her notebook and wrote down only what could survive being found:

Brandt - access logs - Gessler  
Tuesday / Bretz / Gesellschaft  
No new channel outside calendar

Downstairs, the outgoing log already held one line to D. Desmond. In another building her false WISE assessment was still sitting under her own name. They were separate pieces of paper under separate names. If Brandt or Gessler or anyone on her side ever made them touch, the gap between Anna Kessler and Anya Forger would stop looking like cover and start looking like fraud, and WISE would hear Damian's name from the wrong side first.

Her stomach rolled hard enough to make her stand.

Outside the office window, government cars slid through the wet dark and a man at the payphone down the block was losing a fight with numbers. The city looked ordinary. Nobody was looking up. Anya braced both hands on the sill until the wave passed, then went downstairs and looked through the dispatch-room glass at the ministry pouch sealed for the morning run, squat and ordinary on the shelf, with her envelope buried among seating charts and routing slips.

Tuesday was in there now.
