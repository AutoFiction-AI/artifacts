# Chapter 11

After the canal, Anya spent three days inside WISE headquarters, and by now the fluorescent buzz felt lodged behind her eyes.

Not from the reception itself. Crowds gave her the bright-light headache and then let go. This was three days of precision work: engineered elevator rides, timed coffee runs, standing beside colleagues with a dead phone in her hand while their thoughts scraped past hers in fragments, taking the long way to the archive because two analysts from signals usually argued there at 10:15 and one of them had clearance she needed to hear from up close. The most invasive thing she had ever done on purpose.

Yor kept breaking through it anyway. Dominic's records. Garden's code in Dominic's paperwork. The crack that had opened in her mother's head on the service road. But Anya had nothing she could carry into a briefing folder, no way to ask Yor what she'd found without admitting she'd stolen it out of her mind, and no reason to think Yor would tell anyone before she tried to think her way back into obedience. That was the bet Anya had made when she turned away instead of following Yor home. Maybe Yor would go quiet for a few days. Maybe, in those same few days, Garden would clean the trail and Yor would let them. Damian's warning about the WISE leak felt shorter than that. So Anya came to headquarters and stayed.

In her fourth-floor cubicle, coffee went cold beside an access-log spreadsheet. The fluorescents buzzed. Reheated fish from the break room was making the whole floor smell like punishment. Outside, two analysts argued about sourcing methodology while one of them worried about his dead dog and the other about an overdue credit-card bill.

Reading WISE all day got her the same junk everybody else carried around: errands, resentment, money, lunch, whether anyone had replaced the toilet paper on the third floor, whether a mole on somebody's shoulder looked worse than last week, whether the vending machine was still eating coins. Intelligence came in bursts. Between bursts, life kept being stupid and ordinary.

It had started with a single fragment from the diplomatic reception: *the asset inside WISE*. A mole, somewhere in a crowd too dense to pin to one skull. She had tried replaying the room afterward, flat on her bed with a pillow over her face to block the morning light, but telepathy wasn't a recording. It happened once and then it was memory like any other, full of holes and false certainty.

So she'd started the other way. Paper first, the way Loid had taught her since she was sixteen: before you use the cheat, do the work. Access logs. Intercepts. Who sat in which briefing, then somehow the Renewal Front knew the same thing a day later. Grosz had helped by cutting the whole giant bureaucratic mess down to the parts that actually mattered: army planning, contract lanes, the places where decisions turned into parts and shipments. If somebody inside WISE was feeding that machine, the access pattern should touch those lanes.

By Monday noon she had a private grid open across three windows: names down the left, compartments across the top, then print releases, late badge swipes, leave blocks, briefing attendance, routing authority, and a last column she hadn't labeled because the label would have been *things I only know because I stood next to people and stole them.* She let herself write in it anyway. Berger: hates Vienna posting. Dreher: hiding medical thing? Falk: gives nothing. Then she stared at the words until the shame stopped being dramatic and turned useful.

Eleven analysts had the clearance and the opportunity. Two weeks of paperwork got that down to seven. Two had been out of Berlint during key leak windows. Two more never touched the right compartments. One had the right access but spent every suspicious evening in a gambling room loud enough that even telepathy would have had to fight the slot machines. Another was having an affair so obvious in his thoughts that Anya wanted to apologize to every person who had ever accidentally stood near her. Brief reads of colleagues whose surface minds were banal, frightened, horny, or furious in ways that had nothing to do with procurement channels dropped the list again. By Monday evening she had three names.

Anna-Lise Berger had been one of them. Berger's file looked bad in the abstract: access to the right summaries, two after-hours printer releases, a stretch of bitterness sharp enough to read from across a conference table. An elevator ride killed that. Berger's head was full of Vienna resentment, her mother's slipping memory, a dentist appointment she'd been avoiding for three months, the dull ache of being forty-three and professionally overlooked, and a panicked internal reminder to buy incontinence pads before visiting her mother again because the last time she'd forgotten and spent two hours pretending not to notice the wet chair. When the doors opened, Berger smiled at Anya and stepped out still innocent, and Anya stepped out carrying things Berger had never offered her.

She ducked into the stairwell afterward and stood with one hand on the rail until the elevator started moving again. Protect your own secret by prying under everybody else's. Good work. Real principled stuff.

That left two. Martin Dreher and Gerald Falk.

Dreher worked signals analysis on the third floor. He was fifty-one. He drank black coffee with two sugars in the cheap styrofoam cups everybody hated. A desk photo showed his wife, two sons, and a Golden Retriever who looked disappointed to be there. He had been in the building fifteen years and brought Friday pastries from a bakery near his apartment. He was the kind of colleague people described by habit because the habits were what you noticed first.

His file looked worse than Berger's had. Dreher's desk touched the Baltic readiness brief, border-positioning summaries, and the procurement annexes Grosz had effectively put a red circle around. Over the previous month he'd pulled the same bands of material the Renewal Front kept staying a step ahead of. Three times he had released hard-copy packets from the third-floor secure printer and then vanished from the building for somewhere between ninety minutes and two hours. Three times the Renewal Front had later anticipated deployments or inspections that should have stayed inside WISE. On paper it looked clean in the ugliest way.

Anya spent most of Tuesday morning making herself prove it twice.

She checked the access times again. 13:08 on the procurement annexes. 13:14 on the border summaries. Secure print release at 13:23, user tag unresolved. Dreher badged out at 13:41. Leak window opened that evening.

Then another. 14:02, 14:05, 14:11. Print release. Blank calendar block. Dreher gone for an hour and forty-eight minutes. By the next morning the Renewal Front had already shifted material off a route WISE had planned to watch.

Then the third. Same lane. Same disappearing act.

It was enough to make her go downstairs instead of forwarding the pattern immediately. If she was going to light a fire under internal security, she wanted his face in the room with the numbers.

Tuesday morning put him alone in the break room with an empty mug in his hand, staring at the motivational poster above the microwave as if the eagle might tell him what to do next. Anya filled her own cup and stopped within range.

His thoughts hit like cold water.

*Tuesday. He said Tuesday. If the second panel is worse than the first one, if Kessler was wrong, if I've got three weeks, maybe four before it shows, before Eva looks at me and knows. Nine months with her mother and I can't do that to her. I can't.*

The panic broke, re-formed, and tried to pass as work.

*Budget meeting at two. Baltic assessment. Focus.*

Then it slipped again.

*Second referral first. Referral means paperwork. Paperwork means the WISE medical plan. HR sees the claim and I'm done. Cash for one more visit. Kessler at four. Tell Eva it's Wilkinson. Tell her I'm staying late. Please just believe that one more time.*

By the time the thought-stream collapsed into raw fear, Anya's hand had tightened hard enough on the cup to make the styrofoam creak.

"Hey, Martin."

He startled. "Anya. Hi. Morning."

"You okay? You look a little..."

"Fine." Too fast. "Just a long week. You know how it is."

*Don't ask. Please don't ask. Not here.*

"Sure," she said. "Get some rest."

Back upstairs she tried, for twelve whole minutes, to make the read mean anything else.

Maybe Kessler was a cutout. Maybe referral meant the first step in some contact protocol. Maybe cash-only plus no paper trail plus timed absences plus the exact right document pulls was enough even if the fear underneath it wasn't about ideology. People did dangerous things for reasons smaller than belief all the time. Debt. Shame. Family. Disease. A private crisis didn't clear him. If anything, private crises made people vulnerable.

That line held until she opened his work lane and saw how neatly the bad facts stacked.

She pulled Dreher's access history, printer releases, and leave calendar into the same window and stared until the pattern stopped looking accidental. Three leak windows matched three unexplained afternoon absences, each between ninety minutes and two hours. Each absence landed after he'd pulled procurement annexes or border-positioning summaries that the Renewal Front had later anticipated almost exactly. Nothing on his calendar explained those gaps. Nothing in the user-facing system accounted for the hard-copy releases. Today's schedule had another blank block beginning at 3:40.

She checked the routing authority on the packets. Wilkinson had signed the broader Baltic tasking, but not the individual print pulls. She checked the printer room logs. Same unresolved section-code release all three days. She checked Dreher's desk assignment notes from the shared project folder. Half the filenames were routine. Half touched exactly the lanes Damian had warned her were still hot. She checked the archive request tray and found his name again in the handwriting box, request submitted for hard-copy review because "screen comparison unreliable." That might have meant aging eyes. It might have meant he didn't want a digital trail.

At 11:40 she went back downstairs under the excuse of a copier jam and walked past his cubicle. Dreher wasn't there. His desk was too neat for somebody in panic except for one open drawer crammed with antacid tablets, a crushed receipts envelope, and a bakery box folded flat for reuse. The family photo sat angled toward his chair. Next to it was a yellow legal pad with budget numbers in one column and three dates circled hard enough to bruise the paper.

Clinic dates, maybe.

Handoff dates, maybe.

The problem was that both stories used the same evidence.

At 12:06 she came back up and opened Falk's personnel file, willing the answer to be there instead because Falk made more sense as a traitor and less sense as a man with a hidden oncologist. Twenty years older than her, senior enough to move paperwork without being noticed, close enough to Loid that betrayal would actually count. But Falk's recent access looked broad in the ordinary way senior analysts looked broad, and the telepathic passes she had managed on him had turned up nothing except weather, budgets, and the kind of tidy administrative annoyance that clung to men who alphabetized their own tea tins.

By 1:30 the choice had narrowed to something simpler and worse. If she waited for cleaner proof on Falk, Dreher's 3:40 blank block would come and go. If Dreher was the leak and she sat on it because cancer was an uglier secret than treason, then the next warning would reach the Renewal Front before anyone inside WISE moved.

The pattern said four o'clock with Kessler, cash only, no paper trail.

If that was a handoff and she spent the afternoon trying to get a cleaner read on Falk, she'd be waiting through another leak to avoid making herself uncomfortable. Falk was still there. Dreher had a clock on him.

By 3:12 she had written the memo.

The wording was careful. Too careful. Nothing in it exposed how she knew. She listed the paper pieces one by one: overlapping access, weird hours, missing afternoons sitting right on top of the old leak windows, hard-copy packets that seemed to vanish, a man looking like he was about to come apart. Then she cut that last one back to "observable strain" because she couldn't write *I heard him thinking about dying in the break room,* deleted "sudden" from the line above, and printed the page.

Kurtz took it in internal security without reaction. Mid-forties, flat voice, office that smelled like stale paper and orange-peel cleaner.

She read the first paragraph while Anya stood there and said, "Why now?"

"Because there's another gap this afternoon."

"And not yesterday?"

Anya kept her face still. "Yesterday it was a pattern. Today it's a clock."

Kurtz looked back down at the paper. Not suspicious. Not reassuring either. Just evaluating. "If you're wrong, this burns a senior analyst for having a private life."

Too late to pretend that wasn't exactly what she was doing. "If I'm right, he gets the rest of the week."

Kurtz set the memo on the in-tray with one finger. "We'll look."

Counterintelligence yanked two analysts off the Ostanian desk the next morning. IT went rooting through Dreher's terminal traffic and comms metadata. A watcher took the desk opposite his in the bullpen with a legal pad and the posture of a man pretending not to watch the exact person he was watching. By lunchtime the whole third floor was walking funny, compensating around holes nobody was allowed to name while Dreher moved through the middle of it under surveillance he hadn't agreed to and couldn't yet feel.

Anya felt it for him anyway.

She felt it in the way the floor had gone fractionally wrong. Conversations stopped when he approached and restarted too fast after he passed. Printer queues backed up because the extra reviewer assigned to cover him didn't know the third-floor machine needed to be kicked low on the left side to catch the second tray. Somebody in logistics kept thinking *which one is it* every time he saw a security officer walk by. Dreher himself seemed to notice only that people were busier than usual. His surface thoughts stayed trapped inside appointments, numbers, and the repeated impossible task of deciding whether to tell Eva before or after the next scan.

At lunch on Wednesday she passed him in the corridor with a paper cup of soup in one hand and a folder tucked under his arm. He nodded like always.

"Afternoon."

"Hey."

*Don't spill. Don't spill on the report. Tell Eva Thursday. No, tonight. No. Tonight and then she looks at you differently forever.*

Anya made it all the way to the stairwell before the guilt hit hard enough to bend her over the railing.

The investigation ate two days.

Late on the second evening, Kurtz's report hit Anya's secure inbox. Three lines in, she got up from her desk and took it into the bathroom.

Martin Dreher was not selling intelligence to the Renewal Front.

Three weeks earlier, Martin Dreher had been diagnosed with Stage III pancreatic cancer. Dr. Kessler was an oncologist at a private clinic across the city. The referrals were medical. The cash was medical. The blank blocks in his afternoons were appointments, imaging, treatment planning, and time parked two streets over because he couldn't yet drive straight home and tell his wife it was happening to him now, not someday. He'd kept it off the WISE plan because a flagged claim would pull him from active work before he was ready to be looked at like a man leaving.

But Kurtz's report didn't stop there. Counterintelligence had tailed him on Tuesday and Wednesday. Both times Dreher left the building alone, took the tram to Zeidler Private Oncology, signed in under his own name, met with Kessler, then sat in his car on a side street with both hands locked on the steering wheel before driving home. No third-party contact. No alternate route. No communication with any known Renewal Front intermediary before, during, or after the appointments.

The document pulls were real and they were legitimate. Wilkinson had assigned Dreher to the Baltic contingency packet after Geller's intercept note, which meant the procurement annexes and border-positioning summaries Anya had circled were exactly what he was supposed to be reading for the weekly readiness brief. The "missing" printer releases had not disappeared into a handoff. They had gone through a third-floor secure batch queue mapped to the section code instead of individual user badges; security recovered the binders from Wilkinson's locked project cabinet with Dreher's tabs and margin notes still in them. Terminal audit clean. Communications audit clean. Personal finance clean except for the clinic withdrawals.

The leak-window alignment had been the cruelest part. Those documents passed through half the desk in the twenty-four hours before every readiness brief, which was exactly when the real mole needed them most. Dreher's absences had overlapped the leaks because his appointments happened to fall on the same afternoons Falk, Wilkinson, Geller, and two duty officers were all moving through the same circulation chain. Anya had mistaken shared timing for a private channel.

The last page listed the remaining names with access to those packets under the same project code. Gerald Falk was on it.

In the stall, the folder sat open on her lap while warm air pushed through the vent overhead. Somebody came in, used the sink, left again. Another person followed, thinking in loud irritated fragments about a train delay and a tampon she'd forgotten to replace. The thoughts crashed against Anya's skull and kept going.

Every piece she'd read had been true: Tuesday, cash, the second referral, the terror. None of it had been about treason. A man trying to keep chemo off an HR system had sounded, at the surface, exactly like a man preparing a covert handoff, and she had handed that mistake to counterintelligence in a neat memo.

Two analysts lost two days on him. IT burned hours on the terminal of a man who had been reading survival rates on his lunch break. Physical surveillance watched him come out of an oncology clinic with his face wrecked from trying not to cry in public. Somewhere in the building, the actual mole had all that room.

His desk photo came back to her, along with the Friday pastries and the low tuneless humming from three floors down. The flattened bakery box. The antacids. The legal pad with those three dates stabbed into it. She had turned the worst private fear of his life into a security referral and then made half the building rearrange itself around the damage.

Cold metal bit into the scar in her left palm while she held the stall latch and waited for the nausea to level out. At the sink, the face in the mirror looked the same. Trusting it felt harder.

She closed the report, then opened it again because hating herself was not the same thing as fixing the hole.

Falk on the last page. Falk still inside the chain.

She stood at the bathroom sink until the fluorescent hum flattened back into one sound she could stand.

---

Gerald Falk was in his usual corner of the fourth-floor bullpen the next morning with tea and a cable digest. Seeing him there felt less like spotting a colleague than realizing a familiar fixture in the room had shifted half an inch overnight. He had once shown up at her Eden graduation with a silver fountain pen that still lived in her desk drawer. Not a sentimental gift, exactly. Useful. He'd said a person should own at least one pen that made ugly paperwork feel expensive.

The shared printer gave her an excuse to stand near him. Pages fed out. She straightened them one by one and stayed inside his radius.

"You look tired," Falk said without lifting his eyes from the digest. "Eat breakfast."

His mind moved under the words in tidy little rows.

*Budget allocation for Q3 is twelve percent high again. Same trick as last year. Sylvia won't fight the escalation numbers because they're useful. Pension committee at noon. Actuarial adjustment still wrong. Might rain.*

"I'm fine," Anya said. "Long week."

"Isn't there always."

*Wilkinson routing note is sloppy. Geller used one intercept and called it a pattern. Move the extra eyes. Fix it later.*

Forty seconds passed. Then a minute. His thoughts kept moving through budget irritation, pension math, weather, soup rotation. Then, warm and unguarded in the middle of it:

*She doesn't eat enough. Loid used to do the same thing.*

That was all. There was no spike, no concealed channel, no buried panic broadcasting through the walls. After Dreher, the quiet was almost worse. Noise had made her wrong. Silence gave her nothing to be wrong about.

Back at her desk she opened Kurtz's attachment again, this time not for the shame. She pulled the circulation-chain names into a separate note and started working through them one by one. Wilkinson had authority but not enough unsupervised time on the live desks. Geller's access was narrow and loud; if he moved a channel, three people complained in writing. The duty officers rotated too often. Falk sat at the crossing point: senior enough to alter coverage, ordinary enough to vanish into procedure, trusted enough that nobody read his tidy annotations as ambition.

She opened the Wilkinson file that Falk's stray thought had tagged. Falk had annotated it at 8:12 that morning and signed off on a further coverage shift starting the next day at 0600. Two more analysts off live review of the Ostanian diplomatic procurement traffic. Temporary on paper. Forty-eight hours. Bureaucratically clean. The exact desk Damian had spent the last week warning her the Renewal Front still used to move material and instructions.

It wasn't the only clean cut.

She dug back through the previous ten days and found three smaller reallocations with Falk's initials on them. A one-shift analyst loan from procurement review to embassy media traffic. Harmless if you believed Geller's note. A delayed archive pull on border customs chatter because the desk was "overcovered." Sensible if you believed Anya's false assessment had bought them room. A request to widen a watchlist on Westalian student groups while narrowing one on Ostanian freight brokers. Perfectly defensible in isolation. Together they formed a pattern of tiny absences around exactly the lanes Grosz and Damian kept naming.

The first of the edits had landed the morning after Sylvia accepted Anya's report on Damian.

She sat back in her chair so fast it squealed.

There it was. Not proof. Worse than proof, in a way. A shape built out of things she'd made easier for somebody else to do.

Her false report had not created Falk. It had created air. He'd been breathing it ever since.

From the cubicle across the aisle, somebody was thinking furiously about a yeast infection and whether business-casual allowed boots with a heel. From the far hall came a burst of laughter, then the stray thought *if he brings tuna again I'm transferring departments.* Headquarters kept being headquarters while the page on Anya's screen quietly accused her.

She opened her desk drawer and found the fountain pen where it always lay under spare batteries and a pack of headache tablets. Silver barrel, black grip, weighty in the hand. Falk had given it to her outside Eden's assembly hall while Loid was trapped in traffic and Yor was trying not to cry onto Anya's shoulder.

"For forms worth signing," he'd said, and then, because Anya had been eighteen and suspicious of all adults on principle, "It isn't a bribe. You're not important enough yet."

She had laughed. He had, too.

Now she rolled the pen between her fingers and thought about all the things administrative men could kill without ever touching a gun. A desk didn't go blind in one dramatic flash. A desk went blind because somebody wrote TEMPORARY RESOURCE REALIGNMENT at the top of a form and signed where trust expected them to sign.

At 11:20 Falk stopped by her cubicle with his mug.

"You staying buried in coverage tables all day?"

Anya locked the Wilkinson file before turning. "Looks like it."

"Take a lunch break before you start seeing patterns in the carpet."

"Maybe the carpet's suspicious."

One corner of his mouth moved.

*Ask if she's going home this weekend. No, don't crowd her. She already looks strung thin. Wilkinson still can't format a note to save his life. Move the six o'clock handoff. No, not handoff. Coverage shift. Christ, get the wording right.*

The stumble was so small she almost missed it. Not a revelation. Not some obvious crack. Just one word appearing where it shouldn't and getting shoved aside fast enough that somebody without telepathy would never have known it had been there.

Her pulse kicked once under her ribs.

Falk tipped his mug toward the break room. "There's still some decent coffee if you move fast."

"That sounds fake, Gerald."

"It is, but it's less fake than the tea."

He kept walking.

Anya sat there with the locked screen and the pen in her hand and tried to decide whether that one slip counted as anything or whether this was how another innocent man got burned. *Handoff* could have meant the shift paperwork itself. People said the wrong operational word all the time. She knew that. She also knew he had corrected it inside his own head before the thought finished forming.

Not proof.

But no longer nothing.

She spent the next hour building the smallest case she could trust. Not betrayal. Not motive. Just motion. Falk's initials on the coverage changes. Falk on the circulation chain for the Dreher packets. Falk catching and using the gap her false report had opened. Falk's brief internal slip over the six o'clock change. By 1:00 the list fit on half a page and still looked thin enough to hate.

She did not take it to Kurtz.

Not yet.

If she handed internal security another elegant wrong answer, she would deserve whatever came after it. Damian was right about the time pressure, but Damian didn't work inside a building full of people whose private disasters could mimic treason down to the minute. This one she needed to watch happen.

At 16:40 she checked the next day's floor assignment board. The procurement desk coverage shift was still there. 0600. Falk's initials in the approval field. No one had challenged it.

Tomorrow's hunt stopped being abstract. If somebody inside WISE wanted that lane dark before the weekend, 0600 was the hour to watch.

The encrypted call came at nine that night, one-time cipher and frequency rotation. Anya sat on her kitchen floor with her back against the cabinet and had to try the key sequence twice before the line cleared through static.

Damian's voice came on stripped of its diplomatic polish.

"The weather turned."

"How bad?"

"Our friend at the garden center moved up the planting schedule. Public event. Meant to look like the people across the street started it."

It was a diplomatic incident, something visible and meant to read as Westalian provocation.

"When?"

"This weekend. Midweek at the latest. Days, not weeks."

The kitchen faucet dripped once into the sink. Then it dripped again.

"I lost two days on the personnel issue," she said.

"What happened?"

"I was sure about one of them. I was wrong. He was hiding that he's sick. Bad sick."

Silence held on the line. Then, quieter, "I'm sorry."

The words hit harder than they should have.

"I flagged him because the paperwork fit," she said before she could stop herself. "Access history, printer pulls, missing afternoons, all lined up with the leaks. It was all real. It just meant oncology instead of treason."

Damian took a breath. Paper shifted on his end, then stopped. "And the real one?"

"Still inside the same circulation chain. I think it's Falk."

"Think, or know?"

"Think. More than I did yesterday. Less than I want."

"What changed?"

She looked at the note she'd already started on the back of the electricity bill, though there was almost nothing on it yet.

"He signed a forty-eight-hour coverage cut on one of the diplomatic procurement desks. Starts at six tomorrow. Then I went backward and found three smaller reroutes in the same lanes, all after my Damian report went into the system. He keeps making the hole look administrative."

The static hissed softly between them.

"Which desk?"

She gave him the channel designation.

Another pause followed. "All right," he said. "Don't watch speeches, then. Watch deliveries. If the event is this weekend, they'll move parts first. Your person is clearing air for that or somebody is using his paperwork to do it."

"That's more useful."

"I know."

Her shoulder eased a fraction against the cabinet. It wasn't enough to matter.

"There's something else," she said. "He almost thought the wrong word."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning I don't know if I caught a tell or if I'm doing the same thing again and building a whole case out of one second of somebody's bad brain."

"Then don't build the case out of that second."

She shut her eyes.

"Right."

"Use what the paper can survive," Damian said. "Use what you can watch happen in real time. If you're right about him, he's going to need that desk dark for a reason. If you're wrong again, you burn another innocent person. So don't guess. Sit on the lane and see who uses it."

That was the first clean instruction anybody had given her all week.

"Okay."

"Can you get eyes on it alone?"

"I can get there before the shift change. After that I'll decide if I need help."

"From someone you trust?"

She let out one dry breath.

"That list is not having a good week."

He was quiet just long enough for her to picture the look he would be making if they were in the same room. Not diplomatic. Not amused. The other one.

"Anya."

"Yeah?"

"Be careful."

"You too."

"If the schedule moves again, call me."

"I will."

The line clicked dead.

For a moment she stayed on the floor listening to the pipes and the neighbor upstairs moving around in shoes that should have been illegal after nine. Then she pulled the electricity bill fully onto her lap, turned it over, and wrote:

0600  
diplomatic procurement desk  
Falk

Underneath that she added, after a second:

watch, don't report

Then, lower:

Yor one more night.

It was ugly and it was still the calculation. Her mother was somewhere in the city with Dominic's photographs and whatever was left of her faith in Garden. Anya was betting Yor would stay quiet until tomorrow because the other clock had reached six in the morning and a desk inside WISE was about to go blind.

The apartment went dark when she hit the switch. Her head still hurt, but now it was the ordinary version, the screen-stare, skipped-meal kind. She set the note beside the phone, got into bed, and stayed awake long enough to count the hours until six between the faucet drips.
