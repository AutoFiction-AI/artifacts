# Chapter 14

Three days after David Crane died at the Kreuzfeld border checkpoint, Anya stood outside Loid and Yor's apartment with her key in her hand. Loid was home on a Tuesday, radio low behind the door, while Yor moved in the kitchen and Bond's nails clicked across the floor toward her. Home smelled like rice steam and dish soap. Sylvia's voice cut through it anyway. *We lost Crane at the checkpoint.* The floodlights. The broken lane. The gap in coverage Anya had helped cut open with a lie.

She should've come the first morning. Instead WISE had sealed up everyone who'd touched Kreuzfeld: mirrored phones, movement logs, outside contact requests shoved through desks the mole might already own. If she'd walked that scrutiny to the Forger apartment, she would've brought the leak to their door. Tonight the summit scramble had finally cracked the hold long enough for her to get here. If she waited longer, tomorrow happened without them.

She let herself in.

Loid was at the sink in an apron, sleeves pushed to the elbows. Yor had the refrigerator door open. Bond hit Anya at the knees hard enough to make her stumble, his thoughts arriving in three blunt beats. *Home. Her. Good.* For one second nobody had turned and she saw the evening as it had been before she came through the door: dinner half-finished, radio muttering, the kind of ordinary hour Crane would never get back.

"Anya!" Yor shut the refrigerator too fast. "You should've called. Have you eaten? Sit down. No, wait, you're freezing. Sit first."

Loid turned. His face stayed easy. The check behind it did not.

*She came alone. No warning. Her hands. How bad.*

"How's work?" he asked.

It was what a father asked in a kitchen. It was also *tell me what room we're in*.

"Papa." Her voice came out scraped thin. "I need to talk to you."

The reading glasses came off his head. That was the only large movement.

"They killed Crane," she said.

The radio died in the middle of a weather report. Loid had crossed the room and switched it off before she registered him moving. Yor pulled out a chair.

"Sit," Loid said. Then, before she could, "Were you followed?"

"I don't think so." Anya sat because her knees had already started to shake. Bond pressed against her leg. "WISE put everybody from Kreuzfeld on internal hold. Phones copied, exits watched, no outside contact unless it went through review. If I came sooner, I brought a tail here."

Loid didn't nod. "And tonight?"

"Credential rush for the summit. Everybody running in circles. I got a clean hour and took it."

Yor set water in front of her and touched her wrist once. "All right. Tell us."

Anya wrapped both hands around the glass and forced herself not to look at the tremor in the water.

"Crane was on a courier line tied to summit prep. Not official prep. Off-book procurement, access schedules, movement that shouldn't have been riding the same route. Somebody burned the checkpoint before dawn and the line went dark with him." She swallowed. "It wasn't only Crane. The same morning, somebody raided one of Damian Desmond's caches. One of his people went underground. Whoever's building tomorrow wanted both sides blind before lunch."

That got Loid's eyes up in a different way.

"The ministry diplomat."

"Yes."

Yor looked between them. "The summit is tomorrow."

"Yes." Anya made herself keep going. "And it's not a pressure tactic anymore. It's the stage. Opening session. Cameras already live. Delegations trapped in place. If they can make the first footage read Westalian, correction won't matter for the first hour and maybe the day after."

Loid leaned one hand on the table. "What part of that do you know and what part are you building from fragments?"

"Know: opening session, public blame, help from inside the building. Strong read: gunfire first, something camera-clear, with an internal relay feeding the floor." She met his eyes because anything else would look weaker than the truth. "Voss is close enough to matter. He didn't build this to miss the first five minutes."

"How much of this has WISE?"

"Enough to panic. Not enough to see the shape." She took a breath. "And I wasn't sending home contact through a channel I don't trust."

Loid held the line a second longer. *No sleep. Guilt first, then tradecraft. Too much of both.* "That's the whole reason?"

"No." The word hurt on the way out. "The hold was the real blocker. After that..." She looked down at the water. "After that I kept thinking I'd come here with something solid instead of fear. That part was me."

It wasn't absolution. It was only accurate.

Yor had gone very still. Not shocked. Recognizing.

*Dominic's office. The manifests. The watermark.*

"I have something," she said, and she was already in the hall before either of them answered.

Loid looked after her, then back at Anya. "You should've taken the first clean window."

"I know."

Yor came back with a thick manila envelope and set it on the table between the glasses and the soy sauce. Her hands were steady by effort.

"Karl Dominic," she said. "The broker from last month. After I killed him I photographed everything on his desk because the papers felt wrong and I knew if I left with only a feeling I'd talk myself out of it by morning."

She opened the envelope. Grainy photographs slid over the wood: shipping manifests, procurement records, a photographed purchase order with the Garden watermark at the top and three completed eliminations listed below it.

Loid picked up the manifests first. His eyes moved fast.

*Voss. Civilian intermediaries. Defense procurement. Supply chain.*

"Where did he get these?" Loid asked.

"He was holding them, or moving them, or both." Yor touched the page with one finger. "This mark is Garden. Internal. Not city hall paper, not ministry. Ours."

Anya looked at the cleanup sheet again. Three dates. Three names. Neat entries beside rubber-stamped death.

"The Garden took contracts for them," Yor said. "Not officially. Through cutouts. They marked people in the weapons chain as liabilities and sent me after them."

Behind the words sat the one she wasn't saying: *I killed them and never checked who was paying.*

Loid's hand stopped on the Garden sheet. "How many?"

"Three here. More in the photographed records. I don't have the full count."

"And how long have you known?"

"Since Dominic." Yor folded her hands together once, then forced them apart. "Long enough to know I'm not going back."

He didn't rush her.

"If they send a handler?" he asked.

"I don't answer."

"If they leave instructions."

"I don't take them."

That quieted the room more completely than Crane's name had.

Yor looked at Anya first, then at Loid. "I'll help with the summit. But not as Thorn Princess. Not as their asset. As Yor. As family."

She didn't dress it up. She just set it on the table with the photographs.

Loid reached across and covered her hand with his. "All right," he said.

Yor turned her hand under his and held on.

Anya looked down at Bond's head against her knee. His fur smelled like dust and soap and the apartment itself. Safe things. Crane had had safe things too.

Loid let one breath pass, then another, and went back to the table because that was what he did when the wall was already on top of them.

"Your documents corroborate the procurement chain," he said to Yor. Then to Anya: "Your summit read gives it a clock. We still need floor placement, leadership seating, and whatever Voss thinks his deniability looks like tomorrow."

"Damian can get that," Anya said.

The kitchen changed again.

Loid leaned back a fraction. "Why him."

"Because he's already in it." She kept her eyes on the cleanup sheet until that felt cowardly and she had to look up. "His cache got hit, his line's burned, and he can get closer to Voss than I can. If we shut him out, he walks into tomorrow blind and we lose half the floor."

Yor asked quietly, "Do you trust him?"

"Not cleanly." Anya could feel Loid sharpen at that. "Enough that when he tells me Voss will stand somewhere, I believe him."

Loid's face gave away nothing. His thoughts did.

*Useful. Risky. Young. Attached to her in some fashion. Deal with summit first.*

Anya wanted the floorboards to open.

"If he comes here," Loid said, "he comes because we need what he knows."

"That's why I'm asking."

He held her gaze one second longer, then nodded once. "Text him."

Anya sent the message under the table because it made her feel less exposed even though both of them obviously knew what she was doing.

`Need you at my parents'. Tonight. Summit.`

The reply came back fast enough to sting.

`On my way.`

Loid saw the screen light. "Fast."

"He was awake."

"Of course he was," Loid said.

Yor stood because she needed something to do with her hands. "People think worse on an empty stomach," she said. "I know that's not the biggest problem in the room, but it's still true."

Nobody argued.

She put rice and vegetables on the table. Anya got a few bites down because Yor was watching and because Loid said, "Two more," in the exact tone that made it sound medical. No one tried to make the silence friendly. Loid read the manifests while he ate standing up. Anya kept catching pieces anyway.

*Whiteboard. Venue map. Clean paper.*

*Knife case in the hall closet. Flats, not heels.*

*Eat. Keep chewing. Don't think about the checkpoint. Don't think about Crane in the cold.*

She put her chopsticks down before she snapped them.

Loid noticed immediately. "Enough?"

She nodded.

The kitchen changed by stages. Plates stacked. The sauce bottle moved aside. Loid swung the whiteboard off the wall and leaned it against the refrigerator. A venue map went up with two fruit magnets. Yor's photographs lined the table. Bond followed the motion with grave interest, then parked himself beneath the map as if he had seniority.

"Opening session is still the obvious window," Loid said. He marked the south terrace doors, the east service corridor, and the west leadership row. "They need spectacle and attribution before anybody can correct it."

"The south side gives them both," Yor said. She hadn't touched her tea. "Sight lines, panic, glass."

"And a fast exit if the outside team knows the grounds," Loid said.

Anya caught the next piece before he spoke it, the route lines already built in his head.

*If Yor starts on the terrace and they come through the corridor first, she loses the angle.*

"Foyer junction," Anya said. "Mama starts there."

Both of them looked at her.

Too fast. Again.

Loid asked, "Why."

"Because the terrace is too exposed from the start."

"That isn't quite an answer."

She gripped the back of the chair. "Because the junction holds both lines until contact."

Yor measured the distance on the map with two fingers. *Thirty-five if the shoes are wrong. Less in flats.* Anya heard it and blurted before she could stop herself.

"Go in flats first."

Yor's head came up.

Anya wanted very sincerely to stop talking in front of other people forever.

"I mean for running," she said too quickly.

Yor held her eyes for one long beat, then went back to the map. "Yes. Flats first."

Loid's pen hovered. *How is she there before I say it. Pattern. Later.* Then he tapped the south side again. "Even in flats, she loses the terrace if the first burst comes from outside."

The room tightened. They shifted the route once, then again. Loid pushed Yor toward the terrace. Yor pushed herself back inside. The arrows multiplied. Nothing got cleaner.

Bond stood up so abruptly his nails scraped the floor.

Yor half turned. "Bond?"

He crossed the room fast, faster than an old dog with stiff hips had any right to move, and planted himself in front of the refrigerator. He barked once and jammed his nose against the lower edge of the map.

Anya was already reading him.

Low wall. Wet stone. Grass crushed under hard shoes. Dark clothes moving fast along the south edge. Light on glass. Heat. Noise.

Bond barked again, sharper this time.

Loid followed the line of his muzzle. "South perimeter."

The spot Bond had pinned was outside the south terrace where the landscaped grounds narrowed near the vehicle approach.

"That's real," Anya said.

Loid looked at her, then at Bond, then back to the map. A different memory flickered through him. *Government event. Weeks ago. She pushed me off a bad line without saying how.* He didn't ask. He circled the south perimeter in red.

Yor came up beside him and measured again. "Then I don't start on the terrace. I start inside, stay out of sight, and move south on contact. First man at the bend before the terrace line sees me."

Loid nodded once. "Better."

Bond pressed against the refrigerator, vision already fading into ordinary dog distress. Anya crouched and put her arms around his neck.

"Good boy," she said into his fur. "I know."

Loid capped the red marker with a click. "You're not on first contact," he said without looking at her.

Anya stood. "I know that."

"You're not on it at all."

"Papa."

Now he looked at her.

"No," he said. "I need one variable in this apartment that doesn't get shot at."

The sentence landed harder because he said it like a handler and a father at the same time and didn't bother to separate the two.

"You need somebody on the floor who can tell when the room turns," Anya said. "Not after. Before."

"Somebody does not have to be you."

"I know the players better than anybody you're going to put in a corridor."

"That still doesn't make you expendable."

Yor set her mug down very carefully. "She's not saying expendable."

"Then what is she saying."

"That if the room shifts before the shooting starts," Yor said, "she'll catch it faster than I will from a junction and faster than you will from a relay."

Loid's jaw tightened. *No open floor. Not after Crane. Find another angle.*

The bell rang before he answered.

Yor got there first. "Hello. You must be Damian. Come in. It's awful out there."

"Thank you, Mrs. Forger."

"Yor," she said at once. "Mrs. Forger sounds like someone who sends complaint letters."

That pulled a surprised breath of laughter out of him before he could stop it.

He stepped into the kitchen damp from the rain, coat dark at the shoulders, hair flattened at the temples. Bond inspected his shoes, circled once, and sat squarely on his feet with the solemnity of an official seal. Damian froze. The first thought Anya caught came too fast and too scrubbed.

*Tea. Maps. Don't look at the hall. Don't think Eden. Summit first.*

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

Loid came out of the hall wiping his hands on the apron. "Damian. I'm Loid."

The handshake lasted the exact right length. Loid's smile stayed easy. Behind it the inventory clicked into place.

*Good suit. No aide. No sleep. Looked at her first. Personal.*

Anya considered spontaneous death as a practical option.

"Before we go any further," Loid said, "tell me why you're here."

Damian kept one hand on the back of the chair. "Because the same people who killed Crane hit my side before lunch. My cache is gone. Reinhardt's underground. If I keep pretending tomorrow is still a political event with a security problem, more people die."

Loid said nothing.

Damian set his phone on the table beside the photographs. "And because Anya asked."

There it was. Awful. Direct. Impossible to take back.

Loid's thoughts sharpened. *Too honest on the second point. Good. Or reckless.* "If I think you're protecting your own position at her expense, this conversation gets short."

"Fair," Damian said.

Yor, apparently satisfied that the men had completed whatever strange test counted for them, pushed a reheated plate toward him. "Eat if you can. Operations improve when people chew."

Damian looked down at Bond pinning his shoes to the floor, then at the plate, then at the war map on the refrigerator. A different thought rose up, unguarded enough for Anya to catch.

*Fruit magnets on a kill board. Of course this is her house.*

It hit her wrong and almost made her laugh.

Loid didn't give the room time to settle. "Walk us through the parts we don't have."

Damian stepped to the map. The wet young man in the doorway disappeared under the diplomatic polish and the work underneath it.

"Security tightened after the border hit. Too much, too fast," he said. "Voss stays close, but not on the floor. West Gallery. That's where he can watch the hall and still claim distance if it goes bad."

"Can you get near him?" Loid asked.

"Yes, if I look like I'm there to warn him, not expose him."

"Will he buy that?"

"He wants to."

Yor tapped Bond's red circle. "If south is real, I take the foyer junction and move on contact."

"Agreed," Damian said. "If the first shots come from outside, the panic drives inward. That buys the relay seconds."

Loid uncapped a black marker. "Communications room is still my problem."

"Not the main room," Damian said. "Too watched. Secondary technical station off the east service corridor. If somebody's feeding the shooters live updates, I'd start there."

Anya caught the next piece as he thought it, neat and compressed.

*During seating or I lose him.*

"During seating," she said.

Damian looked at her. Loid looked at both of them.

"Why seating," Loid asked.

"Because once speeches start, everybody locks forward," Anya said. "If Voss is pretending to be informal, he'll only do it while the room is still shifting."

Damian let out one breath. "Yes. That's better."

Loid drew a short black line off the east corridor and didn't comment on how fast she'd gotten there.

"Then Anya stays out of the first break," he said. "Diplomatic annex, back row."

"No," Damian said.

The word came too fast. He corrected a fraction too late. "Not the annex."

The kitchen went still enough that even Bond lifted his head.

Loid's voice stayed mild. "Why not."

"Because annex seating goes through ministry roster review now. They tightened it this afternoon." Damian kept his eyes on the map. "East press is cleaner. Better sight line, separate entrance, cleaner exit if the room breaks."

Anya stared at him. He would not look at her.

Loid folded his arms. "You can get her press."

"Yes."

"At what cost."

Damian gave the smallest lift of one shoulder. "Enough."

"Why spend it on her."

There was father in the question now, not hidden well enough to miss.

Damian held his gaze. "Because she's already on too many lines in this operation. I can move one of them."

That wasn't the whole answer. It was honest enough to leave a mark.

Yor glanced between them. "That sounds expensive."

"It is," Damian said.

"Can you afford it?"

He gave a tired breath that wasn't quite a laugh. "No. But I can spend it anyway."

Loid studied him one beat too long, then nodded once. "Do it."

Damian stepped into the hall and made the call where the rain on his coat could drip onto tile instead of the map.

"Petrov. It's Damian. I need one media credential for tomorrow. East Gallery. Full-room access if you can manage it. Yes, I know what hour it is. I also know whose office covered for your nephew in November, so let's skip the part where you pretend you can't."

Yor blinked once. "Oh."

Anya pressed her mouth shut.

Loid wrote `EAST PRESS` on the edge of the board in block capitals and underlined it once.

*He spends favors for her fast. Noted.*

Damian came back in with the phone still in his hand. "Badge by seven. East entrance, not the diplomatic queue. The ministry line got ugly this afternoon."

Loid pointed the marker at Anya. "If the room breaks, you call it. You don't chase anything."

"I know."

"You don't leave the gallery unless the room is already broken."

"I know."

"If it breaks early," Damian said quietly, "she'll see it."

Loid's jaw moved once. He hated the sentence because he agreed with it.

Yor walked the route from the refrigerator to the hall and back again, counting under her breath, then stopped at the red circle Bond had chosen. "If south goes loud, I cut the first man at the bend and keep the rest off the glass. Somebody else makes sure the floor doesn't read me as part of the attack."

"I can do that," Damian said. "If Voss still has half a conscience, that's where I use it."

"And if he doesn't?" Loid asked.

Damian looked at the west side of the map. "Then I use the other half of the room."

Ugly. Practical. Enough.

No one recited the whole plan after that. Loid marked the east corridor. Yor measured the south line one last time in stocking feet. Damian added two names beside West Gallery and crossed one of them out. Anya spoke only when she had to, and even then everybody in the room seemed to wait through the half second before and after her words, as if they were all noticing the same thing and declining to name it.

By the time Loid capped the marker, the whiteboard held only what they could actually carry into tomorrow: south perimeter circled in red, east corridor cut in black, West Gallery in blue, and `EAST PRESS` underlined once between the fruit magnets. Bond had already gone back to sleep with his nose against Damian's shoe.
