# Chapter 5

Anya kept the Gesellschaft Club roster open across her knees while Damian idled half a block past the entrance instead of pulling in. The note that got her here had come through the cultural exchange calendar at noon under a tedious chain about seating cards and missing RSVPs. Forty minutes later the same thread had picked up a question from Brandt's office asking whether Anna Kessler would be attending the club reception on behalf of the Westalian mission. Damian had answered that too, with the exact amount of bureaucratic boredom the occasion required.

If Brandt wanted the calendar, he had it.

If Gessler pulled Kessler, he would find the harmless cultural attaché file the cover was supposed to produce.

None of that made Damian visible outside a private club with her any less loud. It only made it a risk they had chosen instead of one chosen for them.

Soap still clung to his collar over whatever cedar thing he'd put on and then tried to wash off before picking her up.

He checked the side mirror again.

*No gray Volker. No ministry sedan. Bus, florist van, taxi. Fine.*

He had already taken two unnecessary turns around the square before easing back toward the curb. The third pass finally seemed to satisfy him. He parked under a streetlamp that turned the windshield into pale gold and killed the engine without taking his hands off the wheel.

"Did Brandt answer?" Anya asked.

"Only once." Damian kept looking ahead at the club entrance. "He wanted to know whether Kessler was attending in an official capacity."

"And?"

"I told him Cultural had sent a representative because the club had a Westalian quartet playing after dinner and somebody needed to pretend that mattered."

"That sounds insulting to quartets."

"It worked."

He turned then, just enough to meet her eyes.

"Brandt is active," he said. "Contained, but active. Gessler has your name, which means he has your file. If either of them gets curious enough to move from paper to feet, tonight becomes a problem quickly."

"You came anyway."

"Bretz signs on Tuesday."

Tuesday dropped between them hard enough to turn her stomach once.

She looked back at the roster. Getting it had cost him something. She could tell from the way his thoughts kept catching and restarting. *Bretz? Over the phone? Absolutely not.* *Why do you need that list, Desmond?* *Forget it.* He had cut the calls off before curiosity turned into a report. He had not wanted another message sitting in the same calendar Brandt was already checking. He had wanted to bring her in person even less than he wanted to burn a favor.

If the lead was real, it was moving too fast to hand off. If she was lying about something fundamental, he wanted her close enough to watch it happen.

"Tell me again why we're escalating from fake bureaucracy to actual public embarrassment," she said.

"Because if Bretz signs what I think he's signing, whatever Hartig is building won't wait for either of our institutions to organize themselves." Damian took the folder from her lap, opened it, and tapped the first page with one finger. "He arrives around eight. Cognac in the salon, dinner at nine, never stays past eleven. Tuesday is his procurement review. If he's angry about anything, it'll be tonight. If he's frightened about anything, it'll also be tonight."

"And you couldn't send somebody you actually like."

"I don't actually like anyone suitable for this."

"Comforting."

"Useful."

"His wife is Margarethe," he said. "She likes arranging people. If she decides you're decorative, you'll spend the night getting handed from one conversation to another. If she decides you're attached, you stay where she put you."

"So we go in attached."

"We go in as a couple because that's the only version of this room that gets close to Bretz without setting off alarms." He kept his tone level, but his thoughts lost shape around the edges of the word. *Normal. Fine. People do this every day. They hold hands. They sit close. Nobody files a report about it.* "A young man alone looks ambitious. A young woman alone looks suspicious. A couple looks boring. I want boring."

Anya glanced at the club. Stone facade. Iron balconies. Tall curtained windows lit from inside. The place looked older than every government that had used it.

"How boring are we."

"Enough that no one remembers the details tomorrow."

"That isn't an answer."

"We met at a reception in spring. We kept running into each other. You think my work sounds tedious, which is healthy. I know nothing meaningful about yours because people who actually love each other respect boundaries."

"That sounds fake already."

"Good. Real couples sound fake to everyone else."

She snorted.

"If Margarethe asks more," he went on, "you translate sometimes for the mission. Music, visiting faculty, whatever sounds least interesting in the moment."

"I hate that this cover job is believable."

"It should be. We aren't writing a play."

"And if she asks what I like about you."

That slowed him for half a beat.

"Say I listen well."

"Do you."

"Publicly, yes."

She started to say something sharp and heard herself change course. "That's kind of sad."

"Sad works." He straightened his cuffs. "Nobody remembers sad."

She turned a page. "I still think we should approach separately. You work the room. I find Bretz. We converge when I've got a read on his mood."

"No."

He said it quietly enough that she almost missed the edge in it.

"Because the surveillance risk or because you hate fun."

"Because if Brandt put anybody on this place and I lose you in the room, I have to decide whether to keep the operation alive or get you out." He adjusted his cuffs, too precise about it. "I'd rather not make that decision from across a piano."

She looked at his cuffs instead of his face. He was holding himself so still the buttons barely moved when he breathed.

"Fine," she said. "Then let's say the ugly part out loud."

"The cover."

"The cover."

"We're together."

"That is what a couple is, yes."

A pulse jumped once in his cheek.

"Damian."

He looked at her.

"You're doing the thing with your teeth again."

"I'm not."

He was. He stopped.

"If Margarethe peels me away," he said, "you stay near Bretz, not on him. If he shuts down, you let him shut down. I would rather lose a partial lead than have him remember you as the woman who interrogated him over cognac."

"You say that like it's a risk."

"You look interested when you're interested."

"What a character flaw."

"Tonight it is."

She let that one go because he was right and because she had her own questions.

"What about the dog."

He blinked. "The dog."

"There's a note here about Kaiser being protective and territorial. That matters if we're doing boring domesticity inside six feet of a colonel."

Damian took the roster back and checked the margin note she was pointing at.

"He's usually at Bretz's side," he said. "Big, older, badly trained, adored. If he hates you, we have a problem. If he likes you, Bretz probably does too."

"That seems unstable."

"It's a club full of powerful people making decisions based on what feels familiar. Stability isn't really the governing principle."

She closed the roster. "Anything else."

"Two things." He ticked them off on his fingers. "If I say your name once, you look at me. If I say it twice, we leave."

"And the second thing."

His gaze flicked down to her shoes, then back up. "Try not to limp before nine. Margarethe notices weakness like a hawk."

"These shoes are weakness."

"I know."

"You picked them."

"I was trying to avoid anything that would make you look armed."

"You could've said that before I put them on."

"You still would've worn them."

"True."

That got the smallest flicker at the corner of his mouth.

"All right," he said. "One more time. We enter together. I make the introductions. We stay close enough to Bretz to look social. If he gives you an opening, take it lightly. If the room shifts, we walk. We do not push hard enough that he remembers us tomorrow."

"I won't push." She slid the roster under her coat. "If he has anything, it'll be on the surface. Men like Bretz don't hide irritation well."

His thoughts snagged on that sentence. *How would you know that with this much certainty?*

She kept her face still.

"And if it goes bad," he said, "west exit for you, front for me. No acknowledgment in the room, none on the street, none until tomorrow."

"That's almost trusting me."

"It sounds like contingency planning."

"You're very romantic."

He opened his mouth and then didn't say whatever he'd been about to. Gathered the folder instead, opened his door, and came around the car to open hers. Cold air swept in with the smell of damp leaves, coal heat from the club kitchen, and frying oil from somewhere down the block.

*Open the door. Offer your arm. It's a club, not a military exercise.*

She took his arm.

*Oh, for God's sake.*

They walked toward the club.

***

The Gesellschaft Club's entrance hall was polished wood, marble, and portraits of dead men in uniforms, all wearing the same satisfied expression. A chandelier threw hard white light over the ledger on the doorman's stand. Somewhere deeper in the building silver struck china in little precise collisions.

Damian changed the second they crossed the threshold. The tension in him sealed up and the public version stepped forward, warm, easy, faintly amused at the world and perfectly calibrated for it. His hand stayed at the small of her back just long enough to look practiced.

"Desmond," he said. "And guest."

The doorman found the name, lifted his eyes once toward Anya, then waved them through.

The reception occupied a long salon crowded with candles and bad art. Thirty or forty guests stood in clusters around cocktail tables and the bar. Their thoughts slid over her in scraps: heating oil, a bad hip, a parking ticket, a mistress, fish last month, whether anyone had noticed the inheritance fight was back on, why the quartet was playing Schubert again, whether the minister's wife had altered that dress herself because the hem was dreadful.

It was manageable. There was no state-dinner pressure and no wall of noise, just a roomful of self-important strangers and one target.

She found Bretz near a display of harbor scenes. He had cognac in one hand and a dog at his side big enough to block a doorway.

"That's a very big dog," Damian murmured.

"That's a military installation."

Bretz himself looked like he'd been hard once and now lived with pain too long to hide it. His thoughts were not about politics. *Hip again. Sit down now and she'll say I told you so all the way home.* Irritation kept leaking through him in usable flashes, but nothing about him suggested a man careless enough to think his secrets in complete sentences.

"Colonel Bretz," Damian said, stopping at the exact distance that read respectful rather than eager. "Damian Desmond. We met at Eastbridge last spring."

Bretz turned. *Desmond. Younger one. Foreign Affairs. Father was... no.* "You didn't eat the fish."

"The fish was ambitious."

Bretz gave a single rough laugh and shifted his weight to save his hip. "Who's your friend."

Damian's hand tightened once, barely, at her back.

"My girlfriend," he said.

The word scrambled his thoughts at once. *Fine. Cover. Still alive. Nobody is arresting anyone over one word.*

Anya smiled. "Anna."

"Club's decent," Bretz said. "Cognac's better than the conversation."

The dog noticed Anya then. His head turned. A second later the rest of him followed.

It pressed its nose into her palm.

*Oh. Hello.*

"Kaiser likes you," Bretz said, and it was the first unguarded warmth in his voice. "He doesn't like most people."

Kaiser leaned into her leg. His thoughts came through simple and pleased. *Warm. Good. Stay here.*

"He's beautiful," Anya said, because saying *your dog has adopted me* felt unwise.

She scratched behind his ears. His eyes closed. His tail thumped once against the side table.

"Careful," Bretz said. "He broke a lamp in the card room last month and the committee still acts like it was sabotage."

"Damian," a woman called from across the salon.

Anya did not need the file to know who that had to be. Margarethe Bretz arrived in emerald silk, moving like refusal had never once slowed her.

"Damian Desmond, I knew that was you." She seized his arm before he had agreed to any of this. "You look exactly like that actor. What is his name. The one from the picture about the diplomat and the actress."

*No. Smile. Stay pleasant. Do not yank your arm away from a colonel's wife in a club full of old men.*

"Friedrich Engel," Margarethe said to herself a second later. "That's it."

"It's an honor to resemble someone with that much hair," Damian said smoothly.

Margarethe laughed, delighted. "Come meet the Hasslers. Ernst has been saying the ministry needs more young men with shoulders."

"His shoulders are fine," Bretz said into his cognac.

"Come along."

She had Damian moving before he found a polite excuse. He looked back once.

*Let her work. Don't come back too fast. Smile at the shoulders man.*

Anya watched him disappear into a knot of well-dressed strangers. Then she looked at Bretz. Then at Kaiser, who had planted himself against her leg with devotional certainty.

"Your wife is a lot," she said.

Bretz drank. "That's the charitable version."

His surface thoughts followed the same line. *Opera singer in September. Painter in October. Boy'll be there till midnight if she likes the sound of his last name.* Then, softer under it: *She likes this part. Always did.*

There was enough in that to make him human without making him safe.

Anya let herself lean against the wall beside him, casual, almost sheltered by the dog.

"Do you collect these," she asked, nodding at the watercolors.

"God, no. Koenig paints them. We all pretend they're respectable because his wife left him." Bretz shifted again, pain flashing hot and mean through his hip. "I've got a few decent things at home. Feldmann. Some landscapes."

"I love Feldmann."

This was not true. She had looked him up in the car and remembered one cow.

Bretz gave her a flat look that suggested he did not believe her for a second. His thoughts moved right past art and snagged on something sharper. *Tuesday. Numbers before committee. Not through the usual desk. Again.*

That was the first solid piece.

"My father used to say the army wasn't run by generals," Anya said. "It was run by the men with clean cuffs and rubber stamps."

"Your father was right." Bretz scratched Kaiser's shoulder. "At this point I spend more time on procurement paperwork than I ever spent near a firing range."

His mind skipped, not in a speech, just in irritated pieces. *Krueger-Schiff. Four hundred thousand. Second signature.*

It still wasn't enough. She had money, timing, a second signature, and no name.

Anya kept her voice light. "That sounds worse than war."

"War at least has the decency to be honest."

Across the room Damian's thoughts cut through the crowd. *Horse digestion. Still horse digestion. This man brought notes. Why did he bring notes.*

It nearly got her.

"Bad crowd for honesty?" she asked.

Bretz took another drink. "Bad crowd for anything else."

That answer cost him nothing. She needed him half an inch closer to his own annoyance.

"And yet everybody here keeps signing things."

"At my age," he said, "I sign a lot of things I don't respect."

His hand flattened once over his breast pocket. *Six more years. Sign it. Keep the pension. Get home before the leg locks.*

There was the pocket, and there was the pain, and there was a man who had practiced endurance until it turned into obedience. Still no Hartig. Still no channel.

Kaiser chose that moment to dive for her handbag.

It happened fast enough that she only got one hand on the strap before the compact was gone. The dog backed away with it in his jaws, pleased with himself.

"Kaiser. No."

Anya lunged. Kaiser dodged and trotted toward the bar with the compact.

Two women turned to watch. A bartender froze mid-polish. Somebody laughed. Somebody else thought *Not again.*

Bretz bent to intercept the dog and pain tore through him so sharply she felt it before she heard the thought. *Christ. Hip.*

He caught Kaiser by the collar, pried the compact loose, and straightened with one hand braced at his lower back. A folded cream memorandum slipped halfway out of his inner pocket with the handkerchief he dragged free.

Defense Ministry crest.

Office of the Undersecretary.

Strategic National Interest.

It wasn't enough.

He shoved it back into place before she got more than the shape of it.

"I apologize," Bretz said, offering the compact back at arm's length. "He's usually difficult on a smaller scale."

"It's fine," Anya said. The compact was warm and damp. "He's got taste."

Bretz barked out a real laugh. His guard loosened with it. *All right. Desmond's girl isn't useless.*

Kaiser pressed himself against her again, serene and shameless.

Bretz tucked the memo deeper into his jacket with visible annoyance. His thoughts flashed another broken set of pieces at her. *Tuesday. Secondary authorization. Four hundred thousand. Discretionary.* Then the door shut again.

That was almost enough to take outside, which was exactly the problem.

She could have walked. She had undersecretary, money, a second channel, and a deadline. Damian could probably build from there. Instead she heard the thin empty place where the name should have been and decided that going home with a hole in the middle of the lead would bother her more than the risk of one more step.

"Bad phrase," she said, nodding at his pocket, "or bad memo."

He snorted. "It's the kind of phrase bureaucrats use when they want you to do something ugly and feel patriotic about it."

His eyes were still on her now, not the room.

"And you still have to sign it."

Bretz's expression changed. Not much. Enough.

"You listen closely for a diplomat's girlfriend."

He kept his eyes on her over the rim of his glass, too tired to hide the question all the way.

She smiled as if the answer were harmless. "My father used to come home furious at forms he couldn't refuse. It's a recognizable tone."

"What did he do."

Too clean. Too direct. One more sentence and she'd have to build a whole man out of air.

Across the room Damian's thoughts snagged hard. *Fine. Thursday. Yes, of course, delighted. Let go of my sleeve now.* Then, sharper: *She's in trouble.*

He arrived at her side with the faintly winded look of a man who had purchased his own freedom at social cost and intended to invoice someone later.

"Forgive me," he said to Bretz, sliding an easy hand around the back of Anya's coat. "I left her alone for ten minutes and now she thinks she's an auditor."

Anya turned her head just enough to glare at him.

Bretz looked from one of them to the other. The suspicion in him shifted shape. Couples did this. One person got nosy. The other apologized. Social texture, not tradecraft.

"I was being charming," Anya said.

"You were cross-examining a colonel over cognac."

"Is that frowned upon."

"In most clubs, yes."

Damian gave Bretz an apologetic little shrug that cost him dignity and bought them cover. "My fault. I spent forty minutes being educated on horse digestion and committee appropriations by Ernst Hassler, so now she's heard the word budget too many times and believes we're all dying of paperwork."

"You are all dying of paperwork," Bretz said.

"That may be true. But Foreign Affairs at least kills us by memorandum. If Defense has to do it by charts and secondary signatures, I respect you more than is healthy."

That did it.

Bretz let out a sound too tired to be called a laugh.

"Horse digestion I could survive," he said. "Hartig trying to shove the Krueger-Schiff money through a secondary authorization before committee is what makes me drink."

That was it.

The name hit both of them at once. In Bretz's mind it came with the rest of the structure she had been missing. *Hartig. Same damned undersecretary seal. Same line about national interest. Tuesday. Get it signed, move it quiet, let committee find out after.*

Damian did not react with anything larger than a blink. She could feel the answer locking into place behind his eyes anyway.

"That bad," he said.

"That familiar," Bretz muttered, which was as close to treason as he was willing to go in public.

Margarethe's voice rose again from the far side of the room.

"Ludwig, if you hide behind that dog one more time, I'm sending the Hasslers to fetch you."

"Threat noted," Bretz said under his breath.

Kaiser leaned harder into Anya, satisfied with the world.

"We should let you get back to your evening," Damian said.

"You should," Bretz replied. Then, because Kaiser had already made the decision for him and because Damian had paid for the cover with his own embarrassment, he added, "Take him as a recommendation. He doesn't waste affection."

"That's an alarming standard for a dog," Anya said.

"And yet accurate."

Margarethe swept back toward them before the moment could close.

"There you are." Her eyes went straight to Damian, then to his hand still resting on Anya's back, and brightened with vindication. "I told Ernst you were taken. Men never listen unless a woman confirms another woman already did the work."

"A failing of the sex," Damian said.

"All of them." Margarethe turned to Anya without slowing. "Anna, you must bring him next Thursday. My niece has standards and I need a fresh victim."

Damian's thoughts collapsed into one exhausted line. *I knew it.*

"We'll see whether our schedules cooperate," Anya said before he could politely schedule his own funeral.

Margarethe smiled like a woman perfectly willing to decide for them. "Do that."

They said their goodbyes. Damian thanked Bretz, lied politely about the watercolors, and let Margarethe air-kiss both sides of space near Anya's face. Bretz shook his hand. Kaiser watched Anya all the way to the door.

*Come back,* the dog thought, simple and devastating.

She did not look back because she had already stolen enough from one household for a night.

The cold hit them on the club steps.

"Keep walking," Damian said.

He didn't head for the car. He took them past it, across the street, and around the dark edge of the square instead. Shop windows gave back broken reflections of them in motion. He checked each one without turning his head. No gray Volker. No ministry sedan. One couple arguing under an awning. A delivery boy smoking behind the bakery. A tram dragging blue light over the rails two blocks east.

Only after the second corner did he speak again.

"You heard him."

"You heard him too."

"I heard enough."

They crossed into the park because the trees broke sightlines from the street and because neither of them wanted their first clean exchange about Hartig in a parked car someone might already have identified.

"All right," Damian said. "What did you have before I came back."

"Tuesday review. Four hundred thousand. Krueger-Schiff. Undersecretary's office. Strategic national interest. Secondary authorization." She tucked her hands into her coat pockets against the cold. "No Hartig. Not until you decided to make procurement sound like a shared medical condition."

He let out a breath through his nose.

"That wasn't improvisation. That was self-defense."

"You accepted next Thursday, didn't you."

"I accepted enough of next Thursday to get back to you before you had to invent a father."

She looked at him. "I had one almost ready."

"That's exactly what worried me."

It startled a laugh out of her, smaller this time.

"You're welcome," he said.

"I didn't thank you."

He started to answer and then just looked at her. "No," he said. "You didn't."

They reached the bench under the linden tree and stopped. Damian checked the path once more, then finally sat. Anya dropped down beside him and pulled off the borrowed heels with immediate vindictive relief.

The bench held the day's cold.
Moisture had collected on the slats and darkened the wood.
A tram bell rang somewhere beyond the trees and then the sound thinned into traffic.
Leaves skated across the gravel and lodged against her shoes.
Their breath showed white for a second and vanished.
Water moved in the fountain at the center of the square with the slow stubborn sound of pipes that should have been shut for winter.

For a while they just sat there with Hartig's name between them.

"If he's moving discretionary money through a secondary authorization," Damian said at last, "then the timing is worse than I thought."

"Because of the money."

"Because of the speed." He rubbed a hand over his mouth. "A secondary line means somebody wants committee looking at the thing after it's already alive. Four hundred thousand isn't one bribe and it isn't petty theft. That's infrastructure. Shipping, movement, warehousing, maybe components if Krueger-Schiff is handling the freight end."

"So not a man lining his pockets."

"No." He looked out through the trees. "A man building a pipe."

"Bretz thinks Hartig reports up."

"He does." Damian's thoughts were already making their own map. *Hartig doesn't have the courage for a structure this exposed without cover. Undersecretary's office. Somebody above him wants deniability.* "Nobody builds a pipe like that for personal amusement."

"Good. I hate when corruption is boring."

"This one may still be boring. It just might also get people killed."

The words sat in the cold between them. Anya flexed her toes against the gravel hard enough to feel each stone through her stocking.

"Your freedom cost you a dinner invitation."

"My freedom cost me a dinner invitation, a niece with strong opinions about clavicles, and forty-five minutes of charts on horse feed." He loosened his tie another inch. On him it looked almost reckless. "I think Ernst Hassler carries them for comfort."

"What kind of person brings charts to cognac."

"An Ostanian man with money and no one left who can stop him."

"That's bleak."

"It's also accurate."

She tilted her head back against the bench and looked at the branches above them.

"Margarethe likes you."

"Margarethe likes disruption in a tailored jacket."

"Still counts."

"I'm choosing to believe you don't mean that as an insult."

"I haven't decided."

That got a tired half laugh from him.

"You were good in there," he said after a moment.

"So were you."

"I nearly left you to die with Kaiser."

"Kaiser was the least dangerous thing in that room."

"You say that because he liked you."

"He has—" She stopped herself. "He liked me. I'm counting it."

"Even after the theft."

"Especially after the theft."

He turned to look at her. "You did let that run a second longer than most people would have."

There was no bite in it. He was only looking at her, waiting.

She shrugged one shoulder.

"He was enjoying himself."

"That's not an answer."

"It is if you respect art."

He stared at her for one beat, then laughed despite himself. Short. Quiet. Real.

"Fine," he said. "Art."

"Thank you."

"I didn't say it was good art." But he was trying not to smile when he said it.

For one brief stretch the pressure sat under the bench instead of between them, mixed up with her shoes, the dead leaves, and Hartig's name.

"Still," he said, looking back toward the fountain, "I owe you one."

"You owe me shoes without weapons-grade heels."

"That's fair."

"And maybe dinner somewhere without horse charts."

He made the mistake of imagining Margarethe's niece at the exact wrong moment. The thought came through in a miserable, well-dressed blur. *Pearls. Questions about family. Somebody saying promising jawline as if he's livestock.*

That one got her. The laugh came out before she could manage it, loose and too loud in the cold, the real one with the hitch at the end.

Damian went still.

He didn't go abstractly still or privately inward. He turned toward her with the look of someone who had just heard a floorboard move in an empty house.

His thoughts came in bright involuntary fragments. *Eden courtyard. Autumn sports day. Chalk on the lane. East wall. Her laugh getting out before she could stop it.*

He didn't ask the question he'd asked at the gallery months ago. He didn't go backward into the same shape. He sat there listening to the sound of it fade and then said, almost to himself, "There was a girl at Eden who laughed like that."

The park seemed to tip a little under her.

"Same hitch at the end," he went on. "Autumn relay. I spent the rest of the day insisting the handoff had been unfair because I was ten and humiliatingly committed to being right."

The memory in him was more specific than the words. Chalk on his socks. A baton skidding. A branch over the east wall. His small furious face trying to become dignity and failing completely.

"You were probably more upset about your socks than the race," Anya said.

He turned his head.

"My socks."

Too quick. Too specific. She heard it as soon as it left her mouth.

"You went to Eden," she said, trying to make a joke out of the swerve. "I assume stained socks counted as a constitutional crisis."

His gaze stayed on her, not hostile, not easy either.

"That's an absurdly specific defense."

"You started it."

"Did I."

"You brought up childhood athletics in a park at night. That's on you."

He kept looking at her. Under the streetlamp at the end of the path his face had gone younger around the eyes in a way the club never let happen.

"There was a linden tree by the east wall," he said. "She used to climb it because she thought rules were decorative."

He had not meant to say that much. She could hear the surprise of it in the next beat of silence.

Anya looked at the actual linden above them because looking at him felt dangerous.

"She sounds annoying," she said.

"She was."

"You liked that."

"I was ten," he said, which was not an answer.

It might have been the truest thing he'd said all night.

She could see the afternoon now whether she wanted to or not. The chalked lanes. The ugly little baton. Damian furious in polished shoes. Her own knees green from climbing the wall because the baton had bounced the wrong way and nobody else was fast enough or stupid enough to go get it.

He hadn't asked whether it was her.

She hadn't denied it.

That was its own kind of disaster.

"I've been trying to place that sound since the gallery," he said quietly.

It wasn't a demand and it wasn't quite a confession. It was a fact set between them like the Hartig line had been.

Anya rubbed her thumb over the heel strap of one shoe.

"Maybe you should lose more often," she said.

It was not a good evasive line. It wasn't meant to be. It bought her half a second and admitted nothing.

Something in his expression shifted. Not clarity. Nearer to acceptance that he was not going to get a straight answer tonight and that pressing for one might break the strange better thing they had managed to build in the last two hours.

"That sounds like advice from experience."

"Maybe you're just easy to beat."

"Historically inaccurate."

"That's not what the relay says."

He huffed a laugh and looked down at her shoes by the bench.

"You do this a lot," he said.

"Do what."

"Get to the next part before I've said the current one."

Her whole body went still under the coat.

He was watching her hands now, not her face.

"I work in diplomacy," she said, because it was true enough to hide behind and flimsy enough to sound like a joke. "I thought anticipating people was the whole job."

"Not like that."

There was no anger in it, which somehow made it worse.

The question he did not ask moved through him anyway in fragmentary pieces. *Were you there. Why do you keep feeling familiar. What else are you hiding every time you look at me like that.*

She could have asked which part bothered him most. She could have told him about the baton and the wall and the whole stupid afternoon, at least the schoolyard part of it. The tree. The scraped knees. Enough to take some of that look out of his face.

She did not.

The fountain kept up its cold mechanical breathing.

A taxi hissed past the park.

Somewhere behind them two people laughed and kept walking without ever coming into view.

"Still a good operation," Damian said at last.

"Chaotic operation."

He considered that. "Those can be the same thing."

"That's a terrible philosophy."

"It's gotten me this far."

"You're twenty." It came out wrong. Gentle where she'd meant it sharp. "That's not what I—"

"I know," he said.

She smiled despite herself.

He noticed, which felt unwise and also nice.

For another quiet stretch they sat without trying to improve it. The bench stayed cold under them while the park kept moving around the edges of the night. She left every thought half-finished.

*I could tell him,* she thought. About the tree. About the relay race. About the fact that he had not imagined that girl.

She did not.

"We should go," she said. "My feet are freezing."

"Yeah."

He stood and held out his hand. She took it. His fingers were warm.

*Her hand is ice cold.*

She pulled her shoes back on and they walked to the car without talking. They had Hartig's name now. They had the channel. The lead sat under everything else, ugly and unfinished. They had a dinner invitation Damian would rather fake his own death than accept. Her laughter was still in the air between them, but now it wasn't only the laugh. It was the relay, the tree, the ridiculous socks, the whole autumn afternoon that had finally made it out of hiding.

Either way, the fuse was shorter now.

This time it had a whole memory attached to it.
