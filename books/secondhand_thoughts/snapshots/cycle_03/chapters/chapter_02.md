# Chapter 2

A few days later, the car left her two blocks from the Foreign Affairs Ministry. That was standard practice. A private sedan disappeared into evening traffic, and the district swallowed it without comment. There were no diplomatic plates lingering at the curb and no driver to remember a face.

Anya paid him, stepped onto the sidewalk, and took the city in for one second before the gates.

Berlint at dusk looked almost forgiving. Streetlights were coming up under a flat grey sky. Leaves scraped along the pavement in thin circles. The air carried wet stone, car exhaust, and the sweeter diesel stink from the government lot behind the Ministry. Somewhere back the way she had come, a woman argued with a child about ice cream in October. *He'll get sick. Fine. Maybe one scoop. Damn it.* The thought hit first, then thinned out as distance took it.

Maybe later, for normal people. Right now she had to be fake-diplomat people.

Her credentials sat in a slim wallet inside the clutch: Marta Kramer, junior cultural attache, Westalian mission. The cover had depth: payroll, office keys, a desk on the third floor with two locked drawers and a drooping plant she kept almost killing, a supervisor who thought Marta Kramer did decent work when she remembered deadlines and terrible work when she remembered them too late. Boring was expensive to build. WISE had built her boring properly.

Two SSS officers stood at the entrance under the Ministry crest. The taller one checked her papers against the guest list. The shorter one watched her face with professional boredom and a bad lower back.

*Division with remainders again. Mina's going to cry and I won't get home before bedtime and Nora will be furious.*

"Attache Kramer." The taller man handed the papers back. "Main gallery. Second doors on the left."

"Thank you."

Heat met her in the foyer. So did the event.

It wasn't just the general fact of a crowd. It was this crowd. Diplomatic minds came lacquered over: full sentences rehearsed into shape, talking points laid in rows, grudges dressed for dinner. Under that sat the real mess: an aide counting chairs and enemies, a deputy minister thinking about his mistress and a fisheries vote in the same breath, security officers running faces, doors, and distances, spouses sorting one another into triumphs and mistakes by fabric alone. Champagne loosened the stitching. Warmth loosened it further. By the time she crossed the marble, people's thoughts were already slipping out through the seams.

Breathe. Don't snatch at everything. Don't be stupid.

She smoothed the front of her dress, dark blue and respectable, the sort of dress a junior cultural attache wore when she wanted to look serious and forgettable, and walked into the ballroom.

The Ministry did state dinners the way it did everything else: with marble, chandeliers, and the steady suggestion that everyone present ought to feel honored. Warm gold light sat on the floor. White linens glowed under crystal. A quartet near the windows played a Baroque piece nobody cared about. Waiters moved through the room with champagne, wine, and a pale nonalcoholic drink nobody wanted. Ministry catering meant the food would look official and taste tired.

Warm rooms made sloppy thinking. So did alcohol. So did people standing too close to one another and pretending not to notice. Pressure gathered behind her left eye almost at once.

Rule one: keep moving.
Rule two: let the useful thought trip over you by accident.
Rule three: do not puke on foreign policy.

A waiter passed. Anya took a glass of champagne and let herself drift just slowly enough to look social.

Deputy Minister Werfel saw her first and brightened in a way that had nothing to do with diplomacy. "Attache Kramer. Good to see you again."

"Deputy Minister Werfel. How was the recital?"

His face opened. *She remembered. Elena will be ridiculous about this.*

"Wonderful. One missed note in the Handel, but she recovered beautifully."

"I missed six notes the last time I touched a piano, so that sounds heroic."

Werfel laughed, and the exchange stayed easy. He kept talking about his daughter's bow hold and the National Gallery exhibit and whether the ministry had overfunded a restoration nobody would admit liking. Anya nodded in the right places and let the room keep muttering around him.

*Schiller can sign it himself if he wants the appropriation that badly.*

*That is the Trade Minister's wife's dress. No, worse, it's almost the same dress.*

*The pork is dry. How is pork dry in gravy?*

Then a sharper line caught.

*If Defense punts again, south gate sits another week.*

Anya did not look at the source yet. Looking was how you told on yourself.

Werfel was still talking. "I always think modernists improve when they're slightly underfunded. Desperation helps."

"That's comforting. I've been underfunded for years."

He laughed again, took another sip of wine while she took champagne, and another slip came from somewhere near the windows.

*Three weeks. Four if Kleist keeps hiding behind procedure.*

A checkpoint, a timeline, and a name. It wasn't much, but it was something.

She let her attention widen by inches instead of jerking toward it. The woman wore a charcoal suit, looked to be in her mid-fifties, held white wine in her left hand, and never actually drank it. Her thumb tapped once against the stem each time somebody crossed between her and the annex tables. Anya kept her face on Werfel and stored the picture.

"You Westalians are all pretending to like abstraction," he said. "It's a national vice."

"Not all of us. Some of us are pretending to understand it."

That got her another pleased burst from him and a second, more dangerous current from two bodies over.

*Civilian line item on top this time, idiot. Do you want Muller asking why concrete costs like ammunition?*

It was General Brennan, border infrastructure command, thick-necked and excellently tailored, with a mind full of columns and irritation.

*Liaison allocation. Keep it in liaison. Don't put uniforms anywhere near paper.*

It wasn't clean and it wasn't enough. But now it rhymed: south gate, Kleist, civilian line, money pushed sideways.

A cultural secretary from the Ostanian side drifted up before she could peel away. He had a damp upper lip and a folder tucked under one arm because some people would rather die than arrive anywhere without paperwork.

"Attache Kramer. The film week proposal."

Of course it was.

"Yes?"

"Your mission still wants the Murnau posters in the smaller gallery, not the foyer?"

*Please say yes. Please say yes so Culture stops calling me at home.*

"Smaller gallery," Anya said at once. "The foyer eats anything delicate."

Relief showed at once. "Good. Excellent."

"Though if you give us the foyer instead," she added, because Marta Kramer would complain just a little, "I'll still write a beautiful thank-you note."

He almost smiled. "I'll keep that in mind."

He moved on. Werfel got claimed by a trade delegate. Anya finally had room to breathe, which lasted about six seconds before two new thoughts clipped past each other so close they practically sparked.

*Why is Brennan pretending he doesn't know the number?*

*If Griesler folds early, half the room starts forgetting who invited whom.*

She shifted toward the long side of the ballroom and picked up a plate from a passing tray because empty hands invited questions. She ended up with a cheese puff, anchovy toast, and something with a mushroom cap pretending to be elegant. She ate the cheese puff on the move. Salt helped.

Near the center of the room Brennan stood with three men who all looked as if they enjoyed using the word strategic. Numbers rolled through his mind in hard clean blocks until his sciatica barged in over them.

*Hotel chair tonight and I swear to God I'll set the place on fire.*

Apparently the state ran on hidden accounts and bad backs.

So Brennan wasn't the whole answer. Maybe he wasn't even the main one.

She circled wide, letting the event carry her. A deputy's wife mentally drafted a letter to a school headmistress. An undersecretary tried not to think about the ulcer starting under his ribs. A young diplomat composed an apology text to a woman he had definitely lied to. Anya yanked away from that one so fast she nearly clipped a waiter with her shoulder.

*God. No. Not that one.*

The room would not become useful just because she needed it to. It stayed full of everybody else's garbage while she hunted for the one thread that mattered.

At the far end of the ballroom, just beyond the first line of tables, the charcoal-suited woman had turned enough for Anya to catch her profile. She had an administrative face, the kind nobody remembered and everybody regretted underestimating.

*If he asks for the guest log again, tell him I never had it.*

It still wasn't a who and it still wasn't enough. But now there was a he.

Anya took another half-step. The woman's gaze flicked, not to Brennan this time, but to the annex doorway where three retired party men in black suits were pretending not to huddle. One of them laughed with his mouth and not his eyes.

*Desmond should know better than to ask in the open.*

There it was.

But which one? Demetrius, Damian, Donovan himself by proxy? The Desmond name covered too much ground to be useful alone.

A low chime rang from the doors. Staff began steering the room toward tables with the gentle aggression only government hospitality perfected.

Fine, then. She would sit down and watch what rose when people got trapped with food and protocol.

Her place card put her one rank too far from importance. That was good for invisibility and bad for access. Table fourteen sat off to the left of the central aisle, close enough to see the dais and the annex doors if she angled her chair, far enough that nobody important would stay long.

The seating mix looked deliberate in the ugly Ministry way. Anya between a deputy from cultural preservation and the widow of a deceased rail minister, across from a deputy legal adviser, one SSS liaison in formal dress, and Colonel Dreher, retired, who wore his age the way some men wore medals: as proof that time had failed to beat them properly.

The widow spoke first. "Westalis sends its young women to do all the work now, does it?"

Anya smiled. "Only the women who can survive committee minutes."

That got a wheezing laugh, and just enough approval to buy silence for a minute.

Soup arrived. It tasted like warm apology. The quartet kept playing. A vice minister rose to welcome everyone and think resentfully about his tailor while saying peace and dialogue and mutual respect into a microphone.

At the next table, a man whose hair looked shellacked into patriotism checked the annex doors every thirty seconds.

*He was in there twice already. God forbid the boy ever just eat his soup.*

Anya lowered her eyes to the bowl and listened harder.

*You want an attendance list, go ask scheduling. You want April fourteen, ask someone else.*

Across from her, the deputy legal adviser thought in dense little knots.

*No military signatures. That's the entire point. Civilian board, civilian purchase, civilian exposure if it bursts.*

He lifted his spoon and said aloud, "The saffron is subtle."

Anya nearly laughed in his face.

Colonel Dreher had gone from politely indifferent to mildly annoyed at something across the room.

*Another circuit. Christ. He has Donovan's jaw and none of Donovan's patience.*

It still wasn't a name. But now there was a shape in the air: a younger Desmond making circuits, not eating, asking retired men about schedules and lists and things they didn't want to say in the ballroom.

The widow leaned toward Anya. "You know, I always think cultural affairs sounds restful, and then I remember artists."

"They're usually less dangerous than ministers," Anya said.

"Usually?"

*She's prettier than the last one. Shame about the hair.*

"Only usually," Anya said, because that was safer than responding to the rest.

Bread came. Fish came. The pork came later and proved the earlier complaint right even before she tasted it. Around the table people discussed restoration grants, the opening of a museum wing, fuel import negotiations they pretended were not fuel import negotiations, and a concert series nobody wanted to fund but everybody wanted their name on.

Underneath, the room kept leaking.

*South route. Not checkpoint. Stop calling it the checkpoint where anyone can hear you.*

*If Griesler takes the committee copy instead of the cleaned one, we're done.*

*He said forum, not committee. Forum. Why is he still on the forum?*

Useful and useless came through the same door: a philanderer worrying about lipstick on his collar, a woman silently reviewing whether she had over-salted tomorrow's roast, one security officer cataloguing exits, another thinking about hemorrhoids with enough misery to light up the whole room. By the time the main course landed, pain was beating behind both Anya's eyes.

She touched two fingers lightly to the side of her head, then dropped the hand before it became a tell.

The SSS liaison at the table noticed anyway. *Headache. Too much perfume in here.*

"You all right, Attache Kramer?"

"Perfectly diplomatic," she said.

He snorted into his wine.

At the far edge of the ballroom, a dark-haired man moved out from one retired cluster and into another with the calm of somebody pretending not to work very hard. Not tall enough to be Demetrius, she thought first. Then she checked that against what she actually knew and stopped being lazy. Height meant nothing from here. The angle was wrong. All she had was a dark suit, good posture, and the fact that older men who ignored everybody else kept letting him into the corners of their conversations.

The younger Desmond, Dreher had thought.

Anya cut a piece of pork and forced it down.

The vice minister moved from welcome remarks into a toast. Crystal rose around the room. So did the thoughts.

*Smile, smile, smile, then ask him who approved the reallocation.*

*If he says liaison again I'm leaving him to die.*

*Why is Donovan's son talking to Kleist's clerk?*

There it was. It wasn't just retired party fossils; administrative staff were in it too.

Anya drank. The champagne had gone flat.

Dreher followed her line of sight without moving his head. "A dreary breed, diplomats," he said aloud.

"Some of them," Anya said.

"No sense of timing."

*If the boy corners me after dessert, I'll tell him the same thing I told his father twenty years ago. Put it in writing or stop whining.*

That one hit hard enough that her hand tightened on the stem.

So it ran through his father: old Desmond channels, administrative nerves, south gate, civilian paperwork, a forum guest log. None of it was clean, but it was enough for her to smell the shape of something ugly.

Dessert came and went under speeches about heritage, trade, and the spiritual value of exchange programs. The widow of the rail minister confessed aloud that she hated custard. The deputy from cultural preservation admitted, after his third drink, that he thought most public sculpture should be quietly buried. The quartet changed tempo. Chairs loosened. Tables broke apart in waves.

If she waited much longer the room would dissolve into too many conversations at once and she'd lose the thread in the sludge.

She excused herself on the strength of needing air and stepped out into the crush between tables.

A minister's aide near the wall was composing the resignation letter he had been writing in his head for three years and would never send. An SSS officer near the annex doors was on shift change and hated the replacement's shoes. A woman in green had gotten sauce on her sleeve and wanted the earth to open. Then, under all of it, the cleaner line pulled again.

It wasn't dinner chatter or drunk vanity. It was focused, ordered, and running too fast.

Names and days kept flashing by, attached to a list that refused to stay in one place.

Anya turned toward the French doors at the far end and almost made it before Colonel Dreher cut across her path with a fresh drink and the stubbornness of a man who believed public space reorganized itself around him.

"You're with the Westalian mission, yes?"

"Cultural affairs." Her smile held. "Marta Kramer."

"Dreher. Retired, allegedly." He gave her a practiced look that had probably unsettled younger women for decades. "Enjoying the evening?"

*Westalian. Young. Alone. If they sent her to fish, they're getting lazy.*

"Very much."

"Good."

He did not move.

All right, fine. She could run two tracks.

"You seem skeptical," Anya said.

"I distrust any evening where they serve pork under gravy and call it generosity."

That almost got her. "Then we're aligned."

He studied her another second, then huffed once through his nose.

*If Desmond asks me about April again, he can freeze with the smokers for all I care.*

April again. That gave her something firmer.

Aloud he said, "Your people still trying to borrow our print collection?"

"Borrow is a harsh word. We prefer 'temporarily admire from abroad.'"

"Predatory."

He was enjoying himself now, which made escape possible. Anya used the opening the second he gave it, drifting past with a soft apology and a lift of her empty glass.

Cold hit the minute she stepped through the doors. Iron sconces threw weak light across the terrace stones. A couple argued in hushed vicious voices near the balustrade. An older man checked his watch with naked hatred. A woman smoked like she was stealing time from God. The air smelled of rain, tobacco, and wet stone cooling too fast.

And near the far railing, alone, stood the mind she had been following through half the room.

He wore a dark suit and held himself very straight, one hand around a drink he had barely touched, the other flat on the stone as if he had taught it calm by force. Even from a distance she could see he wasn't looking at the view. He was looking at the glass doors, the annex corner reflected in them, the route back in. He was working, not brooding.

The signal sharpened so fast it made her teeth hurt.

*Griesler first. Before month-end. If he panics and runs to committee, it stops being a review and starts being troops at the fence.*

Anya slowed without meaning to.

*Kleist tomorrow if his secretary lies again. Wednesday if I have to go through the father-name door.*

Another fragment, clipped and mean with fatigue.

*Need the forum list. Need the civilian names on the draft, not the cleaned copy.*

It still wasn't enough to hand anybody, but it was enough to turn her.

He shifted, just slightly, and she saw more. His knuckles were white against the glass stem. The drink sat almost full. Whatever expression he wore toward the room had dropped off out here; all that remained was concentration stretched thin over anger.

One more pulse came off him, fast and ugly.

*If they see me before I'm ready, everyone I leaned on burns.*

That did it.

He wasn't feeding the scheme. He was trying to document it before someone could sell it as policy and turn the border red.

Her stomach dropped anyway. Tomorrow's report changed shape on the spot. Sylvia would want the path, the logic, the confidence level. WISE would want sources she could not name because she had stolen them straight out of people's heads. And Damian Desmond, if that was who this was, had not chosen to tell her any of it. He had stepped out for air and she had gone through him like a lock pick.

The answer brought no comfort. It only gave her a worse problem.

He moved to set the glass down, and the terrace light caught his face.

It was Damian Desmond.

He was older, obviously, sharper through the jaw and darker under the eyes, with the soft roundness of Eden sanded out by adulthood and bad sleep. But it was Damian. He was the same age as her, with the same mouth when it went flat with irritation and the same line between the brows when he thought too hard. WISE kept a file on him because WISE kept a file on every Desmond worth breathing near. The paperwork called him a junior diplomat with moderate potential and uncertain loyalties. It had not mentioned the look of somebody holding a roomful of inherited wolves apart with his bare hands.

He scrubbed a thumb once over the edge of the stone.

*No more delays. No more waiting for them to hand me the harmless version.*

The thoughts kept coming, but now she stopped treating them like answers and started sorting them against what she had seen inside: the administrative woman by the windows not drinking, Brennan burying money in liaison lines, Dreher thinking about April and old arguments with Damian's father, the way retired men kept letting him into their corners because the family face still opened doors, the almost untouched drink, the full body stillness of a man using every inch of himself not to pace.

This was what she had come for: not a clean confession, but a pattern under pressure.

And she had it because she had taken it.

Anya stayed where she was, arms crossed against the cold because it looked natural and because the cold had finally reached her skin. The sensible move was obvious: mark him, go back inside, pull one more confirming thread from the room, leave. The actual move was harder. She had enough to ruin him with the wrong framing and not enough to protect him with the right one.

The terrace door opened behind her for a smoking attaché and banged once in the wind. Warm light spilled across the stone, brief and gold. The gust caught the loose front strand of her hair and blew it over her mouth. Annoyed, she set her glass on the railing with a sharp crystal click and shoved the pink strand back behind her ear.

Damian's thoughts broke stride.

His head turned just enough to catch her in the edge of his vision.

*Pink?*

Then the present tore.

*Courtyard. Spring. Bell late again.*

Her fingers dug into her own sleeves.

Sun on paving stones. A white shirt half untucked from running. A schoolbag dropped too hard. Pain and humiliation arriving together.

*You hit me right in the face. God.*

Not spoken, only remembered. He was thirteen again, nose bleeding down the front of his shirt, furious because it hurt and because half the yard had seen it.

The memory jerked, found another scrap.

A wet paper towel shoved at him.
A girl's face gone wide with horror.
That same girl's mouth twitching because the hit had been an accident and also, unfortunately, impressive.

Anya stopped breathing for one beat.

He kept turning the fragments over, not on purpose anymore. The way memories got handled when they leapt out before a person had decided whether they wanted them.

*Same tilt.*

*Green eyes.*

*No, that's insane.*

His attention snagged harder.

*Pink hair. Of course it was pink.*

For one second the terrace, the state dinner, the border, all of it thinned under the stupid bright violence of childhood: Eden uniforms, brick warmed by sun, his own outrage, her own mortified silence before she'd tried to apologize and somehow made it worse.

Then the first edge of a name pushed up.

*An...*

The terrace door opened again.

"Desmond," a man in a grey suit called from inside. "The deputy minister wants you."

Damian straightened at once. The schoolyard vanished. His mind snapped back into neat political lines so fast it felt violent.

"Of course."

He picked up his glass and turned toward the doors. As he crossed past her, six feet away at most, his thoughts were already on appropriations, border posture, who hated whom at the deputy minister's table, whether Kleist would dodge him again if the minister kept him late. His public face was back on. He was a diplomat again.

One brief glance brushed over her. It was automatic, polite, almost dismissive. There wasn't enough light, enough time, or enough reason in the middle of a state dinner to connect a pink-haired Westalian cultural attache to a girl from school who had once punched him in the face by accident and then failed to apologize correctly.

*Westalian. Freezing. Why don't any of them ever bring coats?*

Then he was through the doors and gone into the big hot noise of the ballroom.

He had not recognized her fully. All he had was pink hair, a courtyard, a punch, the front edge of her name.

Anya stayed on the terrace until the cold bit through the dress and into muscle. The flat champagne sweated against her palm. From inside came the muffled churn of the room closing around itself again, ministers reclaiming ministers, aides scooping up bosses, security shifting for departures. Her heartbeat had gone too fast and stayed there.

The bad part wasn't the mission result. The mission result should have simplified tomorrow, and instead it had split it open. She had enough to change WISE's read on the sector and no clean way to use it without dragging Damian, his access, and whoever he had leaned on into the light before he was ready. If she'd been anybody else, it would've been easy.

The bad part was how she had gotten it.

She'd let herself lean in past the point she would have called necessary in anybody else's report. She had taken surface thoughts because she had to, then she had stayed for fear, then for strategy, then for a memory that had nothing to do with the border and everything to do with the small ugly fact that she had no doors in her head and everybody else did.

The woman smoking two meters away crushed her cigarette out and thought, *If he leaves me I'm taking the apartment.* That, at least, was somebody else's problem.

Anya pushed off the railing and went back inside before the terrace became conspicuous.

The ballroom had begun its slow collapse into coats, handshakes, and strategic lingering. She did not look for Damian again. Looking now would have been stupid. She made one clean circuit of the room, caught only static and leftovers, let Brennan's thoughts confirm that he was still hiding money in the wrong place, let the charcoal-suited woman think miserably about a missing guest log, and then took the first exit that did not require another speech.

The SSS officer at the doors glanced at her pass and thought about his bus home. That was good enough.

Outside, the air felt cleaner even with traffic in it.

She walked the first six blocks instead of taking a car. She'd had too many minds in her head already that evening. The government district thinned into broader streets, then apartments, then shops shutting under metal grilles. Her heels bit at the backs of her feet. Wet leaves stuck to the curb. By the time she got to a main road, her headache had turned from sharp to deep and ugly.

She took a tram for three stops because she was tired and immediately regretted it.

*Did I turn the stove off?*

*No, no, no, don't cry here.*

*If he asks where I was, I say paperwork.*

Anya got off one stop early and walked the rest.

Her apartment building smelled like boiled cabbage and radiator dust. Mrs. Voller on the second floor was thinking about game shows loud enough to hear before Anya reached her own landing. By the time she got her door open, the key teeth had left fresh half-moons in her palm.

She kicked off her shoes in the hallway, made it to the sink, and threw up ministry pork, too much acid, and almost no champagne.

So much for not puking on foreign policy. At least the foreign policy had survived.

She ran water until the basin cleared, stood there with both hands braced against porcelain, and waited for the room to stop shifting under her feet. The headache thudded behind her eyes with slow professional patience. Her earrings were suddenly unbearable. She stripped them out, dropped them on the counter, and pressed the heels of her hands against her sockets until color burst under her lids.

Damian's near-formed *An...* came back immediately.

"Great," she muttered to the sink. "Love that."

The apartment offered no comfort beyond privacy. It was one room and a kitchenette, with a desk by the window, a radiator that clanked when it felt philosophical, two chairs, one of them mostly decorative, and a narrow bed under a bookshelf holding equal parts tradecraft manuals and cheap novels she kept buying at kiosks because pretending not to be a spy required hobbies. Tonight the room felt staged for somebody else: somebody who came home from the ministry and wrote harmless notes about cultural exchange schedules instead of deciding whether to lie to her own service.

She changed out of the dress without turning on the bright overhead, traded stockings for old pajama pants and a sweater with a hole by one wrist, tied her hair back badly, and sat at the desk with a legal pad, the evening's seating card, and a blank WISE report form she should not have been looking at until morning.

The pad got the fragments first, raw and out of order.

South gate.
Kleist.
Griesler.
Civilian line item.
Liaison allocation.
Forum list.
April fourteen.
Desmond.
Old files.

She stared at the names until they stopped being words and went back to sounds.

The first version came out too fast because truth usually did when it had nowhere to go.

She rolled paper into the machine and started typing.

Subject identified as Damian Desmond, Foreign Affairs. Off-book activity suggests he is conducting a covert inquiry into procurement irregularities connected to border review channels and Renewal Front-adjacent actors. Access appears to derive from old Desmond political relationships with retired party and committee figures.

Anya stopped with her fingers still on the keys.

There was no source path and no way to explain the confidence level. There was no way to prove why Damian mattered without handing Sylvia a person to squeeze, watch, or burn. WISE would not torture him. That was not the service she worked for. But they would turn him into an operation immediately. They would open him up like a file and map every contact they could reach through him: every older man at dinner who had let him into a corner, every clerk who had given him one more paper than protocol allowed, every frightened administrative woman guarding guest logs with a wineglass in her hand.

*If they see me before I'm ready, everyone I leaned on burns.*

She had taken that straight out of him. She could not unknow it now.

Also, and this part mattered more than she wanted it to, the report was impossible on its own terms. Anya had observed a young diplomat moving between tables. She had observed older officials accepting him. She had observed him take air on the terrace and think like a man trying to keep a border incident from turning into something worse. Everything that made the report true also made it unusable. Sylvia would ask how Anya knew the difference between internal rivalry and a real counter-operation. She would ask why she felt confident about intent. She would ask for the chain.

Anya could lie by omission around telepathy in the field. She did it every day. But this was not omission. This was a report built from the center out on information nobody had chosen to give.

She yanked the page free hard enough to wrinkle it and dropped it on the floor.

A second version came out cleaner, smaller, safer.

Damian Desmond remains a person of interest due to unusual access patterns across ministry and party-adjacent personnel. Current behavior suggests freelance positioning around internal security chatter rather than direct operational control. Continued watch recommended. Renewal Front indicators present but fragmentary.

The sourcing posture was better. The consequences were worse.

This one still put Damian under a lamp. It still told WISE to keep a close enough eye that he would eventually feel it, maybe not tomorrow and maybe not next week, but soon. And if he was using old family channels against Ostanian hardliners, close watch from Westalis would squeeze him from the wrong side at exactly the wrong moment.

She read the page again and felt the problem move under her hands instead of resolving.

Because there was a second lie buried under the operational one. She wasn't only trying to protect a useful line. She was trying to protect the private ugliness of what she had done: the terrace, the stolen fear, the childhood memory that had nothing to do with the assignment until it did, because once Damian almost remembered her, the entire mission ceased to be about a target and became about a person she had known before either of them were old enough to choose any of this.

She had heard him bleed in memory.

That thought sat in her throat like something physical.

Anya pushed back from the desk, stood too fast, and paced once to the window. Across the street a man in shirtsleeves smoked under an awning and thought blankly about football. That much, at least, was merciful. The radiator knocked twice. A car passed. Somewhere down the block a dog barked and got answered by another dog and then another. It was ordinary city noise, ordinary strangers with their ordinary private heads.

And then there was her, sitting in the middle of it.

By the time she sat back down, the second report might as well have been a confession, not to telepathy but to wanting to be the kind of person who could split the difference cleanly, tell enough truth to stay useful, and hold enough back to keep Damian alive, or free, or simply unexamined. That person probably existed in spy novels written by men who had never had to hear a target remember being thirteen.

She tore the second page in half, then in half again.

The room smelled faintly of sick and paper dust. Anya rubbed the heel of one hand over her sternum like that might knock the discomfort loose.

What did she actually know if she forced herself to use only what could live on paper?

Damian Desmond had access.
Damian Desmond had interest.
Renewal Front chatter was real.
Procurement chatter was real.
The room at the ministry was more frightened than it should have been for routine nationalist noise.

She also knew, with the kind of certainty nobody sane could cite in a report, that Damian was working against it.

That certainty would stay in her body whether she filed it or not.

She fed a third page into the machine and sat for a long time without typing. Her fingers rested on the keys, did nothing, moved, and stopped again.

At last she began with the version that could survive questions.

Mid-level diplomat with family visibility. Politically useful name, inconsistent direct authority. Activity at the ministry event suggested internal positioning and information gathering around security-adjacent issues, but present evidence does not support categorizing him as the primary operational threat.

It was still a lie, just neater.

She kept going because stopping there would have been honest in a way the rest of it wasn't.

Renewal Front references overheard at the event remain incomplete and likely include opportunistic posturing by multiple domestic actors. Recommend monitoring as elevated nationalist chatter rather than imminent action pending corroboration.

That line hurt more than the first one had.

Because now she was doing it on purpose.

She wasn't just hiding telepathy or protecting a source path that had never been a source at all. She was steering. She was building a document designed to make Sylvia look away half a degree, a bureaucratic nudge that might buy Damian space and maybe buy herself one more chance to figure out what the hell he was actually doing before WISE stepped on it.

It was the ugliest kind of lie because most of it was professionally legible. Nobody reading it tomorrow would hear a crack in the sentence. Nobody would think she had gone home sick from ministry pork and typed her way into a betrayal with a childhood memory still stuck under her ribs.

Anya reached for the legal pad and added one more line to the raw notes in pen.

He almost had my name.

She stared at that, then drew a box around it as if containment on paper could do anything useful.

This part did not belong in the report at all. It did not belong anywhere. Damian remembering her wasn't an intelligence datum she could file. It was a timer.

The whole problem reduced itself to four bright stupid scraps: pink hair, a punch, a courtyard, An.

By chapter standards of her own life, maybe that still qualified as "probably fine." By any sane standard, it was a problem moving toward her with its head down.

She thought about the look that had not quite become recognition when he passed her on the terrace. It had been automatic and dismissive, then gone. He had not placed her, not yet. But he had reached for it without meaning to, and that meant the memory was close enough to surface again the next time some stupid detail lined up: a laugh, a gesture, Becky saying the wrong thing at the wrong time if the universe felt theatrical.

The report sat in the machine half-finished.

Anya read it from the top, adjusted one phrase, cut another, and added a softer uncertainty where the truth pressed too hard against the edges. By the end it looked like good analytical caution. That was the part she hated most.

She slid the page out and set it beside the torn remains of the first two versions. The desk was a mess of paper and bad choices: one true and unusable, one cautious and still too dangerous, one smooth enough to survive daylight.

She gathered the third version, clipped it into a plain folder, and left the raw notes loose under her palm for a second too long.

Tomorrow morning she would walk into Sylvia's office with neat paper, controlled voice, and a professional assessment built on scraps, observation, and a lie she had now told enough times inside her own head to make it sound trained.

The first two drafts went into the ashtray on the windowsill. She struck a match, touched flame to one corner, and watched the paper blacken inward. The fire ate Damian's name first. Then Griesler. Then April. The room smelled briefly of smoke and chemical ink.

When the ash cooled, she crushed it flat with the matchbook and rinsed the tray clean.

The folder stayed on the desk.

Anya turned out the lamp, stood in the dark apartment with the ministry still humming at the back of her skull, and understood that nothing about tonight had ended on the terrace. The discovery was the easy part. The easy part had already happened.

Tomorrow the lie would stop being a draft and become a record.
