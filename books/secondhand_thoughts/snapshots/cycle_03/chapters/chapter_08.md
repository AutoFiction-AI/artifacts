# Chapter 8

The noise started in the elevator.

It did not come from the machinery. The Concordia's elevators glided without a sound, part of what people were paying for. The man beside Anya said nothing. He had a leather portfolio, a neutral face, and an expensive tie. His thoughts, unfortunately, were having a full panic spiral.

*Left the gas on. No. Did I? I turned the knob.*
*I'm almost sure I turned the knob.*
*If the building burns they'll find the Ackermann notes on my counter and think I wrote a suicide note. Christ.*

Anya adjusted her earring and watched the floor numbers climb. The startle of other people's thoughts had burned off years ago. What stayed was the drag of it, the low constant pull, like carrying a radio under her ribs that never shut up for long.

Tonight was going to be loud.

The elevator opened onto the third-floor lobby. Beyond the double doors, the Westalian trade delegation's autumn reception was already underway. She could hear the quartet through the wood, then glassware, then shoes on polished floor. Under all of that ran the other layer.

*If he says grain tariff one more time I'm leaving.*
*Nice shoes. Who is she?*
*Where is the shrimp. They said seven.*
*Maria's going to assume the worst. Again.*

Two hundred minds, maybe more, all stacked on top of each other. The gas man was right behind her, still thinking about the stove. Before she could step into the room, a side door opened off the service corridor and Damian came through it with a paper folder under one arm and Thursday still all over his face.

He had changed ties between Lang's working group and the reception. The rest of him had not recovered. His jaw was set too hard. The diplomatic smile was present in outline only.

"You look terrible," Anya said.

"That's generous." He came to a stop beside her without quite standing beside her, close enough to talk and far enough to deny later. "Lang's meeting happened."

"And?"

"And I'd like to know why a clerical correction arrived on Ackermann's lap before Lang announced there'd been one."

That cut through the room noise cleanly. "You got the annex numbers?"

"Most of them. Not the first version."

"What was missing from the correction?"

"Two shipping lines and one staffing note." His fingers tightened once on the folder, then eased. "Lang called it routine cleanup. Nobody at that table believed him enough to look relaxed."

"Voss?"

"Didn't attend. Brandt did. Ackermann did. Dietrich drifted through after as if he'd taken a wrong door and stayed for the port policy."

"That's not suspicious at all."

"No, it's charming." Damian glanced at the ballroom doors. Somebody laughed inside. Somebody else thought about cholesterol with total despair. "One more thing. The word review kept coming up."

"What kind of review?"

"That's the problem. At least three kinds." His voice stayed mild, but the irritation underneath had edges. "Lang meant committee review. Ackermann meant border checkpoint review. One of the trade people was thinking about an oversight review tied to budget tables. Same word, three different knives."

Anya let that settle. Inside the room, a server hurried by with a tray of sparkling wine and thought only about not dropping it in front of the ambassador again. A woman near the entrance was trying not to think about her heel blister and therefore thinking about nothing else.

"What do you want from me?" she asked.

"If Ackermann, Dietrich, or Brandt turn up in your range, listen for anything that links the annex corrections to the reception. If you hear review attached to schedules or staffing, remember exactly who said it."

She looked at the folder. "You wrote it down."

"I wrote down what people said out loud. That's not the same thing." His gaze flicked to hers for a beat too long, enough to register the ask underneath. He needed what she did in rooms: observation, intuition, impossible pattern recognition, whatever careful lie both of them preferred on weekdays. "I have the south end and Lang. You take the east and bar side. We compare later."

"You're giving me the side with Ackermann, security, and alcohol."

"I know your talents."

"That sounded like an insult."

"You hear insult because you assume the worst of me."

"I assume the accurate amount."

Something almost softened in his mouth. Then his thoughts cut in around the edges of his calm face.

*If she can read this room the way she reads every room, she'll hear what I missed.*
*If Ackermann moved the paper before Lang spoke, somebody wanted timing more than policy.*
*And if this turns out to be nothing I spent an afternoon writing tariff language for men I can't stand.*

"Go be respectable," Anya said.

"You too."

They went in by different doors.

Warm amber light hit first, then crystal, then bodies. Westalians had claimed the bar. Ostanians had claimed the food. The quartet was playing hard for people who did not care. The ballroom had two exits, a service corridor, and a fully stocked bar. Anya clocked the exits first. Most of the room had probably clocked the bar.

Anya took a glass of white wine from a passing tray because it gave her hands a job and made her look less like she was counting the room. She started moving.

In the first fifteen minutes she got this:

A Westalian trade minister near the terrace doors was building an alibi for his wife in careful little pieces. *Traffic. Then Kessler wanted the export figures. Receptions always run late. If I leave at ten I can say ten-thirty.* It was too detailed and too prepared. It definitely was not about export figures.

At the buffet, an Ostanian general with enough medals to plate a second jacket was thinking about shrimp with the focus of a man inspecting artillery. *Tail came off clean. Good kitchen. Sauce is wrong. Too much lemon. Not enough bite.* He lined four shrimp on his plate like he expected them to hold formation.

By the window, a young diplomat kept rewriting the same breakup text in her head. *We should take a break. No. That's awful. I need space. No. Also awful. I just don't want to feel like this every Sunday anymore.* Her thumbnail had gone white against the stem of her champagne flute.

Near the service entrance, a Westalian aide in a bad jacket was thinking about his boss's speech, his mother's test results, and Ackermann's title all at once. *I wrote it phonetically. He still won't look at the card. Why do I make the cards. Why do I even own pens.*

An older Ostanian deputy minister, moving through the room with quiet gravitational pull, was thinking about natural gas imports and whether her daughter had remembered the cat's wet food. The cat was beating the gas imports without much effort.

Then the first live wire slid through.

A square little man with customs cuff links and a wet upper lip stood near the piano pretending to care about museum lending rates. *If Ackermann asks for annex four again, tell him Lang moved it to review. If he asks where review, say committee. If he asks which committee, laugh.*

That was new. So was the pulse spike that came with it.

Anya drifted another step and caught a different voice, this one from a woman with a trade badge and a fixed social smile.

*Checkpoint schedule holds if the review doesn't interfere. That's all. That's the line. Hold the line.*

It was the same word with different pressure behind it.

That was crowded-room telepathy on useful nights: cat food, affairs, zippers, and then one sentence with teeth. The headache behind her right eye had already started its steady drilling, which meant the room was doing what rooms like this always did.

WISE called this observation. Later she'd turn the stolen parts into clean report language about assessment, body language, and general temperature. Nobody got to write, *I was inside his head and here is the useful part.* Two years in, she could do the translation fast. Two years in, it still felt like cheating even before the lying started.

Across the room, Lang had Damian pinned beside a floral arrangement so expensive it had to be state-sponsored.

Lang was broad, silver-haired, and built like a man who believed chairs should respect him on contact. Out loud he was saying something about pragmatic tariff realism. Inwardly he was nowhere near tariffs.

*Smile at the boy. Keep him warm. Ackermann leaves in twenty. After that it doesn't matter whether Desmond sulks.*
*Who gave Brandt the first copy. Idiots. If he says annex in this room I will bury him.*

Damian's face gave nothing. Damian's thoughts were cleaner.

*You changed the sheet after the room sat down.*
*And now you're quoting my paper back to me like I should thank you.*
*Go on. Mention national stability one more time.*

Anya turned away before Lang could catch her looking. So the reception was not a new set piece after all. It was the second room attached to the first one, the spillover where the careful men went to pretend the knives were decorative.

Yuri Briar stood watch at the east side of the ballroom.

He wore a dark suit with an earpiece almost invisible against his hair. Anybody who'd never worked security would have called his posture relaxed. Anybody who had would have seen the readiness in it. His weight stayed balanced, his hands loose, and his eyes made the same sweep every eight seconds. Anya counted once to be sure.

His thoughts cut through the room with familiar sharpness.

*West entrance clear. Kraus has kitchen corridor.*
*Three extra Westalian staff not on the advance sheet. Logged it. Don't like it.*
*Three is planning, not sloppiness.*

He'd always been good. Anya still remembered him in the apartment years ago, glaring at Loid's miso soup like it might leap out of the bowl and confess.

Then the work track slipped and Yor rushed in.

*Need to call Sis. Thursday? Friday?*
*If I missed it she'll have cooked anyway.*
*It'll be terrible. I'll tell her it's perfect.*

That hit with the usual stupid tenderness. Yuri loved his sister so hard it hurt to overhear. He wasn't built for half-measures in any direction.

Then his focus landed on her.

*There. Cultural attaché. Third government room this month.*
*That isn't culture. That's circuit work.*
*Sis would tell me to stop being suspicious. Sis is not on security detail.*

Cold moved through her stomach. His eyes were on her now, not warmly, not as family by marriage, but as pattern recognition.

Anya slipped into the nearest conversation knot, all bilateral arts funding, ceramics residencies, and exchange fellows. She kept her face interested and her shoulders loose. Nobody in that circle cared whether the Ostenheim program was oversubscribed, which helped.

Four minutes later she peeled off toward the bar and passed within range of Strauss.

Strauss worked economic analysis at WISE. He had a gin and tonic, a good suit, and a tired face. He still looked like the sort of man who alphabetized his worries for efficiency.

*Halverson cut the ugly columns again.*
*Not cut. Shaped. That's the polite word.*
*We kept the numbers that bought us money and buried the ones that cooled the room.*
*Oversight gets the shaped set. Everybody at the desk knows it. Nobody says it where minutes get kept.*

Cold bubbles bit the back of her teeth as she lifted a sparkling water from a tray. Great. So WISE did the same trick in better tailoring. It wasn't a grand revelation, just a man with a drink and a budget line helping a chart lie by standing beside it. She did her own version every time she filed *behavioral indicators* instead of *he thought it so loudly I nearly winced.* It was the same trick in smaller font.

The room didn't change. It just got a little harder to sort clean from dirty.

Near the dais, the ambassador's wife tapped a spoon against her glass for a toast. The room leaned toward her in polite waves. Chairs stayed put, but bodies moved. Clusters compressed, and the thought-noise went from crowded to layered.

*Smile now.*
*Please don't let him mention wheat figures.*
*If I miss the tram this ends in a taxi and my wife is going to kill me.*
*Ackermann is leaving too early.*

The toast itself was nothing but friendship, exchange, stability, autumn light across two nations, and other expensive lies. The useful part lived underneath it.

*Brandt can wait until Monday if the checkpoint review slips.*
*No. Monday's too late.*
*Keep him away from Dietrich until after the revised tables go through.*

Anya stood in the middle of polite applause and felt the shape of the evening change under her feet. The word Damian had handed her downstairs kept surfacing, but it would not hold still. Committee review, checkpoint review, oversight review, maybe more. The syllables stayed the same, but the rooms inside them kept changing.

When the clapping died, Damian cut out of Lang's orbit and reached her near the buffet as if the shrimp had drawn him.

"The shrimp's apparently safe," she said.

"High praise."

"An Ostanian general has been auditing it for forty minutes."

That got a glance toward the buffet. Damian's mouth almost moved. Under the social layer, his attention was already on her read of the room.

"You were right about review," she said. "It keeps showing up and it keeps meaning something else."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough that one customs man has rehearsed lying about annex four so often he's probably going to do it in his sleep."

Damian's gaze stayed on the shrimp. "Did he attach it to Lang?"

"Lang and Ackermann. Another woman tied it to checkpoint scheduling. Strauss is hiding ugly budget columns from oversight."

"Strauss being Strauss isn't my problem."

"It is if all the same people are deciding what counts as ugly."

That landed. He took a breath like he didn't want to grant it but had to. *Not wrong. Unfortunately not wrong.* Out loud he said, "Ackermann's at the east cluster with Dietrich and a shipping attaché. Brandt's pretending to care about fisheries by the windows."

"And Lang?"

"Still collecting gratitude for a paper he mangled."

"Do you antagonize everybody on purpose, or is it just a gift?"

"I don't antagonize people."

"You absolutely do."

"I create conditions for candor."

"That is the same thing."

The crease showed beside his mouth again, faint and unwilling. Then his thoughts shifted back into work.

*If Ackermann moved the sheet, Dietrich knows why.*
*If she hears a name tonight, we stop guessing.*

"South end?" she asked.

"I have to keep looking like a respectable son of the party machine." He reached for a glass he didn't want and gave the room his public smile. "Take east. If Yuri circles, don't get clever."

"That's vague."

"It's as kind as I can make it in public."

He was gone into the crowd before she could answer, all diplomatic posture and contained annoyance. Anya watched him get caught by a defense liaison and hated how easy it was to see the boy under the polish when he wanted to throttle somebody.

Quadrants helped: upper left, upper right, lower left, lower right. A room this big could drown her if she let it turn into one blur. She read, sorted, moved, and kept moving. Cultural attachés were allowed to mingle. They were not allowed to haunt.

She worked east first because Damian had asked and because Yuri already thought she was a problem there.

Ackermann stood near a marble pillar talking to a red-faced shipping man and Dietrich, who looked exactly like the sort of person who would use the word synergy as a threat. Out loud they were discussing cross-border warehousing. Inwardly they were doing something else entirely.

Ackermann: *Do not say checkpoint. Not here.*

Dietrich: *If Brandt wanders over I will break both his hands and call it diplomacy.*

Shipping man: *Smile. Nodding means I understand. I do not understand.*

Anya took a canapé she didn't want and stepped into the edge of their orbit just long enough to matter.

"My office keeps hearing from museum couriers that customs delays are worse on the north route," she said to nobody in particular. "I was wondering if that's seasonal or just bad luck."

The shipping man brightened because he thought she'd offered him an exit. "Transit always worsens in autumn."

Ackermann smiled at her with the particular blank warmth senior officials reserved for harmless young women. "Paperwork worsens in every season, I'm afraid."

*Who invited her.*
*Westalian. Fine. Harmless.*
*If Dietrich says one stupid thing I will have to smile through it.*

"Arts shipping has its own little catastrophes," Anya said.

Dietrich gave her a look like he'd ranked her at once and found the result uninteresting. "Everything has its catastrophes."

His thoughts were less composed.

*Everything has its schedule too. Friday, if review closes. Monday if it doesn't. Not here. Not here.*

She almost stilled. Friday what. Monday what. Before she could edge closer, someone else joined the cluster and dragged the conversation sideways into port fees. The useful thought line vanished under irritation, shoes, hunger, boredom.

Anya stayed another thirty seconds to avoid looking like she had come for anything particular, then peeled away toward the windows with the sense of having seen a thread disappear back into cloth.

A military aide near the curtains was consumed by the question of whether he'd parked in a tow zone.

One junior diplomat within range was thinking with total spiritual commitment about whether his fly was down.

It was.

There was no professional way to solve that. Anya left him to history and kept walking.

Brandt was, unfortunately, real enough. He stood half-hidden by a potted palm with two fisheries men and the face of someone being forced to discuss weather with enemies. Out loud he was dry and competent. Inwardly he was clogged with scheduling.

*If Ackermann says the review holds, the November signature moves.*
*If the November signature moves, Voss asks questions.*
*I do not want Voss asking questions.*

There it was. Not proof, but a shape.

Anya cut behind a server and nearly walked straight into Yuri.

He caught the tray she clipped before the glasses could fall, efficient and without fuss. The server muttered thanks and fled. Yuri set the tray back into the man's hands and turned to her with a professional expression so neutral it took effort.

"Ms. Kessler."

Anya swallowed a pulse spike. "Officer Briar."

"You remember me."

"You're hard to miss."

"That's generally the point." His tone stayed courteous. His thoughts did not. *She moved toward Ackermann. Then Brandt. Not random. Not social.* "Enjoying the reception?"

"Enjoying is a strong word."

"Work, then."

"Culture is work."

"This many times a month?"

There it was: not an accusation, but a pattern laid on the table like silverware.

Anya sipped her water because it bought half a second. "Trade season gets everybody dragged into rooms they'd rather avoid."

"Mm."

*Bad answer. Too ready.*
*She knew my name before I offered it.*
*We've met enough for that. Doesn't help.*

He let the silence hold long enough to be intentional, not rude and not friendly either.

"Stay clear of the east service corridor after eight-thirty," he said. "We're rotating staff and I don't want anybody wandering into the wrong lane."

"Good to know."

"And if anyone asks, you heard it from hotel management."

"That's very generous of hotel management."

One corner of his mouth moved, not quite amusement, more like professional acknowledgment that she had seen the cover and understood it.

"Enjoy the shrimp," he said.

Then he turned away.

Anya stood where he left her and did not look after him. Her skin had gone too tight across her shoulders. Yuri's suspicion had crossed from ambient thought to spoken behavior now. It was still deniable, still fixable, and still real.

Across the room Damian looked up from the defense liaison and caught Yuri walking away from her. His thought flashed clean through the crowd.

*What did he want.*
*Not now. Keep smiling.*

Anya gave the smallest shrug she could manage. Damian's attention snapped back into diplomatic mode before anybody else noticed it had moved.

She worked the west side for ten minutes because east had become expensive. An academic spent five solid minutes thinking about a dead ficus in her office. A banking adviser wanted desperately to leave his wife and even more desperately not to lose her apartment in the divorce. Someone near the bar was planning to steal monogrammed napkins for reasons too bleak to examine.

Then one thought made her stop by a lacquered side table.

*Do not discuss procurement numbers at functions, Dietrich, for God's sake.*

It came from a narrow woman in a navy dress speaking to nobody she liked. Her mouth was still smiling at the ambassador's cousin. Her mind was on the east cluster and the exact kind of stupid that ruined men.

Anya turned her head just enough to see Dietrich again, now with Ackermann and a customs man and one more face she didn't know. The quartet cut into something brighter. Laughter rose near the terrace. The room kept pretending it was a party.

It had only been forty minutes since Damian came out of Lang's meeting downstairs. Already she had annex corrections, checkpoint timing, procurement nerves, and Yuri asking why culture kept putting her next to the wrong people. The evening had stopped feeling broad and started feeling arranged.

* * *

Damian was near the terrace doors again, listening to a woman from the Foreign Affairs Ministry explain port logistics with real interest. That should not have been attractive in anybody. On him it was still weirdly effective.

*If they fix the northern bottleneck the border looks calmer. If the border looks calmer the ministry loses its excuse for the defense allocation.*

Even his thoughts sounded like briefing notes that had gone to school.

Anya waited until the woman excused herself.

"If port logistics ever seduce you away from me, I'd like advance warning," she said.

"I'll try to be humane."

"That'd be new."

"You were talking to Yuri."

He went straight to it. His face stayed pleasant. His eyes did not. Anya read the question sitting behind them before he said it.

*Did he make her.*
*No. If he'd made her he'd still be here.*
*Don't overreact in public, idiot.*

"He warned me away from a service corridor," she said.

"That's almost sweet."

"His internal review was less sweet."

That got a tiny tightening at Damian's jaw. "He's clocking patterns?"

"Third room this month."

"Annoying."

"For me, yes."

For a second they stood shoulder to shoulder like two mildly bored reception professionals discussing traffic. Underneath, the pressure between them was doing something else.

"Anything else?" Damian asked.

"Brandt cares about November signatures and whether Voss starts asking questions if something slips. Dietrich is waiting on Friday or Monday depending on a review he didn't define. Ackermann's customs man has rehearsed a lie about annex four."

"Good." Then, quieter: "Too good."

"That's what I said about Yuri."

He ignored that on purpose. *Friday or Monday. So the schedule isn't stable. Good. No. Bad. Useful. Bad.* "Lang will be at the west side after the next toast. Ackermann keeps orbiting east. If there's a handoff tonight, it uses the crowd between."

"You're optimistic."

"I prefer irritated."

They both looked at the same movement at once: Dietrich cutting across the floor with the half-speed patience of a man who believed people would always open around him. Behind him, Hesse was talking educational exchange with an Ostanian academic near the west pillar. Lentz, the ceramics program officer, hovered one cluster over with the fixed hopeful look of someone who still believed kiln temperatures could interest strangers.

It was a normal room and a normal party, with every person in it carrying a private ridiculousness or private threat, often both.

Then someone by the bar said something about cultural grant compliance in a tone so dry it surprised a laugh out of Anya before she could stop it.

It came out loose and too real, with the little hitch at the end.

Damian looked up from her like he'd been struck.

It was not the polite sweep he gave everybody. This was different: old memory, not present calculation.

*There. By the bar. Elbow tucked in. Glass almost against her ribs.*
*I've seen that before. Where.*
*Eden. Festival cleanup. Henderson's shoes full of soap. Pink hair everywhere and that laugh.*

The laugh. That was the problem. The thought snagged there and went hunting for the rest.

*No. Wait. She used to tilt her head before saying the worst possible thing.*
*This woman just did it.*
*How many people in Berlint have that exact shade. No. Stop.*

Her name hovered at the edge of his mind, not in the thought yet, close enough to burn.

A hand landed on his arm.

The defense liaison had stopped avoiding him. Damian's focus snapped away hard enough that Anya felt the memory sink under the professional layer in real time: name, file, border posture, handshake, tone. The childhood image dropped straight through the floorboards.

She set the glass down and flattened her palm on the polished wood. The cool surface helped a little. Her pulse was hitting hard enough to make the stem rattle against her fingers.

It was too close. Three more heartbeats and school-Anya and attaché-Anya would have clicked together in his head. After that came the questions. Why was an Eden girl wearing Westalian credentials. Why did she keep appearing anywhere his covert life got dangerous. Why did she keep landing on the right answer half a beat before the rest of the room even saw the question.

Hesse was talking educational exchange with an Ostanian academic near the west pillar. Anya slid into the gap and waited through a long, stupid twenty-count for an opening.

"The preparatory courses help with that," she said when the academic mentioned networking. "I did the embassy exchange three years ago and I still keep almost recognizing people from it. Whole week of name tags, forced smiles, and stale conference-room air. Faces blur after that."

Hesse agreed. The academic agreed. Nobody cared, which was perfect.

A few minutes later, blessedly, Lentz started talking to her about wood-firing kilns and glaze chemistry. For three whole minutes Anya got to stand in front of a mind that matched its mouth. There was no second layer and no concealed knife, just ceramics and a minor panic about hotel water ruining an unfinished test batch.

Then Damian appeared at her shoulder with a fresh drink and the bland smile he used when he was absolutely running a probe.

"You did the preparatory exchange?" he asked.

Anya looked at him. "Spring intake."

"At the embassy annex?"

There it was. It was not casual. It was a probe.

Hesse was still nearby, thinking about the annex's leaky roof and the classroom urn that could strip paint. Good. Enough to grab.

"The annex for the seminars, the old ministry rooms for language drills," she said. "Hesse kept pretending the urn wasn't poison."

Damian's face did not change. His thoughts did.

*Fine. The annex. The stale room. Then why did I go chasing the annex off one stupid school memory. Why Henderson. Why that laugh.*

"I was trying to place you," he said aloud.

"Everybody tries at these things," Anya said. "Do enough receptions and every face starts stealing pieces from the others."

"Fair."

He should have left it there. For one second Anya thought he would.

Instead he turned his head toward Hesse with the same mild expression.

"You were with that intake?" Damian asked. "Before the roof finally gave up?"

Hesse blinked.

It was a tiny thing, barely anything, but it was enough.

His mouth shaped a social smile half a beat late. His thoughts were sharper.

*What intake.*
*Roof? I was still in trade that year.*
*Do not look confused. You are too old to look confused at a party.*

Anya felt the air go thin.

Hesse let out a soft diplomatic sound that could have meant yes, no, or pass the salt. "The annex leaked for years. That hardly narrows it."

It was a bad answer, not because it was false but because it was protective.

Damian heard the wobble in it too. Anya saw him hear it.

She cut in before the silence could gather weight.

"He guest lectured once," she said, too fast and then making herself slow down. "Not my intake, I guess. That's why the room stuck harder than the actual teaching. Sorry. I merged two miserable memories together."

Lentz laughed politely because politeness was what she had. Hesse grabbed the offered bridge and walked across it.

"A professional hazard," he said. "Government buildings do blur."

His thoughts were a mess of gratitude and irritation.

*Bless the girl for fixing it.*
*Did I ever lecture there. Maybe once. No. Trade, then transfer. Whatever. Smile and keep it moving.*

Damian looked at her for one beat more than he needed to.

*He didn't know her.*
*Or he didn't know enough to say so.*
*Either way that story is checkable.*

There it was. It was not recognition. In its own way, it was worse.

Anya had not just dodged a memory. She had given him something concrete to keep.

"My apologies," Damian said to Hesse in the same public tone. "I've become one of those men who thinks every old room in Berlint is the same old room."

"A common disease," Hesse said, relieved to have a name for the awkwardness even if it wasn't the real one.

Damian raised his glass slightly to Anya. "I'll let you get back to ceramics."

"Thrilling subject," she said.

"I can tell."

He moved on.

Lentz resumed explaining kiln temperatures with patient enthusiasm. Anya nodded in the right places and heard almost none of it. Damian's thought stayed stuck under her skin:

*That story is checkable.*

Maybe not tonight, and not this minute. But the leverage had changed. Before, he had a feeling and an old photograph and the bad luck of seeing her everywhere. Now he had a witness reaction, a cover anecdote, and the knowledge that when pressed she could build an answer out of almost nothing and make it stand up in public.

This wasn't the same old almost. This one left residue.

From the east side of the room, Yuri tracked her again.

*Bar. Hesse. Ceramics officer. South window cluster.*
*She's not mingling. She's mapping.*
*And Desmond keeps crossing to her. Too much.*

No good answer existed for that. Anya did the only thing she had. She crossed back toward the bar and stayed put long enough to look boring.

That lasted ninety seconds.

Ackermann drifted into range again with Dietrich and the shipping man. Their spoken conversation had become lighter, which only made the thoughts underneath more obvious.

Ackermann: *Friday, then. If review closes Friday.*

Dietrich: *Unless Brandt loses his nerve.*

Shipping man: *I hate these people. I hate these people. I hate these people.*

Anya kept her face on her water glass and listened harder.

Ackermann: *No move until coverage shifts.*

Then somebody bumped her elbow and the sentence shattered into taxi anxiety, dry-cleaning, and a memory of bad oysters. She turned and caught only a woman apologizing with sincere horror.

"So sorry."

"You're fine," Anya said automatically.

It was not fine. Nothing about the room was fine. Friday. Monday. Review. Coverage shift. Annex four. November signature. Voss. Ackermann. Dietrich. Yuri deciding she was a pattern. Damian deciding her lies could be filed for later.

The room did not need one clean revelation to become dangerous. It was already dangerous. The problem was that danger kept arriving in separate dialects and refusing to line up.

By the time the ambassador's second toast dissolved into looser drinking, Anya had started sorting the night into mental columns.

Things she knew:

Lang's meeting and this reception belonged to the same chain.

Ackermann and Dietrich were waiting on some timing point connected to a review.

Brandt did not want Voss asking questions.

Strauss and Halverson were still shaping WISE output to make certain threats look hotter than others.

Things she did not know:

What moved on Friday.

What moved on Monday.

Which review mattered to which lie.

How much of any of it touched Damian's annex numbers.

How much of it touched her.

Then Yuri came back a second time, not to her but to a post two meters beyond her shoulder, and spoke quietly into his sleeve. Out loud it was logistics. In his head it was not.

*If Desmond keeps crossing to the west bar, log it.*
*And log the attaché. If she's in the room when the staff rotation changes, I want it noted.*

Anya set her glass down before she could crush it.

She was now in two separate men's working notes for two separate reasons and neither of them knew the real one.

* * *

By nine o'clock the ballroom had gone thick.

Three hundred people, maybe, enough that the thought-noise stopped sounding like separate minds and turned into pressure. The ache behind her right eye had deepened, her jaw tight, her shoulders tight, her fingers flexing around the water glass without permission. A waiter went past thinking in furious detail about three days of unwashed dishes and a pan of burned rice his roommate claimed was soaking. She told herself to keep moving and keep sorting.

A woman near the ambassador was dying inside because she'd realized too late that her dress matched the ambassador's wife's.

Brandt was still stalling the November deployment signature.

A man by the far curtain was thinking about his sick kid's fever and pretending to follow an energy conversation.

Lentz, poor Lentz, had circled back to whether hotel water would ruin her glaze test.

The room kept handing Anya proof that most private panics were private for a reason and useless to anyone but the person having them.

Then the clean one hit.

*Asset inside WISE is still holding. Move the handoff before review closes the window.*

Anya stopped.

She did not stop dramatically, just enough that the server behind her had to swerve.

The voice came again, sharp as a knife on tile.

*No delay. Coverage shifts after the review. Pull it forward if you have to.*

It was close, very close. Somebody inside this cluster of Westalian staffers, Ostanian counterparts, servers, security, and trailing spouses was within range and thinking about a Westalian asset and a handoff and a review window like it was a timetable problem.

Anya turned her head once, slow.

Twelve faces, or fourteen if the passing server counted. She caught a trade official, a mission analyst she half recognized from a budget breakfast, a woman in green, a bald man by the window, two men talking fishing rights and actually thinking about fishing rights, and a server worrying about his tray angle.

Nobody lit up with WISE. Nobody obligingly thought the whole sentence again.

She cut left through the knot and let a tray nearly clip her shoulder. She got hotel booking, knee pain, an affair, and then nothing.

Back toward the window. Another pass.

*If the tram's late again I'm sleeping at the station.*

There was still nothing.

One more, faster this time, risking obviousness because obviousness was cheaper than missing it.

*Handoff.*

It came thin and already breaking apart.

Then it was gone.

Whoever had thought it had shut the door or moved or let some other trivial human concern flood the surface back over it. The room rushed in to fill the space with parking meters, divorce, border review, and sore feet. The signal was buried again, and the only solid thing Anya had was the phrase sitting under her ribs like swallowed glass.

The part that stuck was *inside WISE*.

No, it was not solid. Slow down.

That was the first correction and it mattered.

She had heard the words *asset inside WISE is still holding.* She had heard *move the handoff before review closes the window.* She had heard *coverage shifts after the review.* All from one nearby mind, or what felt like one nearby mind, before the signal fragmented.

What she had not heard was a name.

She did not have a rank, a desk, or any idea whether the asset was an analyst, courier, clerk, field operative, or somebody who touched their paperwork twice a month and sold the touch.

She did not know whether "inside WISE" meant physically inside the station, administratively inside the service, or only inside the thoughter's understanding of some arrangement they were half wrong about.

She did not know which review it was either. God, not even that. Committee review. Checkpoint review. Oversight review. The whole room had been using the same word to mean different clocks all night.

And thoughts were surface. Fear could dress itself as guilt. Shame could sound like conspiracy. Illness could sound like hiding. She knew that better than most. She'd watched whole rooms condemn themselves for affairs, debt, abortions, lies to spouses, lies to mothers, stupid things done drunk in college, pills in a jacket pocket, a child with a fever at home. Surface thoughts were not sworn testimony. They were weather.

Her heartbeat kept trying to ignore that and jump straight to catastrophe anyway.

Because even with every correction in place, even with every unanswered variable left unanswered, the risk stayed obscene.

Somebody close enough to operational timing, close enough to handoffs and coverage shifts and the word WISE, believed there was a usable asset in her service and believed the window around that asset was about to close.

If the thinker was right, WISE had a mole.

If the thinker was wrong, then people adjacent to the conspiracy believed WISE was compromised and were planning around that belief.

Neither option was survivable enough to ignore.

Anya made herself move again, slower now. It wasn't a hunt. It was a drift. She took a fresh glass she did not want and used it as cover to circle the cluster another time.

The mission analyst by the window snagged her attention for half a second. He had the right kind of clenched face, but the wrong age for that panic and the wrong posture too. His thoughts hit in hard, ashamed bursts.

*If they see the transfer I'll have to tell Sylvia it was for Tomas.*
*Not debt. Not gambling. Hospital.*
*Please don't let anybody ask about the transfer tonight.*

Anya almost laughed from sheer ugly relief and felt sick immediately after. There it was again, the trap built into her own talent. A body in the wrong angle, a mind under pressure, and the story her fear wanted leapt ahead of what she actually knew.

It wasn't him.

A woman in green near the curtain radiated professional blankness for three whole steps before the surface broke into:

*Smile at Ackermann. Smile at Ackermann. Pretend you did not tear your stocking in the bathroom.*

She wasn't it either.

A server came through carrying empty flutes.

*Don't drop them. Don't drop them. If you drop them Lenz will send you home and rent is Friday.*

He wasn't it either.

Again and again the crowd refused to become useful on command. The room had never promised fairness. Tonight it seemed actively offended by the idea.

Someone touched her elbow.

Anya jerked half an inch before she caught it. Damian, close enough that nobody watching would think twice.

"You look pale," he said.

"Occupational hazard."

"From Yuri?"

"No."

He looked at her properly then. Public face still in place. Private attention sharp enough to cut.

*Something moved.*
*She's too focused for a headache and too off for social fatigue.*
*Not here. Don't ask here.*

"Did you get something?" he asked, which in public passed as bland professional courtesy. In private it came out weighted.

Anya had to choose a lie fast and choose one that didn't collapse under its own neatness.

"Maybe," she said.

That was too honest, and she corrected. "Or maybe the room finally turned everybody into the same person. Hard to tell."

His eyes narrowed by a fraction.

"That's not an answer."

"It's what I have."

He held the silence long enough to tell her he knew she was evading and wasn't going to force it in front of three hundred witnesses. His thought stayed with her anyway.

*Later, then.*
*If she bolts after this I follow.*
*No. Can't. Lang's still here. Damn it.*

"Don't do anything stupid," he said, which in another mouth would have sounded paternal and in his sounded mostly tired.

"That's rude."

"It's accurate."

"You stole my line."

"You make yourself easy to quote."

That almost felt normal, which was maybe why it hurt.

He moved off before the pause could turn noticeable. Anya watched him go toward Lang again, toward politics and procurement and whatever old damage kept him walking into rooms like this at all. The thought she had caught lodged harder under that image.

If WISE was compromised, or if somebody credible believed it was, Damian's lane was not clean either. The annex numbers, the checkpoint schedule, the delayed signatures, the things he'd pulled out of Lang's meeting this afternoon, all of it might be sitting in the same web.

Three days from now, she would tell herself following him was protocol. That would not be the whole truth.

For now she still had the room.

She circled once more, forcing herself to catalog instead of leap.

The cluster near the east windows wasn't it; she got fishing rights, bunions, and a daughter applying to medical school.

The bar-side trade group wasn't it either; she got bad cognac, tariff boredom, and one affair too stale to matter.

The security lane gave her Yuri thinking about staff rotation, a misprinted credential, and whether Yor had meant Thursday or Friday. It wasn't that.

The Westalian economic pair by the pillars gave her one man thinking about Halverson's shaped tables and the other thinking about whether Strauss knew he was sleeping with interns. It was disgusting, but it wasn't it.

The cluster itself had dispersed by degrees while she worked. That was the worst part. The thinker could already be across the room, in a corridor, in an elevator, out on the Concordia steps calling a car.

She had missed the face.

No, second correction: maybe she had and maybe she had not. Missing was a conclusion. What she had was smaller and uglier.

She had failed to isolate the face in time.

That failure sat close to guilt because part of her was already counting the cost downstream.

Sylvia would ask how she knew. Sylvia had already swallowed one clean lie from her three weeks ago. Going back now with *Somebody near the bar thought there was an asset inside WISE* would survive exactly one question before collapsing into the real impossible answer.

Loid would ask differently and probably get farther. He would want sequence, range, relative position, names of everyone within twenty meters, whether the thought came with fear or confidence, whether she could reproduce cadence. He would ask the way spies asked when they were trying not to scare you by taking you seriously.

Damian would ask cleaner questions than either of them and keep them sharper.

The truthful answer was still the one answer she'd never given anyone in her life.

Fine.

Then the hunt was hers.

Not because the fragment had settled everything, but because it had settled enough.

She would start with the review cycle, not the abstract word from the room but the actual live reviews touching WISE over the next week: oversight, coverage rotation, border traffic assessments, anything that changed staffing or paper flow. She needed to know who had access, who would know a window was closing, and who would know a handoff had to move before coverage shifted.

She would cross that against Damian's annex numbers and the names from tonight: Ackermann, Dietrich, Brandt, and Lang if necessary, though Lang smelled more like a man covering a dirty table than running the dirt himself.

Look for timing before identity. Timing lied less.

That was the concrete line.

She could do quiet work, ugly work, work that started with calendars, rosters, access lists, watch coverage, and who touched what file and when. She could do it because Loid had taught her how to build a pattern and because telepathy, filthy unfair telepathy, had dropped the first shard in her lap.

She could not go upstairs with a half-truth and call it proof.

She could not pretend the shard was harmless because it wasn't whole.

She could not stop hearing the exact sentence either.

*Asset inside WISE is still holding.*

Still was the word that kept scraping.

Still meant ongoing, or the thinker believed it did. Still meant this was not a brand-new arrangement in their mind. Still meant a line already existed and they were worried about losing it.

Maybe still referred to rumor. Maybe the thinker was passing along someone else's bad assumption. Maybe inside WISE meant something narrower or weirder than the obvious reading.

The corrections kept coming because they mattered.

Anya pressed her thumb hard into the stem of her glass until it hurt. Pain helped, not because it solved anything but because it stopped her from turning urgency into certainty out of sheer desperation for clean edges.

Across the room, Damian found her again. Concern came first, then restraint.

*She's off. What happened. Later. Not in here.*

Anya gave him the smallest nod she could manage.

Then she scanned the ballroom one more time, and the room did not feel warm anymore. It felt searchable in the worst possible way. Anybody could be ordinary until the sentence under their ordinary opened like a trapdoor.

The general at the buffet was still thinking about shrimp, about tail and sauce and lemon and bite. It wasn't him.

The young diplomat by the window had deleted the breakup text and was now drafting tomorrow's apology. It wasn't her.

Yuri checked the cracked watch Yor had given him years ago and thought, with deep private irritation, that he still couldn't remember whether he'd promised Thursday or Friday dinner. It wasn't him.

Strauss worried about Halverson's shaped columns and whether oversight would ask for the raw set after all. It wasn't enough, and it wasn't this.

Ordinary thoughts kept surfacing. Ordinary thoughts kept clearing people by accident. It should have made the room feel safer. It didn't. It only proved how many covers ordinary life could wear.

The quartet brightened. Glasses clinked. Somebody laughed too loudly near the terrace doors. Somewhere in the middle of all that, a usable line into WISE either existed or had been believed into operational reality by people with schedules and windows and handoffs.

That was enough to move on.

She set the empty glass on a passing tray and started assembling the next three days in her head. Tomorrow she would pull review calendars and coverage changes. After that she would map who in WISE could see both the shifts and Damian's lane. Then she would work through everyone from tonight who had business caring when those windows closed.

She'd do it quietly. She'd do it alone until she had something that could survive a question.

And if that meant Damian felt her watching him more closely, or Yuri decided she attended one function too many, or Sylvia asked why she suddenly cared so much about access rosters, she'd deal with that when it came.

For now she went to find her coat with the phrase still lodged under her ribs and the hunt finally shaped enough to start.
