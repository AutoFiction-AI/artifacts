# Chapter 13

Anya was already awake when the phone rang at 6:14 on the first morning of November. She had slept an hour at best. It was probably less. Most of the night had gone to encrypted border traffic on her WISE terminal and the neighbor in 4B thinking, *please please please just sleep, I'm begging you, just one hour.* The baby on the other side of the wall kept crying. The mother's thoughts were louder.

She picked up on the second ring. Sylvia got straight to it.

"At approximately 0340, border control at Kreuzfeld was breached. Crane was moving on forged papers when a three-man team intercepted him. They had his route, his timing, and the cover packet. He died on site."

Anya sat on the edge of the bed with the phone hard against her ear. The baby cried again. The neighbor thought, *please, please, this one time,* and then nothing but blank tired static.

"His body has been recovered," Sylvia said. "The documents have not. I have informed his wife."

Crane. David Crane. Parking space, three months ago. "Chief," every single time. Forty-one. Wife, no kids. He kept border stamps in an Altoids tin in his glove compartment because supply never had the ones he wanted when he wanted them. He had spent the last six weeks running eastern courier lanes Anya had helped make possible.

Three weeks ago she had filed Damian's network as political vanity with a useful pedigree attached. She had written that the chatter hanging off his eastern contacts was posture, not live logistics. She had cut the note about customs clerks and freight schedules. She had downgraded Renewal Front movement on the eastern corridors from active concern to something smaller, duller, safer. WISE moved the Desmond-adjacent traffic off hot watch and into periodic review. Duplicate eyes came off Kreuzfeld. Courier lanes were rebuilt around the thinner coverage picture. Crane drew one of the replacement runs.

That did not mean her report put rifles in anybody's hands. It meant the hole existed. Somebody with routing access had used it.

"There is another development," Sylvia said. "At approximately 0300, an SSS tactical unit executed a search warrant on a residential address in eastern Berlint. The location matches an address from our analytical review last week. The warrant was specific. They knew what they were looking for."

Damian's cache. The apartment. The shelves arranged by region. The false wall behind the kitchen cabinet. The coffee stain near the baseboard that he had left because replacing the carpet meant letting the landlord inside. She had laughed when he said it. He had not.

"The material was seized intact," Sylvia said. "Analysis is pending. The eastern sector is compromised."

Compromised. Broken. Burned open. Gone.

"I have questions about your reporting timeline," Sylvia said. "We will address them at 0800. Attend in person."

"Yes."

Sylvia went quiet for half a second. Not uncertainty. Just the small stop between the work and the next piece of work.

"Crane was a good officer," she said. "We will debrief in the morning."

The line went dead.

Anya stayed where she was. The neighbor in 4B adjusted the baby and thought, *okay, okay, maybe like this,* and the baby screamed harder. The thought blew apart into exhausted misery.

She called Damian.

He picked up at once. "I'm aware," he said. His voice was controlled, too even, and she could hear how hard he was working at it. "My contact in the SSS reached me twenty minutes ago. They took everything."

"How specific?"

"They went to the false wall first. Then the bedroom floorboards. No wasted motion."

Anya shut her eyes.

"Crane is dead," she said.

A pause. "Who?"

"WISE field officer. He was carrying a courier packet through Kreuzfeld."

"I'm sorry."

He meant it. That was the problem. He did not know what he was apologizing for. He did not know she had handed WISE a cleaner version of him than the truth. He did not know Crane's run had existed inside the coverage picture that lie helped build. He did not know the dead space between what she filed and what she knew had finally filled up with a body.

"There's something else," Damian said. "Mertens called an hour ago."

The name hit and brought the rest with it. A ministry official. Careful. Nervous. Two daughters. Saturday football. Photocopies in a gym bag, left in the back seat of his car in the ministry garage because that was the blind spot nobody bothered to search.

"He's out," Damian said. "Told me not to call again. He thinks the SSS got the address through his department's records. He thinks they will come for him next."

"Will they?"

"I don't know. If the files were taken intact, they'll find his name soon enough. I tried to tell him I could arrange something. He hung up before I got there."

Anya stood and went to the kitchen window without deciding to. Morning had turned the glass the color of dishwater. She thought of Crane's parking space. Mertens in a ministry garage with a gym bag. The report she had put in Sylvia's hands. She had drawn a line around Damian and called it strategy. Everything outside that line had paid for it.

*Please just sleep,* the neighbor thought. *Please.*

"Do you think the raid and Kreuzfeld were the same operation?" she asked.

"I think they were two precise hits inside forty minutes," Damian said. "I think both were tied to the same work. The rest is guesswork."

"A good guess."

"Yes." He exhaled. "Still guesswork."

That helped and did not help.

"We need to meet," Anya said. "Not the usual place."

"I know somewhere."

She hung up, stripped off her T-shirt, and stepped into the shower. The water never got fully hot. She let it hit the back of her neck anyway. On the tram west, a man in a wool scarf thought about invoice numbers, a woman in nurse's shoes thought about whether she had time to shave her legs before tonight, and somebody near the back kept replaying *did you lock the cabinet, did you lock the cabinet, did you lock the cabinet* until Anya wanted to turn around and shake him. The city had made room for dawn and deliveries and normal resentment without asking anybody's permission. Crane stayed dead inside it just fine.

The printing shop block was still half asleep. Someone inside was already running a press. The sound came through the walls in steady mechanical shudders.

The basement was underneath the shop, reached through a service door beside a dumpster that smelled like wet ink and cardboard. Damian had a key. He did not explain how. The stairs were steep concrete. By the halfway mark, the railing had leached enough heat from her palm that she shoved both hands into her coat pockets.

The room below was about fifteen feet by twenty, all concrete except for a steel folding table, four mismatched chairs, and one fluorescent tube on the ceiling. Damp had browned the far wall in vertical streaks. Damian was already there with his elbows on the table and his hands folded loose, coat still on, as if he had been holding the same position for an hour. The burn mark near his left cuff stood out darker than the rest of the wool.

She pulled out the chair and sat. Neither of them spoke.

Above them, someone in the shop dragged something heavy across the floor and let it drop. The thud came down through the ceiling, dull and blunt. Damian kept looking at the table.

*Too static. Stupid. Should've moved it. If they took the little ledger, Mertens is finished.*

There was a pale coffee ring on the table, half rubbed away. Anya put two fingers beside it and kept them there.

"They went to the false wall first," Damian said at last. "Straight there. No search pattern. No hesitation."

"So the warrant wasn't just specific."

"No."

"Mertens still thinks the address came through ministry records?"

"He thinks someone pulled an internal property file connected to his department. He thinks the SSS is going to —" Damian's voice went flat. "He thinks they're coming for him. He's probably right. I don't have anything useful to say to that."

Anya nodded.

"Crane was carrying forged driver papers through Kreuzfeld," she said. "The team that hit him knew the exact lane, the exact time, and the cover packet. That did not come off the street."

Damian's gaze dropped to her hand by the coffee ring.

"Then somebody inside your routing handed them the window."

"Yes."

Another thud upstairs. Someone laughed immediately after, bright and stupid and normal.

Damian rubbed his thumb against the side of his index finger. "They had my apartment. They had your courier window. Both before sunrise, and I keep —" He stopped. "I keep trying to make that two separate things and I can't."

"The coordinated part is still a guess," Anya said.

"It doesn't feel like a guess."

"Still a guess."

He looked up at that. The fluorescent light made the scar over his eyebrow look whiter than the rest of him.

"Yeah," he said. "Still a guess."

There it was. Not agreement. Discipline. The difference mattered and she hated that it mattered when Crane was dead.

"Mertens is going to be okay," she said.

He looked at her.

She heard how bad it sounded too late. "No. I don't know that. I just..." She swallowed. "I know he mattered to you."

A drip hit the floor somewhere behind her. Slow. Then another.

"He has two daughters," Damian said. "That's the part I can't stop thinking about."

Anya nodded because anything else would have been a lie.

"Tell me about Crane."

The question landed hard enough that she almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because a man was dead and Damian still wanted him to be a person, not a line item. That almost broke something.

"He called everybody chief," she said. "Even Sylvia, once. He used too much powdered creamer in bad coffee and then pretended he liked it. He whistled when he was waiting at checkpoints, but only the same three bars because he couldn't remember how the song went after that. He could spot a bad stamp on forged papers faster than anyone I know." She swallowed. "He was forty-one."

Damian said nothing.

"He kept spare seals in his car because he never trusted supply. He had one story about a checkpoint dog that he told every time somebody new got assigned east. He always got the middle wrong and the ending right."

The room held it. Upstairs, someone cursed about ink coverage. Somewhere in the pipes a radiator knocked once and gave up.

"He was on a replacement run," Anya said.

Damian's face did not change, but the silence tightened.

"Replacement for what?"

She rubbed at the heel of her palm with her thumb, hard enough to feel the old scar pull. "For coverage we cut."

He waited.

She could have stopped there. She did not.

"I filed the east-sector picture wrong," she said. "I made it smaller than it was. I said it was ego and old men talking war after dessert. I pushed the customs piece and the freight piece down the stack. WISE thinned the eastern watch after that. They opened substitute lanes through crossings they thought were quieter." Her throat felt scraped raw. "Crane took one."

Damian's hands stayed folded. That restraint felt worse than if he had stood up and shouted.

"That's what I can prove," Anya said. "I can prove my report changed the coverage picture. I can prove somebody inside had the new window. The rest is me looking at a dead man and wanting the line to point somewhere clean."

He inhaled slowly through his nose.

"Then don't make it cleaner than it is."

She looked at him.

"Your report helped build the lane," he said. "That doesn't mean you briefed the men waiting on it."

"You don't know that."

"No." His voice went flatter. "Neither do you."

She looked back down at the coffee ring. He was right. He was annoyingly, relentlessly right about her in all the places that had nothing to do with telepathy.

*She's going to take the whole thing into herself if I let her.*

That one landed with force and no new information. She still hated it.

"At the reception," she said, "I caught a fragment about the mole and I let it slide because I couldn't place it. Then I burned two days on the wrong analyst."

"Which analyst?"

She shook her head too fast. "Doesn't matter now."

It mattered. He knew it mattered. Her evasion hung there between them like a smell.

"It matters if you're still guessing at the wrong people," he said.

"I know."

"Do you."

It was not a challenge in his public voice. It was quieter than that, and more exhausted.

Anya opened her mouth, closed it, tried again. "I know I missed something."

"That isn't the same sentence."

Above them, somebody dropped a stack of paper. The slap of it came through the ceiling in a flat burst. Anya almost laughed again, this time because the universe apparently intended to keep humiliating her until she went numb.

"My network is burned," Damian said after a moment. "Your eastern routing is burned. The mole is still —" He rubbed his face with one hand. "Mertens has two kids. I can't even tell him what's coming."

"Yes."

"So if we're still doing this, the shape changes."

She looked up.

"No more fixed caches," he said. "No more apartments holding paper. No more couriers either of us hasn't looked in the eye."

"That's not much of a network."

"It's what's left."

"What about your people?"

He almost smiled and failed. "I have fewer people than I did at dawn."

The drip behind her slowed. Stopped. Started again.

"If I have something for you, I bring it," Damian said. "If you have something for me, you bring it. Nothing sits still long enough to be photographed, copied, or sold."

Anya nodded once. "Okay."

"And if Mertens calls again, I answer once. After that, nothing in writing."

"Okay."

He looked at her for a beat too long, not speaking.

*Don't ask now. Not like this.*

He pulled off his coat and held it out across the table. "Take this."

"I'm fine."

"You're shaking."

"I'm cold."

"Yes," he said. "That's why."

She did not take it. After a second he laid it on the table between them, the sleeve falling over part of the coffee ring.

"I have to be at WISE in half an hour," she said.

"Then go."

It should have sounded like dismissal. It did not. It sounded like the thing you said when there was no useful version of stay.

She stood. So did he.

"What do we actually have left?" she asked.

His gaze flicked to the coat, then to the concrete floor, then back to her face.

"Direct contact," he said. "You and me. That's it."

Anya nodded once and moved past him toward the stairs. When the service door banged shut behind her, his coat was still spread across the table.

She made 0800 with three minutes to spare.

The WISE east annex smelled like wet wool, radiator dust, and coffee that had died sometime before dawn. A security clerk with a razor nick on his chin checked her badge and thought about mustard. Somewhere behind the inner door, two analysts were arguing out loud about a border map while one of them thought, *if you say contour line one more time I will put this pencil through your hand.* Nobody sounded like a building where a man had died an hour ago. The noise of ordinary irritation almost made her sick.

Sylvia was waiting in Briefing Room B with the blinds half closed. A route map had been pinned to the far board. On the table sat a thin gray folder, a stack of copied manifests, and one black-and-white checkpoint photo turned face down. Sylvia did not stand when Anya came in.

"Sit down," she said.

Anya sat.

Sylvia pushed the folder across the table. "Open page three."

It was her assessment from three weeks ago. Clean typescript. Her name at the top. The lie sat there in flat bureaucratic language, smaller than the damage it had done.

Desmond-adjacent freight and customs chatter assessed as aspirational political signaling, not evidence of live operational control over eastern logistics.

Anya did not need to read the rest. She had built the sentence herself.

"Read the next line," Sylvia said.

Anya obeyed.

Associated Renewal Front reporting to remain under periodic review pending independent corroboration. No immediate rerouting recommended on current evidence.

Sylvia waited until Anya looked up again.

"This assessment entered the system as analysis, not source color," she said. "Once it did, eastern desk reclassified the Desmond-adjacent corridor from red watch to amber review. Duplicate surveillance at Kreuzfeld, Seebruck, and Langenau was reduced. Support coverage was reassigned north. Replacement courier windows were generated from the new threat picture." She touched the manifest stack with two fingers. "Crane drew the 0340 Kreuzfeld window."

There it was. Not guilt. Process. Not feeling. Chain.

Anya stared at the paper until the words blurred and came back.

"What did you have when you wrote this?" Sylvia asked.

"Fragments."

"From where."

"I can't give you a clean source line."

"That is not what I asked."

Anya heard the unspoken line under it anyway. *Do not hide behind tradecraft now.*

"Social access," Anya said. "Conversations. Indirect material. Nothing I could cite cleanly."

"Enough to think the chatter was live?"

"Yes."

Sylvia's face did not change. That made it worse.

"Enough to say so at the time?"

Anya looked back at the report.

"Yes."

Silence filled the room. Outside the glass, a junior officer hurried past holding two mugs and thought, *don't drop it, don't drop it,* with the absolute emotional commitment of someone whose whole life briefly was not dropping coffee.

"Why didn't you," Sylvia said.

The question was small. It landed like a punch.

Anya's first answer came out wrong. "I thought if we tagged him active we would blow the only line we had into that circle."

Sylvia kept waiting.

Anya hated that waiting. It left too much room for truth to move.

"I told myself I was preserving access," she said. "I told myself I was buying time."

"By distorting an assessment."

"Yes."

"For how long did you know it was distorted."

There was no version of this that did not sound like rot. "When I filed it."

Sylvia looked at her for a long second.

*You idiot. You absolute, gifted idiot.*

That one did not come through Sylvia's face. It came through clean and bright and vanished so fast Anya almost thought she imagined it.

"Then hear the chain as it currently stands," Sylvia said. Her voice had gone flatter, not colder. "Your report changed the board. Falk's desk signed the downgrade into active routing guidance. Eastern watch thinned the corridor. Replacement windows were created. Someone with access to one of those windows handed Crane's packet to the team waiting at Kreuzfeld. Those are the facts available this morning. I will not let you turn the rest into certainty because certainty is easier to punish."

Anya swallowed.

"And Desmond's apartment."

"Specific warrant. Specific enough to suggest either prior interior knowledge or a records leak on the Ostanian side." Sylvia tapped the face-down checkpoint photo instead of looking at it. "The timing suggests coordination. The channels do not prove it. Ministry records could explain the address. Our reroute explains Kreuzfeld. One operation could have reached both. Two separate leaks could have landed on the same night. Until we know otherwise, treat both possibilities as live."

That was the answer Damian had given in different words. The same ugly shape, twice from two different directions.

"Do you believe Falk signed those changes because he was exploited," Anya asked, "or because he is the mole?"

Sylvia's gaze sharpened.

"I believe Gerald Falk touches half the eastern paperwork because that is his job." The line was clipped enough to count as a warning. "If you are making an accusation, make one with evidence."

"I'm not."

*Not yet,* thought the part of her that Sylvia did not need telepathy to read.

Sylvia leaned back in her chair. "Good. Then be useful instead."

She turned the checkpoint photo over.

Crane stood beside a border kiosk in bad lighting, coat zipped to the throat, one hand lifted halfway to his ear as if whoever took the picture had caught him mid-annoyance. Somebody had circled the truck lane behind him in blue grease pencil. Another photo beneath it showed the same patch of gravel later, darker in one long spill beside the curb.

Anya looked away first.

"By 1700," Sylvia said, "I want every omitted note, every downgraded contact, every crossing affected by this assessment, and every reroute that followed from it. Names if you have them. Desk handoffs if you can trace them. If you touched it, I want the exact shape of the damage."

"Yes."

"Nothing leaves your desk today without me seeing it."

"Yes."

"And Anya."

She looked up.

Sylvia's expression had not softened. That made the next part legible precisely because it did not.

"Do not waste the day performing grief at me. Work."

The route analysis room had twelve terminals and air that never seemed fully used by the people breathing it. Anya took the station nearest the dead radiator because nobody else wanted it. The screen took too long to wake up. While it loaded, the analyst across from her thought about whether a second breakfast counted as a moral failure if the first one had been half a banana eaten standing up. Somebody at the copier worried his wife had stopped loving him because she no longer corrected his tie. The ordinary traffic of private nonsense moved through her skull with the same rights as anything else. She wanted silence badly enough to bite something.

She opened her own assessment first. Then the routing chain behind it.

One lie turned into forms faster than grief turned into sense.

Her report at 09:14.

Eastern desk note, twenty-three minutes later: Desmond-associated freight chatter reclassified amber pending corroboration.

Coverage review that afternoon: duplicate watch at Kreuzfeld no longer operationally justified under current threat picture.

Support transfer the next morning: shadow-car allocation moved north to Nordhafen.

Courier redistribution approved that evening.

Falk's initials sat on two of the three approvals in neat blue script.

Not proof. Just proximity. Too much proximity.

She opened Crane's packet.

Alias: Erwin Baer.

Cover: sugar-delivery subcontractor.

Crossing: Kreuzfeld, lane three.

Window: 03:36 to 03:44.

Support column: none.

Below that sat a field note from two nights earlier, clipped and irritated in exactly the way Crane had been clipped and irritated in person.

Papers serviceable. Guard on lane three still lazy about axle seals. Whoever typed the manifest needs a new ribbon because this one looks like shit.

He had signed it D.C., because everyone knew it was him.

Anya stared at the initials until they stopped meaning anything.

She pulled up her original rough notes from the Desmond file. The sentence she had removed was still there in an earlier draft: contact cluster suggests possible touchpoints with customs scheduling and rail freight timing across east crossings. She had highlighted it once, then deleted it from the final because saying it aloud inside WISE would have brought too much heat too fast. That had been the argument. That had been the shape of the self-justification. Preserve access. Avoid blowing the line. Stay useful longer.

Crane had died inside longer.

A junior analyst stopped at her desk with a stack of memos and thought, *wrong station, wrong station, wrong station,* before realizing she was not the person he wanted. He mumbled an apology and kept moving. Anya nodded without hearing him.

She kept digging.

The downgraded corridor had not only changed courier assignments. It had changed which anomalies triggered immediate review and which ones got rolled into end-of-day summaries. Two customs names she had shoved into a side note rather than the main threat lane never hit urgent follow-up. A flagged fuel invoice sat unanswered for forty-eight hours because the desk handling it was no longer working under red-watch rules. The whole thing was so boring on paper it almost offended her. Nothing in the chain looked like murder. It looked like staffing. It looked like bureaucracy. It looked like a set of adult decisions made in polite language until somebody bled out on gravel.

By noon her coffee had gone cold untouched. By one she had three pages of reroutes, support cancellations, and downgraded anomaly notices. By two she had a list of names she had minimized, a list of crossings affected, and an ugly little cluster in the middle where Falk's desk touched everything without yet touching enough.

She took the printouts to Sylvia's office.

Sylvia read standing up.

"Falk signed these two," Anya said. "The downgrade and the support transfer."

"Yes," Sylvia said.

"That is too close."

"It is close." Sylvia set the pages down. "It is not yet narrow."

Anya hated that she was right.

"Mertens may explain the apartment," Anya said. "If the ministry side leaked the address, and Falk touched the routing, then it could've been two people hitting the same network from different sides."

"Possible."

"Or Falk handed the routing out and somebody else took advantage of the panic on the Ostanian side."

"Also possible."

"That is not very useful."

"Neither is false certainty." Sylvia picked up the pages again. "Keep separating the known from the guessed."

The rebuke landed because it was deserved. Anya stood there anyway.

"If you had to make the call now," she said, "would you pull me off the desk?"

Sylvia took longer than she needed to answer.

"If I wanted to punish you, yes," she said. "If I want the damage mapped before whoever did this moves again, no." She set the pages down for the second time. "So I am not punishing you today. I am using you. Try to deserve the distinction."

That should have felt merciful. It did not.

Anya went back to the terminal and kept working until the words on the screen turned liquid around the edges. At 16:40 she sent Sylvia the first clean reconstruction: the lie she filed, the downgrade it triggered, the coverage cuts that followed, the specific window Crane inherited, the points where uncertainty still started. She left the building after dark with the route map burned behind her eyes and the smell of dead coffee in her coat.

Damian took the long way home.

He walked east through Berlint's commercial district past shuttered storefronts, delivery trucks, and cafes rolling up awnings for a day that had no idea what had happened. The air bit harder without his coat. He had left it in the basement. He did not go back.

Mertens had daughters. Eight and ten. Damian had never asked their names. That had felt prudent. Now it felt cheap. He knew about the football instead. He knew about the gym bag. He knew Mertens took the long ramp into the ministry garage because the short stairs hurt his knee in winter. Useless information, except it was not. It was the part that kept exposure from staying abstract.

By the time he reached his building, rain had started. He let himself in, locked the door, and stood in the entryway listening to the refrigerator hum and the pipes settle. Wet footprints spread on the tile. The apartment looked half inhabited, as it always did when he was too tired to pretend otherwise. Books on the floor. One lamp on. Cold air leaking out of the bedroom because he had left the window cracked again.

He emptied his pockets on the kitchen counter. House key. Basement key. Two coins. A tram ticket gone soft with rain. Mertens's number on a folded scrap of paper.

He looked at the number for a moment, then tore it into four pieces and dropped them into the sink.

The box was in the closet on the top shelf, behind a stack of diplomatic histories he kept meaning to finish and never did. He had opened it once since moving in, late at night after a dinner where he had smiled through opinions that made him want to break the glass in his own hand. He had looked at the photo then and put it back. This time he took the whole box down and set it on the bed.

It held the usual things. A school blazer he had outgrown at thirteen, folded badly. A note from his mother, the ink almost gone. A runner-up certificate from a math competition. Three pens his father had given him for the entrance exams. Second place had always been the safe result in his house. Not good enough to display. Too good to throw away.

And the class photo.

He sat on the edge of the bed with it in both hands. Standard group portrait. Teacher at the side, already tired. Second row, third from the left. Pink hair. Wide grin. Uniform rumpled. Looking somewhere off camera like whatever was there was more interesting than the photographer. Small enough that somebody had probably told her where to stand twice. She was probably seven.

He was not looking for proof. He already had that. Becky's drunken sentence at the gala had closed the last inch of distance. The photo made the certainty worse, not clearer.

Anya Forger.

The problem now was not whether he was right. The problem was what right meant.

The woman at the state dinner had been Anya Forger. The cultural attache who found him too quickly. The woman who stepped into an ambush at exactly the right second. The woman who listened with her whole body and somehow still always seemed half a beat ahead of the words coming out of his mouth.

Did that mean Westalis had put someone near him that early? No. Start over. That did not hold. She had been a child. He had been a child. She had punched him during dodgeball because he had called her a freak and deserved what came after. Nobody spent years cultivating the second son of a family not yet worth the trouble.

The cover could have come later. The family could have been a cover too. Or neither was true. He could not prove any of that from one photo and a stack of memories that refused to behave like evidence.

The memories themselves were insultingly ordinary. Half a peanut butter sandwich shoved at him after Emile stole his lunch. Anya calling a teacher unfair to his face and then getting detention for it. The way she laughed without checking first whether it was allowed. He remembered her getting bored during assemblies and kicking the back of his chair until he finally turned around to glare at her. He remembered the specific satisfaction of glaring at her when he was eight, as if outrage were a kind of rank.

Childhood had not felt like tradecraft. It had felt like childhood. Messy, mean, brief, real.

He remembered the safe house too, in ugly detail. Antiseptic on the cut above his eyebrow. Her fingers cold from rinsing out a washcloth. The way she said sorry like she meant the rib and not everything else. The kiss. There was no point pretending he had forgotten the kiss. There was even less point pretending he had understood it.

Was any of it real?

The girl in the photo was. The woman in the basement had been too. When she said she had filed him wrong, there had been no performance in it. He knew performance. He had been raised on it. He also knew what a person looked like when she was trying to build a complete theory of disaster out of whatever sharp parts were nearest to hand.

What unsettled him was not only that she had lied. He lived among lies. He produced enough of them himself to have lost the right to be shocked on principle. What unsettled him was the shape of her timing. She moved toward danger before warnings fully landed. She answered questions he had not quite asked. She knew where the pressure was before anyone in the room admitted there was pressure at all. At the state dinner he had called it intuition. At the safe house he had called it field instinct. At the gala he had stopped trying to name it because every name he had sounded insufficient.

That still was not proof of anything except that she remained a problem he could not diagram.

He set the photo down long enough to close the bedroom window. Rain tapped once against the glass and slid away. The room cooled immediately anyway. When he came back to the bed, the photo waited where he had left it, pink hair bright even in bad grayscale.

He had been trained from childhood to treat attachment as exposure. Standing in the kitchen with the basement key in his hand, he tried on the neat answer: cut Anya loose, bury whatever was left of the network, and call it prudence. Crane stayed dead in that version too. Mertens stayed exposed. The mole kept breathing.

He dropped the key into the back of the kitchen drawer under a roll of string and three dead batteries. There would be no more fixed rooms. Nothing on paper would sleep behind walls again. If anything passed between them now, it passed hand to hand or not at all.

Then he went back to the bed, slid the class photo under his mother's note, and shut the lid. He would keep working with her. He would not hand her the photo. Not yet.

He put the box back on the shelf behind the unread histories. Rain tapped once against the shut window. The apartment stayed cold.
