# Chapter 10

Under the kitchen light, the milk was going to be a problem. The next evening, Yor knew only that the milk was going to be a problem.

She held the carton at an angle and read the date stamp. Tomorrow. Back into the refrigerator it went, behind the leftover stew, where Loid was less likely to discover it. He had opinions about expiration dates that she considered disproportionate to the actual science of dairy spoilage, but she'd never found a way to say that without sounding like she was defending spoiled milk, which she wasn't. What she was defending was the principle that milk didn't become poison at midnight on the printed date. She was fairly sure about this.

Bond had taken over the middle of the living room rug again, chin pressed into the worn border, sleeping with the calm certainty that everyone else would step around him. His breathing came deep and even, with a faint whistle at the end of each exhale, and one back leg twitched with whatever passed through a precognitive dog's sleeping mind. Yor sometimes caught Anya watching Bond with an expression that suggested she was listening to something Yor couldn't hear, but the habit was so old and so gentle that Yor had filed it years ago among the things about her daughter that she loved without understanding.

Across the hall, Anya's old bedroom door stood half open. The bedspread was smooth. At noon a message had come from an embassy number Yor didn't recognize: *paperwork ran long don't wait up love you*. Too neat. Too bright at the end. Yor had answered with *Eat something hot if you can*, then, because that sounded too much like an instruction, *Only if you want to!* Anya had replied with a peanut sticker and nothing else. That had not helped.

A bento box sat open on the counter. This was not strictly necessary. Loid was at the university for a consulting session, one of the occasional appointments that maintained a cover identity that had long since stopped feeling like one, and he wouldn't be back until evening. But Yor liked making them. The routine was grounding. Rice balls, three, with pickled plum. Tamagoyaki, two slices, trimmed where the browning had become something closer to charring. A thermos of mushroom miso soup from a recipe she'd clipped from a magazine in the city hall break room.

The soup tasted too salty. Not catastrophically, but enough that she could hear Anya's polite silence if she ever served it, the careful way Anya said "It's good, Mama" when what she meant was *This is mostly salt*.

The bento went into the refrigerator with a note: *The soup is maybe a little salty. Sorry! Please don't feel like you have to drink it. But I think it's okay? Yor*

As she dotted the question mark, her phone buzzed.

Not a ring. Just one hard pulse against the countertop, the pattern reserved for numbers that didn't exist in any directory. She set down the pen and picked up the phone.

An address on the northern outskirts. A name: Karl Dominic. A timeframe: tonight. Beneath it, the standard return instruction blinked in smaller text. Confirm completion within forty-five minutes of execution. And at the bottom, the six-digit code that confirmed it was real.

Twice was enough. The message would erase itself in ninety seconds. She set the phone face-down on the counter and went back to the bento box, adjusting the thermos so it wouldn't leak during refrigeration. Once the lid was closed, the knife she'd used for the tamagoyaki got washed, dried, and returned to the block.

Bond opened one eye from the rug, then closed it.

"I'm going out tonight," Yor told him. "Don't eat Papa's dinner. It's in the fridge."

She considered sending Anya another message about coats and late trams, decided she was already hovering in a way twenty-year-olds hated, and didn't. Instead she packed the leftover stew into a smaller container in case Anya came by after midnight hungry enough to stop pretending she wasn't.

After a shower, she dressed in a fitted dark jacket, pants that didn't restrict movement at the hip, and flat shoes with grip soles she'd ordered from a work-supply catalogue under the reasoning that icy sidewalks were dangerous and practical footwear was just common sense. Which was true. The shoes were also excellent for moving quietly across concrete and gravel, which was the actual reason she'd bought them.

In the closet, behind the shoe rack and a box of seasonal clothes she rotated every April and October with a conscientiousness that Loid found endearing and Anya found baffling, sat a locked case. The key lived on a chain around her neck alongside a small enamel peanut charm, a prize from an Eden Academy festival booth, won by a six-year-old Anya and presented with the solemnity of a military decoration. The charm's paint was chipping at the edges, and she'd never replaced it.

Inside the case: two blades, a garrote wire, a compact medical kit, and lockpicks that had been Garden standard issue since before Anya was born. From it she selected the longer blade and the picks. The garrote she left. It was a single target in a compound setting. The blade was cleaner, faster, something she could sheathe when the job was done. The garrote required sustained proximity she didn't enjoy. That wasn't out of squeamishness. It was because the garrote was slow, and slow meant time spent close enough to hear the sound the body made, and that was a sound she preferred to keep brief.

With the blade strapped to the inside of her left forearm, under the jacket sleeve, Yor checked the draw. The motion was smooth. The lockpicks went into her inner pocket. The phone went into the other one, screen down over the return code she was not going to think about yet.

In the hallway mirror, she looked like a woman heading out for the evening. It was a bit severe. She tried the small reception-desk smile she used at city hall when she arrived for her shift. It softened things.

On her way out, she stopped at the hall table where Loid had left a shopping list in his careful handwriting: rice, laundry soap, Bond's joint medication, milk. That reminder had been on her list all day. He would buy a new carton, find the old one hiding behind the stew, pour it out, and give her the expression. Not disapproval exactly. More the gentle bewilderment of a man who had spent decades in intelligence operations and could not fathom his wife's relationship with dairy.

At the bottom she added *milk, please check fridge first!* in her rounder, larger handwriting, and beneath it: *Back late. Don't wait up? There's stew.*

The door locked behind her. The landing smelled of the neighbor's floor wax and the building's old radiators kicking against the October chill.

Then she went to kill Karl Dominic.

---

The compound was forty minutes north of Berlint proper, where the city's residential blocks thinned into industrial lots and gated estates belonging to people who valued privacy over proximity. Yor took the tram to the northern terminal and walked the remaining two kilometers along a service road paralleling a drainage canal. The air smelled of wet concrete, diesel from a freight depot she'd passed a kilometer back, and the faintly vegetal rot of standing water in the ditch.

The tram had been half full. Shift workers folded into coats. A student asleep against the window with ink on two fingers. A woman with grocery sacks and a face so tired it looked arranged that way. Yor stood the whole ride, one hand on the rail, and read the briefing file once before it wiped itself. Karl Dominic. Fifty-three. Arms broker. His supply chain had been linked to the recent military buildup along the Ostanian border: weapons transfers routed through shell companies, warehouse leases, and stamped invoices that made rifles travel dressed as something else. The Garden's assessment classified him as a destabilizing agent. Eliminate. Standard contract.

Standard. The word belonged to the paper trail around every job: assignment number, return code, the deposit arriving on the fifteenth. Take the name. Do the work. Wipe the blade clean before it dried. Be home in time to pack leftovers and answer Loid when he asked how city hall had been. If she stopped in the middle and asked who had checked the file, who had signed off, whose daughter was waiting in some apartment with the light on, the whole mechanism would seize. So she had not asked. She had called that trust.

So had the shipping clerk. Three months ago. A man in the port district whose briefing profile listed arms connections that turned out, when she'd checked afterward, to be invoices for industrial machine parts. Legitimate equipment for a legitimate factory. The man had begged, not for himself, which she could manage, but for his daughter, who was waiting at home. They sometimes begged. She'd trained herself to close that channel years ago. But the invoices. The gap between the profile and the evidence. She'd reported the discrepancy to her handler. The handler had said: *Thank you, Yor. Don't worry about it. These things are reviewed.* And she'd stopped worrying.

Mostly, she'd stopped worrying.

The landscape changed as she walked. Berlint's streetlights gave way to intermittent industrial security lamps, and the sounds of the city, tram bells, traffic, a bar's music leaking from somewhere, faded into the low drone of freight machinery and then, past the last depot, into nothing much at all. The service road was unpaved beyond the terminal's reach: packed dirt and gravel that crunched under her soles. Past a shuttered machine shop came a fenced lot full of construction aggregate, then a drainage pump station where someone had left a folding chair and an empty thermos. The only other living thing she'd encountered in the last kilometer was a cat that crossed the road, evaluated her with the professional indifference of an animal that knew it couldn't be caught, and vanished into a culvert pipe.

Half past ten found her at the compound. The perimeter was a three-meter concrete wall topped with razor wire and motion sensors at the corners. Beyond it: the roofline of the main building, interior lights, the muted geometry of an estate that had been a manufacturing facility before Dominic repurposed it. Sealed loading docks on the east side. The main gate faced south, connected to the highway by a paved access road.

Surveillance settled into place. Three men visible at first glance: one stationed at the gate, shifting his weight foot to foot with the restlessness of a man assigned a post he found boring; one near a cargo van parked in the loading area, its rear doors open; one walking the interior perimeter on a patrol route. She timed the patrol. Four minutes per circuit. Consistent rhythm. The pace of routine rather than alertness. A second look caught more: office lights burning in the southwest corner of the main building, the intermittent shadow of men crossing a conference room farther in, the bright square of paperwork being moved from one hand to another. A live deal, not storage.

The north wall had a vulnerability. A motion sensor housing mounted at an angle that covered the airspace above the wall rather than the wall face itself, a factory installation never corrected. Exactly the mistake that stayed in place when security was maintained by men who filled out forms instead of climbing walls.

When the patrol rounded the south wall, she went up the north wall at the sensor gap and dropped into the compound's northeast corner behind a maintenance shed. The shed smelled of machine oil and dried grass clippings. Thirty still seconds passed while the patrol guard's boots crunched by on the other side, steady and unhurried, the steps of a man completing a task he'd completed a thousand times without incident.

The crunch faded toward the south. She moved.

The man at the cargo van was leaning against the rear bumper, smoking. His sidearm sat on his right hip, holstered, the retention snap undone. It was structural carelessness. He'd never encountered trouble here, and the boredom of guard work had eroded whatever discipline he'd once maintained. Yor came around the van's blind side. The last three meters took less than a second. She put the blade through the base of his skull at the junction where the brainstem lived, and he dropped. She caught his weight against the van's bumper and lowered him to the pavement without noise. His cigarette rolled under the tire.

Inside the van: long wooden crates, unmarked, secured with military-grade packaging straps. The chemical smell of preservative oil seeped through the wood grain. Rifles. She counted twelve crates. Enough for a company.

From there she moved toward the main building.

The loading dock's sealed entrance had a secondary door, service access, a standard pin-tumbler lock that the picks handled in nine seconds. A corridor inside, fluorescent tubes overhead, half of them dead and the survivors flickering with a buzz that set her teeth on edge. An office door on the left, closed. Voices from deeper in the building, male, three or four of them, the measured rhythm of negotiation. The private sale. Buyers in the building. A steel fire door marked MAINTENANCE STAIR sat at the corridor branch, paint bubbled near the bottom from old flooding.

That changed the approach. Buyers meant witnesses and potentially more armed men. She needed the principal and the exit, nothing else.

Two guards in the corridor, stationed outside a conference room. The first noticed her at six meters and reached for his radio. Wrong decision. Yor closed the gap in a stride and drove the heel of her palm into his trachea. He folded with a wet, shocked gasp. The second guard was faster. He'd drawn his sidearm by the time she reached him. But his partner was between them, and the half-second he spent calculating a clear firing line was a half-second he didn't have. Her blade opened the inside of his forearm from wrist to elbow, deep enough to ruin the arm, and the gun clattered to the floor tiles. An elbow below his ear dropped him after that.

The first guard's radio came with her. The channel stayed quiet long enough for a fragment from the conference room to carry through the door.

"Not until Karl says," one man said. "He wants the papers in order."

Dominic liked control. No one would burst into his office uninvited while buyers were waiting to be told they could touch what they'd paid for. That would buy her minutes. Not many.

The corridor branched. Left toward the offices. Right toward the voices. She went left. The buyers were not her contract.

Karl Dominic was alone in a corner office at the corridor's end, behind a desk buried in working stacks of paperwork, speaking into a phone. He was shorter than his file photo suggested, stocky, balding, in a dress shirt with the sleeves pushed past his elbows. The office smelled of cigar smoke layered over cheap instant coffee from the tins stacked in freight depot commissaries. A framed photograph on the desk showed Dominic with a woman on a boat, somewhere sunny, both of them squinting at the camera with the particular resignation of people who'd been told to smile.

He looked up when the door opened.

"No," he said into the phone, already irritated, already mid-argument. "I want the confirmations in front of me before they touch the second shipment. If Voss wants deniability, he can pay for it twice."

Then his face processed confusion, recognition, fear.

He dropped the phone and lunged for the desk drawer.

Yor was faster. She had been faster than every person she'd ever been sent to kill, and twenty years had not changed that. The speed lived below thought, below decision, in the architecture of muscle and nerve that the Garden had trained and she had maintained through thousands of hours of practice she told Loid was yoga. Her blade caught his reaching wrist before his hand found the drawer, and the cut was deep enough to part tendons. He screamed, short and bitten off, with no room left in it for dignity.

He tried to stand. She put the blade through the left side of his neck, angled for the carotid, and held it for the two seconds it took. His legs buckled. He went down against the desk, scattering papers, and slid to the floor trailing a red line down the laminate front.

The phone was still connected. A tinny voice on the other end asked, "Karl? Karl, what happened?"

Yor disconnected the call.

The desk intercom buzzed almost at once. Once. Then again. She looked down. Beside the blotter lay a cream index card in Dominic's thick handwriting.

22:35 buyers seated  
22:40 signatures  
22:45 yard release

The wall clock above the filing cabinet read 22:38.

Wiping the blade on the hem of Dominic's shirt, she sheathed it. Her hands were steady. They were always steady during the work. They didn't shake or hesitate or feel the weight of what they'd done until afterward, when the adrenaline burned off and she was just Yor again in a kitchen with a knife and a cutting board, pausing over carrots long enough to blame the tremor on the cold.

The papers on the desk were organized in working stacks, not abandoned clutter. Tonight's crates. Tonight's buyers. Tonight's payments.

Garden training had been explicit on this point. The Garden provided the briefing; the operative executed the contract. Investigation was a different role, different people, a different level of the organization. For twenty years, that division had been enough.

But the shipping clerk. The invoices that hadn't matched. The handler's voice: *Don't worry about it.* And the daughter, waiting at home.

Yor looked at the papers scattered across Dominic's desk and the ones that had fallen to the floor, some of them soaking up the blood pooling from his neck, and she didn't leave.

The top stack held shipping manifests and transfer orders for military hardware: rifles, communications equipment, explosive ordnance, all routed through holding companies whose names meant nothing but whose addresses landed in Berlint's government district, near the defense ministry. The shipment codes repeated. So did a name. Werner Voss.

The intercom buzzed a third time. In the conference room, chairs moved. Yor set her phone to silent, braced it over the desk, and took the fastest images first: cover sheet, buyer list, Voss transfer page, the order for encrypted field communications equipment that had moved six weeks earlier to an anonymous warehouse near the ministry. The quantities suggested equipping a field unit, not filling a depot. An active operation, being assembled through Karl Dominic's desk.

Under the manifests sat a second packet, clipped to the first on heavier cream stock. Those were Dominic's confirmations.

The radio in her hand hissed. "Gate to corridor," a man said. "We need Karl in two."

These were not freight receipts or customs clearances. They were completion records, the back-office proof Dominic would use to show a liability had been handled and demand payment for it. One look told her why they felt familiar. The forms used the same sequence as the Garden's own closeout notices: assignment number, compensation release, executing party.

The contracting entity was coded. She didn't recognize the designator. The executing entity, on the summary sheet clipped to the front, appeared nine times across four years under a numerical code Yor knew as well as her own address. She'd seen it on her payment records for twenty years, on the deposits that arrived on the fifteenth of each month, on the brief confirmations after each completed assignment, on the secure documents she signed when she accepted new contracts.

The Garden.

The code sat in the same place she usually looked for her own fee. Her fingertips slipped on the cream paper. For the first time that night the room gave a small, ugly tilt under her shoes.

Faster now, she read. There were nine assignments: liability removal and witness management. They were not border threats or coup planners. They were cleanup for bad shipments and men who had seen too much. She photographed the summary page, then the payment table behind it, the release dates landing on the fifteenth like the deposits in her own account. A door latch clicked in the corridor. Someone said something annoyed and too close to make out.

One entry carried a date from three months ago and a district marker from the port. She found the corresponding page halfway down the packet and snapped it before the words stopped blurring. There were the machine-parts invoices again. There was the clerk's name. She saw the cramped office, the stack of stamped forms, the moment he started begging only when he understood she wasn't there to threaten him. He had said his daughter was waiting at home.

The shipping clerk had not been an exception. Or if he had been, this dead man had eight others filed beside him.

There were still pages under her thumb. She did not get them. The hall outside had changed shape. A buyer's voice carried clear now, no longer muffled by conference-room walls. "Where is he?"

Yor took one last image of the page headers still buried in the packet, enough to keep the assignment numbers and dates, then laid the cream stock back where she'd found it. Taking the whole packet would announce the search before she reached the yard. Leaving it meant Dominic's people might not know for minutes which pages had been looked at and which hadn't.

Karl Dominic had not hidden a confession in a lucky drawer. He had been working through tonight's sale with the paperwork laid out exactly as he needed it, shipment on top and cleanup underneath, because men like him believed paper was insurance.

Twenty years. The number landed like a deposit date. For twenty years the Garden had handed her a name, a district, a return code, and she had sharpened blades, wiped sinks, hung up her coat, and told herself Loid's shoes by the door and Anya's bed across the hall stayed safe because she did what was necessary.

Now an arms broker had kept proof that the Garden did cleanup for his supply chain in the same neat stacks as freight paperwork.

What if the shipping clerk had not been a mistake. What if the mistake was every time she'd folded a closeout slip and put it away without looking harder.

No clear next step followed that thought. The blank space frightened her. She had always known what came next.

The first shout went up as she reached the corridor. Not a full alarm, not yet. Just the shocked bark of a man finding the guard on the floor outside the conference room. The clean route was gone. She hit the maintenance stair instead, took the steps in silence, and came out through a lower service bay that stank of solvent and wet cardboard. By the time she crossed the yard, the perimeter patrol was already turning north twenty seconds ahead of schedule.

Back over the wall she went, using the same sensor gap and not enough time. Her left hand caught the razor wire on the descent. She felt the resistance and the tug but not the pain. The pain arrived thirty seconds later on the service road, a line of hot sting across her palm that resolved into a shallow cut running along the old scar from when Anya was eight and learning to cut carrots and the knife had slipped from her small grip and caught Yor's hand instead. Anya had cried for twenty minutes. Yor hadn't minded the cut. She'd held her bleeding palm behind her back so Anya wouldn't feel bad and said, "See? It's nothing. Let's try again."

With the cut wrapped in her jacket sleeve, she kept walking.

Then she saw the ditch.

At the base of the wall, where the service road curved toward the canal, the weeds beside the industrial waste containers had been pressed flat into a human hollow. A cheap thermos lay on its side with the cap screwed back on tight. Beside it sat compact field glasses wrapped in dark tape to kill reflection, a pencil worn down to the metal, and a folded municipal survey map pinned under a broken brick. The compound perimeter had been sketched on the back in quick pencil strokes: south gate, loading dock, patrol route, the north sensor gap she'd used. In the upper corner someone had written `WS-7`.

Somebody had been here long enough to count.

Movement broke beside the containers.

A figure rose. One shoulder lower under a light pack. Weight on the back foot. Small enough to be underestimated.

Yor's mind measured the distance, the angle, the quickest path to the throat.

Then the figure stepped into the spill of security light and it was Anya.

Yor stopped. Her daughter was in dark clothes, not the diplomatic wardrobe, not the outfits of a cultural attaché at Westalian embassy events, but cut from the same logic as Yor's own: cloth that wouldn't shine, snag, or hold the light. Anya's hair was pulled back under a dark knit cap. Mud darkened the knees of her trousers. A compact camera strap crossed her ribs under the jacket. The field glasses on the ground were hers. So was the map.

Anya looked too thin. She always looked thin. Milk and soup were small problems with practical answers. This belonged to the other pile, the ache that stayed lodged behind Yor's ribs because Anya was twenty and an adult and there was no decent way to make an adult eat.

Neither of them spoke for a moment. The canal water moved in the ditch. The compound's lights threw a pale halo against the low October clouds.

"You should eat more," Yor said. "You look thin."

Anya's mouth opened. Closed. Her eyes dropped to Yor's left hand, the jacket sleeve dark and stiff with blood, the makeshift wrapping already soaking through.

"Your hands are bleeding, Mama."

"Oh." Yor looked at her hand as if the blood were a social inconvenience, like noticing a stain on her shirt at a work dinner. "It's, um. It's not bad. I scraped it on a fence. It's really not deep. I just didn't have a, um. I'll clean it when I get home."

Her own voice sounded absurd in the canal dark. Fence. As if Anya were twelve and had found her after grocery shopping instead of outside a weapons broker's wall with surveillance notes pinned under a brick.

Yor looked at the map again. "What are you doing here?"

Anya followed her glance and, for a second, seemed to consider lying. Yor watched the thought pass through her face and go.

"I had a line from the west side," Anya said. "One corridor. It ended here."

The words were plain. The meaning inside them was not.

"I was supposed to watch the gate," Anya said. "See who came out after Dominic. Get a plate number, maybe a face. Something I could use."

She swallowed. "I didn't think it was going to be you."

Yor's eyes went to the taped field glasses, the notes, the careful little pencil marks counting patrol time. Not an accident, then. Not curiosity. Her daughter had come north from some other piece of trouble and laid herself flat in canal weeds to watch a killer's house breathe.

"You were going to follow them?" Yor asked.

"If I had to."

The answer came too fast. Anya heard it too and made a face at herself, small and irritated.

"I mean," she said, "I was going to decide that when I knew who it was. I hadn't really finished the plan."

That was so recognizably Anya that Yor almost smiled. It vanished before it could settle.

Behind the wall, someone shouted again. This time the word *body* carried clearly.

Anya's gaze flicked to the compound, then to the phone-shaped weight in Yor's pocket, then back to her face. A change passed over her there, quick and impossible to name. Not surprise exactly. Not fear. Something like recognition, but pointed in a direction Yor could not place and could not safely ignore.

"They were waiting on signatures," Anya said. "If anything's missing, they'll know fast."

Yor said nothing.

Anya held her eyes. "Don't make whatever call comes next tonight. Just go home."

The night seemed to thin around that sentence.

Yor didn't ask how Anya knew there was a call. She didn't ask what the west side had been, or who had sent her here, or why her daughter now sounded like someone who understood reporting windows and search patterns and the cost of using a burned line one stop too soon. Anya didn't ask why Yor had Dominic's blood on her sleeve, or what was in the pocket she'd glanced at twice, or whether the look on Yor's face belonged to injury or something worse.

Those questions stayed where they always stayed between them, large and untouched. But the space around them had changed shape. It was no longer ignorance. It was arrangement.

"I need to get back," Yor said, gesturing toward the road to the tram station. The old line came to her out of habit and sounded childish now. "It's late."

"Yeah." Anya nodded once. "I'm not coming with you."

"Right. Of course."

Another silence sat down between them. The canal gurgled. A light snapped on above the compound's loading dock and threw a harder glare across the road.

"Were you here alone?" Yor asked.

"Yes. It was just watching. I wasn't going in."

That answer she believed. It did not reassure her.

"Be careful getting home," Yor said. "It's cold tonight. You have a proper coat? Not just that one?"

"I know the route. I've got layers."

"There's stew in the fridge. From yesterday. If you want to stop by. You don't have to. But it's there."

"I'll come by. Tomorrow, maybe."

"And the soup. The mushroom one. It's new. It might be..." Yor glanced helplessly at her bleeding hand as if it had personally interrupted her sentence. "I think I used too much miso. But it's fine. Probably. It's an experiment."

"It's probably not that bad. I'll try it."

Yor wanted to cross the six paces between them. She wanted to put a hand on the back of Anya's head and say something ordinary enough to get them safely through the night. Instead she kept both hands to herself. One was bleeding. The other had touched Dominic's paperwork and carried photographs of the Garden's code in an arms broker's office.

"Goodnight, Anya."

"Night, Mama."

Yor turned toward the tram road. Looking back felt impossible. Even so, before the bend took the canal out of sight, she heard the small sounds of Anya repacking the observation post: thermos cap, brick against gravel, paper folding once and then again.

By the time she reached the dead streetlamp near the service-road junction, the domestic thoughts were trying to reassemble themselves: stew in the fridge, soup too salty, whether Anya had lied about the coat, whether there was enough rice left for breakfast. Under them sat the other things, flat and impossible. Nine contracts. Four years. The Garden's code on Dominic's papers. WS-7 on Anya's map. Her daughter saying *don't make whatever call comes next tonight* in a voice that assumed Yor would understand her.

She tried, uselessly, to fit the pieces together in an order that made sense. Anya had come north from a west-side line with a map and a camera. Dominic had kept Garden work under his shipment papers. Somewhere between those two facts lay a shape large enough to hold her marriage, her daughter, twenty years of contracts, and whatever she had been telling herself all that time. She could not hold the whole thing at once. She could only hold the next practical fact: if Garden touched the canal before dawn, the hollow in the weeds would stop being a question and start being a trail.

The phone in her pocket vibrated once: not a call, just the automated reminder that the confirmation window was closing.

Yor took it out under the dead streetlamp. The screen showed the unentered completion field and eleven minutes remaining. If she answered, Karl Dominic would become just another completed line item. Garden would know she had stayed long enough to touch his desk. They would audit the breach, reconstruct the yard, sweep the north wall, the ditch, the flattened weeds where someone had lain with field glasses and a map marked `WS-7`.

Her thumb rested over the keypad. For twenty years she had never missed a confirmation window. Not once. Completion, code, silence. Then home.

Tonight she slid the battery free instead.

She put the phone in one pocket and the battery in the other. The dark screen reflected nothing back at her. By the time the tram came, the window would die on its own.

Then she kept walking toward home.
