# Chapter 15

The International Conference Center smelled of floor polish, new carpet, and overheated coffee from the press mezzanine, the kind of institutional cleanliness that tried too hard to feel calm. Anya cleared the east-side screening with Damian's credential clipped high on her lapel and a reporter's notebook tucked under one arm. The screener barely looked at her. Across the plaza the diplomatic queue crawled between extra guards and handheld scanners, three lines deep already. Whatever Damian had rerouted her around, it had more teeth than inconvenience.

Her seat in the East Gallery put the whole assembly hall under her.

The Main Assembly Hall curved out below in tiers of dark wood and pale stone. Ostanian delegates sat to the west in sober rows. Westalian delegates mirrored them from the east. A raised platform split the room down the middle, with translation booths above and the south wall all glass beyond it, a clean sheet of window looking out to the terrace and the clipped hedges beyond. If somebody got a line through that glass, the podium would sit in it like a target at a fairground.

Four hundred minds hit her at once.

She braced one hand on the armrest and let the noise come through.

*if this runs past four my daughter will kill me...*

*translator three is missing paragraph six again, useless...*

*coffee's burned, of course it's burned...*

*south service stairs at thirteen fifty, don't look at the doors again...*

Anya opened her eyes.

That could have been security. It could have been catering. It could have been somebody arranging a smoke break they did not want seen by the cameras. In a room this crowded every guilty thought arrived looking important until the next one shoved it aside.

She kept listening.

*Voss is here. Great. So the old men brought their knives after all...*

*if the Westalians say inspections we slow-walk it to committee...*

*did I leave the apartment window open...*

*first report has to sound Westalian or the whole thing turns stupid...*

That one came from somewhere below and to the right, not the press row beside her. It was a male surface voice, flat with concentration. It did not feel like panic or fantasy. She did not turn her head yet. Turning too soon only scattered the source.

On the platform the moderator shuffled his stack of remarks. To his left sat Deputy Minister Gerhardt in his tidy charcoal suit, jaw working once as he reread the note card beside his water glass. The printed summit program inside Anya's notebook put his response at fourteen hundred exactly, second speaker after the Westalian deputy minister. He would have to stand to reach the microphone. He would stand with the south wall behind him.

*when he stands, center mass. Glass won't matter if the angle stays clean...*

Anya's fingers tightened on the notebook.

That thought arrived from closer, from the Westalian overflow seats under the gallery where press and aides blurred together into respectable furniture. She looked down as if checking her notes and found a man in a charcoal suit with a press lanyard he wore too still. Everyone else in the row shifted with boredom, crossed legs, rubbed temples, glanced at phones they were not supposed to have out. He kept mapping the room with his eyes, never landing on faces, only doors, railings, glass, podium.

Her headache started there, a blunt pulse behind her left eye.

She listened harder.

*thirteen fifty they're in place, fourteen hundred the deputy stands, if the first noise comes from Westalian side security writes the rest for us...*

Not one complete thought. Three different ones, caught in pieces and stitched together around the room's geometry: the south service stairs, the south glass, Gerhardt standing at fourteen hundred, Westalian attribution. Bond's red-smeared building vision overlaid the terrace for one sick second, and Anya had to swallow before she could breathe normally again.

It was a staged assassination attempt. They wanted Gerhardt dead on camera with Westalis blamed before the blood dried, the summit over with him and peace going with it.

The headache sharpened when she opened herself wider, trying to hold onto the source.

*don't look at the camera, don't look at the...*

*if the line's compromised, south first, comms after...*

*why is the shrimp cold in the middle...*

The last one almost made her bark a laugh. It also ruined the signal. By the time she cut through the absurdity and reached for the operative mind again, it had sunk back into four hundred other thoughts, some terrified, some petty, most useless.

She scanned the hall anyway. The south doors sat under a discreet security camera. Two floral arrangements flanked the platform. Gerhardt's water glass threw a thin line of reflected light over the podium. A clean shot from the terrace would not need much time. It would only need him upright.

She stood.

She had no earpiece and no radio. The summit's countermeasures would catch any unauthorized transmission before she got half a sentence out. Their plan had dead drops for exactly this reason. She tucked the notebook under her arm and took the side stairs down to the service corridor, forcing herself not to hurry until the gallery doors shut behind her.

The corridor smelled more harshly of bleach and old coffee. Staff and security moved through it in practiced summit silence, the kind built from shoes that knew government floors. Anya walked fast and read what she could as each mind slid past.

*get the interpreter backup to room five...*

*my God, if this blazer button goes now I'm resigning...*

*Falk's in east comms. If the main board drops the cold line on rack three buys twelve...*

She stopped mid-step.

It was Falk.

Gerald Falk in east comms, tangled up with venue traffic he should never have touched. And the rest of the thought was worse: the main board, rack three, twelve.

It was not enough. Not yet. It could be contingency chatter. It could be building tech. It could be the one foggy thread that stole minutes from the live shooter on the terrace if she chased it now.

She hated decisions that required being right only once.

At the east-side fire exit she crouched behind a red extinguisher cabinet, tore a page from her notebook, and wrote fast enough to make the letters lean.

*South terrace 1400. Gerhardt target. Falk in comms room east corridor.*

She added *rack 3? cold line?* then stared at the words.

Question marks killed field notes. So did bad facts.

She struck the line out hard enough to tear the paper, folded the sheet twice, and slid it behind the extinguisher bracket where Loid would check. Fifteen minutes was their agreement. If everything held, he would find it by 13:45. If everything did not hold, the plan was already worse than paper.

On the way back she kept listening.

*if the deputy from Westalis cuts the second paragraph the old bastard will still stand, fine...*

*don't scratch your neck, only guilty people scratch their necks...*

*east rack stays dark unless the board dies, remember that, remember...*

That one vanished before she could even sort whether it came from Falk, from a technician, or from a second operative who only knew one narrow job. She carried it with her anyway.

By the time she retook her seat the hall had thickened with the specific silence that came right before microphones went live. Her temples pulsed in time with it. She put both feet flat on the floor and reopened herself to the room.

The moderator gave introductions. The Westalian deputy minister rose. Gerhardt sat with one hand on his note card, waiting for his turn.

Below her, the man in the charcoal suit did not look at the south doors. He did not need to. His mind kept doing it for him.

*keep the line clean, wait for the stand...*

Anya counted breaths. She tracked the moderator's throat, the South Terrace camera, Gerhardt's hand on the table, the press row's boredom, the flickering surface thoughts of three security officers who were already half focused on Damian in the West Gallery because he had started moving before anyone had invited him to. The summit had too many live wires. She only needed one of them to catch.

It did.

The first stir ran through the West Gallery while the deputy minister was still on his opening paragraph.

Damian watched Werner Voss from across the West Gallery and could feel the paper in his inside pocket as if it had temperature.

The copies were old-fashioned on purpose. Ministry stationery. Handwritten marginalia. Cross-referenced manifests from Dominic's compound against contract records from his father's old channels, then against shell procurement accounts that existed just long enough to move money and vanish. Electronic files could be called fabrication. Paper with old men on it was harder to shrug away.

Voss had taken the best corner in the gallery, near the railing with a National Unity delegate Damian recognized from a defense fundraiser and a clear line to both the floor and the side exit. He was talking without giving her his full attention, eyes skimming the room below between sentences as if the summit mattered less than where the bodies were standing.

Damian put a thumb against the edge of the document stack through his pocket and felt one corner bend.

He had imagined this confrontation in a dozen forms. In most of them Voss denied everything. In some of them he laughed. In one especially adolescent version, he looked afraid as soon as Damian said his name. That version had not survived adulthood.

The actual room was worse because it was real. Cameras hung above. Aides lined the side walls. Ostanian delegates sat below, ready to decide from one wrong word whether they were looking at a whistleblower or a Desmond boy setting fire to his own house for attention.

Voss turned before Damian reached him, as if some private weather instrument had registered him coming.

"Young Desmond," Voss said. "This seems like poor timing."

"I have procurement records tied to your name and Renewal Front channels." His voice came out flatter than he had planned. "So — no. Timing isn't the problem here."

The delegate beside Voss blinked once, then schooled her face. Voss did not look at Damian right away. He looked at the floor below, at the cameras, at the room. Calculation first. Always calculation first.

"Would you excuse us," he said to the delegate.

She took two steps away and no more. Good for her, Damian thought. If this went bad, witnesses mattered.

Voss held out a hand. "Let's see what you're carrying."

Damian set the papers on the railing between them instead of surrendering them outright. The pages made a soft dry sound that cut through the gallery noise like a threat.

"If this is theater," Voss said, "pick a smaller room."

"If it were theater I'd have brought a camera crew."

That got Voss to touch the top page.

His eyes moved quickly. Too quickly for surprise. Damian saw the first real shift not in his face but in the hand holding the paper, a minute tightening at the thumb when he hit the second signature.

He knows this ledger, Damian thought. Not just the money. The exact layout.

"Dominic's archive," Voss said.

"Partly."

"Dominic is dead."

"I know."

Voss turned a page. "And what do you imagine this proves?"

The question irritated Damian more than the content. Voss was not buying time to think. He was buying time to choose the story that would keep most of his skin.

"That Kruger Defense subsidiaries and three procurement shells routed ministry money into Renewal Front channels," Damian said. "That the channels stayed open because people with public offices wanted them open. That your name appears whenever the money stops pretending to be public."

Voss gave the smallest possible exhale. Not a laugh. The memory of one.

"You still think there's a top and a bottom," he said. "One rotten man, one clean floor underneath. That's not how this works."

Damian felt his jaw harden. He made himself loosen it before the movement showed.

"Then tell me how it works."

Voss laid the papers flat and looked directly at him for the first time.

"The idiots wave flags. Fine. Useful. But the men with invoices don't care about flags." He tapped the second page. "Your hawks buy Westalian surveillance through intermediaries. The Westalians buy our military reporting back. Same money. Different stamps."

"That's—" Damian stopped. He had expected a defense. This was closer to an org chart.

"It's how it works," Voss said. "The surprise is yours."

"Names," Damian said.

"You have some."

"Not enough."

"You take out one company and three more open under a cousin's signature. Half the people who'd condemn me are protecting their own procurement counsel from exactly the same audit."

Damian thought of Demetrius for one involuntary, furious second. Not because he knew his brother was in these exact lines. Because he knew the family network had lived near these rooms all his life, breathing the same air, pretending proximity was innocence.

"And the terrace team," Damian said. "Are they abstractions too."

That landed. Not fear. Not even panic. Annoyance, sharp and personal, as if Damian had broken etiquette more than secrecy.

"Careful."

"I'm being—"

"You're being twenty." Voss cut across him without raising his voice. "You walked in here with your father's files and his posture and you think that makes you ready for what happens next."

Damian's hand closed on the railing hard enough that the papers crumpled under his thumb. He forced his fingers open. The top page had a wrinkle across Voss's name now.

"Then stop acting like I'm not already here."

Voss studied him. Below them the Westalian deputy minister moved into his second paragraph. Somewhere across the room a camera rotated, found their corner, and stayed.

"You say my name into those microphones," Voss said, voice lowered but not softened, "and the room splits in five seconds. Half of them condemn me because they should. The other half move to contain the damage before the paperwork reaches their own desks." He let that sit. "Your delegation will be in the second group."

Damian waited.

"And you end up being the Desmond who said it in public. Not the whistleblower. The breach."

"I know," Damian said.

The words came out before he had decided to say them. He saw Voss register the honesty and look briefly unsettled by it, as if he had prepared for defiance and not for someone who understood the cost and was going to do it anyway.

He gathered the papers before Voss could decide to take them and turned toward the floor.

The moderator looked annoyed when Damian requested a procedural point. The Ostanian delegation chair looked alarmed before the microphone even came on. Alarm, Damian thought, not surprise. That was useful too.

"State your point," the moderator said.

Damian did not hear his own first words. He saw the room receiving them.

Heads turned before the sentence had finished. The Westalian deputy minister stopped mid-breath. Two photographers near the side aisle lifted their cameras in the same motion, predatory and grateful.

"I am entering documentary evidence," Damian said, voice steadier now that it was already happening, "that Councillor Werner Voss and connected procurement entities inside our own defense apparatus routed funds and materiel to the Renewal Front. I am also stating for the record that those entities overlap with commercial intermediaries tied to Westalian defense firms represented in this building."

Silence took the hall for half a beat.

Then everything arrived at once.

"Desmond, sit down," the Ostanian chair snapped.

"Moderator, this is improper," said the Westalian deputy minister, already halfway to his feet.

"Security," somebody behind Damian hissed. Another voice, farther left, said, "Jesus Christ."

Damian kept going because stopping would only turn the moment into a request.

"If you want the shell names, I have them. If you want signatures, I have those too. If anyone in this room has been using defense procurement to fund the same people this summit was called to address, the documentation is here. It runs in both directions. It has for years."

The Ostanian chair grabbed for Damian's sleeve and missed. "Do you understand what you've handed foreign press."

"Our contractors handed them the access years ago," Damian said. "I'm handing them the receipts."

The moderator called for order. Nobody gave him any. People were already sorting themselves into camps visible only in posture. One row furious that the allegation existed. Another furious it had been public. A third doing arithmetic with names and committees and tomorrow's headlines. Voss stayed where he was in the gallery, broad and still and unreadable except for the single fact that he was not leaving.

Security moved in along the perimeter. A press aide near the east aisle was already whispering into a handset with both eyes fixed on Damian as if he had turned from a person into a usable event.

He felt it then, not triumph and not panic. Exposure. A kind of cold. He had just broken the summit on purpose and still had no proof it would save more than it ruined.

When the moderator finally forced a recess through the microphones, the room obeyed only because it wanted better angles for the argument.

The Ostanian chair caught Damian by the elbow hard enough to bruise.

"What the hell do you think you're doing?"

Damian looked at the hand, then at the older man's face.

"Late," he said. "I think I'm late."

The chair let go as if Damian had become something contagious.

Across the room, three cameras were still pointed at him. One of the operators mouthed his name to a producer and then mouthed it again, testing how it felt with disaster attached.

Loid found Anya's note behind the fire extinguisher at 13:42.

He knew her handwriting before he unfolded it. The impatient tilt. The pressure marks where she bore down too hard on consonants. The crossed sevens she still wrote the way he had taught her at ten because she had once thrown a worksheet at the wall and announced that numbers were fascists.

*South terrace 1400. Gerhardt target. Falk in comms room east corridor.*

He read it twice, then once more for anything hidden in the slant or the spacing. There was nothing beyond urgency. Anya only embedded extra meaning when she had time. She did not have time.

It was Falk.

The name dropped through him cleanly and took other memories with it. Gerald Falk at WISE briefings, always carrying too much paper. Gerald Falk standing in the Forger kitchen with a dish towel over his shoulder because Yor had apologized for breaking a wineglass and he had started drying the rest before she could get more embarrassed. Gerald Falk handing Anya a fountain pen in a velvet case and telling her serious people used refillable instruments because disposable ones made cowards of the hand.

Loid folded the note once and moved.

The east service corridor ran the length of the venue in unforgiving fluorescent light, concrete underfoot, cleaning solution cut with old wiring and burnt coffee. His cover credentials got him through the first checkpoint. The second he took on timing, stepping through during a shift change he had memorized the night before.

The Communications Room sat at the far end with no window, one heavy door, and radio static leaking around the frame.

He tried the handle.

It was unlocked.

Gerald Falk sat at the main console with a headset around his neck and both hands on the keyboard. Three wall screens showed internal cameras, security doors, and delegation movement tags crawling in color-coded blocks across the building. The center console held venue communications, emergency routing, and a mirrored schedule for the platform speakers. Falk had not just found himself near the operation. He was sitting at its circulatory system.

For one second Loid stayed in the doorway and watched the ordinariness of him: the slight stoop, the neat part in the thinning hair, the dry skin at the knuckles from office soap. That ordinariness had protected him for years. It had also protected everyone who trusted him.

"Gerald."

Falk stopped typing. He did not turn around immediately. His shoulders rose once, fell once, then settled.

"Loid," he said. "You found me fast."

"Terrace team."

Falk looked at the lower monitor and then at the wall clock above it.

"In position by thirteen fifty," he said. "Shot window at fourteen hundred if the room behaves."

Loid saw the south-terrace camera tile in the corner of the wall feed. Two men in civilian jackets crossed into frame and vanished under the overhang. Another paused at the stairs and checked his watch.

He could rip the main cable right now. He could black out the room in two seconds.

He did not.

Killing the board blind might solve one visible problem and shove the rest into contingencies he could not see. Anya's note had not mentioned only the terrace. It had named Falk in the comms room. If Falk was this deep in the plumbing, he had built himself options. Loid wanted them live long enough to watch where they opened.

He opened the door wider without taking his eyes off Falk and coughed twice into the corridor, sharp and deliberate.

Yor was covering the foyer junction for the exact reason that it gave her the shortest line to the south approach. She would hear it if she was anywhere near where she was supposed to be.

Loid shut the door.

"Hands where I can see them," he said.

Falk lifted both hands from the keyboard and rested them on the desk. Compliant, almost patient. That patience made Loid want to break something.

"Abort code," Loid said.

"There isn't one."

"Names, then."

Falk turned his chair halfway. His glasses had slid a little down his nose. He pushed them back up with one finger, buying composure he mostly already had.

"Which names?" He said it like the start of a briefing. "The terrace team? The funding channels? The ministry contacts who looked the other way?"

"It breaks down into whether Gerhardt leaves this building alive."

"Gerhardt is one function."

"Don't do that."

Something old and rigid moved behind Falk's face. It wasn't panic but conviction, which was worse. Loid had expected greed or vanity or some softer corruption with enough room inside it for contempt. Conviction kept men upright while they killed strangers and called themselves principled for it.

"You know what I watched happen in those rooms, Loid," Falk said. "Every assessment bent until it fit a peace nobody was allowed to criticize. Every ugly number sanded smooth because stability photographed better."

Loid took two steps into the room, enough to reach the side console if he had to, not enough to lose the door.

"I questioned plenty."

"And still served it."

"I didn't feed target coordinates to men with guns."

"No." Falk's mouth tightened. "You just made it run smoothly enough that everyone else went home and didn't think about it."

On the wall feed, the camera angle from the assembly floor cut briefly to the West Gallery. Damian was moving.

Loid saw it and adjusted his math. If Damian had started early, then the summit was about to break along more than one seam at once.

"Step away from the console."

Falk stood. He moved slowly, which was almost more insulting than refusal.

Loid took the headset from the desk with his left hand and kept his right free. The channel carried venue security, thin with static and bureaucracy.

"West Gallery movement," someone said. "Stand by."

Good, Loid thought. Let the room keep talking.

Falk watched the headset settle against Loid's ear.

"You aren't killing the board," he said.

"Not yet."

"Because you know I built backups."

"Because I'd rather see them activate than guess where you buried them."

That got him the faintest flicker in Falk's expression, maybe respect or maybe the disappointment of a man who had hoped to keep one advantage a little longer.

"How long?" Loid said. "How long have you been passing intelligence?"

Falk took his glasses off and cleaned them on his shirt, a gesture so ordinary the room felt wrong around it.

"Twenty years," he said, and put the glasses back on.

Loid absorbed the answer and found that he had no room for surprise left. Twenty years crossed administrations, operations, funerals, birthdays, entire internal generations of analysts who had built careers on floors Falk walked every day. Twenty years meant Crane. It meant other people too, people Loid probably still thought of as accidents or attrition because a bureaucracy had filed them that way.

He did not let that thought finish. He could not afford the time.

"Who's on the terrace?"

"I don't have all the names. Cells don't — it's compartmented, Loid. You've run compartmented ops."

"I've never run one that ends with someone shot at a podium."

Falk's eyes went to the wall clock and came back.

"Crane," Loid said. "Was he one function too?"

For the first time Falk looked away.

Agent Crane's death had ripped through WISE three months ago with no satisfying shape to hang on it. The paperwork had called it checkpoint compromise and bad luck, a line item in a dirty border operation that everybody hated and nobody could disprove. Crane had been forty-one. He sent office photos of the same stupid dog every week and laughed before his own punch lines landed. Loid had been the one to tell Anya not to attend the memorial because the service would be mostly analysts and institutional grief, and she had gone very still in the kitchen before saying okay.

The headset crackled.

"Floor microphone live," said a security voice. "Who authorized that?"

Then Damian's voice came thin through a speaker somewhere beyond the wall, not through the headset but through the building itself, amplified into the corridors.

"I am entering documentary evidence..."

Falk smiled with one side of his mouth.

"Interesting timing."

Loid did not look at the wall speaker. He looked at Falk and listened to Damian blow open the summit one floor away. The words came muffled through concrete but clear enough to identify names: Voss, procurement, Renewal Front.

The room's pressure changed instantly. On the wall feed security started moving at the perimeter. The south-terrace tile showed one of the civilian-jacket men pausing at the door, hand to his earpiece. He was waiting for the room below to go noisy, for confusion to make the shot easier to write afterward.

"Your team is about to lose its cover," Loid said. "Call them off."

"They don't need cover. They need noise."

"You don't get a war back by murdering a deputy minister in front of cameras."

"No," Falk said. "But after today nobody gets to sit in this building and call it peace."

Loid felt the impulse to drive him through the console like an electrical surge. He did not move. Violence would have been satisfying and operationally stupid.

On the wall feed the south-terrace camera jolted. A figure crossed too fast for an ordinary guard. Then another. Then the image shook hard enough to smear.

The first shot came through the building as a crack of breaking glass and an alarmed burst on the security channel.

"South side, south side, shots fired, shots fired."

Yor, Loid thought, with cold certainty.

He let one beat pass. Two. Enough for the channel to fill.

"One hostile down."

"No, four possible, four possible."

"Medic to terrace now."

"Who engaged?"

Nobody answered that question. Then somebody screamed into the radio about blood and another voice shouted for the south camera again.

Gerhardt was still alive. Loid let himself know it for half a second and then took it back because the room was not done costing them.

Falk had gone very still.

"Your wife?" he asked.

Loid did not answer.

Falk looked at the feed where the south camera had gone to static and something moved behind his face, half awe, half nausea.

"She's something," he said, almost to himself.

Loid crossed the room so fast Falk barely had time to flinch before the back of the chair hit the console. Not a punch. Not yet. One forearm across Falk's upper chest, enough pressure to interrupt breath without doing damage he could not justify later.

"Leave my family out of your mouth."

Falk swallowed against the pressure. "I can't."

Loid held him there another second, then let him breathe and stepped back. The near loss of control tasted metallic. He could not afford more of it. He forced himself to look at the screens.

On the floor feed Damian was still on camera, now half turned toward the Ostanian chair. Press had clustered. Security was entering the aisles. The summit had cracked, but not cleanly. Nothing about today would be clean.

"What does that mean?" Loid said. "You can't."

Falk adjusted his collar with shaking fingers. It was not fear this time, only damaged breath.

"It means your daughter wrote the blind spot."

Loid's body took the sentence before his mind did. Something locked through his shoulders and stayed there.

"Explain."

Falk seemed to understand, even then, that he finally had Loid's full attention in a way ideology had not managed.

"Two months ago," he said. "Her first assessment on the Ostanian operative she was tracking. It was too neat, too certain. She cut the threat profile, shifted analytical attention away from the sectors we were already using, and wrote it in a voice senior reviewers trust. It wasn't a reckless lie. It was a competent one."

The competence hurt more than the accusation.

Loid knew exactly how a report like that survived. He had taught her. Strip out the one fact that made the whole thing dangerous. Leave the supporting texture intact. Be specific where detail reassures and certain where uncertainty invites a second read. He had spent years turning tradecraft into household instruction because he told himself he was making her safer.

"What did you do?" he said.

"I read it."

"I know you read it."

"I used it." Falk's voice roughened. "I rerouted transmissions through the blind channels she created. Traffic the building stopped prioritizing because your daughter's judgment told it to look elsewhere. The packet that put Crane at that checkpoint went through one of those channels."

Loid did not move.

Crane's dog photo flashed through his head with obscene clarity. Mud on the snout. One ear inside out. The caption had been *heroic counterintelligence support asset declines bath*. Loid had replied with nothing because he had been in field transit and meant to send something later. Later had become memorial flowers on a desk.

Falk watched him take it.

"You don't know why she wrote it," Loid said at last.

"No."

"And you still used it."

"What was I supposed to do, flag it?" Falk's voice went thin. "Report it to the same building that filed it as excellent analysis? Tell Sylvia her best junior operative wrote a blind spot and watch the whole desk pretend to be surprised?"

Loid looked at him and saw a man who had turned his own helplessness into permission twenty years ago and never looked back.

The lower corner of the main console flashed amber.

An outbound queue spiked.

Loid saw it before Falk could hide the look. A new transfer path had opened under the emergency response traffic, copying cached delegation positions, internal security notes, and temporary room assignments into a blind route tagged only with an internal rack designation. It wasn't the terrace operation. It was aftermath, salvage, damage that could keep moving after the shooting failed.

"What's that?"

Falk's eyes flicked toward the lower rack for less than a second. That was enough.

"Nothing you'll stop in time."

At 14:06 the queue bar started climbing.

Loid ripped the main transmission cable out of the console.

The central screens died. Radio static cut to black. Something lower on the east rack clicked once in the sudden hush, hidden behind a booster housing and the nest of cables below it, but Loid already had Falk's wrist in one hand and his other arm twisted up behind his back. Falk struggled once, poorly, more from outrage than strength.

Loid drove him to the floor, stripped off his own belt, and cinched Falk's wrists hard enough to keep the hands useless.

"Should've pulled it sooner," Falk said into the carpet, breath ragged now. "You always wait too long. Think watching is the same as controlling."

Beyond the wall the summit kept breaking apart into evacuation orders, raised voices, and a fire alarm kicking in somewhere too late to matter. Damian's surname surfaced again and again through the noise like a thrown object.

Loid sat back on his heels for one second because standing too soon felt less safe than remaining armed over the man on the floor.

He looked at the dead console, at the dark wall screens, at Falk tied with his own belt, and knew with a precision that made him cold that the room still had not finished telling him what it cost.

Yor was near the Main Foyer when Loid's signal cut through the open space.

Two sharp coughs from the east corridor. Deliberate. Not nerves. Not a man clearing his throat.

It was Loid.

She changed direction at once.

If he was signaling from the east corridor instead of coming himself, then he had found Falk and could not leave him. That made the south approach hers. The conclusion arrived without emotion because there was no time yet for emotion.

She crossed the foyer in measured steps, dark blazer smooth over a cream blouse, credential visible, one hand free. She had left the Garden's favorite tools at home on purpose. One blade rode the thigh holster under her skirt. Another sat in her boot. That would be enough if the room stayed honest about numbers.

Through the glass doors to the South Terrace she saw four men in civilian clothes moving with military economy and trying to hide it from people who were not looking hard. Two were already drawing sidearms. A third was taking position at the sightline to the assembly hall windows. The fourth had a radio in his hand and his weight angled toward the exterior stairs.

There were too many.

Gerhardt was due to stand any second.

Yor pushed through the doors.

The first shooter's round hit the frame beside her head and burst glass across the tile. The noise flattened the space for a split second. After that everything turned clean.

She crossed the distance before he corrected his aim, caught his wrist in both hands, and forced the muzzle high. Bone shifted. His finger spasmed on the trigger and sent the next shot harmlessly into the sky. Yor drove the heel of her hand into the front of his throat with all her weight behind it. Cartilage folded. He dropped, hands clamped to his neck, making one wet torn sound before his head struck stone.

The second man pivoted toward her. He was slower than the first because he had seen what she was now and his body had to waste a fraction of a second on disbelief. Yor went under the barrel, drew the blade from her boot, and cut across the side of his neck so close to the jaw that the blood came first as spray and only afterward as flow. He got one hand to the wound, stared at the red pouring through his fingers, and folded to both knees before the rest of him followed.

The third was already at the railing trying to recover the original sightline to the hall. Yor threw the blade. It hit above the collarbone and went deep enough to knock him back into the metal. He made a surprised coughing noise that turned instantly liquid. By the time the gun left his hand and struck the flagstones, his knees had started to fail.

That made three.

The fourth broke for the stairs shouting into the radio.

Yor caught him halfway down, one hand in his coat, and drove him face-first into the stone lip hard enough to burst his mouth open. Teeth hit the step and bounced white once before the blood covered them. He tried to turn. She took the side of his head in both hands and twisted. The neck went with a dry snap she felt through her wrists.

Then it was over.

It was not quiet. Not really. The hall beyond the glass was already screaming itself into procedure. Alarms had started. Somewhere inside, someone was shouting for medics through a bullhorn. But the terrace itself had gone still in the post-violence way, four bodies dropped where intention had ended, blood moving downhill over flagstone joints and into the clipped grass border.

Yor stood in the middle of it and let her breathing come back under control.

Her hands shook once, a fine involuntary tremor. Then they stopped.

Someone was in the doorway to the west service corridor.

Yuri.

He had his sidearm half drawn. His gaze moved from the body by the doors to the one under the railing, then to the shattered mouth on the stairs, then finally to Yor with blood across her sleeve and one empty hand still held the way it had been a second before the killing stopped.

"Sis," he said.

He knew.

He wasn't guessing or worrying his way toward it. The recognition hit his face with the force of a physical blow. He was not seeing a stranger who happened to resemble his sister. He was seeing the exact body he had grown up trusting do something that belonged to an entirely different life.

Yor swallowed. Her mouth had gone dry in the stupidest possible way. "Yuri. Call it in."

His eyes went to the security camera mounted over the corner of the building, its red light steady and indifferent.

"How long?" he said.

She understood the question and hated it. How long had she done this. How long had he failed to see it. How long had their whole family lived beside one another inside different names for duty.

Glass grated under her shoe when she shifted her weight. She did not answer because there was no answer that fit inside the twenty seconds before response teams hit the terrace.

Yuri holstered his weapon with visible effort and thumbed his radio live.

"South Terrace," he said. Professional voice. Flat enough to carry. "Four hostiles down. Send medical and response."

The dispatcher came back immediately. Yuri kept his eyes on the camera, not on Yor.

"What engaged them?" the dispatcher asked.

Yuri's jaw worked once.

"Unknown Westalian security asset," he said.

The lie came out in his own voice and cost him enough that Yor could hear the price.

She took one step toward him. "Yuri..."

"Go inside," he said.

It was not loud. That made it worse.

"Before the team gets here."

"Yuri."

He still did not look at her. "Please."

The word cut cleaner than the radio lie had.

Yor nodded once and went back through the broken doors.

Boots hammered the stairs less than twenty seconds later. Yuri moved only when they were almost on him. By then his face had returned to the usable shape the SSS trusted. It was not the same as composure. It was a uniform.

"Who engaged?" the first response officer demanded.

Yuri looked at the blood, the bodies, the camera, and the place his sister had just vacated.

"Unknown Westalian security asset," he said again.

This time he had to live inside it.

At 14:07 Anya caught the thought she should have treated like a blade the first time.

The hall had disintegrated into managed panic. The moderator was trying to turn recess into evacuation. Press were already speaking into handsets with their bodies half turned toward the exits. Security minds flooded the room in blunt urgent bursts that hurt almost as much as the gunfire fragments still ricocheting through the crowd memory.

*camera six locked south side, hold that feed...*

*Desmond just detonated his own career...*

*no, he just saved the minister, don't be stupid...*

*green light. good. lower east is still moving. booster's hiding it...*

Anya froze.

Not because the thought was complete. It wasn't. It was one passing surface flare from a man three rows behind her who had already started thinking about his exit route and whether the south stairwell was still usable. But the fragment hit the earlier one in the corridor and locked the two pieces together: east comms, rack three bought twelve if the board dropped, and the lower east channel was still moving with the booster hiding it.

She got up so fast her chair legs scraped.

The headache that followed had teeth. The room tilted left, then right. She grabbed the back of the seat in front of her until the wave passed and pushed toward the side stairs with one hand pressed hard against her temple.

If she stayed, she could keep reading the room and maybe learn what the leak was carrying. If she left, Damian's thread vanished to her and the summit became four hundred muffled radios she could not sort. The decision still was not clean. She made it anyway.

The east corridor lights stabbed at her eyes. Every footfall jarred her teeth. She passed the fire exit where she had left the note. She passed two security officers moving the other way and caught *where the hell is east comms support* from one of them before she hit the Communications Room door hard enough to send it banging into the wall.

Loid was inside, shoulder near the doorway. Falk sat on the floor against the far wall with his wrists cinched behind him by Loid's belt. The main screens were dead.

"Lower east rack," Anya said. Her voice came out thin and wrong. "Something's still live. He kept thinking green light and the booster."

Loid did not ask who *he* was. He moved at once.

The room looked smaller with the main board dead, all dark screens, residual heat from the electronics, and the smell of hot dust and cable insulation pressing close. Falk turned his head to watch them, expression unreadable except for the fact that he was suddenly more awake than a bound man had any right to be.

Loid dropped to one knee in front of the lower rack. Anya went down beside him because standing felt impossible and because if she left the search to sight alone the room would win.

Metal faceplates and bundled cables crowded the rack. Low on it sat a dull gray signal booster, rectangular and innocent-looking, with more empty-looking dead space behind it than the casing should have allowed.

*good, green light*

She reached toward the booster and stopped herself. Her hand was shaking.

"There," she said. "Behind it. Maybe."

Loid took the mounting screws with two fingers and wrenched the booster housing sideways hard enough to bend the bracket.

A smaller device sat recessed in the dark space behind it, no larger than a paperback, one green indicator burning steadily on its face.

Falk closed his eyes.

Loid tore the power lead free.

The green light died.

For a second nobody in the room said anything. The silence after a correct guess never felt like relief. It felt like standing where the next bad fact would land.

Then Loid found the tiny status strip along the relay edge and tipped it toward the ceiling light.

`BUFFER REMAINING 10:48`

Twelve minutes total. Just over a minute spent since the main board died.

The arithmetic held. That made it worse, not better.

"What was it carrying?" Loid said.

Falk opened his eyes again. "Enough."

Loid looked as if he might put Falk through the wall after all. Instead he set the dead relay on the desk with such care that the restraint became its own kind of violence.

Anya was still on the floor. Her knees had gone unreliable. The relay's green light had burned itself onto the back of her eyes. She kept seeing it every time she blinked.

Most of the buffer was left. Most of the damage was not yet spent.

Falk's thoughts were not quiet enough to keep out, even with her skull trying to close around the noise.

*too neat, should've known she'd hear the rest sooner or later...*

*the girl writes like him*

That one was worse than the spoken accusation had been.

Anya looked at Loid.

He was already looking at her.

His face had gone into the blank, controlled arrangement he used when the real reaction was too expensive to show in public. She knew that face from missions, debriefs, hospitals, the three worst days of her life. It was not calm. It was cost accounting.

He knew now. Not just that Falk had exploited the report. He knew why it had worked, what shape of lie had gotten through review, whose lessons were inside it.

Anya opened her mouth.

"I can explain" was too small. "I didn't mean for Crane" was worse. "Damian" was a different disaster. Every available sentence felt stupid before it had even finished forming.

"Not here," Loid said.

He did not raise his voice. He did not soften it either.

Boots sounded in the corridor. Someone shouted for east comms. Loid moved to the door, checked the hall, then came back long enough to haul Falk upright by the arm and march him out ahead of the response team.

The next minutes blurred. Security officers flooded the room. Loid handed Falk over without explaining more than rank required. Anya stood because standing was simpler than admitting she might fall if she stayed down. Nobody asked her a direct question, which felt temporary in the most dangerous way possible.

When she finally got clear of the room and the corridor and the building's bright official interior, the cold outside hit her like a second concussion.

She made it to the stone wall beside the service exit and braced one hand against it.

The summit was still emptying by the front steps. Sirens climbed the street. Camera crews crowded the barricades in hard bright clusters. Every mind within range was doing the same frantic sorting: shots fired, summit blown open, Desmond, Voss, minister safe, who gets blamed, who gets buried, who gets quoted.

Anya bent her head and tried not to be sick.

Crane.

She had spent months not thinking his name all the way through. Thinking around it. Thinking *checkpoint* or *that border operation* or *the memorial Loid said I didn't have to attend*. Now Falk had taken the edges off and handed her the whole thing.

She had written the report because Damian was useful and frightened and not what WISE thought he was. She had written it because she wanted room to move. She had written it because the first lie had worked and competence made the second lie easier. After that the reasons had piled up until they looked like structure.

None of them put Crane back anywhere.

Loid came out a few minutes later with his belt still missing and her folded note in one hand. He stopped beside her, not close enough to touch.

"Can you walk," he asked.

She nodded.

He looked at her for a long second, the way he looked at doors before deciding whether they were safe to open.

"Good," he said. "You're with me until we're home."

"Dad, I..."

"Not here." His voice stayed level. "And not in pieces. All of it."

That was worse than if he had shouted.

He folded the note once more and slid it into his pocket. His hand lifted as if he meant to steady her shoulder, stopped before contact, and dropped again.

Out by the barricades a producer was already saying Damian's surname into a microphone while the cameraman beside him thought *traitor* and the woman on his other side thought *hero* and none of them sounded certain even in their own heads. Above them, somewhere out of sight, camera six still held whatever it had recorded on the south doors. Falk was in custody. Voss was exposed without being simplified. Yuri was living inside a lie he had chosen.

Loid turned toward the street. Anya fell into step with him automatically.

He kept her close enough to stop if she drifted and far enough that their sleeves never touched.
