# Chapter 16

Loid's unfinished questions were waiting back at the apartment, and she was going to answer them. Sylvia had not called her in yet, which meant she would. The summit had ended yesterday. Falk was in custody. Damian had gone into the summit already knowing her real name, and if Anya left the telepathy hidden one more day then she was just adding fresh rot to old rot. Damian had to come first.

She stood outside his door for four minutes before she knocked.

Four minutes gave her three neighbors through plaster and pipes: the woman downstairs arguing with herself about whether to call her sister, the man two doors down rewriting a complaint about the hallway lights until the wording got nasty, somebody above coughing wetly into a sink. It was normal apartment stuff from normal-size brains. Anya put her palm on Damian's door, caught the low hum of him on the other side, and jerked back before the thought shaped itself.

*Not like this. Not before the knock.*

She knocked.

The lock turned after one exact pause. Surprise hadn't done that. Decision had.

When the door opened, Damian was wearing a dark sweater with the sleeves shoved up. He looked like he'd been sitting still too long.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

*She's here. Okay.*

That one came through simple and flat, like he'd set it on the table between them.

He moved aside. She went in.

His apartment was neat in the boring, lived-in way. Books were lined up straight. One glass was drying beside the sink. Policy journals were stacked on the side table with a pen clipped to the top one. The couch still had the dent from his usual spot. One shoe lay half under it and the other had been left by the door. Last time she'd been here she read the place like a floor plan, exits and corners and signal strength. Tonight it just looked like where he took off his shoes and forgot about them, and where he'd spent an hour failing to do anything that counted as work.

The childhood box sat open on the coffee table.

Old school shoes lay on top, and a class photo sat underneath. Beside the box lay a ministry notepad with one sentence started and then gouged out so hard the paper had torn.

Anya stopped.

It wasn't because of the photo. That part wasn't new. Since Becky's gala she'd heard the schoolyard flashing through him in pieces: pink hair, Becky crying, a six-year-old punch, the almost-name catching and slipping away. The bad part was the box open right now. He had been in there tonight.

He didn't offer a drink. Neither of them sat.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

*Yeah. I figured.*

His thoughts were already crowding around the wrong guesses: that the alliance was over, that there was new fallout from the summit, that she was leaving the city. None of it was close.

"Okay."

He leaned against the counter and crossed his arms. It might have passed for casual if you didn't look at the shoulders.

"It's not about the operation," Anya said. "It's about me. About what I can do."

She had rehearsed this in the bathroom mirror that morning with toothpaste on her lip. She'd rehearsed it on the walk over too. Every version sounded stupid. There was a clinical one, a dramatic one, and a tiny one. *I can kind of hear people.* That one made her want to throw herself into traffic.

"I'm a telepath," she said. "I read minds. I've been doing it since I was little. Four, maybe five, I don't know. I've never told anybody. Not my parents, not Becky, not WISE. Nobody."

Silence hit the room hard enough to feel.

*What.*

Then, a beat later:

*She said she reads minds.*

He drew one careful breath that barely moved his shoulders. Thoughts started firing past in broken pieces: the state dinner, the service corridor, Bretz, the safe house, every time she'd answered too fast, every time she'd known something she shouldn't.

*She read my thoughts. All this time.*

"At the state dinner," he said. "That first night. You were reading my thoughts then too."

"Yes."

"Everything in that room. About the operation. About..."

The rest flashed before he said it: her smile, Eden Academy, a pink-haired girl.

"Yes," Anya said again.

His face changed, not because of the confession but because she'd answered the thought.

"Stop doing that." Sharper now. "Can you stop?"

"I can stop reacting. I can't stop hearing it. It's like sound."

He pushed off the counter and crossed to the sink, then back again, as if distance might blunt it. It didn't. The thoughts kept arriving from the same radius, only sharper now because he was listening to himself listening.

*Chair. Faucet. Window latch. Don't think about the safe house. Don't think about anything with a face.*

Anya looked at the floor.

"You can hear me trying not to think about it."

"Yes."

He squeezed the bridge of his nose until the skin blanched. "Jesus Christ."

*Every thought. God. The negotiations. The club. The park. The safe house.*

He caught the edge of the counter hard enough to click the glass beside the sink. Then he let it go and scrubbed a hand over the back of his neck. He had nowhere to put his hands. He had nowhere to put any of it.

*The kiss.*

That thought came up jagged and hot.

*The safe house. She was in my head when I kissed her.*

"Yes," Anya said quietly.

Anger arrived all at once. It wasn't loud, which made it worse. Every word came out too clear, clipped into the politeness he used when he was closest to fury.

"So every conversation we've had. Every time I thought I was telling you something because I chose to tell you. You already had it."

"Yes."

"You used it on purpose."

The answer had to come fast or it would sound like she was shopping for one.

"Yes."

His mouth flattened.

"Say it properly."

Anya swallowed.

"I answered questions before you finished asking them. I changed what I was going to say because I already knew the answer sitting on top of you. Sometimes I stayed because I heard you wanted me there. Sometimes I left because I heard you were about to ask for something I couldn't take cleanly."

He stared at her.

"How many times is sometimes."

"Enough that I can't make it small."

His gaze jerked away again, like the sight of her made the fact worse.

"The safe house. My father. My mother. Things I said because I was trying to be honest."

"I already knew."

He looked away fast, like looking at her hurt. Two steps took him to the window. Both hands braced on the sill.

"My mother," he said to the glass. "At the reception."

"The marigolds on the terrace," Anya said. "Before she got sick."

His breath hitched out of him.

*I never said that out loud.*

Outside, lights kept turning on across Berlint like nothing in this room mattered to anybody else.

Anya let him have the anger. There was no excuse to offer, no speech about not choosing the ability, no little pile of true things that would still be useless here.

"How far does it go?" he asked.

"Surface thoughts. What's on top. If you're remembering something, I get that too because you're thinking it right then. But I can't go digging. I can't pull up things you aren't having."

"Just the surface."

*As if the surface isn't the part that counts.*

He wasn't wrong.

"And it's not truth exactly," Anya said. "That's part of why it's such a mess. Surface thoughts lie all the time. People rehearse things, panic about things, imagine things. I get access. I don't get God."

He turned from the window enough to look at her.

"That's not better."

"I know."

"It's worse in a different way."

"Yeah."

His hand dragged down over his mouth.

"Does anybody else know?"

"No."

"Nobody."

"Nobody."

That landed differently. It didn't ease anything. It made the room stranger.

*I'm the first.*

Anya kept her face still. She had expected fury, revulsion, even a door in her face. She had not expected to hand him the ugliest thing she owned and realize he was the first person who had ever actually touched it.

"I'm sorry," she said. "And I'm not going to do the thing where I tell you not to be angry. Be angry."

"Don't manage it for me."

"Okay."

*Good.* A beat. *No. Not good. I don't know what I want.*

The next wave came through messier. It wasn't clean anger now. It was sick realization: every ugly little private thought handed over without consent, every soft one too.

Then, under all of it:

*She knows the bad parts and she still came here.*

Anya kept her face still. For once she let the thought stay where it belonged, even though she'd heard it.

"I don't know what to do with this," Damian said. His eyes were red around the edges. "I genuinely don't know what to do with the idea that every private thought I've had in front of you was..."

He stopped.

His gaze had dropped to the coffee table, to the box, to the photo half hidden under the shoes.

*Tell her.*

The thought came through clean as a bell.

He crossed the room, pulled out the class photo, and held it up.

"Second row," he said. "Third from the left. Pink hair. That's you."

Anya looked.

She was six years old, mouth open in a grin at something off camera, one hand blurred from waving. Damian was in the front row already looking annoyed at the photographer, tiny and severe in exactly the way he'd been born to be.

"I know who you are," he said. "Anya Forger. Eden Academy, Class Three. You punched me when we were six because I called Becky a crybaby. I've known since the gala."

The shock wasn't *who*. It was *when*.

It was November, this apartment, the photo under his lamp, months stacked behind his voice.

"The gala," she said. "I knew you were close then. I heard that part. I didn't know you went home and made it certain."

"A classmate said your name. I came back here, found this, checked. It was you."

*She caught the circling. Not this room. Not the part where it became a fact.*

That hit harder than the school photo: his desk lamp, this table, his hands on the picture. She hadn't been here.

"At my parents' apartment," Anya said. "When you met my dad. At the summit yesterday. All that time."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

Damian took longer with the next breath.

"Because the operation still mattered. Because saying it in November would've blown everything sideways. And because I kept taking one more day instead. Then another."

He looked back at the photo, not at her.

"That's not noble. It's just what I did."

The notepad with the torn line sat between them. One more day sat there too, written into the paper until it failed.

"And the summit," Anya said. "The badge. East Gallery."

His head came up.

"What about it."

"You moved me there before plenary. Better sight lines, separate entrance, all that. I knew you were keeping me off something. I never knew what."

The pause this time was answer enough before he gave the words.

"The main press intake was under ministry screening after Kreuzfeld. Security was cross-checking credentials for anyone near the central galleries. One of my father's aides was sitting in that room, and he knew Eden rosters well enough to be dangerous. If you'd gone through the main line wearing borrowed papers, somebody had a real chance of putting the cultural attaché and Anya Forger in the same file before the afternoon session. East Gallery used the foreign overflow desk and a side stair. Less scrutiny. No Desmond staff. I kept you off that screening line."

The room seemed to tip a little.

"Because you knew."

"Because I knew."

"You were protecting my cover."

"I was trying to keep it from getting worse."

There was no clean version of that. He knew it. She knew it.

"Why not tell me then," Anya asked, "if we were already standing in the same fire."

"Because if I said it there, in the middle of the summit, we would've had this conversation with half the ministry still watching the doors. And because..." He stopped, the correction visible before it happened. His eyes stayed on the photo, and his thumb worked harder over the paper edge. "Because I didn't want to lose you."

*Both. But mostly that second thing.*

Air went out of Anya in one rough push. Her knees gave up before the rest of her did. She sat on the floor beside the table because that was where her body had decided she belonged.

"So we both did it," she said. "Hidden information. Kept going."

Damian lowered himself onto the couch instead of staying over her. That was good. The distance helped.

"Not the same thing," he said.

"No. What I did is worse."

"Maybe."

"No. It is."

He didn't answer. The photo stayed in his hands. Two children stared up from glossy paper while the adults who used to be them failed to say anything useful.

"When you came to my parents' apartment," Anya said after a while, "what did you think you were doing."

His laugh was short and dead on arrival.

"Trying not to stare at your family photos. Trying not to act like I'd shown up at someone's dinner table carrying a second conversation nobody else could hear."

That made her chest hurt in a new place.

"You could've walked."

"Yes."

"You didn't."

"No."

His thumb moved over the edge of the photo without touching either of the children on it.

"I kept waiting for the girl from school and the woman from the ministry to separate into two different people. They didn't. That did not make any of this easier."

Anya folded her arms around her knees.

"No."

That was the nearest they got to agreement before the room ran out of first-hour words.

---

The second hour went quieter.

At some point Damian had put the kettle on, forgotten it, and done it again. One mug of tea went cold beside the sink. Her water glass sat untouched and sweating on the table. Damian was back in the dent on the couch. Anya had climbed up to the far cushion with her knees tucked under her chin. The box stayed open between them.

The anger was still there. She could feel it the way you feel a bruise through clothes, not every second but enough.

*She told me. Nobody dragged it out of her.*

"Can I ask something?" he said.

"Yeah."

"When you say it's like sound, what does that actually mean."

Anya pressed her thumbnail into the seam of the cushion.

"If it's one person and they're close, it's pretty clear. Not every word. More like... I get the top layer with whatever else is attached to it. Images sometimes. Pressure. If it's a crowd, it smears. On a tram in rush hour it's like six radios in a sack all tuned to different stations. In restaurants you can usually pick a table if you try. Political rooms are awful because everyone thinks in bullet points and panic."

Something almost happened at the corner of his mouth. It wasn't a smile, just a reflex trying and failing.

"Political rooms think in bullet points," he said.

"They do. And people who think they're hiding something are louder than they mean to be."

"How far."

"Next room, easy, if I know who's there. Across a street sometimes if somebody's wound up enough. After that it drops off. Distance helps. Walls help less than you'd hope."

"Sleep."

"Quieter. Mostly. Dreams leak weird. Drunk people are loud. Hospitals are worse. Everybody's afraid and fluorescent lighting somehow makes it meaner."

He looked toward the hall.

"So when I said I needed to think somewhere you couldn't hear me..."

"The stairwell helps. Outside helps more. A car definitely."

"Good."

He sat with that for a beat.

"You built rules for it."

"Had to."

"What kind."

Anya let out a breath through her nose.

"Don't answer thoughts unless I want to set the room on fire. Don't quote exact words back. Don't stay in crowds after it goes white at the edges. Don't get drunk in public. Don't put my back to a wall in a packed place because then I start trying to hear everybody at once and that gets ugly fast."

"And with people you care about."

There it was.

"Stay back enough that it doesn't matter," she said.

"That seems relevant."

"Yeah."

"Did it work."

"Until you."

The silence after that had a different shape, with less impact and more ache.

Damian reached for his glass, changed his mind, and reached again. His fingers tightened around it.

"Did you tell me now because you thought I was about to find out anyway."

"No."

"Then why now."

She could have said because Loid knew too much already, because the summit had blown half the cover structures in the city sideways, because one lie kept breeding new ones whenever she fed it. All of it was true. None of it came first.

"Because after the gala you knew my name and still didn't say it. After East Gallery you were protecting a cover I hadn't even admitted was mine. At my parents' place you sat there carrying something that should've been in the room. Yesterday you walked into the summit with me still taking from you." She rubbed her thumb over the seam until it hurt. "There wasn't going to be a clean day for it. There was just going to be a later uglier one."

He looked down at the childhood box again.

"You thought I was owed it."

"Yes."

"Even if it ended things."

"Especially then."

That thought hit him hard enough to flare bright through the rest.

*She came here to lose me if that's what it cost.*

Anya stared at the water glass. The cold had made a ring on the table.

"I hate that I understand that," he said.

"Yeah."

"I also hate that I understand why you waited."

"Yeah."

The anger shifted again. It wasn't gone, just reorganized.

"Can I ask the ugly question now," Damian said, "or are we already past quota."

"We're way past quota. Go ahead."

That earned the tiniest exhale from him. It still wasn't a laugh, but it was closer.

"When you read someone, do you just get words."

"Not just words. Mostly words, or scraps of words. But feelings come with them when they're strong. Not sentences. More like pressure. Weather. If somebody's furious, the room goes sharp. If somebody's scared, it's loud."

"And mine?"

There it was.

Anya pressed her thumbnail harder.

"This is the part I came here to say and I still don't know how to say it right." She took a breath. "In the safe house, when we kissed, I was reading you. I was trying not to, but you were too close and feeling too much, and I just..." She stopped. "I could feel what you wanted. I didn't have to guess. I could feel it. And my body answered before I'd figured out whether I was answering you or me."

He didn't move.

"Afterward you went to wash the blood off your hands. I stayed on the floor and kept shaking even after you left the room. That part's real. The part after is where I don't know, where it gets..." She started over. "I don't know how much of what I feel for you started as yours. Your feelings hitting me so hard I just carried them home."

"Meaning what."

"Meaning maybe I wanted you because you wanted me and the signal was loud enough that I couldn't tell which one lit first." Her mouth had gone dry. "That's not... I'm not saying none of it's mine. I'm saying I can't sort it. And I'm scared of that."

Nothing in his face changed, but his thoughts tightened.

*Not the telepathy. This.*

"You think you might have kissed me because I wanted it badly enough for both of us."

"I don't know."

*In that room by myself.*

His hand found the glass, tightened, and let go before something gave.

"Have you had that with anyone else," he asked.

"No."

"Never."

"I never let anybody get close enough for it to matter like this. Strong feelings leak through. That's always been true. Crowds are hell because of it, and dating was..." She made a face. "A stupid idea. People are manageable across a table. They're not manageable when they're right there and wanting things."

He looked at her properly for the first time in several minutes.

"So the answer was to stay alone."

"Mostly, yeah."

"Since you were a kid."

"Yeah."

Thoughts moved through him slowly then: her at four years old, a classroom, a train car, no quiet anywhere. He wasn't seeing her memories. He was building bad little pictures from the facts she'd given him, and the loneliness in them came through anyway.

"It wasn't noble," Anya said. "I was scared. That's not the same thing."

"I didn't say it was."

"I know."

He sat back and rubbed at his forehead.

"I can tell you one thing," he said.

Anya waited.

"I can't help with the safe-house part. I didn't know enough then to help with anything. But after the gala? At your parents' apartment, at East Gallery, yesterday at the summit. I knew who you were and I kept choosing the door you were behind."

The words were simple. The thought under them wasn't.

*That's why this hurts so much.*

Anya's throat went tight.

"Okay," she said, because the rest of the language had dropped out of reach.

"That doesn't fix the rest."

"I know."

"I'm not offering it as a fix."

"I know."

His gaze drifted toward the photo again.

"I spent months thinking I was the only one holding hidden information in this relationship." He gave a short, disgusted little shake of his head. "Turns out we were taking turns."

"You were better dressed for it."

That time the air that left him was recognizably a laugh, brief and grim and gone fast.

"Your standards are collapsing."

"Been a bad week."

The quiet after that was not easier, but it was more human.

Damian set the glass down.

"If surface thoughts lie," he said, "how did you decide what counted."

Anya thought about that: a childhood worth of bad methods, twelve-year-old rules that kept mutating into adult ones, the things she'd gotten wrong because somebody had panicked in the wrong direction at the wrong second.

"Pattern," she said. "Context. Whether the thought matches what their body is doing. Whether it's the thought they keep having or just one awful flash because brains are gross. Half the job is knowing a person can think something and not mean it as the thing they'll actually do."

"And the other half."

"Pretending I don't know any of it."

He looked down at his own hands.

"That's not pretending. That's labor."

"Yeah."

"Did you ever hear something from me and decide not to use it."

"All the time."

"Such as."

She hesitated.

"You don't actually want examples."

"You're probably right."

He sat with that for a few seconds, then ignored his better judgment.

"Give me one."

Anya closed her eyes for a second.

"At the reception, before your mother got sick, you were thinking about how she used to straighten your tie too hard when you were little. You were trying very hard to look like a man at a diplomatic event and also thinking about being eight. I didn't say anything because it wasn't mine."

His expression did something painful and private.

"And a worse one."

"Damian."

"A worse one."

She fixed her eyes on the box so she would not have to watch his face absorb it.

"In the safe house bathroom, before you came back out, you were thinking that if you put your hands on me again you wouldn't stop where you should. You hated yourself for thinking it and also didn't stop wanting it. I didn't use that either."

His gaze snapped to the box and stayed there.

Heat ran through the room hard enough that she almost thought she'd said too much even for the question he had asked. Then his thoughts came through rough and ashamed and still stubbornly alive.

*I asked. I asked for that one.*

"Okay," he said at last, voice low and scraped raw. "Okay."

Neither of them moved for a while.

The kettle clicked as metal finished cooling in the kitchen.

"I don't know what any future with you looks like that isn't crooked at the start," Damian said eventually.

Anya gave a small, humorless breath.

"Me neither."

"That's not me asking for an essay."

"Good. I don't have one."

"Do you have anything."

She thought about every rule she'd just listed. Some of them were childish. They were useful anyway.

"Distance helps," she said. "Honesty helps more, apparently. I can leave rooms. I can keep thoughts to myself. I can stop using what I hear to steer things. I can tell you when it's too loud and go. I can screw up and tell you I screwed up instead of pretending I didn't."

"You can do that."

"Yes."

"Consistently."

"No," Anya said, because this was not the place to fake certainty. "But I can mean it consistently."

He accepted that with a face that suggested he hated accepting it and hated the alternative more.

"That's the first honest answer you've given me tonight that makes me want to stay in the room," he said.

It should not have made her want to cry. It did.

"Cool," she said to the childhood box.

His thought arrived a second later, surprised and unwillingly fond in spite of everything.

*That was such an Anya answer.*

She bit down hard on the urge to react to that one.

"What," he said.

"Nothing."

That almost became a smile and didn't.

"Right."

The box sat between them, lid propped against the coffee table leg. There were more things inside than she'd noticed before: a bent school pin, a paper certificate gone soft at the corners, a second shoe buried under the first.

"You kept all that."

"My mother kept it," Damian said. "I failed to throw it away for fourteen years."

"That's more honest than I expected."

"I've had a strenuous evening."

Anya looked at the photo again. Six-year-old Becky wore her hairband too big. Six-year-old Damian looked insulted by sunshine. Six-year-old Anya was mid-wave, as if the camera had caught her halfway through trying to make the future notice her.

"When you found the picture after the gala," she said, "did you hate me."

The answer came too quickly to be diplomatic.

"No."

Then the slower answer followed.

"I was angry at first because it felt like I'd been made stupid for free. Then I was angry because I couldn't tell what part of our alliance was accident and what part was you choosing me." He looked at her now, direct and tired. "Then I kept thinking about you at six. And at the ministry. And in that basement safe house with your ribs turning purple. None of that added up into hate. It added up into a problem I did not want to solve by losing you."

The word problem should not have been tender. In his mouth it was.

Anya scrubbed at one eye with the heel of her hand before anything wetter could happen.

"Okay," she said again.

"You can't keep saying okay like it means one thing."

"It means several things. That's the best I've got."

"Fine."

"Thank you."

He exhaled through his nose.

"I need time," he said.

"Okay."

"I don't mean I'm done. I mean I need to think about this somewhere you can't hear me doing it."

That should have been absurd enough to break the room. It almost did. A tiny near-smile touched his mouth and vanished.

"Yeah," Anya said. "I get it."

"And when you leave, if you can still hear me in the hall, don't stay there trying to measure it."

She looked up.

"Don't check whether I'm calmer. Don't turn this into another information problem because waiting feels worse."

That one hit clean because it was exact.

"I won't."

"You mean that now."

"I mean it now," she said. "And if I screw it up, I'll tell you I screwed it up."

He nodded once. He wasn't satisfied, but he wasn't dismissing it either.

"I'll call you when I'm ready."

Anya stood. Her water glass was still full. The class photo lay face-up on the table now, both of them trapped forever at six.

She got one arm into her coat and stopped. There was one more thing. She had to say it before the hallway took over and turned everything flat.

"I wasn't trying to make this come out okay," she said. "I wasn't trying to keep you. I just couldn't keep hearing everything and then show up here wanting more from you without saying what I was doing. Even if this is the part where you walk."

Damian stood too. Four feet lay between them again. The room was the same, but the air wasn't.

He set his hand on the doorknob without turning it, like he was trying not to pack the thought in anything diplomatic before letting it out.

"Anya, wait."

"Yeah."

"I'm glad you told me."

*I'm glad you told me.*

Her hand stalled on the other sleeve. She had to look at the chain lock instead of his face.

He opened the door.

The hallway was flat fluorescent light. Somebody downstairs had finally called her sister after all. The building kept being a building.

They did not touch.

"Goodnight," she said.

"Goodnight."

Outside, the city came back all at once: dinner plans, sore feet, rent, a teenager worrying about his jacket, a cab driver thinking about soup. Damian stayed at the far edge of her range for half a block, warm and blurred and then less, then gone.

She could have leaned back toward it and listened harder.

She didn't.

For once, not knowing had to be enough.
