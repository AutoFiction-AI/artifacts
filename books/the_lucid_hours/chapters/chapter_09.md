# Chapter 9

On Thursday morning, the flowers arrived at seven-fifteen, which was how Carmen knew the state was coming.

In thirteen months at Lakeview Gardens, she had never seen a living plant in the lobby. The silk ficus by the reception desk didn't count — it had a coating of dust thick enough to write in, and someone had once hung a pine-tree air freshener from one of its branches like an ornament on the world's saddest Christmas tree. But these were real flowers. Grocery-store carnations in a plastic vase, pink and white, their stems already starting to bend from being jammed together too tightly. They sat on the front desk next to the sign-in clipboard like an apology nobody had asked for.

Cliff Moorehead materialized from his office at seven-twenty, which by itself would have counted as a warning. Carmen had worked here for over a year and could count on one hand the number of times she'd seen the administrator before nine. He was wearing a polo shirt she hadn't seen before — navy, still creased from the package — and khakis with an ironed pleat down each leg. He kept smoothing one hand over the new shirt and resetting his mouth into a customer-service smile that slid off as soon as nobody gave it back.

"All right, everyone." He clapped his hands once in the hallway outside the nurse's station, the sound flat and managerial, like he'd practiced it on the drive in. "Surveyors are here at ten. I need all documentation current by nine. All documentation. MDS assessments, care plans, medication records, incident reports. If it's not up to date, it doesn't exist."

Tammy, passing with a stack of linens balanced against her hip, didn't break stride. "So half our patients don't exist."

"Tammy."

"I'm just saying. You want me to chart or you want me to change Mrs. Patterson, because she's been sitting in it since six and I only got two hands."

Cliff's mouth worked for a moment, opening and closing around words that didn't arrive. "Chart first. Then — yes. Prioritize."

"I'm prioritizing Mrs. Patterson's sacrum, Cliff. She's got a Stage Two on her tailbone and she needs turning every two hours and she gets it every three because you won't hire another aide for this wing."

"We're not discussing staffing right now."

"We're never discussing staffing. That's the point." Tammy turned the corner with her linens. Cliff stood in the hallway for another five seconds, then retreated to his office. A moment later, Carmen heard the printer start up — the heavy, grinding sound it made when someone was printing more than ten pages at once.

By eight-thirty, the transformation was visible. Someone — Cliff, or someone Cliff had deputized — had taped a motivational poster to the wall outside the dining room. It featured a sunrise over mountains with the words QUALITY CARE STARTS WITH YOU in a font that suggested clip art and a 3 AM deadline. The edges were still curling from the printer heat. It had been positioned directly over a scuff mark that had been on the wall since before Carmen started, and it covered the scuff but not the nail hole above it, so the effect was less "inspirational workspace" and more "someone is trying very hard."

The carnations had already begun to wilt.

Carmen moved through her morning routines — eight o'clock medications, vitals, the blood sugar check on Mr. Andersen in Wing B, the dressing change on Mrs. Patterson's pressure ulcer — at her steadiest speed, no wasted motion, no second looks at the MAR, every task already placed where it needed to go. She'd worked a state survey at her previous facility in Edison, a smaller place that got dinged for medication storage temperatures and a bathroom handrail loose enough to spin in its bracket. Surveyors came annually, theoretically unannounced, though facilities always seemed to know within hours of the survey team's departure from their regional office. Cliff had sent a staff email at four-twelve in the morning. Carmen's phone had buzzed on her nightstand while Reggie slept.

She was not worried about the surveyors finding the ring's operation. The ring left no physical evidence in accessible places — Agnes had insisted on that from the beginning, with the unbending precision of a pharmacist who'd spent forty-two years understanding exactly what regulatory scrutiny looked like. No pills in drawers, no cash in envelopes, no notebooks in rooms during the day. Everything moved through Marcus and out the building within hours of a meeting. The prepaid phone was powered down and hidden inside a heating vent cover in Harry's room, behind a screw Agnes had replaced with one that turned by hand.

What worried her was the medication count log.

She had documented every discrepancy herself — had written her initials and her license number next to each one, because that was the procedure, and Carmen followed procedure. Six documented variances in the past three months. All on her shifts, all under her name, all explained in the notes column with plausible fictions the charge nurse had taught her to write before Carmen understood she was covering for something larger than charting errors: documentation error, shift-change miscommunication, pharmacy delivery discrepancy. Each explanation was something that actually happened at understaffed facilities with hybrid paper-electronic systems and overnight nurses who'd been hired two weeks ago. None of them were true.

A competent surveyor would see the pattern. Not necessarily what the pattern meant, but that a pattern existed, and that it centered on one nurse.

She'd known this was coming since the moment she stopped counting honestly. She'd just managed not to picture it as a day with an hour attached. And now the date was here, and the carnations were dying in the lobby, and Cliff's motivational poster was peeling off the wall.

Dr. Prasad arrived at nine for her biweekly rounds, moving through Wing A with the efficient speed of a physician who saw forty patients a day across three facilities and couldn't afford to linger. She didn't seem to know about the survey — or if she knew, she didn't change her rhythm. She reviewed charts, wrote orders, asked Carmen about Mrs. Patterson's wound progression and Mr. Andersen's morning glucose, and was gone by nine-forty-five. Carmen envied her. Prasad was a doctor. She came, she prescribed, she left. She didn't sit in the building for eight hours knowing that her license number was on a stack of documents that told a story she'd invented.

---

The surveyor's name was Linda Horvath. She was about fifty, with gray-brown hair cut short enough to be practical and long enough that one section kept slipping forward; every few minutes she shoved it behind her ear with two impatient fingers and kept moving. Her shoes told Carmen everything: black, rubber-soled, scuffed pale at the toes, ugly and clearly comfortable. Hallway shoes. State shoes. She carried a binder thick enough to stop a door, a clipboard with a pen clipped to the top, and a stainless steel coffee thermos she refilled twice from the pot Cliff had set up in the conference room. Real brewed coffee, not the instant packets they usually had — another tell that the state was in the building.

She brought a second surveyor, a younger woman named Keisha who handled the dietary and environmental review while Horvath took clinical. Carmen saw Keisha photographing the dining room steam table with her phone, then crouching to check the temperature gauge on the reach-in cooler, writing something in her own binder. Cliff hovered near both of them with the energy of a man trying to appear relaxed while standing on a nail.

"Can I get you anything?" he asked Horvath for the third time in twenty minutes. "Water? We've got — I set up coffee in the—"

"I'm fine, Mr. Moorehead."

"Cliff. Please."

Horvath didn't use his first name. She opened her binder and began with resident interviews at eleven.

Carmen was doing her late-morning med pass when Horvath entered Room 114A. Harry was in his wheelchair by the window, wearing the blue cardigan he wore every day, staring at the parking lot with his mouth slightly open and his eyes fixed past the employee cars, as if the room had failed to reach him. He'd been in this posture when Carmen had come through with his morning medications at eight — pills set in the cup, water on the tray table, Harry taking them without looking at her, swallowing on reflex, the way he accepted a blanket adjustment or a blood-pressure cuff. She'd watched him do it and had the strange doubled experience of seeing an act she knew was an act performed so well it looked like the thing it was imitating.

Now Horvath sat in the vinyl visitor's chair, the one that exhaled a small puff of disinfectant-scented air when weight hit it, and opened her binder.

"Mr. Zielinski? I'm Linda, from the state health department. I just have a few questions for you today."

A pause. Then Harry's voice, querulous and thin, the voice of a man calling across a distance only he could see: "Eleanor?"

"No, sir. I'm Linda. From the health department. Can you tell me what today's date is?"

Another pause, longer. Harry seemed to be considering the question with genuine effort, his mouth working slightly as though the answer was a physical thing he was trying to locate in a cluttered room. "It's... March? Wait. It's — is it Tuesday?"

"Can you tell me what year it is?"

"Seventy-nine." He said it with sudden confidence, sitting up slightly in the wheelchair, and his voice shifted from lost to certain in a way that was startling. "Seventy-nine, and I'll tell you something, the Yanks are done. Reggie Jackson can't carry them without Munson. Thurman went down in that plane and that was the whole season."

Horvath's pen moved across her clipboard. "Do you know the name of the president?"

"Carter." Harry leaned forward. The words started crowding each other, fresh outrage all over them, like he'd just snapped the paper open at the breakfast table and found somebody willing to hear him out. "Carter, and he's losing Iran in front of everybody. Those people in Tehran are still stuck in that embassy and he looks like he doesn't know where to put his hands. You know what I paid for gas last week? A dollar and two cents at the Exxon on Route 18, and I still sat there half an hour. Eleanor said I should've gone Tuesday. She's right about Tuesdays. Where's Eleanor?"

"I'm not sure who Eleanor is, Mr. Zielinski."

"She was just here. She comes at — what time is it? She brings the — there's a thing she brings." He gestured vaguely at the nightstand. "With the..."

"That's all right. Can you tell me the name of this place? Where you are right now?"

Harry looked around Room 114A as though seeing it for the first time. His gaze traveled across the adjustable bed, the nightstand with its box of tissues and plastic water pitcher, the window with its view of the employee parking lot. He seemed baffled by all of it — not frightened, not distressed, just genuinely unable to reconcile his surroundings with whatever internal geography he was navigating.

"Is this the — no." He rubbed his face. His hand trembled slightly — a tremor Carmen had never seen from him before, which meant it was part of the performance, added today for the surveyor's benefit. "I don't. Where's Eleanor? She'd know."

Carmen stood at the sub-station near the Wing A entrance, charting vitals she'd already charted, and listened. Nine minutes. Horvath spent nine minutes with Harry Zielinski, and Carmen timed it because she couldn't help herself, the way you watch a tightrope walker and count the seconds not because you want them to fall but because you need to know how long a person can sustain the impossible.

Nine minutes, and Horvath never stopped writing. Harry gave her Munson's plane crash, gas lines at an Exxon on Route 18, the embassy in Tehran, and Eleanor six separate times, each return to the name bent a little differently from the one before. Carmen drew one unit too much insulin for Mr. Andersen, caught it, and pushed the excess back into the vial without taking her eyes off Harry's door.

When Horvath left Room 114A, Carmen was at the medication cart, drawing up an insulin dose for Mr. Andersen. She glanced toward Harry's doorway. In the small window above the door handle, she could see his reflection in the glass — he was watching Horvath walk away, and his face was completely still. The man in 1979 was gone. The one running the act was back. He held the stillness for two seconds, three, then let his face slacken again, and the transition was so smooth it was like watching water close over a stone.

---

Agnes was next. Room 108A.

Her roommate, Doris, ninety-one and deep into Alzheimer's, was asleep in the bed by the window, her mouth open, her breathing cycling through a wet click, a pause, and a long drain that carried into the hallway. Agnes sat in the chair by her own bed, hands folded in her lap, eyes fixed on a point approximately fourteen inches above the floor. She did not look up when Horvath entered.

"Mrs. Chen? I'm Linda, from the state health department."

Nothing.

"Mrs. Chen, can you hear me?"

Agnes's eyes moved — not to Horvath but to a different point on the floor, six inches to the right of the first point. Her expression didn't change. She might have been watching a crack in the linoleum. She might have been watching nothing at all.

"Can you tell me your name?"

Agnes opened her mouth. She made a sound — not quite a word, not quite silence. Something between a sigh and a syllable, like a radio tuned to the space between stations. Then she closed her mouth and resumed staring.

Horvath tried three more questions, spacing them out with the patience of a professional who'd been trained to give silence room. Agnes responded to none of them. At one point she raised her right hand — the one with the slight weakness from her stroke, which she normally overrode with the sheer force of her pharmaceutical precision — and touched her own face with the slow, exploratory gesture of someone encountering their own features for the first time. She ran her fingers along her jaw, her cheek, the bridge of her nose. Then her hand returned to her lap.

Four minutes. Horvath wrote a note, closed her binder, and left.

Carmen was passing in the hallway with a blood pressure cuff, heading toward 110A, and she caught a two-second glimpse through the closing door. Agnes was watching Horvath's back with the focused, evaluative expression of a pharmacist watching a technician count pills — not hostile, not amused, just assessing. Her eyes were completely lucid, her posture alert. The room smelled like Doris's incontinence pads. Then the door swung shut and the glimpse was gone.

---

Lola was in the dayroom when Horvath found her.

She'd positioned herself in the chair nearest the window, wearing a pink sweater with a small stain on the collar — coffee, Carmen thought, and probably deliberate. Her gold crucifix caught the late-morning light through the window. Her hands were wrapped around a rosary, the beads threaded between her fingers, and the tremor in her right hand made the beads click against each other in a soft, arrhythmic pattern, like a clock that had lost its mechanism but kept trying.

"Mrs. Fernandez? I'm Linda, from the—"

"Oh!" Lola looked up with such genuine-seeming delight that even Carmen, who knew the act, felt a moment of warmth. "Mija, sit down, sit. You want coffee? The coffee here isn't — well, it's not coffee, it's more like — you know my husband, Tomás, he used to make café con leche, real café, from scratch, his mother's recipe, she was from — where was she from?" Lola looked at the ceiling. "Matanzas? No, that's not right. Somewhere near there. A town outside Matanzas. There was a bakery. She always talked about this bakery—"

"Mrs. Fernandez, I just need to ask you a few—"

"—and the bread, Dios mío, she said the bread was so good you could cry, and Tomás always said she exaggerated about Cuba, you know how sons are about their mothers, but I think she didn't, I think the bread was exactly that good, because when we went to Miami for his cousin's wedding — when was that? Was that before the children or after? No, it was after, because Elena was walking already, she was — she had those little shoes—"

Horvath tried again. "Can you tell me—"

"—pink shoes, with a buckle, and Tomás said they were too expensive but I said what is too expensive for your daughter's feet? Forty dollars. For shoes she'd outgrow in six months and he acted like I'd bought a Cadillac. Men." Lola shook her head with fond exasperation, then turned to Horvath with sudden attention. "Are you married, mija?"

"I — yes."

"He treats you good?"

"Mrs. Fernandez—"

"You look tired. They work you too hard, I can see it. You eat today? You're thin. Not too thin, you look nice, but thin. Tomás's mother, she always said—"

Something shifted in Horvath's face. Not suspicion. Just the small reset of a professional recognizing a conversation she wasn't getting back. She tried once more. "Do you know what medications you take?"

"Medications?" Lola's face went soft with confusion — not Harry's elaborate temporal displacement but something warmer, as if she'd simply wandered off her own sentence and expected to find it again in a minute. "The pills? Oh, the pills. They give me — the girl gives me the — what's her name, la morena, the one with the — the pretty one—"

"Do you know what the pills are for?"

"For?" Lola held up her tremoring right hand, the rosary beads clicking. "This, I think. For the shaking. And for my — for sleeping. And there's a white one, a big white one that I don't like because it's — Tomás, you know, he never took pills. Not one. Not even aspirin. He said his mother taught him that the body knows how to heal itself, which, you know—" She leaned closer to Horvath, conspiratorial. "He was wrong about that. Obviously. But you couldn't tell him."

Twenty-two minutes.

Carmen knew because she was in the dayroom charting vitals for the Wing B residents and couldn't leave without making it obvious she was watching. By the end, Horvath's handwriting had shrunk to the size of pill-count notations. Lola had given her a bakery near Matanzas, Elena's pink shoes, a cousin's wedding in Miami that kept starting over, and Tomás's opinions about pills. She had not given her one answer Horvath could use.

Horvath's handwriting got smaller and faster. She checked her watch twice. The second time, she closed her binder with a finality that was courteous but absolute.

"Thank you, Mrs. Fernandez. You've been very helpful."

"You're leaving? But I didn't finish telling you about the bakery. In Matanzas. Or near Matanzas, I don't—"

"Maybe next time."

"You come back, mija. And eat something. Promise me."

Horvath walked out of the dayroom fast, binder tucked tight against her side. She tucked her hair behind her ears. Her pen clicked against the binder three times in rapid succession.

Carmen looked at Lola. Lola was looking down at the rosary in her hands, turning the beads with her tremoring fingers, her mouth slack now that there was no one to charm. Then she saw Carmen watching and lifted her face into the smile again.

---

Earl and Burt were interviewed together in Room 116A because Horvath was behind schedule.

Earl was in his bed, propped up on two pillows, a three-month-old issue of Time open on his lap to an article about western water rights he'd already read. When Horvath entered, he gave her one flat look, then turned a page he wasn't reading. He coughed — the deep, wet rattle that Carmen had been hearing more of lately, a cough that came from somewhere below the chest and took its time leaving — and settled back against the pillow.

"Mr. Washington, can you tell me today's date?"

Earl looked at her. He looked at the magazine. He looked at her again. Then he closed his eyes.

Horvath waited. She was good at waiting — Carmen could see that. She let the silence sit without rescuing it, pen loose in one hand, eyes on Earl's face.

Earl's breathing deepened. His head tilted slightly left. Within thirty seconds, he appeared to be asleep. He wasn't — Carmen could see from the hallway the way his fingers still held the magazine's edges, gripping rather than releasing — but Horvath couldn't see that from the visitor's angle, and the performance was more than good enough for a surveyor with sixteen facilities left this quarter.

Burt, in the bed by the window, was a different kind of quiet.

Burt Kovacs was eighty-eight and his dementia was real. He'd been playing since before the ring was a concept and long before the ring was a word anyone used — playing clubs in New York and Philadelphia and Budapest, his wife Erzsébet in the audience for the early years, his hands moving over piano keys fast and exact, as if the thinking lived in his fingers. Now those hands lay on the thermal blanket, long-fingered and mostly still, occasionally twitching in patterns that might have been remembered chord progressions or might have been nothing at all.

"Mr. Kovacs?" Horvath leaned forward, speaking clearly. "Can you tell me your name?"

Burt looked at her. His eyes moved across her face the way they moved across most faces now — searching, not quite finding, the effort of recognition visible but the result uncertain. He was alert in the way a person could be alert and lost at the same time.

"Erzsi?" he said softly. Then, in Hungarian, a string of syllables that came out tender and questioning, all of it meant for a woman not in the room.

"I'm Linda. Can you tell me where you are?"

"The music is..." Burt trailed off. He seemed to listen to something that existed only for him — his head tilted, his fingers twitched on the blanket, and for a moment his face held the concentration of a man hearing a passage he wanted to remember. Then the concentration dissolved into something like distress. "Where's — the thing. The book. My book."

Horvath wrote a notation. She asked two more questions. Burt answered one with a fragment about Erzsébet that trailed into Hungarian and ignored the other entirely, staring at his own hands as if they'd been attached to his wrists by someone else and he was still adjusting to the arrangement.

Six minutes. The fastest interview of the day.

Horvath closed the binder on Earl's nap and turned back to Burt, who was rubbing his thumb against his forefinger on top of the thermal blanket as if there were still piano keys there. She waited for something clearer from him. It didn't come.

Carmen went into 116A after Horvath left, ostensibly to check vitals. Earl opened one eye.

"She gone?"

"She's gone."

Earl grunted and reopened his magazine. Burt was still looking at his hands.

When Carmen took Burt's wrist for a pulse, he said "Erzsi?" again, softer this time, and let her turn his hand over as if it belonged to somebody else.

---

Horvath found Carmen at the sub-station at two-fifteen, after lunch.

Carmen had been expecting this. Not with dread, exactly — with the hard, narrowed focus she used when a task required precision under pressure and she couldn't afford to hesitate. She'd rehearsed nothing. Rehearsing would have made her sound rehearsed, and Horvath had been doing this too long to miss the difference between a nurse who was remembering and a nurse who was reciting.

"Ms. Reyes, I'd like to go over some of your medication management documentation."

"Okay." Carmen pulled the second chair out from behind the sub-station desk. "What do you need?"

Horvath sat and opened her binder to a tabbed section. Carmen could see the photocopies — her count sheets, her handwriting, her initials, her license number stamped at the top of each page.

"I'm seeing some variances in your controlled substance counts." Horvath ran her finger down the column without losing her place once, pausing each time Carmen's initials appeared. "Six documented discrepancies over the past three months. Oxycodone, alprazolam, tramadol. All occurring on your shifts."

Carmen's hands were on the desk, fingers laced. She'd placed them there before Horvath had started talking — a deliberate choice, because if she held a pen she'd click it, and if she touched her badge she'd adjust it, and her hands would do the things they did when she was lying and she didn't trust them not to.

"August twenty-second." Horvath tapped one photocopied line with her pen. "Oxycodone, five milligrams. Paper count nineteen, electronic count twenty-two. Your note says overnight documentation error. Walk me through the three-tablet gap."

Carmen felt the back of her neck go damp. "That one was the agency night nurse. eMAR froze again around six, so she counted off the paper narc book and never back-entered before shift change. I caught it at seven-fifteen on handoff. Three tablets gone from the card, electronic still showing twenty-two."

Horvath flipped a page. "September third. Alprazolam, one milligram. Pharmacy delivery discrepancy, corrected invoice to follow. I don't have the invoice."

"It would've gone to admin, not the narc book." Carmen kept her hands laced harder. "Pharmacy sent a short card. Seventy-eight in a ninety-count blister pack. I called from the med room, they confirmed the misfill, and the corrected invoice got faxed up front after lunch. Cliff should have it with the vendor paperwork."

Horvath looked up. "And the two unresolved variances? July eleventh and September seventeenth."

"Tramadol on July eleventh. Oxycodone on September seventeenth." Carmen heard herself getting too crisp and made herself breathe. "I re-counted both with the charge nurse and still came up short. So I documented them as unresolved and emailed Mr. Moorehead, because that was the cleanest thing I could do with it."

"And all six still happened on your shifts."

"Because I count them on my shifts." Carmen kept her eyes on Horvath's binder, not Horvath's face. "If the number's bad at handoff, it lands on me whether I caused it or not."

"The protocol says to notify your administrator."

"I did. On the two unresolveds. In writing." This part was true. Carmen had sent Cliff two emails, dated and timestamped. Cliff had not responded to either one, because Cliff did not respond to things that might require him to do something.

Horvath looked at her. No accusation. Then she dropped back to the photocopies, ran the six entries again from top to bottom, tapped the line about the missing invoice, and glanced toward Cliff's office as if measuring whether the walk and the argument were worth it. Her watch caught the fluorescent light when she turned her wrist. She checked the time, rubbed under one eye with the heel of her hand, wrote a cramped note in the margin, and flipped to a fresh page.

"I'm going to note the documentation gaps as a finding," Horvath said. "You'll need to implement a reconciliation process for the paper-electronic crossover. Both formats should be updated simultaneously at the point of count."

"Absolutely. I've actually been talking to our charge nurse about that."

She hadn't. She wouldn't.

Horvath made a final note, closed her binder, and stood. "Your medication administration records are thorough, Ms. Reyes. I can see you take the documentation seriously."

"Thank you."

"The discrepancies are a concern, but the documentation trail supports your explanations. I'll include recommendations in the report."

Carmen nodded. Smiled — not too much, just the professional acknowledgment of a review that had gone well. Horvath gathered her thermos and binder, tucked her hair behind both ears simultaneously, and headed toward the conference room where Cliff was waiting in a chair he'd positioned to face the door, shoulders bunched and already braced.

Carmen sat at the sub-station for another minute after Horvath left. She unlaced her fingers and found that they'd been pressing against each other hard enough to leave white marks on her knuckles. She flexed them until the color came back.

---

The survey concluded at four with a stack of findings that would keep Cliff busy for two weeks: the medication documentation gaps that Carmen had invented a reconciliation process for, a dietary storage temperature variance Keisha had photographed, two overdue care plan updates, a hand sanitizer dispenser that was empty, and the motivational poster, which Keisha had noted was covering a fire safety placard in violation of corridor clearance standards. Minor deficiencies. Mild enough to let a facility keep operating without penalty while generating enough paperwork to prove the state health department was performing its function.

Cliff ordered pizza. Three boxes from a place on Route 1 — pepperoni, plain, and something with roasted vegetables that no one touched. He set them on the break room table with a two-liter of warm Pepsi and a sleeve of paper cups and told the staff they'd done a great job. He used those exact words. "Great job, everybody." He said it twice. He didn't mention Mrs. Patterson's pressure ulcer, or the staffing ratio that made it inevitable, or the fact that the facility's survey score was a number that measured documentation completeness rather than whether anyone in the building was actually being cared for.

Tammy took two slices of pepperoni and ate them standing by the break room window, watching the surveyors' state sedan back out of its parking space.

"Flowers are dead," she said, chewing.

"They're not dead," Cliff said. "They're wilting."

"They're dead, Cliff. Rest in peace."

Carmen didn't eat. She poured a cup of the warm Pepsi, drank half, and tipped the rest into the break room sink. She finished her shift. She charted the afternoon medications, updated Mrs. Patterson's wound assessment — the ulcer was the same, neither better nor worse, which was what happened when you turned someone every three hours instead of two — and restocked the medication cart for the evening nurse. Normal work. Work she could do with her hands and the front of her mind while the rest of her sat somewhere else entirely, in a place she couldn't yet look at directly.

At six-forty, she clocked out. She walked to her car. She sat in the driver's seat and put the key in the ignition.

She did not turn it.

The parking lot was half-empty — day shift gone, evening shift trickling in, the gap visible in the spaces between cars. Late September light came in low and amber through the windshield, flattening everything into its own photograph. A plastic bag skidded across the asphalt and caught on a shopping cart someone had left near the dumpster. The courtyard trees were starting to turn — just barely, the first suggestions of yellow at the edges of leaves that had been green all summer.

Carmen sat with her hands on the steering wheel, her key in the ignition, her foot on nothing, and looked at the building she'd spent the day protecting.

Beige stucco. Small windows with institutional blinds, half of them crooked. A sign that read LAKEVIEW GARDENS REHABILITATION AND NURSING CENTER in letters that had faded from green to a color that was no longer any color in particular. No lake. No garden. Three scrubby trees in a paved courtyard and a view of Route 1 if you stood on your toes.

Inside that building, Harry Zielinski was either staring at the parking lot or staring at 1979. Agnes Chen was in her room with Doris breathing in the bed by the window, the click, the pause, the drain. Lola Fernandez had her rosary. Earl Washington had his magazine. Burt Kovacs was still studying his hands.

Carmen Reyes, LPN, license number 26NR03891, had lied to the state to protect a controlled-substance diversion operation running out of Lakeview Gardens.

What stayed in her body was the voice she'd used for it. It was the same calm voice she used for med pass, the same pauses, the same dry annoyance at other people's paperwork. Her mother was calling about Reggie. Carmen let the phone ring out. She sat there fifteen minutes while the amber light went gray and the vinyl under her palms cooled. When the screen lit again, she put the key the rest of the way in, started the car, backed out of her space, and drove to her mother's house to pick up her son.
