# Chapter 12

The word she was looking for was aluminum.

Not an important word. She'd been trying to describe the foil over the breakfast tray to her roommate Margaret, who couldn't hear her and wouldn't have cared, and the word had gone missing somewhere between her brain and her mouth. She'd stood there with her hand out, miming the act of peeling back the foil, and what came out was "the — the shiny. The paper that's not paper."

Margaret hadn't looked up from her oatmeal.

Lola sat on her bed after breakfast and tried the word again. Aluminum. There it was, back in place, as if it had only stepped out for a minute. Her heart jumped under her nightgown. She couldn't tell if the word had slipped because she'd been performing confusion for fourteen months and the act had worn a track through her, or because she was seventy-nine and had Parkinson's and sometimes seventy-nine-year-old women with Parkinson's lost words. Both belonged in the room. There was no test for sorting them. No blood draw, no brain scan, no doctor saying this one is the disease and this one, Dolores, is what you taught yourself to do.

She held her left hand up in front of her face. The tremor was there, as it always was — a fine, persistent shaking, worse in the mornings before her carbidopa-levodopa kicked in, worse when she was tired, worse when she was afraid. Right now it was moderate. A ripple, not a quake. She watched it and felt nothing useful. You couldn't argue with a tremor. You couldn't ask it to explain itself. It was just there. The medication helped and didn't help, and some mornings she could button her blouse on the first try and some mornings the buttons won and she asked Tammy for help with the same flat acceptance she'd learned to apply to everything her body did without her permission.

Her roommate Margaret — Margaret Oates, eighty-six, severe vascular dementia, no family visitors in the eleven months Lola had shared this room with her — was being wheeled to physical therapy by an aide Lola didn't recognize. A new one. They came and went so fast that Lola had stopped learning their names, which felt like its own small cruelty but was also just practical. You couldn't mourn everyone who left.

She got up and walked to the window. Outside, October had taken hold. It always seemed to happen with her back turned. The three trees in the courtyard had started to turn — though "turn" was generous. They were mostly just giving up, dropping leaves in patchy clumps onto the pavement below. Beyond them, the parking lot, half-full at this hour. Beyond that, Route 27. If she pressed her face to the glass she could see the Dunkin' Donuts sign, which she did sometimes, not because she wanted a donut but because the sign was bright and unmistakable and it reminded her that there was a world out there that did things she no longer did, like drive and buy coffee and stand in a line and choose.

She was supposed to be confused right now. That was her job, her contribution: the lookout, the smokescreen, the warm old woman who distracted nurses and visitors with stories while the real work happened behind her. In the hallways, during meals, during activities, when the aides came in, when Dr. Prasad made her biweekly rounds — Lola Fernandez was chatty, affectionate, mildly disoriented, calling everyone mijo or mija, telling stories that went in circles, asking what day it was three times in a conversation. The warmth was real. The stories were real, most of them. The confusion was manufactured, and it had been easy at first. Fun, even. She'd been a teacher for thirty-one years, fourth grade, and every day in a fourth-grade classroom was a performance of patience and enthusiasm that sometimes became real by third period. Playing confused was simpler than that. You just let the words tangle, let the sentences loop, let your eyes go soft when someone asked you a direct question. You became a version of yourself that people already expected a seventy-nine-year-old woman in a nursing home to be.

But fourteen months of playing confused had done something she hadn't anticipated. The confusion had weight. It seeped into her like sofrito smell working into the kitchen curtains in Union City, a little each day until the cloth held it whether you wanted it to or not. She forgot words now. She wasn't performing it. She wasn't choosing to stumble for whoever was watching. She reached for a word and found the shelf bare. Aluminum. Thermometer, last week — she'd been trying to tell Carmen her room was cold and she'd said "the thing, the thing that measures" and Carmen had looked at her with that careful nurse's attention and Lola had smiled through it, had made it part of the act, but alone afterward she'd sat on this bed and said thermometer six times fast with her hands balled in her lap. Carnation, the week before that, and that one had frightened her, because Tomás used to bring her carnations, cheap ones from the bodega on Bergenline Avenue, and if she was losing the word for the flower her husband brought her then she was losing a piece of him, and she had so few pieces left.

She thought about Burt. She didn't want to but she did. Burt had fallen two weeks ago and come back from the hospital worse, quieter, the confusion in his eyes no longer intermittent but settled, and watching him — watching a man whose dementia had been real all along, real where hers wasn't, or hadn't been, or might be becoming — had put a sound in her ears she couldn't shake. A kind of hum. Not a sound anyone else could hear. Just the low, steady vibration of a future she was walking toward whether she wanted to or not, a future where the word aluminum didn't come back, where thermometer stayed gone, where the act and the woman performing it merged into one thing and nobody, including her, could tell the difference.

Tomás would have said something about this. Something practical, something slightly impatient, because Tomás had not been a gentle man about worry. He'd been gentle about other things — he could cup a baby bird in his hands with a softness that made you forget those same hands poured concrete for twenty years — but worry made him restless, made him want to fix. "Lola, stop," he would have said. "Either it's happening or it's not, and sitting there scaring yourself doesn't change which one it is."

He was right. He was always right about that kind of thing, and wrong about everything else.

She sat on the bed and let herself think about him. Not a prayer exactly. A visit. Most mornings began like this. Tomás, she said in her head. Amor, mira. The conversation was one-sided and it always had been, because even alive Tomás hadn't been a big talker, and dead he was even less so.

He'd died of pancreatic cancer ten years ago, and he had not gone peacefully. People said passed away, went gently, is in a better place. Lola let them say it. What she had was Tomás in a hospice bed in their apartment in Union City, a bed the insurance company had delivered with more efficiency than they'd ever shown for his actual treatment, and three months of dying that he'd spent furious. Not sad. Not resigned. Not at-peace-with-God. Furious. He'd cursed the nurses who came to change his IV. He'd cursed the hospice chaplain, a kind young woman named Sister Beth who'd tried to talk to him about acceptance, and Tomás had told her to take her acceptance and get out of his house. He meant it. He was dying at sixty-eight and he had poured concrete and raised two children and paid his taxes and fixed the sink when it leaked and gone to Mass when Lola asked him to and now his pancreas was eating itself alive and no one could tell him why.

The last two weeks, the morphine drip ran almost constantly and he was in and out, and the in was worse than the out because the in meant he knew where he was and what was happening to him. He'd lost forty pounds. His skin had the color and texture of wet newspaper. He vomited bile into a plastic basin because his stomach couldn't hold anything, not even water, not even the ice chips Lola put on his lips with a spoon. The morphine constipated him so badly that she gave him suppositories, which she did without complaint, without performance, because she'd also wiped his back when he sweated through his shirts at night and held his penis when he couldn't aim at the urinal anymore, and she'd done all of it because that was what forty-three years of marriage actually meant. Not the vows. The vows didn't know to include any of this.

He'd died at 4:17 on a Tuesday morning in January. She'd been sitting next to the bed, not sleeping, her hand on his arm. His breathing had changed — the rattling, the gaps that got longer — and then it stopped, and the room went quiet in a way rooms with living people in them never were, and she'd sat there for a long time before she picked up the phone. Those minutes were the last minutes she'd ever be in a room with Tomás. Once she called the hospice number, the machinery of death would start — the funeral home, the insurance paperwork, the obituary, the calls to Roberto and Miriam, the casseroles from neighbors who meant well and didn't know what else to do — and she wasn't ready for that. She wasn't ready for the shift from wife to widow, the last role change she'd ever have to make, or so she'd thought at the time.

She'd been wrong about that. She'd made one more: from widow to patient. And one more after that: from patient to lookout, to performer, to a woman who watched through a mask of confusion while pills moved through cookie tins and envelopes of cash went into the pockets of a man everyone believed was her nephew.

Tomás, she thought. Amor. What would you say about all this.

She knew what he'd say. He'd say: "Are you eating? Is anyone hurting you? Then do what you need to do and stop worrying." That was Tomás. He operated from a simple moral architecture: are you fed, are you safe, is someone being cruel. Everything else was negotiable.

Her son Roberto called every Sunday from Chicago. The calls lasted about twelve minutes. She'd timed one once, not to catch him, just out of the bleak curiosity that came from having too much time and not enough to spend it on. Twelve minutes covered his wife Elena, the kids, work, the weather in Chicago, and "how are you doing, Mamá?" She always said fine. She performed mild confusion on these calls now, because Roberto talked to the staff, and if Lola was lucid and articulate on the phone but drifting in the building, someone would notice. So she let her sentences circle. She asked about the grandchildren and then asked again five minutes later. Roberto's voice when she did this — patient, a little too loud, careful — sounded like the voice Helen Chen used when she visited Agnes, and that comparison made Lola's stomach turn.

Her daughter Miriam sent cards from Orlando. Birthday, Christmas, Mother's Day, and random Hallmark cards with flowers on the front and messages like "Thinking of you today!" The exclamation point always felt wrong to Lola, something pushed when it should be quiet. If you were thinking of someone, you could come. But Miriam had two kids and a husband who traveled for work and a mortgage she'd mentioned twice, each time giving a little laugh right after the monthly number, and Orlando was far, and Lola didn't blame her. She didn't blame either of them. They were living the lives America demanded of children — spread out, working, building families in whatever city had jobs — and the alternative was guilt, which helped no one and which Lola had seen do terrible things to other families in this building. The daughter in Hackensack who visited every day and cried every time, until the resident — a man named Phil who had Lewy body dementia and didn't know anyone's name — started getting agitated whenever she showed up. The son who came once a month from Pennsylvania and spent the entire visit staring at his phone. Guilt made people stupid. Lola preferred the cards.

She touched the crucifix at her throat. The gold was warm from her skin. She didn't pray — she hadn't prayed since Tomás died, because she had some things to say to God and none of them were requests — but the crucifix was her mother's, and touching it was a way of touching her mother, dead longer than Tomás and still easiest to reach through the hands. Her thumb found the little cross the same way she'd watched her mother's thumb find it through entire Masses, back when Lola was small enough to fit under her arm.

Ay, Dolores. What are you doing.

She said it to herself in her mother's voice. Not with judgment. With tired wonder.

---

Tammy found her in the courtyard at two o'clock, which was how most of their afternoons together started — not by plan but by routine, by two people who lived in the same small world eventually falling into rhythm without deciding to. Tammy's break was 1:45 to 2:15, and Lola was usually out here by one-thirty because the room was too quiet after Margaret was wheeled back from therapy and fell asleep, and the courtyard, even with its scraggly trees and its view of the parking lot, was outside. Outside still counted for something.

"Move over," Tammy said, and sat down on the bench without waiting for Lola to shift. She was wearing her teal scrubs, the ones with the bleach stain on the left thigh that she'd given up trying to hide, and her hair was in a knot that looked like it had been assembled in the dark, which it probably had been — Tammy's studio apartment, the one she'd moved into after the divorce, had one overhead light and a window that faced a wall. Lola knew this because Tammy had described it three times, each time in the same flat way, as if bad lighting were just another item on a list of things that had gone wrong and she didn't have the energy to rank them anymore.

"Coffee?" Tammy held up two Styrofoam cups from the staff kitchen. The coffee at Lakeview was terrible — thin, vaguely brown, produced by a machine nobody had cleaned since the previous administrator — but Tammy always brought two cups, and she always got it right. Two sugars, milk not cream. It was the kind of thing that shouldn't matter and that mattered more than almost anything.

"Gracias, mija." Lola took the cup with both hands, partly because of the tremor and partly because the warmth felt good. The October air had an edge to it now, a sharpness that meant summer had stopped pretending. Two of the courtyard trees had gone mostly bare, the third holding on to its leaves in scattered orange patches, stubborn or just slow. Leaves littered the pavement around the benches, dry and curling.

They sat. The courtyard was empty except for them and a squirrel working the base of the middle tree, nosing through the fallen leaves for something edible. Lola watched it with a recognition she didn't examine.

"So," Tammy said.

"So."

Tammy drank her coffee and made the face she always made, the one that said this is undrinkable and she was going to finish every drop. "Greg's got a girlfriend."

Lola didn't look at her. She knew from fourteen months of performed inattention, from a lifetime of reading rooms, that Tammy needed her to not make this a moment. She lifted her coffee and blew on it.

"Yeah. Brandy. Her name's Brandy, like the — yeah." Tammy's voice had gone carefully flat, which meant she was working hard to keep it there. "She's twenty-eight. Works at a Verizon store in Bridgewater."

"Ay."

"It's fine. It's been eight months. It's whatever."

It was not fine and not whatever and they both knew it. Tammy worried the rim of her Styrofoam cup with her thumbnail. Her words came out one by one, too careful, too even. The divorce had reached Lola in pieces over these afternoons. Greg had left in March. Before that there'd been the gym woman whose name Tammy never said. Papers were final in July. Tammy had moved into the studio in Perth Amboy with her cat, who'd immediately peed on the mattress. She'd replaced the mattress, not the cat. That was the kind of detail Tammy let out when she was deciding between laughing and doing something worse.

"Twenty-eight," Tammy said again, looking at the squirrel instead of Lola. "I'm forty-three and he picks a girl who wasn't born when I started working here."

Lola wanted to say something real. The want was physical, pressing at her sternum like a cough she couldn't release. She wanted to drop the mask and say: Mija, I watched my husband die in our apartment. He cursed the chaplain and I wiped bile off his chin and I gave him suppositories because nobody else was going to and we didn't talk about dignity because dignity was a word for people who hadn't had to do those things yet. And twenty-eight-year-old Brandy from Verizon is not going to do that for Greg when the time comes, and the time comes for everybody. She wanted to say: your ex-husband is an ordinary man doing an ordinary stupid thing and it'll hurt you until one day it won't and I can't tell you when that day is because grief doesn't keep a schedule, not even the grief for a man who's still alive and just chose someone else.

But she was Lola Fernandez, room 112A. Warm, confused, mildly disoriented. And Tammy was the person who brushed her hair on the mornings the tremor made the brush jump and skitter in her grip. Tammy was the one who knew about the two sugars and the milk. If Lola spoke too clearly, too specifically, Tammy would notice. Tammy was tired, not stupid. She noticed things. And noticing would lead to questions, and questions would lead to the ring, and the ring would lead to Harry and Agnes and Earl and Carmen and Marcus and Burt, and Lola would be the one who'd unraveled everything because she couldn't stand being lied to about coffee by a person she was lying to about everything else.

So she said: "Men." She said it with a wave of her hand, a puff of air, pure performance — dismissing the whole gender as a weather pattern. "Tomás — my husband — he forgot our anniversary once. Thirty years, mija. Thirty years married and he forgot. Brought flowers the next day like that fixed it." She let the story ramble, let it loop in the loose circles her cover required, and the story was true — Tomás had forgotten their thirtieth, and the flowers had been carnations from the bodega, and she'd forgiven him before he walked through the door because forgiveness wasn't something she'd ever had to decide about with Tomás, it was just the climate they lived in — but the truth of the story was separate from its job here, which was to be harmless, to fill space, to be the kind of thing a confused old woman said to a younger woman's pain.

"Greg never forgot an anniversary," Tammy said. "He just forgot to give a shit about the other three hundred and sixty-four days."

"Sí."

"Sorry. Language."

"Mija, I've heard worse." She had. She'd heard Tomás curse God and a chaplain and the pharmaceutical company that made the morphine that wasn't strong enough and the insurance adjuster who'd denied the home health aide request two weeks before he died.

"Yeah." Tammy stretched her legs out. Her shoes were the white nursing clogs that all the CNAs wore, scuffed gray on the sides and collapsed at the heels from the miles she logged every shift — the steps between rooms, the laps around the wing, the back-and-forth to the supply closet for gloves and wipes and the incontinence briefs they went through forty of per day. Lola looked at those shoes and saw fifteen years of work that nobody wanted to discuss over dinner. "She posted pictures on Facebook. Her and Greg at some restaurant with candles. On the table. Candles." Tammy's voice thinned on the word. "We ate off paper plates because I was too tired to wash dishes after a twelve-hour."

"I'm sorry, mija."

"Don't be. I'm just — it's just stupid. The whole thing's stupid."

Lola felt the guilt land under her ribs, and the coffee went sour in her mouth. Tammy brought her coffee and brushed her hair and told her about Greg and the apartment and the bad lighting and the cat, and Lola kept giving her room 112A instead of herself.

The coffee shook in her left hand. She put the cup down on the bench between them.

"You know what the worst part is?" Tammy said.

"Tell me."

"I miss him." She said it as if missing him were something she'd been caught doing. Not the volume. The posture — her shoulders came forward, her chin dropped. "Not the — I don't miss the lying. Or the gym woman. Or the part where he let me pick up doubles every week while he watched videos on his phone. I miss having someone there. In the apartment. I miss noise. It's so quiet, Lola. My place is so quiet. It's like —" She stopped. Picked at the rim of her Styrofoam cup. "You know when you turn the TV off and the silence afterward is louder than the silence before you turned it on? That's what it's like. Every night."

Lola knew that silence. She'd lived inside it for ten years, first in the apartment in Union City where it had been Tomás-shaped — a quiet that filled the exact spaces his sounds had occupied: the scratch of his pen on the crossword, the wheeze in his breathing that she'd told him to get checked and he hadn't, the thud of his boots by the door — and then in this room, where the quiet was different, institutional, a quiet made up of the sounds just outside it: Margaret's breathing, the ventilation hum, the distant call bells.

"I know," Lola said.

And then the words came out before she could stop them. "You're a good person, mija."

Tammy looked at her. Her thumbnail stopped at the rim of the cup. For a beat she looked straight at Lola, not at the squirrel or the parking lot.

"That's sixteen-fifty an hour and a studio with a window facing a wall," Tammy said.

Lola let her eyes slide away to the garbage can, where the squirrel had made it to the lid and was hanging there with its tail twitching. "Ay. That's true."

"Yeah."

"I gotta get back," Tammy said. She stood and her back cracked, two sharp reports like knuckles popping, the sound of a forty-three-year-old body that did the physical work of a much younger person for compensation that assumed she was replaceable. She picked up both coffee cups — hers and Lola's — because that was Tammy, then stood there another second with the cups in her hand, waiting.

"Tammy."

"Yeah?"

Lola had the first two clean words in her mouth: I'm not. She pinched the Styrofoam seam until it bent under her thumbnail. If she said them, Tammy would hear the whole rest of it.

"Tell Brandy I said hi," she said instead.

Tammy snorted. Not a real laugh. The short, hard sound a person made when something was funny enough to crack the surface but not enough to break through. "Yeah, I'll get right on that."

She walked back inside. The courtyard door closed behind her with the heavy pneumatic exhale that all institutional doors made, a sound engineered for a building designed to contain rather than welcome. Lola sat with the bench to herself and the two wet rings where the coffee cups had been and set Tammy's life in order against her knee, counting with her thumb on the fingers of her shaking left hand. Greg. Brandy. Verizon. Twenty-eight. Bridgewater. Studio. Window facing a wall.

She did it again, slower. Greg. Brandy. Verizon. Twenty-eight. Bridgewater. Studio. Window facing a wall.

On the third pass, Bridgewater disappeared.

Not the feeling of it. Just the word. The little Jersey town where Brandy sold phone plans and posed beside Greg in candlelight on Facebook, and Lola could not catch its name.

She sat with that blank spot for a beat, then another. Then she got up, knees crackling, and went to the courtyard door.

Inside, Tammy was pushing the linen cart past the nurse's station. Lola let her shoulders sag, softened her eyes, and put the old familiar drift back in her voice. "Mija?" she called. "Did Margaret eat? I can't — I don't remember if she ate."

Tammy looked over. "She ate, Lola. She's back in bed."

"She ate," Lola said, nodding as if that settled it.

Then she went down the hall at her careful shuffle while Tammy turned back to the cart, the act back in place and Bridgewater still gone.
