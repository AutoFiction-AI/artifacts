# Chapter 3

Devon's podcast was about serial killers. Harry could hear it from his room — thirty feet down the hall, a tinny voice describing some crawl space in Ohio, and beneath it Devon's breathing, slow and even. He was asleep by quarter past eleven most nights, which was close enough to dependable.

Harry checked his watch. Timex, steel band, the one Jack had given him for his eightieth birthday. Eleven thirty-two. Close enough.

He wheeled himself into the hallway. The overhead fluorescents were dimmed to half for the night shift, buzzing at a frequency that had bothered him for three months when he'd first arrived and now just sounded like the building breathing. Room 110 was making the sound again, the low continuous moan that never quite rose to a volume anyone could justify investigating. Harry rolled past. The linoleum had a buckle outside Room 112 that caught his left wheel every time, and he corrected for it without thinking, the way a driver steers around a pothole on a road he drives twice a day.

The nurse's station was dark except for the monitor glow. Devon was in the rolling chair, head tilted back, mouth open, arms folded across the Lakeview polo they made him wear. Twenty-six years old, night shift three times a week, phone face-down on the medication cart playing true crime at a volume that would wake no one. He was paid fourteen-fifty an hour to watch thirty-two residents and he spent most of that time asleep. Harry had done the math on Devon's value to the facility: at current staffing levels, even an unconscious body in the chair met minimum regulatory requirements for overnight aide coverage. The system didn't need Devon alert. It needed Devon present. Devon, to his credit, was reliably present.

Harry wheeled past, watching Devon's chest rise and fall. He wouldn't surface until three, when the podcast ended and his phone alarm went off for a single wellness check before dawn shift arrived. Harry knew this because he'd watched Devon for four months before he trusted the pattern. Four months was a reasonable sample size.

The TV room was at the far end of Wing A, past the supply closet and the handicapped bathroom, as far from the station as the building allowed. During the day it smelled like microwave popcorn and the vinyl cleaner the CNAs sprayed on the chair seats. At night it smelled like the rest of Lakeview: disinfectant and warm skin and the faint chemical sweetness of urine that no amount of mopping ever fully extracted from institutional carpet. The window unit hummed and rattled and pushed air that was cooler than the hallway but not by much. Late August in central New Jersey, and Lakeview's HVAC budget was roughly what Harry would have spent on a mid-tier filing system in 1994.

He wheeled in, checked the hallway behind him — habit, not paranoia — and stood up.

His knees cracked, both of them, left then right. He pressed his palms against his lower back and stretched until something deep in his spine shifted and the pressure released. He was eighty-four and everything hurt in the mornings and most of it hurt at night, but his legs worked. His legs had always worked. The wheelchair was overhead — the cost of running the operation, a prop he'd spent six hundred hours in over the past eighteen months — and the numbness that spread through his left thigh every time he stood was the price of sitting in a chair his body didn't need.

He walked to the card table, pulled the chain on the floor lamp Agnes preferred because the overhead fluorescents gave her headaches, and set up the folding chairs. His faded blue cardigan hung on the back of his wheelchair where he kept it, and he pulled it on because the window unit made the room colder than the hallway and because he was eighty-four and his body didn't hold heat anymore. He positioned the wheelchair at the table's head — he'd sit in it for the meeting, same as always, because the height was right for the work and because standing for two hours was a cost his knees wouldn't cover.

Agnes arrived first. She always arrived first.

She came in without greeting, carrying the pillbox organizer in one hand and two notebooks in the other — the blue one for inventory, the black one for buyer accounts. She wore the same gray cardigan over the same Lakeview-issue nightgown, and her wire-rimmed glasses sat low on her nose because the left arm was bent and she had not asked anyone to fix it. She was seventy-six and weighed maybe a hundred and ten pounds, and she moved through the room with the authority of forty-two years at the Walgreens in Edison, as if everything in it was slightly misplaced and she was the only person who knew where it should go.

She sat down. She opened the blue notebook. She uncapped a pen.

"You are four minutes late."

"I'm right on time. You're early."

"That is the same thing." She arranged the pillbox organizer in front of her and began opening compartments, her precise hands setting each small lid against the table with a hard plastic click a shade louder than the work required.

Lola appeared in the doorway, moving quietly for a woman of seventy-nine with a tremor. She'd come from 112, the room with the buckled linoleum outside it, and she was already in position mode: shoulders squared, eyes on the hall, the warm confusion dropped like a coat she'd hung by the door. In the daytime, Lola was everyone's favorite resident — the one who called you mija and told you a story about her husband that circled back on itself three times before it landed somewhere gentle and unexpectedly sharp. At night she was the best lookout Harry had ever worked with. Different business, same value in a person who noticed everything and looked harmless doing it.

She took her post by the door frame, leaned against it, and held her crucifix. Her hand trembled — it always trembled, and the Parkinson's medication managed the large movements but not the fine ones, so the gold chain shivered against her nightgown in a way that looked like devoted prayer and was actually a neurological condition being put to practical use.

"Coffee?" she said.

"After," Harry said.

"It was your turn to bring it."

"I brought it last week."

"You didn't. Earl brought it because you forgot."

"I didn't forget. I prioritized."

"Ay, prioritized." Lola shook her head. "You prioritize like my husband prioritized the yard. Never."

Earl came in pushing Burt's wheelchair. Earl Washington was eighty-one, six-two when he stood straight, which he rarely did anymore — his back had started rounding years ago and his posture now matched the stoop he performed during the day, which was either ironic or efficient depending on how Harry looked at it. He had large hands, a steelworker's hands, and they rested on Burt's wheelchair handles with the steadiness of a man who'd spent thirty years gripping things that were heavier than a wheelchair.

Burt was asleep. Mostly. His chin rested on his chest and his wispy white hair was going in three directions and his long fingers — musician's fingers, Agnes called them, and she was right, they had the reach — twitched against the wheelchair armrests in some rhythm only Burt could hear. He might have been dreaming about a piano. He might have been dreaming about nothing. Burt Kovacs was eighty-eight and his dementia was the real thing. The rest of them put on confusion for staff. Burt sank into his and came back when he could, and Harry kept that cost on the books.

Earl parked Burt near the table, pulled the canvas bag from the back of the wheelchair, and sat down. He nodded at Harry. The nod covered hello, status, and anything else that needed saying. Earl talked when talk was needed. Otherwise he sat there with the kind of silence that made everybody else sound wasteful.

"How's he doing?" Harry asked. He meant Burt.

"Same."

Earl reached into the canvas bag and set a hardcover book on the card table — Burt's Hungarian-English dictionary, the big one with the cracked spine and the wine stain on the front cover. Burt's wife Erzsébet had given it to him in 1986, or 1987, Harry could never remember which, and Burt still reached for it on his nightstand some nights, running his thumb along the binding the way you'd touch a hand you hadn't held in years. He hadn't opened it to read in a long time. He didn't need to. Agnes had hollowed the center block of pages with a razor blade five months ago and sealed the cavity with packing tape, and the dictionary now held sixty-eight tablets in four pharmacy-grade bags — reserve stock, the buffer that kept their supply chain from running dry between cycles.

Agnes reached across and opened the dictionary without ceremony. She began transferring the bags to her sorting station, arranging them by type along the edge of her blue notebook. Harry watched her hands — precise, economical, the left one slightly slower from the stroke residual but compensating with efficiency. Agnes did not waste gestures.

"Inventory," Harry said.

Agnes didn't look up. "Eighty-two oxycodone, five milligram. Mostly Burt's breakthrough stock. On patch nights he does not use the full order, and if he asks for it, he gets it. One hundred twenty alprazolam, one milligram. Lola's when Lola can spare it, plus the B Wing bedtime refusals the nurses chart before they bother to watch anybody swallow. Forty-five tramadol, fifty milligram. Your card, two rehab leftovers nobody sent back to pharmacy, and fifteen pulled because they expire on the twenty-ninth." She turned the blue notebook so Harry could see the columns — numbers in handwriting so clean it looked typeset. "Non-controlled: metformin, forty count, five hundred milligram. Lisinopril, thirty, ten milligram. Rosuvastatin, twenty, five milligram. The metformin is from Dr. Prasad's most recent round of renewals. She increased Burt's dosage, which means the surplus margin is wider this month."

"Street value on the controlled?"

"Approximately three thousand two hundred, at market rates. At our pricing — with the adjustments for Rosa and the standing discount for Janine — twenty-three hundred, give or take."

"Give or take."

"Give or take forty dollars. I would need to confirm whether Janine wants the full order this month." She uncapped a second pen, red, and made a notation in the margin. The red pen was for corrections and adjustments. The black pen was for confirmed data. Agnes ran two pens the way other pharmacists ran two registers — one for the real count and one for the questions.

Earl had opened his envelope and was spreading cash across his end of the table: fives, tens, twenties, a few fifties. He sorted bills into stacks with the ease of a man who'd counted union dues for three decades. His hands moved through the money the way Agnes's moved through pills — muscle memory outlasting the context that had made it.

"Last month," Earl said. "Three thousand eight hundred forty."

Harry noted it in his own notebook — pocket-sized, pencil, because pencil could be erased and Harry had learned in forty years of accounting that permanence was a liability. That was four hundred off the month before. The gap bothered him, even though he knew the reason.
He slid the notebook into his breast pocket and, with the same motion, checked the hard little rectangle sitting in the lower cardigan pocket. Harry trusted backups more than anybody else's memory.

"Down because Derek missed his last pickup," Earl said. "Marcus says Derek's insurance kicked back in. Something temporary, through his wife's job."

"Good for Derek." Harry marked four hundred off the month. If insurance had covered Derek for a few weeks, that was four hundred dollars they didn't have to bill him. He moved to the next line.

Earl coughed. It came from somewhere deep in his chest — a wet sound that rattled up through his throat and ended in a wheeze that made Lola turn from the doorway. Harry looked too. The cough had a weight to it, a thickness that was different from a throat-clearing or a cold. He'd been hearing it for a few weeks — maybe six, maybe eight, he hadn't been counting precisely, which was unusual for him and which he noted now as its own data point.

"You all right?" Harry said.

Earl cleared his throat and waved the question away with one large hand. He went back to sorting bills.

Harry watched him for another second, then let it go. Earl's dismissals were non-negotiable, and pushing past them wasn't a move that worked with a man who'd spent his career deciding what deserved a response and what didn't. Harry filed the cough. He filed everything.

"Envelopes," he said.

Earl began the separation. Twelve hundred for Marcus's thirty percent — the distribution fee, the cost of having legs that could leave the building. That went into the pickup envelope Marcus would collect during his next visit as Lola's nephew. Four hundred for Earl, which he'd get to Denise in Trenton by way of a story about a VA supplemental benefit that didn't exist. Three hundred eighty for Harry, which he'd send to Jack's business account through a wire transfer he'd route as a drawdown from an investment fund that also didn't exist. Jack's landscaping company was carrying fifteen thousand in startup debt and Harry was paying it down in increments small enough that Jack wouldn't ask questions. Agnes's share — four hundred — went into a plain white envelope she'd take back to her room and slide under the mattress, where it would join the other envelopes in a stack she never counted. She had no one to send it to. She had Helen, who had sold her house and put her here, and Helen didn't need money — Helen needed to be forgiven, and Agnes was not extending that service. Lola's share went to a church in Miami that ran a free clinic and didn't ask where donations came from. Burt's paid for things Medicare wouldn't: the hearing aid that actually fit his ear canal, the fentanyl patch copays when Part D dropped into the coverage gap, the lidocaine patches that kept his lower back from locking up by dinner.

"The lisinopril margins are thinning," Agnes said, still sorting. She held a tablet up to the lamp and read the imprint through her glasses. "The generic price at pharmacy level has dropped again. We should consider replacing it in the lineup with something that has a wider spread. Losartan, possibly. I will check the numbers."

"Check them," Harry said.

"I said I will." She set the tablet down with pointed precision.

Harry let the silence end the exchange. Agnes didn't need prompting and she didn't tolerate it. He turned to the cash on the table and continued recording the distribution in his notebook — small, careful pencil marks, the numbers squared and legible, each entry dated, each amount verified against Earl's count. He'd done this kind of work for forty years in offices with fluorescent lighting not so different from this, except the offices had been in Bergen County and the clients had been small businesses with tax problems and the only criminal liability had been the occasional undeclared cash payment, which was a misdemeanor, not a federal charge carrying a mandatory minimum.

Burt stirred. His eyes opened halfway and drifted across the card table and the people sitting around it without quite finding them. He blinked. His mouth worked for a moment before the words arrived.

"The Blue Note," he said.

Nobody responded. Harry was writing. Agnes was sorting. Earl was stacking envelopes. Lola glanced over from the door but held her post.

"Nineteen sixty-four," Burt said. The words came out clean. "Bill Evans was on the bill. I wasn't. I was hauling gear for Billy Martello — his bass amp, the big Ampeg with the blown speaker that buzzed on the low notes. Billy gave me twenty dollars and two joints for the night, and I sold both joints to a white kid from Connecticut in the men's room for fifteen dollars each."

"You told us," Earl said. There was nothing unkind in it. He stated facts once, evenly, and left them there.

"Thirty dollars for two joints in 1964," Burt said. "You know what that's worth now?"

"Two hundred eighty-seven dollars," Agnes said without looking up. "Approximately. I checked the last time you told this story."

Burt stared at her. Something moved behind his eyes. "That's right," he said slowly. "How'd you know that?"

"Inflation calculator. I verified it."

"I told this story before?"

"Three times." Agnes set down a bag of alprazolam and looked at Burt directly, her glasses low on her nose, her expression clinical. "The details shift. The first time, the joints cost the man ten dollars each. The second time, twenty. Tonight, fifteen."

Burt considered this. Confusion crossed his face, then amusement, then something sadder that was gone almost at once. "Well," Burt said. "Maybe it was different nights."

"Maybe," Agnes said, and went back to her sorting.

Burt's eyes drifted toward the middle distance. His fingers kept their rhythm on the wheelchair arm, tapping out a song or a memory of a song or nothing at all. Harry watched him for a moment, then wrote a number in his notebook and moved on.

With Burt, Harry got thirty seconds at a time: a room name, a year, the price of two joints in 1964. Then Burt's eyes went loose again and his fingers returned to the armrest, tapping at something the rest of them couldn't hear.

"Coffee," Lola said from the doorway. It wasn't a request anymore. It was a ruling.

"Fine." Harry stood — his left knee caught, and he paused until it released — and walked to the counter at the back of the TV room, where a coffeepot sat next to a sleeve of Styrofoam cups and a canister of Folgers decaf that Lola had taken from the kitchen two weeks ago. The facility's coffee was bad. This coffee was the same brand and therefore equally bad, but it was theirs — pilfered and brewed on their schedule, in their room, and Harry had learned at eighty-four that ownership mattered more than quality. It had probably always mattered more than quality. He just hadn't noticed when he'd had both.

He filled the pot from the water cooler and turned it on. The machine was older than Devon and sounded like it. The gurgling was loud enough that Harry paused and listened — Devon's podcast still droning, no change in the hallway, the building settling into its late-night sounds.

"Harry." Lola's voice, low and pointed.

He turned.

"Your socks."

"What about my socks."

"You're not wearing them."

He looked down. She was right. He'd forgotten the compression socks again, and his ankles were swollen enough that the skin puffed over the elastic band of his slipper socks. Dr. Prasad would have called it edema. Harry had been ignoring it for weeks.

"They itch," he said.

"They work. Go put them on."

"We're in the middle of business."

"We're in the middle of coffee. You have time."

"Lola."

"Harry."

He went back to the table without putting on compression socks. Lola made a sound — not quite a word, more like a small, definitive judgment pressed through her teeth — and turned her eyes back to the hallway. The coffeepot sputtered behind them.

Agnes had finished her sorting. The bags were lined along her notebook's edge, each one positioned over the buyer code it corresponded to — letter-number combinations, never names, keyed to the account system in the black notebook. The black notebook was the one that mattered. The blue one was inventory — defensible in the abstract as a retired pharmacist's habit, an old woman who couldn't stop counting. The black one held buyer medical histories, drug interaction warnings, and dosing notes for sixteen people who needed medications they couldn't afford at pharmacy prices. If anyone found the black notebook, the operation was over and the evidence was organized well enough to convict them.

"We have a problem," Harry said.

The room tightened. Earl's hands stopped on the cash. Lola's grip tightened on the crucifix chain. Agnes held her pen above the page.

"The new nurse," Harry said. "Carmen."

Agnes put her pen down. "The one who caught the alprazolam count."

"She flagged a discrepancy — eight pills short across two substances. Reported it to the charge nurse." He paused. "Charge nurse called it a documentation error. Carmen logged it anyway." At Lakeview that was how shortages survived: one nurse signed what she expected to see, the next counted the card by habit instead of tablet by tablet, and the hole kept moving dressed as clerical error. Carmen had counted the pills and watched the throats.

"But she did not move on," Agnes said.

"No." Harry opened his notebook and flipped back three pages. He'd been tracking Carmen Reyes since her second week at Lakeview — shift patterns, documentation habits, how she interacted with residents, how long she spent on medication counts versus how long the average nurse spent. Thirteen months on the job. She worked the 3-to-11, picked up doubles when she needed money, and she needed money often enough that Harry had a rough model of her financial situation that he didn't feel great about having built. Single mother, LPN working toward an RN. Good with residents — genuinely good, not the efficient-but-detached good that most of the staff managed after a year. And she was better with medications than anyone Lakeview had employed in the four years Harry had been here.

"Last week. Med pass." Harry looked at Lola. "She watched you swallow."

Lola shifted at the door. "I was—"

"You were fine. She wasn't checking the chart. She was watching your throat."

"How much attention?" Earl asked.

"Deidre was here nine months. Sixty-three pills short and she never blinked." Harry closed his notebook. "Carmen caught eight in two weeks."

"She picked up a double last Thursday," Agnes said — faster now, the words compressing. "I saw her past midnight."

"One in the morning." Harry watched their faces — Agnes gone still, Earl's expression unchanged but his hands motionless on the envelopes, Lola's grip tightening on the crucifix until the tremor transferred to the chain. "She walked to the TV room. Looked in. Three of us sitting up. Talking."

"Dios mío," Lola said, very quietly.

"We performed. She backed off." He folded his hands on the table. "But she saw it."

"Sundowning," Agnes said.

Harry looked at the doorway again: Carmen in the frame, three people sitting up straight around the table, every mouth closed at once. "She saw a meeting."

"Can you handle it?" Earl said.

"I don't know yet." Harry had been thinking about Carmen Reyes for two weeks. Report, ignore, come in: those had been the tidy categories. Tidy categories were not the same thing as a plan. "She's not Deidre. She's counting on her own shifts. Nobody asked her to."

"What about the count?" Agnes said.

"The eight came from direct pulls," Harry said. "Derek missed pickup, Janine doubled, and Marcus wanted the bags full."

"That was bad arithmetic," Agnes said.

"It was temporary."

"It was theft from live cards," Agnes said. The words came faster now, facts packed tight. "The safe stock is Burt on patch nights, Lola when she can spare the alprazolam, your tramadol when you decide you can stand the hip another night, and the rehab leftovers the pharmacy forgot to reclaim. Everything else comes out of somebody's pain control or sleep and leaves a hole behind. I will not touch the fentanyl patches. I will not bag oxycodone and alprazolam together. And I will not leave Burt hurting because Marcus made promises."

"So what do we do?" Lola asked from the door. Her voice had lost the warmth of the coffee argument and the compression-sock lecture. Quieter now, stripped of stories and digressions, just the question.

"Three clean days," Harry said. "No controlled pickups. Marcus gets told we're dry."

"He'll hate that," Lola said.

"He can hate it somewhere else."

"Good," Earl said.

"On Carmen's shifts, nobody palms anything," Harry said. "If the pill goes in your mouth, it goes down. Agnes moves nothing off live cards."

"That should already be the rule."

"It is now." He turned the pencil once between his fingers, then set it flat on the table. "And I make the counts cleaner before dawn."

Agnes looked at the clock on the cable box. "Before three. Devon's alarm."

"Before three," Harry said.

"And Carmen?" Lola asked.

"If she lets three clean days go, she wanted the numbers fixed. If she keeps digging after clean cards, she's after us."

The coffeepot beeped. Harry stood and poured five cups, including one for Burt, who was dozing but would want it if he surfaced. All decaf. He added two sugars to Lola's — she liked it with milk and sugar, but the kitchen didn't stock milk in single-serve containers and nobody was going to make a trip to the walk-in refrigerator at midnight, so sugar was the concession. He brought it to her at the door. She took it without looking away from the hallway.

"Gracias," she said.

"De nada," Harry said, and his pronunciation was terrible, as always: eleven words of Spanish in sixty years, every one of them mangled. Lola smiled when he tried — genuine warmth, absolutely no respect for his vowels.

He set Agnes's on the table — black, no additions, six inches from her notebook, never closer, because Agnes would not risk a spill near her records. Earl's he placed near his hand. Earl nodded once. Harry sat back down with his own cup and the room eased into the late-meeting rhythm, the business done, the coffee turning the remaining time from an operation into something closer to a visit.

Burt opened his eyes. He looked at the Styrofoam cup in front of him with the slow recognition of a man discovering something he'd wanted without knowing he'd wanted it. He picked it up. His hands shook — that tremor was always real — and coffee tilted toward the rim but didn't spill. He drank. He set the cup down.

"Is this the Blue Note?" he said.

"It's Lakeview, Burt," Earl said. His voice was different from the efficient briefness he used during business — something more careful, pitched to guide rather than inform.

Burt looked around the table. He seemed to register each of them, or something that corresponded approximately to each of them. "I knew a girl here once," he said. "Played flute." He paused. "No. That was somewhere else."

"Drink your coffee, mijo," Lola said from the door.

Burt drank his coffee. The room sat with him while he did, and nobody said anything, and the silence was the kind that didn't need filling — the silence of people who spent their nights together in a room that smelled like vinyl and Folgers and the permanent warm sweetness of a building that held eighty lives and their smells inside its walls. Burt finished his cup and set it down with a small decisive motion, as though he'd made a judgment about the coffee or the evening or the decade, and then his eyes went elsewhere, and that was the last clear thing he said that night.

Harry checked his watch. One fifty-eight.

"We're done," he said.

Agnes repacked the bags into the dictionary and closed it. She stacked her notebooks, capped both pens — black first, then red, always in that order — and tucked everything under her arm. Earl collected the envelopes and slipped them into the canvas bag. Lola checked the hallway: a long look in both directions, her body still against the door frame, the tremor in her hand pausing as if even the Parkinson's understood what was operationally required.

"Clear," she said.

Agnes left first. She moved down the hall with the economy of a woman who had closed a pharmacy counter six thousand times and knew how to leave a space without leaving a trace. Earl went next, pushing Burt's wheelchair, the rubber tires making their small sticky sounds on the linoleum. Burt's head lolled against the headrest. Earl steered with one hand and held the canvas bag against the armrest with the other, and Harry watched them go — Earl's stooped back, his large hands steady on the chair, his roommate asleep in front of him — and thought that whatever Earl Washington's reasons were for doing this, they were not Harry's reasons, and they were probably better ones.

Lola waited for them to clear the hallway, then went. Her slippers whispered on the floor. One hand traced the wall for balance — the medication helped with the big movements, the walking and the standing, but the small adjustments, the corrections that kept her from listing left, those she managed with her fingertips on the wainscoting. She disappeared around the corner toward 112 and didn't look back.

Harry was the last to leave. He always was. He turned off the lamp, rinsed the coffeepot in the small sink behind the counter, dropped the cups in the trash, wiped the card table with a napkin from the stack by the microwave, and pushed the chairs in until they lined up. He stood alone in the dark TV room and listened: Devon's podcast droning its murderous details, the ventilation rattling in the walls, Burt's wheelchair receding down the hall, and beneath everything the deep institutional quiet of a building where most of the people inside it were dying at various speeds and the rest were being paid not enough to notice.

He sat down in his wheelchair. The adjustment was physical and deliberate — a loosening of his spine, a drop of his shoulders, a slackening around his eyes and jaw that he'd practiced in front of the bathroom mirror until he could hold it for hours. He let his hands go soft on the armrests. He let his mouth hang slightly open, the lower lip loose, the expression of a man who was looking at something that wasn't in the room. He became Harold Zielinski, Room 114A, dementia patient, the one who asked about his dead wife Eleanor.

He wheeled himself into the hallway. The fluorescents hummed. Room 110 was still moaning. Devon was still unconscious. The building was exactly as he'd left it two hours ago, and no one awake in it knew that five of its residents had just conducted a business meeting that moved thirty-eight hundred dollars through a distribution network that none of them could have imagined existing in a place that smelled like this and looked like this and paid its night staff fourteen-fifty an hour to sleep through it.

Harry stopped at his door — 114A, the single room, a privilege earned by seniority and the administrative assumption that he needed quiet for his condition. He wheeled in. He transferred to the bed, the movement efficient even though no one was watching and the performance was for empty walls. He pulled the thin blanket to his chest. The window unit rattled. The room was warm and close, the air thick with late August and the stale air of a space one person lived in and never left.

Carmen Reyes sat in his head like a number that refused to reconcile. A report would have brought supervisors, forms, a round of disbelief, the confusion defense. Carmen would work quieter than that. She could start recounting on her own, stay through med pass and watch Lola swallow every pill, ask why Burt came up short on oxycodone before his refill date, close one small gap after another until the extra tablets vanished from the margins.

Harry opened his eyes. By the time anyone upstairs understood what she was looking at, the cards could be clean and the cash already gone.

Outside his window, the parking lot was empty except for Devon's Civic and the overnight nurse's minivan. The streetlight at the lot's edge cast a yellow circle on the asphalt. He had just under an hour before Devon's alarm and less than that before Carmen started turning the night into paperwork.

Waiting had sounded reasonable at the table. In bed it looked like the sort of arithmetic people defended right up until the audit.

Harry sat up. The mattress springs complained. Three clean days required cleaner numbers than the ones they'd left on the books.

He pulled on the cardigan, took the bath towel from the chair, and went back into the hall.
