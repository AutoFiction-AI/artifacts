# Chapter 10

The TV room's fluorescent tube buzzed from a loose ballast when Carmen pushed the door open at one o'clock. Harry sat in his wheelchair at the card table — the one the activities aide used for bingo on Tuesdays — wearing the faded blue cardigan he wore to every after-midnight meeting Carmen had attended. Agnes sat across from him. Lola was at her post by the hallway door, one hand resting on the gold crucifix at her throat, her other hand still in the tremor that worsened at night. Earl sat in the vinyl recliner by the far window with his eyes closed, though Carmen could tell from the set of his shoulders that he was listening.

Three days after the survey — three days after she sat in her car unable to drive, feeling the weight of what she'd told the state surveyor settle into a place she couldn't reach to remove it — she'd found Harry alone in the TV room during evening med pass, leaned against the doorframe while Lola pretended to watch a game show behind him, and said, "Show me all of it."

Harry had said one o'clock. Nothing else.

Four composition books sat under the white buzz, black-and-white marbling gone soft at the corners, Agnes's handwriting packed so tight and even it looked typeset. Agnes did not look up when Carmen pulled a vinyl chair to the table and sat. She kept sorting pill bottles into short rows, labels turned the same way, caps aligned. There were yellow stickers on some caps, red dots on others, a system Carmen did not know yet. The room smelled like dust cooked under fluorescent heat, stale coffee, and the sweet medicinal film that always clung to Agnes's hands no matter how much sanitizer she used.

"You said all of it," Harry said.

He pushed the top notebook toward her.

"Good," Carmen said. "Then don't give me the pitch."

She opened to the marked page. The spread looked less like a confession than a set of books. Agnes had ruled the paper into columns with a pen so sharp it had scarred the cardboard underneath: MONTH, GROSS, MARCUS, JACK, DENISE W., CLINIC, BURT, AGNES, RESERVE. At the bottom she had boxed the summary twice in red: 18 MONTHS. AVG GROSS 4,000. TOTAL 48,000 +/-. On the right-hand page she had copied out the most recent full month in cleaner block letters:

4,000
1,200
500
400
350
500
400
650

Nothing about it looked frantic. Nothing about it looked improvised. It looked like somebody's side business from a garage, except the raw material had come through med carts and return bins and under her license.

"Eighteen months," Carmen said.

"Since early last year," Harry said.

She ran her finger down the newest month. "Thirty percent to Marcus."

"Twelve hundred," Harry said. "Distribution, gas, exposure."

"Exposure is not a cost center."

Agnes capped a bottle without looking up. "It is if you want the medication to leave the building."

"Twelve hundred is still high."

"Then drive it yourself," Harry said.

Carmen looked up. "I'm here, aren't I."

Harry did not answer that. He sat very still with his hands folded on the table. The stillness was one of the things that made him frightening. He never looked like he was working to control himself. He looked already controlled.

She tapped the next line. "Jack gets five hundred. Your grandson."

"Yes."

"For what."

"Landscaping business in South Brunswick. Fifteen-thousand-dollar hole."

"And he knows where the money comes from?"

Harry waited. The fluorescent buzz filled the space where a softer man would have rushed to explain himself. Finally he said, "He thinks it came from a certificate of deposit I cashed before Lakeview."

"So you lie to him."

"I send him five hundred."

"That's not what I asked."

"No," Harry said. "It isn't."

Earl coughed into his fist, deep and wet. Carmen felt it in her own chest hearing it. Harry kept his eyes on her and said nothing else. The silence did what a speech would have done worse. It left the lie sitting on the table with the notebook.

She went to the next name. "Denise Washington."

Earl opened his eyes. "My daughter."

"Four hundred," Carmen said.

"VA supplement," Earl said. "On paper."

He coughed again, took a second to get his breath, then finished. "Shoes for the girls, school fees, field trips. Heat when PSE&G gets cute."

He closed his eyes again. Conversation over.

Carmen's finger moved to Lola's line. "Three-fifty to a church clinic in Little Havana."

Lola was still looking at the hallway. "Iglesia de la Caridad," she said. "They keep regular meds for people who don't have regular anything. Blood-pressure pills, diabetes meds, inhalers when they can get them. A woman comes in wheezing and what is she supposed to do, mija, pray harder?"

"No return address," Harry said.

"Of course no return address."

Lola's tremoring hand tightened around the crucifix once and settled.

"Burt gets five hundred," Carmen said.

"Compression stockings that actually fit," Harry said. "Hearing-aid batteries. Brand-name carbidopa-levodopa when the facility pharmacy substitutes the generic again and he spends two days nauseated and shaking worse."

"And Agnes gets four."

Agnes said, "Yes."

"For what."

"For being correct."

Carmen looked up from the page. Agnes was still sorting, not smiling, not offering the line as a joke. Under the fluorescent buzz it landed as plain fact. Carmen thought of the white envelope under Agnes's mattress, the children Helen brought every other Sunday, the way Helen used that too-loud careful voice like volume itself could cross whatever she thought had been damaged.

"There's no Harry line," Carmen said.

"I already live on the expense side of the ledger," Harry said. "Move on."

Carmen did not move on. She flipped back one page, then another, then forward. The pattern held. Some months gross dipped into the high three-thousands, some months ran over four, but the spine of it stayed the same: Marcus at thirty percent, the same recipients, the same reserve. It was not random or desperate. It was monthly.

"This is what pisses me off," she said. "Not the notebook. The discipline. You made it look normal."

"It's monthly arithmetic," Harry said.

"It's narcotics diversion."

"And arithmetic."

Agnes slid the second notebook toward Carmen with two fingers. The page already lay open. RESERVE / 650 was written at the top in narrow block letters, and underneath Agnes had listed the uses in hard capitals: MONEY ORDERS, GAS, REPLACE EXPIRED STOCK, BUYER BRIDGE, LOSS.

Carmen tapped the last word. "Loss from what."

Agnes answered this time. "Broken seals, wrong strengths, Marcus stupidity, buyers who lie, replacements when a discontinuation changes back before the pharmacy catches up."

Carmen looked from the word to Agnes. "Say that again."

"An order gets reduced or discontinued. We move too fast. A physician changes course. Omnicare takes a day or two to deliver. If the resident needs the medication back before then, reserve buys retail."

"So somebody in here has gone without."

"Briefly," Agnes said. "Twice."

"Twice too many."

"Correct."

The answer irritated Carmen more than an excuse would have. "You're saying that like it's acceptable."

"I am saying it like it happened."

Harry said, "That is what the reserve is for."

"To cover the times you hurt the wrong person."

"To keep the wrong person from carrying all of it," Harry said.

Carmen stared at LOSS again. The word had more moral weight in Agnes's hand than it would have in anyone else's, not because Agnes was soft but because she was not. A softer person would have called it slippage or error or something else that lied by tone. Agnes had written loss and left it ugly.

"What happened," Carmen asked.

Agnes finally looked up. "One resident went without a bedtime trazodone for a night because a discontinuation turned out to be a dose reduction and not a stop. She did not sleep. She screamed at the television until three in the morning. Another case was Burt. The substitute arrived, not the brand. He vomited twice and refused breakfast. Reserve covered the replacement. I changed the screening rules."

"And you kept going."

"Yes."

"Why."

Harry answered before Agnes did. "Because outside this building people were already going without longer than overnight."

Carmen turned to him. "That doesn't make this clean."

"No," Harry said. "It doesn't."

Nobody in the room rushed to make it cleaner than it was. Lola kept watch at the door. Earl's breathing stayed audible in the recliner, wet on the inhale. Agnes straightened a row of amber bottles until the labels lined up like soldiers. The only thing Carmen could hear besides the ballast was the faraway ice machine kicking on down the hall.

She pulled the newest composition book closer. "Buyer list."

Agnes let go of it. "Read."

Carmen opened to diagnosis headers instead of names: LUPUS-SLE, TYPE 2 DM, CHRONIC PAIN - MUSCULOSKELETAL, HYPERTENSION, HYPERLIPIDEMIA. Under each header Agnes had logged dosage, quantity, frequency, refill cadence, with margin notes in red. There were no names anywhere, no numbers she could call, just conditions, requests, and little pieces of medical history gathered secondhand from parking-lot conversations and pill bottles photographed under bad light.

"No names," Carmen said.

"Need is relevant," Agnes said. "Names are not."

Three entries were marked with red asterisks. This time Carmen did not wait to be directed. She turned to the first and bent over it until she could smell the paper.

The first entry was headed CHRONIC PAIN - MUSCULOSKELETAL above an OXYCODONE REQUEST, with current meds in the margin: clonazepam, trazodone, nightly alcohol use.

"Respiratory suppression risk," Carmen said. "If Marcus hands her oxy on top of clonazepam and she's drinking, he can put her down."

"Yes," Agnes said.

"Did he know that? Did he ask?"

"Not enough."

Harry said, "He asked enough to get forty dollars."

Agnes tapped the margin with one blunt fingernail. "I said no."

"And if you hadn't."

"Then she might not wake up."

There was no performance in Agnes's voice, no emphasis laid on the line to make Carmen absorb it. She said it the same way she would have said a refill was due Friday.

Carmen flipped to the second red mark. The note was shorter and meaner: POST-OP PAIN above an OXYCODONE REQUEST, then Agnes's blunt summary that the buyer had first claimed a husband after shoulder surgery and, on the second contact, an aunt. Same phone. Same quantity request. Ninety preferred. DO NOT SELL.

"She changed relatives."

"Mid-sentence," Agnes said.

"Marcus brought her twice?"

"He thought desperation and lying were the same thing," Harry said.

"They aren't," Carmen said.

"No," Harry said.

"What happened after you cut her off."

Agnes shrugged once. "She found somebody else. That is the usual result."

"And that doesn't bother you."

"Everything bothers me," Agnes said. "That is not the same as saying yes."

Carmen heard the small scrape of Lola's orthopedic shoe pivoting on linoleum. Lola had not moved from the door, but she had angled her body back toward the table now, listening harder.

"You screen from gossip," Carmen said. "From what Marcus remembers and what buyers tell him and what pill bottle he can get a picture of through a windshield. That's not medicine. That's triage done in a parking lot by amateurs."

"Yes," Agnes said. "And still safer than Marcus alone."

"That's a terrible standard."

"It is the one available."

Carmen hated how often that sentence could be true inside Lakeview.

She turned to the third red mark. This one was almost boring in its consistency: LUPUS-SLE, hydroxychloroquine 200 mg, ninety count, same dose, same request interval. Agnes had added: old bottle verified, joint swelling observed, no sedatives, no escalation.

She read it twice, then looked at Harry. "This is the buyer you're sending me to."

"Yes."

"Convenient."

Harry said nothing.

Agnes did. "She is the cleanest case."

"Of course she is."

"Yes."

Carmen waited for Harry to deny that the whole thing had been staged around the safest moral sample on the page. He did not. The silence did not absolve him. It confirmed the calculation.

"So Rosa is your best argument," Carmen said.

"Rosa is one argument," Harry said.

"The easiest one."

"Yes," Agnes said.

"Then why should I see her first."

Harry folded his hands tighter. "Because you asked why anybody buys from us. She can answer that better than I can."

"And the rest of them."

"The rest of them are why Agnes keeps writing NO in red."

Carmen looked down at the pages behind Rosa's. There were more red marks further in. Not as many as there should have been if every buyer lied, not few enough to make the room feel noble. Enough to make the operation look like what it was: not a charity, not a cartel, not even a consistent ethic. A system run by old people with partial information and too much confidence in their own standards.

"What about the ones you say no to," Carmen asked. "They just disappear."

"They go somewhere," Harry said.

"Somewhere worse," Lola said softly from the door. "Usually worse."

That quiet sentence did more damage than a speech. Carmen could picture the alternatives too easily: fake pills pressed in somebody's basement, fentanyl in a baggie, steroids bought from a friend of a friend, Facebook groups full of maybe-medication and maybe-scam. She could also picture the people inside this building whose medications became columns and percentages and reserve losses. Both pictures sat there at the same time, refusing to cancel each other out.

Agnes turned another page. "My information is stale by the time Marcus brings it."

Carmen looked up.

"You see order changes first," Agnes said. "Dose reductions, discontinuations, pharmacy substitutions. If I had current lists, I would catch more of this before it reached the lot."

"You're asking me to help."

"I am asking you for current information."

"For a black-market med reconciliation."

"Name it whatever lets you sleep."

"I sleep fine," Carmen said, which was such obvious bullshit Lola made a small sound through her nose.

Harry said, "You don't have to pull anything."

"I know I don't."

"Then don't. Tell Agnes when something changes."

"And then what. You adjust your menu."

Agnes said, "I stop Marcus from handing oxycodone to a woman on clonazepam with a bottle of sauvignon blanc in her refrigerator. If you prefer not to help with that, say so clearly."

Carmen looked at the first red note again: clonazepam, trazodone, nightly alcohol use. It was exactly the kind of incomplete med list you got when people outside care settings pieced their treatment together from old specialists, urgent cares, borrowed transport, and the memory of one doctor who retired six years ago. Agnes was right about the risk. Agnes was also asking Carmen to join the record-keeping side of a crime.

"I tell you about changes," Carmen said. "That's it. Dose reductions, discontinuations, substitutions if they matter clinically. I am not pulling pills off carts. I'm not altering charts. I'm not getting cute."

"That is a start," Agnes said.

Before Harry could close the notebook, Carmen reached for the older one and turned it open herself. FACILITY MONTHLY MEDICATION COST PER RESIDENT was written on the left-hand page. UNINSURED RETAIL on the right. The institutional side of the equation was ugly enough to make the numbers feel fictional: monthly medication costs that ran past two thousand dollars for one resident without anybody at corporate blinking because billing abstractions and managed-care discounts kept the pain off-site. On the retail side the same drugs looked like bus fare turned into extortion.

Hydroxychloroquine, 200 mg, ninety count: 389.
Rosuvastatin, 20 mg, thirty count: 212.
Metformin, 500 mg, sixty count: 87.
Oxycodone, 5 mg, thirty count: 241.

Carmen did not need Agnes to narrate the math. She could follow the route herself: physician orders, facility pharmacy, bulk purchasing, med carts, return bins, discontinued cards, delayed pickups, pills piling inside the institution because it cost less to let them accumulate than to verify anybody still needed them. Then the other side of the same road: people outside paying retail with no coverage, cutting tablets, skipping doses, pretending a prescription from last year was still current because a doctor's visit cost money they did not have.

"Surplus on the left," Carmen said.

"Deficit on the right," Agnes said.

"Surplus in the building," Harry said.

"Not in the residents," Carmen said.

Harry gave her a short look that might have been agreement if he had been a man who wasted agreement out loud.

She closed the notebook. The cardboard cover made a dry smack on the table. "You already set up the meeting."

"Thursday," Harry said. "Your shift starts at three. Be in the lot at two-thirty. Silver Civic, far end."

"You picked the clean case."

"Yes," Agnes said.

"And you're still going."

"Yes," Harry said.

"Why."

This time Harry took a second before answering. "Because even the clean case isn't clean. You should see that too."

Carmen handed the composition book back to Agnes. Agnes took it without fumbling, quick and certain, and stacked it under the others. Lola returned her full attention to the hallway. Earl coughed once more, harder this time, and pressed the heel of his hand to his sternum until the spasm passed. Carmen sat another second in the white buzz of the room with the numbers still moving behind her eyes: forty-eight thousand total, four thousand a month, twelve hundred to Marcus, five hundred to Jack, four hundred to Denise, three-fifty to the clinic, five hundred to Burt, four hundred to Agnes, six-fifty to reserve. There was enough ugliness in it to ruin everybody and enough order to make ruin look administrative.

Then she got up and left before any of them could mistake staying seated for agreement.

---

Rosa drove an early 2000s Civic — silver paint going milky at the fenders, a St. Christopher medal on the rearview mirror, a striped beach towel folded over a tear in the passenger seat. She was parked at the far end of Lakeview's lot, away from the entrance and the employee spaces, in the spot where visitors parked when they did not want to be seen arriving or did not want anyone to remember them leaving. Carmen knew the spot. Families used it when they came to admit a parent they would not visit again.

Thursday. Two twenty-five. Carmen had arrived early, crossing the lot in her scrubs with her badge clipped and her bag on her shoulder. The afternoon was warm for late September, holding onto summer even with the courtyard trees starting to turn. A supply van was backed up to the loading dock, beeping every time the driver touched reverse. The air smelled like hot asphalt, diesel, and whatever somebody in dietary had burned at lunch.

Rosa got out of the car when she saw Carmen coming. She was fifty-two and moved like someone whose body charged her for every step. Her dark hair was cut close, practical. She wore a fleece vest over a long-sleeved shirt despite the warmth. Carmen clocked the cold sensitivity before Rosa said a word.

"You're the nurse," Rosa said.

"Yeah."

"Harry said you wanted to talk."

"I wanted to understand the costs."

Rosa gave a small laugh with no humor in it. "Everybody wants to understand the costs once it isn't theirs."

She leaned back into the car, grabbed a CVS bag off the passenger seat, and shut the door with her hip. The bag was fat with papers: receipts, pharmacy printouts, old bottles with labels still stuck to them, the paper trail of being uninsured in America.

"I do this every few months," Rosa said. "I go in and ask again. Same prescription, same question. They say the number out loud, I thank them, I leave, I go sit in my car and want to throw up."

She took out a folded receipt, then another, then a pharmacy estimate printout stapled to a coupon that had still left the price impossible.

"Hydroxychloroquine, two hundred milligrams, ninety count. It was three eighty-nine at CVS this week, three sixty-two at the Walgreens on Route 1 if I used their little loyalty card, and four twelve at Rite Aid before that one closed. Costco was cheaper one month but still over three hundred, and I don't have a membership, so who gives a shit."

"And from Marcus."

"Forty."

The number sat between them in the heat.

"Cash," Rosa said. "Exact if I can manage it. I count the tablets before I drive away."

"You trust him that much."

"Trust." Rosa snorted. "I trust he wants forty dollars and I need the pills. That's what I trust. I don't tell him where I live. He doesn't ask me how my week was. Works out."

She pulled an amber bottle from the bag and handed it to Carmen. The label was almost rubbed white from living in a purse. Hydroxychloroquine sulfate, 200 mg. Directions and refill history old enough to be humiliating.

"Sometimes there's a typed sheet in the bag," Rosa said. "Sometimes it's handwritten. Spacing, expiration dates, don't take it on an empty stomach unless you like nausea, all that. Whoever writes it isn't fucking around."

"Agnes," Carmen said before she meant to.

Rosa lifted one shoulder. "Probably. The old pharmacist lady. All right. She can stay mean if the instructions stay right."

They stood beside the Civic while cars went by on Route 27 and the supply van kept beeping behind them. Wind pushed a paper cup across the lot and trapped it against the curb by Rosa's front tire.

"I was at a dental office in Woodbridge," Rosa said. "Receptionist there, twelve years. I answered phones, chased insurance authorizations, called people to remind them about cleanings they were never going to keep anyway. The dentist retired. The new owner brought his own staff. I got two weeks and a card with a fruit basket on it."

She folded the receipts again along lines she had already memorized.

"No COBRA because it was a small group plan. I checked. Marketplace was five-forty a month for the premium and a three-thousand-dollar deductible before anything useful happened. At the temp agency I was making sixteen an hour. You can do that math faster than I can."

Carmen could. Rent, gas, food, a car that needed a new starter, a utility bill, a premium, a deductible, and a disease that did not care what your hourly wage had been at the dentist's office. The corridor Rosa was describing was one Carmen knew by instinct even before she knew the policy language for it. Too much money for Medicaid. Not enough for the kind of coverage that kept a body functional.

"You have a rheumatologist now?" Carmen asked.

Rosa laughed again, still without humor. "I have memories of a rheumatologist."

"Labs."

"Not since last year."

"Eye exams."

"Two years." Rosa looked at her. "I know what the drug can do. I also know what lupus does when I stop taking it. Those are the two options on the menu."

That landed harder on Carmen than the receipts had. Even the clean case was not clean. Rosa was not getting monitored the way she should have been. She was not buying a safe legal substitute. She was buying a partial version of medical care from a parking lot and calling it survival because survival was the honest word.

"What happens when you don't take it," Carmen asked.

Rosa's face changed a little, not softening, just losing patience with the fact that she had to make the case all the way down to symptoms.

"Flares," she said. "Joints first — knees, hands, ankles if I really get lucky. Then fatigue so bad you sit on the edge of the bed and bargain with the room. Mouth ulcers. Fever that isn't a real fever. Everything hurts and none of it looks dramatic enough for anybody to care."

She held up her right hand. The knuckles were swollen, skin stretched shiny over the joints. The inflammation had a wet look to it, as if the hand had been overfilled.

"Last time I ran out before Marcus, I started cutting tablets in half with a pill splitter. Then quarters. Then every other day because I wanted the bottle to keep lying to me a little longer. Three weeks like that and I couldn't open a jar. Couldn't button my pants. Couldn't turn my apartment key without using both hands."

She rested the bad hand on the roof of the Civic and flexed it once, winced, stopped.

"I had just started a warehouse data-entry job. Sitting down work. Easy, right? Except your fingers still have to bend. I called in one day, then another, then a week. They let me go after day four and the temp agency stopped calling back. That's what happens."

She said it flat, like she had told it before to people with clipboards and warm eyes and no power to fix any part of it.

"Can I see your left hand," Carmen asked.

Rosa held it out. Carmen took it. The metacarpophalangeal joints were warm and boggy with active synovitis. She turned the hand gently and felt the swelling over the ring-finger knuckle. Rosa did not flinch, but Carmen could feel the effort of not flinching in the tendons.

For a moment it was only the hand in hers: heat in the joint, fluid in the tissue, the thick tender swell over the ring finger, the stale lotion smell on Rosa's skin, the tiny tremor of somebody holding still because being examined by a stranger in a parking lot was preferable to not being examined at all.

"This one's worse," Carmen said.

"I know."

"You should have imaging."

"With what money."

Carmen let go. Rosa put her hand back in her vest pocket and kept it there.

"Harry told you I was the clean case," Rosa said.

Carmen said nothing.

"He did. I can tell."

"Agnes did."

"Same difference."

Rosa leaned against the car again. Her body looked tired in the specific way Carmen recognized from patients who spent all day negotiating with pain instead of resting from it.

"Look, I'm not buying oxy in a Walmart lot," Rosa said. "I'm not nodding off in my car. I'm trying to stay on the med I was prescribed so my hands keep working long enough to get another job with benefits before my body gets even dumber ideas."

"That still doesn't make this safe."

"No," Rosa said. "It doesn't."

"You could be getting bad storage, expired stock, wrong manufacturer, no monitoring, no nothing."

"Yes."

"You know that."

"I'm standing in a nursing-home parking lot buying prescription drugs from somebody's nephew. Of course I know that."

Carmen didn't have an answer for that.

"Then why keep doing it."

"Because forty isn't three-eighty-nine." Rosa looked at her own knuckles. "I told you about the marketplace. I told you about the temp job. I stop taking the med, my hands go, I lose the work, and then I'm really —" She shook her head. "You felt it."

She bent to pick up one of the papers that had slipped out of the CVS bag. It was not a receipt. It was a printed estimate for a comprehensive eye exam and retinal screening from a clinic in Edison, the kind of sheet someone carried around because throwing it away would make the problem feel official.

"I keep everything," Rosa said. "Not because it helps. Because if I don't keep it, I start thinking maybe I imagined the numbers."

She shoved the paper back in the bag and looked at the building over Carmen's shoulder.

"I'm not stupid about Marcus either. I count before I leave. I don't ask him for extra. Once he offered to see if he could get me pain pills too and I said no because that's not what I need and I don't need a new disaster. Another time he told me he could maybe do prednisone and then came back and said no because the old lady pharmacist didn't like the blood-pressure history. Good. I don't need people improvising with my kidneys."

That detail snagged Carmen. "He offered first."

"Yeah. He offers things. He's a guy with inventory."

"And Agnes says no."

"Seems like it."

"You keep coming back anyway."

Rosa folded her arms tighter. "I come back for the hydroxychloroquine. I don't need to be in love with the operation."

The beeping from the loading dock stopped. For a second the lot went quiet except for traffic on Route 27 and the metallic tick of Rosa's cooling engine.

"If I report it," Carmen said, and heard how formal she sounded saying it, "you know what that could turn into."

"Yeah." Rosa did not blink. "Cops, board, whatever else rich people call when they want a mess cleaned up by force."

"And you're still here."

"I am."

"Why."

Rosa's mouth tightened. "Then I go back to stretching ninety days into one-forty. Cutting tablets. Trying to make the bottle last." She shifted her weight and winced when her knee caught. "Report it if you — I can't stop you. Just don't tell yourself you helped me."

They looked at each other in the heat.

"I don't know what I'm doing yet," Carmen said.

"OK." Rosa opened the driver's door and got in. She put the CVS bag on the passenger seat. "My refill's in November. Just so you know."

She pulled the door shut. The engine turned over on the second try — hesitation, catch, a belt complaining once before it settled. Carmen stepped back as Rosa pulled out of the space, brake lights glowing at the exit before the Civic took a left and merged into the afternoon traffic on Route 27 and was gone.

Carmen went in through the front doors with four minutes left before shift. The silk flowers Cliff had put out for the survey were still on the reception desk, already collecting dust along the fake petals. Her badge hit her scrub pocket when she clipped it on. At the sub-station Devon was awake now, staring at a glucometer like it had insulted him. Somewhere down the hall a television audience laughed too hard at something not funny.

In the medication room the day nurse was waiting with the controlled-substance log open beside a fresh pharmacy tote. Burt Kovacs's refill sheet was clipped to the top; generic carbidopa-levodopa had been substituted again. In the return bin, a discontinued trazodone card still held twenty-six sealed tablets, expiration next spring. Beside it sat two blister packs of rosuvastatin with nine tablets left in one and eleven in the other, both marked for destruction because the dose had changed and the family did not want the overfill.

"Cliff wants recounts on anything that even looks funny," the day nurse said without preamble. "Survey's got him twitching. If it's off, say it's off before I sign out."

Carmen set her bag down and washed her hands. The water in the med-room sink never got fully hot. She dried them on the brown paper towel that left lint in the webs of her fingers.

"I know how counts work," she said.

"I'm saying it because he said it six times at huddle and twice in my face."

The day nurse slid the log toward her. Carmen looked at the ruled lines, the initials, the dosages, the official story of what had moved and what had not. Controlleds were the only medications in the room that got treated like they were real. Everything else could disappear into delay, substitution, family confusion, pharmacy backlog, physician indifference, or a return bin with no hurry attached. But the narc book mattered. The narc book had signatures. The narc book had her license number waiting one bad audit away.

She opened the drawer and started counting with the day nurse watching. The plastic card pressed against her fingertips, tablet wells caught under her thumb, and sharp medicinal dust hung in the air. She said the numbers out loud and then said them again. Her hands moved automatically, but her mind kept splitting the room into paired columns the way Agnes's notebook had: official, actual; institutional, retail; clean case, not clean; harm inside, harm outside.

The day nurse pointed with her pen. "Burt's sheet came in. Generic again. He'll puke all over me tonight and then tell me I'm poisoning him."

"He's not wrong about the nausea."

"I know he's not wrong. I'm just tired."

Carmen initialed the line for receipt of the pharmacy tote. The pen clicked against the clipboard when she lifted it. In the return bin the trazodone card sat there like a small dumb sermon. Twenty-six tablets no one in administration would fight over because they were not controlled and no reimbursement report would ever care whether they were destroyed today or next week or never. On Agnes's pages the same kind of accumulation had become reserve, bridge, loss. On Rosa's side of the wall it had become the distance between forty dollars and three hundred eighty-nine.

"You good?" the day nurse asked.

Carmen counted again.

There was the official number. There was the second number behind it, the one that would turn the room hard and bright if she spoke it aloud. If she said it, Cliff would be in here within ten minutes with his tie crooked and his survey voice on, wanting signatures and incident forms and a chain of custody that would end, sooner or later, under 26NR03891. Maybe that was what should happen. Maybe that was the clean version. But clean was not the same as harmless and she had spent three days learning how little those things overlapped.

She thought of Harry telling Jack the money came from a certificate of deposit. She thought of Agnes writing LOSS without flinching. She thought of the woman on clonazepam and trazodone and nightly alcohol use who might stop breathing if Marcus worked alone. She thought of Rosa's hand in hers, hot and swollen, and the eye-exam estimate folded in the CVS bag like a bill from another planet.

"What'd you get?" the day nurse said.

Carmen wrote the official number where the form required it, kept the second count in her head, and went out to start med pass.
