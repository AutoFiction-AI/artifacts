# Chapter 5

Melanie's hard block numbers sat in Carmen's head all through the next day.

That night, Carmen waited until Devon left the station.

He did this every night around twelve-thirty — walked to the kitchen, ate whatever the dietary aide had left out, came back in twenty minutes smelling like butterscotch pudding. She'd never had a reason to track his schedule before. Tonight she had timed it on her watch, standing at the sub-station pretending to chart, marking the moment he pulled out his earbuds and shuffled toward the kitchen in his untied Nikes. Twelve thirty-one. She gave him two minutes' head start.

The hallway was dim at this hour, half the fluorescents off, the others buzzing at a frequency she could feel at the base of her skull. Wing A stretched ahead of her — a single corridor of closed doors and the faint ammonia smell that never fully left the linoleum, no matter how many times the overnight aide mopped. The AC had been struggling all week. Early September in central New Jersey and Lakeview Gardens was still holding heat like a kiln, the building's cinder-block walls radiating warmth back into the hallways hours after dark. She could feel it through the soles of her sneakers.

She carried her clipboard. She didn't need it — she wasn't charting, wasn't rounding, wasn't doing any of the things a clipboard authorized her to do at twelve-thirty in the morning. But the clipboard was habit, the same way the badge clipped to her scrub top was habit. The tools of a nurse who belonged in this hallway at this hour. Cover.

She passed Room 110 and heard Mrs. Delacroix making her sounds — a low continuous murmur that rose and fell with her breathing, each exhale catching just enough in her throat to turn air into voice. Carmen had checked on her at eleven. She was fine, or she was the same, which was what fine meant in Room 110.

The TV room was at the far end of the wing, past the supply closet and the handicapped bathroom, the farthest common area from the nurse's station. Not visible from Devon's desk. That was the point — the ring's point, she understood now, the reason they met here and not in someone's room.

The door was open. The television was off. Harry sat in his wheelchair at the card table, his back to the hallway, a newspaper folded in his lap. The only light was the green-white glow of the emergency exit sign above the far door, casting the room in a color that made everything look submerged.

Carmen stepped inside and closed the door behind her. The latch clicked.

"Mr. Zielinski." She stayed standing. "I need to talk to you."

Harry didn't move. His head was tilted forward the way it always was when he was sleeping — chin to chest, mouth slack, the posture of a man who'd lost track of the room and the hour and possibly the year. His breathing was slow, wet, heavy. A thin shine of drool on his chin.

"Harry."

She used his first name. She had never done that before — not in thirteen months of med passes and evening check-ins and gentle redirections when he wandered toward the fire exit asking for Eleanor. He was Mr. Zielinski, the way all her residents were formal names, because the formality was part of the care and Carmen did not cut corners.

He held the performance for two more seconds. She counted — one one-thousand, two one-thousand — watching for the seam, the place where the act would start to peel. She'd seen him break character once, in the hallway, and it had been fast, a light switch. But that had been accidental, a caught moment. This was something else.

Harry lifted his head. His spine straightened — not dramatically, he was eighty-four and his back didn't snap to anything, but the slouch was gone, the way a folded thing unfolds when you pick it up and shake it out. His shoulders squared against the back of the wheelchair. His chin came up. And his eyes, which she'd watched drift and search and lose focus a thousand times during med pass, locked onto her face with a precision that was almost physically uncomfortable. Like a flashlight pointed directly at her when she'd been expecting candlelight.

He wiped the drool from his chin with the back of his hand. One clean motion. Quick and economical, thumb catching the wet shine at the corner of his mouth. A man cleaning himself up because the costume was off and he didn't need the prop.

"Close the other door," he said.

His voice was different. She'd heard him coherent before — the tramadol correction, the hallway at 2:40 AM. The confused Harry spoke in fragments, rising intonations, questions that dissolved before they finished. This Harry's voice was flat and level. He finished what he said.

"It's closed," Carmen said.

"The other one." He nodded toward the far wall, where a second door led to the back hallway. She hadn't noticed it was cracked open — two inches, enough for sound to carry.

She crossed the room and pulled it shut. When she turned back, Harry had folded the newspaper and placed it on the card table. She could see the crossword from here. Completed, in ink, in handwriting that was small and square and precise — nothing like the shaky scrawl in his chart, the trembling Harold Zielinski who couldn't remember how to spell his own last name for the intake assessment.

He waited. He didn't ask why she was there. He sat in his wheelchair with his hands folded on the table, his faded blue cardigan pulled tight across his narrow chest. The room thinned out around that silence until it felt like she'd have to break it herself.

Carmen stayed standing. The clipboard edge bit into her palm. She loosened her grip before the plastic cracked.

"I know what you're doing," she said.

"Okay."

"The medications. I ran a full manual count last night — you're forty-seven pills short across four controlled substances. Oxycodone, alprazolam, tramadol, and hydrocodone." She heard herself falling into med-pass language, the clinical vocabulary that made everything procedural and clean. She stopped. "You're stealing pills. You and the others. Agnes, Lola, Earl — I've seen the meetings, Harry. The TV room, after midnight. I've seen you walk. Heel to toe, steady, at two-forty in the morning with a towel under your arm. You're clear-headed. You know exactly what you're doing. You're running something."

Harry's expression didn't change. He didn't deny, didn't flinch, didn't offer the scrambling panic she'd half-expected. His attention sharpened, but only by a hair. Not surprise. Just confirmation.

"Sit down," he said.

"I don't want to sit down."

"Then stand." He pulled the vinyl chair nearest to him away from the table, angling it toward her. The metal legs scraped on the linoleum, a sound that in the daytime would have been nothing but at this hour cut through the quiet like something tearing. "But you're going to want to hear this sitting."

Carmen sat. The chair exhaled under her weight. Old vinyl, cracked along the arms, the cushion held together with facility-issue tape that had gone amber and stiff. She could feel the split upholstery through her scrubs.

"How long," she said.

"Eighteen months. Give or take."

"How many of you."

"Five. Plus distribution."

"Names."

"You know the names."

She did. She'd known since the hallway, since the TV room at 1 AM weeks ago when the animated voices had gone slack at the sight of her, since the count sheets that kept coming up short on her shifts and nobody else's. She knew. She was asking him to say it because saying it made it something she could document, respond to, process through the professional channels that were supposed to exist for moments like this.

"Why are you here, Carmen?"

"Because forty-seven pills are missing and they were dispensed under my license number."

"I know that. That's not what I asked."

He watched her. His pale eyes — she'd always thought of them as watery, unfocused, the eyes of a man looking for someone who wasn't in the room. They weren't. They were sharp and light and specific, the color of old ice.

"Why are you here. In this room. Talking to me."

Carmen pressed her palms flat on her thighs. The fabric of her scrubs was damp — the room's heat, or her own, she couldn't tell.

"Because you need to understand what you've done to —" She stopped. Started again, lower. "Every pill that went missing came through my med pass. My name on the count sheet. My license number." She leaned forward. Her voice was getting quieter, which was what happened when she was truly angry — she didn't get louder, she got smaller, more exact. "If the state board pulls my records, I am the one they see. Not you." Her hand found the badge and she turned the clip between her fingers, the sharp laminate edge biting her thumb. "You're an eighty-four-year-old man in a wheelchair. A judge looks at you and it's supervised care, it's a facility failure. Me, I lose my license. I go to prison." She swallowed. "I lose Reggie."

The room was quiet enough that she could hear the ventilation system laboring two hallways away. The AC compressor kicked on and off in cycles — she'd timed those too, during long overnight shifts with nothing to count but the intervals between mechanical sounds. Right now it was off, and the air in the TV room sat heavy and still.

Harry hadn't moved. He let her words hang there in the heat until she could feel sweat cooling under her arms.

"You done?" he said.

"Don't talk to me like this is a board meeting."

Something shifted in his face. His mouth tightened a fraction. Acknowledgment, maybe. The recognition that a number was real.

"Fair enough," he said. "You're scared. I hear that." He waited. "But you need to hear me now."

"I'm looking at a federal crime."

"You're looking at five people." He counted them on his fingers, not for drama but the way he counted everything — precisely, by the unit. "Me. Agnes. Lola. Earl. Burt. Burt's eighty-eight. He's got actual dementia — not like me, actual. Agnes is seventy-six with stroke residuals. Lola has Parkinson's. Earl's an eighty-one-year-old veteran." He stopped there, the way he'd stopped before when he'd started to say something about Earl. Whatever it was, he kept it. "We live here. We don't leave."

"That doesn't change what you did."

"It changes what happens if you report it." He pressed his hands flat on the table. She could see his fingers clearly in the exit sign's light — long, thin, steady. She'd watched those same hands tremble over a medication cup, shake while reaching for a glass of water, fumble a spoon in the dining room. They weren't shaking now. "You call the police. The state. The DEA. Pick one. What happens."

"They investigate."

"They investigate. Then they charge. Possession with intent — Schedule II, federal mandatory minimums." He let that sit. "For five nursing home residents. One of whom can't tell you what year it is."

"I know what the charges would be, Harry."

"I'm not talking about charges." He pressed his thumb against the edge of the table. "Agnes in a courtroom, a hundred and ten pounds. Lola shaking. Burt not understanding why someone put handcuffs on him."

"So your plan is — what, look pathetic?"

"We don't need a plan for that." He shifted in the wheelchair. His left knee popped — she'd heard it before, always the left, and now she wondered how long he'd been performing the wince that went with it when actually it was just a knee.

He unfolded the newspaper. Under the crossword were three business envelopes, a pharmacy notice folded into quarters, and a money-order receipt clipped to one of the envelopes with a silver paper clip bent almost straight. He slid the receipt across the table with one finger.

"Read it."

Carmen looked down.

*Denise Washington. Trenton, New Jersey. $400.00.*

The name hit her from Earl's chart before it hit anywhere else. Emergency contact. Daughter. One 609 number, no alternate.

"Earl's daughter," Harry said. "First Monday every month. He tells her it's a VA benefit because that's easier than telling her her father sells oxycodone out of a nursing home."

Carmen looked up.

"His pension pays the building and leaves him fifty dollars. Five-zero. That's the whole number. You report tonight, that envelope doesn't go out. Denise gets nothing from him this month except a call she can't use."

"You're trying to buy me with somebody else's daughter."

"No." He tapped the receipt once. "I'm telling you the damage has a name on it." He slid the receipt back under the newspaper. "Then there's the facility. Cliff's running a building where five residents moved pills for eighteen months under his nose."

"And Lakeview gets cited."

"Shut down. Maybe." He watched her. "A hundred and twelve residents who had nothing to do with this get relocated."

He let that settle.

"You know where they go," he said.

"Wherever has beds." Carmen heard herself say it flatly, without inflection, because she did know. She'd worked per diem at three other facilities before Lakeview. She knew what wherever-has-beds looked like — the places that smelled worse, where the call bells rang three times before anyone answered, where the food was Sysco institutional and the medication errors weren't careful accounting like this but genuine neglect.

"Wherever has beds," Harry repeated. "That's on you. If you report."

"That's not fair."

"No."

They sat with that. The institutional clock on the wall — round, white-faced, never accurate — read twelve-fifty-one. Carmen could hear, very faintly, Mrs. Delacroix's murmuring from down the hall. A continuous sound, almost restful if you didn't think about what it meant.

"What do you actually want?" Harry said.

She opened her mouth. She didn't know. That was the problem — she'd come in here with a fact, with forty-seven pills and a count sheet and a license number, and she'd expected the fact to tell her what to do. The fact didn't tell her anything. It just sat there. Forty-seven pills. No next step attached.

"I want it to stop," she said.

"Okay. We stop. Flush everything, shut it down." He paused. "Then what."

"The count stabilizes. I go back to charting normally."

"And the forty-seven pills already in the system?" He watched her. "Your name, your shift, your signature. The three oxy from six weeks ago, the five alprazolam the week after." He waited. "You flagged those. And moved on."

Carmen's stomach shifted. She'd been so focused forward — what else could go missing, what else could go wrong — that she hadn't looked at the trail behind her. The count sheets she'd already signed. The discrepancies she'd reported to the charge nurse and then accepted the charge nurse's dismissal of. Months of documentation that showed, at minimum, sustained failure to catch what was happening on her shifts under her license.

"Even if we stop tonight," Harry said, "you're exposed. Any audit pulls your dispensing records. Chronic shortages on your shifts, documented and signed." He paused. "That's not a charting error."

"So you're telling me I'm already screwed."

"I'm telling you that calling the police puts your license number on the first line." He watched her. The exit sign's glow made his face look carved from something pale and cold, but his eyes kept moving, catching every shift in her mouth, every breath she took too fast. "Give me a week."

"No."

"One week. Let me show you the whole picture. The buyers, their situations. Where the money goes. Who depends on it. Then you decide."

"I've already decided."

"No you haven't." His voice was quiet. His hands stayed still on the table. "You didn't go to Cliff. You didn't call the hotline."

She started to speak.

"You came here at midnight," he said. "And you shut both doors."

Carmen's hands stopped moving. Her nails were sunk into the split vinyl arm of the chair, caught there without her noticing. She pulled them free and felt the tacky drag of old tape against her fingertips.

He was right.

She had come here. Not to Cliff's office, not to the phone in the break room where the state board hotline poster hung in red block letters. She had come to the TV room. At midnight. She had closed the door behind her and sat down when he told her to. None of that belonged to the clean version of this story, the one with a report and a supervisor and somebody else taking over. She was in a warm dark room making private terms with a resident, and she could not tell anyone that without making herself part of it.

"One week," she said.

It came out rough, forced through something in her chest that didn't want to let it pass.

"One week. You show me whatever you think I need to see. Then I decide." She leaned forward. "And nothing moves in that week — no pills sorted, nothing packaged, nothing goes out. The operation stops while I'm looking at it."

Harry considered this. The pause stretched. She could almost feel him sorting inventory, timing, the people already waiting on the other end. His face didn't change.

"Okay," he said.

Carmen stood. Her scrubs peeled away from the chair's vinyl with a sound like tape pulling off skin. She picked up her clipboard from the floor where she'd dropped it — she hadn't noticed dropping it, didn't remember the moment, which bothered her because Carmen noticed things, that was the whole problem, she noticed too much.

"We had trouble with the pharmacy," Harry said. "Few years back."

He said it flat, with no heat in it at all. Carmen had her hand on the door.

"Cost savings. Half our prescriptions got delayed or substituted." He paused. "That's not the whole story. But that's where it started."

"I didn't ask how it started."

"I know," Harry said. "But you will."

Carmen pulled the door open. The hallway's half-light poured in, fluorescent and thin. The TV room's green glow stayed in her eyes for a second after she stepped out. She walked away without looking back.

---

She sat in her car in the parking lot with the engine off and the windows down.

September hadn't broken the heat yet. The air at one-fifteen was as heavy as it had been at noon, damp enough to sit on her skin. The dumpster smell reached her parking spot anyway, sweet rot under hot asphalt. Crickets worked the strip of weeds by the curb hard enough to sound mechanical.

She kept seeing his back come up straight. The crossword filled in ink. The hand that had trembled over pill cups wiping his chin clean in one pass. The bent paper clip. Denise Washington, Trenton. Four hundred dollars. Eighteen months. Five people. Forty-seven pills under her name.

Her phone buzzed. She'd forgotten she had it — she must have grabbed it from the medication room on the way out. Her mother: *You okay mija? Reggie had bad dream, he's asleep now. Don't worry. Call me tomorrow.*

The text was forty minutes old.

Carmen looked down at her hands. Crescent marks from the chair arm still striped her fingertips. Her license number sat under every shortage she'd signed.

She opened the message thread and typed, *Got stuck finishing paperwork. Kiss him for me.* She stared at *paperwork* until the word looked stupid and harmless. Then she sent it anyway.

*You didn't go to Cliff. You didn't call the hotline. You came here at midnight. And you shut both doors.*

She could still report it in the morning. Cliff came in around eight-thirty if he bothered to come in at all. The state board hotline number was on the poster by the break-room microwave, red block letters above a coffee stain shaped like Florida. She knew what the call would sound like because she'd made versions of it before — not about diversion, never that, but about pressure ulcers nobody had charted and a kitchen aide working with a fever and a med cart left unlocked during shift change. Date. Time. Nature of concern. Witnesses. Immediate risk to residents.

This one fell apart halfway through.

She had the manual count. She had the discrepancies on her shifts. She had a private conversation in a dark room with both doors shut and no witness but Harry Zielinski, who by breakfast would be back in his wheelchair asking for Eleanor. She had an addressed envelope she hadn't confiscated, a name from Earl's chart that was now attached to a number and a city. If she wanted to make the clean report, she'd have to leave Denise out of it. If she wanted to tell the whole truth, she'd have to explain why she knew about Denise in the first place. Either version made her look dirty.

She leaned back against the headrest and closed her eyes. The vinyl was hot against her scalp. Somewhere inside the building Mrs. Delacroix would still be making that low engine sound in her throat. Devon would come back from the kitchen sleepy and sugared, sit at the station, maybe put one earbud back in. Earl would be in bed or pretending to be in bed, coughing into the dark with fifty dollars to his name after Medicaid took its share. Denise would be asleep in Trenton or not asleep, either one, waiting on money she thought came from the government. Reggie was across the river with his grandmother, seven years old, having bad dreams about whatever seven-year-olds dreamed about when the grown-ups around them were tired all the time.

She opened her eyes.

The thing that made her sick was not that Harry had an argument. It was that he had paperwork. Numbers. Names. The shape of a system. Carmen knew how much damage a form could do once someone filled in the blanks. She also knew how fast a person turned into a line item when the form got far enough from the body. The forms never had a box for who could absorb the hit and who couldn't. They just had signatures.

She set the phone face-down on the passenger seat and started the car. The AC coughed hot air at her knees before it cooled. In the rearview mirror, Lakeview stayed low and lit against the dark. She didn't go back inside for the number on the poster. She drove.
