# Chapter 17

Carmen was in the medication room doing the evening count when she heard it. Not the words at first — just the cadence. Every nurse learns the difference between the TV room's daytime murmur and the local news anchor's emergency tone, the slight tightening in the broadcaster's delivery that means something happened nearby, something with a suspect, something with a mugshot.

She set down the clipboard and walked to the TV room.

It was mostly empty at 8:40 PM — dinner was over, the last visitors had signed out at eight, and most of the Wing A residents were back in their rooms. Burt was dozing in the recliner nearest the screen, his mouth open, one hand curled around the armrest like he was holding onto something in a dream. The volume was up too loud because nobody had adjusted it after Mrs. Petroski had gone back to her room, and Mrs. Petroski was functionally deaf.

The anchor was describing an arrest in Edison. Prescription drug distribution. A man named DeShaun Briley, thirty-seven, picked up during a traffic stop with approximately four hundred tablets of oxycodone, eighty alprazolam, and an undetermined quantity of hydrocodone in the trunk of his vehicle. Possession with intent to distribute, state and federal charges. Bond set at seventy-five thousand.

Carmen didn't recognize the name. She didn't recognize the face in the mugshot — a heavy young man, scared-looking under the flat police lighting, his left eye slightly swollen like he'd been rubbing it or hadn't slept. But she knew that number. Marcus's new buyer had been taking orders that size.

Her hand went to her badge and stayed there. The news anchor moved on to a car accident on Route 18.

She stood in the doorway for another thirty seconds, watching the screen without seeing it, while her mind ran the chain. Briley to Marcus. Marcus to Lakeview. Lakeview to the count discrepancies documented under her name, her license number, her signature on every shift-end log for the past seven months. If Briley had Marcus's phone number in his contacts — if he had a text, a single text, with Marcus's name or a location or a time — investigators would pull Marcus's records. And Marcus had visited this building every week for over a year, signing in at the front desk as Lola's nephew, his name in a visitor log that no one cross-referenced because no one at Lakeview cross-referenced anything.

Carmen turned off the TV room light on her way out. Burt didn't stir.

She went to Harry's room. He was sitting up in bed reading the Trenton Times, his faded blue cardigan buttoned wrong — top button through the second hole — and his reading glasses taped at the left temple where the arm had broken six weeks ago. He looked up when Carmen knocked on the open door, and his face told her he already knew.

"You saw it," she said.

"I saw it." He folded the paper and placed it in his lap. "Edison. That's twenty minutes from here."

"It's the volume buyer. The quantities match."

Harry took off his glasses. Without them, his eyes were sharper — the lenses were for reading, but they also softened his face, made it rounder, made him look more like the befuddled old man he played during daylight hours. "I've been trying Marcus's phone for an hour. His personal one. Goes straight to voicemail."

"Since when do you have Marcus's personal number?"

Harry gave her the flat look he reserved for obvious arithmetic. He didn't answer.

"When's the last time he came in?" Carmen asked.

"Ten days ago. He hasn't been here since the meeting." That was what they called it — the night Marcus had been confronted, had argued back, had been cut off by Earl in a single sentence that ended more than the conversation. "Sit down, Carmen."

She didn't sit down. She kept one hand on the doorframe, nails pressing into the paint. "If they trace Briley to Marcus — any connection, a phone record, a text, anything — they follow Marcus here. They pull the visitor log and they see his name every week for fourteen months. They look at the medication records and they see my count discrepancies, documented and signed. Every one of them mine."

"Sit down."

"I can't sit down right now."

Harry put his glasses on the nightstand and lined them up parallel to the edge, beside the pen and folded paper already there. "We clean house," he said.

"Everything?"

"Everything we can touch. Pills. Agnes's notebooks. The phone. The visitor log and your old count sheets stay where they are."

Carmen leaned against the doorframe. The hallway behind her smelled as it always did at night — disinfectant and the warm-skin scent of people who didn't go outside, the fading trace of dinner's meatloaf, hand sanitizer from the dispensers every fifteen feet. Familiar smells. She'd worked in this hallway for thirteen months and stopped noticing them, same as she'd stopped noticing her own apartment most days. Now each layer came back sharp and separate because she might be leaving all of it under circumstances she didn't choose.

"I'll tell Agnes," she said.

"Tell her about the notebooks."

"I know."

She found Agnes at 9:15. Agnes was still awake — she rarely slept before midnight, a habit from four decades of pharmacy shifts that ended at 10 PM. Her roommate, Mrs. Delacroix, was asleep and snoring wetly in the bed nearest the door, the sound of a woman whose body worked hard just to breathe. Agnes was sitting upright in the far bed with a crossword puzzle book open on her lap. She hadn't filled in any squares.

"I heard it," Agnes said before Carmen could speak. "The volume on the TV was up. Mrs. Petroski's hearing."

"Harry wants everything gone tonight."

Agnes closed the crossword book. Her hands were steady — the left-side weakness from her stroke showed in her gait when she walked, a slight drag in the left foot, but her grip had recovered fully within the first year — and she placed the book on the nightstand with the same deliberate precision she placed everything. Pharmacist's hands. Everything in its location. "My notebooks."

"Yeah."

"There are four. Composition books. Black-and-white marbled covers." Agnes's voice was clinical, but the words came faster than usual — closer together, compressed under pressure. "Under my mattress. Under the fitted sheet, between the mattress and the mattress pad. Not under the bed frame."

Carmen looked at her. Agnes's face was what it always was — precise, impatient, stripped clean. But her hands had moved from the crossword book to the mattress edge and were pressing down, her fingertips whitening against the hospital-grade fitted sheet.

"I'll come get them after Devon falls asleep," Carmen said. "Eleven-thirty."

"I'll have them out."

---

Devon was asleep by 11:45. Carmen checked twice — at 11:30, when he was still scrolling his phone with the audio off, and again fifteen minutes later when the phone was face-down on his chest and his breathing had gone slow and even. He'd picked up the overnight because the regular night aide had called out, and Devon worked overnights like he worked every shift — minimally, resentfully, one earbud in, his attention dimmed to whatever frequency let him avoid being fired for a third time. He was twenty-three. He'd keep sleeping.

Carmen went to Agnes's room first.

Agnes was standing by her bed in a housecoat that had belonged to someone else — the facility distributed donated clothing to residents whose families didn't bring enough, and Agnes's daughter Helen sent the same three outfits in rotation, all a size too large because Helen still pictured the woman she'd been during the stroke recovery, fifteen pounds lighter and unable to say the word "prescription" without it coming out wrong. The housecoat was a faded floral print with a zipper up the front. Agnes held the notebooks out in both hands, palms flat under them so the spines wouldn't bend.

Four composition books. Black-and-white marbled covers. The kind sold at every CVS and Walgreens in New Jersey for two dollars and forty-nine cents, the kind Carmen had bought for Reggie's school supplies in September.

She took them. They were heavier than they looked — filled cover to cover, no blank pages. The top one fell open in her arms and she saw Agnes's handwriting: small, square, pharmacist's print that could have been typed. Columns of drug names with dosages. Buyer codes — not names, just two-letter abbreviations. Expiration dates noted in red ink. Margin notes: *Do not combine w/ warfarin. Check renal function. Ro — skip if on new antibiotic, interaction risk. Jn — monitor for signs of tolerance escalation (none observed).* One page had a hand-drawn table of pill counts going back months, each row dated, each column a different controlled substance, every number precise to the individual tablet.

Eighteen months of pharmaceutical expertise applied to an illegal operation with the same rigor Agnes had applied to forty-two years behind a pharmacy counter in Edison. These notebooks were evidence of a federal crime. They were also the most careful, most specific, most conscientious pharmaceutical records in the building — better than what Carmen charted, better than what Dr. Prasad's prescriptions produced, better than anything the state survey had reviewed.

"I'll burn them behind the loading dock," Carmen said.

Agnes looked at the notebooks in Carmen's arms. She was a small woman — five-two, gray hair cut short because she could manage it without help, wire-rimmed glasses reflecting the hallway's fluorescent light. She didn't say anything.

"Agnes."

"Go," Agnes said. Her voice was flat and fast.

Carmen went.

She started with the pills. Harry had the remaining stockpile in two shoeboxes in the back of his closet, behind a row of Reader's Digest condensed books that had come with the room. The shoeboxes were the part they could still erase: hidden stock that tied the signed discrepancies in the med binder to a real operation. She retrieved them while Harry sat in his wheelchair by the window, watching the dark parking lot.

"Thirty-six oxycodone," he said without turning around. "Fourteen alprazolam. Twenty-one tramadol. Some non-controlled."

"It doesn't matter anymore, Harry."

"No." He turned the wheelchair around. His hands were on the wheels, and the motion was smooth but slow. Carmen couldn't tell if the slowness was exhaustion or age or the performance settling back into his body.

"I suppose it doesn't," he said.

Carmen carried the shoeboxes to the handicapped bathroom at the end of Wing A and locked the door. She flushed the oxycodone first, then the alprazolam, tramadol, metformin, lisinopril, rosuvastatin — all of it in batches the old plumbing could handle. The white tablets hit the water looking like the mints her mother kept in a dish by the front door. The pipes knocked in the walls. By the time the last bag was empty, forty-five minutes had passed and sweat had gone through her scrub top.

When she unlocked the door and stepped into the hallway, Earl was standing outside the medication room.

He was leaning against the wall, breathing through his mouth. His nighttime clothes — sweatpants, a flannel shirt with a fraying collar — hung on a frame that had been losing weight for months, though Carmen only registered it now, standing in the half-lit hallway with an empty shoebox in each hand. His big hands were flat against the wall behind him, the posture of a man who needed the wall more than he wanted to admit.

"Earl, you should be in bed."

"Harry said help."

"I don't need—" She stopped. She looked at him. He was 81 years old and his cough had been getting worse since at least September — she could hear it through the walls during her shifts sometimes, a deep wet rattle that sounded like it was pulling something loose — and he was standing in the hallway at half past midnight offering to carry things. "Okay. Take these back to Harry's room."

She handed him the empty shoeboxes. He took them and turned down the hall, and three steps in the cough hit — a real cough, not for show, the kind that bent him forward at the waist and made his shoulders shake. He stopped and leaned against the wall and coughed into a handkerchief he pulled from his pocket, and the sound of it filled the hallway, wet and thick and rattling. When it passed, he folded the handkerchief and put it back without looking at it. Carmen had been a nurse long enough to know what that meant — the not-looking. You don't check the handkerchief when you already know what you might find and you've decided not to find out.

"Earl."

"I'm fine." Said once. Flat. Finished. He kept walking.

Lola was at the junction of Wing A and the main hallway, white ceramic mug braced in both hands, her eyes moving between Devon's desk and the nurse's station. Lakeview Gardens was printed on the mug in chipped blue letters. The coffee shook in small rings. Her jaw was set tight.

"Mija," she said when Carmen passed. "How much longer?"

"Almost done, Lola. Stay here."

Lola nodded. She brought the mug closer to her chest, holding it against the front of her nightgown, and her gold crucifix clinked against the ceramic.

Carmen got the notebooks from where she'd left them on the supply closet shelf and went out the back. The loading dock sat in the spill of a single yellow bulb — concrete, rusting ramp, dumpster, chain-link fence, dead grass. Not visible from the front.

She dragged a metal trash can to the center of the dock, tore pages from the first notebook, and lit them with a match from the break room. The smoke went white and thin and smelled like school paper.

She was midway through the second notebook when Agnes appeared at the back door.

Carmen hadn't heard it open. Agnes was standing at the edge of the loading dock in her donated housecoat and slippers, her arms at her sides, the cold November air moving through the thin fabric. The firelight caught her glasses and made her eyes invisible behind two small rectangles of reflected orange.

Carmen stopped tearing pages. "You should be inside. It's cold."

Agnes didn't move. She watched the fire. The pages curled, blackened, fell apart — handwriting she'd put down over fourteen months, catalogues of medications and dosage calculations and interaction flags, the whole pharmaceutical architecture of the operation documented with the rigor she'd applied to every prescription she'd filled for four decades. The paper kept resisting for a second before it gave.

"I started those notebooks the first week Harry asked me to join," Agnes said. Her voice was faster than usual — the words closer together, compressed, more data per sentence. Her hands were at her sides and they were not sorting, not organizing, not giving themselves a job. They were just hanging there. "Fourteen months of inventory. Every tablet accounted for, every interaction flagged, every expiration date tracked to the day. I maintained buyer profiles. Rosa's lupus medication schedule. Derek's insulin timing and when the metformin needed to coordinate. Janine's tolerance levels — I was monitoring for signs of escalating use, which I did not observe." She paused. "Nobody asked me to keep those records. I kept them because that is what a pharmacist does."

Carmen tore another fistful of pages and fed them into the trash can. The fire was hotter now, the draft pulling the flame up, the metal warming under her hand when she steadied the can's rim.

"This is the only professional work I have done in three years," Agnes said.

Carmen looked at her. Agnes did not fold her arms against the cold or step back from the smoke. The toes of her slippers darkened against the damp concrete, and she kept watching the pages cave in.

Carmen went back to burning. By twenty past one, all four notebooks were ash. Agnes stayed there until the marbled covers blackened, curled, and dropped into the gray powder at the bottom of the can. Then she turned and went back inside without saying goodnight.

Earl appeared in the hallway as Carmen came through the back door, carrying Lola's cookie tin.

"Lola said to get rid of it," Earl said. He was leaning against the wall again, his hand braced on the doorframe of the linen closet. His breathing was shallow and wheezy.

"I'll take it." She held out her hand and he gave her the tin. She dumped it in the dumpster.

The prepaid phone was last. She deleted the seventeen texts, carried the cheap flip phone to the loading dock, crushed it under her heel, snapped the SIM card in half, and threw both into the dumpster.

When she came back inside, her hands smelled like smoke and hot plastic. She washed them at the sink in the medication room with the orange antibacterial soap, then washed them a second time. Her hands still smelled like burning.

---

By four in the morning, it was done.

Carmen sat in the TV room. The overhead light was off. The television was off. The room was lit only by the hallway fluorescents coming through the open door, a blue-white half-light that made the vinyl chairs look colorless and the walls look like they were receding. She could hear the building's heating system — a low drone that had been the background noise of her working life for thirteen months — and Burt's snoring carrying down the hall from 116A, and, if she listened, Earl's cough. One cough, then silence, then two more, muffled by the wall and the closed door but audible in the 4 AM stillness of a building where nobody was supposed to be awake.

Harry wheeled himself in at 4:15. He'd changed into his blue cardigan — the good one, the one he wore to every ring meeting. He parked his wheelchair by the card table where Agnes used to spread out her notebooks and the ziplock bags and the envelopes of cash. The table was bare except for an old coffee ring, and Harry looked at it.

"Done?" he said.

"It's done. Pills, notebooks, phone. Lola's tin."

Harry nodded. His hands rested flat on his thighs, still for once, the skin spotted and thin over the swollen knuckles.

"This is over," Carmen said. "No more pills, no more buyers, no more anything."

Harry didn't argue. He'd argued with Marcus — coldly, precisely, with the weight of every number he'd ever counted — but he didn't argue with Carmen. Not tonight.

"Fourteen months," he said.

Carmen rubbed the back of her neck. The ponytail was loose and the elastic was barely holding. "Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Add it up." She could still smell the smoke on her scrubs. "Not tonight."

Harry looked at her. The hallway light caught the side of his face — the thin skin over the cheekbone, the pale eyes. He didn't argue with that, either.

They sat. The building settled around them — the heating system cycling, the squeak of a mattress from someone turning over in a room down the hall, the small mechanical sounds of a facility built in the late seventies and maintained the minimum amount required to pass state inspection. Through the window, the parking lot was dark and empty. Beyond it, the courtyard's three trees stood bare, their branches sharp against the brown suburban sky, all the leaves gone since late October.

"What are you going to do?" Harry asked.

"Go home. Sleep. Come back tomorrow and do my job."

"That's not what I mean."

She knew what he meant. She didn't answer. She tightened her ponytail with both hands and looked at the empty card table. The coffee ring sat near one corner. Beside it the laminate held a few neat dents where Agnes had pressed hard enough with her pen to mark the table through the pages. Carmen set her thumb over them, then pulled her hand back.

"I'm going to check on everyone," she said, and stood up.

"Carmen."

She stopped at the doorway.

Harry was looking at his hands. "Thank you," he said.

She nodded. She left.

Agnes was in bed, awake, staring at the ceiling with her glasses still on. The ceiling was acoustic tile, water-stained in one corner from a leak that maintenance had patched but not painted. Mrs. Delacroix's snoring was the only sound. Agnes's housecoat hung over the chair by the window, one sleeve dusted with gray ash. Carmen stood in the doorway for a second, then moved on.

Lola's room. Lola was asleep, curled on her right side, the rosary beads wound through her left hand. Her right hand rested on the pillow near her face, and even in sleep the tremor was there — a fine vibration in her fingers that moved the beads against the cotton pillowcase, a faint clicking sound like something small and persistent ticking down.

Earl and Burt in 116A. Burt was asleep, had been asleep through everything — his decline so steady now that the ring ending meant nothing to a man who could no longer remember what the ring was. He was on his back with his mouth open, his wispy white hair pressed flat against the pillow, his long musician's hands slack on the blanket. The Hungarian-English dictionary that used to hold the pill stash was gone — Carmen had moved it to the supply closet months ago, after his fall, and it was still there, hollowed out and empty, a dead artifact of a dead operation. Earl was in the other bed with his eyes closed, but his breathing was wrong — too controlled, too careful, the breathing of a man who was pretending to sleep so nobody would ask him how he was feeling. Carmen stood in the doorway and listened to his breathing and heard, beneath it, the faint wet rattle that had been there for months and was not getting better.

She went back to the nurse's station and did her charting. Overnight rounds: checked on Mr. Kovacs at 11 PM, 1 AM, 3 AM. Checked on Mr. Washington, Mrs. Fernandez, Mrs. Chen. Medication count documented as correct for the cart and cabinet at end of shift. The discrepancy reports from earlier shifts were still clipped in the binder under her name. What was gone was the off-book evidence: the shoeboxes in Harry's closet, Agnes's notebooks, Marcus's texts. She signed the log with the same signature she signed everything — C. Reyes, LPN — and put the clipboard back on its hook.

The front door beeped at 5:48 AM.

Tammy came around the corner in scrub pants and a winter coat, travel mug in one hand. She set the mug on the desk, hung her coat in the locker-room nook, clipped on her badge, and stopped when she saw Carmen.

"You're still here."

"Picked up the overnight." The excuse came out too easily. "Devon needed coverage and I was already—"

She stopped.

"I stayed," she said.

Tammy looked at the loose hair, the red around Carmen's eyes, the smoke still caught in her scrubs. Then she picked up her mug again and said nothing.

Tammy put her bag in her locker. She started her rounds.

Carmen gathered her things. She signed out on the overnight log. She put on her coat — it was the same coat she'd worn last November, a black puffy jacket from Old Navy that she'd bought on sale for twenty-nine dollars and that had a broken zipper she kept meaning to fix — and slung her bag over her shoulder and walked toward the exit.

She passed Harry's room.

The door was mostly closed. Lakeview policy required doors to remain openable from the hall, not shut fully, but Harry had pulled it to within an inch. Through the gap, in the strip of visible room, Carmen saw him sitting in his wheelchair by the nightstand. He had something in his hand. A phone — not the prepaid, which was in pieces in the dumpster behind the building, but a different one. A cell phone she had never seen. Small, black, a flip phone like the prepaid but not the prepaid. He was holding it to his ear.

He was talking. His voice was low and Carmen couldn't hear the words from the hallway — just the rhythm, the cadence of short sentences delivered with the controlled precision of his real register. Not the performance. Not the wandering mumble about Eleanor and what day was it and who were these people. The real Harry, at ten minutes to six in the morning, calling someone on a phone that Carmen didn't know existed, saying things she couldn't hear to a person she couldn't identify.

She stood in the hallway for three seconds. She could have pushed the door open. She could have walked in and asked who he was talking to and why there was another phone and why the man who had just sat in the TV room and agreed that it was over was already making calls before the sun came up.

She didn't.

She kept moving. At the front door she pushed out into the morning. The parking lot was dark and cold and the wind came across it flat, unbroken — the courtyard's bare trees did nothing, and the building didn't block it from this angle. Carmen walked to her car with her coat unzipped because the zipper was broken and her hands in her pockets because they were cold and still smelled like smoke despite the soap, despite the washing, despite the fact that the notebooks and the pills and the phone were all gone now and there was nothing left to smell like except the memory of having done it.

She got in the car and sat with her hands on the steering wheel and the engine off. The dashboard clock said 5:53. In an hour and seven minutes she needed to be at her mother's house to drop off Reggie's lunch, and in two hours and seven minutes Reggie needed to be at school, and in nine hours and seven minutes she needed to be back here for her regular shift. The math of her life, the same math, the same numbers that didn't change because she'd spent the night flushing pills down a toilet and burning a pharmacist's notebooks on a loading dock. Forty-seven thousand a year. Six-eighty a month in loan payments. Twelve hundred rent. Her license number on every shift log, every count sheet, every controlled substance record at Lakeview Gardens Rehabilitation and Nursing Center.

She started the car. She drove home.
