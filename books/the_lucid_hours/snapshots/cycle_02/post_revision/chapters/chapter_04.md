# Chapter 4

The charting system at Lakeview was a hybrid — half-digital, half paper — that nobody had fully committed to in either direction. Carmen sat at the Wing A sub-station at 2:37 AM, entering vital signs from her 1 AM rounds into the computer while the paper backup waited on a clipboard beside her. The terminal was old enough that it hummed. The screen cast a blue-white wash over her hands and the laminate counter, which was peeling at the corner where someone had picked at it during hundreds of similar overnight hours.

She'd taken the double for the same reason she always did. The car insurance was due in nine days and she was $140 short. The overnight differential was $3.50 more per hour, not enough by itself to close a $140 hole. The whole double would do it and still leave gas money if she didn't eat out. She hadn't eaten since the turkey sandwich from the vending machine at 10 PM, and she could feel the deficit in her concentration, a lag between noticing something on the screen and understanding what it said.

The building made its night sounds around her. The HVAC cycled on with a low shudder she felt in the counter surface before she heard it. Room 110 — Mrs. Delacroix — produced the same low, continuous murmur she'd been making for the seven months Carmen had worked this wing, a sound Carmen had stopped hearing the way you stop hearing a refrigerator. A toilet flushed somewhere in B Wing, probably Devon using the staff bathroom, which meant he was at least awake, which was an improvement over last Tuesday when she'd found him face-down on his folded arms at the nurse's station with a podcast about cryptocurrency playing through one earbud.

Carmen finished the vitals entry for Room 112A — Lola Fernandez, blood pressure 128 over 76, pulse 72, temp 97.8 — and tabbed to the next field. Her eyes were dry. The building's air was always dry, but at night the dryness concentrated, and by 3 AM every shift her contact lenses felt like they'd been sitting on a dashboard in July.

She'd been thinking about the pill count. It kept surfacing with a splinter's timing: absent until she moved wrong, then suddenly all she could feel. Three oxycodone, five alprazolam. The charge nurse's dismissal. Lola's mouth opening for the pill check with the smoothness of someone who'd practiced the motion. The voices in the TV room — four, five people talking in complete, coherent sentences at one in the morning, and then silence and slack faces the moment she appeared in the doorway.

She couldn't make the pieces add up to anything she was ready to name. The count was wrong, and the patients were wrong, and those two facts pointed in a direction she didn't want to follow because following it meant paperwork and phone calls and an investigation that would land on her shifts, her counts, her license number on the dispensing log.

She clicked save on the vitals screen and stretched her neck. The sub-station sat at the elbow of Wing A's L-shape, which meant she could see down the short leg of the hall — six rooms, all doors closed except 118, whose occupant, Mr. Petrelli, insisted on sleeping with his door open because he'd been a fireman and believed in egress — but the long leg, where the sicker residents and the singles were, angled out of sight past the linen closet.

Movement registered in her peripheral vision before she consciously looked. A shape in the long hallway, just past the angle.

She turned her head.

Harry Zielinski was walking.

Everything familiar about Harry's gait was gone: the shuffle she'd guided through a hundred med passes, the arthritic list, the wall-seeking hands she'd documented in his mobility assessment three months ago when she marked him as requiring assistance for ambulation.

He was walking. Upright. His spine was straight, or as straight as eighty-four years would allow — a natural curve instead of a hunch. His feet moved with deliberate spacing, each step placed and lifted, heel to ball, like a man who trusted his knees. His cardigan hung open over his pajama top and swung lightly with each step. In his right hand he carried something wrapped in a white bathroom towel, pinned close to his thigh and meant to disappear.

He was thirty feet away. The hallway was at half-light — every other fluorescent panel was on, the facility's overnight energy-saving measure that turned the corridors into alternating bands of pale light and weak shadow. He moved through them steadily.

Carmen's hands were on the keyboard. She didn't move them. She watched with the full attention she gave a patient whose numbers had slipped out of range. Alarm hadn't caught up yet.

Harry reached the intersection where the long hallway met the short one, where the sub-station was, where she was sitting in the blue-white light of the monitor with her name badge clipped to scrubs she'd been wearing for twelve hours, and he looked up and saw her.

He stopped.

They looked at each other across the linoleum.

It lasted three seconds. In the first second, Harry's face was open — unguarded, the face of a man who'd been alone in a hallway doing what he needed to do, and the expression on it was ordinary irritation at being interrupted, the look you'd give someone who walked in while you were on the phone. In the second second, she saw him recognize what she was seeing and what it meant. His eyes, which she'd spent a year documenting as confused and intermittently oriented, were clear and sharp, moving over her face, her badge, her hands — taking stock, making calculations, reaching conclusions. In the third second, his jaw set, and something crossed his face that she would later decide was resignation.

Then he put his left hand on the wall. His weight shifted. His shoulders dropped forward into the familiar hunch, and his knees bent slightly, and his right foot turned inward, and she watched him rebuild the performance from the ground up, piece by piece, like an actor stepping back into costume — the posture first, then the grip loosening on the towel-wrapped object, then the face going slack, the eyes losing focus, the lower lip dropping.

"Eleanor?" he said. His voice was cracked and querulous, the voice she heard every shift. "Is that you? Eleanor, I was looking for — where did you — the bathroom. I was trying to find the bathroom."

Carmen stood up. Her badge swung against her chest and then settled.

"It's Carmen, Mr. Zielinski." Her resident voice came out — steady, clear, warm enough. "Let me help you back to bed."

She crossed the thirty feet between them. The linoleum bit cold through her sneakers; the HVAC always dropped at night. His breathing was even. He wasn't winded or confused. He held it a little shallow, like he was bracing for what she might do next.

She took his left arm. The forearm was thin — bone easy to feel through skin and pajama cotton — but the muscle under it was live and ready. At the same moment his right hand tightened around the towel-wrapped object against his thigh. The terry cloth puckered under his fingers.

For a second she thought about reaching for it. Peeling the towel back. Calling Devon over and making him look. But then she saw the whole stupid scene as it would happen: an aide half asleep, an old man folding toward the floor, her own voice getting louder while Harry stared past her and asked for Eleanor. Whatever was in the towel would still be whatever she said it was.

"Here we go, Mr. Zielinski. Back to bed."

That choice seemed to register. The weight he gave her was exact — enough dependence to occupy both her hands, not enough to actually need her strength. He was making her hold the act with him.

"Eleanor makes the bed," he said, mumbling. "She always makes the bed. I can't — the corners. She does the corners."

She walked him into Room 114A and guided him to the bed. Room 114A smelled like him — warm, stale skin, the sweet-sour note of medications processed through old kidneys, the faint ammonia ghost that never fully left any room where someone lived in a diaper. He sat on the edge, and when she lifted his legs onto the mattress he went limp a beat too neatly, controlled even in surrender.

The towel disappeared under the blanket with him. By the time she pulled the covers to his chest, his hand was already resting on it, and his knuckles were white.

"Good night, Mr. Zielinski."

He'd closed his eyes. His face was slack.

She stood in his doorway for a moment. The hallway was empty and half-lit and silent except for Mrs. Delacroix's distant murmur and the HVAC cycling down to its lowest register, a sound like the building exhaling.

She looked at the man in the bed and understood that the three seconds in the hallway had not ended in the hallway. He had seen her see him, then walked her all the way back here and let her decide whether to make this a scene with nothing in her hands. The towel was under the blanket because she'd let it get there. The night three shifts ago when she'd heard clear voices in the TV room. The pill count. The tramadol correction — two-fifty, not two hundred, said with the quick certainty of someone who'd been paying attention. Every separate anomaly she'd been collecting clicked into the same shape.

She went back to the sub-station and sat down. The vitals screen had gone to sleep. Her reflection stared at her from the dark monitor — tired eyes, hair escaping the ponytail, the particular gray look that twelve hours of fluorescent light put on brown skin.

She sat there for eleven minutes. She knew it was eleven minutes because when she looked at the computer clock it said 2:52, and when she stood up it said 3:03. She wasn't blank. She was putting the order together: Harry first, then the cabinet, then whatever came after.

---

The medication room was off the central nurse's station, between the two wings. It was the size of a large closet — one door, no window, a locked cabinet bolted to the wall, a counter with a sink, and a plastic chair nobody sat in because there was barely room for it. The room smelled like rubbing alcohol and the fine, dry dust that came off pill bottles when you handled hundreds of them.

Carmen locked the door behind her, which was protocol for controlled substance counts. She pulled the keys from her hip — the medication key was the small brass one on the ring with seven others, and by now she could find it by touch alone.

She opened the cabinet. The controlled substances were on the top two shelves, each in its own labeled container, each with a count sheet clipped to the front. The count sheets were simple: date, shift, count, initials. Two signatures per shift — outgoing nurse and incoming nurse, verifying the same number. Carmen's initials were on most of the evening entries. The overnight nurse's initials — Maria or Dolores or whoever they'd pulled from the float pool that week — were on the rest.

She started with the oxycodone. Five milligram tablets, prescribed to four residents on Wing A and two on Wing B. She removed the container, opened it, and counted. She counted slowly, touching each tablet with the tip of a tongue depressor as she'd been taught in pharmacology, pushing each pill from one side of the tray to the other. Eighty-six tablets should have been in this container according to the last verified count plus pharmacy deliveries minus dispensed doses.

She counted sixty-seven. She counted again. Sixty-seven.

Nineteen short.

She wrote the number on the back of a count sheet and moved to the alprazolam. One milligram tablets, prescribed to three residents. Expected count: one hundred forty-four. Actual count: one hundred twenty-eight.

Sixteen short.

The tramadol was next. Fifty milligram capsules, four residents. Expected: eighty-eight. Actual: eighty.

Eight short.

She opened the hydrocodone container. Ten milligram tablets, two residents. Expected: thirty-six. Actual: thirty-two.

Four short.

She stood at the counter with the numbers on the back of the count sheet. Nineteen, sixteen, eight, four. Forty-seven pills.

The ventilation hummed above her. She could hear, faintly through the closed door, the rise and fall of Mrs. Delacroix's murmur and the building settling into its own weight, a ceiling creak every few minutes, then a soft tick in the ductwork.

Forty-seven pills across four controlled substances. Not concentrated in one medication, which made a single patient's hidden stash harder to believe. It suggested movement. More than that, she was still inferring.

She checked the count sheets again. Her own initials appeared at the start of each evening shift, and at the end, the numbers matched — she'd counted correctly every time. Which meant the pills were leaving between her departure and the start of the next verified count. Overnight. When Devon slept through his shifts and the hallways belonged to whoever was awake in them.

She thought about Harry's walk. The posture, the stride, the clear eyes. She thought about the rolled towel pressed against his leg. If the towel held pills, that would be proof. She hadn't seen inside it.

The tramadol correction from her first week surfaced. Two-fifty, not two hundred. The exact number didn't prove he was keeping inventory. It proved he knew the counts better than he should have. Combined with the missing pills and the walk, it pointed at him hard enough that looking elsewhere would have been a lie.

Forty-seven pills. Dispensed under her license. Missing from her shifts.

She put the containers back in the cabinet. She locked it. She folded the count sheet and put it in her scrub pocket, then took it out, looked at the four numbers she'd written, folded it again, and put it back.

She unlocked the medication room door and stepped into the nurse's station. Devon's phone was propped against the monitor now, the screen showing a man in a garage talking about engine rebuilds. Devon was tipped back in the office chair with his mouth slightly open, not quite asleep but not registering her either.

Carmen walked past him. She went to the supply closet, got a fresh set of vitals equipment she didn't need, and carried it back to the Wing A sub-station, where she sat down and set it on the counter beside her. If Devon looked up, he'd see routine. If Harry noticed anything at all, it would look like routine too.

One call to the supervisor would turn routine into a discrepancy form with Carmen's initials at the top. She needed another nurse's hands on the cabinet before that happened.

She didn't do it at three in the morning. Not with forty-seven pills missing under her initials and nothing that tied them to Harry except a hallway, a towel, and her word that an eighty-four-year-old resident had walked thirty feet like he belonged there. By the time the supervisor got here, Harry would be back in bed asking for Eleanor. Devon would say he hadn't seen anything. The log would still point first at Carmen.

So she sat on it until dawn and let the night keep happening around her. At four she made her scheduled rounds. The rounds gave her a reason to keep moving through Wing A without announcing that she was watching it. Most of the wing was asleep. Mrs. Delacroix's murmur leaked out of Room 110. Mr. Petrelli snored through his open door with one foot hanging off the mattress, and Carmen tucked it back under the sheet without waking him. In Room 112A, Lola Fernandez was curled on her side with one hand under her cheek and the other wrapped around her gold crucifix, and she looked like someone's grandmother, which she was.

In Room 114A, Harry Zielinski was on his back with the blanket pulled to his chin and his eyes closed. No towel in sight. He looked like a frail old man dreaming about his dead wife, and Carmen stood in his doorway and watched his chest rise and fall and knew he was awake and knew he knew she was there and knew the performance was still the only thing either of them could afford to do in the dark.

She finished her rounds and went back to the sub-station. She charted, answered two call bells, helped Devon turn a patient at five, and drank the coffee she'd brought in a thermos that was now cold and tasted like the inside of the thermos. The folded count sheet stayed in her pocket until the sky at the end of Wing A went from gray to a weak pink and the first breakfast cart rattled out of the kitchen.

The day nurse, Melanie Russo, came in at 6:53 with wet hair at the nape of her neck and a Dunkin' cup sweating in one hand. She set the cup down, looked at Carmen's face, and didn't bother with good morning.

"What happened?"

"I need a narc count before you touch anything."

That got her moving. Carmen took her into the medication room, shut the door, opened the cabinet, and slid the count sheets into the light. She read off the expected numbers while Melanie counted with the tongue depressor, pushing each pill into a neat line, lips moving a little as she went.

"Sixty-seven," Melanie said.

"Count it again."

Melanie counted again. "Still sixty-seven."

Carmen pulled the alprazolam, then the tramadol, then the hydrocodone. Melanie counted all three twice. The numbers didn't move.

"One twenty-eight. Eighty. Thirty-two." Melanie set the tongue depressor down and looked at Carmen. "Jesus. That's nineteen short on the oxy, sixteen on the Xanax, eight tramadol, four hydro."

Now someone else had said it out loud: forty-seven pills.

Melanie reached for the count sheet and wrote the actuals in hard block print. 67. 128. 80. 32. She initialed beside each line, then flattened the page with her palm like maybe the numbers would change if the paper stopped buckling.

"You already call Janice?"

"Not yet." Carmen heard how flat her own voice had gone. "I wanted the count witnessed first."

Melanie looked at her for a second too long, then back at the sheet. Through the door came the beginning of day shift: wheels, voices, somebody laughing too loud at the station, Mrs. Delacroix's low murmur still working through the wall like a motor that never shut off.

The shortage sat on the page in Melanie Russo's handwriting now. Harry could still fold himself back into the hunch and the dead-wife questions. The pills were no longer only Carmen's word.
