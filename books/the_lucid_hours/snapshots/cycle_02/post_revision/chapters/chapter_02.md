# Chapter 2

The oxycodone was short before Carmen opened the cabinet.

She'd known it the way she sometimes knew a patient had spiked a fever before taking their temp — something in the air of the medication room, in the stillness of the count log left open on the clipboard where Denise, who covered the 7-to-3, had signed her end-of-shift count in handwriting so loose it looked like she'd done it while walking. Carmen uncapped her pen, ran her finger down the column for Schedule II narcotics, and started counting.

Oxycodone 5mg: the log said sixty-two tablets. Carmen counted the blister packs twice. Fifty-nine.

She counted again, slower, peeling back the foil edges to make sure she wasn't doubling a punch-through. Fifty-nine. She wrote the number on a sticky note and moved to the alprazolam.

The alprazolam was five short.

The medication room was a closet off the central station, barely wide enough for one person and the locked cabinet. It smelled like alcohol wipes and the faint chemical sweetness of pill dust that collected in the cabinet's corners no matter how often they wiped it down. Carmen's scrub top stuck to the small of her back. The AC had been cycling on and off all week — Cliff had put in a maintenance request, which meant nothing would happen until after Labor Day.

She wrote the alprazolam number on the sticky note beneath the oxycodone number. Three short, five short. She checked the tramadol and the lorazepam. Both matched.

She found Pat Sorvino at the nurse's station, eating string cheese and scrolling through something on her phone with her free hand. Pat was the charge nurse tonight, an RN who'd been at Lakeview eleven years and had developed the specific equanimity of someone who'd seen enough medication discrepancies to classify them by probable cause before anyone finished the sentence. She wore compression stockings under her scrubs for the varicose veins she blamed on twelve-hour shifts and two pregnancies, and she kept a tube of Biofreeze in the pocket of her scrub jacket that she applied to her lower back every two hours with the regularity of a med pass.

"Count's off," Carmen said. "Three oxy, five alprazolam."

Pat looked up from her phone. She took a bite of string cheese and chewed it slowly, and Carmen could see her running the mental calculation — how much of a problem this was, who it was going to land on, whether it was worth the paperwork.

"Did you count the returns?"

"There aren't any returns logged since Tuesday."

"Hm." Pat set her phone down. "Was Denise's count off when she signed out?"

"Her count matched. It's my count that doesn't."

"Yeah, that's what I mean. It's probably a charting error. Denise isn't — you know how it is. The overnight girls don't always recount. She might've carried Brenda's number forward."

"That's eight pills, Pat."

"I know what it is." Pat pulled a strand of cheese apart. "Document it. I'll flag it for the DON in the morning. Put it in the incident log, write up the discrepancy form, and make sure you note that the prior shift count was unverified."

"It's going under my name."

"It goes under whoever found it, which is you, which is also who's on the floor right now, which is also you." Pat wasn't being cruel. She was stating the architecture of accountability as she understood it. "Look, I'm not saying don't worry. I'm saying this happens. Two, three times a year we get a count that's off and it's always a charting issue or a pharmacy delivery that didn't get logged. Document it right, and if it comes back, it comes back with your documentation showing you caught it."

Carmen went back to the medication room and filled out the controlled substance discrepancy form. The form asked for the dispensing nurse's name and license number. She wrote her name and her LPN license number, the one she'd memorized the day she got it, the number that was her. If anyone ever pulled this form — a surveyor, an auditor, the Board of Nursing — the first thing they'd see was Carmen Reyes, LPN, and a count that didn't match.

She filed the form in the red folder at the back of the binder, behind the tabs for incident reports and fall assessments, where discrepancy forms accumulated like parking tickets nobody planned to pay.

---

The evening med pass was the longest part of her shift. Forty-seven residents on Wing A, each with a medication administration record specifying exactly what they took, when, and how. Carmen loaded the cart at 5:00, started at Room 100, and worked her way down the hall toward 120. Most of the residents were routine — she could do this in her sleep, and on some nights when she'd picked up a double, she nearly had. Hand the cup, watch them swallow, check the mouth, chart it, move on. The cart's left wheel squeaked, a rhythmic complaint she'd reported twice and stopped mentioning.

Lola Fernandez was in 112A. Carmen knocked, entered, and found Lola in the recliner by the window, her silver hair freshly brushed — Tammy would've done it on the evening round — and her gold crucifix catching the last daylight through the blinds. Lola smiled the way she always smiled, wide and warm, a smile that turned the entire hallway into a kitchen where someone was making coffee for you.

"Mija, you're here. Is it time already?"

"It's time, Mrs. Fernandez. Three tonight." Carmen set the paper cup on the tray table: lisinopril, sertraline, the alprazolam.

Carmen watched.

She'd watched hundreds of patients take medications. Most of them — the real ones, the ones whose cognition had thinned to lace — approached the pill cup with wariness or confusion. They picked pills up and looked at them. They put one in their mouth and forgot the others. They chewed when they should've swallowed. They tongued pills into their cheeks and Carmen would find them later, pasty and half-dissolved, on the pillow or in a tissue. Giving medications to genuinely impaired patients was messy, imprecise, and frequently required a second attempt with applesauce.

Lola picked up the cup. All three pills went into her mouth in one motion. She drank the water Carmen held out, swallowed, and opened wide before Carmen had to ask. Her tongue lifted. Her cheeks were clear. Nothing was pocketed.

It took four seconds. There was no hesitation, no fishing a tablet back with her tongue, no pause to remember the cup. The swallow was cleaner than most alert residents managed. It was too clean.

Carmen marked the MAR. Lisinopril, check. Sertraline, check. Alprazolam, check. She paused on the alprazolam.

Five short in the cabinet. Lola swallowing like she knew exactly what Carmen checked for.

"Thank you, mija," Lola said, and patted Carmen's hand. The tremor in Lola's fingers was real — Carmen could feel it against her skin, the fine irregular vibration of early Parkinson's — and the warmth in her grip was real too, and those two real things made everything else harder to question.

"You're welcome, Mrs. Fernandez. Goodnight."

Carmen rolled the cart to 108A. Agnes Chen's room. Agnes's roommate, a woman named Dorothy who hadn't spoken in eleven months and received nutrition through a PEG tube, lay motionless in the far bed. Agnes sat on the edge of her own bed, looking at nothing.

"Mrs. Chen, I have your evening medications."

Agnes didn't respond. She didn't look at Carmen. She sat with her hands flat on her thighs, palms down, as though she were steadying something that wasn't there.

"Mrs. Chen? Your medications."

Agnes turned her head. Her wire-rimmed glasses caught the overhead fluorescent, blanking her eyes for a moment. Then she said, quietly: "Not now."

"You're refusing tonight?"

"Not now," Agnes said again, which was either confirmation of the refusal or a repetition of the phrase that was all she had. Carmen had heard both from patients. She waited. Agnes looked away.

"Okay. I'm going to chart that you refused. You can ask for them later if you change your mind."

Agnes didn't respond. Carmen marked the MAR — lisinopril refused, rosuvastatin refused, aspirin 81mg refused — and noted the refusal in the chart. Agnes had the right. Every resident had the right. But Agnes hadn't refused in the four months since Carmen started working this shift, and Carmen added the refusal to the sticky note in her head next to the short count and Lola's four-second swallow.

She finished the med pass at 7:40, twenty minutes behind because Mr. Gruber in 104 had needed to tell her about the Cincinnati Reds game and Mrs. Okafor in 118 had refused her blood pressure medication, then changed her mind, then refused again, which was completely normal for Mrs. Okafor and meant nothing except that Mrs. Okafor liked having someone to talk to for a few extra minutes.

Carmen clocked out at 11:15. In the parking lot, sitting in her Civic with the windows down because the car had been baking in the August sun for eight hours and the seats burned through her scrubs, she called the scheduling office and picked up the overnight shift. Eleven to seven. Eight more hours on top of eight.

"You sure?" the scheduler said. "That's — you just worked a full."

"I'm sure. Sixteen hours, yeah."

She needed four hundred dollars by Friday. The car payment. Her mother couldn't watch Reggie past midnight, so she called Gloria and asked — not asked, negotiated, because her mother worried and the worrying came out as conditions.

"Just tonight, Mami. He's already asleep. I won't wake him. I'll pick him up at seven-thirty."

"You work too much, Carmen."

"Yeah."

"You sound tired."

"I'm fine."

"That's what your father used to say, and then he'd fall asleep at the table."

"Mami, I have to go."

She sat in the car for another minute. The parking lot was half-empty, lit by two sodium lamps that turned everything yellow-orange. She could see Lakeview's flat roof and the HVAC units that hadn't been serviced since March. She could see the windows of Wing A, dark now except for the blue glow of televisions in two rooms. Room 110, where Mrs. Davila moaned through the night in a way that the staff had learned to hear as ambient. Room 116, where Earl Washington and Burt Kovacs shared a room and never seemed to interact, one barely responsive and the other genuinely somewhere else.

She went back inside.

---

Devon Watts was the overnight aide, twenty-six years old, working toward his associate's in something he changed every semester. He was a good kid who cared about the residents and was terrible at staying awake past midnight. By 12:30 he was at the nurse's station with his earbuds in and his head propped on his hand, scrolling through his phone with the decreasing coordination of someone losing a fight with sleep. By 12:45 the phone was on the desk and his chin was on his chest. Carmen didn't wake him. She'd need him if someone fell or pulled a call bell hard enough, and he slept light — she'd tested this. But she didn't need him for rounds.

The overnight shift at Lakeview was a different building than the day. The fluorescents in the hallways dimmed to half, casting a light that was less illumination than announcement — we are still here, the power is still on, this is still a place where people are being cared for. The sounds compressed: no call bells, no television chatter from the common room, no meal trays, no visitors. What was left was the building itself. The ventilation system's hum, deeper at night. The click of the thermostat that controlled the AC that didn't work. Somewhere in Wing B, a toilet running. The soft shuffle of Carmen's sneakers on linoleum that had been waxed so many times it was more wax than floor.

She did her first round at midnight. Checked the fall-risk patients: Mr. Gruber in 104, who sleepwalked and had a bed alarm that went off twice a night; Mrs. Okafor in 118, whose new blood pressure medication made her dizzy and who'd already fallen once, catching the bedrail on the way down and bruising her entire forearm a deep eggplant purple that her daughter had photographed and emailed to Cliff; Burt Kovacs in 116, whose dementia made him unpredictable at night, though tonight he was asleep in a position that looked uncomfortable — half on his side, one arm dangling, his mouth open and his breathing slow and ragged and old.

She charted vitals on the two residents who required overnight monitoring. She restocked the supply cart. She ate a granola bar from her bag and drank water from the fountain outside the medication room, the water warm and tasting faintly of copper.

At 1:10 she started her second round. Wing A stretched ahead of her, the hallway narrowing in the half-light. She passed 100, 102, 104. Usually by this hour she'd be in the Civic headed to her mother's, not still inside Lakeview. Whatever happened on Wing A after midnight usually happened without her. Tonight the car payment had bought eight extra hours, and at the far end of the hall she heard why the room sounded different: not a television, but people trying and failing to keep a conversation down to a whisper.

A voice said, sharp enough to carry, "She wrote it down."

Another voice came back immediately, lower and rougher. "Then stop saying it so loud."

Then a wet cough scraped through the quiet, followed by voices tripping over one another. It wasn't a TV left on low. It wasn't the loose, looping talk of residents sundowning. Someone made a point. Someone else snapped back.

The TV room was at the far end of Wing A, past the last rooms, around the corner from the hallway where the sub-station sat empty. Carmen's sneakers didn't squeak. She came around the corner and looked through the open doorway.

Three residents sat in the vinyl chairs around the card table. The television was off. The overhead light was off. The floor lamp had been dragged in from the corner and set close to the table. Papers were spread between them. A pen lay uncapped by one hand.

She recognized Lola's silver hair in the lamplight. She recognized Harry Zielinski in the wheelchair, sitting forward with his chin up and both hands planted on the armrests. The third person turned just enough for Carmen to catch the profile. Earl Washington.

Carmen's foot hit the doorframe. She didn't mean to. Her toe caught the aluminum strip where the carpet met the hallway linoleum, and the sound was small, a tap, barely there, but the room went quiet like she'd fired a gun.

Earl moved first. His hand came off the table, gathered the papers in one sweep, folded them once, and slid them under Harry's wheelchair cushion into the dark gap between the vinyl seat and the metal frame.

Harry dropped first. His shoulders rounded, his head went down, his face slackened, his hands loosened in his lap. Lola let her mouth fall open and her eyes go soft. Earl lowered his chin to his chest and closed his eyes.

The floor lamp hummed. Lola blinked at Carmen and said, "Is it morning, mija?" The slur in her voice wasn't the one Carmen heard after dinner or baths, when fatigue and tremor pulled her words apart. This came out slow but shaped.

"It's one in the morning, Mrs. Fernandez. Let me help you back to your room."

"Oh." Lola pushed at the chair arm as if she'd only just realized where she was. "I was looking for the bathroom. Ay, I thought I had time."

She stood, and warm urine ran down her calf and onto the tile.

The smell hit at once, sharp ammonia under the room's old-upholstery odor. Carmen caught Lola under both elbows before she could slip. The vinyl chair gleamed dark where she'd been sitting.

"Okay," Carmen said automatically. "It's okay. I've got you."

Over Lola's shoulder she saw Earl get to his feet and take the lamp by the stem. Harry's chair had already edged back from the table. The folded papers were gone under the seat cushion, and then Lola's full weight tipped into Carmen and there was no way to keep watching without letting an old woman fall in her own piss.

Carmen walked Lola to 112A with one arm around her waist and the other holding the wet hem of her nightgown off the floor. Lola apologized the whole way in a soft, embarrassed murmur that might have been real or might have been another layer of the act; Carmen couldn't tell anymore. In the bathroom she got Lola onto the toilet, peeled the soaked brief down, wiped urine from her thigh and calf with a washcloth from the sink, found a clean brief in the second drawer, and changed her. By the time she had Lola back in bed with a dry gown and the call bell tucked against her hand, Harry and Earl had had minutes, not seconds.

Lola patted Carmen's wrist. The tremor was there, real and irregular under the skin. "Good girl," she said. "Goodnight, mija."

"Goodnight, Mrs. Fernandez."

Carmen went back to the TV room. It was empty. Harry's wheelchair was gone with the papers hidden under its cushion. Earl was gone. The floor lamp was back in its corner. The card table had nothing on it except a half-empty box of tissues and a copy of People magazine from March, set square in the center as if nothing else had been there.

She stood in the room and listened to the building breathe: the ventilation hum, the toilet in Wing B, the distant murmur she'd taken for a television.

Sundowning. Her brain reached for it anyway, because it was a chartable word and chartable words made things smaller. She'd seen sundowning on this floor: people up at three in the morning insisting they had to catch a bus, arguing with a husband dead twenty years, wandering half-dressed toward an exit that stayed locked. Mr. Dietrich in 106 had once put on his shoes and tried to get to a job he'd retired from in 1998.

Sundowning meant loops, fear, the same wrong fact over and over. It was not papers on a card table. It was not an uncapped pen. It was not one resident warning another to keep his voice down and all three of them going slack before she'd taken a second step into the room.

Carmen walked back to the nurse's station. Devon was asleep. She sat in the chair next to his and looked at the count log and the red folder with the discrepancy form that had her name and license number on it and the sticky note in her pocket with two numbers — three and five — and she added them together with what she'd just seen and the total didn't make sense in any clinical framework she owned.

Three oxycodone. Five alprazolam. Lola's four-second swallow. Agnes refusing for the first time in four months. Harry correcting her tramadol count two shifts ago with the precision of a man reading a number off a page. And now this: a room full of people who were supposed to be gone, sitting up in the dark, doing something that looked like work.

She woke the station monitor and opened the overnight nursing notes. For Lola she wrote the smallest true thing she had: resident found awake in TV room at 0115, redirected to room, no distress noted. Then she checked 114A and 116A, found Harry and Earl back in bed, and put the same narrow truth in each chart: awake in common area during rounds, in room on recheck, no injury, no acute complaint.

That was chartable. The part that mattered wasn't. She had not heard a single clean sentence, had no papers in her hand, no witness besides Devon asleep in the next chair with his mouth open. If she called Pat at one in the morning and said three residents who spent all day in some version of confusion had been holding a meeting in the dark, Pat would hear the count discrepancy first and the story second. Eight missing controlled pills under Carmen's license, and now a report she could not defend beyond I know what I saw. That was how you got yourself treated like the problem before you had anything solid enough to prove there was one.

So she didn't file an incident report. She didn't page the DON. She left the minimal notes in the chart because they were true, and kept the rest in her head, where it felt less defensible and somehow more dangerous. A theory was useless. Proof was what counted. She sat at the nurse's station with Devon breathing beside her and the dark hall in front of her, and listened for wheels on linoleum.
