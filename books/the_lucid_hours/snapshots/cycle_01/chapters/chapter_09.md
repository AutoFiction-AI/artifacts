# Chapter 9

The flowers arrived at seven-fifteen in the morning, which was how Carmen knew the state was coming.

In thirteen months at Lakeview Gardens, she had never seen a living plant in the lobby. The silk ficus by the reception desk didn't count — it had a coating of dust thick enough to write in, and someone had once hung a pine-tree air freshener from one of its branches like an ornament on the world's saddest Christmas tree. But these were real flowers. Grocery-store carnations in a plastic vase, pink and white, their stems already starting to bend from being jammed together too tightly. They sat on the front desk next to the sign-in clipboard like an apology nobody had asked for.

Cliff Moorehead materialized from his office at seven-twenty, which was its own kind of unprecedented. Carmen had worked here for over a year and could count on one hand the number of times she'd seen the administrator before nine. He was wearing a polo shirt she hadn't seen before — navy, still creased from the package — and khakis with an ironed pleat down each leg. His face had the specific look of a man whose salary depended on a number generated by people he couldn't charm, and he was trying to figure out charm anyway.

"All right, everyone." He clapped his hands once in the hallway outside the nurse's station, the clap of a man who'd read a book about leadership sometime in the nineties and hadn't updated his material. "Surveyors are here at ten. I need all documentation current by nine. All documentation. MDS assessments, care plans, medication records, incident reports. If it's not up to date, it doesn't exist."

Tammy, passing with a stack of linens balanced against her hip, didn't break stride. "So half our patients don't exist."

"Tammy."

"I'm just saying. You want me to chart or you want me to change Mrs. Patterson, because she's been sitting in it since six and I only got two hands."

Cliff's mouth worked for a moment, opening and closing around words that didn't arrive. "Chart first. Then — yes. Prioritize."

"I'm prioritizing Mrs. Patterson's sacrum, Cliff. She's got a Stage Two on her tailbone and she needs turning every two hours and she gets it every three because you won't hire another aide for this wing."

"We're not discussing staffing right now."

"We're never discussing staffing. That's the point." Tammy turned the corner with her linens. Cliff stood in the hallway for another five seconds, then retreated to his office. A moment later, Carmen heard the printer start up — the heavy, grinding sound it made when someone was printing more than ten pages at once.

By eight-thirty, the transformation was visible. Someone — Cliff, or someone Cliff had deputized — had taped a motivational poster to the wall outside the dining room. It featured a sunrise over mountains with the words QUALITY CARE STARTS WITH YOU in a font that suggested clip art and a 3 AM deadline. The edges were still curling from the printer heat. It had been positioned directly over a scuff mark that had been on the wall since before Carmen started, and it covered the scuff but not the nail hole above it, so the effect was less "inspirational workspace" and more "someone is trying very hard."

The carnations had already begun to wilt.

Carmen moved through her morning routines — eight o'clock medications, vitals, the blood sugar check on Mr. Andersen in Wing B, the dressing change on Mrs. Patterson's pressure ulcer — with the focused calm of someone who knew what was coming and had decided how she would handle it. She'd worked a state survey at her previous facility in Edison, a smaller place that got dinged for medication storage temperatures and a bathroom handrail loose enough to spin in its bracket. Surveyors came annually, theoretically unannounced, though facilities always seemed to know within hours of the survey team's departure from their regional office. Cliff had sent a staff email at four-twelve in the morning. Carmen's phone had buzzed on her nightstand while Reggie slept.

She was not worried about the surveyors finding the ring's operation. The ring left no physical evidence in accessible places — Agnes had insisted on that from the beginning, with the unbending precision of a pharmacist who'd spent forty-two years understanding exactly what regulatory scrutiny looked like. No pills in drawers, no cash in envelopes, no notebooks in rooms during the day. Everything moved through Marcus and out the building within hours of a meeting. The prepaid phone was powered down and hidden inside a heating vent cover in Harry's room, behind a screw Agnes had replaced with one that turned by hand.

What worried her was the medication count log.

She had documented every discrepancy herself — had written her initials and her license number next to each one, because that was the procedure, and Carmen followed procedure. Six documented variances in the past three months. All on her shifts, all under her name, all explained in the notes column with the kind of plausible fictions the charge nurse had taught her to write before Carmen understood she was covering for something larger than charting errors: documentation error, shift-change miscommunication, pharmacy delivery discrepancy. Each explanation was the kind of thing that actually happened at understaffed facilities with hybrid paper-electronic systems and overnight nurses who'd been hired two weeks ago. None of them were true.

A competent surveyor would see the pattern. Not necessarily what the pattern meant, but that a pattern existed, and that it centered on one nurse.

She'd known this was coming since the moment she stopped counting honestly. She'd just managed to not think about it as a specific event on a specific date. And now the date was here, and the carnations were dying in the lobby, and Cliff's motivational poster was peeling off the wall.

Dr. Prasad arrived at nine for her biweekly rounds, moving through Wing A with the efficient speed of a physician who saw forty patients a day across three facilities and couldn't afford to linger. She didn't seem to know about the survey — or if she knew, she didn't change her rhythm. She reviewed charts, wrote orders, asked Carmen about Mrs. Patterson's wound progression and Mr. Andersen's morning glucose, and was gone by nine-forty-five. Carmen envied her. Prasad was a doctor. She came, she prescribed, she left. She didn't sit in the building for eight hours knowing that her license number was on a stack of documents that told a story she'd invented.

---

The surveyor's name was Linda Horvath. She was about fifty, with gray-brown hair cut short enough to be practical and long enough to still need tucking behind her ears, which she did every few minutes — a gesture so habitual it seemed less like grooming and more like a kind of punctuation. She wore shoes that told Carmen everything: black, rubber-soled, scuffed at the toes, ugly and clearly comfortable. Those were the shoes of a woman who walked facility hallways for a living and had stopped caring what her feet looked like years ago. She carried a binder thick enough to stop a door, a clipboard with a pen clipped to the top, and a stainless steel coffee thermos she refilled twice from the pot Cliff had set up in the conference room. Real brewed coffee, not the instant packets they usually had — another tell that the state was in the building.

She brought a second surveyor, a younger woman named Keisha who handled the dietary and environmental review while Horvath took clinical. Carmen saw Keisha photographing the dining room steam table with her phone, then crouching to check the temperature gauge on the reach-in cooler, writing something in her own binder. Cliff hovered near both of them with the energy of a man trying to appear relaxed while standing on a nail.

"Can I get you anything?" he asked Horvath for the third time in twenty minutes. "Water? We've got — I set up coffee in the—"

"I'm fine, Mr. Moorehead."

"Cliff. Please."

Horvath didn't use his first name. She opened her binder and began with resident interviews at eleven.

Carmen was doing her late-morning med pass when Horvath entered Room 114A. Harry was in his wheelchair by the window, wearing the blue cardigan he wore every day, staring at the parking lot with the emptied expression of a man who had misplaced the present tense entirely. He'd been in this posture when Carmen had come through with his morning medications at eight — pills set in the cup, water on the tray table, Harry taking them without looking at her, swallowing with the mechanical indifference of someone for whom pills were stimuli, not decisions. She'd watched him do it and had the strange doubled experience of seeing an act she knew was an act performed so well it looked like the thing it was imitating.

Now Horvath sat in the vinyl visitor's chair, the one that exhaled a small puff of disinfectant-scented air when weight hit it, and opened her binder.

"Mr. Zielinski? I'm Linda, from the state health department. I just have a few questions for you today."

A pause. Then Harry's voice, querulous and thin, the voice of a man calling across a distance only he could see: "Eleanor?"

"No, sir. I'm Linda. From the health department. Can you tell me what today's date is?"

Another pause, longer. Harry seemed to be considering the question with genuine effort, his mouth working slightly as though the answer was a physical thing he was trying to locate in a cluttered room. "It's... March? No. No, it's — is it Tuesday?"

"Can you tell me what year it is?"

"Seventy-eight." He said it with sudden confidence, sitting up slightly in the wheelchair, and his voice shifted from lost to certain in a way that was startling. "Seventy-eight, and I'll tell you something, the Yanks are done. Reggie Jackson can't carry them again. Munson's gone, did you hear that? Thurman. Plane crash. That was — was that this year?"

Horvath's pen moved across her clipboard. "Do you know the name of the president?"

"Carter." Harry leaned forward, and his voice took on the urgency of a man who'd been reading the newspaper and needed someone to listen. "Carter, and he's going to lose Iran. I'm telling you. Eleanor won't listen to me about it but I've been watching the news and that Shah is finished. We've got people over there, embassy people. If Carter doesn't get them out — you know what the price of gas was last week? A dollar and two cents at the Exxon on Route 18. A dollar and two cents. That's — Eleanor would know what it was before—" He stopped. The urgency drained from his face. He looked at his hands. "Where's Eleanor?"

"I'm not sure who Eleanor is, Mr. Zielinski."

"She was just here. She comes at — what time is it? She brings the — there's a thing she brings." He gestured vaguely at the nightstand. "With the..."

"That's all right. Can you tell me the name of this place? Where you are right now?"

Harry looked around Room 114A as though seeing it for the first time. His gaze traveled across the adjustable bed, the nightstand with its box of tissues and plastic water pitcher, the window with its view of the employee parking lot. He seemed baffled by all of it — not frightened, not distressed, just genuinely unable to reconcile his surroundings with whatever internal geography he was navigating.

"Is this the — no." He rubbed his face. His hand trembled slightly — a tremor Carmen had never seen from him before, which meant it was part of the performance, added today for the surveyor's benefit. "I don't. Where's Eleanor? She'd know."

Carmen stood at the sub-station near the Wing A entrance, charting vitals she'd already charted, and listened. Nine minutes. Horvath spent nine minutes with Harry Zielinski, and Carmen timed it because she couldn't help herself, the way you watch a tightrope walker and count the seconds not because you want them to fall but because you need to know how long a person can sustain the impossible.

Nine minutes of a man whose mind could run the financial architecture of a pharmaceutical distribution network answering every question with a 1978 that never existed — but a 1978 so detailed, so internally consistent in its specifics, that Horvath wrote steadily the entire time. Harry mentioned the score of a Yankees game that hadn't been played, the price of gas at a specific station on a specific highway, a presidential crisis he'd conflated with events from four years earlier in a way that mimicked the temporal drift of genuine dementia — the past collapsing into itself, years stacking on top of each other like transparencies on an overhead projector.

He asked about Eleanor six times. Each time, the question arrived with a slightly different inflection — confused, then concerned, then plaintive, then matter-of-fact, then lost again — as though her absence was a wound he kept rediscovering from different angles.

It was the best acting Carmen had ever seen. Not because it was flawless but because it wasn't. Harry let his face go slack at slightly wrong moments, let his eyes wander mid-sentence to track something only he could see, produced the kind of micro-errors that looked like a broken mind rather than a performing one. A bad actor would have been too consistently confused. Harry was inconsistent in exactly the right ways, the ways that real dementia was inconsistent — patches of startling clarity inside the fog, sudden conviction about a false fact, the confused person's particular stubbornness about details that didn't matter.

When Horvath left Room 114A, Carmen was at the medication cart, drawing up an insulin dose for Mr. Andersen. She glanced toward Harry's doorway. In the small window above the door handle, she could see his reflection in the glass — he was watching Horvath walk away, and his face was completely still. The man in 1978 was gone. The man who ran things was back. He held the stillness for two seconds, three, then let his face slacken again, and the transition was so smooth it was like watching water close over a stone.

---

Agnes was next. Room 108A.

Her roommate, a ninety-one-year-old woman named Doris who had advanced Alzheimer's and hadn't spoken a recognizable word in eight months, was asleep in the bed by the window, her mouth open, her breathing a thick, slow rasp that was audible from the hallway. Agnes sat in the chair by her own bed, hands folded in her lap, eyes fixed on a point approximately fourteen inches above the floor. She did not look up when Horvath entered.

"Mrs. Chen? I'm Linda, from the state health department."

Nothing.

"Mrs. Chen, can you hear me?"

Agnes's eyes moved — not to Horvath but to a different point on the floor, six inches to the right of the first point. Her expression didn't change. She might have been watching a crack in the linoleum. She might have been watching nothing at all.

"Can you tell me your name?"

Agnes opened her mouth. She made a sound — not quite a word, not quite silence. Something between a sigh and a syllable, like a radio tuned to the space between stations. Then she closed her mouth and resumed staring.

Horvath tried three more questions, spacing them out with the patience of a professional who'd been trained to give silence room. Agnes responded to none of them. At one point she raised her right hand — the one with the slight weakness from her stroke, which she normally overrode with the sheer force of her pharmaceutical precision — and touched her own face with the slow, exploratory gesture of someone encountering their own features for the first time. She ran her fingers along her jaw, her cheek, the bridge of her nose. Then her hand returned to her lap.

Four minutes. Horvath wrote a note, closed her binder, and left.

Carmen was passing in the hallway with a blood pressure cuff, heading toward 110A, and she caught a two-second glimpse through the closing door. Agnes was watching Horvath's back with the focused, evaluative expression of a pharmacist watching a technician count pills — not hostile, not amused, just assessing. Her eyes were completely lucid, her posture alert. Then the door swung shut and the glimpse was gone.

A seventy-six-year-old woman with forty-two years of pharmaceutical expertise, a woman who could calculate drug interactions in her head faster than the pharmacy software, who had once nearly lost her license for refusing to fill a contraindicated prescription because she'd rather lose the license than lose a patient — sitting in a vinyl chair in a room that smelled like Doris's incontinence pads, pretending she didn't know what language was.

---

Lola was in the dayroom when Horvath found her.

She'd positioned herself in the chair nearest the window, wearing a pink sweater with a small stain on the collar — coffee, Carmen thought, and probably deliberate. Her gold crucifix caught the late-morning light through the window. Her hands were wrapped around a rosary, the beads threaded between her fingers, and the tremor in her right hand made the beads click against each other in a soft, arrhythmic pattern, like a clock that had lost its mechanism but kept trying.

"Mrs. Fernandez? I'm Linda, from the—"

"Oh!" Lola looked up with such genuine-seeming delight that even Carmen, who knew the act, felt a moment of warmth. "Mija, sit down, sit. You want coffee? The coffee here isn't — well, it's not coffee, it's more like — you know my husband, Tomás, he used to make café con leche, real café, from scratch, his mother's recipe, she was from — where was she from?" Lola looked at the ceiling. "Matanzas? No, that's not right. Somewhere near there. A town outside Matanzas. There was a bakery. She always talked about this bakery—"

"Mrs. Fernandez, I just need to ask you a few—"

"—and the bread, Dios mío, she said the bread was so good you could cry, and Tomás always said she exaggerated about Cuba, you know how sons are about their mothers, but I think she didn't, I think the bread was exactly that good, because when we went to Miami for his cousin's wedding — when was that? Was that before the children or after? No, it was after, because Elena was walking already, she was — she had those little shoes—"

Horvath tried again. "Can you tell me—"

"—pink shoes, with a buckle, and Tomás said they were too expensive but I said what is too expensive for your daughter's feet? Forty dollars. For shoes she'd outgrow in six months and he acted like I'd bought a Cadillac. Men." Lola shook her head with fond exasperation, then turned to Horvath with sudden attention. "Are you married, mija?"

"I — yes."

"He treats you good?"

"Mrs. Fernandez—"

"You look tired. They work you too hard, I can see it. You eat today? You're thin. Not too thin, you look nice, but thin. Tomás's mother, she always said—"

Something shifted in Horvath's face. Not suspicion — the recalibration of a woman who'd done this job long enough to recognize when a conversation had left her control and wasn't coming back. She tried once more. "Do you know what medications you take?"

"Medications?" Lola's face went soft with confusion — not Harry's elaborate temporal displacement but something warmer, more like a woman who'd simply lost the thread of a conversation she'd been enjoying. "The pills? Oh, the pills. They give me — the girl gives me the — what's her name, la morena, the one with the — the pretty one—"

"Do you know what the pills are for?"

"For?" Lola held up her tremoring right hand, the rosary beads clicking. "This, I think. For the shaking. And for my — for sleeping. And there's a white one, a big white one that I don't like because it's — Tomás, you know, he never took pills. Not one. Not even aspirin. He said his mother taught him that the body knows how to heal itself, which, you know—" She leaned closer to Horvath, conspiratorial. "He was wrong about that. Obviously. But you couldn't tell him."

Twenty-two minutes.

Carmen knew because she was in the dayroom charting vitals for the Wing B residents and couldn't leave without making it obvious she was watching. Twenty-two minutes of Lola Fernandez spinning a fabric of her dead husband, her daughter's baby shoes, a bakery in Cuba that may or may not have existed, a trip to Miami that kept restarting from different beginnings each time she circled back to it, and a detailed account of Tomás's opinions about pills that somehow became a story about their first apartment in Union City. She never answered a single one of Horvath's questions. She never refused to answer, either. She was warm and delighted by the company and utterly, comprehensively useless as a source of clinical information, and the warmth was real — that was the thing. Lola genuinely liked people. She genuinely wanted Horvath to sit and talk. The performance worked because it was built on something true.

Horvath's handwriting got smaller and faster. She checked her watch twice. The second time, she closed her binder with a finality that was courteous but absolute.

"Thank you, Mrs. Fernandez. You've been very helpful."

"You're leaving? But I didn't finish telling you about the bakery. In Matanzas. Or near Matanzas, I don't—"

"Maybe next time."

"You come back, mija. And eat something. Promise me."

Horvath walked out of the dayroom with the stride of a woman who'd just lost twenty-two minutes she couldn't recover. She tucked her hair behind her ears. Her pen clicked against her binder three times in rapid succession.

Carmen looked at Lola. Lola was looking down at the rosary in her hands, turning the beads with her tremoring fingers, and her face — just for a moment, before the performance rebuilt itself — was tired in a way that had nothing to do with the talking. Tired from the distance between the woman telling the story and the woman choosing which story to tell.

---

Earl and Burt were interviewed together in Room 116A because Horvath was behind schedule.

Earl was in his bed, propped up on two pillows, a three-month-old issue of Time open on his lap to an article about western water rights he'd already read. When Horvath entered, he looked at her with the flat, uninvested expression of a man who had seen too many people come through this room to put energy into another one. He coughed — the deep, wet rattle that Carmen had been hearing more of lately, the kind of cough that came from somewhere below the chest and took its time leaving — and turned a page he wasn't reading.

"Mr. Washington, can you tell me today's date?"

Earl looked at her. He looked at the magazine. He looked at her again. Then he closed his eyes.

Horvath waited. She was good at waiting — Carmen could see that. Patient in the practiced way of someone who'd sat through a hundred of these silences.

Earl's breathing deepened. His head tilted slightly left. Within thirty seconds, he appeared to be asleep. He wasn't — Carmen could see from the hallway the way his fingers still held the magazine's edges, gripping rather than releasing — but Horvath couldn't see that from the visitor's angle, and the performance was more than good enough for a surveyor with sixteen facilities left this quarter.

Burt, in the bed by the window, was a different kind of quiet.

Burt Kovacs was eighty-eight and his dementia was real. He'd been playing since before the ring was a concept and long before the ring was a word anyone used — playing clubs in New York and Philadelphia and Budapest, his wife Erzsébet in the audience for the early years, his hands moving over piano keys with the speed and intention of someone who thought in music. Now those hands lay on the thermal blanket, long-fingered and mostly still, occasionally twitching in patterns that might have been remembered chord progressions or might have been nothing at all.

"Mr. Kovacs?" Horvath leaned forward, speaking clearly. "Can you tell me your name?"

Burt looked at her. His eyes moved across her face the way they moved across most faces now — searching, not quite finding, the effort of recognition visible but the result uncertain. He was alert in the way a person could be alert and lost at the same time.

"Erzsi?" he said softly. Then, in Hungarian, a string of syllables that sounded like a question asked with great tenderness to someone who wasn't in the room.

"I'm Linda. Can you tell me where you are?"

"The music is..." Burt trailed off. He seemed to listen to something that existed only for him — his head tilted, his fingers twitched on the blanket, and for a moment his face held the concentration of a man hearing a passage he wanted to remember. Then the concentration dissolved into something like distress. "Where's — the thing. The book. My book."

Horvath wrote a notation. She asked two more questions. Burt answered one with a fragment about Erzsébet that trailed into Hungarian and ignored the other entirely, staring at his own hands as if they'd been attached to his wrists by someone else and he was still adjusting to the arrangement.

Six minutes. The fastest interview of the day.

It was funny and it wasn't. The other performances — Harry's Oscar-caliber 1978, Agnes's pharmaceutical amnesia, Lola's twenty-two-minute filibuster, Earl's tactical nap — were comedy. Burt was the thing the comedy was built on top of. The confused old man the others were pretending to be was the confused old man Burt actually was, and the gap between their pretending and his being was the gap the whole operation lived inside of.

Carmen went into 116A after Horvath left, ostensibly to check vitals. Earl opened one eye.

"She gone?"

"She's gone."

Earl grunted and reopened his magazine. Burt was still looking at his hands.

---

Horvath found Carmen at the sub-station at two-fifteen, after lunch.

Carmen had been expecting this. Not with dread, exactly — with the specific, concentrated focus she used when she knew a task would require precision under pressure and she couldn't afford to hesitate. She'd rehearsed nothing. Rehearsing would have made her sound rehearsed, and Horvath had been doing this too long to miss the difference between a nurse who was remembering and a nurse who was reciting.

"Ms. Reyes, I'd like to go over some of your medication management documentation."

"Sure." Carmen pulled the second chair out from behind the sub-station desk. "What do you need?"

Horvath sat and opened her binder to a tabbed section. Carmen could see the photocopies — her count sheets, her handwriting, her initials, her license number stamped at the top of each page.

"I'm seeing some variances in your controlled substance counts." Horvath ran her finger down a column of numbers with the steady tracking of someone who read count sheets the way other people read newspapers. "Six documented discrepancies over the past three months. Oxycodone, alprazolam, tramadol. All occurring on your shifts."

Carmen's hands were on the desk, fingers laced. She'd placed them there before Horvath had started talking — a deliberate choice, because if she held a pen she'd click it, and if she touched her badge she'd adjust it, and her hands would do the things they did when she was lying and she didn't trust them not to.

"Right. I documented those." Her voice was level, professional. The voice of a nurse who'd been through audits before, which she had — just never from this side. "We've had some issues with the overnight handoff. The hybrid charting system — we're half electronic, half paper for the controlled substance logs — it creates gaps. There was a period over the summer where the night nurse was documenting counts on the paper log but not entering them in the system, so when I'd do my end-of-shift count, the electronic total didn't match the physical count."

It came out the way she needed it to. Not too smooth — she paused where a nurse recalling a genuine frustration would pause, frowned at the memory of the documentation mess, let her voice carry the specific irritation of a competent person whose work had been made to look sloppy by someone else's failures. The story was the kind of thing that actually happened at facilities like Lakeview, where systems were held together with habit and inertia and the night nurse might have been hired off a staffing app last Tuesday.

Horvath wrote something on her clipboard. "You attributed three of the six discrepancies to overnight documentation errors."

"Three to overnight, one to a pharmacy delivery that came in short — I called the pharmacy on that one, they confirmed the miscount on their end and sent a corrected invoice — and two I honestly couldn't track down. I documented them as unresolved variances. That's what the protocol says to do."

"The protocol says to notify your administrator."

"I notified Mr. Moorehead on both unresolved discrepancies. In writing." This part was true. Carmen had sent Cliff two emails, dated and timestamped. Cliff had not responded to either one, because Cliff did not respond to things that might require him to do something.

Horvath looked at her. It was not an accusatory look. It was the look of a woman who had reviewed controlled substance logs at dozens of facilities this quarter and had this exact conversation at half of them. The same discrepancies, the same explanations, the same hybrid systems and overnight handoffs and pharmacy miscounts. The infrastructure of American nursing home care produced these gaps as reliably as it produced bedsores and staff turnover, and Horvath had been swimming in it long enough to know that most of the time the paperwork problems were real and the explanations were true and the system was simply broken in the ordinary way.

Carmen could see the competence in how Horvath read the log — not scanning but tracking, following the numbers across columns the way Agnes tracked dosages, catching the pattern of Carmen's initials recurring next to every variance. In a different world, with fewer facilities to cover and more hours in the day and a mandate that extended beyond documentation review into actual investigation, Horvath might have looked harder. Might have pulled the pharmacy invoices. Might have cross-referenced Carmen's shift schedule against the specific medications that came up short and noticed that oxycodone and alprazolam — the drugs with the highest street value — were disproportionately represented.

But Horvath had seventeen more facilities to visit before the end of the quarter. The skin under her eyes was dark and loose, and her pen had been clicking against her binder for the past ten minutes in a rhythm that suggested not enough sleep and too much coffee.

"I'm going to note the documentation gaps as a finding," Horvath said. "You'll need to implement a reconciliation process for the paper-electronic crossover. Both formats should be updated simultaneously at the point of count."

"Absolutely. I've actually been talking to our charge nurse about that."

She hadn't. She wouldn't.

Horvath made a final note, closed her binder, and stood. "Your medication administration records are thorough, Ms. Reyes. I can see you take the documentation seriously."

"Thank you."

"The discrepancies are a concern, but the documentation trail supports your explanations. I'll include recommendations in the report."

Carmen nodded. Smiled — not too much, just the professional acknowledgment of a review that had gone well. Horvath gathered her thermos and binder, tucked her hair behind both ears simultaneously, and headed toward the conference room where Cliff was waiting in a chair he'd positioned to face the door, his posture suggesting a man who expected to be told something he didn't want to hear.

Carmen sat at the sub-station for another minute after Horvath left. She unlaced her fingers and found that they'd been pressing against each other hard enough to leave white marks on her knuckles. She flexed them until the color came back.

---

The survey concluded at four with a stack of findings that would keep Cliff busy for two weeks: the medication documentation gaps that Carmen had invented a reconciliation process for, a dietary storage temperature variance Keisha had photographed, two overdue care plan updates, a hand sanitizer dispenser that was empty, and the motivational poster, which Keisha had noted was covering a fire safety placard in violation of corridor clearance standards. Minor deficiencies. The kind of findings that let a facility continue operating without penalty while generating enough paperwork to prove the state health department was performing its function.

Cliff ordered pizza. Three boxes from a place on Route 1 — pepperoni, plain, and something with roasted vegetables that no one touched. He set them on the break room table with a two-liter of warm Pepsi and a sleeve of paper cups and told the staff they'd done a great job. He used those exact words. "Great job, everybody." He said it twice. He didn't mention Mrs. Patterson's pressure ulcer, or the staffing ratio that made it inevitable, or the fact that the facility's survey score was a number that measured documentation completeness rather than whether anyone in the building was actually being cared for.

Tammy took two slices of pepperoni and ate them standing by the break room window, watching the surveyors' state sedan back out of its parking space.

"Flowers are dead," she said, chewing.

"They're not dead," Cliff said. "They're wilting."

"They're dead, Cliff. Rest in peace."

Carmen didn't eat. She poured a cup of the warm Pepsi, drank half, and tipped the rest into the break room sink. She finished her shift. She charted the afternoon medications, updated Mrs. Patterson's wound assessment — the ulcer was the same, neither better nor worse, which was what happened when you turned someone every three hours instead of two — and restocked the medication cart for the evening nurse. Normal work. The kind of work she could do with her hands and the front of her mind while the rest of her sat somewhere else entirely, in a place she couldn't yet look at directly.

At six-forty, she clocked out. She walked to her car. She sat in the driver's seat and put the key in the ignition.

She did not turn it.

The parking lot was half-empty — day shift gone, evening shift trickling in, the gap visible in the spaces between cars. Late September light came in low and amber through the windshield, the kind of light that made everything look like a photograph of itself. A plastic bag skidded across the asphalt and caught on a shopping cart someone had left near the dumpster. The courtyard trees were starting to turn — just barely, the first suggestions of yellow at the edges of leaves that had been green all summer.

Carmen sat with her hands on the steering wheel, her key in the ignition, her foot on nothing, and looked at the building she'd spent the day protecting.

Beige stucco. Small windows with institutional blinds, half of them crooked. A sign that read LAKEVIEW GARDENS REHABILITATION AND NURSING CENTER in letters that had faded from green to a color that was no longer any color in particular. No lake. No garden. Three scrubby trees in a paved courtyard and a view of Route 1 if you stood on your toes.

Inside that building, Harry Zielinski was sitting in his wheelchair, possibly staring at the parking lot, possibly staring at 1978, and there was no way to know which from out here. Agnes Chen was in her room with Doris's breathing for company, her hands folded and her mind running pharmaceutical calculations that no one would ever need again. Lola Fernandez was holding her rosary and the story about the bakery near Matanzas was over, and the distance between the woman who told it and the woman who chose to tell it was the distance Lola crossed every day and couldn't talk about with anyone. Earl Washington was coughing into a magazine. Burt Kovacs was looking at his hands.

And Carmen Reyes, LPN, license number 26NR03891, had sat across from a state health department surveyor and manufactured a coherent, plausible, procedurally fluent explanation for the documented evidence of a controlled substance diversion operation running out of a nursing home in central New Jersey. She had done it without raising her voice or touching her badge or clicking a pen. She had done it with the specific competence of a nurse who understood exactly which excuses a surveyor with too many facilities and not enough time would accept without further investigation.

She had done it without hesitating.

Her phone buzzed on the passenger seat. Her mother — probably about picking up Reggie. She let it ring.

She sat in her car for fifteen minutes. She watched the light change from amber to something grayer. She felt the steering wheel under her hands, the vinyl warm from the closed car and the day's last sun, and she noticed that her grip was tight enough to feel her own pulse in her fingertips.

She wasn't crying. She was somewhere else — not before tears or after them but in a different country from tears entirely, a place where the thing she'd done was settling into the fact of her life the way sediment settles into water, becoming part of the composition. She had lied to a government official. She had lied fluently, competently, and well. She had protected five elderly people running a prescription drug operation out of a facility that couldn't be bothered to turn its patients on schedule, and she had done it because she understood why they did what they did, and because the alternative would have destroyed them and probably destroyed her, and because when the moment came she discovered she was good at it.

That was the part that sat in her like a stone she'd swallowed. Not that she'd lied. That she'd been good at it. That the same professional competence she'd spent seven years building — the documentation, the precision, the procedural fluency that was supposed to be the thing that made her trustworthy — was exactly what made her an effective liar. The skills were the same skills. The voice was the same voice.

She turned the key. The engine caught on the first try. She put the car in reverse, backed out of her space, and drove to her mother's house to pick up her son.
