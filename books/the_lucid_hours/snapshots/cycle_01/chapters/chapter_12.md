# Chapter 12

The word she was looking for was aluminum.

Not an important word. She'd been trying to describe the foil over the breakfast tray to her roommate Margaret, who couldn't hear her and wouldn't have cared, and the word had gone missing somewhere between her brain and her mouth. She'd stood there with her hand out, miming the act of peeling back the foil, and what came out was "the — the shiny. The paper that's not paper."

Margaret hadn't looked up from her oatmeal.

Lola sat on her bed after breakfast and tried the word again. Aluminum. There it was. Back in place, as if it had never left, and she couldn't tell — this was the thing, this was the thing that woke her up some nights with her heart fast under her nightgown — she couldn't tell if she'd lost it because she'd been performing confusion for fourteen months and the act had gotten into her, worn a groove, or because she was seventy-nine years old with Parkinson's and sometimes seventy-nine-year-old women with Parkinson's forgot words. Both were true. That was the problem. Both were true and she couldn't tell which one was happening at any given moment, and there was no test for it, no blood draw or brain scan that would come back and say this forgetting is real and this one is theater and this one, Dolores, this one we're not sure about.

She held her left hand up in front of her face. The tremor was there, as it always was — a fine, persistent shaking, worse in the mornings before her carbidopa-levodopa kicked in, worse when she was tired, worse when she was afraid. Right now it was moderate. A ripple, not a quake. She watched it and felt nothing useful. You couldn't argue with a tremor. You couldn't ask it to explain itself. It was just there, the way weather was there, and the medication helped and didn't help, and some mornings she could button her blouse on the first try and some mornings the buttons won and she asked Tammy for help with the same flat acceptance she'd learned to apply to everything her body did without her permission.

Her roommate Margaret — Margaret Oates, eighty-six, severe vascular dementia, no family visitors in the eleven months Lola had shared this room with her — was being wheeled to physical therapy by an aide Lola didn't recognize. A new one. They came and went so fast that Lola had stopped learning their names, which felt like its own small cruelty but was also just practical. You couldn't mourn everyone who left.

She got up and walked to the window. October had arrived while she wasn't looking, the way it did, and the three trees in the courtyard had started to turn — though "turn" was generous. They were mostly just giving up, dropping leaves in patchy clumps onto the pavement below. Beyond them, the parking lot, half-full at this hour. Beyond that, Route 27. If she pressed her face to the glass she could see the Dunkin' Donuts sign, which she did sometimes, not because she wanted a donut but because the sign was bright and specific and it reminded her that there was a world out there that did things she no longer did, like drive and buy coffee and stand in a line and choose.

She was supposed to be confused right now. That was her job, her contribution: the lookout, the smokescreen, the warm old woman who distracted nurses and visitors with stories while the real work happened behind her. In the hallways, during meals, during activities, when the aides came in, when Dr. Prasad made her biweekly rounds — Lola Fernandez was chatty, affectionate, mildly disoriented, calling everyone mijo or mija, telling stories that went in circles, asking what day it was three times in a conversation. The warmth was real. The stories were real, most of them. The confusion was manufactured, and it had been easy at first. Fun, even. She'd been a teacher for thirty-one years, fourth grade, and every day in a fourth-grade classroom was a performance of patience and enthusiasm that sometimes became real by third period. Playing confused was simpler than that. You just let the words tangle, let the sentences loop, let your eyes go soft when someone asked you a direct question. You became a version of yourself that people already expected a seventy-nine-year-old woman in a nursing home to be.

But fourteen months of playing confused had done something she hadn't anticipated. The confusion had weight. It pressed down on her actual mind the way water pressed on a sponge — slowly, invisibly, until the sponge was heavy and couldn't remember being dry. She forgot words now. Not performing it, not choosing to stumble for the benefit of whoever was watching, but actually reaching for a word and finding the shelf bare. Aluminum. Thermometer, last week — she'd been trying to tell Carmen her room was cold and she'd said "the thing, the thing that measures" and Carmen had looked at her with that careful nurse's attention and Lola had smiled through it, had made it part of the act, but alone afterward she'd sat on this bed and said thermometer six times fast with her hands balled in her lap. Carnation, the week before that, and that one had frightened her, because Tomás used to bring her carnations, cheap ones from the bodega on Bergenline Avenue, and if she was losing the word for the flower her husband brought her then she was losing a piece of him, and she had so few pieces left.

She thought about Burt. She didn't want to but she did. Burt had fallen two weeks ago and come back from the hospital worse, quieter, the confusion in his eyes no longer intermittent but settled, and watching him — watching a man whose dementia had been real all along, real in the way hers wasn't, or hadn't been, or might be becoming — had put a sound in her ears she couldn't shake. A kind of hum. Not a sound anyone else could hear. Just the low, steady vibration of a future she was walking toward whether she wanted to or not, a future where the word aluminum didn't come back, where thermometer stayed gone, where the act and the woman performing it merged into one thing and nobody, including her, could tell the difference.

Tomás would have said something about this. Something practical, something slightly impatient, because Tomás had not been a gentle man about worry. He'd been gentle about other things — he could cup a baby bird in his hands with a softness that made you forget those same hands poured concrete for twenty years — but worry made him restless, made him want to fix. "Lola, stop," he would have said. "Either it's happening or it's not, and sitting there scaring yourself doesn't change which one it is."

He was right. He was always right about that kind of thing, and wrong about everything else.

She sat on the bed and let herself think about him, the way she did most mornings. Not a prayer exactly. A visit. She talked to Tomás in her head — not as delusion, nothing like that, but as habit, the way you talked to someone you'd shared a bed with for forty-three years. The conversation was one-sided and it always had been, because even alive Tomás hadn't been a big talker, and dead he was even less so.

He'd died of pancreatic cancer ten years ago, and he had not gone peacefully. That was the thing about the euphemisms people reached for — passed away, went gently, is in a better place — they were engineering projects, little bridges built over something people didn't want to look down at. What was underneath: Tomás in a hospice bed in their apartment in Union City, a bed the insurance company had delivered with more efficiency than they'd ever shown for his actual treatment, and three months of dying that he'd spent furious. Not sad. Not resigned. Not at-peace-with-God. Furious. He'd cursed the nurses who came to change his IV. He'd cursed the hospice chaplain, a kind young woman named Sister Beth who'd tried to talk to him about acceptance, and Tomás had told her to take her acceptance and get out of his house. He meant it. He was dying at sixty-eight and he had poured concrete and raised two children and paid his taxes and fixed the sink when it leaked and gone to Mass when Lola asked him to and now his pancreas was eating itself alive and no one could tell him why.

The last two weeks, the morphine drip ran almost constantly and he was in and out, and the in was worse than the out because the in meant he knew where he was and what was happening to him. He'd lost forty pounds. His skin had the color and texture of wet newspaper. He vomited bile into a plastic basin because his stomach couldn't hold anything, not even water, not even the ice chips Lola put on his lips with a spoon. The morphine constipated him so badly that she gave him suppositories, which she did without complaint, without performance, because she'd also wiped his back when he sweated through his shirts at night and held his penis when he couldn't aim at the urinal anymore, and she'd done all of it because that was what forty-three years of marriage actually meant. Not the vows. The vows didn't know to include any of this.

He'd died at 4:17 on a Tuesday morning in January. She'd been sitting next to the bed, not sleeping, her hand on his arm. His breathing had changed — the rattling, the gaps that got longer — and then it stopped, and the room went quiet in a way rooms with living people in them never were, and she'd sat there for a long time before she picked up the phone. Those minutes were the last minutes she'd ever be in a room with Tomás. Once she called the hospice number, the machinery of death would start — the funeral home, the insurance paperwork, the obituary, the calls to Roberto and Miriam, the casseroles from neighbors who meant well and didn't know what else to do — and she wasn't ready for that. She wasn't ready for the shift from wife to widow, the last role change she'd ever have to make, or so she'd thought at the time.

She'd been wrong about that. She'd made one more: from widow to patient. And one more after that: from patient to lookout, to performer, to a woman who watched through a mask of confusion while pills moved through cookie tins and envelopes of cash went into the pockets of a man everyone believed was her nephew.

Tomás, she thought. Amor. What would you say about all this.

She knew what he'd say. He'd say: "Are you eating? Is anyone hurting you? Then do what you need to do and stop worrying." That was Tomás. He operated from a simple moral architecture: are you fed, are you safe, is someone being cruel. Everything else was negotiable.

Her son Roberto called every Sunday from Chicago. The calls lasted about twelve minutes. She'd timed one once, not to catch him, just out of the bleak curiosity that came from having too much time and not enough to spend it on. Twelve minutes covered his wife Elena, the kids, work, the weather in Chicago, and "how are you doing, Mamá?" She always said fine. She performed mild confusion on these calls now, because Roberto talked to the staff, and if Lola was lucid and articulate on the phone but drifting in the building, someone would notice. So she let her sentences circle. She asked about the grandchildren and then asked again five minutes later. Roberto's voice when she did this — patient, a little too loud, careful — sounded like the voice Helen Chen used when she visited Agnes, and that comparison made Lola's stomach turn.

Her daughter Miriam sent cards from Orlando. Birthday, Christmas, Mother's Day, and random Hallmark cards with flowers on the front and messages like "Thinking of you today!" The exclamation point always felt wrong to Lola, something pushed when it should be quiet. If you were thinking of someone, you could come. But Miriam had two kids and a husband who traveled for work and a mortgage she'd mentioned twice with the specific worry that meant it was too much, and Orlando was far, and Lola didn't blame her. She didn't blame either of them. They were living the lives America demanded of children — spread out, working, building families in whatever city had jobs — and the alternative was guilt, which helped no one and which Lola had seen do terrible things to other families in this building. The daughter in Hackensack who visited every day and cried every time, until the resident — a man named Phil who had Lewy body dementia and didn't know anyone's name — started getting agitated whenever she showed up. The son who came once a month from Pennsylvania and spent the entire visit staring at his phone. Guilt made people stupid. Lola preferred the cards.

She touched the crucifix at her throat. The gold was warm from her skin. She didn't pray — she hadn't prayed since Tomás died, because she had some things to say to God and none of them were requests — but the crucifix was her mother's, and touching it was a way of touching her mother, dead longer than Tomás and missed in a way that lived in Lola's hands, in the specific shape her fingers made when they closed around the small gold cross.

Ay, Dolores. What are you doing.

She said it to herself the way her mother would have. Not with judgment. With tired wonder.

---

Tammy found her in the courtyard at two o'clock, which was how most of their afternoons together started — not by plan but by routine, by the way two people who lived in the same small world eventually fell into rhythm without deciding to. Tammy's break was 1:45 to 2:15, and Lola was usually out here by one-thirty because the room was too quiet after Margaret was wheeled back from therapy and fell asleep, and the courtyard, even with its scraggly trees and its view of the parking lot, was outside. Outside still counted for something.

"Move over," Tammy said, and sat down on the bench without waiting for Lola to shift. She was wearing her teal scrubs, the ones with the bleach stain on the left thigh that she'd given up trying to hide, and her hair was in a knot that looked like it had been assembled in the dark, which it probably had been — Tammy's studio apartment, the one she'd moved into after the divorce, had one overhead light and a window that faced a wall. Lola knew this because Tammy had described it three times, each time in the same flat way, as if bad lighting were just another item on a list of things that had gone wrong and she didn't have the energy to rank them anymore.

"Coffee?" Tammy held up two Styrofoam cups from the staff kitchen. The coffee at Lakeview was terrible — thin, vaguely brown, produced by a machine nobody had cleaned since the previous administrator — but Tammy always brought two cups, and she always got it right. Two sugars, milk not cream. It was the kind of thing that shouldn't matter and that mattered more than almost anything.

"Gracias, mija." Lola took the cup with both hands, partly because of the tremor and partly because the warmth felt good. The October air had an edge to it now, a sharpness that meant summer had stopped pretending. Two of the courtyard trees had gone mostly bare, the third holding on to its leaves in scattered orange patches, stubborn or just slow. Leaves littered the pavement around the benches, dry and curling.

They sat. The courtyard was empty except for them and a squirrel working the base of the middle tree, nosing through the fallen leaves for something edible. Lola watched it with a recognition she didn't examine.

"So," Tammy said.

"So."

Tammy drank her coffee and made the face she always made, the one that said this is undrinkable and she was going to finish every drop. "Greg's got a girlfriend."

Lola didn't look at her. She knew from fourteen months of performed inattention, from a lifetime of reading rooms, that Tammy needed her to not make this a moment. "Mm."

"Yeah. Brandy. Her name's Brandy, like the — yeah." Tammy's voice was flat in the way that meant she was working hard to keep it flat. "She's twenty-eight. Works at a Verizon store in Woodbridge."

"Ay."

"It's fine. It's been eight months. It's whatever."

It was not fine and not whatever and they both knew it. Lola could hear the specific frequency of Tammy's not-fine, the way her consonants sharpened and the space between words stretched, as if she were testing each one's ability to hold her weight before she set it down. Tammy's divorce had surfaced over these courtyard afternoons in pieces, not chronologically, not as a story she was telling, but the way someone emptied a bag onto a table: a detail here, a fact there, scattered and unsorted. Greg had left in March. He'd been seeing someone before that — not Brandy, someone else, a woman from his gym whose name Tammy had never said out loud, which told Lola more than the name would have. Papers were final in July. Tammy had moved into the studio in Perth Amboy with her cat, who'd immediately peed on the mattress. She'd replaced the mattress, not the cat. That detail had been delivered with the particular deadpan that meant Tammy was choosing between laughing and something worse.

"Twenty-eight," Tammy said again, looking at the squirrel instead of Lola. "I'm forty-three and he picks a girl who wasn't born when I started working here."

Lola wanted to say something real. The want was physical, pressing at her sternum like a cough she couldn't release. She wanted to drop the mask and say: Mija, I watched my husband die in our apartment. He cursed the chaplain and I wiped bile off his chin and I gave him suppositories because nobody else was going to and we didn't talk about dignity because dignity was a word for people who hadn't had to do those things yet. And twenty-eight-year-old Brandy from Verizon is not going to do that for Greg when the time comes, and the time comes for everybody. She wanted to say: your ex-husband is an ordinary man doing an ordinary stupid thing and it'll hurt you until one day it won't and I can't tell you when that day is because grief doesn't keep a schedule, not even the grief for a man who's still alive and just chose someone else.

But she was Lola Fernandez, room 112A. Warm, confused, mildly disoriented. And Tammy was the person who brushed her hair on the mornings the tremor made the brush jump and skitter in her grip. Tammy was the one who knew about the two sugars and the milk. If Lola spoke too clearly, too specifically, Tammy would notice. Tammy was tired, not stupid. She noticed things. And noticing would lead to questions, and questions would lead to the ring, and the ring would lead to Harry and Agnes and Earl and Carmen and Marcus and Burt, and Lola would be the one who'd unraveled everything because she couldn't stand being lied to about coffee by a person she was lying to about everything else.

So she said: "Men." She said it with a wave of her hand, a puff of air, the way her performed self would — dismissing the whole gender as a weather pattern. "Tomás — my husband — he forgot our anniversary once. Thirty years, mija. Thirty years married and he forgot. Brought flowers the next day like that fixed it." She let the story ramble, let it loop the way her cover required, and the story was true — Tomás had forgotten their thirtieth, and the flowers had been carnations from the bodega, and she'd forgiven him before he walked through the door because forgiveness wasn't something she'd ever had to decide about with Tomás, it was just the climate they lived in — but the truth of the story was separate from its job here, which was to be harmless, to fill space, to be the kind of thing a confused old woman said to a younger woman's pain.

"Greg never forgot an anniversary," Tammy said. "He just forgot to give a shit about the other three hundred and sixty-four days."

"Mm."

"Sorry. Language."

"Mija, I've heard worse." She had. She'd heard Tomás curse God and a chaplain and the pharmaceutical company that made the morphine that wasn't strong enough and the insurance adjuster who'd denied the home health aide request two weeks before he died.

"Yeah." Tammy stretched her legs out. Her shoes were the white nursing clogs that all the CNAs wore, scuffed gray on the sides and collapsed at the heels from the miles she logged every shift — the steps between rooms, the laps around the wing, the back-and-forth to the supply closet for gloves and wipes and the incontinence briefs they went through forty of per day. Lola looked at those shoes and saw fifteen years of work that nobody wanted to discuss over dinner. "She posted pictures on Facebook. Her and Greg at some restaurant with candles. On the table. Candles." Tammy's voice thinned on the word. "We ate off paper plates because I was too tired to wash dishes after a twelve-hour."

"I'm sorry, mija."

"Don't be. I'm just — it's just stupid. The whole thing's stupid."

Lola felt the guilt arrive the way it always did when Tammy told her something real — not as a pang or a wave but as a weight, a physical addition to what she was already carrying, settling in next to the grief and the tremor and the fear about the words she kept losing. She was deceiving this woman. Every afternoon, every cup of coffee, every time Tammy sat on this bench and said things that mattered to her, Lola was receiving them through a screen of performed confusion, and Tammy didn't know. Tammy brought her coffee and brushed her hair and told her about Greg and the apartment and the bad lighting and the cat, and Lola took all of it and gave back a character. A warm character. A character who cared, because Lola did care, that was the terrible part — the caring was real and the person doing the caring was a fraud, and those two facts existed in the same body and she couldn't separate them any more than she could separate the performance tremor from the Parkinson's tremor.

The coffee shook in her left hand. She put the cup down on the bench between them.

"You know what the worst part is?" Tammy said.

"Tell me."

"I miss him." She said it the way someone confessed to a crime they were ashamed of. Not the volume. The posture — her shoulders came forward, her chin dropped. "Not the — I don't miss the lying. Or the gym woman. Or the part where he let me pick up doubles every week while he watched videos on his phone. I miss having someone there. In the apartment. I miss noise. It's so quiet, Lola. My place is so quiet. It's like —" She stopped. Picked at the rim of her Styrofoam cup. "You know when you turn the TV off and the silence afterward is louder than the silence before you turned it on? That's what it's like. Every night."

Lola knew that silence. She'd lived inside it for ten years, first in the apartment in Union City where it had been Tomás-shaped — a quiet that filled the exact spaces his sounds had occupied: the scratch of his pen on the crossword, the wheeze in his breathing that she'd told him to get checked and he hadn't, the thud of his boots by the door — and then in this room, where the quiet was different, institutional, a quiet made up of the sounds just outside it: Margaret's breathing, the ventilation hum, the distant call bells.

"I know," Lola said.

And then, because the words pressed against her and she had to let some of them out or they'd crack something inside her, she said: "You're a good person, mija."

She said it in her performed voice. She said it with the warm, slightly drifting delivery she used for everything. But she meant it from underneath the performance, from the place where Lola was still entirely herself, where the confusion hadn't reached, where she was a woman who recognized goodness because she'd spent enough years around its absence to know the difference.

Tammy looked at her. For a second — less than a second, a flicker — something crossed her face. Not suspicion of the ring. Nothing that specific. Just a flicker of attention, the way a person looked when a sentence arrived with more weight than its packaging suggested. As if Tammy had heard something underneath the warm old woman's voice that didn't match the warm old woman's diagnosis.

Then it passed. Tammy looked back at the squirrel, which had climbed halfway up the garbage can near the courtyard entrance and was evaluating its options.

"Good people get paid shit," Tammy said.

"That's true."

"Yeah."

They sat. A car pulled into the parking lot, idled for a minute near the entrance, then backed out and drove away. Someone who'd come to the wrong place, or come to the right place and changed their mind. Lola had seen it happen before — family members who pulled in and sat in their cars for ten, fifteen minutes, working up to the doors. She understood. This building was hard to enter and harder to leave, because leaving meant driving away from someone who couldn't drive with you, and that act, repeated every visit, accumulated into something that felt like a judgment even when it wasn't one.

"I gotta get back," Tammy said. She stood and her back cracked, two sharp reports like knuckles popping, the sound of a forty-three-year-old body that did the physical work of a much younger person for compensation that assumed she was replaceable. She picked up both coffee cups — hers and Lola's — because that was Tammy. She picked up the cups. She remembered the sugars. She came back.

"Tammy."

"Yeah?"

Lola looked at her. She wanted to say: I'm not confused. I haven't been confused. I remember every word you've said to me about Greg and Brandy and the apartment and the cat. I'm lying to you every day, every time you brush my hair and bring me coffee and sit on this bench and tell me things that cost you something. I'm lying and you deserve better than a friend who isn't real.

She almost said it. The words were right there, lined up, ready to walk out of her mouth and ruin everything.

"Tell Brandy I said hi," she said instead.

Tammy snorted. Not a real laugh. The short, hard sound a person made when something was funny enough to crack the surface but not enough to break through. "Yeah, I'll get right on that."

She walked back inside. The courtyard door closed behind her with the heavy pneumatic exhale that all institutional doors made, a sound engineered for a building designed to contain rather than welcome. Lola sat with the bench to herself and the two wet rings where the coffee cups had been and the leaves collecting in the corners of the pavement and the last of the afternoon light angling across the parking lot.

Her left hand trembled against her thigh. She put her right hand over it, covering it, the way you'd hold a small animal that was shaking — not to stop the shaking but to let it know the shaking had been noticed.

The word came back to her. Aluminum. It had been there all along, she supposed. It was the not-knowing that was the thing. Not the forgetting — everybody forgot, that was just the tax on being alive long enough — but the not-knowing which kind of forgetting it was. Whether the word had gone because she'd spent fourteen months teaching her brain to lose things on purpose, or because her brain was losing things on its own now, because seventy-nine years and Parkinson's and grief and a daily performance of incapacity had, together, begun to do what each one alone might not have managed.

She said the word once, out loud, to the empty courtyard. Aluminum. Just to prove she could.

The trees didn't answer. The parking lot didn't care. She sat there until the light changed and the air got cold enough that her tremor worsened, and then she went inside and became the person everyone expected her to be.
