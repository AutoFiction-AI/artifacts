# Chapter 7

Carmen got to the school twelve minutes early because she'd overestimated the lights on Route 27. She parked behind a silver Lexus with a stick-figure family decal on the rear window — two parents, three kids, a dog — and cut the engine to save gas.

Mid-September, but the heat hadn't broken all the way. It was looser now, the air starting to think about fall without committing. She cracked the window. Reggie's elementary school was a single-story brick rectangle behind chain-link, its entrance banner reading WELCOME BACK VIKINGS in bubble letters some PTA parent had hand-painted with more enthusiasm than skill. The crossing guard stood at the curb in an orange vest, talking to another parent about something that required hand gestures.

Her mother usually did pickup. But Gloria had a rheumatology follow-up in Edison — her hands again, the swelling that flared and eased on its own calendar — and wouldn't be back until five. Carmen had the day off. Her first in eleven days, if she counted the half-shift she'd swapped with Denise on Tuesday, which she counted, because she'd driven there and back and used the gas.

She checked her phone. No messages from Lakeview. No messages from anyone. She'd been doing this for five days now — picking up the phone, checking the screen, waiting for something she couldn't name — ever since the night she'd told Harry she wouldn't give him a week and then given him one anyway. Two days left.

She put the phone facedown on the passenger seat and watched a line of kids file out the side door. Whatever was happening at Lakeview wasn't happening right now. Right now she was a mother in a pickup line, and the only math that mattered was the grocery list folded in her bag and the $74 she'd budgeted for it.

Reggie came out at 2:47, carrying his backpack on one shoulder because both straps were apparently uncool even in second grade. He climbed into the back seat and buckled himself in without being asked. He'd started doing that sometime in September, and each time it happened she felt the quiet pride of a milestone she'd missed noticing when it first appeared.

"Mom. Did you know sea turtles can hold their breath for five hours?"

"I didn't know that." She pulled out of the line, checking the mirror. "Is that for your project?"

"Yeah. Five hours. That's like from school to bedtime."

"Almost."

"Can we get poster board? Ms. Aldridge says we need poster board for the presentation part."

"What color?"

He considered this with the seriousness it deserved. "Blue. Because the ocean."

"We'll see what they have."

She drove to the ShopRite on Route 18. Not the closer one on Easton Avenue, which had better produce but charged thirty cents more per pound on chicken, and she went through three pounds a week. The parking lot was half-full — mid-afternoon on a weekday, the good hour before the after-work rush hit. She got a cart with a stuck front wheel because she didn't feel like walking it back for another one, and Reggie walked beside her with his hand on the cart's edge, steering them toward the cereal aisle by habit.

"We're going around," she said. "Produce first."

"Can we get Cinnamon Toast Crunch?"

"We'll see."

They wouldn't. It was $4.79 a box and the store-brand version was $2.49 and tasted close enough that Reggie had stopped noticing after the first bowl. But she didn't say no yet because saying no required a reason, and sometimes he forgot to ask again by the time they reached that aisle.

She worked the list. Bananas, sixty-nine cents a pound — she took four. A bag of Gala apples, $3.99 for the three-pound bag. The Honeycrisps were $6.49 and she didn't look at them. Carrots, a head of broccoli, a bag of yellow onions. She picked up the cherry tomatoes at $3.49 a pint, held them for two seconds, put them back, and took a can of diced tomatoes for eighty-nine cents instead.

Reggie held the apples while she compared unit prices on chicken thighs. Bone-in skin-on was $1.89 a pound. Boneless skinless was $3.29. She did the math on yield — bone-in lost maybe twenty percent to waste, but the bones went into stock, which gave her soup base for two dinners, which dropped the per-meal cost below the boneless even before she factored in the labor of stripping the meat. She put two family packs of bone-in thighs in the cart. $7.12 total.

Rice, $3.49 for the five-pound bag — still half a bag at home, skip it this week. Dry black beans, $1.29. Eggs, $2.99 for the eighteen-count. She could never bring herself to buy the dozen at $2.49; the per-egg math was wrong and the per-egg math was the only math. Bread, $1.89, store brand. Peanut butter, $2.69 — she was loyal to this brand because Reggie liked it and some things she refused to substitute.

In the cereal aisle Reggie stood in front of the Cinnamon Toast Crunch and performed a silence Carmen recognized from Gloria. The wordless occupation of a position, patient and obvious.

"Next time," she said.

He picked up the store brand without argument. That was worse than complaining. It meant he'd learned the difference between what you wanted and what the budget allowed, and he was seven, and she hated that he already knew.

The poster board was in the school supply section, wedged between folders and glue sticks. Blue, $1.29. She put it in the cart and he held it flat across the top of the bags like he was transporting something fragile and important.

At checkout the total came to $68.43. Under budget by $5.57, and she'd come back Thursday for milk and whatever fruit was on manager's special. She paid with her debit card and watched the number leave her checking account in real time on her phone's banking app. $411.18 remaining. Rent was due in sixteen days.

She loaded the bags into the Civic's trunk while Reggie sat in the back with the poster board across his lap, tracing waves in the air with his finger.

---

At home — the apartment was on the second floor of a two-family house, on a street where the sidewalks buckled over tree roots — she unpacked groceries while Reggie spread his project across the kitchen table. He had a library book about sea turtles with a cracked spine, a printed worksheet from Ms. Aldridge with guiding questions, and six markers that were all running low because markers were always running low.

"Mom, loggerheads can weigh three hundred and fifty pounds."

"That's like three of me."

He looked at her, calculating. "Maybe two of you."

"I'm choosing to take that as a compliment."

She seasoned the chicken thighs with adobo and garlic powder and put the pan in the oven at 375. Not the broiler — the broiler ran uneven and she couldn't afford a burned batch. Rice on the stove. She washed the broccoli and cut it into pieces Reggie would eat, small enough to seem manageable, not so small she'd be standing at the counter with a knife for twenty minutes.

While the chicken cooked she sat with him and helped draw a sea turtle on the poster board. She wasn't good at drawing. The turtle came out looking like a worried football with flippers. Reggie studied it with generous gravity, said "That's OK, Mom," and carefully drew his own version next to hers. His was better in every way — rounder shell, actual flippers, an eye with expression in it. He labeled it LOGGERHEAD SEA TURTLE in careful block capitals. The second G was backward.

"They eat jellyfish," he said. "And they don't even get stung."

"How?"

"Their throat has, like —" He made a shape with his hands, fingers pointed inward. "Pointy things. Spines. Inside their whole throat. So the jellyfish can't sting when they swallow."

She watched his face while he explained this — the concentration, the pleasure of knowing something she didn't — and felt the thing she always felt when Reggie showed her who he was becoming. Not pride exactly. Something closer to fear. He was growing into someone who would need things she wasn't sure she could give him.

They ate at the kitchen table with the poster board moved carefully to the counter. The chicken was fine. The rice was fine. Reggie ate the broccoli without complaint, which meant he was too distracted to notice it, because he was telling her about leatherback sea turtles. They could dive deeper than submarines. "Well, some submarines." And plastic bags in the ocean looked exactly like jellyfish, and the turtles ate them by mistake and died, and there was a photo in his library book of a dead turtle cut open and its stomach was full of plastic.

"That's really sad," she said.

"Yeah." He ate a piece of chicken, chewing thoughtfully. "Ms. Aldridge says we can write to our senator about it."

"You should."

"Will they write back?"

"Probably someone in their office will. Like an assistant."

He considered this. "That's not the same."

"No," she said. "It's not."

She cleared the table. He took a bath — he was old enough now to do most of it himself, which was a relief she felt guilty about the way she felt guilty about every convenience that arrived from him needing her less. She checked behind his ears, the back of his neck. He smelled like the strawberry shampoo in the bottle shaped like a fish, which cost $1.80 more than the store brand and was the one expense she didn't negotiate because it was the only shampoo he didn't fight her on.

Homework: a reading comprehension worksheet and eight math problems involving subtraction with regrouping. She sat on his bed while he worked at his desk, his pencil pressed too hard against the paper the way it always had been, since pre-K. The lead broke on problem six. She sharpened it and handed it back. The school's occupational therapist had said he'd self-correct. The occupational therapist outside the school, the one who could actually work with him regularly, cost $150 an hour and wasn't covered by her insurance. So the pencil grip would self-correct, or it wouldn't, and either way she couldn't afford to find out.

He finished at eight-fifteen. She read to him — three chapters of a book about a dog who solved mysteries, borrowed from the library because books were free if you returned them on time. She was religious about returning them on time.

He fell asleep at twenty to nine. She stood in his doorway for a moment. His breathing had that particular open-mouthed child-sleep sound — deep and unguarded, with a small whistle on the exhale that the pediatrician said was just his adenoids being seven-year-old adenoids, nothing to worry about. His poster board leaned against the desk. The worried-football turtle and the better turtle, side by side. LOGGERHEAD SEA TURTLE. The backward G.

She pulled his door most of the way shut and went to the kitchen.

---

The dishes were done and the chicken was portioned into containers — two lunches, one dinner reheated over rice on Thursday — when she called Yolanda. Nine-twenty. Late enough that Reggie wouldn't wake, early enough that Yolanda would still be doing what she did every night: watching old ER episodes on her laptop with the sound low while her cat sat on the keyboard and she pretended this bothered her more than it did.

Yolanda picked up on the third ring. "Hold on." The sound of the cat being lifted and set down on something. "OK. What's up?"

"Nothing. Just calling."

"You sound off."

"I'm tired."

"You're always tired. This is a different kind."

Yolanda had been an ICU nurse at University Hospital in Newark for nine years. She read voices the way she read monitors — baseline deviations registered before the person talking knew they were deviating. She was also the kind of person who bought name-brand everything and then complained about money, which Carmen found both maddening and faintly admirable, a willingness to choose pleasure over math that she herself had never managed.

"I'm fine. How's work?"

"Garbage. We're short three nurses because two called out and one quit, and HR sent a temp who didn't know where the crash cart was. Nine years and I'm still showing people where the crash cart is."

"Rough."

"That's just Tuesday." A sigh that rattled through the phone's cheap speaker. "Oh — you hear about Dana? Dana Whitfield?"

Carmen didn't know a Dana Whitfield. "No."

"She was on Three South. Med-surg. Been here, like, four years, maybe five. Solid nurse. Not flashy, but she knew her stuff. She had a thing with controlled substances — a documentation issue."

Carmen's hand was resting on the counter. She moved it to the dish towel, folded the towel into thirds, set it on the edge of the sink.

"What kind of issue?"

"Pharmacy sent up the wrong count on a hydromorphone order. Actual pills didn't match what the pharmacy logged as dispensed. Two tablets off. She documented what she received, the pharmacy says their count was right, and now it's her word against theirs. Board pulled her license pending investigation."

"For two tablets."

"For two tablets." Yolanda's voice had gone flat, the way it did when she was past anger and into the duller terrain on the other side. "She swears it was a pharmacy error. Maybe it was. Doesn't matter. She can't work while the investigation's open. It's been — what did Keisha say — like eight months? No income for eight months."

"What's she doing?"

"Moved in with her sister somewhere. Bloomfield, I think. She had an apartment, couldn't keep it. She's got a kid — twelve, thirteen? She's doing DoorDash."

Carmen looked at the containers of chicken on the counter. Two lunches. Thursday dinner. Three-day cycle.

"That's —" she started, and didn't find the rest of it.

"I know. And the part that really gets me, even if they clear her? She's got eight months of gap on her résumé that she'll have to explain at every interview for the rest of her career. 'What were you doing from January to August?' 'Oh, I was under investigation for drug diversion, but they cleared me.' You know how that sounds."

Carmen knew how that sounded.

"Anyway." The cat returned — Carmen heard the small thump and Yolanda's automatic "Get off, Nelson." She'd named the cat after the hospital's parking garage because that's where she'd found him, two years old with a torn ear and a personality disorder. "How's Reggie?"

"Good. He's doing a project on sea turtles."

"Cute. He's such a weird little scientist."

"He's not weird."

"He's a little weird. In a great way. He told me dolphins sleep with one eye open and then stared at me to see if I'd try it. That's weird, Carmen."

The laugh was right there, the shape of it in her chest and moving upward, but something was sitting between her ribs and her throat and what came out was a flat "Yeah."

Yolanda paused. The pause was her reading the monitor.

"You sure you're all right?"

"I'm fine. Long stretch. I haven't had a day off in — yeah. I'm just tired."

"Come over Saturday. I'll make that rice thing. We'll drink wine and talk shit about men we don't even have."

"Maybe. Let me see how the schedule looks."

They talked for another ten minutes — Yolanda's upstairs neighbor who played reggaeton at 3 AM, a patient on the unit who'd coded and come back swinging at the respiratory therapist, Nelson's vet bill for a tooth extraction that cost $387 and which Yolanda described with the specific outrage of a person who'd spent that money on a cat's mouth without hesitating and then needed someone to be appalled on her behalf. Carmen listened and laughed at the right places and gave the right responses and didn't say any of the things she couldn't say.

After they hung up she sat at the kitchen table.

The apartment was quiet. The refrigerator hummed the way it did every forty minutes or so, a rising drone that sounded like a question the compressor kept asking itself. Through the shared wall she could hear the downstairs tenant's TV — a laugh track, muffled, the ghost of someone else's evening.

She opened the calculator on her phone.

$47,000 a year. Gross. After federal, state, Social Security, and Medicare, her take-home was $2,998 a month. She knew this number the way she knew Reggie's birthday and her blood type. It arrived on the first and the fifteenth, half and half, and it was gone by the time the next one came.

Student loans: $680. She'd consolidated three years ago and the monthly payment had actually gone up because the interest rate adjusted and she hadn't understood the fine print well enough to argue it. $38,000 remaining. She'd be forty-four when they were paid off if nothing went wrong, if she never missed a month, if the rate didn't adjust again.

Rent: $1,200. The landlord had raised it $75 in June. Carmen had said thank you because the alternative was a $1,400 apartment farther from Reggie's school, and the difference in gas money would have eaten the savings.

Car payment: $400. A 2019 Civic with 94,000 miles. The transmission had made a sound last month — a low grinding on the upshift between second and third — that she was choosing to interpret as normal.

Car insurance: $200.

$2,998 minus $2,480 left $518 a month. For everything else. Her phone, $68, because work required the apps and the scheduling portals. Gas — Lakeview and back, Reggie's school, her mother's house, the ShopRite that was farther but cheaper — $160 in a careful month. Food for two: $74 a week if she bought bone-in thighs and store-brand cereal and never once looked at the Honeycrisps.

The $518 covered those things and then it was done. What was left — Reggie's school supplies, the field trips with their $12 permission slips she couldn't send back unsigned, the shoes he outgrew every four months, the copays, the dentist twice a year at $180 because her insurance covered one cleaning and she wasn't going to skip his second — came from doubles. Fourteen in the last two months. Five hundred, sometimes eight hundred extra a check, before taxes. Her mother watched Reggie those nights for free, which was not free, because Gloria's time had value and the fact that her mother never mentioned this was a cost Carmen carried differently but carried all the same.

The doubles were the margin. The doubles were the entire gap between making it and not making it.

If she lost her license, the $47,000 disappeared. The doubles disappeared. She had nine years of nursing experience and an LPN credential, and if the board suspended her — not revoked, not convicted, just suspended pending investigation the way they'd suspended Dana Whitfield for two hydromorphone tablets — the nine years became a gap she'd have to explain at every interview for the rest of her working life. She could do retail. She could drive for DoorDash. She could move in with her mother, put Reggie in Gloria's second bedroom, and try to rebuild from the floor of a career that had taken her five years of night school and $38,000 in debt to build.

She closed the calculator. The screen went dark.

Reporting what she knew wouldn't make her safer. Even if they believed her — even if the board decided she'd been a victim of circumstance and not a participant, which was less true every day — the investigation alone would freeze her license. Months of the $518 going to zero while the $2,480 stayed exactly where it was. Months of $680 in loan payments with no income to make them from. The math didn't have an answer. It had a shape — a box with her and Reggie inside it, dimensioned to the dollar, no room to move in any direction.

She turned off the kitchen light and walked down the hall.

Reggie had rolled onto his side. One arm hung off the mattress, his hand open, fingers loosely curled the way they went when he was fully gone. The nightlight — a blue plastic turtle she'd found at a garage sale for fifty cents, which he'd carried around the house for two days before allowing it to be plugged in — cast the room in the color of shallow water. His breathing, the small whistle.

The poster board leaned against the desk. The worried-football turtle and the good turtle, side by side. LOGGERHEAD SEA TURTLE. The backward G.

She stood there. The feeling wasn't about right and wrong. It wasn't about the ring or Harry or the pills counted and miscounted under her license number. The feeling was arithmetic. Reggie's breathing and $518 a month and a woman named Dana Whitfield who was delivering strangers' food in her car because two tablets didn't match.

Carmen pulled the door most of the way closed.

She went back to the kitchen and stood with her hands on the edge of the counter, looking at the window above the sink. Her own face looked back at her from the dark glass — thirty-one, hair still up, the shadows under her eyes that had been there so long she'd stopped noticing them in bathroom mirrors but always noticed in reflections she didn't expect. The refrigerator asked its question and answered itself.

She didn't make a decision. She didn't need to make one tonight. She had two more days.

She turned off the light and went to bed.
