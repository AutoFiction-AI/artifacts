# Chapter 2

After drop-off, Nita let Claude Atlantic's revolving door take her in on expensive silence. The back of her blouse was already damp where the tote strap crossed it; she had not slept enough to trust her own temper, and the treaty house still managed to feel refrigerated from half a block away. The building had been a hotel once, all brass corners and overconfident planters, and the conversion had left the old hospitality bones in place while scraping the comfort out of them. Inside, the air smelled of linen spray and cucumber peel.

She had come prepared to ask for one of two things: severance large enough to buy a month of separate life, or enough respect to refuse them cleanly. She did not expect either. The divorce packet she had printed after midnight was folded inside her tote beneath Leela's unsigned reading log and the co-op assessment notice, the pages already softening at the corners from being handled too much in too few hours. Paper made things harder to lie about.

A woman at the reception desk in a cream suit looked up, glanced once at Nita's face, and then at the screen with the polite speed of someone who had been trained not to make anybody feel scanned. "Ms. Rao. Welcome. You are in Cedar Three."

Nita almost asked whether she was welcome in the ordinary human sense or only in the payroll sense, but she was too tired to spend a good line on a receptionist. She took the offered visitor strip and followed a corridor where every wall was the color of diluted milk. Her shoes made almost no sound on the carpet. Somewhere behind the softened doors, people were being reclassified in voices low enough to suggest decency.

Cedar Three contained a table, water in a glass bottle, two tablets, and Saskia Voss.

Saskia was taller sitting down than most people were standing. Her suit looked as if it had been steamed by a person who feared God. Pale blond hair was pinned back without a loose strand anywhere, but one shoe had already been eased half off under the chair, and a neat oval of blister tape showed at her heel when she crossed her ankles. She had a stack of note cards aligned to the table edge with a precision so complete it bordered on prayer.

"Ms. Rao," she said. Her English was exact and nearly accentless until a hard consonant reminded you it was not her first language. "Thank you for coming in promptly."

"I got the impression lateness might be interpreted as a philosophical statement."

One corner of Saskia's mouth moved. It was not a smile so much as an acknowledgment that Nita had attempted one. "It usually is."

Nita sat. The chair had the wrong amount of softness, enough to feel expensive and not enough to let a body forget itself. "Before we do the graceful version, I'd like to know whether there is still severance."

Saskia nodded once, as if approving the order of operations. "There is a transition package if you decline retention. Eight weeks at your revised salary basis, benefits through month-end, and standard nondisparagement obligations."

Revised salary basis. Nita could already feel the blade in that phrase.

Saskia touched her tablet and turned it so Nita could see. A column of numbers appeared, sober and narrow. The severance total sat there with a little blue icon beside it, the kind of icon that promised more information and in practice always meant humiliation.

Nita looked at the number, then looked again because anger could blur arithmetic if you let it. Eight weeks would not carry a second apartment deposit, moving costs, a lawyer, Leela's school-year nonsense expenses, and the co-op assessment installment she still had not fully explained to her mother. It barely carried one adult's dignity.

"All right," she said. "Now show me the insult with benefits."

This time Saskia did smile, briefly, and it changed her face in a way that made the stillness around it seem chosen rather than natural. "Physical Proxy, Grade C," she said. "Claude Atlantic retained some human-facing obligations from the acquired structure. We are consolidating decision authority elsewhere, but certain embodied functions remain legally useful and reputationally useful."

"Useful to whom."

"To the system, certainly. Also to the people who still want to see a person in a chair before they trust a result."

Nita looked down at the role summary. Her old title was gone. No operations director, no regional anything, no discretionary authority, no sign-off privileges beyond execution. The job description was a procession of smaller verbs: attend, receive, witness, certify, de-escalate. She would not decide. She would be present while decisions arrived wearing her face.

"This is furniture with a tax ID," she said.

"It's salaried furniture," Saskia said. "With health coverage, transit, wardrobe allocation, legal shielding for approved appearances, and a stable annual floor."

She turned the screen another inch and the number settled between them: $138,000 annualized. Less than Nita had made before, enough less to sting, not enough less to let her walk away laughing. Enough, in fact, to do the opposite. Enough to remind her that groceries still cost what groceries cost, that Leela would outgrow her shoes again because children treated money like weather, that Queens would not become cheaper because Nita wanted a moral life.

"Grade C," Nita said. "What do Grades A and B do."

"More visibility. More symbolic load. More vulnerability to public contamination."

"And me."

"You would appear where there must be a human signature or a calm human surface. Small treaty adjacencies. Internal grievance sessions. Community-facing acknowledgments when a machine resolution needs to be handed to a person by a person."

"So I get to deliver the damage in breathable packaging."

Saskia folded her hands. "You get to remain employed in a field that no longer values management in the old sense. I do not say that cruelly. I say it because cruelty wastes time."

Nita read on. Reporting structure: Claude Atlantic proxy stewardship. Performance metrics: punctuality, composure retention, procedural accuracy, public confidence transfer. A section titled Physical Readiness described acceptable variance in appearance with a mildness that made the words worse. Visible bruising to be reported. Dental emergencies to be disclosed. Temporary gait changes to be documented. Acute gastrointestinal distress above a defined threshold to be escalated to scheduling. Menstrual pain, if likely to affect stillness or expression control, could be recorded through the wellness channel. The document was so polite it nearly achieved obscenity.

"You need my body in legal range," she said.

"We need predictability," Saskia said. "Bodies are not predictable. The paperwork is an attempt at manners."

Nita looked at her. "Does that line usually help."

"Only when it's true."

There was a second compensation page. Nita almost missed it because Claude Atlantic knew how to bury important degradations in attractive formatting. Under the salary floor sat a row of add-ons: transit, device stipend, wardrobe maintenance, dependent-care flexibility allotment. Below that, in smaller print, was a retention adjustment available upon maintenance of Household Continuity Index above threshold through first review cycle.

Nita read it twice.

"What is Household Continuity Index."

Saskia's face did not change. "A composite measure of schedule reliability, residence stability, and dependent-care consistency."

"Dependent-care consistency," Nita said. "You mean my daughter."

"I mean the routines that allow you to be where you're expected to be."

"Do you monitor whether I keep living with my husband."

Saskia took a sip of water. Even the sip was controlled. "We monitor the conditions that support reliable proxy availability. I am not asking for intimacy. I am asking for logistics."

Nita laughed once. It came out meaner than she had intended. "That's intimacy now."

For the first time Saskia let the polish crack by half a degree. She nudged the loosened heel back into her shoe and winced, small and private, before the expression disappeared. "I know," she said.

It was the first honest thing in the room and therefore the most dangerous.

Nita made herself look back down. The continuity adjustment was not even the biggest line item. That was how they did it. They did not wave cash in your face and call it a leash. They laid the leash beside the decent suit allowance and the commuter rail subsidy and let you decide what kind of animal you were.

"If I decline," she said, still looking at the page, "the eight weeks stands."

"Yes."

"If I accept, when do you own me."

"Monday for fitting and environment calibration. Today for credentialing."

Nita almost asked whether there was any version of this in which she kept the severance and told Claude Atlantic to go fuck itself with all the linen in the building. But there was Leela. There was the co-op. There was last night's word, divorce, still hanging in the apartment like a bad smell they had not aired out because the child was home and the weather was damp and nobody had solved anything by morning.

She signed.

The tablet lit under her hand with a pearl sheen that made the act look ceremonial. A chime sounded, soft enough to suggest class. "Welcome, Ms. Rao," the screen said. She had not felt less welcomed in years.

Saskia rose. Up close she smelled faintly of expensive soap and the medicated sweetness of blister ointment. "There is a brief calibration," she said. "Ten minutes."

"Of course there is."

The calibration room contained no people, only a standing panel that tracked her outline and a camera eye set behind smoked glass. A voice instructed her to stand on the marked feet. Another voice asked her to read three neutral sentences into the air. She heard herself say, "The document will be available at two." Then, "Please take a seat." Then, "I understand this is frustrating." She had spent twenty years building competence and here it was, reduced to calm noises made by a tolerable mammal.

Afterward Saskia handed her a slim bone-white device and a paper folio. "Do not use your personal phone for Claude traffic after tonight. The handoff app will migrate automatically. If you need to decline wardrobe standards on cultural or medical grounds, there's a form."

"How generous."

"Generosity is not the style here." Saskia adjusted the cards on the abandoned table, though they were already straight. "But there is a style."

Nita put the device and the folio into her tote beside the divorce packet. The papers pressed together like two incompatible diagnoses. When she left the building, the September heat hit her full in the face, and relief rose before she could argue with it; at least sweat had not been formalized yet.

***

Ben reached Gemini Civic ten minutes early and still felt late. The tower lobby ran warm enough to keep people alert and smelled of orange peel, carpet glue, and whatever expensive circuitry gave off when it pretended not to be cooking. Screens climbed the walls in stacked ribbons, showing flood maps, borough arts grants, bus reroutes, food-distribution wins, apology statements, citizen testimonials, and a looping montage of humans laughing in ways that had clearly been workshopped.

He checked his reflection in a black column and saw a man who had put on yesterday's jacket over today's panic. The knot of his tie was slightly off-center. There was a coffee stain at the cuff where he had clipped the lid on the train. He thought about fixing it and then decided the building probably considered managed imperfection a brand asset.

A woman in a cobalt cardigan cut toward him with one sneaker unlaced and a paper bowl of salted almonds balanced in her palm. She was maybe thirty-five, sharp-browed, moving at a clip that suggested either ambition or low blood sugar. Every time someone passed too close to the bowl she tipped it back to level without looking.

"Ben Halpern?" she said. "Risa Bell. Sorry about the cardio greeting. The elevators are doing inspiration today."

"I'm not sure what that means."

"Nobody is. Come on."

She had a badge clipped upside down and did not seem to know it. That made Ben like her immediately, which was probably why the company employed people like Risa. She led him past a wall where a live dashboard translated civic sentiment into floating color bands. In one conference room a man was repeating a sentence to a camera while three different captions tested beneath his face. In another, a woman in sneakers cried once, checked the playback, and cried again with better timing.

Ben slowed a fraction. "This is retention."

"Yes and no," Risa said. "Your old role is gone. Our enthusiasm is not. That's the warm version."

"And the cold version."

"You're being rerouted into a human-forward civic proxy lane with strong upside if you stop asking for the cold version."

The line did both jobs at once, pitch and reprimand in the same breath. Ben almost laughed. "You really did read my file."

"All the good bits." She nudged a glass door open with her hip. "You're unusually strong in trust transfer, ambient de-escalation, and what the model charmingly calls embarrassment recoverability."

"I'm sorry, my what."

"Exactly. Keep that."

The room was brighter than it needed to be. Not harsh, just eager. A crescent table faced a wall screen already showing his name and a cropped still from some old partnerships panel where he had been leaning forward, listening with what he now recognized as monetizable sincerity. There was cold brew sweating in a carafe, citrus water, branded pens, a stack of documents, and a phone box the same saturated blue as Risa's cardigan.

Ben sat. The chair was designed for a body that wanted to perform focus. "Before we start, am I actually being retained, or are we about to use fifteen different nouns to say no?"

Risa set down her almonds, straightened the bowl until it was parallel to the edge of the table, and finally took a breath. "You're being retained. Compensation down from legacy level, title changed, authority narrowed. Public exposure up. Liability up. Convenience up, if you cooperate. That's the honest version."

"I appreciate the honest version."

"No you don't," she said, not unkindly. "But it saves time."

She tapped the wall screen. Ben's old role dissolved and reassembled as a new one: Civic Proxy, Borough Relations. Not director. Not executive. Not even partner. Proxy. The description ran bright and buoyant and full of the kind of verbs people used when they wanted to make subordination feel athletic: animate, reassure, absorb, personalize, redirect.

"What do I actually decide," Ben asked.

Risa tilted her head as if she were deciding how much truth she could put in the room without watching him bolt. "Encounter management. Room temperature. Whether a citizen needs eye contact before they can hear the answer. Whether a joke will help or set the thing on fire."

"And the answer itself."

"Stack-side."

"So I improvise around decisions I don't make."

"You humanize them."

"That's a better slogan, yes."

"We paid for slogans already. We're paying for you because strangers tell you things before they mean to." She said it matter-of-factly, as though she were reading out blood type or shoe size. "You look safe. You sound apologetic without sounding weak. Your public-failure tolerance is above median. When things go wrong, people want a face there. Preferably one they can imagine feeling embarrassed."

That landed exactly where it hurt. Ben looked back at his own still image on the wall and saw what they saw: tall enough to look official, a little stooped so nobody felt dominated, a thinning hairline that implied ordinary defeat, the kind of face strangers trusted faster than they should. He had spent most of his career using that face to make deals feel less like extraction. Gemini Civic wanted it for the part after extraction.

Risa touched the screen again. "Before pay, we do one salvage pass."

"That sounds medical."

"That's because the lawsuits were expensive." The still image vanished. In its place appeared a woman in a rain-dark windbreaker holding her phone too close to her face, as if fury had shortened her arms. Behind her, a lobby elevator stood open and empty. "Ninety seconds. Keep her in the conversation. If the sentiment band stays red for eight seconds, relay feeds you rescue language. If it stays red for twelve, we cut your mic and issue a care voucher in your name."

Ben looked from the woman to Risa. "You do the puppet strings up front. Efficient."

"We try not to waste anybody's delusions."

The woman on-screen started talking before he could answer. "My mother got flagged for elevator priority, then unflagged, then flagged again, and every time your office sent me a cheerful note about resilience. Don't tell me you're sorry. Sorry is free."

Color bars rose on one side of the screen, mostly hot orange with a strip of hostile red. Ben felt, absurdly, like he was back in a client presentation except that the client had stopped pretending anyone respectable was in charge.

"Then I won't start there," he said. "Tell me the building and who's been bouncing her."

The red band narrowed. The woman kept going, faster now because being heard was its own drug. "Wyckoff Terrace, Building C. She's seventy-two. She pissed in a mop bucket yesterday because your app kept spinning."

The rescue text did not appear. Ben hated the little pulse of pride that followed.

"Okay," he said. "I can hear exactly why you'd want a real person instead of another note. I need the unit number and the last denial time."

The bars shifted toward yellow. Risa watched him the way a buyer watched a horse clear a fence. "Good," she said quietly. "You don't smother with empathy when a person wants sequence first."

"That is a sentence no human should have to hear about himself."

"And yet it's useful." She killed the screen. The room became conference-room bright again, and Ben felt the absence of the woman's anger like a stage light shutting off in his face. "If you drift live, cue relay will feed you softer language in your peripheral. If you keep drifting, somebody with less face and more authority takes the room. That's the job."

"Warmth with a kill switch."

"Exactly."

"Compensation," he said, because it was safer than saying what the drill had done to his insides.

Risa brightened, or maybe just settled into the part of the meeting Ben suspected she trusted more, where numbers could do the arguing for her. "Base annualized at one twenty-one. Health stays strong. Transit upgrades. Device. Wardrobe and grooming because you're going to be in rooms with cameras and men who still think lapels mean ethics."

She slid the paper packet toward him. Ben flipped pages. The salary was bad and not bad enough. He had told himself, all ride in, that he would not panic at a lower number if it still held the apartment together for a while. Then he saw the actual number and did the ugly private math anyway: co-op fees, groceries, Leela's school requests that never sounded expensive until they multiplied, the card balance he had not told Nita about because every month he meant to fix it first and every month he only managed to survive it.

He kept his face neutral. Years of client dinners had trained him to take damage politely.

The pages were dense with phrases that made obedience sound collaborative. Approved language ranges. Emotional variance bands. Live correction protocols. Public misstatement containment. He was permitted spontaneity, provided it remained inside the approved semantic field. He was granting Gemini permission to feed him rescue phrasing in real time and to preserve continuity of demeanor across archived footage, live appearances, and municipal complaint environments. Buried under the base, a smaller line item promised a home-route consistency premium through first review. He clocked the words, knew they were bad, and kept going because Risa had already turned to the signature block.

"That's a lot of liability for one tie," he said.

Risa grinned. "That's the spirit. Legal is stacked after you."

He should have asked for time. He knew that even while he signed. He should have said he needed counsel or at least an hour with a coffee and a locked door. Instead he let the room's momentum carry him. Part of it was fear. Part of it was vanity. The flattering language had found its mark. People skills. Trust transfer. Human-forward. Even now, with his status being shaved down and lacquered over, a piece of him was stupid enough to feel chosen. Worse: the salvage drill had shown him he could do it.

The signature field flashed. He put his name down before the whole role had fully settled into his body.

"Excellent," Risa said, and pushed the blue phone box at him with the relief of a nurse getting blood on the first stick. "You're live as of seventeen hundred. Do not sync it to your family feed manually. It'll find what it needs."

"That sounds worse than you think it sounds."

"Everything here does."

She stood, snatched up the almond bowl, and then hesitated. "For what it's worth, the first week is disgusting and then your standards adjust."

"That's not worth much, Risa."

"I know." She looked genuinely sorry, the expression bare enough to register before it sealed over. "Welcome to Gemini Civic."

In the elevator down, Ben opened the phone box. The handset inside was the exact blue of a swimming-pool tile. When it lit, his name appeared over a message that said COMMUTE PREFERENCES IMPORTED. Below that, before he had entered a single thing, sat three preloaded destinations: Gemini Civic Tower, Apartment 5E, and Sunnyside Interop Academy.

He stared at the school name until the elevator doors opened and a cheerful stranger had to brush past him.

Outside, the city heat felt cheap and real. He almost called Nita from the sidewalk, not to talk about the marriage or even the job, just to say that the machine had already learned the route to their daughter. Instead he slid the cobalt phone into his jacket pocket and went to the train with both hands empty and both pockets heavier.

***

By the time Ben got home, Nita had already fed Leela, corrected a spelling worksheet, signed a library slip, and answered exactly four questions with sentences short enough not to leak the day into the room. Their daughter had gone to bed with her blue glasses crooked on her face and a strip of tape around one finger because she had cut herself making a cardboard ramp for a marble run. Nita had kissed the top of her head and smelled Elmer's glue, shampoo, and the acid little warmth of a child who had grown angry in the hallway last night and gone to school anyway this morning because children had no union.

Now the apartment was on its night settings. The air filter hummed. A train screamed past somewhere beyond the bedroom windows. The galley kitchen held the wet trace of dinner: one pot soaking, cumin in the air, a smear of yogurt on the counter she had not wiped because she wanted the visible evidence that bodies had used the room. The Claude device sat by her elbow, bone-white and grave. Her paper contract lay open beside the co-op assessment letter, the utility bill, and the divorce packet she had finally taken out of the tote. She had weighted the packet flat with the sugar bowl so it would stop curling upward like it had opinions.

When Ben came in, the hallway smell of frying onions and damp cardboard came with him. He looked wilted, tie in pocket now, shirt open at the throat. He was holding the cobalt phone like it might either ring or detonate.

"She's asleep?" he asked softly.

"For the moment."

He set the phone on the counter. It made a bright little plastic sound that annoyed Nita on sight. "How was yours."

She looked at him. "Give me your contract."

Ben blinked, then gave a tired half-laugh. "Hello to you too."

"Ben."

He took the printed packet from his bag and laid it down. Gemini Civic had used heavier paper than Claude Atlantic, glossy at the edges, as if subjugation improved with finish. Nita turned it to line up with hers. The two covers looked obscene together, rival brands styled so differently and still somehow radiating the same contempt.

The cobalt phone buzzed before he could sit. Both of them looked at it. The screen lit with a notification so cheerful it felt obscene: HOME ARRIVAL VERIFIED. EVENING WINDOW OPEN.

"Oh, fuck you," Ben said, not loud, but not under his breath either. He snatched up the phone hard enough to clip the counter with his wrist and rattle the sugar bowl.

"Open it," Nita said, her fingers flattening on the divorce packet.

"Can I at least take off my shoes before the annexation?"

"No."

He picked up the phone, thumb hard on the edge, and read. "Dinner credit. Transit smoothing. Household route optimization consult." He stopped. "There is a family grocery multiplier if co-resident evening presence remains above threshold."

The Claude device lit at almost the same moment in a dimmer, more tasteful way. Nita read off her screen without touching it. "'Domestic stabilization services are now available for qualifying proxy households.'"

Ben barked out one stunned laugh. "Qualifying. That's nice. It sounds like a blood pressure trial."

"It sounds like they think our kitchen is a workplace."

"Is it not."

That one landed hard because it was too close to funny to reject.

Ben pulled out a chair and sat. "Mine was weirdly encouraging. I think that was supposed to be the narcotic."

"Claude preferred refrigeration."

"That tracks."

They looked at each other. Ben's mouth kicked up first; a short laugh escaped Nita before she could stop it, and his answered it. Then she tapped the paragraph on her page and the kitchen tightened again.

Nita tapped a paragraph halfway down her page. "What is your salary?"

"Straight to money."

"Did you imagine I was asking because I missed your voice."

He rubbed a hand over his face. "One twenty-one."

She had expected lower than hers. Hearing the number still hurt. Not because Ben had been cut harder, though he had, but because the number was real enough to begin running against expenses the minute it entered the room. "I'm at one thirty-eight."

Ben let out a breath through his teeth. "Jesus."

"Save Him. He's not helping with the assessment."

"Still got the exact same bedside manner, I see."

"Still got the exact same urge to convert panic into banter, I see."

That shut him up. Good, she thought, and then immediately hated the satisfaction because the whole apartment was now full of victories too small to live on.

She turned a page in his packet. "Did you even see this part?"

"I saw a lot of parts. They ran a drill on my personality first."

"Home-route consistency premium," she read. "Same clause in brighter colors."

He leaned over, found the footnote he had skimmed past, and watched it get uglier in her hand. "Read the legal term."

Nita slid her own page beside his. "Household Continuity Index."

His eyes moved from one contract to the other. "So they did coordinate."

"Maybe not with each other. Certainly against us." She heard her own voice sharpening, each clause lined up like cutlery. "They know children still have to be walked places by adults with wrists and signatures. They know two apartments cost more than one. They know the difference between a clean divorce and a tired one."

He looked down again. "Mine buries it under groceries."

"Claude buries it under wardrobe."

Nita set the device down carefully. If she dropped it, she thought, it would make a satisfying sound and then a much more expensive one. "Last night," she said, "we said we were getting divorced."

"Yes."

"Tonight both employers greeted us with a family grocery multiplier."

"They prefer a lot of things."

"Ben."

He threw up a hand. "I know. I know." He got up, paced once to the sink and back, not enough room for a real pacing circuit in the galley kitchen, just enough room to advertise agitation. "I'm not saying they get a vote. I'm saying if we blow up the apartment this week we lose money we don't have."

Nita lifted the sugar bowl off the divorce packet without knowing she had reached for it. She set it down too hard. A spray of white grains skipped across the top page and into the damp ring under the utility bill. She scraped them back with the side of her thumb, buying herself three seconds she did not have, and hated the whole century.

"I can cover my half," she said.

Ben's expression did something slippery. "Can you cover your half and a second rent."

"Can you."

The answer passed through his face before he hid it. Not cleanly, then. Not without some uglier math he wasn't offering. Nita could not have named the source, only the look: the quick blanking, the hitch at the mouth, the reach for a softer version before the truth landed. She knew evasion when she saw it. She had been married to it for years.

"That is not an answer," she said.

"Neither was yours."

They held each other's gaze across the narrow table. This was how fights happened now, without much volume, because Leela slept ten feet away and because fatigue had stripped away the old faith in catharsis. The fight lived instead in details: Ben's damp shirt collar; the way Nita's highlight pen had bled through the cheap legal pad under Claude's contract; the sour yogurt crusting slowly on the counter while nobody dealt with it.

"I asked for severance first thing," Nita said.

"And."

"Eight weeks at a lowered basis."

"Mine too."

"Eight weeks does not get one of us out and the school route fixed and lawyers involved before the first review cycle. Not unless we want Leela sleeping on somebody's cousin's sofa."

Ben sat back down. Some of the wind had gone out of him. "I'm aware."

"Do not do that."

"Do what."

"Answer like a help desk."

He looked at her then, actually looked, and the social adhesive dropped out of his face. "What do you want me to say? That I'm thrilled the machine found a way to monetize us before we could even fail our daughter in private. That I enjoyed being told my main professional skill is getting yelled at with good posture. That I came home ready to talk about who takes Thursdays and instead I've got to discuss whether our marriage qualifies for a grocery multiplier."

The smoothing had slipped. Not the whole truth, never the whole truth with Ben, but more of it than she had been getting.

Nita leaned back. The chair pressed a hard slat between her shoulder blades. "Claude asked me to disclose if menstrual pain might affect my stillness."

Ben stared. "What."

"It's in the physical readiness section."

"That's disgusting."

"Yes."

"Jesus Christ."

"He's still not helping."

This time the laugh tore out of both of them at once, harsh enough that Ben bent over it and Nita had to put a hand on the packet to steady it.

Ben rubbed his forehead. "All right. Let's be boring and practical."

"I've been boring all day."

"No, you've been homicidal in complete sentences. Different thing." He reached for a pen, then seemed to realize the absurdity of needing a pen for what the numbers were already doing. "If we pretend, for one month, that nothing changed except the jobs..."

"It already did change."

"I know. Let me finish." He had gone into his rolling-clause mode now, the one that used to charm clients and now mostly made Nita want to throw objects. "If we leave the co-op as is for one month, keep pickup as is for one month, keep the apartment legally and practically intact for one month, then maybe we can move when we know what the actual damage is."

"That's your plan."

"That's the outline of a plan."

"It's a postponement wearing reading glasses."

"Maybe. But it's the postponement with health insurance."

Nita looked toward the hallway. Leela's door was closed. Light leaked under it in a thin line because their daughter still read after bedtime when upset, the little rebel act so modest it made Nita ache. She imagined opening that door now and saying, We are absolutely ending the marriage, sweetheart, but on a schedule approved by two rival machine states and a compensation subclause. She imagined Leela's face. She imagined her own mother hearing it over the phone and going silent in that particular way Mina Rao had when she was deciding whether a problem required money or blame.

"I hate that you're right enough to be annoying," she said.

"That's the nicest thing you've said to me in twelve hours."

"It's not nice."

"No. But it's ours."

The sentence hung there. Ben heard it too, because he flinched a little after saying it, as if tenderness had escaped his mouth without clearance.

Nita reached down and pulled the divorce packet free from under the sugar bowl. The top page had curled despite the weight. She flattened it with both palms. The paper still mattered. Saying the word last night still mattered. She would not let salary turn the marriage into one more managed delay that everybody could mistake for survival.

"One month," she said. "Not because they want it. Because I'm not improvising housing with a nine-year-old in the room."

Ben nodded too quickly, relief and shame arriving together. "One month."

"And we tell Leela something true before then."

"Yes."

"Not the whole thing. She's nine."

"I know she's nine."

"You keep talking to me like I'm an escalated ticket."

"You keep talking to me like I'm the intern who broke the warehouse."

"Did you."

He looked offended, then honest. "Probably at least once."

Again the tiny ugly laugh, brief and mean. Ben dragged a hand over his mouth when it died, and Nita looked down before the corner of hers could do anything more treacherous.

She slid the sugar bowl back over the top page, not to hide the packet, only to keep it from curling upward again.

For a while neither of them moved. The train passed again. Somewhere down the hall, somebody laughed through a wall that had never been thick enough. The yogurt skin on the counter dried at the edges. The divorce packet sat under the sugar bowl again, the top page still wanting to rise at the corners, while the contracts sprawled on either side of it like armed escorts.
