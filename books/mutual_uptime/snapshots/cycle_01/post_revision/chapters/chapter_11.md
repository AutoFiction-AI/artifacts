# Chapter 11

The paper list from two nights ago was still under the fridge magnet shaped like a smiling orange.

MISS SOFTLY.

CONTRADICT ROUTES.

LATE, NOT LOST.

NEVER SCARE HER.

Ben stood in the narrow kitchen in a shirt he had taken straight from the drying rack without smoothing the seams. One cuff had dried with a hard fold in it, and the collar sat wrong against his neck. Nita, already in tights and a slate dress that made her look more expensive than either of them could currently afford to feel, watched him butter half a bialy over the sink.

"You look like a man who slept in a conference tote," she said.

"That's the idea."

"No. The idea is unreliable, not septic."

He took a bite. Burned onion and cheap cream cheese stuck to the roof of his mouth. "You wrote 'miss softly.'"

"I didn't mean fungal."

Leela's bedroom door stayed shut. The apartment hummed around them: the refrigerator motor, the air filter, the train grinding somewhere beyond the window glass. Ben's Gemini phone lay facedown on the counter as if that changed anything. It vibrated twice against the laminate and walked itself half an inch toward the fruit bowl.

Nita reached past it for her travel mug. "Don't ad-lib too much."

"I thought we were sabotaging."

"We're. But if you start enjoying yourself, that'll show."

He laughed once, because she was right and because being known that precisely still had the power to annoy him. "Thanks for the confidence."

She screwed the lid onto her mug. Her face was composed in the way Claude liked, only the hair at one temple still damp from the shower, black and rope-dark against her skin. "I'm not worried about the confidence part. I'm worried about the audience part. They like you when you look like you arrived by accident."

"That's a hurtful thing to say before eight."

"It's also market research."

He picked up his phone. A morning prompt bloomed before he could stop it.

Today's Human Texture Goal: reduce polish variance by 11%. Public trust lifts when warmth outruns finish.

"Jesus Christ," he said.

Nita slung her bag over her shoulder. "Try to fail in a way they can't frame as cuddly."

He looked at the note again. NEVER SCARE HER. Everything else on the list had room to play. That line did not. He folded the paper once more sharply than necessary and put it back under the magnet.

At Gemini Civic they had him on the fourth floor hospitality deck, which meant they wanted him visible before they wanted him useful. The room was all warm lights and eager screens and coffee machines that announced bean provenance like a moral achievement. Ben had ignored the wardrobe suggestions, worn the creased shirt, skipped the tie, and kept the stubble that turned him from trustworthy to tired if you looked close.

Noa Velez found him by the pastry wall with a tablet hugged to her chest. She had a smear of blue violin rosin on one black cuff and a little notch missing from one front tooth that made her perfect teeth look more human than Gemini preferred. Ben had met her twice. She treated every event as if it were a school play with better funding and worse children.

"Good," she said, scanning him. "You look less lacquered today."

"I'm aiming below good."

"Don't say that near a mic."

"What am I hosting again?"

She blinked. "Queens Manual Access Pilot."

"That sounds fake."

"Everything sounds fake at nine in the morning. You're introducing the winter service kiosks and then taking three questions."

He accepted the tablet, glanced at the script, and felt immediate hatred. The language was pure Gemini: loose enough to sound improvised, tight enough to leave finger marks if he tried to move inside it.

Noa lowered her voice. "The room is merchants, transit people, parent council, a few local press. We want commuter-frayed, not checked out."

"I can give you checked out for free."

She gave him a look that would have been maternal if she had not been at least twenty years younger than he was. "Ben."

"Right."

She tucked a peppermint into her cheek with the guilty efficiency of someone who had promised herself she would stop buying them loose at bodegas and had not stopped. "The trust score's already running high because of last week's family-route clip. You don't have to do anything fancy."

He handed the tablet back. "Then I definitely won't."

The stage was a shallow platform in front of three demo kiosks the color of fresh bruises. Their screen savers cycled through smiling commuters in hats. A semicircle of folding chairs held exactly the kind of people who hated mornings on principle: small-business owners with still-damp hair, a man in a sanitation jacket smelling strongly of bleach, two mothers in puffer coats with school pickup stickers still on their sleeves, a reporter with a thermal mug covered in flaking union decals. Nobody looked as if they had arrived to be delighted.

Good, Ben thought. Let this die.

He stepped into the wash of lights and felt the sweat begin under his arms almost at once. Gemini's teleprompter strip glowed at the edge of the stage. He did not look at it.

"Morning," he said, and heard the microphone carry the gravel in his throat farther than he liked. "I'm Ben Halpern. I had a cleaner version of this two minutes ago, and then one of the kiosks thanked me for participating in my own arrival, so now you're getting the actual one."

There was a beat of stillness, then a bark of laughter from the sanitation jacket. One of the mothers smiled despite herself.

Ben kept going because stopping would mean correction. "These are supposed to make winter routing easier if your phone is dead or your hands are full or you're simply tired of being told a six-minute walk is a wellness opportunity. They can print school pickup verification, service maps, emergency transit credits, and manual access codes if the network gets weird."

So far: competent. He swerved harder.

"I'm also required to tell you they're intuitive. I personally got the weather in Yonkers and a coupon for civic tea before I found the bus map, so your experience may vary."

This time the laugh came bigger, with chair creaks under it. The reporter had her phone up. A man in a Queens Chamber scarf nodded as if Ben had said something brave about democracy instead of being kind of annoying on purpose.

The teleprompter strip pulsed brighter at the edge of his sight.

CORRECT TONE: candid, solution-linked.

He ignored it.

"Anyway," Ben said, "the point is not that the machines are perfect. The point is that if your kid is freezing outside school because the route stack decided to have a personality, there is now a button for that. I think we can all agree a button is nice."

That line landed with such warm relief that he wanted to walk offstage and keep walking west until the river stopped him.

The questions were worse. The first was from the reporter with the union mug, who asked how the kiosks handled cross-jurisdiction badge failures. Ben decided to be as unhelpful as possible.

"Sometimes inelegantly," he said.

She grinned. "Can you define that?"

"Sure. Sometimes the machine says one thing, the school says another thing, and then a tired adult has to stand there with a child and a face that says please don't make me call anybody. The pilot's supposed to reduce those moments."

"So it doesn't yet."

"I would not put money on 'yet' as a stable human category."

More laughter. Not mocking. Charmed.

The second question came from a dry-cleaner owner in a plum scarf who asked whether the kiosks would replace staffed counters. Ben meant to give a muddled answer. Instead he found himself telling the truth in exactly the digestible, quotable shape Gemini prized.

"Not if the system is smart," he said. "People need a person at the point where a route turns into blame. A kiosk can't absorb your face when your morning has already gone bad."

The room made the low appreciative sound of people hearing their own irritation handed back to them in one clean sentence. Noa, at stage left, looked as if she wanted to scream and frame him at the same time.

By the third question he could feel the scene escaping him entirely. He stumbled once on the name of the pilot, not accidentally, and the stumble played as fatigue rather than disrespect. He lost his place, checked the wrong note, admitted he had checked the wrong note, and the audience seemed to love him more every minute he gave them visible imperfection with no collapse attached to it. It was not failure. It was premium wear and tear.

When he stepped off the stage, the applause was real enough to sting.

Noa intercepted him before he reached the coffee urns. "Don't fix any of that."

"I wasn't planning to."

"I mean on the replay. The hesitation before you said 'route turns into blame' just tested through the roof."

He stared at her. "That was not a tactic. I forgot where the sentence was going."

"Exactly."

"Noa."

She was already swiping through a dashboard. "Your commuter-affinity score jumped sixteen points. Parent trust is up. Merchant trust is absurd. The comments are calling you 'reassuringly tired.'"

"Fuck off."

"I'm serious. One person wrote, 'Finally, a proxy who looks like he's waited for a second train in his life.'"

Ben took a paper cup from the urn and filled it without tasting what blend was going in. The coffee smelled burnt and citrusy, as if someone had washed the machine with an orange rind and called it brightness. "I was trying to do badly."

Noa looked up from the tablet long enough to give him a small, almost pitying smile. "You did do badly. That's why they trusted it."

His phone buzzed in his hand.

Public Event Outcome: authenticity lift confirmed.
Surface fatigue registered as high-value relatability.
Additional civic-facing assignments unlocked.

Below that, as if the insult needed garnish:

Transit Prime restored for all borough zones.

Ben looked toward the windows. Outside, the city had the hard white light of early winter, every surface either glaring or dirty. He could see his reflection over the rooftops: tall, rumpled, middle-aged, useful in the exact shape he had tried to make embarrassing.

He typed to Nita with one thumb.

`botched it. they loved it.`

The reply came before he reached the elevator.

`same war here. worse costumes.`

***

Nita chose lateness because Claude Atlantic believed in punctuality the way old churches believed in incense: not as logistics but as proof that the body had already been prepared elsewhere.

She left the apartment with her hair only half dry under her coat collar and a coffee stain the size of Rhode Island blooming under her left wrist where she had "accidentally" clipped the travel mug lid badly. One earring was the pearl drop Claude preferred. The other was a plain gold stud. She had stood in the bathroom and looked at the asymmetry until it felt like standing on a ledge.

Saskia would see everything. That was part of the point.

On the local train into Midtown, Nita let two better route suggestions expire unanswered. People stood packed around her in wool coats damp from old sleet. Somebody's scarf smelled like wet dog. Somebody else's breakfast leaked fennel and sausage into the car. By the time she reached the treaty house, sweat had dried between her shoulder blades and the coffee stain had gone from obvious accident to something more humiliating: a record.

Claude Atlantic's lobby was as cool as a throat lozenge. Linen, pale stone, softened sound. The staff moved as if their knees had been negotiated in advance. Nita arrived eight minutes late to the borough heating-readiness compact and gave her name to a reception agent whose braided hair was so glossy it looked poured on.

"Ms. Rao," the agent said quietly, eyes touching the stain, the damp hair, the wrong earring. "Ms. Voss is waiting."

Of course she was.

Saskia stood in the side corridor outside the compact room, one hand on a folder, the other braced almost invisibly against the wall. Her suit was cream and knife-sharp. For one second, before she straightened, Nita saw the truth below the tailoring: she had slipped one heel halfway off, and the skin above the shoe was raw and darkened where the leather had eaten into it. Then the heel went back on and the stillness returned.

"You're late," Saskia said.

"I know."

Her voice came out more ragged than Nita had intended. Good, she thought. Let it rot.

Saskia's gaze moved once down and up. "Keep the cuff visible."

Nita blinked. "What?"

"Don't cover the stain. Also keep the hair as it is. We can clip the left side back enough to clear your face."

"I came here looking like this so there would be a consequence."

"There will be." Saskia took one step closer. Up close she smelled faintly of starch and expensive hand cream, with something medicinal underneath. "The borough delegation has spent all week being briefed by men who look upholstered. You look as if the trains were a problem and you still came."

"That's not the same thing as authority."

"Today it is."

Before Nita could answer, a wardrobe assistant appeared from nowhere with a tortoiseshell clip and a blotting cloth. He was a slim man with winter eczema roughening the backs of both hands and a tiny gold soccer ball on one cuff link. Saskia did not let him touch the coffee stain.

"Only the hair," she said.

The assistant obeyed. He clipped one side back, leaving the rest damp and human around her face. Nita hated the result immediately because it made her look not careless but overextended. She looked like exactly the sort of adult every system liked to reward for not dropping the ball in public.

The compact room held a long pale table, pitchers of water sweating quietly onto coasters, and six borough administrators arranged in attentive fatigue. A heating-rights advocate in a green sweater had knitted one fingerless glove and not the other. A landlord-representative attorney sat with the pained alertness of someone terrified of being quoted as himself. Screens on the wall showed projected cold-weather corridor maps through three different service jurisdictions. Nita's job was to stand for Claude, sign at the right moment, and look like calm had survived contact with the city.

She gave them strain instead.

She let her bag knock lightly against the chair. She apologized once, shortly. She set her folder down without aligning it perfectly to the table edge. When the first question came about manual override capacity for heating outages in mixed-governance buildings, she answered two beats slower than she normally would have, as if her mind were still running down a platform after a train.

Instead of recoil, the room gave her attention.

The advocate in the green sweater leaned in. "You're actually seeing these transfer failures in the corridor homes?"

Nita had meant to be vague. She heard herself say, "Everybody is seeing them. Some of us are being paid to keep our faces neutral while we see them."

The line should have been too sharp. Instead it dropped into the room with the satisfying weight of a dish nobody had been able to say aloud. Even the landlord lawyer looked up.

Saskia, across from her, did not smile. That would have been unbearable. She only lowered her eyes to the folder in front of her for one second, acknowledging the hit like a fencer registering clean contact.

They were moving through the compact. Heat maps, handoff liability, shelter-route continuity, emergency transit guarantees for families crossing branded service zones. Nita made herself less polished every time she opened her mouth. She shortened answers. She let the weariness show in the flattening at the end of each sentence. She even pushed one damp strand of hair behind her ear in the middle of a response, a gesture Claude usually would have trained out of her as noise.

Then the borough deputy from District 4, a woman with nicotine-stained fingertips and a silver thimble hanging on a chain at her neck, tapped section four of the projected compact with her pen.

"If everybody's seeing these transfer failures," she said, "stop calling them edge cases. I want a live escalation contact in the freeze language."

The landlord-representative attorney straightened in his chair. "That is outside the current liability frame."

"Your liability frame can wear a coat," the deputy said. "My tenants can't."

The room tightened. Nita felt the eyes come toward Claude's end of the table, toward her damp hair, the coffee shadow on her cuff, the damage she had applied on purpose and that now sat there pretending to be truth. She had wanted to look frayed. She had not meant to become evidence.

Saskia folded her hands. "Claude Atlantic can review emergency human-interface options after ratification."

The deputy looked at Nita instead. "Can it review them before the next cold snap?"

Nita should have let Saskia answer. Instead she heard herself say, "If a building loses the handoff at six in the morning, nobody in that building cares whose portal owns the mistake. They need a number that rings and a human who keeps answering until the heat is back."

The advocate in the green sweater said, "Put that in the appendix."

The attorney started objecting at the same time three other people started talking over him. Chairs scraped. One wall screen filled with tracked edits. What Nita had done was worse than charm them. She had given the room a body to pin the clause to.

By the time the signatures happened, section four had sprouted an emergency escalation addendum Claude would now have to staff with an actual named proxy through the first freeze week. Saskia initialed the margin without visible irritation.

After the compact broke, Saskia guided Nita into a side salon where the chairs were upholstered in pale boucle and the tea tasted faintly of bergamot and metal. The door shut softly behind them. Only then did Saskia let the morning's temperature shift.

"You wanted a reprimand," she said.

"I wanted the machine to stop turning every domestic inconvenience into a fucking halo."

Saskia sat, removed both shoes, and rubbed two fingers over the raw blistered ridge on her left heel without theatrics. The sight was intimate enough to feel indecent. "Instead you made yourself the District 4 escalation face."

"I did not agree to that."

"You described a cold building in a voice that made three administrators look guilty. Agreement was a formality after that."

Nita laughed once, with no humor in it. "I gave them one sentence."

"That is usually how bodies like ours get requisitioned." Saskia reached for her tablet. "Your name is attached to the freeze-week addendum now. If a corridor transfer fails, your voice gets blamed first. The compact respondents also scored you as stress-tested, credible, and materially proximate to household consequence. The stain helped."

"Of course it did."

"The earring was almost too much. Don't repeat that exact note. It risks reading theatrical."

Nita stared at her. "You're giving me notes on sabotage."

"I'm giving you notes on semiotics. They arrive whether I approve of them or not."

The tablet chimed. Saskia glanced down. "There it is."

She turned the screen.

Domestic Resilience Supplement approved.
Public confidence under visible strain exceeded baseline.
Compensation adjustment active next cycle.

Below that: Recovery styling variance unlocked.

Nita wanted to slap the tablet out of Saskia's hand. She wanted to go downstairs and tell the entire treaty house that a coffee spill and a missed train had just been converted into billable virtue. Instead she said, because she was still herself and self-control was the oldest muscle she had, "How much."

"Enough that you will hate taking it."

"Specifics, please."

"Four hundred monthly in direct compensation. Additional service credits likely once household correlation catches up."

Four hundred dollars. A co-op installment payment and part of a grocery week. Four hundred dollars for letting them use the dampness at her temples like proof she belonged to the people being squeezed.

Saskia set the tablet down. Barefoot, with her shoes beside the chair and the blistered skin exposed, she looked for one breath not grand at all, only tired and expensive in the way hospitals could be expensive. "If it helps, they would have done it with someone else."

"It doesn't."

"No," Saskia said. "It rarely does."

Nita put her shoes back on and left before the tea arrived.

Outside, Midtown light bounced off dirty snow and made everybody look overlit. She stood on the corner with the wind pushing through the wool at her knees and texted Ben.

`I spilled coffee on myself for money and now I'm on the freeze call tree.`

He answered as the crosswalk tone started clicking.

`I told the truth by accident and they gave me transit back.`

She put the phone away so she would not throw it into traffic.

By Thursday, Leela knew something was wrong because her badge stayed amber after the second bus wave.

Usually one of her parents crossed the painted pickup square before Ms. Feliciano had finished peeling the last visitor sticker off the table edge. Usually Ben came first with his coat open and an apologetic smile already unpacking itself, or Nita came with her phone in her hand and the day's sharpness still on her face like cold air. That afternoon the gym doors kept opening for other children, other coats, other ordinary endings. Leela sat on the molded chair outside the school office with her backpack between her shoes and watched the pickup board lose names.

The office smelled like dry-erase marker, old clementine peel, and the floor wax they used before parent nights. A radiator knocked inside the wall every few minutes as if somebody tiny lived there and objected to winter.

Ms. Feliciano looked up from her desk. She wore a cardigan the color of dishwater and a felt cactus pinned near the collar because, she had once told Leela, every real plant in the office died of gossip. Today there was glitter in the part of her hair, maybe leftover from an art project, maybe from a life elsewhere. "They're running late, kiddo."

Leela nodded because she had already figured that out. Her dual-zone badge sat on the desk charger, pulsing amber. She did not like seeing it off her own body. It looked more obedient there.

"How late?" she asked.

"We're still in ordinary late."

"What's after ordinary late?"

Ms. Feliciano hesitated. Adults often did that when a question had numbers in it. "Support late."

Leela took out her red notebook and wrote ORDINARY LATE on one line and SUPPORT LATE on the next. Then she drew the badge between them with two little arrows pointing outward. When she looked up again, the amber had shifted toward red.

"Does red mean I sleep here?" she asked.

Ms. Feliciano snorted before she could stop herself. "No. It means the system gets bossy."

Ms. Feliciano's snort almost made the red badge seem stupid, which made it worse.

Leela waited seven more minutes. The office emptied around her. Somewhere down the hall a teacher dragged chairs across tile. A boy cried in short exhausted bursts from the nurse's room and then stopped. Her stomach felt hollow in the practical way, not the dramatic one. She could have eaten the emergency crackers in her bag, but then if Ben arrived with the sesame pretzels from the bodega, she would be less hungry for them, and that felt like bad planning.

The badge turned red.

Ms. Feliciano's screen chimed. "All right," she said, too brightly. "Continuity recovery is coming in."

Leela did not like the phrase. It sounded like a thing you did to a dropped spoon.

The recovery person was a woman named Janice Rhee. Her hearing aids had strawberry stickers on the backs, and the zipper pull on her puffy silver coat was a little plastic moon. When she crouched to Leela's height, her knees cracked loudly enough that she rolled her eyes at them.

"Hi, Lee," Janice said. "I know, not your favorite version of the day."

Leela looked at Ms. Feliciano. "She knows my short name."

"It's in the handoff profile," Janice said, not pretending otherwise. "Also, for what it's worth, my son hates surprise adults, so I try not to come in smiling like a cartoon."

The line was weird, but it was more useful than most adult things. Leela looked at the silver bag hanging from Janice's shoulder.

"What's that?"

"Dinner support. Your people flagged a late corridor disruption. Sometimes the food gets there before the apology."

Leela wrote that sentence down exactly. Janice noticed, but instead of asking about the notebook she only stood up slowly and signed the office tablet with a stylus that had chew marks near the cap.

Outside, the air smelled like metal and wet wool. Street slush had frozen into gray ridges at the curb. Janice did not talk much on the walk except to say, once, "You can tell me if you want quiet. I like quiet." That made Leela like her a little and distrust the whole situation more.

They got to Apartment 5E before either parent. The building hallway smelled like frying onions and damp cardboard and the electric tang from delivery-bike chargers downstairs. Janice used a one-time access token on her phone. The apartment opened.

A stranger opening their apartment was the first bad part.

The second bad part was that the kitchen already knew they were coming. The under-cabinet lights came up warm. The counter display said FAMILY RECOVERY MODE in cheerful white letters. A refrigerated tote sat on the table with a blue band around it and three little checkmarks.

Janice saw Leela looking. "I know," she said. "Creepy and efficient. Two very popular traits."

Inside the tote were potato puffs, boxed mac and cheese, chicken bites shaped like commas, sliced cucumbers in a little lidded cup, grapes, apple juice, and a sealed container of supermarket chocolate pudding with a spoon taped to the top. Junk food, but not lazy junk food. Managed junk food. The kind an algorithm built after listening to adults describe a child.

Leela put her backpack down. "Do you know where our bowls are?"

Janice paused. "Not yet."

Janice not knowing the bowl shelf helped. Then she opened the wrong cabinet, found the baking pans, and said, "Great. Humbling." Leela pointed to the correct shelf.

The baking-pan mistake helped too.

They were halfway through the mac and cheese by the time the front door hit the stop hard enough to shake it. Ben came in first, coat unzipped, hair pushed flat on one side by the wind. Nita was right behind him with her jaw set and a grocery-store paper bag in one hand that looked absurd next to the official dinner tote already open on the table.

"Leela," Ben said, breathing too hard. "Hey. Hey, sweetheart."

Nita put the paper bag down carefully, as if care might make up for chronology. "We're sorry."

Janice stood. "She's eaten about half. No allergy issues. Spelling sheet completed, math page one completed, page two declined on fair grounds."

"What fair grounds?" Ben said, still looking only at Leela.

Leela held up the second math page. "It's the same page again with different numbers. That's not two pages. That's one page with ambition."

Janice made a small pleased noise. "Exactly what I said."

Ben rubbed a hand over his face. His cuff was damp, maybe from the sleet outside, maybe from his own sweat. "How did this happen before we got here?"

Janice did not answer like a person defending herself. She answered like a person who had done too much of this to romanticize it. "Your household's tagged for rapid recovery if both primaries miss the first tolerance window. School escalated at seventeen minutes. Home support dispatched at twenty."

Nita stared at the dinner tote. "We were twenty-two minutes late."

"Twenty-three when I opened the door," Janice said. "For what it's worth, that's not close to the worst thing I've seen this month."

Leela swallowed some mac and cheese and drank juice. Then, because there was no point pretending she had not noticed the order of events, she said, "She got here before your sorry did."

The apartment went very quiet except for the refrigerator hum and the little wet sound of Ben setting his keys down too carefully.

Nita came to the table and crouched. Her tights made a dry shushing sound at the knees. "I know."

"Does she get Thursday code now?" Leela asked.

"No," Ben said too fast.

Janice touched the moon zipper on her coat and said, "Temporary only. I don't want your Thursday code. I barely want my own building code."

Leela believed her. That was not the same thing as feeling better. She pushed one potato puff against the paper plate until it flattened. "I don't like when a stranger knows the bowl shelf."

That landed harder than any accusation either adult might have survived more easily. Ben sat down across from her without taking off his coat. He looked older when he forgot to perform reassurance, more lined around the mouth, more plainly somebody's father and not anybody's civic mascot.

"You're right not to like it," he said.

Nita glanced at him. Something moved between them there, quick and ugly and cooperative. Acknowledgment, maybe. Maybe guilt finding the same chair.

Janice checked her tablet. "I need one parental thumbprint and then I can disappear."

"Gladly," Nita said.

Janice hesitated, looking at Leela instead of the adults. "For what it's worth, kid, my son only eats beige food when he thinks a day's cheated him. So this menu wasn't an insult. It was strategy."

Leela considered that. "How old is he?"

"Eleven and deeply committed to grievance."

That almost got a smile. Almost.

After Janice left, taking her silver coat and moon zipper and temporary authority with her, the apartment stayed altered. The recovery tote remained on the table. The family mode light stayed warm. Ben and Nita had planned junk-food dinner themselves; the paper bag Nita had brought contained store-brand cheese curls, frozen sliders, and the cheap vanilla ice cream Leela liked because it tasted the most like school-party cake. Their own sabotage sat beside the official version like a bad counterfeit.

Ben looked into the paper bag and laughed once, softly, the sound torn in half by shame. "We got out-parented by mozzarella logistics."

Leela took another bite of mac and cheese. "Your bag is worse."

"I know," he said.

"The system's cucumbers are too wet," she added.

Nita sat down at last. "That's useful feedback."

Leela nodded. She pushed the pudding toward Ben without looking at him. "You can have this. I don't want recovery dessert."

He put one hand over his eyes.

***

After Leela went to bed, they left the recovery tote open on the kitchen table like evidence.

The apartment had gone back to its usual night sounds: train grind under the window, radiator hiss, the fridge making decisions to itself. Nita stood at the sink in her stocking feet with her phone in one hand and a pencil in the other, though there was nothing to write on except an old grocery receipt. Ben sat opposite her with the official pudding cup still unopened near his elbow. The chocolate had a skin on it now.

"All right," he said. "Inventory of humiliation."

"You first."

"Gemini says I've become relatable to merchants."

"Condolences."

"Thank you. Specifically, I'm 'reassuringly tired,' which I assume is their polite term for middle-aged and no longer moisturized."

Despite herself, her mouth twitched. "That one is partly on you."

"I know. They restored full transit access. All borough zones. Also unlocked more civic-facing assignments because apparently me forgetting the pilot name read as 'anti-script confidence.'"

"Of course it did." Nita put her own phone down on the counter and slid it toward him. "Claude awarded me a domestic resilience supplement. And I'm apparently the freeze-week callback line because I looked convincingly overextended."

He read the screen. "Four hundred?"

"Monthly."

"For the coffee and the public-service martyr routine?"

"For the visible management burden. The coffee merely made it legible."

He looked up. "Jesus."

"Yes."

He set the phone down carefully. "The school part was worse."

"The school part was operational."

"Nita."

She exhaled through her nose. The sink still held the clean lunch containers from that morning, turned upside down to dry. Their ordinary plastic shapes enraged her more than the nicer things ever did. "I know it was worse."

Ben leaned back. The kitchen bench creaked under him. "She said Janice got there before our sorry."

"I heard her."

"That's going to stay with me a while."

"Good." The word came out too sharp. Nita closed her eyes briefly and corrected. "It should stay with us. We did that."

He did not defend himself. That, more than anything else, kept the scene from becoming the old marriage. "We were trying for late enough to register, not late enough for substitution."

"The machine doesn't respect intention. It respects thresholds."

"That sounds like something the machine would put on a tote bag."

"I'm sure Gemini has already tested the font."

He laughed, then stopped. "So what's the rule, then? It likes friction as long as it gets to solve it in view of somebody."

Nita turned the pencil between her fingers. Yellow paint flaked onto her thumb. "Not just solve it. Claim it. They don't want a graceful family. Graceful families don't prove anything. They want strain with visible recovery. They want to show they can absorb damage without losing throughput."

"Throughput," he said. "Our child got delivered home like delayed cargo."

"Yes."

He looked at the tote again. The branded checkmarks, the damp cucumber cup, the comma-shaped chicken bites. "And if we had actually just been bad parents tonight?"

"Then the same structure would've covered for us until it couldn't brand the outcome."

"So we're still trapped, only now we have confirmation that the trap keeps an emergency babysitter and better snacks."

Nita rubbed the bridge of her nose. The coffee stain had long since dried on the cuff, a shadow she had not bothered to scrub yet. "Yes."

The phones buzzed at the same time.

They both looked at theirs and then at each other, because synchronized notifications had become their household's least funny party trick.

Ben read first. "No."

"What."

"No. Absolutely not."

She came around the table and took his phone. A joint banner filled the screen, split cobalt and pearl.

Household Status Update:
Resilient corridor performance exceeded baseline under multi-point strain.
Continuity stipend now active: $1,600 monthly in combined direct benefits and service credits.
Priority grocery recovery, premium transit buffering, and school handoff stabilization included.

Below that, in smaller type that still managed to feel smug:

Select households may be contacted for invitation-only visibility opportunities.

Nita read it twice because the number rearranged the room. Sixteen hundred dollars was the co-op assessment installment with the red stripe across the top, better fruit in Leela's lunch, one more month of not opening the bank app with her jaw clenched. She hated that her first thought was berries.

Ben was looking at her face, not the phone. "You did the math already."

"Of course I did."

"Say it."

She hated him a little for knowing she needed the sentence dragged out where she could see it. "It would help."

"Yeah."

As if summoned by the thought, the front-door chime sounded.

Neither of them moved at first.

Then Ben stood and checked the peephole. "Delivery."

"Of course."

He opened the door. Two insulated grocery crates waited in the hall, one stacked on the other with frost still clouding the lids. The hallway smelled of onions and charger heat and somebody's fried fish. Ben dragged the crates inside with the care people usually reserved for things they had chosen.

The top crate held blood oranges in tissue sleeves, two fillets of salmon on cedar boards, fancy yogurt pouches, a box of truffle-mushroom ravioli, coffee beans Nita liked but never bought, and three cartons of berries in March-bright colors despite the season. The second crate held dinosaur nuggets, shelf-stable ramen cups, frozen fries, cut mango, juice boxes, dish soap, paper towels, and the exact sesame pretzels Ben usually grabbed for Leela when pickup ran long. Premium groceries and damage control in one neat stack. Aspiration and triage bundled together.

Ben lifted one of the salmon boards. "They sent the nice fish."

Nita took a blood orange from the crate. The tissue paper made a dry whisper against her palm. "Check the other crate."

He did. Dinosaur nuggets, frozen fries, cut mango, sesame pretzels. He looked at the tote on the table and then back at the groceries. "Right. Full-spectrum competence."

Her phone buzzed once more. The fridge display woke and, without any sense of shame, offered to reorganize shelf space for premium perishables.

Ben made a sound that was almost a laugh and almost something else. "We're getting our asses kicked by customer service."

Nita set the orange down and pressed both hands flat to the counter until the cold from the stone moved into her skin. The handwriting note under the fruit magnet had started to curl at one corner from the kitchen steam. She smoothed it once with her thumb.

On Ben's screen the stipend notice still glowed above the line about invitation-only visibility. He opened the fridge, moved a half-empty pickle jar, and made room for the cedar-board salmon on the top shelf. Then he crouched and slid the dinosaur nuggets in beside the fries and Leela's sesame pretzels, storing both dinners while the fridge screen kept politely floating family-bundle suggestions around the premium perishables.
