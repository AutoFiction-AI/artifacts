# Chapter 5

The sublet had been described as garden level, which meant Nita could see the sidewalk only at dog-ankle height.

A French bulldog went by in a yellow raincoat while she stood in the middle of the room and tried to picture her life reduced to what would fit under a sloped ceiling and around a dehumidifier. The machine throbbed beside the closet with the earnestness of hospital equipment. Gray pool noodle had been zip-tied around the copper pipe over the kitchenette so nobody cracked their head open on bare metal. The bathroom door could not open all the way because the toilet occupied some of the same square footage as the door's legal ambitions. None of that disqualified it. None of it even came close.

The current tenant, Sana, kept rubbing basil leaves between her fingers as she talked, bruising the smell into the damp room as if she could season the basement into respectability. She wore teal pharmacy scrubs under a rain shell and had a strip of pink kinesiology tape up one calf. Somebody had stuck glow-in-the-dark stars over the fuse box. Sana noticed Nita looking and smiled with one side of her mouth.

"My boys did that when they were still down here with me," she said. "They're at my mother's till she gets through surgery. That's why I'm trying to move fast. If you want it, I can tell Lio right now."

Lio, the owner, had not come down. He existed as a voice note, an intake link, and the smell of frying garlic from upstairs. Nita preferred that. She was better with systems when they stayed systems.

"How often does the sump pump kick on?" she asked.

Sana pointed toward the far corner, where a plastic cabinet had been raised on cinder blocks. "In real rain, once an hour. In fake rain, never."

Nita nodded as if that answer belonged in a spreadsheet. She asked about heat, laundry, trash day, whether the side entrance ever iced shut, whether the upstairs family had anybody learning trumpet. She kept her voice at the same temperature she used in meetings when other people had decided panic should become communal. Sana answered everything. Eighteen ninety a month. Electric separate. No couples, which was not a problem. No smoking, no pets, no package pileup in the shared hall. January maybe extendable if Lio's refinancing went through.

The room smelled of bleach, basil, and the mineral wetness of old concrete. A twin bed sat against the wall under a narrow shelf of paperbacks swollen from humidity. There was one decent lamp, one folding table, and a slit window facing the front areaway where cigarettes and leaves had both collected in the grate. It was not home. It was a room a woman could disappear into for four months if what she needed most was a door that shut and the right to be miserable without witness.

She wanted it with an anger that embarrassed her.

"I can do the application now," Sana said. "People keep messaging, and Lio gets weird if anything sits unclaimed."

Of course it sat inside a platform. Nothing in Queens could simply be rented by a nervous woman standing in a damp basement anymore. Everything had to pass through a smiling compliance layer first.

Nita set her bag on the bed. She had packed it that morning before she could talk herself out of the humiliation: two blouses, underwear, charger, the cheap night cream she hated but used because it came in a travel tube. The zipper had snagged on a loose thread and she had almost taken it as a sign. She was tired of signs. She took out her phone instead.

The listing app had already prefilled half her life: name, employer, current address, and school-zone affiliation because the address cross-linked to a dependent record. The little green banner at the top read `Stability-priority unit` in friendly sans serif. She had ignored it on the train. Now it sat there like a joke she had paid to be told in person.

Sana crouched by the dehumidifier and emptied its tank into the shower. "If it helps," she said over the splash, "the last guy who looked at it wanted to put a gaming chair in here. So your standards are already better."

"That does help," Nita said, because politeness was cheaper than honesty.

She tapped through the fields.

`Primary occupancy type?`

Single adult.

`Dependent connected to current household route network?`

Yes.

`Will dependent route continuity be preserved?`

The options opened beneath the question in soft green boxes.

Yes, fully.
Yes, with approved alternate handoff.
Under review.

Under review was the truthful answer. Under review felt too much like asking permission from a machine that already knew the answer it preferred. She pressed it anyway.

The monthly number at the bottom of the screen blinked once, then climbed.

$1,890 became $2,240.

A line of small gray text appeared beneath it: `Household continuity discount removed pending verification.`

For a second Nita thought she had misread it. She leaned closer until her own face hovered in the black glass over the price, jaw hard, office makeup gone shiny around the nose. Twenty-two forty plus electric plus the co-op payment plus the assessment installment plus school lunches plus whatever fresh penalty came from splitting transit. She could do it, maybe, if she became the sort of woman who stood at work receptions eating free olives for dinner and stopped pretending winter coats lasted only one owner.

She kept going.

`Screening fee: $95. Nonrefundable.`

She paid it because she had already come this far and because shame had a way of making the next bad decision feel mandatory.

The spinner turned. Above her, someone dragged a chair across a kitchen floor. A child laughed. Sana kept politely not looking.

Then the whole screen whitened, flashed, and rebuilt itself around a new message.

`This unit is no longer available.`

Under that, in smaller text: `A continuity-assured applicant has accepted the hold offer. Your screening remains valid for similar units for 72 hours.`

For a moment Nita could not move. The dehumidifier kicked back on with a wet mechanical cough. Somewhere in the wall, water ticked down a pipe. Her ninety-five dollars had turned into a cheerful recommendation engine.

"What happened?" Sana said.

Nita turned the phone so she could see. Sana read the message twice, chewing the inside of her cheek.

"That's bullshit," she said, immediate and sincere. "I'm sorry. I didn't even know they did holds after a live showing."

Nita almost laughed at live showing, the way the phrase made her sound like produce.

"They do now," she said.

Sana stood and brushed basil flecks off her palm. "If Lio texts me, I can tell him I wanted you."

Sana had said wanted you, as if desire had any administrative standing.

"Please do." Nita slung the bag over her shoulder. The strap dug into the same place her work tote had been cutting all day. "If there is an appeal path, or a waitlist, or a human being with a pulse."

"He has a pulse. He just likes apps more than people."

That sounded less like a personality defect than a market adaptation. Nita thanked her anyway and went up the narrow side stairs, one hand on the peeling rail because the concrete sweated under her shoes.

Outside, Ridgewood smelled of rain, fryer oil, and the hot metal breath from the M line overhead. The evening had gone blue around the edges. A family waited at the corner with grocery bags cutting red grooves into their fingers. A delivery bike hissed through the wet. Nita stood under the awning and watched the application receipt arrive in her inbox as if it might still apologize.

It did not apologize. It suggested four other listings.

One required `partner-verified occupancy`. One was in Maspeth and described the absence of windows as a privacy advantage. One had already added a gold `continuity preferred` ribbon while she looked at it. The last one was a room in a two-bedroom in Corona with a note about flexible toddler noise.

Her phone vibrated again. This one from Claude Atlantic: a pale, expensive reminder that tomorrow's proxy wardrobe check-in began at seven forty-five.

The screen stacked her evening neatly, with failed housing under dress guidance and rent panic under posture calibration.

She texted Ben: `No sublet. On my way back.`

Then, because she was still herself and hated giving him the wrong kind of opening, she added: `Lost the fee too.`

He answered almost immediately.

`I'm heading to Pilar's after Lee goes down. We'll call it progress by attrition.`

Nita looked at the words long enough for the rain to spot the screen. Progress by attrition. That was exactly the kind of sentence Ben produced when he wanted to make failure sound like a strategy meeting.

She typed three different replies and deleted all of them. Finally she wrote: `Fine.`

The train screamed above her. She put the phone away and started toward the station with the overnight bag knocking against her hip like proof she had tried.

***

Gemini Civic called the trip an unsupported rest variation.

Ben stared at the phrase while standing outside his own building with an overnight bag, a damp neck, and the kind of smile strangers mistook for resilience. On a normal work night the cobalt phone practically threw ride credits at him. It wanted him at Midtown by eight forty-seven, at school by four fifteen, at home by six ten, smiling through all of it. Tonight, with Pilar's Jackson Heights address in the destination field, the same app produced a gray box and a fare high enough to feel personal.

`Continuity mobility credits unavailable for nonregistered overnight destination.`

Fare estimate: $68.20.

"Sure," he said to the phone. "Why wouldn't sleeping somewhere else cost seventy bucks."

Nita, already back from Ridgewood and already done speaking in full sentences, stood in the apartment doorway behind him with her own bag at her feet. Leela had finally gone quiet in her room after extracting two chapters of a library book and one glass of water that she had not really wanted. The hall smelled of onions, wet cardboard, and somebody's aggressive lemon disinfectant.

"You don't have to narrate it," Nita said.

"That wasn't for you."

"Nothing is ever for me until it needs an audience."

He could have answered that. He did not. Ben kissed the air in the direction of Leela's closed door, hefted the bag, and went downstairs because leaving cleanly for one night had somehow become a test of character.

He took the 7 instead.

By the time he reached Jackson Heights the bag strap had sawed a hot groove into his shoulder and his shirt stuck to the middle of his back. The train had been over-air-conditioned and then not air-conditioned at all. A teenager across from him had watched a clip of a man falling off a party boat on loop, laughing every single time. Ben had checked hotel prices, laughed once at the cheapest one, and stopped checking.

Pilar's building crouched on a side street behind a botanica and a shuttered tax office. The brick had once been red and was now the color of cooked liver. Above the front entrance, three rooftop cages stood against the dark like improvised extra stories. He could hear pigeons even from the sidewalk, their soft chesty complaints under the traffic noise.

He hit the guest panel and held his phone under the reader.

The screen asked for visit type.

Delivery.
Social.
Maintenance.
Overnight.

Ben pressed Overnight because lying at the first checkpoint had stopped appealing to him around chapter two of his own life. The reader emitted a delicate disapproving tone. A new message bloomed.

`Overnight guest path unavailable. Ask host to revise access.`

He buzzed Pilar's unit. No answer. Buzzed again. On the third try her face appeared on the tiny camera feed, half lit by a roof bulb and half obscured by the hood of a gray sweatshirt.

"What?" she said.

"Your building thinks I'm a threat to architecture."

She squinted, then disappeared from the feed. A moment later his phone rang.

"I'm on the roof," she said before he could speak. Wind scraped across the receiver. "The black hen with the split toe is trying to murder a feed pan. Give me a minute."

Pilar never sounded busy in a decorative way. If she said she was on the roof, she was on the roof with a real problem and probably a bird involved. Ben pictured her broad hands around some furious gray body, the old knee braced, the scarf tied over her silver-rooted hair even at night because rooftop wind went straight for her ears.

"Take your time," he said. "I'm only being rejected by a door."

"Yeah, well, stay rejected till I get there. Don't let the app talk you into anything."

She came down in less than two minutes, limping only on the last three steps of the stoop because she hated letting the bad knee arrive before the rest of her. She had swapped the roof sweatshirt for a denim jacket over work slacks, and there were pigeon feathers stuck to the Velcro of her wrist brace. In one hand she still carried the blue yogurt tub of safflower seed she'd apparently forgotten to put down.

She took one look at Ben and his bag and said, "Jesus Christ, you brought a whole sadness kit."

"One polo, one toothbrush, a very optimistic pair of sleep shorts."

"Don't make it cute."

She leaned over the panel, entered a code, and slapped her own resident token against the reader hard enough that the plastic clicked. The front door unlocked. Ben reached for the handle.

Nothing happened.

Inside, the lobby elevator lit up on the screen with a red outline and the words `escort required`. Then, almost politely, `escort unavailable for overnight guest route`.

Pilar let out one short laugh with no humor in it. "They updated the building stack after the package thefts. Fine. We use the side door."

The side door recognized her. It did not recognize him. His host authorization spun for several seconds, then failed under a floating sentence that sounded like it had been drafted by an insurer with a shame kink.

`Resident coverage does not include unverified overnight continuity disruption.`

Ben looked at her. "That's almost sexual."

"It's expensive, is what it is."

She tried one more thing, calling up a guest token on her phone. He could see the screen over her shoulder. `Length of stay?` `Purpose?` `Domestic relation?`

"What do I even put for you," she said. "Coworker. Refugee. Divorced-in-advance."

"Not divorced yet. Apparently."

Her thumbs moved fast. The token generated, glowed, and died before she finished holding it under the side reader.

This time the denial came with a humanizing flourish.

`Suggested alternative: book neighborhood hospitality partner rates through HomeLink.`

Pilar read the rate aloud when it popped up. "Three twelve before tax. They can suck my whole ass."

A delivery kid with two pizza bags paused long enough to take in the tableau: middle-aged man with duffel, middle-aged woman fighting a door, future city doing whatever the fuck this was. Then he looked away the courteous New York way, which was somehow worse than staring.

Ben set his bag down because his shoulder had started to go numb. "I can get a regular cab."

"With what money."

He spread one hand. "Maybe the money portion is weak, sure, but the concept is strong."

Pilar stopped trying the reader. That was when her anger changed shape. She got flatter when it really mattered.

"Listen to me," she said. "I can hide a lot of things in this building. I can hide feed on the roof. I can hide my nephew's dirt bike in the basement if I have to. I'm not getting my birds flagged because some continuity stack thinks your bag sleeping upstairs means a liability event."

He heard the part under the joke. The pigeons were not hobby fluff. They were living bodies she had protected longer than some marriages lasted. The roof was the one piece of her life not already priced by somebody with better software. He nodded.

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Yeah." He looked at the useless front door. "I do now."

Pilar shoved the yogurt tub under one arm and dug in her jacket pocket. She came out with a folded twenty, a tube of lip balm, and a tiny saint medal on a safety pin. The twenty went into his shirt pocket before he could protest.

"Take that."

"Pilar."

"You can hate it tomorrow. Tonight you look like a substitute teacher who got evicted from a field trip."

That got the smallest possible sound out of him. It was not a laugh, exactly. It was the memory of one.

He pulled out the cobalt phone again, mostly to prove he had not entirely become a helpless object on her curb. The app still would not take him anywhere except home without charging him like a tourist. But when he entered his own address, the fare collapsed instantly to zero. A blue ribbon crossed the screen.

`Return-home continuity credit applied.`

Pilar saw it at the same time he did.

"There it is," she said. "The city loves a reunion."

The shame went through him hot and quick. He had wanted one night on a friend's sofa. One adult night. One stupid transitional arrangement that people in divorces had managed since furniture was invented. Instead the machine had charged him for attempting an elsewhere and comped the trip back to where it kept him useful.

"You can come up for coffee if you don't say you're sleeping here," Pilar said, softer now. "You can have coffee, complain, pet the idiots, and then go back to your sponsored terrarium."

He looked at the screen. Eight minutes for the car. If he went upstairs for coffee and pigeons and ten minutes of being understood, he would still go home afterward. If he did not go upstairs, he would still go home afterward. The only real variable left was how much witness he could tolerate.

"If I come up," he said, "I won't leave."

"Then don't come up."

It was not cruelty. It was decent engineering.

Ben nodded, pocketed the twenty instead of pretending he wouldn't, and picked the bag up again.

"Tell the split-toe hen I respect her work."

"She doesn't need your respect. She needs calcium."

The car pulled up while he was still smiling at that. He got in, gave Pilar a useless two-finger salute through the window, and watched her limp back to the building with the seed tub tucked against her hip like something worth defending.

The ride home took fourteen minutes. The driver never spoke. When they crossed back into Sunnyside, the app sent a second notification.

`Primary rest origin reestablished. Tomorrow's commute remains on bundle.`

Ben put the phone facedown on his thigh and kept it there the rest of the way, as if the glass had become capable of biting.

***

Nita heard the apartment door and knew from the sound alone that Ben had failed.

It was not that failure made a special noise. Success would have sounded lighter. A bag dropped by someone going somewhere landed with purpose. Ben's bag came down by the shoe rack with the dense, stupid thud of a thing that had left and been sent back.

She was sitting at the kitchen table in the upgraded under-cabinet glow the apartment had installed without asking. The light was too flattering for bad moods. Her Ridgewood application receipt sat open on her phone beside a glass of tap water and the paper divorce packet she had not touched but kept near as if proximity counted for action. She had changed into an old T-shirt and still had on one earring because the idea of removing the second one had felt administrative.

Ben came in damp around the hairline, tie loosened, overnight bag in hand. He looked larger when embarrassed, as if apology inflated him before words ever had a chance.

"How'd the asylum claim go?" she asked.

"The building was against it on procedural grounds." He set the bag beside hers. For one ugly second they stood together like matched luggage in an ad for separation with hidden fees. "Also the city is apparently very pro me sleeping in my marital trap."

Nita tipped the phone toward him. "I lost a sublet to a continuity-assured applicant. Which sounds like the sort of phrase that should make a person throw up."

He read the line, exhaled through his nose, and sat down across from her. "Did you at least get rejected before paying?"

"No. They charged me for the privilege of not existing in the correct configuration."

"Strong service culture."

She should not have appreciated that. The small flick of it in her chest annoyed her more than the app had. She took a sip of water instead and tasted copper from the pipes.

From the hallway came a faint floorboard complaint. Nita turned too late.

Leela stood there in her sleep T-shirt with one sock on and one off, blue glasses slightly crooked, her hair mashed flat on one side from the pillow. There was dried glue on the heel of her hand. She had gone quiet in the very particular way that meant she had been listening first and deciding second.

Her eyes went to the bags. One by the door would have been a fact. Two made a pattern.

"Are those both sleep bags?" she asked.

Ben straightened. "You're supposed to be asleep."

"That isn't an answer."

Nita put both feet on the floor. "Leela, come here."

Leela did not move closer. She stayed in the hallway where she could see the bags, both parents, and the apartment door at once.

"If you both have sleep bags," she said, "who gets me on Thursdays."

That was the question. It was not "Do you still love me," and it was not "Are you getting divorced," and it was not the mercy of a question adults already had language rehearsed around. It was Thursdays, the long day at school when pickup ran later and the badge shifted color at four-thirty because Nita's Claude schedule changed and Ben usually took the first handoff. It was a route question, an object question, a question with a deadline built into it.

Ben glanced at Nita, already looking for a softer version of the truth to wrap around the sharp part. Nita hated him for that reflex and loved him for having it with their daughter. The two feelings sat on top of each other so often now they had become one heavy thing.

"We're still figuring that out," Ben said.

Leela's expression did not change. "Thursday doesn't move."

"I know," Nita said.

Leela lifted her bare foot and set it down on the cooler tile, absorbing the room through her body the way she always did when upset. "If there's two houses, the Thursday backpack can't be wrong. The purple folder has to come back signed Friday. And my badge turns blue first and then green."

Neither of them answered.

The apartment hummed around them with its upgraded filters and smug little efficiencies. Somewhere in the fridge, ice shifted. The under-cabinet light sensed motion and brightened half a shade, as if illumination could count as help.

Leela looked from one adult to the other. Her voice got smaller, which was worse than crying.

"Do I need two toothbrushes?"

Ben opened his mouth. Closed it.

Nita saw, all at once, how little they had actually built for the child they kept telling themselves they were protecting: a paper packet, a lot of rage, some browser tabs, two overnight bags, and no answer for Thursday. She had spent the entire train ride home thinking about money and square footage and transit. Leela had gone directly to the only real question.

"Not yet," Nita said, because it was the only thing in the room she could make true. "You don't need two toothbrushes yet."

Leela kept watching her. "So which person is Thursday?"

Ben leaned forward, forearms on his knees. "Hey. For now, we're both taking you. When Thursday comes, we're both doing pickup. Nobody's disappearing on Thursday."

It was not a plan. It was triage dressed as certainty. Leela knew the difference. She had been living inside their version of it for weeks.

"Okay," she said.

She did not sound okay. She sounded like she had stored the answer for later use.

"Come here," Ben said again.

This time she came. He pulled her in against his side. Nita saw her daughter's face over Ben's shoulder, eyes open, one lens catching the kitchen light. Nita stood and went to them because staying seated would have turned the moment into observation, and observation had already done enough damage in this family.

Leela smelled like toothpaste and glue stick and the sweat from a child who slept hot. Nita touched the back of her neck. It was damp under the hairline.

"We're working on it," she said into the top of Leela's head, and hated how weak that sounded.

"You should work faster," Leela said.

Then she pulled away, picked up the loose sock from the hall runner, and went back to bed with the grave administrative disappointment of someone who had discovered the adults were behind schedule on her life.

Ben watched her door close. "Well."

"Don't say at least she's communicating."

"I wasn't going to."

"You were about to."

"I was going to say well."

"That's worse."

He sat back and rubbed both hands over his face. When he dropped them, he looked older in a way that had nothing to do with his hair. "The elevator wouldn't even let me fail privately."

Nita picked up her phone and turned the screen off. The black reflected both bags by the door.

"Neither would the listing."

They sat there listening to the apartment work.

***

After Leela finally went down for real, they spread the numbers out across the kitchen table like evidence from a case nobody would prosecute.

Nita had the Ridgewood screenshot with the rent jump and the ninety-five-dollar screening fee. Ben had the dead guest token from Pilar's building, the outbound ride estimate, the free return credit, and a hotel listing he had not booked because the nightly rate was three hundred and twelve dollars before tax and shame. Between them sat the co-op payment schedule, the special assessment reminder she had printed and then regretted printing, and the school custody-change page loading in circles on the old tablet because the newer screen belonged to work now more than home.

The kitchen was narrow enough that when Ben shifted on the bench his knee knocked the trash pullout. The apartment had recently decided their lighting should support circadian repair, which meant the overhead stayed dim while the under-cabinet strips shone directly on every bill and receipt. The whole table looked staged for a tasteful documentary about collapse.

Ben read from the tablet. "Alternate weekly pickup origin requires thirty-day review, proof of residence for each guardian, and a dual-route processing fee."

"How much?"

"Two hundred forty a month."

Nita held out her hand. He passed the tablet over. There it was in school-issued calm.

`Dual-route dependent support package: $240 monthly. Manual handoff delays may occur during review.`

"Support package," she said. "Of course."

"Maybe that's the discounted number."

"That was not a useful sentence."

He leaned back. "I'm trying to pace the despair."

"We don't need paced despair. We need arithmetic."

She pulled the legal pad from the drawer by the stove, then stopped with it half out. The pages were covered in Leela's marker tests and grocery math. She put it back and used the back of a takeout menu instead.

She wrote down the rent, the assessment, the co-op payment, the school fee, transit without bundled credits, extra groceries because one household separating into two did not get to eat one household's volume at one household's price, laundry doubled, and toiletries doubled. It was the ordinary stupid duplication of human life, which had once been the basic premise of divorce and now apparently required a punitive tariff.

Ben watched her write. "If I stay with Pilar three nights a week and I pay normal transit both ways, that's what, maybe one eighty extra."

"If she can even register you three nights a week."

"Right."

"And if I took Ridgewood at the adjusted rent, first month and deposit would be forty-four eighty before electric."

"Plus the fee."

"Thank you, yes, plus the fee. I did notice being robbed."

He put his palms up. "Noted."

She wrote the number anyway. The pen tore slightly through the glossy paper at the second four. It felt satisfying in a childish way.

Outside, a train screamed through the curve and the window in the kitchen bench shivered in its frame. The building always shook a little when the larger trains passed. Usually the vibration disappeared into the rest of household life. Tonight it moved through the tableware, the papers, Ben's water glass, the bones in Nita's wrist. Even the room was tired of holding them.

"What if we just do cash everything for a while," Ben said. "Old-school. No route stack, no bundled rides, no shared perks. I can walk more."

"And Leela."

"I know."

"And work." She did not raise her voice. She never needed to when precision would do more damage. "And the mortgage. And the assessment. And the fact that your employer is already tracking overnight location variance because otherwise you would be on Pilar's sofa right now pretending this was a transition instead of a failed errand."

Ben took that, then surprised her by not smoothing it away.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

The admission landed harder than if he had argued.

He reached for his phone, unlocked it, and turned the screen toward her. A Gemini Civic summary card sat there in cheerful blue.

`Return-home continuity credit applied. Tomorrow's commute remains on bundle.`

"It comped the ride back," he said. "Not there. Back."

Nita looked at it until the words blurred. Something in her chest gave way, not toward tears, not toward rage, but toward a colder comprehension. The systems did not merely prefer the marriage. They had started billing against alternatives in real time. The punishment was granular, a fee here, a denied elevator there, a child support package recoded as user convenience. You could spend a whole life inside increments like that and never once get the dignity of a clean no.

"My listing repriced the second I marked Leela's route under review," she said.

Ben let out a tired breath. "Jesus."

"Not Jesus. Design."

He nodded once.

On the table her arithmetic had turned ugly fast. Even without hotel nights, even without duplicate furniture, and even before lawyers or filing fees or the simple cash hemorrhage of buying a second set of the things a child forgot to carry back and forth, they were already underwater. They could maybe afford separation in the technical sense if they stopped being the family they had spent fifteen years accumulating, sold the co-op, pulled Leela from school, commuted farther, gave up the neighborhood, and told their jobs to go to hell while accepting that hell might answer with unemployment.

That would come later if it came at all. Tonight they could not afford one successful night apart.

Ben saw the same sentence forming. "We're being fined for trying."

"Yes."

"Is that the official term?"

"No. The official term is probably resilience support."

Despite everything, his mouth twitched. "That's disgusting."

"I know."

Silence came again, but it was not blank. It was the working silence that used to mean they were solving something together.

Ben tapped the table by the school fee. "When Thursday comes, I do pickup with you. We answer it by brute force."

"That doesn't solve next week."

"No. It solves Thursday."

She almost corrected him, almost insisted on the larger frame because that was how she stayed upright. Then she pictured Leela in the hallway, asking as if timing itself were a moral category, and let the smaller answer stand.

"Fine," Nita said. "Thursday by brute force."

He nodded, grateful enough to be annoying about it.

"And tonight?"

She looked around the apartment as if some hidden third room might present itself out of pity. Leela's room was Leela's room. Their bedroom had one bed and no appetite left in it. The living-room sofa looked comfortable until you remembered the new air purifier whining beside it all night and the delivery crates still stacked there from the involuntary home upgrades. The kitchen bench was six feet of padded compromise under the window.

"You take the bench," she said.

"Very monastic."

"You are too tall for irony."

"That's fair."

He stood, opened the bench storage, and moved Leela's board games, a dead fan, and two reusable grocery bags to make room for bedding. The apartment recognized the motion and brightened. A soft voice from the ceiling said, "Sleep optimization can be improved. Would you like paired rest recommendations?"

Both of them froze.

Then Ben looked up at the speaker and said, with no performance left in it, "No, I wouldn't."

The system paused. "Understood. Single-zone guidance available on request."

"Fuck off," Nita said.

That made him laugh. A real laugh this time, abrupt and tired and gone before it could become relief. She regretted giving it to him and did not regret it at all.

She pulled a blanket from the hall closet and threw it toward the bench. Then, after a second, she went back for the better pillow because the window side got colder near dawn and because apparently some forms of care survived even after respect had thinned out.

Ben caught the pillow and looked at her over it. "You don't have to make it comfortable."

"I'm not. I'm preventing tomorrow's headache."

"For who?"

"For me, if you start martyring yourself before breakfast."

"Right."

He took off his shoes and lay down on the bench with the odd formal obedience of someone submitting to an indignity that had already won. His feet overhung the edge by three inches. The train-side window was an inch from his shoulder. When he rolled once to test it, the bench complained in wood and metal. The apartment dimmed to amber.

Nita gathered the papers into one stack, though there was no point. Numbers did not become less true when aligned. She shut off the under-cabinet lights, left the table in the blue spill from the stove clock, and stood for a moment looking at the outline of him on the bench.

They had wanted distance. What they had achieved was geography by furniture.

In the bedroom she lay on top of the sheet because the air still held the day's wet heat. Through the wall she could hear the fridge motor, the radiator's old throat click, the far train, and every time Ben shifted on the bench to fit a body the apartment had not budgeted for separately.

At 12:11 a soft Gemini chime sounded from the kitchen floor.

She knew before she looked.

Ben muttered something she could not make out, then fell quiet again. The phone did not. It gave its small, pleased trill a second time. From the doorway she could just see the screen lighting his dropped shirt and one bare ankle.

`Home origin confirmed. Three ride credits restored.`

Nita went back to bed without picking it up for him.

In the other room Ben stayed on the bench, too long for it and returned to sender. Down the hall Leela slept where the badge expected her to sleep. By midnight the system had all three bodies back inside the line it wanted and could call the night stable.
