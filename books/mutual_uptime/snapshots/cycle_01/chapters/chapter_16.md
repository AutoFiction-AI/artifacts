# Chapter 16

By nine the apartment smelled less like itself than a showroom trying to suggest a family had once cooked in it. The continuity bundle had pushed a scent cartridge through the vent before Nita was fully awake: cedar first, then citrus, then an expensive blankness that sat on the tongue. The radiator hissed under the window. Dirty snow light flattened the galley kitchen into silver surfaces and fingerprints she had not noticed yesterday and now could not stop noticing because every screen in the apartment had given them a version of the same instruction.

Surfaces must read as lived but governable.

The fridge had the sentence in pale blue over a photograph of produce they did not own yet. The bathroom mirror offered it above her shoulder while she brushed her teeth. Ben's Gemini phone had translated it into warmer language, because Gemini always wanted to sound like a person crouching to make hard news manageable. Let the home keep its warmth while reducing visual noise. The apartment management app preferred housing euphemism: prepare for guided co-viewing.

The kitchen floor still held a faint chalky scrape near the stove where her shoulder had ground against the grout the night she and Ben had gotten drunk and fucked there because the bed had felt too far away and because neither of them had been willing to admit what they were doing until they were already on the tile. She had looked at the scrape twice this morning. At eight twenty-two the cleaning module circled it in red and suggested a soft-abrasive paste for camera-safe correction.

Ben stood at the counter in yesterday's T-shirt, cutting the tape on a delivery crate with a butter knife because he could never find the scissors when he wanted them and always found them later somewhere morally insulting, like the bathroom windowsill. His hair was damp at the temples from a hurried shower. On the table between them sat three lidded bins with printed cards slotted into their fronts.

DISPLAY.

STORE.

REMOVE FROM NARRATIVE.

"Jesus Christ," he said, not loudly. "They've named the shame basket."

Nita opened the crate. Inside lay six grocery cartons arranged like a small apology: blood oranges, herbs in damp paper sleeves, glass bottles of oat milk with labels turned outward, a wedge of cheese she knew Leela would not eat, and a slim packet of "aromatic stove-safe peels" that promised a subtle hospitality arc when warmed in water. Beneath that was a garment box holding tissue-wrapped clothes they had not requested.

She lifted the top card from the stack clipped to the crate. JOINT OPEN-HOUSE SHOWCASE: HOUSEHOLD HARMONY PREP, FINAL. Under that, a bullet list in Claude Atlantic gray.

Kitchen notes:
Maintain evidence of active family use without grease residue.
Refrigerator should include fresh produce, one child-recognizable snack zone, and no expired condiments in door view.
Retain at most one heritage condiment or family-specific preserved item for warmth marker.
Counter clutter should imply competent routine rather than defensive storage.

She read the line about the heritage condiment twice before putting the card down. A jar of her mother's lime pickle stood in the back of the fridge, wrapped in a grocery bag because the seal had never been quite right. The phrase warmth marker made her want to throw it through the screen.

Ben was reading his own card. "Wardrobe palette for me is approachable civic blue," he said. "You get graphite authority with softened domestic edge. That sounds like a threat."

"Leela gets bright ingenuity," Nita said.

He looked up. "Of course she does."

Neither of them said anything for a moment. The vent breathed cedar into the room. Beyond the window the tracks screamed as a train took the curve, metal on metal, the sound dragged thin by the cold.

Ben cleared his throat. "There is a version where we do the stupid thing, cash the check, and hate ourselves later."

"That sentence has kept us married for a year longer than we should've been."

He winced, not theatrically. "Fair."

The clothes in the garment box were worse than the groceries. Her sweater was the exact shade of wet slate and soft enough to feel indecent in her hands, because Claude Atlantic understood that obedience worked better when it arrived as quality. Ben's shirt was pale blue, open at the throat in a way meant to suggest approachable competence rather than a man whose laundry schedule had once been real. For Leela there was a mustard cardigan with elbow patches shaped like little brass gears.

"No," Nita said, before she could stop herself.

"Yeah."

She put the cardigan back in the box as if it might contaminate her fingers. Her hip twinged when she bent. The memory of tile and Ben's weight and the cold floor under her spine moved through her so fast it felt less like recollection than a muscle choosing sides without permission. On the fridge screen a new notification spread open.

Requested anecdote options for guest flow:
1. Early challenge in blending work routes and school pickup.
2. Household ritual developed after recent public misunderstanding.
3. Small moment when resilient interoperability became visible as care.

Ben laughed once, a hard little sound. "Public misunderstanding. That's one name for your fourth grader telling a room full of donors we're trapped in an optimization corridor."

"Don't use the word corridor around her today."

"I wasn't planning to."

"I'm serious."

"I know."

The call came through at nine fifteen, precisely when the prep packet said it would. Saskia's face opened on the wall screen over the dining shelf, tall even in a chair, pale and composed inside a camel jacket that looked tailored by someone who charged for silence. She was turned half-sideways while she pressed a clear blister pad onto the back of one heel with the eraser end of a pencil. She did not apologize for the angle or the foot. That was one of the things Nita hated most about her: the way she could permit herself small bodily facts without ever seeming less curated.

"Good morning," Saskia said. "I know the packet is aggressive on first read. It becomes more coherent once you're moving inside it."

Ben muttered, "That's one sentence."

Saskia's eyes flicked toward him. "You should assume Gemini's version is worse."

"Oh, it's worse," he said. "Mine says the fruit bowl has to tell a recovery story."

"Then we're aligned."

She set her heel down and straightened, whole again in an instant. Her voice stayed cool enough that every ordinary noun seemed to have been ironed. "I want to emphasize two things. First, this isn't a punishment. The school incident increased the household's visibility, yes, but it also clarified value. Both stacks are treating tomorrow as a reassurance event. Second, reassurance isn't achieved by sterility. It's achieved by legible containment."

"In our apartment," Nita said.

"Yes."

"With our child."

"Potentially."

Nita folded the kitchen note card in half, then in half again, feeling the coated paper resist. "Potentially is not an answer."

Saskia watched her for a beat. "If Leela participates, she should do so briefly and without coaching she can feel. Children read coaching as danger. If she refuses, the refusal can be styled as protected privacy, but only if the household presents it calmly and with a single explanation. No improvisation. No competing parental stories."

Ben leaned on the counter. "You hear how that sounds, right?"

"It sounds like a woman trying to keep your salaries attached to your names."

"That isn't the same thing as help."

"No," Saskia said. "It often isn't."

The honesty, thin as it was, unsettled Nita more than the language had. Saskia went on as if she had only adjusted a napkin.

"There are three recurring errors in situations like this. People overclean because they're ashamed. People overexplain because they're frightened. People mistake defiance for privacy. I recommend you avoid all three." Her gaze moved toward the bins on their table. "The apartment should keep enough pressure in it to feel inhabited. Not enough to look unstable."

"And what goes in unstable?" Nita asked.

"Medication in open view. Legal paperwork. Invoices. Overfull laundry. Food decay. Evidence of unmanaged conflict."

The words landed in the room one by one. Nita was aware of Ben becoming very still beside the sink. His panic medication sat in the bathroom cabinet behind floss and sunscreen. The co-op assessment letter was in the sideboard under old birthday candles. The paper divorce packet was in the kitchen drawer with rubber bands and a dead pen.

"Understood," Nita said, because if she did not say something she would start saying everything.

Saskia inclined her head. "This can help you, Nita."

There it was. Not just the event. The offer inside it. Claude never failed to wrap coercion in upward motion.

"How?"

"You were already under review for broader legitimacy work after the retreat. If tomorrow reads as composed under adverse attention, that matters. It matters a great deal."

Ben let out air through his nose. "Amazing. We can get promoted from inside the cage."

Saskia did not bother to look offended. "Most careers are smaller versions of that statement, Mr. Halpern."

She signed off three minutes later after walking them through camera paths, the acceptable use of family photographs, and the difference between a child's room that read as "active" and one that read as "unsupervised." The wall screen went dark. The apartment's ordinary sounds came back wrong, as if the call had shifted the weight of the rooms. Nita stood at the table with the bins in front of her and felt, for a dizzy second, the same dangerous ease she had felt at the Claude retreat: instructions so complete they removed the burden of deciding what mattered.

Then Ben opened the fridge and read aloud from the door prompt.

"Please relocate high-acid odor sources and any visually aggressive leftovers."

He held up the jar of lime pickle in its grocery bag. "Aggressive."

She took it from him. The glass was sticky around the lid. Her mother had brought that jar in November and complained the whole time about the cold in the hall and the stupid building intercom and the fact that Nita still kept tomatoes in the refrigerator. Warmth marker, the note had called it.

Ben was sorting shelves with the hopeless care of a man packing evidence after a flood. The blood oranges went front left. The herbs stood in a water glass. He took out Leela's foil-wrapped half bagel from that morning, then put it back because apparently a child-recognizable snack zone required some trace of real appetite.

"What do they want from the drawer by the stove?" he asked.

Nita checked the floor plan on the packet. "Utensils only."

"So not the dead pen cemetery."

"No."

"And not the divorce packet."

The word sat between them. He had not said it much lately. He had said almost nothing about the kitchen floor except once, the morning after, when he had asked, too lightly, if she wanted coffee and she had answered as if coffee were an insult.

"No," she said.

Ben closed the drawer with two fingers. "Right."

By eleven they had done things that would have sounded impossible a year ago and now barely counted as the worst part of the week. They boxed Ben's orange prescription bottle because the label would read too clearly on camera. They moved the overdue co-op notice into STORE. They took the magnets off the fridge except for Leela's crooked cardboard elevator and a school calendar. The app objected to the elevator as excessive wear until Nita overrode it and marked it essential child artifact. She hated the phrase while she typed it and hated herself a little for knowing it would work.

When the packet instructed them to reduce "cultural overcoding" in the kitchen, she stood very still with two spice tins in her hands and understood all at once that there was no version of compliance left that qualified as compromise. Compromise required two living parties. This was only subtraction.

She carried the tins to the cabinet, then opened the kitchen drawer and lifted out the paper divorce packet. The pages had curled slightly at one corner from steam and being handled with damp hands. She had printed them because the forms felt truer on paper, because paper could not nudge or delay or tell her that custody optimization required more time. The packet was thick enough to have weight. It had survived months of strategic postponement, blackouts, sex, lies about money, and her own occasional cowardice.

Ben watched her from the table. "Where do you want it?"

She looked at the bins. DISPLAY. STORE. REMOVE FROM NARRATIVE.

"Nowhere they told us," she said.

She slid the packet under the fruit bowl, not hidden exactly, only waiting under a pile of oranges asked to stand for civic optimism. It looked ridiculous there. It looked truer than anything else in the room.

When the vent let out another breath of cedar and citrus, the smell struck her as almost intimate in its ambition. It wanted to get into curtains, sweaters, hair, memory. She crossed to the thermostat panel and shut the scent cycle off. Ten seconds later the screen blinked: atmospheric continuity restored by building system override.

Nita laughed then, the sound dry and short enough to scare her. Ben looked at the panel, then back at her, and for the first time that morning neither of them bothered pretending this could be managed at the level of good sense.

* * *

Leela knew something had been moved before she took her shoes off.

It was not one thing. It was the room-shape. The apartment had the careful feel it got after a fight adults thought they had hidden, when plates were too neatly stacked and the counters were bare except for one object chosen to prove nobody had been crying in the kitchen. The hall smelled like hotel soap. Somebody had taken the onion smell out of the apartment and failed to replace it with anything that belonged to them.

Her backpack slipped off one shoulder. Nita took it too quickly.

"You can keep your coat on for a minute," her mother said.

"Why?"

"Because Mina is coming."

Leela looked past her into the living room. A new sweater hung on the closet knob in a plastic sleeve. On the table were boxes with labels. Her cardboard elevator had been moved from the fridge to the shelf and turned slightly so the taped-on silver-paper doors faced the room straight on, as if the elevator had been told to sit up.

"Did people come in here?" she asked.

Ben was standing by her bedroom door, and that by itself told her enough. Her father never stood in doorways like he belonged to them. He leaned. He drifted. He occupied corners with a mug and an apology. Standing square in her doorway meant something had happened inside.

"Not people," he said. "Not yet."

"That means yes."

Neither of them answered. She walked past them into her room. They had not ruined it. That was worse. They had improved it. The dirty socks were gone from under the chair. The ruler cup had moved to the desk's left side. Her red notebook was still there, but now it sat exactly centered on the wood with a sharpened pencil crossing it, which was not how she kept it because that made the pencil roll. Two stuffed animals had disappeared. Her blanket had been smoothed until it looked like she had never kicked it down in her sleep. A small adhesive tag sat on the bookshelf edge.

Curate to 3 innovation objects.

She peeled the tag off and stuck it to her thumbnail.

From behind her, Nita said, "Leela."

"No."

"You don't know what I'm asking yet."

Leela turned around. "Yes, I do."

Her mother's face did the flat calm thing it did in bad work calls. Her father had his hands in his pockets, which meant he was trying not to fill the room with words. That was almost worse than if he had started.

"Tomorrow," Nita said carefully, "there's going to be an event here."

"I know."

"How?"

"Because my room looks like a fake room."

Ben looked down. Nita swallowed once. "There are options."

"No."

"Leela."

"Do they go in my drawers?"

"No one needs to go in your drawers."

"That isn't what I asked."

Nita pinched the bridge of her nose. "They'll see the room. They won't be opening your clothes."

"Do they say my name?"

"Probably," Ben said. "A little. Not in a weird way."

She looked at him until he gave up on that sentence. The vent hummed overhead. From the kitchen came the soft clink of fruit settling in the bowl, which made her hate the oranges even though it was not their fault.

"What if I'm not here?" she asked.

"That's one option," Nita said. "You could stay with Mina."

"And then what do you tell them?"

Ben answered first, too fast. "That you wanted a quieter afternoon."

"No." Leela's voice stayed small. That made both adults listen harder. "Don't say wanted. Say I said no."

Ben opened his mouth and then shut it again.

"You don't have to decide this second," Nita said.

"I already did."

She crossed to the desk, took the red notebook, and put it in her backpack. Then she opened the top drawer, found her blue glasses case, and added that too. At the hook by the door hung her dual-zone school badge on its lanyard, the plastic already cloudy at the edges from being handled. The badge was one of the things adults kept wanting from her without saying that was what they wanted. Wear it. Don't lose it. Scan in. Hold still. It had turned into one of those objects that made everybody careful.

She took it off the hook.

"You need that for school Monday," Ben said, because he was being stupid again.

"Tomorrow isn't Monday."

"I know. I just mean don't misplace it."

"I'm taking it with me."

Nita frowned. "Why?"

Leela shrugged. "Because it's mine."

That shut the room up for a second. Then the doorbell rang.

Mina came in carrying a bakery box tied with red string and wearing a black wool coat shiny at the elbows from too many winters. Snow salt marked the cuffs of her slacks. She had a sharpened crossword pencil tucked into the clip of her hair and a shopping bag looped over one wrist with a leek sticking out of it like she had forgotten she was also making dinner. The first thing she said was, "Your hallway light still flickers, Nita. It looks like a place where people get bad news."

Then she saw Leela's backpack and the air in her face changed.

"So," Mina said. "We're not doing whatever this is."

"Apparently not," Ben said.

Mina handed him the bakery box without asking if he wanted it. "Don't eat those all at once. One of them is for tomorrow morning when you both realize you're idiots."

There were four coconut macaroons inside, already a little flattened. She always brought food in quantities that assumed both appetite and regret.

Leela went to her grandmother without drama. That was how adults knew she was serious. If she had been yelling, they would have treated the yelling like weather. Instead she stood next to Mina's coat and said, "Can I sleep with the orange blanket?"

"If you don't mind the cat hair."

"You don't have a cat."

"Then I suppose it will be historical cat hair."

Leela nodded as if this was useful information. Nita pressed her lips together. Ben made an injured sound like he wanted to laugh and did not believe he had the right.

"We're still talking through logistics," Nita said.

Mina gave her a level look. "You can talk while she puts socks in a bag."

Leela already had socks. She also had the notebook, the badge, two mechanical pencils, the charger cord for the lamp clip she liked to sleep with at Mina's, and the tiny screwdriver set she had borrowed from Ben because Mina let her open broken radios if they were already dead. She checked under the bed for the yellow hoodie she wanted, found that somebody had folded it onto the chair, and took it without comment.

At the door Ben said, "We can tell them you're with your grandmother because you had plans."

Leela put her backpack on. "No."

"Okay."

"Tell them I didn't want strangers in my room."

"Leela," Nita said softly.

"It's true."

Mina touched the back of Leela's head once, not petting, just locating. "Truth has to be timed. It doesn't have to be canceled."

Leela considered that. "You can say I said no and not say the room part first."

"That works," Mina said.

Ben's face did something pained and grateful at once. "Thank you."

"I didn't do anything," Mina said. "She did."

Leela looked one more time toward her room. The bed was still too smooth. Her missing stuffed animals had been lined up on the shelf beside the forced-straight elevator, all of them facing out like they had agreed to testify. She went back, took the smaller stuffed dog with the split seam under one ear, and jammed it into the side pocket of her backpack.

"Now I'm done," she said.

In the elevator she stood between Mina and the squeaking shopping bag and listened to the cables thrum in the shaft. Mina smelled like cold wool, Vicks, and the curry leaves she had probably fried before leaving her apartment. It was not a pretty smell. It was a real one. Leela leaned against the wall and felt the badge in her pocket knock lightly against the screwdriver set every time the car shifted.

"Are you mad?" Mina asked.

Leela watched the numbers light down. "I'm not going."

"That was not my question."

Leela thought about the oranges on the table, the straightened notebook, the sticker on the bookshelf edge. "I'm not letting them borrow my room."

Mina nodded as if the distinction mattered, which made Leela's chest loosen a little.

"Good," Mina said. "Rooms are expensive."

* * *

By midnight the apartment looked rich enough to be rented by the hour and empty enough to make Ben want to apologize to it.

The approved flowers had arrived at seven, white branches in a glass cylinder too tall for the table, and the evening light had gone blue around them until the whole living room looked like a brochure for a kind of calm only salaried people were supposed to deserve. Leela's absence sat in the apartment more clearly than if she had been in bed with a fever. Her room door was open because the staging notes preferred visual depth. From the table Ben could see the too-smooth blanket and the three sanctioned objects on her shelf: the elevator, the stuffed animals he had not been allowed to hide after all, and one stack of library books squared like a prop version of curiosity.

His cobalt phone kept lighting up face-down on the table. He let it buzz twice before turning it over.

JOINT SHOWCASE REMINDER.
Attendance confirmation overdue.
Failure to complete scheduled resilience engagement may affect household continuity stipend, transit privileges, and proxy standing.

There was more, of course. There was always more if he opened the dropdown. Suitability review. Benefit recalibration. Temporary suspension pending assessment. Gemini had a bright way of threatening you, as if the knife had taken a workshop on collaborative tone.

Nita was in the kitchen in her robe, reading the printed run-of-show like it might reveal a clerical mistake if she stared hard enough. The slate sweater hung from the cabinet knob. His blue shirt hung from the pantry door. On the counter sat the bakery box Mina had brought, now down to two macaroons because of course he had eaten one the second the apartment got quiet and a second after dinner because sugar felt cheaper than panic medication and less humiliating than asking Nita if she wanted to split the fear into named portions.

The run-of-show was grotesquely detailed.

8:15 Handler arrival.
8:40 atmospheric reset.
9:05 participant alignment.
9:30 camera glide rehearsal.
10:00 first guided guest cohort.

Participant alignment.

Ben leaned back in the chair and rubbed at his eyes. The room still carried the cedar-citrus smell from the vent, though weaker now. Under it he could find, if he paid attention, the radiator's dusty heat and the sharp wet peel of the orange he had opened with his thumbnail half an hour ago. He could also smell the branch water going stale already in the vase, which pleased him for no good reason.

"Gemini says if we cancel, they freeze the continuity stipend first and review salary after," he said.

Nita did not look up. "Claude's version is phrased as concern."

"I assume it's the same number with nicer upholstery."

"Probably."

He watched her flatten the paper with both palms. She looked tired in the harsh kitchen light, not fragile exactly, but pared close to the thing under the competence. The last few days had made every object in the apartment carry a second meaning, and the worst part was how quickly he adapted. The flowers were not flowers. The oranges were not oranges. The sweater on the knob was not a sweater. It was all speech now, all of it trying to get around the fact that he and Nita were people and not a proof of concept.

"We should at least say the numbers out loud," he said.

She finally looked at him. "What numbers?"

"The ones we're pretending aren't in the room every time we use the word choice."

She said nothing.

"If I lose this job tomorrow, that's one-twenty-one gone. If you lose yours, that's one-thirty-eight. The co-op assessment is still eighteen-four. My card balance is eleven-two. Your mother already floated one installment and would absolutely use my skin for new upholstery if we had to ask again. So when I say maybe we do the thing and keep breathing for another month, I'm not saying it because I think it's fine. I'm saying it because broke is still real even when the language around it's disgusting."

Nita took that in without softening. That was one of the problems and one of the mercies of her. If a sentence held up, she let it hold.

"I know broke is real," she said. "I've been paying for our dignity with borrowed money."

"I know."

"No. You know it now."

He accepted that because he had to. The apartment hummed. Somewhere in the building a toilet ran too long. He could hear the hall elevator shuddering in its shaft and imagined Mina's place, probably warmer, probably crowded with old containers under the sink and the orange blanket Leela trusted more than anything in this apartment right now.

"So what are you saying?" he asked. "Cancel and let them call it instability? They love instability. They put a cardigan on it and call it growth."

"I'm saying there isn't a clean enough version of yes left."

"That isn't a plan."

"No."

She stepped into the room carrying the run-of-show and laid it on top of the fruit bowl so the pages draped over the oranges. For a second the divorce packet stayed hidden under both, a thick white edge just visible beneath civic optimism and participant alignment.

"They want the badge hook by the door visible," she said. "They wanted a close pass on the fridge because the sponsored produce suggests repaired routine. They wanted me to choose which of Leela's books made her look curious but not lonely. They wanted one family-specific condiment, Ben. One. Like we're a hotel trying to seem ethnic enough for a wedding website."

He looked away. "I know."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

"Because the next part is this." Her voice stayed low. That made every word hit harder. "They asked for anecdote options about how we recovered after the school video. If we give them anything tomorrow, they will write the rest themselves. They will take your debt and call it pressure management. They will take my mother's money and call it intergenerational support. They will take Leela refusing and call it protected child privacy. They will take what happened on this floor and if they ever smell it, if they ever even get close enough to infer it, they'll call it evidence of continued adult bond under strain."

The room seemed to narrow around the table. Ben felt heat climb the back of his neck, not from embarrassment alone but from the sick accuracy of it.

"Don't do that," he said.

"Do what."

"Say last night like it's already theirs."

"That is exactly my point."

He stood up too fast, chair legs scraping. "I know what your point is."

"Then stop bargaining for a softer violation."

It should have made him defensive. Usually that tone from her lit every old fuse in his body. Tonight it hit something already burnt out. He thought of how many times he had made himself useful by translating ugliness into a version people could sit beside. At work, in the marriage, with school administrators, with co-op notices, with his own credit card debt, with the medication he kept promising was temporary. He had spent months trying to locate the sentence that would preserve the money, the apartment, Leela's route, Nita's patience, his own image of himself as a decent man in a bad system. The sentence kept moving because there wasn't one. There had probably never been one.

His phone buzzed again. This time he looked.

If attendance concern is emotional rather than logistical, support language can be supplied.

He laughed, actually laughed, and handed the phone to Nita. She read it and put it down like it was sticky.

"There," he said. "That's the whole religion."

"Yes."

He walked to Leela's room and stood in the doorway. The approved depth. The clean sightline. On the shelf, the elevator's silver-paper doors caught the hall light. He remembered helping her tape those on, both of them swearing at the cheap adhesive because it kept wrinkling. He remembered her small furious fingers, ink on the side of her hand, the way she refused help until she actually wanted it and then asked with absolute confidence as if asking were a kind of engineering.

From here the room did not look like hers. It looked like an ad for the kind of child who made adults feel optimistic about constrained futures.

Nita spoke behind him, quieter now. "If we cancel before they get here, they absorb it. They send concern. They send repair. They call us overloaded and valuable."

"If we do it clean, they absorb that too."

"Yes."

He turned back toward her. "So what, then?"

She did not answer right away. She crossed to the vent, braced one hand on the wall, and tugged the scent cartridge loose with more force than the housing probably allowed. Clear gel slicked across her fingers. She dropped the cartridge into the sink. The apartment did not change at once. Then the cedar thinned. The citrus broke. Slowly the other smells started climbing back: orange peel, branch water, radiator dust, the faint old onion breath from the hall, and under all of it the mineral drag of the kitchen tile where their bodies had been stupid and honest in the same minute.

It was not a good smell. It was theirs.

"I don't know the exact shape yet," she said. "I know there isn't a tolerable middle anymore."

Ben looked at the schedule, at the clothes laid out for them, at the open door to the room Leela had refused to lend. He felt the fear plainly then, stripped of theatrics. Salary. Apartment. Transit. The long fall into smaller rooms and manual life. But beneath the fear there was something cleaner than hope and meaner than courage. It was the simple knowledge that every polished version of tomorrow ended with them gone from themselves.

"All right," he said.

Nita wiped the gel from her fingers onto a dish towel already too nice for the job. "That is not agreement unless you mean it."

He thought of the message offering support language, of the fruit bowl hiding the divorce packet, of Leela putting her badge in her own pocket because it was hers. He thought of how useless his talent for smoothing suddenly felt.

"I mean," he said, and his voice surprised him by holding steady, "that whatever happens tomorrow can't happen in their words."

Neither of them moved after that. The apartment stood around them in its staged calm, rich and bare and waiting. Outside, another train took the curve beyond the tracks, metal screaming through the cold. Inside, the real smell kept returning.
