# Chapter 18

Nita had learned the laundromat's schedule before she learned the building's tenants. At four ten the big front-loaders downstairs dropped into spin, and the floor of her living room answered with a low shiver that ran through the chair legs and into her ankles. If Ben and Leela were on time, she usually felt that tremor a minute or two before she saw them through the taped window.

The apartment over Wash World had been advertised as a one-bedroom with family flexibility, which meant the back room could fit a full mattress if the door stayed half-open and the front room could fit a child on a folding pad if the child did not require dignity. The rent was lower than the co-op's monthly hemorrhage had been and higher than the place deserved. The toilet only flushed cleanly if she held the handle down and counted to four. The stove leaned a fraction to the left, enough that oil gathered at one edge of the pan unless she shoved a folded takeout menu under the front leg. The radiator hissed like something angry being talked down in another room. Everything she owned smelled faintly of detergent and hot coin metal.

She had not turned any of it into virtue. That would have been another performance, just with cheaper props.

Leela's folded mattress was propped against the wall for the afternoon so there was room to walk. Nita had spread the green blanket over it anyway, trying to shake out the habit of doing care work only once it became visible. A plastic set of three drawers stood beside it. Socks in the top, underwear in the middle, T-shirts at the bottom. That was the system. Not elegant. Not optimized. Just labeled in black marker on masking tape because if Leela asked where things were every ten minutes Nita would eventually snap, and if Nita snapped the whole arrangement would start to feel like punishment instead of logistics.

On the windowsill sat a mug holding two pencils, one pair of kid scissors, and the brass key to the apartment. The key still gave her a stupid little pulse of anger when she picked it up. After months of smart-lock tokens and access windows and household continuity scoring, what finally opened her front door was a bad copy from the hardware place on Roosevelt that had made three identical keys for twelve dollars cash and handed them over on a ring shaped like a cartoon slice of pizza.

Below her, the washers kicked into spin.

Nita went to the window. Dirty snow had collapsed into gray ridges along the curb. A delivery bike hissed through slush. Three floors down the laundromat's front sign blinked in a patient, half-dead way that made even its promise of cleanliness feel tired. Across the street, Ben and Leela turned the corner together.

They no longer moved with the brisk accidental authority the old route had given them. No optimized crossing signal winked them through traffic. No school badge on Leela's chest flashed them into priority. They waited with everyone else at the light, Ben with his shoulders hunched against the wet cold, Leela in the yellow coat that was getting short at the wrists. Her backpack was plain black now. The rectangle where the dual-zone badge had been clipped looked cleaner than the rest of the fabric, a ghost patch near the zipper.

Ben carried a canvas grocery bag and Leela carried the backpack herself. Good, Nita thought automatically, and hated the thought a second later. The child carrying her own bag was not proof of resilience. It was a nine-year-old carrying her own bag.

The buzzer had died the first week and the landlord's nephew had fixed it by explaining that he would fix it soon, so Nita went down the stairs before they could knock. The building smelled the same every day: bleach from the laundry, damp cardboard from somewhere in the hall, cumin and frying onions from the second floor, and the scorched electrical note of too many chargers sharing one outlet. Halfway down she passed a basket of unmatched socks outside 2B with a handwritten sign that said TAKE IF YOURS. Queens, she thought, had always understood mutual aid as an accusation.

By the time she reached the front door, Leela was already peering through the laundromat window.

"The third dryer from the end rattles harder than the others," Leela said as soon as Nita opened the door. "I think it has a shoe in it."

"Could be one of mine," Ben said. "I've had a rough month with footwear."

He tried a smile. It did not travel all the way up his face. The cold had reddened the skin around his eyes, and there was a fresh nick on his jaw where he had shaved too fast or with a bad mirror. He lifted the grocery bag a little.

"I've got her stuff," he said. "And the school envelope. Ms. Mendez put three sticky notes on it because she thinks paper only counts if it comes with attitude."

Leela snorted. "She licks the envelopes first. It's disgusting."

Nita stepped back to let them in from the sidewalk wind. "Come upstairs before my heat budget escapes under the door."

The stairs were narrow enough that Ben had to angle the grocery bag in front of him. Leela went first, counting landings under her breath the way she did when she was tired and trying not to show it. At the top she let herself into the apartment as if she had always done it, even though this was only the fourth week and only the second time she had used her own key without Nita standing beside her.

She went straight to the plastic drawers.

"Top is socks," she said.

"Top is socks," Nita said.

"I remembered."

"I know."

Ben set the grocery bag on the table. The table was a folding card table with one cracked corner and a leg that always threatened mutiny if somebody leaned too hard. Nita had jammed a coaster under it. Ben noticed. Of course he noticed. He noticed the same things he had always noticed when disaster entered a room through carpentry and appliances rather than through words.

He looked around once, taking in the narrow stove, the line of mugs on the sill, the mattress against the wall, the damp gloves hanging over a chair. He did not say the room was nice. He did not say they would figure it out. For the moment, that counted as tact.

"Blue folder in the backpack," he said. "Red notebook in the side pocket. One glove was wet so I stuck it on top instead of sealing it in with the papers. Also, apparently the school needs us to sign the temporary pickup form again because their manual override expires every week, which feels less like policy than revenge."

He slid the manila envelope out of the bag. Three yellow notes really were attached to it. Ms. Mendez's blocky handwriting filled each one in a different color marker:

SIGN BOTH PARENTS

RETURN MONDAY

PLEASE DO NOT STAPLE CASH

Nita made a sound that was not quite a laugh. "She thinks we're the kind of people who staple cash?"

"She thinks everyone is the kind of person who ruins paper on purpose."

"That seems sane."

Leela had the backpack open on the floor now, unloading with the stern attention she once gave to Lego instructions. Math folder. Red notebook. One paperback with a bent corner. Pencil pouch. A Ziploc of almonds twisted shut around too much air.

"Where does the charger live?" she asked without looking up.

"Here for now," Nita said.

"What does for now mean?"

"It means here until we buy another one."

"I bought another one," Ben said quickly. "It's in the side pocket. It was the cheap one at the pharmacy, so it's about four inches long and humiliating, but it works."

Leela found it and held it up between two fingers. "This is a baby charger."

"That's the charger your father could afford without insulting the cashier by putting it back."

Ben let out a breath through his nose. "Accurate, but brutal."

Leela considered the charger again. "Okay. It can stay here."

The approval in her voice hit Nita harder than any complaint would have. A child should not have to grant operational clearance to a cable.

She took the envelope and looked for a pen. The first one on the sill was dry. The second wrote, but only after she scribbled hard on the back of a grocery circular until the ink woke up. She signed where the line said Parent One. The label made her think of treaty houses and performance metrics and adult bodies reduced to numbered placeholders. She shoved the thought aside and handed the pen to Ben.

He signed Parent Two with the fast looping hand he used when he wanted the act of signing to look friendlier than it was.

"There," he said. "The republic survives."

"Don't start."

"I didn't finish."

Leela peeled off her coat and hung it on the chair instead of the hook. The chair dipped under the weight and caught itself. "Can I keep one toothbrush in each place," she asked, "or am I supposed to remember a wet toothbrush in my backpack forever?"

"One in each," Nita said.

"Already handled," Ben said.

He reached into the grocery bag again and set down a pharmacy pack with two cheap toothbrushes, blue and green. Then a tube of toothpaste. Then a packet of hair ties. Then, after a beat, a folded wad of bills.

"This is for the class photo thing," he said. "And for the crackers, because I used the last sleeve at my place and forgot to replace them before this morning."

Nita looked at the money, then at him. The bills were damp from his wallet. Tens, fives, singles. The exact kind of cash a person handed over when his bank balance did not permit the abstraction of generosity.

"You don't need to do a reimbursement audit every handoff."

"I know. I'm not auditing." He rubbed his thumb over the corner of one bill. "I'm trying not to turn your kitchen into the place where all the missing food gets explained by weather."

Under pressure he always wanted to soften the shape of shame until everyone in the room could sit on it without bruising. Even now, without the old apartment and without any system score floating above their heads, he reached for charm like a clean towel. Nita understood the reach well enough to resent it on sight.

"You ate crackers," she said. "You didn't embezzle pensions."

"Give me time."

Leela looked up from the backpack. "If I've got two toothbrushes, do I get two deodorants when I need deodorant?"

Ben coughed into his fist. Nita felt the laugh arrive before she could stop it.

"We're not advance-purchasing your puberty," she said.

"You should," Leela said. "You buy everything late."

That shut all three of them up for a second, because it was true in a way that reached beyond deodorant.

Ben broke the silence first. "You also have glue sticks in there," he said. "Before anybody accuses me of emotional underfunding."

Leela dug again and produced a two-pack. "Only purple ones."

"Purple turns clear."

"It says that. It doesn't always."

Nita tucked the money under the mug with the pencils. "Fine. Leave the cash."

Ben nodded once, as if they had concluded a minor treaty and both disliked the terms. He leaned against the table, careful of the bad leg, and glanced toward the green blanket over the folded mattress.

"How's she sleeping?"

He asked it plainly. No joke, no sideways packaging. That made it harder to answer.

"Like somebody sleeping over a room full of machines," Nita said. "First night she thought the dryers were thunder and came into the bed at two. Second night she slept through everything. Last night she kicked the wall so hard I thought we'd lost the plaster."

"I was testing vibration," Leela said.

"Of course you were."

"At mine she sleeps with the hall light on," Ben said. "The studio gets weird when the radiator shuts off. It sounds like someone thinking bad thoughts in the pipes."

Nita looked at him then, really looked. The collar of his coat had gone shiny at the edges. His hair wanted cutting. His face still had that open, trustworthy arrangement strangers gave their money and stories to before they had earned the right, but late winter had taken the easy padding off it. He looked older than he had in the co-op kitchen, and not in any way that made experience sound noble.

She wanted, suddenly and stupidly, to ask whether he had enough blankets in the studio. Instead she said, "Leela, put the wet glove on the radiator before it molds into a new species."

Leela obeyed. Then she paused, hand on the backpack zipper, and frowned.

"Do I keep the old badge?"

The question landed in the middle of the room with the quiet force of something heavy set down carefully.

"You don't have to wear it," Ben said.

"I know that."

She pulled the badge from the front pocket. The lanyard was gone. Just the plastic card, cloudy from use, with its old photo of her looking annoyed in a school polo she had already outgrown. The edges were gummy where she had peeled off the continuity sticker.

"It still opens the science lab," she said. "Probably."

"Then keep it if you want it for the science lab," Nita said.

"Not because of them."

"Not because of them."

Leela slid the card into the top drawer with the socks as if she were storing a broken but still interesting part.

Outside, a truck backed up down the block with a long electronic beep that used to be one more city sound and now, in the absence of all the old household prompts, sounded almost personal. Ben pushed away from the table.

"I should go before I become dinner infrastructure."

"You were never dinner infrastructure," Nita said. Then, because the sentence had come out closer to kind than she intended, she added, "Mostly because you forgot to buy dinner half the time."

"That sounds more like us."

Leela did not look up. "I'm making the elevator after homework. The laundromat is the basement."

"Why?" Ben asked.

She pointed at the floor with one glue stick. "Because it shakes."

He stood there a second longer, bag empty at his feet, as if there were still some official language he ought to supply before leaving. There was not. That was one of the few honest gifts of the new arrangement. Nobody was grading the handoff for warmth. Nobody needed the family to model continuity with well-managed faces.

At the door he said, "Text if the school changes anything."

"I'll."

"And if the charger dies, I kept the receipt. I think."

"Go home, Ben."

He gave Leela the kind of small salute he had used on her since preschool when he wanted to leave without making departure dramatic. She returned it with the glue stick.

Then he was on the stairs, his steps uneven because the second tread down from the landing tilted left. The building swallowed him one floor at a time.

Leela came to stand beside Nita at the window. Together they watched him step out under the blinking laundromat sign and turn toward his studio three blocks away, shoulders up against the cold. No route shimmered open for him. No transit credit cut in. He just waited for the light to change and then crossed when the regular crowd crossed.

"Do elevators need two doors," Leela asked, "or can the basement just be a flap?"

Nita thought of every sealed threshold that had once arranged their life for them, every door that had opened because the right badge was near enough, every door that had stayed politely closed when the system decided they belonged elsewhere.

"For this one," she said, "a flap is fine."

Leela nodded as if that settled an engineering dispute. She went back to the table, pulled the red notebook toward her, and began to draw the shaft.

The dryers below kicked again. The glasses on the sill trembled once and held.

***

Ben's studio was narrow enough that every evening began with a choice about what kind of failure he could tolerate. If he left the bed down, he could sit on it and eat over his chest like a man in temporary trouble. If he folded it into the wall, he got back a strip of floor, the milk crate, the chair, and the temporary illusion that trouble had signed a lease and become decor.

He folded the bed up.

The latch caught on the second try. The room immediately became more honest. One narrow closet with no door. One window looking at a brick wall painted with old water stains. One hot plate on top of a cabinet that had once held office files and now held his socks, a pan, three bowls, and the mug full of soy sauce packets he had been hoarding because paying for a whole bottle still felt faintly immoral. The bathroom was a pocket behind a folding door that never shut unless he kicked the frame with his heel.

Leela had texted from Nita's phone at five nineteen.

bring the small screwdriver

and if you have eggs

not a lot just enough

Nita's message had come thirty seconds later.

I've got rice. If you're coming, bring the screwdriver, eggs, and whatever passes for a vegetable in your current life.

He had stared at the screen longer than the words required. It was not an invitation. It was groceries plus hardware. Still, in the new economy of their separate lives, groceries plus hardware counted as being expected somewhere.

He checked the fridge. Four eggs, one half-cabbage wrapped in a damp towel, two scallions gone limp at the roots, and the last spoonful of chili crisp Mina had pushed on him after the move with the expression of a woman who did not approve of him but approved even less of him eating plain food. He packed all of it into a tote with the small screwdriver, a handful of washers, and the tape measure Leela had forgotten last time.

On the walk to Nita's, the neighborhood gave him back his correct size. Nobody waved him through anything. The crosswalk timer made him wait like everybody else. He had once spent weeks furious at that kind of ordinary delay because the optimized route had trained his body to accept convenience as a right and interruption as persecution. Now the wait felt half insult, half cleansing. The city no longer cared whether he got to the next block on schedule. The city had gone back to not knowing his name.

It also meant he got splashed by a bus at the corner and had no one to blame but weather.

By the time he reached the laundromat building his cuffs were wet. He climbed the stairs with the tote banging his knee and could already hear Leela talking at full engineering volume through the door.

"If the counterweight is lighter than the car, that isn't a bug," she was saying. "That's gravity getting to win."

Nita opened the door before he knocked. She wore an old gray sweater with the sleeves shoved to the elbow and her hair caught back in one of the black pharmacy ties he had brought over at the handoff. There was flour on one wrist. Not enough to mean baking. Enough to mean she had been using the counter for more than paperwork.

"You're dripping," she said.

"The bus had opinions."

"Shoes off, then."

He toed them off by the door. The apartment smelled different at dinnertime than it had at handoff. Less like new defeat. More like onion, warm rice, and the sweet chemical soap-breath rising through the floorboards from the laundromat below. Leela had claimed the table completely. Two detergent boxes stood upright as the sides of an elevator shaft. A strip of cardboard marked floors in black marker: ROOF, 3, 2, 1, BASEMENT. A yogurt cup full of washers sat at her elbow like treasure.

"You brought the hardware," she said, relieved enough not to hide it.

"I brought eggs and the illusion of structural integrity."

"Good. I don't need actual integrity yet."

Nita took the tote from him and peered inside. "You found a vegetable."

"A cabbage is technically several vegetables held together by stubbornness."

"That tracks."

She handed him the tote back and turned toward the stove. He saw then that the paper divorce packet was wedged behind the sugar jar on the shelf over the counter, edges curling a little in the damp heat. It had survived the walkout, the move, the last few weeks of forms and calls and people who wanted to know where the child would be on which night. It sat there with the rice and the tea like a document nobody loved but nobody could afford to misplace.

He looked away before the sight of it could turn into anything theatrical.

"Wash those scallions," Nita said. "And sign the permission slip while your hands are still clean."

She slid the school form across the counter without looking at him. He signed where the line waited, then weighted the paper under the sugar jar again. Nita had already signed. Her name angled harder on cheap paper than it ever had on glossy proxy documents. He liked that about it and hated that he noticed.

The sink was too small for the cabbage. He had to tear leaves off and rinse them in sections. Behind him, Nita scraped cold rice into a pan with a wooden spoon. The stove clicked twice before the burner caught. Leela kept up a running explanation of the elevator's design flaws, pausing only when she needed both hands for tape.

"The problem is the car tips if the string rubs the corner," she said.

"That sounds less like a problem and more like a design note," Ben said.

"It's a problem if the passenger is soup."

"Fair."

"The passenger isn't soup," Nita said. "The passenger is probably a Lego man."

"Lego men still deserve standards."

Ben chopped scallions on the plastic board Nita had balanced over half the sink. The board slipped once and his knuckles brushed hers as she reached for the oil. Their hands knew each other too quickly. Just skin, a brief dry catch of contact, but his body answered before the rest of him could file the moment properly. He felt the memory of the kitchen floor from weeks ago in one sharp useless pulse, her mouth against his shoulder, the cold tile under his knee, the morning after hard as a judge.

He pulled his hand back first.

"Sorry."

"It's a small kitchen," Nita said.

She did not sound angry. She sounded exact, which was worse for him because exactness still meant she was in the room with him, not somewhere safer behind performance.

He tipped the chopped scallions into the pan. The oil answered with a quick spit. Nita added cabbage. He cracked eggs into a bowl and beat them with a fork that bent slightly under pressure.

"Did you get more tape?" Leela asked.

"In the tote."

"The good kind?"

"No," Nita said before he could answer. "The kind your father and I can buy this week."

Leela rummaged and produced a cloudy roll. "This isn't the good kind."

"Then use it on the ugly parts," Ben said.

"There are only ugly parts right now."

"Excellent. Then the tape is perfectly matched."

That got a quick grin out of her, sudden and sideways. Ben felt the room loosen by half an inch.

He poured the eggs into the pan. They ran around the cabbage in yellow streams and caught on the rice. Nita handed him the soy sauce packets without comment. He tore them one by one and squeezed brown lines over dinner like a man fueling a machine too old to warrant fresh parts.

"You know," he said, "there was a time in our lives when this would have counted as rock bottom."

"No," Nita said. "There was a time in our lives when this would have counted as content."

He laughed once, because that was true enough to sting.

Leela had moved to the floor now, holding the cardboard shaft upright between her knees while the tape set. "Can I keep the screwdriver here?" she asked.

"Why here?" Ben said.

"Because the basement is here."

"That's not how tool distribution works," Nita said.

"Then buy another screwdriver."

"With what."

The question came out of Nita too fast, stripped of all cushioning, and the room tightened at once. Ben saw her hear herself a second too late. Leela froze with the tape in her hand.

He stirred the rice. "I'll get another screwdriver," he said. "Not tonight, but soon."

"You don't have to do that," Nita said.

"I know. I said I'll do it."

For a beat she looked as if she might argue anyway, because fighting the offer was easier than receiving it. Then the pan demanded attention and she turned back to the stove.

"Fine," she said. "Then buy one with a handle she can't destroy in a week."

Leela relaxed enough to snip tape with her teeth.

They ate at the card table because there was nowhere else to eat. Nita on the folding chair. Ben on the milk crate. Leela cross-legged on the mattress she had pulled down halfway and used as a bench. The fried rice was mostly cabbage and egg with the memory of chili crisp moving through it like gossip. It was hot, salty, cheap, and enough.

Outside, somebody shouted in Spanish down on the sidewalk. Downstairs the dryers turned over with the regular muscular thump that had become the apartment's pulse. The window sweated a little in one corner where the tape seal had failed.

Ben watched Leela eat for a minute before he let himself start. Since the move she attacked dinner with the concentration of a child who had understood exactly how many kitchens now stood between hunger and being fed. Fork up, rice gone, question ready.

"If the basement is laundry," she said, "does that mean the roof should be pigeons?"

"Whose pigeons?" Ben asked.

"Any pigeons."

"No pigeons," Nita said. "The project is already structurally dishonest."

"That's rude to my project."

"Your project is held together with pharmacy tape and revenge."

"Then it's from Queens."

This time Nita laughed too, short and surprised enough that Ben had to look down at his plate. Not because the laugh meant anything large. Because it did not. Because it was only a laugh over cheap rice and cardboard, and because a part of him still reached toward that sound like an idiot dog who had once been fed at this table and hoped the rules might briefly revert.

He did not reach. He kept eating.

Leela pointed her fork at the notebook beside her plate. "The next one is taller. It needs another floor and maybe a person who pulls from the top if the string gets stuck."

"That isn't an elevator," Ben said. "That's a small labor dispute."

"It still moves people."

"She has a point," Nita said.

"I'm worried by how often that's true."

Leela swallowed and wiped her mouth with the back of her hand before either adult could stop her. "At school the real elevator lies."

Nita looked up. "How."

"It dings before it's all the way there. So people think they can go."

Ben felt, not for the first time, that his daughter had been issued a plain child's face over a temperament that had already grown calluses in all the wrong places.

"Then your elevator doesn't have to lie," he said.

"It has to say if it's stuck."

"That seems fair."

"And the basement door can be a flap because nobody important uses the basement."

Nita set down her fork. "That isn't true."

The correction was quick, almost reflexive. Ben saw the rest of the sentence build behind her eyes. The machines were downstairs. The laundry was downstairs. The place where soap, heat, and coin labor kept everyone's clothes from smelling like defeat was downstairs. The old world had run on hidden floors and called them support.

Leela looked between them. "I meant nobody dressed nice."

"Still not true," Nita said, gentler now. "Just draw the flap."

They finished eating. Ben scraped the last of the rice from the pan into a plastic container for Nita's lunch or Leela's snack or whoever got hungry first tomorrow. That was one of the useful things about scarcity. Leftovers stopped pretending they had owners and started answering whoever opened the fridge at the right hour.

When he carried the plates to the sink, Nita was already there, sleeves shoved higher, running water in a thin cautious stream so the drain did not panic.

"I can do these," he said.

"I know."

"That wasn't me trying to earn points."

"I know that too."

He dried one plate with the dish towel and realized halfway through that it was one of Leela's old T-shirts cut into a square. Of course it was. Nothing in the room had escaped reassignment.

"We're better at this part than I expected," he said quietly.

Nita rinsed the spoon, shook it once, and set it in the rack. "No," she said. "We're willing to do this part. That's not the same thing."

There it was. The clean cruel precision he had once hated in fights and once relied on in meetings and still, despite himself, trusted more than comfort. He nodded because there was nothing else honest to do.

"Okay," he said.

From the table Leela called, "I need both of you."

They went.

The elevator shaft stood between two stacks of paperback books to keep it from leaning. Leela had tied the string through a hole she had punched with the screwdriver. The car itself was a cut-down tea box with one side reinforced by a library card she had decided was too old to matter. A yogurt cup full of washers hung behind it as the counterweight.

"It goes up," she said, "but only if someone stops it from scraping."

"Show us."

She loaded the car with a green eraser, then pulled the string. The tea box climbed two floors, tilted, and jammed at 3 with a complaint of cardboard on cardboard.

"See," she said.

Ben crouched on one side. Nita knelt on the other. The laundromat's next spin cycle rose through the floor under all three of them, a steady industrial tremor like a second pulse.

"Try again," Nita said.

Leela pulled. Ben kept one finger against the back corner of the car so it stayed square. On the other side Nita lifted the flap of the second-floor door just enough that the box did not catch.

The car moved. Not smoothly. Not elegantly. It shuddered past 2, hesitated at 1, dropped half an inch, then found its line again and made it to the basement.

Leela's whole face changed.

"Okay," she said softly. "Okay, that's better."

Ben stayed crouched there a second longer than the elevator required. Across the cardboard shaft Nita's hand was still braced at the door, flour caught pale in the lines of her knuckles. The divorce packet sat on the shelf behind her. His studio waited three blocks away with its folded bed and his one chair and tomorrow's cold sink. Nothing in the room suggested repair. The arrangement was expensive in time, ugly in places, and one missed paycheck away from fresh humiliation.

It was also theirs.

Leela set the eraser back in the car, thinking hard. "Next time bring more washers," she said. "It drops too fast with only four."

Ben looked at Nita. Nita was already reaching for a pencil.

"Bring six," she said.
