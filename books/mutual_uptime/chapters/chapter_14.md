# Chapter 14

Nita woke to the scrape of scissors on construction paper.

The apartment was still dark in the corners, radiator hissing like it had a private grievance with the wall. She had slept for maybe ninety minutes in two broken pieces. The browser tabs from last night were still open on her phone: the co-op assessment portal, Ben's card balance, Mina's transfer from three weeks ago with the note `For the windows`, as if a lie told in parentheticals counted less. The co-op envelope still pinned the two apartment listings where she'd left them, minus the scissors Leela had already taken from beside the stack. Her eyes found the kitchen table and stayed there.

Leela sat cross-legged on one of the dining chairs in her school leggings, blue glasses sliding down her nose, trimming blue arrows out of construction paper with the concentration of a bomb technician. Her fingers were streaked navy from a leaking marker. Ben stood at the counter in yesterday's T-shirt, peeling district logos off the edge of a cardboard trifold because Leela had pasted them on crooked and now wanted them "less sponsored."

"You should eat something before we go," Nita said.

Leela did not look up. "I had a banana."

"Half a banana," Ben said. "The rest became engineering material."

On the table beside the scissors was the Future Citizens Week packet from Sunnyside Interop Academy, printed on thin paper that had already fuzzed at the folds. FAMILY PARTICIPATION STRONGLY ENCOURAGED, the header said. Students will present a household system, service role, or civic routine that contributes to our shared future. A blue sidebar invited proxy households to offer "real-world examples of embodied civic trust." Sponsors' logos ran along the bottom in a neat row, small enough to look tasteful and large enough to pay for the glossy certificates. Gemini Civic had one. Claude Atlantic had one. So did three banks, a nutrition brand, and a transit insurer whose mascot looked like a happy subway card.

"Let me hear it once more," Nita said.

Leela stuck another arrow to the board. "You heard it."

"I heard one version."

"That was the version for when you were brushing your teeth."

Ben turned, holding a peeled sponsor sticker in two fingers. "That sounds like there are multiple versions, which is not what anyone wants before eight in the morning."

Leela shrugged. "There are subheadings."

Nita could still feel last night's argument sitting in the room like dirty dishwater that had not gone down the drain. Ben's debt. Her money from Mina. The ugly intimacy of exact numbers. They had stopped fighting only because Leela had flushed the toilet at 12:14 and both of them went quiet at once, the way people did when they remembered the child in the next room had her own ears and no exit. This morning required ordinary clothes, ordinary breakfast, ordinary school faces. Nita hated how quickly adulthood demanded costume changes.

"Subheadings are fine," Ben said. "We love subheadings. We love orderly civic expression. We just want to make sure none of them end with me getting called into a conference room."

Leela finally looked at him. "Then don't sit near the door."

Ben's shoulders hitched once. Nita dropped her eyes to the board before the laugh could get loose.

Nita reached for the board. Leela let her take it, which worried her more than refusal would have. The title, in block letters cut from orange paper, read MY FAMILY HANDOFF ROUTE. Beneath it were a drawing of their building, a drawing of the school, and three arrows in different colors. There was nothing obviously dangerous there. There were stickers of buses and one badly drawn pigeon that had to be Pilar's influence.

"This is fine," Nita said, which in her own mouth meant only that disaster had not yet announced its preferred shape.

Leela slid her red notebook into her backpack.

"Do you need that?" Nita asked.

"It's my notes."

"You have notecards."

"The notecards are the short notes. That's why they're called that."

Ben set a plate down in front of Leela. Toast cut into four squares, peanut butter dragging in pale stripes. "Eat two and I'll pretend we live in a civilization."

Leela ate one square because her father was standing there and because refusing now would create a delay. Nita watched her chew and hated the fact that she now calculated breakfast in the same mental column as school pickup, bonus credits, debt minimums, and the apartment's latest prediction that "stability-forward households" saved 11 percent on winter utilities. Everything useful had become contaminated. Even peanut butter.

They walked the twelve-minute route because the route stack was running "normal crossing conditions" and not opening the preferential lights. Early winter in Sunnyside came with a hard white sky and a film of old salt along the curb that never quite melted. Parents in puffers and office coats moved toward the school with poster boards, cupcakes, plastic trays of fruit, and the patient expression of people prepared to clap for children before going back to work. Leela wore her dual-zone badge on its lanyard under her coat, though the metal edge still clicked against the zipper with every step. She always tucked it in when she was anxious, as if the badge worked better close to the ribs.

At the school entrance a hand-painted banner drooped over the doors: FUTURE CITIZENS WEEK. The paint had bled in one corner where someone had carried it through wet weather, and a second-grader's paper skyline peeled loose from the cinderblock wall underneath. The schoolishness of it was almost aggressive. Snow boots lined the hallway below a bulletin board full of self-portraits. A volunteer grandmother in a knitted Mets hat tried to pour coffee from an urn that was losing the fight. Marker smell sat on top of floor wax and damp wool. The sponsorship only arrived on top of that, in the form of QR codes taped beside student art and table tents that said this refreshment station was made possible by CivicHarvest Snack Cubes.

Deshawn stood near the classroom door helping his daughter knot the belt of a silver puffer that kept twisting backward. He had clean work boots on and a thermos with a faded stingray sticker. His face looked more tired than ever, the night-shift gray under the eyes still there, but his daughter's braids had fresh green beads on the ends and he had clearly redone them this morning with the care of a man who refused to send a child into public looking half-finished.

He straightened when he saw them. For one second Ben looked as if he might reverse direction down the hall.

"Morning," Deshawn said, plain and not unkind. "Coffee's bad enough to qualify as chemistry. Don't let them fool you."

"Thanks," Ben said. "Useful intelligence."

Deshawn glanced at Leela's board. "That looks serious."

"It's about the route," Leela said.

"Everything's about the route in this building." He handed his daughter a clementine from his coat pocket. "Maya, not in class. I mean it."

Maya rolled her eyes with the full-body despair only fourth-grade girls and saints could manage. "I know."

Nita saw Ben absorb the whole exchange like a bruise pressed on from the outside. There was not time to manage that too. Ms. Cepeda appeared in the doorway with a clipboard and a peppermint in one cheek, her mustard cardigan hem chewed into threads by chair wheels or life.

"Welcome, welcome, come in, find your tiny seats," she said in the exhausted sing-song of an adult who had already repeated the same sentence twenty-seven times that morning. "We're running a little behind because one of the projectors decided citizenship was optional."

The classroom had been rearranged for parent attendance. Student desks were pushed to the sides. Tiny blue plastic chairs formed three rows in the middle. At the back, a table held supermarket cookies, cut fruit going brown at the edges, and a school tablet on a charger that kept slipping out. On the board behind Ms. Cepeda, someone had lettered OUR HOUSEHOLDS, OUR FUTURE in bubble letters surrounded by paper gears and buses. Each child's project leaned against the wall: sanitation maps, nurse uniforms made of felt, a bakery family tree, an essay on "why crossing guards are civic heroes" illustrated with glitter.

Nita sat with her knees high and her coat folded over her lap. Ben ended up one chair away because that was how the rows fell, and neither of them made a joke about the distance. Leela took her place with the other students, board balanced against her shins, red notebook on top of it like an afterthought with teeth.

Three presentations came before hers. One boy explained his mother's work in municipal compost outreach and forgot the word "anaerobic" halfway through, so the whole class got to watch him wrestle it to the ground. A girl named Suri brought in her grandfather's taxi medallion and said it weighed "like a stupid necklace for a truck." The room laughed in the right places. Parents took photos because children on stools under classroom lights made ordinary adults stupid with love and boredom at the same time.

Then Ms. Cepeda smiled at Leela and said, "Next we have Leela Rao-Halpern, who is presenting on family route systems."

Leela carried the board to the front without visible nerves. That was one of the terrible things about her. She did not look brave because she did not yet know what deserved fear. She propped the trifold on the easel, set her red notebook beside it, and adjusted the school tablet until the first slide appeared. Nita saw the title MY FAMILY HANDOFF ROUTE in larger digital letters and exhaled once.

Leela began.

"Hi. This is my family handoff route. It's twelve minutes if the lights are normal and eight if the crossings decide to help."

A map of Sunnyside appeared. Their building, the school, three turns, two crosswalks. The class leaned in because maps made everything look like a game.

"This is our apartment building." She pointed with the capped end of a marker. "It has two bedrooms and a smart lock that likes marriage."

The adults laughed. It was soft and reflexive and almost affectionate. Ben's mouth twitched before he got it back under control.

"This is my school badge." Leela held it up. The laminated surface caught the fluorescent light. "It has two home permissions and one school permission. If the wrong adult checks me out, the office app makes the microwave noise."

More laughter. Ms. Cepeda smiled uncertainly.

"My mom works for Claude Atlantic. My dad works for Gemini Civic. They're physical proxies. That means they have to go places in person so the computers don't get blamed."

Two parents in the second row let out a late bark of laughter. Then a few adults turned in their chairs to measure Nita and Ben against the kind of parent the school brochure liked to photograph.

Leela turned a page in the notebook.

"In September my parents said they were getting divorced. That means not married anymore. They're still trying."

The room made a sound that was not laughter and not yet silence. Nita's hands went cold inside her sleeves.

"Leela," Ben said, too low for anyone but the first row.

Leela did not look at him. "This is a chart of how many times bags got packed." She lifted the board's side flap. Six blue bars, drawn with ruler precision. "This is a chart of how many times anyone actually moved out." A single red bar sat flat on the bottom like a joke told by a child who did not know what mercy was. "You can see there is a difference."

Some of the kids laughed because the graph looked funny. One of the fathers in the back raised his phone. Then another.

"Let's keep phones down, please," Nita said, already half standing.

Ms. Cepeda, alarm finally reaching her face, put out a hand without conviction. "Maybe just be present with the students, everybody."

Leela kept going.

"My mom looked at another apartment and it disappeared while she was brushing her teeth. My dad tried sleeping at Auntie Pilar's and the ride credits stopped, so he had to call from a laundromat with no chairs. We still use the same route because the route works even when the people on it don't."

The first row of adults stopped smiling. More phones came up.

"Leela," Nita said again, sharper now.

Leela looked over at her then, honestly puzzled. "You said to be factual."

A father by the coat hooks barked a laugh, and the woman with the baby smiled without lowering her phone.

Ben was already moving down the row, whispering apologies to knees and tote bags, reaching the back where a woman with a sleeping baby strapped to her chest was filming one-handed over a paper plate of supermarket cookies.

"Could you not?" he said. "Please. That's my daughter."

The woman blinked at him, surprised to be spoken to as a human instead of an audience. Glitter from some earlier volunteer activity clung to her wrist. "She's incredible."

"She's nine."

At the front of the room Leela held up another page from the notebook. It had times written in two columns, Claude in gray, Gemini in cobalt.

"Sometimes after a bad fight we get better groceries," she said. "Blood oranges after the kitchen one. Fancy yogurt after the bag one. Once we got sea-salt crackers and nobody liked them."

Now the room was silent except for one kid opening and closing his glue stick.

"The paper said to say how our household helps the future. I think my household helps by staying in the same arrangement even when it makes everybody mean. Because I'm part of the route and the route is useful. Thank you."

She finished, not triumphant, not upset, not even especially relieved. She gave the class the same small nod she gave when she finished a math problem on scratch paper.

A few parents started clapping. The rest joined for two beats and then let their hands fall while Nita was already at the front, taking the notebook from the easel so fast one of the binder rings clicked against her wedding band. On the school tablet, Leela's final slide still glowed: three arrows moving between apartment and school, none of them leaving the page.

In the back row, a parent hit replay.

* * *

By the time Ben got into the hallway, the clip had already acquired the greasy speed of something too neat to remain private.

He could see it happen hand to hand, not metaphorically but literally. A father in scrubs sent it to somebody and then turned his phone sideways to show a mother in a camel coat. The woman with the sleeping baby had tears in her eyes and kept saying, "I mean, it's devastating," without stopping the recording. Two kids from another class drifted over because adults were suddenly making the same face adults made near fender-benders.

Ben spent the first five minutes behaving like the version of himself everybody had spent years rewarding. He kept his voice warm and his palms out. You could reason with people if you made it easy for them to feel generous.

"I'd really appreciate it if you'd delete that."

"It's just for my sister."

"I know. I still need to ask."

"I'm not posting it publicly."

"Thank you. Please don't send it either."

A man in a fleece vest with a volunteer sticker peeling off his chest said, "This matters, though." He had a violin case by his leg and an expression that suggested he believed history would thank him for ordinary rudeness. "People should know schools are doing this."

"Schools are doing what?" Ben asked. He could hear himself getting brighter, which was the last warning sign before he got truly angry. "Letting a fourth grader talk about her family? Having folding chairs?"

The man's mouth twitched. "Come on."

"No, really. Come on to what?"

Nita emerged from the classroom with Leela and Ms. Cepeda. Leela had her coat zipped wrong. One blue mitten trailed from its clip. She was not crying. Ben wished briefly, selfishly, that she were. Tears would have given the room instructions. Her dry calm left everybody free to project noble things onto her while her parents stood there holding the bill.

Ms. Cepeda touched Nita's elbow and then thought better of it. "I'm so sorry. I looked over the board. I swear I did."

"The board wasn't the problem," Nita said.

Her voice was quiet enough that people nearby leaned in. Ben knew that tone. It meant she was selecting words with blood already in her mouth.

"The notes," Ms. Cepeda said helplessly. "I thought they were notes."

"They were."

Leela looked up at Nita. "Did I use too much context?"

For one beat nobody answered. The fluorescent lights buzzed. Farther down the hall, a kindergarten class sang something about community helpers in voices too shrill to survive memory intact.

Nita knelt to fix Leela's zipper. Her hands were efficient and not gentle. "No. You used all of it."

"Was that wrong?"

Nita's fingers stopped on the zipper tab. Ben saw the whole miserable machinery of parenthood in that pause: tell the truth, protect the child, do not recruit her into your own shame, do not lie so hard she starts thinking reality itself is optional.

"It wasn't wrong," Ben said, stepping in because somebody had to. "It was just... public."

Leela considered that. "The assignment was public."

There it was again, the clean mechanical logic that made adults sound like drunks around her.

Assistant Principal Nwosu appeared from the office with a lanyard full of keys, a navy blazer over sneakers, and the face of a woman whose morning had just turned expensive. She was carrying a stack of participation certificates because schools never stopped being schools, not even during minor catastrophes.

"Mr. Halpern, Ms. Rao, could we step in here for one second?" she asked.

The office smelled like copier heat and old coffee. A student-made papier-mache globe occupied too much desk space. Leela sat in a plastic chair by the fish tank and pressed one finger against the glass while an orange molly ignored her. The tank's filter hummed louder than anyone's breathing. Ben took the chair closest to the door without meaning to. Nita remained standing.

Assistant Principal Nwosu folded the certificates into a neater stack she did not need. "First, I want to say Leela presented beautifully."

Nita laughed once. It had no amusement in it at all.

"Second, we're asking families in the hall not to circulate student videos. Ms. Cepeda is handling that now."

"Ask harder," Nita said.

"We can request. We can't seize devices."

"Then send a schoolwide notice."

"We're drafting one."

Ben's phone vibrated, then again, then twice more. He looked down because nerves were stronger than judgment. One notification came from a parent thread, another from the school-pickup thread, and a third from an unknown number. The first clip had already been cut tighter than the original. Someone had trimmed away the beginning map and left in the smart-lock line, the divorce line, the graph, the blood oranges. It was only forty seconds now. Forty seconds was perfect. Forty seconds fit inside a lunch break, inside outrage, inside affection, inside a sponsorship deck.

He locked the phone and put it face down on his thigh.

"There's also the media release," Assistant Principal Nwosu said carefully. "For open school events. It covers in-room capture for educational and community use."

"Community use," Nita repeated. "What an elastic little phrase."

"I know." The principal's voice softened. "I do know."

That made it worse somehow. Ben believed her. She looked like a woman who bought her own classroom tissues and had once cried in a supply closet over a district budget spreadsheet. None of that changed the fact that the room had done what rooms did now: turned a child's exactness into shareable proof before the adults nearest her had found their feet.

His phone buzzed again. This time it was a text from Deshawn.

`Maya has Leela's purple folder. Also tell her she didn't do anything wrong.`

Ben stared at the message until the letters blurred, then looked up to find Deshawn in the doorway holding the folder and a single-serve pack of cheddar crackers.

"Sorry," he said. "Maya grabbed the wrong thing."

Leela stood and took the folder from him. "Thanks."

Deshawn crouched enough to be eye level with her. "You hungry?"

She nodded after a second.

He handed her the crackers. "Maya won't eat orange food unless it's shaped like a moon, so these are safe."

Leela almost smiled. "They taste dusty."

"Yeah. That's school cuisine."

Ben felt the old stairwell from chapter seven flash through him like a light shone under a door. It wasn't desire, not exactly and not right now. It was the humiliating fact of being seen by a person who knew too much and still offered crackers because children had to eat.

Deshawn stood and looked at Ben, not searching, not forgiving. "Hall's a mess. Want me to walk her to the library corner while you deal with this?"

Nita answered before Ben could. "Please."

Leela hesitated. "Am I in trouble?"

"No," Nita said.

"Then why can't I stay?"

"Because we need five stupid adult minutes."

Leela absorbed that. "Okay."

She went with Deshawn, crackers tucked under her arm with the folder, red notebook clamped flat against her chest. Assistant Principal Nwosu watched her go, pressed her lips together, and let the breath out slowly.

"We'll send the notice," she said.

"It won't matter," Ben said.

He checked his phone again because not checking had become impossible. The clip was already outside the school threads. Somebody had posted it to a borough account that specialized in transit failures and public-system absurdities. The caption read: NINE-YEAR-OLD EXPLAINS MORE ABOUT PROXY LABOR THAN CITY HALL EVER HAS. Views climbed while he watched: 3,200. 4,100. 5,900.

Nita read over his shoulder. "Oh, for fuck's sake."

That got Assistant Principal Nwosu's full attention, because profanity from controlled parents always sounded like a structural problem.

"We need to get ahead of it," Ben said.

"With what?" Nita asked. "A more tasteful version of our daughter's humiliation?"

"With context."

"She already provided context. That is the entire problem."

The office phone rang. Nobody moved to answer it for three beats. When the secretary finally picked up at the front desk, her murmur was low and tense. Ben could hear the words `yes, she's a student` and `no comment` and then, somehow worse, `please email the district office`.

By dismissal the school steps had the look of a place something bad had happened that did not qualify as an emergency. Parents kept glancing over while pretending not to. Two older kids reenacted "the smart lock that likes marriage" for each other near the bike rack, and one of them laughed hard enough to hiccup. Leela heard. He saw her hear. She did not ask what was funny. She just tugged her badge farther under her coat and said, very quietly, "Does it still work tomorrow?"

Nita was on the phone with Mina by then, speaking in the clipped logistical register that meant fear had decided to dress as competence.

"Tonight," she said. "If you can. No, she's fine. No, she's not fine. I know. I know. Please."

Ben stood beside her with his own phone going hot in his hand. The parent-thread numbers looked ridiculous now. Forty-six thousand views. Someone had added captions. Someone else had mirrored it. Gemini's cobalt icon flashed at the top of his screen with a meeting request for 6:00 p.m. Claude Atlantic's pale gray icon had already hit Nita's phone twice. Neither message mentioned Leela by name in the preview. That, more than anything, made him feel sick.

Mina arrived in twenty minutes, carrying a reusable grocery bag full of cut guava in a takeout container and a paperback she had been meaning to finish since October. She wore a camel coat with a dry cleaner's safety pin still clinging to one cuff and looked at both of them once, thoroughly, before she hugged Leela.

"I brought socks," she said, because that was how her kind of love moved through the world: never empty-handed, never theatrically.

"I don't need socks," Leela said.

"Then you can disrespect them later."

That got the smallest real smile of the day.

Mina fell into step beside them for the walk home. Leela looked from her to Nita. "Should I bring the notebook tonight?"

Nita answered too fast. "Yes."

So the notebook would go too. Of course it would. That little red record had already become more valuable to everyone than it had any right to be.

* * *

By the time Mina took Leela after dinner, the apartment felt wrong with the overnight bag gone from the hall and Leela's shoes no longer tipped sideways by the radiator.

Nita came back in first and left her coat on because taking it off would have implied she planned to rest. The hum of the appliances sounded louder than usual, opportunistic. Somebody in the building was frying onions. Ben shut the door, checked the smart lock out of pure superstition, then checked it again. She put the kettle on and never lit the burner. He stood with his phone in one hand and his visitor sticker still on his sweater, curled and filthy at the edges.

For forty-five minutes neither employer called directly. They staged the room with materials.

Claude Atlantic dropped a calendar hold titled REPUTATIONAL ALIGNMENT TOUCHPOINT. Gemini sent a packet called FAMILY TRUTH RESPONSE KIT, then replaced it twelve minutes later with a cleaner version and a brighter cover image, as if ethics lived in saturation. There were talking points, posture notes, and one line Nita had to read twice because rage had made her stupid for half a second: preserve the child's uncoached observational register.

Ben opened the Gemini packet at the table. "Jesus Christ."

"Keep going."

He did. "'Do not overcorrect into apology. Audiences respond to pressure acknowledged with warmth. Preserve lived texture. Avoid defensive legal phrasing. Preferred framing: a working family navigating visible complexity.'"

Nita leaned both hands on the counter. The laminate was cold and slightly tacky where the surface had worn down near the edge. "Visible complexity. That's what they call a kid getting laughs on the divorce slide."

His phone buzzed again. A voice note this time, not from management but from Pilar.

"Put her on speaker," Nita said.

Ben did. Wind hit first, then pigeons, then Pilar's voice, flat as a wrench laid on concrete.

"You two alone?"

"Leela's with Mina," Ben said.

"Good. Don't confuse embarrassment with damage. Gemini's comms people are passing that clip around like somebody found a saint in homeroom."

Nita closed her eyes.

"How bad?" Ben asked.

"For you? Bad. For them? Your kid looked honest, poor enough to hurt, and funny by accident. That's catnip." A wet irritated coo sounded in the background. Pilar muttered at it in Spanish, then came back. "And if either stack asks for the notebook, the answer's no. If they ask twice, the notebook is suddenly somewhere nobody can inventory. Don't let anyone with a badge near your mother's buzzer."

"They're not getting the notebook," Nita said.

"Good," Pilar said. "Keep one thing." The line clicked dead.

Claude Atlantic finally called.

Saskia appeared on the kitchen tablet from what looked like a hotel room or a room designed to resemble one. The room behind her had a pale wall, a controlled lamp, and no visible clutter. She was in a cream blouse that should have looked soft and instead looked expensive. Her face was immaculate, but when she shifted the camera Nita saw one bare heel wrapped in a square of blister padding, white against the carpet. The damage was human and discreetly managed.

"Nita," Saskia said. "Ben."

Ben gave a short nod from the table. He had no role in the call and knew it.

"The clip has crossed three major municipal feeds and at least two labor-law mirrors," Saskia said. "Sentiment is sympathetic. The relevant point is that Leela reads as precise."

"Precise," Nita repeated.

"Uncoached. Specific. Credible."

Ben laughed, abrupt and ugly. "That's one way to say our daughter told a room full of parents we can't get divorced."

Saskia turned her head a fraction. "Mr. Halpern, I'm not recommending celebration. I'm recommending control."

There it was again, the thing Nita had once found almost beautiful: the narrowing, the smooth reduction of catastrophe into posture and sequence. She could feel, even now, the old dangerous relief of listening to somebody who refused to panic in complete sentences. Then Saskia chose the next sentence carefully enough to make Nita hate her.

"Claude can put educational privacy counsel on retainer tonight," she said. "We can also dispatch a secure courier to image the notebook from its current location before school tomorrow. If local media begin hovering at pickup, we would prefer Leela not attend in person. I need your authorization by eight-thirty."

No one spoke for a beat. The refrigerator motor kicked on. Somewhere upstairs a chair leg scraped.

"No courier is touching her notebook," Nita said.

"Then I need an attendance decision."

"You don't get one tonight."

"Support is time-bound."

Ben's cobalt phone chirped. He looked down, then pushed it across the table so Nita could see. Gemini had turned the packet into a live prompt.

`FAMILY CALIBRATION SESSION 8:30 P.M.`

`Confirm parental participation`

`Confirm child artifact access`

`Temporary continuity-resilience bonus: +$420 equivalent`

`Activate educational privacy retainer`

Nita stared at the words `child artifact access` until they blurred.

"They're bidding on her notebook," Ben said.

"They are pricing urgency," Saskia said.

"That's the same sentence in a nicer dress."

Saskia ignored that. "If you refuse coordination, don't pursue public takedown language tonight. A panic response would frame the child as mistaken rather than exact, and the exactness is what currently protects household trust."

Nita gripped the counter harder. "My daughter is not a trust asset."

"No," Saskia said. "She's evidence. That's why speed matters."

The line sat there between them like something with a pulse.

"We're not available for content," Nita said.

"Refusal is easiest before compensation posts," Saskia said, and signed off.

The kitchen went ugly and small again. Ben sat down hard enough to rattle the silverware tray. Nita stayed standing because if she sat she might not get back up.

"Compensation," Ben said.

"Mm."

"They're not even hiding it."

Her phone buzzed with a photo from Mina. Leela sat at the little table in her grandmother's kitchen, socks on after all, blue glasses pushed up in her hair, doing math homework beside the takeout container of guava. Mina's hand was in the corner of the frame holding a mug. On the table, because of course, was the red notebook.

Nita called immediately.

"Ma," she said when Mina picked up. "If anybody from school, Claude, Gemini, or something pretending not to be any of those buzzes, you don't open the door."

Mina did not waste time asking why. "Fine."

"Keep the notebook there. Keep her badge there too."

"Already in the kitchen drawer."

That almost took Nita's knees out. "Good."

"Do you need me to keep her tomorrow?"

Nita looked at the Gemini prompt still glowing on Ben's screen, at the kitchen tablet gone black, at the co-op balance still open on her own phone under Mina's photo.

"Maybe," she said. "I'm not giving them the answer tonight."

"Then don't." Mina hung up the way older women did when a sentence had already become sufficient care.

Three minutes later the apartment buzzer sounded.

Ben flinched so hard his chair feet squealed on the tile. He looked at Nita. She looked at the wall panel. Building services had already accepted the drop.

When he opened the door, two branded deliveries were waiting on the mat. Gemini had sent a fruit basket wrapped in cobalt ribbon: pears, blood oranges, grapes polished to a fake shine. Claude Atlantic had sent linen spray and a thick card about composure in periods of increased visibility. Tucked between them was a slim household-services slate already awake.

`Authorize in-home documentation consultation by 10:00 p.m.`

`Priority artifact intake available`

`Priority benefit value: +$420 equivalent`

Ben picked up the slate. His thumb hovered over the bright confirm field.

"Ben."

"It's four hundred and twenty dollars and a privacy lawyer."

"It's first crack at her."

He looked from the slate to Mina's photo of Leela bent over fractions in borrowed socks. His visitor sticker was still curled on his sweater. The fluorescent light showed every bad angle in his face.

"I know," he said.

The slate asked `Are you sure?` after he pressed decline, as if certainty were a customer-service preference. He pressed it again, harder.

The refusal registered. Then the wall panel by the fridge lit anyway.

In response to increased public visibility, your continuity bundle has been upgraded to Resilient Family Plus. New monthly benefit value: +$420 equivalent.

Ben let out one dry, bitter laugh. Nita set the composure card by the microwave without reading the rest, with the fruit basket crowding one side and the live slate on the other still offering privacy counsel and priority artifact intake in its bright obedient type. The systems had translated the afternoon into groceries and a number before Leela had even finished her homework at Mina's. Somebody had already assigned a value to their daughter's handwriting.
