# Chapter 17

By dusk the bakery windows were gray again. The dust had laid itself over the glass in a fine, patient skin, as if the town had not already spent the afternoon pawing through Cora Vale's life and pricing it by the lot. Hester stood at the stove with her apron tied too loose at the waist, stirring onions through bacon grease while a pan of cabbage buns browned in the oven. Every few minutes the spoon stopped. She bent, coughed into a square of linen, folded the linen shut, and went on.

Thea had stopped pretending not to see.

"Let me take that," she said when the coughing fit finally forced Hester's palm flat against the stove ledge.

"And have you stir onions like a woman punishing them for their sins?" Hester straightened. Her eyes watered, but the look she gave Thea was dry as old wood. "No."

She always pocketed the blackest crust for herself, as if bitterness were a private taste she did not care to explain. Tonight the burnt heel of one loaf already sat wrapped in a scrap of wax paper by her elbow, waiting for later. Above her left ear, two hairpins crossed in a slanted X. Thea had never once seen them match.

"You can't keep coughing that way."

"Watch me." Hester gave the onions another turn. "Slice the cabbage finer. Dell chews like he's racing somebody."

The kitchen behind the shop was hot from baking and close with yeast, coffee grounds, flour, and the bitter syrup Hester kept for her chest in a brown bottle by the lamp. A damp towel covered the bowl of risen dough. The shelf above the sink held the sealed dust jars in a crooked row, their glass gone amber in the weak light, each lid ringed with old wax. Thea tried not to look at them for long. Since the auction she had not wanted any surface in Morrow to remember another thing.

She cut the cabbage on the scarred board, working the knife through the pale curls, and listened to the town outside settle into its boarded, emptied evening. A truck changed gears on Main and faded. A screen door rasped somewhere down the block. Then only wind against the alley and the faint rattle of cooling tins.

"Will she come?" Hester asked.

Thea knew which she meant. She kept her eyes on the knife. "She said yes."

"And Del?"

"He was with her."

Hester snorted once, not quite a laugh. "That boy has been with her since he was put in her arms, and half the trouble is that neither of them has ever known where one of them ended and the other started."

Thea slid the chopped cabbage into the waiting bowl. She wanted to ask whether Hester thought that had changed today, with Ezra's letter spoken out loud before half the town, with August Poeschel standing there to hear his own work named for what it was, with Cora asking for the truth even though it cost her the last fiction she had been able to live inside. She wanted to ask whether Hester thought any of them could still be repaired, or whether survival at this point only meant learning how to travel damaged.

But Hester hated questions that wanted blessing disguised as wisdom.

So Thea said, "The dough's ready."

"I can see that."

Hester dusted the table, then stood with both hands braced on the edge before she trusted herself to lift the bowl. The movement was small. The fatigue in it was not. Under the kitchen heat her face had gone the flat color of bread dough. When she drew breath, the air seemed to catch somewhere low and have to be hauled the rest of the way up by force.

Thea moved closer anyway. "At least let me fill them."

"You can fill them. If you overstuff them, I'll throw the lot in the alley."

That was permission. Thea took it.

They worked without grace and without needing any. Thea pinched dough into rounds, spooned in cabbage and onions and the stingy shreds of salt pork Hester had saved for the occasion, then folded the edges shut. Hester moved along behind her, turning each seam under with the side of her thumb, swift and exact even now.

"You're nervous," Hester said.

"You can tell from my bread?"

"I can tell from the way you keep listening for the door."

Thea almost denied it. That was still one of the oldest habits in her, to take her own feeling and dress it in grammar until it looked manageable. Instead she set another bun on the tray and said, "I don't know what she'll want from me tonight."

"Probably the truth. Just not all of it at once."

"That's one of your sayings, not advice."

"No." Hester leaned one hip against the table until the wave passing through her chest eased. "My sayings are prettier. Advice is uglier and more useful."

Thea smiled despite herself. It felt strange on her face after the day.

When the first knock came, it was Dell's way of knocking: one hard rap, then two quicker ones after it, as if the first had merely announced that the rest were serious. Hester was already reaching for the oven door. Thea wiped her hands on her skirt and went through the shop.

Cora stood in the back entry with dusk on her shoulders and Dell half behind her, tall enough now that he filled the narrow doorway even slouched. Auction dust had dried into the hem of Cora's skirt and gone pale in the blunt ends of her cut hair. She still held herself like a woman braced against weather, but there was no house left to defend in the set of her body now, only habit. Before she even looked fully at Thea, she bent to push the loose strip of screen back into the frame where it had sprung near the latch. She could not cross a threshold without mending something if there was anything to mend.

Dell had a carpenter's pencil behind his right ear and a folded newspaper timetable sticking out of his back pocket, the edge gone soft from being handled. His left forearm lay bound and awkward against his middle. He had tried to wash up, but dust still sat in the seams beside his nose and in the whorl at the temple where his hair refused every comb it ever met.

"You said six," he told Thea, sniffing past her into the kitchen. "I count better than that."

"I made eight," Hester called. "One for each of us and two for greed."

"Then I came to the right house."

The answer was quick enough to sound easy. Thea heard the strain under it anyway, the way his voice tried at manhood and slid back toward boy if pain caught it wrong.

Cora met Thea's eyes at last. There was flour on Thea's sleeve. There was a crease in Cora's face from the hat ribbon she had worn all afternoon and taken off too late. For one long second Thea knew the exact shape of Cora's mouth and did not know whether she would ever be allowed near it again.

"You can hang your coats there," she said, and hated how schoolmarm the sentence sounded as soon as it left her.

Cora's mouth moved once at one corner. Not a smile. Not nothing. "We're not staying long enough to need them."

Hester came in from the kitchen with the first tray, and the room altered around her. Not lighter. Just arranged.

"You'll stay long enough to eat sitting down," she said. "If anybody starts in on plans before I've got soup in bowls, I'll box ears, broken arm or no broken arm."

Dell's gaze went to the spoon rack as if he were judging whether she could still reach one to throw. "I'd like to see you try."

"You would not," Hester said. "You're vain about your face."

That got him. He ducked his head so they would not see the grin come all the way in.

The table in the kitchen had been pulled out from the wall for four and then amended for a fifth with a chair that had once belonged in the front room, its cane seat mended with clothesline. Cora, without thinking, folded a square of old seed catalog and shoved it under the short leg before anyone sat. The wobble vanished. She took the chair nearest the stove, as if warmth were a thing that should go first to the oldest body in the room and could only be trusted to do so if she kept watch on it.

The soup was potato, onion, and milk, thick enough to hold heat. The buns came out scorched on top in spots and shining with a smear of butter Hester must have been hoarding. There was pickled beet relish in a chipped bowl, and a plate of sliced pears preserved in thin syrup that proved Hester had been lying for weeks when she said she had no sugar to spare.

The sight of it hit Thea harder than if Hester had set down a roast goose.

"You extravagant old fraud," she said.

Hester lowered herself into her chair with a care she tried to pass off as annoyance. "It's not extravagance if it clears the shelf. Eat."

For the first minute only spoons sounded. Steam came up against faces still powdered with the day. Dell burned his mouth, swore into his spoon, and looked around as if daring correction.

Hester tore a bun in half. "Profanity's allowed if it's descriptive."

"Then that soup's hot as hell."

"That's better."

Cora had not yet looked directly at Thea again. She ate fast at first, the way people do when they have spent a day being watched and want at least one human act to go unobserved. Then the food slowed her. Some of the drawn steel left her shoulders. She took another bun. Thea watched her notice, without comment, that Hester had left the caraway off half the batch. Hester knew Cora had hated the taste since she was twelve and swallowed a mouthful of the seeds on a church picnic to prove she was not delicate. Thea did not know how many people in Morrow still remembered a thing so small. Maybe only the women who had kept one another alive this long.

"I heard old Pritchard offer ten cents for our washstand like he was doing the wood a favor," Dell said around his third spoonful.

"He'd haggle with his own coffin," Hester said.

"He rattled the drawer first." Dell mimed it with his good hand, sharp and contemptuous. "Three times. As if he thought money might hatch in there if he worried it."

Cora set her spoon down. "Mrs. Pritchard tried all the cupboard latches in the kitchen. Every one."

"Maybe she meant to buy one," Thea said.

Cora looked at her then, fully this time. Weariness had made her eyes clearer, not duller. "She doesn't cook. She only inventories."

Dell laughed once, surprised into it. Hester's answer followed on top of his.

"That's a profession in this town."

The laugh stayed. It moved around the table, thin at first, then truer. Thea felt it in her ribs like something loosening.

Hester used the opening. "Now. Since we're not discussing plans and I refuse to let this meal turn into a funeral with spoons, somebody tell me the worst song ever written."

"All of them," Dell said.

"Coward's answer."

"That one about the red river," he said. "Any fellow with a weak jaw sings it when he's had beer."

"He doesn't need beer," Cora said. "He only needs dusk."

"And somebody to ignore him," Hester added.

Thea said, "There was a boy in normal school who used to sing sentimental hymns at girls by the library steps."

Dell stared. "At them?"

"At them."

"Did it work?"

"Not on any girl with sense."

"Then the song wasn't the trouble," Hester said.

Thea surprised herself by laughing. "No. The trouble was his hair oil. You could smell him before the second verse."

"There you are," Hester said. "No song survives pomade."

Cora broke off a piece of bun and sopped her bowl. "Ezra used to sing work songs under his breath and leave out half the tune. Just the lines about mules or rain barrels. If the part was about a girl, he never remembered it."

It was the first time she had said his name without the whole room tightening around it. Thea saw the change happen, saw Dell hear it too. He looked down into his soup, not hiding from it exactly, only making space.

"Otto couldn't carry a tune if you nailed it to his coat," Hester said. "But he had three dirty verses for every church picnic song ever printed, and he knew precisely when to save them till the children had run off."

"You sang them with him," Thea said.

Hester sniffed. "Only so he wouldn't make a fool of himself alone."

"Merciful woman."

"I wasn't."

Dell pointed his spoon at her. "You're smiling."

"That's windburn."

The pears went around. Hester made everyone take some. Outside, the light thinned to the color of old paper. The lamp on the table gathered them tighter inside its reach. The bakery walls kept out the worst of the evening wind, but not all of it. Thea could hear grit brush the outer brick with the sound of something testing a knife.

"Books, then," Hester said, as if she had written the order of topics in advance. "Miss Bell, since that's your trade. Best one and worst one."

"You make it sound like flour grades."

"Everything is a flour grade if you live long enough."

Thea thought of the schoolhouse shelves, the atlas with Alaska still looking like a rumor, the McGuffey readers with names inked inside from children now in Colorado or dead or merely gone where Morrow no longer got to count them. She thought of the geography book with its cut-out hollow and the letter that had split her life at the seam.

"Worst," she said, "is any reader that tells children the country is orderly."

"That's cheating," Dell said. "That's not one book."

"Then the blue civics text with the eagle on it."

"Good," Hester said. "It always looked smug."

"Best," Thea said, and found herself cautious for no reason she could name. "A railway timetable, if you're truly going somewhere. A cookbook written by a woman who's survived company. A novel with enough weather in it that you trust the people before they speak."

"That's three," Dell said.

"I teach. We multiply."

He considered that. "Best book's a repair manual."

"For what?"

"Anything with bolts." He shrugged with one shoulder. "A machine tells the truth if you can read the marks."

Thea watched him say it. He still pretended, when it suited him, that books and school had slid off him like rain off oilcloth. Yet there was the pencil behind his ear and the timetable soft in his pocket, and he had read Ezra's letter with his face set still as split stone while half the town listened. Seventeen, she thought. Seventeen, and already he had learned to hide whatever in him was tender by disguising it as function.

"Then what's the worst?" she asked.

"Any poem printed in a farm paper."

Hester barked out a laugh so sudden it tipped into coughing before she could close her mouth against it. The cough doubled her over. The linen came up too slow this time. Thea saw the string of spit go gray across Hester's knuckles before it disappeared into cloth. Cora half rose. Hester waved her back down without looking up, the refusal sharp as a slap.

It passed. Not quickly. But it passed.

When Hester sat again, she took a sip of water and said, voice rougher, "That answer stays."

Dell's ears had gone red. He hated fright showing on him almost as much as pain. He tore the remaining half of his bun into small hard pieces and arranged them by size beside his plate.

"You're right, though," Thea said to him, because she knew he needed the floor returned without pity. "Farm-paper poems are usually about sunsets written by men who have never looked up from a ledger."

"Or calves," Cora said. "Bad poems love calves."

"Too easy," Hester said.

"I've seen them." Cora's mouth shifted again, closer to a smile now. "Weak little verses about dew on a nose."

"You have become literary, Mrs. Vale," Thea said.

"Don't start."

But there was warmth in it, and it went under Thea's skin like drink.

The talk wandered after that because Hester let it and because none of them could bear, for one hour, to make every sentence earn its keep. Yeast came next, naturally, because the buns were good enough to deserve explanation.

"People kill starter by fussing over it," Hester said. "They want to warm it, feed it, stir it, worry at it every ten minutes. Leave it alone half the time and it behaves better."

"You're talking about people," Dell said.

"I'm talking about dough. If it wounds you, improve yourself."

"Is that why you hide your crocks under towels?" Thea asked.

"That's because dust has no manners."

Cora ran a thumb over the seam of her bowl. "I always thought bread ought to have some meanness in it. Sweet bread doesn't trust the world enough."

"There she is," Hester said softly, almost to herself.

Thea did not ask what she meant. She knew. This was the Cora who had not vanished at auction, only been driven so far inward all afternoon that Thea had feared whatever remained might never turn its face outward again.

Dell leaned back, wincing despite himself when his ribs reminded him of the movement. "If California's all oranges, maybe the bread out there tastes foolish."

"Who told you California's all oranges?" Hester asked.

"Magazine ad. At the depot last spring. A woman in white gloves holding half a fruit like it was a jewel."

"That wasn't for you," Thea said.

"I know that." He rubbed one thumb over the edge of his spoon. "Still looked like it smelled better than here."

Cora lifted her eyes. "Does it?"

The question was aimed at Thea. Not because Thea had been to California. She had not. But because of the four of them, she had spent the most time pretending maps could answer anything.

"Not all of it," Thea said. "I imagine some of it smells like hot tar and fish and the back side of money. But the depots I've seen in other places smelled different from ours. Cinders. Wool coats damp from weather. Coffee. Once, in Omaha, there was a crate of bananas split open and turning black, and the whole platform smelled like sugar trying to rot politely."

Dell stared in real pleasure. "Bananas."

"I didn't say you could afford one."

"Didn't ask if I could."

Cora said, "What about Lincoln?"

Thea had not been there in years. Long enough that the memory came back with pieces missing and the pieces missing mattered less than the fact of motion itself.

"Coal smoke," she said. "Wet newspaper. Perfume if somebody's trying too hard. The lunch counter grease. Sometimes oranges in winter if the right car comes through. And people hurrying like they mean to get somewhere before the day closes on them."

"Sounds proud," Hester said.

"Mostly it sounds loud."

Cora looked at the lamp chimney, at the flour in the cracks of the table, at nothing Thea could name. "Maybe every place smells different once you know you're allowed to leave it."

No one answered immediately. The sentence had come out of her without warning, plain as weather, and it sat with them.

Dell broke it first, mercifully. "Nebraska smells like singed wheat and axle grease."

"Cow manure," Hester said.

"That too."

"Old coffee grounds," Thea said.

"Wet wool in March," Cora said.

"Hot iron after rain," Dell added.

"You all missed the obvious," Hester said. "It smells like dirt. Which would be a comfort if it ever stayed where it belonged."

They laughed again, quieter this time, because the truth of it had teeth.

By the time the bowls were empty and the last of the pears taken up in syrup with bread, the room had changed shape. The day had not become smaller. The loss had not softened. But the table had made a place big enough to hold a few things besides grief, and that felt almost indecent in its relief.

Thea stood to gather plates. Cora stood at the same time, their chairs scraping together.

"I'll wash," Cora said.

"You don't have to."

"I know that."

The words were ordinary. The pressure under them was not.

Dell, as if hearing what ought to be given privacy and refusing to look as if he heard it, scooped up the cutlery one-handed and carried it to the sink. "If I'm sent out of the room, I'm taking a bun with me."

"Take two," Hester said. "And bring me the blue tin from the pantry after."

He paused. "The round one or the square?"

"Round. Don't go inventing."

He went, and Hester leaned back with her hands on her lap, not resting exactly, but borrowing a minute from her own body by force.

At the sink Thea poured water from the kettle into the dishpan and added cold till the enamel stopped ticking. Cora rolled her sleeves once. Her hands were still rough with dust from the sale ring and the house and whatever else the day had asked her to touch. When Thea passed her the first plate, their fingers met in the wet.

Thea almost pulled back. Cora did not.

"I was ugly with you," Cora said, looking down at the plate she was wiping. "At the house. Before."

Thea had thought about that fight in enough versions to have worn grooves in her own mind. In some, she won it cleanly. In some, Cora did. The truth had never let either woman come out whole.

"You wanted him easier than he was," she said.

"I wanted the dead to stay useful." Cora's mouth tightened. "That's uglier than I said it then."

"Yes."

Cora nodded once, accepting the answer as if it were a tool she had asked to borrow. "I asked you to choose me over the truth because I was frightened of what was left if I didn't. That wasn't fair."

The plate in Thea's hands was already clean. She kept washing it anyway. "No."

"I know." Cora set the dried plate aside, lined exactly with the edge of the drainboard. "I'm not asking you to make it smaller."

Thea's throat hurt, but not in any stock way she could name. It was the steam, the lamp heat, the scrape of Cora's thumbnail on old crockery, the fact that all afternoon Thea had stood in public with Ezra's letter shaking only once in her hands and now one plain apology from the woman she loved made the whole room feel less solid.

"I hated you for being right where it cost me," Cora went on. "I don't mean I stopped wanting you. I mean I hated you in the same breath. I figure that's worth saying plain."

Thea let out something that might have become a laugh in another life. "That's unpleasantly honest."

"Well."

"I hated you for asking it," Thea said. "And I understood why you asked. That was worse."

Cora's hand flattened on the rim of the dishpan. The skin over her knuckles was split white from weather and lye soap both. "Can you stand to go on with me if I don't promise pretty?"

Thea turned then. Cora was close enough that the heat of her reached through damp cloth. Not a kiss. Not nearly. But close enough for danger to remember its name.

"I don't want pretty," Thea said. Her voice came out too formal for a beat, and she heard it. She started again. "I don't want another rescue story either. I want to know if when we leave this town, I leave it alone."

Cora looked at her for a long second. "No," she said, and Thea's heart dropped before the rest arrived. "Not alone."

It would have been enough if Hester had not chosen that moment to cough again, harder, in the other room.

Both women turned. Hester was waving them off already, furious at being the occasion for concern. Dell came back from the pantry with the round blue biscuit tin tucked under his good arm and stopped when he saw her.

"I'm fine," Hester said.

"That's a lie," Dell said.

"Yes," she said, surprising them all by not denying it. "Set the tin down."

The round blue tin had once held Danish butter cookies and now had a dent in the side where Otto, according to Hester, had dropped it off the wagon seat twenty years ago and sworn for a full mile. Dell put it in the middle of the table. Thea dried her hands. Cora rolled her sleeves back down as if the talk at the sink could be folded with the fabric and carried privately a little longer.

Hester laid her palm on the lid. Her hand looked broader than ever with the veins standing at the wrist.

"I didn't ask you here to feed you only," she said. "Though that was part of it, and don't let anybody tell you otherwise. People think a meal is decoration. It's not. It's a way of saying I know you've still got a mouth and a gut and tomorrow will need both."

Dell sat again, slower now, wary. The pencil remained behind his ear.

"Open it," Hester said to Thea.

Thea lifted the lid. Inside, wrapped in flour sacks and old hand towels, lay six jelly jars packed close, each sealed with wax around the lid. Dust sat inside them in shades from pale wheat to darker brown, not much in any one jar, never more than a hand's skim. Tucked along one side was a small paper packet folded from old ledger stock and tied with thread gone soft from age.

Even before Hester touched it, Thea knew what that packet was.

"Otto's seed," Hester said. "Buffalo grass. Saved from before everybody around here decided roots were less important than bragging."

Cora's face changed in a way almost no one but Thea would have seen. Not sentiment. Recognition, then fear of sentiment, then the work of holding both still.

Hester went on. "It won't make anybody rich. That's one virtue in its favor. It sits low, takes hold deep, and minds its own business. If you give it a patch and a little luck, it keeps the ground from lifting and traveling. Otto kept saying we ought to lay it back around the basin edges where the water used to spread and soak instead of run off. Men with more acreage than sense laughed at him till the whole county started blowing east."

Dell leaned over the tin, intent as if the packet were a carburetor part. "It'll still grow?"

"If the world's not entirely rotten." Hester's mouth bent. "Seed wants to outlive us. That's its main talent."

She tapped one jar with her finger. The glass gave a small bright note.

"These are yours now too. All of them. I've kept them long enough."

Thea looked up sharply. "Hester."

"Don't begin. I know what I said." Hester drew breath carefully, then another. "I kept thinking I'd carry them myself till there was a better moment. My chest has informed me that waiting on better moments is for fools and Methodists."

"You're Lutheran," Dell said automatically.

"Exactly."

That got a quick crooked smile from him, but it vanished almost at once.

"You know the rules," Hester said. Her voice had flattened into its working tone, the one she used for recipe weights and weather warnings. "Each jar holds one true thing. Open it and you've spent it. Don't crack the seal because you're curious. Don't open one to win an argument. Don't open one just to feel worse on purpose. Use them when memory's in danger of being scrubbed clean or turned holy. Use them when one of you starts lying about how it was."

Her gaze moved from Thea to Cora to Dell and did not spare anybody.

"That includes lies you tell out of love," she said.

No one answered.

Thea touched one jar through the towel. It was cool. Lighter than she expected and heavier too, because her hand supplied the rest.

"How many?" Dell asked. "To each, I mean."

"You're already dividing them," Hester said.

"Somebody has to carry them."

"Good. Then carry them with your head on." Hester nudged the packet toward the center. "The seed isn't a relic. Don't make a church object of it. If you've got ground fit for it someday, plant it. If you haven't, then give it to ground that deserves it. Saint's Basin first, if you're wise. After that, anywhere you mean to keep from turning traitor."

Cora put two fingers lightly on the packet, then took them away. "Why us?"

Hester gave her a look so direct it bordered on insult. "Because who else is left with enough sense to know it isn't magic? Because I'm tired. Because memory that sits on my shelf after I'm gone is only another thing for dust to cover. Because Otto didn't save seed for me to die preserving a tin."

The sentence dropped into the room with the force of a pan set hard on a table.

Dell's head came up. "Don't talk like that."

"Like what?"

"Gone."

Hester's face softened, which on her looked almost stern. "Del. I've got no use for pretend. Not from doctors, not from preachers, not from family. I'm here now. That's what counts now. Tomorrow can count itself when it arrives."

Thea saw the boy in him then, not because he looked younger but because he looked betrayed by arithmetic. He stared at the jars, jaw set, and the timetable in his back pocket crackled when he shifted.

Cora spoke before the silence could harden. "All right," she said. Practical first, because that was how she touched pain when she meant to keep moving. "We wrap them in towels and keep them low in the valise. Not the carpetbag. Del, the valise with the shirts. It'll ride steadier."

Dell scrubbed the heel of his hand over his mouth. "Need sawdust or flour sack around the bottoms."

"I've got flour sacks," Hester said.

"And the seed?" Thea asked.

"Inside my handbag," Cora said at once, then checked herself, glancing around the table as if she had spoken too quickly and shown more than she intended. "If that's agreeable."

It was not only the safest place. It was the closest.

Hester saw everything and looked away just enough to give them the mercy of not naming it.

"Good," she said. "Now that the dead and the dirt have both had their say, we can come to the living. Where are you three going?"

There it was. No ceremony. No easing in. Just the question laid bare between the spoons and the lamp.

Dell shifted first. "West."

"That's not a place," Hester said.

"It's a direction."

"Only useful if you're a compass."

Thea nearly smiled, but the question had struck too near the spot in her that had been braced all evening. She could feel her own old training rise, the instinct to answer carefully enough that no one could accuse her of wanting too much. She forced herself not to hide inside it.

"I can get us to Lincoln," she said. "At least I think I can. The library may not want me now, and I know that. But I've still got the letter, and I've got enough saved to make the fares that far if we're careful."

Cora's head turned. Pride and shame crossed her face so fast Thea could not have separated them if she tried.

"I don't want you buying me passage like a charity case," Cora said.

"I'm not."

"It would look like it."

"To who?"

"To me."

There was the true objection. Thea felt her own temper stir, not hot, only sharp.

"Then let me say it correctly," she said. "I'm not purchasing you. I'm asking whether we travel the first leg of this road together. If we do, then what I've got is ours for that leg. If we don't, then I'll keep my money and my dignity and you can keep yours, and everybody can die respectable."

Dell stared at her, impressed despite himself. Hester closed her eyes briefly, either from fatigue or approval.

Cora let out a breath through her nose. "You always know how to make a sentence put on stockings."

"You understood it."

"I did."

Thea made herself stay still. This was the beat where she usually over-explained and spoiled whatever honesty had just arrived. She bit back three further clarifications and let the room do some of the work.

Dell spoke into the gap. "I've got money too."

Cora turned on him. "From what?"

"From fixing Pritchard's pump and the Reiners drill in August. And from the threshing belts I sold Mayberry after the south shed came down." He lifted his good shoulder. "Not a king's ransom. Enough that I don't need to sit there while you two talk like I'm freight."

Hester nodded once, as if he had finally said the minimum acceptable thing.

"Good," she said. "You're not freight."

"I know I'm not freight."

"Then stop looking wounded when adults forget it and correct them."

Dell's ears reddened again, but he did not duck his head this time. "Fine. I'm correcting everybody now. I can go to Lincoln. After that I might go farther. I still want California, or the Corps, or anything with engines that aren't half dead already. But I'll go to Lincoln first. I won't slip off a platform and leave a note under a plate if that's what you're worried about."

Cora had been worried about exactly that. It showed.

"I wasn't going to say it that way," she muttered.

"You were thinking it that way."

"Maybe."

The honesty of it pulled the meanness out of the exchange before it could root.

Thea said quietly, "Lincoln first gives us a place to stand and decide from there. It doesn't have to be more promise than that tonight."

Cora looked at the jars, at the packet of seed, at Dell's bent wrist resting beside the tin, at Hester's face already graying with tiredness around the mouth. When she spoke, the words came in the order they always did with her when something mattered too much: practical thing first, feeling after if it could not be avoided.

"We'll need to sort what we can carry. Clothes. Papers. Del's sling wraps. The letter." She touched the edge of the tin. "These."

Then she lifted her eyes to Thea.

"Far as Lincoln," she said. "I can promise that. I can get on that train with you and Del and not turn coward halfway to the depot. Beyond it, I won't tell stories yet. I don't know what I'll be once I'm not on that farm, and I won't lie to keep you near me."

Thea had thought the clean sentence she wanted might sound grander than that. It did not. It sounded smaller and truer and therefore hit deeper.

"All right," she said.

Hester's gaze sharpened. "That isn't enough from you."

Thea almost laughed from nerves alone. "You run a tyrannical supper table."

"Yes."

Thea folded her hands in her lap so no one would see them shake. "All right," she said again, clearer. "Lincoln first. After that, if I want something more or different, I'll say it before it turns to poison. I won't stay silent and call it virtue. And I won't ask either of you for forever while the ash from today is still in your hair."

Dell absorbed that with a seriousness beyond his years and beyond his patience, but he did not sneer at it. That was its own kindness.

"Good," he said. "Then mine is this. Lincoln first. After that, I get to say what I want too. Out loud. Not after I've done it."

Cora swallowed. The muscles in her jaw worked once.

"Yes," she said.

Thea would have trusted almost any other mother to gather him in then, to spoil the hard-won truth with comfort. Cora only reached across the table and set her hand over his for a single second, weathered palm to narrow wrist, then took it back. It was more moving than an embrace would have been because it was exactly all she could bear in front of witnesses.

Hester leaned back. The effort of the conversation had cost her. That was visible now in the bluish hollows at her temples and the way she was measuring each breath as if she meant to make the supply last.

"There," she said. "A plan narrow enough to be honest. Miracles happen."

"You don't believe in miracles," Dell said.

"I believe in bread rising and people occasionally surprising me. That's enough wonder for one life."

She pushed the tin gently toward Thea. Then the seed packet toward Cora. Then, with one blunt finger, she tapped Dell's folded timetable where it still showed from his pocket.

"And you," she said, "if you insist on going west eventually, at least learn the names of what you'll be leaving behind. Fool boys dream better when the map has labels."

He blinked. "I know the labels."

"Then stop hiding them in your pocket like contraband."

For the first time all evening he grinned without flinching, full and young and impossible not to love.

"Yes, ma'am," he said.

Hester clicked her tongue. "Don't 'ma'am' me like a deputy sheriff."

Outside, the wind had dropped. In its place came the strange after-silence that belonged only to dust country, when even a town's emptiness seemed to listen. The lamp flame shivered once in the chimney and steadied.

They packed the jars before anyone could say something that would cheapen what had just been said cleanly. Dell fetched flour sacks and cut strips from one with his pocketknife. Thea wrapped each jar in towel and cloth, tucking them into the blue tin so glass would not knock glass. Cora slid the seed packet into her handbag between a comb and the small purse where she kept household cash. Hester sat and watched, correcting the arrangement once, then again, because of course she did.

When the work was finished, Cora stood with the handbag strap in one hand and the blue tin waiting by Thea's chair.

"I'll come back for the rest in the morning," she said to Hester.

"Come back because I said so, not because you owe me a courtesy."

"Both, probably."

Hester considered that and let it pass.

Dell got to his feet. "I can carry the tin."

"You can carry yourself to the door," Cora said.

"Arm's broke, not both legs."

"And the jars will ride with Miss Bell tonight," Hester said, settling the dispute before it could grow teeth. "You can all rehearse your competence tomorrow."

Dell looked ready to argue, then saw Hester's face and thought better of it. That too was a kind of growing up.

At the back door Cora paused. Dell had already gone through to the alley, drawn by the cool air and the chance to spit the taste of medicine and pears from his mouth where no one would comment on it. Hester had turned her head into her shoulder and was coughing again, quieter now, which was worse.

Thea stood with the tin in both hands.

Cora touched the lid once, not taking it. "I meant what I said," she said, low enough that only Thea could hear over the cough and Dell's boots outside.

"So did I."

"Lincoln first."

"Lincoln first."

Cora's hand slid from the tin to Thea's wrist. Flour from the towel marked her thumb white. The touch lasted only a beat. It was enough to make Thea feel, all at once, how starved she had been for any contact not shaped by duty or audience.

"Don't let me make fog again," Cora said.

It was not romantic. It was better.

"Then don't ask it," Thea answered.

Cora nodded and went out into the alley.

The screen door snapped. Dell said something she could not catch. Cora answered, and even without the words Thea heard the changed note in it, not healed, never that easy, only less defended than when she had arrived. She stood in the doorway until their steps faded toward the street.

Behind her, Hester's coughing stopped. The silence after it ran too long. Thea turned fast enough to slosh the jars inside the tin, but Hester was still upright in her chair, one hand over her chest, the other reaching blindly for the burnt heel of bread she had saved for herself.

"Don't look at me like that," Hester said hoarsely when she found it. "Put the tin under your bed and bank the stove. Tomorrow's not done with us yet."

Thea set the jars down and crossed the room.

Later, with the stove checked, the door barred, and the blue tin tucked beside her narrow bed upstairs, she lay awake listening to the bakery settle around the night's cold. Below her, Hester moved once across the boards, then not again for a long time. In the room the jars held their little weights of weather and witness. On the chair back hung Thea's dress with flour on the sleeve where Cora had seen her in it. In the alley, somewhere beyond sight, a freight gave one low mournful call and kept going west through the dark.

Lincoln first, Thea thought.

It was not forever. It was not safety. It was only the first true distance any of them had managed to name.
