# Chapter 3

Before the ovens had enough heat to wake the room, Hester Blum stood at her back table with six washed fruit jars, a bent teaspoon, and a square of muslin she had boiled the night before because if she was going to behave like a fool, she would at least do it cleanly.

The bakery still held its night smells. Yeast had gone slack in the sponge crock. Coffee grounds sat damp in the pot from yesterday. Flour lived in the boards. Wood smoke cooled in the iron throat of the range. Under all of it sat the storm, the same way it sat under everything now, as if Black Sunday had taken a room in every house in Morrow and did not mean to pay. Dust lay on the sill above the dough trough, in the groove of the cake case glass, on the enamel breadbox, and over the little mirror by the washstand in the curtained room where she slept. She had wiped once already since the storm and there was still enough grit left to write a lie in.

Yesterday, leaning across the cake case to see whether the front pane had gone permanently blind, she had breathed on the glass and seen Otto's hand there for one blink: not his face, not enough for comfort, only his hand setting two survey stakes against a wall. The hand had gone before she could even say his name. That could have been grief. She had been living with grief long enough to know it could dress itself in plain clothes and pass for ordinary sight if you let it. After Marta died, there had been mornings when every white apron in town looked like her back from across the street until the woman turned and proved to be someone else.

Cora's story about the Vale front-room window had not helped. Hester had believed her and not believed her in the same breath. A widow could tell the truth and still be misled by wanting. So could an old woman who had buried a husband and a daughter and kept on measuring yeast with the same hands. The dead did not become reliable because you missed them.

She lifted the teaspoon and drew it along the sill above the dough trough. Dust gathered with a dry, soft hiss. It was not like flour. Flour had weight, obedience, and purpose. This stuff skated. It clung to the spoon and then seemed to think better of it, fleeing into the cracks in the grain. Hester scraped it into a jar, covered the mouth with muslin while she worked the next spot, and tamped on the glass lid with the heel of her palm. The old zinc ring caught crooked. She loosened it, tried again, and felt the threads take.

She kept the jars separate because not keeping them separate would have been the act of a person already determined to believe. She took one from the cake case, one from the dough-room sill, one from the breadbox lid where the dust had settled in a gray fur over chipped white enamel, one from the bedroom mirror, and one from the back windowsill above the sink. The last came from the shelf over Otto's old weather notebooks, where nobody had touched anything in three days because she could not bear more cleaning that did not matter.

With the carpenter pencil she used for flour tallies, she marked paper scraps and slid them under each jar: case, trough, breadbox, mirror, sink, shelf. It looked ridiculous. It looked worse than ridiculous. It looked like the sort of private nonsense people forgave in a woman as long as they could tell themselves she had been softened by loss. Hester had worked too long and too publicly for that. Men who had eaten her rye and sweet rolls for twenty years would come in grinning and ask whether she had started bottling ghosts now. Women would lower their voices and say poor Mrs. Blum had not been right since Marta's lungs gave out. There was no appetite in town for an old baker who might be correct about anything larger than salt.

Her chest caught. She turned her head and coughed into her apron. The cough dragged from deep enough to make her eyes water. When she wiped her mouth, the spit on the cloth held a faint gray in it. It was dust again. It was always dust. It sat in the folds of her throat the way old bitterness sat in a family, never leaving cleanly, only thinning and returning.

She set the jars in a row on the sill above the range, where the first easy warmth from the waking stove could reach them without making the glass sweat. The sky outside the back window had barely begun to lift from black to soot-gray. No customer would come yet. No wagon would rattle the alley. This was the one honest hour left in town, the hour before people put on their voices.

Hester picked up the jar marked case and cupped it between both hands. The glass was cool enough to ache. She held it until the chill softened, then breathed across the curved side the way she had breathed across the cake-case pane. Her own fog bloomed on the outside and slid away.

Nothing happened.

She tried the jar from the breadbox. She warmed it longer, rubbed her thumb over the shoulder, and held it near the side of the stove. The dust inside lay still as sifted ash.

There was nothing there either.

The third jar, from the sink window, showed only her own bent face warped by glass and dawn. Hester let it down hard enough that the bottom thudded the table.

"Fine," she said to the room. "Then fine."

She was angry now, which helped. Anger cleared a path through shame. It was better to be cross than hopeful. Hope, at her age, was a thing with too many small teeth.

She reached for the jar marked shelf, more to finish the nonsense than out of faith. Dust lay inside it in a thin drift against one curve. She held it in both palms and let the heat of them build. The glass warmed. She tipped it once, letting the dust slide. Then she breathed across the side.

At first she thought it was the same nothing. The gray inside shifted, no more than that, and she nearly set it down. Then the dust gathered itself in a narrow dark line, not loose now but drawn, as if an invisible finger were dragging through wet ground. Another line crossed it. A tabletop formed around those strokes. The curve of a shoulder followed, then a cuff with flour rubbed into the seam. It was Otto.

He stood bent over a board laid across two chairs, the old way he did when he worked on maps at home because the bakery tables were always needed for dough. The jar gave her his good side first: the left cheek rough with missed whiskers, the little dent near the ear where a horse had kicked dirt at him when he was twenty and handsome enough to think it would never matter. His wedding ring, flattened on one edge from years of survey chain and kneading troughs, clicked against the board as he tapped an inked oval. The nail of his first finger was split to the quick, exactly as it had been all that spring he kept driving stakes into hard ground and refusing gloves.

"There," he said, and even in the jar his voice came to her dulled, as if through a wall. "That black stays wet."

His finger pressed again. The oval darkened. It was not ink now but ground. She saw, with the sick clarity that belonged to dreams and evidence and nothing in between, a patch of meadow gone almost blue with held water while the land around it lay the color of dry bread crust. A fringe of reed grass moved. Then Otto's hand swept over the place, measuring distance in his old practical way, and the image thinned. Dust ran back into dust. The jar showed only gray smears on curved glass.

Hester did not move for a moment. Her hands had gone so tight around the jar that the rings of the lid had printed themselves into the heel of her palm.

She had not thought about the split in Otto's fingernail in years. She had forgotten the exact shape of the dent by his ear. More than that, she had forgotten which corner of the basin map he used to tap when he argued. Grief could give a woman a face. It could give her a sentence if she had heard it often enough. But it could not, not honestly, return a particular fingernail and a forgotten patch of ink she had not held in her mind since before the bank and the men from town called the basin useless water.

She set the jar down carefully this time and went to the drawer under the counter where she kept salves, seed envelopes, and what remained of the papers she had refused to burn. The map lay folded in waxed paper under Otto's weather notebook from 1931 and a bundle of Marta's relief-sewing coupons. Hester unwrapped it with hands that were less steady than she cared for. The paper had gone thin at the creases. She opened it on the table and smoothed the corners flat.

There it was. Otto's cramped hand looked plain and stubborn as ever. Field lines crossed the page. Notes about runoff crowded the margins. A sketched ditch showed where later men had wanted it cut cleaner. South of the Vale acreage he had made a dark, thumb-sized oval and written beside it, "holds wet longest." The mark sat in the same crooked place the jar had shown him touching.

The kitchen seemed to change size around her. It did not seem larger. It seemed smaller and sharper. The stove ticked. Somewhere in the alley a board banged once and settled. Hester looked from the map to the jar and back again.

"All right," she said, very quiet.

It was not an answer to Otto. It was not a prayer. It was an admission that the thing on glass and enamel and dust was not widow's vapor, not unless the whole town had gone mad in exactly matching ways. It was fact of some sort, ugly and poorly mannered, which was often how fact first came through a door.

She was still standing there with the map open when the back latch lifted.

Dell Vale came in sideways, as if even the door ought not to know he was using it. He had no hat on. His hair stuck up pale with sleep and dust, and the cracked toes of his boots had been rubbed with stove blacking in an effort to make them look less broken. It worked only in dim light, but seventeen was old enough to try. He shut the door behind him with the care of someone accustomed to old hinges, then saw the jars on the sill and the map on the table and stopped.

"You weren't open," he said.

"And yet you're here."

He shrugged without answering that. The movement made him look younger than his jaw wanted to. His left cuff hung open where the button had gone missing. He had machine grease at the base of one thumbnail and a clean stripe on his trouser leg where he had wiped his hand and immediately regretted it. Hester liked him better for the regret. Boys who still regretted making a mess had not yet turned into the men who left all of it to women.

"I can come back."

"If you were going to come back, you'd have used the front door like a Christian." Hester folded the map once, not enough to hide it entirely. "Shut that draft out proper and sit down."

He obeyed before deciding whether obedience suited him. That too was seventeen. He lowered himself onto the flour bin by the wall, knees up, hands loose between them, trying for a roughness he had not finished growing into. His eyes kept flicking to the jars.

Hester poured the last inch of yesterday's coffee into a cup and set it on the table. She cut the heel from a loaf of molasses bread, spread it with the hard end of apple butter, and slid that over too. Dell took the bread first. Hunger had better manners than pride some mornings.

"Ma said you saw one," he said after a bite. Apple butter had darkened the corner of his mouth; he wiped it away with the back of his wrist before the sentence was done. "On your glass."

"Your mother shouldn't have told you every strange thing in her line of sight."

"She didn't tell me every strange thing. She told me enough to stop me thinking she was worn out and seeing tricks." He chewed once more and looked at the jars instead of at Hester. "Then I saw you carrying those in from the wash yard yesterday."

"So now you've come to save me from myself?"

"No." He swallowed. "I've come because if there's something to it, I need to know what sort."

The cup sat untouched by his hand. That was how she knew it mattered. Dell could drink coffee hot enough to raise blisters when he was pretending he had no nerves.

Hester sat opposite him and put both palms flat on the table. "You need to be more exact than that."

He gave her the wary side of his face, the one he used with men offering advice and girls offering pity. "Do I."

"Yes."

"Fine." He drew breath, let it go, and began again with the irritation of someone forced to show his work. "If that thing on our window was real, then either he signed something or he didn't. Either August has been making up a story for months, or he hasn't. Either my father got trapped or he lied to us. Pick one. I'd like to stand on something that isn't moving."

Hester let the words settle. The boy had come for certainty, but not the kind he could boast about. She heard the shape of another fear under the first one: not only that Ezra had lied, but that Cora had built her next year of life on whatever version of Ezra let her keep breathing. A son could want the truth and still dread being the one who carried it into his mother's kitchen.

"And you thought I'd have one of your choices lined up for you in a jar."

He looked at the map now. "Do you?"

"No. I've got worse than that."

His mouth tightened. "What does that mean?"

Hester reached for the map and unfolded enough to show the dark oval. "It means I saw Otto just now. Not enough to make a religion out of him. Enough." She tapped the mark. "He touched this place."

Dell leaned in despite himself. He had Ezra's quick, practical hands, though not Ezra's way of pretending they were calmer than they were. "What's that."

"Saint's Basin."

"South low ground?"

"Before your time, it was more than low ground." She watched him take that in. "Otto used to say the wet there held the rest of the fields together longer than folks understood. Then men decided standing water was wasted acreage and mosquito trouble and something to tame. They cut and tiled and called it improvement."

Dell followed the sketched lines with his eyes. "That's on our side of the road."

"Quarter mile south of your west field, near enough."

"I thought Saint's Basin was farther." He frowned. "Pa said it used to be over by the Miller place."

"Your father said a number of things in the last year that were easier to live with than they were to prove."

The sentence landed harder than she intended. Dell sat back. The bin creaked under him.

"So it's true, then," he said. "All of it."

"Don't start lying to yourself in the other direction. One map mark and one jar don't make all of it."

He almost smiled, but the expression failed halfway. "You keep saying jar like that helps."

"Would you prefer omen. Sign. Warning. Miracle."

"No." He rubbed his thumb over the rim of the coffee cup at last. "I don't need church. I need to know what the hell he did."

Because he had said "he" and not "August" or "Pa," Hester stood and took down another jar.

"You may not get what you came wanting," she said.

"I know."

"Do you."

This time he looked at her square. The softness had gone out of him in a way that made her hate the whole season. "I know enough not to ask Ma first."

That, at least, was plain.

She brought the jar marked mirror to the table. If there was anything decent in the world, it would stay dead and gray. Hester did not like the part of herself that had begun choosing jars by intuition, but there it was, operating beneath reason like yeast beneath dough. She set the jar between them and held it steady while the boy leaned in.

"Touch it," she said.

"Why."

"Because warm hands do as much as breath sometimes."

"Sometimes."

"If I knew more than that, I'd be making money off it."

The corner of his mouth twitched. That helped. She would take any sign he had not turned to rawhide in the last month.

Dell laid both hands around the jar. His fingers were long and still awkwardly large for the rest of him. They were mechanic's fingers, or musician's if he had been born in another county to people with spare time. The gray dust inside did nothing. He looked at Hester as if to ask whether she was mocking him.

"Keep hold," she said.

She bent and breathed across the curved glass. Their fog mingled briefly on the outside. Inside, the dust shifted. Dell's hands tightened so quickly the lids gave a small metallic creak.

The gray did not draw a map this time. It rose in a sheet, then folded itself inward, making depth where there had been none. A kitchen came up, close and narrow, with blue-checked oilcloth on a table and a wall clock whose pendulum stuck at one side before it carried through. It was the Vale kitchen. There was no mistaking it. Hester knew the crack in the plaster by the pantry door and the nick in the table edge from when Cora had dropped the cream separator handle two winters back.

Ezra stood at the table in his shirtsleeves. He was thinner than Hester remembered him from the year before his death, cheeks sharpened, collarbone working under skin when he swallowed. His hair had gone flat from sweat and one side needed cutting. On the back of his right thumb the pale crescent scar from splitting kindling showed when he lifted a folded paper. He smoothed the fold once with the heel of his hand, then again, then a third time, exactly as he used to do with seed bills, school notices, church programs, anything he could not leave imperfectly square.

Outside the window, Cora's shadow crossed the muslin curtain in a blur of skirt and clothespins. Ezra heard it before Hester did. He jerked his head toward the door, snatched open the middle drawer of the table, and shoved the paper under the warped oilcloth lining instead of inside the drawer itself. The drawer stuck on its nail, as it always did. He hit it shut with the heel of his palm, wiped his hand on his trousers, and turned toward the stove with such practiced speed it made Hester's stomach go cold.

By then the shadow had passed the window. The image blurred. Dust fell. The kitchen flattened back into glass.

Dell had gone still in the bad way, not the calm way. The jar sat clamped between his hands while the color left his face one strip at a time.

"That's ours," he said.

Hester did not answer. There was no useful answer to that.

"That drawer catches on the nail." His voice had thinned. "I fixed it with him. It still caught."

He took one hand off the jar and rubbed it hard down his mouth, as if he could erase the sight before it reached the back of his head. Hester saw the effort it cost him not to break the cup or throw the whole thing against the wall. He had his father's hands and his mother's temper and none of the years required to manage either.

"Could've been anything," he said.

"Yes."

"Could've been seed receipts."

"Yes."

"Could've been nothing that mattered."

"No." Hester kept her voice level. "Don't turn stupid now."

He flinched, which was not the same as offense. It looked more like relief that someone had stopped him before he got farther into the lie.

"I wasn't," he said, and then, because the sentence did not hold, he tried again. "I was trying to think."

"Think better."

He looked at the dead jar again. "He hid it from her."

"He did."

"Why."

"Because paper with figures on it can shame a man more efficiently than a beating." Hester heard Otto in the sentence and did not apologize for that. "Because delay feels like mercy while you're doing it. Because he might have meant to tell her after supper, or after harvest, or after one more decent week, and men are always surprised to find that time is not sitting around waiting on them."

Dell gave a short, ugly laugh with no humor in it. "That sounds like men according to women."

"Usually the reliable source."

That got a breath out of him, not quite a laugh. He let it go and bent forward, forearms on knees, hands loose now between them. The open cuff on his left wrist had folded back, showing a burn scar no bigger than a dime where he had once grabbed a muffler too soon after shutting off the truck. Hester remembered bandaging it while he insisted it was nothing and winced only when she turned away.

"If I tell Ma that," he said, "and it's not the bank paper, then I've just put a hole in him for nothing."

"If you tell her nothing and go on waiting for weather to do your work, that's a choice too."

"I know that."

"Do you."

He looked up, irritated now because irritation was easier than hurt. "Would you stop asking that."

"When you stop being seventeen."

He snorted. Then his face changed again, sudden and miserable. "I don't want August telling it first."

There it was. It was not heroism or appetite for truth as a clean moral good. It was fear of whose mouth would shape the story if Dell did not get there sooner. Hester understood that fear better than she understood half the prayers she had heard in church.

She reached for the map.

"Then we go where Otto pointed."

Dell's head came up. "What."

"Saint's Basin. Not to fix your father. Not to condemn him either. To see whether the land still carries the mark of what was done to it."

"Today?"

"I've got bread to make and you've got chores unless Cora has died in the last ten minutes."

"She hasn't."

"Then not this minute." Hester spread the paper flat between them. "But soon."

He slid off the flour bin and came to the table. Up close, he smelled of sweat gone cold, machine oil, and the cheap lye soap Cora bought when money pinched hard. He bent over the map the way boys bent over engines, all defiance gone in the face of a thing that might answer if properly handled.

"That's the road here," he said after a moment, finger hovering rather than touching. "And that's the windbreak that used to be thicker before the blight took the middle trees. This bend..." He squinted. "If that's right, then the basin edge would run behind our south wheat, not straight across. It'd cut catty-corner."

"Otto said the ground remembered curves better than men did."

"Ground doesn't have to prove itself to anybody." Dell traced the shape in air again. "There's a place down there where the wheat always comes up meaner on the rise and then goes greener for a strip after a wet spring. Not green exactly now. Nothing's green now. But different. The binder used to bog there when I was nine."

"Your father noticed?"

He took his time answering. "He cussed it enough."

That was not nothing. Hester watched the boy stand in the space between what he had just seen and what he still wanted to believe. He was doing it honestly now, which was harder to watch than simple denial would have been.

"If Otto was right," she said, "they didn't just dry out a nuisance patch. They cut the wet out of the whole quarter. That changes wind. Hold. Yield. Everything after. Men in town called it progress because the ground looked flatter and their boots stayed cleaner." She tapped the dark oval. "If Ezra had paper about this, then his shame and your mother's debt and August's piety have all been sleeping in the same bed."

Dell's eyes flicked to hers. "You think Pa knew."

"I think he knew enough to hide a paper from his wife."

"That's not the same."

"No. It isn't. Which is why we look."

He put both hands on the table. The boards complained. "Ma can't know yet."

Hester's first answer rose hot: that Cora was not a child to be managed, that secrecy had already done enough work in this town, that she had no wish to join another line of people keeping a woman outside her own life. But Dell was not asking to keep Cora ignorant forever. He was asking for a day, maybe two, to bring her something firmer than a vision in a jar. The request was wrong in the ordinary way and right in the way seasons sometimes forced on people.

"She'll know if she asks me direct," Hester said.

"All right."

"Don't make me a liar for the sake of your stomach."

"I won't."

"You say that now."

He held her gaze. His face had gone older while they talked, not by much, only by the small amount a bad fact could age a person in one kitchen. "If we find nothing, I shut up and put the drawer back the way it is. If we find something, she gets the truth from us before it comes out of town mouths. That's what I'm asking."

Hester looked at the jars cooling on the sill. They sat gray in glass, nothing and not nothing. Otto's hand was gone from his jar. Ezra had gone from his. The world had already spent those witnesses. Waiting for the next storm meant waiting for chance, and chance had too often favored men with papers and dry collars.

Outside, the eastern light strengthened enough to show the alley ruts through the window. A wagon rolled somewhere on Main Street. In another fifteen minutes the first customer would come wanting coffee, bread, gossip, or credit. The town would put its hands all over the day and smear it.

Hester folded the map along its old creases and slid it back into the waxed paper. She chose a smaller sack for it instead of the drawer, one she could tuck under her apron if need be.

"Tomorrow at first light," she said. "You do the horse and chicken work before your mother wakes enough to count your footsteps. Then you come by the alley. Bring a spade if you can manage it without a speech from Cora."

Dell nodded once.

"And Dell."

"What."

"Eat the rest of that bread. Investigations don't keep a boy upright."

He almost grinned then, and because he did not quite manage it, the expression hurt her more than if he had broken down. He took another bite obediently. Apple butter shone dark on the crust.

"If this turns out to be nothing," he said through the bread, "you'll say so."

"I always do."

"Even if it makes you look foolish."

Hester screwed the lid tighter on the dead jar between them. "Especially then."

He swallowed and set down the last of the heel. "All right."

It was not agreement exactly. It was enlistment.

When he left by the back door a minute later, he carried his shoulders differently, as if the weight had shifted but not lightened. Hester watched through the flour-fogged pane while he cut across the alley toward the side street, head down against a wind too small to deserve the name and still full of grit enough to sting.

Then she turned back to her table, to the row of jars that had stopped being foolish in the dark and the dough that still had to be punched down before breakfast trade. The practical work remained. That was almost a comfort.

She set Otto's map beside the sponge crock where she could reach it, rolled up her sleeves, and drove both fists into the risen dough. It gave way warm and alive under her knuckles, breathing out sour sweetness and trapped air. Outside, the light climbed over Morrow's boarded storefronts and thin trees and all the ground men had called improved until it began to die. Hester was done waiting for weather to speak first. In the morning she and Dell would go south to Saint's Basin and make the land answer in daylight.
