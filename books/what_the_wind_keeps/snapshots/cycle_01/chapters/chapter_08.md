# Chapter 8

The key stuck twice in the swollen lock, and when the door finally gave the schoolhouse breathed stale chalk and old dust into Thea's face. She stood a moment on the threshold with her satchel against her hip and let her eyes adjust to the dimness. Seven desks still stood in rows. Three more had been shoved to the back wall after the McClearys and both Rausch boys went west in the night without waiting to tell anybody goodbye. The Nebraska map hung crooked above the stove. A thumbprint of gray dirt had blurred the panhandle. Somebody, months ago, had carved a careful D into the lid of the back desk and then worried a groove through the tail of it with a pocketknife.

Inside her satchel, under the attendance ledger, lay the school board's note telling her to prepare a final inventory of books, slates, hinges, stove pipe, and salvageable furniture. The handwriting belonged to Mrs. Byers, who had once brought lemon cakes for Christmas programs and now wrote as if the room had already become lumber. Thea kept the note folded. If she opened it first thing, the day would belong to that hand. She set her satchel on the desk, unhooked the windows, and swept narrow paths between the benches with the broom kept behind the cloak hooks. Dust lifted, glittered in the flattened yellow light, and settled back onto the floorboards as if it had nowhere else left to go.

She wrote the date on the blackboard. Beneath it she wrote, in a firmer hand than she felt: Spelling. Arithmetic. Dinner. Song. The word Dinner looked faintly ridiculous among the rest, which was part of the point. Yesterday she had sent the remaining children home with instructions to bring whatever food could be spared and no apologies tied to it. It had been a bold thing to write in a town where every egg had a cash value and every loaf already belonged partly to somebody else. Still, if the school was going to die, she was not going to let it do so by inches and account books.

Irene Dvorak came first, eleven years old and already argumentative in the manner of a woman twice her age, with her dead brother's suspenders clipped over a flour-sack dress because she did not trust buttons to hold. She stamped her shoes on the threshold, sent up a cuff of powder, and said, "Are we truly having lessons, or are we only clearing out?"

"We're having lessons," Thea said. "You may save disappointment for later."

Irene considered that and nodded as if she had granted permission. She went to her desk and took out a pencil sharpened with such fury it looked bitten at the tip.

Opal Jensen followed with her lunch tied in a blue-checked rag and the same chipped front tooth she worried with her tongue whenever she was thinking hard. After her came Paulie Reed, who smelled faintly of axle grease even at eight because his uncle had him under a truck at dawn more often than in church, his cowlick standing up through the dust like wire. Mabel Kruse arrived last among the children, long-legged and thirteen and mortified by nearly everything, with a paring knife in her pocket for pencil work and a hem let down twice in the same month.

They looked at the board, at Dinner, at the room with its back-row ghost desks, and then at Thea. She felt the whole morning balance there. If she faltered, the day would collapse into the dry official business waiting inside her satchel.

"Copy your spelling words," she said. "And if anybody asks, tell them I haven't gone soft. I'm merely lowering standards in a historically memorable fashion."

That earned her what she had wanted: Opal's startled bark of laughter, Mabel pretending not to smile, Paulie climbing into his seat too fast and nearly tipping it over.

Dell came in without knocking, which had always been Dell's method with buildings that had once belonged to him. He had grown too tall for the room years ago, but he still entered it as if he expected his younger body to be waiting somewhere inside. His hair had gone almost white at the ends from dust and sun, and he had a burlap sack slung over one shoulder that clinked when he dropped it by the stove. Nails, hinges, and a bent latch spilled half out of the mouth.

"Found these in the shed out back," he said. "Thought maybe the board would want to count every damned screw before it lets the place rot."

Thea did not look at the carved D in the back desk. "Language."

Paulie grinned. Mabel rolled her eyes in the superior manner of the newly old.

Dell leaned one shoulder against the wall and looked everywhere except at the open desks. "I ain't staying. Mrs. Blum said she'd be along."

"Then you may not stay from the back desk," Thea said. "If you're going to haunt the place, do it properly."

The children's faces turned toward him. The room waited to see if he would bolt. Dell's mouth twitched with unwilling amusement. He crossed to the back row, folded himself into the desk with his knees almost against his chest, and muttered, "Seat got smaller."

"You got larger."

"Wasn't my doing."

That did it. Even Mabel laughed outright. Something in the room eased. The pressure did not disappear. It merely shifted a half inch off their throats.

Thea gave them words that belonged to no test and would help none of them with county advancement: weather, thimble, machinery, meadow, necessary. Irene objected to necessary on principle. Paulie inserted an extra a into meadow and defended it stoutly. Mabel misspelled machinery the first time and went red enough to show through the dust on her cheeks. Dell, from the back desk, kept his eyes on the scarred wood and still supplied answers under his breath before anybody else could reach them.

"If you're going to cheat," Thea said, "you might at least do it in a way that flatters the teacher."

"I wasn't cheating."

"You were vibrating the answer into the room like a telegraph wire."

"I was talking to myself."

"Then say something less useful."

He ducked his head, but she saw the corner of his mouth give. Dell in company always carried himself like a boy walking with a nail in his boot. If he slowed down enough to take it out, somebody might notice where he hurt. Yet when she set Paulie to the board with a column of subtraction and he froze halfway through the borrowing, Dell could not help himself. He got out of the desk, took the chalk, and worked the problem in three quick strokes, all angles and impatience.

"You don't carry the one there," he said to Paulie, not looking at him. "You'll gum the whole business."

"Mr. Vale," Thea said.

"I ain't a mister."

"No. But you're also not a substitute teacher."

Paulie peered at the figures. "You write mean."

"So do you."

"Enough," Thea said, though she was smiling when she turned back to the class. "If Mr. Not-a-Mister Vale would like to return to his illegally occupied seat, the lawful business of education may continue."

Dell snorted once through his nose and sat down again. He left the solution on the board. The chalk dust on his fingers made his hands look older than the rest of him.

By ten the room had warmed. Flies worried the window corners. Thea set the arithmetic slates aside and took out the dog-eared reader with the loose spine, but the lesson kept slipping free of her. Paulie asked whether buffalo were truly as numerous as the book claimed or whether writers lied from habit. Irene wanted to know why Nebraska had a legislature if August Poeschel decided everything worth deciding. Mabel, who had been quiet all morning, said the train west of Holdrege carried whole families on the roof now according to her cousin's postcard, and Opal asked whether California smelled different from here or only looked it. Ordinary questions, absurd questions, frightened questions wearing their work clothes. Thea answered what she could and deflected what she could not. She heard herself doing it, the careful classroom balancing act, and hated how much practice she had at making uncertainty sound civilized.

At noon she pushed the front table against the wall beneath the blackboard and laid out the food as it arrived. A loaf from Hester's bakery appeared first, still warm enough to dampen the towel around it, dark crust cracked along the top where the oven had caught it hard. Then came a stoneware jar of pickled cucumbers from Mrs. Jensen, who did not come in, only handed them through the door with beet-red knuckles and a muttered, "Use them up before they go soft." Opal untied her rag bundle to reveal two cold boiled potatoes and half an onion. Paulie had brought three radishes with the greens still on and an apple the size of a ball bearing. Irene, affronted by scarcity, produced a jar of gooseberry preserves wrapped in yesterday's newspaper as if unveiling silver.

Hester Blum arrived carrying a tin of butterless biscuits against her stomach, her apron striped with flour and her dust cough tucked away somewhere behind her ribs by what looked like force of character alone. She set the tin down and took in the table with one sweep of her sharp old eyes.

"That'll pass for a feast if nobody gets proud," she said. "Miss Bell, if this is school policy from now on, I should've hired on years ago."

"The pay is worse than you think," Thea said.

"So is mine."

Paulie reached for a biscuit before anybody had sat down. Hester slapped the back of his hand with two dry fingers, not hard enough to sting.

"You'll keep all five of those if you wait thirty seconds," she said. "A man who steals hot bread makes a fool of himself in public. I've seen it happen."

"Who?"

"Otto, twice. One time with raspberry jam down his shirt."

That gave them another laugh, and because the laughter belonged to bread and not to survival it rang differently in the room. Thea felt it in the boards under her shoes.

The second adult to arrive was Cora.

She came in with a lard tin wrapped in a clean dish towel and the look she wore in town when she intended to give nobody a loose thread to pull. Her hair had been pinned up in a hurry and one ash-blond strand had already worked free by her ear. Fine dust lay in the seams of her cuffs. Thea noticed that before she noticed anything else. She always noticed what the weather had managed to do to Cora.

"Dell forgot his dinner on the table," Cora said. "So either you feed him or he starts gnawing the furniture."

"I don't," Dell said.

"You would if the day ran long enough."

Her voice was spare and flat enough for anybody listening, just Mrs. Vale being practical in Miss Bell's schoolhouse. That was the trouble with loving her in daylight. Every safe sentence came wrapped around the one Thea could not answer.

Thea took the tin from her. The handle bit into her palm through the towel. Inside were hard-boiled eggs already shelled and halved, the yolks mashed with a little mustard and more care than the town had any business permitting itself. Six eggs. Thea knew exactly what six eggs meant at the Vale house now. She said only, "Thank you, Mrs. Vale."

Cora's fingers brushed hers once at the handoff. Nothing. Less than nothing. A touch that could belong to crockery. But Thea felt the room take notice all the same, not because anyone had seen too much, only because secrecy trained the nerves to imagine witnesses everywhere. Hester had turned to cut the loaf and was talking to Irene about whether gooseberries deserved sugar. Dell was prying open the biscuit tin with an expression of concentrated innocence. Even so Thea could not stop the stupid leap in her chest, or the sharper feeling under it: resentment that gratitude had to sound like schoolroom propriety when last week Cora's mouth had been against the inside of her wrist.

Cora glanced once at the table. "Looks respectable."

"That's more than can be said for the singing to come," Hester said.

"Who's singing?"

"All of them. It's the price of biscuits."

"Then God help the biscuits," Cora said.

There it was, brief as the touch had been: the line of dry humor Thea knew from lamplight and bed-edge and not from classrooms. She wanted to look up too fast, which was exactly why she did not.

They ate at desks because the wind had risen outside and sent dust scratching along the south wall in low steady passes. The children traded food in solemn violation of every ordinary rule: a potato for half a radish, a biscuit for a spoon of gooseberry, an egg half for a pickle. Hester spread preserves with the back of a knife and passed pieces down the row. Cora, after a token refusal, sat on the windowsill with her hat in her lap and peeled the shell fragments from the towel one by one, a small practical occupation for hands that had no safe place to rest. Dell pretended not to watch whether she ate enough.

For ten minutes the talk was not about departure, debt, or weather. Paulie wanted to know whether coyotes would eat soap if given the chance. Irene claimed her uncle in Broken Bow had once sung so badly a mule kicked through a fence to escape him. Mabel, after prolonged resistance, admitted she had read half a novel hidden inside her civics book and did not regret it. Hester said novels were the only respectable place left for foolishness. Cora said foolishness had always found more economical rooms than that. Thea listened to them and felt, with a kind of ache she had not earned, how quickly a table could begin to resemble a town if enough people sat down without an agenda.

The bad song began because Paulie asked for it with his mouth still full and because Hester, who enjoyed disorder as long as somebody else had to sweep it up, insisted the room could not call itself a schoolhouse on its last decent day without music. Thea chose "Red River Valley" not because it was wise but because everyone knew at least part of it and because she had no strength for pretending the word leaving had not set up house in Morrow already.

"If you sing it like a funeral," she said, "I will mark you all absent in spirit."

"Can you do that?" Opal asked.

"With a sufficiently vindictive pen."

They started too low. Mabel went flat on purpose until Irene elbowed her. Paulie rushed the words, then slowed so much on the next line he nearly toppled the whole thing into a hymn. Dell tried to keep out of it and failed after the second verse when Hester fixed him with a look older than the state and said, "You've got a sound box same as anybody. Use it."

"I'd rather dig fence posts."

"That can be arranged after you sing."

So he sang, grudgingly, with that adolescent roughness still undecided whether to become a man's voice or break apart trying. It made the others laugh, then steadied them. By the time they reached the final chorus they were not good, but they were loud, and there was a difference. Cora had bent her head, smiling into the edge of her hat. When she looked up once, just once, Thea saw open amusement there instead of the defended set she carried through bank offices and public roads. It was almost enough to make the room dangerous.

The song ended in a clatter of wrong notes and laughter. Hester gave a single dry clap. "There," she said. "If the bank closes the school after that, it's acting out of mercy."

Even Dell laughed at that, though he ducked it quickly, rubbed one thumb over the carved groove in his desk lid, and went still again. Thea saw the motion. She said nothing. He had scratched that letter years ago with all the secret seriousness boys gave to wood they expected would keep them forever. Now his hand dwarfed it.

After dinner the school day thinned in the ordinary way. Thea read aloud while Paulie swung one leg and lost the thread every third sentence. Mabel copied a page of penmanship without complaint, which was practically a public miracle. Irene and Opal quarreled over whether Nebraska rivers counted if they had gone to mud. Dell fixed the loose latch on the north window with one of the screws from his sack and refused to admit he was doing maintenance for a building slated for death.

Then the hour arrived when children begin to lean toward the door no matter what a teacher says. Thea dismissed them one by one with leftover biscuit wrapped in paper and instructions to bring back the borrowed readers only if the readers survived summer. Irene marched out with her suspenders crosswise and her preserves jar empty. Opal tucked the onion skin from dinner into her pocket because she hated waste even in its smallest form. Paulie left still singing the wrong line of the chorus. Mabel paused on the threshold long enough to say, without looking directly at Thea, "It wasn't bad, today," and then was gone before the reply could embarrass either of them.

Cora had left earlier, after gathering the towel and the empty tin. She had said she needed lamp wicks from the mercantile and had not looked back from the doorway. Thea had watched the dust mark her shoulders through the window until she turned the corner. Wanting more than that from a public leave-taking felt childish. She wanted it anyway.

Dell stayed.

He remained in the back desk after the others had gone, one boot braced against the floor rung, scraping chalk dust into pale crescents with his thumbnail. The room around his long body looked built for a version of him already missing. Thea stacked copybooks and waited him out. She had taught enough boys on the verge of manhood to know that if she asked the real question first, she would get only the lie.

"You may go," she said at last. "School isn't a prison unless the county improves its funding considerably."

"I know."

"Then why are you still here?"

He shrugged without lifting his eyes. "Seat's cooler than outside."

"Liar."

"Maybe."

He ran the pad of his thumb over the carved D again. "Forgot I'd cut that."

"You were very proud of your initial."

"Didn't have much else to brag on at ten."

"You had long division."

"Hell of a courtship."

She laughed before she meant to. Dell glanced up, surprised into almost-smiling. Beneath the wariness and the adolescent meanness he put on like another shirt, there was still the boy who used to sit scowling through recitation and then hand in copywork neat enough to shame half the room. He hated being seen in that continuity.

"You miss it," Thea said, softer now.

He straightened too fast, the way he did whenever tenderness got near him with its hands out. "I didn't say that."

"No."

"It's just a room."

"Yes."

He looked around at the maps, the scarred desktops, the cracked glass over the alphabet strip, all of it. "Thought it'd feel better when it shut," he said. "Thought I'd be glad to see the end of one more thing telling me where to sit." He rubbed his palm down his thigh as if trying to wipe the words off after he'd spoken them. "It doesn't. That's all."

Thea set the last copybook down. "No," she said. "It doesn't."

He absorbed that in silence. Outside, a wagon rattled past on the road toward the depot, the iron tire catching stones. Somewhere farther off a screen door slapped twice and kept slapping in the wind.

"Mrs. Blum's got men coming tonight," Dell said finally. "She's making bread and plans at the same time, which ought to be illegal."

"Plans for what?"

He gave her a sideways look, measuring what he wanted to tell and what belonged elsewhere. "South of our west field. By the old basin. She says if we wait till July the ground will set like crockery and stay shut. Couple fellows from north road might come with spades if their wives let them. August sure as hell won't like it."

So there it was, not rumor anymore but shape. Hester and Dell had found something with enough reality to gather bodies around it. Thea thought of Cora sitting on the windowsill with the egg towel in her hands, saying almost nothing because public rooms made speech expensive. Ten days to auction, August said. South of the fields, Dell said. Every road in town presently led either outward or toward a fight.

"Does your mother know?" Thea asked.

"Knows enough." Dell stood up, the desk groaning under the release. "I told you because you'll hear it by supper and think folks have gone touched. Maybe they have. Still."

"Still."

He picked up the sack of screws and latch pieces. At the door he paused without turning. "You ought to keep the books a while longer," he said. "Not for lessons. Just because."

"That's not a category the county recognizes."

"To hell with categories."

Then he was out, long strides down the path, hatless in the glare, heading toward the bakery and whatever Hester meant to begin.

The room changed when he left. Schoolrooms always did once children were truly gone; they became a species of stage after the actors had stripped the place of noise. Thea moved through the desks, righting the ones Paulie had skewed, collecting onion skin and pencil shavings, stacking the slates. She should have opened Mrs. Byers's note then. She should have begun the inventory of state property, counted the readers, tested the hinges Dell had just repaired, and prepared to hand back a key and a ledger like any respectable woman facing the end of her employment.

Instead she sat at the desk and drew the folded Lincoln timetable from between the pages of the grammar book where she had hidden it. The paper had softened along the creases from being handled in secret. Thursday morning train east, connection onward. If she left before dawn, if the roads held, if the depot agent still honored the fare as quoted, if she arrived in Lincoln looking more like a librarian than a dusted-out schoolteacher with too many private entanglements, then there was still a life on the other side of the paper.

She had tried to keep that possibility clean. That was the only way she could bear it. On paper, Lincoln was oak tables and catalog drawers, steady salary, a room of her own, a hat she could brush once and have it stay brushed. On paper, leaving had nothing to do with Cora's body in the dark or Dell's unfinished face in the back desk or Hester coughing over a table of men who meant to go dig up an old civic sin before August could lawyer it dead. On paper, departure was professional advancement, prudence, a woman taking the chance she would be a fool to refuse.

The blackboard had gone gray again while she sat there. The children had smudged the lower edge with cuffs and shoulders; Dell's subtraction problem remained ghosted beneath the afternoon light. Thea rose, took the rag, and wiped a broad wet stroke across the slate where Song still stood in chalk.

The memory came up under her hand.

Not all at once. First the black brightened as if lamplight had been lit behind it. Then sound, thin and scratchy, a fiddle sawing at a dance tune fast enough to trip a person into joy or injury. The schoolroom filled itself backward. Benches shoved to the walls. Paper chains drooping from the rafters. Women in good dresses gone shiny at the elbows. Men with collars open and faces red from coffee and heat. A pie table against the far window.

Ezra Vale stood in the middle of it in shirtsleeves and stocking feet, younger than the photographs on Cora's dresser and broader through the chest than grief had allowed Thea to imagine. His suspenders hung loose at the hips because someone had dared him to dance without the boots. He slapped one palm against his thigh, missed the beat, caught it badly, and launched into a clumsy clog that had all the grace of a gate blown half off its hinges. Laughter cracked around him. Not cruel laughter. The kind a room gives a man who has made himself ridiculous on purpose because other people need a reason to feel good in their own skins.

Cora was there against the wall, not young exactly, not girlish, only unguarded. She had a pie plate braced to her skirt with one hand and the other over her mouth. Her laughter bent her at the waist. Dell, smaller by years and all elbows, beat time with both palms on a desktop until somebody shooed him back. Ezra kicked too hard, nearly slid, windmilled for balance, and grinned straight at Cora.

"I paid fifty cents for that pie," he said, breathless and pleased with himself. "I'm earning every bite."

Then the rag passed once more over the slate and the schoolroom went back to dust, chalk, and late light.

Thea stood with the damp cloth in her hand until the water ran down her wrist and spotted the floor. She had not wanted Ezra sanctified. The town already did enough of that work for the dead, especially dead men, sanding them smooth until the living could not get a grip on what had actually been suffered near them. But she had not wanted this either, not the absurd competence of his body gone foolish for a laugh, not Cora's face opened by delight that belonged to a season before widowhood and debt and secret bedclothes. In some mean compartment of herself, Thea had preferred him burdened, ashamed, complicated by bargains and lies. Easier to love beside a ghost if the ghost could be reduced to a caution.

He would not stay reduced. That, she understood as the rag cooled in her fist, was part of what the dust kept doing to them. It refused clean enemies and clean martyrs. Ezra had signed something he should not have signed. Ezra had probably hidden things. Ezra had also once danced like a fool in his stockings so a roomful of tired people could laugh and so Cora would laugh with them. Cora had loved a man who could do that. Thea had not been wrong to love her afterward. She had only been wrong whenever she pretended the dead man could be made simple enough to step around.

Outside, the wind hit the south wall in a stronger sheet, then slackened. The timetable crackled in her other hand where she had carried it up from the desk without knowing she had done so. Thursday morning. Connection onward. Lincoln by afternoon if the trains behaved and the dust did not.

South of the Vale fields, Hester was gathering men and spades. In the Vale kitchen, Cora would be rinsing the egg tin and counting the remaining money in her head because counting it on paper made the shortage look official. Dell would be pretending he had not sat through spelling and song like a boy with nowhere better to put his grief. Mrs. Byers wanted an inventory. August wanted obedience. Lincoln wanted a woman who could arrive on time and leave the rest of herself behind.

Thea looked at the blackboard again, at the place where Ezra had vanished back into chalk haze. Then she folded the timetable once along its old crease, once across it, and tore it clean down the middle. The sound was small. It still felt decisive enough to alter the air. She tore the halves again, then the quarters, until Lincoln was only a few black letters stranded on separate scraps in her hand.

She dropped the pieces into the empty stove and laid one match to them from the desk box. The paper curled fast. A corner flared blue, then orange. For a second the printed departure times shone brighter than the ash around them. Then they blackened, collapsed inward, and were gone.

Tomorrow could carry forms and keys and the polite murder of a public room. Tonight there was bread at Hester's bakery, a fight gathering over Saint's Basin, and the long west road back to the Vale house where Cora might or might not be waiting in the yard when she got there. None of that promised anything clean. It was not safety. It was not wisdom by the standards of anybody who read employment letters with common sense. It was, however, a choice.

Thea closed the damp rag inside the cupboard, took up her satchel, and finally opened Mrs. Byers's note only long enough to see the list of items to be counted. Desks. Maps. Window glass. Stove pipe. She folded it shut again and tucked it under the ledger. Outside, the sky had gone the color of rubbed brass over town. She locked the schoolhouse, tested the knob once, and started west with dust gathering at the hem of her skirt and the taste of smoke from the burned timetable still faint on the back of her tongue.
