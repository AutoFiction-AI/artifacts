# Chapter 13

The county inventory sheet rasped against Thea's palm as she stepped into the schoolhouse. The room had already begun to look less like a place children used than a room somebody meant to empty. The blackboard still held a pale fan of chalk where she had scrubbed out sums three days ago, but the desks stood in uneven ranks now, two of them already gone home with families who had asked whether the district would mind if they took what had once belonged to their own children. The map of the United States had been rolled up and tied with string. The flag lay on her desk like a folded shirt waiting to be claimed by a body larger than the one it had fit.

The county sheet called the closure a suspension pending consolidation. It made the thing sound temporary, almost courteous. Thea had read the line three times the evening it arrived and still wanted to take a penknife to the word suspension. Nothing in Morrow had been suspended. It had been starved. The school had not closed on one day. It had thinned one desk at a time, one set of overshoes left too long under a bench, one child absent for planting and then absent for good, one family westbound under quilts and rope and dust.

She set her satchel by the stove, unpinned her hat, and opened the east windows as far as the swollen frames allowed. Heat came in anyway. It had a baked, dirty smell, as if the road outside were being toasted and ground at once. Dust lay in the window tracks, in the grooves of the floorboards, in the leather strap around the globe's equator. When she drew her finger along the ledge it made a clean line and then clouded over again almost at once.

On the desk sat the district ledger, the key ring, three official forms, and the stack of copybooks she was meant to box for whichever larger town ended up swallowing Morrow's pupils. She was to count primers, readers, slates, chalk, composition tablets, maps, stove tools, and ink bottles. She was to note what was salvageable and what was not. She was not, apparently, to note the reason half the third readers still smelled faintly of camphor because their owners had kept them near sickbeds. She was not to note that little Elsie Brand's blue hair ribbon was still wound through the hinge of her desk lid because Elsie had gone to Colorado so abruptly in May that her mother had forgotten to come back for it. She was not to note that Owen Reece, who had once hidden dried grasshoppers in the coal scuttle, had quit school not because he had become dull or lazy but because his father coughed pink into a handkerchief and needed him in the field.

She made herself begin with the books.

There were twenty-one first readers, though only sixteen could honestly be called usable. The cardboard corners had furred open in the damp spring years before the drought, and later dust had worked into the cloth bindings until each cover felt skinned. The second readers were fewer. Two McGuffeys had their backs gone entirely, pages sewn together long ago with white thread by hands that had not been hers. The state geography books lived on the highest shelf because the younger children did not need them and the older ones had dwindled to almost none.

Thea climbed onto the chair by the back wall and began taking them down one at a time.

The room was quiet in a way it had never been quiet when school was only between days. A living schoolhouse kept little sounds in it even when empty: the ping of cooling stove iron, a marble lost in a crack, laughter seeming to sit up in the rafters and wait for morning. This quiet was flatter. It felt official. She could hear a fly worrying itself against a pane and the faint scrape of her own skirt against the chair rung. Outside, somewhere down the block, somebody hammered crate boards together. The rhythm carried the whole length of town now and seemed to say the same thing at every door: pack, nail, go.

She set the first three geography books on the desk and dusted the spines with her handkerchief. *Our Nation and Its Lands.* *The World and Commerce.* *Nebraska and the Great Plains.* Old state texts, thick and earnest, full of rivers that still seemed dependable because the pages had been printed before the weather turned mean for good.

The fourth one did not come free.

She tugged and felt paste tear with a dry little rip. The book slid forward all at once, heavier than it ought to have been, and struck the desktop hard enough to startle her off the chair. It bounced once and fell open on its face.

At first she thought some boy had cut the middle out years ago to hide marbles or licorice, because the pages had spread around a dark square cavity the size of a man's hand. Then she saw the care of it.

The outer leaves had been pasted solid along all three edges, trimmed true with a blade that knew how to keep a line. The hollow itself had not been hacked. It had been worked down through the paper in patient layers, each cut exact enough that the corners met clean. A folded packet of oilcloth lay inside. Beneath it rested another fold of paper gone soft at the creases.

Thea stood very still, one palm on the desk.

There were plenty of men in Morrow who could cut straight, but not many who would take that much time with paper. Ezra Vale would have. He had carried impatience in his body, but when something frightened him badly enough he turned meticulous. She had seen it once at the Christmas program three winters earlier when one of the boys snapped a ruler before the recitations began. The child had gone red and near tears, certain he would be whipped at home for breaking school property. Ezra, who had come to bring in stove wood and stay for Dell's reading, sat down on the back bench with his pocketknife, shaved the splintered end clean, and whittled the length true again while the children sang. He had sanded it with the side of a matchbox until the ruler looked merely old instead of ruined. When the boy thanked him afterward, Ezra had shrugged like a man embarrassed by being watched doing something gentle.

The oilcloth packet had been tied with plain thread. The paper underneath showed pencil marks through the fold. Thea knew before she touched either of them that she had not found a prank.

She looked toward the door, though no one had entered and no wagon had stopped outside. Her own breath came back at her off the hot room. She set the official inventory sheet aside so carefully it was almost reverent, then drew the book nearer and lifted the contents out.

The thread broke at once. The cloth opened on two sheets of lined paper, folded small, the blue rule faded with age and handling. Ezra's hand ran across the first page. She knew it from notes he had sent with Dell about chores, from the one apology he had written when Dell missed a week in lambing season, from labels on seed jars in the Vale pantry. The letters were plain and serviceable, a little right-leaning, as if even in writing he wanted to get the whole thing finished before courage ran out.

Under the letter lay the map.

It was not a school map. It was drawn on thin paper, copied from something older and more exact. A rough oval marked the basin south of the Vale fields. Narrow lines showed the tile run and diversion ditch. In one corner, in smaller pencil, were figures that looked like measurements and a hand different from Ezra's, steadier and finer. Otto Blum's, Thea thought. Or Ezra copying Otto so carefully that his own hand had almost disappeared trying to behave like another man's.

On the margin someone had written: *holds spring water here until June if left alone.*

Lower down: *If cut, north field dries faster but wind takes what roots no longer hold.*

Thea lowered herself into the nearest pupil desk because her knees had gone uncertain. The seat bit the backs of her thighs. It had been built for a child and punished adults for forgetting it.

The first line of the letter read:

Cora,

If you are seeing this, then I did not say it living. Miss Bell may have found it first. That is no insult to you. It is only that she handles books, and you would have no call to come hunting in a school shelf. I put it here because men can search a house top to bottom and never think to weigh a geography in the hand.

Thea let the page lower an inch.

He had not known about her and Cora; she did not believe that for an instant. He had known, though, that the school would likely outlast everybody except the woman paid to tend it, and he had used that knowledge the way desperate people used any dependable thing left within reach. The thought made her skin prickle under her dress. Even dead, he had put paper in her hands and made her part of the household's accounting.

She read on.

I kept waiting for a day when there was only one wrong thing to say, and there never was. There was the note and the seed bill and the taxes and Dell's boots and the kitchen roof. Then there was my pride sitting on top of all of it.

You know Otto warned against touching Saint's Basin. Hester warned too, in her fashion. I heard them. I signed anyway.

Thea's thumb stuck to the edge of the page. Dust had worked into the paper fibers. She eased it free and continued.

August laid the note and the tax paper in front of me. He said the south meadow sat wet and useless and that if the basin was cut and tiled there would be one more strip to sow and maybe one more season to breathe. He said if the acreage looked cleaner he could carry me a little longer. I let him say it. Then I said yes.

I told myself a wet place was not a moral thing. I told myself wheat fed people and standing water did not. The plain truth is I was scared and sick of coming in from the field to see you making supper out of almost nothing. I wanted one more season and put my name where it should not have gone.

Thea looked up from the letter and found the schoolhouse gone strange around it. She saw the cracked varnish on the desktop, the chalk rubbed into the grain, and the row of blank hooks by the door where no coats would hang in autumn. She had known, in the broad sense, that debt made cowards and liars. But hearing the bargain in Ezra's own hand was different. He had traded a meadow for time at the bank, and he had told himself it was necessity. August was not clean in it, but neither was this accident.

She read the next paragraph once, then again because the first pass had gone by in a rush of heat.

I went down there when they opened the line. I did not tell you that part. The men were joking because men will joke over almost any damage so they do not have to hear it while it is happening. They had the shovel bites already in and the tile stacked. When the first water found the cut, it ran quick and ugly, brown as dishwater and alive with things that had been still a moment before. A frog came out in the wash and slapped itself against the bank. One of the men kicked it back with his boot because the ditch was where the work was, not the bank. I knew Otto had been right before the last spade went in, and I let it stand because I had already signed and did not know how to be a fool in front of other men after I had been one alone.

The page trembled very slightly where she held it. The movement was too small to call shaking, but it kept the words from settling in one place.

She could smell the basin now though the schoolhouse sat blocks away from it: wet clay broken open in the sun, old roots, the bitter mineral smell that had come off Dell's clothes after the dig. She saw again, because she could not help it, the way his face had gone gray under the dust when they pulled him out, the left arm wrong, the boy he had saved crying into Hester's apron. It would have been simpler if Ezra's letter had delivered a name for the villain and left the rest alone. Instead it put his hand on the first wrong and August's on the next and left the land itself carrying both.

Thea read further.

Afterward I told you less than half. That was mine. I said the basin was men's talk and not for the kitchen. That was a lie. It was our ground and your labor and Dell's breath the same as mine. I kept it from you because I could not bear to hear the whole sound of it in the room.

August's crookedness came after. He said he could make the paper hold for another season if I signed what he put down and let him tend the rest. I believed what kept me moving. When I later saw the date was not what he told me, I was sick behind the shed. He cheated me, but he did not put my hand to the first wrong.

Don't let them say I knew nothing. I knew enough to be ashamed, and then I knew more and kept quiet.

Thea pressed her lips together until the taste of chalk found its way onto her tongue.

It was no absolution for August, no rescue for Cora, and no plea for mercy. Ezra had written like a man finally sick of the tidier lie.

She turned to the second page.

I copied Otto's basin marks because I wanted paper against paper. The line looked ugly there too. Keep them together.

I won't ask forgiveness. I had chances to speak and let them rot.

Do what you think you must with this. Burn it if that is kinder. Show it if the bank starts calling me a fool who never knew. I won't ask you to lay me away better than I was. I loved you poorly in this. That is the plainest sentence I have.

Ezra

Thea sat with the paper open across her lap and heard, from somewhere out on Main Street, a truck backfiring twice before it caught. A dog answered, and then the quiet took the room again.

The room remained hot and official. A dead fly lay in the trough of the window frame. The county's form still waited under her elbow with its lines for usable maps and damaged readers. Yet August had lost the comfort of standing alone in the story. Ezra stood in it beside him now, not as prey only but as a man who had signed, watched, understood, and then gone home without telling his wife what he had done.

What came next in her mind was Cora. Cora would have to read *I signed anyway* and fit it against every small mercy Ezra had left behind: the careful seed labels, the mended harness, the coat peg that still hung empty by the door. Dell would have to hear that his father had not simply been cornered by a banker but had helped start the damage that later rose back up through the basin and into his own broken body. The truth in her lap did not free either of them. It only gave their hurt another edge.

She read the last line again. *I loved you poorly in this.* That sentence hurt Thea in a place she was ashamed to name. There were nights in the Vale house when Cora came apart only after the lamp was turned low and Dell had gone quiet in the next room, nights when desire and grief lay so close together Thea could not have said which one she had touched first. Ezra had always been there anyway: in the money tin, in Dell's face, in the peg where his coat had hung. Carrying this letter home would wound Cora, but it would also drag the dead man out of the gentler place grief had made for him between them. Thea could not pretend she was free of wanting that.

She went back to the map. Otto's notes, or Ezra's careful copy of them, were small enough she had to lean close. The old wet meadow lay there without romance. The page listed measurements, depths, and the direction of runoff. One note by the basin had been smudged and redrawn. Another, near the north edge, read: *buffalo grass held here before plow.* The paper refused myth. The basin had held water until June, the roots had held the ground, and men had cut it open because they thought need gave them leave.

Burning the packet would still be easier. She could strike a match from the box in her desk drawer and feed each page to the stove's black mouth. August would remain the whole visible sin. Ezra would remain the trapped husband Cora had been surviving on. Dell would be spared one more fracture in a summer already crowded with them. And Thea would become one more careful person in Morrow deciding what somebody else could bear.

The idea sickened her not because silence was always wrong. Silence had saved her often enough. Silence was how she boarded in a widow's house without becoming a story men could use against both of them. Silence was how a hand found another hand in the dark and lived to do it again the next night. But there was a difference between refusing a town's hunger and helping a lie stay neat inside a family because love preferred the neat version.

She laid the letter on the desk and stood too quickly. The little chair screeched back. The sound seemed rude in the empty room.

At the washbasin in the corner she poured a thin stream from the pitcher and sluiced her wrists. The water came off the enamel already warm. Dust turned to paste on her skin and ran into the bowl. She watched it collect there, gray-brown, the color of every road in town, and thought of how often people spoke of being soiled as if dirt itself were moral. The basin water cared nothing for guilt. It took what came to it. Paper was harder.

She folded the map with greater care than she felt and slipped it back under the letter. The oilcloth had gone soft at the corners. She wrapped both pages and paper together and held the bundle in one hand, testing the weight. It was absurd how little physical heft a ruined story required.

The geography book still lay open on the desk, its carved-out middle naked to the room. Thea touched the inner wall of the cavity with one finger. The paper edge rasped her skin. Ezra had not cut this in a panic. He had done it over time, probably at a table by lamplight, pages pressed under a board so the knife would not drag. She pictured him stopping every few minutes to listen for Cora's step or Dell's. She pictured him pasting the edges and setting the thing near the stove to dry, then carrying it back to the schoolhouse under his arm on some errand respectable enough not to draw notice. Shame could make craftsmen out of people who had never before wanted such work.

She ought to finish the inventory.

The thought arrived and nearly made her laugh. The county could wait for its numbers. Still, she did not want to leave the room looking as if a storm had passed through her and nothing had been put right after. There was some stubbornness in her that refused to let revelation scatter things without at least one civilized answer.

So she wrote.

Under *state geographies*, she entered the count. Under *damaged beyond use*, she marked one. Her hand remained steady, which seemed to her either admirable or shameful. She tied the map roll more tightly. She boxed the copybooks, slid the globe into its crate, stacked the readers by grade. When she came to the attendance register, she paused.

The year's pages bore attrition in three inks. September names were full and neat. Then the first margin notes began: withdrawn to help at home. Moved to Denver. Out sick. Returned? The question mark had been hers on a day she still believed people came back. By June the page looked like a field after grasshoppers, with empty rows and names that had no final explanation. A school died by ledger before anyone admitted it in speech.

Thea closed the register and tied it shut.

At last there was nothing left uncovered except the hollowed book. She considered putting the packet back just until evening, just until she could think in a room that was not full of children gone. But that, too, would be a choice in favor of delay, and delay had its own appetite. It grew quickly once fed.

She lifted the front board from the geography and let it fall shut.

The cavity made the book close wrong. She could feel the altered weight in it now instantly, and the fact that she had not felt it before annoyed her almost as much as it impressed her. Ezra had counted on habit. People touched schoolbooks as units, not as singular things. To them it had been one more volume to stack, one more spine to dust, one more lesson nobody finished. He had hidden his confession inside the town's thinning future because nobody respected that future enough to inspect it closely.

Thea slid the packet into her satchel, then took it out again.

The leather bag sat by the stove where anyone looking might search it out of ordinary curiosity. She did not trust ordinary curiosity in Morrow. She tucked the bundle instead inside the front of her dress, between chemise and corset cover, where the paper lay warm against her breastbone and scratched faintly when she breathed. Hiding it there felt too close to claiming it, and the thought made her flush. It was not a lover's letter. It was worse. It was evidence.

She put on her hat, gathered the keys and forms, and stood for a moment in the center of the schoolhouse.

This, then, was the end of her wage in Morrow. Fifty-two dollars a month had once seemed hardly worth the trouble of a boarding room and county oversight and the small daily humiliations of being observed by every woman who thought a single teacher ought to know her place. Now the money gone missing from the house ahead of time seemed to echo in the room. There would be no children in September, no salary envelope, and no excuse after this for why she might still be seen walking the road west to the Vale place except the true one, which could not be said aloud in town and would not become any safer for being true.

She took one last look at the desks.

Children had sat there and picked scabs and whispered and copied spelling words badly and grown. They had coughed dust into handkerchiefs and traced rivers they would never see. They had stood for recitation with patched elbows and dirty collars and the stubborn dignity of the poor. Some of them would never finish school anywhere. Some would spend the next years in labor camps or on relatives' floors or married too soon because hunger made decisions faster than age did. The county form wanted numbers. The room gave her bodies instead.

She blew out a breath she had not known she was holding, though there had been no lamp lit to answer it, and locked the door behind her.

The late afternoon light was white and pitiless. Across the street, Mrs. Linder was shaking a rug over a line, though there was no point in it; the rug would collect another skin of road within the hour. She did it anyway with the furious thrift of a woman refusing surrender one beating at a time. Thea nodded to her, received a distracted nod back, and started toward the road west before she could talk herself into stopping at the bakery first.

Hester would have seen too much in her face. Hester saw through flour and weather and politeness with equal efficiency. She would ask what had happened in the schoolhouse, and Thea would either lie badly or tell the truth in the wrong order. Neither appealed.

The walk out of town felt shorter than usual, perhaps because her body had already chosen the destination and left the rest of her to catch up. The letter shifted against her skin with each step, a small dry reminder that she was carrying something capable of breaking a house open.

At the edge of Morrow she slowed.

Beyond the last clustered buildings the Vale road ran pale between fields that had once promised more than they meant to give. Heat shimmered over the stubble. To the south, beyond sight but not beyond thought, Saint's Basin lay with its opened seep and its warning boards and its history newly wet enough for people to fear. Ahead, at the farm, Cora would be moving through the late chores with Dell laid up inside, August's deadline crouched just beyond the day, and no reason yet to suspect that the dead had found another way to interfere.

Thea put her hand, flat and instinctive, over the packet hidden in her dress.

Before the Vale yard came into view, she would have to decide whether the packet went into Cora's hand in private or onto the kitchen table where Dell, laid up and listening, could hear the dead speak too. The choice rode against her breastbone with every step.

She kept walking.
