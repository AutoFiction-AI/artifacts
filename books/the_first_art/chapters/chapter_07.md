# Chapter 7

The next afternoon, every half hour Gavin had to stop reading and press his fingers against his thighs until the joints loosened.

The sub-collection was colder in December. The chill came out of the limestone itself, dry and mineral, nothing to do with the weather outside, and it made his left hand tremor harder — the same fine shake the mass detention had left behind, sharpened now until the manuscript's notation blurred when he held the pages up to the wall's glow.

He sat on the floor with his back against the shelves, the manuscript spread across his knees, his notebook open beside him on the stone. The passage had been defeating him for three days.

The notation was doing something he hadn't seen before. The earlier sections — the ones describing the First Art's principles, magic as agreements that could be renegotiated, the substrate beneath spells — had used symbols that moved in pairs and small groups, showing direct correspondences between concepts. This section's symbols moved in larger patterns. Slow, layered, interconnected. When he translated one cluster and wrote the meaning down, the act of comprehension seemed to shift the patterns around it, as though the page were adjusting to his understanding. He wasn't sure if the adjustment was in the manuscript or in his perception. The distinction had been getting less clear for weeks.

He'd been translating the section using Babbling's framework — structural invariants, repeating patterns, mapping known elements onto unknown positions — and it worked, but slowly. Each evening he got a few more sentences into English, or something near English. The translations sat in his notebook in his own bad handwriting, crossed out and rewritten and crossed out again.

The section was about origins. Not the First Art's principles, which the earlier passages had covered, but its source. Who — or what — had created the substrate that Gavin was learning to perceive.

He'd been translating the key concept as "practitioners," which he'd known was wrong even as he wrote it. The beings the manuscript described did not practise the First Art the way a wizard practised spells. The relationship was different at a level that English kept flattening.

He tried the passage again. The symbols rearranged themselves in their slow configurations as he read, and this time — on his fourth attempt at this particular cluster — something aligned. Not a dramatic shift. More like the sensation of a sentence snapping into meaning when you'd been reading it as nonsense for days. The structural invariants held. The correspondences checked.

He wrote in his notebook: *Their relationship to the substrate was not functional. It was —*

He stopped. The word he wanted sat somewhere between "constitutive" and "fundamental" and "identical," and none of those words were right.

He tried again: *The substrate was not something they used. It was something they were.*

It was still wrong. Too simple. The notation showed a relationship more nuanced than identity. The beings were not literally made of the substrate the way a table was made of wood. It was more like — and he knew the analogy would break, because analogies always broke here — it was more like the relationship between a fish and water, except the fish was also water, except the water was also the fish's thoughts and its social structures and its architecture and its history, except there was no moment where the fish had been separate from the water and then started using it. The separation didn't exist. Hadn't ever existed.

He wrote:

*Think of it as: magic was not their tool. Not their environment. It was the medium they lived in and through. As fundamental and unconscious as air is to humans, except air is outside the body and this was not outside them at all.*

He looked at what he'd written. It was clumsy but usable. The notation kept its shape under the wall's glow, patient as ever. He could feel how much of the structure was still standing outside the sentence.

He'd taken to calling them the Builders in his notes. The manuscript didn't name them. They were referred to with a notational structure that implied something closer to "those who made the medium specific" or "the ones who gave the substrate its current local configuration." Neither of those was a name. The manuscript had been written for an audience that already knew who they were, the way a Hogwarts textbook didn't bother explaining who Merlin was. Gavin was reading a text written for readers who no longer existed, in a room that those readers had probably built.

He worked through the next section. The notation described the Builders' activities — their works, their constructions — in terms that the Babbling framework could only partially map. They had made stable configurations of the substrate that persisted. What a wizard would call enchantments, though the word reduced something vast to something small. When a wizard enchanted an object, they applied a spell to a thing — a layer on a surface. The Builders worked with the substrate itself. They adjusted the underlying agreements about what a thing was and how it related to everything around it. A wizard enchanted a ceiling to look like the night sky. The Builders could have edited the rules that defined what a ceiling was, what "looking like" meant, what the night sky was permitted to be.

Gavin sat with this for a while. The wall's faint glow was steady — a luminescence he'd been reading by for months without much thought. It had a depth to it now that it hadn't had before his perception changed. He could sense the substrate structure underlying the glow, a faint architecture that produced the light — something stable and intricate and very old, set down long before the castle was built above it. He'd assumed it was some early enchantment, a Founders-era spell that had persisted through centuries of ambient magical reinforcement. It wasn't. It was a substrate configuration. One of the Builders' works. He was sitting inside their work, reading about them by their own light.

Everything Gavin had learned in five years of magical education — every spell, every potion, every transfiguration — operated in a thin top layer on something these beings had shaped the way he might doodle on parchment in Binns's class. The whole system that wizards used was a residue. Left-behind infrastructure. Nobody remembered who had built it, the way you didn't think about who had built the road when you walked on it.

His hand shook. He pressed it flat against the cold stone until the tremor passed, and then he kept reading.

---

The next section was shorter, and it took longer.

Gavin worked through it symbol by symbol, checking each translation against the structural invariants, writing possible meanings in his notebook and scratching most of them out. Outside the sub-collection — up through corridors and staircases, past the seventh-floor entrance that only sometimes connected to the same hallway — it was getting dark. In December the daylight was gone by four. Down here there was no way to know.

After two hours he had a sentence he was reasonably sure about:

*They are not here.*

The room gave a small, sick lurch around him. Nothing had moved. The shelves were still cut into the stone. The manuscript was still open on his knees. But the corner where the wall met the floor to his left stopped reading as a corner for a second or two. It looked thin. Not even thin. Uncommitted. As if he could put his hand through the limestone and come out nowhere his body knew how to stand in. His stomach dropped so hard he slapped his palm against the flagstone just to feel the cold answer him back.

Three words of English content for a full passage of notation, because "here" was doing work that English couldn't match. "Here," in the manuscript's terms, was not a location. It was a category of existence — a specific mode of being that included occupying physical space, existing on a planet, maintaining a particular relationship to the substrate in its current local form. The Builders were "not here" the way a dog whistle was absent from a room full of human ears: still sounding, just pitched past the range of the listener.

The manuscript was precise about one thing, and Gavin read it three times to make sure his framework was giving him the right reading.

They had not died.

Whatever had happened to the Builders, the manuscript treated them as continuous. The notation had separate structures for cessation — he'd seen them in earlier passages describing the collapse of specific substrate configurations — and none of those structures appeared here. The page handled the Builders the way it handled anything still in force: as an ongoing fact.

They had left.

He sat with the word. In English it came out like travel. It made the Builders sound as if they had set off somewhere you might follow if you had the right map. Leaving implied a direction — from somewhere to somewhere else. A departure through space, the kind of thing you could draw on a map. The notation showed something else: a hinge turning inside reality itself. Its representational movement — the slow shifting of symbols that depicted a *from* and a *to* — gave him a *from* he could understand. This planet. This substrate. This mode of existence where you had a body and occupied a position and knocked against physical things.

The *to* was there on the page. The notation could show it. The symbols moved through a configuration that represented a destination, or a state, or a new mode of being — Gavin could see the notation doing it, could watch the symbols arrange and rearrange in a pattern that meant *where they went*. He tried to hold the whole configuration in his head and the wall-light narrowed to a seam. Pressure stabbed behind his eyes. For one stupid second the page did not seem to be resting anywhere at all. It was just present, hanging in front of him without position, and his right hand clenched hard enough on the edge that the material bit into his skin.

He couldn't follow it. The meaning was there and his mind slid off it the way his eyes slid off an optical illusion — perceiving the shape but unable to resolve it into sense. The symbols kept folding into a state with no distance in it, only changed conditions. The Builders had stepped clear of location altogether and become a different kind of thing, one the manuscript could hold steady on the page and Gavin's human cognition could not track.

They had relocated. He wrote it in his notebook anyway and stared at how flat it looked. It still suggested trunks and routes and arrivals. What the page showed was more like one set of rules giving way to another while they passed through intact.

He put the manuscript page down and pressed both hands against the floor. Both were shaking — his left from the persistent damage, his right from something else. From holding a page that had rewritten what he thought the world was.

The sub-collection room was three metres by four. The shelves were cut into the limestone and most of them were empty. The ceiling was low. The light glowed steadily from the walls, old and stable and made by beings who no longer existed here in any sense that Gavin could track. The entire magical system that ran the castle above — every portrait that moved, every staircase that shifted, every self-lighting candle, every ghost that drifted through walls as a sustained agreement between death and the substrate's rules — was built on top of what they had left behind. Leftovers. The residue of a civilization that had decided to become something else, and had gone, and what remained was what remained, and nobody knew.

He picked the manuscript up again. There was still more.

---

The last section he translated that night was about what the Builders had done before leaving.

They had set wards.

That was Gavin's word, and it was as inadequate as everything else, but it was the closest thing in wizarding vocabulary. In class — in the version of Ancient Runes that was still being taught, the version that had nothing to do with the notation in his hands — wards were protective spells. You set them around a house, a room, a doorway. They could be keyed to specific people, configured for specific threats, maintained with periodic renewal. The concept was familiar. Every student knew what wards were.

What the Builders had set was not familiar.

The wards covered the planet.

Gavin worked through the passage twice. The notation was unambiguous. It wrapped the whole globe in one continuous structure, running over oceans and under continents alike. Planetary scale was not a grand undertaking to the Builders but an ordinary exercise — the equivalent, maybe, of a wizard warding their house before a holiday.

He sat on the cold floor and worked through the next part of the passage, and this was where the translation gave him the most trouble, because the concept underlying the wards' function was the hardest thing he'd tried to render into English.

Every time he thought he had the structure, something in the room went missing in a way that was not missing. His satchel by his boot was the first. He looked straight at the brass buckle and could not make it stay a thing. His eyes kept slipping off it as if the leather had been greased. Then the nearest shelf did the same. He could see the line of old manuscripts and blank stone and still not get purchase on the fact of them. He reached out and put his fingers against the shelf just to feel the cut limestone under his hand. The effect broke at once. A second later it came back when he looked down at the page. He bit the inside of his cheek until he tasted blood and forced himself through the passage again.

The wards bent notice. They took Earth and slid it out of whatever counted as a valid target for attention. The planet still turned inside them — wizards and Muggles and flobberworms and all the ordinary mess of life carrying on — but any awareness coming from outside found nothing for its grip to catch.

He tried several translations of the key passage:

*They remove Earth from a category of detection.*

*They subtract this planet from the set of things that can be perceived.*

*They edit the rules of attention so that Earth is not the kind of thing attention can land on.*

The third was closest, and it was still wrong around the edges. The wards rewrote the rules of notice itself. Inside them, Earth kept all its ordinary traffic — weather, wars, schoolchildren trying to levitate feathers. Outside, attention passed over the planet as cleanly as a hand over polished stone, because Earth no longer fit the shape of a thing that could be seized by a look.

Like pulling a folder from a filing system and leaving the papers where they were. The loss was in the index.

Gavin closed his notebook. He looked at his hands, which had stopped shaking at some point during the last section, the way a headache sometimes vanished when your attention was somewhere else. He checked his watch. Quarter past nine. He'd been in the sub-collection for five hours. His knees had gone past aching and into a numb stiffness that he'd have to walk out, and his lower back felt welded into its position against the shelf.

He put the manuscript and the notebook into his bag and stood up. The standing wasn't graceful. He bent and straightened and bent again until his legs remembered how to hold him. He stretched, and the stretch was clumsy and ordinary and human, and his body was a body in a castle on a planet, and the planet was inside a structure set by beings who had used the substrate as a medium of existence before becoming something else and going somewhere he couldn't follow, and the structure was designed to make the planet unnoticeable, and the question he hadn't translated yet — because the section hadn't covered it, or because he hadn't reached the right passage — was: unnoticeable to what?

The wards existed. Wards warded against something. The manuscript had described what the wards did with careful precision and had not yet described what they protected against. Or maybe it had, somewhere in the pages he hadn't translated, in the sections he hadn't decoded, in the slow patient configurations of symbols that kept rearranging themselves while he lagged behind them.

He walked out of the sub-collection through the sequence of stone rooms — three of them, connected by low archways, each emptier than the last. The limestone gave way to the castle's regular masonry at the seventh-floor corridor, and the torches here were burning at half-strength, which was standard after nine o'clock in December though nobody could say whether it was conservation or the Carrows tightening the atmosphere.

Gavin walked past sleeping portraits and the occasional suit of armour standing at attention in its alcove, and every one of these things — the spell-animated paintings, the enchanted metal, the torches' self-regulation — drew on a system the Builders had put down. He could feel it now, faintly, the way he'd been able to since the breakfast in the Great Hall when his perception had first shifted. The substrate ran underneath everything, quiet and continuous, and the castle's spells rode it the way ink rode paper. Portrait eyes tracked him because somebody had once taught paint a narrow trick. The armour held its stance on memory alone. Even the torches were only fire following an old instruction. And all of it drew on groundwork a vanished civilization had laid down the way you might lay paving stones — functional, durable, unremarkable to the people who had done it.

The whole castle was recent and local, threaded through something older and much larger, and the species that lived in it was using the residue of a departed civilization without knowing that the residue was residue.

The corridors were empty. He passed a window on the sixth floor and the glass was cold enough to feel from two feet away. Outside, the grounds were dark under December cloud cover, the distant edge of the Forbidden Forest a darker line against the sky. Snow sat on the mountains beyond the lake. The world looked the way it always looked at this hour, at this time of year — a Scottish castle in winter, nothing visible that suggested anything beyond what the eye reported — and it was wrapped, all of it, in a structure that made it invisible to things Gavin couldn't name yet, and the structure was the last act of beings who had become something else and gone.

He kept walking. The stairwell between the fifth and fourth floors was the coldest part of the route, always, because the window on the landing didn't close properly and the draught cut through robes and jumper alike. His left hand tremored in the cold. He tucked it under his arm and took the stairs carefully, because the steps here shifted position after midnight and it was nowhere near that yet, but a fall would still be a stupid way to end the evening.

---

The common room was warm. That was the first thing, always — the warmth of the Hufflepuff common room after the castle's corridors, the heat from the fire and the adjacent kitchens bleeding through the walls. It smelled like bread tonight, bread and something with onion. The barrel entrance closed behind him and the cold began to drain from his robes and his fingers and his feet.

The round room with its low ceiling and warm-toned walls and the plants growing in the window boxes — it looked the same as it had looked every night for five years. Earthy, enclosed, lit by candles and firelight that moved and flickered in ways the sub-collection's steady glow never did. Students were scattered in the usual places. Third-years working at the big table by the window where the plants grew in their indirect light. A loose group of younger students near the fire, quills out, textbooks open. Someone had left a Gobstones set half-played on the floor by the hearth, the stones sitting in a constellation that would make sense to whoever came back to finish the game.

Miren's chair was empty — she'd gone up already, or she was somewhere in the castle doing DA work that Gavin knew about in broad terms and didn't ask about in specific ones. They hadn't talked much since the argument. Not fighting, exactly. Just careful. Duncan's usual corner of the sofa was also empty. He'd been going to bed earlier and earlier as the term wore on, and Gavin hadn't found a way to ask about it that didn't feel like prying into something Duncan was deliberately managing by himself.

He dropped his bag by his usual chair and sat down. The fire found his face first, then his hands. His left hand tremored faintly against the armrest.

He should eat something. He'd missed dinner again.

"Excuse me?"

A first-year was standing next to his chair. She was small — conspicuously small, the kind of eleven-year-old who hadn't hit any growth spurt yet and barely reached Gavin's shoulder even when he was sitting. Her school robes were new but already looked slept in, the hem dragging on one side where a stitch had come loose. She was holding her Charms textbook against her chest with both arms, and in her right hand she gripped a quill she'd chewed until the feather end was a flat, damp mess.

"Sorry to — I mean, I was wondering if you could help me with — it's the Levitation Charm." She said the last part quickly, the way you said something embarrassing — get it out, get it done. "I can't get it to work."

"The — yeah, sure. Sit down."

She sat in the armchair across from him and opened her textbook to the Levitation Charm chapter. Her name was written on the inside cover in careful, rounded handwriting: *Ffion Pryce*. Under the name she'd drawn a small picture of a cat. The cat was terrible — enormous head, no whiskers, body like a sausage — but she'd coloured it in carefully with what looked like Muggle crayons, orange and white stripes, and had written *Mr Paws* underneath in tiny letters.

"So what happens when you try it?" Gavin said.

"Nothing. The feather just sits there. I've been trying since October and everyone else got it weeks ago and I just —" She stopped and chewed on the ruined quill end.

"Right. Can I see you try?"

She pulled a practice feather from her bag — white, crumpled from being carried around — and set it on the low table between their chairs. She held up her wand, which was short and pale and looked like willow.

"Wingardium Leviosa." Swish and flick.

The feather sat there.

"Okay," Gavin said. "The wrist movement looks right to me. I think. Let me —" He took out his own wand and tried to demonstrate the flick, and immediately realized he wasn't sure about the precise angle. He'd learned this spell four years ago and hadn't thought about the mechanics since. His own Levitation Charm worked — it just worked, the way tying a knot worked, without thinking about each step. "The flick might need to be sharper? Like more of a snap at the end. And — make sure the emphasis is on the right syllables. Win-*gar*-dium Levi-*o*-sa."

"That's what Professor Flitwick says."

"Yeah. So. Right. Do that."

She tried again. Nothing.

"Okay, um." Gavin rubbed the back of his neck. He was not good at this. Teaching required being able to break down something you did automatically into separate steps, and he couldn't find the steps. The spell worked when he cast it. He didn't know why it worked and didn't know why it wasn't working for her. "Try — this is going to sound thick, but try not thinking about the feather. Think about the space above the feather. Where you want it to be. Like, aim your — I don't know, your wanting? — at the empty air where you want the feather to end up."

Ffion looked at him with the expression of an eleven-year-old receiving advice she suspected was nonsense but was too polite to say so.

"I know it doesn't make sense," he said. "I'm not great at Charms, honestly. I just — that's what worked for me. Sharp flick, right emphasis, and sort of really clearly want the feather up and not down."

She adjusted her grip on the wand, chewed the quill, and set her jaw in a way that made her look briefly older.

"Wingardium Leviosa."

The feather twitched. It shifted on the table but didn't rise.

"That was something," Gavin said. "Again. Same thing but sharper on the flick."

"Wingardium Leviosa."

The feather rose. Slowly, wobbling, uncertain — but up. Three inches. Five. It hung there, shaking slightly in mid-air, held by a connection between intention and substrate that Ffion couldn't see and Gavin could. He could see the arrangement underlying the charm — the fit between the caster's intention and the substrate's current state that made "up" a valid direction for a feather to travel — but it did not look self-contained anymore. The charm sat in the room like a tap screwed into pipework hidden in the walls. Ffion's wand movement had caught on rules the Builders had laid down long before feathers or schoolbooks or first-years. The feather was not just going up. It was borrowing old buried machinery to do it.

"Do that again," Gavin said.

Ffion blinked at him. "Did I do it wrong?"

"No. Sorry. No. Just again."

She lifted her wand. "Wingardium Leviosa."

The feather rose two inches and dropped.

Gavin leaned forward before he meant to. The older structure had been there again — a brief clean hitch into something larger, there and gone before he could get a proper look at where the little classroom charm bit into the buried works.

"Again," he said.

Ffion didn't cast this time. She looked at him instead, the quill end paused halfway to her mouth. "Is something wrong with it?"

With it. As if the feather could be wrong, or the spell, or the room. Gavin opened his mouth and nearly told her that the whole planet had been edited out of being noticed and that the spell she was struggling through was riding on the leftovers. What came out was, "No. Nothing's wrong. I just thought I saw — no, sorry. It's fine. You had it."

She kept looking at him for a second, uncertain now in a way she had not been when she'd asked for help. "All right," she said, but carefully, as if she was answering an older student who might be odd in ways first-years were not supposed to comment on. Then she tried again. The feather rose faster this time, higher, steadier. She held it for close to twenty seconds before her focus broke and it drifted down.

"I did it."

"Yeah. You did."

"I actually —"

"Keep going. It'll get steadier."

She grinned, though there was still a faint crease between her eyebrows now, and immediately tried again. The feather rose. It hung. It held.

"Thank you. I've been — everyone else got it ages ago and I thought I was just..." She trailed off. The sentence didn't need finishing. "Thanks," she said, and gathered her things and went back to her table near the fire. She put the feather down, looked over at him once as if checking he was still only a tired fifth-year in an armchair, and cast the charm again. It rose. She made a small, private sound of satisfaction, a quick breath through her nose, and Gavin looked away because the moment belonged to her.

He sat in his chair and watched the fire. Someone had left a pair of socks drying on the hearth guard and they gave off the faintly sour smell of damp wool left too close to the heat. The common room was quiet in the way it got after ten — settling, soft, the scratch of quills and the murmur of people not wanting to go to bed yet because the fire was here and the dormitories were cold.

He pulled his notebook back out.

He turned to a clean page and wrote: *Earth is under wards.*

He stared at it. It looked stupid immediately. Like the opening line of a bad prophecy, missing only the dramatic capitals. He wrote underneath it: *Attention bent away.* Then: *Built by —*

He stopped with the nib touching the paper. *Builders* made them sound like men who put up walls. *Not here* sounded like sulking. *Left* made the whole thing feel like a departure board. The notebook kept dragging everything he'd learned back down to school size.

His left hand tremored once against the paper. He flattened it there until the shaking eased. The fire cracked and shifted. The bread smell drifted from the kitchens through the wall. At the table near the hearth, Ffion Pryce was working through her Levitation Charm again, the destroyed quill clamped between her teeth, Mr Paws the terrible crayon cat face-down on the table beside her textbook. The feather rose. It hung. It fell. She put it back and this time, when it would not go cleanly, she leaned toward the girl beside her and asked something Gavin couldn't hear.

Gavin looked at the two and a half lines in his notebook and shut it. He slid the notebook back into his bag. There was no version of them he could say to Miren tomorrow that would not sound mad, and no version he could hand to Babbling that would come with proof attached, but that was not the whole of it now. He could still see the little crease that had appeared between Ffion's eyebrows when he'd nearly said too much. He did not want another first-year looking at him like that because he couldn't keep a feather as a feather in his own head for five minutes. He didn't go back to the sub-collection. Not tonight. Tomorrow night the manuscript would be there, in the same small cold room, in the same patient notation that kept rearranging itself for a reader it hadn't been designed for. The rest of the questions — what the wards protected against, why the Builders had thought a planet needed hiding, what existed outside the wards that made concealment worth the work of a civilization that could reshape reality as a medium of thought — would wait in the pages he hadn't reached, in the slow configurations of symbols that showed things his mind couldn't quite resolve, in the quiet of a room lit by a light that a vanished civilization had left on when they stepped through a door that wasn't a door into whatever they'd become.

He closed his eyes. The fire was warm. Across the room, when the feather dipped again, Ffion turned to the girl beside her instead of to him. Gavin let her. The feather rose, settled, and rose again without him.
