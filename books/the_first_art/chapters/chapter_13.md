# Chapter 13

Gavin hadn't slept. After the notation blurred into shapes on the page, he'd shoved the manuscript back into his trunk and climbed until the castle ran out of stairs. The Astronomy Tower was the coldest place in the castle, and he hadn't brought a cloak.

The stone floor leached heat through his robes as he leaned back against the parapet and pulled the cloth tight around his chest. Wind came through the gaps between the crenellations in bursts that flattened his hair and then died and came again. He'd been up here for a while. An hour, maybe. Long enough that his hands had stopped just being cold and had moved into the numb stage where the fingers didn't bend right when he tried to make a fist.

March in Scotland. The thaw had started but hadn't committed — patches of snow still on the mountains, the grounds mostly mud with green pushing through in places, the lake half-frozen and half-not in a way that looked like a decision the water couldn't make. From up here the Forbidden Forest was a dark shape pressed against the hills, and below it the lawns sloped down to Hagrid's cabin, which was dark. Hagrid might be in the forest tonight. Gavin didn't know. He hadn't paid attention to Hagrid's schedule in months. He hadn't paid attention to most things in months.

He'd come up because the sub-collection was too small. The walls of those cut-stone rooms had started to press. Fourteen weeks of working there — the limestone smell in his clothes, the faint glow of stones that predated everything else in the castle — and the air had begun to feel used. He'd kept walking out of the dormitory, out of the common room, up the stairs past the library landing, then up more stairs past the Dark Arts classroom, dark now with the door closed, and up again. He'd ended at the Astronomy Tower because it was the highest place he could reach, because there was sky here, because the walls opened.

A telescope mount jutted from the parapet near his left shoulder. Cold iron, bolted into stone. He touched it without thinking and then left his hand there because the metal was cold enough to feel sharp and the sharpness was something real and immediate and unrelated to anything he'd been translating. The manuscript was in his trunk, four hundred vertical feet below him, and he could feel the distance like a taut thread in his awareness. That was new — the sense of where it was when he wasn't with it. Not a pull, exactly. More like being aware of a sound source even when you weren't listening to it.

The sky was clear. No moon worth noticing — a thin paring low over the western mountains, already going down. The stars were hard pinpricks, the kind you got when there was nothing between you and them and the nearest city light was forty miles south. The constellation Gavin always looked for — Orion, the only one he actually knew — was high and tilted over the forest, three belt-stars in a line.

He looked up.

And the wards were there.

They didn't arrive suddenly. There was no flash or sound or anything dramatic. They were there the way the stars were there — present all along, perceivable now because something in him had adjusted, except it wasn't his eyes that had adjusted and it wasn't darkness they'd adjusted to. His substrate perception had been deepening since January, since the quill, since the nosebleeds started. At Christmas he'd felt the wards as pressure — something leaning on glass — and in the sub-collection he'd caught edges of them, fragments, like hearing a few notes of something you couldn't quite place through a wall. But up here, on the tower, with the sky open above him and no stone ceiling or castle architecture between his perception and what was overhead, the wards came clear.

They were enormous.

That was the first thing. They extended farther than the sky — not across it but through it, occupying a layer of reality that the sky didn't know about. His brain did its best: it rendered what his restructured cognition was perceiving as something between structure and weather, something that had spatial qualities because spatial was how his mind handled large-scale pattern. The wards arced overhead in interlocking curves that went past the horizon in every direction. They weren't lines, exactly, but if he'd had to draw them on a piece of parchment they'd have been lines — sweeping, intersecting arcs, each one an agreement about what Earth was and what could notice it.

Back against the parapet, mouth slightly open, he looked at something no one else in the school could see.

Beautiful wasn't what he'd expected. The manuscript had described the wards as structures, functional, engineered, and he'd pictured something utilitarian — a fence, a wall, a shield. They were nothing like that. They were patterned in ways that reminded him of the interference shapes you got when you dropped two stones in still water and watched the ripples cross, except each ripple was itself a structure of extraordinary intricacy and the patterns where they crossed were more intricate still. They nested. A large curve contained within it smaller curves that mirrored the large one's shape at a different scale, and those contained smaller ones, and at whatever resolution his perception could manage there were more — more detail, more structure, more. It was like looking at a snowflake and realizing the snowflake was the size of a continent.

He didn't have words for what they were made of. They weren't made of anything in the way a wall was made of stone. They were agreements — the manuscript's term, the best his translation could manage — rendered in the substrate itself. Each arc was a statement about visibility and attention and category, woven into the grammar beneath magic with a precision that made wand work look like a child scratching letters in dirt. The Builders had done this. Beings for whom magic was air had sat down — or whatever they did instead of sitting — and written, into the deepest level of how reality organized itself, a set of interlocking agreements that said: this planet is not here. Do not notice it. There is nothing to notice.

And it had worked. For however many thousand years, it had worked.

Gavin pulled his knees up to his chest and wrapped his arms around them. The wind pushed at him and he barely registered it. His hands were numb. He was cold clean through to the bone, the way you got after sitting on stone in March without a cloak for too long, and the cold wasn't important because the wards were beautiful and they were failing and he could see where.

The thin places. He'd known about them from the manuscript — the sections he'd translated in December, the passages about degradation and maintenance protocols designed for a civilization that had left. But knowing was words on a page. Knowing was concepts he'd assembled from imperfect translation and then panicked about on the sub-collection floor. Seeing was different. Seeing was worse.

The thin places weren't holes. They were areas where the interlocking curves had weakened — where the nested agreements had become less forceful, less definite, the way a path worn through grass wasn't a gap in the grass but a place where the grass had thinned. The ward structures in these areas still existed but with diminished conviction. The patterns were still there. They just weren't saying what they said as firmly.

He could see three from the tower. Two were large — spanning what felt like enormous tracts of the ward surface, though his sense of scale was unreliable because the wards weren't operating at a scale where human distance measurements meant much. One was above the Forbidden Forest and slightly east, an area where the intersecting arcs had separated from each other enough that you could perceive what was between them. The other was further north, barely at the edge of what he could resolve, a wide zone where the nested structures had degraded unevenly — some scales still strong, others thinned to almost nothing, like a rope with half its strands frayed through.

The third was small. It was almost directly overhead.

He looked at it. It was a place where the ward patterns had worn smooth, like the letters on an old gravestone that the rain and the wind had been at for centuries. The agreements were still legible. Still present. But they'd lost their precision. The grammar that said *this planet is not here* was, in this small patch, saying something more like *this planet is probably not here*. The filter was thinner. Not gone. Thin.

And through the thin places, he could sense what was beyond them.

He'd expected, if expected was the right word for something he'd been dreading for months, that perceiving it would be different from reading about it. He was right. It was different. But not in the direction he'd feared. He'd been afraid of a presence. Something that would notice him noticing it, something looking back, something with intention. A monster, even though the manuscript said it wasn't one. You could be told something wasn't a monster and still picture a monster because that was what human minds did with threat. They gave it a face and a direction.

It didn't have a face. It didn't have a direction.

What was beyond the wards wasn't a thing. It was a condition.

The first time he let himself lean into it, the skin under his fingernails went dead. The stars above the thin place split into separate distances his eyes couldn't hold together, some seeming close enough to touch and others so far his sight slid off them, and the tower gave a quick sick sideways tilt that made his stomach clench. The stone had not moved. His sense of where he was had.

He swallowed. His ears stayed blocked, the way they did on a fast descent, but there was no relief-pop waiting on the other side. Air went in and out of him and still left a raw panic in his chest, as if breathing had stopped being a complete answer. On this side of the wards, the substrate had rules. Magic operated according to a grammar. Agreements were possible. Things could stay what they were because there were structures underneath them making that statement hold.

Through the thin place overhead, he could feel those structures tail away by degrees. They weren't breaking. They were thinning. Like climbing higher and higher until the air still existed but no longer had enough in it for lungs to use: words going short, fingers clumsy, balance giving bad reports. Except it wasn't air. It was the substrate itself becoming less applicable, less able to support the fact of Earth. The agreements thinned, and with them the conditions that let magic and life remain legible to themselves.

The manuscript had used two analogies that kept resurfacing whether he wanted them or not. The first was the gap between wizards and flobberworms, not predator and prey but a scale so lopsided the smaller thing fell below the shape of notice. The second was altitude. It wasn't weather battering the wards or something trying to break in. It was the breathable layer ending farther down than it used to, the livable band narrowing century by century while the upper air stayed exactly what it had always been. The high air didn't want to suffocate you. It was simply where breathing quit working.

With his knees against his chest and his numb hands clutching his own elbows, Gavin felt the truth of that shift from translated words into something his altered perception could actually touch. It was worse than he'd imagined because the condition beyond the wards was indifferent. If it had wanted to destroy Earth, that would have been terrible but comprehensible. There would have been a relationship, however predatory, and relationships implied shared categories. But this was worse. It occupied its own terms, and out there the terms that included Earth did not obtain.

Only the wards kept the question from arising. They said *nothing here* and the condition, because it didn't care enough to look closely, because close looking wasn't even the right category, accepted the statement. But the wards were old and no one had maintained them and the statements were getting quieter.

His nose kept running in the cold. The tremor in his left hand made the wool at his sleeve jump in little jerks. Wind shoved at the tower, and the thought came in flat and miserable: the adults couldn't help with this.

Not because they were unwilling. Because they couldn't perceive it. You couldn't fix what you couldn't see, and you couldn't see this without the First Art, and the First Art required months of cognitive restructuring through a manuscript that existed in a single copy in a sub-collection that didn't appear on any map. The adults were busy with a war. Dumbledore was dead. Snape was whatever Snape was. The Ministry had fallen. The Order of the Phoenix was scattered and hiding. Every competent witch and wizard in Britain was occupied with a war about blood purity and political power and none of them — not one — knew the whole place might just stop working.

Gavin was not smart enough.

That was the thing that settled in his stomach like swallowed ice. He'd known it before — Miren had pointed it out, gently, and she'd been right. His Ancient Runes marks were mediocre. His Transfiguration was worse. He'd failed his mock OWL. The manuscript had been designed for minds categorically different from his, and even with the cognitive restructuring he was still Gavin Tench, average at everything, exceptional at nothing, a kid whose best Switching Spell turned teacups into lumps of clay. He'd managed one successful First Art edit — a quill that was slightly less visible — and it had given him a nosebleed that lasted twenty minutes and blurred his vision for an hour. The wards were a planetary-scale structure built by beings who used magic the way humans used oxygen, and the maintenance protocols in the manuscript called for interventions of a sophistication so far beyond what he'd achieved that the gap between his ability and what was required was not meaningfully different from infinity.

At fifteen he didn't know enough. He couldn't do enough. The problem was too large and he was too small and the people who might have helped were killing each other over something that would be irrelevant if the wards failed.

He stayed where he was.

Time passed. He wasn't tracking it. The stars moved in the slow way stars moved — Orion's belt shifting westward, the paring of moon gone now behind the mountains, the sky rotating around its pivot point. The wind died and came back and died again. His body was cold in a way that had gone past discomfort into a kind of background fact. The stone under him was stone. The iron telescope mount was iron. His robes were damp from the wind and the beginning of frost that was forming on every cold surface.

The ward-structure hung overhead, beautiful and degrading. The three thin places. The condition beyond, patient and indifferent, above the line where breath worked.

He could have gone inside. He didn't.

---

The sky began to change.

It happened the way dawn always did — slowly at first, the deep black overhead lightening to a dark blue that was almost black but not quite, the stars fading from the east as the light came up behind the mountains. The ridgeline caught the first change: a thin line of grey along the peaks, then something warmer creeping down the western faces as the sun found the mountains from below the horizon.

The ward-architecture was still there. It hadn't disappeared — it wasn't optical, had nothing to do with visible light — but Gavin's attention loosened. The part of his perception that rendered the ward structures grew tired the way any sense grew tired when you held it at maximum effort for too long. The curves receded. They were still present if he reached for them, a faint architecture of agreements arcing overhead, but they stopped dominating his awareness and the morning came in to fill the space they left.

The morning.

March dawn in the Highlands. The sky went from blue-black to blue-grey to a pale, clean blue that promised nothing warmer than what was already here. Frost had settled on the parapet during the night — Gavin noticed it now, thin and white along the edges of the crenellations, on the iron of the telescope mounts, on the stone floor around him where his own body heat hadn't reached. His robes were stiff with it. His trousers were damp and cold against his legs. He was very cold and he noticed that now, finally, the way you noticed cold when the thing that had been overriding it went quiet.

Below him, the grounds resolved in the growing light.

The lawns were patchy — dead brown and muddy where the last snow had melted, green in strips where the grass was coming back, churned up near the Quidditch pitch where somebody had been walking back and forth for no reason anyone could see. The lake was a flat grey mirror, still partly frozen at its northern edge where the sun didn't reach until afternoon. The Whomping Willow was a bare skeleton across the water, leafless, its branches motionless in the still air. The greenhouses caught the early light and threw it back in irregular flashes, their glass dirty from a winter's worth of weather.

Hagrid's cabin. Smoke was coming from the chimney now — thin, grey, the kind you got from a fire that had burned low overnight and was being coaxed back to life. The pumpkin patch beside it was bare dirt, nothing growing yet, but the soil had the dark, wet look of ground that was thawing and would be ready for planting soon. A light came on behind the curtains. Fang barked once — a deep, muffled sound that carried up through the morning air.

The Forbidden Forest was waking. The trees at its edge were still bare but there was a different quality to them in the March light — the branches not quite empty, buds present though not open, a thickening at every tip that would be leaves in a month. Birds were starting up. Not many. A few early songs from the forest edge, thin and territorial, staking out the morning the way birds did when spring was still a question rather than an answer.

Gavin watched a crow land on the Quidditch pitch and walk across the mud with the deliberate, important stride of a bird that had business. It stopped, tilted its head, jabbed at something in the ground, came up with nothing, and walked on.

Below the tower, in the courtyard, the first students appeared.

Two Ravenclaws — blue-trimmed scarves, walking close together, heading toward the Great Hall. One said something and the other laughed, the sound carrying up in the way sound carried on cold mornings, thin and clean. A first-year came out of the entrance hall and crossed the courtyard at a trot, small inside a coat he was wearing over his robes, late for something or cold or both. A group of older students followed, talking, their voices a murmur from three hundred feet up.

Gavin watched them.

They didn't know. None of them knew. They were worried about the Carrows and the war and whether Harry Potter was alive and whether the DA's last message drop had been discovered and who would be called up in Dark Arts today and what was for breakfast. Those things were real. Duncan's bruises were real. The Cruciatus was real. Those fears had names and timings and bodies attached to them.

Then the things below him he couldn't bear to lose started crowding in at once. The Hufflepuff common room, too warm and smelling of bread through the wall. The plants in the windows doing fine on bad light. Miren arguing Quidditch standings nobody could use this year. Duncan, before he'd learned to sand himself down, muttering about Sheffield Wednesday and his mum's horrible thing with tinned peaches. The first-year Gavin had coached through Levitation in December, face going bright when the feather finally rose. The boy in the courtyard now, kneeling on cold stone to tie his shoe. Fang barking once from Hagrid's cabin. The crow on the pitch walking like it owned the place.

He loved this world, not as a big idea but in its exact bits. Dirty greenhouse glass. Ink on a student's cheek. Socks that didn't match. Toast eaten on the run. Weeds in the flagstones because nobody had time for weeds.

The boy in the courtyard got his shoe tied, shoved himself upright with one hand on his knee, and went inside for breakfast.

Gavin's grief landed there, in the inventory. It wasn't for himself first; he'd be dead too if the wards failed, him and his manuscript and his mediocre understanding and his nosebleeds and his one blurry quill. The grief was for the boy who'd tied his shoe, for the Ravenclaws who'd laughed, for the first-year with the feather, for Miren and her arguments, for Duncan whose worst problem should have been the Carrows because the Carrows were the kind of problem that ended. It was for his parents writing letters about the garden and the cat. For Hagrid stoking his fire. For every person in the Great Hall eating breakfast under the enchanted ceiling with no idea what was over the real one.

He still wasn't smart enough. Fine. That hadn't changed. But nobody else even knew there was work to do. Waiting for an adult to notice was useless. Trying to explain it again would only burn time and turn it back into rubbish in his mouth. He would go to class. He would keep his head down. He would hand in what he could and let the rest rot. Every hour he could steal would go to the manuscript and the maintenance protocols and the parts he still couldn't read. He would learn enough to do something, or wear himself out trying, but he was done waiting for somebody better to turn up.

More students crossed the courtyard. The sun cleared the mountain ridge and the grounds went from grey to the gold-brown of early Scottish spring — mud turning amber, the green patches of grass brightening, the lake catching light and throwing it in a line toward the forest. A door opened somewhere below and the breakfast sounds drifted up — faint, muffled, the clink of tableware and the low communal murmur of people eating together.

Gavin wiped his nose on his sleeve. His fingers left a damp streak on the wool. He looked up one more time. The ward-structure was there, faint now in the morning light but perceivable if he reached — the arcs and intersections, the nested agreements, the three thin places with their worn-smooth grammar. The condition beyond, patient as geology. The small patch almost directly overhead where *this planet is not here* had become something less certain.

He stood up. His knees cracked. His lower back ached the way stone made you ache when you'd sat on it for most of a night, a deep bruised feeling that went into the hip joints. His feet had gone past numb into the territory where he wasn't sure they'd hold him and he stood slowly, one hand on the parapet, waiting until the pins-and-needles started and he could feel the stone under his soles. The telescope mount was warmer now under his other hand — sun-warmed on its eastern face, the iron going from cold to cool.

He went down the stairs. The spiral staircase was dim after the open tower and he went slowly, one hand on the wall, feeling the stone under his fingers change from the wind-smoothed outer blocks to the rougher interior masonry. The castle was waking around him. Footsteps in a corridor below. A door. The particular morning sounds of a school that was occupied and frightened and still, despite everything, functioning as a school.

He reached the bottom of the stairs and stepped into a corridor that smelled like breakfast: eggs, toast, and the warm-bread smell that meant the kitchens were going and the house-elves were doing what they did and the day was starting the way days started.

A second-year he didn't know passed him, walking fast, tie loose and shirt untucked, a piece of toast in one hand. The boy glanced at Gavin — his rumpled robes, his frost-damp hair, whatever his face looked like after a night on a cold tower — and kept going. The boy had a smudge of ink on his cheek. His socks didn't match. He was eating his toast in the efficient, distracted way of someone who had somewhere to be and hadn't given a single thought that morning to what was overhead.

Gavin watched him disappear around the corner and then walked toward the Hufflepuff common room. He'd need to change into dry robes before class. He had Charms first period and a Potions essay due tomorrow that he hadn't started. Those things were still facts. Smaller ones. Schoolwork was camouflage now, something to keep him moving through the day without drawing notice while he spent the real hours somewhere else.

He went down the stairs toward the barrel entrance. The corridor near the kitchens smelled like bread, yeasty and warm and alive. Through a doorway he caught a glimpse of a house-elf carrying a platter of sausages, small and serious-faced, absorbed in the work of feeding people.

The barrel entrance was ahead. He could hear voices inside — Hufflepuffs getting ready for the day, the sounds of a common room doing what common rooms did in the morning.

He tapped the barrel in the right rhythm and went in.

The warmth came first — the fire up, the room heated, the contrast with the tower so sharp that his face prickled and his hands began to ache as the blood came back. Students sat at tables, eating and talking. A sixth-year was complaining about a Herbology chart. Someone near the windows laughed hard enough to snort. A copy of the *Daily Prophet* lay on a side table with its front page open to the war, black type big enough to catch the eye from across the room.

The table nearest the hearth was half breakfast and half something else. It held plates, mugs, and a bowl of porridge skinning over. Beside them sat a stack of bandages, a tin of salve, three bread rolls wrapped in napkins, and a folded bit of parchment with times written down the side in Miren's blunt hand.

Miren was there with Duncan and two younger Hufflepuffs Gavin recognized by face and not by name. Her plait was coming loose at the nape of her neck. The bruise along her jaw had gone yellow-green around the edges. Duncan had both hands around a mug and was wearing his tie properly for once, which usually meant he felt awful enough to need one thing to stay in place.

Miren looked up and took him in all at once: damp hair, frost-stiff robes, face gone grey from the cold.

"Where've you been?"

"Tower," Gavin said. "Couldn't sleep."

Her eyes narrowed. "All night?"

He nodded once.

One of the younger girls pushed a plate toward him. "Take some before it's gone weird."

"Thanks." His voice came out rough. He took a piece of toast because refusing would have started a different conversation.

Duncan tipped the teapot a little in his direction. "Tea's still warm. More or less."

Gavin poured with both hands because the left one was doing the fine shake again. Nobody pointed that out. The tea tasted of tannin and metal and the fact that it had been sitting too long. It was still hot enough to hurt his mouth, which helped.

On top of the rota by Miren's elbow sat a folded square of parchment with his name on the front in Professor Sprout's rounded handwriting. There was a thumbprint of soil near the crease.

Miren tapped it. "That came up from the greenhouses."

He opened it.

It was short and bad-tempered in the way Sprout's notes got when she was too busy to be polite. He was missing the peat-acidity chart, the seed-tray observations, and one set of pruning notes. If he had decided Herbology no longer applied to him, he could report after lunch and explain the theory. Sprout would provide the time.

He folded the note once, then again until it was a hard little square, and put it in his pocket.

Miren watched him do it. "You were meant to hand those in on Saturday."

"I know."

The two younger Hufflepuffs stood. One picked up the wrapped rolls, the other the bandages. Miren gave them both quick instructions in a low voice: which corridor, which knock, don't hang about if they heard steps. They nodded and went. Gavin watched the bread disappear into a schoolbag between Charms notes and a bottle of ink.

When they were gone, Miren pulled the rota a little closer to him. "Can you do kitchens corridor after supper? Just half an hour. We're moving two first-years and I'm short."

Gavin looked down at the times. Seven to seven-thirty. Exactly the clean piece of evening he used for getting out of sight before the common room settled for homework and curfew.

"I can't."

Miren stayed still. "Can't because you've actually got something, or can't because of the manuscript?"

There wasn't a good answer that wasn't also the bad answer.

"It's — yeah. Because of that," he said.

Duncan kept his eyes on his mug. "I can stand in a corridor."

"No," Miren said, without looking at him.

"It's only half an hour."

"No."

Gavin could have taken the opening Duncan had just made. He could have said yes after all. Half an hour wasn't that long. Half an hour was nothing if you were talking about ordinary life. But he'd spent the night looking at a worn place overhead where *this planet is not here* had started to fray into something weaker, and the arithmetic had changed. Half an hour was half an hour.

"I'm sorry," he said, and meant the sentence but not enough to change it.

Miren pulled the rota back. She scratched out the empty slot and wrote her own name in it, hard enough that the nib caught in the parchment fibers.

"Fine," she said. "I'll do both."

Nobody said anything for a few seconds. The common room noise carried on around them: plates knocked together, the fire settling in the grate, somebody by the stairs asking whether today was Binns or Babbling first period and getting the answer wrong twice before anybody fixed it.

Duncan drank his tea. "Sprout had us repot the biting geraniums last week," he said quietly, still looking at the mug. "One of them got a girl through the glove and she started crying in front of everybody. Sprout pretended not to notice and gave her the blunt secateurs. It was sort of decent of her."

It was such a Duncan line that for a moment Gavin nearly smiled. It was not a point or a rescue attempt, just an odd little fact from somebody else's day, offered into the gap because the gap was there.

"That does sound like her," Gavin said.

Miren was looking at him now with the expression she got when she had run out of patient versions of the same thought. She was not angry yet. She was closer than that.

"You've got Charms in twenty minutes," she said. "And after lunch, apparently, you've got Sprout."

"Yeah."

"Are you going to do any of the things people are actually asking you to do?"

He held the mug between both hands and watched steam fail to rise from it. The true answer was no, except for the bare minimum needed to keep doors open and adults off him. Saying it that plainly in front of Duncan felt filthy.

"Some of them," he said.

Miren let out a breath through her nose. It was not a laugh. It was not quite that.

"All right," she said. "Then do the ones you're doing."

He finished the tea because it was there and stood up before he had to answer anything else. The blood was back in his feet now, enough to hurt.

"I need to change," he said.

Duncan gave him a small nod. Miren was already rewriting the bottom half of the rota, moving names around to cover the slot he'd left open.

In the dormitory his bed curtains were still half-open from the night before. He stripped off the wet robes and pulled on a dry shirt, a jumper, and a fresh robe with one cuff coming unstitched where he'd caught it on a trunk latch last week. His fingers fumbled the buttons twice before they worked. On the bed lay his Charms book, the unfinished Potions essay, and the Herbology growth log with three days of blank boxes in the middle like teeth knocked out.

He looked at the lot of it and, for one clean second, saw the day the way everyone else would have seen it: missed work, annoyed teachers, Miren downstairs doing his half hour because he wouldn't, Duncan trying to make things easier by pretending he could. They were ordinary failures, the kind that had names.

Then the other scale came back and crowded it out.

He put the Potions essay under the bed. He slid the Herbology log into the bottom drawer of his trunk without filling the missing boxes. He took the Sprout note out of his pocket, folded it smaller, and tucked it into his Charms textbook so he wouldn't forget where to be after lunch. Then he added his wand and the thin notebook where he'd been copying maintenance passages in pencil from the manuscript.

That was the day's shape. It was enough school to stay in motion and enough apology to stop people asking for the wrong sort of explanation. Every other usable piece would go somewhere under the castle.

When he came back through the common room, Miren had tied the supply bag shut and slung it over her shoulder. The rota on the table now had her name in two places. She did not look up. Gavin took that, put the Sprout note deeper into the Charms book, and went to class.
