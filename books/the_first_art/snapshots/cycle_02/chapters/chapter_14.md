# Chapter 14

The line was: *I will complete my assignments on time.*

By Monday afternoon, one of the consequences had caught up with him in Herbology.

Gavin wrote it for the fifteenth time, and the word *complete* came out looking like *conplete* because his hand was doing the thing again, the fine tremor in his left fingers that had been with him since November. He pressed harder and the quill dug a shallow trench through the parchment. The ink bled sideways into the tear.

He crossed it out and started the sixteenth line.

Professor Sprout was at her desk at the front of the room, marking third-year essays with the stub of a quill she'd broken that morning on a Venomous Tentacula pot that needed splitting. She held it like a pencil sharpened to its last inch, her thick fingers cramped around it, earth worked permanently into the creases of her knuckles. A pot of something small and yellow-green sat at the corner of her desk — she kept turning it a quarter-rotation every few minutes, chasing the afternoon light that came through the greenhouse-adjacent windows. Her attention had stayed on the essays for twenty minutes straight. The red annotations in the margins were detailed, careful, twice the length of anything the students had probably written themselves. Gavin suspected it was the sort of habit that cost her hours, and that she kept doing it for the same reason she still watered the Greenhouse Three seedlings every morning even though no one was taking N.E.W.T. Herbology this year. That, he thought, was how you got through a year like this: keep doing the old jobs and don't admit what the school had become.

The classroom windows were cracked open. April pressed through them: cut grass, warming stone, the distant sound of someone laughing on the grounds below. The courtyard had daffodils. Gavin could see them from where he sat, bright yellow things pushing up through the beds that nobody had maintained since September, doing it anyway because daffodils did not need institutional permission to exist.

*I will complete my assignments on time.*

He was not going to complete his assignments on time. He had not completed an assignment on time since February, possibly January. The Herbology worksheet on Fanged Geranium propagation cycles had been due eleven days ago and was still in his bag, blank except for his name in the upper-right corner, which he had written and then forgotten about because the properties of Fanged Geraniums were not relevant to anything, and what was relevant was the resonance pattern in the seventh ward layer that he'd found two nights ago in the sub-collection.

The degradation wasn't random. He'd known that since the Astronomy Tower, known it in his body and his restructured perception, but now he could see the structure of the non-randomness — how the substrate agreements that held the wards in place thinned along lines that looked, from the angle his cognition could hold them at, like the grammatical equivalent of wear. Not breakage. Forgetting. The wards were forgetting what they agreed to do, the way a word you say a hundred times in a row loses its shape and becomes just noise. The agreements that made Earth invisible to the thing outside were losing their coherence through something that resembled — this was the closest he could get — semantic erosion. The meaning was leaking out.

And if the forgetting followed a grammar, then the grammar could be read. And if it could be read, maybe it could be written back.

*I will complete my assignments on time.*

His handwriting was terrible. It had always been terrible. His mother had sent him a handwriting-improvement book in second year, a Muggle one with dotted letters you were supposed to trace, and he had used it for a week and then lost it under his bed. The lines he was producing now were worse than second year, though — the tremor in his left hand made his m's look like n's and his t's forked unpredictably, and the overall effect was less a sentence than an illustration of someone having a sentence happen to them without their full cooperation.

He was trying to hold two things at once. The ward degradation grammar, which occupied most of his working mind and demanded an attention he didn't have good words for — like listening to a melody and trying to remember the shape of each note after it passed — and the physical task of moving a quill across parchment to produce something that looked enough like English to satisfy a woman who was not checking anyway. The second thing kept losing.

The seventeenth line. He got through *I will complete my* and then the resonance pattern surfaced again — the relationship between the seventh layer and the substrate beneath it, how the agreements stacked, each one depending on the coherence of the one below it like a sentence depending on the grammar depending on the idea of language itself — and when he looked down at the parchment he had written *I will complete my substrate* and stopped with the quill still touching the page, a bead of ink pooling under the nib.

He crossed it out and started the eighteenth line.

This was his life. This was what it looked like from the outside: a fifteen-year-old sitting in a chair that wobbled on its left front leg, writing lines for missing a worksheet about Fanged Geraniums, with bad handwriting and ink on his fingers. From the inside it was the same, except that behind the handwriting and the wobbling chair, the planetary wards were thinning in a pattern that suggested they might have less time than he'd originally thought, and the question of whether the degradation grammar was readable — actually readable, not just perceivable — was the most important question anyone on Earth was currently working on, and the only person working on it could not spell *complete* without turning it into *conplete*.

He did not think this with any particular sense of irony. He was not the sort of person who stood outside his own situation and observed how absurd it was. He was just in it. The absurdity was structural, like the wobble in the chair, and equally beneath notice.

"Mr. Tench."

He looked up. Sprout was watching him over her broken quill, her face patient in the particular way that meant she was about to say something kind and completely wrong.

"Is everything all right?"

"Yeah. Yes, Professor. I'm fine."

"Because I've noticed — and I'm not the only one who's noticed — that your work this term has been..." She paused. She turned the yellow-green pot another quarter-turn and pulled a dead leaf from one of its lower stems with the practiced, unconscious motion of someone who had been tending plants for thirty years. "We understand the pressure everyone's under. This has been a difficult year for all of us. But your OWL examinations are in June, and if your marks continue on their current trajectory—"

"I know."

"Do you? Because you've failed your Transfiguration mock, your Potions practical was — I'm told it was not good — and now this." She gestured at his bag, where the blank Herbology worksheet presumably lived. "I'm not trying to add to your burden. I'm trying to make sure you're aware that there are consequences that will outlast this year."

OWL examinations. The phrase sat in his head for a moment, inert, like a stone he'd picked up and couldn't remember why he was holding. OWLs were in June. The wards were thinning now. If the degradation pattern he'd identified in the seventh layer was consistent across the other layers — and he thought it was, he was almost sure it was — then the rate of erosion suggested something he wasn't ready to calculate, something measured in years or decades, not centuries, and in that context OWLs were exactly as urgent as the governance structure of an ant colony to a person standing in the path of a flood.

But he could not say that. So he said, "I'll do better, Professor."

"I hope so." Sprout held still for another moment, the broken quill hovering over the essay, and Gavin had the sense she was deciding whether to push further — whether he was a student who needed pressing on or leaving alone. She settled on leaving him alone, and Gavin was grateful because the alternative was saying something insane.

"Finish your lines," she said. She went back to marking. The red quill moved across the third-year essay in her cramped, precise hand, and she adjusted the pot one more time, and the afternoon continued.

He finished the last five lines quickly, his handwriting degrading with each repetition until the final one, *I will complete my assignments on time*, was legible only in the sense that a person who already knew what it was supposed to say might be able to reconstruct it. He brought the parchment to Sprout's desk. The page rustled in her hand. She set it down more carefully than he expected and took a breath through her nose, and Gavin couldn't tell whether that meant pity, frustration, or just the worn-out patience of a teacher who'd watched too many students come apart this year for too many different reasons.

"You can go," she said.

He packed his bag. His nose had dripped without him noticing — just a drop, the kind he got now when he'd been thinking hard about the substrate for too long — and there was a small bloodstain on the cuff of his sleeve that could have been ink. He wiped his face with the back of his hand and left.

---

Miren was in the corridor outside. She had been waiting.

She was sitting on the stone windowsill with her legs drawn up, her bag beside her, looking at the grounds below. The late-afternoon light caught the bruise on her jaw, the one that had been there since March — faded now to a muddy yellow-green that she'd stopped trying to hide with her hair. She didn't talk about it. Whatever had happened, whoever had done it, she'd absorbed it into the set of things she carried this year without comment.

She turned from the window when he came out and slid off the sill. Her whole body had the braced stillness Gavin had learned meant she'd decided to have a conversation she'd been putting off, as if she were about to step into cold water.

"Can we talk?" she said.

"Yeah, sure."

"Not here."

She led him to an empty classroom down the corridor — one of the ones that had been used for Carrow detentions earlier in the year and then abandoned for a different room on the floor above. The desks were shoved against the walls. There were scorch marks on the stone floor and, in the corner, a dark stain that might have been ink and might not. Nobody had cleaned it. Miren sat on a desk and put her feet on the chair below it. Gavin set his bag down and leaned against the windowsill.

"So—"

"I've been keeping track," she said.

That was how she started. Not with accusation, not with heat. With specifics. She pulled a torn piece of parchment from her pocket — not proper notes, just a list in her quick, slanting hand.

"You've missed dinner fourteen times since February. Fourteen. You weren't in the common room twenty-two nights out of the last thirty. You failed your Transfiguration mock. You failed your Potions practical. You just sat detention for a Herbology worksheet, which—" She looked up from the parchment. "Gavin, it's a worksheet. You fill in boxes."

"I know."

"You did lookout for the supply run in March and you nearly got Emily Watkins caught because you weren't watching the corridor. You were staring at the ceiling with your mouth open."

"It wasn't the ceiling, it was—" He stopped. It had been the ceiling, technically. He'd been looking at the way the charm-work in the stone above him layered against the deeper substrate structure, and the pattern had been relevant to the resonance problem, and Emily Watkins had been twenty feet away carrying stolen Dittany, and he had not been watching. "That was bad. I know."

"Duncan got pulled out of Ancient Runes last week. Did you know that?"

"No."

"He was gone for three hours. He came back and wouldn't say what happened. He won't talk to anyone except me, and what he says to me is 'I'm fine,' which is what he always says, and he's not fine, he's—" She stopped herself. She breathed. Her fingers pressed flat against the desk on either side of her hips. "You were in the same room, Gavin. You were sitting four seats away from him when they came and took him out, and you didn't notice."

He hadn't noticed. He'd been in Ancient Runes, in the same room as Duncan, and he had not registered when Duncan was removed because his mind had been in the seventh ward layer, tracing the degradation pattern, and the classroom and its inhabitants had been as distant as a conversation in the next room when you were reading.

"I—"

"Don't." Her voice was flat. Not loud, not cruel. Just done with the soft versions. "Don't do the sorry thing. Don't tell me you've been stressed or you haven't been sleeping or it's the manuscript. I know about the manuscript. You told me about it months ago. You showed me the blurry quill. And I tried — I really tried to take that seriously. But you're worse now, not better, and everyone can see it, and I can't keep—"

She pressed her lips together. Outside the window, the courtyard was golden with late-day sun, and someone was crossing it toward the Great Hall with a bag of Quidditch equipment over their shoulder, walking fast, ordinary, going to dinner.

"I need to say something and I need you to actually hear it." She put the parchment back in her pocket. "From where I'm standing — from where anyone standing near you is standing — you found a book. The book was interesting enough that you stopped doing homework. Then you stopped eating with us. Then you stopped coming to the common room. Then you stopped showing up for things that actually matter. Duncan is getting hurt and you're in the sub-collection. People are running supply chains and message networks and hiding first-years and you're in the sub-collection. And when I ask you what you're doing down there, you tell me the planet is in danger from something I can't see and you can't prove, and—"

"That's not—"

"—and you're the only person who can fix it." She let that sit. "Gavin. You're a fifth-year who can't pass Transfiguration. That's not me being mean, it's true. You've never been the person who solves the thing nobody else can. You're not. You know you're not."

She said it without cruelty. That was the worst part. She was being careful with him — the same care she showed Duncan when she asked how his day had gone. Direct but not unkind, saying the true thing because pretending it wasn't true would be worse.

"I know how I sound," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yeah." He rubbed the back of his neck. The classroom smelled like old spell damage — a burnt-copper smell that had sunk into the stone and would probably never come out. Through the window behind him, the grounds were green and gold. "I know it sounds like I've cracked up. Or I've been alone too long and I've built something in my head that isn't there."

"That's what it sounds like."

"It's not what's happening."

"Then tell me. Really tell me. Not the version where you start talking about substrates and I watch you give up on me halfway through the sentence."

He looked at her. She was sitting on the desk with her feet on the chair and her hands flat on the surface and the fading bruise on her jaw, and she was asking him — properly asking, for what might be the last time — and he owed her an answer that was not the careful, hedged, half-explanation he'd been giving her for months.

"OK." He ran his hand through his hair. His palm came away damp. "Right. OK. When you cast a spell. Any spell. There's the bit you do. Wand, words, what you mean. Under that there's... support, I suppose. That's not right. It's more like floorboards and the beams under them. Most people only ever have to think about the boards. I've been looking underneath."

"You've said this before."

"I know. But that underneath bit — it wasn't always there. Something made it. Not people. Not wizards. Something else. And before it left, it put—" His eyes had moved to the wall behind her. He could feel it happening and couldn't stop it. The substrate in this room was layered — centuries of charm-work deposited on the old Hogwarts stone like geological strata, warming charms and sound-dampening charms and structural reinforcements — but beneath all of that, beneath the castle itself, the ward structure pressed against his awareness like a sound below the threshold of hearing against a dog's ear. Cold without temperature. Presence without substance. The thinning.

"What are you looking at?" Miren said.

"Sorry." He pulled his attention back. "I keep doing that. They put a kind of cover over the planet. Not a wall. More like making the whole place easy to miss. So whatever's outside would pass over us. And it's wearing out. I can see it. I know you can't verify that, I know there's no way for me to prove it—"

"You've told me this."

"Yeah."

"And you're the only person who can see it."

"I think so. I don't know. Maybe there are others, somewhere, but—"

"And you're working on fixing it."

"I'm trying to understand how it works. I can't fix something I don't—" He trailed off. His left hand was tremoring and he put it in his pocket. "I'm still learning. I'm bad at it. I know I'm bad at it."

Miren was looking at him. Not her usual look when she thought he was deflecting, or making excuses, or retreating into the manuscript to avoid what was happening in the real world. Something else. He'd seen her look at things this way before — at corridors she thought might have someone around the corner, at common room conversations she was trying to read — a concentrated, careful attention that took in more than most people bothered to.

"Gavin," she said, more quietly. "When you were looking at the wall just now. Your eyes were tracking something. Moving. Like you could see something there."

"I can. Sort of."

"There's nothing there."

"There is. You just can't—"

"I know. I can't see it." She was quiet for a moment. Her gaze moved from his face to the wall behind her and back. "You really believe this."

"It's not about believing. I can see—"

"I know. You can see it." She let out a breath and rubbed her thumb against the desk edge. Gavin had the uneasy feeling the row was sliding into something else now, the ready anger giving way to something less certain and harder to manage. "That's — honestly, Gavin. That's worse."

"What?"

"If you were just being lazy, I could work with that. If you'd checked out because the year was shit and you couldn't handle it — fine, I'd get it, I'd yell at you and you'd feel guilty and maybe you'd come to dinner. But you actually—" She stopped. "You tell me the planet's dying and your hand won't stop shaking and now you're bleeding again and half the time you go blank while I'm still talking. I don't know what to do with that."

"My nose isn't—" He touched his upper lip. His fingers came back red. He wiped it on his already-stained sleeve. "That happens when I think about the substrate too long. It goes away."

"It's blood coming out of your face."

"Yeah, and then it stops."

"When did you last eat?"

He tried to think. Toast at breakfast, probably. Or had that been yesterday? The schedule of meals kept losing its edges, how everything lost its edges when the resonance pattern pulled him under. He knew this was a problem. He was not unaware.

"That's what I thought," Miren said.

---

The light in the classroom had shifted. The sun had moved past the window and the room was dimmer, the scorch marks on the floor fading into the stone's natural gray. The courtyard below was emptying — students drifting toward dinner, the Great Hall's noise just barely audible through the castle's walls, the clatter and hum of an ordinary evening in a school that was not, this year, ordinary.

Miren hadn't moved from the desk. But the tightness had gone out of her shoulders, and the room felt flatter now that the anger was spent. What replaced it sounded worse to Gavin: tired, precise sadness.

"Here's what I'm asking," she said. Her voice was even. "There's a supply run tonight. Ernie needs lookouts. Hannah needs someone to carry healing stuff to a second-year in Ravenclaw who's been hiding from Dark Arts in an empty classroom. I'm going. Come with me."

He could do it. Find Ernie Macmillan. Stand in a corridor for an hour while stolen healing supplies changed hands. Watch for Filch. Pass the signal. Carry bandages to a girl too scared to go to class.

And while he did, the ward degradation pattern would continue its slow erasure, and the resonance structure he'd been mapping would continue to lose coherence, and the thin places would get thinner, and the time he spent watching for Filch would be time he did not spend on the only problem whose solution space included everyone on the planet, including the people the Dittany was for, including Miren, including Duncan, including the scared second-year hiding in a classroom, all of them inside the sphere of what the wards protected, all of them unaware that the protection was fading, all of them safe only because something built by a departed intelligence was still, barely, remembering what it was supposed to do.

"I'm working on the thing that makes all of that irrelevant," he said.

He heard the word as soon as it left his mouth. Grandiose. Cruel. The kind of word people used when they'd decided their own problem sat above everybody else's. But once it was out he could not find a cleaner one. The scale in his head stayed the same.

"Irrelevant," Miren said.

"That's not what I—"

"It's what you said."

"I know. It came out wrong. I don't mean it doesn't matter, I mean—" He pushed both hands into his pockets, the tremoring one and the still one. "If I stop now, it keeps getting worse. The wards are a bigger problem. That's all. I can't do both. I've tried. My head doesn't split that way."

"So you chose."

"I didn't choose. I just — ended up here."

"You chose this, Gavin. You keep choosing it. Every night you go down there instead of — that's you choosing."

He stayed where he was. Blood touched his upper lip again. He tasted iron.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"I know." Miren slid off the desk. She was standing now, her bag in her hand, and she looked tired in a way that was not about sleep. "But you're not here, Gavin. You're sorry and you're not here, and I don't — I don't know what that does for anyone."

There was a pause. Outside, someone called a name across the courtyard — sharp, bright, ordinary — and then the sound faded into the general noise of the castle settling into evening.

"I'm going back to the common room," Miren said. "The run leaves in ten minutes. If you want to come, come now. If you don't, I'm done asking."

He didn't move. He just stood there with his hands in his pockets and his nose starting to bleed again, a thin warmth on his upper lip.

"Right," Miren said. "OK."

"It's not that I don't care."

"I know it's not." She pushed her hair back. The plait was half undone — she never re-did it during the day, just let it unravel until evening. "But I come to get you and two seconds later you're staring past me at something I can't see. I'm done pretending the blood and the shaking and whatever's happening to your eyes is normal. I'm done being the one who comes to fetch you when you don't want to come back."

"So you're—"

"I'm going to go do what I can do. What's in front of me. And you're going to stay here and do whatever it is you do." Her voice caught — not a sob, just a hitch on the word *do*, which she pushed past. "And maybe that's how it is now."

She stood there for a second, like she was waiting to see whether he'd finally cross the room. He didn't.

"Miren."

"What?"

He wanted to say the wards would keep thinning whether he ate dinner or not, whether he walked a corridor or not. He wanted to say she was standing inside the same problem as Duncan and the girl in Ravenclaw and every person in the castle. But in his head it all turned into the sort of sentence that sounded mad before you reached the end of it, so he left it alone.

"I don't know how to explain it better than I have," he said.

"Yeah." She nodded. "I know."

She stood in the doorway for a moment. The corridor behind her was warm with torchlight and the last of the afternoon sun, and the bruise on her jaw was almost invisible in that light, and she was his best friend, had been his best friend since they were eleven years old and sitting next to each other at the Sorting because their surnames put them close in the alphabet. One hand stayed on the frame. She didn't say anything else, and Gavin had the sense that if he let her walk away now, this version of them was over.

"Be careful," she said. "Whatever you're doing. Please."

"I will."

"Eat something."

"OK."

She left. Her footsteps moved down the corridor — quick, sure, the walk of someone who knew where she was going and what she was going to do when she got there — and then they were gone, and Gavin was alone in a classroom with scorch marks on the floor and a stain in the corner and silence.

He wiped his nose. He sat on the desk she'd been sitting on. It was still warm where she'd been, and the warmth faded as he sat there, and the classroom got dimmer as the last of the light moved past the window, and the castle hummed with the sound of hundreds of people going to dinner and he was not one of them.

Outside, the daffodils in the courtyard had closed for the evening. The grounds were empty. The sky above the castle was the pale, washed blue of an April evening, and behind the sky — above it, through it, in a direction that was not a direction — the wards held. Thinner today than yesterday. The seventh layer's resonance pattern flickering in a way that he was almost, almost able to read, the degradation grammar just past the edge of what his restructured cognition could reach, like a word on the tip of his tongue in a language he was still learning.

She could count missed dinners and lookout shifts and blood on his sleeve. He could see the seventh layer thinning over the castle. The two facts sat beside each other and did nothing to help.

He sat for a while. Then he opened his bag and took out the manuscript.

Its surface was warm against his fingers, as it always was — the not-parchment material holding some residual energy from whoever had made it, or from the agreements encoded in its shifting notation, or from something else entirely that he did not yet understand. He turned to the section he'd been working on. The notation moved, rearranging its representational relationships in response to his attention, and the ward-structure diagrams — he didn't have a better word for them — opened like a sentence continuing from where he'd left off.

He worked.
