# Chapter 18

The substrate went quiet so gradually Gavin only noticed when the next impact failed to come.

It was like noticing a headache ending — not the sudden absence of pressure but the slow realization that it had been there and now it wasn't. For hours the battle's output had saturated everything: hundreds of spells fired simultaneously, each one flexing the agreements that made it possible, the accumulated noise of it drowning out the fine structures he needed to perceive the way a brass band drowns out a whisper. He'd stayed where he'd ended the night, on the limestone floor with the manuscript open on his knees, trying to catch the pattern before it flattened back into notation, but every time his perception started to settle, another volley of combat magic dragged the substrate sideways and the pattern dissolved.

It had to be dawn by now. Time had a different quality in the sub-collection even on normal nights — the old stone didn't carry sound from the castle above, and the self-lit walls held the same faint glow whether it was noon or midnight. But the substrate carried everything, and through it Gavin had felt the fighting as a continuous distortion, the magical equivalent of trying to read during an earthquake. He'd tried to work anyway. He'd accomplished nothing.

The last scattered jolts thinned out one by one — a single spell, then silence, then another, then silence that held — until the quiet spread outward through the substrate and the final resonance died away. The agreements were still there, still intact, still governing how magic worked, but nobody was using them hard enough to make the whole system ring.

Gavin closed his eyes and felt the wards.

They were there. Overhead and everywhere, the vast architecture the Builders had left behind, the planetary-scale structures that filtered Earth from the kind of attention that would unmake magical life as casually as a footstep unmakes an anthill. He couldn't see them with his eyes — that wasn't how it worked — but through the restructured cognition the manuscript had built in him over months, he could feel their shape the way you feel the shape of a room in the dark: edges, distances, the places where the structure was thick and the places where it had worn thin and let through a quality of awareness that didn't belong here.

They'd worn a little even since he'd checked them upstairs. He couldn't tell if the battle had done it — the massive expenditure of war magic stressing substrate joints that were already worn — or if it was the ordinary daily degradation he'd been tracking for months, as indifferent to whatever the wizards below were doing to each other as rot in roof beams is indifferent to the family eating under them.

He opened his eyes and looked at the manuscript. The notation had settled. He could read it again.

He read for what might have been half an hour. The section on ward-substrate coupling that the battle had disrupted — the Builders' notation for the interrelation between active magical use and the passive structures that held the wards in place. He'd been close to understanding something about it before the fighting started, a connection between the resonance patterns of the wards and the way the substrate responded to load, and he'd lost it in the noise, and now the thread was there again, fragile, waiting for him to pick it up.

He picked it up. He followed it for two pages. The notation shifted under his perception, meaning surfacing and sliding away before he could hold it, and he concentrated until his nose started to bleed.

He pinched the bridge of his nose with his left hand and kept reading with the blood running over his fingers. The tremor in that hand was worse today — it always got worse when he was tired — and the blood pooled in the crease between his thumb and forefinger and dripped onto his robes. He didn't wipe it.

The footsteps reached him before the person did. The sub-collection's corridors carried sound in odd ways — the old stone bounced echoes around corners, arriving before they should have, so he heard her for nearly a minute before she appeared in the doorway. He knew it was Miren from the rhythm. She walked faster than most people, even when she wasn't in a hurry.

She stopped in the doorway and stood there and looked at him.

Gavin lowered the manuscript page. He wiped his nose on his sleeve, smearing blood across the back of his hand, and looked up at her.

Stone dust had gone to paste in her hair, turning the dark plait grey-white where it hadn't come loose, and it had come loose on one side so the hair hung over her ear in a knotted tangle she hadn't bothered to fix. A cut on her forehead, shallow but long, still seeping along the edge of her hairline. Her robes were torn at the shoulder and there was something dark on her left side — blood, not hers, from the way it had soaked into the fabric in a broad stain rather than spreading from a wound. The old bruise on her jaw, the one from February that had never quite faded, sat underneath a new one, fresher, already purpling along the same cheekbone as though the same bone kept catching everything meant for her face.

She was breathing hard. She'd come quickly through the damaged corridors, the route to the sub-collection that was already unreliable on normal days and must have been worse now with the battle's damage to the castle's upper structure.

"It's over," she said.

Gavin nodded. He'd felt it in the substrate — not the specific moment of Voldemort's death, but the cessation of combat, the pattern of magical output collapsing from hundreds of simultaneous sources to scattered individuals to nothing.

"I know," he said. "I felt the fighting stop."

She stood in the doorway for another moment. Then she came in and sat down against the opposite wall, three feet from him, and closed her eyes. She let her head fall back against the stone. The faint light from the walls caught the cut on her forehead and the dried blood and the dust in her hair. It put years on her. She had the used-up look of someone who'd been awake for thirty hours and had spent most of them in the worst night of her life.

Neither of them said anything for a while.

The sub-collection's quiet was its own kind. The mineral smell of limestone. The dry cold that had nothing to do with weather. The light from the walls, steady and faint, casting almost no shadows because it came from everywhere at once. Above them, somewhere in the castle's newer construction, something creaked and settled — the building adjusting to damage, the same sounds an old house makes after a storm, except the storm had been a war and the house had been full of children.

"Harry killed him," Miren said. Her eyes were still closed. "In the Great Hall. I wasn't there for it — I was outside, near the greenhouses. But they said the spell rebounded or something. People were cheering." She paused. "It didn't sound like cheering. It sounded like — I don't know. Something else."

"Yeah," Gavin said.

Miren opened her eyes. She looked at the ceiling — the limestone, unmarked, older than the rest of the castle by centuries. A single crack ran across it that hadn't been there before, a thin fracture line where the battle's concussive force had reached even this deep.

"People died, Gavin."

"I know."

"Do you want to know who?"

He put the manuscript page down. He set it carefully on the floor beside him, the pages aligned, their faint warmth fading into the cold stone, and he gave her his full attention. It felt strange. He'd been giving all his attention to the notation for hours and redirecting it was like turning a heavy piece of furniture. But he turned it.

"Yeah," he said. "Tell me."

She told him.

Marsh. The sixth-year who'd done the Carrow impression at Christmas dinner, the one who'd practised it for weeks beforehand and revealed it to the table with the timing of someone who understood that comedy was mostly about waiting, and the table had laughed, and Duncan had laughed so hard that pumpkin juice came out of his nose, and Gavin had laughed too because it was Christmas and they were scared and Marsh had made them briefly not scared. He'd stayed to fight. A wall had come down on him in the third-floor corridor. They'd found him under the rubble afterward, and he'd been dead for at least an hour by then, and a first-year had been sitting beside the pile of stone because she hadn't known what else to do.

Colin Creevey, the Gryffindor. Gavin thought he was fifteen, maybe sixteen — he couldn't remember. Creevey had snuck back into the castle to fight after being evacuated. He'd been too young to be there. The specifics of how he'd died were unclear, or Miren hadn't heard them, or she'd decided not to repeat them.

A Ravenclaw girl named Anand whom Gavin had sat next to exactly once in Herbology. She'd had dirt under her fingernails the whole lesson and she'd known the Latin name for every plant in greenhouse three and she'd corrected Professor Sprout on a soil acidity question, quietly, without embarrassment, and Sprout had conceded the point with a small nod that said she was impressed. A killing curse. Anand hadn't known it was coming.

There were others. Miren named them in a flat, even voice, one after another, the way you read a list you've already read too many times. Some Gavin recognized from corridors, from classes, from the muffled year they'd all shared in this damaged school. Some he didn't. A few were adults — Lupin, who'd taught Defence in Gavin's second year, the one with the patches on his robes and the tired kind voice that made you feel like he actually wanted you to learn the material. Tonks, the Auror, who Gavin had never spoken to but had seen in the corridors once, her hair bright pink against the stone grey of the castle walls.

Miren told him all of it. When she was done she put her hands on her knees and pressed down, and he could see her fingers whiten with the pressure against the fabric, as though she needed to feel the floor was solid.

"There are more," she said. "I don't know all the names yet. They're still — the Great Hall's a triage ward. Madam Pomfrey's got people everywhere, and there are house tables flipped on their sides for barriers, and they've laid out the..." She stopped. Started again. "Some of them are Death Eaters. Nobody's moving them yet. Nobody's sorting who's who."

"Are you hurt?" Gavin asked. He was looking at the cut on her forehead, the dark stain on her robes.

"This isn't mine." She touched the blood on her side. She didn't say whose it was. "The cut's nothing. I've had worse from Quidditch."

She hadn't played Quidditch this year. Nobody had. But it was something to say, and they both let it sit there without correcting it.

"Duncan?" Gavin said.

"Alive. He's upstairs." Something shifted in Miren's face — the flat reporting voice cracked for a moment and something else came through, not grief exactly but a kind of bewildered tenderness. "He was in the Room of Requirement with the younger kids during most of it. He came out near the end. He's been carrying water to the Great Hall for the past two hours. Him and two third-years with jugs. Just going back and forth."

Gavin could picture it. Duncan, thin and careful, his fair hair unwashed, the split lip that had healed badly, carrying water jugs because they were the thing in front of him that needed doing. Not dramatic. Not the kind of bravery anyone would write about. Just the repeated walk between the kitchen corridor and the Great Hall with something heavy in his hands, because people were hurt and thirsty and he could carry water.

"OK," Gavin said.

The quiet settled again. Somewhere above them, distantly, a sound that might have been crying or might have been the castle's stressed stone — the sub-collection filtered the world above through old limestone so that everything arrived distant and approximate, like hearing rain through a thick wall. It was impossible to tell pain from architecture from this deep.

Miren was watching him. It wasn't the way she'd looked at him in the classroom during the battle — that had been something between fury and horror, the look of someone seeing a person they loved choose something monstrous with perfect calm. This was different. Quieter. She was studying him with the careful attention of someone trying to map a landscape that had changed since the last time she'd looked. Not with shock — she'd had time to process — but with the steady focus of someone who needed to understand what she was looking at before she could decide what to do about it.

"You worked through it," she said. It wasn't a question.

"I tried. The substrate was — I couldn't do much. The noise from the fighting disrupted everything."

"The noise."

"The magical output. Hundreds of people casting simultaneously, it saturates everything I'm trying to perceive. It drowns it out."

"So the battle was an inconvenience."

The word sat between them. Miren said it without heat — not as an accusation but as a statement she was testing the weight of, the way you test whether a step will hold before you commit your weight to it.

"I didn't mean it like that," Gavin said. Then he stopped, because he had meant it like that, and she knew it, and lying about it now would be worse than the truth. "I mean. Yeah. For the work. It was."

She nodded. Slowly. She wasn't approving, just taking it in.

"Marsh is dead," she said. "Anand. Creevey. Lupin. All of them. And for you it was a disruption to your work."

"I know what that makes me sound like."

"Do you?"

"Yes." He looked at the dried blood on his hand — his blood, from the nosebleed, already browning against his skin. "I don't know what to do with that. I can't — I can hear it when I say it. I know what it makes me."

There was a long silence. Long enough that Gavin could hear the faint persistent drip of water finding its way through battle-damaged stone above, trickling down through new cracks in old limestone to places it hadn't reached before. A new path through old rock.

"I saw the curse," Miren said. Her voice had changed. Lower. Slower. She was feeling her way through something she'd been turning over for hours, maybe longer. "During the battle. I was near the east staircase and there was a Death Eater — not one I recognized, nobody important — and he cast something at you and it just stopped. The light was there and then it wasn't. Like somebody had turned it off."

Gavin remembered. Or didn't, exactly — he'd been thinking about the ward-substrate coupling problem, and the curse had been an interruption he'd dealt with reflexively and immediately let go of. His perception had registered the incoming spell as a substrate event, identified the agreement that made it valid, and edited that agreement locally so the curse was no longer a thing that existed. The whole process had taken less time than blinking. He had continued walking.

"I saw that," Miren said. "And I saw you keep walking like nothing had happened. Like a killing curse — it was green, Gavin, I know what green means — was the same as someone bumping you in a corridor."

"I wasn't thinking about it."

"I know." She was quiet for a moment. "That's what's frightening."

Another silence. Miren drew her knees up and rested her chin on them and looked at him across the three feet of limestone floor, and her face was very tired and very young and not angry. That was the thing he kept coming back to. She wasn't angry anymore. The anger had been there for months — the slow burn of watching someone she cared about choose something she couldn't see — and it had been there in the classroom during the battle, white-hot and desperate. But it was gone now, or used up, or it had turned into something else that didn't have a name, a quiet exhausted recognition that sat somewhere between grief and acceptance.

"The things you told me about," she said. "The wards."

"Yeah."

"They're real."

It wasn't a question. Gavin looked at her.

"I can't see what you see," she said. "I know that. I'm not going to pretend I understand any of it. The substrate, the Builders, the — whatever it is out there. I don't understand it. But I watched you dissolve a killing curse without noticing and then refuse to stop a war because it wasn't worth the interruption, and I've been thinking about that while I was pulling Creevey's body off the lawn, and the only way any of it makes sense — the only way you're not just..." She stopped. Started again, more carefully. "The only way you're not completely broken is if what you told me is true."

Gavin looked at the blood on his hand. He rubbed the dried flakes with his thumb, feeling them crumble against his skin, and the touch of it — the specific grain of dried blood against his own thumb — was the thing that was real right now, more real than anything he could say.

"It's true," he said.

"Yeah." Miren pressed her forehead against her knees and spoke into the fabric of her robes. "I know it is. I think I've known for a while. Since the quill, maybe. Or since the way your eyes go when you're looking at something I can't see. Like you're reading a page that isn't there." She lifted her head. "You really see them? The wards? Right now?"

"I can feel them. It's not visual, exactly. It's more like — " He stopped. The old problem. The words didn't exist for what he perceived because the perception wasn't built for human language, and every time he tried to describe it he ran into the same wall, the same gap between experience and speech that had been widening all year. "They're there. They're always there now. And they've worn down since yesterday."

"Because of the battle?"

"Maybe. Partly. They've been thinning for centuries. The battle might have — I don't know. The substrate took a lot of stress from all that combat magic. But I haven't been able to check properly. The noise only just stopped."

"And beyond them. Beyond the wards."

"It's still there."

Miren held his gaze. Her eyes were dark and red-rimmed and steady. "Are you going to fix them?"

There it was. The question he'd been asking himself since December, since the first time he'd felt the wards directly from the Astronomy Tower, since the manuscript had shown him the scope of what was failing and the scale of what failure meant. The question that had no good answer because the honest answer was the worst one.

"I don't know," he said.

Miren waited. She could do that — listen with a fierce attention that was more intense than speech, a quality of listening that had always made Gavin feel she could see further into what he was saying than he could himself. She was doing it now. And it was different from all the times before, because this time she was listening to something she was prepared to believe.

"The wards were built by something that — they weren't human, Miren. They weren't even close to human. They used magic the way we use... I don't know, air. It was their medium. Their environment. And they built the wards as they were leaving, and the wards were designed for that kind of intelligence to maintain, and I'm a fifteen-year-old who failed his Transfiguration OWL and can't do a proper Switching Spell." He looked at the manuscript on the floor beside him. The notation on the exposed page shifted slowly, relationships between concepts drifting and reconfiguring, and he could read about a third of what it was saying. "I've been working on this for seven months and I can read maybe a third of the notation on a good day. And the parts I can read — the maintenance protocols, the coupling mechanics — they're the introductory sections. The Builders' equivalent of a primer. For children. For their children, who could do things I can't even imagine. And the primer is the hardest thing I've ever tried to understand."

"So you can't fix them."

"I don't know. I might not be smart enough. The problem might be too big." He picked at the last flake of dried blood on his knuckle. "But nobody else can see them. Nobody else knows they're failing. Nobody else is working on it."

"Just you."

"Just me."

Miren looked at him for a long time. The thin line of dried blood from her hairline to her eyebrow had darkened to a rust colour, and the dust in her hair had settled into the creases of her plait so that it looked grey in the sub-collection's pale light. She looked older than fifteen, older than she had an hour ago, like someone who had survived something and was still deciding what surviving it meant.

"Right," she said.

She stood up. She was slow about it — stiff, bruised, running on nothing — and she steadied herself with one hand against the wall and stood there looking down at him. He was sitting on the floor of the sub-collection with the manuscript beside him and the blood on his hand and the faint light from limestone walls and the work that would outlast everything she'd just survived and everything she was going back to.

"Miren."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry about Marsh."

Something crumpled in her face. Just for a second — a flash of grief that she put away with the speed of someone who'd been putting grief away all night because there was too much of it to hold and she'd been holding it for hours and would hold it for years but not right now, not here, not standing in a room full of old stone where a different kind of loss was measured on a different kind of scale.

"He was funny," she said. Her voice was thick. "He was really funny and he was seventeen and he stayed because he said someone had to make sure the Carrows knew what their own faces looked like when they were scared. He practised that impression for weeks. He had Alecto's walk down and everything."

Gavin didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that was adequate to a seventeen-year-old who'd been brave and funny and was now under a pile of stone, and the silence was better than the wrong words, so he let it be.

"I'll come back," Miren said. "Or — I don't know. I'll come back."

"OK."

She turned and walked out of the sub-collection. Her footsteps echoed in the corridor — fast, then fading, then they were gone — and the old quiet reasserted itself, the quiet that had been here before the castle was built and would be here after, and Gavin was alone.

---

He sat with the names for a while.

Marsh, Anand, Creevey, Lupin, Tonks. He thought about Marsh at Christmas, screwing up his face into Alecto's hard little walk until Duncan laughed pumpkin juice through his nose. He thought about Anand's hands with soil worked into the lines of them. He thought about Creevey coming back when he should have stayed gone. Dead was a stupidly small word for what had happened to them, how physical it was: stone falling, green light, a body under a sheet.

The crack in the ceiling let in a line of morning light and, with it, a smell the sub-collection never had on its own: old smoke, plaster dust, blood. Somewhere above him something heavy scraped over stone, stopped, then scraped again.

Gavin got up and went upstairs.

The corridor outside the hidden stair looked half collapsed and half abandoned in a hurry. One section of wall had broken open to the morning, so cold air came straight in off the grounds carrying damp and ash. Spell burns blackened the stones around the Great Hall doors. A suit of armour had come apart and been shoved against the wall in pieces. One metal hand lay on the floor by itself.

The Great Hall stood open. House tables were on their sides with blankets hung over them for screens. The floor between them was streaked with muddy footprints and water and blood thinned pink by bad mopping. Madam Pomfrey was shouting for dittany from somewhere he couldn't see. Near the doors, a body under a white sheet still had one shoe showing, the laces dark and wet. Duncan came past with a glass jug in each hand and a third-year on either side of him carrying their own. Water slopped over all three of them. Duncan's sleeves were shoved up. His hands shook hard enough to make the jugs clink together, and he kept walking.

Gavin stood there until somebody inside the hall started crying in the flat, astonished way people cried when the work paused long enough for them to hear themselves. Then he turned back.

The manuscript was where he'd left it, open on the limestone floor, the page still faintly warm under his fingers when he sat down again.

Overhead the wards held, thinner than yesterday. Beyond them the pressure remained, patient and wrong. He sat in the beam from the cracked ceiling and found his place. His nose started bleeding again before he finished the first paragraph. Blood struck the margin, spread, and sank into the page. He leaned closer, turned the page with his shaking left hand, and read.
