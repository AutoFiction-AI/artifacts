# Chapter 6

The passage had been resisting him for three days.

Not the notation itself — he could read the notation now, or at least follow its structures the way someone who had studied a language for a term could follow a newspaper article, guessing at half the words and filling in the rest from context. The problem was that this section of the manuscript was describing relationships between agreements, and each relationship assumed he understood things he did not, and each of those assumed a framework he could not quite see, and the whole thing kept folding in on itself like a sentence that referenced its own grammar.

He had the manuscript spread on his bed with the curtains drawn, the sheets of not-quite-parchment weighted at the corners with his Potions textbook, a sock, his wand, and a quill he wasn't using. His left hand was smudged with ink where he'd been making notes in the margin of his Ancient Runes textbook, which was the only notebook he had that Miren wouldn't borrow. Outside the dormitory windows the light was already gone. Late November in Scotland meant dark by half four, and the windowpanes had that black-mirror quality where you saw the room reflected back rather than the grounds.

He was working through the same three lines he'd been stuck on since Tuesday. The notation seemed to show two agreements overlapping, occupying the same conceptual space without canceling each other, and he could almost see how that related to the way a Levitation Charm rested on a substrate structure more complex than the charm itself — the way a word sat on top of a grammar the speaker never thought about. Almost. The almost was the problem. He kept reaching for it and it kept not being there.

The dormitory door opened. Ernie Macmillan stood in the gap, his prefect's badge slightly crooked, his face holding the particular expression of someone delivering news they already knew was bad.

"Everyone to the Great Hall. Now."

"What's—"

"Now, Tench. Everyone means everyone."

Gavin closed the manuscript inside his Runes textbook and slid both under his pillow. He straightened his robes — he hadn't changed out of them, hadn't been to dinner — and followed Ernie into the common room, where students were already pulling on shoes and buttoning cloaks with the hurried, tight-shouldered movements of people who had learned that summonses were not optional.

The walk from the Hufflepuff common room to the Great Hall was short — two minutes, same level — and by the time Gavin reached the entrance corridor he could see it wasn't just Hufflepuff. Students poured out of stairwells from every direction. Ravenclaws coming down from their tower in a quiet column, Gryffindors in a tight group that moved like something trying not to scatter, Slytherins walking a bit faster and looking a bit less frightened, though not by much. A first-year near the entrance was crying without making noise, her face wet and her expression controlled in a way that no eleven-year-old should have known how to produce. A prefect — Gavin didn't see which house — had a hand on the girl's shoulder and was steering her forward with the practised efficiency of someone who had done this before.

The Great Hall had been cleared. The four house tables were gone, vanished or shunted against the walls, and the stone floor was bare and cold and enormous — the full length of the Hall laid open, hundreds of students filing in to stand shoulder to shoulder under the enchanted ceiling. The ceiling showed the November sky: low cloud, no stars, the kind of solid darkness that settled over the Highlands and stayed until morning. The candles floated in their usual constellations but the light they cast felt wrong without the tables beneath them, as if the room had been undressed.

Amycus Carrow stood at the far end, where the staff table normally sat. He was sweating. His robes were plain black with the sleeves pushed up to his elbows, and the skin of his forearms was pale and lightly freckled in a way that Gavin wished he hadn't noticed because it made Carrow look like a person standing in a kitchen, not a man with a wand pointed at five hundred children. His sister Alecto stood a pace behind him, arms folded across her chest, her chin slightly raised. She wore rings on three fingers — heavy, dark-stoned — and her hands looked like they'd been dressed for an occasion.

Carrow let the silence build. He was not sophisticated about it, just willing to wait while the sound drained from a room full of frightened students, and the waiting worked. Students stopped shuffling. Someone's shoe squeaked on stone and the sound was enormous. The first-year near the door stopped crying, or at least stopped audibly.

"Someone," Carrow said, and his voice carried without amplification, pitched to fill space the way someone who had been drilling others would pitch it, "thought they'd have a laugh."

He paused. Gavin was near the middle of the crowd, between a Ravenclaw boy a year younger than him — fourth-year, with ink on his fingers and the compressed, swallowing look of someone trying not to be sick — and a Hufflepuff girl he thought was named Chambers. The Ravenclaw was pressing his thumbs against his fingertips one at a time, methodically, some private counting sequence that kept his hands busy and his mind on something other than the front of the room.

"Dumbledore's Army, Still Recruiting." Carrow's lips pulled back from his teeth. The expression was not a smile, but it used the same muscles. "Painted on the wall outside this very Hall. Letters high as a man. Charm-resistant paint. Somebody put real thought into it."

He rolled his wand between his fingers, a slow rotation, and his eyes moved across the crowd the way you'd scan a shelf for a particular item you didn't expect to find.

"Now, I could ask who did it. Could drag you up here one by one and find out. But that'd take all night, and I've got better things to do with my evening." He looked at the crowd. "So here's what's going to happen instead. You're all going to learn a lesson. Every single one of you."

The lesson was the Cruciatus Curse.

He demonstrated first. He pointed at a boy near the front row — a fourth-year Gryffindor, red-haired, freckled, who flinched when Carrow's wand came up but did not step back because there was nowhere to step and the boy behind him was pressed against his shoulder blades. The curse hit and the Gryffindor went down.

The sound a person made under the Cruciatus was not a scream in any way that Gavin understood screaming. It was the sound the body made when every nerve fired simultaneously with a pain signal that had no physical source, no location, no injury to correspond to. The boy's back arched off the stone and his mouth was open and the sound that came out was high and airless and sustained, a single note that went on longer than Gavin would have thought a person could hold without breathing. His hands clawed at the floor, fingers scraping stone, and his heels drummed and his head rolled and there was no part of his body that was not trying to escape a thing that existed inside every part of his body.

And beneath it — under the sound, under the visible — Gavin saw something flicker. The same kind of layered structure he'd perceived beneath the first-year's Levitation Charm at breakfast weeks ago, except this one was larger and organized around a single function. It looked like a set of instructions for how nerves should fire and in what order and with what intensity. An agreement about pain, written in the space between the wand and the boy. As specific and arbitrary as a recipe.

Then Carrow lifted the curse and the boy lay on the stone gasping and twitching, his hands opening and closing, a dark stain spreading across the front of his trousers because the curse had taken everything from him, including that. A girl near the front stepped toward him and Carrow said "Leave him" without looking at her and she stopped.

"Right," Carrow said. "Pair up."

The silence in the Hall changed quality. It had been the silence of fear. Now it was the silence of people understanding what was about to be asked of them.

"You heard me. Every two. Face each other. You're going to practice."

Nobody moved. Alecto stepped forward and began walking through the crowd, pointing at pairs, grabbing shoulders and turning bodies into position. She moved with brisk physical competence, the rings on her fingers catching candlelight as she shoved a tall Ravenclaw girl into place opposite a Hufflepuff boy who looked like he was going to faint.

"When I say practice," Amycus said, "I don't mean wave your wand about and hope for the best. I mean cast the curse. On your partner. You want to know what it feels like to fight for the right side, this is where you start."

Gavin was paired with the Ravenclaw boy — the one with ink-stained fingers who had been counting with his thumbs. Up close, the boy had a narrow face and brown eyes that were trying very hard to be steady and were not quite managing it. A fourth-year. Gavin didn't know his name.

"I'm not going to," the boy said, very quietly, with a strange, careful politeness, as if he were declining a second helping of pudding.

"Yeah," Gavin said. "Me neither."

They stood facing each other with their wands out because Alecto was circling and not having your wand out earned a visit. Around them, students raised wands with shaking hands and pointed them at people they ate meals with. Nothing happened for most of them. The Cruciatus required genuine malicious intent — the desire to cause suffering, not the pretense of it — and most of the students in this room were incapable of producing that even when they were frightened enough to try. Wands moved and the air stayed still and the failure was a kind of mercy, though Carrow was not inclined to see it that way.

"Put your back into it!" He was walking through the pairs now, watching. "I want results. You — Goyle — that's pathetic. Again. Again."

A few managed something. A Slytherin boy hit his partner and the girl staggered sideways, clutched her arm, made a sound that was not quite a cry but close. Not the full curse — something diluted, a fraction of what Carrow had demonstrated — but enough to hurt. The Slytherin boy looked at his own wand as if it had done something he hadn't meant.

Across the Hall, Gavin could see Miren standing three rows forward, paired with a Gryffindor girl. Neither of them was casting. Duncan was a few pairs away from Miren, standing opposite a Hufflepuff sixth-year, and his face held the careful blankness Gavin had been seeing more of since October — the expression of a Muggle-born boy who had learned exactly what attracted attention and was trying with everything he had to produce the opposite. His wand was up. He was not casting. His partner was not casting at him. They stood there like two people in a queue, waiting for something to be over.

Gavin stood opposite the Ravenclaw and did not cast. He was not thinking about the boy. He was not thinking about the room. He was thinking about the third line of the passage he'd been working on — the one where two agreements seemed to occupy the same conceptual space, and whether the relationship between them was hierarchical or parallel, and what it would mean if agreements could nest inside each other the way clauses nested inside sentences, and—

"Not trying, are we?"

Gavin looked up. Carrow was three feet away. His face was damp, the lopsided set of his jaw making him look like he was permanently bracing against a wind from the left. His wand was at his side but not relaxed — held the way a man held a tool he was about to use.

"Sorry, I—"

"Sorry." Carrow repeated the word the way he'd repeat an insult, tasting it. "Sorry. That's nice. That's lovely. What's your name?"

"Tench."

"House?"

"Hufflepuff."

"Right. Tench, Hufflepuff." Carrow raised his wand. His lips were wet. "Let me show you what you're meant to be practicing."

The Cruciatus hit Gavin in the chest and everything that happened next was that his body stopped being his.

Every nerve fired. Not a signal of damage — there was no damage, nothing cut, nothing broken — but the full saturating experience of pain from everywhere simultaneously. His legs gave out because the signal to stand was overridden by the signal that everything was wrong. He hit the stone floor with his shoulder and then his head, and the impact registered as a dull, distant thing underneath the primary thing, which was the pain. It was not like being burned or cut or hit. It did not have an analogy. It was the body's entire capacity for suffering activated at once, every channel open, every nerve reporting the same impossible message, and his mind could not process it because there was nothing to process — no source to locate, no injury to protect, no muscle to brace. His back arched. His hands curled into fists and his fingernails cut into his palms and he could feel that too, the small specific pain underneath the impossible one. His mouth was open. He could hear himself making a sound that was high and continuous and did not belong to him.

The floor was cold under his cheek. That registered. Stone, and gritty, the grit pressing into his skin. His jaw was clenched so hard his teeth were grinding and he could feel one of his back molars shifting in its socket. There was a taste in his mouth — copper, and something chemical behind the copper — and he realized he had bitten through the inside of his cheek.

Then it stopped.

It stopped and his body did not stop. His muscles had been clenched against nothing for however long the nothing had lasted — three seconds, ten, a year — and they didn't know it was over. He lay on the floor of the Great Hall and shook. His jaw ached. His shoulder throbbed where he'd hit stone. His skin was still reporting at full volume, every sensation turned up past where the dial was supposed to go: the cold of the floor, the grit, the fabric of his robes twisted against his ribs, the air moving across the sweat on the back of his neck. Everything was too much and too present and his hands were opening and closing and his left hand was doing something he could not control — a fine, rapid tremor that pulsed through his fingers and up into his wrist as if a wire inside him had come loose and was vibrating.

He got up. Not because he was brave or defiant, not because getting up proved anything about who he was. He got up because the curse was over and lying on the stone floor of the Great Hall while Amycus Carrow looked down at him was worse than standing. His legs shook. He locked his knees and held his shaking left hand against his thigh where the tremor wasn't visible, or less visible, and he stood there and breathed.

"Better," Carrow said, and moved on.

Carrow continued through the room. He hit two more students — a Ravenclaw girl who screamed and a Gryffindor boy who didn't — and then stood at the front again and watched the pairs with his arms folded and his wand hanging loose in his right hand, patient now, the lesson delivered. The Ravenclaw boy opposite Gavin had gone very white. His counting had stopped. His ink-stained fingers were trembling too, though Carrow hadn't touched him.

It lasted another hour. Maybe longer. Time had lost its edges inside Gavin's body after the curse. Students continued to point wands at each other and mostly fail and occasionally succeed, and the occasional successes got quieter as the session went on, the gasps smaller, the staggering more controlled, because the students who could produce the curse were learning what it felt like to hurt someone they knew and the lesson was not the one Carrow intended. Gavin stood and breathed and pressed his left hand against his leg and the metallic taste did not leave his mouth.

When Carrow finally dismissed them — "Think about that next time one of you wants to play artist" — the students poured out of the Great Hall in a dense, quiet mass. Nobody pushed. Nobody spoke above a murmur. The red-haired Gryffindor boy, the first demonstration, was walking under his own power, his jaw rigid and the dark stain on his trousers visible to everyone who looked and seen by no one, because not seeing it was the kindness they could manage. The first-year from the entrance was being steered by the same prefect, her steps mechanical, her face the color of the corridor walls.

The Ravenclaw boy — Gavin's pair partner — passed him at the doors. He nodded at Gavin, a small, precise gesture, like he was closing a parenthesis on something they had shared without sharing it. Then he turned up the stairwell toward Ravenclaw Tower and Gavin went the other way.

His left hand trembled the whole walk back.

---

The Hufflepuff common room was warm and smelled like boiled carrots from the kitchens next door. The fire was burning. Students came through the barrel entrance in ones and twos and filled the low-ceilinged space with the particular quiet of people who had been afraid together and were now safe and did not know what to do with the distance between the two.

A sixth-year girl was crying on the sofa by the fire — not the gasping kind, the slow, leaking kind that looked like it could go on for hours, the kind that came from a place below where crying was useful. Two friends sat on either side of her. One had an arm around her shoulders; the other had fetched a cup of tea from somewhere and was holding it near her hands without insisting she take it. A boy in Gavin's year — Collins, the one who played chess in the common room most evenings and always set up the board with the white queen on the wrong square — was sitting on the floor against the wall with his knees up and his wand beside him on the flagstones. He'd put it down and his body language said clearly that he did not want to pick it up again.

Miren came in with Duncan. She had her hand on his arm, not guiding him, just resting there, a point of contact. Duncan's face still held the careful nothing, but his hands were in his pockets, and Gavin could see through the fabric that they were clenched. They sat on the bench near the fire. Miren said something to Duncan that Gavin couldn't hear. Duncan shook his head once, a minimal movement, and Miren didn't push.

Gavin sat in the armchair by the window — the one slightly too far from the fire and slightly too close to the draft that came through the garden-level glass. His left hand was still trembling. He pressed it flat against the chair arm and felt the tremor vibrate into the wood, a fine buzzing that was visible if you looked and invisible if you didn't.

His jaw ached. His shoulder hurt where he'd hit the floor. His legs had that wrung-out feeling, muscles that had been clenched too hard for too long and were now loose in a way that didn't feel like relaxation but like something broken. The taste of copper sat on his tongue, underneath everything else, a residue. These were the facts of what had been done to him and he noted them the way he would note the symptoms of a flu: present, specific, not interesting.

What he was actually angry about was that it was half past eight and the evening was gone.

He noticed the thought. He was aware of it arriving, settling into the front of his mind, shouldering aside the things that should have been there — the outrage, the horror, the solidarity with the crying girl on the sofa and the boy who couldn't touch his wand and Duncan with his hands clenched in his pockets. Those feelings existed somewhere. They were present the way the ache in his shoulder was present: real, available if he reached for them, not the thing his attention went to when left to itself. What his attention went to was the passage about overlapping agreements, the one that had been on the edge of yielding before Ernie opened the dormitory door, and the three hours that had been taken from him, and the fact that the manuscript was under his pillow and his left hand was shaking too badly to hold a quill.

He looked at the common room. At the girl crying with her two friends. At Collins on the floor. At Miren sitting with Duncan, her hand on his arm, her face held still in the way she held it when she was most upset — not frozen but controlled, as if she'd decided what her face was going to do and would not allow it to deviate. These were people dealing with something that had happened to all of them. These were his friends, or close enough. And his dominant emotion, the one that sat in the front of his chest like a fist, was irritation about his schedule.

This was wrong. He knew it was wrong. He knew that the correct response to being tortured by your teacher was not to calculate lost study hours. Not wrong the way cruelty was wrong or cowardice was wrong — wrong the way a compass pointing south was wrong, a fault in the mechanism itself. Something had shifted. The manuscript had shifted it, or the perception it had opened in him had shifted it, and the shift was not something he had chosen or could undo by choosing.

He wanted to go to his trunk. He wanted to pull the manuscript out and find the passage and try to work through it, even with his hand trembling and his jaw aching and the taste of blood in his mouth. He wanted that more than he wanted to sit in this room with these people and feel the things he was supposed to feel. The wanting was specific and sharp and it frightened him a little because it was not how a person should feel after what had just happened, and he could see that, and seeing it did not change it.

He stayed in the chair. He did not go to his trunk. This was the one concession he could make to the version of himself that still understood how to be a person in a room with other people: he would sit here for a reasonable period and he would not pull out the manuscript and he would not work. He would sit.

He sat. The fire cracked. The crying girl's friend finally persuaded her to take the tea. Collins leaned his head against the wall and closed his eyes. The draft from the window pressed cold against Gavin's left side and his left hand buzzed against the chair arm and he stared at the fire and thought about overlapping agreements.

---

Miren came to him an hour later.

She crossed the common room with a deliberateness that said she'd been building to this since they sat down — watching him from the bench, watching him sit alone by the draft and not go to anyone, not speak to anyone, not look at anyone for longer than it took to register they were there. She sat in the chair opposite his, the one with the lopsided cushion that listed to the right, and she looked at him and did not speak for long enough that the silence became the opening statement.

"How's your hand?" she asked.

"It's fine. Just a tremor. It'll stop."

"When?"

"I don't know. Probably soon." He moved his left hand to his lap, where the shaking was less obvious. It wasn't less obvious. "How's Duncan?"

"He went to bed." She watched Gavin's face. "He didn't get hit. Carrow went after the Gryffindors mostly, the ones who weren't trying hard enough. But Duncan stood there the whole time and didn't move and didn't speak and I think standing through it was worse for him than getting hit would've been. Because he knew Carrow was looking at the Muggle-borns specifically and deciding whether to make a point, and Duncan was waiting for that, for three hours, and it didn't come, and now he doesn't know if it'll come tomorrow instead."

"Yeah."

"Yeah." She repeated it, flat.

The fire was lower. Most of the students had gone to their dormitories. The crying girl had been led away by her friends. Collins was still on the floor against the wall, either asleep or pretending to be. The common room had that late-evening quality where the warmth turned stale and the firelight moved across the low ceiling in long, uneven strokes and the room felt like it was settling, sinking into itself.

"You weren't there," Miren said.

Gavin looked at her.

"In the Hall. You were standing there and you weren't there. I could see you, Gav. You were three rows behind me. And when Carrow was walking through the room you were somewhere else. Your eyes were open and you were looking at things and you weren't seeing any of it."

"I was—"

"Don't tell me you were fine. Don't tell me you were handling it differently. You were doing the thing you do now where you go inside your head and the rest of us stop being real." She said this without raising her voice. Her hands were in her lap, still. Her body was held in that contained way she had when she was most upset, as if motion would crack something open that she'd decided to keep shut. "I've been watching it for weeks. But tonight I watched you get Cruciated and get up and your face didn't — you weren't angry at Carrow. You were angry at something else. I could see it."

"I know," Gavin said.

"Do you?"

"I know it looks like— Mir, I know. I wasn't. Present." The word came out like something he'd reached for and found at arm's length. "I've been working on something and it's taken up— I can't stop thinking about it. Even when I should be thinking about other things. Even tonight."

"Working on something."

"The manuscript. The one I've been translating. I know you've noticed—"

"The library thing." Her voice got shorter. "The book you've been translating for weeks. The one you disappear to work on until one in the morning. The one you won't talk about."

"It's not that I won't talk about it. It's that I can't— when I try to explain what it actually—" He stopped. Started again. "It's not a book. Not exactly. The notation in it, the symbols, they describe something underneath magic. Not a spell, not a technique. More like a layer underneath spells. Like, if a spell is a sentence, this is the—" He could hear himself fumbling and knew every word was making it worse. "The rules that let spells happen. The grammar. Except grammar's wrong too because it's not language, it's—"

"It sounds like a school project."

"It's not a school project."

"It sounds like a school project that you've decided is more important than—" She stopped herself. She did not gesture at the room, at the students who had gone to bed shaking, at the common room that smelled of cold carrots and fear and exhaustion. She didn't need to. The room did the work.

"I'm not saying it's more important."

"What are you saying, then?"

Gavin opened his mouth. The honest answer was too large and too strange and did not have words that would survive being spoken aloud in this room, in this moment. He had found a layer of reality beneath magic. The manuscript had changed his perception and he could see things now that he could not see before and the change could not be reversed and it frightened him and it also felt like the most real thing in his life, more real than the Carrows, more real than OWLs, and he knew that was wrong but knowing it was wrong did not make it less true.

What came out was: "I'm saying what I've found matters. I think it matters more than I can explain right now."

Miren looked at him. Her eyes were steady and dark and the anger in them was the kind that came from caring, which was worse than the kind that came from contempt because it meant she was still paying attention.

"We got Cruciated tonight," she said. "All of us. Every student in this school stood in the Great Hall and got tortured by our teacher. Collins can't pick up his wand. There's a fourth-year Gryffindor walking back to his dormitory in pissed trousers because Carrow used him as a demonstration. Duncan's upstairs right now lying in bed wondering if tomorrow's his turn. And you're sitting here telling me your manuscript matters."

"That's not—"

"That's what you're telling me. That is exactly what you're telling me. Because you came back from that Hall and sat in that chair and didn't say one word to anyone and the only thing you're upset about is that it took three hours out of your evening."

She was right. She was exactly right and he couldn't argue with her because the evidence was his own behavior, which he had not thought to manage because the idea that he should perform the correct emotional response to being tortured hadn't occurred to him as a thing that needed performing. His emotions had been occupied elsewhere.

"Mir—"

"I sat with Duncan for an hour. You were right there. Ten feet away. You didn't come over. You didn't ask him if he was all right. You sat by the window and stared at the fire and I could see you — I could see you were working on something in your head. After that. After all of that."

"I know."

"You keep saying you know. Great. So knowing it, what are you going to do? Keep going? Keep disappearing to that library room until one in the morning while the rest of us actually deal with what's happening?"

"I can't explain it to you because the explanation doesn't work in—" He was doing the thing where he started sentences and abandoned them, reaching for formulations that kept not existing. "What I can see now, since reading it — the way magic works underneath, the structure of it, it's not what anyone thinks it is. It's not just a different kind of spell, it's a completely different—" His left hand was trembling against his thigh. He put his right hand over it, pressing the fingers flat. "I know what it looks like."

"It looks like you've checked out."

The words landed in the space between them and sat there.

"I haven't checked out."

"Then come back in." She said it simply, like it was simple. "Come help with the first-years. Come sit with Duncan. Come do one thing that isn't about the manuscript for one evening. Just tonight. That's it."

"I will. I'll do that. Yeah."

But she had heard the hesitation before the agreement. The half-second where his attention had snagged on the manuscript under his pillow, the passage waiting for him, the problem that was more present to him than the problem in front of him. He saw her register it — her mouth tightening, her shoulders settling into something that wasn't anger anymore but was worse than anger. A kind of tired clarity. The look of someone who had asked a question they already knew the answer to and had gotten the answer they expected.

"Right," she said. "Fine."

She didn't slam anything. She didn't raise her voice. She stood and looked at him for a moment with an expression he couldn't read because it was composed of too many things — frustration and worry and disappointment and something underneath all of those that might have been grief, the early stages of it, the kind that came when you watched someone leave a room they were still physically inside. Then she turned and walked to the dormitory corridor and the common room absorbed her departure the way it absorbed everything this year — quietly, without comment, with the fire still burning and the draft still pressing through the garden-level glass.

Gavin sat in the chair. His left hand trembled. The copper taste had faded to a residue, barely there, the body's diminishing record of what had been done to it.

He thought about going after her. He thought about knocking on the dormitory door and saying what he hadn't managed to say — about the substrate, about perception, about how the manuscript had changed the way he saw magic at a level he did not yet understand and could not yet describe. He thought about telling her that when Carrow cast the Cruciatus on the Gryffindor boy, Gavin had seen the architecture of the curse, the layered structure beneath it, and it had looked like the same kind of agreement that sat beneath a Levitation Charm, just organized around a different function. Pain and levitation, built on the same grammar. And wasn't that important? Wasn't that worth knowing? Didn't the fact that torture and floating feathers had the same underlying structure mean something that mattered more than—

He stopped the thought. He stopped it because he could hear where it was going and where it was going was exactly where Miren had said he already was: a boy who had decided his book was more important than his friends' suffering. And she was not wrong about what it looked like. She was wrong about what it was — or he believed she was wrong, which was not the same thing — but she was right about everything she could see, and everything she could see was everything he could show her, and he could not show her the substrate because showing it was not possible.

He sat until the fire burned low and Collins opened his eyes and looked at Gavin across the dim common room with an expression that was just exhaustion, just a boy who had been on the floor for too long, and then Collins picked up his wand — carefully, with two fingers, as if it might be hot — and stood and went to bed without speaking. Then Gavin was alone.

He went to his dormitory. He lay in bed. He did not take the manuscript out from under his pillow, though he could feel it there, the slight warmth of the not-quite-parchment through the cotton pillowcase, and his hand — his right hand, the one that didn't shake — rested on the pillow next to it.

His left hand trembled against the blanket. The dormitory was dark. He could hear Duncan's breathing from the next bed over, too even, too controlled, the breathing of someone who was awake and managing it.

Miren was right about what she could see. She was right that he had checked out of the room he was standing in. She was right that his anger was pointed wrong. The only thing she was wrong about was the part she couldn't know — that the manuscript was not a distraction from what mattered but might, in a way he could not yet prove or articulate or even fully understand, be the thing that mattered most.

Or he was wrong. He was a fifteen-year-old Hufflepuff with mediocre marks in Ancient Runes and a shaking hand and a tendency to believe that the thing consuming his attention was the most important thing in the world, and maybe Miren was right about all of it, the visible and the invisible, and the manuscript was just a manuscript and he had checked out and the checking out was the whole story.

He didn't know. His hand shook. Duncan breathed. The manuscript waited under his pillow, warm and patient, and somewhere in its pages the passage about overlapping agreements held the shape of something he had almost understood before the world interrupted.

He lay in the dark and did not sleep for a long time.
