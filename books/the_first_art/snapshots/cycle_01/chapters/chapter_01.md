# Chapter 1

The compartment smelled like dust and old upholstery charm. There were three of them in a space meant for six: Gavin by the window, Duncan beside him with his bag on his lap, Miren across with her feet tucked under her on the seat. The train pulled out of King's Cross eleven minutes late, and the sound was wrong even before they cleared the station.

Usually the Hogwarts Express was loud from the start. The sound was a layered thing — compartment doors banging open, someone's cat loose in the corridor, older students shouting for friends or sweets, the trolley rattling before anyone was settled. Today the corridor was quiet. The doors stayed shut. When someone did pass outside their compartment, Gavin could hear their footsteps on the carpet, which he had never been able to hear before over the noise.

"She didn't come," Miren said. She had been watching the platform through the grimy window as London slid past, and now she turned with the expression she wore when she was working herself up to something, though the something hadn't arrived yet. Her dark hair was in the tight plait she always wore for doing things, and she had a sunburn across her nose and the tops of her cheeks — she'd told them in a letter that she'd spent August helping her mum repaint the house in Lewisham, the whole outside, on ladders. The sunburn was peeling at the edges. She picked at it when she was thinking.

"She wasn't there. I watched the whole platform."

"Her family might've come through a different entrance," Gavin said.

"There's one entrance, Gav."

"Right. Yeah."

"That's four from our year. Four that I know of. Collins, Abiodun, Singh, and now her. And that's just Hufflepuffs."

Duncan hadn't said anything since they'd settled into the compartment. He was looking at the stretch of seat beside Miren — the empty space where someone should have been sitting — and his hands were on his bag and he was holding very still. He'd been holding very still since King's Cross. On the platform, Gavin had seen Duncan's parents standing together in their Muggle clothes — his mum in a rain jacket despite the clear weather, his dad in the kind of shoes you wore to a job interview even though there was no interview. His mum had pressed a Tupperware container of sandwiches into Duncan's hands and Duncan had tried to give it back and she'd pressed it in again and they had both been smiling and the smiles had been terrible.

Duncan was Muggle-born. Both parents, no wizarding relatives, no pureblood grandfather to claim. He had been a name on a list since August, the list the Muggle-Born Registration Commission had drawn up, and the fact that he was on the train at all was a decision his family had made that Gavin couldn't fully imagine the weight of. Come back and be watched, or stay home and be found.

Gavin didn't know what to say about any of it. He had spent his own summer doing OWL revision in his bedroom in Swindon, which now felt like something that had happened in a different country. His parents were both wizards, both unremarkable, both fine. His dad worked in Magical Transportation — the scheduling division, not the policy side — and his mum did part-time apothecary supply. They had been worried about this year, but in the general way parents worried, which was to send extra socks and tell him to keep his head down.

He kept his head down naturally. It wasn't something he had to work at.

"Have you heard anything about what it's actually going to be like?" Miren asked. She was asking both of them but looking at Gavin, because Duncan had the expression that meant he would rather not be in this conversation. "The Carrows. Amycus and Alecto. Do you know what they're planning?"

"Snape's headmaster," Gavin said. "Everyone knows that."

"I know everyone knows that. I mean what are the Carrows going to do? With us. In lessons."

"They've made Dark Arts mandatory. Not Defense Against."

"I know. And Muggle Studies. That's mandatory now too." She stopped herself. The anger was coming up, the thing she had been building toward, and she was trying to say it carefully because everything she was about to say pressed against Duncan in a way that couldn't be helped but shouldn't be made worse.

Duncan opened his bag and took out a textbook. Advanced Potion-Making. He opened it to somewhere near the middle and looked at it.

"Muggle Studies is going to be —" Miren lowered her voice. "It's not Muggle Studies anymore. You know that. It's going to be about how Muggles are animals, or whatever they — it's propaganda. And they're making everyone take it."

"I know what it is," Duncan said. His eyes hadn't moved on the page.

"Yeah. I know you know."

They were quiet. The train was past the city now, into the green stretches of middle England, and the sky was flat and grey and had been grey since morning. Gavin could hear the rain start on the roof, a faint drumming that made the compartment feel smaller.

Duncan closed the textbook. "Did you hear about the Potions change?"

"What change?"

"Slughorn's staying on, apparently. He wanted to leave, but." Duncan shrugged with one shoulder. It was a minimal movement, precise, the kind of shrug that conserved energy. "Someone told me on the platform."

"Well, that's something," Miren said. "At least Potions won't be —"

"Won't be what?"

"I don't know. Something."

They went quiet again. None of them slept. The train moved through the Midlands and the countryside looked the same as it always had and the rain came and went and came back. Miren got up once to look down the corridor and came back without saying what she'd seen, which was probably more closed doors and fewer faces than there should have been.

Sometime around Doncaster, Duncan opened the Tupperware. The sandwiches inside were ham and English mustard on white bread, cut into triangles, each one wrapped separately in cling film. He held the container out without looking at either of them. Gavin took one. The bread was soft and the mustard had bite and it tasted like somebody's kitchen — a specific kitchen in Sheffield where a woman in a rain jacket had wrapped each triangle carefully and pressed the whole container into her son's hands twice on a platform because once hadn't been enough. Duncan ate one himself, slowly, looking out the window at fields that were beginning to darken. Miren shook her head when he offered and Duncan didn't push it, and the three of them sat with the open Tupperware between them and the rain on the roof and the quiet that shouldn't have been there.

Gavin read through his Transfiguration notes, which he'd been meaning to review since July and hadn't. He was going into his OWL year and he was average at Transfiguration, average at Charms, slightly below average at Potions, passable at Herbology, bad at History of Magic in the specific way that everyone who wasn't Hermione Granger was bad at History of Magic. His best subject was Ancient Runes, and even there he sat in the comfortable middle of the class. His end-of-year marks the previous June had been a smooth line of Acceptables with a single Exceeds Expectations in Care of Magical Creatures, which Professor Grubbly-Plank had given him, he suspected, because he'd been the only student who hadn't flinched when the Fire Crab sneezed.

He wasn't bothered by any of this. Some students agonised over marks — Ravenclaws especially, though there were a few in every house — and Gavin understood the feeling the way you understood a foreign custom: recognisable but not applicable. He wanted to pass his OWLs. He would probably pass most of them. He didn't know what he wanted to do after school, and the not-knowing didn't bother him either, which bothered Miren, who had plans.

"What do you think they're going to do about Quidditch?" Miren said sometime in the late afternoon, when the rain had stopped and the hills were getting steeper through the window.

"Probably still have it," Gavin said.

"How can they still have Quidditch when they're —" She stopped. "I don't know. Maybe they will."

"They will," Duncan said. It was the most he'd said in hours. "That's the thing you keep normal so the rest of it looks normal too."

Miren looked at him. Gavin looked at him.

"My dad talks about it," Duncan said. "About his work. When management changes something big, they keep the Christmas party going." He seemed to hear himself — the way the analogy had reached for his Muggle life and pulled it into this compartment, this train, this world that was becoming specifically dangerous for people whose fathers compared Death Eater governance to office politics. He went quiet and put his textbook back in his bag.

"That's smart," Miren said after a moment. "That's actually — yeah. The Christmas party."

"Right."

The light outside was changing, going long and golden over the Scottish hills, and Gavin could see first-years pressing their faces to windows further up the train. He remembered that — the first sight of Hogwarts from the lake, the sheer overwhelm of it, the towers and the lit windows and the sense that you had come somewhere vast and ancient and specifically yours. He wondered what the first-years would feel this time. He wondered what the castle would feel like now.

The train was slowing. The corridor filled briefly with noise — trunks dragged over carpet, someone's owl protesting, a prefect's voice calling something about leaving belongings. They were normal sounds, but there were fewer of them, and in the spaces between lay a quiet that didn't belong on the Hogwarts Express on the first of September.

Miren stood and pulled her robe straight. She'd been picking at her sunburn again and there was a flake of dead skin on her collar that Gavin thought about mentioning and didn't.

"Right," she said. "Let's go."

Duncan put his bag over his shoulder. He had packed light — no owl, no extra trunk, just the one bag. Gavin wondered if that was because he'd packed in a hurry or because he'd packed to leave in a hurry. He didn't ask.

They stepped off the train into a September evening that smelled like wet pine and lake water and the particular cold that Hogsmeade station always had, the kind that settled in your ears and stayed. The platform was less crowded than it should have been. Gavin could count the heads around him. He had never been able to count heads on this platform before.

The Great Hall looked right and was wrong.

The four house tables were in their places. The enchanted ceiling showed a clear September night, deep blue and scattered with stars, and the floating candles threw warm light over the golden plates and goblets. The house banners hung behind the staff table. The ghosts drifted near the high windows. It was all correct, all in order, and none of it landed the way it was supposed to.

The seats were the problem, or the absence in them. Every table had gaps — not one or two empty spots but long stretches of bench where nobody sat, the spaces visible and specific. The Hufflepuff table was missing perhaps a dozen students. The Gryffindor table looked worse. The Slytherins were nearly full, which Gavin noticed and then felt bad for noticing, and then stopped feeling bad because it was a fact and facts didn't require guilt.

He sat between Miren and a sixth-year named Fenwick who was trying to read a letter under the table without anyone seeing. The letter was long and the handwriting small and Fenwick's hands weren't steady. Duncan sat across from them with his hands flat on the table and his face pointed at the staff table, at the chair in the middle.

Snape was in Dumbledore's chair.

Gavin had known this. Everyone had known this since the summer. But knowing it and seeing it were different things, and the seeing settled in his stomach in a way the knowing hadn't. Snape sat very still, his black robes precisely arranged, his expression a kind of practised blankness that was nothing like Dumbledore's openness or warmth or any of the qualities you associated with someone sitting in that particular chair. He looked at the students the way you looked at a room you were responsible for managing.

On either side of Snape, in chairs that hadn't been at the staff table last year, sat two people Gavin had never seen.

The man was squat and heavy-shouldered, with a lopsided face and teeth that caught the candlelight wrong. He was sweating, though the Great Hall was cool, and he'd pushed the sleeves of his robes up past his forearms like someone about to do physical work. He kept shifting his weight and looking out at the students, scanning the tables with the quick, unsettled movements of someone who was both excited and nervous about what was coming. The woman beside him was similar enough in the face to be related — siblings, Gavin thought — with the same dense, graceless build and an expression that was steadier than her brother's, more certain. She didn't look at the students. She looked at the staff, at the other professors, and something in that look made Professor Sprout, who was nearest, angle her body slightly away.

The Sorting went quickly. Fewer first-years than usual — a short line of eleven-year-olds who looked even smaller than first-years usually did, dwarfed by the emptiness of the Hall around them. Professor McGonagall read the names and her voice was the same voice it had always been, crisp and Scottish and giving nothing away, but she held the scroll differently. Gavin couldn't identify what was different. Her grip, maybe. The angle of her wrist. Her hands held the scroll the way Duncan's hands had held his bag on the train.

The new students sat down at their tables and looked around for what was supposed to come next — the cheering, the welcome, the older students pulling them into seats and loading their plates and asking about their houses with the performative enthusiasm of people who remembered being that small and scared. Some of it happened. The Hufflepuff prefects tried. But the trying was audible in a way that succeeding never was, and the first-years' faces settled into polite confusion as the effort showed through.

Snape stood. The Hall went silent — not the gradual settling that had preceded Dumbledore's speeches, where you could hear stragglers whispering and someone dropping a fork, but an immediate total silence that started at the staff table and moved through the room like a door closing.

"The appointments to the staff this year are as follows," Snape said. His voice carried without effort. "Amycus Carrow will teach Dark Arts." Not Defence Against the Dark Arts — just Dark Arts. "Alecto Carrow will teach Muggle Studies, which is now compulsory for all students."

The squat man — Amycus — surveyed the students with an expression Gavin recognised from a specific type of adult: the kind who had been given authority late and meant to use it while it lasted. He looked at the students the way Gavin's uncle looked at his allotment when the neighbour's cat had been through it. Ownership and the intent to correct.

Alecto Carrow did not look at the students. She was still watching the staff, and Professor Sprout was still angled away, and between those two positions Gavin could read something that didn't need to be spoken.

"The school rules are unchanged," Snape said. "They will be enforced."

That was it. No welcome-back, no feast-opening remarks, no commentary about the year ahead. Snape sat down. The food appeared on the plates — roast chicken, potatoes, bread, pies, all of it the same abundance as every year — and the silence held for three seconds, four, five, before someone at the Ravenclaw table picked up a serving spoon and the sound of it broke whatever was holding the room and people began to eat.

Gavin took a piece of chicken and a roll. He ate because the food was there and because not eating would be conspicuous. The chicken tasted like chicken. The roll was warm. None of it felt like the start of term. It felt like eating in a waiting room.

"Don't look at them," Miren said quietly, as Fenwick beside Gavin craned toward the staff table. Fenwick pulled back. Miren was already eating, steady and deliberate, the way she did everything when she was angry. She buttered her roll with more force than bread required.

"Muggle Studies is compulsory," Duncan said. His voice was flat and carefully empty. "That's interesting."

"It's not interesting. It's —"

"I know what it is."

They ate. Around them the Hufflepuff table produced the sounds of a meal — silverware on plates, glasses being filled, low conversation — but the conversation kept stalling and restarting, circling the same ground without settling. A fourth-year asked if Hogsmeade weekends would still happen and nobody knew. A seventh-year said something about Quidditch tryouts and the word landed and sat there like it belonged to a different school.

Gavin looked at the enchanted ceiling. Clear sky, bright stars, the Milky Way running faintly above the floating candles. It was the same sky that had been there every September, and it was beautiful, and he was the only person at the table looking up at it.

From the staff table, Amycus Carrow laughed at something. The sound was loud and wrong in the half-quiet Hall, and several students flinched. Not dramatically — a scattering of small movements: heads dipping, shoulders tightening, a fork paused between plate and mouth. The kind of flinch that was already becoming habit, three hours into the year.

Professor Sprout looked down at her plate. McGonagall did not.

Gavin reached for another roll and then pulled his hand back because the basket was closer to Duncan and Duncan hadn't eaten anything.

"Do you want —" Gavin started.

"Not hungry." Then, as if correcting: "Maybe later."

People were leaving. That was the other wrong thing — usually the feast went on, dessert appeared, the talk got louder, the first-years warmed up, and the older students stayed because the Great Hall on the first night was the whole point, the feeling of being somewhere together. Tonight students were standing up in ones and twos as soon as the dessert appeared, heading for their common rooms, a few of them carrying napkins wrapped around biscuits as if they were stockpiling. The Gryffindors left first, a group of them moving together without discussion. The Ravenclaws followed. Even the Slytherins thinned out, though a few stayed and drifted toward the staff table with the ease of students who had nothing to fear from this particular change.

"We should go," Miren said.

They went. Through the corridors to the Hufflepuff basement, past the barrels, through the round door into the common room that smelled, as it always did, of earth and warm wood and whatever the kitchens were making next door. The common room was the same — the plants in the windows, the round porthole-like panes showing nothing but the castle's stone foundations and dark earth, the copper fixtures and the low ceiling and the fat yellow armchairs that had been there since Gavin's first year.

He sat down in one of them and the upholstery was warm from the fire and the cushion had the shape his body already knew and for a moment, just that moment, the wrongness of the evening pulled back and he was a fifth-year in his common room on the first night of term and the year ahead was just a year.

Then Miren sat beside him and said, quietly, "We need to figure out who's missing. All the houses. I'm going to start a list." She had a piece of parchment already — she must have brought it from home. She unfolded it on her knee and Gavin saw names in her small, definite handwriting, the missing Hufflepuffs she'd been counting since August, and now she was adding columns for the other houses, leaving blank spaces for names she didn't have yet. Her quill moved in quick, certain strokes. She wasn't angry anymore, or the anger had turned into something she could do, which was better than the kind that sat in your chest and didn't go anywhere, which was Gavin's kind.

Duncan went upstairs without saying good night.

The first weeks were an education in the distance between what a school looked like and what a school was.

The timetables were issued. The classes met. Homework was assigned and collected and returned with marks in the margins. The mechanisms of Hogwarts continued to function, and the students moved through them the way Gavin imagined fish moved through water that had gone slightly off — the temperature wrong by two degrees, something chemical in the current, not enough to kill but enough that every movement required more effort than it used to.

Dark Arts under Amycus Carrow was a classroom with the desks shoved against the walls and the air smelling of something burnt. Muggle Studies under Alecto was worse in a way that was harder to name: a room full of students being told, in a tone of reasonable explanation, that everything about Duncan's parents — about Duncan's entire life before Hogwarts — was degenerate, primitive, something to be studied the way you studied a disease. The calm of her voice made the content more obscene, not less. Duncan sat through it. Gavin sat two rows behind him and watched the back of Duncan's head stay perfectly still and gave up trying to think of something to say afterward because nothing he could think of was adequate to the situation and saying something inadequate felt worse than silence.

Transfiguration was the same as it had been. Charms was the same. Herbology, Potions, History of Magic — these ran as they always had, the professors doing their work with a carefulness that felt deliberate, as if maintaining their subjects was a form of resistance, or at least of insistence. Professor Sprout gave longer assignments than usual and spent more class time on them, and Gavin suspected this was her way of keeping students in her greenhouse and out of the corridors where the Carrows walked.

The corridors between classes had changed. Students moved quickly and in groups, not lingering the way they used to — nobody sat on the stone window ledges to talk, nobody leaned against the walls outside classrooms waiting for friends. The Carrows walked the upper floors between lessons, separately, and their presence pressed on the hallways before they appeared, the way a cold draft pressed on a room before you found its source. Students learned to check the corridor ahead before turning a corner. Gavin learned it too, without thinking much about it, the way you learned any small habit that kept you from being noticed.

In the evenings the common room was fuller than it had been in other years. Students who used to study in the library or wander the castle after dinner came straight back and stayed put. The room was warm and the fire was always going and the Carrows were not there, and that was enough. People did homework and played Gobstones and talked in the low voices that had become the standard volume for indoor conversation. Miren worked on her list most nights, comparing what she heard from other houses with what she could count at meals. She had a system — parchment organised by house, names in columns, absences marked in red — and several people gave her information without being asked. Duncan did his homework with the lamp pulled close and his handwriting neat and small, and he went to bed early, and he didn't talk about what happened in his classes with the Carrows. His hands sometimes shook when he packed his books away, and he was careful to set them down slowly so the shaking wouldn't show.

Gavin studied. He attended class. He did his homework with the slow thoroughness of someone who needed thoroughness because quickness wasn't available to him. He was mediocre at all of it and the mediocrity was familiar and in a strange way comforting — a piece of his life that the year's strangeness hadn't touched.

Three weeks in, on a Tuesday night in late September, he was in the library working on a Binns essay. The topic was the Statute of Secrecy: its origins, its implementation, its major revisions. Binns had assigned it with the same droning indifference he brought to everything, as though the political crisis currently restructuring the wizarding world had no bearing on a historical analysis of wizarding secrecy legislation, which maybe for a ghost it didn't.

The library was nearly empty. Madam Pince sat at her desk with her lamp and her eagle-feather quill and her expression of permanent suspicion, which had deepened this year into something closer to fear. She watched the few remaining students the way a shop owner watched customers she didn't trust but couldn't refuse to serve. The Restricted Section behind its rope was darker than Gavin remembered, though he might have been imagining that — this year lent itself to imagining things darker. Two Ravenclaws studied at a table near the windows. A first-year had fallen asleep on an open copy of One Thousand Magical Herbs and Fungi, her cheek pressed against an illustration of a Bouncing Bulb. Gavin sat at his usual spot, a table near the back, close to the History of Magic shelves, and worked through his essay with the steady, uninspired competence that characterised everything he did.

He was checking a citation in A History of Magic — Bagshot's account of the Wizards' Council debates preceding the Statute, somewhere in the seven-forties — when he turned a page too far and his eye caught on a footnote at the bottom of page 742.

It was small type. Parenthetical. The kind of footnote that existed to show the author had been thorough rather than to convey anything the author thought mattered. Bagshot's footnotes were like that — dense, fussy, mostly about sourcing decisions, the scholarly equivalent of showing your working.

This one read:

*The question of a pre-wand magical substrate, occasionally raised by Continental theorists of the Sardinian school, was investigated by the author during the preparation of this work. A manuscript purporting to describe such a substrate was consulted in the Hogwarts collection (sub-collection, uncatalogued) and found to be of uncertain provenance and doubtful reliability. The notation system employed bears no resemblance to any known runic or proto-runic script. The manuscript's claims regarding the nature of magical law are inconsistent with the established theoretical framework and are not treated further in this text.*

Gavin read it. He read it again.

His essay was about the Statute of Secrecy. The footnote had nothing to do with the Statute of Secrecy. He had only seen it because he'd turned one page past what he needed, and Bagshot had buried it in print small enough to say she'd looked into it while also saying she didn't think much of what she'd found.

Pre-wand magical substrate. He didn't know what that meant, exactly. Wand magic was magic — the thing they learned, the thing the school taught, the thing that made the wizarding world wizarding. A substrate beneath it would be like finding out there was a floor under the floor you'd been walking on. Which was probably nonsense, which was probably why Bagshot had dismissed it, but the phrase sat in his head with the particular weight of a thing that was either nothing or not nothing, and he couldn't tell which yet.

The manuscript was in the Hogwarts collection. A sub-collection, uncatalogued. Somewhere in the school, in a part of the library system that didn't appear in the catalogue — he had used the catalogue enough times to know he'd never seen a section labelled "sub-collection." Bagshot had found it, or someone had shown it to her, and she had read it and decided it was unreliable, and she had written this footnote, and that had been that.

He looked up. Pince was still at her desk. The Ravenclaws had gone. The first-year was still asleep on her textbook, and her arm had shifted so that her hand hung off the edge of the table, fingers loose. The library was quiet with the particular quality of Hogwarts at night — the building settling around itself, the faint sounds of portraits shifting in their frames, the wind outside and the creak of old wood adjusting to the cold.

Gavin folded the corner of page 742 down. It left a small crease, barely visible.

He went back to his essay. He finished the paragraph about the Wizards' Council vote of 1692, wrote two more sentences about enforcement mechanisms, and put his quill down because his hand was cramping and he was tired and the library was closing in ten minutes anyway. The essay was adequate. It was always adequate.

He packed his bag. He shelved the reference books he'd pulled but kept his own copy of A History of Magic — the one with the dog-eared page — and put it in his bag on top of his Transfiguration notes.

The corridors back to Hufflepuff were cold. The castle was colder this year on the lower floors — someone had said the Carrows had adjusted the heating for the basement levels, though Gavin didn't know if that was true or just the kind of thing people said when they needed the administration's pettiness to have a shape they could point at. His footsteps echoed on the stone. A portrait of a round-faced witch in a green hat watched him pass and said nothing, which portraits usually didn't, late at night, unless you startled them.

He tapped the barrel in the rhythm Professor Sprout had set for the year, and the round door swung open, and the common room was empty except for the fire, which was low, and the smell of bread from the kitchens. He crossed to the stairs and went up to the dormitory and got into bed without lighting his lamp because his roommates were asleep and the curtains around their beds were drawn and the only light was the faint glow from the window, which was underground and showed nothing but the pale roots of some plant pressing against the glass from outside.

He lay there. He thought about the essay, which he'd hand in tomorrow and which would come back with an A in Binns's faded ink. He thought about the Charms homework he needed to start. He thought about Duncan not eating at the feast and going to bed without a word and what it would be like to sit through a class designed to teach you that your parents were less than human.

He thought about the footnote. Pre-wand magical substrate. A manuscript in the Hogwarts collection, uncatalogued, in a part of the library system he'd never seen referenced anywhere else. Bagshot had looked at it and decided it was nothing — uncertain provenance, doubtful reliability, a notation system she couldn't read. She'd written her footnote and moved on and nobody, as far as Gavin could tell, had followed the thread.

It was probably nothing. It was almost certainly nothing. Bagshot was a real historian and she'd dismissed it and that should have been the end of it. But the thread was there now, loose and visible, the way a loose thread on a jumper was there once your finger had caught on it — not because you'd been looking for it but because it had been there all along and now you knew about it and it would still be there tomorrow, waiting to be pulled or left alone.

He'd probably look into it. Not urgently. Not with any particular expectation. Just the way you opened a drawer you'd never opened before — not expecting anything, just wanting to know what was inside.

He rolled over. The pillow was cool on the other side. From the next bed he could hear Ernie Macmillan breathing in the slow, controlled way that meant Ernie was awake and pretending not to be, and from somewhere deeper in the castle the pipes clanked once and the building shifted around them, settling into the night the way something old and heavy settled when it was finally still.

Gavin closed his eyes. The footnote sat in his bag in the dark, on a dog-eared page, in small type, waiting for nothing in particular.
