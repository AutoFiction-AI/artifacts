# Chapter 11

In January the quill was still the same one he'd been using since October. A school quill, cheap, the vane split on one side where he'd pressed too hard during Babbling's mid-term assessment. He'd brought it to the sub-collection because it didn't matter — the exercise didn't require a good quill — and because he'd failed with it so many times that switching to a different one would have felt like superstition.

He set it on the stone shelf and sat back. The air in the sub-collection was cold and dry and smelled of limestone, the way it always did. Since Christmas he'd also been unable to miss the slight give in the castle's magic, the sense that the wards were holding with less to spare than they had before. The walls gave off their faint luminescence — not torchlight, not Lumos-light, something older that came from the stone itself and cast no shadows. The manuscript lay open beside him, its notation shifting slowly on the not-parchment surface, but he wasn't reading it tonight. He'd memorized the relevant passages weeks ago. What the notation described wasn't complicated. The execution was.

The substrate around the quill was visible to him the way it had been visible since November — a structural quality in the air, not a glow or a shimmer but a kind of commitment. Everything in the room had it. The shelves, the stone, the air itself. The agreements that governed what things were and how they behaved, the rules that said stone was hard and air was transparent and quills were visible. He could see them the way you could see grain in wood once someone pointed it out. You couldn't unsee it afterward.

The agreement governing the quill's visibility was the one he'd been working on. Not because visibility was easy — nothing about this was easy — but because it was legible. The rule that said this object was visible, that light would interact with its surface and reflect into eyes and produce the experience of seeing a quill, had a shape he could perceive. A structure. A kind of spatial grammar around the quill that specified the terms of its participation in being seen.

For six weeks he'd been trying to loosen it.

The technique, if you could call it that, was not a spell. There was no wand movement, no incantation, no magical energy involved. What the manuscript described was a cognitive operation — a way of engaging with the substrate directly, the way you might reach into the grammar of a sentence and alter a rule so that the sentence still worked but worked differently. A Disillusionment Charm cast invisibility onto an object. What Gavin was trying to do was soften the rule that made visibility apply. He was not trying to hide the quill. He was trying to change the terms under which the quill participated in being seen.

The distinction was, he understood by now, the entire point of the First Art. But understanding the distinction had not helped him accomplish it. Every night for six weeks he'd sat in this room and reached for the structure and tried to adjust it and nothing had happened. The agreement stayed firm. The quill remained stubbornly, completely visible. He'd tried approaching the rule from different angles, at different speeds, with different degrees of focus. He'd tried thinking about it in the manuscript's notation, in Babbling's translational framework, in plain English. Nothing caught. The substrate was there, he could see it, and it would not move for him.

Tonight he did not expect anything different. That was maybe the relevant thing. He was too tired to hope. It was past midnight. He had Transfiguration at nine and would fail whatever exercise McGonagall set because he'd been failing all of them since autumn. His left hand had the tremor tonight — the one from the Cruciatus, which came and went without pattern, a faint judder in his ring and little fingers that made his handwriting even worse than usual. He was cold. The sub-collection was always cold but January cold was different from November cold, a settled permanence in the chill, as though the stone had given up pretending it would ever be warm.

He looked at the quill. He looked at the agreement around it. He reached.

It was the same nothing as every other night. The substrate was there, structured and visible and completely indifferent to his effort. He held the reach for a while — the way you'd hold a stretch past the point of discomfort on the chance that something might give — and felt the familiar blankness of a tool that didn't engage. The manuscript said the First Art was natural, that the Builders had practiced it the way humans breathed, that it was not a learned skill but a way of being in relation to the substrate. But Gavin was not a Builder. He was a fifteen-year-old Hufflepuff with bad Transfiguration marks and a split quill and hands that shook.

He let go and sat back against the stone wall, which was the temperature of January in the Scottish Highlands filtered through six feet of castle. The quill sat on the shelf, solidly and irritatingly visible.

He should go to bed. He was going to feel terrible in Transfiguration. McGonagall had started giving him the look she reserved for specific disappointment — not the general kind she distributed across the class when nobody got the exercise, but the individual kind that meant she'd expected something from him once and had recently revised the expectation downward. He didn't want to see that look on four hours of sleep.

He rubbed his face. His fingers were numb. The tremor in his left hand had settled into a steady rhythm, the faintest vibration, like a string that someone had plucked and forgotten about.

He reached for the substrate one more time, the way you checked a locked door a second time even though you'd just checked it, more habit than hope. The agreement around the quill. The rule that specified its visibility. He could see its shape — something like a weave, if you could weave with obligation instead of thread. The terms that bound this particular arrangement of feather and dried ink to the category of things that light acknowledged.

He didn't push. He didn't try harder than he had any other night. He was too tired for effort. What he did was hold the reach and let his attention rest against the structure of the agreement the way you'd lean against a wall — not trying to move it, just being present against it. The manuscript's notation for this operation, as near as he could translate it, was something like releasing a held emphasis. Not force. Something closer to forgetting to insist.

The agreement softened.

It happened without transition. One moment the rule had the structure he'd been looking at for six weeks — committed, definite, the quill existing firmly in the domain of the visible. The next moment the structure was different. The edges of the agreement blurred outward as though the rule had relaxed a grip it hadn't known it was holding. The quill was still there. Still on the shelf. Still a quill. But the terms of its visibility had changed. The weave was the same weave, but looser, the obligations between light and surface and eye widened to something approximate where they had been exact.

Gavin stared at it.

From his perception — from the substrate-aware perception that the manuscript had built in him since November — the quill looked exactly the same except for the loosened rule around it. He could see the object and the changed agreement at once: the cheap quill on the shelf, and around it the rule gone slack like a knot tugged loose. Both were real, both were there, and only one of them belonged to ordinary sight.

But when he tried to see the quill the way he would have seen it a year ago — before the manuscript, before any of this — when he tried to look at it with just his eyes, the way Miren would look at it or Duncan or McGonagall or anyone — the quill was wrong. It was there, but less there. The light from the walls brushed its surface and slid off, as if the feather had forgotten how to catch it. It wasn't invisible or transparent. It was something that had no word. The quill was present in the room but didn't hold its place inside sight. His eyes wanted to slide past it. When he forced his gaze to stay, the quill wavered at its edges like something seen through old glass.

He was still looking at it when the pain arrived.

It started behind his eyes — a hard spreading pressure, as though something inside his skull had tried to wedge itself into a space too small for it. His vision smeared. The faint stone-luminescence of the sub-collection stretched sideways, the walls running together at their edges, and he couldn't pull the room back into focus. He blinked and his face was wet and he touched his upper lip and his fingers came away dark.

A nosebleed.

He tilted his head forward and pinched the bridge of his nose. Blood ran between his fingers and dripped onto the stone shelf next to the quill. The quill that he had altered. That he had actually, genuinely altered — not with a spell, not with his wand, but by loosening the agreement that governed its participation in being seen.

His vision stayed bad. The sub-collection's stone walls smeared when he moved his head, and the manuscript's notation on the open page swam into illegibility. His brain was protesting. The First Art taxed cognitive architecture, not magical energy — the manuscript had been specific about this. The human mind could engage with the substrate, but the engagement cost something structural. The Builders' minds had been built for this the way lungs were built for breathing. Gavin's was not.

He sat on the floor of the sub-collection with his head forward and blood spotting the knees of his trousers and thought: he'd done it.

The feeling sat low and strange in his chest. He had crossed a line. Theory had turned into practice. He could edit the substrate. He could change the rules. The thing the manuscript described was real and he could do it and the crossing was — the manuscript was right about this too — irreversible. He could not go back to being the person who had only read about the First Art. He was now the person who had practiced it.

His nose bled for another ten minutes. His vision cleared slowly, in stages, like a lens being adjusted by someone with an unsteady hand. When he could see properly again the quill was still altered. The loosened agreement held. He hadn't been sure it would — the manuscript was unclear on whether edits persisted or decayed — but the quill sat on the shelf in its changed state, less visible, less committed, as though the rule had accepted its new configuration and found no reason to return to the old one.

He picked it up. It felt the same in his fingers. The same weight, the same split vane, the same slightly waxy texture of a cheap school quill. The substrate didn't change how things felt to touch. It changed how they participated in the agreements that governed reality. Visibility was one agreement. Solidity was another. The quill was still solid because he hadn't touched that rule. He'd only loosened visibility.

He put the quill in his pocket. He cleaned the blood off the shelf with his sleeve, which didn't clean it so much as spread it into a faint pinkish smear on the stone. He closed the manuscript, folded it carefully into the leather wrapping he'd cut from a ruined bag, and put it back on its shelf in the innermost room. Then he climbed the cold stairs out of the sub-collection and into the seventh-floor corridor.

The corridors connected tonight in the straightforward pattern, the one that took ten minutes instead of thirty. The castle was silent — past midnight, no patrols, the Carrows asleep or at least out of the upper floors. Gavin walked through the dark corridors and the substrate around him was the same as always, the agreements governing stone and air and torchlight steady and undisturbed, and for a moment the fact that he had changed one of those agreements felt improbable, as though it had happened to someone else in a different part of the castle. But the quill was in his pocket. The blood was on his trousers. His head ached in a way that was new, specific, unlike any headache he'd had before — a dull wrongness behind his eyes, as though something back there had been rearranged and hadn't settled yet.

He was on the third-floor staircase when he realized he was smiling. A small, private, stupid smile that had nothing behind it except the fact that something had worked. He stopped smiling when he noticed it, because it felt wrong — too easy, too clean an emotion for what had just happened. But it kept trying to come back.

The Hufflepuff common room was almost empty when he reached it. A fourth-year girl he knew vaguely — Leah something, she sat two rows behind him in Herbology and was always losing her dragon-hide gloves — had fallen asleep on one of the sofas with a Potions textbook on her chest. The fire had burned down to a low orange mound that gave off more light than heat. The room smelled the way it always did: earth, warm wood, and underneath that the yeast-and-onion drift from the kitchens next door, where the house-elves were baking tomorrow's bread.

He went to bed. His hand still had the tremor. His nose had started bleeding again, faintly, a slow warmth on his lip that he pressed his sleeve against. He lay in the dark with the quill in his pyjama pocket and the barrel ceiling low above him and listened to the dormitory's sounds — the steady breathing of one boy, another's occasional mutter from whatever he was dreaming, the small settling noises of the castle adjusting itself in the cold. He knew he should care about the nosebleed, about the headache, about the visual distortion that had taken most of an hour to clear. He knew these were the costs. But the quill was in his pocket and it was altered and he had done it.

He slept. He dreamed about the agreement around the quill, or about something adjacent to the agreement, the way dreams were adjacent to the things that produced them. When he woke up his pillowcase had a brown stain where his nose had bled in the night.

---

He found Miren after Herbology the next day. She was in the common room, sitting cross-legged on the floor near the fire with a spread of parchment around her and a quill behind her ear that she'd clearly forgotten was there. There was ink on her left thumb and on the side of her hand where it dragged across the page. She was writing in the tight, rapid shorthand she'd developed for DA supply logistics — a notation that nobody else in Hufflepuff could decipher, which was less by design than because her handwriting under pressure was atrocious.

"Hey," he said.

She looked up. "Hey." She studied his face for a moment — she did that now, a quick check, her eyes doing a sweep that took in his colour and his posture and whatever it was she was looking for. "You've got something on your —" She gestured at her own upper lip.

He wiped his nose. Dried blood came away on the back of his hand. "Yeah. Nosebleed."

"Another one?"

"It's fine." He sat down beside her, careful not to sit on the parchment spread. She moved one sheet out of his way without looking at it. "Can I show you something?"

Miren set down her quill. The logistics parchment could wait, apparently — she made the choice to be present when someone asked her to be present and then figured out the scheduling later. She had a smudge of soil on her neck from Herbology that she hadn't noticed, and a scratch on her forearm from the Venomous Tentacula that Professor Sprout had them repotting this term. Sprout kept insisting the repotting was a normal part of the fifth-year syllabus, but three students had been bitten last week and Gavin suspected the Carrows had just stopped funding replacement plants for the less dangerous species.

"All right," Miren said.

He pulled the quill from his pocket.

From his perception — from his — the quill was the same as last night. The substrate agreement around it was still loosened, the visibility rule relaxed in its new configuration. He could see the change the way you could see a door standing open that used to be closed. Still a door. Just differently positioned.

He held it out to her.

Miren looked at the quill. He watched her look at it. He watched her eyes try to settle on the object and not quite manage — the gaze sliding sideways, catching, sliding again. She squinted.

"What've you done to it?"

"I've changed it. Not with a spell. With —" He stopped. Started again. "You know the thing I've been working on. The substrate. The agreements."

"The First Art thing." Her voice was careful. Not dismissive. Miren had decided somewhere around Christmas, he thought, to stop arguing about whether the First Art was real and start treating it the way you'd treat a friend's religion — with respect for the belief if not for the content. It was kind of her. It was also exactly wrong. "What about it?"

"I changed the quill. Last night. I edited the — the agreement that governs its visibility. Not a charm. Not a spell applied to the object. I changed the underlying rule."

Miren looked at the quill again. She reached out and took it from his hand. Turned it over. Held it up to the common room's warm firelight. The quill wavered in her grip, its edges indistinct, the split vane blurring into the air around it. Her fingers were solid around something that didn't look entirely solid.

"It's blurry," she said.

"Yeah, but —"

"It's like a Disillusionment Charm. A not-great one. Like..." She tilted her head. "Like a second-year's, maybe? It's still pretty visible. You can see the shape."

"It's not a Disillusionment Charm."

"I know you're saying that. I'm telling you what it looks like."

"And you're right. That's the problem. From your — from where you're seeing it, it does look like that. It looks exactly like a bad Disillusionment Charm. But what I did is different. A Disillusionment Charm applies an effect on top of the object. What I did — I changed the rule underneath. The quill isn't hidden. It's less... it's less subject to being seen."

He could hear himself. He could hear how it sounded. The difference he was trying to make — paint on the wall versus the wall itself, which felt to him like the whole point of all this — sounded, spoken aloud in the Hufflepuff common room, like a boy explaining why his homework was actually better than it looked.

Miren turned the quill over one more time. She was trying. He could see her trying.

"Less subject to being seen," she repeated.

"I know it sounds —"

"No, I'm listening. I'm just — Gav, what's the difference? Actually. If the result looks the same and a Disillusionment Charm does the same thing —"

"It doesn't do the same thing. It does a thing that looks the same. A Disillusionment Charm puts a coat of paint on the quill that makes it hard to see. What I did is — I went into the wall the paint's on and changed the wall so paint doesn't stick the same way. The quill isn't painted invisible. The rule that connects it to visibility is... wider. Softer."

He watched her face. She was listening with the focused attention she gave to things she was determined to understand, her head tilted slightly, one hand resting on her knee. But what she was hearing was a distinction without a difference, because the evidence — the only evidence he could offer her — looked identical either way. The blurry quill. The blurry quill that looked exactly, precisely, maddeningly like a bad Disillusionment Charm.

"Can you show me the difference?" she asked. "Like, is there a test? Something that would work differently on a Disillusionment versus what you're saying?"

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Thought.

"A Finite Incantatem would remove a Disillusionment Charm," he said slowly. "It wouldn't affect what I did. Because what I did isn't a spell. There's nothing to cancel."

Miren's eyes sharpened. That was a test she could work with. "So cast one. Cast a Finite on it. If the blurriness stays, you're right."

"I can't. I mean — I can, but it wouldn't prove anything. You'd just see a still-blurry quill and you could say the Disillusionment was layered, or that my Finite was weak, or —" He stopped. It just went in a circle. Every way of proving the difference needed the other person to see the substrate, and there was no way to hand someone that sight without first putting them through the same change the manuscript had already made in his head. The proof was behind the same locked door as the sight.

Miren was quiet for a moment. She handed the quill back to him.

"I'm not trying to..." She paused. Restarted, which was unusual for her. Miren didn't usually need a second run at a sentence. "I don't think you're making it up. I think you believe it. And maybe you're right, I don't know. But what I can see is a blurry quill, Gavin. That's it. That's all I've got."

"I know."

"And you got a nosebleed doing it?"

"Yeah."

"So." She looked at him. He could see the expression assembling itself on her face — concern layered over frustration layered over something else, something that might have been grief if grief could be that tired. She'd been wearing some version of this expression since Christmas. "You stayed up all night, you gave yourself a nosebleed, and you made a quill a bit blurry. From where I'm sitting."

"From where you're sitting. Yeah."

"I'm not being —"

"I know. You're not being anything. You're telling me what you see."

"Right."

They sat with that for a moment. The common room fire crackled and shifted, a log settling into the grate. The second-years in the far corner were playing Exploding Snap — the soft slap of cards and the occasional small bang that made everyone flinch slightly this year, because loud noises meant different things now. The kitchen smell had shifted to something with cinnamon in it, probably whatever the elves were testing for Saturday's pudding.

"How's the DA stuff going?" he asked, because he didn't know what else to say, and because she was his friend and her life was not a sounding board for his demonstrations.

Miren glanced at the parchment spread on the floor. "Fine. Seamus wants to do another graffiti run but Neville thinks we should wait until after the Slytherin match because the Carrows'll be watching the corridors." She rubbed the Herbology soil off her neck, noticed the ink on her thumb, and let that go. "We're low on dittany again. Hannah's been trying to nick some from the hospital wing but Pomfrey's started locking the supply cupboard, so." She shrugged. The shrug carried more weight than it looked like it should — the specific weight of someone whose supply logistics had life-or-death stakes and who was fifteen years old and keeping track of them in illegible shorthand.

"Do you need help with —"

"No." Not sharp. Just certain. She'd said it this way before. "I need you to sleep and eat and stop bleeding from your nose. Those are the things I need from you."

He put the quill back in his pocket. "Okay."

"Okay." She picked up her logistics quill — the one she'd recovered from behind her ear — and turned back to her parchment. He sat beside her for a while, not talking. She wrote, her hand moving fast, the scratch of quill on parchment steady and purposeful. After a few minutes she paused and looked at him sideways.

"Is your vision doing that thing again? Where you look past things?"

"No. I'm just sitting."

"All right." She went back to writing. He sat there until his head started to ache again and the edges of the room went soft, the fuzziness at the margins of his vision that meant the cost of last night was not finished with him yet. Then he got up and went upstairs.

---

The dormitory was empty. The other boys were at dinner. He could hear the Great Hall's muffled rumble distantly — two floors up and sideways, the institutional clink and scrape of a school meal, the low hum of a student body that was smaller and quieter than it should have been. He should eat. He would eat later, probably. Or not. Food had become something he forgot about and then remembered when his hands shook from something other than the Cruciatus remnant.

He sat on his bed and took the quill out of his pocket.

It sat in his hand, less visible than a quill should be. He could still make out the split vane and the dried ink at the nib. The loosened agreement around it was perceptible to him as a changed texture in the substrate — like a patch of softer ground in a field, a place where the rules gave more than they did elsewhere.

He held it up to the dormitory light. The quill wavered in the glow. Its edges were soft. Looking at it felt like looking at something through water, something present but not holding still in the right way. He set it on the bedspread to free a hand for his handkerchief and immediately lost it. His eyes slid to the blanket fold beside it. He had to pat the coverlet twice before his fingers found the shaft.

He stared at the quill for a moment, then pulled a bit of spare parchment from the nightstand drawer. Fine. He'd do a practical test. He put the parchment on his knee, dipped the nib in the ink bottle, and tried to write his name.

The G came out badly. By the a his gaze had slipped off the nib again and the letters climbed uphill across the page. He stopped with Gav skewed toward the corner, the ink line frayed where he'd pressed too hard while trying to keep track of where the point actually was. That was one consequence. He had turned a cheap school quill into something nearly useless for homework.

His nose started bleeding again. A warm trickle ran down his left nostril. He found the handkerchief in his bedside drawer — already stained brown from this morning, the Scourgify he'd done before breakfast apparently not up to the task — and pressed it to his face. The blood was bright against the cotton. His vision blurred again, softly, at the edges. The dormitory's shapes — bed, trunk, nightstand, someone's Quidditch magazine on the floor, a sock that had missed the laundry pile — went liquid, as if the room were seen through rain on a window.

He was still sitting there with the handkerchief to his face when the room tightened.

The round window clicked in its lead frame. Every hair on his forearms lifted. The wall sconce opposite his bed thinned from a steady amber tongue to a blue needle edged in orange. The change was small, maybe two seconds, but the whole dormitory felt narrower in that interval, as if the stone had drawn half an inch closer to his ribs. On the bed the altered quill lost another degree of itself. The shaft did not just blur. Its middle seemed to go missing, nib and vane hanging in the air with barely enough quill between them.

Then the pressure eased. The sconce swelled back to amber. The window went still. The quill settled into its usual half-seen state on the blanket.

Gavin wiped his nose and looked at the quill. The wards had thinned again. It was not enough for anyone else in Hufflepuff to wake up and say the room felt wrong. It was not enough to leave a mark you could point at tomorrow morning. But the room's magic had registered it, and the first thing he'd changed had registered it worst of all.

He cleaned the handkerchief with a Scourgify that mostly worked — his wand still did ordinary spells, and he was still mediocre at them — and the worst of the blood vanished, leaving a faint pinkish ghost on the cotton. Then he set the quill on the nightstand instead of putting it back in his pocket. If the wards shifted again, he wanted the loosened thing where he could see what happened to it.

He turned on his side. Through the round window he could see the January dark, the grounds invisible except for the faint light from Hagrid's cabin far below and the deeper black of the Forest beyond it, its edge just visible against the sky. The quill sat on the nightstand, not quite holding still in the sconce-light. He watched it until sleep took him.
