# Chapter 17

By noon the day after the wake, the F train spat Marisol out at Stillwell with a blast of platform heat that lasted exactly one staircase. At street level the wind took her jaw in both hands and twisted. She pulled her scarf up until wool scraped her mouth, then walked toward the boardwalk with her shoulders pitched forward like she was climbing, not crossing flat ground. January had stripped Coney down to steel, plywood, and bolts. Ride cars sat locked in place over the empty lots, each one with a skin of salt dust. The vendors' shutters were down. The gulls had the place to themselves and sounded meaner when there were no children throwing fries.

She had picked that hour because noon looked less dramatic in court. Noon was practical. Noon was two adults meeting in daylight to discuss publication boundaries, not a daughter arranging an ambush after a public funeral fight. She repeated that to herself as she stepped onto the boards and felt the hollowness under them, the old wood knocking lightly against its own nails when the gusts hit. Her phone kept buzzing inside her coat pocket. Unknown number, unknown number, then one text from a local arts reporter who had somehow found her new prepaid line: Are you prepared to comment on allegations of defamation by the Rivas estate? She turned the phone facedown without opening the message.

Gloria stood half a block away near a locked lemonade stand, exactly where she had said she would be. She wore the same black puffer she wore to church and to funerals, too warm for buses, never warm enough for wind. Her hair was pinned so tight it made her cheekbones look sharper. One gloved hand held a dented blue thermos by the neck. The other rubbed her right knee in small circles through her wool skirt, a quick private motion she stopped doing as soon as she saw Marisol looking.

Neither of them hugged. Gloria lifted her chin once, a greeting stripped to minimum parts.

"You're late."

Marisol checked her phone. "It's twelve-oh-three."

"That is late when your knees are made of broken glass."

"You picked the place."

"I picked a place where nobody from church is going to pretend they came for sea air." Gloria looked over Marisol's shoulder, taking inventory of empty benches, closed bathrooms, locked arcades. "Sit. If we stand the whole time I'll freeze and you won't hear anything because you'll only be feeling sorry for me."

They sat on opposite ends of a bench painted a summer color that looked obscene in winter, a peeled robin's-egg blue with rust bleeding through the slats. The ocean was the color of dishwater. Every third wave slapped hard enough to send spray over the railing and onto their boots.

Marisol kept her tote on her lap. Inside were copies she had printed at the shop at six that morning: the certified death certificate, the witness line with Salvador's full name and address, portions of the warehouse shift logs, stills from Ada's conservation scans, a transcript section from the roof-garden tape. She had highlighted nothing. Highlighting looked prosecutorial. She wanted record, not argument. At least that was what she told herself at six-fifteen while the copier jammed twice and she smacked it with the heel of her hand harder than necessary.

Gloria unscrewed the thermos cap and poured coffee into it like it was a little cup. Steam tried to rise and got yanked sideways by the wind.

"You did what you wanted at the wake," Gloria said. "You got your audience. You got your article. You got your dramatic exit. Why am I here?"

"Because that wasn't the end." Marisol looked straight out at the water, not at her mother. "I've got to turn in manuscript pages in two weeks if I want any chance of keeping this commission alive. Even if the museum pretends I'm dead, the book is still happening. I called you because I need to decide what goes in and what doesn't before everybody else decides for me."

"Everybody else already decided for you, mija. Estate lawyer decided. Curator decided. Internet decided."

"I'm not talking about comments." Marisol's fingers had gone numb around the tote handles. She flexed them one by one. "I'm talking about what I put my own name on."

Gloria drank and grimaced. "This coffee is shit. I made it too weak." She capped the thermos and set it between her boots. "Say what you came to say."

Marisol reached into the tote and pulled out the stack in a plastic sleeve. The wind tried to peel the top page back; she flattened it with her palm.

"I'm done carrying this in secret like contraband," she said. "This isn't rumor anymore. It's stamped paper, signatures, and dates anybody can check. If I don't publish the record, I'm another person helping bury him."

"You think we buried him because we enjoy dirt?"

"I think you lied for thirty years and then asked for gratitude."

Gloria gave a short laugh without humor. "There's that line you practiced on the train." She pointed at the sleeve. "You brought your evidence bundle like you're serving papers. Fine. Keep going."

"I need to know one thing before I lock pages," Marisol said. Her throat felt raw, as if she had already been yelling. "I need a clean yes or no from you. Did Salvador lie about being my father, or not?"

For a few seconds Gloria said nothing. The wind filled the gap, pushing through bench slats and coat seams and every soft place in Marisol's body. A gull landed two benches down, watched them hopefully, then thought better of it and flew off.

Gloria turned her face toward the water and spoke without looking at Marisol.

"No, he didn't lie about that. Salvador is your biological father."

The sentence did not hit all at once. It arrived in parts, each word separate, each one with enough space to breathe around it: Salvador. your. biological. father. Marisol felt the old burn scar on her left wrist tighten under the cold as if the skin there had memory of another weather. She waited for the part where Gloria would add maybe or probably or he believed. That part did not come.

"Say it again," Marisol said.

"I'm not repeating it for theater."

"I'm not asking for theater."

Gloria's jaw set. "He's your biological father. Tomas raised you from the first sonogram to the day he died. Both are true." She finally looked at Marisol, eyes watering from wind and not from tears. "If you need me to sign something, I'll sign."

The boardwalk seemed to tilt slightly under Marisol's feet. She set the sleeve down before she dropped it. Somewhere behind her sternum a craving lit up, sharp and chemical, not for grief, for anesthesia. She could taste cheap vodka from a plastic cap as clearly as if she had unscrewed one. She put both hands between her knees and pressed until the ache in her knuckles beat the ache in her mouth.

"Since when?" she asked.

"Since before you were born."

"No. Don't give me that broad answer. Dates."

"You love dates when you're afraid." Gloria pulled her gloves off with her teeth and rubbed warmth back into her fingers. "Fine. You want dates. Spring of ninety-two I was cleaning surgical rooms at Methodist nights, and your father, Tomas, was working double shifts, then not working, then swearing he would get clean by sheer stubbornness. Salvador had a studio visit in the hospital foundation office. Somebody introduced us because I spoke Spanish and his assistant didn't show. I translated forms for an hour. He gave me money for a cab and I refused it because I had pride and twenty-six dollars in my pocket and blisters on both heels. Later he came to where I worked with a box of bakery cookies for the whole floor like that made us even. That's how it started. Stupid and ordinary."

Marisol swallowed hard. "You had an affair."

"Yes."

"While married."

"Yes."

"While Tomas was in and out of detox."

"Yes." Gloria rubbed her knee again, harder. "You want me to decorate it? It was ugly. I was tired, angry, lonely, and full of resentment that could power a city. He was famous and attentive and smelled like turpentine and expensive soap. I was stupid enough to think attention was the same thing as rescue."

Marisol nodded once, too fast. The motion made her dizzy.

"Did Carina know?"

"Not then. Not now in full."

"Great. So this is just me again. Special treatment."

"Don't be childish. This is not a prize." Gloria's voice shifted from flat to command. "Listen with your whole head. Tomas knew before you were born. I told him at ten weeks because I thought he was going to leave and I wanted him gone if he was going. He sat at our kitchen table with his hands flat, one hand shaking, and asked only one question: Will this child have my name? I said yes if you want it. He said then we move forward and we never weaponize blood in this house. Those were his words."

Marisol laughed once, a dry bark. "Never weaponize blood. He says that and then he dies and you make him a saint in protest posters."

"You still think I did that alone." Gloria leaned forward, forearms on thighs, as if she were about to pray but could not decide to whom. "Tomas chose the martyr story with me. Not every detail. But the spine of it, yes."

"No."

"Yes." Gloria turned toward her fully now. "He chose it when he was alive, before the final relapse. He knew exactly what overdose would do to your life and Carina's and mine. He had watched what people say at funerals for addicts: no casseroles, no scholarship envelopes, just whispers and somebody counting silverware. And he had two daughters, one paycheck, and a body that didn't keep promises."

Marisol felt anger rise, then stall, then reform into something messier. "You're telling me a dead man asked for myth-management."

"I'm telling you a living man asked for survival math." Gloria opened her bag, dug out a folded envelope wrapped in clear tape, and held it up before handing it over. "I brought this because I knew you wouldn't believe me without paperwork. You got that from me, by the way."

Inside the envelope was an old photocopy with a union logo half faded away and a handwritten note on the back in blocky black marker. The photocopy listed bereavement payout categories and emergency household support for deaths tied to labor actions. The note said: If it is overdose, no fund. They will call it personal failure. If it is line action, girls keep apt + school. T.

Marisol read it twice. The marker had bled into the paper fibers years ago, turning the letters fuzzy at the edges.

"This could be anyone's T," she said, and heard how thin the protest sounded.

"Turn it over."

On the front, clipped to the form with a rusted staple, was a pay-stub copy with Tomas Vega's name and partial employee number.

"He asked Benny from the warehouse union office for the categories," Gloria said. "Benny refused to lie on official forms. He gave Tomas the list and told him not to be stupid. Tomas came home with this in his pocket and didn't even take off his boots before he did the speech about legacy. Legacy, as if we were Kennedys."

Marisol stared at the note until the marker strokes blurred.

"He still overdosed," she said.

"Yes."

"He still left us."

"Yes."

"He still let me walk around reading poems about a murder that didn't happen."

"Yes." Gloria's voice was quiet now, almost hoarse. "And he also put your school uniform money in an envelope every August and wrote the amount on the outside in pencil. He also made arroz con gandules every Christmas Eve even when his hands shook. He also sat through your first rehab family day and let a twenty-two-year-old counselor tell him how to breathe. People do filthy things and loving things with the same hands. That's what I kept trying to tell you and you heard only the part that made me look guilty."

The wind shoved a sheet of sand across the boards, a dry scraping hiss that sounded like a match being struck. Marisol held the form down against her knee and tried to separate fact from narration. Paternity yes. Tomas knew. Tomas signed onto the lie. The martyr script had been strategy before it became folklore. None of that erased the years of manipulation, the dinner-table deflections, the way Gloria weaponized silence like a kitchen knife kept sharp for one specific task.

"How did he die that night," Marisol asked, "and don't say 'in a motel' like it's a weather report."

Gloria shut her eyes once. When she opened them, she did not look away.

"He called Salvador from a pay phone near the warehouse because he was ashamed to call me drunk," she said. "Salvador told me later he could hear traffic and retching between words. Tomas said he messed up and needed thirty dollars and a place where nobody knew him. Salvador picked him up because he had a car and I didn't. They went to a motel off the highway in Queens because that was what was open at two in the morning for cash. Tomas used in the bathroom. Salvador was in the room yelling through the door like that helps. Tomas came out sweating, sat on the bed, and his breathing got shallow fast. Salvador called 911. He gave his real name because the desk clerk already had his ID on the copy machine. Tomas seized once, hard enough to knock his jaw against the nightstand, and then he went still."

Marisol pressed her tongue to the roof of her mouth until pain sharpened. The details were too physical to reject as invented. Jaw on wood. Desk clerk with ID. Cash motel. These were not martyr details. Nobody building a heroic myth started with a copier at a roadside front desk.

"And then what," she said.

"Then the police came and wrote everything down in that language they use, as if bodies are appliances that malfunction," Gloria said. "Salvador called me from outside while they were still in the room and said, Gloria, he's gone, and he cried like a child and I hated him for crying first. I took a cab I couldn't afford. By the time I got there they had zipped the bag."

Her hand shook once as she reached for the thermos. She stopped before touching it.

"At the morgue the next day," Gloria went on, "I told Salvador we would not let this become the story. I said overdose would stain the girls forever. He said we could keep private records private. Tomas's note was in my purse. The union fund list was in my purse. I made the call to Father Ignacio about a vigil near the river because protest language was already in the neighborhood air that month, and people were ready to believe what sounded righteous. Salvador helped with printing because he had money and guilt and contacts. That is the truth. Not clean. Not forgivable. Truth."

Marisol's first impulse was to tear the photocopy in half and let the wind have it. Her second was to tuck it back into the envelope so carefully the corners would not bend. She did the second.

"You turned a death into a branding package," she said.

"I turned a death into rent, tuition, and two girls who didn't get spat on at school," Gloria snapped. "Do you think I'm proud? I'm not proud. I'm telling you the numbers because numbers fed you. The union widow assistance paid six months when Tomas became a public symbol. Church fundraisers covered your braces. Your first piano lessons came from condolence cash left in little envelopes with saint cards. Later, when you were twenty-one and drinking your liver into the ground, I sold my ring and paid the rehab intake deposit in hundred-dollar bills because that was all the pawn place would give me. I'm not saying this for medals. I'm saying this because you keep writing me as a villain in a neat black coat and I've always been wearing an apron with bleach holes."

The word villain made Marisol flinch because it was accurate in a way Gloria did not intend. Villains were easier to publish than mothers. Villains made clean arcs: reveal, condemn, transcend. Mothers asked for footnotes and second drafts and revisions that never ended.

"I didn't ask you to pay for my rehab by lying about my father," Marisol said.

"No, you asked for vodka and then asked me to not leave."

Marisol sucked air through her teeth. "Cheap shot."

"Accurate shot." Gloria exhaled through her nose and looked down the length of the empty boardwalk as if waiting for a bus that would never come. "You want to burn me in print, do it. But don't pretend it's pure ethics. Some of it's revenge. You're my daughter. I recognize the face."

The sentence landed because it was not wrong. Marisol had told herself this chapter of the book would be accountability. It was accountability. It was also hunger. Hunger to pin each adult to the page and make them hold still while she named what they had done to her childhood, her sobriety, her body. Hunger to never again be the one everyone edited around.

She pulled her day-one chip from her coat pocket, thumbed the ridged edge until the metal warmed. Blue paint had started to wear off where her nail caught it. Day one had turned into more than day one, but she still carried it because it reminded her that wanting and choosing were separate verbs.

"Listen," she said, voice rough. "I'm not promising absolution. I'm not giving you closure. I'm deciding form, not forgiveness."

Gloria made a small circular gesture with her wrist: continue.

"Factual record stays. Cause of death stays. Witness signature stays. Timeline stays. I'll use facsimiles where legal lets me. Where it doesn't, I'll quote certified language and cite source. I include my own relapse, explicit, because otherwise I'm performing innocence and I'm not innocent." Marisol tapped the plastic sleeve. "What I won't do is publish every private wound just because I can make it lyrical. No full motel address. No body details that don't change accountability. No names for people who didn't choose this fight."

"And me?"

"You'll get your name because you had agency and you used it."

Gloria nodded once, accepting the hit.

"Carina?"

"Initial only unless she gives consent in writing. Leo doesn't get anything identifying. Not his school, not his photo, not the block where he lives, nothing searchable." Marisol felt a pulse in her throat settle by a fraction. "Salvador's paternity stays off the public page for now. I'll hold it in the private archive with a sealed note in case legal testimony is ever required. If Carina wants the full story from me, she hears it from my mouth, not from a review."

Gloria stared at her, expression unreadable.

"You'd sit on that after everyth--"

A wave cracked against the rail hard enough to swallow the rest.

"I said for now. I didn't say forever." Marisol rubbed her wrist where the burn scar tugged. "And don't start with martyr math. Accuracy is a duty. Punishment is a choice. I'm done mixing them up."

A gust hit hard enough to rock the bench. Gloria grabbed the thermos before it tipped and tucked it under her coat like contraband.

"People are going to call you a coward," she said. "They're going to say you left out the ugliest parts because they made you look soft."

"People already call me unstable, ungrateful, opportunistic, drunk, hysterical, strategic, and dramatic depending on the day. I can survive coward." She gave a humorless smile. "What I can't survive is becoming the same machine that chewed him up and called it honor."

"He chose it," Gloria said.

"He chose with a system's hand on his throat," Marisol shot back. "It was built to punish addicts and broke families. Calling that choice doesn't make it clean. I can write that without pretending he had no agency. That's the point. Complex isn't the same thing as exonerated."

For the first time since they sat down, Gloria's mouth softened at one corner.

"That sounded like your good poems," she said.

"Don't compliment me right now."

"I'm not complimenting. I'm identifying when you're clear." She shifted, winced, and straightened her leg. "What about me reading pages before publication? Last week's article called me a nurse and skipped fifteen years of housekeeping like mops don't count."

Marisol considered the question longer than Gloria expected; she saw surprise register and vanish.

"You can read sections that include your direct quotes and factual claims," Marisol said. "Not to veto. To challenge errors. You don't get editorial con--"

"I don't want editorial control. I want to not be misquoted into cartoon evil."

"Then give me clean dates and receipts."

"I can do dates."

"Can you do truth when it's ugly?"

"I'm sitting on a frozen bench in Coney Island admitting I fucked a famous painter while married and lied for decades. What more ugly do you require?"

Marisol barked out a laugh before she could stop it. "Fair point."

They sat in silence for a minute, maybe two. The ocean kept hitting the pilings in a rhythm too slow for panic and too hard for calm. Marisol watched a strip of caution tape snap against a locked roller coaster gate and thought about all the words she had wanted from Gloria since she was twelve: I'm sorry, I was scared, I chose wrong, I chose you, I chose me, I chose all of it and none of it was enough. She got none of those exactly. She got records, dates, a union form, and a mother whose hands were red from cold because she had insisted on being precise.

"Did he love me," Marisol asked quietly, "or did he love the role?"

Gloria did not answer right away. When she did, her voice had dropped into the register she used at hospital bedsides, practical and unwilling to lie for comfort.

"Both," she said. "He loved you in the daily way, which is less glamorous and more reliable. He packed your lunches, sat through parent conferences, and ran beside your bike until his lungs gave out. He also loved being seen as your father because it made him feel less like a man losing to drugs. You can hate that and still keep the lunches."

Marisol nodded, then shook her head, then laughed at herself for doing both.

"You are impossible," Gloria said.

"Genetics or environment?"

"Both," Gloria said again, and this time they both smiled, brief and unwilling.

The smile did not heal anything. It did not erase the wake, the years, the lie. It made room for one breath that did not taste like metal.

Marisol pulled a legal pad from her tote. The top page already had a heading in block letters: BOUNDARY TERMS - BOARDWALK CONVERSATION. She hated how procedural it looked, but she needed a record that could not pretend to remember differently by Tuesday.

"I'm writing this down now so nobody edits today into a cleaner version," she said.

"You always did narrate your own disasters."

"Occupational hazard." She clicked her pen. "Hybrid manuscript. Documents braided with poems. Certified language stays where I can legally quote it. No motel address. No identifying details for minors or private parties who didn't choose this."

Gloria listened with her head tipped, like she was hearing medication instructions.

"And me?" she said.

"You stay named. You had agency and you used it." Marisol kept writing. "You can read sections that quote you directly to challenge factual errors. No veto power."

"Carina?"

"Initial only. Leo gets no name, no school, no block, nothing searchable."

Gloria gave a short, incredulous laugh.

"An initial is decoration, mija. At nine this morning a reporter called your sister's school office and asked if Leo was the boy from that wake headline. They are already triangulating."

The pen stopped in Marisol's hand.

"What?"

"Carina was at the front desk when the call came in. She lied and said wrong family." Gloria's voice went flat with effort. "You keep one breadcrumb in print and they'll be at pickup line with cameras."

"I didn't know that."

"Now you do. Change it."

Marisol stared at what she had written, then drew a hard line through `Carina initial only / Leo non-identifying mention permitted`. The ink tore the damp top layer of paper.

"Fine," she said, and rewrote beneath it. "No public mention of Leo at all. Carina appears only as 'my sister' unless she signs consent. Paternity stays in restricted archive unless family consent or court order forces disclosure."

Gloria's shoulders dropped by half an inch.

"Add one more," she said. "No saint language. Not for Tomas, not for Salvador, not for you. No halos, no devils."

Marisol wrote: `No sanctification frame.`

"And if you print that I'm your mother, get my work history right," Gloria went on. "Housekeeping first, then home health. I'm tired of being either a cleaning-lady punchline or an angel nurse."

"Housekeeping, then home health," Marisol said, writing each word slow enough to feel it.

She capped the pen and folded the page into quarters. She slid it into the taped envelope with Tomas's note.

"I need one more thing," she said.

Gloria's jaw tightened. "I already gave you blood and paperwork."

"I need your voice on record saying Tomas chose the lie. Audio only."

"For legal defense?"

"For accountability, including mine. I don't want to paraphrase this and make it pretty."

Gloria looked at the water, then back at Marisol.

"Not here. Wind will eat half the words." She rubbed her knee through the skirt seam. "Thursday, my kitchen, fan off, clock battery out, kettle unplugged so it doesn't hiss on tape. You bring questions in advance."

"Deal."

"And there is a condition." Gloria held her eyes. "You call Carina with me on the line before any draft pages with family material leave your laptop."

"I was going to give her a week--"

"No." Gloria shook her head once. "Tonight. She already thinks I chose you over her."

The refusal came up fast in Marisol's throat and sat there. Then she nodded.

"Tonight," she said.

Gloria stood with effort. Her first step buckled at the knee and she swore, low and tired.

"Walk me to the station," she said. "Not because we're healed. Because there's black ice under the overpass and my knee will fold if I hit it."

"Practical I can do."

They walked side by side without touching, a half step between them that felt deliberate and fragile. The boardwalk gave way to cracked sidewalk, then to fryer oil from one corner place doing winter business for workers and kids cutting school.

At the station stairs Gloria paused, breathing hard. The wind thinned underground, replaced by damp concrete and hot metal.

"Call her now," Gloria said. "If we wait, she'll think we wrote terms over her head."

Marisol put the phone on speaker and tapped Carina's name. It rang three times.

"What," Carina said, no greeting.

"It's me. I'm with Mami," Marisol said.

A sharp breath came through the speaker. "Of course you're."

"Listen first. I'm cutting Leo from the manuscript completely. No mention, not even coded. For you, it's 'my sister' unless you sign otherwise."

Carina laughed once, hard and mean. "You don't get points for not doxxing my kid."

"I'm not asking for points."

"Then hear me. If his name, age, school, or anything searchable appears once, I'll get legal aid and make your month disappear in paperwork." Carina's voice shook on the last word. "I'm done being collateral for your art project."

"Understood," Marisol said.

Carina disconnected.

Gloria took her phone back without comment, nodded once, and headed down the stairs.

Marisol stayed at the landing until her mother vanished around the tiled bend. Her mouth flooded with saliva so fast it hurt. The craving had shifted from ache to command. She pulled out her blue chip and dug the ridged edge into her palm until skin blanched.

She texted Luis: `At Stillwell. Craving is loud. Need instructions now.`

On the corner by the station, the deli cooler glowed white. She opened the glass door and reached for a tiny vodka bottle before she had formed the decision in language. The glass bit cold through her glove.

Her phone rang. Luis.

"Where are your feet?" he said.

"Outside Stillwell. Deli at the corner."

"What's in your hand."

Marisol looked at the bottle. "Vodka."

"Put it back."

"I'm at the counter already."

"Then say you changed your mind and put it back."

The cashier watched her, bored and impatient. Marisol set the bottle aside.

"Not this," she said. "Coffee and a roll."

Luis stayed quiet until she tapped her card and stepped outside with a paper cup burning through the seam.

"Good," he said. "Meeting on Fifth starts at six. You walk there now. Keep me in your ear till you hit the basement stairs."

"I'm shaking."

"Walk anyway."

She walked. Salt grit slid under her boots. Twice she passed liquor-store windows with neon beer signs buzzing blue against the dark. Twice she slowed. Luis kept asking practical questions: street name, next corner, whether she'd eaten three bites yet. By the time she turned onto Fifth Avenue her jaw hurt from clenching.

At 5:58 she pushed through the church side door and followed the burnt-coffee smell downstairs. Folding chairs crowded the basement, fluorescent lights hummed overhead, and wet wool steamed off coats. Luis sat in the back row and nudged an empty chair out with his boot.

When her turn came she said, "I almost bought a bottle twenty minutes ago and I wanted it bad enough to taste the first swallow." No one clapped. A woman across from her nodded once and passed the sugar packets.

After the meeting she rode home with coffee breath and a headache needling behind both eyes. In her room she spread the documents across the narrow desk and weighted each corner with whatever was heavy enough: a chipped mug, the Spanish-English dictionary from college, the rent notice she had stopped opening, her copy of Vallejo with the spine cracked in three places.

She drafted the lawyer email first, attaching the handwritten terms and adding a new line: `Minor removed completely after active contact risk. Please review for residual identifiability.` She drafted Ada next: `I made a call today. Hybrid form. Hard redactions. No public paternity disclosure. I'll explain if you want.`

Then she opened the boardwalk scene and started cutting. She replaced each `Leo` with a black bracket. When a sentence leaned toward moral speechifying, she stripped it back to weather, skin, paperwork, and the sound of Carina's voice saying collateral.

Past midnight the radiator clicked and failed and clicked again. The room smelled like old paper and marker ink. Her palm still carried a crescent from the chip edge.

At 2:07 she printed ten pages, stacked them, and wrote one margin note to herself in block letters: `KEEP THE DAMAGE VISIBLE.` Ten pages was not a manuscript yet, but it was a start she could carry into spring.
