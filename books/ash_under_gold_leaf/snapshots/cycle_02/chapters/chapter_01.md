# Chapter 1

Marisol stood across from the museum steps with her tote strap digging into her shoulder and the October wind pushing damp air up Flatbush Avenue like a warning. The building looked expensive in the way old certainty looked expensive, all stone certainty and carved faces that had never wondered whether twenty dollars would clear before midnight. A school group spilled out of a bus, bright jackets, lunch coolers, one kid already crying because he had left a dinosaur water bottle at home. She let them pass, then checked her phone again even though she had checked it on the train and once on the sidewalk outside the bagel place.

The sobriety app was plain black text on a white screen, stripped of medals and fake confetti. It read: 30 months.

She held her thumb on the screen until it dimmed, trying to pin the number there by force. Thirty months. Not a holy number, but long enough that people had started using words like stable around her, long enough that her mother had stopped smelling her breath before handing her coffee, long enough that she had begun to believe the body's old hunger had finally lost interest in her. She knew better than that. Hunger did not lose interest. Hunger took notes.

She crossed at the light and climbed the steps. Her left wrist prickled where the old burn scar tightened in the cold. Inside, the museum air turned dry and filtered, solvent clean under the perfume of polished stone. Security ran her bag through the scanner, eyed the spiral notebooks and recorder she had packed, and waved her toward a temporary desk where a volunteer handed over a plastic badge sleeve and a one-page map with too many arrows. The volunteer had glitter nail polish flaking off in crescent moons and whispered the cafeteria warning like contraband: the coffee was drinkable only before noon.

Chief Curator Nora Feldman met her outside Administration in a charcoal suit and museum-issued tote stuffed with folders and what looked like a pair of black flats. Nora was taller than Marisol had expected, pale enough that the skin under her eyes showed lavender from fatigue, and she blinked in the quick, overused way of someone who had spent too many years under track lighting reading donor emails. She shook Marisol's hand once, firm and brief.

"Thanks for being early," Nora said. "Intake always takes longer than anyone admits. Come on."

Her office sat behind two keycard doors and a wall of framed exhibition posters that made every decade look cleaner than it had been. Nora motioned Marisol into a chair, opened a file, and slid a contract addendum across the desk.

"Before we talk aesthetics," Nora said, "we need to clarify institutional posture. The residency is attached to the Rivas retrospective. Donors are underwriting both. That means deliverables have a reputational horizon, not just an artistic one."

Marisol read the first paragraph, then the next. She had seen versions of this language in grant packets and adjunct contracts, but this version had sharper teeth. If commissioned material caused donor withdrawal, legal exposure, or public claims of defamation, the museum could suspend publication support, revoke access, and claw back stipend funds. There it was in polished legal English: if you embarrassed the money, the money got to erase you.

"This looks less like a poetry residency and more like a nondisclosure agreement in costume," she said.

Nora did not flinch. "It is both. We are paying you four thousand dollars to produce public-facing text in connection with a living artist whose estate and collectors are extremely litigious. I need you to understand where the lines are before you start stepping near them."

"The project brief said I could write against sanctification."

"Against simplistic sanctification," Nora said. "Not against the institution's ability to function next season."

She said institution the way some people said weather, too large and too indifferent to take personally. Then she leaned back, kicked one heel off under the desk, and flexed her toes before slipping on one of the flats from her tote. Human, suddenly, with a blister she had not budgeted for.

"Let me put this in plain English," Nora said. "We need language that gives donors a legacy story they can stand beside at dinner without choking on it. Complexity is fine. Public bloodletting is not."

Marisol felt the old flare in her chest, that instant heat when somebody asked for complexity with guardrails. She thought about rent arrears waiting in a drawer back in Sunset Park. She thought about the copy shop manager who kept scheduling her for closing shifts because she never complained. She thought about the way people in funding rooms said voice when they meant labor.

"You commissioned me because I don't write brochures," she said.

"Correct," Nora said. "We commissioned you because your work carries edge without collapsing into spectacle. I'm asking you to keep that balance inside a legal framework."

Nora tapped the final page. "Sign here, here, and here. Then we'll activate your badge."

Marisol signed. The pen dragged once where her hand trembled, a tiny shake she hoped Nora would read as cold. Nora stacked the pages, initialed two corners, and passed over a lanyard with MARISOL VEGA printed in black block letters.

On the margin of her own notebook, Marisol wrote donor-safe grief, stared at it, then crossed the phrase out so hard the paper thinned.

"Your primary contacts are me for curatorial matters and Dr. Ada Park for conservation coordination," Nora said. "You're meeting both now. Rivas requested an introductory walk-through before public hours."

"Requested me specifically?"

Nora's mouth made a brief, tired line that might have been amusement. "Rivas requests weather patterns and blood chemistry. We negotiate what we can."

They moved through a corridor that narrowed near the conservation wing, where the air got even drier and smelled faintly of ethanol and old canvas. Ada Park waited by a steel door with a ring of keys and a tablet tucked under one arm. She was lean, close-cropped hair tucked behind one ear, and the scar across her right knuckle pulled white when she tightened her grip on the tablet. Her hands looked overwashed, skin cracked at the cuticles from solvents and gloves.

"Ms. Vega," Ada said, offering a nod instead of a handshake. "I finished humidity checks in Gallery Three. You're clear to enter, but don't touch anything that isn't designated touch-safe. If a label looks loose, tell me. Don't fix it yourself."

"I grew up Catholic," Marisol said. "I can follow shrine rules."

Ada's mouth twitched. "Good. Same posture, less incense."

Nora swiped her card. The lock released with a hard mechanical click that sounded too loud in the quiet hall.

Gallery Three was still half asleep. Lights were up at maintenance level, paintings floating in dim rectangles, ladders pushed aside, one crate still open near the far wall. The room held that charged pre-opening silence museums liked to call calm, when every object waited to be looked at and every employee waited for something to go wrong first. A wheelchair sat near the central bench, but Salvador Rivas stood beside it with one hand on the backrest, tall even bent, shoulders pitched forward in a way that made every breath look negotiated.

He wore a dark suit jacket over a black T-shirt, collar loose at the throat. His skin carried a yellow cast under the gallery lights. A tremor moved through his right hand in small, persistent pulses as he folded and unfolded a linen handkerchief into sharp squares. When he looked up, his eyes were clear, almost bright.

"There she is," he said. "The museum's poet with a deadline."

Nora stepped forward. "Salvador, this is Marisol Vega, our resident writer. Marisol, Mr. Rivas."

"Salvador," he corrected. "If I have to die in public, we can skip honorifics."

His voice had theatrical weight, but there was gravel under it, a scrape that sounded painful when he pushed volume. He held out his left hand. Marisol took it. His skin was cool and dry, painter's callus still thick at the base of his fingers.

"Vega," he said, still holding her hand a second too long. "From where?"

"Flatbush," Marisol said. "Originally Sunset Park."

"No. The name. Vega from where?"

Nora shifted. "Salvador, we can do biographical context after we review process."

He ignored her. His gaze stayed on Marisol like he had found a word he recognized in a language he had pretended to forget.

"Your mother's name?" he said.

The question landed in her body before it landed in thought. "Gloria Vega," she said. "Why?"

The tremor in his hand sharpened once. He let go of her and sat in the wheelchair with careful annoyance, turning the motion into an insult someone else had scheduled.

"Because I knew a Gloria Vega with hair pinned so tight it looked like discipline," he said. "Worked nights. Knew exactly how to stretch two chickens across five people."

Nora cut in, voice polished. "For today's purposes, we're orienting Marisol to approved materials. Interviews will be scheduled through my office with either me or a designated staff member present, and recordings route through Legal review before transcription."

Salvador laughed once, no humor, then coughed into the handkerchief. The cough took too long. Ada took one step forward, then stopped when he lifted two fingers without looking at her. When he caught his breath, he spoke to Nora without taking his eyes off Marisol.

"No," he said. "If you put three people in a room, I perform. If you put Legal in the room, I lie. If you put this woman and me in a room, maybe I tell the truth before my pancreas finishes eating me and my right hand forgets line pressure."

Nora did not look away. "No witness means contested notes. Contested notes turn into defamation exposure, and then donor counsel shuts the whole project down."

"Then build medical guardrails, not a chaperone," he said. "Ada outside the door. Panic button on the wall. Let the room smell like solvent and old canvas. No curator script in my ear."

He turned the chair slightly, now addressing Marisol directly. "What are you writing?"

"A poem cycle tied to your retrospective," she said. "And notes for a larger manuscript about my father's death."

"Your father the martyr," he said softly.

The room thinned. Even the vents seemed to hush.

"You know about that?" Marisol asked.

He picked at the edge of his handkerchief, folding it again. "Brooklyn is small. Grief is loud."

Nora stepped between them by half a pace, not enough to block, enough to remind them she could.

"Salvador," she said, "we're not discussing unvetted personal history in an unscheduled intake."

He looked up at her, almost kind. "Nora, if I wanted your season donor-safe, I'd die quietly and leave you three paragraphs about brushwork, underpainting, and varnish bloom. You invited a poet because you wanted voltage. Voltage burns insulation."

Ada shifted her weight, key ring clicking softly against her thigh. "We need to decide access parameters now," she said. "I've got a treatment window in forty minutes, and this room has to clear for lighting tests."

Practical, clipped, no drama. Marisol was grateful for the blunt timing. It kept the moment from floating off into metaphor.

Nora exhaled through her nose and spoke in that legal cadence again, each noun set down like a paperweight.

"Fine. Concrete terms," she said. "Ninety minutes max because your vitals crash after that. Door closed but unlocked. Ada's in the corridor with a med line. Any notebook handling gets logged as potential estate evidence."

Salvador's mouth curled. "I can do ninety. I can do unlocked. But if someone steps in to steer language, the notebooks stay in storage with the cracked canvases and bad gesso."

He leaned forward in the chair, tremor running through his hand harder now, and for the first time Marisol saw fear under the performance, not fear of death exactly, fear of speech failing before he could spend it.

"I've got notebooks," he said to Marisol. "Forty years, maybe more. I call them the Saint Ledger Notebooks when I'm being dramatic. Dates, names, thumbnail sketches, pigment mixes, where the ground failed, lies I told myself and then crossed out. You don't get them with donor handlers sipping oat milk outside the glass."

Nora's voice cooled another degree. "If you withhold material after documented terms, we'll record noncooperation and proceed without your private archive."

"Do what you need," he said. "I don't have time left for your script."

A pulse beat in Marisol's throat hard enough to feel at her gums. This was the pivot she had not expected before noon on day one. She could hear two futures opening at once. In one, she stayed procedural, kept Nora in the room, delivered tasteful language about light and mortality and civic memory, and maybe left with enough stipend money to pay part of December. In the other, she stepped into a room alone with a man famous for staging truth like a set piece and hoped she could still tell document from trap when he started talking about her family.

She asked the only question that mattered. "What are you asking for exactly?"

"Three closed-door sessions to start," Salvador said. "Ninety minutes each, timed to when pain meds are low enough that I can think and high enough that I can sit up without vomiting. Before the tremor eats my hand-memory and every line turns to mud. Keep the curator out of the room, and keep donor observers out too. You can bring one notebook. No recorder until I say yes."

"That isn't acceptable," Nora said.

"Then you can publish your wall labels without me."

Nora turned to Marisol, and now the institutional voice dropped, just for a second, into human fatigue.

"If you agree to this," she said quietly, "you're taking on risk personally and professionally. There won't be a witness if he later disputes your notes. There won't be institutional protection if this becomes a claims fight. Defamation exposure, indemnity clawback - no, direct personal exposure if this goes bad. It lands on you. I need you to hear that clearly."

Marisol heard it. She also heard the scrape of late rent against her name, heard the manuscript in her backpack like a body she had to carry uphill, heard her father's saint story rattling in its frame.

"I hear you," she said.

Salvador watched her with unnerving stillness. His right hand shook against the armrest, but his face was calm, waiting.

The word yes pressed against her teeth before caution could assemble. She tasted metal, felt the burn scar on her wrist pull tight in the cold air, and let the risk land where it landed.

"I'll do it," she said. "Closed door. Me alone."

Nora's eyes closed briefly, one controlled blink held too long. "I need written acknowledgment that this deviates from standard protocol," she said. "And a scheduling structure through my office, no side texting, no private transport."

"Fine," Marisol said.

"You'll submit contemporaneous notes after each session."

"Fine."

"If at any point he is medically unstable, staff can enter regardless of interview status."

Salvador made a dismissive sound. "If I pass out, by all means, preserve me."

Ada stepped in before Nora could answer. "I'll set the room with a panic button and med line in reach," she said. "And I'm putting a chair by the wall for when either of you gets dizzy. That's not negotiable."

Salvador gave her a tired, almost affectionate look. "You're the only adult in this building, Ada."

"I know," she said.

Nora straightened the folder in her hands, trying to square the moment by squaring paper. "We'll draft terms by end of day," she said. "Marisol, stay after and we'll process the access addendum."

The meeting broke without ceremony. Ada moved toward the far wall to check a label seam, already back inside practical tasks. Nora took a call the second her phone lit up, voice instantly donor-smooth: "Yes, of course, we are fully aligned on messaging." Salvador sat in his chair, eyes on the triptych across from him, fingers worrying the handkerchief into smaller and smaller squares.

As Marisol turned to leave, he spoke without looking at her.

"Bring a pen that doesn't skip," he said. "The cheap ones always fail when the sentence gets ugly."

Outside the gallery, the corridor felt colder than before. By the time she reached the public atrium, her jaw hurt from clenching. She went down the service stairs instead of taking the elevator, hand sliding along chipped paint, and came out near the loading dock where staff smoked under a NO SMOKING sign that everyone ignored. Rain had started, light and mean, enough to slick the curb and darken the concrete.

The craving arrived so fast it felt like being shoved from behind. Not a thought first. Mouth first. Saliva flooding, pulse snapping awake, the body remembering vodka's clean burn before her conscience had finished forming a sentence. She could see the liquor store two blocks down, bright refrigeration light, discount sign in the window, a shelf of half-pints behind plexiglass. Her legs had already angled that direction.

She stopped at the corner and dug for her phone with hands that did not feel attached to her. Her contact list had three people pinned at the top. She stared at Luis's name long enough for the screen to threaten sleep, then hit call before she could decide she was being dramatic.

He picked up on the third ring. Background noise: echoing announcements, a bus kneeling, somebody laughing too loud, and the wet snap of the orange peels he was always tearing into strips at dispatch to keep his hands busy.

"You okay?" he said.

No hello. Just a gate.

"No," she said. "I'm not drinking. Yet. But my body thinks that's the plan."

"Where are you?"

"Museum loading dock."

"Can you see a store?"

"Yeah."

"Turn your back to it."

She did, facing a chain-link fence and stacked milk crates wet with rain.

"Now what happened," Luis said. "Facts, not theater."

His voice was steady in that specific way that annoyed her when she wanted sympathy and saved her when sympathy would have killed her. She told him about intake, the contract language, donor clauses, Salvador saying her mother's name like a match strike, closed-door demand, Nora warning her she could get buried if it went bad.

Luis listened without interruption except for one "mm-hm" when a truck backed up and beeped through her sentence. When she finished, he asked, "Did you say yes?"

"Yeah."

"Why?"

She leaned against the fence, cold metal through her coat. "Because if he's telling any truth at all, it's in private. Because I need the material. Because I can't keep writing around my father like he's a stained-glass window. Pick one."

"Those are reasons," Luis said. "I'm asking which one was driving the bus."

A laugh broke out of her, sharp and mean. "You're doing transit metaphors now?"

"I'm at work. Adaptability. Answer me."

She closed her eyes. Behind her lids she saw Salvador's hand shaking against the armrest, the way he had said martyr like he knew the architecture of that word from the inside.

"The father thing," she said. "That was driving."

"Okay. That's honest." Pause. Paper shuffling on his end. "You got a notebook on you?"

"Always."

"Write three facts before you write one feeling. Date, time, who said what exact words. Then write what you wanted to hear and didn't. Don't call your mother yet. Don't call anybody you want to perform for."

"You think I'm performing right now?"

"I think you're scared and smart, and that's a dangerous combo when liquor is two blocks away. Write it down so your future self can't lie about today."

Rain thickened, ticking against the fence and her phone screen. She crouched under the shallow overhang by the dock door, pulled her notebook from the tote, and clicked her pen.

"I'm writing," she said.

"Good. Read me the first line when you're done."

She wrote: October 27, 2026. 10:42 a.m. Gallery Three intake with Nora Feldman, Ada Park, Salvador Rivas.

The letters steadied as she continued. Nora said donor clauses allowed suspension and clawback for reputational harm. Salvador recognized Gloria Vega's name before any family prompt. Salvador requested three closed-door sessions, no curator present. Nora warned no witness means no protection in a claims fight. Marisol accepted.

"Read it," Luis said.

She read. He let silence sit for one breath, then two.

"That's usable," he said. "Now feelings, one sentence only."

She looked at the wet page, ink feathering at the edge where rain had touched it.

"I wanted him to be just an artist," she said slowly as she wrote, "and now I think he's a door I can't close."

"There you go," Luis said. "You don't have to walk through today. You just have to stay sober today."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Yeah," she said, angry because he was right to ask. "I do."

"Then next step. Send the curator your acceptance in writing with terms clear. Keep it factual. No poetry in email. Then get coffee with sugar and protein, because your blood sugar sounds like trash and low blood sugar lies louder than donors."

"You flirting with me, Lou?"

"I'm old enough to be your worst decision. Go eat. Text me a photo of food, not coffee, by noon."

She smiled despite herself. "Bossy."

"Alive," he said, and hung up.

Marisol stayed crouched until her legs went numb, then stood and wiped rain off the notebook cover. The liquor store sign down the block hummed in her peripheral vision, still there, still offering the exact fast mercy she had trained herself not to call mercy. She turned away from it and walked back inside, shoes squeaking on the service corridor linoleum.

At a staff desk outside Administration, she opened her laptop. Her right hand skidded on the trackpad and she missed her password twice before forcing both palms flat against the warm plastic. She had bitten the inside of her cheek somewhere between the dock and the elevator; copper sat on her tongue.

Before she finished the subject line, a security supervisor stopped beside her desk.

"Ms. Vega? Curatorial flagged your badge," he said. "Until Legal countersigns your addendum, you're restricted to Admin and public floors. No Gallery Three access without escort."

He set a paper temporary pass beside her wrist and moved on.

Subject: Interview Protocol Confirmation - Rivas

Nora,

Confirming that I accept the three-session closed-door structure requested by Salvador Rivas, with scheduling routed through your office and contemporaneous notes submitted after each session. I understand this deviates from standard witness protocol and carries dispute risk. I acknowledge temporary badge restrictions pending signed addendum language.

Thanks,
Marisol Vega

She read it once, deleted temporary, sent it.

Her inbox chimed before the sent icon stopped spinning.

From: Nora Feldman
Cc: Legal, Security Operations
Report to Conference B at 11:30 for risk acknowledgment and access terms. Until completion, no unsupervised contact with Mr. Rivas.

Then she opened a new document titled MARTYR MANUSCRIPT - FACT LOG and pasted the notes from her notebook, line by line, before memory could sand them smooth. She added a final entry:

Accepted terms: three private sessions, no in-room witness, dispute exposure acknowledged.

When she leaned back, the museum hummed around her, carts rolling, printers spitting labels, a child somewhere in the lobby asking too loudly whether dead artists still got paid. Through the window she could see rain stippling the plaza and, beyond it, the city moving with complete indifference to whatever she had just agreed to. Her hands still shook, but the shake had changed. Not appetite now. Aftershock.

Thirty months sober, she reminded herself, pressing her thumb against the ridge of her burn scar until the skin hurt.

Thirty months, and now this.
At five thirty she'd be in the museum's education room, legal pad squared, trying to get one concrete, verifiable claim before either of them started performing.
