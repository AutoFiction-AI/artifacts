# Chapter 12

Three days later, Marisol stood outside 2B with the folder braced against the hallway wall, clear sleeves already arranged: death certificate, toxicology summary, incident report with witness signature, shift log, motel invoice. She wanted the first thing in the room to be paper, not another speech.

Certified copy paper had a different weight from printer paper. Thicker, powdery, official in the hand. She had learned that in Municipal Record Room three days earlier, while a clerk named Imelda watched her fill out line 6B again because she had put “relationship: daughter” where the form wanted “requestor status code.” The clerk had slid the corrected form back and said, almost kindly, “Don’t fold these. If they crease they look tampered.”

She had not folded them. She had spent twenty-two dollars at a copy shop near Borough Hall to make duplicates and keep the originals in clear sleeves. She had highlighted nothing. She had promised Luis on the phone she would not turn evidence into performance. “Read them straight,” he had said. “No speech. Let the words do the cutting.”

The lobby light was out again. On the second-floor landing, somebody had taped a notice about package theft over an older notice about rent increase, both curling at the corners from steam.

She listened first. Pot lid clatter, Leo’s voice trying to remember the planets, Carina saying, “Start over and breathe.” Gloria’s rosary beads clicked once against glass. That house sound, all of them layered, made Marisol press two fingers against her sternum and count to four before she knocked.

She knocked.

Carina opened the door with a dish towel over one shoulder and a school district lanyard still around her neck. She had changed out of office shoes into socks with little basketballs on them, but she was still in the navy slacks she wore to P.S. 261. Her chipped front tooth flashed when she inhaled through her mouth, not a smile.

“You said seven,” Carina said.

“It’s six forty-eight.”

“You’re keeping minutes now?”

“I brought the documents.”

Carina looked past her toward the stairwell and then back at the folder under her arm, the clear sleeves catching hallway light. “Come in. Wipe your boots. Leo, shoes off the table.”

Leo sat at the kitchen end of the table with his science folder open and a glue stick cap between his teeth. He had drawn three pigeons in pencil margins, each with one leg banded in red marker. Next to his notebook was a jar lid full of sunflower seeds. He had started feeding pigeons off the fire escape and had begun assigning each one attendance marks like they were students. He was nine and took inventory the way accountants and children did: with zeal and no mercy.

He glanced up when Marisol came in. “Tia Mari, I got one to land on my hand yesterday, but Mama said don’t tell Abuela because she thinks they got diseases.”

“Everything’s got diseases,” Gloria said from the stove. “That is why God made soap. Wash your hand again.”

Gloria stood with her weight tilted off her right knee, stirring a dented pot of arroz con gandules with one hand and pressing the other into the small of her back. Her hair was pinned into the same tight knot she wore even when she slept. Two prayer candles burned at the corner shelf: Sacred Heart and Virgen de la Caridad, wax rivers down both sides. The apartment smelled of sofrito, café bustelo reheated too many times, and hot wax.

Marisol put her tote down and set the folder on the table, not the edge where spills happened, the center where homework happened and bills got sorted and Leo lost his baby teeth. “I need both of you to sit,” she said.

Carina snorted without humor. “You need?”

“I asked for tonight because I’m done doing this in hallways.”

Gloria did not turn around. “If this is about accusing, you can accuse after Leo eats.”

“It is about evidence.”

“Everything is evidence with you now.”

Leo looked between faces, alert in the way children got when the weather changed indoors. “Am I in trouble?”

“No, mi cielo,” Carina said at once. “Eat your chicken.”

He stabbed a piece, chewed, and kept watching.

Marisol pulled out three sleeves and laid them side by side. Left to right: death certificate, toxicology summary, incident report with witness signature. Then she added the Red Hook shift log copy, the one with Tomas’s name and clock-out time the night before he was supposed to have died at a protest uptown. She laid a fifth sheet, motel invoice with date and room number, because she had found it in Gloria’s old accordion folder when she came for laundry last month. She had not told Gloria she had copied it.

Paper spread across plastic tablecloth printed with faded oranges. An exhibit, yes, but also impossible to ignore.

Gloria finally turned off the burner and came to the table, wiping her hand on her apron. Carina remained standing, one palm on Leo’s shoulder. Her thumb moved back and forth over the seam of his T-shirt without her seeming to notice.

Marisol tapped the first page. “Certificate number 26-11-78403. Cause of death: acute combined fentanyl and heroin toxicity. Location: Harbor Motor Lodge, room 214. Date: November 12, 1992.”

The year scraped through her on the way out. Thirty-four years of family story, and the number on the page did not blink.

Leo stopped chewing.

Carina said, “Not in front of him.”

“He hears all of it anyway.”

“Then give him one thing this house doesn’t do: a filter.”

For half a beat, Marisol wanted one too. She wanted Leo to keep one clean shelf in his head for another year.

Gloria leaned over the table and read the heading without touching the page. Her lips moved around the English words before sound came. “You went to get this certified.”

“Yes.”

“You paid for this.”

“Yes.”

“With what money?”

The question landed like a slap. Even now Gloria could move the argument from death to bookkeeping in one sentence.

Marisol stared at her. “That’s what you took from this?”

“I asked a question.”

“Copy shop shift. Credit card. Doesn’t matter.” She moved to the third page. “Witness line. Read it.”

Gloria did not.

Carina did. She bent from the waist and read fast, like she read school emails she already knew she would hate. “Witness: Salvador Rivas. Address—” Her jaw tightened. “He was there.”

The speed of it told Marisol Carina was not surprised by drugs, only by the name in ink.

“He signed it.”

Leo pushed his plate away. “Who’s dead?”

The question hit the back of Marisol’s neck like a hand. She had wanted truth; she had dragged timing in by force.

No one answered.

Carina straightened. “Leo, bedroom. Take your folder.”

“I didn’t finish—”

“Now.”

He looked at Marisol once, then grabbed the folder, jar lid, glue stick, and dragged his backpack down the hall. His socks whispered on linoleum. A minute later his door clicked shut. No music started. He was listening.

The room lost its only soft thing.

Gloria lowered herself into a chair with a small breath she could not hide, knee stiff. “You wanted us in one room,” she said. “We’re here. Say what you came to say.”

Marisol sat too, because standing made it feel like court and she was tired of pretending she was judge. “I came because this can't stay in pieces. I needed you and Carina at the same table with the same pages. No more separate stories.”

Carina pulled out the chair opposite and sat hard. “You got your little tribunal. Congratulations.”

“I'm not trying to humiliate anybody.”

“Then stop laying out my father like case files.”

“Our father’s story has been case files since before either of us could read.”

Gloria’s eyes cut to Marisol. “Watch your mouth.”

“Why? Because it sounds ugly? It's ugly.”

Gloria tapped the death certificate once with one finger. “You think this paper tells everything? That one line is a life?”

“No. It tells enough to stop the lie.”

“What lie?” Carina asked, but there was no curiosity in it, only anger putting on a coat.

Marisol looked at her. “Don’t do that.”

Carina held her gaze for a beat, then looked away first. She grabbed the dish towel, folded it into a square, unfolded it again. “Fine. I won’t do that. You want plain? I knew he overdosed.”

Marisol felt the table edge under her palm as if it had tilted. “Since when.”

“Since I was seventeen.”

The heater pipe hissed and went silent.

Carina kept her hands busy with the towel. “Ma told me after I found the motel receipt in the church cookbook. She said if you found out then, you’d blow up your life and maybe ours. She was probably right.”

Marisol laughed once, a dry split sound. “So both of you watched me give readings about him getting beaten by cops. You watched me stand in front of rooms and build him into a saint and you said nothing.”

Carina’s eyes flashed. “You were building him before anybody could stop you.”

“I was thirteen, Carina.”

“And by thirty-four you were still cashing it. Grants, panels, residency, all of it under Vega Daughter of the Martyr.”

Marisol flinched as if slapped. “You think I wanted this?”

“I think you liked being the one with a tragic origin story that got applause.”

“Don’t.”

“You don’t get to ‘don’t’ me. You dragged this into my kitchen while my kid does homework in the next room.”

Gloria raised a hand. “Enough, both.”

Marisol turned to her. “No. Not enough. You don't get to referee after thirty-four years of fabrication.”

Gloria’s voice came soft first, which was always danger. “Fabrication fed you.”

Marisol blinked. “What?”

“You heard me.” Gloria sat straighter, command tone clicking into place. “When Tomas died, he had no legal job papers worth anything, no union death benefit we could claim clean, no insurance, no savings. He had drugs in his blood and a borrowed social number in half his records. You think I walked into an office and told them all that and they handed me help?”

She pointed at the death certificate with two fingers, nails short and squared. “You put this cause of death on benefit forms in 1992 and you get denied. Widow emergency cash? Denied. Rent relief? Denied. Church fund? Maybe not denied but cut in half because now it's ‘his fault.’ Then, when you girls hit school, forms start asking extra questions. Immigration asks why addresses don't match and why signatures don't match and where your husband worked and under what name. One question turns into ten, ten turns into a visit.”

Marisol opened her mouth.

Gloria did not let her. “You were a baby. Carina wasn't even born. I had one knee that already hurt from cleaning twelve floors at Kings County and a landlord who wanted cash by Friday. I did math, not poetry. I did survival math.”

Gloria set both palms on the table. The plastic cloth popped, and Leo's fork jittered against his plate.

Carina stared at Gloria now, not at Marisol. “You told me it was for her. You never said benefits.”

“What good would that do you at seventeen?”

“I wasn’t stupid at seventeen.”

“I know you weren’t. That's why I told you enough, not all.”

Marisol felt heat climb her neck. “You forged protest flyers.”

Gloria shrugged once. “We printed memorial cards with church people. Not flyers.”

“You told me the police beat him and dumped him.”

“I told you a story you could carry to school without getting spit at.”

“No one would spit at me for having a father with addiction.”

Gloria barked a laugh that held no amusement. “You were not in those offices with me. You were not in line at HRA when the woman at window three said, out loud, ‘Another junkie widow.’ You were not at St. Dominic’s when one old man asked if you girls had inherited sickness. Don't tell me what no one would do.”

Marisol looked at the candle shelf because if she looked at Gloria’s face she would either scream or fold. Wax had spilled down and hardened in milky ribs. She remembered being nine, tracing those ridges while Gloria filled out forms at the table, numbers and more numbers, sometimes stopping to shake her cramped hand.

Memory was cruel: it gave you detail too late.

Carina swallowed. “You still let her write poems about state murder.”

Gloria answered without turning. “By then the story had roots. If I yanked it, what did I replace it with? ‘Your father died in a motel with a needle and a witness who disappeared’?”

Marisol said, “His name is here. He didn't disappear.”

She tapped Salvador Rivas’s signature. Ink slanted, old but legible. As she shifted the page, the plastic sleeve nicked her index finger. A bead of blood rose fast. “He told me he was there. He told me other things too.”

Carina narrowed her eyes. “Like what.”

Marisol felt the room tilt again, this time from inside. She had not planned to say it tonight. Luis had said stay with documents, no detours.

“Tuesday, roof garden, eight-seventeen, wind hard enough to shake the light strings,” she said, then stopped and swallowed. “He said there’s a chance he’s my biological father.”

Silence cracked open so hard she heard Leo’s pencil drop in the back room.

Carina stared as if Marisol had switched languages. “What?”

“He said he and Ma were together the spring before I was born. He said Tomas knew there was doubt.”

“I know they were together,” Carina snapped. “That's neighborhood history. He means what, exactly?”

“He means he might be my father.”

Carina looked to Gloria, then back to Marisol, then to Gloria again. “Did you know this?”

Gloria’s face did not move. “I knew there was doubt.”

Carina went very still. “Doubt as in gossip, or doubt as in you believed it?”

“Doubt as in I believed it,” Gloria said. “I never proved it. Tomas knew and told me we were raising you as his. That was the decision.”

Marisol stared at her. The cut on her finger stung where she had smeared blood on the sleeve. “So you finally said it. Out loud.”

Carina pushed her chair back an inch. “You both just dropped paternity into my kitchen with Leo ten feet away.”

“It's not a grenade. It's relevant.”

“It's relevant to my son hearing names through drywall and trying to do planets while this house comes apart.”

Carina flicked two fingers toward the folder when she said “relevant,” like the word itself was greasy.

Marisol rubbed her left wrist over the old burn scar, hard enough to hurt. “You think this is career management?”

“I think everything is content to you now.” Carina’s voice shook and she hated that it shook; Marisol could see her fighting for control the way she fought with jammed copy machines at school, knuckles white on the lid. “You came in with a clear sleeve packet like we're your panel discussion. You say ‘evidence’ every three minutes. You already blew up the museum with that poem, now you’re back for family footage.”

“I didn't record this.”

“Not with a mic. With your head.”

Gloria said quietly, “Carina.”

“No, Ma, let me finish.” Carina turned fully to Marisol, words fast, clipped, whole. “I held your hand after your first detox, remember that? I shaved your legs because you were shaking too hard. I lied to your students and said you had the flu when you were in bed sweating through blankets and cursing at shadows. I did that because you were my sister, not because it would make good pages. I'm not giving you this house to burn for another ‘raw truth’ post.”

Marisol felt the old shame come up like bile, acidic and immediate. She tasted metal. She made herself keep eye contact. “You want my accounting? Fine. I drank. Chapter six of my life, congratulations, everybody got receipts. I lost the workshop. I lost stipend access. I'm paying interest on debt I can't touch. I'm not here because this helps me. I'm here because if we keep lying, Leo grows up under the same script and then we do this all over again with him.”

From the hallway, Leo said, “I can hear you.”

Carina closed her eyes for one second. “Headphones, baby.”

“I forgot where they’re at.”

“Top drawer.”

“Not there.”

Carina stood, went to the hall, and came back with a cracked pair wrapped in blue tape. She handed them through the doorway and waited until his door shut again. When she returned to the table she did not sit.

Gloria’s hands were flat on the plastic cloth. “No one is giving Leo the script you got. I will die before that.”

Marisol said, “Then tell him his grandfather used.”

“At nine?”

“At whatever age he starts asking why grownups whisper.”

Gloria shook her head. “There is truth and there is timing.”

Marisol’s laugh came out as almost a sob and she hated that sound. “You have been saying timing for thirty-four years.”

Gloria’s eyes flashed wet for the first time. “Because there was never a safe time. There was only less dangerous and more dangerous.”

She took a breath and went on, slower, each clause set down like plates. “I did not lie because I liked drama. I lied because paperwork wanted clean widows and clean children, because immigration wanted names that matched and I had none that matched, because landlords wanted no police in their buildings, because schools asked no questions if you said martyr and looked them in the eye. That word opened doors. Overdose closed them.”

Marisol said, “And what did that word do to me.”

Gloria looked at her, long enough that Marisol had to stop tapping her thumb against the folder edge. “It gave you a father you could love without flinching until you were strong enough to flinch.”

The sentence was almost beautiful, which made Marisol angrier.

“You don't get to call this love and walk away clean,” Marisol said.

“I'm not clean.” Gloria pushed up her sleeve and showed the dented white line near her elbow where blood draws had scarred. “I worked nights mopping chemo vomit at the hospital and came home to two girls and one story that held. I held with it. That's not clean. That's work.”

Carina looked exhausted all at once, as if the argument had physically climbed onto her back. She picked up the toxicology page, read two lines, put it down. “I can’t do this with Leo here,” she said.

“Then send him to Mrs. Colon downstairs,” Marisol said.

“It's a school night.”

“It's also the rest of our lives.”

Carina gave her a look sharp as broken glass. “Everything is apocalypse with you.”

“And with you everything is logistics so you never have to pick a side.”

Carina’s mouth opened, closed. She laughed once, the old cutting-joke laugh with no joke inside. “You think I don't pick sides? I pick Leo eating breakfast. I pick lights staying on. I pick Ma not limping to a second shift because you needed catharsis at dinner.”

Marisol shoved the folder away from her, papers sliding two inches across plastic. “I'm not asking for catharsis. I'm asking you to stop helping her keep this alive.”

Carina leaned over the table, both palms down. “You don't get to tell me what helping looks like. You disappear for weeks into museum drama and lovers and meetings and then storm in here with documents like subpoenas. You're not the only person this happened to.”

Marisol stood so fast her chair legs screamed on linoleum. “I know I'm not.”

“Then act like it.”

She realized she was breathing through her mouth, quick shallow pulls that made her dizzy. She forced herself to sit back down. Luis’s voice in her head: Slow is not surrender.

“Okay,” she said, quieter. “Tell me what that looks like.”

Carina blinked, not expecting the drop in volume. “It's not turning every wound into an argument you win.”

“I'm not trying to win. I'm trying not to hand Leo the same fake prayer card we got.”

“You always are.”

“I'm trying to get one honest page in this house before he memorizes the wrong one.”

Carina said, “Survive where?”

Marisol frowned. “What?”

“You heard me. You keep talking like this is a panel. Fine. Practical question. Where are you sleeping tonight, after that paternity bomb with Leo listening through drywall?”

“I've got options.”

“You have debt and a frozen stipend.”

“I've got options.”

Carina’s stare did not move. “Name one.”

Marisol did not answer fast enough.

Carina gave a small nod as if confirming a number she already knew. “Exactly.”

Gloria rubbed her forehead with two fingers. “Nobody is throwing anybody out. We're angry. We'll talk tomorrow.”

Carina turned to her mother. “No. We won’t.”

“Carina.”

“No. I mean it.” Carina faced Marisol again, voice steady now, frighteningly steady. “After what just came out, you can't stay here tonight.”

Gloria sucked in a breath through her teeth. Down the hall, Leo’s bed frame thumped once, then went still.

Marisol stared. “You’re joking.”

“I'm not.”

“Where am I supposed to go, Cari?”

“I don’t know.”

“You don’t know.”

“I don’t. I'm done choosing between my son trying to sleep and this paternity fight eating my whole apartment.”

Gloria half-rose from her chair, winced, sat back. “Carina, enough.”

Carina did not look at her. “Ma, if she stays, this keeps going all night. We’ll still be at this table at midnight arguing who fathered who while Leo pretends to sleep. Tomorrow he has spelling and I’ve got payroll submission and you’ve got Mrs. Kaplan at seven. I can't keep running a paternity hearing and a home in the same twelve feet.”

Marisol felt her heartbeat in her gums. “So that’s it. You eject me and call it boundaries.”

“I'm setting one boundary.”

“Tonight?”

“Yes. Tonight.”

Marisol looked to Gloria. “Say something.”

Gloria’s eyes were red-rimmed and hard at once. “If you both keep shouting, neighbors call landlord. We already got one warning in October. I can't lose this apartment.”

“That's your answer?”

“My answer is everybody breathe.”

Carina said, “My answer is pack.”

Marisol pushed back from the table and stood. Her legs felt boneless. She wanted to throw the folder, tear the pages, drink, run, none of it in that order. Instead she said, “Fine.”

The word came out flat and wrong.

She went down the short hall to the room she used when she crashed there, technically Leo’s second bedroom now, really a storage room with a narrow bed and stacks of church bulletins Carina designed on weekends for extra cash. The suitcase under the bed still had one wheel missing from the summer trip to Queens she had taken and never written about. She dragged it out. Dust streaked her palm.

She packed like someone else was moving her body: three pairs of jeans, two sweaters, underwear rolled tight, notebook, charger, toothbrush, small bottle of ibuprofen, blue day-one chip from the nightstand, AA meeting list folded in quarters. Her hand hovered over the framed photo of Tomas holding toddler Marisol at Orchard Beach. She left it. She took the photocopy instead, thinner, less claim.

In the living room Gloria and Carina were speaking low and fast in Spanish, the way they did when they wanted precision without audience. Marisol caught fragments through the hall: `nene`, `renta`, `mañana`, `por favor`. Leo’s door opened a crack. One eye in the gap.

“Are you leaving for work?” he whispered.

Marisol crouched so they were level. “No, bug. Just for a few days.”

He looked at the suitcase. “Did you and Mama do a fight-fight?”

She almost lied. The lie came easy, muscle memory. She stopped it with effort that made her jaw ache. “Yeah,” she said. “We did.”

“Can I text you my pigeon chart?”

A laugh caught in her throat and turned into something rougher. “Please do. I need updates on every bird criminal.”

He nodded solemnly. “The one with the red band steals from the fat one.”

“Write down the evidence.”

“I always do.”

He closed his door gently.

Marisol stood and lifted the suitcase. The broken wheel made it drag crooked, a scratch-scratch against the hall floor. She carried it the rest of the way.

At the kitchen table the pages were still laid out, damp now at one corner where a glass had sweated. Death certificate, toxicology, witness signature, shift logs, motel receipt. Nothing disputed except what they meant.

Carina had her keys in hand. “I’ll walk you down.”

“Why, you think I’ll steal the silver?”

“We don't have silver.”

“Exactly.”

“Stop.” Carina’s voice cracked for the first time all night. “Please just stop.”

Marisol saw then how much she was shaking, tiny tremors in both hands. Not rage now. Fear and guilt and exhaustion braided tight.

Gloria touched the back of a chair, not sitting, not standing fully. “Take your scarf,” she said to Marisol. “It's twenty-eight degrees.”

The ordinary instruction undid her more than the eviction had. She grabbed the scarf from the hook without trusting herself to answer.

At the door, Carina said, low enough for Gloria not to hear, “Don't call me for a while.”

Marisol looked at her. “How long is a while.”

“I don’t know.”

“Emergency?”

“If Ma’s in the hospital, I’ll call.”

“That's not what I asked.”

Carina swallowed. “No calls. No popping in. No surprise apologies at school pickup. I need quiet. Leo needs quiet.”

The hallway smelled like bleach and somebody’s fried fish. The light buzzed overhead.

Marisol nodded once. “You got it.”

Carina opened the door and stepped back.

On the landing Marisol turned, expecting Gloria to have come out anyway, to press leftovers into her hand or say one of the old goodnight prayers she used to mutter when Marisol came home late from readings. Gloria stayed in the kitchen doorway, one hand on the frame, face unreadable from that distance.

“Ma,” Marisol said.

Gloria answered in Spanish, voice tired, almost formal. “Ve con cuidado.” Go carefully.

Not come back. Not call me. Go carefully.

Marisol carried the suitcase down two flights because the wheel kept catching. Outside, wind came hard between buildings and made her eyes water at once. She stood by the curb and checked her phone.

Battery thirty-one percent.

Bank app: checking balance $46.12.

Credit card available: $112, which was not available, not really.

One text from Luis from an hour earlier: `How'd it go? Keep me posted before bed.`

One from Ada from yesterday, still unanswered: `Thinking of you. No pressure to respond.`

She typed to Luis: `Bad. Got kicked out. No drink.` She erased `No drink` because it sounded like a child asking for credit, then put it back because facts were facts. She hit send.

The suitcase handle pinched the skin between thumb and forefinger. The cold made the metal taste of panic rise again in her mouth.

A cab slowed, saw the suitcase, sped up when she did not raise a hand fast enough.

She called Mr. Han, the Sunset Park landlord. Straight to voicemail. Twenty seconds later his auto-text hit: `Rent past due. Pay $400 tonight or lock changes 10:00 a.m.`

She typed to Ada: `I got put out. Any chance I can crash on your couch tonight?` The message flipped to delivered and sat there. No typing bubbles. No reply.

The bus shelter at the corner had one panel cracked and taped in an X. She rolled the suitcase in, sat on the bench, and pulled her scarf up over her mouth. Two teenagers in puffer coats shared earbuds and laughed at something on a phone. A woman in scrubs counted singles from her wallet twice, then a third time, lips moving with each number.

Marisol took the folder out and checked the pages again by streetlight, as if one might have changed while she was upstairs.

Witness: Salvador Rivas.

Cause: acute combined fentanyl and heroin toxicity.

Room 214.

Facts sat still. People did not.

The bus app said the next B41 southbound would arrive in twelve minutes, then updated to fourteen, then eleven, algorithmic mercy. Marisol opened Notes and typed three words: `No stable housing.` She stared at them until they blurred and added a fourth: `Tonight.`

She put the phone down and pressed thumb and forefinger against the bridge of her nose until stars burst in her vision. She was not drunk. She was not safe. She was not done.

When the bus finally came, she stood, lifted the crooked suitcase, and climbed on, heading south with Mr. Han’s deadline ticking and no clean plan for where she would get off.
