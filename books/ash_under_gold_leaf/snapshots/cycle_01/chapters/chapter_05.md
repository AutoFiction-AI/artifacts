# Chapter 5

By the time Marisol got off the B61 at Van Brunt, the rain had stopped pretending it was weather and settled into the fabric of everything. Her cuffs were wet above the ankle. Her left sock had started to slide down inside her boot and bunch under her arch. In the lot outside the warehouse, puddles held diesel rainbows that never broke apart, no matter how hard the wind pushed them. She stood there a second with her tote pressed to her ribs, watching a forklift reverse into a loading bay, its warning beep flat and patient, like a hospital monitor that had stopped caring whether anyone lived.

The Red Hook annex sat three blocks from the water, a brick shell with patched windows and a roll gate that looked as if it had lost fights with three different storms and one union election. This was where everything the museum did not have room to remember ended up: busted pedestals, old registrarial binders, obsolete labels, transit crates with three layers of tape and no legible handwriting left. A paper sign on the side door read `RING BELL IF YOU HAVE BUSINESS`. Somebody had crossed out `BUSINESS` in marker and written `A WARRANT`.

Marisol rang anyway.

The woman who opened had silver glitter on two fingernails and a fleece vest with a chess-club patch stitched over the chest where a name tag had once been. She did not bother smiling.

"You museum?"

"Residency project," Marisol said. "I emailed yesterday. I'm looking for handling manifests tied to T.V. inventory tags. Late nineties through early two-thousands, if those are boxed by year."

The woman leaned on the metal door and looked her up and down, not hostile yet, but measuring labor against nuisance. "You got a letter?"

Marisol handed over Nora's signed authorization and watched the woman's mouth tilt when she saw the letterhead.

"Everybody comes with letters," she said. "Nobody comes with dry boots. Come in before you freeze and drip on my desk."

Her name was Sonia, according to the old embroidered strip on her vest. She kept a mug of black coffee beside a stack of intake slips and did not let the mug out of sight while she spoke, as if someone had tried to steal it before. A small inhaler sat beside the keyboard with a purple rubber band around it. When she coughed, she reached for it without looking.

Inside, the annex smelled like wet cardboard, rust, and that old-paper sweetness that turned sour at the edge. The lights buzzed. Radiators clanged when they woke up and then fell silent before doing anything useful. Marisol signed a visitor ledger with a pen chained to the table and followed Sonia through aisles of steel shelving where crate labels had been corrected so many times that each box looked like it had lived three names.

"You said T.V.," Sonia called over her shoulder. "Could be Tomas Vega. Could be transfer-verified. Could be temporary vault. Could be Tuesday volunteer if somebody got funny with abbreviations."

"That's the problem," Marisol said.

"That's every problem."

Sonia showed her two systems that did not agree with each other and seemed proud of neither. The old paper dispatch books lived in plastic bins, sorted by year in handwriting that changed every few shelves. The digital terminal by the office door had started in 2012 and treated anything earlier like folklore. Between them sat thirty years of partial memory and water damage. Sonia gave Marisol a rolling cart, two nitrile gloves with split seams at the thumb, and a sympathetic look she hid by turning to cough into her sleeve.

"Lunch is when it is," Sonia said. "Bathroom sticks. Kick low, not high. If you find mold that blooms green, call me. If it's black, call Jesus."

Marisol laughed once, because that was either laugh or leave. Then Sonia disappeared back toward the office, and the place closed around her with forklift whine, dripping pipes, and the soft scrape of her own gloves against paper.

She started with 1999 because the underpainting code Ada had found ended in `99-RH`. She kept a legal pad on top of the cart and wrote each box ID, each mismatch, each missing run. Half the bins held call sheets from exhibits nobody remembered. One contained outdated emergency plans and a laminated card that still listed the smoking area as "rear stairwell." Another was full of condition reports that had fused together from damp, so each page lifted with the slow resistance of skin from a bandage.

By eleven, her neck hurt and her hands smelled like mildew and latex. She found two references to `TV-HANDLING`, both marked relocated, both with no destination line. She found one dispatch sheet that looked promising until she realized `T.V.` meant `thermal vault` in the registrar's shorthand used that year. She crossed out three pages of notes and forced herself not to tear the paper. She had promised Luis she would write facts before feelings. She wrote: `No complete sequence yet. Multiple abbreviation conflicts. Do not decide narrative early.`

At noon, rain started again on the roof with that fine metallic tapping that could make ten minutes feel like an hour. Marisol ate a bruised pear standing in aisle F and watched water bead down a shrink-wrapped crate labeled `LIGHTING GEL ARCHIVE / 2008`. Nobody stored lighting gels in a crate that size. She set down the pear, cut the tape with her key, and peeled back plastic gone brittle with age.

Inside sat six ledger binders wrapped in contractor trash bags, each one tagged in grease pencil: `DOCK - HANDLING - NIGHT SHIFT`.

She stood very still for one breath, then another, then said, "You have got to be kidding me," to the empty aisle.

The binders were swollen but readable if she lifted each page with both hands. The pages held names, initials, punch-in times, cargo IDs, and overtime signatures. She photographed pages as fast as she could before the battery warning hit, then wrote line references on her pad. `T. Vega` appeared on one log, then two, then a run of six entries in the same week. Beside his initials, someone had penciled `RIGHT HAND LIMITED` on one supervisor note, then circled it in red with no explanation.

The first clean hit made her chest go tight. It was not panic and it was not joy. It was the opposite of both. She could feel information changing weight in real time, like a crate tipping one inch before anybody yelled to grab it.

She rolled the cart toward the office to ask for access to a copier and found two men and a woman already there, wet jackets steaming by the radiator. The woman stood with her thumbs hooked in her belt loops and spoke with the flat volume of someone who had trained herself never to shout in rooms where managers could claim she was hysterical. A union decal sat half-peeled on her phone case, covered by a kid's dinosaur sticker.

Sonia looked up when Marisol approached. "This is her."

The woman turned. "You Marisol Vega?"

"Yes."

"Deena Morales, Local 42 steward." She did not offer a hand. "I hear you're pulling handler logs for some book project."

Marisol held the folder to her chest. "I'm verifying timeline records connected to my father's death."

"Yeah?" Deena clicked her tongue. "Because from where I stand, it looks like another museum person mining dead workers for content while our guys still get blamed for every crate that cracks."

"I'm not writing about damaged crates."

"You're writing about us, though."

"I'm writing about my family."

"Same machine," Deena said. "Different poster."

Sonia busied herself with forms, eyes down. One of the men coughed into his fist and pretended to read a safety bulletin upside down.

Marisol felt the old instinct rise, the polished voice she used in donor rooms, every verb selected, every edge sanded. It would not work here and she knew it. "Listen," she said. "I'm not here to hang this on handlers. I'm trying to establish where Tomas Vega actually was the week he died. That's it."

"Then establish it without stripping names," Deena said. "You know what happens when legal reads old logs? They use dead men to discipline living ones. They call it compliance review and pretend it's neutral."

"I'm not legal."

"You work for people who are."

"For now."

Deena watched her for a long second and then gave a short laugh that had no humor in it. "There it is. The artist truth speech." She tapped the folder in Marisol's hands with two fingers, not hard but deliberate. "You publish anything with active members identified, we file against the museum and you can explain to your residency director why her Friday went to shit."

Marisol felt heat climb her neck, anger braided with embarrassment because Deena was not wrong about the machine, just wrong about her. "I'm not trying to feed anybody to HR," she said. "I need dates, shift codes, and confirmation of one person."

"Confirmation from who?"

"Anyone who actually worked that dock run and remembers him."

The room went quiet enough to hear the rain patter through the gutter seam outside.

One of the men at the radiator, short and bowlegged in a canvas jacket gone shiny at the cuffs, lifted his head. He had been scraping a lottery ticket with a transit token and pocketing the silver flakes as if they were worth saving. A hearing aid whistled once when he adjusted his cap.

"You mean Tomas with the busted hand?" he said.

Deena turned. "Benny, don't."

"What, don't? She asked a question." He folded the half-scratched ticket and tucked it into a tobacco tin with a faded Virgin of Guadalupe sticker on the lid. "If she's lying, she's lying. But that kid had a hand you remembered."

Deena's jaw tightened. "We're not doing witness theater for residency art."

"I retired three years ago," Benny said. "You can't grieve me for speaking." He looked at Marisol. "You his daughter?"

"Yes."

"Which one?"

"Marisol."

Something shifted in his face, not recognition exactly, but the click of a detail finding its slot. "He kept your school photo in his lunch pail. Third grade, maybe. Missing front tooth. He showed everybody for a week."

Marisol swallowed so hard it hurt. She had one third-grade photo with a missing tooth, the one Gloria hated because her bangs were crooked. She had never seen it in any lunch pail. "You remember that?"

"I remember because he dropped mustard on the corner and spent twenty minutes trying to clean it with club soda," Benny said. "Made it worse." He rubbed at his own knuckles as he spoke, a habit like he was still checking for pain. "Right hand got jammed in a chain hoist two summers before. Middle and ring finger never straightened right after. He wore that gray support glove, cut the tips off so he could still feel nylon straps."

Marisol looked down at her notes where `RIGHT HAND LIMITED` sat in blunt pencil. She could see the words without reading them.

Deena stepped between them a little, not enough to block, enough to claim space. "Benny, you're giving personal history in an unrepresented conversation."

"I'm giving what happened," Benny said. "That boy worked nights and still showed up on time for days when he had court with his landlord. Nobody put that on posters."

Marisol lifted her eyes. "Posters?"

Benny glanced at Deena, then back. "After he died, people printed him like a symbol. Fist up, profile clean, no glove, no limp in the hand. That's fine if you need a symbol. I'm saying the real man was tired all the time and carried two braces in his jacket because one smelled bad."

The air in the office felt suddenly thin. Marisol became aware of her own palm digging crescents into the folder cardboard. Deena blew out a breath and looked toward the door.

"I've got to keep members from getting dragged," she said to Marisol, voice flatter now, less combat, more policy. "That's my whole job. You understand me?"

"Yeah," Marisol said. "I do."

"Then hear this clearly. No full names. No active badge numbers. No published pages without redaction."

"I can do that."

"Can and will are different verbs."

"I will."

Deena studied her face one more moment, then nodded once, a tiny movement that read less like trust than temporary truce. She picked up a clipboard and turned to Benny. "You coming to the safety committee or are you going to keep gambling your pension one scratch-off at a time?"

Benny grinned, showing the gap where a molar had gone missing. "Committee puts me to sleep. Lottery keeps me humble."

When Deena and the others stepped back out into the rain, Sonia let out a breath she had been holding long enough to rattle. "You always walk into weather like this?" she asked Marisol.

"Lately, yes."

"Well, copy fast. Copier jams if the paper is damp. Which everything is."

The copier lived in a cinder-block side room with one fluorescent tube and no heat. Marisol fed pages by hand, flattening each ledger sheet against the glass so the warped corners did not cut off dates. She copied shift logs first, then dispatch slips attached with rusted staples, then a run of shipping sheets stamped `PIER TRANSFER / NIGHT RECEIVING`. Her fingers went numb from pressing paper flat. Every third page jammed. Every jam left gray toner on her thumb.

She worked until her phone battery hit four percent and her stomach cramped from too much coffee and one bruised pear. At some point Benny appeared in the doorway with a vending-machine hot chocolate and set it beside her without speaking.

"It's mostly sugar," he said after a minute. "But warm is warm."

"Thanks."

He watched her fight another jam. "You're going to want the overtime addendum pages too. They're where supervisors wrote who couldn't lift alone." He tapped the stack. "That's where I remember the hand note."

"Can I ask you something?"

"You already are."

"Did anyone talk to you when he died? For records, I mean. Museum, police, anyone."

Benny snorted. "Police asked if he used. Museum asked if he clocked out. Nobody asked who paid him cash to cover double shifts. Nobody asked why half the logs for that month went missing and then came back with fresh hole punches." He pointed to a binder edge where the holes were clean circles through swollen paper. "See that? Somebody rebuilt sequence after the flood. Maybe to save it. Maybe to edit it. Depends how kind you feel that day."

Marisol stared at the holes. They were too neat. "Who had access?"

"Everybody and nobody," Benny said. "Night foremen, registrar clerks, sometimes outside movers when management got cheap. We didn't run a courtroom. We ran a dock."

"Did Tomas ever mention a protest?"

"He mentioned rent. He mentioned his mother's blood pressure. He mentioned a girl with poetry notebooks he didn't want riding the subway alone after dark." Benny gave her a sideways look. "That part sounds like you."

She looked away first.

"People can be political and exhausted at the same time," Benny added. "I'm not saying he didn't march. I'm saying anybody who tells you one clean story about that year is selling you something."

When he left, she sat on the copier stool and drank the hot chocolate gone lukewarm, grainy at the bottom. She did not cry. Her body wanted to, that throat burn and nose sting, but the room was too cold and practical for crying. She wrote three lines in her legal pad instead.

`Benny ID: chain hoist injury to right hand (M/R fingers contracture), cut-tip glove.`

`Poster image omitted disability marker.`

`Records show manual resequencing post-flood.`

By the time the annex closed, she had forty-eight copied pages clipped into three bundles with yellow routing slips. Sonia let her use an old envelope reinforced with packing tape and wrote `RETURN IF FOUND` in fat marker across the front before handing it over.

"You coming back tomorrow?" Sonia asked.

"Probably."

"Wear better socks."

Outside, the rain had thinned to mist, the kind that floated more than fell and still found your neck. Marisol walked to a bodega on Van Brunt where the copy machine took coins and the owner let her use an outlet if she bought something. She bought a two-dollar coffee and a bag of stale plantain chips, then sat under a flickering Pepsi sign and scanned every copied page to a cloud folder titled `RH_MANIFEST_VERIFY`.

A man at the counter argued about Lotto numbers while a little girl in a puffer coat practiced spelling words from a worksheet: `harbor`, `witness`, `calendar`. On `calendar` she got stuck, tongue between her teeth, and whispered the syllables until she nailed it. Marisol listened and kept scanning.

Around eight, with the folder finally synced and her phone warm in her hand, she started assembling the timeline on loose receipt paper because she had run out of legal-pad pages. She made one column for family story, one for archive record, and one for confidence level. She wrote dates in block letters, then rewrote them with time stamps where available.

The family story as she had received it since childhood was ritual-grade and clean: Tomas spent his last evening preparing banners for a dockworkers solidarity march; he slept at the parish office; he was killed the next day when police broke the protest line near the piers. Every year Gloria lit a candle and said the sequence the same way, no stray details, no place for questions.

The records in front of Marisol smelled like toner and mildew and refused to stay clean.

`Night receiving log:` Tomas Vega clocked in 21:18 on June 11.

`Overtime addendum:` stayed for emergency re-crating when rain breach hit bay door, signed out 04:52 June 12, supervisor initials M.C.

`Pier transfer sheet:` cargo transfer to museum annex departed 05:35 June 12, handler line includes `T. Vega` and note `RH glove`.

`Union dispatch scratch copy:` reassigned to evening shift June 12 pending hand clearance.

If he signed out at 04:52 and was still on a transfer sheet at 05:35, he had not spent that night sleeping in a parish office. Maybe he went to a march later. Maybe he never made it there. Maybe the story had stitched one week to another because stitches held better than bare skin. But the version printed on memorial cards, the one that made cause and sacrifice line up like saints in a stained-glass panel, no longer survived first contact with ink.

Marisol sat back and rubbed her eyes until stars pulsed behind them. The bodega radio played old salsa under static. Somebody near the beer fridge laughed too loud, then coughed until it turned ugly. She thought of Benny saying `one clean story` like it was a product with a barcode.

She opened a blank note to text Luis and typed: `Found logs. He worked night before.`

She deleted it.

Then she typed: `Need to sit with this before I call.`

She deleted that too.

What she finally sent was: `Got documents. I'm heading home.`

He answered in under a minute. `Eat first. Then home.`

She almost laughed. Luis as sponsor was also a dispatcher. She bought an empanada that tasted like old fryer oil and ate it standing by the window while buses hissed at the curb.

On the ride back to Flatbush, she kept the envelope on her lap with both hands, thumbs resting over the taped seam as if body heat could dry paper retroactively. At Atlantic she switched to the train and got wedged between a nurse asleep on her feet and a teenager practicing trumpet fingering on an invisible instrument. Every stop, more damp coats, more voices, more metal screech. Her shoulder blades ached. Her right wrist clicked each time the train lurched.

She tried to line the facts in chronological order and could not stop seeing the places where somebody had touched the record. She kept seeing fresh hole punches through old wet paper. She kept seeing abbreviation systems that let one person disappear inside three different meanings. She kept seeing missing boxes that reappeared under wrong labels. She also kept seeing a steward ready to fight before she asked her first question, because she had watched institutions turn documents into weapons too many times to count.

At Franklin Avenue, a man boarded with a bouquet wrapped in bodega plastic and held it above everyone's head so the flowers would not get crushed. Marisol watched water drip from the stems onto his sleeve and thought about how much labor went into keeping anything intact long enough to hand it to somebody else.

By the time she climbed the apartment stairs, the hallway smelled of sofrito, coffee, and candle wax from Mrs. Soto's door shrine. The smell was both home and church. She stood outside her own door and listened before unlocking, a habit from years of shared apartments and late-night dread. The apartment was quiet inside. Carina had Leo overnight at their mother's, which meant she had one table, one lamp, and no witnesses.

She spread the copies across the kitchen table under the weak overhead bulb and rebuilt the sequence again, slower this time, using sticky notes for uncertainty. `Confirmed by record.` `Likely.` `Unknown.` `Narrative artifact.` She wrote until her lower back cramped and the coffee went cold.

At 12:14 a.m., she found the line that settled it past reasonable argument. A supplemental handling sheet, carbon copy almost gone, showed `T. Vega` assigned to crate stabilization beginning 23:40 on June 11 with a notation in different ink: `approved despite hand restriction - union notified.` The same form had a receiving stamp from 07:10 June 12. The alleged protest death, according to every family vigil card in Gloria's china cabinet, happened June 12.

It was the night before, and he had been on shift.

He had not been at a parish office, not where the script had always placed him.

Marisol leaned over the sink and let cold water run over her wrists until the skin went numb. She was not shaking from revelation. She was shaking from accumulation: wet paper, bad light, Deena's warning, Benny's hand rubbing his knuckles while he described a glove nobody had ever mentioned, her own notes multiplying into a version of her father that did not fit in the frame she had inherited.

In the mirror above the sink she looked older than she had that morning, not by years, by friction.

She came back to the table and wrote one sentence at the top of a fresh page:

`Chronology has been curated.`

Then she crossed out `curated` because it sounded like museum language, too clean, too clever. She wrote:

`Somebody arranged this.`

That was closer.

At 1:03, she sealed the copied logs in a plastic tote and slid it under her bed between winter boots and a box of old workshop handouts she had never thrown out because throwing out paper still felt like inviting amnesia. She set one copy set aside for off-site storage, as Luis had drilled into her after hearing too many stories about floods and landlords and sudden "accidents." She did not trust her own apartment with evidence anymore. She did not trust the museum. She did not trust a narrative that arrived pre-smoothed.

Before sleep, she opened the third-grade school photo album she had not touched in years and flipped until she found the missing-tooth picture with the crooked bangs. The corner was clean. There was no mustard stain. She pressed two fingers to the glossy edge anyway, trying to imagine the image riding in a dented lunch pail beside a thermos and two oranges.

Outside, a truck hit a pothole hard enough to rattle her window. Somewhere down the block, somebody shouted, then laughed, then shouted again. It was city noise, ordinary and relentless.

Marisol slid the album back into the drawer, turned off the lamp, and lay awake with the copied dates bright behind her eyes.

She had what she came for: a factual line through the fog, thin but real.

She also had the shape of the silence around it, and it was bigger than one family lie.
