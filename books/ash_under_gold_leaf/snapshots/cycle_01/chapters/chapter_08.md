# Chapter 8

The F train doors folded shut behind Marisol and smeared her reflection into the dark glass: coat collar wet at the neck, hair still flat from the hat she had shoved into her tote, eyes too bright for someone on day three. She stood with her hand around the center pole and counted stations the way she counted minutes in meetings, an old trick Luis had taught her for cravings and panic and any other kind of hunger that lied about urgency. There were seven stops left, then six, then five. She had texted Ada that she would bring dinner, then spent eleven dollars she could not spare on pork dumplings and two bottles of yuzu seltzer because she was not going to walk into Ada's apartment with wine, not even as a joke, not even sealed.

Outside the station the wind came off the avenue and slid under her sleeves. Sunset Park looked rinsed clean and mean in winter: halal carts wrapped in steam, nail salons still lit past nine, men smoking in front of shuttered storefronts while a delivery bike rattled by with a milk crate tied down by old bungee cords. She climbed Ada's block with her shoulders up by her ears, fighting the urge to check her phone for unread messages from work, from Luis, from nobody. Her account balance had dipped below three digits after the workshop penalty and rent transfer, and she could feel that fact as a second heartbeat every time she touched her wallet. She wanted tonight to be one room with one person and food that tasted like anything besides burnt coffee from church basements.

Ada opened the door in gray sweatpants, one sock on and one bare foot planted on the hardwood because she had been painting her toenails and forgotten to finish. A bottle of dark green polish sat on the floor beside her, cap off, brush balanced across the mouth like a bridge. She looked at the dumpling bag, then at Marisol, and said, "You brought enough for six people. Were you expecting an ambush?"

"I panic-bought," Marisol said.

"At least you panic-bought correctly. Come in before my hallway turns into a wind tunnel."

The apartment was one long rectangle with the kitchen in front and a bed shoved behind a folding screen painted with cranes. Ada had pinned grocery receipts to the side of her fridge with tiny magnets shaped like tools: a scalpel, a clamp, a mini palette knife. Each receipt had one number circled in pen. Marisol had asked once what the circles meant, and Ada had said, deadpan, "Evidence that six dollars for scallions is a hate crime." Tonight there were three fresh circles and a fourth note in block capitals: `BUY RICE BEFORE CIVILIZATION COLLAPSES`.

Marisol laughed, genuinely, and felt the laugh surprise her body on the way out.

"I knew you'd appreciate that one," Ada said. "Take your shoes off. The downstairs neighbor filed a formal complaint about mysterious ceiling impacts. I think she means me dropping books, but I'm not giving her ammunition."

Marisol stepped out of her boots and lined them beside Ada's sneakers without being asked. She set the dumplings on the counter and reached for plates.

"Don't touch that drawer," Ada said lightly. "That's where all the cursed takeout chopsticks live. They stab through plastic and then hold a grudge."

"You sound like my mother talking about pressure cookers."

"Pressure cookers deserve fear. Chopsticks deserve respect. Different category."

It should have stayed easy. The easy part almost made it harder.

Ada filled a pot, added ginger slices, scallions, and miso, then slid frozen dumplings into the simmering broth with the same careful hand she used on varnish swabs, thumb steady, wrist loose. Marisol watched her from the narrow strip of counter where the cutting board fit and tried to listen to the domestic sounds like proof that life could still be ordinary: knife on wood, radiator hiss, a muted Korean drama through the wall next door, somebody upstairs dropping what sounded like a bowling ball and then apologizing in Spanish.

"I had to break up a fight today," Ada said.

Marisol looked up. "At work?"

"At the bodega. Two grown men screaming about whether an avocado could be trusted. Not price. Trustworthiness. One of them held it like evidence in court."

Marisol barked a laugh into her sleeve. "What was your ruling, Dr. Park?"

"I told them both to squeeze less and buy more. They ignored me because I was carrying tofu and apparently that disqualifies you from fruit law."

"You'd look good on a bench. Tiny gavel. Terrifying eye contact."

Ada nudged a bowl toward her. "Eat before the noodles die."

They sat at the small table pushed against the window. Outside, the laundromat sign across the street blinked `OPEN 24 HRS` with one dead letter, so it read `OPE 24 HRS`, and Ada claimed it looked like the city had become a minimalist poet against its will. Marisol nearly snorted broth through her nose when Ada performed a solemn reading of the sign in an avant-garde voice and then bowed to nobody.

It was humor unrelated to anything, with no strategy and no extraction. Marisol could feel how rare that had become by how quickly her shoulders dropped.

Halfway through the bowl Ada set her spoon down and wiped her fingers on a napkin. The shift in her face was small, almost polite, which meant she had rehearsed this part.

"I need us to say a few things out loud," Ada said. "Now, not after dessert, because we don't have dessert and also because I hate fake casual transitions."

Marisol felt the old warning flare along her ribs. "Okay."

"First boundary. I'm not your back door into conservation files. No screenshots, no metadata questions, no 'just tell me what this code means' at midnight." Ada spoke quietly, eyes on Marisol, no sharpness in it and no wiggle room either. "If it's museum-restricted, it's restricted. If that makes me less useful to you, then we should know that now."

Marisol looked at the table grain and then up again. "You're right."

"Second boundary." Ada folded the napkin in half, then in quarters, precise and almost absent-minded. "Don't show up at my lab. Don't show up here without asking first unless somebody is bleeding. I need one place in my life that is not under donor panic. This apartment is that place."

"I haven't done that."

"You haven't. I'm saying it before we hit the ugly part of stress and start pretending nobody saw the cliff."

Marisol nodded. She could feel heat climbing the back of her neck, part shame and part relief, because the rules were clear and clarity beat guessing.

"Third," Ada said, softer now. "I can't be your only support line when things go bad. I can be one person. I can't be the whole system."

Marisol swallowed broth that had gone lukewarm. "You mean Luis. Meetings."

"Yes. I mean your life bigger than me." Ada tapped her own knuckle scar against the edge of the bowl once, a small metronome click. "And I mean this personally too. If we're in this, I need your words before your performance. If you're wrecked, say wrecked. If you're angry, say angry. Don't give me polished suffering."

That line landed cleanly because it was exactly the trick Marisol used when cornered: a beautiful sentence in place of the fact.

"Okay," she said again, but the word came out thin.

Ada studied her for a beat, then asked, "Did you drink after your mother cornered you outside the meeting hall?"

The room did not move. The radiator clicked. Someone in the building corridor laughed and coughed and kept walking. Marisol's hands went cold all at once.

She had rehearsed six versions of this confession on the train, each one calibrated for maximum honesty and minimum damage. None of them survived contact with Ada's face.

"Yeah," she said. "I did. Vodka. In a bus shelter on Church. I drank enough to black out the corners and then I went home and kept going. I threw up in my sink. I missed a paid shift the next day and lost the workshop slot. I reset at a meeting two nights ago. Day one chip."

Ada did not flinch, did not rush in with comfort. She asked, "What day are you on now?"

"Three."

"Did Luis hear it from you?"

"He did."

"Are you planning to hide this from me after tonight?"

Marisol almost said no too quickly, and stopped herself. "No. I don't want to."

"Those are different sentences." Ada leaned back in her chair and crossed her arms, not closed, just steady. "Pick one you can keep."

Marisol looked at the steam ghosting above the bowls, then back at Ada. "I won't hide it from you."

Ada nodded once. "Good."

There was no speech about bravery and no halo for confession. It was just a condition met, one line item checked.

"I'm not going to play rescuer," Ada said. "I don't have that in me, and even if I did it would still be bullshit. I can do care. I can do honesty. I can do hard stops if you start lying or using me to dodge your program."

"That's fair."

"It's not fair. It's necessary." Ada picked up her spoon, then put it back down without eating. "And I need one more boundary because this part matters to me. If we touch tonight and you leave your body, we pause. No guessing game, no pushing through because you think you owe me. I will ask you direct questions and I expect direct answers."

Marisol's throat tightened. "Okay."

"And if you want to stop, you say stop. If I want to stop, I say stop. No wounded-artist tragic monologue after."

Marisol laughed once in spite of herself. "You really know me."

"Unfortunately," Ada said, dry as chalk, and reached for her bowl.

They finished eating in a quieter rhythm that was neither broken nor repaired. Marisol helped clear plates. Ada let her wash and took over drying, standing close enough that Marisol could feel warmth through two layers of cotton every time they passed a bowl between them. They talked about nothing that mattered to the mystery: the laundromat cat that had started stealing socks, the museum gift shop trying to sell a tote with a typo that read `IMPRESSIONISM IS A MOO` under a Monet cow painting, Ada's failed experiment with instant pot congee that had fused to the pot like masonry.

"I had to soak it overnight," Ada said, affronted. "Then my neighbor texted to ask if I was doing construction."

"You were. Rice demolition."

"I respect that this is bullying, but I also deserve it."

She smiled when she said it, a quick flash that made Marisol feel both wanted and called to account at the same time.

When the kitchen was clean Ada turned off the overhead light, leaving only the lamp near the bed and the city glow through the window. The room narrowed around them. Marisol leaned against the counter and watched Ada cross the apartment to feed a sourdough starter in a glass jar labeled `JUROR 7` with blue painter's tape.

"You named your starter after a person who sends people to prison?" Marisol said.

"After a person who asks one annoying, useful question before making a decision," Ada said. She stirred flour and water into the jar with a chopstick. "I aspire to due process in fermentation."

Marisol laughed again, then felt her chest pull tight so fast she almost winced. She wanted to step forward, to touch Ada's shoulder, to stop standing in the doorway of every room in her own life. She also wanted to run for the train and call it mercy.

Ada set the jar down and faced her. "Where are you right now?"

There it was, that blunt question stress always drew out of Ada.

"Half here," Marisol said. "Half in that bus shelter."

"Do you want to stay here?"

"Yes."

"Do you want me to touch you?"

"Yes." Marisol's voice scraped on the word. "But slow."

Ada crossed the room and stopped within reach without closing the distance all the way. "Show me slow."

Marisol put one hand on Ada's wrist first, then on her waist, waiting for a flinch that never came. Ada's skin was warm from dishwater. Her hands smelled faintly of soap and solvent, the museum living in her pores no matter how many times she scrubbed. Marisol kissed her once, careful, and Ada answered with her palm at the back of Marisol's neck, not pulling, just present.

They moved to the bed because there was nowhere else to go. The springs complained when they sat. Marisol's coat and sweater landed on the floor in a heap next to Ada's single polished toe, dark green and imperfect. Ada touched the burn scar on Marisol's left wrist with two fingers and waited. Marisol nodded. Ada kissed the scar, then the inside of her elbow, then her mouth again with more pressure.

For a while Marisol stayed in it: the friction of denim against her thighs, the cool air on her stomach when her shirt lifted, the rasp of Ada's breath when Marisol bit her lower lip too hard and Ada made a noise that was half laugh, half warning. Marisol slid her hand under Ada's T-shirt and felt ribs, muscle, the shallow dip above her hipbone. Ada shivered and said, "Yeah," like permission and surprise at once.

Marisol wanted to be good at this in a way that had nothing to do with sex and everything to do with deserving. She caught herself performing even now, trying to calibrate touch for effect, trying to earn safety through technique. The thought turned sour and familiar.

Ada pulled back enough to look at her. "You're gone again."

Marisol blinked. The ceiling came into focus: one water stain shaped like a coast she could not name. Her jaw ached from clenching.

"I know," she said.

Ada rolled away a few inches but kept one hand on Marisol's forearm. "Do you want to stop?"

"No."

"Do you want to keep kissing and stay above the waist?"

Marisol shook her head.

"Do you want to keep going the way we were?"

Marisol took a breath that hurt on the way in. "I want to keep going, but I need to not feel like I'm being graded."

Ada's mouth twitched. "By me?"

"By me."

"Okay." Ada tucked a strand of hair behind Marisol's ear with unnecessary gentleness. "Then no grading. Tell me one thing you want right now. Specific."

Marisol closed her eyes, opened them. "Your hand on my throat. Not tight. Just there."

Ada's eyes searched hers. "Like this?"

Her palm settled under Marisol's jaw, thumb resting by her pulse, pressure light and steady. Marisol let out a sound she had been holding since she stepped off the train. Her body came back in pieces: skin first, then breath, then hunger without apology.

"More?" Ada asked.

"A little."

Ada adjusted and kissed her again. This time Marisol stayed. She pulled Ada's shirt over her head and ran her mouth along the scar on Ada's right knuckle when Ada braced herself on the mattress, a small pale line that caught the lamplight. Ada hissed, then laughed into Marisol's shoulder.

"That is absolutely unfair," Ada said.

"You can file a complaint with the avocado court."

Ada kissed her hard enough to shut her up, and Marisol thanked God and chemistry and dumb luck for humor surviving in this room.

They undressed each other with occasional clumsy elbows and one near-disaster when Marisol's foot caught the fitted sheet and nearly launched them both off the bed. Ada snorted into Marisol's neck and said, "Romance," then guided her back with a hand flat between her shoulder blades. Marisol felt the weight of that hand and trusted it.

When Ada slid down the bed and kissed the inside of Marisol's thigh, Marisol's first instinct was to apologize for every inch of herself: the old razor nick near her knee, the bruise blooming yellow where she had knocked into a copier tray, the ghosted bloat still lingering under her navel from relapse dehydration. Instead she grabbed the sheet and said, voice shaking, "Yeah. Stay there."

Ada looked up once for confirmation, got it, and kept going, unhurried, attentive, ruthless in the best way. Marisol's fingers tangled in Ada's hair. She did not go elegant. She went loud enough that Ada reached up and pressed two fingers to Marisol's mouth, smiling without breaking rhythm.

"Thin walls," Ada murmured.

Marisol bit those fingers lightly and laughed against them, then lost the laugh to a moan she could not contain. The room narrowed to touch and sound and the smell of miso still hanging in the air.

Afterward Ada climbed back up, breathless, cheeks flushed, and lay half on top of Marisol with one knee between hers. Marisol rolled them and kissed Ada until Ada's control frayed, until Ada grabbed Marisol's wrist and whispered, "Ask me," through clenched teeth.

"Can I touch you here?" Marisol asked, hand hovering at Ada's hip.

"Yes."

"Can I keep going?"

"Yes, keep going."

The words were simple and exact and hotter than anything performative. Marisol moved the way Ada asked, listening. Ada's body answered in shifts: breath stuttering, shoulders going rigid then loose, a short curse in Korean that Marisol did not understand and did not need to. When Ada came, she covered her own mouth with the heel of her hand and laughed helplessly into the pillow after, hair stuck to her face, looking younger and more wrecked than Marisol had ever seen her.

They lay still for a long minute, both of them sweating despite the winter draft. A siren passed somewhere three blocks away, then faded. The radiator knocked again like a stubborn tenant.

Ada was the first to move. She reached for the water bottle on the floor, took a drink, handed it to Marisol. "You okay?"

"Yeah." Marisol took water and tasted metal from the cap and salt from her lip. "I'm okay, actually."

Ada raised an eyebrow. "Look at you, using contractions after all."

Marisol laughed weakly. "I deserved that."

They sat with their backs against the wall, sheet around their waists, knees touching. The lamp cast a yellow wedge across Ada's thigh and the edge of the folded screen. Marisol could feel the old instinct to seal the night in pure feeling, to call this healing and stop there. Ada had already taken that escape route away from her, and Marisol was grateful.

"I need to say one more thing," Marisol said.

Ada nodded but did not rescue her with an easy expression.

"When I drank, I wasn't trying to have one bad night and then bounce back pretty. I wanted annihilation for about forty-five minutes. I wanted to not be a person with a mother and a body and rent due. I bought the bottle because I knew exactly how fast it would shut things off." She rubbed the heel of her hand over her sternum. "I'm saying this because if I pretend it was a slip, I'll lie again."

Ada listened with her chin tucked, eyes narrowed in focus. "Thank you for not making it pretty," she said. "And I need to match that." She pulled the sheet higher over her chest, then met Marisol's gaze. "I don't have capacity for chaos right now. Work is already a pressure cooker and Nora is three emails away from combusting. If this turns into missing nights, mystery texts at 2 a.m., or me guessing whether you're alive, I'm out. Not as punishment. As self-preservation."

Marisol took that in without arguing. "That's fair."

"Necessary," Ada corrected, gentler this time.

"Necessary."

"Here's what I can do." Ada ticked points off on her fingers. "I can have dinner with you when we plan it. I can answer honest texts. I can listen when you say you're craving. I can ask questions when you get shiny and evasive. I can sleep with you because I want to, not because I think it'll keep you sober."

Marisol felt heat rise again at the bluntness, but she nodded.

"And I can't be your workaround for files," Ada said. "I can't be your crisis hotline every night. I can't pretend this is low stakes because it's romantic."

"I hear you."

Ada angled her body closer until their knees locked. "Tell me your side. Your terms."

No one had asked Marisol that in years without hidden trapdoors.

She took her time. "I can tell you the truth even when it's ugly. I can call Luis before I call you if I'm at the edge. I can keep meetings daily this week. I can stay out of your lab unless invited. And if I start treating you like access instead of a person, I want you to call it immediately. Don't wait to be polite."

Ada's mouth softened at one corner. "I was never going to wait to be polite."

"I know. That's one of your charms."

"Top three, easy."

They both smiled, small and tired. The smile felt earned, not decorative.

Ada leaned over and kissed Marisol once, light. "I also have a petty boundary."

"Oh no."

"If you ever bring those chili-lime tortilla chips into this apartment again, we're done."

Marisol stared. "That's your hard line?"

"They smell like citrus cleaner and disappointment." Ada shuddered theatrically. "I won't live like that."

Marisol laughed so hard she folded forward and the sheet fell off her shoulder. She had not expected laughter after confession and sex and terms and risk, but there it was, loud and unpretty and real. Ada laughed too, then grabbed the sheet and wrapped it around both of them like a truce flag.

Later, after another glass of water and a quick shower where they took turns under the weak pressure and argued about whether body wash could legally call itself "ocean breeze" in a city that smelled mostly like diesel and wet stone, Ada sat at the edge of the bed rubbing lotion into her solvent-dry hands. She did each finger slowly, methodically, as if counting bones. Marisol watched and thought about care as labor, not aura.

"What meeting tomorrow?" Ada asked.

"Seven a.m. basement on Lincoln."

"Text me when you're out. Not for permission. Just so I know the day started."

"I will."

Ada clicked off the lamp and settled under the blanket, back against the wall, knees up, making space for Marisol to curl into her side. In the dark, the city reduced itself to hum and occasional horn. Marisol listened to Ada's breathing settle and let her own follow.

She did not mistake this for redemption, and she did not mistake it for safety either. It was a contract with body heat attached, desire with rules, and trust with the cost printed clearly at the bottom.

Marisol lay awake another ten minutes, maybe fifteen, feeling the blue plastic edge of her day-one chip in the pocket of her jeans on the floor, knowing it would still be there in the morning, small and hard and impossible to poeticize. She reached for her phone on the nightstand, typed one message to Luis before she could talk herself out of it.

`Saw Ada tonight. Told her about the drink. Day 4 tomorrow. Going to Lincoln 7am.`

He answered fast enough that she knew he was on his break route.

`Good. Keep it boring and honest.`

Marisol set the phone face down and slid back under the blanket. Ada, half asleep, reached for her without opening her eyes and found Marisol's wrist, thumb resting on the scar as if taking a pulse. Marisol let that touch anchor her. Outside, a truck downshifted on Fourth Avenue. Somewhere in the building, someone laughed in their sleep.

In the dark she could feel how the night had changed the leverage between them. Ada knew the worst recent fact and stayed. Marisol had what she wanted and no alibi left.

She closed her eyes and listened for morning.
