# Chapter 13

The sublet in Sunset Park heated itself like a grudging old animal. The radiator clanged twice, hissed, went quiet, then clanged again as if it had changed its mind. Marisol sat at the card table that passed for a desk and tucked her feet under her thighs to keep feeling in her toes. Her one suitcase stood open by the wall where she had left it three nights ago, half unpacked, with a sweater sleeve hanging out like a tongue. The landlord had taped a polite reminder to her door that morning, the kind with a smiley face and an amount underlined twice: 2,400.
Three nights earlier she had gotten off the B41 with that same crooked suitcase, paid Mr. Han four hundred from the sidewalk before the lock deadline, and taken the key before he could change his mind.

She moved the notice under her water glass so she could not look at it while she worked. Then she looked at it anyway.

The draft file on her laptop was called "catalogue_essay_v6_FORREAL.docx," because by version four she had started naming files like dares. The first paragraph still punched hard and false.

Salvador Rivas built a cathedral out of stolen grief.

She read it aloud into the room and heard exactly what Luis would hear: theater, heat, no risk. The sentence let her stand outside the blast radius. It turned him into a cartoon priest with blood on his hands and turned her into the clean witness who had arrived just in time with a flashlight. She knew better than that by now. Cathedral went first, then stolen, then the whole first line, and she sat with the white space.

Outside, a B63 bus exhaled at the corner. A delivery guy swore at a frozen lock and kicked it until the metal gave him an answer. The room smelled like stale coffee, damp wool, and the cheap lemon cleaner the super used in hallways when he wanted to seem diligent. She pressed her palm to the burn scar on her left wrist, the old reflex when she needed to keep from pacing, and opened three windows side by side: death certificate, Red Hook shift logs, and the transcript she had made from the roof garden tape before counsel started sending warnings.

She had always pretended she wrote by ear, by accident, by weather. That was the mythology she could sell to students who wanted permission to wait for lightning. The truth sat in front of her now: timestamps, columns, and choices that made enemies.

By late morning she printed the draft at the corner copy shop where she still picked up two shifts a week. She used her employee code and watched thirty-one pages crawl out of the machine, her own words looking more expensive than her bank account. Back upstairs she taped page one to the wall and page two under it. A thick line down each sheet split the columns she labeled "Documented" and "Claimed."

Line by line, she put every sentence on trial.

When she wrote, "My mother weaponized holiness," she wrote a question mark in red and asked herself, Where is the document for intention. There was none. There were only actions and consequences: a forged narrative, twenty-eight years of repetition, a daughter built inside a lie sturdy enough to survive school forms and church anniversaries. She crossed out weaponized and replaced it with repeated.

When she wrote, "Tomas chose heroin over us," she felt her throat close on the cruelty of the sentence and the accuracy of the drug. The certificate did not contain the word chose. It held the colder phrase acute combined fentanyl and heroin toxicity, typed in municipal black ink above a signature block that did not shake. She wrote that phrase into the essay exactly as printed. Then she added the room she did know: motel carpet, fluorescent bathroom light, a man she loved and never met alive, and the witness line where Salvador Rivas had written his full name and home address in careful cursive.

She left the paternity question in brackets for now.

By noon her neck ached from leaning forward and her right thumb was gray with graphite. She shut the laptop, pulled on her coat, and walked to the church basement on Fourth Avenue where the noon meeting met in a room that was always one degree too hot. Her socks were still damp from yesterday's slush. Folding chairs squealed against linoleum. The coffee on the back table smelled burnt enough to count as ritual. Someone had put out off-brand cookies that tasted like powdered drywall.

Luis was already there, heavy coat unzipped, transit uniform layered underneath: navy fleece, reflective stripe, MTA patch rubbed soft at the edges. He moved with the careful hitch in his left leg that got worse in cold weather, and he stirred sugar into his coffee with the rounded end of a plastic knife because he said the thin stirrers snapped like promises. When he saw her, he tapped the chair beside him with two fingers and did not ask how she was. He knew better than that opener when she looked as wired as she felt.

After the meeting, people drifted toward daylight in twos and threes. A man with nicotine fingers kept talking about baseball statistics to nobody in particular. A woman in a chef's coat asked if anyone had a spare MetroCard swipe and got one. Marisol and Luis stayed seated while volunteers stacked chairs around them.

"You brought pages?" he said.

She slid five printed sheets from her tote. "I brought the part that might get me sued."

"Everything gets you sued now. Read me your first paragraph."

She read the current opening, voice low because the room carried sound: "This essay is not a eulogy. It is a record of edits made to survive shame, poverty, and institutions that call myth protection. It concerns one painter, one family, and one dead man whose body was used to purchase dignity after dignity after dignity."

Luis held up a hand. "Stop there. That last part is clean writing, and you know I don't care about clean writing. Whose dignity are you naming?"

"My mother's. Mine. Carina's. Maybe his too."

"Maybe is lazy in a sentence like that. You scared of saying his name?"

"I just said his name in line one."

"No, you said painter. You made him a type. I'm asking if you can write Tomas and leave it there without turning him into your project mascot."

She hated him a little for being right in public fluorescent light. "I can write Tomas."

"Write him like a person then. Not a symbol you inherited, and not a cautionary poster."

She looked at the pages and found the margin note where she had written "DON'T ORNAMENT THE DEAD" in all caps. "I'm trying."

He gave her the look he used when bus drivers blamed traffic for routes they had left late. "Try is not a route. What did you do this morning. Specific."

She answered the way he liked, in sequence. "I cut adjectives that made me sound righteous. I replaced motive claims with actions. I added my relapse as fact, not confession theater."

"Where."

"Page four."

"Read it."

Her mouth went dry. She read anyway. "On November twenty-second, after confronting my mother with freight logs and a death certificate, I bought a half-pint of vodka from a deli on Rogers and drank in a bus shelter while pretending to wait for the B44. I reset my sobriety date the next morning and told my sponsor before noon."

Luis nodded once. "That is closer. Do you see what changed?"

"I put myself in it."

"You put cost in it. Exposure without cost is gossip with better fonts."

She looked up. "You think this is gossip."

"I think you're smart enough to weaponize truth when you're hurt." He leaned back and winced as his knee popped. "I also think you can choose not to. That's why we're still talking."

The volunteers had finished stacking chairs and turned off half the fluorescent panels. The room dropped into a softer buzz.

"Tell me the line you're proudest of," he said.

She pointed. "Here. 'He repainted the witness out of his own canvas and called the surface finished.'"

"Fine line. What is it doing in this paragraph."

"It's the thesis."

"No. It's a punchline." He tapped the paper. "You throw that line, everybody claps for your anger and nobody asks what you owe the people still alive."

She wanted to tell him she owed nothing, that they had spent decades spending her childhood like petty cash. Instead she heard herself say, "What if exposure is the amends."

He took a long breath through his nose. "Sometimes it's. If you stole money and you return the money with interest, that's amends. If you break a thing and tell everybody who broke it, that might be amends too. If you tell the story so you can feel superior, that's performance."

"How do I prove what this is."

"You can't prove motive clean. You can prove method." He folded his hands over the paper, thick fingers scarred at the knuckles from a job he never talked about. "Ask practical questions. Did you verify. Did you include your own damage. Did you cut what you can't support. Did you ask for consent where consent matters. Did you leave room for the dead to stay human."

He pushed the pages back toward her. "I dispatch buses all day. If one stop lies, the whole line bunches and people freeze on corners. You're building a route. Don't fake the stops because you want applause."

She laughed once, harsh and brief. "You always do transit parables when you're mad at me."

"I'm not mad. I'm busy." He stood, favoring his left leg, and shrugged into his coat. "Call me before you send anything. If you dodge that call, I will assume you're drinking or grandstanding."

"I don't love those options."

"Love better options then."

He left her with the burnt coffee smell and the stack of marked pages. She sat another ten minutes, listening to pipes knock inside the church walls, and wrote in the margin of page one: "EXPOSURE IS NOT AMENDS UNLESS YOU PAY."

Back in the sublet she opened a new file named "catalogue_essay_v7_accountability." She kept the old drafts in a folder called "BAD HALOS" so she could not pretend they had never existed. Window light thinned to dishwater gray. Her fingers stiffened in the cold between radiator cycles. She typed in bursts, then stood to tape revised pages over old ones, building a paper sediment on the wall until the original version disappeared under edits.

She cut the self-congratulatory scaffolding first. The sentence about "speaking truth to power" went into the trash. So did "as a daughter of immigrants," which she had been using as shield and credential in the same breath.

Instead of another ethics paragraph, she drafted the parish-hall memory she had kept dodging: Sister Elena pressing a white envelope into Gloria's palm after the martyr speech, the room smelling like pernil and candle wax, Carina staring at her shoes while Marisol said thank you and did not correct a single fact. The envelope paid their gas arrears. By Christmas, the story had hardened into family liturgy.

For the relapse section she forced herself to stay in sequence. The deli clerk unscrewed the half-pint because her hands were shaking too hard for the cap. Vodka hit her cracked lip, sweet then chemical. Fifteen minutes later the warmth flipped to tremor so hard she sloshed liquor down her coat sleeve and missed two calls from Luis she was too ashamed to return.

Under that she named the motive she had kept dressing up: she posted the accusatory poem not only from rage but because she wanted to beat counsel to the story before legal language could lock it down.

As the room tipped toward evening, a subject line from estate counsel flashed across her screen: NOTICE OF PRESERVATION OF CLAIMS. Any use of roof-garden audio beyond already contested excerpts, the email said, would trigger immediate injunctive action. She cursed out loud, opened a new footnote column, and tagged every volatile line to a document number.

The Gloria section fought back. One draft turned her mother into a tyrant with a rosary. Another turned her into a saint of sacrifice. A third balanced both so neatly it sounded like an ethics textbook. She killed all three, opened the legal pad where she had copied Gloria's exact words from the parking lot confession and from the dinner explosion, and underlined the verbs. Not believed, felt, intended. Verbs with weight: forged, told, hid, paid, worked, prayed, lied.

She built the paragraph with those:
My mother did not invent poverty, addiction, or the state's appetite for neat stories. She did choose repetition. She told the martyr version at school assemblies, parish dinners, and family birthdays because each retelling bought us a little social oxygen. The lie became rent currency, scholarship language, and a shield against the shame that follows overdose into every room.

It still sounded like indictment, but not like revenge porn. She kept it.

Her phone rattled across the table. The landlord left a voicemail in English and then Spanish: eight hundred by noon tomorrow or he would start showing the room and change the lock if she was out. Marisol checked her balance, swallowed, and sent three hundred anyway. The confirmation email landed instantly; her chest stayed tight.

She reached four pages she did not hate before she remembered to eat. After microwaved rice and eggs swallowed standing up, the count hit seven and a headache throbbed behind her right eye. She reread the section on Salvador until the words blurred.

That section was the hardest because his facts came wrapped in performance. She had tape evidence of his admissions, but she also had his habits: deflect with aesthetics, flirt with remorse, offer one concrete fact only when cornered. If she wrote him as a monster, she lied. If she wrote him as tragic genius, she lied harder.

She went back to the tape transcript and marked every place where he changed tense. Present tense when he described paint, past tense when he described people. She built a paragraph around that fracture.

Salvador Rivas spoke about pigments as if they were organs and about people as if they were weather systems he endured. He called concealment composition. He called revision mercy. In the same interview where he admitted he signed as witness to Tomas Vega's death, he described his own silence as tenderness. The record shows both claims. This essay refuses to separate them for his comfort or mine.

She left the maybe-father line unclosed but stripped the fog: "He also claimed a biological tie to me that is not yet supported by DNA testing, an amended birth certificate, or any sworn filing; at this stage the claim rests on his interview statement and my mother's partial admission." She stared at partial admission until her jaw clenched and she bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste blood. Then she flagged the sentence for counsel review and kept drafting.

Not long after, her phone buzzed with a number she did not know. The area code was Manhattan. She let it ring twice before answering.

"Marisol Vega?"

The voice belonged to a woman who sounded tired and brisk at once, the tone of someone who had repeated bad news all week and still bothered to pronounce every name correctly.

"Yes."

"My name is Cora Batista. I'm on Salvador Rivas's palliative team tonight. I got your number from his paper folder, the blue one with your name in Sharpie and three exclamation points." She paused, and Marisol heard a monitor chirp in the background, then the soft clatter of pill cups. "He asked this afternoon whether the poet was coming back. I said I didn't know."

Cora spoke fast but not carelessly, with the slight rasp of a smoker trying to quit. Marisol pictured her before she saw her: scrub top under a cardigan, hair pinned up with whatever was handy, one eye on the clock and one on a patient who could turn on a sentence. Later she would learn the cardigan was bright yellow with two loose threads at the cuff, and that Cora kept a mechanical pencil tucked behind her ear because she did Sunday crossword puzzles during quiet stretches to keep herself awake.

"Is he lucid right now?" Marisol asked.

"For pieces. Not for long. I'm calling because those pieces are getting shorter." Cora lowered her voice, and Marisol heard a door close on the other end. "Mornings are your best shot. Between about eight-fifteen and nine, before pain meds stack. After that he drifts, and sometimes he starts arguing with people who are not in the room."

Marisol sat down hard on the chair edge. "How much time are we talking. Days. Weeks."

"I don't predict dates for families, and I don't predict dates for people who are not technically family. I can tell you trend. Trend is down." Cora exhaled. "He sleeps more, eats less, and forgets where his hands are. Last week he could hold a thread for twenty minutes. Tonight he lost one in four."

Marisol pressed her thumb into the burn scar until it hurt. "I need one more interview."

"Then ask for one, in writing, tonight. Keep your questions tight. No autobiographical orbits. He does best with concrete prompts." Cora made a small sound that could have been a laugh. "Think less memoir, more deposition with feelings."

Despite herself, Marisol smiled. "You're good at this."

"I'm good at triage. Different skill." Cora's voice softened for one beat. "Listen, my kid has a Regents math exam tomorrow and I still haven't figured out how to explain matrices, so I'm not in the mood for poetic delay. Send the questions. I'll put them in his morning folder."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. He might wake up mean." She cleared her throat. "But he still asks for you. That means something, even if we can't make it noble."

The line clicked off.

Marisol held the dead phone against her ear another second, listening to the absence. Then she opened her email and started a new message addressed to Cora, cc to the general account the nurse gave her, no museum addresses, no curator language.

Subject: "Request for Final Interview Window - Focused Questions Attached"

She typed the first sentence three times before it sounded like a person instead of an applicant.

Thank you for contacting me. I am requesting one final recorded interview with Salvador Rivas in the next available morning lucid window.

She attached a one-page document and forced herself to keep it to six questions.

1. On what date did you first learn Tomas Vega had died, and who notified you.
2. What factual basis do you claim for saying you are my biological father, and who can corroborate it.
3. Why did your name appear as witness on the municipal death record.
4. Who proposed the martyr narrative first, and what specific facts did each person agree to repeat.
5. What material support, if any, did you provide to Gloria Vega and her daughters in exchange for silence.
6. What did you repaint or remove in works that originally referenced Tomas Vega, and what responsibility do you admit now, in your own words, without metaphor.

She stared at question five. It could break everything open or get the interview canceled before sunrise. She left it in.

Before sending, she added one line beneath the list: "I understand fatigue and confusion may limit answers; I prefer short factual responses over polished statements."

She hit send at 9:12 p.m. and felt the old immediate panic after irreversible action. It felt too much like the night of the poem post, the same adrenaline spike, the same urge to check her phone every ten seconds. She put the phone in the sink and turned the faucet on cold for a count of thirty, forcing her hands under the water until the skin went numb.

Then she went back to the essay because dread without labor always turned theatrical in her body.

Before she opened the ending, Luis texted: "You send yet?"
"Sent request. 8:20 a.m. if stable," she wrote back. "I'm sober."
His reply came ten seconds later. "Call me after pages. If you spin tonight, you don't go in there alone."
She set an early alarm labeled CALL LUIS and turned the phone facedown.

The final section had to declare form and motive. She had drafted twelve endings already: one triumphant, one sorrowful, one clinical, one that tried to sound like Baldwin and one that accidentally sounded like a grant report. She opened a blank page and wrote in plain English first: "This is not an attempt to punish one man or absolve one mother. It is an attempt to stop lying in inherited patterns." She replaced punish with expose, then changed it back to punish because that was the truer word for what she had wanted at noon.

She rewrote the relapse paragraph with no rhetorical framing: "I drank on November twenty-second because I wanted speed and anesthesia more than I wanted honesty. The first swallow tasted like mouthwash and pennies. By minute fifteen my hands stopped shaking; by minute twenty they shook harder and I dropped my MetroCard into the gutter. I missed Luis's calls and called that silence privacy until morning proved it was lying." She read the lines aloud and waited for the internal objection that this was self-indulgence. The objection did not come. What came was a quieter fear: that readers would use her relapse as a discount code against every other claim. She wrote that fear into the draft too, one sentence, then cut it. The chapter did not need anticipatory defense.

A little after ten, Cora replied from her phone.

"Received. 8:20am tomorrow if stable. Bring ID. One recorder only. If he tires, we stop."

No greeting, no signature flourish, only terms. Marisol appreciated the clarity so much it made her throat sting.

She set three alarms and kept writing past midnight, shaping transitions, trimming verdict words, checking every assertion against paper evidence. Closer to one, she printed the full draft, fourteen pages now, and spread them across the floor. She crawled from page to page with a pencil, circling any sentence that told the reader what to feel instead of showing the cost. She crossed out nine circles.

On the final pass she hunted soft verbs: was betrayed became learned; was destroyed split into paid and hid and drank and posted; villain narrowed to witness twice removed. She left one sentence ugly on purpose because the polished version lied: "I wanted him to suffer before he died."

That sentence terrified her. It also made the rest of the chapter honest enough to stand.

Near two she saved the document as "catalogue_essay_v8_SUBMIT_after_interview.docx" and exported a PDF copy to guard against panic edits in the morning. She slid the printed stack into a manila envelope with the death certificate copy, the shift log excerpts, and the blue day-one chip she carried like a small hard planet in her pocket.

She did not pray. She sat on the edge of the bed with her coat still on and listened to the radiator knock the same uneven rhythm as before, like someone asking to be let in and then changing their mind.

Tomorrow had an hourly map: Cora's 8:20 window if Salvador stayed lucid, then Church Avenue by ten if she wanted Carina to open the door. Tonight she had a draft that no longer pretended innocence.
