# Chapter 15

Three mornings later, Marisol got off the B41 with the cuffs of her jeans wet and black from slush, and the paper folder under her coat already curling at the corners from her body heat. She had printed the release packet at the copy shop in three sets, ten-point font, legal margins, every clause she had fought over with a borrowed template and two nights of insomnia. The top page still smelled faintly of toner and the hand lotion she kept behind the register to stop her fingers from cracking. On the back of the folder she had written, in thick blue marker, the only instruction that mattered: GET THE SIGNATURE BEFORE MORPHINE.

She stood in front of Methodist's palliative entrance and pressed two fingers to the black glass. Cold needled through her skin. Her reflection floated there: a runner-thin face, hair braided badly because she had done it in the dark at six, and a burn scar near her left wrist half-hidden by the cuff of her sweater. She took out her phone. A text from her landlord sat on top of everything else, timestamped 7:12 a.m.: `Need at least $600 today or I post notice.` Under that, a group thread from Carina with Leo's latest pigeon drawing and no direct message to her. Under that, nothing from Ada.

Inside, the air changed from wet December metal to over-clean citrus and old fear. At the desk, a volunteer handed her a flimsy sticker with VISITOR in red block letters and told her to keep it visible. The volunteer had gray braids wrapped in a Yankees scarf and a voice like she sang in church, and she called everyone baby in exactly the same tone, even the man cursing at the vending machine down the hall. Marisol peeled the backing and pressed the sticker onto her coat pocket, careful not to crumple the folder.

Room 614 had Salvador's name on a printed strip half peeled from the plastic holder. The last two letters hung loose and breathed every time the heat kicked on. Through the glass panel she could see him turned toward the window, bed raised, the oxygen cannula slack against his upper lip like a misplaced thread. His skin had the papery yellow she still could not get used to, as if somebody had glazed him and forgotten to fire him. The right hand with the tremor rested on top of the blanket, fingers curved around nothing.

When she opened the door, a nurse in navy scrubs was adjusting the pump. Her badge said MINA ORTIZ in peeling white letters, and she had tiny planets painted on her thumbnail and ring finger, not careful little stars but full planets with crooked rings and visible brushstrokes. Mina looked up, recognized Marisol from the previous visits, and gave her a quick nod.

"You got him at a good window," Mina said quietly. "He's been awake since five because he says the birds in this city start fighting before sunrise." She tapped the folder's corner with one planet-painted nail. "You still doing papers."

"Yeah," Marisol said. "I need ten minutes where he's clear. Maybe fifteen if he gets stubborn."

"He gets stubborn every day ending in y." Mina checked the infusion line, then tucked a blanket edge under Salvador's elbow with a practiced sweep. "I can hold the eight-thirty dose for a little. Not long. He hurts when I do."

"I know." Marisol swallowed. "Thank you."

Mina gave a shrug that was almost embarrassed. "My brother kept saying tomorrow right up till there wasn't one. I hate tomorrow." She touched Salvador's chart once, then left, letting the door click soft behind her.

Marisol pulled a chair close enough to smell him. It was not decay, not exactly. Hospitals did their best to erase rot with detergent and bleach and dry air, but there was still a sweet-sour edge under it that made her think of pears left too long in a bowl. Salvador opened his eyes without turning his head.

"You walk like your mother when you're angry," he said. His voice was dry paper. "Heel first. Like you want the floor to confess."

"Good morning to you too."

"It isn't morning in here. They took that from me days ago." He blinked, chin tipping toward the folder. "That's the execution order."

"Release authorization. Interview audio. Notebook quotations. Derivative rights for publication and performance." She sat, set the folder on her knees, and tried to keep her hands still. "You asked me to finish the catalogue essay. This is how I can finish it without getting buried in court before page one."

He made a small sound that might have been a laugh. "Court was always waiting at the bottom of the stairs. I just had better shoes."

"Salvador."

He turned his head then, slow, eyes watery but focused. In this light he looked both enormous and already gone. "Read me the ugly parts. Not the polite parts."

She opened to the marked pages. "Clause six. Estate retains ownership of originals, but grants me nonexclusive license to quote recordings and notebooks in the manuscript currently titled *Ash Under Gold Leaf* and related readings. Clause seven. I indemnify the estate against claims arising from false statements that are not supported by documentary evidence."

"There it is," he said. "False statements. Their favorite paint thinner."

"I didn't write that one. They did."

"Then read clause eight."

She turned a page. "Clause eight says no excerpt can be used in a context that implies criminal conduct by identifiable living persons unless independently corroborated by public records or technical documentation."

"Identifiable."

"Yeah."

"Meaning your mother. Your sister. Nora. Half the board." He shut his eyes for a second, then opened them again. "They call it caution so they can call truth defamation."

"Then sign anyway." She heard her voice harden and did not pull it back. "Sign, and I can fight from inside the language they gave me. If you don't sign, I get nothing from you that I can print."

"You have your death certificate." His lip twitched. "Not mine. Tomas's."

"That's not enough for what this is."

"It is enough if you stop asking permission."

"I got suspended for stopping permission. My stipend got frozen. My badge doesn't open a bathroom now. I'm behind on rent and I'm one missed shift from losing the room I already can't afford. I don't need advice about heroic disobedience from a man who died famous. I need your signature."

The words landed in the room like glass. She heard the edge in them as soon as they left her mouth and hated it, but she did not take them back. Salvador stared at her, then closed his eyes again and let out a breath that rattled.

"Good," he said. "You finally sound like the person writing about me." He licked his lips. "Pen."

She grabbed one from her tote, a cheap black roller with COPY LAND stamped in blue on the barrel. His hand shook when he reached for it, so she held the folder against a hardcover book to make a flat surface and steadied the paper at the margin with two fingers. He signed the first line with patient violence, each letter dragged like he was carving into wet plaster. S-a-l-v-a-d-o-r came out legible enough. On the second page he made it through Rivas and stopped, breathing through his mouth.

"You can rest for a minute," she said.

"No." He tried to lift the pen again. It tapped the page twice, leaving two black dots that looked like fly shit. "Where's the witness line?"

"Last page."

"Always the last page."

His right shoulder jerked once. The pen fell from his fingers and rolled onto the blanket. For half a second Marisol thought he was joking, staging some little theater of fragility to control tempo again. Then his chest pulled in too hard and stayed there. His jaw worked, and a thin string of spit slid to the corner of his mouth. The monitor began to alarm in quick high beeps.

"Salvador?" She stood so fast the chair legs screamed against linoleum. "Hey. Hey, look at me."

His eyes were open but unfocused, fixed above her shoulder. His hand clawed at the blanket, knuckles white under jaundice. She hit the call button with the side of her fist and shouted for help. Mina was in the room before the second alarm cycle, then another nurse, then a resident with an exhausted ponytail and a stethoscope that kept slipping off her neck. Someone lowered the bed. Someone turned him. Someone said DNR status, comfort measures, call respiratory anyway.

Marisol got pushed toward the wall without anyone touching her. She watched the room turn into choreography she could not interrupt. Mina leaned close to Salvador's ear and said his name three times like she was trying to pull him back by sound alone. The resident drew up medication with a hand so steady it made Marisol furious. Salvador's breath came in rough wet pulls, each one farther apart than the one before. His face, already yellow, went waxen at the mouth. Marisol thought of pigment names because she could not think of death directly: Naples yellow, old ivory, a thin wash of gray over everything.

He stopped moving his hand first. Then the jaw unclenched. Then the next breath did not come.

The alarm changed tone, lower and continuous. Mina checked the monitor, got the resident's nod, then called the time from the clock above the sink. Her voice was formal when she said it. Formal and kind at once, which was a thing Marisol had not known was possible until then.

No one looked at Marisol for several seconds. The resident wrote something in the chart. The second nurse pulled the sheet up to Salvador's chest and smoothed it twice even though it was already smooth. Mina turned off the alarm and adjusted the cannula out of habit, then stopped with it in her hand and let it fall.

"I'm sorry," Mina said.

Marisol did not answer. She stepped to the bedside and put her fingers on Salvador's wrist, as if a pulse might still be hiding somewhere she had not checked. His skin was already cooling at the knuckles while his forearm stayed warm, heat leaving in layers she could not negotiate with. She bent until her forehead touched the blanket over his hip, wool scraping her skin, and breathed in detergent, sweat, morphine, and the sour-metal smell of the room.

When she straightened, her vision narrowed to a bright tunnel. She grabbed the sink rail, hard enough that her nails bent backward, and gagged once without vomiting. A wall sanitizer dispenser sat inches from her face. She hit it by reflex, and the ethanol stink bloomed sharp and sweet. Her mouth flooded with the memory of freezer-burned vodka, plastic cap digging her palm, throat already opening. For one humiliating second she almost brought her slick hand to her lips.

She wiped her palm on her jeans instead, fast, like she was erasing evidence. Paper whispered under the vent, and she turned to the folder still open on the bed tray. There were two complete signatures and one partial signature, while the witness line was empty, the notarization block was empty, and the final page was untouched.

"He was signing," she said. Her voice came out thin. "He was in the middle of it."

Mina followed her gaze. She pressed her lips together. "I can't witness something that wasn't finished before death. If legal needs chart timing, they'll get chart timing."

"Can you write that he intended to sign?"

"I can write what I observed." Mina met her eyes and did not flinch. "What I observed is that he started and then he died."

Marisol nodded once, because if she did not nod she would scream. Mina put a hand on her shoulder for a brief moment, squeezed through wool and bone, then left her with the body.

Marisol stood there until the room cooled around her. She picked up the pen from the blanket and wiped it clean with a tissue, not because it mattered but because she could not bear leaving his spit on the barrel. Then she gathered the papers, stacked them square, and slid them back into the folder with hands that were now shaking hard enough to rattle the clip.

Her phone buzzed as she stepped into the corridor. Unknown number. She answered.

"Ms. Vega, this is Malcolm Price for the Rivas estate." The voice was male, clipped, expensive, the kind that had practiced not needing volume. "I understand Mr. Rivas passed at approximately 8:47. I'm on-site. Could we speak in consult room B in ten minutes?"

She almost hung up. Instead she said, "You were waiting?"

"I was nearby anticipating execution of documents. Given events, we need immediate clarity."

"Yeah," she said. "Clarity."

Consult room B was too warm and smelled of stale coffee from a machine down the hall. Malcolm Price stood when she entered. He was in his early fifties, trim suit, dark tie with a tiny repeating pattern of rowing oars, and he had a permanent pale groove where a wedding ring used to be. He touched that groove while he introduced himself, thumb circling skin that was already smooth. On the table in front of him sat a leather portfolio and a paper cup of tea he had not touched.

Nora sat at the far end with her glasses off, pinching the bridge of her nose where eye strain gave her headaches. She had kicked off her heeled boots and switched into foldable black flats, the same emergency pair Marisol had seen peeking out of her tote at board meetings. She looked older than forty-five that morning, not softer, just worn thin.

"I'm not here for condolences," Marisol said, staying standing. "Let's skip that part."

Malcolm gave a small nod. "Understood."

Nora said, "Sit, Marisol. You're shaking." Her voice had the institutional polish stripped out of it. "Please."

Marisol sat because her knees had started to feel unreliable.

Malcolm opened his portfolio and slid a copy of the release form between them. A yellow tab marked the signature page. "I reviewed what nursing scanned to administration. Two pages are signed. The execution page is not. Witness and notary blocks are blank. Under New York estates law and this contract's integration clause, the instrument does not execute."

"He signed," Marisol said. "You can see his name."

"Partial signature does not vest rights."

"He died with the pen in his hand."

"I'm not disputing sequence. I'm stating legal effect."

Instrument. Execute. The words scraped the back of her teeth; he'd already translated Salvador's last breath into filing language.

Nora leaned forward. "Malcolm, give me a narrow lane. One excerpt window pending probate."

He kept his eyes on the table. "No interim lane on this fact pattern."

"Fact pattern?" Marisol repeated. "He was choking and still trying to write."

"I mean unverified allegations about living individuals and institutions." Malcolm folded his hands. "Litigation cost is coercive even when facts are arguable. The estate will not expose itself, the museum, or third parties to foreseeable claim."

Her palms slicked against the vinyl chair. Under his voice she could still hear the flatline tone, low and continuous.

"Third parties?" Marisol laughed once, raw and joyless. "My mother's name is in the underlayer. The death certificate has his witness signature. I'm not pitching rumors off Reddit."

"Mr. Rivas told many versions. Dying declarations are not magic."

"This isn't a murder trial. It's a book, and it's my life."

"Exactly." Malcolm tapped the table lightly with two fingers, as if keeping time to music only he could hear. "Books multiply publication risk by jurisdiction. You publish paternity without DNA, allege coordinated deceit for financial benefit, or imply institutional fraud, we seek injunction. If injunction fails, we pursue damages."

Nora's eyes stayed on the table. "Marisol, you still have a public-record spine. Municipal documents, direct observation, independently sourced facts."

Heat pooled under Marisol's coat, sweat threading down her spine. If she lost her temper here, she'd lose the room and the record with it.

"Without the interview tape?"

"Without estate-owned recordings or notebook excerpts," Malcolm said. "Yes."

"And the notebooks?"

"Restricted assets pending inventory and probate." He took a breath, then finally let a trace of weariness into his face. "Ms. Vega, nobody is forbidding writing. We are forbidding use of material you do not have rights to use."

"You're forbidding me from writing the part that matters."

"I'm preventing a defamation case that will consume you and everyone attached to you."

"You don't give a shit about me."

"I give a great deal of attention to foreseeable harm." He slid a one-page notice across the table. "This preserves all rights and requests immediate confirmation that no excerpts from recordings or notebooks will be disseminated."

Marisol did not touch the paper.

Nora said softly, "Take counsel before you respond."

"With what money?" Marisol snapped, then looked away. The room hummed with forced air and fluorescent buzz. She could feel sweat between her shoulder blades despite the cold outside. "My stipend is frozen because your board panicked about a poem. My rent arrears are two-four. My card is at six-eight. Don't tell me to hire counsel like you're recommending a brunch place."

Nora flinched but did not argue.

Malcolm gathered his portfolio with careful hands. "I'm sorry for the timing."

"No, you're not," Marisol said. "You're efficient. That's different."

He stood. "Efficiency is how I keep families from being eaten alive in public." He let the silence sit for a second longer, ring groove flashing pale as he reached for his coat. "I know that sounds obscene right now."

"It does," Marisol said.

After they left, Marisol stayed in the consult room with the stale coffee smell and the legal notice she still had not touched. She took her blue day-one chip out of her pocket, rolled it between her fingers, and watched her hand shake anyway. She wanted a drink with a level of specificity that frightened her: not whiskey, not wine, just cheap vodka from the corner store on Flatbush where the guy with the lazy eye did not ask for ID if you were buying only one bottle and looked tired enough. Her mouth flooded. The first swallow rose in memory so hard she could taste plastic cap and freezer burn.

She put the chip between her teeth until the metal edge hurt her gums. Then she took out her phone and opened Luis's number. She did not call. She typed: `He died before signing. Estate blocked tape. I want to drink.` She hit send before she could edit it into something noble.

By the time she walked out of the hospital, sleet had started. Not snow yet, just hard wet pellets that hit her cheeks like thrown sand. She zipped her coat to the chin and headed toward the bus stop, folder under her arm, legal notice folded into quarters in her back pocket like an unpaid bill.

Halfway to Flatbush, her phone buzzed with a message from an unknown ProtonMail address.

`Need to talk. Not phone. 5:30, branch library by Grand Army, basement stacks. - A`

She read it twice, then a third time, and felt her pulse change.

***

The basement stacks at the library smelled like dust, damp carpet, and radiator heat that never reached the floor. A security guard watched videos on his phone behind the desk and barely looked up when she came in. At a back table near the city-planning section, Ada sat with a canvas tote at her feet and a paper cup of barley tea gone cold. She had tied her hair up with a yellow pencil, and the skin on her hands was cracked at the knuckles despite thick ointment, pale lines across brown skin like unfinished seams. A roll of white finger tape stuck out of her coat pocket.

When Marisol sat, Ada did not hug her. She did not offer condolences. She slid a packet of gum across the table.

"You smell like hospital," Ada said. "Chew one."

Marisol took a piece, unwrapped it with fingers that still would not stop shaking, and put it in her mouth. Peppermint hit hard enough to make her eyes water.

"He died at eight forty-seven," she said.

Ada nodded once. "I heard at noon. Conservation got a lockdown email at twelve nineteen. Everything tied to Rivas moved to legal hold. Server permissions changed. Physical storage locked."

"They blocked the tape." Marisol leaned forward, voice low. "Estate counsel said truth is defamation risk if it has names in it."

The sanitizer reek from upstairs still sat in her throat, sweet and chemical.

"That's not wrong, legally." Ada's tone was flat, technical. "It's also not neutral."

Marisol pressed her tongue against her back molars until the ache steadied her. "Why this cloak-and-dagger email?"

Ada let out a breath. "Because my work account is monitored right now and my phone is on museum device management. Because if I say one dumb thing on a recorded line, they can call it intent and terminate for cause. Because I still need to pay rent next month." She rubbed the cracked knuckle of her right hand with her thumb, then stopped herself. "And because legal forced a decision at two-oh-seven."

Marisol went still.

"What decision?"

Ada pulled a printed email from her tote and flattened it between them before she touched the manila folder. The subject line read `LEGAL HOLD ACTION REQUIRED - RIVAS MATERIALS`. Midway down, one sentence was bolded: `Re-index all Rivas conservation files as undated study references and remove donor-family cross-links by 16:00.`

"General counsel sent that," Ada said. "Then they put us on a call and watched my cursor while they told me to click acknowledgment."

Marisol scanned the page. "Did you click it?"

"No." Ada held her gaze. "I exported the live index with timestamps, pulled checksums on every flagged file, wrote refusal in the audit log, and walked out. Then I built this." She set the printout aside and pulled a thin manila folder from her tote, keeping one hand on top of it. "Your signature route died upstairs. These technical files are museum custody, not estate custody. Different leverage."

"You told me in October you couldn't share raws."

"I couldn't then. I can now if I eat the consequences." Ada met her eyes. "I'm not giving you cute screenshots and pretending we slipped. I'm giving you a chain-of-custody packet that can stand up in a fight."

Marisol's heartbeat thudded against the table edge. "What packet?"

"Multispectral composites of *Witness at the River*. Full-resolution IR reflectography TIFFs from the October session. Cross-section notes from varnish removal." Ada tapped the folder. "And two pieces legal buried before I could show you. Underlayer inscription revealed by channel isolation: `G.V., Dec 1991, cinco meses.` Plus a 1992 intake note where Rivas asked for overpaint on the male figure and removal of a dedication line with your mother's first name."

Marisol stared at her.

"That doesn't prove paternity," she said.

"No. It corroborates timeline and relationship proximity before your birth. It kills deniability that they were strangers." Ada leaned back, jaw tight. "It's evidence, not closure."

"If you hand me that, they torch your badge."

"Probably." Ada's mouth twisted. "Or they suspend first so they can sound merciful in HR language."

"Why do it?"

Ada rubbed the tape seam on her knuckle before she answered. "Because I spend my days lifting nicotine varnish off other people's lies. I can tell myself I'm neutral while I do it. I can say process over story. That email made it plain what process meant this time." She gave a tiny humorless laugh. "I like my job. I like my mortgage autopay. I also like sleeping without feeling like I swallowed bleach."

Marisol swallowed hard. "I don't want to be the reason you lose your life."

"You're not that important," Ada said, and for a second there was the dry private wit Marisol had missed. Then Ada's face went serious again. "Listen to me. If I do this, you don't turn me into a saint in your pages. You don't write me as collateral damage that proves your purity. You use the files exactly, with context, and you carry the blowback with your name on it."

"I can do that."

"Can you?" Ada's voice dropped lower. "You were very good at making yourself the tragic center when things got bad. You were also drunk in that bus shelter after your mother's confession, and you still haven't told half the people you owe. I'm helping evidence, not your myth."

The words hit clean and left no room to hide. Marisol nodded slowly.

"You're right," she said. "I can still slip into performance. I know that." She placed her palm flat on the table, as if taking an oath in a cheap courtroom. "I won't make this about my redemption."

Ada studied her for a long beat, then pushed the folder halfway across.

"Good. Because this next part is deliberate."

Inside were printouts of file hashes, accession numbers, and a draft email already composed on museum letterhead. Subject line: `Evidence Preservation Copy - Witness at the River Conservation Records`. Recipients: Marisol's personal email, Ada's personal email, and a legal-discovery address that included estate counsel and museum general counsel. CC: Nora Feldman.

Marisol felt her stomach drop anyway. "You're copying them?"

"Yes." Ada's voice was precise now, almost classroom. "If I send this in secret, they can call it tampering. If I send it in the open with checksums and timestamps, they can still discipline me but they can't argue fabrication. This is traceable by design."

"They'll know it was you in three seconds."

"Correct." Ada zipped her tote closed. "We're not doing a thriller. We're doing a record."

Marisol barked a short laugh that broke in the middle and turned into something close to a sob. She covered her mouth with her fist and breathed until it passed.

"When?" she asked.

"Now. Public library Wi-Fi is trash but stable enough for a small file package. Full-res set uploads from my home connection tonight." Ada pulled a laptop from her bag, opened it, and typed in a long password without looking at the keys. Her fingers moved quick despite the taped knuckle.

She attached three compressed files first: IR composites, intake memo scans, chain-of-custody report. Then she pasted the hashes into the body and added one sentence above them: `Sending this preservation copy in the interest of evidentiary integrity after legal hold notification.`

"That's too clean," Marisol said quietly.

"It needs to be clean." Ada kept one palm flat on the table. "Messy gets eaten."

She hit send.

The progress bar crawled. Twelve percent. Twenty-eight. Forty-nine. Marisol watched as if the number itself were a pulse on a monitor.

At one hundred, the screen flashed SENT 5:58 PM.

For two seconds nothing happened.

Then Ada's phone lit with a calendar alert she had not made. Another ping, this one email. She opened it. Her face did not change, but she exhaled through her nose in a way Marisol recognized as impact.

"What?"

Ada rotated the phone across the table so she could read.

`Dr. Park, effective immediately you are placed on paid administrative leave pending investigation into unauthorized dissemination of restricted conservation records. Building and systems access suspended at 18:00. Please arrange return of badge and museum-issued equipment by 10:00 tomorrow. - HR Compliance`

Marisol read the message twice and felt her skin go cold from the inside.

"I'm sorry," she said. "Fuck. Ada, I'm sorry."

Ada locked the screen and put the phone face down. "Don't apologize like a tourist. We both knew the toll before the bridge." She reached into her tote, took out her museum badge on a retractable clip, and set it on the table between them. "I hate that this little plastic rectangle can decide whether I touch paintings tomorrow." Her voice thinned for the first time. "I really hate that."

Marisol ran her thumb once along the badge photo. Ada wore a blue lab coat in it, with a neutral expression, sharp eyes at the corners, and hair shorter than now. The photo did not show the cracked knuckles, the finger tape, or the way she lined up objects exactly parallel when she was trying not to panic.

"What do you need tonight?" Marisol asked.

"I need to back up my personal drive before legal asks for devices. I need to email my mortgage officer because leave pay can get delayed in payroll audits. I need to call my mother and tell her no, I didn't get fired for sleeping with an artist, and yes, she can stop saying `I told you so` in three languages." Ada put the badge back in her bag with deliberate care. "And I need you to open those files in front of me so I know they arrived intact."

Marisol pulled out her phone. The email was there, time-stamped, headers visible, attachments heavy with numbers. She opened the first scan. A ghost image bloomed in grayscale: underpainting figure, male torso turned three-quarters, the letters T.V. near the shoulder where the visible surface now carried only river dark. She opened the second file. The inscription emerged as pale strokes under later pigment: G.V., Dec 1991, cinco meses.

She felt the room tilt slightly.

"Jesus," she whispered.

"No," Ada said. "Chemistry."

Marisol opened the intake note scan. There it was in a conservator's old handwriting from 1992, slanted and impatient: `Artist requests overpaint over male figure + remove dedication line incl. G.V.` Two names had been blacked out in old marker, but the redaction had thinned with copying and age, so the first letters still bled through like bruises.

"It's enough to corroborate," Ada said. "Not enough to absolve anybody."

"It's enough to keep them from saying I made it up."

"Then don't make anything else up either." Ada packed her laptop and stood. "You have the files. Now you have the burden."

The sentence sat between them like something heavy set down on purpose.

When they reached the library doors, sleet hit sideways across Flatbush, tiny hard beads that bounced off the concrete before they melted. Marisol wanted to kiss Ada, to hold her wrist, to give her some visible thanks that was not language. Ada read her face and shook her head once.

"Not tonight," Ada said. "I don't want tenderness used as a receipt."

"Okay."

"Go home. Duplicate everything. Three encrypted copies, two print sets. Don't text details to anyone. And call Luis before the liquor store calls you."

Marisol nodded. "Are you going to be okay?"

"I'm going to be employed by a process for a while." Ada gave a small, tired smile. "That's not the same as okay, but it's survivable."

She pulled her hood up, checked traffic out of habit like somebody who had grown up crossing too many dangerous intersections, and stepped into the weather. In thirty seconds she was another dark coat in a city full of them.

Marisol rode the train to Sunset Park with her phone in her fist and her jaw clenched hard enough to ache. In the car across from her, two teenagers argued over a cracked screen protector, and an older man slept upright with a takeout bag against his chest like a child. Ordinary city evening. Nobody knew she had a dead man's unsigned release in her tote and a live woman's career in her inbox.

In the sublet, the radiator hissed and failed to heat the room past fifty-eight. She put water on for instant coffee, opened her laptop, and began duplicating files exactly as Ada had instructed. Folder by folder, checksum by checksum. External drive, encrypted cloud vault, offline archive. She printed two hard sets and labeled each page with date and source so nobody could claim she had shuffled order. Her fingers cramped twice. She kept going.

At 9:12 p.m. Luis replied to her earlier text.

`Good. You told the truth fast. Call now.`

She called.

"You drink?" he asked before hello.

"No."

"You planning to?"

"I planned it all day. I didn't do it."

"Keep it plain. What happened."

She told him in sequence: death at 8:47, incomplete execution, estate block, Ada's transmission at 5:58, HR leave at 6:03, craving sharp enough to taste freezer-burned vodka in her throat. She did not edit for dignity. She did not leave out the parts where she had wanted to disappear.

Luis listened through dispatch static and paper shuffling. When she finished, he cleared his throat.

"You're in consequence country now," he said. "No shortcuts. Tonight you secure records and stay sober. Tomorrow you make the next right call without drama."

"What's the next right call?"

"First, don't post online. Second, get legal advice that isn't from panic. Third, treat Ada's risk like debt, not romance."

The line burned because it was true.

"Copy," Marisol said.

"Eat something salty and with protein before bed. Your body can't tell grief from depletion half the time."

She made eggs in a scratched pan and ate them standing at the sink. Then she called Luis back as instructed, read him her plan for the morning, and set two alarms.

At 11:03 p.m., Nora wrote from her museum account.

`Marisol - You have received materials now under active dispute. I cannot advise you formally. Off record: preserve original files, preserve metadata, do not discuss contents by text. I am sorry this landed on Ada. - N.F.`

Two minutes later came Malcolm Price, subject line in all caps: NOTICE OF PRESERVATION DEMAND. Keep all materials. Do not alter. Do not disseminate. Direct all correspondence through counsel.

The instruction was to preserve and not use, to hold and not speak, and to carry evidence like contraband and call that ethics.

Marisol opened the IR composite again. Beneath the finished surface, the hidden figure leaned toward someone outside the frame. The inscription glowed pale under black paint and varnish: G.V., Dec 1991, cinco meses.

Proof had a temperature. It did not warm anything.

She thought of Salvador's hand clawing the blanket while the pen rolled out of reach. She thought of Ada setting her badge on the library table, the clip still rewinding by reflex after she let go. Twelve hours between those gestures. Two institutions, same lesson about who got to authorize a story.

At midnight she clipped the unfinished release pages to the chain-of-custody report and wrote a sticky note in block letters: `HE DIED BEFORE THE WITNESS LINE. SHE SENT THE FILES ANYWAY.`

Then she opened a clean notebook page and drafted the first paragraph she would have to live inside:

`At 8:47 a.m. on a sleet morning in December, Salvador Rivas died before completing a release for publication use of his recorded testimony. At 5:58 p.m. the same day, Dr. Ada Park transmitted conservation records with full metadata through traceable channels, accepting immediate professional risk to preserve evidentiary integrity. I possess those records because she chose consequence over cover.`

She put the pen down and rubbed the bridge of her nose until color burst behind her eyes.

If she used the files badly, Ada's leave became waste.

If she used them well, Ada still paid.

Marisol set the blue day-one chip on top of the paper stack, turned off the lamp, and sat in the dark until her breathing slowed enough to sleep.
By dawn, the calls about Saint Brigid's would begin.
