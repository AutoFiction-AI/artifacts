# Chapter 14

Sleep never came on the ride in. The bus dropped him on East Fourth under a sky the color of rubbed nickel, with wind coming off the desert carrying grit. Eli stood on the curb with the green document folder under his arm and his duffel between his shoes, counting how many breaths he could take before the left side of his chest pinched. Four before it pinched. Five if he held his shoulders still and lied to himself.

The casino hiring office sat behind the parking garage instead of inside the building where guests saw chandeliers and mirrored escalators. Out back there were loading docks, dented linen carts, and a row of smokers in maintenance jackets passing a lighter down the line like a relay baton. The laundry intake door opened and shut every few seconds, and each time it opened he got a burst of wet heat and detergent so strong it tasted chalky.

A woman in a burgundy security polo took his ID, glanced at the Pennsylvania address, and clipped a paper badge to his shirt that said VISITOR in red block letters. She had a tiny silver hoop through the right side of her nose and chewed cinnamon gum with the tired determination of somebody staying awake on purpose.

"You're for laundry?" she asked.

"Whatever starts fastest."

She snorted once, not meanly. "That answer always means laundry."

She pointed him toward a trailer office with fake wood paneling and a hand-lettered sign that said EMPLOYMENT. The trailer shook each time a truck hit the dock bumpers outside, and each shudder lit up the old crack under his left ribs. Inside, the air conditioner rattled loud enough to compete with the slot-machine spill leaking through the wall.

The woman behind the desk had already split his forms into two piles before he sat: usable now and fix later. "Tasha Bell," she said, still typing. "If this packet misses cutoff, payroll punts you a week." Her hair was pulled into a low braid held with a pencil, and there was a half-finished crossword next to her keyboard with three squares darkened in hard pen because she'd changed her mind and pressed too deep. She wore rhinestone cat-eye readers on a beaded chain and kept pushing them up to the top of her head every time she looked at his papers, then dragging them back down for the next line. The old compliance reflex slid into place in him anyway: answer fast, look steady, never admit how thin the paperwork really was.

"You've got out-of-state ID, no local lease, and your social on a sticky note." She tapped the note with a fingernail. "Please tell me you've got the card."

"I lost it in the crash. I can get a replacement sent."

"Okay." She clicked through screens. "Background and E-Verify can still clear with the number if it matches. If it doesn't, there's no fix-it-later window. They cut you. You understand?" The word cut landed in his gut harder than it should have. He had used it himself for years when someone else's job was on the line.

He pressed his thumb against the edge of the desk until the nail blanched.

She looked up at him long enough to make him answer with words.

"I understand."

"Good. Don't coast through this one on yeses."

She spun the monitor toward him and walked him through two different entities with two different logos: Silver Sage Resort for night-audit coverage and Sierra Textile Services for laundry. Same building, same elevators, separate payrolls, separate supervisors, separate ways to avoid paying overtime on the same exhausted body. He read every line because he still had the old habit of reading contracts, and he still heard Cal Bristow in his head saying You can either know what you signed or you can explain later why you didn't.

Tasha watched him read, arms folded. "Most people autograph first and panic after. You're either careful or buying time."

"Careful."

"Careful costs you shifts around here."

She said it like a weather report.

The laundry manager came in before he finished the tax form. Her name tag said DEENA L., and she wore black work gloves even indoors, the fingertips dusted pale from lint and bleach. A pink inhaler peeked out of her cargo pocket every time she sat down. She did not sit down now. She stood behind his chair, scanning him like she was deciding whether his spine would hold up under cart weight.

"How old are you?" Deena asked.

"Fifty-two."

She made a face like she was tasting pennies. "Any lifting restriction?"

He felt the rib flash hot when he inhaled. "No."

Tasha gave him one quick side look, then went back to the screen.

Deena continued. "Graveyard. Ten p.m. to six-thirty on the laundry floor. If audit's short, I loan you upstairs till ten-thirty. We run on pounds and minutes, not intentions. Miss in your first two weeks and I strip your locker by lunch. Step off line without radioing and I reassign your station before your cart cools. Bring me doctor's-note drama before ninety days and I'll swap you for the next warm body."

There was no edge to her voice. That was the edge.

Eli asked, "What's the rate?"

"Laundry is fourteen twenty-five. Audit support is sixteen ten. First check holds an eighty-dollar uniform and shoe deduction. Temporary badge until paperwork clears. Badge doesn't clear, your last week sits in review."

"Sits where?"

"In review." Deena lifted one shoulder. "Could be two days. Could be three pay cycles. Depends who's mad."

Tasha snapped her gum and kept typing.

Outside, something metal hit concrete and clanged twice before it settled. Eli looked at the numbers on the sheet again. Fourteen twenty-five. Sixteen ten. Four doubles and even one short laundry swing would put him in range to send real money, not apology money. If he did not get that much, he stayed in motion and called it progress while everyone else paid for it.

He signed.

Deena slid a locker key across the desk. "You start tonight. Bring ibuprofen if you take it. We don't stock meds because legal won't let us."

"I've got some."

"You'll need more."

She turned to go, then looked back. "And don't call me ma'am. Makes me feel dead."

---

The laundry floor lived in permanent midnight no matter what the clock said. No windows, only sodium lights and strip fluorescents that made everybody's skin look waxy and tired. Conveyor belts carried sheets overhead in gray loops. Industrial washers thumped with a bass note he felt in his sternum before he heard it. Steam rolled out each time the press doors opened, and the heat sat on his shoulders like wet wool.

Mateo Rivas showed him the sort line and did not waste words. He was maybe thirty, maybe younger in the face and older in the hands. He had a tiny tattoo of a sparrow at his wrist and a white athletic tape wrap over his left thumb, refreshed so cleanly it looked like ritual. When he opened his locker to stash his lunch, Eli saw a crayon drawing taped inside the door: three stick figures under a huge yellow sun and the word DAD in crooked block letters.

Mateo caught him looking and grinned without embarrassment. "My kid says that's me in the middle. I told her I don't have that much hair."

"You probably had more hair in her version."

"Everybody's got more hair in her version."

He handed Eli earplugs from a box mounted to the wall. "Use these or go deaf in installments."

Deena ran orientation by walking backward in front of them, yelling over the machines with one hand on her inhaler like a talisman. "Whites and sheets on line one. Towels on line two. Blood bins flagged red, chemicals flagged yellow. Don't play hero with needles. You see sharps, you stop and call me. If you get stuck and don't report it, I write you up and you get tested. No exceptions."

Another worker muscled past hard enough to clip Eli's hip with a linen cart, then shouted "Behind!" after she had already moved through him. She did not slow down. Her name patch read JENNY as she dumped fresh folded towels onto line two, and someone had stitched a tiny smiling cactus above the J with green thread, the careful kind of handwork that had to be done at a kitchen table by lamplight.

By midnight Eli's shirt stuck to his spine. By one his left side began sending sharp narrow warnings every time he lifted a wet sheet bundle from the washer mouth to the feed table. The cracked rib had felt almost civil in bus seats and short shifts. It turned mean in heat.

He adjusted by using his right side more, then overcorrected and yanked his old knee when a wheel on the cart caught in a floor seam. Pain moved through him in layered bands, not dramatic, just relentless and cumulative, each band collecting until he stopped being able to pretend they were separate problems.

Mateo noticed the hitch in his step on their first break. They stood in a cinder-block room with two vending machines, a dead ficus in the corner, and a mural of Lake Tahoe sunrises painted directly over a crack that split the wall from ceiling to tile.

"You move like my tio after roofing," Mateo said. "You busted up?"

"I was in a car wreck a month ago."

"And you took laundry. Jesus." Mateo cracked open a can of peach energy drink and took two fast swallows. "You got kids?"

"Two."

Mateo tipped the energy-drink can toward the locker-room door. "I do this because my ex and I switch daycare every six months like it's a hostage exchange. You?"

Eli looked at his hands. The skin between his thumb and forefinger had gone chalky from detergent splash and dried out enough to sting.

"I owe money I shouldn't have touched."

Mateo absorbed that without asking for the story. "Yeah. Welcome to the building."

When break ended, Deena caught Eli swallowing two ibuprofen dry and said, "Take four next time."

"Label says two."

"Label isn't pushing carts for you."

At three forty the audit supervisor came down because crossover was short and recon could not wait. Her name was Kendra Shaw. She pointed her metal clipboard at him before she reached him, already assigning his lane. She wore a black cardigan over uniform slacks and shoes with tiny silver cats embroidered on the toes, the kind of hidden detail people gave themselves when the rest of life had to be plain. Her travel mug smelled like cardamom.

She skimmed his temporary badge and frowned. "You're the provisional?"

"Yeah."

"Keep your hands off anything guest-facing. You're back-office recon. Folio and cage envelope have to reconcile before I call finance. You see a variance, you flag me before it leaves the room. Bury one and your access dies before sunrise. And when your body's about to give out, tell me before you hit tile. I need clean numbers, not a workers-comp incident."

"Got it."

Kendra looked at him again, maybe at the sweat-dried salt on his collar, maybe at the stiffness he was no longer hiding well. "You got reading glasses?"

"No."

She slid a pair of cheap +1.25 readers from the pocket of her clipboard and set them on top of the folio stack. "Use mine. Bring them back. I only lend these to people who hand them back."

The recon room felt like another planet after laundry: overcooled, quiet except for printer chatter and the hum of fluorescent ballast. He sat at a steel table and matched cage envelopes to folio summaries while Kendra keyed corrections into a terminal that still ran a green-text interface older than June. The work should have felt easier because it was seated and clean. Instead his body shook when it finally stopped lifting, and his rib throbbed in time with the printer.

At nine twelve he misread a seven as a one and Kendra caught it before he finalized.

"You're drifting," she said.

"I'm fine."

"No, you're not. And I can't file 'I'm fine' when finance asks why a variance almost posted. Tell me what failed."

He stared at the line item, then told the truth in the smallest possible shape. "Left side pain. I'm compensating and losing focus."

"You're initialing a coaching slip." She pulled a yellow form from her clipboard, wrote in tight block letters, and pushed it toward him. "Provisional recon error plus pain disclosure puts you on laundry-only for tomorrow's crossover. Two clean laundry shifts, no variance noise, and I'll review you back into audit."

He initialed.

"I'll take the two shifts."

When he gave her back the borrowed readers at ten thirty, she said, "Deena asked if you can cover four doubles and a five-hour laundry swing this week. I approved the hours, but you're still laundry-only until that hold clears."

He thought about Marisol in the Glenwood Springs motel bathroom: his palm on the sink, his mouth on the burn mark on her forearm, Nora's name buzzing on his phone until the screen went dark. Marisol had asked whether Nora knew, and he had said, "Not yet," like delay was an answer instead of a choice. While he kept that lie warm, Nora covered the mortgage and June lived on a cut meal plan.

"I can stay upright."

Kendra's expression did not soften. "Then prove it. Bring me two clean nights and I take the hold off."

---

By the fourth night he learned where to stand to catch the only cross-breeze on the floor, the two-second strip near the receiving door where outside air slipped in when trucks backed to dock three. He learned Jenny sang old nineties country under her breath when she sorted stains, and that Deena took exactly thirty-seven steps from her desk to the press line because she counted when her asthma got bad. Mateo rubbed a quarter between finger and thumb before every call to his daughter, not for luck exactly, more like friction to keep himself in place.

Pain had hierarchies. The rib was bright and sharp when he lifted. The knee was dull and mean when he pushed. The palms burned from hot damp cotton sliding faster than his skin wanted to allow. By sunrise each day all three merged into one low electrical field, and he stopped assigning names to parts.

On Thursday, Tasha called him into the employment trailer after shift and closed the door.

"E-Verify kicked a tentative nonconfirmation," she said. "Birth year mismatch."

He felt the room shrink around the word nonconfirmation.

"I entered what was on your I-9," she added. "Could be a keystroke. Could be your old record from when you used Ian for that prepaid account and then forgot where else you used it. People do dumb things when they're trying to disappear."

His face warmed, then cooled. "What happens now?"

"I filed the contest notice. You've got eight federal workdays, and the clock started when I printed this. Until Social Security clears it, you're a conditional hire. Conditional hire means managers can trim or move your schedule and call it operational necessity." She tapped the desk. "Deena can build that paper trail in her sleep."

"So she can cut me."

"She can cut you, park a check in review, or bounce you between entities, as long as the paperwork reads clean."

Outside, the dock alarm beeped three slow notes as a trailer reversed. Tasha slid a printed form across the desk, along with a pen chewed nearly in half.

"Sign here acknowledging notice."

He signed.

She watched him cap the pen and added, almost gently, "You don't have to tell me your story. But if you're trying to send money somewhere fast, conditional status is blood in the water. They'll work you to wire and then call it operational necessity."

He folded the notice into the green folder and felt the paper edge catch a small cut on his thumb. "Then I take the hours while I've got them."

"That's one strategy."

"What's the other strategy?"

"Keep enough spine to walk when they start stealing twice."

He left without answering because he did not know which theft she meant.

Deena posted the weekend schedule ten minutes later. Next to his name: LAUNDRY 2200-0630 / AUDIT 0630-1030, Friday through Monday, plus a laundry-only swing Tuesday 2200-0300. Four doubles and a five-hour fifth night. He stared at the sheet until Mateo shoulder-bumped him.

"They gave you the punishment tour," Mateo said.

"Looks like it."

Mateo whistled through his teeth. "You either pissed her off, or she likes your work, which here is the same thing."

At midnight, halfway through loading king-size sheets into the tunnel washer, Eli's rib seized so hard his left hand opened on reflex and dumped half a load onto the wet concrete. Deena appeared at his side before he could bend.

"Stop." She keyed her radio. "Line one needs a mop and a hazard sign." Then to him, quieter: "Badge."

He blinked. "What?"

"Badge. No crossover tonight." She peeled the temporary audit sticker off his plastic and pocketed it. "I logged a safety drop and sent Kendra the incident. You're laundry-only until she clears you."

"I'm still standing."

"You dropped sixty pounds of wet linen."

"It slipped."

She held his gaze for one long beat. "You want audit access back, tighten up. I can't babysit you."

She walked away before the mop crew arrived.

He finished the load with his right arm shaking and his breath sliced into shallow pieces. On break he sat on an overturned milk crate behind the dock because the break room lights made his eyes buzz. The desert air was cold enough to sting the sweat on his neck. He took his shirt hem in both hands and pressed fabric hard against his left side until the spasm eased.

The flip phone vibrated in his pocket. Unknown number. He answered on the second buzz.

"Theo," the voice said. "Don't get dramatic, it's me. I'm on a borrowed line because mine's dead again."

Eli straightened instinctively though nobody could see him. "Hey."

"I got your text with the hours screenshot. That's real money if they keep you."

"If they keep me."

"You sound wrecked."

"I'm working."

"No shit. Listen. If you send anything this week, send it with a memo line Nora can track. Don't do anonymous cash apps and then expect trust points."

Eli let a truck engine idle fill the gap before answering. "I wasn't planning anonymous."

"Good. Also, June asked if you'd called me." Theo paused. "I said yeah, because you had. She didn't ask anything else."

The words hit his cracked side like a cart handle, knocking half a breath out of him. June asked if you'd called me. Not How are you, not Where are you exactly. Just a systems check.

"Thanks," Eli said.

"Don't thank me. Just keep receipts. And don't die in a laundry room trying to sprint through fourteen grand in two weeks."

"I won't."

Theo gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "You don't get to promise that to me."

The line clicked dead. Borrowed phone, borrowed minutes, son setting terms like a foreman assigning safety tasks. Eli sat in the dark until Deena's voice came over the dock speaker calling all hands back to line one.

---

Saturday bled into Sunday without a clean border. He measured time in machine cycles and audit print runs, in how many ibuprofen remained in the orange bottle, in whether the left side caught at four a.m. or held until six. He started taping his rib before shift with cloth athletic tape from the casino gift shop because the pharmacy had closed when he got off. He applied it in the locker room mirror with his shirt off, watching his hands work around a bruise blooming yellow at the edges and violet at the center.

Mateo came in one morning while Eli was rewrapping and leaned against the lockers, unwrapping a breakfast burrito from foil. "You ever think about just quitting and doing something your body can survive?"

"Every hour."

"So why don't you."

Eli smoothed the tape and pulled his shirt down. "Because surviving is not the same problem as now."

Mateo chewed, swallowed, and lifted two fingers in a tired salute. "Fair."

Then, after a beat: "My girl hates dinosaurs. Everybody thinks kids love dinosaurs. She says they're just birds with anger issues. That's why I work this shift, by the way. Daycare's cheaper if I drop her before six."

It was such a specific opinion that Eli smiled before he meant to.

"Birds with anger issues."

"Exactly. She's six and already funnier than me."

They clocked in.

By Sunday night, after two laundry-only shifts and a signed clean-floor log from Deena, Kendra gave him back a temporary audit sticker and began handing him more complex reconciliations without comment: group booking adjustments, disputed minibar charges, count-room slips that did not match the nightly revenue summary. He moved slower than he wanted and still faster than the temp before him, according to Kendra, who finally said on Monday, "You're useful enough that I argued to keep your hours even with the paperwork flag."

"Why?"

She shrugged, eyes on the screen. "Because I hate retraining people. And because you don't bullshit me when you're wrong."

A printer jammed. She opened the panel and tugged out a wrinkled strip of receipt paper with two blackened fingertips. "Don't confuse that with friendship."

"I don't."

"Good. I just put you down as primary overflow for weekend recon. That means extra audit coverage after laundry when someone no-shows. Better rate, worse spine. Decline one call and I pull your recon access; you're laundry-only until I can train another closer."

His first full check posted Tuesday night. Gross: $813.35. Deductions bit hard: taxes, uniform, shoe holdback, cafeteria charge he did not remember making and would have to dispute later. Net: $603.18.

He stood by the employee kiosk while slot jackpots chimed through the wall in fake celebration. His forearms still trembled from the tunnel washers, and this check was enough to hit Nora's account like proof he wasn't just apologizing by phone anymore.

He bought a money order in the twenty-four-hour pharmacy and sent five hundred to Nora with the memo line Theo had told him to use: MORTGAGE ARREARS - OCT WK 2. The clerk behind the bulletproof glass counted the bills twice, then made him read the memo line back before she printed the slip. She wore a University of Nevada hoodie and had silver glitter on her eyelids that caught the fluorescent light every time she blinked. Then she slid the receipt under the slot.

"Keep the stub," she said. "People always lose the stub."

"I won't."

He tucked it into the green folder behind June's tuition statement and felt a narrow strip of steadiness, the kind that held for one minute before the ground moved again.

When he reached the motel room he rented by the week, the carpet smelled of old smoke and lemon cleaner fighting to a draw. He sat on the edge of the bed, shoes still on, and opened June's contact thread on the flip phone. Three outgoing calls from the past month, one answered for twenty seconds before she said she had to run, then nothing except his voicemails stacked like unsold inventory.

He looked at the time. 11:47 p.m. In Pittsburgh it would be 2:47 a.m., but June lived by campus hours and library shifts now, strange pockets of sleep and work he no longer understood. He could wait and call at a civilized time. He could overthink until the next shift started.

He called.

It rang five times and dropped to her voicemail. The greeting was simple and brisk: "It's June. Leave it."

He held the phone tight enough the plastic creaked.

He saw the Glenwood motel mirror fogged white, Marisol's braid dark against his wrist while Nora's name flashed on his screen and rang out unanswered.

"Hey, Junie, it's Dad." He stopped, reset. "It's me. I'm in Reno, doing casino laundry and night-audit support. I sent five hundred tonight with your mom's name in the memo so she can track it. I lied to your mother from a motel bathroom in Glenwood, and while I hid there Nora carried the mortgage and you rationed meal swipes."

A siren wailed two streets over and faded.

He continued, voice rough from steam and lint. "I'm ashamed of what I did to both of you. I miss you every day. If your meal plan's still cut, text me the gap and I'll send it."

"You don't have to call me back. Good night."

He ended the message and sat with the dark screen reflected in the motel window. Across the parking lot, a man in pajama pants smoked under the ice machine and watched the empty road like he expected someone to pull in and save him from his own choices.
He did not hit replay. Hearing himself say ashamed had already scraped him raw.

Eli set the phone facedown, then picked it up again two minutes later to check for a response he knew would not be there. Nothing. He set an alarm for 8:30 p.m. and lay back without undressing, one hand over the taped rib as if he could hold the bone together by pressure alone.

The room heater kicked on and rattled like loose dice in a cup. He closed his eyes and pictured Nora opening the transfer receipt, then June deleting his voicemail before he got to the end. Tomorrow meant another shift he could not miss, another night of lint in his lungs and pain taped tight to his ribs. None of it comforted him. It only kept him pointed east.

At 1:16 a.m., before he slept, he checked the phone again.

No new voicemail. No text. No missed call.

At 8:30 p.m. the alarm dragged him upright into the same ache, the same lint smell still trapped in his hair, the same badge waiting on the table by the door. Deena had texted the shift line at noon: I NEED YOU FOR EXTRA AUDIT COVERAGE AFTER LAUNDRY. PLEASE CONFIRM. The laundry-only hold was off.

He typed back with his thumb.

`I'll be there.`

He clipped the badge to his shirt and left before the ache could talk him out of it, heading for the laundry corridor where the load cards waited above Dryer Three.
