# Chapter 17

By the time Eli reached the house on Beechwood, the sky had gone the color of old dishwater and the porches up the block had their blue-white holiday lights on timers, blinking into life one railing at a time. His duffel strap cut into the ridge where his cracked rib had healed wrong, and the ache rode with each step. The front walk had the same hairline split by the third flagstone, the same rust stain under the hose spigot, the same bent mailbox post he had promised to reset three summers in a row and never did. Nothing looked dramatic. That was part of what made it harder to stand there.

Nora opened the front door before he touched the bell. She wore scrub pants under a heavy cardigan and her hospital badge was still clipped to the hem as if she had forgotten it was there. Her wrist brace, the beige one she used on lift-heavy weeks, showed at her cuff. She did not step forward for a hug and she did not step back to make him feel unwelcome. She moved aside exactly enough for him to enter.

"The basement door is open," she said. "Bag can go down first. Then come up to the table."

He nodded. "Okay."

The house smelled like radiator dust and lemon cleanser. It also smelled faintly, cruelly, like home: the pine cleaner June hated because it made the kitchen smell like a dentist office, the wool throw on the couch, the onions Nora had chopped before she left for shift this morning and forgot to cook. Eli set his duffel on the basement stairs and touched the railing, where one spindle had a strip of black tape from a failed repair. He had put that tape there the week before June's sixteenth birthday. It was still there.

In the basement, Nora had cleared space beside the old workbench. A narrow bed sat against the cinder-block wall with a folded comforter and two towels stacked at the foot. His winter coat hung on a screw hook. A plastic drawer tower held clean socks, plain T-shirts, underwear, and one set of thermal layers. No decoration, no speech. Practical mercy.

He came back up and found the dining table laid out in stations. At one end sat his green document folder, swollen now with transfer receipts and pay stubs from Reno. At the other sat a legal-size envelope with Nora's handwriting: `SEPARATION TERMS - READ FULL`. Between them lay a yellow pad with boxes drawn in ruler-straight pencil lines, dates down the left edge. Nora had already filled the first three rows.

She pointed at the chair across from her. "Sit."

He sat. The chair legs made that scraping noise he had always meant to pad with felt. The sound bounced in his teeth.

Nora slid the yellow pad toward him. "I'm going to do this once, cleanly. If you need clarification, ask. I'm not doing emotional sidebars while we cover terms."

He kept his hands flat on the table. "All right."

"This is legal separation, not a rehearsal separation. Papers are ready. We sign tonight. Filing goes in Monday morning." She tapped the first row. "You live in basement space only. You can use kitchen, basement bath, and upstairs hall bath for shower. No entry to my bedroom. No entry to June's room. No knocking on June's door unless there is blood or fire."

He looked down at the columns she had drawn: `RULE`, `START`, `REVIEW DATE`, `FAIL CONDITION`, `ACTION`. Her handwriting stayed small and even all the way down, as if she had charted this like intake notes.

"Understood," he said.

"Second. Finance transparency." She tapped the next row. "Every Friday by 8 p.m., you send me photo copies or PDFs of pay stubs, current checking balance, and any cash transfer over fifty dollars. Any cash tip or side cash gets logged. You do not hold hidden cash reserves. You did that once. We are done with that."

His mouth tasted like the burnt coffee from the bus station. "I'm not hiding anything."

"Good. Then documentation is easy."

She kept going. "Third. Household contribution. You deposit four hundred from each weekly check to the mortgage arrears account until current. Then it shifts to June's tuition shortfall. Theo still gets copies because he's been running the transfer calendar and I trust his follow-through."

Eli nodded again, because he had agreed to this on the phone and because hearing it in the room made the consequences less abstract and more like furniture: heavy, unavoidable, in his way.

"Fourth. Time limit on residence." Her finger moved down. "Basement arrangement runs eight weeks from tonight. Review at week six. If there is another breach on money, lying, or house access, it ends same day. You find alternate housing immediately."

He rubbed his thumb over a nick in the table finish. June had carved that with a compass when she was thirteen and pretending she could restore old furniture from YouTube videos. He had laughed instead of stopping her. The mark sat there now like a record.

"What counts as lying?" he asked. "I'm not arguing. I need exact."

Nora watched him for a second, maybe checking whether this was performance. "Material omission. If a fact would alter decisions about money, safety, or June's schedule, and you hold it back, that counts."

"That's fair."

"Fifth." She took a breath before saying June's name. "No private reconciliation scene with June. If she wants to talk one-on-one, she'll ask. You do not corner her on stairs, in the car, in the kitchen at midnight, none of that. She made that boundary in writing and I'm honoring it."

Eli felt the old instinct rise, the one that reached for language and tried to shape him back into reasonable. He pushed it down.

"Okay," he said. "I can do that."

"You will." Nora slid the legal envelope across to him. "Now the legal pages."

He opened the packet. The first page had both their full names in block type and the phrase `PETITION FOR LEGAL SEPARATION` in the top margin. The language ran cold and specific: separate sleeping quarters, household expense allocation, temporary custody schedule for adult dependent support while tuition obligations remained active. He got snagged on the phrase `marital misconduct not adjudicated at filing stage` and felt the floor tip a fraction.

Nora's pen clicked once. "Read all of it."

He did. It took twenty minutes and two trips to the same paragraph because his eyes kept sliding to the line about marital home occupancy ending no later than January thirty-first. Outside, a plow truck grated by at the corner, blade bouncing sparks against the manhole cover. Inside, the baseboard heat kicked and hissed like a warning whisper.

When he finished, Nora turned the signature page toward him.

"Do you want me to call the attorney and put her on speaker for questions?" she asked.

"No." He picked up the pen. "I understand it."

He signed where the tabs marked his name. He initialed each page. On the asset disclosure line he wrote `none beyond listed wages and account balances` and did not flinch at the truth of it.

Nora signed after him, her wrist brace making her letters slightly angular. She put the pages back in order and clipped them.

Then she pulled one last sheet from her folder, not legal, just lined paper with June's rounded handwriting.

"She wanted this included," Nora said. "Read it now."

He took the page.

`Dad,`

`I'm not doing surprise emotional talks. If you need to say something, say it when everyone is there. If I say I'm done, conversation is done. No guilt voice mails if I don't pick up. No coming to campus without asking me first. If you keep it practical and don't bullshit me, I'll stay in the room.`

`- June`

The final period was punched deep enough to emboss the back.

Eli put the paper down carefully. "I can follow that."

Nora looked at him in a way that hurt more than anger. "You can follow lots of things. I need you to keep following them after the feeling changes."

He almost said `I know.` He did not. That sentence had become a trick in his mouth.

Instead he asked, "What else tonight?"

"Dinner at seven. June is on the six-fifteen bus from campus. You can help cook if you can do it without turning it into a scene."

"I can cook."

"You can chop and wash." She stood, gathered the papers into two stacks, and slid one into the legal envelope. "And Eli. You sleep downstairs tonight. No exceptions."

He nodded. "I heard you."

In the kitchen, he washed rice while Nora trimmed chicken thighs. Their elbows nearly touched at the counter, and each time he shifted to give her room he felt the small humiliations of relearning space in his own house. The faucet squealed when he turned it halfway, the same broken washer as always. Nora swore under her breath and smacked the side of the old fridge when it started a loud rattle. He reached for the door at the same time she did, and both of them pulled back.

"Sorry," he said.

"Just hold it for a second." She opened the freezer, pressed the upper hinge with her palm, and the rattle stopped. "There. It sticks when it frosts."

He held the door while she rearranged a bag of peas and a container of broth.

It was ridiculous, the tenderness of that one task.

At six-twenty, the side door banged and June came in with wind-reddened cheeks, a canvas library tote, and a puffer jacket patched with duct tape at one sleeve seam. Her hair was in a loose knot that had half-failed on the bus. She looked older than when he dropped her at the dorm and younger than the messages she had sent him.

She saw him, paused, then toed off her boots in one quick motion.

"Hi," she said, not warm, not cold, just placed.

"Hey, June."

Nora looked up from the stove. "Soup's almost done. Wash up."

June hung her tote on the chair and pulled a cracked lip balm from her pocket, rolling it over her mouth while she scanned the kitchen. Her eyes landed on the legal envelope, then on Eli's green folder, then back to him.

"So paperwork happened," she said.

"It happened," Nora answered.

June gave a short exhale through her nose, almost a laugh. "Cool. Nothing says welcome home like legal stationery."

Eli said, "You hungry?"

"I ate a granola bar at four and I regret every life choice that led to that, so yeah."

Somewhere between the rice steaming and the soup finishing, the room found a rhythm that felt like real life instead of a hearing. June told Nora that the campus laundry machines now charged extra for hot water and she was becoming an expert in cold-cycle misery. Nora said hospital parking was up another twenty dollars a month and she was considering arson as a budgeting strategy. June snorted. Eli set bowls and spoons. For three minutes, maybe five, they sounded like people who had known each other for years and liked parts of one another.

Then silence climbed back in.

They sat at the table with chicken broth, rice, chopped cilantro, and lime wedges in a chipped blue bowl. June stirred hot sauce into hers until it turned brick red.

"This is good," she said to Nora. "Did you use the cheap thighs or the less cheap thighs?"

"The sale ones."

"God bless clearance meat."

Eli smiled before he could stop himself. June caught it and looked away.

She ate three bites, then set her spoon down. "Okay. Ground rule check. We're all here, so if there's any big reveal stuff, do it now, not in hallway drive-bys."

Nora's eyes flicked to Eli and back to June. "Tonight is logistics and house boundaries."

"Logistics are fine. I like logistics. They're honest." June turned to Eli. "What's your schedule?"

He swallowed soup too hot and felt it burn down his throat. "I start at Monongahela Distribution on Thursday. Part-time. Four-thirty in the morning to ten-thirty, Monday through Friday. Loading and returns processing."

June nodded once. "Pay?"

"Nineteen twenty-five an hour. No benefits first ninety days."

"Bus route?"

"Two buses. First one at three-fifty from Forbes."

She made a face. "That is aggressively bleak."

"Yeah."

"You taking this because it's close, or because they're the only people who called back?"

"Both."

June leaned back in her chair, balancing it briefly before letting it drop. "Okay. Practical question two. If your check gets weird for a week, what happens to mortgage and tuition?"

Nora answered before he could, her voice clipped clean. "Mortgage gets first four hundred from each of his checks until arrears clear. Tuition shortfall account gets next transfers. Theo tracks transfers in shared sheet. No exceptions."

June pointed her spoon at Nora. "Thank you. That's the answer format I like."

Then she turned back to Eli. "And if you get embarrassed and decide to disappear again?"

He kept his palms on the table so she could see they were steady. "Then I lose the house arrangement same day. And if I vanish, you don't come looking. You call Theo and Mom, and legal takes over."

June watched him for a full breath. "At least you listened."

"I'm trying to."

"Don't say trying like it's an achievement badge." She added more lime to her soup, squeezed hard enough that seeds dropped in. "Just do the boring thing and keep doing it."

Nora said quietly, "June."

"I'm not yelling."

"I know."

The three of them ate for another minute. The spoon taps and the radiator clicks became the room's metronome. Eli heard the old house settle around them, the tiny tick in the dining room clock that always sped up in winter, the traffic hiss from wet tires on Murray Avenue. He had spent months in motel corridors listening for strangers. Hearing these familiar sounds again felt less like relief than accountability made audible.

June broke the quiet first. "Library cut my hours from twelve to nine this month. Budget freeze."

Nora frowned. "Can you absorb that?"

"Not if textbooks keep pretending they're luxury goods. I'm okay for now. I picked up one peer-note job for intro psych. Seventy bucks if the professor approves."

Eli said, "I can cover books in January if--"

June held up a hand. "No. Don't promise future money in a sentence with `if`. That exact sentence genre is banned."

He nodded once. "You're right."

She looked almost surprised he did not push back. Then she reached for another chicken thigh and said, lighter, "Also your basement smells like wet socks and old paint. That's not judgment. That's data."

"It always did," Nora said.

"True. I think the dehumidifier died in the Obama administration."

"I bought a new filter last spring," Nora said.

June grinned despite herself. "See? Family romance."

Eli laughed, a short real sound, and Nora gave him a look that might have been warning or might have been fatigue. Humor passed through the table like a brief warm draft, then thinned.

When bowls were empty, June stood and stacked dishes. "I'll rinse."

"I've got it," Eli said.

She hesitated, then nodded toward the sink. "Fine. But no martyr dishwashing performance. Just wash."

He took the plates. Soap scent rose sharp and clean. June dried beside him with quick efficient motions, flicking water off each fork before slotting it away. Nora wiped the table and aligned the chairs, each scrape deliberate.

June said, without looking up, "Tomorrow I need a ride to campus by eleven. Bus works if schedules explode."

"I can drive you," Eli said.

She shook her head. "Mom has day shift. Car stays with her. You can carry my bag to the stop. That's enough."

"Okay."

"That is not punishment," she added. "It's what I can do right now."

He set a bowl in the rack. "I hear you."

Nora moved to the counter and opened the legal envelope again, checking the signature tabs like she did not trust paper to stay still on its own. Her jaw worked once before she spoke.

"June, go put your laundry in. I need a private minute with him."

June stopped drying. "No private catharsis, remember?"

Nora did not look up. "Not catharsis. One legal question."

June considered, then dropped the towel on the counter. "Door stays open."

She went upstairs two steps at a time. Her door shut halfway and reopened; she was listening, making sure.

Nora stood at the sink with both hands braced on the metal rim. The house hummed.

"There's one line item missing from your disclosure," she said.

Eli knew before she turned around.

"Was there someone else while you were gone?" she asked. "Do not manage this answer."

The plate in his hand slipped against the soap and knocked the faucet. Water sprayed across his sleeve. He turned the tap off.

"Yes," he said.

Nora's face did not move. "Who."

"Her name is Marisol Vega. She managed the motel where I worked near Glenwood Springs."

"For how long?"

"From October into December." He forced himself to stay in plain language. "We had sex. More than once. It was a relationship, not one mistake. I ended it before I came back."

The stair above them creaked. June stood halfway down in sock feet, hand on the banister, expression stripped blank.

Nora asked, "Did you use family money on her?"

"No. We ate together after shifts. Sometimes I bought groceries for the motel kitchen with cash from my pay, same as other staff. I did not send her money, and I did not move money to her."

Nora's fingers tightened on the sink edge until the knuckles blanched. "You didn't think this belonged in the packet you signed forty minutes ago?"

"I knew it did." He set the wet plate down because his hand had started shaking, soap suds sliding toward the drain. "I was going to tell you tonight. I didn't know when."

"You didn't know when." She repeated it like a lab value she did not trust. "You had two thousand miles to figure out when."

June came down two more steps and stopped. "Jesus," she said softly. Then louder: "You couldn't keep one disaster at a time?"

Eli turned toward her. "June--"

"Don't `June` me like I'm the one who needs regulation." Her voice climbed, then snapped back down to controlled. "Did Mom know before this?"

Nora answered. "No."

June looked from one to the other, then laughed once, ugly and breathless. "Great. So we did legal night and truth night in one package deal. Efficient."

Nora asked, "Was it after I left you the filing voicemail? The one with tuition cuts?"

"Yes," he said. "It had already started before that. It continued after. Then I ended it in December, before I left Reno."

Nora shut her eyes briefly. "So when you were sending transfer receipts and telling me you were coming back to face this, you were also still in that relationship."

"For part of that stretch, yes."

She nodded once, a sharp downward motion like a hammer strike. "Thank you for finally being exact."

June came all the way down then, grabbed her tote off the chair, and slung it over her shoulder even though she was staying in the house. "I'm going to Maya's for two hours," she said to Nora. "I need not to hear pipes and legal words."

"It's icy," Nora said automatically.

"I'll text when I get there."

She turned to Eli before he could answer anything. "Boundary update. Don't message me tonight unless somebody is in an ambulance."

Then she was at the side door, boots half-laced, coat zipper snagging and then ripping free in one hard motion. The door slammed and the cold came in for a second before the spring pulled it shut.

The house held still.

Nora looked at the wet dishes stacked at the sink as if she had never seen them before. "I should have asked this before signing."

He said, "If you want to amend terms, we amend."

Her laugh came out flat. "Amend. Listen to us."

She pulled out a chair and sat, both hands pressed against her forehead. After a moment she spoke without looking up.

"Was she in love with you?"

"I don't know. She cared about me. I cared about her. I used that and I also meant it. Both are true."

Nora lowered her hands. "Do not bring her humanity into my kitchen like it earns points."

"It doesn't."

"Did you tell her you loved her?"

"Yes."

Nora's jaw flexed once. She stood and walked to the pantry, then stopped with her hand on the knob, not opening it. "I need the practical before I can breathe," she said. "Here's the practical. Terms stand. Filing stands. But we add one condition."

He waited.

"Zero romantic or sexual contact while you are living in this house. If that boundary breaks, you are out same day. I'm not managing another layer."

"That is reasonable."

"I'm not asking if it's reasonable. I'm telling you."

"I understand."

She nodded toward the table. "Write it down on the yellow pad. Date and initials."

He sat and wrote in block letters because his cursive had gone jagged:

`NO ROMANTIC / SEXUAL CONTACT WHILE RESIDING IN HOUSE. BREACH = IMMEDIATE EXIT.`

He dated it and initialed. Nora initialed below his.

For a long minute neither of them spoke. Upstairs, June's room creaked as the radiator expanded, and from outside came the distant whine of a siren heading toward Forbes. Eli folded the dish towel once, twice, then set it down.

"I ended it because coming back halfway wasn't going to hold," he said. "That doesn't clean anything up. It's just the sequence."

Nora stared at the new line on the pad. "You keep wanting sequence to sound like morality."

"I know."

She gave him a look that was almost pity and almost contempt and maybe both. "Go downstairs. I'm done for tonight."

He took his folder, the signed packet copy, and the page in June's handwriting. In the basement he set all three on the workbench beside a jar of mixed screws and a dead flashlight. The dehumidifier coughed and failed to start. He kicked its side lightly and it whined back to life.

He sat on the edge of the narrow bed, unlaced his boots, and listened to the house above him: Nora moving from room to room with her precise footsteps, one cabinet door shut too hard, the sink running, then silence. He did not sleep for hours. At 10:41 his flip phone buzzed with a text from June.

`At Maya's. Safe. Back by 11.`

He typed `Thanks for the update.` and stared at the screen before deleting `for the update` and sending only `Thanks.`

No reply came.

He woke before the alarm at 3:12, throat dry from basement heat and old paint dust. The cinder-block wall near the ceiling sweat a dark line where cold seeped through from outside. He dressed in two shirts and his cleanest pair of work pants, packed the steel-toe boots Theo had mailed him in November, and climbed the stairs carrying them by the laces so the heels would not bang and wake anyone.

Nora was already in the kitchen in scrub top and fleece vest, hair damp from a fast shower. She was spooning grounds into the coffee maker. Her eyes looked grainy with too little sleep.

"Bus?" she asked.

"Three-fifty."

She nodded and set a travel mug on the counter without looking at him. "There's oatmeal in the pot."

"Thanks."

He ate standing up. The oatmeal had clumped and gone thick, and it was still the best thing he had tasted in weeks. Nora checked something on her phone, then turned the screen toward him. It was the weather app: freezing rain until noon.

"Text when you clock in," she said. "Not because I care about your employer. Because routine matters."

"I will."

She lifted her mug. "June got back at ten-fifty-three. She's sleeping."

He wanted to ask if June had said anything. He did not.

At the bus stop the shelter glass was tagged with a marker drawing of a penguin in sneakers, and someone had taped a church flyer over half of it. Eli stamped his feet against the cold and watched his breath cloud the pane. Two high school boys in marching-band jackets argued about fantasy football lineups. A woman in a grocery uniform smoked with one hand cupped against the wind. Nobody looked at him twice.

Monongahela Distribution sat in a converted machine shop by the river, a rectangle of corrugated metal and sodium lights with `MD LOGISTICS` half peeled off the front sign. The loading bays breathed diesel into the dark. Inside, conveyor belts clattered under fluorescent glare that flattened every face.

A woman at the desk took his ID and pointed him toward a small office with a crooked calendar of Labrador puppies. `D. KELLEY - FLOOR SUPERVISOR` was stenciled on the door.

Darryl Kelley rose when Eli knocked. He was in his mid-forties, broad through the chest, with a shaved head and a Steelers beanie pulled low. A tiny silver charm shaped like a sewing thimble hung from the beanie edge on a safety pin.

"My kid hates that I still wear this," Darryl said, catching Eli's glance. "She made the charm in seventh-grade home ec. I keep it on anyway so she can stay annoyed at me. Sit."

Eli sat on a plastic chair that bent at the middle.

Darryl slid a clipboard across the desk. "I read your app. You've done hospital compliance, motel nights, casino laundry. That is a fun timeline."

"It's a timeline," Eli said.

Darryl snorted. "Fair. Here is ours. Part-time means thirty-two hours unless volume drops. Shift starts four-thirty, ends ten-thirty if trucks behave, eleven-thirty if they don't. We don't do heroic overtime speeches. We do pallets and labels."

"I can do that."

"Pay is nineteen twenty-five, weekly direct deposit. Ninety-day probation. Miss two starts in a month, you're gone." Darryl tapped the clipboard. "No drama clause is unofficial but real. If you need day-shift therapy or court, tell me before Thursday schedule posts."

"I've got a weekly legal meeting on Mondays at seven p.m. That won't hit shift."

"Good. You got lifting limits?"

Eli considered lying for one second, then remembered the yellow pad line about material omission. "Old knee stiffness on right side. Healed rib fracture left side. I can lift fifty with form, no issue. If I twist wrong, I slow down, I don't disappear."

Darryl watched him and nodded. "That is a useful answer. First week you'll run returns lane with Chen. He talks like he's mad even when he likes you. Don't take the tone personally."

He stood and opened a locker cabinet, pulling out a high-vis vest and scanner holster. "Bathroom is at end of aisle three, not by break room like civilized people. Break room coffee tastes like radiator water. If you bring your own, hide it or everyone steals it."

Eli took the vest. The reflective strips were cracked at the edges from repeated wash cycles.

One bay over, forklifts beeped in reverse and a pallet slammed onto concrete hard enough to vibrate the office window. Darryl winced, then yelled through the door without turning his head, "Eddie, stop trying to murder the floor. It did nothing to you."

A voice yelled back, "It looked at me wrong."

Darryl sighed. "Welcome aboard."

The work was exactly what he needed and exactly what it cost. Returns lane meant ripping open battered cartons, matching damaged inventory codes to a screen, repacking what could be salvaged, and sending the rest to scrap bins that smelled like wet cardboard and machine oil. By six fifteen Eli's shoulders burned. By eight his lower back had gone tight and his right knee clicked on every bend. Chen, a compact man in wraparound glasses with bright orange earplugs looped around his neck, moved fast and narrated everything in a running stream of complaints about packaging tape quality, football officiating, and municipal pothole policy. He was impossible to interrupt and impossible not to like.

At break, Eli stood near the dock door and drank bitter coffee from a paper cup while freezing river air leaked through the seam. He checked his phone: one text from Nora.

`You in?`

He sent `Clocked in at 4:27. Lane work.`

Three dots appeared, disappeared, returned.

`Received.`

Nothing else. It was perfect.

When shift ended, Darryl initialed his training sheet and said, "You kept pace. Don't be a hero tomorrow. Heroes pull hamstrings."

Outside, the freezing rain had turned to sleet and left a granular crust on parked cars. Eli rode two buses home with his boots damp and his hands stinging back to life. On the second bus, an older man in a construction jacket fell asleep against the window and snored through each stop. Eli watched neighborhoods slide by in gray bands: rowhouses, corner stores, laundromats, a church sign that read `NO ONE IS BEYOND RETURN` with three letters burned out so it read `NO ONE IS BEY ND RET RN`.

At the house, he came in through the side door and found June at the table with her laptop open, earbuds hanging around her neck, economics notes spread in color-coded stacks. She wore an oversized sweatshirt with the college logo and had drawn a tiny frowning face on the back of her left hand in black pen.

She glanced up. "How bad was it?"

"Less bad than casino laundry. More bad than dishwashing."

"That is a weird unit system, but I accept it." She took an earbud out. "Mom left soup in the fridge. Also she taped a calendar on the basement door with your shift blocks and my class schedule. If you need the bathroom after ten, text first because pipes scream and wake me."

"Got it."

June clicked her pen against the notebook edge twice, thinking. "I'm not ready for emotional talks. That hasn't changed."

"I know."

"But if you're carrying boxes at four-thirty and sending money when you said you would, that matters. I'm not pretending it doesn't."

He set his lunch container by the sink and met her eyes. "Thank you."

She shrugged. "Don't make it ceremonial."

Then, almost as an afterthought, she reached into her tote and tossed him a small object. He caught it: a new dehumidifier filter in plastic wrap, brand name printed in blue.

"Bought it at the hardware place by campus," she said. "Basement smelled like haunted gym socks. Consider this community service."

He laughed, tired enough that it came out rough. "You didn't have to."

"I know." She looked back at her screen. "Put it in before bedtime or I'll file a smell complaint."

He went downstairs, swapped the filter, and listened to the machine settle into a lower steadier hum. On the workbench he set his first-day badge beside the legal packet and the yellow pad copy Nora had given him. The new line about zero contact sat under his signature in blunt block letters.

At six-thirty Nora came home from shift, dropped her keys in the ceramic bowl by the door, and stood in the kitchen doorway with her eyes closed for a full ten seconds before speaking.

"How was work?" she asked him.

"Hard. Stable. I can do it."

She nodded, opened the fridge, and started portioning leftovers into containers with the calm efficiency of triage.

"Transfer day is Friday," she said.

"I know."

"We review six weeks from tonight."

"I know."

She clicked the lid onto the last container and finally looked at him. "I do not forgive you. Not today. Maybe not ever. But I can work with true information."

He swallowed. "That's what I have now."

"Keep having it."

June came down wearing running tights and one of Nora's old wool hats, left eyebrow nick visible where the knit pulled back. "I'm going around Schenley before dark," she announced. "Anybody need anything from the corner store?"

Nora said, "Milk."

Eli said, "Nothing."

June nodded. At the door she paused and looked over her shoulder at Eli. "Bag to bus stop tomorrow at ten-thirty, right?"

"Right."

"Don't be late."

"I won't."

She left. Nora turned back to the sink. Eli stood for a second in the middle of the kitchen, hearing the old fridge start its rattle and Nora's palm strike the side panel to quiet it. Without discussing it, he moved to hold the door while she adjusted the frozen peas.

It took three seconds. It felt like work.

That night he set two alarms for 3:20 and 3:28 and laid out his socks, thermal shirt, and bus card on the folding chair by the bed. He wrote Friday's transfer amount on the yellow pad in pencil and circled it twice. Above him, footsteps crossed the hall, paused at the bathroom, crossed back. The house was full of people and limits and sound.

He did not mistake that for grace. He accepted it as terms.
