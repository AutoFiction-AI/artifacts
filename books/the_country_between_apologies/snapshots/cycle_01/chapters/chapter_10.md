# Chapter 10

The discharge nurse set his belongings on the bed tray in a clear plastic bag that still smelled like smoke and antifreeze. Eli watched her knot the handles as if she was wrapping lunch for somebody else. The adhesive strip on his forehead tugged when he blinked. His left side hurt in a clean, bright line from his armpit down to his flank, a pain so exact it almost felt mechanical, as if a thin metal rod had been slid under his skin and snapped.

"No lifting over ten pounds for at least ten days," the nurse said, tapping the packet clipped to his chart. Her name badge read KATE K., and one of the cartoon kidneys on her scrub top had a coffee cup in its little hand. "No twisting if you can avoid it. Don't drive on the pain meds. You start feeling short of breath, you come back. Not tomorrow. Right then."

"I don't have a car."

She gave him the look people used when they were trying to keep sympathy from turning into involvement. "Then that's one instruction you'll have no problem following."

He signed three forms in a slanted hand that did not look like his own. The top sheet listed diagnosis codes he had to sound out in his head: closed rib fracture, scalp contusion, observation stay. The final page was the part that mattered: balance due after insurance adjudication. He stared at the printed line, then at the estimate under it. The estimate came to two thousand seven hundred forty dollars. The number sat there with the same calm font as everything else, as if it had arrived through weather and not choices.

He put the packet in the green document folder, now bowed at the corners from melting snow in the crash bay. The folder already held June's tuition statement, one mortgage delinquency notice with red bars across the top, and a folded legal pad page where he had written dates in Theo's handwriting because Theo was the one who had circled them in the atlas: call by Friday, transfer by Monday, do not miss. He had missed most of them.

The nurse left. He stood alone in the room in hospital socks, one shoe untied because bending hurt, and checked his inventory like he was clocking warehouse stock. His wallet held a Pennsylvania license, one debit card with less than ten dollars behind it, eighty-six in cash, three quarters, two dimes, and one nickel. His prepaid flip phone had a crack at the hinge and enough battery for maybe two calls. His atlas was water-swollen, with the cover torn at the top seam, but Theo's marker routes were still legible through the wrinkle. His toiletry bag held a toothbrush, a razor, and motel soap wrapped in tissue. His medications were a bottle of ibuprofen 600 mg with twenty tablets and six tramadol tablets in a childproof vial that rattled like dice. His clothes included jeans cut open at the left thigh where the EMT shears had gone through, a thermal shirt stiff with dried blood at the cuff, and one clean pair of socks from the clinic's charity closet. His keys were useless. His car was a compacted block at an impound lot thirty miles away, and even if it had still been drivable, RidgeLine owned more of it than he did.

He had enough weight in his hands to feel poor and enough empty space to feel erased.

The hallway outside his room carried that layered hospital smell of bleach, old coffee, and warmed plastic tubing. A TV in the waiting area ran a morning show no one was watching. The host laughed too hard at a blender demonstration while an elderly man in a neck brace slept upright with his mouth open. Eli took a seat by the vending machines because standing hurt and because sitting did not cost anything yet.

He opened the flip phone. There was one bar on the screen. Three messages from Nora sat there, all from the night before. Two more came from an unknown Colorado number. He listened to Nora's latest message with the volume low enough that no one else could hear.

"I got your text," she said. Her voice had that ICU-floor flatness, efficient from fatigue. "I'm glad you're alive. I need facts, Eli. Where are you, what injuries, and when you can transfer anything at all. I'm not asking for speeches. Call me with numbers."

There was no insult and no plea. It was just triage.

He closed the phone and held it in his palm until the warmth from his hand spread into the cheap plastic. He did not call her back. If he called with nothing, he'd be performing contrition and she would hear it. She had always heard it.

At the vending machine he bought a peanut-butter cracker pack because he could afford one and because swallowing pills on an empty stomach made his gut feel like wire. The machine rejected his first dollar and took his second. He ate slowly, each chew making his side flash. He timed breaths to the hum of the soda compressor.

On the bulletin board by the public phones, between flyers for church suppers and a missing blue heeler, somebody had pinned a half-sheet ad with greasy thumbprints at the corners:

**HOT SPRINGS MOTOR LODGE - WEEKLY RATES**

**NIGHT HELP NEEDED / FRONT DESK + HOUSE RUNNER**

**ASK FOR MARISOL**

A number was written in marker and crossed out once, then rewritten.

He copied it onto the margin of his discharge papers and dialed from the flip phone before he could overthink the movement. Four rings. A woman's voice answered, low and rough with morning.

"Front desk."

"Hi. I saw your help ad. My name's Eli. I'm at High Valley right now. I can do nights, desk, laundry, whatever you need."

A silence stretched long enough that he thought the call had dropped.

"You local?"

"No."

"Do you have a car?"

"Not anymore."

"Do you drink on shift?"

He looked at the vending machine glass and at himself reflected in it: adhesive strip on his forehead, stubble gone gray at the chin, collarbone shadowing like he had dropped weight in one night. "No."

"You lying to me?"

"Not about that."

Another beat passed.

"Come by at two," she said. "If you can walk from the bus stop, you can work enough to prove something. Don't come with a story. Come with ID."

She hung up.

He sat with the dead call tone in his ear and felt the negotiation settle in his body before his mind caught up. He had a place to go for one afternoon. That was more than he had when he woke up.

He changed in the bathroom stall, cursing quietly when he tried to pull the torn jeans over his left leg. A man at the sink glanced over once and looked away hard, the way people did in bus stations and emergency rooms when shame came into view. Eli used two paper towels to soak them, then folded one into a pad and tucked it under his shirt at the rib to keep fabric from scraping.

Outside, mountain sun hit cold and thin. He waited for the county bus under a shelter that rattled every time a truck came off the interstate ramp. A teenager in steel-toe boots sat at the other end scrolling his phone, grease up to his wrists, knuckles split. They did not speak. When the bus pulled up, Eli climbed the steps sideways so he would not twist his torso. The driver watched him do it and pretended not to notice.

The ride into Glenwood Springs traced the river and then bent past strip malls, welding supply yards, and a chain pharmacy with half its letters burned out. He used the window reflection to read his own face without claiming to. The scar on his forehead was not dramatic, just unmistakable: a pink hard seam over the right brow, fresh enough to shine.

The Hot Springs Motor Lodge sat one block off the main road behind a cracked signboard where two bulbs were dead in the word SPRINGS. The office windows were tinted green. The parking lot had an old Pepsi machine laid on its back beside the dumpster, doors tied shut with orange extension cord. Steam from the nearby mineral pools gave the whole block a wet-metal smell on top of bleach.

Inside the office, the air conditioner rattled louder than the radio. A woman stood behind the counter with a ledger open to a page of hand-written room numbers. Black hair in a loose braid, gray at the temples, no makeup, burn mark pale and shiny on her right forearm where her sleeve had slid back. She wore a motel polo under a denim overshirt with one cuff darkened by bleach. A yellow pencil sat tucked behind her ear. She pulled it out and tapped it against the counter once when he came in.

"Eli?"

"Yeah."

She looked at his forehead, then at the way he held his left elbow away from his ribs. "You got hit by weather or by a man?"

"Weather."

"Weather doesn't fill out incident reports." She held out her hand. "ID."

He passed his license across. She read it, looked back up, then turned the card over like she expected a trick on the back. "Pennsylvania," she said. "Long way to limp for this pay."

"I didn't pick the route."

"Nobody does, that's the lie everybody tells."

She slid the license back and pointed to a chair by the wall. The vinyl seat had a split that showed yellow foam. "Sit. Don't fall in my lobby."

He sat.

She kept standing, pencil against the ledger. "I had a night guy quit three days ago because he thought it was cute to skim cash from late check-ins. It wasn't cute. So here's what this job is. You run desk from ten p.m. to six a.m. You check IDs, take cards, set key packets, and you don't freelance policy. After midnight you strip vacated rooms the morning crew didn't get to, you haul laundry to the back machines, and you keep the coffee urn alive for workers rolling out before dawn. At four-thirty you start breakfast trays for the crews that pay extra."

"Okay."

"Don't answer okay yet. Listen first." She took the pencil and drew a line under an entry in the ledger. "Five nights this week to start. Forty hours if nothing breaks. Twelve dollars an hour cash on Fridays. If you miss a shift, no cash advance and no argument."

Eli nodded.

"Room fourteen is open. Weekly rate is four-twenty. If you work all five nights, I drop it to one-sixty and you pay that Thursday before shift. You trash the room, it's back to full rate plus damage. You smoke in there, full rate plus cleaning. You drink on shift, you're gone before sunrise. You bring anyone into that room without telling me, you're gone."

He felt each number click into place in his head like dowels. Four eighty in if he lasted the week. One sixty out for a bed and a door that locked. Three twenty left before food, bus fare, meds, and whatever tiny amount he could send east without insulting everyone.

"I can do it," he said.

Marisol leaned on the counter and watched him for a second that stretched. "You can do what?"

"Show up sober. Work the shifts. Keep my head down."

"No. Try again."

He swallowed. The cracker pack and ibuprofen sat heavy in his gut. "I can do this week."

Her mouth twitched, not a smile exactly. "That's better. Men who promise me months are men who disappear by Tuesday."

She opened a drawer and set out a single form, photocopied too many times so the lines were soft and gray. The form asked for name, emergency contact, medications, allergies, and the person to call if he was found unconscious. He stared at the last line.

"Need that?" he asked.

"Yeah," she said. "I had a line cook seize in the laundry room last winter and his people didn't know where to find him for ten hours. I don't do that again."

He wrote Theo's name and number. The pen dragged where the form toner had gone glossy.

Marisol took the sheet, scanned it, then pulled a key from a metal board and slid it toward him. The key fob was a green plastic trout with one eye rubbed off.

"Bathroom fan in fourteen screams like an animal," she said. "Kick the bottom of the housing twice and it quiets down. Towels are short because one guest thought six was a personality. If somebody asks for extra after midnight, make them bring dirty ones back first. I don't run a charity."

She rounded the counter and, before he could stand, crouched to lift a crate of rolled pool towels from the floor. She moved it one-handed onto the desk and nodded at it. "Show me."

"Show you what?"

"Show me you can move ten pounds without turning gray."

The crate was probably fifteen. He slid it off the desk and hugged it high against his chest, trying not to flare his ribs. Pain hit so hard his eyes watered. He set the crate down three steps away and kept his face blank.

Marisol watched him without flinching. "You're busted up."

"Cracked rib."

"Which side?"

"Left."

"Then don't try to be a hero with laundry bags. Use the cart and make two trips. I don't need brave. I need the job done and no blood on my tile."

He breathed shallow and waited for the hot stripe in his side to cool. "Got it."

"You got six tramadol in your pocket and you're already planning how to stretch them," she said. "Am I right?"

He looked at her.

She held up both hands. "I've managed workers a long time. It's not a psychic gift. If you have to take one, take it with food and set an alarm so you don't double-dose because you're tired. Keep the bottle at the desk, not in your room. Night minds lie."

"I know."

"Maybe you do. Maybe you don't. Either way, that's the rule now."

She stepped back behind the counter and flipped the ledger closed. The pencil went behind her ear again, precise as a ritual. "Shift starts at ten. Room's ready now. Sheets are clean. Blanket is not pretty but it's warm. You need a bus map, there's one taped by the coffee machine."

He started to thank her.

She lifted two fingers, stopping him. "Don't call this kindness. This is labor for room. I'm not your aunt and I'm not your confessor. Understand me."

"I do."

"Good. Then we won't fight tonight."

Room fourteen faced the back fence where the hot-springs steam drifted through chain link and made everything smell faintly of pennies and eggs. Inside, it had one bed with a quilt patterned in faded burgundy diamonds, a table bolted to the wall, a lamp with a bent shade, and carpet that had been cleaned so often it had gone stiff. The bathroom mirror had a crack in the lower right corner like a lightning bolt.

He set the green folder on the table and laid out his life in rows. He put his cash in one stack and his medications in another. He set the phone charger by the outlet that only worked if the plug was wedged with folded cardboard. He opened the atlas to the Colorado-Utah pages, where Theo's circles around cheap fuel stations were now useless without a car.

He took off his shirt and looked at his left side in the mirror. Purple and yellow bruising spread from the lower ribs like old storm clouds. A scrape ran across his hip where the seat belt had held and burned. He pressed the heel of his hand against the bruise until pain turned from sharp to dull. The clinic had not wrapped his chest, said it risked pneumonia. They had told him to breathe deep even when it hurt. He tried. The fourth breath made him cough and then he had to sit on the closed toilet lid and wait out the sparks in his vision.

He took half a tramadol, then ate half a packet of salted almonds from the vending machine Marisol had in the office because she had been right about food first. He set alarms on the flip phone for pill timing and for shift start. He did not trust his body clock anymore. The crash had knocked time loose in him; day and night came like rooms he stumbled into by mistake.

At eight-thirty he walked back to the office in clean jeans and the least stained shirt he owned. Marisol was at the counter with reading glasses low on her nose, doing the crossword from yesterday's paper between check-ins. She filled answers in pen, not pencil. A hard choice for a puzzle person.

"You early," she said without looking up.

"Couldn't sleep."

"Good. Then your first assignment is coffee. Machine in back. If you burn it, you drink it."

The back room held two industrial washers, one dryer that thumped every rotation, shelves of detergent, and a sink stained with rust around the drain. He measured grounds the way he used to measure audit risks: cautious, exact, trying not to overcorrect. The first batch was too weak. He dumped it and started over before she could taste it.

By ten-thirty two roofers from Grand Junction checked in, boots leaving mud crescents on the lobby tile. Eli copied IDs, ran cards, handed key packets without fumbling. Marisol watched from the office doorway, arms folded. When the men asked if the mineral pool was open all night, she answered without moving her eyes from Eli's hands.

"Pool closes at eleven. Quiet hours start at ten. You wake my long-stay folks, you're buying all their breakfasts."

One roofer laughed. The other did not. They took the keys and left.

At midnight she handed Eli a clipboard with room numbers marked in blue. "Two checkouts from construction crew, one from a family that left at dawn. Strip, bag, wipe hard surfaces, restock soaps. Don't tuck beds pretty; morning crew does corners. I care about clean, not decorative." She tapped room 22. "There's probably glitter in there from that bachelorette party. Glitter is herpes. It never dies."

He almost smiled.

The cleaning cart wheel stuck every third turn and squealed on concrete. In room 18 he stripped sheets damp with old sweat and chlorine. In room 22 glitter had indeed migrated to the sink, the coffee maker, and the floor vent as if it had reproduced. Bending to reach under the bed sent pain lancing through his left side so hard he had to brace both hands on the mattress and breathe through his teeth.

At 1:15 a.m. the flip phone alarm buzzed in his pocket. He took half a tramadol with water from the housekeeping sink. He sat on the toilet lid in room 22 until the wave eased, staring at the wallpaper border of little pine trees and moose heads. Somebody had drawn a cartoon penis on one moose with ballpoint pen years ago and housekeeping had never fully scrubbed it out.

By two, laundry waited in blue mesh bags bigger than his torso. The first bag he tried to shoulder made his knees buckle. He dropped it, cursed out loud, and looked around even though no one was there to witness it. He could hear interstate traffic beyond the back fence, a long hiss and brake chirp, strangers moving east and west with destinations that had names and arrivals.

He found the rolling cart and loaded the bag in sections, carrying pillowcases first, then sheets, then towels. Three trips instead of one. Marisol had been right. Pride was expensive.

Around 2:40 a.m. a man in a Broncos hoodie banged on the office window with his room key, face flushed and wet with pool steam.

"Ice machine ate my quarter," he said.

"Machine's free for guests," Eli said.

"Then it ate my dignity too."

"Try that sentence again sober."

The man blinked, then barked out a laugh and leaned against the counter. "You new?"

"Tonight."

"You don't look like motel people."

"Nobody does at first." Eli grabbed a backup bucket from under the desk and slid it over. "Here. On the house, as they say in houses that are not this one."

"You from around here?"

"Nope."

"Where from?"

"Desk side of this counter," Eli said, and the man laughed again, took the bucket, and left.

At 3:10 the roofers called down for extra towels. Eli held to rule.

"Bring me your dirty ones and I'll swap." 

"We're not hauling wet towels across the lot at three in the morning, man."

"Then you're waiting till six."

Silence on the other end, then a muttered "Jesus" and a click. Four minutes later one roofer appeared barefoot in the lobby carrying a dripping heap. Eli traded two clean towels and made himself keep his tone neutral. His old job voice came back without asking: policy as a shelter against argument.

At 3:30 he bent to pull a bag of trash from the office can and felt something grind under the broken rib. The bag slipped from his hands. Coffee grounds and pastry wrappers fanned across the floor tiles. He stood very still, staring down, because movement had become an equation with pain as the answer every time.

Marisol came out of the back hallway with a mop handle over one shoulder. She took in the trash on the floor, his face, the way he was holding his breath.

"Sit," she said.

"I can clean it."

"You can in two minutes. Sit for thirty seconds now or ten minutes later." She pointed to the chair by the vending alcove.

He sat, not gracefully.

She knelt and scooped the worst of the coffee grounds with gloved hands, then stood and handed him the dustpan. "You don't have to prove anything to the tile," she said. "Tile does not care."

"Feels like everybody else does."

"Everybody else is asleep." She tipped her chin toward the hall. "Look."

The motel was briefly still. He heard only the dryer thump and highway hiss. Fluorescent light buzzed over the desk, turning the laminated tourist brochures flat and ugly. In the quiet he could hear his own breathing, ragged and shallow, like he had been running uphill with a box.

"Pain at what number?" she asked.

"Seven. Maybe eight when I twist."

"Take the other half now. Then for the rest of shift you work from chest height. No floor scrubbing. No lifting wet bags solo. You prep trays and monitor desk. I finish the haul."

He looked up fast. "That's your work too."

"Yes," she said. "And right now my work includes not having you in an ambulance again before payday."

He felt heat climb his neck, embarrassment hotter than pain. "I'm not asking for special treatment."

"Good, because you're not getting that. You're getting allocation. Learn the difference."

He opened the pill bottle with both hands and dry-swallowed the half tablet. It scraped down his throat. She watched to make sure he did not chase it with coffee.

At four-thirty he laid out breakfast trays with wrapped muffins, bruised apples, yogurt cups, and plastic knives that bent if you looked at them wrong. His movements slowed and became deliberate. He reached, placed, wrote room numbers, and breathed. Then he repeated the same sequence. The work became a metronome.

The first early crew came through at five in reflective jackets and hardhats, smelling of diesel and wintergreen dip. One of them nodded at the trays.

"You the new guy?"

"Yeah."

"Coffee's decent. Last dude made motor oil."

"Noted."

The man peeled a banana, glanced at Eli's scar, and did not ask questions. The mercy of that landed harder than praise.

By five-thirty Eli could feel the tramadol flattening edges rather than killing pain. The rib still hurt every time he reached for the upper shelf. It felt like someone had tied a knot around his left lung and pulled one end with each inhale. But he was upright. The shift had shape, with a beginning, a middle, and an end. He had not left.

Marisol reappeared with damp hair and a fresh shirt, as if she had changed in one of the staff bathrooms. She carried two small paper cups of coffee and set one beside him.

"You didn't steal from my drawer," she said. "You didn't flirt with guests. You didn't die. That's a passing grade for night one."

"High standards."

"I like attainable standards."

She sipped and winced. "Still a little bitter. Use less at first scoop."

"Got it."

She set her cup down and rubbed the burn mark on her forearm absentmindedly with her thumb, a quick back-and-forth motion that looked practiced. "You call your people yet?"

He looked at the logbook. "No."

"You're going to have to. The silence bill keeps compounding whether you look at it or not."

He almost told her that bills were all he looked at now. Mortgage arrears. Tuition. The clinic balance with its calm font. Instead he said, "I know."

She gave a short nod, accepting the limit. "Clock out at six. Sleep. Be back tonight ten sharp. If you're late, call before, not after. I hate after."

At six-oh-two he locked the office drawer, handed her the key ring, and walked back to room fourteen with the sun coming up pale over the ridge. A delivery truck idled by the dumpster, back door open, crates of eggs stacked to the roof. The driver smoked with one hand and scrolled his phone with the other, smoke drifting sweet and chemical in the cold.

Inside room fourteen, Eli took off his shoes and lowered himself onto the bed in stages, palms flat, breath counted. The quilt smelled of industrial detergent and old heat. He put the green folder on his chest like a weight plate and opened it.

He wrote numbers on the back of the clinic discharge sheet. Week one motel wages if he worked full shifts would be $480. Room cost if he worked full shifts would be $160. Bus fare, food, laundry soap, and over-the-counter pain meds would run maybe $95. A possible transfer east, if there was no extra damage and no missed nights, would be $225.

He stared at the last line until it blurred. Two hundred twenty-five dollars was an insult to what he had already burned through. It was also more than zero, and zero was what he had been sending in every direction for weeks.

He opened the flip phone and typed a text to Nora.

Working nights at a motel near Glenwood Springs. Cracked rib, no car. First pay Friday. I can send a small transfer then. I'll call with exact amount Thursday.

He read it twice for performance, deleted "small," and sent.

Then he opened a new message to Theo.

Using your atlas still. Job tonight onward. Put your number as emergency contact with motel manager. Sorry for that surprise.

He sent that too.

He sent no message to June. He looked at her name and closed the phone.

He set two alarms, one for medication and one for 8:45 p.m. The bathroom fan screamed when he turned it on. He kicked the housing twice, and it stopped, exactly as promised.

He stood at the sink and washed the coffee grounds from his hands. A black crescent of grime came out from under his thumbnail and spiraled down the drain. The mirror gave him back a face he recognized in pieces: the old forehead under the new seam, the mouth that could still make rules sound reasonable, the eyes that had spent weeks looking past exits.

When he lay down, the rib woke again in small deliberate stabs that matched his heartbeat. He put a folded towel under his left side to keep from rolling and watched morning light crawl across the cracked bathroom tile. Outside, a guest dragged a hard-shell suitcase over concrete, the wheels rattling like bones in a cup.

He had a key, a shift, a price, and a woman at the desk who would throw him out if he tried to convert pity into credit.

It was not redemption. It was a routine.

For now, routine was the only kind of shelter he could afford.
