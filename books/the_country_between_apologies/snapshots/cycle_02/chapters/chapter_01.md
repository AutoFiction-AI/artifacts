# Chapter 1

The dorm loading lane had turned into a stalled parade of hatchbacks and rental SUVs, everyone idling under a heat that felt sticky and metallic. A volunteer in a neon vest kept lifting both palms and calling, "Blue pass to the right, green pass keep left, don't block the fire lane," like he was directing aircraft. Eli kept one hand on the steering wheel and one on the stack of color-coded move-in passes June had printed and clipped together. The dashboard clock read 9:30. The paper edges had softened from sweat.

June had one knee up on the seat, lacing and unlacing the same running shoe while she watched the chaos through the windshield. Her dark hair was braided tight today, practical, and the little nick through her left eyebrow caught when she turned into the light. She looked like she was about to warm up for a meet, not move into a brick building where she did not need anyone to wake her up ever again.

"You still want me carrying the fridge?" Eli asked.

"I want you not throwing your knee out trying to prove anything," she said. "You can do the lighter bins and the fan. I'll take the fridge with whoever's nearest and not seventy."

"Fifty-two is not seventy."

"Right. Sorry. Very youthful fifty-two."

She smiled when she said it, quick and affectionate, already halfway somewhere else. Eli smiled back and heard himself doing the tone he used at board meetings, dry and easy, like nothing was harder than finding parking.

"For the record," he said, "I'm in peak moving shape. I stretched this morning."

"That's terrifying."

The line lurched. He eased the sedan forward six feet. The trunk was packed tight enough that the green document folder had to ride in the back seat beside him, buckled in under a bag of extra towels. Tuition statements, immunization forms, housing contract, copies of everything because Nora trusted paper more than apps. Mortgage letter, too, folded into the back sleeve because mail did not stop arriving just because your daughter went to college. He kept glancing at the folder as if it could spill.

A student volunteer tapped his window and pointed them toward the parking garage under the dorm. "One cart per family, thirty-minute unload, then move your car," she said. She had a strip of silver stars under each eye and a whistle lanyard crowded with enamel frogs. "If you need command hooks, table six. If you need a screwdriver, ask Malik by the elevators, not me. I broke two yesterday and got demoted."

"Noted," June said, laughing.

Eli thanked her. She jogged to the next car without waiting for anything else.

The garage ramp was tight and steep. Eli's right knee complained when he climbed out and bent to pop the trunk. Before he could get the latch fully up, June had already swung the mini fridge onto the dolly with a freshman boy in a Pitt baseball cap who introduced himself as Len and immediately apologized for the sweat running off his chin.

"My mom made me wear this hat for sun," Len said, wiping his forehead with the heel of his palm. "Now I'm just cooking in it."

"Take it off, then," June said.

"I can't. She does visual checks."

Len's mother appeared a minute later, short and square-shouldered, carrying a cardboard caddy packed with spices in labeled jars. She set it down like breakable glass and told Len, "Paprika top rack, cumin second, and label whatever you leave in the fridge. I'm not decoding mystery containers in October." Then she turned to Eli and June with a bright, tired smile. "First one to college. I packed him a rice cooker, three extension cords, and a fire blanket. If anything sparks, he calls maintenance before he calls me."

"You're doing great," Eli said, and he sounded convincing enough that she laughed.

He did the same thing two more times on the way to the elevator. Good luck. Big day. You must be proud. He heard himself giving out tiny, polished lines like business cards and watched other adults do the same. Everyone was one nervous joke away from crying. Nobody wanted to be first.

Up on the fourth floor, the hallway smelled like cardboard dust and industrial cleaner. Doors stood open with names taped crookedly at eye level. June's room had one window facing a wall of old maples and a view of the loading lane where cars looked toy-small and temporary.

Her roommate was kneeling on a quilt, sorting pens into a ceramic mug with hand-painted fish around the rim. "Hi," she said without standing. "I'm Sloane. Sorry this looks intense. If I don't sort these now, they migrate, then I can't find the 0.5s, then -- whatever, bad day. Blue-black, black-black, weird colors, dead ones."

She wore giant noise-canceling headphones around her neck like a collar, one ear cup held together with pink electrical tape. On the bed behind her sat a clear plastic shoe box full of tea bags arranged by mood: Focus, Calm, Emergency.

"June," June said. "That's very organized and also terrifying."

"Terrifying is fair."

Eli introduced himself and set down two bins by the empty desk. Sloane's father stuck his head in behind him, tall and sunburned, with a campus map folded into exact quarters in his shirt pocket. He apologized for being gone so long because he had gone looking for ice and come back with five granola bars and no ice. Sloane finally looked up from her pen system and said, "Dad, bars on the shelf, map in your pocket. We're fine." He took that with practiced grace.

They made the room in layers: mattress pad, fitted sheet, lamp, fan, power strip, milk crate under the lofted bed. June moved fast, deciding in one glance where everything belonged. She hung a string of postcard-sized prints over her desk and stepped back, head tilted. Eli held out the tape without being asked. He could still do useful things in this world if he stayed quiet and paid attention.

At noon, an RA named Tasha stopped by with a clipboard and a voice that carried over two open doors. She had chipped black nail polish and a tiny silver key hanging from one hoop earring. "Floor meeting at six, mandatory. Quiet hours start Sunday, not tonight. If your mini fridge leaks, text the number on this card. I don't see DMs until after midnight. Parents, parking enforcement starts in twelve minutes, so move your cars now if you haven't."

"They're efficient," Eli said.

"It's plate readers," Tasha said. "Ticket first, tow second."

June snorted. "Okay, that's terrifying."

"Good," Tasha said. "Drink water. Keep your door cracked till ten if you want people to meet you. If you need me, knock." Then she moved on, tapping the next door frame with her pen.

By one o'clock the room looked inhabited. June's running shoes were lined up in military order under the bed, except for one pair with ripped laces she refused to throw out. Her phone lay face down on the desk buzzing every thirty seconds with group-chat notifications. Through the open window came a steady mix of horns, rolling carts, and parents pretending they had not just gotten lost in the same three-block loop.

Nora texted while Eli was flattening empty boxes in the hallway.

`How's room setup?`

He sent back a photo of June and Sloane wrestling a duvet into a cover, both of them cursing and laughing.

`Good. Organized chaos.`

Three dots appeared, vanished, returned.

`Thanks. Need ETA later. I start at 7 tomorrow and June's med forms are in your folder.`

Then, after a pause:

`Also trash night. Don't forget.`

The phone warmed in Eli's palm while his jaw locked on that last line. He pictured the kitchen at home exactly as it would look tonight: white envelope from the mortgage servicer on the counter where Nora left important mail, reusable grocery bag hanging from the pantry knob, June's cereal still on the top shelf because she said dining hall food made her feel like she was eating inside a fluorescent bulb. The ordinary weight of the place sat on him harder than the mini fridge had.

He typed, deleted, typed again.

`I'll be back before dark.`

A lie smooth enough to send in two seconds.

He slipped the phone into his pocket and kept breaking down boxes until the cardboard edges burned his thumb.

At two, June checked the orientation app and announced that she had exactly forty-five minutes before her hall welcome. "There's this thing on conflict resolution and consent and where to get free printing," she said. "You don't have to sit through any of that, by the way. Parents have their own programming if you want to learn how to let go and cry in public."

"I'll pass," Eli said.

"Coward."

"Strategist."

She shoved a roll of tape into his chest. "Can you take this and the trash down? We have no floor space left and I'm trying not to start college in a landfill."

"On it."

When he came back, she was standing in the center of the room with her hands on her hips, scanning everything one last time. She nodded to herself in a way that was so Nora it made his throat hurt. Then she grabbed her lanyard, checked for her ID card, and turned to him.

"Okay. So."

"So."

"You don't have to do the weird drawn-out goodbye," she said. "I'm not dying."

"I had a speech prepared."

"Please don't."

"It was very moving. People in the hall were going to clap."

"Dad."

She stepped in and hugged him hard, quickly, one squeeze and out. Not little-kid clinging. Not performative, either. Just exact.

"Thank you for all this," she said into his shirt. "For real."

"You're welcome."

He opened his mouth on a sentence he could not hand her in a hallway full of strangers and open doors and somebody's speaker blasting old Kendrick down the corridor: I moved fourteen thousand out of emergency this morning, and if I keep driving tonight I might not turn back.
His jaw locked. He bit the inside of his cheek until he tasted blood, then swallowed.

She pulled back and looked up at him, eyes sharp now. "You're going straight home, right? Mom said tomorrow's a mess and she needs the forms tonight."

The question came out casual, but she watched him like she watched race splits on her phone, waiting for the number that told the truth.

"Yeah," he said. "I'll head out as soon as I get the car."

"Cool. Text when you're back so she doesn't spiral."

"I will."

"And don't do that thing where you stop for gas and accidentally buy, like, twelve jerky sticks and call it dinner."

"I hate that you know me this well."

"I know your sins." She kissed his cheek, already moving toward the door. "Love you."

"Love you too, Junie."

She winced at the nickname but did not argue, which felt like mercy.

He watched her join a cluster of freshmen in the hall. She slid into their rhythm in under ten steps, matching their pace and jokes, already half turned toward a girl in a thrifted denim jacket who was explaining where to find the cheapest coffee before eight a.m. June did not look back.

Eli stood in the doorway a little too long until Sloane's father came in carrying an armful of hangers and said, "You all set?"

"Yeah," Eli said, stepping aside. "All set."

The elevator down was full of parents holding empty bins like props from a finished play. A woman in scrubs with a tote bag that read NURSES ARE A FORCE OF NATURE kept dabbing under her eyes with the back of her wrist and laughing at herself each time. A man in golf clothes was trying to set up autopay for his son's meal card and swearing quietly at the app because it kept rejecting a security code by one digit. Eli recognized the panic in both of them and gave neither of them anything except a small nod.

In the garage, the air was ten degrees cooler and smelled like hot rubber and damp concrete. He loaded the empty bins into the trunk, slid behind the wheel, and closed the door. The sound in the car changed immediately. Outside noise flattened into a hum. He could hear his own breathing and the faint rattle in the passenger door that Nora had been telling him to fix for six months.

He turned the key to accessory but not ignition and sat there.

A shuttle bus hissed to a stop two levels up. Tires chirped. Someone dropped a metal water bottle and cursed. Time moved in chunks. He watched people leave with blank carts and return with children and lamps and rolled rugs, then leave again alone.

On the floor behind the passenger seat he spotted a crushed granola-bar wrapper from June's last track meet, still wedged under the mat despite two vacuum runs. There was a ponytail elastic looped around the seat-belt buckle, stretched and translucent from sun. A yellow highlighter cap had fused to the cup-holder liner in August heat and would never come out cleanly. He had spent years driving this car to school pickups, urgent-care visits, weekend grocery runs where June raided the cart for cherries before checkout. He had cursed the crumbs and receipts and sock lint. Now the mess looked like proof that someone had lived here with him, and the thought of cleaning it all out tonight felt like stepping into a room after the music stopped.

His phone lit up.

`Nora: You rolling?`

His thumb hovered over the keyboard, then dropped. By the time he finally exhaled, the screen had dimmed.

At 7:12 this morning, before they left the house, he had stood barefoot in the kitchen with the refrigerator humming and moved fourteen thousand dollars out of the emergency account into the old personal checking account he had not used in years. His thumb had hovered over confirm long enough for the banking app to time out once. He had logged back in and done it anyway.

He could still see the balance shift as clearly as if the numbers were painted on the windshield.

He had told himself it was temporary, a forty-eight-hour buffer. In plain language, he had moved fourteen thousand dollars out of the family emergency account, lied to Nora about leaving campus, and was preparing to miss Monday's audit call while mortgage arrears and June's tuition deadline sat in the green folder beside him. His shirt stuck to his back. His pulse thudded high in his throat.

The green folder was on the passenger seat now, buckled in where June had been. A corner of the mortgage envelope stuck out of the sleeve. He pulled it free, unfolded it, read the due date he already knew, then folded it back with careful edges and slid it in again. He checked June's med forms. He checked the tuition bill. He checked a printout of his own calendar from work with Monday's audit call circled at 8:00 a.m. in Nora's neat handwriting.

He set everything back where it had been and did not start the car.

Nora texted at 3:18.

`Need a real ETA, Eli. I've got to pack lunch and chart before sleep.`

He could hear her saying it, not angry yet, just narrowing the frame to tasks because that was how she kept anxiety from turning feral. He typed,

`Just leaving campus now. Traffic was locked up.`

That was the first deception that felt like an action and not a sentence. He sent it and sat another twenty minutes in the same parking space.

A family in matching orientation T-shirts loaded back into a minivan three spots over. The father had a sunburn line around his wedding ring where he must have taken it off for golf all summer. He waved at Eli through the windshield with that reflexive driver-to-driver politeness and then backed out, careful, patient, gone.

Eli took his own ring in his fingers and turned it once. The silver edge pressed a pale groove into skin that had shaped itself around metal for twenty-two years.

He started the engine at 3:47.

He drove out of the garage and followed signs through campus traffic that moved like clogged blood. Students crossed against lights in packs. A city bus cut in front of him and stalled half in the lane while the driver lowered a ramp for a passenger with a rolling walker. Eli waited. Nobody honked. He drummed the steering wheel with his thumb and watched heat shimmer above the hood.

At a red light near the bridge, his phone buzzed with a new message from June.

`Forgot to ask - did you leave the Brita pitcher in my desk crate or on the floor by bed?`

`Left side of the desk crate.` he typed.

`hero`

Then:

`Text mom when you're home pls. She's pretending she's chill and she's not chill.`

His throat tightened on the three words, gratitude and accusation braided together so tightly he could not separate them.

`Will do.`

He did not put the phone back into the cup holder right away. He held it in his palm until the light changed and the car behind him gave one polite chirp.

By the time he reached the interstate split, the sky had gone that late-afternoon white that made every sign too bright. Eastbound lanes rose to the right, the direction of home, hospital badges, garbage bins at the curb, the couch indentation where Nora always fell asleep on her side after a double shift. Westbound carried him toward the airport, then farther, names on signs he had never needed for anything.

His phone lit up in the cup holder: Nora calling.

He had taken this interchange hundreds of times. His body knew the right lane by memory. This time he stayed middle through the first decision point, then left as if he were avoiding a slow truck, then left again while the phone rattled against hard plastic.

A tanker truck boxed him in on the left and an on-ramp dumped a line of cars into his lane on the right. He checked his mirror, signaled, and eased onto the wider westbound artery with Nora still calling.

He could still answer and say it cleanly: I took fourteen thousand from emergency, I'm not coming home tonight, and I'm leaving you with mortgage letters and tuition deadlines until I decide to turn around.

The eastbound exit slid past in his side mirror and disappeared.

He let the first call die. She called right back.

He did not pick up.

He kept going.

Outside the city the lanes widened and the noise changed from stop-and-go brakes to steady interstate roar, tires on old seams in the asphalt making a rhythm that settled into his chest. Billboards took over the horizon: injury lawyers, casino buses, a church with SERVICE TIMES in red LEDs missing one segment so S looked like 5. On the shoulder, a shredded retread lay like a dead snake. The car smelled like stale fries and lemon cleaner from the dashboard wipes in the glove box, with a faint fermented edge where June had spilled cold brew in the cup holder that morning.

On the first long curve, the green folder crept toward the edge of the passenger seat and he shoved it back with two fingers without taking his eyes off the road.

He drove with both hands on the wheel, back straight, speed exactly at the limit. If anyone looked in, they would see a competent middle-aged man in a clean polo, a green folder on the passenger seat, and nothing visibly wrong.

By the time her second call dropped to voicemail, the city skyline was gone behind him. The car pointed west and held there.

Eli Mercer kept driving.
