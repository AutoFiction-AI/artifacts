# Chapter 15
The load card above Dryer Three listed king sheets, forty-two pounds, and no bleach. Eli read it only after he pressed his left hand to the warm steel and judged the heat for himself. At one-forty in the morning, the laundry corridor under the casino sounded like weather trapped in a pipe: washers banging into high spin, ductwork answering with a deep metallic moan that ran the length of the room.

His rib caught when he bent for the lint trap. The old crack on his left side did not stab anymore, but it still gripped him if he twisted wrong, a hard wire under the skin that yanked his breath short. He waited it out, one palm on his thigh, then stood and shut the trap with his forearm. Keep earning. Keep moving. Keep his head clear enough to send money east on Friday and another piece on Tuesday. He had texted Deena he'd cover extra audit after laundry; the night had no soft edge left in it. If he treated every shift like numbers on a page, he could stay in front of shame for another week.

Mateo Rivas rolled a linen cart past him and whistled the same four bars from a norteno song he had never finished. He always stopped before the melody turned sad. The glitter bracelet on his wrist flashed in the fluorescent light, cheap pink plastic beads with SOFIA spelled in silver letters. His daughter had made it at school, he had said on Eli's first night, and he wore it even when the floor supervisor called it unsanitary and told him to cut it off.

"You're stacking those too high," Mateo said, nodding at Eli's cart. "You hit that door frame again, Ms. Kline's gonna act like it's an assault on corporate property."

"Then you'll have to testify for me," Eli said.

Mateo grinned and bared the chipped incisor he kept promising to fix when overtime finally stuck. "For twenty bucks and a coffee, I'm your star witness."

They moved in parallel for the next hour, two men in gray uniforms with their names heat-pressed above the pocket, passing each other at the ironer and the supply cage without speaking much. Eli liked Mateo because he talked in useful specifics and then shut up. He did not ask where Eli came from. He did not perform concern. He liked to fold pillowcases into perfect squares and line them by size, because that small order mattered to him.

At two-thirty-five, Eli crossed to the audit desk upstairs to cover the hour between slot counts and room reconciliations. The casino floor had thinned to insomniac regulars and men who had lost enough money that they played slower, trying to reverse arithmetic by force of care. He keyed in room charges from handwritten slips, checked signatures against ID scans, and printed a folio for a drunk bachelor party that had tried to expense three trays of wings to a room already checked out. The front glass doors breathed cold each time they opened. Outside, Reno wind dragged grit across the parking lot in long, dry scratches.

Mateo came up once for guest towels and stood beside Eli's chair longer than needed, rubbing his sternum through his shirt.

"You all right?" Eli asked.

"Heartburn," Mateo said. "That burrito from the machine was a felony."

"Take ten." Eli tapped the clock in the corner of his monitor. "We have room."

"If Kline catches me sitting, she'll write me like a parking ticket." Mateo gave Eli a look that tried to make it a joke. It missed by a fraction. "I'll hit the men's room and get back down."

He went through the service door and disappeared toward the employee hallway.

Eli kept entering charges. At 2:41 and again at 2:44, he glanced at the clock and kept entering charges. He heard the floor shift in layers: slot chimes, a woman laughing too hard near blackjack, vacuum motors from housekeeping doing a late pass under the lobby chairs. At 2:49, Reina from housekeeping came around the corner with a roll of trash liners tucked under her arm and a smear of blue disinfectant on one cheek.

"You saw Mateo?" she asked.

Eli looked up. "He went to the restroom."

"He's in there too long." She bounced once on her heels, not from impatience but from the way she always moved when worried, quick weight shifts like a boxer between rounds. "I knocked twice. No answer."

The clock in Eli's screen ticked to 2:50. He stood too fast, felt the rib catch, and waited half a breath before following her through the service corridor.

The employee restroom sat behind a coded door near the break room, where the smell of fryer oil mixed with industrial lemon cleaner. The light inside hummed. One stall door was shut. Under it, Mateo's shoes pointed at the tile, toes inward.

"Mateo," Eli said, knuckles to the metal partition. "Hey. You with me?"

Nothing.

He knocked harder. The door rattled but the lock held.

Reina was already backing up, hand on her radio. "I call security."

"Call 911 first." Eli kept his voice flat because flat voices made other people move. "Tell them possible overdose, male, thirties, not responsive, employee restroom near south service elevator." He hit the door with his shoulder. Pain flared along his left side and down his back, bright enough to blur the edges of the sink. The latch gave halfway but jammed against dead weight.

He shoved again. The door opened four inches. He rammed it once more and got twelve. Mateo lay wedged between the toilet and the partition, knees folded under him, cheek against the tile. His skin had the gray cast of paper left in rain. His lips were blue at the center.

Eli dropped beside him. "Mateo. Open your eyes." He pressed two fingers against Mateo's neck and felt for pulse through sweat and stubble and his own shaking hand. There was something, a thin flutter that vanished when he pressed too hard. He eased up and it came back, slow.

Reina's voice snapped over the radio in rapid English and Spanish, then she looked at Eli. "Ambulance coming. Security says they're bringing the kit."

"Good. Unlock that outer door and keep people out." Eli tipped Mateo's head back and lifted his chin. A sour chemical smell sat under the lemon cleaner. There was residue on the sink edge, white streaks dragged thin with water. Mateo's chest barely rose.

Eli put his ear near Mateo's mouth. There was faint air, and it was not enough.

He had done mock drills for years in conference rooms with carpet squares and laminated response cards while people checked email under the table. This was not that. Mateo's hand was cooling fast against the tile. The SOFIA bracelet had twisted to the inside of his wrist, beads digging into skin.

"Come on," Eli said, pitching his voice low so it might reach him before the words did.

Boots slapped in the hallway. Darryl from security came in with a red overdose pouch, breath sawing hard enough to wake a faint whistle in his hearing aid. He dropped to his knees before the radio chatter finished.

"Naloxone," Darryl said, already kneeling. "You got respirations?"

"Slow. Pulse is there but weak."

"Give one now." Darryl ripped the plastic open and shoved the spray into Eli's hand. "Tilt his head. Full press."

Eli slid the nozzle into Mateo's right nostril and pressed until it clicked. He counted out loud because counting kept panic from grabbing the wheel.

"One thousand one. One thousand two."

Nothing changed. Mateo's jaw stayed slack, breath still shallow and far apart.

"Mask," Darryl said.

Reina handed over the pocket mask from the wall first-aid box with hands that shook only at the fingertips. Eli sealed it over Mateo's mouth and nose and gave two slow breaths, watching for chest rise. The first barely moved him. The second lifted more.

"Again," Darryl said, tapping the second hand on his taped watch. "Two minutes. If he doesn't wake, second dose."

Eli breathed for Mateo, steady and deliberate, forcing himself not to rush. The tile dug through his pant leg into his knee. His left rib burned each time he leaned forward, and sweat ran down his spine under the uniform shirt. The hallway noise narrowed to this room, this chest that would not decide whether to keep working.

A memory cut in without warning: Theo at nineteen on the basement futon in Pittsburgh, eyes half-open and unfocused, mumbling that he was fine while his speech smeared together. Eli had stood in the doorway with his briefcase still in his hand, telling himself it was probably weed, probably nothing, because he had an early audit and no room left in the day for catastrophe. Nora had found the pill bottle an hour later, with an orange cap and no label.

"Eli." Darryl's voice pulled him back. "Second dose."

Eli switched nostrils and pressed again.

Mateo jerked once, a full-body shudder that knocked his heel against the stall base. For one second Eli thought that was it, a recovery script exactly how the training video sold it. Then Mateo retched hard, thick fluid and bile across the tile and Eli's sleeve. His eyes flew open but did not focus.

"Roll him. Roll him now." Darryl and Eli turned him onto his side, clearing his airway while Mateo coughed wetly and tried to push them off with weak, angry motions.

"Easy," Eli said. "You're okay. Stay on your side."

"Don't," Mateo rasped, then coughed again so hard his shoulders shook. "Don't call my mom."

"Nobody's calling anybody yet," Darryl said, firm as a drill sergeant and gentler than his words. "You breathe. That's your only job."

Sirens rose outside and cut at the curb. Two paramedics came in with a stretcher and monitor bag, moving fast but not frantic. The woman at the head introduced herself as Nia, a crescent-moon tattoo tucked behind her left ear, and dropped to Mateo's side without looking away from his chest. Her partner clipped a pulse oximeter to Mateo's finger and called out numbers that made Eli's stomach go cold.

"Sat seventy-nine. Resp six and shallow."

Nia looked at Eli. "What did he take?"

"Unknown. Found down in stall around two-fifty. Two intranasal naloxone doses, about three minutes apart. Rescue breaths with barrier mask."

"Any trauma?"

"No head strike that I saw."

She nodded once, all business, then spoke to Mateo in a voice meant to cut through fog. "Mateo, listen. You're at Silver Mesa. You overdosed. We're putting oxygen on you and taking you in."

Mateo tried to sit up. Nia put a hand to his shoulder and kept him down with controlled pressure.

"Sofia," he said, or maybe only the first syllable.

The bracelet glittered again when he reached for air.

The medics loaded him, secured straps, asked Darryl for camera timestamps, and wheeled him out through the service corridor. The whole thing took nine minutes. It felt like one long inhalation that never turned into exhale.

Eli stayed on one knee after the stretcher cleared the hallway, hand braced on wet tile, waiting for his pulse to come down. It did not. When he pushed himself up, the room tipped and he caught the sink with his forearm hard enough to jar the rib. He bent over the toilet and dry-heaved until his throat burned.

When he straightened, Mateo's vomit was still on his sleeve and shoes. Reina had the mop bucket in place, gloves hanging from one finger.

"No," Eli said, reaching for the roll of red biohazard bags. "I'm not leaving this all on you."

They worked in silence for three minutes, bagging soaked paper towels and gloves while the exhaust fan buzzed over them. Eli scrubbed his forearms to the elbow in the sink until the skin went blotchy and raw, then stood there staring at the running water like he had forgotten what came next.

"I got the rest," Reina said quietly. "Go do your report before she starts."

She meant Kline.

Ms. Kline waited by the audit desk with a clipboard tucked against her chest, thumb flicking its edge in a fast metronome. One open tin of cinnamon mints sat by her elbow.

"Mr. Mercer," she said, using the formal tone she used when she wanted witnessable professionalism, "I need a written statement with exact times and actions. Corporate risk's going to ask for sequence before dawn."

Eli nodded and sat, because standing made his leg shake. He typed while she dictated prompts.

2:49 notified by housekeeping of unresponsive employee. 2:50 attempted contact. 2:51 forced entry. 2:52 called 911 via housekeeping radio. 2:53 naloxone dose one. 2:56 naloxone dose two. 2:59 EMS arrival. 3:08 transfer to stretcher.

He knew how this language worked. It converted panic into bullet points and stripped every sentence down to defendable verbs. It mattered. It kept people employed. It kept lawsuits contained. It also made him want to put his fist through the monitor.

Kline read over his shoulder. "Good. Add that security officer Darryl Pike assisted with airway management. Keep it factual, no conjecture regarding substances."

"I know," Eli said.

Her eyes flicked to him, registering tone. "I know that was rough. We still need your station reopened. The front desk line is already backed to the velvet ropes."

Eli's jaw locked. He tabbed into the wrong field twice, backspaced hard enough to tear a line in the printout, and heard the policy distance in every uncontracted word.

Eli looked past her to the camera feed wall. In one screen, slot machines blinked red and gold in absolute indifference. In another, two guests argued over a taxi. Life had resealed over the crack before the body was even out of the building.

"Give me ten minutes," he said.

"You have three."

"I need ten."

Kline pressed her lips together, then checked the staff board clipped near the printer. "You're still in probation status. Refusal to work assigned post can be coded as abandonment."

He almost laughed at the word. Abandonment had become the coin everyone carried for him, stamped with different logos depending on who was spending it.

"Code it however you want," he said. "I'm taking ten."

He stood before she could answer and walked through the service exit into the loading dock alley, where cold desert air hit the sweat on his shirt and turned it to film. The dumpsters hummed from compactors cycling two bays down. A truck idled at the dock, reverse alarm chirping in slow, patient beeps while a driver in a ski cap smoked and stared at nothing.

Eli sat on an overturned milk crate by the cinder-block wall and put his head in his hands. His palms smelled like latex, bleach, and somebody else's vomit. He rubbed them on his pant legs until the skin burned.

He had seen people in crisis before, professionally, in antiseptic offices and committee meetings where the stakes arrived as charts. He had reviewed wrong medication counts, unattended bed alarms, and residents found in stairwells. He had written corrective-action memos with verbs like mitigate and remediate. He had believed, on his best and worst days, that if he could keep sequence tight he could hold collapse at the edges.

Inside the restroom, sequence had mattered. It had also not been enough. Mateo might still die by sunrise. The body did not negotiate with checklists.

Another memory opened, uglier this time because he had spent years sanding it down. Theo at twenty-two, three months before rehab, locked in the upstairs bathroom at home while Nora pounded the door with the heel of her hand hard enough to bruise. Eli had been on speakerphone with his boss in the hallway, whispering that he might be ten minutes late, trying to keep his voice neutral because he hated sounding out of control. Theo had finally opened the door with pupils like pinholes and a nosebleed he kept wiping with toilet paper that disintegrated across his fingers. Eli had said, "Get cleaned up," like they were late to church.

He bent forward until his forehead touched his knees. Gravel pressed through the thin fabric at his ankles. He had told himself his job required composure, that somebody had to keep income steady, that panic did not fix addiction. All of that had been partly true and mostly convenient.

The burn mark above his eyebrow itched where scar tissue tightened in cold. He scratched at it and looked at his watch. 3:19 a.m.

His first urge was old and automatic: lock this down, get back upstairs, finish shift, send money, never mention tonight to anybody who could use it as proof against him. He could already hear the script forming in his own voice. He had handled it. Everybody was fine. He could get back to work.

He saw Mateo's bracelet again, cheap beads cutting half-moons into skin while Eli counted breaths.

Everybody fine was a lie that arrived before evidence.

The second urge came slower, which made him distrust it less. Call Theo before the shift swallowed the night and turned this into another private file in his head. Call while his hands still shook. Ask for structure he did not control.

He hated how much that sounded like surrender. He hated that surrender might be the only useful word left.

His flip phone sat in his locker beside the green folder. He went back in through the dock, ignored Kline's glance like a held blade, and walked to the staff room. The folder was bulging with crumpled wire receipts, debt notices, and the copy of Nora's voicemail transcript he had printed at the public library because hearing it once had not been enough punishment. The pages tracked mortgage arrears, the medical plan, and June's meal plan cut.

He pulled the phone, the folder, and Theo's old road atlas from the locker. The atlas was soft at the corners now, pages dog-eared and stained with coffee, Theo's handwriting in the margins where he had marked cheap motels and truck-stop showers. At the back, Eli had started listing transfers with dates and amounts in pen that skipped on damp paper. The list looked like a child's attempt at order: 180, 240, 90, 300. It had no cadence and no system, only guilt converted to whenever-he-could.

He went to the employee stairwell where the camera had a blind angle under the landing and called Theo.

It rang five times. On six, Theo answered with ambient warehouse noise behind him, forklift beep and an air compressor kicking on.

"Yeah."

Eli swallowed. "It's me."

"I know your number, Dad." Theo sounded awake in the blunt way people on early shifts sounded awake, no warm-up in the voice. "You okay?"

The right answer was no. Eli started with logistics anyway.

"I can only talk a few minutes. I'm on break. One of the night guys overdosed in the restroom. We got Narcan in him, EMS took him out, and now management wants everything normal in nine minutes."

Silence on Theo's end, not empty, measured.

"You hurt?" Theo asked.

"No."

"He alive when they rolled?"

"Yeah. Barely, but yeah."

Another pause. Eli could hear Theo moving, maybe stepping away from a loud bay door. When Theo came back, his tone had shifted into the clipped precision Eli remembered from the rehab years, the voice he used when he wanted facts to stop lying.

"Why are you calling me right now?"

There it was. Theo offered no sentiment tax and no small-talk detour.

Eli leaned against the cold rail. "Because I can't keep doing this as a private project."

"Say it cleaner."

"I need help managing money transfers. And I need somebody who'll call me on my bullshit the day I start spinning stories."

"Okay." Theo let that sit for half a second. "What happened tonight that made this matter now instead of last week?"

Eli closed his eyes. He could have dodged. He was tired enough to tell the truth by accident.

"He went gray on the tile," Eli said. "Same age you were when we started dragging you to intake and pretending we had this under control. I heard myself doing that old thing, numbers first, body second. I don't trust myself to run this alone anymore."

On the other end, Theo exhaled through his nose, a soft static burst.

"All right," Theo said. "I'll help. Terms first."

"Yeah. Give them to me straight."

"By Friday noon your time, I get a photo of your pay stub and every wire receipt. Not when you remember. Noon. If a transfer bounces, I hear it from you before Mom does."

"Friday noon. Stub and receipts. No surprises."

"Money targets stay fixed until I say otherwise. Friday, five hundred minimum to mortgage until arrears drops under two grand. Tuesday, two hundred to medical."

Eli pressed his thumb into the split in his knuckle. "Five hundred works if doubles hold."

"If doubles don't hold, you tell me before Friday morning. You don't spring a shortfall after the cutoff."

Theo kept going, voice dry as chalk. "I build a shared calendar tonight. Sunday and Wednesday, six p.m. Central, you pick up. Miss one, I call once. Miss two in a row, I step off and you call Mom yourself with the numbers."

"If I miss two, I don't get to hide behind you. I get it."

"No direct promises to June about money, school, or home dates. Facts only. What happened, what got sent, what's next."

Eli let that one burn for a beat. "I won't sell her a date I haven't paid for."

"And this part matters." Theo's voice flattened another notch. "You don't save me for blood-on-the-floor nights. You call on a boring Tuesday too, or this is theater."

Eli looked down at his hand, skin raw and red over the knuckles.

"I can do that if it's on a clock," he said. "If it's scheduled, I show."

"Good. Then first proof is tonight." Paper rustled near Theo's mouthpiece. "Cash-on-hand photo after shift. Exact bills. No rounding."

Eli pictured the little spiral anyway, cheap black cover and grease-dark edges, Theo's block letters marching down the page. Theo did not trust memory when a life could disappear in one bad afternoon.

The numbers made Eli flinch less than he expected. They were bigger than what he had been sending and smaller than what guilt wanted. They looked possible.

"I can hit that if I keep double shifts," Eli said.

"Then keep them until you can't, and tell me before you can't."

Forklift horns blared in Theo's background and a voice called his name.

"I got to move," Theo said. "I'm texting you an app link and backup paper version because your flip phone hates links. Take a photo of your current cash after shift and send it. No rounding, no estimates."

"Yeah. I'll send exact bills, no rounding."

Theo hesitated, then added, quieter, "And eat something before you sleep. Adrenaline lies to you."

The words hit his sternum like a palm shove.

"You too," Eli said.

"I'm already on oatmeal." A brief huff that might have been a laugh. "Cafeteria raisins. Pray for me."

The line clicked dead.

Eli stayed in the stairwell another minute listening to the building breathe through vents and pipes. He had expected humiliation and got it. He had also expected contempt and got procedure with care threaded through it so tight he almost missed it.

When he stepped back onto the floor, Kline pointed at the desk clock.

"You took twelve," she said. "The lobby's chewing through us."

"Then dock me twelve," Eli answered, and sat.

She stared long enough for him to hear the slots again, then set a revised task list beside his keyboard.

"Finish guest folios, then inventory discrepancy report. Don't discuss the medical event with guests."

"Understood."

He worked until dawn under lights that flattened everything to one color. At four-thirty he reconciled minibar charges from three rooms. At five he corrected a laundry variance where a cart had been logged twice. At five-forty he printed the incident addendum Darryl requested and walked it to security.

Darryl was rewrapping tape around his watchband at the desk.

"Hospital called," he said without preface. "He came up, then dropped again in triage. ICU took him. Could still swing bad."

Eli nodded once, because gratitude felt too large for his throat and fear had not moved an inch.

"Good work in there," Darryl said. "Most people freeze."

"I almost did."

"Almost ain't the report." Darryl tapped the paperwork with a thick finger. "Kline filed a probation addendum. Corporate risk wants a supplemental interview this afternoon."

Eli gave a dry half-laugh that sounded more like a cough and headed out.

Morning in Reno looked bleached, mountains cut sharp against a pale sky, snow only on the highest lines. He took the employee bus down South Virginia to the grocery counter with the MoneyGram window because wired money sent before sleep counted more than promises made after.

The grocery lights made every surface look the color of old milk. Eli stood at the counter with his check, his ID, and the green folder spread open like a legal argument.

"Two sends?" the clerk asked.

"Two."

"Fee on each line."

"I know."

He sent five hundred twenty to Nora's account with MEMO: MORTGAGE ARREARS - WEEK 1 STRUCTURED. He sent two hundred to High Valley Medical Center with his account number copied from the bill. Nineteen dollars in fees. Two stamped stubs slid under the glass.

He took photos of both with the flip phone, then another photo of the cash left in his wallet laid flat on the counter, exactly as Theo had asked: three twenties, one ten, two fives, four ones.

Theo replied in less than a minute.

`Receipts and cash count logged. Wednesday is the first boring-day call. Don't make me chase you.`

Eli leaned against the storefront window and closed his eyes. He was so tired his bones felt hollowed out. A man in paint-splattered boots stepped around him to buy a money order. Somebody in the parking lot played corridos through a blown car speaker. A delivery truck dropped metal ramps with a crash that echoed off the cinder block and made him flinch.

He imagined Mateo in an ICU bay under bright light, oxygen mask swapped for tubing, bracelet cut off for an IV, Sofia maybe still waiting at school for a pickup that might not happen. He imagined Kline forwarding his name to corporate risk with PROBATION in the subject line. He looked at the eighty-four dollars left in his hand and did the next transfer math twice, because one lost shift would wreck Friday.

On the bus back to the motel room he rented by the week, he opened the atlas to the blank inside cover and copied Theo's check-in dates in block letters so tired he printed like a child.

In his room, he set alarms for noon proof and the Wednesday call, drank a glass of warm tap water, and lay on top of the blanket without taking off his boots. The ceiling had a stain shaped like a coastline. The heater kicked on, coughed dust, and settled into a low continuous whir.

His body kept replaying the night in fragments: the stall door resisting his shoulder, Mateo's heel striking tile, Darryl saying again, Nia's hands moving fast at the mask seal. Between fragments, another image slipped in and stayed. He saw Theo at twenty-two, outside the rehab intake office in a plastic chair, eyes hard and scared, asking, "You staying for the family session or not?" Eli had said he had a meeting. He had gone to the meeting.

The phone buzzed once with a text from Kline.

`3:00 PM supplemental interview. Mandatory for probation file.`

He set one more alarm for 2:15 and put the phone on his chest.

On the nightstand, the green folder sat open to the two stamped MoneyGram stubs, clipped where he'd see them when he woke.

Sleep came in drops, then took him all at once. Nothing had been handled. Mateo could still die before lunch. One missed interview could cost him shifts. One missed check-in could end Theo's oversight and put him back inside his own lying voice.
