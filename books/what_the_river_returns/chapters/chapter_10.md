# Chapter 10

At eight, by the time Lena parked below the levee, the catfish oil had been hot for three hours and everybody at the long tables smelled like cornmeal and river wind. The volunteer fire tent snapped and sighed in the gusts coming off the dark water. Cicadas ran a high electric line through the trees. Somebody's speaker kept losing signal, coughing in and out between old soul and church announcements. The whole setup should have felt harmless. Instead, she caught herself clocking exits before faces.
The red RECEIVED 5:58 P.M. stamp from the duty window still pulsed behind her eyes, all countdown and no comfort.

She had left her unit in the courthouse lot and borrowed Beau's mud-spattered Tahoe, because people in Mercer talked different when they did not see county decals. It was the same strategy she hated when Clay used it, charm where force would leave bruises, but this was not Clay's room. This was folding tables and paper trays and women arguing over tartar sauce recipes while they filled to-go orders for men already halfway drunk.
Hollis liked this crowd for easy handshakes and visible loyalty laps. She did not spot his cruiser on first pass, which did not mean he was not already in somebody else's line of sight.

Beau killed the engine and patted his shirt pocket twice, checking for the roll of antacid he carried now that he had crossed forty and would still eat anything dropped in hot grease. He wore clean jeans for once and a short-sleeve button-up with tiny blue shrimp stitched into the cloth, the kind of shirt that pretended to be funny so people looked at that instead of his badge belt tan line. Lena wished she could borrow that ease along with the truck.

"You sure he's gonna stay?" Lena asked.

"Otis stays till the raffle, always," Beau said. "He thinks every raffle's rigged and keeps showing up to prove it."

Otis Mayfield sat near the far edge of the tent where the light reached him in stripes. He had his guitar case upright between his boots like it was another person at the table. The man was all elbows and knuckles, the skin on his hands polished thin with age and strings. His plaid shirt was pressed hard enough to cut paper, and he still wore the black armband from his sister's funeral six months ago, though he had unpinned the flower and replaced it with a brass safety pin shaped like a fishhook. He salted his tea before he took a sip, a habit he swore kept his voice from cracking, and nobody around him bothered to argue.

Lena carried two plates over and set one down in front of him. "Mr. Mayfield. You said if we came where the fish was fresh, you'd talk."

Otis looked at the plate, then at her. "I said I'd listen while y'all chewed. That ain't the same thing as talk." His gaze cut to Beau. "Boy, this slaw's too sweet. Somebody got scared of vinegar."

"That'd be Sister Almeta," Beau said. "You tell her that to her face, she'll beat you with the serving spoon."

Otis snorted. "That woman already did, in '92, and I deserved worse. Sit down. You standing over me like intake at county lockup."

Lena sat. The bench bowed under all three of them. Grease popped in the fryers. Somewhere behind the tent a kid practiced trumpet scales with no mercy for the right notes. She kept feeling for the edge where small talk ended and risk began.

"I need to ask you about the old icehouse," she said.

Otis pinched a hush puppy in half and looked at the steam like he could read it. "There you go. You couldn't let a fish supper be a fish supper."

"I can eat and ask." Lena broke her own fillet with her fingers. It burned. She welcomed that. "You told Reverend Holt there used to be night hearings down there. Off the books. Before men got dragged to the trees."

He held the hush puppy over his plate until the steam thinned, and the little humor in his face flattened. "Night hearings," he repeated. "That's what y'all calling it in paperwork? Makes it sound like folks got a chance to appeal."

"What do you call it?"

"I call it men got tuned up till they signed whatever white folks needed signed. Then a day or two later everybody acted surprised there was a rope in play." He took one careful bite and chewed like his jaw hurt. "Language matters, Detective. You're too smart to pretend it doesn't."

Beau wiped his fingers and leaned back. "She ain't pretending. She needs what you remember, that's all."

"All?" Otis laughed once, sharp. "You asking me for fifty years of ghosts and you saying that's all."

Lena let that sit. The wind pressed the tent wall inward, then released. She could feel her old instinct to move fast, stack questions, box uncertainty before it spread. That was what command liked. That was what got witnesses shut.

"Start smaller," she said. "How many nights did you see people brought there?"

Otis rubbed his thumb over the edge of his cup. "Seen? Two, maybe three. Heard about plenty more. Most nights you did not look direct. Looking direct got noticed."

"Who brought them?"

"Sheriff trucks. Deputy trucks. Sometimes private cars with county men riding shotgun." He glanced toward the raffle table, where numbers were being shouted into a microphone too loud for the speaker. "Headlights off once they hit levee road. One lantern at the loading door. You could see that from the cutbank if you were dumb enough to be out there."

"What year?"

"You think I kept a diary?" He pointed at his temple with two fingers. "Memory don't run neat. It comes like rain in spots. I know one night was before they paved 17, because I still had red clay stuck in my truck tires. I know one night was after my first baby was born, because I remember hearing her cry when I got home and feeling thankful for the sound." He looked down at his plate. "She died at twenty-eight. Blood clot. I still hear that newborn cry easier than yesterday morning."

Lena nodded once. "I'm sorry."

"Don't do polite on me." He reached for a piece of fish from her plate without asking. "If you want useful, ask useful."

She slid her plate closer. "What did the place smell like?"

Otis blinked, surprised, then gave her the first unguarded half-smile she'd seen. "Now that's a question from somebody who's actually been in bad rooms." He tapped the table twice. "Ammonia. River mud. Old blood that got hosed but not gone. Burlap rot. Diesel off idling engines. You'd get a little mint too, from somebody chewing to keep from puking."

Beau scrubbed his palm down his thigh and set his cup down harder than he meant to. "You ever go inside?"

"Once." Otis said it flat and did not lift his eyes. "A deputy told me to play for a supper there, said they'd pay cash. I was nineteen and stupid and needed gas money. I walked in with my guitar and saw a boy tied to a chair with baling wire around his wrists. No shirt. Back striped up so raw the skin had started shining." He touched the black armband at his sleeve as if checking it was still there. "A man at a table was writing names in a ledger and not looking up. I put my guitar back in the case and walked out before anybody decided I was useful. They docked me half pay for leaving early."

No one at their table spoke for a few seconds. The tent noise carried on around them, indifferent and bright.

"You can put that in a statement," Lena said quietly.

Otis barked a laugh that made two nearby women turn their heads. "No, ma'am. You can put that in your own head. Statement means my name on county paper, and county paper leaks faster than a jon boat with dry seams."

"We'll keep your name out of it till I can back up what you gave us-"

"Don't sell me courthouse weather reports." He pointed a greasy finger at her. "You can't promise what that building won't do."

She could not. Not honestly. Clay treated confidentiality like campaign yard signs, useful until the wind changed.

"Then give me place," she said. "No names. No oath. Just place."

Otis chewed the inside of his cheek and looked away toward the levee crown. Kids ran up and down the slope with glow sticks, shrieking every time one of them wiped out in the grass. The sound made him smile despite himself.

"You remember Junior Tate?" he asked Beau.

"The one with three thumbs if you count that knot?"

"That's him. Dumbest smart man I ever knew." Otis chuckled. "Junior said if you couldn't find trouble in Mercer, look for where the road forgot its own name. Icehouse was on one of those. Past the fertilizer scales. Past a lightning-split persimmon tree that leaned like it was praying wrong. Back then, there was a culvert with one corner busted and a rusted buoy somebody dragged up from the river hanging on a post."

Lena repeated it under her breath until it stuck. Fertilizer scales. Persimmon. Broken culvert. Buoy.

"That helps," she said.

"Helps ain't testimony." Otis wiped his fingers clean with a napkin, then folded the napkin into a neat square and set it under his cup like a coaster. "Don't get greedy because I ate your fish."

The raffle winner got called, and half the tent erupted when it turned out to be Sister Almeta herself taking home a pressure washer she'd donated. Otis laughed so hard he wheezed and pounded the table. Beau laughed too, and Lena felt her own shoulders drop for the first time all day. It was stupid and local and not for leverage. For two minutes she let it be exactly that.

When the noise settled, Otis's face hardened again.

"One more thing," he said. "Night court wasn't just beatings. I watched two deputies pin a cane farmer to a feed table. One held a revolver under his jaw while the other mashed his thumb into an ink pad and onto a blank deed. He hit the wrong line because his hand was shaking, so they bent his thumb back till it popped and made him stamp it again. By sunrise his wife was loading quilts into a wagon and a Mercer Trust truck was backing into his field."

Lena felt that sentence in her chest like a fist. The ledgers from last week were columns and shell names. Here was the bridge between ink and blood.

"If that happened in the icehouse office, there'll be pressure grooves in the desk and carbon ghosts in any ledger pages they missed," she said. "That's recoverable. That's a searchable site."

Otis nodded once. "Floor scale by the loading door had a tin cash box under it. Signed pages sat there till daylight pickup."

He stood, joints cracking, and hooked two fingers through the handle of his guitar case.

"Interview's over," he said. "I got to play two songs so these people can pretend they're dancing in rhythm."

He walked toward the makeshift stage beside the raffle table, moving slow but steady. Lena watched him go and understood exactly what she had and what she did not. Human context, no sworn testimony. A handful of landmarks and a memory that bled at the edges.

Beau leaned close. "We can work with that."

"Maybe," she said. "Maybe we just heard enough to know how little a courtroom will take."

"Courtroom ain't tomorrow." He popped two antacids, chewed, and grimaced. "Search is."

By the time Otis finished his second song, the sky had gone from purple to a flat iron gray and the first heat lightning was crawling behind the trees. Volunteers started breaking down tables. Oil drums got capped. Somebody brought out lidded buckets for leftovers and argued over whose freezer had room.

Lena and Beau waited by the back of the Tahoe while she spread photocopies across the tailgate. Parcel maps, floodplain overlays, trust transfer records from the warrant fight, and a county roads sheet so old it still marked a ferry crossing that had washed out before Lena was born. Wind kept lifting corners. She pinned them with her phone, Beau's flashlight, and a plastic tub of tartar sauce packets somebody had abandoned. Every corner that popped loose kicked her pulse higher in her throat: if she could not keep paper in place, she could not keep this case in place either.

Otis came offstage toweling sweat from his neck with a Saints hand towel gone almost white from washing. He set the guitar case down and looked at the papers without touching them.

"You still here," he said.

"Needed daylight while we've got it," Lena said. "Can you point where those landmarks sit now?"

"Now?" Otis's voice went flint-hard. "Now half that land belongs to folks who bought history cheap and call it development." He tapped one parcel with his nail. "This one used to be a bait shed. That one used to be Bell family row crop. That one used to be where my cousin got his jaw wired shut for arguing taxes." Lena bit the inside of her cheek and tasted iron. The parcels stopped being neutral shapes and turned into injuries with fence lines.

Beau angled the flashlight lower. "Storm's rolling in. Which approach still holds if we move before dawn?"

Otis glanced past them toward the generator where two men in damp ball caps watched the tailgate instead of cleanup. One lifted a phone, screen bright as a lure.

"That's your new problem," Otis said. "You stand here jawing ten more minutes, Clay hears I fed you roads and sends boys to scrub the place before sunup. And once this rain breaks hard, east access turns to soup and washes tire marks into the ditch."

Lena followed his look, then folded one parcel map in half and shoved it under the tartar-sauce tub. "Then don't feed us roads out loud. Show me once, we move quiet."

Otis crouched by the guitar case. Instead of opening it for the instrument, he reached under the neck rest and slid out a long envelope spotted with old grease. He sat back on his heels and held it in both hands for a moment like he was deciding whether the paper weighed more than wood.

"I drew this after my nephew asked where not to park when he goes noodling," he said. "Told him none of this and still sent him another way." He offered the envelope to Lena but did not release it yet. "You don't get to call this evidence in open court unless you can prove what you find without me."

"I understand." Lena wrapped her fingers around the envelope. It was soft at the folds and smelled like cedar oil and old paper. Otis let go.

Inside was a hand-drawn map on the back of a church revival program from 2019, lines done in blue ballpoint and retraced in pencil where his hand had shaken. The river bend was there, the levee spur, the drainage ditch, and an X boxed three times over a rectangle labeled ICE. He had marked the broken culvert with a tiny square and written busted corner. He had drawn a persimmon tree as a crooked fork and added, in cramped letters, struck by lightning but still living.

He had also written three names in the margin and crossed two out so hard the paper almost tore. The third name remained: M. Kearney. No title, no date.

"Who's that?" Lena asked.

Otis's jaw tightened. "Dead two years now. Used to keep scales at the fertilizer yard. He saw trucks. That's all you need." He pointed at the map. "Road in from the east washes out after hard rain. If you're going before dawn, come from levee side and kill lights after the second cattle guard. There is still chain-link on the north wall with a cut big enough for a body."

Beau whistled low. "You been back there recently."

Otis looked offended. "I fish. I check traps. I keep my own weather. You think history sleeps because y'all stop asking it questions?"

Thunder rolled, close enough to shake the tent poles. People cursed and ran for trucks with covered pans clutched to their chests.

Lena folded the map along its old crease, then changed her mind and flattened it again so she could photograph both sides. She sent the images to her secure case folder and watched the upload crawl in weak signal. Fragile lead, fragile chain.

"You need anything from us tonight?" she asked.

Otis barked a humorless breath. "Yeah. Leave before somebody sees us talking too long and decides to make it useful." He picked up his guitar case and rose with a grunt. "And Detective?"

"Yeah?"

"When you write whatever you write, don't make me brave. I ain't brave. I'm old, and I'm tired of carrying a county's teeth marks in my sleep." He jerked his chin toward the map in her hand. "That paper's what tired looks like."

He walked into the rain before she could answer, head down, case swinging against his leg. Halfway to the parking lot he turned to shout back at Beau.

"You still owe me twelve dollars from that Saints bet, liar."

Beau shouted after him, "You changed the spread after kickoff!"

Otis raised one hand without turning around.

By the time they got into the Tahoe, the storm had opened all the way. Wipers knocked hard from side to side. Water sheeted off the windshield and turned the levee lights into smears.

Lena set the envelope on her lap and kept one hand over it as if it might slide away. The map was not testimony. It was not chain-clean. It could be attacked six different ways before breakfast. It was also the first object in this case that told her exactly where to stand.

Beau pulled onto the road slow, headlights cutting through rain and gnats and low fog rising off ditch water.

"You thinking pre-dawn?" he asked.

"We have to," Lena said. "If anybody else hears about this, we lose the site."

"Clay'll want to be first through the door for cameras."

"Then we move before cameras wake up."

She looked down at Otis's cramped lettering, at the note by the tree struck by lightning but still living, and felt the old split inside her catch like barbed wire: procedure and memory, paper and blood, what could be proven and what had always been known. The rain drummed louder on the roof. Somewhere behind them, under a collapsing tent and a generator hum, a man with tired hands packed up his guitar and walked home through the weather he trusted more than any courthouse in Mercer County.

Before she slept, she slid Otis's map and envelope into a gallon freezer bag from the fish-supper cleanup, the plastic still carrying a faint fried catfish and lemon pepper smell. Then she ran the approach through once more in her head, levee side, second cattle guard, lights out, and set her alarm for before dawn.
