# Chapter 1

By 7:18 a.m., the tin siding of Voss Cotton Gin had started to ring with heat. The sound came in thin pops, like knuckles cracking in a quiet room. Lena Waller ducked under the yellow tape at the office door and the air changed from raw sun to bleach, metal, and blood already turning sweet.

Hank Voss lay on his back between the desk and the filing cabinets, shirt gone dark from sternum to waistband. Someone had cut his throat deep enough that cartilage showed white through the red. His right hand was skinned at the knuckles, and one fingernail had torn loose down to meat. He had fought for something, then lost to it.

Beau Broussard stood by the door with his scene log in one hand and a roll of antacid tablets in the other, shaking two into his palm without looking. He had the weathered, wide face of a man who had spent too many years squinting off boat ramps, and this morning he had a crescent of zinc sunscreen across his nose where he had missed a spot with his thumb.

"Perimeter's holding on the office side," he said. "Loading bay's ugly. We had six workers try to come back in for their lunch pails before I got a second deputy over there."

Lena knelt by Voss's left shoulder. Her right knee scar, old surgery from a blown ligament and a younger version of herself that thought she healed fast, gave a quick sting against the concrete. She ignored it and leaned in close enough to smell copper and old coffee on the dead man.

"Any shell casings?" she asked.

"None. No obvious weapon in the room. Front safe's closed. Cash drawer's open, but there are twenties still in it."

"Signs of forced entry?"

"Nope. Back office door was unlocked when first shift came in."

She took that in, then stood and looked where Beau was not looking: the second body, laid three feet from Hank as if someone had measured the gap.

The second man was Black, lean, and half-rigid with an unnatural waxy sheen over skin that should not still have held to the face this long after death. The neck showed an old deep ligature groove under new staining. Mud clung to one heel. The shirt was a plain white button-down, clean except for two rust-colored handprints at the chest, like the person who placed him there had paused to adjust him.

Lena felt, stupidly, the urge to apologize to him.

She moved closer and saw the chipped left incisor. The break was not fresh. It had healed years before whoever killed him. She had seen that tooth in a photograph that had lived in church binders and memorial programs since she was a teenager. She straightened, pulse jumping once, hard.

"Who called it in?" she asked.

"Darlene McKee. Payroll clerk." Beau tipped his chin toward the doorway. "She's outside with EMS. Couldn't keep her from puking."

"Bring her in when she's steady."

He gave her one look. "Len. You thinking what I'm thinking?"

She did not answer him yet.

Outside, cicadas drilled from the tree line past the gravel lot. A drone of voices had formed behind the tape, workers in sweat-dark shirts and steel-toe boots trying to see around deputies. Beyond them, two local TV vans were already wedging into the shoulder. Someone had called the media before the blood cooled.

Sheriff Clay Reddick arrived in a white Tahoe that still carried campaign magnets from spring primaries. He came through the tape with his tie straight, his heavy shoulders set, and a pair of reading glasses tucked into the front pocket of his pressed shirt. The glasses had a cracked temple wrapped in clear tape. He kept using them anyway.

"Detective Waller," he said, voice warm enough for church foyer conversation. "Tell me what I can stand in front of."

"You can stand in front of a locked scene and say we're processing," Lena said. "Anything beyond that is guessing."

Clay glanced at Voss, then at the second body, and the warmth in his face thinned. "Hank Voss is our victim. The other one is a prop until I hear different from the lab. We do not reward whoever staged this with a circus."

"He's not a prop." Lena kept her tone flat. "He's a body."

"You know what I mean. We can call this a homicide with postmortem contamination and keep public order intact."

The phrase public order landed in her ear the way other men said grace.

Lena crouched again near the second body and forced herself to track facts instead of fear. The fingertips were stained dark, not in a lividity pattern. The shirt cuffs were buttoned wrong, left through right hole. The soil under the nails was fine and pale, cemetery clay, not the black grit from the gin lot. Whoever moved him had done it recently and with refrigeration or chemicals, because the Delta heat should have bloated and broken this tissue already.

She stood and pulled her phone. "Dispatch, this is Waller. I need county archive clerk on line now."

Clay watched her with a small patient smile that meant irritation. "Lena, don't start naming history in front of my deputies."

"I'm not naming anything. I'm verifying identity."

He inhaled slowly. "We're not turning this into 1963 before breakfast."

"Then don't." She kept her eyes on her phone. "Let me do my job."

The archive clerk, Nita Fellows, answered on the third ring, breathless and chewing something crunchy. "Records."

"Nita, I need the 1962-63 Mercer Colored School yearbook page for Isaiah Bell. High-res scan. Text it to me and Beau right now."

There was a silence, then the crackle of a desk chair. "That's from the memorial litigation packet, right? I can pull it. Give me three minutes."

"Two if you love me."

"I don't love nobody before coffee, Detective. Hang tight."

Lena ended the call and went back inside. Dr. Naveen Rao was at the doorway now, tying on gloves, his voice already moving ahead of him.

"I can do field observations," he said. "I can't certify much in this heat before transfer. Whoever set your second decedent here changed decomposition trajectory somehow. Could be cold storage. Could be surface preservative. Could be both."

Rao wore clean scrubs under an old denim overshirt and a wristwatch with a busted strap held on by black thread. He refused to replace it because it had been his father's. Lena knew that because he had once told her at two in the morning while they waited for tox screens and stale vending machine coffee.

"What can you tell me now?" she asked.

He crouched at the second body, careful and close. "There are older injuries consistent with hanging trauma at the neck and hyoid region, at least from external exam. There are newer transfer artifacts on the shirt and forearms. This body did not lie here all night."

Clay gave a dry laugh from behind them. "You can tell all that in five minutes?"

Rao did not look up. "I can tell enough to stop you from making statements you can't retract."

Beau's phone buzzed. Then Lena's.

Nita's text came through with a scanned yearbook portrait: Isaiah Bell, senior class, 1963. The face was narrow. The left incisor was chipped. The ear was bent slightly at the top like it had healed from a hard hit in childhood. Beneath the photo, the quote line from school editors read clean and banal and unbearable.

Lena held the phone beside the dead man's face. The same tooth. The same ear.

The world did not tilt. It narrowed.

She turned the screen to Clay. "It's Isaiah Bell."

He looked, jaw locking once, then handed the phone back like it was wet. "It's a resemblance."

"It's enough for provisional ID pending prints and dental."

"Prints from 1963?"

"We have church burial records and a county death certificate from June 1963. We have family to notify if this confirms. We treat him as identified until disproved."

Clay's voice softened again, that false courtesy she had learned to hear as pressure. "Lena, listen to me carefully. If that name gets out before we control narrative, this county catches fire. You know that."

She did know. She could already picture New Hope Missionary's bulletin board by noon, Ms. Ida reading the text chain with her hand over her mouth. She could picture Hollis's friends posting jokes and threats before lunch. She could picture donors calling Clay, and Clay calling everyone else.

She also knew what happened when scenes got politically expensive: bags opened, logs rewritten, timestamps smoothed until a body turned into paperwork.

"Then we lock it down now," she said. "Both decedents, full trace collection, independent transport witness, and immediate state-lab intake."

"State lab is twenty-four hours out."

"Not if you mark emergency historical-custody risk."

Clay stared at her for a beat too long. "You are jumping levels."

"I'm preserving evidence."

He pulled a folded handkerchief from his pocket and wiped the back of his neck. The sun was climbing hard, and his face had gone shiny at the temples. "Fine. You get your transfer request. But this stays internal until I brief city hall."

"I need that in writing."

"You have my word."

"I need it in writing."

Beau made a small sound in his throat that might have been a cough and might have been a warning.

Clay's eyes went flat. "Draft it and bring it to my vehicle."

Lena nodded, because nodding cost less than speaking right then.

Darlene McKee came in fifteen minutes later with a deputy escort. She was in lemon-print house slippers, the kind with foam soles, because she had come to run payroll and not to stand in blood. Mascara had dried in streaks under both eyes. She kept touching the gold cross at her throat until her knuckles whitened.

"I opened at six-ten," she said, voice shaking. "Mr. Voss liked me early on Mondays because the truckers came with invoices. I walked in and saw his shoe first. Then I saw the rest and I screamed till my chest hurt."

"Did you touch anything?" Lena asked.

"No, ma'am. I dropped my keys. I still left them right there by the copier."

"Anybody else had access overnight?"

Darlene swallowed. "Everybody's got a key who matters. That's what Mr. Voss used to say."

"Name them."

"Him. Me. Floor manager. His nephew Trey. Two maintenance men. Sheriff's office, one copy for alarms."

Lena looked up. "Sheriff's office?"

Clay answered for Darlene. "Standard alarm protocol for commercial properties. Not unusual."

It might have been standard protocol, and it might not have been. Lena wrote it down.

By 10:05 the first evidence coolers were sealed. Lena watched each strip of tamper tape go down with date and initials. She signed chain-of-custody twice, then made Beau countersign. She put Rao's transfer notes in a separate pouch and stapled them herself.

When the county van rolled out toward Jackson with deputy escort, camera crews were already at the gate filming through heat shimmer. A man in a union shirt shouted at the lens that Voss had stolen years from every worker on the line. Another man shouted back that somebody wanted race war before election day. Neither man was wrong enough to ignore.

Lena stood in the grit and sweat and diesel smell and watched the van until it took the curve toward the highway and disappeared.

Then she turned to canvass the people who were still alive.

At noon, the break room fans were pushing hot air in circles and losing. Lena set up at a scarred laminate table with Beau beside her and a digital recorder between them. Every worker she called in had the same line ready: did not hear anything, did not see anything, arrived at six, found chaos, waited outside.

The answers were too clean and too matching.

The fourth interview was Alonzo Pierce, union steward, forearms roped with old burns from machine jams. He set his cap on the table and lined it up with the grain in the laminate before he sat.

"You want truth?" he said. "Truth is folks are scared of losing jobs and scared of getting blamed for this. Those are two different fears, but they feel the same in your gut."

"Who told them what to say?" Lena asked.

Alonzo rubbed his jaw. "Nobody had to. We all know how this goes. First headline says worker rage, second headline says lock everybody up, third headline says management was a saint and we are all animals."

Beau leaned forward, elbows on knees. "You got anything concrete, Lonzo? I can fight rumor all day, but it won't hold up in court."

"Concrete?" Alonzo gave him a bitter half-smile. "Concrete is your office had an alarm key in this building. Concrete is cameras in dock three were dead since Friday and nobody fixed them. Concrete is Mr. Voss told us payroll would be late again while he ordered a new bass boat."

"That last one isn't homicide evidence," Lena said.

"No, ma'am. That's character evidence."

She wrote it anyway.

The ninth interview was the one that shifted the floor.

Calvin Rusk, senior mechanic, came in with his hearing protectors hanging around his neck and a paper sack of pecans he cracked one-handed when he got nervous. He wore a stitched patch on his shirt that read RUSK but the R had been repaired twice with pale thread. He sat down, took a pecan from the sack, and did not eat it.

"Mr. Rusk," Lena said, "you were on overnight maintenance last night?"

"I was here till four-thirty. Dryer line three kept spiking temp, and if that line catches, we all cook."

"Did you leave straight from here?"

"I did."

"Did you see anybody at the office before you left?"

He stared at the recorder. "I already told Deputy Kane I didn't see nothing useful."

"Tell me again."

He rolled the pecan between thumb and forefinger until shell dust coated his nail beds. "I got a daughter at Delta State," he said quietly. "She starts sophomore year next week. I can't lose this job because I said the wrong name in the wrong room."

Lena nodded once. "I'm not asking you for gossip. I'm asking what your eyes saw."

"My eyes saw a county refrigerated van by dock two at about four-twelve."

Beau straightened. "County how?"

"Gray stripe down the side, faded seal on the door. Same van your office sent last winter when that inmate died in lockup and y'all needed extra cooling because morgue compressors were down. I know that squeal in the belt. I replaced that belt for Fleet in January."

Lena felt the back of her neck go cold despite the heat. "Who was driving?"

"Could not see full face. White man, deputy-style haircut, baseball cap, long sleeves even in this weather." Calvin cracked the pecan in his fist by accident and winced. "He came out the office side with two others. They loaded something long under a tarp. Then they left by the east gate, no lights."

"Did you report this to anybody last night?"

"No, ma'am. I figured maybe Mr. Voss had legal disposal, maybe spoiled lint bales, maybe something above my pay grade. Then I came in this morning and saw tape everywhere and I thought I might puke."

"Why tell us now?"

Calvin looked up for the first time. His eyes were wet and angry. "Because if this turns into y'all saying one of us workers butchered a boss in his office and dragged some dead Black man in there for fun, I'm not carrying that lie for the rest of my life."

Silence held for a second. The fan rattled overhead.

Beau clicked his pen and wrote the statement header. "We'll take this signed and witnessed."

Calvin gave a humorless laugh. "Can you keep my name out of the six o'clock?"

Lena answered before Beau could. "I can flag you as confidential source in initial report and move your full statement under protected annex until probable-cause filing. I can't promise nobody ever says your name. I can promise we fight for it."

"That's the best offer I've heard all day," Calvin said.

Word spread through the break room in under ten minutes even though no one had heard the actual statement. Fear moved faster than facts. Two workers walked off shift. One of them slammed his badge on the table and said if the sheriff wanted a suspect he could arrest every man who had ever touched a cotton module hook. Another worker whispered that if county vans were moving bodies before dawn, nobody in that room would sleep this week.

By three-thirty, Lena had twenty-two interviews, six near-duplicates, three meaningful contradictions, and one lead that smelled like insider logistics. She also had three missed calls from Clay and a text from county communications asking for "approved language" before afternoon press.

She did not answer either.

At 8:47 p.m., the sheriff's briefing room smelled of old paper, stale coffee, and the sharp lemon cleaner the night janitor used when she was angry at people who left sugar rings on furniture. Fluorescent lights flickered just enough to tighten everybody's shoulders.

Clay sat at the head of the table with a legal pad and no jacket, tie loosened but still knotted. County PIO Cora Denning sat to his right with perfect hair that had survived twelve hours of humidity and a notebook full of highlighted talking points. Beau was at Lena's left, rubbing a thumb over the dent in his wedding band.

Clay opened with a long, level statement.

"Before we discuss tomorrow's operational schedule, I need alignment on public posture. Mercer County requires stability. We have one confirmed homicide victim, Mr. Voss, and one unidentified decedent placed at scene by unknown persons. Our message is that we are pursuing all leads while discouraging rumor and historical speculation that inflames public fear."

Cora nodded as she wrote. "For on-camera language, I can keep it to: active investigation, no confirmed motive, community partnership requested."

Lena waited until Clay looked at her. "No."

The room went still.

Clay folded his hands. "Explain your objection, Detective."

"I will not call the second decedent unidentified. We have provisional ID support from archival photo match, morphology, and burial record cross-reference pending formal confirmation. If we suppress that and it leaks in an hour, we look corrupt before we even get to day two."

Cora jumped in, careful voice, media-trained calm. "Detective, with respect, naming Isaiah Bell tonight could trigger demonstrations at New Hope and the courthouse before dawn. We don't have crowd staffing for that."

"We don't control demonstrations by lying," Lena said.

Clay's tone stayed polite. "No one is lying. We are sequencing facts."

"You're sequencing optics."

Beau exhaled like a man watching a bridge cable fray. "Can we split this? We can say historical identity is under active forensic review without locking the name till dental confirms."

"That's closer," Lena said. "But I also need state-lab priority intake on all trace from both bodies and the office scene. Not just tissue, everything. Fibers, latent lift sheets, soil, transfer prints from the shirt."

Clay tapped his pen once. "County lab can handle standard processing."

"County lab sits three doors down from the same chain-of-custody corridor that just produced an impossible body placement." Her voice dropped, harder now. "I am not accusing our techs. I am saying system exposure is obvious. If we keep this local, defense will shred us and they will be right."

Cora looked between them. "If we escalate to state tonight, we signal loss of confidence in county operations."

"We already lost confidence," Lena said. "People were filming our tape line by nine this morning. The question is whether we deserve any confidence back."

Clay's jaw worked. He leaned back and gave her that falsely gentle tone again.

"Lena, you've had a long day. I respect the effort. I also have to manage the whole county, not just evidence lockers."

Her knee scar throbbed under the table, hot and precise. She thought of the handprints on Isaiah's shirt, the way they had looked like a person arranging furniture for company.

"Then manage this," she said. "I am requesting in writing: state-lab priority, dual-chain witness on every transfer, and immediate lock audit on all county refrigerated vehicles with movement logs from midnight to six."

Beau let out a low whistle that he turned into a cough.

Clay stared at her for three long seconds. Then he smiled without mirth.

"You are very comfortable issuing requests in this room."

"I am comfortable documenting risk."

"No." He held up a hand as she started to speak. "You will listen now. We are not running a public seminar on civil-rights trauma while election signs are still in yards. We are not handing activists and donors fresh ammunition every hour. We are not letting one scene collapse this department into panic."

Every noun in his sentence was an institution. Every verb was control.

Lena answered in short declaratives. "A man is dead. Another man was taken from a grave and staged. A county vehicle was seen at that scene before dawn. If we narrow language to protect campaigns, we become part of the crime."

Cora set down her pen.

Clay looked at Beau. "Do you endorse this position?"

Beau rubbed the bridge of his nose. "I endorse not getting our case laughed out of court. State-lab help gives us cleaner chain."

It was not support. It was enough.

Clay stood, palms on the table. "Fine. State lab gets priority intake. Cora, draft a statement that says historical identity is under forensic confirmation and that outside lab support has been requested in the interest of transparency." He turned to Lena. "You will clear every further communication through me. No off-script comments. No side briefings. If I hear one more direct call to records or media from you, I reassign your scenes and you can inventory parking lot fender-benders until Christmas."

He did not raise his voice. He did not need to.

"Understood," Lena said.

It tasted like swallowing chalk.

Meeting broke at 9:36. In the hallway, Beau walked beside her to the stairwell.

"You poked him in both eyes at once," he said.

"He had both eyes on the wrong thing."

"Yeah." Beau looked down at the vending machine, considered it, then kept moving. "You did right. Just know he don't forget this kind of right."

Outside, the night had not cooled. The air felt wet enough to drink. Cicadas sawed from the courthouse trees. Across the street, three satellite trucks idled with mast lights up, and a small crowd had already gathered at the barricades with handmade signs and church fans. Someone had printed Isaiah Bell's senior portrait and taped it to a poster board in the time it took to hold one meeting.

News moved faster than command. So did grief.

Lena stood on the courthouse steps and watched the evidence courier pull out for Jackson under state escort, red taillights shrinking down Front Street. She felt the familiar split open inside her: one part procedure, one part rage, both of them awake.

She took out her notebook and wrote the next day's first line in block letters so there would be no argument later.

LOCK THE LOGS BEFORE THEY LEARN HOW MUCH WE KNOW.
