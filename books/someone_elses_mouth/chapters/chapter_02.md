# Chapter 2

By the third morning on Signal, his name was already doing rude things to the room.

At 6:54 her phone rattled hard enough to tap the spoon in yesterday's mug. The box fan in the window clicked on its bad bearing every ninth turn, a cheap stubborn metronome. She had told him about the click the night before because he had asked what kept waking her. His message said, "You up or did that fan finally throw itself into traffic?"

She smiled before she was fully awake, which annoyed her. Thirty-five, divorced, behind on captions, six weeks out from a tax bill with teeth, and there she was smiling at a screen before she had even peed. She let the phone sit for what she hoped counted as normal-person timing. Then she wrote back, "Still here. Fan's all talk."

His reply came while she was standing at the sink with a dry mouth and one hand under cold water for no reason except the apartment had already started heating up.

"I don't trust it. If you disappear I'm blaming appliance negligence."

Then a photo: coffee in a paper cup balanced on a black road case, the lip of a loading dock behind it still blue with morning. His thumb cut into one corner of the shot. A strip of fluorescent tape crossed the case like a small emergency.

"Today's office."

Not a shirtless mirror picture. Not a gym selfie with a caption about earning it. A paper cup, a road case, dawn.

She took the phone into the bathroom with her, which was pathetic, but she did it anyway.

By seven-thirty she was at her desk in a sports bra and old shorts, hair knotted up, left heel hooked on the chair rung, cleaning captions on a condo-board Zoom from Cherry Hill. The auto transcript had turned reserve study into deserve slutty three times and the treasurer had a wet whistle on every s. Below her screen a folded IRS notice held down a cable that would not stay in place. The tailor shop downstairs hauled up its metal gate with the sound of a train dragging itself out of sleep. Somewhere on the block a scooter coughed awake, failed, then tried again.

Her phone lit.

"Did deserve slutty pass, or did they table it?"

She laughed out loud, alone in the room. She had mentioned that error once, quick, while brushing her teeth. Most people heard the general category of what she said and answered the outline of it. He kept the nuisance, the throwaway piece, the exact stupid phrasing.

"Still under debate," she typed. "The condo future hangs in the balance."

"Thoughts and prayers."

She put the phone face down, made herself work twelve minutes straight, and then turned it back over just to see if there was more. There was.

"Eat something."

"Bossy."

"You told me coffee on an empty stomach turns you into a fugitive."

She had said that, too, as a joke the night before while she was explaining why she had once left an ex in a Target because he wanted to browse decorative baskets at eleven in the morning. She stood up, went to the kitchenette, and ate half a peach over the sink while the juice ran down her wrist and the fan pushed hot dust against the back of her knees.

Fast answers were cheap in the first week. Plenty of men sent them because they were working a slot machine, pushing the same bright buttons in six windows and waiting to see where the bells went off. Eli's attention came in at an angle. He didn't come back with "how's your day going" every two hours like a customer service script. He returned to the crooked little things she had tossed off without ceremony and handed them back with his thumbprint on them.

At nine he sent a voice note from somewhere loud enough that his first word blurred.

"Hang on."

She heard him move. A door banged. The noise changed shape, generator to alley, broad metallic roar thinning down into a truck beep and somebody swearing about a lift gate.

"There. Better. I was gonna say, if that condo board approves deserve slutty as a budget line item, I need you to steal the minutes."

His voice had the same near-the-ear patience as the first note, but now there was sleep roughing the bottom of it. He sounded like somebody who had worked late and still had to show up again, which she trusted more than if he had sounded glossy. He laughed once at his own joke, quietly, like he was trying not to carry into the phone as performance.

She listened to it twice. The second time with the left earbud pinched just right between finger and thumb because the cable had started cutting out again at the split.

"You're bad for productivity," she wrote.

"I contain multitudes."

"No, you contain OSHA violations and a paper cup."

"Rude. Also accurate."

The condo board argued about roofing bids until ten-thirteen. She cleaned their stumbles, marked their crosstalk, turned their human mess back into respectable text. Every time the phone buzzed she told herself she would finish the current speaker first. Most of the time she did not. He sent a picture of a coil of XLR cable like a sleeping snake and another of a road case with a caster blown clean off. He sent one line about a drummer who had gotten barbecue chips on a monitor console and sounded actually annoyed, not trying to seem charming. Ten minutes later, after a gap full of work noise, another message came in: "If one more guy calls every cable in this building 'the aux,' I'm walking into the river." Only then did he ask if the tailor downstairs still played boleros on Saturdays when pressing wedding shirts. She had mentioned that on Hinge, back when it still felt like a game, because she could hear the music through the floorboards and it made her feel as if other people's ceremonies were stored directly under her bed.

"He only does that when he's in a good mood," she wrote.

"How do you know?"

"Because he sings along and he can't sing."

"Devastating. I respect him more now."

At eleven-thirty her client sent an email marked gentle reminder and she felt the bottom drop out of the flirtation for a second. Gentle reminder always meant where the fuck is my file. She answered with a lie about final formatting, then worked hard enough to make the lie true. The room got hotter. Her thighs stuck to the chair. Sweat gathered under her bra band and along the inside of her elbows. She turned the fan higher and it answered with a louder click.

The phone buzzed again.

"Go stand in the freezer aisle for five minutes before you pass out."

She stared at the screen.

Two nights ago, laughing at herself for how broke and overheated August could make a grown woman, she had told him that when the apartment got unbearable she walked to the Acme and loitered by the frozen peas pretending to compare store brands until her skin stopped feeling rented. She had not said it as a meaningful thing. It had embarrassed her a little as soon as it was out. Now he had reached back for it, and the line snagged for a beat on the command buried inside it. Her ex had been good at dressing instructions up as concern and then acting wounded if she heard the leash.

"I'm not gonna faint in produce," she typed.

"Fair. That came out bossier than I meant."

"Little bit."

"Go get cold anyway."

The phone came with her to the closet before she'd admitted any decision. By the time she dragged on a shirt decent enough for daylight, she was already headed for the door.

The block was all white glare and hot brick. A SEPTA rumble passed somewhere deeper in the city, too low to separate from the traffic. At the corner a man in slides was hosing blood and soap from a butcher's back step into the gutter. By the time she got to the Acme her scalp was damp under her hair knot. She stood with the peas anyway, one hand on the freezer handle, and texted him a photo of frost crusting the inside glass.

"Medicinal," she wrote.

His answer came fast enough that she pictured the phone already in his hand.

"You look hotter than the peas."

She stared at that one long enough for an elderly woman reaching for waffles to give her a look. What got her was the way he had attached the line to the actual small indignity of her standing red-faced in a grocery freezer, not to some fantasy version of her. Men said prettier things than that all the time, usually before disappointing her in some bigger and more boring way. He had let the stupid scene stay stupid and reached for her inside it anyway.

She bought yogurt, two limes, a discounted rotisserie chicken, and the cheapest seltzer. On the walk home he sent another voice note.

"Okay, one actual useful thing. Our monitor guy killed two cables today by yanking them at the jack. Don't buy new earbuds yet. If you show me yours later, I can probably tell whether it's the same stupid break."

She stopped at the crosswalk with a bag cutting into her fingers.

This one was not memory. It had come straight out of whatever mess he was standing in, a piece of his own day handed over still warm.

"You're offering remote triage now?" she typed.

"Against my better judgment."

"I wasn't abusing them."

"That's what every cable killer says."

Back upstairs she delivered the condo-board file nine minutes past the lie she'd told. The client replied with thanks and a reminder about tomorrow's rush job. The reminder should have pissed her off cleanly. It didn't. Something else ran under the day now, bright and private. She ate chicken standing at the sink with one foot on the cool strip of tile by the radiator and listened for his next message like listening for weather.

At four-seventeen he called without warning.

Her whole body moved before her head did. She wiped her hand on a dish towel, saw grease on the screen, wiped that too, then answered voice-only because the camera icon felt too naked at first.

"Hey," she said, trying to sound as if she always picked up on the first ring.

"Hey. You busy?"

Behind him there was a hiss, then the unmistakable shriek of somebody checking a mic through a PA.

"Supposed to be," she said.

"I can call later."

"No. It's fine."

"You sure?"

"Yeah."

He was quiet for a beat, not suspicious, just listening.

"You hiding from me on purpose with the voice thing, or are you in a hostage situation?"

She sat down on the edge of the bed because her knees had gone treacherous. "I look rough."

"Lena. I'm standing next to a dumpster in a loading dock. I promise we're not doing glamour."

She laughed in spite of herself.

"Okay," he said, softer. "Come on."

She hit the camera button before she could decide not to.

For a second they both had the wrong angle. She saw the lower half of his face, stubble darker than she'd pictured, throat, black shirt damp at the collar. He saw probably one eyebrow and her ceiling. Then the images corrected.

He was handsome with the day still on him. The loading dock light hit him greenish from above and made the hollows under his eyes visible. Sweat had flattened a piece of dark hair across his forehead. Behind him a cinderblock wall held a dented EXIT sign, and beyond his shoulder she could see the open mouth of the dock, a strip of late afternoon white and the black ribbed side of a truck. Somebody in the distance rolled a case over a metal threshold and the clatter came clean through the speaker. He looked down once, off-camera, and said, "Give me one sec," to somebody else with the absent courtesy of a man in the middle of work. Then he looked back at her.

"Okay," he said. "Hi."

She felt the stupid heat rise up her neck. "You have a face."

He smiled. It changed him more than the profile photos had, pulling him off center into something more crooked, more alive.

"Bad news?"

"No. Just weird. I had you filed as ear-only."

"Sounds intimate."

"I didn't mean it that way."

"I know. I'm still taking it."

He tipped the phone farther back and she got the full picture at once: black T-shirt, ink on the inside of one forearm, a strip of white spike tape wound twice around his ring finger and hanging loose because he'd been fidgeting it to death. It was such an ordinary, specific thing to have in his hand that something inside her settled. Men assembled out of profile photos did not absentmindedly destroy stage tape while waiting for a woman to turn on her camera.

"You weren't lying about the heat," he said. "You look flushed."

"That's hot-girl branding. Very strategic."

"Sure. And the sweat?"

"Also branding."

He laughed, but he did not do the gross thing where a man acted as if he had caught her performing womanhood badly. He just leaned his shoulder against the cinderblock and looked at her like the fact of her in a hot apartment was enough.

"Show me the earbud," he said.

"What?"

"The cable. The left one that keeps cutting out. Hold it up."

She almost said no on reflex, because the request was so practical it felt more intimate than if he had asked to see her collarbone. Instead she reached for the earbuds where they'd been lying on the bedspread in a snarl and held the split near the camera.

"There," he said immediately. "See that bend right above the jack? You're pulling the stress there every time you wrap it. Tape it two inches up, not right on the split. And stop winding it around your hand like you're taking revenge on it."

She stared at him. "Are you spying on me?"

"No. I just know exactly what people do to cables."

"People?"

"You, mostly."

That mostly landed low in her, almost embarrassingly low.

"I don't even have tape," she said.

He lifted his free hand and showed her the loose white strip on his finger. "You live above a tailor. Steal some. Or use a Band-Aid until tomorrow."

"That sounds sanitary."

"You're not eating it."

Behind him a voice shouted, "Eli, line check in two."

He turned half away. "Yep."

Then, back to her: "I gotta go in a second."

The disappointment crossed her too nakedly because he smiled again, smaller this time.

"Hey," he said. "I'm gonna call you later, okay? After load-out if you're still up."

"That depends."

"On?"

"Whether my glamour schedule allows it."

"Please pencil me in between the peas and deserve slutty."

She was still smiling when the call ended.

For a few seconds she sat with the dead screen in her hand, listening to the room settle back into fan click and street noise. The truck, the shouted line check, the sweat-dark collar, the tape worrying his finger all stayed in her head. He had somewhere to be, people tugging at him, a truck waiting at the dock, somebody yelling line check, and he had still turned back to her.

Her phone buzzed again.

"And drink water. You always forget when you're working."

She went downstairs, knocked on Mr. Velez's half-open door, and borrowed a strip of electrical tape from a shelf crowded with thread cones and garment bags. He handed it over without asking questions, still ironing in an undershirt through August. Back upstairs she bound the earbud cable where Eli had said. The left side came back clean, no static, no cutting. She stood in the middle of the room with one bud in, listening to the condo-board treasurer whistle his way through a final agenda correction. The repaired side held.

At six she opened Hinge because habit was stronger than intention. Three new messages waited there. One man whose profile picture was just a beard in a bar mirror had written, "How's your week beautiful." Another wanted to know if she liked spontaneous road trips, which in app language usually meant he owned a Subaru and a secret grievance. A third had simply sent, "Trouble."

She looked at them long enough to become irritated by their existence, then closed the app without answering.

At ten-forty-eight, after a shower and two false starts on tomorrow's rush transcript, Eli sent a single line.

"You awake?"

She looked at the time, looked at the open file, looked back at his name, and wrote, "Yeah."

She moved the laptop aside to clear space on the bed before the phone even rang.

* * *

By Saturday night she had moved her entire day around three possible windows in which he might be free, which was humiliating and also true.

She did the grocery run before noon because he had a load-in at one. She sent a file early rather than better because he said he might have twenty minutes before soundcheck. She showered at seven instead of waiting for the air to cool because she did not want to be standing half wet in a towel if he called. She told herself this was temporary, first-week nonsense, the kind of thing adults noticed and corrected. Then her phone lit and she answered before the correction could happen.

The long call came late enough that the neighborhood had changed registers. The tailor was dark downstairs. The block voices had thinned to two men arguing softly at the corner and the occasional scooter ripping too fast down a side street. Somewhere nearby a television laugh track kept arriving in short trapped bursts through somebody's wall. Lena had one lamp on, low, and the sheet only over her shins because the apartment still held the day.

He called voice-only from what sounded like a bathroom.

"Please tell me that's a prison break," she said by way of hello.

He laughed, tired. "Hotel ventilation. If I stay in the room you won't hear me over it."

She could hear the room around him in pieces anyway: a distant ice machine dropping a tray, a muffled door somewhere down the hall, the hard slick echo of cheap tile.

"Glamour schedule," she said.

"You were warned."

"How bad is it?"

"The pillows smell like bleach and some guy's scalp. The towels could exfoliate a boat. I think the carpet's damp on purpose."

That made her laugh hard enough that the sheet slipped off one knee.

"You're impossible," she said.

"I'm right."

"You're probably right."

"Thank you. Record this, because it won't happen again."

At night he sounded looser, less polished in the right ways. Words bumped into each other. He left little silences where she could hear him breathe and shift the phone from one hand to the other. Once he said, "Hang on," and she heard him spit toothpaste into a sink. The uglier parts of being in a body did more for his reality than any smooth line could have.

They talked about bad venue coffee, about whether captioning other people's arguments ever left residue in her own head, about the woman in the apartment behind his who practiced trumpet badly enough that he could identify her scales through the wall. He spent five annoyed minutes describing a stage manager who had labeled six power strips with the same strip of tape and then blamed everybody else when half the backline died. He said the hotel vending machine had only neon cookies and a protein bar the color of drywall, and she could hear him tear into one anyway, disgust and wrapper crackle together. Only once did he reach back for her: a catering tray had tried to serve him pale melon and he had thought of her mother's rule about salting cantaloupe in August because that was how real sweetness worked.

He kept handing her the day as it happened, ugly little sounds and all.

At some point after midnight she lay all the way down on the bed and put the phone on her sternum. The heat of it seeped through the thin shirt she slept in. She had turned off the lamp. The room was fan noise and the occasional long rolling groan of a bus. His voice came straight into the dark.

"You getting tired?" he asked.

"A little."

"You should sleep."

"You should stop talking, then."

"Rude."

"You're the one in a bathroom."

"That's fair."

He was quiet a moment. Then: "Can I ask you something that might annoy you?"

Every stupid caution she'd managed not to feel all day arrived at once and took a seat on the edge of the bed.

"Probably," she said.

"You still on Hinge?"

She stared at the ceiling, though there was nothing to see. The question should not have felt loaded. They had matched there less than a week ago. Normal people did not appoint one another to anything this fast. Normal people especially did not do it when one of them existed mostly as a warm rectangle in the hand.

"Technically," she said.

"Technically is a very evil answer."

"It's an honest one."

"Fair."

He did not rush in to claim ground. He did not go hurt or sulky. That somehow made it worse.

"I paused mine tonight," he said after a second.

She rolled onto her side. "Why?"

"Because it started feeling gross."

"Gross how?"

"Like I was checking in on you and then flipping over to a stranger asking if I wanted to rank condiments. It made me feel like an asshole."

She laughed despite herself. "That's most of the app."

"I know."

"You could just not answer people."

"I could. I did. Then the whole thing started feeling noisy, and I don't want noise right now."

He paused, then added, "And yeah, maybe this is selfish, but I don't like picturing your phone lighting up with trout guys while I'm in a hotel bathroom talking to you."

She went still. There it was: appetite with a little grab in it.

"That's a lot for week one," she said.

"I know."

Across the room the laptop still threw a thin blue square over the desk, the rush transcript she'd promised by eight stalled in the ugliest section. Another late rush meant a revision penalty, and the $4,200 tax notice under the cable was not going to shrink because a man with a good voice wanted clarity. At the same time another, older alarm tripped: the marriage came back to her in its early form, practical preferences and gentle instructions, the kind you could mistake for devotion until even the order of her day started answering to them.

"I'm not giving you a speech," he said. "I'm telling you where my head's at."

She let the silence sit.

"This is exactly how people get stupid," she said.

"Probably."

"It's how I got divorced."

He breathed once on the line, steady, neither backing off nor pressing.

"I'm not him," he said.

"No shit."

"Okay."

"It's been five minutes," she said, and immediately hated how thin it sounded.

"I know."

"You don't know me."

"A little."

"A little isn't enough."

"Then don't pause anything for me," he said. "But don't keep me in one ear and a parade of dentists in the other and call it casual. I can't do that part."

She expected reassurance there. Instead he had made himself inconvenient.

"Jesus," she said.

"You asked."

"I'm not asking you for a promise you don't mean," he said. "I'm saying I don't want to act casual if I'm not casual. I'd rather sound stupid than pretend this doesn't have teeth."

The sentence landed differently now, with some grab in it, some want that did not care about being perfectly fair.

"You're making this weird," she said, but there was no edge in it now.

"It was already weird."

"Helpful."

"Come on, Lena. You know what I mean."

She did. Her rush file was still glowing across the room. She'd already sent work early for him, walked to Acme because he'd told her to get cold, started checking the clock against his load-in and load-out before her own deadlines. The speed should have felt fake. It didn't.

"Do you ask everybody this in week one?" she said.

"No."

"How noble of you."

"I'm serious."

"Why me?"

He did not answer immediately. Somewhere in the hotel hallway a door opened, voices passed, then shut away again.

"I keep thinking about your day when I'm in mine," he said. "You tell me one thing about yourself and then I hear the rest of the day around it. And I don't want to be half paying attention to you while somebody named Chris sends me a fish picture."

She laughed with her hand over her mouth. "Chris?"

"It's always Chris."

"Sometimes it's Matt."

"Matt's bad, too."

"Agreed."

"See? That's intimacy."

She felt the give happen anyway, clean and humiliating, like a latch slipping after she'd tested it twice. The sheet had twisted around one ankle. The phone was warm against her palm. Outside, a bus exhaled at the corner and moved on.

"What if this is just first-week brain damage?" she said.

"Then we'll know later."

"That's not comforting."

"It's honest."

He was infuriating for not trying to sell her anything. The choice sat there between them, clean and ugly, hers if she took it.

Her phone buzzed against her hand. Another app notification. Reflex made her glance down.

Hinge: 2 new messages.

The bright little flame icon looked ridiculous next to his name still warm in her ear. She opened the app. Faces came up in orderly little cards: the Manayunk civil engineer holding a trout like evidence, the East Passyunk dentist with adventure twice in his bio, the South Jersey teacher whose paragraph already felt like homework. She had been leaving them there for the version of herself that still wanted backup.

"You still there?" he asked.

"Yeah."

Her thumb hovered over Pause Profile. This was still a man in a phone; that was the sensible part. Then she looked at the cards again and thought about the whole day already stuck to him: peas, earbud cable, pale melon, toothpaste in a hotel sink. The row of men on her screen looked dead on arrival.

"You don't have to do anything tonight," he said, misreading her silence in a way she liked. "I'm not timing you with a stopwatch."

"I know."

She hit pause.

The app asked if she was sure.

She hit yes.

The bright flame dimmed to a neutral little outline, the whole decision reduced to her thumb lifting off the glass and a gray icon staring back.

"Lena?" he said.

"I paused it."

There was a small sound on his end, not triumph exactly. Air leaving him.

"Okay," he said.

"Don't make that noise."

"What noise?"

"That satisfied one."

"I'm not satisfied. I'm trying not to be weirdly happy."

"That's the same thing in a different shirt."

"Fine. I'm weirdly happy."

She smiled into the dark. What felt dangerous was how fiercely she wanted to keep it to herself for now. June would have a joke ready before Lena finished the sentence. Farah would narrow her eyes and ask the practical question. Gabe would turn it into teasing. She didn't want any of their fingerprints on it yet. She wanted one night with it warm in her palm and no chorus laid over it but his voice.

"You don't have to pause yours just because I did," she said, because she heard herself sounding more exposed than she wanted.

"I know," he said. "I already did."

"Right."

"Also, for the record, I wasn't talking to any Chrises worth saving."

"That's noble."

"Thank you."

Sleep would have been the adult move. She stayed on another forty minutes anyway, talking about nothing that would look important in a transcript. The exact shape of the dent in his rental-car seat. Whether the frozen chicken pot pies at Acme were edible or just structurally interesting. The trumpet woman behind his apartment and the kid on Lena's block who kept practicing wheelies badly enough that every attempt ended with a metallic slam and an adult swearing. He asked her to put the phone near the window for a second so he could hear the buses and scooters. She did. He said he liked cities best at the hour when everybody who meant trouble was already either committed to it or asleep.

When he yawned she heard it all the way through, not polite, jaw-cracking, body-first. It made her tenderness for him feel indecently domestic.

"Go to bed," she said.

"You first."

"I'm already in bed."

"Then close your eyes."

"That's too intimate."

"You paused the app."

"You're insufferable."

"And yet."

She did close her eyes because it let his voice move closer.

When they finally hung up, the rush transcript was still open on her laptop, untouched for an hour. The time in the corner said 1:43. The ugliest section was still half-marked, and her client wanted the cleaned file by eight.

She got out of bed, crossed to the desk, and read three lines without taking in a word. Then she opened email instead.

"Running into an export glitch on my end. I'll have the rush file to you by nine," she wrote, and sent it before she could revise the lie into something nobler. It might cost her part of the invoice. It might cost her the next rush if the client got tired of excuses. Relief came first anyway.

The IRS notice was still under the cable on her desk. The fan still clicked every ninth turn.

She picked the phone back up, turned the volume to loud, and opened her alarm app.

He had said he would be awake before call time and probably free for ten minutes around seven. She was already rearranging whole days around scraps like that.

She set the alarm twenty minutes earlier than work required and left the phone on the pillow beside her head.
