# Chapter 9

Eleven days before October 15, Lena had four browser tabs open and all of them were the same lie in different fonts: available balance.

Rain dragged itself down the window by her desk in slow gray ropes. The tailor downstairs had his radio on low and was arguing with somebody through the floorboards, one machine voice and one human one, both irritated. Her caption file sat half-finished on the monitor, a celebrity divorce hearing with clean subtitles and filthy money, but the words would not stay in order. Every time she tried to catch the next line, her eyes slid back to the yellow sticky note she had slapped on the lamp two weeks ago.

OCT 15 - 4200.

IRS and Pennsylvania, as if writing the names down would make them more like people she could bargain with.

Her checking account held $684.17. Her regular savings had $1,918.43. The credit-union subaccount she had nicknamed Q3 TAX still sat at $4,200.00, round and accusing. In the online bank, under the nickname ROSA, there was $5,071.86 left from the trailer sale, the part she kept pretending was not normal money. Winter money. Dentist money. A radiator-dies-in-January-and-the-landlord-finds-new-ways-to-be-deaf money. The original $18,600 had looked thick and adult when it hit, big enough to feel like a plan. By early October it had already become what all money became around her: rent, invoices that came late, the Visa she kept saying she would kill next month, SEPTA taps, groceries, three hundred and fifty dollars for a stolen piece of sound gear she had sent because Eli had sounded too proud to ask, a train to D.C., drinks in a bar where she had sat in a dress with the tags folded into her bag like a little paper tooth.

There were numbers she could move and numbers she had promised herself she would not. The promise had not been to anybody noble. It had been to the version of her that woke up at three in the morning and checked accounts in the dark.

Signal buzzed again.

Eli: You awake enough for something ugly?

Then, before she could answer:

Eli: Voice note. Don't listen while you're trying to type. I know how your face gets.

That made her smile in spite of herself, which was annoying. He had never seen her at the desk in person, but he had built a whole private geography of her anyway: the headphone dent in her hair after three hours, the coffee gone cold by her mouse, the way she pinched the bridge of her nose when somebody said the same stupid thing twice and she had to subtitle it both times.

She put the hearing on pause and listened.

"Hey. I'm sorry. I'm trying not to sound like a fucking scammer and that's making me sound more like one, so I'm just gonna say it plain. The payroll from the Wilkes gig still hasn't cleared. It was supposed to hit yesterday after load-out, and now payroll's telling me maybe tomorrow, which is great, love living in maybe. Noelle's weekly place wants the rest by ten a.m. or they put her stuff out, and I already fronted the storage unit and the locksmith because the ex took the keys when he smashed up the apartment, which is a sentence I still can't believe I'm saying out loud. I'm short thirty-one hundred. If the answer is no, say no clean, okay? Don't make it nicer for me than it is. I just... I hate asking you with your tax shit coming up. I know what month we're in."

The note clicked off. Rain worried at the window, tires made a little wet rasp turning the corner on Moyamensing, and her own breath sounded too loud in the room.

Thirty-one hundred dollars stood there between her and the rest of her life.

It was not the first time money had come into them, as Eli put it, but the earlier transfer had fit inside the story she preferred about herself. Somebody's van window got smashed after load-out, a piece of kit disappeared, she covered the gap, and he had made her feel repaid before any actual repayment had happened. It had been three hundred and fifty dollars and a little thrill. This was a request with a back and teeth. Thirty-one hundred was the kind of number that made the rest of her life get up and move over.

She listened again because dread liked rehearsal. On the second pass she heard the part that had gotten her on the first: I know what month we're in. Nobody else in her life said that about taxes except people who also owed them. Everybody else said, did you do your taxes yet, like they were homework or a doctor's appointment, one ugly day to get through. Eli said it like a season, like a weather front, something that sat on your chest for weeks.

She typed, deleted, typed again.

Lena: I'm up.

Lena: Let me look.

The three dots appeared almost immediately, then disappeared. He was trying not to crowd her. He was good at that.

She got out the narrow legal pad she used when app math started feeling too fake. The paper was soft from kitchen steam and being shoved under plates. She wrote the numbers down in pencil.

Checking 684.17
Savings 1918.43
Q3 Tax 4200.00
Rosa 5071.86

Then under it:

Rent 995 on the 5th
PECO 118.34
Internet 86.19
Visa min 93
Groceries / SEPTA / existing life

Thirty-one hundred sat on the page looking almost elegant. It would have been easier if the number had been messier. $2,947.16 would have felt like a real emergency. $3,100 had the broad clean face of a decision.

She did the subtraction once and then again slower. If she pretended ROSA did not exist, the way she usually did, she could not do it. If she counted ROSA but kept the mental line she had drawn under five thousand, the one that said winter starts when furnaces fail, she still could not do it without opening Q3 TAX. If she opened Q3 TAX, then the yellow sticky note over her lamp stopped being a reminder and became a threat.

Her phone lit again.

Eli: Also you don't owe me an answer fast. I just need the honest one.

He called it the honest one. He always said things like that, words that let her think he was making room when he was also standing in it.

She went to the kitchen sink and opened the cabinet over the stove, where envelopes went to die: a stale wedding invitation, a SEPTA receipt furred with lint, forty-two dollars in cash from a bottle-shop shift folded around a coupon for seltzer, and a MetroCard from the D.C. trip she had kept for no reason except self-punishment. In the junk drawer she found six dollars in quarters, two transit tokens from years ago, and a little velvet box that had once held earrings but now held nothing.

She set the coins on the table anyway, because poverty had its rituals and one of them was making the pathetic pile visible.

Her mother's ring knocked softly against the mug when she reached for coffee. She looked at her hand.

Rosa's signet ring was not delicate and had never pretended to be, with an oval gold face, the R nearly rubbed smooth, and one side nicked from decades of dishes and trailer cabinets and cigarette lighters and whatever else her mother banged her hands against while living. Lena had been wearing it since the funeral, except for one week that summer when her fingers swelled in the heat and she had put it on a dish by the bed because the skin beneath had gone tender and pale. She had put it back on two days later and felt steadier, which was embarrassing, but there it was.

She held the ring with her thumb. The metal was warm from her hand. There were things she owned that had belonged to Rosa, a blender that leaked from the base, three embroidered pillowcases, a box of costume jewelry that turned ears green, but the ring was the only thing that had stayed in motion. It had knocked against coffee mugs, keyboard keys, wine bottles. It had weight. It left a little crescent mark in avocados when she forgot it was on.

She looked at the legal pad again.

The number still sat there, broad and clean enough to feel deliberate.

The apartment felt smaller when she thought about saying no. The desk shoved against the window, the bed two steps away, the storage bins under the table, the air full of hot dust from the laptop fan even though it was raining now; all of it pressed in at once. She did not think he would stop loving her if she said no. That would have been easier to fight. What she thought was worse: he would understand. He would take care of her embarrassment for her. He would say, baby, of course, don't even worry, and then she would spend the next week imagining Noelle's boxes in an alley because Lena had decided October was a bad month to be generous.

She picked up the ring hand again and hated how quickly her mind had moved there. It had skipped right past should I and maybe and gone straight to mechanics.

She thought of the pawnshop on Passyunk, the one with the fading violin in the window and the neon OPEN that only lit up the O and the P. She had been inside once with Farah years ago when Farah needed a bridge week before an insurance check came through. Farah had sold two silver bracelets she called ugly even while her hand stayed on them too long. The place had smelled like old metal and lemon cleaner and a little despair, though maybe that last part had come from the people waiting.

Lena sat back down, typed before she could stop herself.

Lena: Give me an hour.

He answered with a heart and then, a second later, because he knew not to leave it at a heart when she was frightened:

Eli: Thank you for even looking.

That thank you landed on her like he already knew the answer.

She changed into jeans that were dry, found her rain jacket, and almost texted June. Her thumb hovered over the thread. June would know the name of a place that loaned on jewelry without acting like they were doing you a favor. June would also ask three questions too many, not because she wanted the truth but because she liked to hear how people justified themselves while they were still in the act. Lena did not have the energy for June's voice getting careful.

She put the phone away and went out.

October had gone from sticky to soaked in less than a week. The block smelled of wet cardboard, diesel, and the sweet rot from the bags the restaurant around the corner always left too close to the curb. Somebody had chalked a hopscotch grid on the sidewalk and the rain was carrying the numbers sideways. Lena walked fast, head down, her hood shifting with every gust. She passed Farah's shop and did not look in. The window was fogged from the inside and the handwritten ORANGE WINE tag she had lettered on Saturday had bled at the edges where some customer with a damp sleeve had smudged it.

At the corner she stopped under an awning and took the ring off her finger a quarter inch, just to see.

It did not want to go.

She twisted. The skin bunched. She swore softly and shoved it back down, the panic immediate and stupid, as if the ring were offended she had practiced.

The pawnshop door was heavier than she remembered. A bell announced her in a tired metal clack. The place was narrow, deeper than it should have been, with guitars hanging behind wire and a row of laptops in a glass case like sedated animals. A television in the upper corner played a daytime court show with the sound off. Behind the counter sat a woman in a maroon cardigan with one cuff pinned up by a safety pin. Her reading glasses hung on a beaded chain against her chest. A crossword lay open beside the register, blue pen crossing out more than filling in.

She looked up once, took in Lena's wet jacket and face, and did not bother with false brightness.

"Pawn or sell?"

"Pawn," Lena said.

"Good. Selling is where people get dramatic."

The woman held out her hand without introducing herself. Her thumbnail was painted silver glitter, chipped down to the middle. Lena set the ring in her palm, then had to take it back because it was still on her finger. The woman waited without pity or hurry.

"Bathroom?" Lena asked.

The woman reached under the counter and slid over a bottle of hand lotion instead. It was cucumber melon, the kind every nail salon on earth had used in 2004 and apparently one pawnshop on Passyunk still did.

"Try that. Soap makes it swell worse if you're already pissed off."

Lena put a dab around the knuckle. The ring moved, then stuck, then moved again with a little obscene wet pop. Her finger underneath looked naked and undercooked, a pale groove pressed around it so sharply it seemed drawn on. She hated how fast the hand stopped feeling like hers.

The woman turned the ring under the lamp. The TV flashed a bailiff's silent outrage above her head. She peered through a loupe, then set the loupe down and wrote something on a yellow pad.

"Fourteen karat. Nice weight, but the face is worn. You want a pawn, I can do six twenty-five. Ninety days. Interest is what it is. You miss the window, it becomes my ugly ring."

"Six twenty-five?"

"For a pawn. I can offer more if you sell it, but you said pawn."

Lena heard herself say, "It was my mother's."

The woman looked up. For the first time something like a personal expression crossed her face, not softness exactly, more annoyance at the universe for being repetitive.

"Honey, if they weren't somebody's mother's, ex's, grandmother's, I wouldn't have half this inventory."

Then, less sharp:

"Pawn keeps the story open."

Lena laughed once, ugly and surprised. She wiped rain off her upper lip with the back of her hand.

"Fine," she said. "Do the six twenty-five."

The woman pushed a form toward her and tapped the line for ID. Her name, as it turned out from the little plastic badge half-hidden by the cardigan, was Sonia. Sonia counted the bills twice, licked her thumb between stacks, and slid the money across with the ticket.

"Keep that dry," Sonia said. "And don't wait till day eighty-nine if you want it back. Everybody waits till day eighty-nine and then starts telling me stories about miracles."

Lena folded the ticket so small it disappeared inside her wallet. The cash stayed hot from Sonia's hand. On her way out, she almost turned back and said actually never mind, actually give it back, actually I'm still a daughter before I'm whatever the hell this is. But the bell had already clacked once. Sonia had already gone back to her crossword. The hand that had worn Rosa all morning was empty now except for lotion smell.

On the sidewalk Lena tucked the bills into the inside pocket of her jacket and looked at her bare finger again. Rain dotted the skin where the ring should have been, collecting in the pale groove and then slipping off. She sent Eli a single message.

Lena: I'm on my way to the bank.

For a second there was nothing. Then:

Eli: Lena.

Eli: You don't have to destroy yourself for me.

She stood under the failing O and P of the OPEN sign and stared at that line until anger gave her enough heat to move. If he had meant it, he would not have asked. If he had not asked, she would not be standing in the rain with six hundred and twenty-five dollars in pawnshop cash and cucumber-melon lotion drying on the finger her mother used to rap against tables when she wanted everybody to shut up.

She put the phone away and kept walking.

* * *

Freedom Credit Union had the same depressed carpet it had when she opened her first account there at nineteen, a blue meant to hide salt and shame. The lobby smelled like wet umbrellas and printer toner. A fake ficus leaned in the corner with dust stuck permanently to the lower leaves. Lena stood in line behind a man arguing softly with his phone and a woman depositing rent money in an envelope already gone translucent at the edges.

The pawnshop cash sat in her jacket pocket like a fever.

By the time she reached the teller window she had gone past sick and into a kind of bookkeeping trance. She had already made the decisions in the notes app while walking over.

Keep ROSA above five thousand.
Keep enough in checking for groceries and transit.
Break Q3 TAX.

That last line looked cleaner on the phone than it did in her head. Breaking the tax money was not dramatic in a movie way. No sirens. No federal man bursting through the credit-union doors. It meant that on October 15 she would either pay less than she owed or float part of it on the card and call that strategy. It meant she had taken the only round number in her life and bitten a chunk out of it.

The teller was a young guy with a saint medal outside his shirt and a strip of blue athletic tape wrapped around his right pinkie. He kept flexing that finger without seeming to notice. His nameplate said MARCUS.

"What can I do for you?"

"I need to deposit cash and send a wire."

Marcus gave the faint blink people gave when you said wire instead of transfer, as if you had arrived from an older, sadder economy.

"Domestic?"

"Yeah."

"You'll need this." He slid the form through the slot and turned it so the boxes faced her. "There's a twenty-five dollar fee."

The fee made her almost laugh. Of course there was a fee. Every ugly thing in her life came with a smaller ugly thing clipped to it.

She filled in the form with Eli's bank information from the screenshot he had sent after her last message: account number, routing number, name: Elias Ward. She had never needed his full legal name until a financial institution asked for it. Seeing it printed in her own hand gave it a squat documentary weight that made her stomach shift.

Amount: $3,100.00

Purpose of transfer:

She wrote personal and hated the word immediately. Personal sounded like a lotion or a boundary problem. What she meant was this is the shape my humiliation takes today.

Marcus took the paper back and typed, his taped finger hovering over the keyboard like it had its own opinions.

"From checking?"

"Partly." She swallowed. "Can you move twelve hundred from Q3 TAX to checking first?"

"Sure."

He clicked through screens. "And the rest from savings?"

"Yeah. Savings and checking."

He did not ask why somebody had a subaccount named Q3 TAX and was emptying it for a personal wire. He did not ask why she was depositing six hundred and twenty-five dollars in twenties and fifties that smelled faintly of somebody else's hand cream. He asked for her ID, verified the address above the tailor shop, and then slid the screen around so she could confirm.

Checking transfer in: $1,200.00
Cash deposit: $625.00
Wire amount: $3,100.00
Fee: $25.00

Available after transaction: low enough that the exact figure looked vulgar.

Lena signed.

The pen was chained to the counter. The chain rattled when her hand shook, not a cinematic tremor, just enough to knock the plastic flower in the teller cup against the partition twice.

Marcus stamped three pieces of paper and tore one free for her.

"You're all set. It should hit same day unless their bank gets weird."

"Banks get weird," she said.

He smiled at that, quick and involuntary. "Yeah. They do."

She folded the receipt once, then again. Across the top it said WIRE TRANSFER RECEIPT in a font too cheerful for what it meant. Q3 TAX would now be three thousand even. The neatness of the number disgusted her. It looked planned.

Outside, the rain had gone finer, more mist than weather. She stood under the credit union awning because moving felt impossible for a minute. Through the glass she could see Marcus already bent over the next customer's problem, blue tape flashing when he counted cash.

She took a picture of the receipt and sent it.

Lena: done

The answer came before she could lower the phone.

Eli: Jesus, baby.

Then a voice note came through.

She should have waited. She knew that. She pressed play anyway.

"Okay. Shit. I got it. I got the notification while I was downstairs arguing with the front desk, so if I sound out of breath, that's why. She's upstairs filling out another form. Lena, fuck. I don't have a graceful version of this. You saved my ass. You saved hers too. I'm gonna put this back where it belongs as fast as I can. I know what that cost you. I know you didn't do that lightly."

His voice was low and urgent and relieved in exactly the ratio that would have made her weak even if there had not been a receipt in her hand proving she had paid to hear it. The rain ticked on the awning over her head. Somebody on the sidewalk laughed too loud, then coughed. A bus sighed at the corner.

Relief came first. That was the part that made her hate herself.

The relief was physical and quick, a drain opening somewhere behind her ribs. The waiting was over. The arithmetic was no longer hypothetical. Noelle, whether as disaster or sister or just a room full of boxes and a ruined lock, had been dealt with for today. Eli was not on the other end of the thread growing more desperate while Lena stared at columns and acted like numbers were morality. She had done the thing. There was nothing left to decide.

And braided right through that relief, inseparable from it, came the other feeling: the comprehension that the calm had an itemized cost. She had paid $3,125, counting the fee, and a ring her mother had worn through half Lena's life to make the sound in her body stop.

She leaned her forehead against the wet glass of the credit union window. It cooled her just enough to keep her from crying in public, though crying was not actually the pressure in her. What she felt was uglier and flatter. She felt as if she had swallowed a receipt.

Her phone buzzed again.

Eli: I'm standing by a dead ice machine shaking like an asshole.

Another message followed, because silence always loosened him a notch:

Eli: They gave her the room for the week. That's because of you.

She typed with both thumbs because her right hand wanted the ring and there was no ring to touch.

Lena: I'm fine.

He answered immediately.

Eli: You're not fine.

Eli: You don't have to tidy it up for me.

That should have made her angry. Instead it made the back of her eyes sting. He could always find the sentence that left her shame intact and still made it feel held, as if he had reached into her chest and come back with the right wording already warm in his hand. Lena was not asking what to call that today. Today she was asking a smaller one: how to get from the credit union to her apartment without spending any more money.

She walked home because a bus fare now felt obscene.

The city had that rinsed October look she always liked when she was not frightened, brick gone darker, leaves pasted flat to the curb, steam lifting from the halal cart even in the drizzle. A guy outside the laundromat smoked under an umbrella and watched traffic as if it had personally offended him. Two schoolgirls in damp hoodies argued over an eyeliner pencil. A delivery cyclist nearly clipped her and shouted sorry without slowing down.

Everything ordinary stayed ordinary. That felt insulting.

At Farah's corner she almost turned in anyway, not to confess, just to stand by the register where the citrus peel bowl lived and hear Farah say something dry about distributors. The shop would smell like cork dust and wet cardboard and orange rinds. It would smell like a place where money moved in cases and bottles and not by invisible, humiliating leaps from one phone to another. But if Farah looked at her too closely Lena would talk, and if she talked she would either lie or tell the truth, and she did not want either one.

She kept going.

Upstairs, the apartment was exactly as she had left it: divorce-hearing captions frozen on the monitor, coffee cold enough to have a skin, legal pad open with the numbers still lined up like little gravestones. Her chair scraped loud on the floor when she sat. She opened the credit-union app again because self-harm came in many respectable forms.

Checking: $302.17
Savings: $1,000.43
Q3 TAX: $3,000.00
ROSA: $5,071.86

The balances looked fake in their own new way, as if the app had misunderstood her life and simplified it.

Three thousand in Q3 TAX meant she was now twelve hundred short before the deadline, and that was before rent, utilities, or the minimum on the Visa. The app did not show the ring. There was no line for mother's hand, temporarily converted to pawn ticket.

She opened the wallet and took the ticket out. The loan window was ninety days. Item description: LADIES 14K SIGNET RING, ENGRAVED, WORN FACE. Loan amount: $625.00.

Worn face, the ticket called it.

She put the ticket under the yellow sticky note on the lamp. OCT 15 - 4200 stared down at it like a supervisor.

Signal chimed.

Eli was calling.

She let it ring once longer than necessary, out of meanness, or dignity, or because she wanted one second in which he needed her voice and had not yet gotten it. Then she answered.

"Hey," he said, and the relief in him was not performed so much as beautifully arranged. "You home?"

"Yeah."

"Okay. I just needed to hear you inside somewhere."

She heard movement on his end, a door closing, then the hollow room tone she had come to associate with backstage hallways and cinder-block stairwells. He had made spaces for her in his sound. That was one of the things that had gotten her. He never sounded like a generic wherever.

"I can't thank you enough," he said. "I know that's thin compared to what you just did."

"It's a little thin, yeah."

He took that without flinching. "Fair."

There was a pause, not dead air, a pause he knew how to fill without crowding. She could hear him breathing. Somewhere behind him a machine hummed, steady as a cheap fridge.

Lena looked at the legal pad. The pencil lines had smudged where her sleeve hit them.

"I pawned my mother's ring."

The silence on the other end was brief and perfect and made her blood run cold for one strange instant, because it was exactly as long as a decent man would take to absorb that and no longer.

"Fuck," he said, quiet. "Lena."

There it was, the right amount of horror. It was not so much that she would have had to comfort him, and not so little that she would have felt alone.

"You should be mad at me."

"I'm mad at the whole situation."

"That is not the same thing."

"No," he said. "It's not."

Rain tapped the window again. The tailor downstairs laughed suddenly at whatever was on his radio, a bark that came up through the floor. Lena rubbed the pale groove where the ring had sat and got lotion smell all over herself again, all cucumber melon and pawnshop.

"I figured you'd move numbers around," he said. "Not that."

"You also asked."

"Yeah."

She waited for him to defend the ask, or justify Noelle, or say something about repayment schedules and payroll portals. Instead he said, "I know."

That was what he was good at. He knew innocence would insult her and explanation would cheapen the room, so he gave her recognition instead.

It worked on her even now. Maybe especially now. The part of her that had been braced all day eased a little more. She hated that too. She hated how responsive her body still was to being handled carefully.

"I'm gonna get this back to you," he said. "The money first. Then the ring. I don't care if I gotta sell half my kit."

The promise was so direct it should have felt suspicious. Instead it put a stupid pressure behind her eyes.

"Don't say half your kit like it's romantic."

His breath broke into a laugh, small and tired. "That's the least romantic phrase anybody's ever said to you, probably."

"Top ten."

"Okay," he said. "What do you still have to get through tonight?"

What she needed was for him to already be real in a way that could withstand the math. What she needed was to walk back to Passyunk and put Rosa's ring on again and feel the skin close around it like a decision had not just happened. What she needed was not to have felt that first hot wave of relief when his note came through, because that relief was proof of something she did not want to know about herself.

Instead she said, "I need to finish a caption file and pretend I didn't just do that."

"Then let me at least keep the rest of tonight from getting any stupider," he said. "Dump the dead coffee. Put on the mean headphones. Finish the file. When you're done, text me anything. A word, a period, I don't care."

Mean headphones was her phrase, from some offhand joke she had made in week two, and he returned it to her now with interest. She closed her eyes.

"Okay," she said.

"Lena."

"What."

"I love you."

The room did not change. Nothing cinematic happened. No thunder cracked. The rain stayed rain. The paused celebrity hearing stayed frozen on a woman with perfect blowout hair and a caption that read YOU MOVED THE MONEY WITHOUT TELLING ME. Down on the street somebody leaned on a horn and then let it go.

But Lena heard the sentence as if it had weight, as if it had landed on the table with the legal pad and the pawn ticket and the yellow note.

She had said something close enough at Saint Rocco night that the distinction no longer mattered. He was only answering now, or formalizing, or using a word that had already been doing the work for weeks. Even so, she felt the syllables in her body like another debit.

"I know," she said, because saying it back right then would have felt less honest than the money had.

He did not punish her for that either.

"Okay," he said again, easy, warm, as if I know were a tender answer and not a dodge. "Text me later."

When the call ended, the apartment got louder: rain, fridge, the tailor's radio, and the tiny tap of her fingernail against the desk where the ring should have made a thicker sound.

She reheated the coffee in the microwave and it came out tasting burned and metallic, but she drank it anyway. Then she put on her actual mean headphones, the over-ear pair with one side taped, and tried to return to the divorce hearing. The first line she captioned after all of it was a man saying, under oath, that he had only moved funds temporarily.

She stared at the sentence until the words blurred.

Then she laughed, once, hard enough to feel it in her nose, because if the room was going to insult her that openly it could at least have the decency to be funny.

She corrected the timestamp, typed the line clean, and kept going.

Every few minutes her thumb found the groove in her finger and came away with nothing. Every few minutes her eyes slid to the lamp, where the pawn ticket sat under OCT 15 - 4200 like a second deadline she had made for herself.

By evening the rain had thickened again. The window filled with water. The apartment dimmed early. Lena worked until the captions moved on instinct and the numbers in her head became a lower, steadier ache.

By then she no longer needed the legal pad to do the math. She was twelve hundred short for October 15, the pawn ticket gave her ninety days, and twenty-five dollars was gone just to push the money through. His name kept showing up with those numbers now, sliding in beside rent and the Visa minimum whether she wanted it there or not.

That did not thin the love. It only dirtied it, which was worse.

Around eight, Signal lit the desk.

Eli: You fed me and saved me in the same day. That's a lot of woman.

It was exactly the kind of line that would have melted her yesterday. Tonight it did something more complicated and more final. It warmed her. It disgusted her. It made her put the phone face down and hold the bare finger against her mouth until the cucumber-melon smell had finally gone.

At nine she left the laptop open on the desk, washed the mug, and put the legal pad in the drawer with her passport, the Wildwood paperwork, and the envelopes she used for taxes. She left the pawn ticket under the lamp. She wanted to see it when she woke up. She did not trust any version of herself that would have tried to tidy this away overnight.

Near midnight, another message came through: a picture of coiled cable on a concrete floor, the toe of his boot in one corner.

Eli: load-out's a mess. i'll call when i can.

In bed, she set the phone on the pillow beside her face and left one earbud in. The bare finger kept brushing the sheet as if looking for its usual resistance. The absence had texture. It snagged her attention every time she turned over. She stayed awake longer than she meant to, stupidly alert for the phone, until the lit lamp and the stalled caption file on the desk finally blurred into sleep.
