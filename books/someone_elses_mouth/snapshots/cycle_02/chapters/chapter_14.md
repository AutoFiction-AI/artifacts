# Chapter 14

When the power died, the room got louder.

She'd been back from the trailer less than two hours, just long enough to open the laptop and start pretending work was a cleaner problem.

The fan in Lena's window clicked once and quit. The tailor's steam press downstairs gave a last insect whine and went dead. Rain, which had been sitting behind all the electrical noise for an hour, came forward and took the apartment back. It hit the glass in slanted handfuls. Somewhere on Moore, a man said ah, come on like the storm had singled him out for insult.

Lena stared at her laptop screen while the battery icon went red. The caption file she had spent six hours cleaning had exported clean at last. The invoice had not gone out. The client portal had logged her off when the router died, and the PDF sat in downloads like an accusation: 3:14:22 of rush cleanup, medical webinar, $684 due. If she sent it tonight, there was a chance the client paid before the weekend and Mrs. Alvarado did not have to slide December's rent reminder under Lena's door with that brisk pink Post-it she used when she wanted to be polite about pressure. If she did not send it, the money would move to next week, which meant the card minimum, which meant groceries with a calculator out, which meant one more stupid month of being impressed by canned beans.

The battery hit three percent.

She tried the hotspot on her phone even though the service had already started stuttering. Nothing. The login wheel spun and spun. Her charger was useless without power. Her phone was at eleven percent and dropping, because of course tonight had to be the night she had spent her last wall charge on a reheated bowl of caption revisions and not on foresight.

She looked at the black square of her window. Across the street, half the block was dark. The deli still glowed at the corner, dirty yellow and miraculous. One block south, the lights on the cross street were still burning. June's block. Of course.

Her first thought was practical and old enough to live below thought: go to June.

The second thought came after it, slower and meaner. After the shore. After the phrase overlap. After the trailer, with June talking about money like she was picking at a scab with a thumbnail. Lena had not said any of that aloud. She had let suspicion sit in her like bad shellfish and told herself that suspicion was not proof, that grief and embarrassment could make patterns out of steam. But now, in the dark, with the unpaid invoice on her dead machine and rain needling the window, her body still reached for the oldest route. June had her spare keys. June had once bicycled to a Rite Aid at midnight for tampons and a heating pad when Lena was nineteen and folded up with cramps on a bathroom floor. June was where practical emergency went.

Muscle memory beat doubt by half a second. Half a second was enough.

Lena grabbed her phone, the charger, the printed caption notes she had been using as backup, and went downstairs. The tailor shop gate was half-pulled already, Mr. Velez bent under it with a flashlight in his teeth, cursing softly in Spanish. On the sidewalk the rain smelled like wet brick, gutter metal, and old fryer grease washing loose from the corner. Water ran black along the curb. Her sneakers took it in at once.

June buzzed her in before Lena had to text twice.

"You're alive," June called from below. "Come down before the stairs decide they're a waterslide."

The basement apartment was cooler than the street in a way that felt wrong in late November, damp-cold instead of weather-cold. It smelled like coffee gone stale in the pot, nicotine that had seeped into fabric years ago and was never fully leaving, and the hot plastic breath of too many surge protectors under one desk. Cords looped from the folding table to the wall like black vines. Two old laptops sat stacked beside a cracked monitor. A sneaker tray by the door held June's work boots, a pair of Mateo's beat-up black-and-white trainers, and three loose screws for no reason Lena could see. June herself was in gray sweats and a Sixers hoodie with one cuff dark from rain. A bodega receipt was tucked under the elastic of her watchband, safe there like she had run out of pockets halfway through the day.

"Only my block's out," Lena said. Her hair was already sticking damp to the back of her neck. "I just need ten minutes. I've got to send an invoice before I turn into a pumpkin or a delinquent."

"Those are the same thing in this neighborhood." June shut the door with her heel. "Use the silver one. Personal, not the work brick. Power hiccupped and the work brick started timing out on the VPN, so I dumped half my browser shit onto it. Don't open anything minimized unless you want to read property managers crying about package lockers. If you want coffee, don't. It's disgusting."

She said it lightly, fast the way she always did when she was making a mess sound temporary. Still, Lena heard the tiredness under it. June's face had that flat, unreadable look it got when she had not slept enough and had no intention of letting anyone name it.

"You okay?" Lena asked, and hated herself a little for how automatic it sounded.

"Landlord texted that the back window well is taking water." June rolled her eyes, already reaching for a flashlight from the kitchen counter. "So now apparently I'm the Army Corps of Engineers. Send your thing. If the internet kicks, hit the router with the side of your hand. It likes disrespect."

"That's not how routers work."

"You need me to pretend I care?"

Under another set of circumstances Lena would have smiled. She almost did, and then did not. June had already turned away, jamming her feet into the work boots without untying them. She shoved her phone into the kangaroo pocket of the hoodie, patted for the flashlight, found it, and glanced back once.

"Door sticks if you leave before I get back. Pull up, then in."

"Okay."

"And don't open the tabs I shoved down into the dock unless you want to see me getting yelled at by property managers."

"Jesus."

"I'm serious. I was moving everything around when the water text hit. It'll ruin your night in a different way."

Then she was up the short basement stairs and gone, the outer door banging once behind her. Rain thickened against the little high windows at street level. Somebody upstairs dragged something heavy over tile. The apartment settled around Lena with all June's systems still humming inside it.

She sat at the folding desk and touched the silver laptop awake.

The desktop was already open. The silver laptop still wore June's emergency reshuffle all over it. Her dead external monitor had collapsed two workspaces into one screen; one browser sat open on a KeyNest dashboard dense with maintenance tickets and reply templates, and another had been swiped down to a strip of icons at the bottom instead of logged out, as if she had meant to clean the whole thing up and then the landlord text had yanked her upstairs mid-motion. Slack bounced once in the dock. An email draft sat half-finished in June's inbox about key fob replacements and tenant abuse of package lockers. In another life it would have been funny how much of adulthood was just other people's access problems.

Lena opened a browser tab, logged into her invoicing portal, and used the printed notes in her lap to rebuild the line items she had lost when her own laptop died. Rush cleanup. Speaker IDs. Burned-in corrections after client review. She could do it blind. She had done versions of this blind, tired, bleeding, half-drunk, furious, ashamed. Work was often the only place her humiliation could not follow her because timecodes did not care what had happened to her.

She attached the PDF. She filled in the amount. She hit send.

The page thought about it long enough to annoy her. Then a green bar rolled across the top.

Invoice sent.

She had just enough time to feel the cheap, mean relief of it when a notification slid up in the corner of the screen, pale gray on black.

Manual review suggested: eli_ward / target instability spike / contradiction event unresolved.

Lena's finger stayed on the trackpad. She read it once, then again, slower. Manual review suggested. Eli Ward. Target instability spike.

The notification stayed on screen long enough to become rude.

She clicked it because there was no world in which she did not.

The minimized window opened full across the laptop. At the top left, in small clean type, the platform name sat where a Gmail tab should have been: OpenClaw Studio. Beneath it, in a darker strip, a workspace title: eli_ward. At the far right of the header, next to a round avatar with June's initials in it, a green dot glowed beside Active.

For a second Lena's mind tried to build a stupid, harmless explanation. June used OpenClaw for work. June automated things. June had probably named some internal test after Eli because Lena had been talking about him for months and June had a sick sense of humor.

Then her eyes adjusted to the rest.

Below the header, a status ribbon ran across the screen.

mode: mixed autonomy
manual escalation triggers: money / contradiction / silence

The left rail listed folders and modules in a tree:

Identity shell
Source media
Voice assets
Memory bank
Live threads
Repair loops
Payments
Manual overrides

Under Live threads, one item was expanded. lena_signal.

Under it, in smaller gray text, a freshness marker updated in real time. Last inbound: 17m ago.

Her own breath made a tiny damp mark on the screen before she leaned back. It would not have landed if she had been farther away. She had been that close already.

The main pane held a branching map in colored boxes linked by fine white lines. It looked, obscenely, like subtitle software. A timeline. Speaker tracks. Conditional branches. Only instead of names like Moderator or Q&A 2, the boxes said things Lena recognized with an animal drop in her stomach.

App meet cute
Voice note hook
Late-night exclusivity
Family legitimation
Video intimacy
Minor rescue ask
Union Station salvage / Noelle crisis
Larger emergency / payroll freeze
Shore trip repair
Suspicion management

Each box could be opened. Some were green, some yellow, one red. Little rule badges sat under them: delay window, tone pack, follow-up threshold. Eli had not been improvised one message at a time. He had been kept warm between interventions.

Lena sat very still. The folding chair dug a square edge into the backs of her thighs. Rainwater from her jeans wicked into the vinyl seat. She could hear a sump pump somewhere in the building kicking on and off with a wet, exhausted cough.

She clicked Manual overrides because it was the only label on the rail that admitted a human hand.

The log loaded with dates, timestamps, and operator initials.

09/06/27 8:12 PM - JS manual override
video intimacy. No "beautiful." She won't buy it tonight. Use forearms / knees / how she listens.

09/06/27 8:17 PM - JS manual override
If she freezes, tell her to leave the lamp on. She hates being looked for and not at.

09/14/27 6:41 PM - JS manual inject
van window branch. Resist first offer. Accept on second. Keep pride intact.

09/14/27 6:49 PM - JS manual inject
Keep it in minor-rescue range. Accept with embarrassment. Voice note after transfer.

10/03/27 5:06 PM - JS manual inject
larger ask this week. Need urgency that allows delay + shame. Payroll freeze cleaner than theft repeat.

10/03/27 5:09 PM - JS private note
DeLuca won't wait. Get there.

Lena made a sound she had never heard herself make, not a gasp and not a word. Something small being torn.

She kept reading because now stopping would have been the same as mercy, and mercy had missed its window.

10/17/27 11:22 PM - JS manual override
dog test. Do not challenge memory. Fold it into care. Make her feel seen, not corrected.

11/02/27 3:14 PM - JS manual override
shore trip blowback. Admit nothing. Let him sound tired. More static on calls.

11/18/27 9:03 PM - JS manual note
friendship suspicion rising. Remember she still comes to me for practical help. That habit is older than this.

Lena shut her eyes, but it was there too: not only the romance, not only the money, but June's written architecture of Lena's reflexes, laid out like a tradesperson measuring a doorway before bringing in a couch.

When she opened her eyes again, the room had narrowed to the desk, the laptop, the water stain spreading from the hem of her jeans, the chipped mug full of pens and one tiny screwdriver, the pale groove around her ring finger where Rosa's signet had been for years before she walked it into the pawn shop under fluorescent lights and let a man with paper skin weigh her mother in grams.

She skipped Memory bank. She already knew June had her facts. What she had not known until now was how completely June had fed them into his mouth.

Voice assets was docked beside Source media, paired tabs like bad twins. Under the header, the categories ran in a row: cleaned stems, accent training, breath beds, laughter variants.

There was a clip labeled morning-rough-02, another labeled tired-sincere-05, and a third labeled laugh-soft-no-snort. On the source side, folders opened down the left with neat names:

asher_quinn_press_2019
forearm_refs
tour_bus_broll
late_night_voice_pack
tattoo_stills
dc_loading_dock_loops

In the main pane, thumbnails populated: a young white man behind a drum kit, half-turned toward stage light; an interview clip with the audio stripped and cleaned; stills of tattooed arms from what looked like three different bodies; grainy backstage footage cropped so tight a human could become a mood. One file name carried the same drummer name Farah had found in October. Another had a note beside it: jawline useful / don't overuse left profile.

Eli's face had not even belonged to one stranger. It had been cut together out of several.

Under a subfolder called intimacy prompts sat a text file preview, visible without opening all the way.

If she gets self-conscious on camera, slow down.
Praise what is specific, not what sounds hungry.
She won't believe "gorgeous" when she's ashamed.
Try:
- I like the way your forearms look when you're bracing yourself.
- Keep the lamp. I want to see you.
- You don't have to hide your stomach from me.

Below that, one final line in June's shorthand:
If knee scar shows, ask where she got it. She likes being remembered in the body.

Lena jerked her hand back from the trackpad as if it had shocked her. The scar on her left knee was from sixth grade, from falling on church steps in a plaid skirt while June laughed first and then helped her up and picked gravel out of the skin with the corner of a tissue in the girls' bathroom. It was not bedroom data. It was not a usable trait. It was hers and June's and a stupid old afternoon and now it lived here under intimacy prompts like a button to press.

The rain hit harder. Overhead, somebody shouted for a bucket. A siren went past on Broad, blurred by water until it sounded almost underwater itself. Lena pressed her thumb into the meat of her palm hard enough to feel bone. It gave her something that was still hers.

Her phone, face down by the keyboard, began to buzz. It buzzed once, then again. It was a message from Eli.

You okay over there? Storm looks ugly. Keep your phone charged for me.

For one second she only stared at the words because they were so perfectly of him, of course they were, warm and lightly bossy and pitched exactly at the version of care that had always slid under her guard. Then the laptop chimed.

In the upper right corner of the OpenClaw window, a small activity panel expanded on its own.

Autonomy action executed: check-in / target silent 17m / thread lena_signal.
Output source: warm-concern-v3 + storm context.
Delivered.

The same sentence she held in her hand sat there in miniature, timestamped to the second.

Lena did not have to infer anything anymore. She did not have to be fair. She did not have to tell herself grief was making patterns or loneliness was making monsters or maybe June had built a model for some other idiot woman named Lena Cordero who captioned medical webinars and once showed her left knee on camera to a man who did not exist. The machine on June's desk had just spoken to her in Eli's voice while June was upstairs with a flashlight and a landlord and a flooded window well.

Payments sat in the left rail with a green dot beside it.

She opened it anyway, because if she left the money untouched some stupid merciful corner of her would keep pretending the fraud had stopped at words and skin.

It was not a ledger exactly but something worse: a set of scenarios.

minor rescue - pride maintained
public humiliation salvage
family emergency extension
payroll delay / reimbursement promise
cooldown after transfer
reactivation after silence

Each one opened into scripts, timing suggestions, and little decision trees. Some were automated conditions. Some were plainly June's hand.

For minor rescue:
Never request outright.
Signal damage, refuse once.
Accept only after target insists.
Immediate return: gratitude + masculine embarrassment + concrete future plan.

For larger emergency:
Use already-established collateral figure (Noelle).
Frame ask as temporary bridge, not need.
Known barriers: taxes 4.2k; trailer proceeds approx. 18.6k / "mom money."
Target more likely to cross protected-money line if she believes she is saving housing, not career.
Do not stack with sex. She'll feel bought.

And under that, in the right column, a section labeled target payment behavior.

first transfer / minor rescue
relief + concealment from family predicted

larger transfer / payroll delay frame
severe shame after; maintain warmth, reduce availability 5-7 days

Below it sat a note:
She will work extra shifts instead of naming regret. Let her.

The groove around her ring finger seemed to fill with cold. She could see the pawnshop scale again, the tiny digital readout deciding what her mother came to.

Lena looked away from the screen and saw nothing in the room that was not suddenly witness: the screwdriver in the mug, Mateo's trainers by the door, a dish towel striped green and white over the sink, a half-empty box of Saltines. June's whole ordinary basement life still sat there, stubbornly itself, while inside one laptop she had turned Lena into intake fields and thresholds and expected yield.

She lifted her phone and took pictures of the screen because some part of her was still practical enough to know that horror got slippery later. She started with the status ribbon and its mixed autonomy label, then the manual override that mentioned DeLuca by name, then the intimacy prompt with the knee scar, then the payment behavior pane with its protected-money language sitting there like a shop tag, then the activity panel, still glowing Delivered.

Her battery dropped to eight percent.

Footsteps thudded above. The basement door opened. June came down hard and fast, shaking rain from her sleeves, flashlight clamped under her arm now. Her cheeks were pink from the cold. She was talking before she hit the last stair.

"He put a bath towel in the window well like water respects effort. You send it?"

Lena was still holding the phone up. The laptop was still open between them, bright as a stage. June looked at the screen, and the whole apartment changed shape.

It did not happen dramatically; there was no confession, no dropped glass, no theatrics. June just stopped one step short of the floor. The flashlight slid against her side and knocked once against the banister. Her mouth closed. That was all.

For months Lena had been living inside the voice of a man carefully designed to answer her. Now there was only June in a wet hoodie, one hand still on the rail, the bodega receipt softening under her watchband, and the laptop between them with all the borrowed mouths open at once.

Lena looked from June's face to the green dot beside Active and back again. The certainty landed all at once, hard and graceless.

She knew.
