# Chapter 6

At the sink in her underwear, Lena drank coffee gone bitter on the second reheat and opened her banking app before she was fully awake. Under checking, after PECO took its bite overnight, sat $264.18. The screenshot from 11:42 p.m. still sat in her camera roll. Cash App: $350.00 sent to Eli Ward. Green check, confetti animation, the whole infantile little celebration for having made herself poorer because he had been standing in a parking lot with broken glass in his voice, because he had said tell me when it lands, because there had been no way to do the thing halfway once she'd decided to do it.

Enough to act like nothing was wrong if she got careful. Enough to make the next two weeks uglier than she'd planned. She tried out sentences the way she tried out caption lines before sending them to a client.

He didn't ask.

It wasn't rent money.

Adults helped each other.

That one held longest. Better to call it help than stand at the sink building a case against herself before breakfast.

She opened her savings and looked, just to feel less cornered. What Rosa had left in cash still sat there in its larger, quieter block, obscene in a different way. Lena had already subdivided it in her head into taxes, Visa minimums, rent, winter, whatever bad surprise came next. She hadn't touched that. Last night had come out of checking, out of the breathable part of her life, the part meant for groceries and SEPTA and whatever cheap dinner let her pretend she lived like a person with options. That distinction mattered for maybe three seconds before her brain supplied the obvious answer: money moved, and then it was moved.

Gabe texted while she was buttoning her shorts.

`Girls want to know if phone drummer fixed his apocalypse`

A second bubble came right behind it.

`Also Maite's making pernil Sunday if you can stop by`

Lena smiled despite herself. Gabe could never leave one subject alone long enough to look casual. Somewhere in his louder, brighter house the twins were probably climbing him while he tried to type with one thumb, and Maite was yelling from the kitchen that if she wanted any skin she needed to come early. She could hear the room without hearing it.

She typed, deleted, typed again.

`He's not a drummer.`

She deleted it. It was too corrective. It sounded too much like she cared.

`He's okay. Whole thing was a mess. We had to rain check.`

Before Gabe could answer she sent the second one.

`I picked up a shift Sunday though. Tell Maite I love her and I'm sorry.`

The dots appeared. Disappeared. Came back.

`Farah got you in chains`

`Only retail chains. The sexy kind.`

`That sentence just made me lose my appetite`

She could do this. She could keep the transfer where it belonged: between her, June, the receipt, and the man whose voice had gone low and stunned when it hit.

Outside, South Philly was already hot enough to smell yesterday's beer sweating out of the sidewalk. A scooter coughed past. Somebody upstairs from the tailor shop was fighting in Dominican Spanish with the windows open, not because they wanted an audience but because the building trapped heat like a grudge and nobody got privacy in September. Lena locked up, checked that the screenshot was no longer sitting naked on the home screen, and walked to Farah's with her bag knocking damply against her hip.

Farah was on a step stool when Lena came in, reaching for a row of skin-contact bottles no one on the block bought unless they wanted to perform a personality at dinner. She had one foot braced against the shelf, silver flashing at her temples under a messy knot, and the whole shop smelled like cork dust and orange peel and the cold wet mineral note of the white-wine case that had leaked near the back fridge.

"You look like a court date," Farah said without turning around.

Lena dropped her bag behind the counter. "Morning to you too."

"I'm being warm. If I wanted to be rude, I'd ask if you slept in your eyeliner on purpose."

"I wasn't wearing any."

Farah got down from the stool and looked at her properly. She had a strip of price-tag backing stuck to one palm and a pencil tucked through the neck of her T-shirt. "Even worse. What happened?"

Lena went for the version that had the fewest moving parts. "The date didn't happen. His van got hit after load-out. Somebody smashed a window, grabbed part of his kit. It turned into one of those stupid nights where everybody is standing around in a parking lot pretending they're calmer than they are."

"Jesus." Farah peeled the paper off her palm and flicked it at the trash. "He had insurance?"

"I didn't ask for the whole fucking claim sequence."

"You didn't ask because you were trying not to look like a payroll auditor." Farah's mouth tipped, not kind exactly but not cruel either. "Did he at least sound embarrassed?"

Lena heard the question under the question. "Yeah."

"Good. Men should sound embarrassed sometimes. It keeps the blood moving."

Lena laughed. It came out thin. She turned on the register, listened to its little waking beep, and hated the way everything with money announced itself. The drawer popped. Receipt tape curled like a tongue from the side.

Farah watched her a second longer. "You want tea?"

"I'm fine."

"That wasn't a wellness check. I don't want you passing out into the Beaujolais."

"I had coffee."

"Then God help the customers."

The first hour moved the way shop hours always did on hot weekends: slow until it was suddenly not. A guy from two blocks over came in for two warm six-packs and spent five minutes arguing with the price of ice as if Farah personally had frozen it with malice. A woman in scrubs bought a bottle of sauvignon blanc and the cheapest dark chocolate bar from the side rack, then stood at the card reader squinting at the total long enough that Lena pretended to rearrange bags so the pause would have some place to go. The bell over the door kept making its brittle sound. The AC kept losing to the weather.

Between customers Lena checked her banking app once, then again five minutes later as if numbers might feel less accusatory on the second look. $264.18. Rent wasn't due yet. The Visa minimum would sit on the twenty-second like a little knife with a calendar invite. One caption invoice was supposed to clear Tuesday, maybe Wednesday if the client felt slow and holy. She locked the phone and slid it under the counter, like hiding it from herself counted as discipline.

Near noon Farah cut open a box of shelf talkers and said, "I can get Sami to cover Friday close if the distributor actually sends the rose this time."

Lena was breaking down cardboard in the back, the corrugation rough against the heel of her hand. "I'll do it."

Farah kept writing on the case stack with her marker. "You already have Saturday."

"I can do Friday too."

That made Farah look up. "You can. That's not what I said."

Lena shrugged, too fast. "I've got a gap."

"If you're broke, say you're broke. Don't say gap like you're briefing a hostage negotiator."

"Broke-adjacent, then."

Farah set the marker between her teeth, capped it, and came farther into the back room where the floor always smelled faintly of wet cardboard and old cork. "I don't need a confession, Lena. I need to know if you want hours or if you want distraction, because those are different payroll categories."

The question landed harder because it was better than the one Lena had been bracing for. She leaned the flattened box against the wall. "Hours."

Farah studied her another second, then nodded once. "Friday close. If the truck's late, you stay till inventory's done. That gets you closer to one-eighty than one-twenty."

"Thanks."

"Eat before you come. I mean it. You get mean when your blood sugar drops and then you start telling customers the truth."

"I tell them plenty of truth."

"You told that man last month that his favorite cabernet tasted like mulch and divorce."

"It did."

Farah gave her the smallest smile. "See."

They worked the rest of the shift in pockets: silence, then bursts of talk, then silence again. Farah told her the landlord next door was trying to raise commercial rent on a nail salon nobody had painted in six years. Lena took a call from a subtitling vendor who wanted a turnaround so stupid it almost qualified as an insult. A college kid came in asking for orange wine "that didn't taste like feet," and Farah sent him to the fridge with such regal contempt that Lena had to bite the inside of her cheek not to laugh.

When the lull came, Farah pushed half a bruised peach across the counter on a paper towel. "Eat."

Lena looked at it. "That's a depressing amount of fruit."

"It's a realistic amount of fruit. Eat it."

The bruise had gone dark as tea at one side, but the rest was cold from the shop cooler and sweet enough to make her feel briefly restored. Juice ran down the heel of her thumb. She licked it off before it could drip on the register keys.

Her phone buzzed with a message from Eli. She saw only the start in preview.

`I know last night got ugly and I don't want you...`

She turned the screen facedown so fast Farah noticed.

"Oh, so the musician lives."

"Apparently."

Farah was slicing a case strap with her box cutter. "You don't have to narrate this to me like a courtroom sketch artist. I'm just saying if he starts acting like his emergency moved into your apartment, that's a bad sign."

"He didn't."

"All right."

It was only two words, but Farah had a way of putting entire ledgers into them. Lena went back to stacking shelf tags. She could feel the message under the phone like heat under a door.

After clock-out she took the long way home so she would not have to walk past Gabe's place and smell dinner in the windows. At the corner store she stood in front of the fridge and did the arithmetic that had become second nature since her divorce: eggs or decent coffee, laundry card reload or the good detergent, sliced turkey or just peanut butter again. She left with a dozen eggs, a bag of rice, two cans of black beans, and the store-brand coffee that tasted faintly like wet pennies unless she drowned it in sugar. The guy at the register gave her twelve cents in change and a receipt so long it looked accusatory.

By the time she climbed the stairs above the tailor shop her T-shirt was stuck to the middle of her back. The apartment held the day's heat with vindictive loyalty. She fed the window unit two sharp slaps before it rattled into obedience, changed into an old tank top, and opened the subtitling file Farah's call had reminded her to accept because she could no longer afford the dignity of saying no to nonsense.

It was a Hungarian film, or maybe just Hungarian enough to her ear that it made no difference. The vendor had sent translated dialogue in a sidecar document and audio that crackled in one channel. Lena did not understand a single word in the original. What she had were timecodes, an English approximation, and two actors fighting in a kitchen so small their voices kept bouncing off tile.

She spent forty minutes deciding whether a line needed `you always do this` or `you always do this to me`, because one version meant habit and the other meant indictment, and the translation note from the vendor only said EMOTIONAL ACCUSATION. The man's voice went up at the end as if pleading. The woman's cut through him in flat strokes. A pan hit something hard. A chair scraped. Lena timed the captions to breath and collision more than to language, feeling for the moment one mouth took over from the other.

At 9:17 Eli sent a voice note. She knew from the waveform shape before she opened it that it wasn't short. She left it unopened and kept working.

In the film the woman crossed the room in hard shoes and said something that made the man laugh once, sharp and humorless. The translation offered `Then keep your gift.` A beat later came `Nobody asked you to be noble.` Lena rolled back the waveform, listened to the original again, and changed `gift` to `favor` because it sounded less ceremonial and more cruel.

By ten she had billed three hours she would maybe get paid for in fourteen days. Friday close would add somewhere between one-twenty and one-eighty, depending on the truck and whether Farah remembered to count the inventory time right. Three hundred and fifty dollars could be reduced, if she worked at it hard enough, into separate harmless pieces: two extra shop shifts, one ugly foreign-language file, coffee she did not buy, dinner she cooked herself. It was only money. Money went and came. Money was not a verdict unless you let it be.

She finally set the headphones down because the Hungarian couple were still screaming and she could feel their argument in the bridge of her nose. In the silence after, the building gave her the neighborhood back: TV laugh track through the wall, somebody dragging a cooler down the sidewalk, a SEPTA rumble far enough away to feel like furniture moving in the earth.

Eli's note was still waiting.

She looked at it, then at the rush file still half done, then at Friday close written in Farah's blue marker in the photo she had snapped of the new schedule. By midnight her eyes felt sanded. Her hands smelled like peach skin and cardboard and the faint metallic ghost of coins.

She put the phone facedown again and went back to the arguing lovers.

***

June's place was cooler by eight degrees and smelled like cigarettes nobody was currently smoking, old plastic, and the cumin from whatever had been microwaved an hour ago. Lena had texted first to see if June wanted the bruised half of Farah's free Manchego, and June had answered `come down if you're alive / school portal wants a body count.`

The basement apartment looked as if every cord in South Philadelphia had molted there. Old laptops sat shut and stacked near the wall. A box fan chopped the air in one corner. Mateo was at the folding table in a maroon school hoodie with one drawstring missing, his beat-up headphones hanging around his neck, one ear pad held together with black electrical tape. He was clicking a mechanical pencil open and shut with the concentration of a person trying not to say what he really thought.

June barely glanced up from the district website on her screen. "Tell me you brought cheese and not sympathy."

"I brought very judgmental cheese."

"Good. Sit down. This thing wants three proofs of address, which is adorable because children famously control housing."

Mateo did not look up. "It also wants my emergency contact to be somebody who picks up the phone before noon, which feels biased."

June stabbed the keyboard once. "Use me first and your mom second."

He stopped clicking. "She'll get weird about it."

"She can get weird after you stay enrolled."

The sentence could have come out hard. In June's mouth it came out practical, almost bored, like she had already spent the emotional version somewhere else and only the useful part was left. She wore work boots unlaced and an old black tank top, and there was a nicotine stain on the fingers she used to flatten Mateo's crumpled attendance form. Her face looked younger when she was tired, not prettier exactly, just stripped down to the bones Lena had known since middle school.

Lena put the cheese on the table between a geometry workbook and a stack of photocopies. "What did he do?"

Mateo's eyes stayed on the paper. "I was late."

June snorted. "He was late nine times in eleven days and skipped one advisory because the vice principal decided headphones were a gateway drug."

"I took them off."

"You took them off after she said your name three times."

"That still counts as taking them off."

Lena sat on the arm of the couch because every chair in June's place had paperwork on it. "That's a very lawyerly defense."

Mateo glanced at her then, brief and sideways. His acne was worst across the forehead this month, angry little marks above the line where the headphones lived. "It's a factual one."

"There he is," June said. "The boy speaks in full paragraphs only when he's technically right."

A small smile tried Mateo's mouth and gave up. He went back to the form.

Lena reached for one of the photocopies. It was a temporary guardian authorization, half the boxes already filled in by June's dense all-caps. Another sheet was an attendance contract with phrases like RESPONSIBILITY and IMPROVED OUTCOMES that made Lena want to bite somebody. There was a printed email from a counselor noting that continued lateness could affect access to after-school programs.

"This is for the lab?" she asked.

Mateo's head came up fully at that. "Yeah. If they pull it, I can't stay for comp-sci. Which is stupid, because I'm late because I'm walking my cousin to middle school three mornings a week, and then they're gonna make me miss the only useful thing in that building."

"There it is again," June said. "See how he suddenly has a TED Talk."

He ignored her. When he cared, the words came cleaner, almost adult. "Mr. Nguyen said if I keep the seat I can help with the district app thing for the robotics team. If I lose it because of homeroom, that's insane."

Lena liked that about him, the way whole opinions were apparently stored under the blank surface and only came out when the subject deserved it. "Did you tell them that?"

"I told them the cousin part. I'm not telling them about comp-sci because then it sounds like I'm negotiating."

June shoved the temporary guardian form toward Lena. "Can you read line seven? My eyes are fried."

Lena read it aloud. "`If parent or legal guardian is unavailable due to treatment, displacement, or other documented hardship...`"

Mateo said, "That is such a gross sentence."

"Most official sentences are gross," Lena said.

June leaned back, rubbed both hands over her face, then sat forward again before the exhaustion could harden into stopping. "All right. We'll upload the rehab letter, your mother's ID copy, and the lease. Then tomorrow I go to that feral check-cashing place on Snyder and get this notarized."

"You work tomorrow," Lena said.

"I work all the time."

"You also need sleep sometimes."

June gave her a look that said sleep was a hobby for richer people. "If the portal rejects one more PDF, I'm setting the school on fire. Teo, sign there. No, not like you're forging somebody else's death certificate. Write your whole name."

Mateo bent over the paper. His handwriting was unexpectedly neat, each letter separated from the next with stubborn care.

Lena had come because she wanted, if she was honest, a verdict. Not a big one. Something June could say while lighting a cigarette on the back steps: you did a kind thing, you didn't do a dumb thing, he sounded real about it, stop chewing the inside of your cheek. But June's attention was split into ten necessary strips. A school portal was failing. Mateo needed proof of residence and a signature and probably dinner. June's phone kept vibrating with work Slack notifications she ignored with practiced hatred. There was no room in the apartment for Lena's private moral accounting without taking up space somebody else needed for actual trouble.

June clicked to another page and swore under her breath. "Who designed this? A narc with a grudge?"

"What if," Mateo said, still not looking up, "you uploaded the file they wanted."

"What if you shut up and hand me the rehab letter."

He passed it over. His fingers had printer ink on them. A fine line of graphite marked the side of his hand from the pencil.

Lena said, "I can print things upstairs if you need the Wi-Fi not to die."

June answered without looking away from the laptop. "Bless you. They're rejecting the lease scan because apparently JPEGs are immoral now."

"I'll do it."

"You don't have to."

"I know."

That got June to look at her, finally, directly. There was a second where Lena thought June might ask the real question. Instead she took in Lena's face the way Farah had at the shop, precise and fast.

"You look tired," June said.

"Everybody keeps saying that like it's news."

"It's not news. It's me checking if you're about to become work for me."

The line was half joke, half neighborhood affection, and under other circumstances Lena would have thrown it back. Instead she heard in it the whole old arrangement between them: June as the one who could fix a mess if Lena named it. June as the person who knew where the copier still worked at nine p.m., which landlord took partial cash, which form actually mattered. June as the person who had heard enough of her life to sort panic from inconvenience inside thirty seconds.

"I'm fine," Lena said, and hated how quickly it came.

June held her gaze one beat longer, then went back to the laptop. "Good. Then print the lease and steal that kid a plate before he tells me he's not hungry."

"I'm right here," Mateo said.

"And yet you never feed yourself."

"I had cereal."

"That was yesterday."

"It was this morning."

June looked at Lena. "See? This is my glamorous life."

There was affection in the complaint, old and automatic. Mateo rolled his eyes but the color had come back into the room a little, the pressure distributed. Lena went upstairs, printed the lease from her phone because June's printer had died a year ago and still lay under the couch like a pet nobody admitted was dead, then came back down with the pages warm in her hand.

By the time she returned, June had coaxed the portal into accepting three of the four uploads and was on hold with somebody from attendance. She stood with the phone pinned between shoulder and ear, rummaging in the fridge one-handed. "Yes, I understand that's the policy," she said, voice flat with manufactured patience. "I'm asking what you want from me that I haven't already sent you twice."

She pulled out leftover rice, a container of shredded chicken, half an onion in plastic wrap. Mateo had opened the Manchego and was shaving slices off with a butter knife that was absolutely not meant for precision work.

"Use a real knife before you lose a thumb," June said into the air, not sure whether she was addressing him or the woman on the phone.

Mateo muttered, "You know thumbs are useful," and kept cutting.

Lena took the butter knife from him and did it herself. The cheese had sweated a little in the walk over. She could feel the softened edge give under the blade. June, still on hold, pointed with two fingers at the microwave, then at Mateo, then at her own mouth: feed him. Lena did.

None of it was noble. That was the part that made it persuasive. The rice clumped because the container had dried out in the fridge. Mateo wanted hot sauce and found the wrong one. June got transferred twice and only raised her voice once, which for her counted as sainthood. While the rice turned in the microwave, Lena saw the stack of work printouts under the attendance forms: KeyNest spreadsheets, maintenance tickets, somebody's lease-renewal dashboard. June's whole life looked like interfaces that assumed a cleaner reality than the people using them could provide.

When the school finally picked up in human form, June's voice changed. It did not get softer. It got straighter.

"Hi. Yes, thank you. Salazar. S-A-L-A-Z-A-R. I'm his aunt and current guardian, and I'm looking at the portal right now. It says the parent-treatment document is unreadable, which is interesting, because I can read it just fine."

Mateo stopped pretending not to listen.

June listened, eyes on the table, then said, "No, his mother isn't available for an in-person signature. That's what treatment means. Right. So if I bring the notarized form and the lease tomorrow, he keeps his lab access in the meantime? I need you to say yes or no to that, not give me a poem."

Lena looked away, smiling into the cheese because if June caught her enjoying this she would start performing. Mateo's pencil finally went still.

After another minute June got the answer she wanted, or something close enough to weaponize later. She hung up and exhaled through her nose.

"Tomorrow by three," she said. "They'll keep you in comp-sci if I show up with the notarized form and if you stop strolling into first period like a visiting dignitary."

Mateo's whole body loosened a fraction. He hid it by reaching for the rice. "I wasn't strolling."

"You were spiritually strolling."

He ate two bites standing up, still half folded into himself. "Thanks."

June waved a hand. "Be worth the paperwork."

It was as close to tenderness as she let herself get when he could hear it. Mateo heard it anyway. He took his plate to the couch and sat with his knees high, headphones back around his neck, eating with the concentration of a kid who had been pretending not to be hungry for too long.

Lena watched June stack the papers back into order. Her fingers were quick even tired. She smoothed the corners, clipped the forms together, slid the rehab letter into a plastic sleeve so it would not get ruined in her bag tomorrow. There was nothing dramatic in it. That was why it landed. A woman could do all this and still be funny at block parties, still fix fans at Sunday dinner, still know exactly how much lighter fluid to bring to the grill. Ordinary care had never looked pure in their neighborhood. It looked annoyed and underfunded and relentless.

"You want a drink?" June asked. "Water, not feelings."

"That's rude," Lena said.

"It's efficient."

Lena took the water. The bottle had been in June's freezer too long and thin ice floated at the lip. She drank, felt the cold hit the back of her teeth, and said, "I sent him three hundred and fifty dollars last night."

Mateo looked up so fast his fork clicked the plate. June did not move. "To the man you still haven't met in person."

"Yes."

"Eat on the couch, Teo."

"I'm still gonna hear this from the couch."

"I know. Go."

He went, plate in hand, all adolescent reluctance and naked interest. June waited until he had dropped onto the cushions before looking back at Lena. "Three-fifty."

Shame made Lena exact. "His van window got smashed after load-out. Somebody took part of his kit. He said not to worry about it. I sent it anyway."

"You sent rent-and-groceries anyway."

"I picked up Friday close." The defense came out too fast, already rehearsed. "And I took a rush file. It's not like I'm pawning furniture."

June's eyes narrowed just enough to count. "Did he ask you, or did he do that thing where a man describes a disaster until a woman volunteers to rescue him?"

Lena hated that June had a phrase ready for the exact shape of it. "He wasn't running a script."

"That's not what I asked."

"No. Not straight out." Lena set the bottle down harder than she meant to. "He was embarrassed."

"Embarrassed men can still cash the app."

The sentence landed clean enough to make Lena straighten. "He sent a voice note."

June lifted her chin. "Play it."

Lena should have refused. Instead she pulled out the phone, opened the thread, and thumbed the waveform. Eli's voice filled the table, low and careful.

"Hey," he said. "I know you hate being made into a saint, so I'm not doing that. But I need to say this clean. You saved my ass."

She stopped it there.

From the couch Mateo said, "That's not better."

June ignored him. Something small and unreadable moved through her face, then flattened back into practicality. "All right. He can say thank you in a full sentence. That's more than most."

"So I'm not insane."

"I didn't say that." June twisted the cap back onto her own bottle. "I said he cleared the first bar. If he makes you feel like an ATM with bangs after this, kill him."

Mateo groaned into his plate. "That sentence was still disgusting."

"You're sixteen. Everything is disgusting to you."

"It should be."

June stood and reached for her cigarettes, then remembered she was not going upstairs yet. She tucked the pack under the guardian forms instead. "If you're gonna do stupid, make him be explicit about what he owes you. Money, time, all of it. Don't sit there inventing nine versions and picking the meanest one. Ask."

It wasn't a blessing exactly. June had made the number sound as ugly as it was. But she had also made Lena put it in the room and play the note instead of sanding herself down against it alone. That was closer to useful than comfort. June still had real paperwork on the table, real obligations that needed a notary stamp and a working printer. Lena's question, next to that, looked embarrassingly optional. Even so, she left with something firmer than a half answer: make him say what the money meant out loud.

She stayed another twenty minutes. Mateo found a typo in the attendance form and became briefly eloquent about bureaucratic illiteracy. June heated herself a plate only after he had eaten half of his. A Slack message from KeyNest finally drew a hard curse out of her, not because of anything grand, just because somebody wanted an updated tenant spreadsheet by morning and had typed `gentle reminder` in the subject line. Lena left when the apartment started to feel too full of other people's tomorrow.

Outside, the night had that warm September drag where sound traveled farther than it should. Somebody was playing old bachata from a parked car. A couple argued at the corner in voices flattened by exhaustion. Lena walked home with the warm lease-copy smell still on her fingers and June's terms knocking around in her head.

At her kitchen table she ate rice from the container she'd brought back because June had shoved leftovers at her on the way out. Her subtitling file waited on the laptop. The Hungarian lovers were paused mid-accusation, mouths open in a silence that looked almost comic without sound.

She opened Eli's note again and let it run past the line she'd cut off downstairs.

His voice came in a little rougher than usual, like he had not fully slept. The parking-lot chaos from last night had not dropped away so much as thinned into residue. Traffic hissed behind him, and every few seconds something loose smacked metal, like the taped-over van window was still catching air.

"Hey," he said. "I know you hate being made into a saint, so I'm not doing that. But I need to say this clean. You saved my ass."

He took a breath and kept going before the pause could turn into performance.

"Not just with the money. The money mattered, obviously. I mean, yeah, right away. But last night was one of those garbage public nights. There was glass in the lot, Marcus was digging around for a trash bag, I had this roll of tape in my hand like that was gonna fix a damn thing, and I could feel myself turning into one of those guys everybody's polite to because he's obviously having the worst night there. That's a gross feeling. And you didn't make me do the whole thing where I explain it six different ways so it sounds legitimate. You were there. With me. That part got me."

Lena sat very still.

"I'll pay you back," he said. "That's not even a question. I'm not trying to make this bigger than it is. I just, I could breathe again for a minute after that, and that wasn't nothing. So. Thank you."

The note ended before it could get slick. There was no speech about fate and no big promise about next time. Just his breath at the end, and the tiny awkward noise of somebody tapping the phone before the silence turned awkward.

Lena played it again.

The second time she caught the places where he had started to correct himself and let the first phrasing stand. She caught the specific shame of public mess, the thing she understood immediately because humiliation was always louder when other people could see it. She caught, too, that he had said `with me` instead of `for me`. It was not charity or rescue. It was company.

The transfer receipt was still buried in her notifications somewhere, green and stupid. The extra close at Farah's waited on the fridge under a magnet shaped like a tomato. The eggs she had bought instead of takeout were lined up in their carton like minor witnesses. None of that vanished. The money had left her account. The next shift would still smell like cardboard and spilled sauvignon blanc. She would still be eating beans and rice by the sink, doing subtitling math in her head, telling herself the store-brand coffee was a preference and not a budget line. But Eli had given her something she could brace against the expense: he had said with me, not for me. He had made the loss feel less like a stupidity tax and more like the cost of staying beside somebody through public humiliation.

She hated how badly she wanted that version. She hated, too, how relieved she felt when it settled in her chest and stopped rattling around loose.

On the laptop the Hungarian woman was still frozen in her kitchen, mouth sharp around a word Lena could not hear until she hit play. Lena slid the headphones back on, returned to the line she had been leaving half wrong all night, and changed `Keep your favor` to `Then keep it.` The cut was cleaner. The cruelty sat closer to the bone without having to explain itself.

Her phone buzzed once with a follow-up text from Eli.

`Get some sleep, okay? I know you pretend you're made of wire.`

That made her laugh softly, against her will. She had told him that once, weeks ago, talking about how caption deadlines stacked in her back and wrists until she felt soldered together. He had saved it. Used it right.
