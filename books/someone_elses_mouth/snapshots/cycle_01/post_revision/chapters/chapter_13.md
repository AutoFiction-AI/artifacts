# Chapter 13

The trailer always took a second shove after the key turned, as if Rosa were on the other side with her hip against it, making visitors prove they meant to come in. Lena leaned her shoulder into the aluminum door and the old mix hit her at once: salt dried into fabric, mildew in the bathroom grout, old sunscreen baked into vinyl, and beneath that the sweet powdery note that had been her mother's exact idea of clean. The place had cooled since summer, but the fiberglass walls still held heat the way a cheap pan held grease.

June came up behind her with two flattened banker boxes tucked under one arm and a grocery bag looped around the other wrist. She had on work boots with one split lace knotted back into usefulness and an Orioles cap so sweat-stained at the brim it looked found rather than owned, and a paring knife wrapped in a dish towel stuck out of the grocery bag like she was reporting for kitchen triage.

"There she is," June said, looking past Lena into the dim trailer. "Rosa's palace of mildew."

Lena stepped aside. "You say that like you aren't the one who insisted we come today."

"Because if I let you pick the date, we'd be here at Christmas finding religion in a Tupperware drawer."

They had spent most of the drive back down in a workable silence, the radio low, the traffic already thin in late October. After the wet stupidity of the Wildwood trip earlier in the week, silence had begun doing more than silence usually did. It had edges now. Still, June had shown up at seven with coffee and contractor bags and the kind of competence that always made a job look briefly survivable.

Inside, the trailer had the same stubborn arrangement Rosa had kept for years, as if she expected weather to negotiate with her if the cushions sat right. The dinette vinyl was printed with fake blue rope. The fridge stood open and unplugged, one back foot still propped on a folded ShopRite circular to stop the wobble. Twist ties hung looped on a nail near the sink. A casino cup on the counter held pens that had all dried out except one chewed-down marker. Rosa had gone to the hospital that spring and never come back, but the room kept her habits in position for her: breadbox empty and clean, dish towels folded in thirds, a coffee tin of spare change under the radio, the bathroom hand soap watered down to its last translucent inch.

June set the boxes down and opened windows in quick practiced snaps, tapping each latch twice when it stuck. "I brought real knives," she said. "Your mom believed in blades with the engineering integrity of butter."

"She said sawing builds character."

"That was because she liked watching other people work."

Lena smiled despite herself. It came easier in here than it had in the car. June belonged to this trailer nearly as much as she did. There had been whole Augusts when Rosa would take both girls down for three days at a time because it was cheaper than camps and safer than letting them roam the block with wet hair and opinions. June knew where Rosa hid the cards, which burner on the stove clicked twice before catching, how hard you had to slam the bathroom cabinet before the mirror stopped shivering.

They started in the back bedroom, which sounded more dignified than it was. It held two narrow beds and one dresser that never fully closed, with sand still trapped in the window track from one summer when Rosa had kept insisting she would come down one more time after chemo made food taste like nickels. Lena stripped fitted sheets that had gone cool and papery in the dark. June crouched by the dresser and came up with a stack of elastic-waist shorts, a church carnival visor, and a cosmetic bag gummy with exploded lip balm.

"She kept every free moisturizer anybody ever gave her," June said. "This woman would've survived the end times on hotel lotion and pepper packets."

"Don't throw out the little green tubes."

June looked up. "You want them?"

"No. I just don't want to watch you throw them out."

June gave her a quick sideways look, the kind that meant she'd heard the rule and wasn't going to test it. "Fine. Memory lotion can stay."

Lena turned to the closet because it was easier than explaining the difference between useless and unbearable. Rosa's beach chair still leaned in there, webbing frayed white at the edges. Above it sat the old plastic drawer unit where she kept towels, off-brand antacids, and the school things she had never thrown away. Lena pulled one drawer free and found what she had half expected: two plaid uniform skirts, a cardigan with the school crest half peeled off, and a manila envelope full of forms.

"Jesus," June said. "Look at us."

She shook out a class photo. Twelve-year-old Lena stood in the second row with her hair ironed straight for once, jaw set as if the camera had personally annoyed her. Next to her, June looked small and furious in the universal way of underfed Catholic-school girls, chin tipped up, shoelace untied.

"You looked cute," Lena said.

"I looked like I bit people."

"You did bite people."

"Only once, and she deserved it."

The laugh that came out of Lena was real enough that it surprised her. June heard the surprise and let it sit there without touching it. She sorted the forms with nicotine-yellowed fingertips, quick and neat. There were tuition notices, a field trip permission slip, and one of those annual registration packets with its stubborn little boxes for `Mother`, `Father`, `Parish`.

"Remember this shit," June said. "Like if they printed `Father` hard enough one was gonna appear."

Lena took the packet from her. Rosa had signed both lines in the same slanted hand, then crossed one out and written `guardian` in the margin. "My mom used to get mad at forms."

"Your mom got mad like it was a municipal service."

"And your mom?"

June shrugged one shoulder. "Left it blank till the office called. Then she'd write `N/A` so hard the pen cut the paper."

Lena could see June's mother at the Salazars' kitchen table, cigarette burning down in the ashtray, filling out school forms with the expression of someone being insulted by stationery. Outside, gulls kept making those ridiculous rusty-hinge sounds over a trailer park that was mostly shut down for the season. Somewhere farther off, a hammer hit aluminum siding in a dull patient rhythm.

June flipped to the next page. "Emergency contact. Also hilarious."

"Rosa used to put Mrs. DeSanto from upstairs."

"My mom put nobody. Which, to be fair, was accurate."

The tenderness in that could have turned sentimental if either of them looked at it too long. June saved them by tossing the papers into a keep pile and standing up with a grunt. "Kitchen before I lose the will to live."

The kitchen still held Rosa in smaller, meaner ways. There were rubber bands around the dish soap pump because she never trusted factory closures, a freezer shelf lined with paper towels to catch drips from a tray that had not held ice in months, bread stored in the refrigerator even in winter because she said shore air turned everything to sponge, and a margarine tub full of clothespins and two AA batteries. Lena touched the tub and felt, stupidly, as if the plastic might still be warm from her mother's hand.

June unpacked the grocery bag onto the counter: tomatoes wrapped in a deli sheet, white bread, a small jar of Hellmann's gone sweaty at the glass, pepper, salt, paper towels, the knife. "I wasn't eating one more stale pretzel on your emotional field trip."

"That was a research trip."

"That was hydroplaning into humiliation with you because you had a theory."

Lena flinched before she could stop it. June noticed. Of course she noticed. Her face changed for less than a second, the joke slipping off as she recalibrated.

"I brought lunch," she said, quieter. "Don't make me regret the tomatoes."

So Lena washed them at the sink while June found plates that had to be rinsed again for dust. The faucet coughed rust first, then cold clear water. Tomato skin gave under her thumbs with that faint waxy drag she had always loved. June cut thick slices, practical and unsentimental, and salted them from high up because she said it spread better that way. She spread mayo edge to edge on white bread and ground black pepper until the plate looked dirty.

"Your mom always overdid the pepper," Lena said.

"Your mom understood structure."

"My mom understood cheap lunches."

"Those are not different sciences."

They ate on the trailer steps because the light outside was too good to waste on fake blue rope vinyl. October had thinned the whole park down to exposed geometry: empty lots, shut shutters, a few stubborn geraniums going dark in planters, the narrow lane between trailers bright as a knife blade. The tomato sandwiches ran immediately. Seeds slid onto Lena's wrist. Mayo stuck to the heel of her hand. June ate in fast efficient bites, then licked pepper off her thumb and looked out toward the patch of scrub that hid the bay.

"Remember your mom making us hose our feet off before we came in?" she said.

"Because we tracked half the beach into the beds."

"No, because I tracked half the beach in and you followed me around like a witness."

"You always ran."

"I still do."

That landed lightly enough that Lena let it pass. June had already moved on, pointing with the crust of her sandwich at the trailer across the lane where an inflatable palm tree still leaned half dead against a deck. "Rosa loved making fun of those people."

"She said anything inflatable on purpose was an admission of defeat."

June laughed with her mouth full. For a minute they were exactly the girls they had been here: sunburned, sticky, talking shit before somebody called them inside. Lena could almost believe the silence from the drive had been only tiredness, the wet awful Jersey trip already flattening into anecdote.

Then June reached behind her for the next box to fill and caught hold of a shallow plastic file case Lena had set by the door. The lid popped open in her hands. Inside were envelopes labeled in Rosa's cramped block letters: `CAR`, `BILLS`, `LENA IMPORTANT`, `WARRANTY`, `CHURCH`, `DON'T SHRED`.

"God," June said, but this time she did not sound amused. She lifted the `LENA IMPORTANT` envelope between two fingers. "She archived your disasters."

Lena swallowed the last wet bite of tomato. "What?"

"I'm saying your mom saved every piece of paper connected to your life like she was building a case file for the defense." June thumbed through the contents. There was a dental bill, an old lease addendum, and a postcard from the apartment Lena had shared with her ex and hated almost immediately. "Look at this. She gave your mess its own category."

"Everybody has paperwork."

"Not like this."

The air had shifted so gradually Lena only noticed once it was different. The gulls had gone quiet. Somewhere a screen door banged twice in the wind. June set the papers down in a neat stack that looked angrier than a mess would have.

"What are you actually saying?" Lena asked.

June took a breath through her nose. When she spoke again the joke was gone cleanly, as if she had put it away for later. "I'm saying the buyer paperwork in your tote says eighteen-six after fees, and everybody gets to call that sad money because it came out of your mother. In September DeLuca taped a red notice to my basement door for eighteen hundred in back rent and fees, and by dinner I'd had three people explain cigarettes to me like nicotine was why Mateo needed a place to sleep."

Lena stared at her. "That's bullshit."

"Is it?"

"Yeah, June, it is."

Lena felt the vinyl edge of the stair tread pressing a hard line into the backs of her thighs. Tomato seeds were drying on her wrist. "That eighteen-six is already taxes, Visa, and whatever winter costs. It's not padding. And when DeLuca pulled that shit, I told you he was a parasite."

"I know."

June reached for the pepper and screwed the lid on with more care than the task required. "You told me in your kitchen. The school office said it to my face. Mateo misses enough mornings to get put on attendance probation, and that vice principal asks if there's a more stable adult in the house now. Same year your husband leaves and Rosa fronts the Moore deposit, people carry your boxes upstairs and call you brave for landing on your feet."

"Rosa loaned me that deposit," Lena said. "I paid her back in caption checks, and I didn't say one thing about you over Mateo. I said that office was treating him like a threat and you like bad wiring."

June's voice had gone cooler, more precise, which was always worse than when she raised it. Lena could hear the care with which she selected each word, the way she did when she was pinning down a contractor on the phone or explaining to Mateo why a thing could not happen the way he wanted it to.

"I'm not saying you had it easy," June said. "I'm saying even when you hit the sidewalk, the story lands on what happened to you. Mine lands on what I did wrong."

Lena let out a short unbelieving sound. "What the fuck does that even mean?"

"It means Farah drops soup when you stop functioning. I get asked for receipts."

The sentence sat between them with the impossible solidity of furniture. Lena could have argued each line item. She wanted dates and counterexamples and a fair hearing and maybe even a spreadsheet, which was how she knew she was hurt. But the trailer did not make room for anything clean enough to call abstract. The place smelled too strongly of dead sunscreen and damp paper. Her mother's labeled envelopes were right there on the step between them, proof of a form of care so specific it almost looked like surveillance.

"You're acting like I won something," she said finally. "My mother died. The trailer's getting sold. None of this feels padded."

June looked out at the empty lane instead of at her. "No," she said. "But even grief came with assets."

Lena stared.

June rubbed the side of her thumb over the serrated edge of the paper plate. "Forget it."

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Say something ugly and then act like I'm dramatic for hearing it."

Now June looked at her. Her face had gone unreadable in the tired way Lena knew from years of late nights and bad jobs and family emergencies that were never allowed to become true emergencies because June kept getting there first. "I said what I said."

Lena wanted to stand up, or throw the plate, or force the thing open until it broke into usable pieces. Instead she wiped her hand on a paper towel that immediately tore. "Fine," she said, and heard how flat it sounded.

They cleaned up lunch in a silence not nearly blank enough to count as peace. June washed the knife and dried it on the dish towel she'd brought from home. Lena carried three trash bags to the dumpster and came back sweating inside her sweatshirt even though the day had turned. When she passed the bedroom doorway she saw June kneeling by a box of keepsakes, wrapping framed photos in newspaper with the same efficient hands that had once braided Lena's hair before the eighth-grade dance because Rosa had been at work late. The sight should have softened something. Instead it made Lena think, with a quick cold shame, of all the ways care could also be a way of learning where a person broke.

By the time the sun had shifted off the steps, the trailer was half emptied and Lena could not stop hearing the morning again with different captions under it. Jokes she had known for years came back labeled in another voice. Whole stretches of friendship started to look itemized.

***

By late afternoon the place had that flat October light Rosa always hated, the kind that made even clean rooms look vaguely condemned. Empty surfaces gave sound back too fast. A hanger clacked in the closet and it was suddenly the loudest thing in the trailer. Lena carried a box of kitchen things to the car, came back for another, and realized the rooms were already beginning to feel less like absence than inventory.

June was in the bathroom on her knees, unscrewing the cheap plastic caddy from the wall because she said the buyers would try to charge for anything left attached. "They're gonna act like this thing is custom millwork," she called.

"Leave it," Lena said.

"No. It'll take thirty seconds."

"June."

June sat back on her heels and looked over her shoulder. "Len. I'm saving you from bureaucratic theater. Let me have this."

The almost-joke in it was careful, offered rather than thrown. Lena let it stand. She went into the back bedroom to clear the closet's top shelf and found, shoved behind a beach bag, Rosa's blue quilted housecoat still on its plastic hanger. The cheap satin had gone dull at the seams. One cuff was darkened where sunscreen or gravy or both had once met fabric and then become permanent. Lena pulled it down and the pockets made a little crackling sound.

She knew before she checked that something would be inside, probably tissues or a receipt or a cough drop fused to the lining. Rosa never used a pocket once.

Lena slipped her hand into the right pocket and came out with two hard tissues, a casino players card, and three strawberry candies stuck together in a red glossy lump.

The sight of them hit in a place that had nothing to do with reason. It was too exactly her mother: Rosa keeping purse-candy in a housecoat she wore to fry flounder, Rosa pocketing things to deal with later and then leaving them there forever, Rosa in this trailer with the TV loud and the air conditioner rattling, shouting for Lena to close the damn screen before the moths got ideas.

Lena sat down on the edge of the stripped bed because standing had become a complicated instruction. The mattress bowed without sheets over the plastic cover Rosa believed in on principle. The housecoat lay across Lena's lap, slick and cool as fish skin. When she brought it closer the old powder smell was still there under everything else, mixed now with closet dark and the medicinal sweetness of the candies.

June appeared in the doorway and took the whole picture in at once. She did not ask if Lena was okay. One of the reasons Lena had loved her so long was that she usually knew better than that.

"Oh," June said, seeing the candies. "Jesus."

Lena laughed once and the laugh broke halfway through. "Of course."

June leaned her shoulder against the doorframe. "She kept those for me."

Lena looked up.

June nodded at the sticky red lump in Lena's palm. "Those awful strawberry things. She knew I'd steal two before I even sat down. She used to hide them in whatever pocket she had because Gabe would wipe out the whole bowl."

The memory entered the room so plainly that Lena could see it: June at fourteen, all elbows and appetite, checking Rosa's robe pockets before she said hello. Rosa smacking her hand away without conviction. The trailer then still full of wet towels and radio static and the smell of oil heating in a pan.

"You always wanted the mints," June said.

"I still want the mints."

"Which is insane. Those taste like church vestibules."

Lena looked at the candies again. Her eyes were running now, enough that the red cellophane blur doubled. She wiped her face with the heel of her hand and immediately got old powder on her skin from the sleeve. "I can't tell what's trash in here."

"Then don't decide right this second."

June came into the room, took the hanger from Lena with a gentleness so practical it barely announced itself, and laid the housecoat flat on the bed. She patted the pockets once, found another tissue, and threw it away. "Take the robe," she said. "If you leave it, you'll think about it every time it gets cold."

"It smells weird."

"Yeah. That's the point."

Lena laughed again, more cleanly this time. June folded the robe into a square that was not pretty but would fit in a box. Then she picked up the strawberry candies and held them out.

"You want these?"

"Absolutely not."

June slipped them into the front pocket of her hoodie. "Then I do."

That, more than anything, steadied Lena. Gross pocket candy was June's kind of mercy: no ceremony, no speech about memory or healing or taking what remains. Lena wiped her nose on a paper towel and stood up.

"You gonna cry again if I take the ugly useful stuff out of the kitchen?" June asked.

"Probably."

"Good. Stay hydrated."

Back in the main room, they finished the last hour in a version of teamwork so old it felt like muscle memory. June stacked plates by size and rejected sentimentality with a running low commentary. "Keep the opener. Nobody ever regrets a can opener. Leave the shell lamp, it has bad intentions. Take the old towels. Ugly useful stuff is all that survives families." Lena wrapped mugs in the advertising circulars Rosa had saved under the sink. The paper left ink on her fingertips. Outside, the light thinned further. A few year-round residents were already pulling down flags and folding chairs, whole lives reduced for weather.

In the silverware drawer Lena found a Polaroid curled at the corners: herself and June on the trailer steps in bathing suits, both trying not to grin and failing. Lena's hair was plastered flat from the bay. June had one knee skinned open and did not seem remotely bothered by it. On the white strip at the bottom Rosa had written `my girls` in blue ballpoint.

Lena held it a second longer than she meant to. Then June reached across, took it from her, and slid it carefully into the side pocket of Lena's tote without comment.

For a minute after that, something almost simple returned. Innocence was long gone, maybe since earlier that day, maybe months ago. What came back was an older habit of alliance. June carried the heavier box without being asked. Lena found the trailer keys on the hook by the door, looped through a faded dolphin float from some long-closed mini-golf place, and dropped them into her pocket. June remembered the buyer paperwork. Lena remembered the phone charger. Between them, the job kept moving.

When the last box was in the trunk and only the cleaning bucket remained by the door, June lit a cigarette outside by the gravel strip, one hand cupped against the wind. She almost never smoked in front of Mateo and had gotten sneaky about it in the city, but down here, with the trailer half stripped and the lane nearly empty, she did not bother pretending anything. The smoke came off sharp and ugly in the salt air.

"Five minutes," she said. "Then we lock up."

Lena nodded and went back inside for one final walk-through. The counters were empty, the beds were bare, and the fridge had been wiped out so hard it smelled faintly of bleach and old onion. Without the dish towels and the radio and the half-useful clutter, the place looked smaller than grief had allowed. She touched the dinette table once on the way past, the fake blue rope pattern slick under her palm.

Her phone buzzed in her back pocket.

The sound was so ordinary it took a second to feel invasive. It was Signal, from Eli.

She had barely answered him since the shore. He had responded to that new thinness the way he responded to everything at first, with patience so calibrated it nearly counted as style. He had sent a check-in text in the morning and a picture of bad coffee from somewhere he claimed to be loading out, all of it carrying the same no-pressure, baby, just-here ease. Today she had left him unopened since nine.

Now there were two messages and a voice note.

`You still down there?`

`Please tell me you ate something.`

The voice note was nineteen seconds. Lena did not want to play it. She played it anyway.

His voice came warm and low into the stripped trailer, too close to the ear as always.

"Hey. Open a window in there, okay? That trailer traps heat. Eat a real thing before you drive. And don't leave the ugly useful stuff behind just because it looks like junk. Bring home what still works. Call me when you're moving."

Lena stood very still with the phone at her ear long after the note ended.

Outside, gravel crunched under June's boots. The cigarette ember flared once, then dropped. Inside, the trailer gave back Eli's silence as if it had belonged in the room all along.

The phrase kept returning: ugly useful stuff.

June had said it less than an hour ago, standing at the sink with a stack of plates in her hands. Maybe Lena had given Eli that phrase months ago. She had given him enough to build a whole man out of. That was part of the problem. But now, in the emptied trailer with her mother's robe boxed up and the smell of old powder still on her wrist, the note did not sound contaminated by memory alone. It was the ranked little orders in it, the brisk practical mercy, the way care arrived as triage. The phrase was June's, but so was the posture under it. Eli's softer voice only made the theft easier to hear.

Proof would've been cleaner. Contamination got into everything.

"Len?" June called from outside. "You coming?"

Lena locked the screen, set the phone facedown on the dinette for one second, then picked it back up. "Yeah," she said.

She turned off the last light though there was still enough day to see. At the door she looked back once more at the trailer's narrowed rooms, all the old care marks still visible even with the objects gone: the circular under the fridge foot, the rubbed-clean patch by the light switch, the hairline crack in the cabinet mirror where Rosa had shut it too hard for years. Love stayed in a place like that as wear. Use stayed there too. Habit stayed there too.

Outside, June ground out the cigarette and held out her hand for the keys without thinking. Lena almost gave them over. Then she stopped, curled the dolphin float deeper into her own fist, and said, "I've got it."

June's hand dropped at once. "Okay."

Lena locked the door herself. The key bit her palm on the turn. By the time she got into the passenger seat for the ride back, every kind thing June had ever remembered about her seemed to have acquired a second use.
