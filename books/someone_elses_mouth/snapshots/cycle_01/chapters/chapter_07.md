# Chapter 7

The plastic fastener holding the dress tags to the side seam kept scraping Lena's ribs through the lining. She had tucked the paper tag flat under her bra band so it would not show through the fabric, but every time she bent over the bed to check her overnight bag it sprang loose again, a little dry scratch against her skin.

She had bought the dress at the Marshalls on Oregon because the receipt gave her thirty days and because the blue was dark enough to survive one careful evening. The tag said $39.99. The round-trip train had cost $78.40 if she did not miss the ride home. She had spent another eleven dollars on a tiny roller of amber oil at Rite Aid because the bottle of perfume she already owned still smelled, to her, like courthouse carpet and the winter her divorce papers came through. By eight-thirty that morning she had already shaved her legs, under her arms, and the backs of her thighs in the thin bathroom light, balancing one foot on the closed toilet lid like a woman much more graceful than she was. She nicked her ankle and pressed a wad of toilet paper to it until the bleeding stopped. In the overnight tote she packed black underwear that still had some elastic left in it, a toothbrush in a sandwich bag, travel deodorant, ibuprofen, charger, and one condom from the three-pack she had bought so quickly the night before she had not looked the teenage cashier in the eye.

The foil square sat in the inside pocket against her wallet like a dare.

She checked the time again. She checked Eli's last message again, though nothing in it changed because she looked harder.

`Tell me what color so I can picture you correctly.`

When she had written back `blue`, he had answered almost at once.

`Blue on you is never gonna be wrong. Meet me under the big clock at 4:15? I might smell like dust and gaff tape.`

That had come in at 7:06, while she was still in her old sleep shirt and standing at the sink with toothpaste foam on her tongue. She had been carrying it around since then, the sentence with its easy certainty, the fact of a man in another city already imagining her body moving through the day toward him.

She had not told Gabe. If she told Gabe, the whole thing would become family material immediately. He would ask what train. He would ask what time she expected to be back. He would tell Lena not to be stupid in the affectionate older-brother-reversed way he had developed since the divorce, as though he had been born thirty-three and married. She had not told Farah either. Farah's face would have gone too careful. Farah would have handed over a hug or a joke or some bottle-shop wisdom about men being, in the aggregate, not worth dry cleaning, and Lena could not have borne even practical tenderness before she had something solid to show for this.

She had told June, because June already knew everything and because the part of Lena that still wanted witness had wanted one person to know she was getting on the train.

June's answer had come at 7:21. `Text when he's 3D.`

That was June at her gentlest, a joke hiding a blessing inside it. Lena had smiled at the screen and hated herself a little for needing that much from eight words.

At 30th Street she bought coffee that tasted like somebody had filtered it through copper pennies. Families sprawled across the seats under the high ceiling with their bags around them like barricades. A woman in lavender scrubs slept open-mouthed against a backpack. Two college boys argued over whether they had time to buy egg sandwiches before boarding and kept saying bro with the worn reflex of men tapping a loose tooth. The departure board clicked and shifted above everybody's heads. Lena loved that board in a workman's way. It changed without drama. It told the truth in red blocks.

On the platform she kept one hand over the opening of her tote as if she were carrying cash. The dress moved differently from anything else she owned. It whispered when she walked. In the train window, with the station dimly reflected over her own face, the blue made her look less tired than she felt. She took that as a good sign, which was stupid, but the whole trip rested on a structure of little stupidnesses already. She sat by the window and watched the industrial backs of Philadelphia pull away, brick and steel and a seam of graffiti bright enough to look freshly wound. The seat fabric smelled like old rain and detergent.

Eli kept her company all the way past Wilmington. Not constantly. He was too good at this to overplay it. A picture of a cable snake on a scuffed stage floor with the toe of his boot in frame. A voice note low enough that she had to press the phone closer to her ear.

"You on the train?"

His laugh had that warm grain in it, the one that made her think of felt and amplifier hum.

"Tell me when you see marsh. I always miss it when I'm on no sleep."

She did tell him. She told him about the flat pewter stretch outside the window and the white birds standing in it like dropped napkins. He sent back, `There you are doing location scouting for me.` He asked whether she had eaten. He reminded her to drink water because train coffee did not count. He said it lightly, not as a correction, and that kind of care had become one of the things she wanted from him most: not flattery, not even desire exactly, but the irritating, impossible miracle of being remembered in real time.

By Baltimore the whole car smelled faintly of fries and somebody's body spray. Lena went to the bathroom and changed shoes because the heeled sandals looked better with the dress and because she had packed flip-flops for the return like a woman planning sensibly for morning-after feet. In the mirror the station light had found the fine broken capillaries at the side of her nose. She dabbed on more concealer. Her hands shook only a little. When she came back to her seat, Eli had sent a message with no punctuation at all, which always made him feel more bodily to her, less composed.

`you close`

`twenty minutes maybe`

`I'm looking at that clock already in my head`

The last message sat with her all the way into Washington.

Union Station hit her first as brightness. White vault overhead, polished floor, the noise of wheels and rolling bags multiplying under the ceiling until it all became one hard public sound. Everybody looked temporary and intent. Lena stepped off the escalator into the main hall with her tote cutting into her shoulder and saw the clock immediately, bigger than she had pictured from his photos. She went to the bathroom first because arriving too early and then standing there in plain sight felt worse than making him wait one minute. In the stall she fixed the dress where it had ridden up under the bag strap and checked that the paper tag still sat flat inside the seam. In the mirror she rubbed away the shine above her lip and pinched color into her cheeks like she was seventeen.

At 4:08 she walked out and took her place under the clock.

He was not there, which was fine, because she was early.

At 4:12 he texted.

`Running ten late. Load-out bullshit. Don't let me walk in and catch you giving me that disappointed face.`

She smiled in spite of herself and typed back, `You have never seen my disappointed face.`

`I know exactly what it looks like.`

At 4:24 she was still under the clock, with a family from Ohio unpacking themselves onto the benches nearby and a man in a Georgetown hoodie pacing in little aggravated diagonals while he argued into his earbuds about receipts. She checked the entrance every time somebody tall with dark hair came through. She checked it less gracefully every five minutes after that. At 4:31 she wrote, `You close?`

He answered at 4:33.

`I hate this. Another ten. I owe you first drink and then some.`

That was still within the range of ordinary inconvenience. Train stations were made to hold inconvenience. That was practically their religion. Lena walked the perimeter of the hall once because standing in one spot made her feel visible in a way walking did not. She paused at the window and looked out at the traffic with no interest in traffic. She took out her phone and put it away again.

At 4:47 she called him.

Straight to voicemail.

The voicemail itself did not panic her. Reception in big public buildings was always weird. Batteries died. Men carrying cables ignored their phones. Whole adult days went crooked. She told herself all of that in a series of brisk internal sentences that sounded, annoyingly, like caption notes. At 4:51 she sent, `I'm gonna sit somewhere. Tell me where you are when you can.`

There was a bar off the main hall with brass railings and televisions no one seemed actually to be watching. Lena chose a stool with sightline to the doors and set her tote on the one beside it so nobody could take the space where he might still land. The bartender was a middle-aged woman with shellacked green nails and a paper-crane tattoo on the inside of her wrist; every few minutes she straightened the stack of coasters by the register with two swift taps, as if untidiness offended her at the molecular level.

"What can I get you?" she asked.

Lena almost said water because water was what a reasonable woman waiting for a slightly late date ordered. Instead she heard herself say, "Gin and tonic. Light on the tonic."

"Smart," the bartender said, and slid a glass of ice water over too.

Lena thanked her too quickly.

The drink came cold enough to hurt her teeth. She made it last until 5:10. At 5:03 Eli sent nothing. At 5:07 he sent nothing. At 5:10 Lena finished the gin anyway because the ice had gone thin and flat and she could feel herself becoming the sort of woman the whole room could read correctly.

The bartender came back. "You eating or just drinking your dinner?"

"I think I'm meeting someone."

The words sounded worse out loud than they had in Lena's head for the last six weeks.

The bartender gave no visible sign of pity, which made Lena grateful enough to want to cry. "Kitchen's decent if he keeps being an asshole."

"Thanks," Lena said, and looked down at her phone so she would not have to meet the woman's eyes.

At 5:14 another message finally arrived.

`Phone on fumes. Not ghosting you. Swear to God. Give me ten.`

This one should have calmed her. It did for almost a minute. Then it became another thing she had to hold against the shape of the last hour. Ten minutes. Another ten. Phone on fumes. Swear to God. Each explanation kept the structure intact while quietly changing what it meant. She could feel herself, with humiliating clarity, doing the work required to keep embarrassment from hardening into knowledge. If she left now, she would be leaving because she had been stood up. If she stayed, she was still a woman delayed.

She stayed.

***

By 5:38 she had ordered fries she did not want because an empty bar stool with one untouched drink in front of it would have been unbearable. The fries arrived in a metal basket lined with fake newsprint. Grease darkened the paper in expanding circles. The television over the bar played a baseball game with the sound off while a closed-caption crawl underneath lagged two pitches behind, the catcher crouched and rising before the words caught up. Lena watched the delay more than the game. She could not help it. It felt like professional deformity and prophecy at once.

At 5:41 Eli called.

She answered so fast she almost dropped the phone.

"Hey."

What came back first was noise. Traffic. A car horn close enough to clip the mic. Somebody dragging something heavy over pavement.

"Lena."

His voice was breathless and lower than usual, as if he were trying not to shout.

"I'm so sorry. I'm so fucking sorry."

The apology landed first as relief. A body. A voice. Whatever else was wrong, she was not talking to nothing. She closed her eyes and let that hit her before she did anything smarter with it.

"Where are you?"

"Not where I said I'd be. I know. I know." He exhaled hard, and for a second the line crackled. "Noelle's place got blown apart last night. Her ex showed up drunk and went through it. I'm over here trying to get her out before he circles back. I should've called. I know I should've called."

Lena opened her eyes. The bar came back around her in detail so sharp it felt punitive: the lemon wedge softening in her glass, the paper napkin half dissolved under the sweating base, the couple at the high-top sharing crab dip with the calm concentration of people who had successfully arrived somewhere together.

"What do you mean, blown apart?"

"I mean he kicked the bathroom door in and ripped the shower rod down and dumped her mail in the sink. She keeps all her bills in one of those blue Danish-cookie tins like she's ninety. He turned the water on over the whole thing. He smashed this stupid rabbit lamp her aunt gave her when she first moved out. I'm standing in broken glass right now trying not to cut through my shoe."

It was too many details and therefore, perversely, easier to believe.

"Jesus."

"Yeah." She heard him say something away from the phone, his voice blurred by distance. When he came back he sounded closer and rougher. "She keeps apologizing to me like she did this. I swear I'm not making up an artful fucking tragedy because I'm late. I know how this sounds."

That line, the ugliness of it, the fact that he heard how it sounded before she had to say it, slid under her anger in a way she hated.

"Where is she now?"

"Downstairs. Crying in a Corolla with three trash bags and a basil plant. One of the bags ripped because of course it did." He let out a short exhausted laugh that died immediately. "Lena, I'm sorry. I know you came all the way down here."

Her hand went to the paper tag under the seam and pressed it hard enough that the little plastic barb dug her finger. She pictured a blue cookie tin tipped on its side in a flooded sink. A rabbit lamp broken at the base. A woman she had never met holding a basil plant because there were always things people saved that made no sense to outsiders. The story had the stupid frayed edges real messes had. It did not arrive packaged for belief. It spilled.

"Did she call the cops?"

"They came. They took notes. They said because he was gone they couldn't do much till she filed on Monday unless he came back while they were standing there, which was apparently too much to ask of everybody." He made a noise between a laugh and a swallow. "My phone's at four percent. I'm trying to find a charger in this apartment and everything is wet or smashed or under a man named Craig's foot from downstairs. He keeps saying, 'You gotta move, sweetheart, you gotta move,' like volume is logistics."

Lena could see Craig immediately, which again made the whole thing worse and more plausible. A big man in slides, bossing grief around because bossing was the only useful thing he knew how to do.

"Okay," she said. "Okay. Just breathe for one second."

He did, obediently. She heard it. She also heard, under everything, the part of herself taking the scene and setting it in place like an object she would rather own than the alternative.

"You should go," he said. "I mean back to Philly. Not like go away, Jesus. I just mean don't sit there waiting on me while I do this. I hate that you're there by yourself."

The shame of being observed alone in a bar moved through her body again, clean and hot. She looked automatically at the tote occupying the stool beside her, the one she had kept empty for him. The bartender was wiping glasses. Nobody else cared. That almost made it worse.

"I've already been sitting here."

"I know." His voice gentled. That careful tone, curated or not, still found her every time. "I know exactly how shitty that is."

She wanted him to be able to know it. She wanted his knowing to redeem it by half a degree.

"Tell me what you need," she said.

There was a pause. Brief. Long enough to register.

"I don't need you doing that."

"I'm not asking if you want me to."

"Lena."

"What do you need?"

The line rustled. Voices moved around him. A car door slammed.

"Right now? Charger. Coffee. Probably a room for her if we can't get the lock changed tonight. But I'm not asking you for money from a sidewalk while my life is a cartoon. I'm not that guy."

He said it with just enough disgust that she felt the old, stupid protective impulse rise in her chest immediately, fierce as a cough.

"I didn't say you were."

"No, but the universe did. Loudly."

In the mirror behind the bar she saw herself smiling despite the burn behind her eyes, smiling because he had made room for her embarrassment too, because he had turned his own into something shared. The knot inside her loosened.

"Take care of your sister," she said. "Text me when you're plugged in. If she needs a room, say that. Don't do the proud thing."

He let out a breath that sounded half wrecked, half relieved. "You're unreal."

"No," Lena said, and it came out rougher than she intended. "I'm in a bar by Union Station eating fries that taste like fryer oil from 1998. That's extremely real."

He laughed, genuinely this time or well enough that she could not tell the difference.

"God. I wish I was there."

"You were supposed to be."

"I know."

The grief in those two words was not hers, but it fit neatly enough over hers to warm it.

"Go," she said. "I'll wait to hear from you."

"Get yourself home safe first."

"Text me when you can."

"I will."

The line cut. Whether because his battery died or because he ended it, she had no way to know.

For a full minute Lena sat with the dead phone in her hand and felt the room rearrange itself around the new version of events. Not stood up. Interrupted. Not foolish. Caught in somebody else's catastrophe. Not alone for nothing. Alone because adult life was a crooked machine and she happened, today, to be standing where it kicked.

She ordered another gin.

The bartender raised one eyebrow before pouring it. "That kind of night?"

"Apparently."

"You need a menu?"

"I need him to stop being in crisis."

"That's not on the menu," the bartender said. "Chicken tenders are."

Lena laughed, which hurt more than crying would have.

She did eat the tenders. She ate them because food made everything look less dramatic and because the bartender had been kind without making Lena explain herself. Halfway through, a cluster of conventioneers came in wearing matching lanyards and took over the high-top near the door. One of them, a woman with a flowered notebook and a smoker's rasp, spent ten minutes complaining about panel attendance before switching mid-sentence to the fact that her mother had started hiding money in soup cans again. The room filled and emptied around Lena while she waited for the next message and tried not to feel how precisely public her body was. Every time the door opened she looked up. Every time it was not him.

At 6:27 a message came through, then a second, then a blurry photo.

`Found a charger.`

`Noelle's okay. Shaky, furious, keeps saying she's stupid for giving him the spare key.`

The photo showed two black contractor bags, a cracked mirror leaning against a wall, and the white ceramic ears of what might once have been a lamp sticking out of a grocery crate. At the edge of the frame she could see dark wet dirt or plaster or cat litter, impossible to tell. The picture was bad enough to be convincing.

Lena zoomed until the pixels broke apart.

`You don't have to stay mad and nice at the same time btw.`

She typed three versions of her answer before sending one.

`I'm not staying nice out of charity. I'm pissed. I'm also not twelve. Keep helping her.`

He reacted with a heart before sending, `That was hot, unfortunately for my current circumstances.`

She laughed again, quieter this time. The humiliation did not leave. It only changed clothes. It became something she could carry with a little more dignity because there was now a sister and an ex and a bathroom door and a cookie tin in the story with her.

By the time she paid her tab it was after seven and the paper tag in the dress had worked loose entirely, a small flag scratching her side every other step. The receipt was ugly: drinks, fries, tenders, tax, tip. She folded it once and shoved it into her wallet on top of the Marshalls receipt, one bad decision laid neatly over another. In the station bathroom she looked at herself under the fluorescent lights and saw a woman who had done too much to arrive here for the evening to count as minor inconvenience. Her lipstick had worn off at the center of her mouth. The concealer under her eyes had gone grainy. The dress still fit, but now it smelled faintly of fryer grease and tonic.

She touched the overnight bag. Toothbrush. Underwear. One condom. The small, awful museum of intentions.

Her train home was not for another forty minutes. She sat near the gate and watched families gather themselves into rows. A toddler in dinosaur pajamas ran at full speed into his father's legs and bounced. A young man in a Howard hoodie slept sitting up with both arms crossed over a cello case. Above them, the announcements kept arriving in that compressed station voice, all sibilance and metal. Delayed. Boarding. Final call. Words that moved bodies without ever having to care about them.

Eli sent a voice note while she waited.

"Okay. We got her to a place on New York Avenue that's ugly but has a lock and sheets. She's in the shower. I finally plugged in. I know today was a fucking disgrace on my end. I know it. Thank you for not making me defend every terrible minute of it while I'm still in it. Text me when you're on the train?"

He sounded exhausted. He sounded grateful. He sounded, most fatally, relieved by her in a way that let her imagine she still had power inside the wreck.

`Getting on now,` she wrote back. Then, before she could think too hard, `If the room cleaned you out, let me know. I'm serious.`

The typing bubble appeared so quickly it felt like his pulse.

`Don't.`

Then:

`Okay maybe let me see what damage is when I can think. I hate even writing that.`

Then:

`You should be here for good reasons, Lena. I wanted today to be good reasons.`

She boarded the train with that line inside her, protecting it as if it had weight.

***

By the time the train pulled into Philadelphia the sky had gone a hard dirty violet and then broken open. Rain sheeted across the platforms at 30th Street so thick the lights smeared in it. Lena stood under the awning with everybody else for two minutes telling herself she would wait it out, then gave up and ran with the rest of them toward the doors because there was no waiting out weather that committed this hard.

The hem of the dress drank everything the street threw at it. By the time she got down into the subway and then back up near home, her calves were freckled with grit and rain, the thin straps of her sandals had raised red lines across her feet, and the paper tag tucked inside the seam had gone soft as bread. The plastic fastener still held. The cardboard had started to bleed dark blue into the lining.

She let herself into the apartment and stood just inside the door listening to the fan. The room smelled like warm dust and old coffee grounds and the wet city she had carried in on her clothes. Her bed was unmade in the exact hopeful hurry she had left it that morning. The bathroom light was still on. She had a brief, crazed urge to strip the dress off and wad it into the trash so she would not have to look at evidence of how carefully she had arranged herself. Instead she set the tote down, sat on the bed without unzipping it, and called June.

June answered on the second ring. "You home?"

Lena had not even said hello yet. "Yeah."

"You sound like hell."

"I think I got stood up and then maybe didn't."

A beat. Not long. Just enough to show June was taking the sentence apart.

"Do you want me to come over?"

The question loosened something in Lena immediately, almost humiliatingly. "If you're around."

"I'm around."

"You don't have to bring anything."

"I'm not coming empty-handed to a disaster. Open the downstairs door when I buzz."

June arrived twenty-three minutes later carrying a Wawa bag gone translucent at the bottom with grease and rain. She always knocked with the side of her fist, two quick raps like she expected doors to be stubborn. Her hair was damp at the temples, darkening the little silver streak she pretended not to have. One bootlace trailed untied, collecting water from the hallway. She smelled like wet concrete and cigarettes she had not yet gotten to smoke.

"Move," she said softly when Lena opened the door.

Lena moved.

June set the bag on the table and pulled out fries, two bottles of Diet Coke, a packet of aspirin, and a slice of chocolate cake in a clear clamshell crushed at one corner. "The cake got in a fight with my bag. It lost."

"You didn't have to get cake."

"I didn't get it for emotional reasons. It was there."

That was also, in its way, gentleness.

Lena sat back on the bed because standing made the room tilt. June took one look at her dress hem and clicked her tongue.

"You trying to return that?"

"I was."

June crouched and touched the soaked edge between two nicotine-stained fingers. "Not if the dye from the tag ran. Maybe if you steam it and lie with conviction."

Lena laughed once and then, because her body was cheaper than her mind, nearly cried instead.

"Okay," June said, straightening. Her voice went quieter, tidier. The joke left it. "Walk me through it."

Lena did. She started with the train because starting with the station felt too naked. She told June the time of the first text and the second and the call that went to voicemail and the bar and the bartender and the voice note and the photo and the room on New York Avenue. Under stress she could feel herself getting exact enough to be annoying, but she could not stop. "At 4:12 he said ten late. At 4:33 he said another ten. At 5:14 he said phone on fumes. At 5:41 he called. I know that sounds insane. I know I sound insane."

"You sound like somebody who got put in a bad position and keeps replaying the tape." June handed her a Diet Coke, already open. "Drink that."

Lena drank because being told helped.

June held out her hand for the phone. Lena gave it over. June read the messages without commentary, her face doing that unreadable flattening it did when she was tired enough or concentrating hard enough to stop performing. She zoomed in on the photo. She listened to the voice note once. Rain ticked at the window. The fan chopped the room into blunt little pulses of air.

"Well?" Lena said.

June set the phone down on the bed between them. "Well, he should've called before you were sitting there dressed and visible. That's one thing."

Lena flinched at visible.

"I'm not saying that to be a dick," June said. "I'm saying that's the part he owns, no matter what the sister situation is."

"So you think there is a sister situation."

June made a face. "I think the details are too stupid not to belong to somebody. Nobody invents the cookie tin unless they've seen a cookie tin."

That was exactly what Lena had thought, and hearing it from someone else felt like oxygen.

"Right?"

"Probably." June pushed a wet strand of hair behind her ear and looked back at the phone. "Also a lock change or a hotel room is a real problem, not a melodrama problem. If her place got turned over and the ex had a key, tonight is expensive whether he's a saint or an asshole."

"He said he wasn't asking."

"That doesn't mean there isn't a number sitting under the conversation."

Lena pulled the Diet Coke away from her mouth. "You think he's gonna ask."

"I think if a woman is in a motel on New York Avenue because her ex wrecked her bathroom, somebody's paying for it."

Practical language. Systems, money, consequence. It was one of the reasons June was so often right about things before other people were right. Lena both relied on it and resented it. Tonight she wanted it.

"If he asks," Lena said slowly, "is that automatically bad?"

June looked at her over the bottle she had just opened for herself. "That's not the question."

"Then what's the question?"

"Can you afford to help without rewriting the story afterward so you don't have to feel stupid?"

That landed cleaner than comfort would have. Lena stared at the damp dark line drying across the floor where June's boot had dripped.

"I don't know."

"Okay. That's honest." June reached into the Wawa bag and shoved fries toward her. "Eat one thing with salt before you make any moral commitments."

Lena took a fry. It had gone limp in transit and still tasted good.

"I hate that I came back with this as the version of the day." She heard the rawness in her own voice and hated that too. "I packed a toothbrush. I packed underwear. I bought condoms. I bought a dress I literally left the tags on because I thought maybe if everything went great I could still be responsible about it after. Who does that?"

"You do," June said. Not cruel. Not kind. Just factual. "You make backup plans even for your fantasies. That's very on-brand."

Despite herself, Lena barked a laugh. "Fuck off."

"There she is."

Lena scrubbed at under one eye with the heel of her hand and came away with mascara grit. "If this was nothing, June, I don't actually know what to do with that."

June went still for a beat. It was subtle, but Lena knew her well enough to catch it: the way she settled deeper into silence when something mattered. When June answered, her voice had lost every trace of banter.

"Then don't decide it's nothing tonight."

The sentence entered Lena like medicine and like poison.

"You really think that?"

"I think tonight was humiliating. That's different from nothing." June looked back at the phone. "And I think if you cut it off this second because you got embarrassed in public, you'll never know which pain you're actually choosing."

Rain hammered the sill harder. Somewhere downstairs a television rose and fell through the pipes. Lena could smell the wet hem of the dress now, the train and the bar and city water drying into fabric. June sat on the edge of the chair with one knee bouncing once, then stopping. She did not touch Lena. She rarely did unless contact had a task attached to it.

The phone lit between them.

Eli.

Lena snatched it up too quickly. June did not comment.

`Noelle's asleep finally. I'm sitting on the floor because the chair looks like it has bedbugs. Good news is she kept the basil alive. Bad news is the room cost more than my dignity.`

Then:

`Ignore that. Gallows humor. Long day.`

Then, after a pause long enough to feel like choice:

`Can I ask you something ugly or should I wait till tomorrow`

Lena stared at the words until they blurred.

"There it is," June said quietly.

"Don't."

"I'm not doing anything."

The phone buzzed again before Lena answered.

`I can cover some tomorrow if payroll hits when it's supposed to. Right now I'm short 280 and I hate every atom of writing that sentence to you. If the answer's no, say no. I mean that.`

Two hundred eighty dollars. Not an invented giant sum. Not a romance-novel ransom. A room in a bad motel and probably a drugstore charger and coffee and one stupid fee nobody warned you about till check-in. It was exactly the kind of number a real emergency could produce and a bad lie could hide inside.

Lena opened her bank app. Her checking account sat at $912.67. Rent would hit next week. Her Visa minimum was due on the twenty-second. Taxes waited farther off with their patient open mouth. Two hundred eighty dollars would hurt. It would not destroy her. That was almost worse. Destruction would have made the choice clearer.

"Say no if you want to say no," June said.

"Do you think I should?"

June leaned back and exhaled through her nose. "I think if you send it, call it what it is. A one-night bridge. Not proof of love. Not proof you're not a sucker. Just money for a room."

"You make everything sound like a spreadsheet."

"Because money is one."

Lena looked at the dress pooled around her knees, at the darkened hem and the warped paper tag hidden in the seam. She thought of the empty stool beside her in Washington. She thought of the couple sharing crab dip and the bartender pretending not to measure pity. She thought of the overnight bag on the train seat with its obscene little inventory of hope. If she said no now, the whole day stayed exactly as it had looked from the bar: a woman alone in a dress that wasn't even really hers, pretending delay because reality was worse. If she said yes, then the day had consequence. The trip had touched something. Her humiliation had bought entry into an emergency instead of proof of her own stupidity.

It was not a noble calculation. It was only the one that hurt less.

She typed, deleted, typed again.

`I'll send it. One night only. Don't turn this into a pattern.`

The answer came back before she had even switched apps.
