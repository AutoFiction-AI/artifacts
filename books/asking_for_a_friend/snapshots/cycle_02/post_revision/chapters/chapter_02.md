# Chapter 2

She gave it an honest week.

That was the deal Nora had struck with herself on the subway home from the engagement party, her new shoes pinching in a way that felt like penance, Simone's laugh still ringing in her ears from the platform where they'd said goodnight. One week of genuine, good-faith swiping. She would approach the app the way she'd approach an unfamiliar neighborhood on foot — with the patience to walk every block, an openness to what might be around the next corner, and a willingness to believe the good stuff sometimes hid behind a long stretch of nothing.

By Wednesday she had a taxonomy.

The largest category was what she privately called the Assertion Men — guys whose profiles read like ad copy for a personality they'd workshopped in the shower. *Fluent in sarcasm. Equally at home in a dive bar or a five-star restaurant. Looking for my partner in crime.* Every third profile promised to make her laugh, a guarantee Nora found roughly as credible as a restaurant promising its food was edible. Their photos showed them skydiving, holding fish, raising glasses at rooftop bars where everything was backlit and nobody looked quite real. One man had answered the prompt "I'm looking for" with "someone who doesn't take herself too seriously," which Nora suspected meant he wanted a woman who'd laugh at his jokes and not ask where he'd been until two in the morning.

Then there were the specialists. A man whose six photos featured him posing with six different large, visibly sedated cats — a tiger, two lions, what might have been a cheetah, and two animals Nora couldn't confidently identify — each in a different country, as though he'd built an international itinerary around the opportunity to crouch next to an unconscious apex predator. A man who had answered every single Hinge prompt with a reference to his podcast about male vulnerability — "The key to my heart is: honestly just listen to episode 47, I lay it all out there." A venture capitalist who listed, in his bio, the dollar amount of his last funding round, as if Nora might want to date a Series B.

She matched with a few. A high school teacher named Greg who was perfectly pleasant and whose messages had the careful precision of a well-set table — everything in its place, nothing spilled, nothing that made her want to sit down. They exchanged thirteen messages over two days, each one slightly more effortful than the last, until he asked what she liked to do for fun and she realized she would rather reorganize her bookshelves alphabetically by translator than answer that question one more time. She let the conversation lapse and felt guilty about it in a way that also annoyed her, which seemed to be the specific emotional texture of online dating — guilt and irritation stirred into the same lukewarm cup.

A man named Patrick opened by telling her that her smile was "not what you'd expect from someone in publishing," which she chose to interpret as a compliment until she realized she had no idea what it meant, and then she stopped trying. Another man — his name was either Todd or Troy, she'd already let the memory soften at the edges — learned she was a book editor and responded in under forty seconds with a three-paragraph pitch for his novel about a retired detective solving crimes in a wine bar. *For fuck's sake.* She screenshotted that one and sent it to Margo at work, who replied with a skull emoji and the words *you deserve this for downloading the app in the first place.*

By Friday she was swiping in the bath, which felt like a new low. The phone was propped against the shampoo bottle on the tub's edge, her thumbs still damp, the screen stippled with water droplets she kept smearing away with the heel of her hand. The water had gone tepid fifteen minutes ago and the profiles were starting to blur together into a single composite man — holding a guitar, standing next to a tall thing to suggest his own height, claiming to love hiking without specifying that he'd ever hiked anywhere that wasn't a paved trail in Prospect Park. She was about to close the app — not delete it, not yet, because the deal was seven days and she had her professional integrity to think of — when she landed on a profile that made her stop the way a sentence in a manuscript sometimes made her stop: not because anything was wrong, but because something was unexpectedly, specifically right.

---

His name was Caleb. Caleb Mora, thirty-two, Portland. His first photo was taken on a trail — not a posed trail shot with the lighting of a cologne ad, but an actual photograph of an actual person on an actual hike, slightly sweaty, squinting into the sun, his smile caught mid-formation as if someone had said something funny just before the shutter clicked. He was good-looking in a way that didn't announce itself — dark hair, warm brown skin, a face that was interesting before it was handsome.

The second photo was him in a kitchen, flour on his hands, a wooden cutting board in front of him and something in a cast-iron pan behind him releasing a lazy thread of steam. The third was a dog — a medium-sized, slightly scruffy mutt with one ear that stood up and one that flopped, pressed against his leg with the full-body devotion of an animal that had decided, irrevocably, that this was its person. The fourth photo was the one that got her: him laughing at something off-camera, his head thrown back, his eyes squeezed nearly shut, one hand reaching toward whoever was behind the lens as if to steady himself against the force of whatever was funny. She'd seen enough performed laughter on this app to know the difference — the careful arrangement of features versus the involuntary surrender to delight. This was the second kind.

She scrolled down to his prompts.

*I geek out on:* "Soil composition. I know that sounds like a joke but I'm a landscape architect and the difference between sandy loam and clay loam is legitimately the most interesting thing I can bring up at a dinner party, which probably explains my dating life."

*The book I'm reading right now:* "The Understory by Lena Mori. Someone described it as 'a novel that takes the forest more seriously than most novels take people' and I haven't stopped thinking about that."

Nora sat up in the bath so fast that water sloshed over the side and pooled across the tile.

She had edited *The Understory*. She'd spent four months inside that manuscript, line by line, arguing with Lena Mori over comma placement and defending the structural digression in the third section that their publisher had wanted to cut. It was one of her favorite projects — a book that sold modestly and reviewed well and that she was privately proud of in a way she rarely mentioned, because taking pride in your work as an editor was difficult to explain to people who assumed the writer did everything. Most people didn't understand that a good editor left fingerprints on every page, invisible ones, the kind that only showed up under the right light.

And here was a man — a stranger, in Portland — reading it. Not performing it, not listing *Infinite Jest* or whatever novel was currently functioning as a personality signifier on the profiles of men who wanted to seem interesting without doing the work of actually being interesting. He was reading her book. And what he'd said about it — *a novel that takes the forest more seriously than most novels take people* — that wasn't from any review she'd seen. It sounded like something a person had actually thought.

His third prompt: *Together, we could:* "Walk very slowly through a farmers' market, disagree about which cheese is best, and then agree that we should just buy both."

She liked his photo — the laughing one — and typed a comment before she could talk herself out of it: "Okay but which cheese are you arguing for. This matters."

She set the phone on the edge of the tub and stared at the ceiling and waited for the notification buzz and did not get it. She got out of the bath, wrapped herself in a towel, mopped up the water she'd sloshed across the floor with a sock that was already on its way to the hamper, pulled on old sweats, got into bed, and checked the phone again.

Nothing.

She told herself that this was fine, that not everyone checked their phone at nine-thirty on a Friday, that he was probably at dinner or with friends or simply living his life in the way that people who didn't stare at their phones in the bathtub apparently did. She put the phone face-down on the nightstand and picked up the galleys she'd brought home from work — a debut novel about competitive birdwatching that she was three chapters into and liking more than she'd expected — and read the same paragraph four times without absorbing a word.

Her phone buzzed.

He'd matched. His first message: "Comté. Always Comté. But I'll hear your case."

She laughed — not the evaluative half-smile she'd been giving profiles all week, but an actual, audible laugh that surprised her in the quiet of her apartment, where the only sounds were the baseboard heater ticking and someone's bass bleeding faintly through the shared wall. She typed back: "Manchego, and I won't apologize for it."

"Manchego is a strong opening argument. Are you one of those people who pairs it with membrillo? Because if so I might need to rethink my position."

"I'm one of those people who eats it standing in the kitchen at eleven p.m. straight off the cutting board, so no. But I respect the membrillo commitment."

"Okay that's better. The membrillo people have a slightly intimidating energy. Like people who iron their sheets."

She was grinning at her phone. She was aware that she was grinning at her phone — that she looked like every person she had ever privately judged on the subway for grinning at their phone — and she didn't care, because the October dark outside her window had changed quality somehow, had become the kind of darkness that felt like privacy rather than absence. A car alarm was going off somewhere on the next block and someone down the hall had started running their dishwasher and none of it bothered her.

They talked for two hours.

He asked what she did, and when she said she was a book editor, his response wasn't a joke about red pens or a pivot to his own creative ambitions but a question: "What do you look for when a manuscript first lands on your desk? Like, before you've read enough to judge the writing — is there something in the first few pages that tells you to lean in?"

Nobody had asked her that. Not on a date, not at a party, not even most of her friends — and she had smart friends who cared about books, but there was a difference between caring about books and being curious about the specific, granular mechanics of caring about books, and he had found that difference with an ease that, if she'd been paying a different kind of attention, might have looked less like instinct and more like aim. She told him about the feeling — she'd never really tried to put it into words — of reading a first page and sensing that the writer was paying a certain kind of attention, a kind she recognized, as if you'd been listening for a particular frequency and suddenly the signal was there. "It's less about the prose and more about the noticing," she typed. "Like the writer and I are interested in the same corner of the room."

His response came fast: "I think that's how I feel about certain landscapes. There are places that feel like they already know you. That sounds insane. I'm going to pretend I didn't type that."

"Don't pretend. I liked it."

"You liked the insane thing?"

"I liked that you said it and then got embarrassed. That's a good ratio."

She shifted onto her side, the phone on the pillow next to her face, the blue glow from the screen the only light in the room now. They moved from cheese to books to the specific pleasure of doing work you cared about, which turned into a conversation about why that pleasure always felt slightly embarrassing to admit, as if enjoying your job were a moral failing in a world that expected you to endure work rather than love it. He told her about landscape architecture — how it was basically the practice of imagining a space fifteen years from now, when the trees you'd planted were tall enough to give shade and the paths had developed the particular wear pattern of a place people actually used. "You're designing a future you won't see for a long time," he said. "It's an act of faith disguised as a blueprint."

She told him about the novel she'd been trying to write for three years. She almost never mentioned it — it had calcified into a sore spot, a thing she carried but couldn't look at directly, and the few times she'd brought it up with friends the conversation had turned into either encouragement she didn't want or practical advice she couldn't use. But saying it to a stranger on her phone in the dark felt different than she expected, less like a confession and more like setting something down on a table between them to see if it held weight.

He didn't tell her to just sit down and write every day. He didn't ask what it was about. He didn't suggest she try a writing retreat or an MFA or National Novel Writing Month. He seemed to know, without being told, every wrong answer she'd ever gotten to this particular confession — which should have been strange, a stranger navigating the edges of a sore spot with the sureness of someone who'd been warned where not to step. It didn't feel strange. It felt like being met. He said: "Three years is a long time to keep something alive. Whatever's keeping you from finishing — the fact that you haven't given up on it probably says more than the fact that it's not done."

She stared at that message for a long time. The car alarm had stopped. The bass through the wall had gone quiet. Her apartment was silent in the specific way Brooklyn apartments were silent after midnight — not truly quiet, but the noise had shifted register, from the sounds of people being awake to the sounds of a city talking to itself. She was lying on her side with the phone on the pillow next to her like a second face, and the blue light from the screen had become the only source of warmth in the room, which she knew was not literally true but felt true in a way that resisted correction.

"That's either very kind or very perceptive," she typed. "I can't tell which."

"Can it be both?"

"I'll allow it. But I'm noting it for the record."

"There's a record?"

"I'm an editor. There's always a record."

He sent back a single emoji — the face with the monocle — and she laughed again, quietly this time, and the laugh settled into something warmer and less definite, something that sat in her chest the way the first sentence of a good book sometimes sat in her chest: not a conclusion but a promise that more was coming, that the thing she'd just encountered was the beginning of something worth following. She wanted to keep talking, but she also wanted to stop here, in the place where everything was still possible and nothing had been tested yet. "I should sleep," she wrote. "But this was — this was really good."

"It was. I'm glad you have strong opinions about cheese."

"Goodnight, Caleb."

"Goodnight, Nora."

She put the phone face-down on the nightstand. The screen was still glowing through the back of the case, a faint rectangle of blue against the wood, and she didn't turn it over. She lay in the dark and felt something she hadn't felt in long enough that she had to search for the word for it — not happiness, exactly, and not excitement, but the specific, fragile state of having been surprised by a person, of finding that a stranger you'd never met had a shape that fit against yours in a way you hadn't anticipated and couldn't quite explain. She pulled the covers up and the sheets were cold and she pressed her feet together for warmth and she did not check the phone again, which was, she decided, a form of discipline she could be proud of.

---

She lasted until Saturday morning.

The screenshot she sent Simone was from the cheese conversation — the early part, before it deepened, before she'd told him things she hadn't told people she'd known for years. She chose the cheese part because it was funny and light and because sending the deeper stuff would have felt like handing over something she wasn't ready to share yet, even with Simone — even though Simone was the person she shared everything with, had shared everything with since the freshman orientation event at NYU where they'd bonded over their mutual, incredulous contempt for the icebreaker they'd been forced into, and had been narrating their lives to each other in an unbroken stream ever since.

*i think i found a human person on this app??* she typed, and attached the screenshots.

Simone's response came fast — she was always fast, her phone seemingly fused to her hand in a way that Nora found both impressive and faintly concerning. Three messages in quick succession:

*OKAY WAIT*

*scrolling*

*the cheese thing????? Nor. Who IS this person.*

Then: *Name. Age. Location. Profession. I need the full dossier immediately.*

Nora typed it out, smiling at her phone the same way she'd been smiling at it the night before, but the pleasure was different now — this was the pleasure of narrating, of turning an experience into a story for the one person whose reaction she most wanted. *Caleb Mora, 32, Portland, landscape architect. He has a dog. He's reading a book I edited.*

*Which book??*

*The Understory.*

*SHUT UP. He's reading The Understory?? How does a landscape architect in Portland even know about The Understory?*

*I KNOW. That's what I said.*

*Okay show me the profile. All the photos. I need photos immediately.*

Nora went back to Hinge and screenshotted Caleb's profile — the hiking photo, the kitchen, the dog, the laugh — and sent them one by one.

She watched the typing indicator pulse.

*he's cute. He's actually cute. Not cute-for-a-dating-app, actually cute. And the dog looks like a real dog. Most guys borrow a friend's dog for photos and you can tell because the dog is looking at them like who the fuck are you. This dog lives with him.*

*His name is Bruno* Nora wrote.

*The dog has a NAME. Nora. This is significant.*

*You're reading a lot into a dog.*

*I'm reading a lot into a man who reads books by women, cooks with flour on his hands, and has a dog with a name and opinions. Do you know how rare this is? I spent my morning eating cold pad see ew for breakfast because I ran out of coffee and couldn't be bothered to put on real shoes, so trust me, I have perspective on what constitutes a functional adult right now.*

Nora laughed. Simone's ability to read a photograph for subtext was one of her sharpest qualities — she noticed things about framing and body language that Nora missed, the designer's eye applied to human surfaces. And Simone's willingness to narrate her own small disasters was what made these exchanges feel balanced, like they were both characters in the same story rather than one of them performing for the other.

*Did you actually talk though?* Simone wrote. *What did he SAY? What did YOU say? I want a full transcript.*

*We talked for two hours. Si, he asked me what I look for when I first read a manuscript. Like, a real question. Not a polite question.*

*Two HOURS?? Nora. That's not a match. That's a date.*

*It was not a date. It was a conversation on an app.*

*At what time did this conversation take place?*

*...nine-thirty to eleven-thirty.*

*Friday night. Phone. In bed. Two hours. Nora Kaplan, you went on a date.*

*I hate you.*

*You love me. Are you going to text him today?*

*I don't know. Is it too soon?*

*It is never too soon. Text him. Be exactly who you were last night. Tell me everything he says back.*

Nora set the phone on the kitchen counter and looked at it the way she might look at an animal that was about to do something unpredictable. The October light through her window was thin and sharp — the kind of fall morning that made everything look more defined than usual, the buildings across the street revealing edges and textures she normally didn't notice, the fire escape on the brownstone opposite catching the sun in a way that turned the rust almost beautiful. She poured coffee and drank it standing up and then she picked up the phone and opened WhatsApp — they'd exchanged numbers at the end of last night's conversation, a transition that had felt as significant as a door swinging open on a room she hadn't known was there — and typed: "Good morning. Here's my controversial take: manchego on toast with honey. I know. I'm a monster."

His response came in four minutes. "I'm not going to pretend I'm not intrigued. Walk me through the honey ratio."

She took her coffee to the couch and sat cross-legged and began to type. He had a theory about honey on cheese that involved a beekeeper in the Willamette Valley, and she had a counter-theory involving her mother and a jar of buckwheat honey that tasted, she told him, like the inside of a barn. She was halfway through typing *barn honey* when she realized the coffee had gone cold in her hand and the October light had moved all the way across the kitchen floor to warm her feet through her socks. She didn't get up to reheat the coffee.
