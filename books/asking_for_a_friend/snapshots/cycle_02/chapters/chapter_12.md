# Chapter 12

She kept coming back to the phrase, circling it the way she'd circle a problem sentence in a manuscript — the same three words, orbiting, refusing to resolve.

*You're being a lot.*

Nora had been on Simone's couch for two hours. She'd arrived still wearing her coat, her face already wrecked from crying on the train, and Simone had taken one look at her and pulled her inside without asking what happened — the grace of a friendship where asking wasn't always necessary. The coat came off. The sweatshirt went on, an old NYU crewneck of Simone's, soft enough that the cotton had gone nearly translucent at the elbows. The wine appeared. The couch accepted her.

Now she was tucked into the corner with her knees drawn up, turning the words over like a stone she couldn't stop touching.

*You're being a lot.*

She'd read books about this. Not self-help — she'd rather swallow glass — but novels where women were told they wanted too much, felt too much, were too much, and she'd always known, reading those scenes, exactly what the author wanted her to feel, and she'd felt it obediently and then closed the book and gone on with her life. It was different when the words arrived on your phone screen at 11:47 p.m. from a man you'd been patient with for months, a man you'd sent money to without being asked, a man whose face you'd only ever seen in photographs, because you'd been so goddamn understanding about the broken laptop camera and the bad reception and the cold he was fighting.

A man you'd tried to surprise by flying across the country because you wanted — you only wanted — to stand in the same room with him.

"It's such a specific phrase," she said. Simone was sitting on the other end of the couch with her legs folded under her, holding her own wine with both hands, a strand of dark hair caught at the corner of her mouth that she hadn't bothered to push away. "Being a lot. Not 'this is a lot' — that would be about the situation. 'You're being a lot.' That's about me. That's a character note."

"It's a shitty thing to say," Simone said.

"Right, but it's also the kind of thing you can't argue with, because the act of arguing with it proves the point. If I push back, I'm being a lot about being told I'm being a lot. It's a closed loop."

She lifted her wine. Simone's apartment was warm in the January way of old Williamsburg radiators — the unit under the window hissed and clanked against the wall, pushing heat with the persistence of something that didn't know how to stop, and the air smelled like the candle Simone always had going, jasmine and something synthetic underneath, sweet enough that Nora noticed it every time she walked in and forgot it within fifteen minutes. The window had condensation on the inside. Through it, the street was a smear of yellow lights and the occasional bass thud from a car that passed too close to the building.

"I just wanted to see him," Nora said. "I keep going over this, and the thing I can't get past is that I wasn't asking for anything unreasonable. We've been talking for three months. Three months, Simone. I haven't heard his voice. I haven't touched him. I haven't been in the same room with him. And when I said, okay, let me come to you, let me show up in your actual life, his dad had a stroke — which, I feel terrible about, obviously — but then I said I'd come to Chicago instead, and that's when he —"

She stopped. Took a breath. The wine was a Malbec, Simone's Malbec, from the place on Bedford with the handwritten labels — twelve dollars, which Simone had once told her was her ceiling for wine because she'd done the math on what a glass cost at that price point versus going out. Nora didn't do that math. She bought wine by finding something she liked and buying it. She was starting to understand that this was a luxury, not a personality trait.

"I feel pathetic," she said.

"You're not pathetic."

"I'm crying on your couch about a man I've never met in person. By any reasonable metric, that's pathetic."

"By any reasonable metric, most of dating is pathetic. I went out with a guy last month who told me about his crypto portfolio before the appetizers came. I sat there. I stayed for the main course. Who's pathetic?"

Nora laughed, which hurt — her face was swollen from crying, the muscles around her eyes still tender from the contortion of it. She pressed the heels of her hands against her cheekbones. "What if he's right, though? What if I'm too much?"

"He's not right."

"But what if this is what I do? What if I climb inside a relationship before the other person has even opened the door, and then I stand there wondering why they're looking at me like I broke in?"

Simone was quiet for a moment. She shifted on the couch, resettling her weight, and when she spoke her voice had the particular steadiness it got when she was being careful about something. "Nor. You didn't break in anywhere. You bought a plane ticket to visit a guy you've been dating for months. That's not breaking in. That's showing up."

"He didn't see it that way."

"Then that's his problem. Not yours."

Nora wanted to believe that. She pressed her cheek against the arm of the couch, which was nubby and cool and smelled faintly of Febreze — Simone used it because she couldn't afford to have the upholstery cleaned. The cushion sank under her weight. Simone had bought this couch secondhand two years ago, and it had developed a topography of its own, the springs giving way in patterns that recorded who sat where and for how long.

"I sent him money," Nora said. Not for the first time tonight. "For the dog. For his mom. And he told me I was being a lot."

Simone didn't answer immediately. She took a sip of wine, and in the pause Nora heard the radiator cycling, the tick and ping of old pipes expanding inside the wall, and somewhere above them a neighbor walking from one side of their apartment to the other, the footsteps traveling across the ceiling like a sentence being read.

"People are strange about money," Simone said. "Especially when they're the ones who need it."

"Is that what you think? That he's embarrassed?"

"I think —" Simone started, and then stopped. She put her wine on the floor beside the couch — she had a habit of doing this, setting things down when her hands needed to be empty — and Nora saw something cross her face, fast, almost too fast to catch: a tightening around the mouth, a quick inhale, as if she were about to speak and the words had gotten stuck sideways in her throat. Her eyes went to the window and stayed there.

"What?" Nora said.

"Nothing. I think you should let yourself feel shitty tonight and figure out the rest tomorrow."

"Very therapeutic."

"I didn't say I was a therapist. I said drink your wine and feel shitty and let me sit here with you while you do it."

Nora felt the wine warming her hands through the glass, and with it a loosening in her chest — not because the hurt was smaller but because Simone's terrible couch and terrible candle and twelve-dollar Malbec were holding it, absorbing it, giving slightly under its weight.

They talked. Not about Caleb, for a while — about other things, drifting between subjects with the ease of women who'd been narrating their lives to each other for seventeen years and could shift registers without announcement. Simone told a story about a coworker named Priya who'd been stealing lunches from the office fridge, and the Slack channel the team had created to track it, and the anonymous note someone left that said THIEF in Sharpie on a Post-it, and the fact that the thief had responded with another Post-it that said PROVE IT, and how Priya — who sat two desks from Simone and had a collection of jade plants on her monitor stand and who Simone was reasonably sure was not the thief — had started bringing two lunches to work out of pure solidarity with whoever was. Nora laughed until the laughing hurt her face again and the hurting reminded her of Caleb and the reminder made her stop.

"Can I tell you something terrible?" Nora said.

"Always."

"When he said that — 'you're being a lot' — part of me thought, okay, this is what it takes. This is the price of admission. This is what it costs to be with somebody. You give and you give and eventually they tell you the giving is too much, and you learn to want less so they'll stay."

"That's not terrible. That's honest."

"It's terrible because I'm willing to do it. I know exactly what it sounds like — I'd edit it out of a manuscript in a heartbeat — and I'd still do it. I'd learn to want less if it meant he'd stay."

Simone pulled one leg up onto the cushion and turned toward Nora. "You remember sophomore year? Psych 101, the exam week where we were so sleep-deprived we walked to that diner at three in the morning and ordered pancakes and you told me you didn't think you were lovable in the way that made people stay?"

"Vaguely."

"I told you that was bullshit, and it was, and it still is. You're not too much. The right person would cross the country to see you, not the other way around."

Nora felt the tears start again — not the hard crying from earlier but something slower, seeping, the kind that didn't require any contortion of the face. She wiped her cheek on the sleeve of Simone's sweatshirt and the cotton came away dark.

"I don't know what I'd do without you," she said.

She meant it completely, unselfconsciously, without the usual editorial instinct to qualify. She meant it like a first draft — before revision, before the second pass where you added the hedge and the caveat and the ironic distance. Raw.

Simone opened her mouth.

The pause that followed was long enough that Nora looked up, and what she saw on Simone's face was something she couldn't name — a kind of held suspension, every muscle engaged in the act of not doing something, someone standing at an edge and deciding between jumping and stepping back. Her eyes were wet. Nora hadn't seen Simone cry in years — Simone didn't cry, not visibly, not in front of anyone who might remember it — and the wetness sat on her lower lashes without falling, held there by surface tension or by will.

Then Simone smiled. It was the right smile — warm, steady, arriving at exactly the right moment — and she said, "You'd manage. But you shouldn't have to."

They watched a movie, or they put on a movie, which wasn't the same thing. Simone chose something they'd both seen, a romantic comedy from the late nineties they'd watched enough times that neither needed to follow the plot, which meant they could talk during it or not talk during it and neither option was a failure. Simone got a blanket from the closet — the thick knitted one, cream-colored, a birthday gift from someone Nora couldn't remember — and they shared it. This meant Simone shifted to the middle of the couch and their knees touched and then their shoulders touched, and at some point Nora's head was on Simone's shoulder and the movie was still going and she was watching the actors' mouths move without processing the words. The radiator was still clicking. The candle had burned down to a sputtering disk of flame inside the glass.

Nora fell asleep before the credits. She felt Simone reach for the remote, and the dialogue dropped away, and then the lamp went off behind her eyelids, and Simone settled back and Nora adjusted her weight against Simone's shoulder without fully waking. The apartment went still. Outside, the street was doing its late-night recital — a siren tapering off somewhere toward the bridge, a voice below the window, the subway rumble that traveled through the building's foundation and that you only noticed when everything else stopped. She slept fully, without dreaming, for the first time in weeks — her body surrendered to the couch and to the person beside her, the person she trusted more than anyone alive.

She slept all the way through.

---

She woke to the sound of coffee being ground.

Simone had a hand-crank grinder, an ugly little wooden box she'd found at a flea market in Red Hook, because Simone always found things at flea markets that were exactly right for a fraction of what they should have cost. Using it required focused, methodical labor that Simone seemed to find calming and Nora found psychotic. The handle made a rhythmic crunching — beans splitting, giving way — and it pulled Nora out of sleep into the disorientation of waking somewhere that wasn't her bed.

The couch. Simone's couch. The blanket was tucked around her and Simone's spot was empty but still warm, which meant she hadn't been up long. Nora's neck was stiff from sleeping at an angle, the tendons along her left side pulled tight, and her face had the swollen immobility of a mask — a residue she could feel when she moved her jaw. Simone's sweatshirt smelled like Nora's own body now, the lavender detergent overtaken by sleep and wine and the salt of dried tears.

The light through the window was the gray-white of late January in Brooklyn — noncommittal, diffuse, a sky that had been overcast so long it seemed to have forgotten what it was covering for. Last night's condensation had frozen in fern patterns on the inside of the glass.

She found her phone wedged between the cushions. 11:14 a.m. She hadn't slept that late in months. Notifications stacked on her lock screen — work emails, a text from Margo that said *coffee?*, a push alert about something she couldn't bring herself to care about — and underneath them, nothing from Caleb. His silence sat among the notifications like a gap between words where a word should be.

From the kitchen — not really a kitchen, just the six feet of counter and appliances that occupied one wall of Simone's studio — the grinding stopped. Water ran. The kettle clicked.

"Morning," Simone said, without turning around. She was wearing a T-shirt and flannel pajama pants, her dark hair pushed behind her ears, and she had the look of someone who'd been awake for a while — there was already a mug rinsed and set upside down on the drying rack, a first cup she'd had alone.

"What time did you get up?"

"Nine-ish. You were out. I didn't want to wake you."

"I was out." Nora sat up, and the blanket pooled at her waist, and the room's cold found her arms through the thin cotton. She tilted her neck to one side, then the other. Something cracked. "Your couch is going to kill me."

"My couch is doing its best."

Nora looked at her phone again. The blank space where Caleb should have been. She opened WhatsApp and tapped his name — his profile photo, laughing at something off-camera with Bruno half-visible at the edge of the frame, loaded at the top of the thread. The last messages were from last night: his, cold and brief, a wall she'd hit at full speed. She scrolled up past the fight to the messages from before — the warm ones, the ones where he asked about her day and told her about a client's backyard in the West Hills and sent a photo of Bruno asleep on his drafting table. Those messages were still there. The warmth was still there, preserved in the scroll, and the contrast with last night's exchange was what made the fight real — not the cruelty of what he'd said, but the proof that he'd been capable of something else before he said it.

She started typing. Deleted it. Started again. Composing an apology to a man she was angry at and hurt by and still, against her own better judgment, invested in — the cursor blinking, the words assembling and collapsing — felt like working on a sentence she wasn't sure belonged in the manuscript but couldn't bring herself to cut.

*I'm sorry about yesterday. I should have talked to you before booking the flight. I just wanted to see you. That's not an excuse, that's just what's true.*

She read it back. Changed nothing. Sent it before her brain could overrule her thumb.

The kettle whistled. Simone lifted it off the burner and poured water into the French press, and the sound of water meeting ground coffee was domestic and ordinary, and it steadied her. Nora watched the back of Simone's head and thought about how many mornings of her life had included Simone in some form — mornings in the dorm, mornings after parties, mornings after breakups, mornings that were just mornings, not recovering from anything, just starting — and how the accumulation of all those mornings amounted to its own kind of relationship, quieter than romance but more load-bearing than romance had ever been for her.

The typing indicator appeared. Three dots, pulsing.

Her thumbs pressed white against the phone's edges. She watched the dots disappear and return, each cycle a held breath, the interval between sending a question into silence and finding out what the silence had to say.

The message arrived.

*Nora. I was wrong. I was scared about my dad and I was overwhelmed and I took it out on you, and you didn't deserve any of it. You wanted to see me. That's not being a lot. That's being someone who gives a damn, and I'm sorry I made you feel like that was something to apologize for. I've been sitting here all night thinking about how I'd feel if someone wanted to fly across the country just to stand in the same room as me, and the answer is I'd feel like the luckiest person alive, and instead of saying that I said something I can't take back. I'm not asking you to forgive me right now. I'm just asking you not to give up on me.*

She read it twice. The words were warm and specific and they reached into the exact bruised place in her chest and held it — not pushing the hurt down but acknowledging it, looking at it directly instead of around it. He hadn't made excuses. He hadn't performed a generic apology. He'd named what he did and what it cost her and what she deserved instead, and reading it felt like the first sip of something hot after hours in the cold, a heat that started in her throat and loosened things she hadn't known she was holding clenched.

"He apologized," she said, and her voice came out smaller than she'd meant. She cleared her throat. "Caleb apologized."

"Yeah?" Simone turned from the counter. She had two mugs, and she crossed the four steps of the studio — that was all it took to traverse the apartment — and held one out. "What'd he say?"

Nora showed her the phone. Simone took it with one hand, read the screen, and her face settled into a look Nora recognized — brows lifting, mouth softening, the quick nod that meant *this checks out*. It was Simone processing good news on someone else's behalf, an expression Nora had seen hundreds of times and never once doubted.

"That's really good, Nor." She handed the phone back. "That's someone who knows he fucked up and isn't trying to spin it."

"You think?"

"Someone who writes an apology like that at —" She glanced at the time stamp. "— six in the morning, who's clearly been up all night? That's not someone who doesn't care. That's someone who cared too much and got scared."

Nora took the coffee. It was exactly how she drank it — oat milk, no sugar, added while the coffee was hot enough to shift its color to the precise shade of brown that meant the ratio was right. Simone had been making her coffee this way for seventeen years, since the dorm, since they'd discovered they took it identically and treated the coincidence as evidence of cosmic compatibility. Nora wrapped her hands around the mug and let the heat soak into her palms and her knuckles and the pads of her fingers, which were still cold from the apartment's January air.

"I want this to work," she said. She wasn't asking for permission — not exactly — but she was asking for something. For Simone to see her choosing this and to stand next to her while she chose it, even if the choice was risky. Especially then.

"Then let it work," Simone said. She sat down beside Nora, and the couch dipped, tipping them toward each other because the springs on that side had given up months ago. "You're not stupid. You're not naive. You're someone who knows what she wants and isn't going to apologize for wanting it."

"You literally told me last night not to apologize for being too much."

"I'm consistent."

Nora smiled. The muscles around her eyes protested — still tender, still keeping count of last night's damage. She sipped the coffee. She read Caleb's message again, slower this time, letting it work at a lower frequency, a second reading where the architecture showed through the feeling.

Outside, a truck was backing up on the street below, its warning beeps entering through the single-paned window. The radiator had gone quiet between cycles. The candle from last night was a pool of cold white wax in its jar, a faint char smell mixing with the coffee steam.

"I'm going to text him back," Nora said.

"Good."

She typed: *I'm not giving up on you. But we need to talk about meeting. For real. When your dad's better. I can wait, but I can't wait forever.*

Simone leaned over, reading. "That's exactly right," she said.

The message sent. The checkmarks turned blue. Nora put her phone face-down on the cushion and drank her coffee and looked at Simone's apartment — the small, precise space, the desk against the window where Simone worked, the shelf of books that were mostly Nora's recommendations, the single framed Hopper print above the radiator, a woman alone at a counter, which Simone had bought at a Red Hook flea market years ago and hung with a level because Simone didn't do anything crooked.

She thought: this is enough. This morning, this coffee, this person who has known me longer than anyone except my mother. This is the part of my life that doesn't require faith.

Simone pulled the blanket back over both their laps and picked up her own mug and they sat there, side by side, while the January morning slowly committed to becoming a day. They didn't talk. They didn't need to. The silence between them was the kind that could only exist between people who'd long ago exhausted every performative reason to fill it — a silence that felt, from inside it, like proof that some things held.
