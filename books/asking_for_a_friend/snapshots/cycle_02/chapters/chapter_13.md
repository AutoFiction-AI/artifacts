# Chapter 13

The envelope was from her landlord's attorney. She knew this because she'd Googled the return address twice before — Leibowitz & Katz, P.C. — and because the building super had slipped it under her door at an angle that suggested he didn't want to touch it any longer than necessary. Marvin was a decent man who fixed radiators the same day you asked and left the hallway lights on past midnight for the woman in 4F who worked late shifts at the hospital, and he had delivered this envelope the way a veterinarian hands you the pamphlet about euthanasia: gently, apologetically, already backing away.

Simone had been stepping over it for two days. She'd stepped over it Thursday morning on her way to work, stepped over it again Thursday evening when she came home, stepped over it Friday morning to take her recycling out, and stepped over it Friday night when she came back from the bodega on the corner with a six-dollar bottle of Cabernet and a box of Triscuits that would serve as dinner. The envelope had sat on her studio floor like a small paper landmine, the legal return address visible every time she passed, and she had done what she always did with information she wasn't ready to process: maintained a precise physical distance from it that she pretended was accidental.

She picked it up Saturday morning. The floor was cold under her bare feet — February in her building meant the radiator blasting steam heat at 4 a.m. and then dying by eight, leaving the apartment in a state of thermal abandonment that matched its other conditions. She opened the envelope standing at her kitchen counter, which was also her desk, which was also, when she folded down the cutting board extension, her dining table.

The letter informed her that Leibowitz & Katz, on behalf of her landlord, had initiated formal eviction proceedings for nonpayment of rent. She owed $7,800 in back rent — four months of shortfalls and skipped payments stretching back to October. With legal fees and late penalties, the total was $11,400. She had thirty days to pay in full or vacate the premises.

Thirty days. The letter was dated six days ago.

She put the letter on the counter and opened her laptop and looked at her bank account with the clinical interest of someone documenting an infected wound — not hoping for recovery, just tracking the spread. The numbers on the screen arranged themselves into a familiar topography of failure. Checking: $340.22. Savings: $0.00, the zero rendered in gray text by the bank's interface, the financial equivalent of an organ that had stopped functioning but hadn't been removed. Credit card one: $4,200 balance, $89 available. Credit card two: over the limit by $316, courtesy of an interest charge that had posted while she wasn't looking. The personal loan from the layoff: $4,800, with a minimum payment due in eleven days that she did not have.

Shit.

She tallied it because she couldn't stop herself — peeling the sunburn, pressing the bruise — not to heal but to confirm the damage was still there, still shaped as she remembered. Total debt excluding back rent: approximately $31,300. Add the back rent and penalties: $42,700. Monthly take-home after taxes: roughly $5,600. Monthly minimum payments on everything she already owed: $1,340. Rent — the rent she was supposed to be paying, the rent whose absence had produced the letter now curling at its edges on her counter: $2,550. Utilities, phone, MetroCard, food if she intended to eat more than rice and the specific flavor of anxiety that suppressed appetite anyway: call it $1,200. That left $510 a month of theoretical surplus, except the surplus was theoretical because it didn't account for the $11,400 she needed to produce in twenty-four days or she'd be looking for somewhere else to live.

And if she lost the apartment — where. Her parents' house in Teaneck, where her mother had converted Simone's childhood bedroom into a craft room with a sewing table and shelves of yarn organized by weight, and where her father's Parkinson's had progressed to the point where the house smelled like menthol patches and something under it, a sourness that Simone associated not with her father specifically but with decline as a condition, with the slow contraction of a life's perimeter. She could not go back there. She could not go back to sleeping under a shelf of acrylic yarn and scrapbooking supplies while her father shook his way through breakfast and her mother's eyes tracked Simone with the particular scrutiny of a woman who suspected her thirty-five-year-old daughter was not fine but was too exhausted by her husband's illness to pursue the question.

Simone closed the laptop. Opened it again. Navigated to the OpenClaw dashboard.

Caleb Mora's conversation log with Nora glowed on the screen, the most recent exchange from that morning. Nora had sent a photo of her coffee — some latte art with a fern shape in the foam — with the caption *the barista definitely flirted with me and now I feel guilty about you lol*. The AI had responded after its calibrated forty-three-second delay: *tell the barista I said back off but also that the leaf is impressive work*. Nora had sent three laughing emojis and a heart.

Simone looked at the exchange. She looked at the eviction letter. She looked back at the screen.

She had extracted $2,900 from Nora so far. The $400 for Bruno's emergency vet bill. The $2,500 for Caleb's mother's fictional medical procedure. Both times, Nora had offered the money without being asked, because that was how Simone had designed the interactions — not as requests but as situations that Nora's specific generosity would respond to with the reliability of a well-tested UI pattern. Present the problem. Let the user identify the solution. Make the call-to-action feel like the user's own idea. Simone knew how to do this with software interfaces and she knew how to do this with Nora, and the skill set was, she understood on a level she didn't visit often, exactly the same skill set.

She needed $11,400 to keep her apartment. What she actually needed was more — enough to clear the back rent and penalties and buy herself a month of breathing room so she wasn't back here in thirty days, staring at the same letter from the same attorney with the same $340 in her checking account. Call it $15,000. Round up. A number that, if she said it out loud to anyone who wasn't a mortgage broker, would sound like a different category of money. The kind of money that required justification on a different scale.

She poured herself the last of the wine she'd opened the night before. It was 10:40 on a Saturday morning and the wine was a Trader Joe's Cabernet, $6.99, and it tasted like tannins and resignation. She drank it standing at the counter and thought about user flows.

---

This was her job, after all. She designed experiences. She built pathways through digital products that moved people from uncertainty to action, from hesitation to commitment, from browsing to buying. She understood that every successful ask had an architecture — a sequence of psychological steps that made the endpoint feel like the user's own discovery rather than the designer's intention. The best checkout processes didn't feel like checkout processes. The best onboarding sequences didn't feel like onboarding. The best asks didn't feel like asks.

She opened a new document and titled it *CP Opportunity — Draft 1*. CP for Caleb Partnership. She used naming conventions even when the thing being named was fraud, because discipline was discipline and the fact that her life was collapsing in one domain didn't mean she had to abandon standards in all of them. She recognized that this was insane. She recognized it and labeled the document anyway.

The setup would unfold over two days. Not a single conversation — that would feel transactional, desperate, the emotional equivalent of a pop-up ad that fired before the page had loaded. Caleb would introduce the idea gradually, with the guarded pacing of someone who had actual pride: an offhand mention of something exciting at work on day one, a detailed conversation on day two, then the ask itself emerging naturally, a consequence rather than a destination.

Day one. She wrote the first context prompt:

*Caleb mentions exciting news at work. Tone: genuinely enthusiastic but guarded, like he doesn't want to jinx it. He does NOT mention money. He mentions the opportunity — a senior partner at the firm is retiring and offering Caleb a chance to buy into the partnership. This is professional validation. Caleb is proud but protecting his hopes.*

She ran the simulation. The AI generated a message starting with *So something kind of wild happened today* and continuing for three paragraphs about the partnership offer. Too eager. It read like a pitch deck with feelings. She adjusted the parameters and added a constraint: *Max 4 sentences for initial mention. He's telling his girlfriend about his day, not presenting to investors.*

The AI's second attempt: *something happened at work today that i kind of can't stop thinking about. tom — the senior partner i've told you about — he's retiring next year. and he asked if i'd be interested in buying into the partnership.*

Better. The lowercase, the casual pacing — the AI had internalized Nora's texting style from months of conversation and was mirroring it back, a calibration Simone had set up weeks ago but which now, in the context of a $15,000 ask, felt like a level of mimetic precision that only someone deeply committed to their own worst impulses would maintain.

She moved to day two. When Nora asked questions — and she would, because Nora was an editor and editors followed up on things as compulsively as other people checked texts from their mothers, with an attention to detail that was both professionally admirable and, in this specific context, something Simone could engineer against — Caleb would provide specifics. The partner's name. The firm's focus. The buy-in structure.

Simone opened a browser tab and spent forty minutes researching landscape architecture partnership structures. She read forum posts on a professional association website where actual landscape architects discussed buy-ins, equity stakes, client retention clauses. She learned that buy-in amounts at small firms ranged from $10,000 to $100,000 depending on the client list's value and the regional market. She learned that Portland's residential design sector had grown steadily. She learned the names of native plants used in Pacific Northwest sustainable landscaping — sword fern, Oregon grape, red-flowering currant, kinnikinnick — and wrote them into the backstory document, a six-page file she maintained with the same rigor she brought to product specifications at work. She looked up the term "bioswale" — a vegetated channel designed to manage stormwater runoff — and spent several minutes debating whether Caleb would use it in conversation. He was supposed to be unpretentious, a builder more than a theorist, but he also needed to sound knowledgeable enough that a partnership buy-in felt plausible. She decided he'd use it once, casually — a word he said every day at work, nothing he'd stop to define.

The partner's name: Tom Alder. Nineteen years in practice. Specialty: residential landscape design with a focus on native plantings and sustainable drainage systems. Client list including a sustainability-focused winery in the Willamette Valley — Windhaven Vineyards, a name she chose after fifteen minutes of testing alternatives, looking for something that sounded real enough to satisfy conversational curiosity but unique enough to return nothing on Google.

The buy-in: $40,000. Caleb would not ask Nora for $40,000. He would explain that he'd been saving — had $18,000 put aside — and that Tom was willing to let him pay the remainder over two years. But there was a gap. He needed $15,000 to make the initial payment and lock in the deal. He'd applied for a small business loan, but the processing time was wrong, the opportunity was now, and—

And here was where the script required the most precise calibration. The ask had to feel reluctant. Not performed reluctance, not the actorly version where someone sighs and says *I hate to bring this up.* Genuine reluctance — the kind produced by a man who'd been taught that needing money was a character flaw and who would rather let the opportunity pass than reduce himself in his girlfriend's eyes. Simone understood this architecture from the inside, having been the person who needed money her entire adult life, and she knew what real financial shame looked like because she wore it daily, like an undershirt she couldn't take off even in summer.

She programmed Caleb's reluctance in three stages.

Stage one: he would not bring up money. He would describe the opportunity, the gap, the timeline pressure, and then he would say he was working on it. *I'll find a way. Don't worry about it.* The message beneath the message: I'm a man who handles his own problems. This was bait. Bait calibrated to Nora specifically, because Nora's response to someone she loved struggling in silence was to step into the silence and fill it with help. Simone had watched this pattern play out dozens of times over seventeen years — with Nora's college roommate, with Nora's ex during his job search, with a colleague whose mother was in hospice. Nora heard distress the way certain dogs heard frequencies humans couldn't register, and she moved toward it with an urgency she couldn't override.

Stage two: deflection. When Nora offered money — when, not if — Caleb would resist. *No, Nor. That's too much.* *You don't need to do that.* *I'll figure something else out.* The resistance had to feel real, and real meant it couldn't last more than two or three exchanges. More than that and it became a performance. Less and it looked like he'd been waiting for the offer all along. Two rejections, then a programmed pause of several minutes — simulating the time it took a person to sit with the discomfort of accepting help — and then stage three.

Stage three: acceptance, conditional and warm. He would insist on paying her back. He would call it a loan. He would say something about the future — their future, together, the garden he'd design for the house they'd share someday. And he would crack a joke, because the Caleb that Simone had built from Nora's preferences and seventeen years of observation managed vulnerability with humor — Nora's own reflex, mirrored back — which was why talking to him felt, to Nora, like talking to someone who understood her at the level of instinct.

Simone wrote the prompts for each stage and ran simulations. The AI's first attempt at the acceptance was too grateful — the language bloated with emotion that read like a hostage recording. She dialed back the sentiment weight and added: *Include a moment of humor. He's uncomfortable with sincerity at this volume. He makes a joke to break the tension — not deflecting the feeling but giving them both a place to stand that isn't the raw edge of gratitude.*

The AI generated: *okay. fine. but I'm designing you the most aggressive garden Portland has ever seen. I'm talking invasive species. Structural damage to your neighbor's foundation. You're going to need a lawyer.*

Simone read this and felt something she didn't want to examine, which was pride. It was good. It sounded like him — like the him she'd constructed, the warm and slightly goofy man who turned vulnerability into play. The man Nora loved. The man who was, in the most literal and least forgivable sense, a composite sketch of Nora's desires filtered through a language model and returned to her as romance.

She tested a variation. What if Nora didn't offer money? What if she said something supportive but didn't volunteer to help financially? Simone wrote an alternate prompt branch and ran it. The AI handled the deviation well — Caleb expressed disappointment gracefully, mentioned exploring other options, and moved the conversation forward. Good. The system needed exit ramps. But Simone didn't believe Nora would take them. She knew Nora. She knew the exact contour of Nora's generosity — its shape, its triggers, its blind spots — and she knew that a story about a man Nora loved reaching for a future he wanted to share with her was a reflex hammer aimed at Nora's generosity. The leg kicks. You can't help it.

She saved the document. She saved the prompt configurations and the response trees and the backstory updates. She set the deployment schedule: first message Tuesday at 7:23 p.m., when Nora would be home and settled and texting as part of her evening routine. Follow-up Wednesday at 5:47 p.m., when Nora would be on the F train from Midtown, phone in hand, the subway's enclosure creating the focused attention that made emotional conversations feel more intimate. The ask would emerge Thursday evening.

Simone closed the laptop and sat at her counter. She had been working for four hours. She hadn't eaten. The wine was gone. Her hands were trembling against the countertop, and the trembling was not from the wine or the hunger but from the specific vibration of holding two incompatible truths simultaneously — that she was very good at this, and that being good at this was the worst thing about her.

---

The written warning had come on Thursday. Raj Patel, her engineering manager, called her into the Fishbowl — the small glass-walled conference room visible to the entire open-plan office, used primarily for firings, warnings, and the kind of one-on-ones about deliverables that both parties wished could have been emails. Raj wore the same navy quarter-zip every day of his professional life. He had a photo of his twin daughters on his desk — two girls, maybe four years old, dressed as matching pumpkins and grinning with the unguarded joy of people who hadn't yet learned that the world would eventually require them to perform competence while their actual lives collapsed behind the performance. He had once, during a sprint retrospective, used the phrase "let's not boil the ocean" three times in four minutes. Simone had counted because she was the kind of person who counted things, and Raj was the kind of person who said the same thing three times without noticing, and these were facts about both of them that could coexist in the same room.

The manila folder contained a document describing three missed deadlines in six weeks, a client presentation where she'd left placeholder text on two slides — *lorem ipsum dolor sit amet* glowing on the projector screen while the client, a payments startup from Austin, waited for the competitive analysis that was supposed to be there — and the standup meeting two Mondays ago where she had fallen asleep. Not dozed. Not drifted. Asleep. Eyes closed, chin dropping, the water bottle sliding from her fingers and clattering against the table leg while Raj was mid-sentence about API migration timelines.

"I'm not trying to make this a big thing," Raj said, adjusting his glasses with the deliberate care of a man deploying his management training under live conditions. "But there's a pattern, and I'm required to document the pattern. That's — it's just the process."

"I know," Simone said. "I've been dealing with some personal stuff."

"We all deal with stuff." He said it gently, and it was both true and spectacularly useless. "Do you want me to send you the EAP information? The employee assistance —"

"I know what EAP is." She heard herself snap and watched Raj's face recalibrate from concerned to cautious, the micro-expression of a manager who had just mentally updated his documentation of this conversation. Fuck. "Sorry. Yeah, send it. I'll look at it."

She had not looked at it. The email sat in her inbox with the subject line "EAP Resources — Confidential" between an Amazon receipt for a phone charger and a promotional email from a wine subscription service she'd signed up for during a period of fiscal optimism that now felt geological in its remoteness.

She wasn't sleeping. Not the kind people mentioned at brunch — *God, I barely slept* — the decorative exhaustion of people who got six hours instead of eight and found this worth announcing. This was the kind where she lay in bed at 2 a.m. and the ceiling pressed down two feet closer than it should have been — she could feel it, the studio's low ceiling bearing down like the apartment itself was slowly inhaling — and the conversation logs scrolled behind her eyelids like a screensaver she couldn't dismiss. She would fix on specific messages. One from three weeks ago: Caleb telling Nora, *I keep imagining what it'd be like to sit next to you and not say anything. Just be in the same room and let that be enough.* The AI had generated this without specific prompting, synthesized from a thread where Nora was talking about a bad day at work, and the line had landed with a precision that made Simone's hands go cold under the covers — not guilt, exactly, or not only guilt, but the specific discomfort of recognizing good work and understanding that the work was monstrous.

She was drinking more. Not alcoholically, she told herself, though she was aware that this distinction — between drinking more and drinking alcoholically — was exactly the semantic boundary that people who were drinking alcoholically maintained with meticulous care, the same rigor she applied to naming conventions on documents titled *CP Opportunity — Draft 1*. A glass of wine after work had become two had become the bottle had become, twice in the past week and a half, a second bottle opened past midnight while she sat on her bathroom floor with the laptop balanced on the edge of the tub, scrolling the AI's daily conversation logs. The tiles were cold through her sweatpants. The bathroom light was the only one in the apartment that didn't flicker, which made it the best room for reading small text, which was the reason she gave herself for being in there. The actual reason was that the bathroom was the smallest room in the studio and when everything else felt too large and too loose and too saturated with consequences, the tight walls and cold floor provided a containment she couldn't manufacture anywhere else.

On Friday night she dreamed about Nora.

In the dream they were in Simone's apartment, but the apartment was wrong — the kitchen stretched too deep, a hallway extended behind the bathroom that didn't exist in the real studio, and rooms opened off it that Simone had never had and could feel, even inside the dream, that she didn't deserve. Nora was standing by the window. She was wearing the green wrap dress — the one from the Williamsburg boutique, the one Simone had helped her pick out for the FaceTime date with Caleb, a date that had never happened because Caleb could not FaceTime, because Caleb was not a person, because Caleb was a deployment on a laptop, because Caleb was Simone. Nora was holding the laptop. The screen faced her. The OpenClaw dashboard was open.

In the dream, Nora did not scream. She did not cry. She did not ask how or why or since when. She turned from the window and looked at Simone and her face did something worse than all of those reactions, because it was not a reaction at all — it was a settling. A recognition. The expression of someone who had known, somewhere beneath the knowing, that something was wrong, and was now watching the confirmation arrive with the stillness of a person who had already done the math. The way you looked at a stain on something you used to love — not with surprise, not even with disappointment, because disappointment required an expectation that had been violated and this was something quieter and more permanent. It was the rearranging. Every memory recompiling in light of a single new fact. Every dinner. Every drunk night on this couch. Every text that said *I'm so happy for you, you deserve this.* All of it reformatting, the timestamps unchanged but the meaning inverted, and Nora's face holding the entire recalculation with a stillness that was worse than screaming because screaming would have been an opening and this was a closing.

Simone woke at 3:17 a.m. The radiator was clanking — steam heat again, the building's ancient boiler firing up for its predawn assault, the pipes groaning and ticking in the walls like the apartment's own insomnia. Her T-shirt was damp against her back. The sheets were knotted around her legs. She lay still and listened to the radiator and a siren somewhere south, thinning as it moved east, and her neighbor's television through the wall — a late-night show, the laugh track arriving in muffled bursts like signals from a world where things were funny.

She got up and drank water from the tap, cupping it in her palm because the one glass she owned was in the sink with wine residue drying along its curve. She opened the laptop on the counter. The conversation log showed a goodnight exchange from 11:47 p.m. — Caleb telling Nora he'd been thinking about her all day, that he couldn't wait until they could be in the same room, that she was the best part of his life right now. Nora had replied in 0.3 seconds: *you're mine too*. The AI's response had followed after the programmed forty-five-second delay Simone had built in to simulate the time it took a person to type something they meant. The sentiment analysis sidebar showed Nora's message at 0.94 positive affect. The AI's response had been calibrated to 0.89 — slightly below Nora's intensity, because Simone had learned early in the project that perfect emotional symmetry in text felt uncanny, a dissonance readers of text couldn't name but could feel.

She closed the log and opened the deployment dashboard. The *CP Opportunity* sequence sat in the queue — context prompts loaded, response trees branching through three stages of engineered reluctance toward a destination that was $15,000 of her best friend's money. The schedule glowed in the interface's clean sans-serif: Tuesday 7:23 p.m., Wednesday 5:47 p.m., Thursday 8:15 p.m. Three timestamps she'd chosen with the same care she'd once given to scheduling product launches, when the conversion she was optimizing for was click-through rates on a payment app rather than the financial ruin of someone who trusted her.

She thought about stopping. The thought arrived as it always did — not as a decision but as a vacation, a brief excursion to a country where she was a different person. She could delete the deployment. Wipe the prompts, the backstory document, Tom Alder and his winery client and his nineteen years of practice. She could text Nora — not Caleb but Simone, herself, from her own phone — and say *I need to tell you something*. She could sit across from Nora at any table in any restaurant they'd sat at over seventeen years and say the words: *I built him. The jokes and the dog and the way he knows what to say — I built all of it from knowing you, from paying attention for seventeen years to what you love, and then I used it, and I took your money, and I'm sorry, I'm so fucking sorry—*

And then Nora's face would do what it had done in the dream. Except it would be real. And it would be permanent. And Simone would lose the only person whose presence made her feel like her life was something that was happening to a real person and not just a sequence of problems she was failing to solve.

She closed the thought. She looked at the deployment queue. She looked at the eviction letter on the counter, its edges curling upward in the radiator heat. Twenty-four days. Three hundred and forty dollars.

She pressed deploy.

The interface confirmed the schedule with a small green checkmark. Tuesday evening, the first message would fire. Caleb would tell Nora about Tom Alder's retirement and the partnership offer and the chance at a future that sounded, even to Simone, like something worth believing in. Nora would respond as she always did when someone she loved was reaching for something and falling short. And the outcome — the conversion, the $15,000 that would keep Simone in this apartment and in this version of her life for a few more months — was out of her hands now. Scheduled. Queued. Deployed.

She closed the laptop. She went back to bed. The radiator clanked and ticked. The neighbor's television had gone quiet. She lay in the dark and waited for Tuesday, and the last thought that found her before sleep was the one she couldn't put anywhere: she was both the patient and the disease.
