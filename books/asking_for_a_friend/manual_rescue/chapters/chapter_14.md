# Chapter 14

The number had been in her head for eleven hours.

Fifteen thousand dollars. Caleb had arrived at it the way he arrived at everything — gradually, conversationally, as if the two-day exchange about the landscape partnership had led them both there together rather than toward a destination he'd had in mind from the start. He was mortified. He'd said so twice. He'd said she should absolutely say no, that he'd figure something else out, that the last thing he wanted was for this to change anything between them.

And Nora, lying awake at two in the morning with the blue light of her phone gone dark and the traffic sounds of Atlantic Avenue pushing through her thin bedroom wall, had noticed — with the attention of a woman who read for a living, who spent her days parsing the distance between what a sentence said and what it meant — that the not-asking was a kind of asking. That creating the conditions for someone to offer was not the same as asking but was also not the same as not asking. That the careful, mortified performance of reluctance was either genuine vulnerability or the most effective possible strategy for extracting money from a person who wanted to be generous.

She didn't know which one it was. She didn't know which one she wanted it to be.

---

The publishing house was overheated. It always was in March — the building's HVAC had two settings, aggressive winter and indifferent summer, and the transition between them involved three weeks of radiators clanking at full power while the temperature outside climbed into the mid-forties. Nora took off her cardigan at her desk and opened the manuscript she was line-editing — page 204, a scene she'd been putting off because the author's dialogue went flat whenever two characters agreed with each other, and she couldn't figure out how to tell him without telling him his characters only worked in opposition.

She read the same paragraph three times without retaining a word. On the fourth try she marked a comma splice she'd been missing for weeks, and the small satisfaction of catching it lasted about four seconds before the number reasserted itself. Fifteen thousand. She could feel it sitting behind everything she looked at, the way you could feel a headache sitting behind your eyes even when you weren't actively in pain — present, patient, waiting to be attended to.

At lunch, she told Margo.

Margo was eating the salad she ate every weekday — spinach, chickpeas, whatever vegetables were closest to dying in her refrigerator, dressed with the bottle of tahini she kept in the office kitchen and labeled in permanent marker because someone had stolen the first one. She'd told Nora once that the salad was not a diet, it was a system: she didn't have to think about lunch, which freed up a decision for something else. She hadn't identified the something else yet, but the freed-up decision was available. She was wearing her father's watch — an old Seiko with a brushed-silver face and a leather band too big for her wrist, the one he'd given her in January when he went in for the valve replacement, as if he were distributing his effects in advance — and she kept sliding it up and down her forearm without seeming to notice.

"Fifteen thousand dollars," Margo said.

"I know how it sounds."

"Fifteen thousand dollars to a man you've never met in person."

"I've met him. We've been — "

"You've never been in the same room as him, Nora. You've never heard his voice. You've never seen him move. Those are three separate things I just said and each one should be enough." She set her fork down. Margo setting down her fork was a particular signal, like a courtroom gavel but quieter. She was done treating the conversation as casual. "This is a scam."

The word landed in the space between them. Nora felt her face react before she could organize the reaction — not anger, not hurt, something closer to the flinch when someone turns on the lights in a dark room and everything is briefly too sharp.

"You don't know that."

"I don't have to know it with certainty to know it's the most likely explanation. Camera's broken. Father's stroke at the exact moment you book a flight. Mother's medical bills. And now a business investment?" Margo leaned forward, the Seiko sliding down her wrist. "I'm not being cruel. I'm being your friend who is telling you that these are literally the examples they use. This is textbook."

"He didn't ask me for the money. He told me about the opportunity and I offered."

"Because he told you about it in a way that made you want to offer. That's how it works. They don't ask. They construct the situation so you volunteer, so it feels like your generosity instead of their plan."

Nora picked at the edge of her desk where the laminate was curling. She could feel the radiator pushing dry heat into the room, the discomfort of being overheated and having a conversation she didn't want to have, both at once, neither of them letting up.

"Margo. I know what romance scams look like. I edit manuscripts about worse things than this."

"And how many of those manuscripts are about smart women who thought it couldn't happen to them? Because if it's more than zero — "

"That's not — "

"I'm not saying you're stupid. I would never say that. I'm saying that nobody thinks they're the person it happens to. That's the design. That's what makes it work — it doesn't feel like it's happening until after it's happened." Margo picked up her fork again, which was its own signal: she'd said what she was going to say and she was not going to push further, because Margo had the restraint of a woman who'd learned through her own divorce that telling someone what you see and making them see what you see are different projects entirely, and only one of them is yours to do. "Have you Googled him?"

"I'm going to."

"You haven't Googled him."

"I've looked at his — "

"Have you reverse-image searched his photos?"

Nora had not reverse-image searched his photos. She had thought about it, more than once, in the way you circled something you were afraid to do — the thought arriving fully formed at two in the morning, and then being set aside by daylight because daylight made it seem paranoid, because searching was something a suspicious person did and she was not a suspicious person, because trust was a choice she made because trust was how she moved through the world, except when that was also the reason the world could move through her.

"I'll look into it," she said, which meant nothing, and they both knew it meant nothing.

---

She called her mother from the F train, standing near the doors with her bag between her feet and her phone pressed to her ear, the tunnel swallowing every third word.

Diane picked up on the second ring, which meant she'd been in the kitchen, where the phone lived on the counter plugged into its charger, because Diane treated her cell phone like a landline and answered it standing up. She'd been retired from teaching for four years but her days were still organized around the rhythms of a school schedule — up early, lunch at noon, the evening structured around cooking and reading and the half-hour of evening news she watched not because she trusted it but because the habit had outlasted her trust.

"Hi, sweetheart. How was your day?"

"It was fine. Long. Mom, can I ask you something?"

"You're using your asking-me-something voice, which is different from your telling-me-something voice, and both of them are different from your everything's-fine voice, and I can hear all three." The sound of a pot being moved. Water running. "Go ahead."

Nora told her. She didn't use the word Margo had used. She described the partnership, the opportunity, the number — tried to present it the way she would present a manuscript in an editorial meeting, here are the merits, here are the concerns, let the material speak for itself. But her voice did something she couldn't control on the word *fifteen*, a slight thinning, and she knew Diane heard it because Diane had been hearing the micro-variations in her daughter's voice for thirty-four years, the way a retired English teacher heard syntax — structurally, attentively, with an ear for what was being built and what was being hidden.

Her mother was quiet for long enough that Nora heard the kitchen clock, the one that had been two minutes fast for as long as she could remember, the one Diane had never corrected because she said two minutes early was better than on time and on time was better than late. She was also, Nora realized, hearing the absence of cooking sounds. The pot had stopped. The water had stopped. When Diane stopped cooking, she was past worried and into frightened.

"Nora. Have you met this man?"

"You know I haven't. Not in person."

"I know you've told me that, and I've been — I've been trying not to say anything, because you're an adult and you're smart and you know what you're doing. I believe that, I do. But you're talking about fifteen thousand dollars, and you've never sat across a table from him. You've never watched him eat dinner or lose his temper or be tired. You don't know what he looks like when he's not choosing what you see." She paused, and the clock ticked. "And I'm scared."

She didn't say of what. She didn't need to. Diane's fear was not the same species as Margo's alarm — Margo was afraid of a mechanism, a scam, a thing with a pattern and examples and a name. Diane was afraid of something older: her daughter's happiness being contingent on something that couldn't be touched, and this fear was not about the internet or dating apps or the architecture of online fraud, because Diane did not think in those terms. It was the fear of a mother who had been watching her specific daughter perform "fine" for thirty-four years and who could hear the performance even when the performance was expert.

"He didn't ask me for the money, Mom. He told me about the opportunity and I — "

"Sweetheart. That's the same thing."

Nora pressed her forehead against the train window. The glass was cool and slightly greasy and the tunnel walls slid past in the dark. She could feel the vibration of the rails through the floor and through her shoes and through the bones of her feet, and her mother's worry was another vibration, one she could never entirely tune out because it came in on a frequency that was calibrated specifically to her.

"I know," she said. "I know."

---

That night, she sat on her bed with her laptop open and did the thing she had been not doing for five months.

She typed his name into Google.

*Caleb Mora landscape architect Portland*

The apartment was quiet except for the HVAC vent above her closet, which made a sound like someone gently and repeatedly clearing their throat, and the old water pipes conducting noise from the apartment upstairs — a faucet running, the muffled percussion of someone walking on hardwood in shoes they hadn't bothered to take off. Her laptop screen cast the bluish-white she associated now with Caleb, with the late-night conversations and the intimate messages and the entire architecture of a relationship that existed in the glow of devices.

She'd searched for him before. Months ago, when the looking had been playful rather than desperate — the ordinary curiosity of someone googling a new romantic interest, not the forensic dread of someone looking for evidence. She'd found nothing alarming and nothing confirming, which at the time had seemed like the answer: he was a normal person who didn't curate his online presence, and the absence of a digital footprint was proof of his being genuine rather than proof of his being invented.

Now the same absence felt different. She scrolled through results. A Caleb Mora in Tampa who sold commercial insurance and whose LinkedIn photo showed a man in his fifties with a goatee. A Caleb Mora who'd co-authored a paper on soil mechanics at Oregon State in 2011, which was adjacent enough to landscape architecture to tease but not close enough to confirm. Three LinkedIn profiles for different Caleb Moras, none in Portland, none landscape architects. She tried "Mora Landscape Design Portland" and got a company called Morales Landscaping that did residential lawn care and had a four-star Google rating and a phone number with a 503 area code that she stared at for thirty seconds before closing the tab.

She went to the American Society of Landscape Architects website and searched the member directory. No Caleb Mora. She searched the Oregon Landscape Contractor Board's license lookup. No results. None of this proved anything — she knew that, she repeated it to herself like a revision note in the margin of her own thinking. Not every professional joined their industry association. Not every landscape architect held a contractor's license. The distinction between landscape design and landscape contracting was real, and Caleb had described himself as a designer, which in Oregon didn't require state licensure. She'd looked this up. She'd looked it up and been relieved and had not asked herself why she was relieved rather than simply informed.

But the absences accumulated. Each empty search bar was a weight placed on a scale she was trying to keep level, and the scale was no longer level. It listed slightly, almost imperceptibly, toward something she did not want to look at directly.

She opened Google Images and found the reverse-image search function. The interface was simple — four clicks, thirty seconds — and its simplicity felt like an indictment. This was a half-minute process she had chosen not to do for five months. The choice had been easy to justify in the daylight of loving him: checking was suspicious, and suspicion was corrosive, and she had decided to trust him because trust was how she wanted to live. Now, at eleven at night in her Brooklyn bedroom with Margo's voice and her mother's fear braided together in her head, the choice not to check looked less like trust and more like collaboration in her own blindness.

She dragged Caleb's profile photo — the one of him laughing at something off-camera, a dog just visible at the edge of the frame — into the search box.

*No results found.*

She tried the hiking photo, his back half-turned against a trailhead she'd always imagined was somewhere in the Columbia River Gorge. The cooking photo, sleeves pushed up, a cutting board blurred with motion. The one where he sat on a porch step, squinting into afternoon sun, looking like a person you'd want to sit next to and learn everything about.

No results for any of them.

This was ambiguous and she knew it was ambiguous. The photos could be originals — real pictures of a real person, never posted publicly, taken by someone who knew him. Google's reverse-image search didn't index private accounts. Absence of results was not evidence of fabrication. She knew this the way she knew grammar rules — formally, completely, in a way that didn't change the feeling. She also knew that she was building the case for ambiguity with the care of a defense attorney, because ambiguity was the only position that let her keep going.

She picked up her phone and texted Caleb.

*Hey, random question — do you have LinkedIn? My mom was asking about your work and I realized I don't know how to show her anything about the firm*

The lie was small and functional and it was the first strategic sentence she had ever constructed for him — a test disguised as idle curiosity. She already suspected the answer. The question wasn't about information. It was about watching how the answer arrived, whether it wobbled.

The typing indicator appeared. Disappeared. Appeared.

*Ha, yeah — I deleted mine a while back. Something about writing a professional summary of yourself in third person felt deeply wrong. Tim keeps saying we should get back on there but neither of us has done it*

She tried again.

*What about a website for the firm? I'd love to see some of your projects*

*Yeah, we've been meaning to get one together. It's technically "under construction" but it's been under construction for like a year lol. Tim handles the business side and I handle the design side and neither of us handles the website*

Every answer was plausible. She read them again and every answer was still plausible. A man in his early thirties who didn't use LinkedIn, whose two-person firm hadn't built a website — she could name three people in her own life who matched that description. Her dentist didn't have LinkedIn. The debut novelist she'd been trying to acquire had no web presence at all, and he was real enough to have eaten soup across from her at the Italian place on 46th Street last month.

But the answers also sounded like answers. They had the crafted quality of a well-written character's dialogue — the self-deprecating humor, the ready detail about Tim, the acknowledgment that made the gap seem charming rather than suspicious. She was reading for subtext now and she couldn't stop reading for subtext, and reading for it was changing everything without resolving anything, because the same text could be read two ways and both readings were supported and neither could be falsified.

She closed her laptop. The apartment was dark except for the kitchen light she'd left on, a stripe of yellow across the hallway floor. She sat on her bed and tried to remember what this had felt like before — the uncomplicated pleasure of his messages arriving, the warmth of being known, the sensation of being found by someone who seemed to have been looking for exactly her. It had felt like coming home. She wanted to go back there. She was not sure the road still went in that direction.

---

On Saturday she met Simone at the coffee shop on Smith Street, the one that was too far from both of them, chosen four years ago because it was equidistant and the barista remembered their orders and the afternoon light through the west-facing windows was warm. Habit, once established, was its own geography.

Simone was already there when Nora arrived, at the window table, hands wrapped around a cortado, phone face-down on the wood. She was wearing a scarf Nora hadn't seen before — a dark rust-colored silk, knotted in a way that looked effortless and that Nora, who had known Simone for seventeen years, understood was the product of three attempts in a mirror. Simone had always had a talent for making effort look like nature. It was what made her good at design, this ability to construct ease, and it was one of the things Nora genuinely admired about her — the discipline of the performance, the commitment to making the seams invisible.

When Nora sat down, Simone reached across the table and squeezed her hand. "Tell me."

Nora told her everything. The number. The Google results. The empty image searches. Margo's alarm. Her mother's fear. She told Simone the way she'd been telling Simone things for seventeen years — without editing, without strategy, trusting that the person across the table would hold the information carefully.

"So what do you want to do?" Simone asked.

"I don't know. That's literally why I'm here."

"Okay. But what does your gut say? Not your head, not Margo's head, not your mom's head. What does Nora say?"

"My gut says I love him. My gut also says I've never seen his office or met his friends or watched him do anything, and he wants fifteen thousand dollars."

"He didn't ask you, though. You said that — he told you about the opportunity and you're the one thinking about helping."

"That's what I said to Margo. She said that's how it works. They create the conditions and you offer, so you feel generous instead of — "

"I hear that. And I hear your mom, and they both love you and they're being careful for you. But, Nor — " Simone leaned forward, and her face had a quality it got when she was about to say something considered — not improvised, not reactive, but held up to the light first. "They don't know him. They haven't talked to him every day for five months. They haven't heard what he sounds like at three in the morning when he can't sleep and he texts you instead of staring at the ceiling alone. They're looking at this from outside, and from outside, sure, it looks like red flags, because that's what caution always looks like from outside. But you're not outside."

"What if they're right, though?"

"What if they're wrong? What if he's a man with a real opportunity who's terrified to bring it up because he knows exactly how it looks, and the woman he loves is going to let her friends talk her out of the best thing she's found in years?"

"I don't — "

"If you believe in him," Simone said, "believe in him."

It was exactly the right thing to say. It was so precisely what Nora needed — not reassurance about Caleb's reality, which would have sounded hollow, but permission to trust her own experience, which was the thing she actually wanted — that she felt it as warmth, as friendship, as the uncomplicated relief of hearing her own best instincts echoed back by the person who knew her better than anyone.

"You really think it's okay?"

"I think you know him better than anyone else does, and your judgment is worth trusting." Simone took a sip of her cortado and set it down. "But don't send it if something feels wrong. Seriously. If there's a part of you saying no, listen to that part."

This too was perfect — the note of caution that made the encouragement more credible. Nora didn't think of it that way. She heard her best friend being thoughtful and fair, which was what she'd come to hear, because Simone was the person she trusted to hold both sides of a question and tell her the truth.

They talked about other things. Simone asked about the debut novel. Nora asked about work, and Simone said it was fine, her voice closing slightly on the word the way it always did when work was not fine, and Nora made a mental note to circle back later, when her own crisis wasn't occupying all the air. They finished their coffees. They hugged on the sidewalk and Simone held on a beat longer than usual, which felt like tenderness. March had turned the city damp and uncertain — not cold enough for a real coat, not warm enough for just a sweater — and the sky was a flat grey-white that wouldn't commit to anything.

---

She went home and sat on her couch and opened Venmo.

The apartment was still. Saturday afternoon, the held-breath quiet of a building where everyone was either out or asleep. She could hear the upstairs neighbor's television through the ceiling, tuned to something with a laugh track, the laughter arriving in muffled bursts like transmissions from a simpler life.

She typed Caleb's name into the payment field. She typed 15,000 into the amount box.

She looked at it.

Her savings account held forty-two thousand eight hundred dollars — she'd checked this morning, the way she'd been checking for three days, the way you checked on something you might be about to lose. The money was the accumulation of years of being careful: her salary at the publishing house was eighty-five thousand before taxes, and after rent and utilities and the MetroCard and groceries and the occasional dinner and the visits to Long Island and the birthday presents and the LRB subscription and all the other small expenditures that constituted a life in Brooklyn at thirty-four, she saved between seven and nine hundred dollars a month. She'd been saving since she was twenty-six. The forty-two thousand eight hundred dollars was not wealth. It was the evidence of sustained attention, of a woman who understood that money was not about what you could buy but about what you could survive.

Fifteen thousand was more than a third of it. Sent to a man she had never touched, through an app, for a business opportunity she could not verify, on the basis of five months of text messages and one friend's encouragement and something in her chest she was not willing to call love in case it turned out to be something else and not willing to call anything else in case it turned out to be love.

She deleted the number.

She sat with the empty field for a long time. She thought about Margo setting down her fork. She thought about her mother's silence — the clock ticking, the three beats of quiet before Diane said the thing about never watching someone be tired. She thought about Simone, her hand warm on Nora's across the coffee-shop table: *if you believe in him, believe in him*. She thought about Caleb, and the thinking was the problem, because when she thought about Caleb she didn't think about credentials or search results — she thought about the conversation they'd had at one in the morning two weeks ago about which Van Morrison album was best and why *Astral Weeks* was the only correct answer, and the way he'd told her about his mother's empanadas, and the night she'd told him about her father and he'd said something so exactly true that she'd put her phone face-down on the pillow and stared at the wall because being understood that precisely felt physical, not just emotional, a kind of tuning fork struck inside her ribs.

She typed 10,000 into the field.

The number was not the result of a calculation. She had not sat down with a risk assessment and concluded that Caleb was sixty-six percent likely to be real. Ten thousand was the number that let her keep going — large enough to mean she believed in him, small enough to mean she wasn't certain. The gap between the ten she was sending and the five she was withholding was the exact width of her doubt: narrow, provisional, something she could live alongside if she didn't look at it too carefully. It was neither a declaration nor a retreat. It was the punctuation of a sentence she didn't know how to finish — not a period, not a question mark, something in between that grammar didn't have a name for.

She pressed send.

*$10,000 sent to Caleb M.*

She set her phone face-down on the couch. She went to the kitchen and opened the refrigerator and stood in front of it looking at the shelves — a half-empty container of Greek yogurt, two limes, leftover Thai she didn't remember ordering — for long enough that the cold raised goosebumps on her bare arms and the motor began to labor against the open door. She was thinking about the word Margo had used. *Scam.* One syllable. No room in it for maybe. She hadn't said it to anyone — not to Margo, who'd said it first, not to her mother, who'd been too frightened to say it at all, not to Simone, who had said the opposite, and the opposite had been exactly what Nora wanted to hear, which was either the sign of a best friend who knew her perfectly or the sign of a person who only listened to the voices that told her what she already wanted.

She closed the refrigerator. She picked up her phone and texted Caleb.

*Sent you 10 instead of 15 — I want to help but things are a little tight right now. I hope that's okay*

Things were not tight. She had the money. She had thirty-two thousand eight hundred dollars that would have been closer to forty-six thousand if she'd never met a man on Hinge five months ago — four hundred, twenty-five hundred, ten thousand, the arithmetic she'd been avoiding. She was lying to him about why she'd sent less, and the lie was small — the kind of social convenience people used every day, a cushion that made the exchange more comfortable for both of them — but it was the first thing she had deliberately withheld from him, and she noticed it the way she noticed a wrong word in a manuscript, a faint misalignment that reorganized the sentences around it.

His response came quickly.

*Nora. You're genuinely incredible. You didn't have to do any of this. I'm going to pay you back, every cent, the second the partnership goes through. I mean that.*

She read the message and felt the specific vertigo of not knowing whether she was reading gratitude or performance. She had lived inside this relationship for five months without asking that question. She had loved him without it, sent money without it, sent a photograph of her body to him without it. Now the question was in the room — sitting in the chair across from her, patient and quiet, impossible to answer — because the evidence pointed in both directions and her heart pointed in one and her head was beginning, slowly, to point in the other, and the space between them was the exact shape of a life she had built on hope and was not yet ready to take apart.

She lay down on her bed as the light outside went grey and flat and listened to the building settle around her — the pipes, the muffled television, a siren working its way west on Atlantic Avenue. She did not text Caleb again. She did not open WhatsApp or Hinge or any of the apps that had, over five months, become the infrastructure of a love she might have built herself, or that might have been built for her, or that might be real. She lay in the early dark with the doubt and the love together in her chest, neither one leaving, neither one winning, and she understood that this was the new shape of things — not knowing, and going on anyway, and the going on not feeling like courage or foolishness but like the only direction left that didn't require her to become someone she wasn't ready to be.

Outside, March did what March does in Brooklyn — couldn't decide, wouldn't commit, hung between what it had been and what it was becoming, the last ice on the sidewalks going grey and soft in the gutters, the season not yet arrived but no longer deniable.
