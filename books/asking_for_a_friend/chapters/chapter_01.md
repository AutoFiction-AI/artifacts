# Chapter 1

The ring was a pear-cut sapphire, and Nora knew this because Lindsay Huang had already described it three times to three different clusters of guests, each telling calibrated to the audience — the gemological details for the couple's jeweler friend, the proposal story for the college girls, and for Nora and Simone, who had walked in twelve minutes ago knowing almost no one, the full emotional arc: how Tyler had hidden the ring in a hollowed-out copy of *Giovanni's Room* because that was the first book Lindsay ever lent him, and how she'd found it while packing for a trip and burst into tears before he could get down on one knee, and how he'd said, "Well, you ruined the whole thing," and then got down on one knee anyway on the bedroom floor with packing cubes scattered around them.

It was a good story. Nora had to admit that. A story with specific details and a beat of comic self-undermining and an ending that made you want to believe in the institution even if your own evidence was mixed. She would have cut the line about the packing cubes being from Away — the brand specificity was distracting — but otherwise it held up under close reading.

"She's told it before," Simone murmured, leaning in so her mouth was near Nora's ear. They were standing by the drinks table in a converted loft in Bushwick — someone's friend's studio, cleared out for the party, the art replaced by string lights and the furniture pushed against the brick walls. The October air coming through the propped-open windows was perfect, which felt like the city showing off for people who were already winning. "That 'ruined the whole thing' line is too polished. She's been workshopping it."

"It's a good line, though."

"Oh, it's a great line. Tyler didn't come up with it. Lindsay wrote that line and put it in his mouth like a ventriloquist. Which is also how their relationship works, from what I can tell."

Nora laughed — a real one, the kind Simone could always produce, arriving before she'd decided whether the observation was fair. Tyler Huang-to-be-Park, whom Nora had met exactly once at a birthday dinner seven months ago, was standing across the room with his arm around Lindsay's waist in a way that looked both possessive and slightly performative, like he'd seen a photograph of a happy couple and was recreating the pose. He was an environmental lawyer, Nora remembered, or maybe an environmental consultant — one of those jobs that sounded more important than it probably was in practice. He had a nice face. Slightly too much jaw. He was wearing a shirt that someone had definitely picked out for him.

"You're being mean," Nora said. "They're happy."

"They're extremely happy. I'm not contesting their happiness. I'm just noting that their happiness has a stage manager." Simone took a sip of the wine they'd both been poured without being asked — a rosé that tasted like it cost twelve dollars and wanted you to think it cost thirty. "Also, did you notice he calls her Linds? Nobody calls a Lindsay 'Linds' unless they've decided they're the kind of couple that has nicknames."

"Simone."

"What? I'm celebrating. This is me celebrating." She raised her glass in the direction of the happy couple. "Mazel tov, Linds."

Nora shook her head and drank the rosé, which was getting warmer in her hand. The loft was crowded in the way Brooklyn parties were always crowded — forty people in a space designed for twenty, everyone slightly too close, the music too loud to ignore and too quiet to dance to. The playlist was somebody's Spotify algorithm: Phoebe Bridgers into Frank Ocean into something acoustic and vaguely Celtic that didn't belong. Nora recognized maybe six faces. Simone recognized fewer but performed like she recognized all of them, because Simone could talk to anyone for twelve minutes and make them feel like they'd been seen.

This was what they did, Nora and Simone. They went places together. Engagement parties, birthday dinners, gallery openings, the occasional wedding where Nora knew the bride and Simone came as her plus-one and ended up charming the groom's father. They'd been doing this for seventeen years — since freshman orientation at NYU, when Simone had sat down next to Nora at a dining hall table and said, "You have the face of someone who's also pretending they already have friends here," and Nora had said, "I actually do already have friends here," and Simone had said, "Great, then you can introduce me to them," and that had been that. The origin story of a friendship, the kind you tell at each other's eventual weddings, except Nora was thirty-four and Simone was thirty-five and neither of them had produced a wedding yet, which was fine, which was genuinely fine, which Nora had explained to her mother so many times that the explanation itself had become a kind of evidence against its own thesis.

She watched Lindsay accept a hug from a woman in a jumpsuit who was already crying. Nora didn't know the woman in the jumpsuit. She didn't need to — already clutching Lindsay, holding her left hand up to the light and gasping at the ring as if she hadn't seen the Instagram post two weeks ago. Nora had seen the Instagram post two weeks ago. The ring looked exactly like the Instagram post. The caption had been *holy shit holy shit holy shit* with a long row of emojis, and Tyler had commented a single red heart, and Nora had double-tapped and felt something she'd spent two weeks not examining.

"You okay?" Simone asked.

"I'm great. Why?"

"You have your manuscript face on."

"My what?"

"The face you make when you're reading a manuscript and you can see where it's going and you wish it were going somewhere else. You're standing at a party looking at an engaged couple with your manuscript face."

"I don't have a manuscript face."

"You absolutely have a manuscript face. I've watched you read in public. Your whole left eyebrow does a thing."

"My left eyebrow does not do a thing."

"Your left eyebrow is doing the thing right now."

Nora touched her eyebrow, which was absurd, and then laughed, because Simone had made her touch her own eyebrow at a party, and Simone was grinning at her in the way that meant she'd been aiming for exactly that — the laugh, the broken tension, the moment redirected away from whatever Nora had been standing in and toward the shared frequency of their friendship instead. This was Simone's talent, or one of them: she could feel the temperature of Nora's mood before Nora had fully taken her own reading, and she could adjust it without making a production of the adjustment. It was a form of care so practiced it was almost invisible, like the way a good editor could fix a sentence without the writer ever noticing it had been broken.

A man Nora didn't know brought them a plate of cheese without being asked. He had a beard and a wedding ring and kept looking back toward a woman across the room who was deep in a conversation that clearly didn't need him.

"You two looked like you needed cheese," he said.

"We always need cheese," Simone said, and he laughed too hard and drifted back toward a cluster of men who were all wearing the same kind of boot.

Simone picked up a cracker. "He was nice."

"He was terrified. He's at a party he didn't choose and he doesn't know a single person here and he brought us cheese because he needed a mission."

"Okay, but the cheese is good." Simone ate the cracker. "The Gouda situation is actually excellent. This is a well-curated cheese plate. I'll give Lindsay that."

"You'll give Lindsay the cheese plate."

"I'll give Lindsay the cheese plate, the loft, and the ring. The ring is beautiful. I'm not a monster."

"But?"

"But I'm not crying about it in a jumpsuit. I'm not going to stand here and pretend that watching someone else get the thing is the same as getting the thing." She said it lightly, but there was something underneath it — a harder surface, briefly exposed, like a rock at low tide. Then she ate another cracker and the surface was covered again. "Anyway. When's the last time you went on a date?"

The question landed sideways, the way Simone's questions sometimes did — arriving in the middle of a different conversation as if it had always been part of this one.

"What?"

"A date. Dinner with a person. Romantic intent. Eye contact across a table. When?"

Nora calculated. The actual answer was eight months ago, a setup from Margo — a guy named Peter who was a literary agent and who had spent forty-five minutes talking about his clients' advances as if money were a personality trait. They'd split the check and she'd walked to the subway feeling like she'd just sat through a pitch meeting. She'd texted Margo: *he's not a person, he's a LinkedIn profile in a blazer*. Margo had texted back: *I know, I'm sorry, I owe you a drink and also better judgment*.

"Recently enough," Nora said.

"That's not an answer."

"It's a perfectly good answer."

"It's a deflection. I know your deflections, Nor. I've been cataloging them since 2007."

"Fine. It's been a while."

"How much of a while?"

"A while while."

Simone looked at her. She had a way of looking at Nora that was equal parts affection and dissection — like she was both hugging Nora and taking her apart at the same time, gently, to see how the pieces fit. "You know you're allowed to want things," Simone said. "You're allowed to want what Lindsay has. You don't have to stand here and be the cool single friend who's above it."

"I'm not above it. I'm beside it. There's a difference."

"There really isn't."

The party shifted around them. Somebody opened another bottle of the rosé. Somebody else changed the music to something with a heavier bass line, and three women Nora didn't know started dancing near the windows. Lindsay was showing her ring to a new group now — the fourth or fifth telling. Tyler had drifted to the kitchen area, where he was opening beers with a wall-mounted opener and talking to another man, his shoulders three inches lower than they'd been during the toast. Nora watched him and thought: he's happy but he's also exhausted, and neither of those things cancels the other out, and probably that's what it looks like from the inside of the thing everyone here is celebrating.

She refilled her wine and talked to a woman named Grace who worked in textile design and had opinions about linen that were surprisingly interesting. She talked to a man named Dev who turned out to be Tyler's college roommate and who had the rare quality of asking follow-up questions. She told Dev she was an editor and he asked what kind and she said literary fiction and he said, "So you're the person who decides if a book is good," and she said, "I'm the person who decides if a book is good enough to fix," and he laughed and she felt the small pleasure of having said a true thing in a way that also functioned as a joke.

Simone appeared at her elbow. "We should go."

"We just got here."

"We got here an hour and twenty minutes ago. We've reached the part of the evening where the music gets louder and the conversations get worse and someone's going to suggest a drinking game."

"You don't know that."

"I absolutely know that. Tyler's roommate — the one you were just flirting with —"

"I wasn't flirting."

"— he's going to suggest Never Have I Ever within the next fifteen minutes. He has that energy. He looks like a man who peaks at Never Have I Ever."

Nora looked across the room at Dev, who was, in fact, now rummaging through Lindsay's cabinets with the purposeful energy of someone looking for shot glasses.

"Oh my god," Nora said.

"I'm a prophet. Let's get out of here before someone asks if I've ever kissed a girl and I have to decide whether to perform bisexuality for a room full of fucking strangers."

They said goodbye to Lindsay, who hugged them both and said, "Thank you so much for coming, it means so much," with the glazed warmth of someone who had been receiving congratulations for ninety minutes and was running on fumes and champagne. Nora said congratulations and meant it. Lindsay's hand was small and warm and the sapphire caught the string lights and threw a tiny blue flare across the wall and for a second Nora felt the thing she'd been not-feeling all night — not jealousy, exactly, but its quieter cousin. The awareness of absence. The recognition that Lindsay had something Nora had assumed would arrive for her without effort, like a package she'd forgotten she'd ordered, and at some point she'd stopped checking the tracking and now she wasn't sure it had ever shipped.

She let go of Lindsay's hand and followed Simone down the stairs and out onto the street.

---

October in Brooklyn at ten-thirty at night was the best weather in the world for walking and the second-best weather in the world for feeling sorry for yourself. The air was cool and specific — leaves decomposing on somebody's stoop, garlic from a restaurant's exhaust fan, that particular New York smell of cooling asphalt after a warm day, like the city breathing out. Nora and Simone walked toward the Jefferson Street L stop, shoulder to shoulder, their heels loud on the sidewalk. Simone had switched to flats the moment they hit the street — she kept a pair of foldable ballet flats in her bag the way other people kept an umbrella, a preparation for the walk that always came after the performance.

"Rate the party," Simone said.

"Six."

"You're being generous. That was a five at best. The cheese was a seven. The rosé was a four. Tyler's toast was a three because he cried and he didn't earn the crying."

"People are allowed to cry at their own engagement party."

"They're allowed. It doesn't mean it's earned. He teared up during the part about how she makes him want to be a better person. You don't get to cry at a cliché. You have to say something specific and true and then you can cry."

"You have standards for when people are allowed to cry?"

"I have standards for everything. That's my problem." She said it like a joke, but she held the word *problem* for a beat too long, and Nora felt the shape of something underneath it — not sadness, exactly, but a weight that Simone carried with such consistent grace that Nora sometimes forgot it was there.

They descended into the station. The L platform was mostly empty — a couple pressed against each other near the far end, a man in a Carhartt jacket reading his phone with his whole body angled away from everyone else, a woman with a stroller staring at nothing, her face somewhere else entirely. Nora and Simone stood near the yellow line and waited. The air underground was warmer than the street, trapped and slightly sour — metallic, human, close.

"So," Simone said. "You didn't answer my question."

"Which question?"

"The date question. You deflected and I let you because we were at a party and I'm not a monster. But we're not at a party anymore. We're in a subway station and I'm asking you directly. When's the last time you went on an actual date?"

"Peter. The literary agent. March."

"March." Simone let the word sit. "Nor, it's October."

"I'm aware of what month it is."

"That's seven months."

"I can do math."

"Have you been on the apps at all?"

"I deleted them."

"All of them?"

"All of them. After Peter I decided I'd rather be alone than sit through another dinner where a man explains his relationship to his own ambition for ninety minutes."

"Not all dates are Peter."

"Enough of them are."

The train came — a gust of warm air first, then the headlights, then the sound, which always hit Nora's body before her ears fully processed it, a pressure in the chest. They got on and sat in the orange seats. The car was half-full, that late-evening mix of people going home from places and people going to places, everyone sealed inside their own evening. A teenager across the aisle was watching something on her phone without headphones, the tinny audio drifting through the car like a small provocation nobody had the energy to address.

"I got the raise, by the way," Nora said. She hadn't meant to mention it — or she had meant to mention it but hadn't meant to mention it here, on the train, dropped into the middle of a conversation about her dating life like a non sequitur. "Priya told me Friday. Four percent. It's not huge but it puts me over eighty-five."

"That's great," Simone said. "Nor, that's really great." Her voice was warm — completely warm, genuinely pleased, the voice of a best friend hearing good news. But something happened in her face that was not her voice. It was fast — less than a second, a flicker across her features like a shadow thrown by a passing car — and Nora would have had to be watching for it to catch it, and she wasn't watching, she was looking at her own hands in her lap, because talking about money always made her look at her own hands. By the time she glanced up, Simone was smiling, and the smile was real, and whatever had been underneath it was already gone.

"Eighty-five is good," Simone said. "You should be making more, honestly. You're the best editor they have and you've been there five years. But eighty-five is good."

"I know. I'm not complaining."

"You never complain. That's also your problem."

"I have so many problems."

"You really don't." Again, the joke that wasn't entirely a joke. Simone's humor had this quality — a double floor, a trapdoor you could step over if you weren't looking for it. Nora almost never looked for it. You didn't excavate your best friend's throwaway lines. That wasn't how friendship worked. You took the surface because the surface was what was offered, and you trusted that the deeper thing, if it existed, would surface when it was ready.

"I think you should download Hinge," Simone said.

"No."

"Hear me out."

"I've heard you out. You've been making this argument since June."

"Because since June I've watched you go to four engagement parties and two weddings and a baby shower, and every time we leave one of these things you have this look on your face like someone switched your coffee to decaf without telling you. You're not unhappy. You're just — under-caffeinated."

"That's a terrible metaphor."

"It's a great metaphor. I'm a metaphor genius. Download Hinge."

"Hinge is where hope goes to die."

"Hinge is where hope goes to be tested. Some of it dies. Some of it survives. That's the process."

"You sound like a brochure."

"Download the app, Nora."

The train slowed for Lorimer Street.

"This is me." Simone stood, pulled her bag over her shoulder, and hugged Nora — quick and tight, the choreography of a thousand goodbyes, Simone's chin on Nora's shoulder, Nora's hand on the back of Simone's neck, two seconds, done.

She stepped onto the platform. "Think about the app."

"Goodnight, Simone."

"Download the app." The doors closed and Simone was still mouthing something through the glass — *the app*, probably, always the app — and then the train pulled away and Simone was gone and Nora was alone in the orange seat.

At Bedford Avenue she got off. On the platform, a group was waiting to board — louder and younger, dressed for a night that was still beginning rather than ending, laughing about something from a previous location. Nora's chest tightened. Not sadness, exactly. Just the awareness of a sound you'd been hearing all along — the refrigerator hum, the traffic, your own solitude underneath all of it — suddenly audible because something made you stop and listen.

She was alone on the stairs, and then she was alone on the street, and the October air was still beautiful but now it was beautiful in a way that was pointed, the way a compliment from the wrong person could be pointed. She walked the four blocks to her apartment with her keys already in her hand because that was a habit she'd never unlearned from her mother, who had grown up in a different New York, where a woman's keys between her fingers meant something more urgent than just being ready to open a door.

---

Her apartment was warm and quiet and smelled like the candle she'd left burning, which was almost out now, the wax pooled and the flame guttering against the glass. She kicked off her shoes and dropped her bag on the chair by the door and stood in her living room in the particular silence of a one-bedroom apartment occupied by a single person at eleven o'clock on a Saturday night.

The apartment was fine. It was good, actually — rent-stabilized, which in Brooklyn was basically a miracle, a thing people congratulated you on the way they congratulated Lindsay on the sapphire. She had books on every surface, real books, the physical kind, manuscripts and galleys mixed in with the ones she'd bought and the ones she'd been sent and the ones she'd stolen from the office free shelf. She had a couch she liked and a kitchen that was too small but functional and a bedroom with a window that faced a brick wall but got morning light for about forty minutes, which was enough. She had a life that looked, from the outside, like a life that was working. A good job. A best friend. A mother who called twice a week. A body that still did what she asked it to do. An apartment she could afford alone, in a city where that sentence meant something.

And she'd stood at that party tonight watching Lindsay Huang show her ring to a room full of people and she'd felt the thing she always felt now at these events — not the big, cinematic jealousy, not the kind you could turn into a funny story for Simone on the subway. The other kind. The quiet one. The one that lived in the gap between what she had and what she'd assumed she would have by thirty-four. She'd never made a plan, exactly. She wasn't a woman who had a timeline on her vision board — she didn't have a vision board, she would have edited the concept of a vision board out of any manuscript that crossed her desk. But somewhere in her twenties she'd absorbed a schedule from the culture or from her mother or from the sheer weight of weddings attended, and the schedule said that by now there would be someone. Not a husband, necessarily. Not a ring. Just a person who knew how she took her coffee and who would be there on a Tuesday night when Tuesday night was hard.

She'd had that. She'd had Marcus, for three years, and Marcus had been good — kind, smart, a public radio producer who read actual books and who made her laugh and who wanted to understand her. The relationship hadn't ended with a fight or a betrayal or a dramatic revelation. It had ended the way a lot of relationships ended: slowly, and then all at once, and then with a conversation on a Sunday morning where they both agreed, with a politeness that was its own kind of violence, that they had become roommates who sometimes had sex and that neither of them wanted to be the one to say it first but now someone had said it and so it was over. She'd cried after he left, and then she'd rearranged the bookshelves, and then she'd called Simone, and Simone had come over with wine and the kind of companionship that didn't require Nora to narrate her own feelings but just to sit in them while someone she loved sat next to her.

That was two years ago. Two years in which she'd gone on sporadic dates — Peter the literary agent, a man named Jordan who worked in sustainable architecture and who had kissed her outside a bar in Carroll Gardens and then never texted again, a woman named Asha whom she'd met at a publishing event and gone to dinner with once and liked enormously and then never followed up with because she wasn't sure enough of her own desire to pursue it and wasn't ready to examine why — and then she'd deleted the apps and told herself she was fine and mostly she was fine. Fine was a real state of being, not just the absence of something worse. She believed that. She mostly believed that.

She brushed her teeth and washed her face and got into bed and picked up her phone and opened Instagram and saw Lindsay's post — a photo of the ring on her hand, the caption *I said yes before he finished asking*, sixty-seven likes in the first hour — and she closed Instagram and opened her email and there was nothing and she closed her email and she sat there with her phone in her hand and the screen's light on her face in the dark room and she thought about what Simone had said.

*Download the app.*

It wasn't that she didn't want to. That was the thing she couldn't say to Simone on the subway, the thing she couldn't shape into a joke or a deflection. She wanted to. She wanted to badly enough that the wanting itself felt like a concession — an admission that being fine was not, actually, enough. That she was lonely in a way that her job and her mother and her best friend and her books could not fix, because the loneliness was specific, it had a shape, and the shape was the empty side of her bed and the silence in her apartment at eleven o'clock and the fact that when something good happened — when Priya told her about the raise, when she finished editing a manuscript she was proud of, when she read a sentence so beautiful it made her want to call someone — the person she wanted to call didn't exist yet.

*Fuck it.*

She opened the App Store. She typed *Hinge*. The icon was a little stylized H that looked like a door, or maybe she was projecting. She tapped the download button and watched the circle fill — the tiny progress bar, the small mechanical process of the phone acquiring the software that might or might not change her life, the way everything consequential happened now, through a screen, in a bed, alone.

The app opened. She entered her name, her age, her location. She chose six photos — two from the last year, one from a trip to Vermont, one that Simone had taken at a rooftop bar in July where the light was good, one professional headshot from the publisher's website, and one of her smiling at something off-camera that she chose because she looked happy in it and then wondered whether that was the same as being happy in it. She answered the prompts: *A life goal of mine* (edit a book that changes how someone sees the world — she almost wrote something funnier but the earnest answer came out first and she didn't change it). *I'm looking for* (she typed and deleted three times: *someone real* sounded like a dating profile cliché, *someone who reads* sounded like a test, *someone* sounded like desperation, so she wrote *someone who can make me laugh without trying too hard* and thought, *shit* — she was a professional editor who couldn't write her own Hinge prompt without overthinking it).

She finished the profile and looked at it and it was fine. She looked fine. She looked like a woman who had her life together and was choosing to be on this app, not driven to it. The performance of choosing versus the reality of need — this was the gap she lived in now, the gap she'd always lived in, the gap between the person she showed Simone on the subway and the person who was lying alone in bed at eleven-fifteen on a Saturday in October, downloading a dating app because she'd watched a woman she barely knew show a ring to a room full of people and felt the absence of something she didn't know how to name without flinching.

She closed the app before she could start swiping. She put the phone on the nightstand, screen down, and lay in the dark and listened to the sounds of her building — someone's television through the wall, bass-heavy and murmuring, the creak of the radiator beginning its nightly argument with itself, the distant bleat of a car alarm that nobody would attend to — and she thought: *I did it. I downloaded the thing. I'm on the app. I'm a person on a dating app.* The thought was equal parts relief and humiliation, which was, she was starting to suspect, the ratio at which most honest things arrived.

She fell asleep with the candle burned out and the phone dark on the nightstand and the app installed and waiting, a small door she had opened and not yet walked through.
