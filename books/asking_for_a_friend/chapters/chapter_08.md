# Chapter 8

Five weeks. She had been seeing Caleb Mora — she used the word *seeing* loosely, the way you might describe a phone conversation as a meeting — for five weeks, and she had never watched him laugh.

She knew what his laugh looked like in text. *Ha* followed by a period meant he was being polite or buying time. *Haha* meant genuinely amused. *LOL* he never used, which she respected — it was one of those small taxonomic details she'd filed away during their first night of messaging, back when she was still testing whether this person was real or merely plausible. Once, when she'd told him about a manuscript she was editing in which the author had described his protagonist's eyes as "cerulean orbs," Caleb had sent five crying-laughing emojis in a row and then: *I need you to know this man exists. Someone sat at a computer and typed cerulean orbs with his real fingers and then presumably went to bed feeling good about it.*

She could picture him doing it — at his kitchen table in Portland, coffee going cold, Bruno sprawled at his feet with his chin on Caleb's shoe, that loose, easy smile she'd memorized from his profile photos. She'd stared at those photos enough times that she could reconstruct his face with her eyes closed: the slightly crooked nose, the dark hair that curled at his temples, the way his eyes narrowed when he smiled so you couldn't tell if they were brown or very dark green. But she'd built all of this from still images, from eight photographs that never moved, and she wanted — she'd been wanting for weeks now with an urgency that embarrassed her — to see him in motion. To watch his mouth form the words he'd been typing. To find out if he was the kind of person who talked with his hands, or who looked away when he said something vulnerable, or who had a laugh that was quieter than you'd expect from his texts.

She wanted to FaceTime.

She'd first suggested it around week two, a Tuesday night, both of them in bed — separate beds, separate cities, separate time zones, a fact that sounded romantic in a Nora Ephron way but mostly meant that when she texted *goodnight* at midnight his phone might not even buzz for three more hours. *We should do a video call*, she'd written, trying to make it sound casual rather than desperate, which was the emotional tightrope of all modern dating communication. *I want to know if you're as cute as your photos or if you've been using strategic angles this whole time.*

He'd said yes, absolutely, he'd love that. But his laptop camera was broken. Some kind of firmware issue — he'd spilled water near the keyboard a month ago and the camera had died a slow, undramatic death. He was ordering a replacement part from the manufacturer. *Soon*, he'd written, and then a heart emoji, and then almost immediately, as if the heart had been too naked: *Fair warning though — I look like a startled deer on video. All forehead. You've been warned.*

She'd laughed. She'd told him she looked like a hostage on FaceTime, all chin and overhead lighting, and he'd sent back: *So we'll be two badly lit mammals staring at each other in mutual horror. I'm in.*

The replacement part didn't arrive.

The second time, the following Tuesday — she noticed, later, that it was always a Tuesday, as if the desire to see him operated on a weekly cycle she couldn't override — she'd suggested they just use their phones. Front-facing cameras. The technology that had been standard on every device sold since roughly the Obama administration. He'd said great, perfect, how about Thursday evening after his site visit? Thursday evening came, and he was still at the site — a residential garden installation in Lake Oswego, behind schedule because of the November rain, which in Portland apparently fell with the persistence of a personal grievance. The reception was terrible. He tried anyway: she got two minutes of stuttering, frozen video and crackling audio that sounded like he was broadcasting from inside a dishwasher, and one glimpse of what might have been his forehead above the rim of a hard hat before the call dropped entirely.

*That was objectively the worst date of my life and I was standing in six inches of mud the whole time*, he'd texted afterward. *Let me make it up to you. This weekend, my apartment, decent Wi-Fi, I'll even comb my hair.*

They did not try again that weekend, because Caleb came down with something — a cold, or maybe the flu, he wasn't sure, but he felt like death and looked worse and he didn't want her first real look at him to involve a swollen nose and a Nyquil glaze. He sent a photo of the tissue pile on his nightstand as proof, a small mountain of crumpled Kleenex next to a mug of something and what appeared to be an entire sleeve of Ritz crackers. Bruno's ear was visible at the bottom edge of the frame, pressed against Caleb's leg. *He hasn't left my side in two days*, Caleb wrote. *Either he loves me or he's waiting for me to die so he can eat the crackers.*

The cold lasted a week and a half. Each day brought an update — his temperature, the color of what he was coughing up, which she didn't need to know but which felt, perversely, like a form of intimacy, because who shares the details of their mucus with someone they're trying to impress? Only a person who had forgotten to perform, who was too miserable to be strategic. She found this comforting.

She found everything comforting if she looked at it from the right angle, and she was aware, in the way you're aware of a car alarm five blocks away — registering it, choosing not to respond — that she was looking very hard for right angles. The broken camera and the bad reception and the cold formed a sequence. Individually, each was perfectly ordinary. People's laptops broke. Job sites in the Pacific Northwest had bad cell service in November. Men in their early thirties got sick. But taken together, the three excuses shared a common outcome: she still didn't know what Caleb looked like when he moved, when he spoke, when he was sitting in his own apartment doing nothing. And she was choosing — it was a choice, she could feel the muscles of the choice working — not to arrange these facts into the shape they were obviously trying to make.

She opened a browser tab on her phone one night, typed *boyfriend won't video chat*, stared at the autocomplete suggestions — *boyfriend won't video chat red flag, boyfriend won't video chat catfish, boyfriend won't video chat is he married* — and closed the tab so fast her thumb cramped. She wasn't Googling that. She wasn't the kind of person who Googled that. She was a thirty-four-year-old senior editor at a publishing house; she evaluated narratives for a living; she knew what a red flag looked like because she'd read six hundred manuscripts about women who ignored red flags and she'd wanted to reach through the page and shake every single one of them. She noticed things. She was a professional noticer.

She was also a person who wanted this to be real badly enough that the wanting had developed its own gravity, pulling everything toward a version of events in which the broken camera was just a broken camera and the bad reception was just bad reception and the cold was just a cold and Caleb Mora was a man in Portland who liked her and owned a dog and would, eventually, appear on her screen with his crooked nose and his curling hair and his startled-deer face, and she would see him, and he would be real, and the waiting would have been worth it.

She picked up her phone and typed: *How's the cold? If you say you're still dying I'm going to FedEx you soup.*

He responded in under a minute — he always responded quickly, which she'd catalogued early on as devotion and had not revisited the categorization: *Getting better. Down to only four tissues an hour. Bruno keeps trying to lick my face, which is either love or an attempt to harvest my snot. Unclear on his motives.*

She sent back a laughing emoji and put her phone on the nightstand and stared at the ceiling of her Brooklyn apartment, where a crack in the plaster had been slowly branching since August like a river delta she was too lazy to report to her landlord. The radiator clicked and ticked in the corner, doing its nightly impression of a telegraph machine. Three floors up, someone was playing piano — not well, but with conviction, the same four bars of something she almost recognized repeated over and over — wrong each time, circling back each time, as if this next pass might somehow land it.

She understood that person.

---

The next afternoon, Margo dropped a manuscript on Nora's desk hard enough to rattle her pen cup — four hundred pages of autofiction about a man who achieved spiritual clarity through competitive CrossFit — and settled into the chair beside Nora's desk that had become, through accumulated lunchtimes, exclusively Margo's. No one had assigned it. Margo had simply sat in it enough Tuesdays and Thursdays that the chair had developed a kind of social magnetism, and now even the interns navigated around it the way you navigate around a reserved table at a restaurant.

"I hate this," Margo said, unwrapping a sandwich from the deli on Forty-Fourth. She was wearing the reading glasses she'd bought at a bodega for six dollars — tortoiseshell frames with tiny rhinestones at the corners, completely absurd, which was why she loved them. She pushed them up her nose with her ring finger, a tic so automatic she didn't seem to know she did it. "This man did seven hundred burpees and now he thinks he understands the human condition. He literally writes, and I'm quoting, 'The barbell is a metaphor, but it is also literal.' What does that even mean?"

"It means he thinks he's Camus."

"He's not Camus. He's not even in the same area code as Camus. He's a man who discovered muscle failure and mistook it for existentialism, and I have to write a straight-faced pass on this bullshit by Friday." Margo bit into her sandwich and kept talking before she'd finished chewing — she'd stopped being tentative about things sometime around the divorce, and the habit had extended to her table manners. "How's the boyfriend?"

Every time someone used that word — *boyfriend* — Nora felt a small rearrangement in her chest, like a book being reshelved. She hadn't used the word herself, not yet, not out loud, but she also hadn't corrected anyone who did, which was its own kind of declaration. "He's good. Getting over a cold. He sent me this whole saga about Bruno trying to eat his Ritz crackers while he was sleeping."

"Cute." Margo chewed, watching Nora with the steady, practical attention she brought to everything — manuscripts, lunch, the emotional lives of the people she cared about. "So. Have you seen him yet?"

"I see his photos every —"

"On video. Have you FaceTimed. Have you watched this man move in real time and heard his voice come out of his face."

The questions weren't questions. They were the same question wearing three outfits, and they landed in the place Nora had been carefully keeping empty — the place where the Google search had lived for forty-five seconds before she'd closed it.

"We've tried," she said, and heard her own voice shift into a register she recognized from her authors when she'd flagged a structural problem and they were explaining why it was actually intentional. Defensive. Slightly too detailed. "His laptop camera broke, and then he was at a job site with no reception, and then the cold —"

"That's three reasons."

"They're three separate things that happened."

"They're three versions of the same outcome, which is that you haven't seen this person." Margo set down the sandwich. She took off the rhinestone glasses and placed them on the desk, and without them her face was simpler and more serious, the bones of her concern showing through. She'd been through her own romantic catastrophe — married at thirty, divorced at thirty-five, the whole arc compressed into five years that had left her with a settlement she never discussed, an ex-husband who'd started a joint Instagram with his new girlfriend captioned things like *Sunday meal prep with my partner in crime*, and a bullshit detector that ran at an industrial frequency. "I think he sounds great. I think the texts are sweet and he seems like a real person. I'm just asking because it's been more than a month and that's — it's a lot of texting and not a lot of face."

"We're going to. He's getting over being sick. Next week."

"OK."

"You don't sound like you think it's OK."

"I said OK." Margo picked up the glasses, examined a rhinestone. "If I meant something other than OK I'd say something other than OK. You know me. I don't do subtext — I leave that to the authors who can't write it."

Nora laughed, and the laugh was mostly real and partly a door she was closing on the conversation. Margo let it close. That was one of the things Nora valued about her — she knew when to push and when to stop, which was the same skill that made her a decent editor and a good friend, and if the question sat in the air between them for the rest of the lunch hour, neither of them acknowledged it, and when Margo went back to her desk she squeezed Nora's shoulder once, briefly, and let the pressure say what she didn't.

Nora turned back to the CrossFit memoir and read three pages without absorbing a word. The cursor on her screen blinked in the margin where she was supposed to be making notes, and she thought about how Margo's question had the same shape as the Google search she'd closed — *boyfriend won't video chat* — and she thought about the fact that she edited books for a living, that her entire professional existence was built on catching inconsistencies — she saw what didn't add up, she noticed the gaps between what people presented and what they meant, and she thought about how none of that seemed to apply to her own life, which was either a sign that her feelings for Caleb were strong enough to override her professional instincts or a sign that her professional instincts were exactly as useless as her authors always claimed they were when she found the holes in their plots.

She picked up her phone and texted Caleb: *My colleague just asked me if you're real. I told her you were. Back me up here.*

He responded in forty seconds: *Define real. Philosophically? Because I've been reading this book about simulation theory and I'm not sure any of us are real. Bruno might be. He has a very convincing corporeal presence, especially when he's sitting on my feet.*

She smiled at her phone without knowing she was doing it — unguarded, unconscious, the whole feeling right there on her face, and Margo, across the open-plan office, saw the smile and said nothing.

Four days later, a Friday evening. Nora was on her couch under the blanket her mother had given her two Christmases ago — heavier than any blanket had a right to be, pressing her into the couch cushions with an insistence that somehow felt like being held. She was reading a manuscript, an actual good one for once, a novel about a woman running a bakery in the Outer Sunset in San Francisco that managed to be funny about grief without ever letting the comedy defuse the sadness. She'd been reading for an hour, her tea going cold on the side table, the radiator doing its clicking, when her phone buzzed.

*Hey — so something happened and I'm kind of freaking out and I don't want to dump this on you but I also don't know who else to tell right now*

Then, three seconds later:

*Bruno swallowed a tennis ball. The whole thing. He was playing in the yard and I turned around for maybe two minutes and when I looked back he was making this sound I've never heard a dog make*

Then:

*I'm at the emergency vet. They did an X-ray and it's stuck in his small intestine. They say he needs surgery — emergency surgery, tonight, or it could become a full obstruction and then we're talking about something much worse*

Then, after a pause in which the typing indicator appeared and disappeared three times:

*I'm sitting in this waiting room that smells like antiseptic and dog piss and there's a woman across from me whose cat ate a whole ribbon off a gift bag and she keeps telling me it was a satin ribbon, like the thread count is relevant to the cat's intestinal situation. We're both just sitting here staring at the wall. Sorry — I know this is a lot. I just needed to tell somebody.*

"Shit." She said it out loud, to her empty apartment. She sat up so fast the manuscript slid off her lap and fanned across the floor. She typed: *Oh my god, Cal, is he going to be OK?*

*The vet says the surgery is pretty routine for this, they see it all the time apparently, tennis balls and socks and one time a whole corn cob. But they walked me through the risks and the anesthesia and all I kept thinking was that I wanted to hold his paw when they put him under and they don't let you do that*

Her throat hurt. Not the dramatic, literary kind of hurt — the actual kind, the physical tightness you get when someone you care about is scared and you're in a different time zone and you can't do anything useful. She typed and deleted three responses before settling on: *He's going to be fine. He's a dog who can swallow a tennis ball whole — he's clearly indestructible.*

*That's one way to read it. The other is that he has the survival instincts of a golden retriever at a barbecue.*

She almost laughed. Even panicked, even sitting in a fluorescent waiting room at an emergency vet clinic, he was still funny, and the fact that he could be funny while scared made her want to reach through the phone and put her hand on his arm.

*How much is the surgery?*

The typing indicator appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

*They quoted $3,800. I don't — I'm not thinking about the money right now. He ate a tennis ball and he's my dog and I'm going to fix it. I'll deal with the bill later.*

There it was. The specific, stubborn pride of a person who would rather absorb the blow than ask for a cushion. She recognized it — not just generally, but specifically, the way her own father used to carry grocery bags until his fingers turned white rather than make two trips, the way he'd once called a cab to the hospital for chest pains rather than wake Diane because he didn't want to worry her. The people she loved were all like this: they'd rather hurt quietly than impose. And the thing about people like that was that the only way to help them was to offer before they could refuse, to make the offer casual enough that accepting it didn't feel like a concession.

*Let me help with the vet bill*, she typed.

*Nora. No. That's not why I told you.*

*I know it's not why you told me. That's exactly why I'm offering.*

*We haven't even met in person. You can't just — this is too much. I'll figure it out.*

*I'm not asking you to let me cover it. I'm asking you to let me help. There's a distinction and I'm an editor, so trust me when I say the distinction matters.*

Another long pause. She imagined him in the waiting room, the fluorescent lights washing out the color in his face, the antiseptic smell, the woman with the ribbon-eating cat glancing over, the awful shared intimacy of people waiting to find out if the creature they love is going to be OK.

*You're going to make me cry in this waiting room*, he wrote. *In front of the cat lady.*

*Good. She could probably use the reminder that men have emotions. Think of it as a public service.*

*I can't let you cover the whole thing. I've got some savings. But honestly — even a couple hundred would take the edge off. I'd pay you back. I mean it.*

She opened Venmo before she could talk herself out of it. She found the account — C. Mora — a handle he'd shared weeks ago, back when they'd been joking about hypothetically splitting a dinner tab and he'd sent it as proof that he was a man who went halves. She typed four hundred dollars. Not the full thirty-eight hundred — that would have been too much, more weight than help, more monument than gift — the other person would have to carry it like a debt. Four hundred was enough to mean something without meaning everything. She hit send. The little animation played — the blue checkmark, the confirmation, the cartoon money traveling from one icon to another. Two taps and a thumb scan. Four hundred dollars, gone.

*Done*, she typed. *It's for Bruno. Please don't make it weird.*

*It's already weird. It's the best weird. You're the best weird person I've ever met.*

She put the phone face-down on the couch cushion and sat there for a moment, the manuscript still on the floor, the blanket bunched in her lap, the jazz pianist upstairs starting their nightly practice — those same four bars, looping, patient, wrong in the same places every time. She felt good. Not abstractly good — specifically, physically good, warm in a way that had nothing to do with the blanket: she was a person who, when someone she cared about was scared and alone in a fluorescent waiting room, sent help. She didn't deliberate. She didn't calculate. She acted, and the acting felt like a declaration of who she was, and Caleb knew who she was, and the knowing was part of why this thing between them — this strange, screen-bound, five-week-old thing that she'd never been able to touch — felt more real than anything she'd had in years.

She picked up the phone and texted Simone: *Are you around tomorrow? I need to tell you something that's either very generous or very stupid and I genuinely can't tell which.*

*Always around for you. Coffee at Daly's? 10?*

*Yes. Bring your honest opinion. I may need it surgically removed from me afterward.*

Saturday morning. The coffee shop on Bedford that was spelled either Daily or Daly — the hand-painted sign was ambiguous and neither Nora nor Simone had ever asked, which meant they'd been calling it two different things for three years, a fact that still made Nora laugh when she thought about it. November had fully committed to itself now: the trees along Bedford were stripped bare, and the cold had the thin, metallic quality of a New York almost-December, it slipped inside your collar and stayed.

Simone was already at the window table when Nora pushed through the door, wearing an oversized denim jacket Nora hadn't seen before — thrifted, probably, because Simone had an eye for secondhand clothes that managed to look curated rather than salvaged, a talent that Nora, who bought her clothes new and at full price without thinking about it, had always admired without examining. Simone was peeling the label off her water bottle in long, careful strips, a habit she'd had since college — some combination of fidgeting and perfectionism that produced neat little scrolls of damp paper on the table beside her cup. Her nails were painted a dark reddish-purple that was chipping at the tips, and there were shadows under her eyes that her concealer hadn't quite handled, and she looked up when Nora sat down with a smile that was warm and immediate and made Nora feel, as it always did, like she had just arrived at the one place in the city where she was fully expected.

"I ordered you the usual," Simone said, pushing a latte across the table. Oat milk, no sugar. She'd been ordering for Nora since 2009.

"You're a saint."

"I'm an enabler. There's a difference." Simone tore off another strip of label. "Tell me the generous-or-stupid thing."

Nora told her. All of it — the texts from Caleb, the tennis ball, the emergency vet, the woman with the ribbon-eating cat, the $3,800 quote, the way he'd deflected the financial question with that flat, proud *I'll figure it out*, the way the not-asking had reached inside her and grabbed something, the Venmo, the four hundred dollars.

Simone listened the way Simone always listened — leaned forward, eye contact, small sounds at exactly the right moments that told you she was tracking every beat. Her phone was on the table between them, face up, the screen dark. At some point while Nora was describing the text exchange, the screen lit briefly with a notification — a quick flash of color and text too small to read from Nora's angle — and Simone's eyes moved to it for less than a second, a flick so fast it barely registered, before returning to Nora's face. She didn't pick the phone up. She didn't turn it over. She just let the screen go dark again and kept listening.

"So," Nora said. "Is it stupid? Margo would say it's stupid. Margo would say I've never even FaceTimed this man and now I'm bankrolling his dog's intestinal surgery."

"Margo's not wrong about everything. She went through some real shit with her marriage, and now she's got her guard up — I get that." Simone said this warmly, one friend being fair about another. "But her pattern recognition doesn't automatically apply to your situation. She sees someone who won't video chat and goes straight to worst case. That doesn't mean worst case is what's happening."

"She asked me if I'd ever seen him on video."

"What'd you tell her?"

"That we've tried. Which is true."

"It's true." Simone wrapped both hands around her coffee. Her ring finger had a mark where she'd been chewing on the cuticle, a tell Nora associated with work stress — Simone's hands always showed what her face didn't. "Is the FaceTime thing bothering you?"

"Not — I don't know. Not the way it bothers Margo. It's more like..." Nora stared at her latte. "It's like reading a manuscript where every page is brilliant but you can't see the author photo. You know the writing is real. You can feel the person behind it. But there's this one thing that won't come into focus, and you keep turning pages hoping the next one will resolve it."

"That's a very you way to describe a relationship."

"Everything's a manuscript if you squint."

Simone laughed — short, warm, the corners of her eyes crinkling. "So the money. Four hundred bucks. For a dog you've never met, owned by a man you've never FaceTimed."

"When you say it like that —"

"I'm not saying it like anything. I'm describing the facts." Simone leaned forward. "Here are some other facts. You care about him. His dog is sick. He didn't ask you for money — he told you what was happening because you're the person he tells things to. And you helped, because that's what you do. Four hundred bucks isn't going to wreck you, Nor. You spent more than that on that jacket last month."

"That jacket was on sale."

"Everything's on sale if you squint." Simone paused. She lifted her coffee, took a deliberate sip, and set it down, and something in her face went still — not cold, but focused, as if she were choosing her next words carefully. "You know why it worked, right? The fact that he didn't ask. If he'd come out and said *can you Venmo me*, you'd have hesitated. But the pride, the *I'll figure it out* — that's what got you. Because you can't stand watching someone carry something alone when you could lighten it." She reached across the table and touched the back of Nora's hand — briefly, easily, a reflex seventeen years had worn so smooth it no longer required thought. "It's not stupid. It's generous. That's who you are."

The sentence landed somewhere below Nora's sternum, in the place where the opinions of the people who truly knew her went directly into the bloodstream, bypassing every filter she'd built. Simone knew her. Simone had known her since they were eighteen and scared and pretending not to be, since the first week of freshman orientation when they'd discovered they both hated the same campus coffee shop for the same specific reason — the music was too loud to think and too quiet to dance to, a limbo that offended them equally. Seventeen years of knowing someone built a kind of authority that no amount of Googling could replicate. When Simone said *that's who you are*, it carried the weight of evidence.

"Thanks, Si."

"For what?"

"For not making me feel insane."

"You're not insane. You're a person who sent money to a sick dog she's never met, which is honestly the most Nora Kaplan thing I've heard all year."

They stayed for another hour. Simone told her about a client at work who wanted an app interface that was "intuitive but also complex, like a really good cocktail menu" — Simone did the client's voice, a nasal tenor with the cadence of someone who'd just taken a weekend design-thinking workshop, and Nora laughed hard enough that the woman at the next table looked over. Nora told Simone about the CrossFit memoir, about the barbell-as-metaphor line, and Simone said, "That man discovered lactic acid and thought it was enlightenment," and Nora laughed again, and the laughter felt like breathing, like something her body needed and could only get here, at this table, with this person.

The November light came through the window in pale, cold sheets. Nora's hands were warm from the mug. Simone had finished peeling her water bottle label and was rolling the strips into a single tight cylinder between her thumb and forefinger, the way she always did, a neat little monument to a nervous habit she'd never bothered to break. Outside, Bedford Avenue did its Saturday morning thing — strollers and dogs and people in puffy coats carrying bags from the farmers' market — and somewhere in Portland a dog named Bruno was in surgery or out of surgery and a man named Caleb was waiting or already relieved, and Nora had helped, and it felt correct, and the small hard stone that Margo's question had lodged in her throat was still there but quieter now, pushed down by the coffee and the laughter and Simone's hand on hers and the specific, precious, irreplaceable weight of being told by the person who knew you best that the thing you'd done was not insane but generous, that it was not stupid but brave, that it was exactly, precisely who you were.
