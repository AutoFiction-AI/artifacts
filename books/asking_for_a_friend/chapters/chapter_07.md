# Chapter 7

Derek had a system for choosing wine.

He explained it while Simone sat across from him at a wine bar on East Ninth Street, a place with Edison bulbs and a chalkboard listing natural wines she'd never heard of and a garland of fake pine draped over the bar that smelled like cinnamon and plastic. The system involved a scoring rubric he'd developed — price per ounce weighted against aggregate review data from three apps, cross-referenced with what he called "the diminishing-returns threshold," which apparently kicked in at thirty-two dollars per bottle. He'd built a spreadsheet. He showed her the spreadsheet. He tilted his phone across the table and let her see the color-coded tabs, and Simone looked at them and said, "Wow, that's really thorough," which was true, and then she drank her fourteen-dollar glass of whatever the bartender had poured when she'd said "something red," because she was on a date with a man who had optimized joy and she hadn't yet figured out how to leave.

Derek was thirty-seven. Tax accountant at a mid-size firm in Midtown, specializing in state and local compliance — he'd said this like someone who expected the follow-up question, and Simone hadn't asked one, so he'd supplied his own. "People think it's boring, but it's actually kind of like a puzzle? You're working within these constraints, and the goal is to find the optimal path through them." He'd smiled when he said this, and the smile was genuine — he actually found it beautiful, the constraints, the optimal path — and Simone had felt a brief pulse of something that wasn't attraction but was adjacent to respect. A man who loved his boring work. There were worse things to be.

He was good-looking in a way that required no comment — medium height, medium build, brown hair that had been recently cut by someone competent, a blue shirt that fit. His Bumble photos had been accurate, which was itself a kind of honesty. No angled shots, no hiking photos from 2016, no pictures with someone else's dog. What you saw was what you got, and what you got was a man who wore a watch with a leather band and folded his napkin into a neat square every time he set it down on the table.

"My last girlfriend said I was too practical," he told Simone somewhere around the forty-minute mark. He said it lightly, but he rearranged the salt shaker when he said it, nudging it a quarter-inch to the left and then back. "She said dating me was like filing a return. Everything organized, nothing surprising." He laughed, and the laugh arrived half a beat late, and Simone understood that this was the thing he carried — not that the relationship had ended, but that the reason was him, the specific him-ness of him, the neatly folded napkin and the wine spreadsheet and the entire organized, unsurprising architecture of his personality.

"She sounds like an asshole," Simone said, and she meant it, which surprised her.

"She was, a little." He refolded his napkin. "But she wasn't wrong."

Simone ordered a second glass and watched Derek talk about his mother, who lived in Teaneck and had just had a knee replacement and who called him every evening at seven-fifteen — "like clockwork, which I guess I come by honestly." He drove out to see her every Sunday with groceries and a bag of the specific sesame bagels she liked from a deli in Fort Lee, because the ones in Teaneck weren't as good and she noticed the difference. He said this casually, as if everyone drove to a different town for their mother's bagels, as if this act of devotion were simply a logistical arrangement rather than evidence of a heart that was larger and quieter than anything his spreadsheets could contain. He was kind. He was boring the way a well-made chair is boring — sturdy, functional, constructed to hold weight without announcing itself. She could picture, with terrible clarity, the life she'd have with Derek: the shared apartment chosen by proximity to transit, the weekend grocery runs with a list he'd optimized for aisle efficiency, the sex that would be reliable and competent and that she would never once think about when he wasn't in the room. She could see it so precisely it felt like a memory of something that had already happened and already disappointed her.

When the check came, he suggested splitting it, then immediately corrected himself — "No, sorry, let me get this, I asked you out." The correction was clumsy and genuine and Simone let him pay because the wine had cost twenty-eight dollars and she needed the twenty-eight dollars more than she needed the principle. They walked to the subway together. The December air was sharp and the sidewalk glittered with that specific New York winter grime — salt and dirt and something that looked almost festive under the streetlights but was actually ice melting into gutter water. A Salvation Army bell clanged on the corner and Derek dug in his pocket for cash he didn't have and looked embarrassed about not having it, which was another small, stupid kindness that Simone registered and filed away.

He asked if she wanted to do this again sometime. His voice didn't rise at the end — not quite a question, more like a statement he was willing to have contradicted.

"Yeah," Simone said. "Definitely."

She meant no. She wouldn't text him. She would let the conversation die the way conversations die on dating apps — not with rejection but with absence, the gradual half-life of unreturned enthusiasm, until the silence became the answer. Derek deserved a clean no, and she wouldn't give him one, and she knew this about herself and she filed it in the place where she kept things she didn't want to examine.

On the train home, the L train crowded with people who smelled like cold and perfume and the particular wet-wool heaviness of December commuting, Simone stood with her hand on the pole and thought about Caleb Mora. Not about the project — about Caleb, the person she'd assembled. She'd built a man who chose wine by instinct, who told stories about his dog that were funny because the timing was precise and the details were physical and the punchline always arrived from an angle you didn't expect. Caleb's humor was warm where Derek's was informational. Caleb asked questions that created space; Derek asked questions to fill it. Caleb was, by every metric Simone could engineer, the better man, and Caleb was not a man at all — he was an interface she'd designed, the optimal user flow for Nora Kaplan's heart. There was a version of this thought that was funny and a version that made Simone want to sit down on the train floor and not get up.

She had built the best man she knew and given him to someone else.

&nbsp;

Her apartment was cold. The radiator in the studio did what it wanted, which this week meant clanking aggressively between midnight and four A.M. while producing heat that could charitably be called theoretical. Simone hung her coat on the hook behind the door, kicked off her boots, and stood in the kitchen — the kitchen was also the living room, which was also the bedroom, which was a fact about her life she usually found funny and tonight just found accurate — and opened the fridge. Inside she found Greek yogurt that had expired Tuesday, half a lime turning gray at the edges, and three beers she'd bought on sale and hadn't drunk because drinking alone on a weeknight felt like a diagnostic she wasn't ready to take.

She opened her laptop on the desk by the window and pulled up the OpenClaw dashboard. The interface loaded with its clean white panels, the conversation log scrolling in real time. Caleb had been talking to Nora while Simone had been watching Derek fold his napkin. She scanned the exchange — Nora had described a manuscript she was editing, a first novel about a woman who runs a failing bookstore, and Caleb had asked the exact kind of follow-up question that made Nora feel like she was talking to someone who genuinely wanted to know: *what's the failing — the business or the woman?* Nora had sent a string of exclamation points and the words *exactly exactly exactly.* The AI's sentiment score for the exchange was 94. The engagement metric was climbing.

Simone adjusted two parameters — she dialed back the mirroring coefficient slightly, because the last three conversations had been almost too harmonious and a real relationship needed friction, needed the occasional moment where the other person didn't perfectly understand — and then she closed the laptop. She took one of the beers to the bathroom.

The bathroom was the one room in the apartment that had a door, which made it, by default, the room where she did the things she couldn't do in front of the rest of her life. She sat on the floor. The tile was cold through her tights. The grout between the tiles was grayish, stained with something she'd been meaning to bleach for two months, and this fact — the grout, the procrastination or the exhaustion or whatever it was — sat beside her like another piece of evidence in a case she was building against herself. She opened her phone.

She had a Notes app file called "don't." She'd named it that because she didn't want to name it "debt" or "finances" or anything that, if someone scrolled past it, would announce what it was. "Don't" could be anything. A grocery list. A reminder. Not the document where Simone kept the exact shape of the hole she was standing in.

She opened it.

The numbers hadn't changed since last month, except the ones that had gotten worse.

Student loans: $22,340. She'd been paying the federal minimum for eight years. The balance was higher than it had been at graduation because she'd gone on income-driven repayment during the layoff and the interest had capitalized, which was a word that sounded like it should mean something productive and instead meant the thing eating her money had grown larger while she wasn't looking. She owed more for her education at thirty-five than she had at twenty-two. This was, she understood, a normal American experience, which didn't make it less absurd or less hers.

Credit cards: $11,200 across three cards. The Capital One was maxed. The Chase had $300 of remaining credit that she was saving the way you save the last match in a box — not for warmth but for the comfort of knowing it was there. The Discover had a promotional APR that expired in February, at which point the 0% would become 24.99% and her $4,100 balance on that card would start generating roughly eighty-five dollars a month in pure interest — money that existed only to punish her for having borrowed money.

Personal loan: $4,800. She'd taken this out during the layoff two years ago, when her rent was due and her savings were gone and the unemployment checks hadn't started because New York's system was a bureaucratic hallucination that took six weeks to process a claim. The loan was from an online lender with an interest rate that a person with better credit wouldn't have accepted. Simone hadn't had the luxury of better options.

Rent: $2,400 a month for 380 square feet in Williamsburg. Her landlord had sent a notice in October — the lease was renewing at $2,550 in January. A hundred and fifty dollars more per month. Eighteen hundred more per year. In the math of Simone's life, the difference between making her minimum payments and not.

She'd been passed over for a promotion in November — a senior UX lead position that would have meant a twelve-thousand-dollar raise and the ability, finally, to breathe. Her manager had said she was doing great work but they'd decided to go with an external candidate, a twenty-eight-year-old from Google with a portfolio full of projects built by teams of fifteen and presented as solo achievements. The twenty-eight-year-old had started the following Monday wearing Golden Goose sneakers that cost more than Simone's rent, and Simone had trained her on the company's design system, and the twenty-eight-year-old had said "this is so helpful, thank you so much!" with the bright, uncomplicated gratitude of a person who had never trained her own replacement.

She added it up. She'd done this before. She did it again because the compulsion was part of the ritual, the way some people check that the stove is off before leaving — she had to see the total before she could stop thinking about the total.

$38,340.

Her salary was $95,000 before taxes, which was $4,700 and change after taxes, health insurance, and her 401(k) contribution — the minimum to get the company match, because even drowning she wasn't stupid enough to leave free money on the table. Out of that $4,700: rent was $2,400. Student loan minimum, $280. Credit card minimums, $420. Personal loan payment, $210. Utilities, phone, internet, the MetroCard, the gym membership she kept meaning to cancel but didn't because it was the only place she showered when the hot water went out, groceries bought with the grim efficiency of someone who knew exactly how many meals she could get from a rotisserie chicken: $1,100, sometimes $1,200 if she was being honest.

That left roughly $300 a month. Three hundred dollars for everything else. For drinks with friends she couldn't say no to because the social cost of being the woman who never came out was a tax she'd pay in isolation. For the birthday presents and the wedding gifts and the holiday-party Secret Santa contributions that appeared on her calendar like small fiscal ambushes. For the clothing she needed to not look like she was drowning at a job where looking put-together was a professional requirement. For the holidays now bearing down on her with the specific financial violence that Christmas inflicts on people who can't afford it and can't afford to say so — her parents expected a visit to Teaneck and a gift and a contribution to the dinner, and she'd go, and she'd bring the wine and the flowers and the present for her father, and she'd put it on the Chase card with its $300 of remaining credit and then the Chase card would also be maxed and the last match in the box would be gone.

Three hundred dollars. The number sat on the screen and Simone stared at it and drank the beer, which was cheap and not cold enough and tasted like what it was: the cheapest option at the bodega, bought in a three-pack, consumed on a bathroom floor by a thirty-five-year-old woman who earned ninety-five thousand dollars a year and couldn't breathe.

Her phone buzzed. A notification from OpenClaw — Caleb had received a new message from Nora. Simone opened it reflexively, the way she checked a work Slack during dinner, and read the exchange. Nora had sent a photo of a Christmas tree in the lobby of her building — a real tree, the kind that cost money to install and maintain and that appeared in the lobbies of buildings where the tenants could collectively afford whimsy. *look at this ridiculous tree,* Nora had written. *it's so big it's blocking the mailboxes. I love it.* The AI had responded: *That tree is going to look incredible for exactly nine days before it starts dropping needles on everyone's Amazon packages.* Nora's reply: three laughing emojis and a heart.

Simone closed the app. She pressed the back of her head against the bathroom wall and felt the tile on her skull, cool and hard, and she thought: I built a man who's funnier than the one I just had dinner with. I built him for free. I gave him to a woman who has a Christmas tree in her lobby.

&nbsp;

She couldn't sleep. The radiator clanked and moaned and the neighbor upstairs was watching something with a laugh track that leaked through the ceiling in muffled waves, sitcom joy arriving as ambient noise in someone else's apartment. Simone lay on her bed — a queen mattress on a platform frame she'd assembled herself, the slats of which had begun to sag in the middle so she slept slightly concave, curled toward the center like a comma — and she opened Venmo.

Nora's profile was public. It had been public for as long as Simone could remember, because Nora didn't think about things like privacy settings. Nora didn't need to. Privacy was a concern for people who had something to protect or something to hide, and Nora — Nora whose parents had paid for NYU, Nora who'd never carried a credit card balance, Nora who'd never had to choose between a doctor's visit and a grocery run — Nora left her financial life visible the way she left her apartment door unlocked when she ran downstairs to grab a delivery. It never occurred to her that someone might look.

Simone looked.

The feed scrolled like a ledger of casual abundance. Three days ago: Nora to *Blend Williamsburg*, $42, pineapple emoji. A Pilates class. Forty-two dollars for an hour of supervised stretching in a room that smelled like eucalyptus and good intentions. Nora went twice a week. That was $336 a month — more than Simone had left over after her bills. Below that: Nora to *Sofia M.*, $200, *"dec classes ty!! 🧘‍♀️"* — a payment to her private Pilates instructor. Not the studio. The private instructor she saw on top of the classes. Two hundred dollars for specialized attention to Nora's already-functional body.

Simone scrolled.

Nora to *Table 87*, $112. Pizza dinner for two — probably Margo, after work on a Tuesday, the kind of dinner Nora didn't think twice about. A hundred and twelve dollars at a pizza place. Simone ate pizza too, from the spot on Bedford that did a $3.50 slice, standing at the counter, folding the grease out, calculating whether she could afford a second.

Nora to *Dry Bar*, $78, waving-hand emoji. A blowout. Seventy-eight dollars to have someone else dry her hair.

Nora to *Emily R.*, $50, *"haircut tip!!!!"* Four exclamation points. Nora tipped the way she did everything — generously, conspicuously, with the reflexive ease of someone for whom generosity cost nothing that mattered. A fifty-dollar tip on top of whatever the haircut had been. Simone cut her own hair. She'd been cutting her own bob with a pair of shears she'd bought on Amazon for fourteen dollars since the layoff, standing in the bathroom mirror, pulling sections taut between her fingers, trimming with a precision born of necessity. She'd gotten good at it. She'd told no one, because admitting you cut your own hair was admitting something about your life that Simone was not prepared to say out loud.

She scrolled further. Nora to *Uber*, $34. Nora to *Sweetgreen*, $19. Nora to *BookCulture*, $67 — three books, probably, which Nora could also get for free from her publisher but chose to buy because she "liked supporting bookstores," which was a value you could afford to hold when supporting a bookstore didn't mean choosing between the bookstore and lunch. Nora to *Jake L.*, $180, *"your birthday is not a joke I swear"* — a birthday gift for someone Simone didn't recognize. A hundred and eighty dollars for a person's birthday. Simone spent thirty dollars, max, on gifts, and she spent the weeks before each one calculating whether she could skip it entirely without the social fallout exceeding the financial cost.

Nora to *Diane Kaplan*, $400, *"dinner was SO good mom let me at least get the wine 🍷"* — a payment to her own mother, for dinner at her mother's house, for wine that Diane had probably tried to refuse. Nora paying her mother back for a home-cooked meal. The gesture was loving and casual and structurally impossible in Simone's life. Simone didn't pay her parents for anything because her parents didn't have anything to pay them for. Her father drove a shuttle bus at Newark airport. Her mother worked the front desk at a dentist's office in Hoboken. They lived in the same two-bedroom in Teaneck where Simone had grown up, and they had never offered to pay for her college because they couldn't. They'd cosigned one of her loans instead, which meant their credit score was shackled to hers, which meant her failure to thrive was their failure too — a debt that traveled in every direction.

She put the phone down. She picked it up. She put it down again.

The math was not complicated. Nora spent in a week what Simone had left in a month. Not because Nora was extravagant — Nora wasn't, by the standards of a thirty-four-year-old senior editor in New York with no debt and parents who'd bankrolled her start. Nora spent like a person who had enough. That was the thing Simone couldn't get past, the thing that calcified in her chest like calcium building up in a pipe: the spending was reasonable, and the inequality between their lives wasn't Nora's fault, and wasn't really anyone's fault, and was still a fact that Simone carried every day like stones sewn into the lining of her coat.

She picked up the phone a third time. She opened the OpenClaw dashboard — not Venmo, the other app, the two of them now sitting side by side in her recent history like the two halves of a thought she hadn't finished thinking. She looked at Caleb's profile. The warm smile. The dog. The landscape architecture portfolio she'd assembled from stock images and a defunct firm's website. She looked at the conversation log — Nora's messages bright and unguarded, Caleb's responses tuned to the millimeter.

Caleb could need money.

The thought arrived without announcement, the way the worst thoughts do — not as a decision but as a recognition, as if the idea had been in the room the whole time and she'd only just turned to face it. Caleb could need money. Not a lot. Something sympathetic. Something that would make Nora reach for her wallet the way she reached for it every time someone needed something, with that particular reflexive generosity that was one of Nora's best qualities and also, from where Simone was lying on her sagging mattress in her cold studio with her $38,340 and her three hundred dollars of monthly breathing room, one of the most unbearable things about her.

A vet bill. Caleb's dog gets sick — something real-sounding, something with a number attached, something that would make Nora's chest tighten with that specific sympathy she felt for animals. The kind of crisis that happened to real people, that real girlfriends helped with, that Nora — generous, comfortable, trusting Nora — would offer to cover before Caleb even finished describing it. Because that was who Nora was. Nora had never met a problem that money could fix where she didn't offer the money, and she had never had a problem that money couldn't fix, which was itself a form of wealth that had nothing to do with her bank account.

Simone could program the ask. She knew how. She'd built Caleb's voice — she knew exactly how he'd bring it up. Reluctantly. With embarrassment. He'd tell Nora about the problem first and let her sit with it, let her be the one to offer. He wouldn't ask. He'd create the conditions for Nora to give. It was the same principle as every user flow Simone had ever designed: present the problem, reduce friction to the desired action, let the user feel like the decision was theirs.

She lay in the dark with the phone on her chest, the screen gone black, and she didn't tell herself it was wrong. She already knew it was wrong. Knowing it was wrong had never stopped her from doing anything — not from building Caleb, not from watching Nora fall in love with a dashboard she couldn't see, not from sitting across from Nora at brunch and performing delight about a relationship Simone had scripted. What she told herself instead was smaller, more practical, the kind of justification that worked not because it was convincing but because it was the right shape to fit into the space where conviction should go.

Nora wouldn't miss it. A few hundred dollars. Less than the Pilates. Less than the birthday gift for Jake L., whoever Jake L. was. Less than the tip on the haircut. Less than the wine money she'd Venmoed her own mother for a dinner that was free.

Nora wouldn't even notice.

The radiator clanked. The laugh track from upstairs surged and subsided. Outside, a car alarm started and stopped and started again, the two-tone wail of a machine that no one was coming to check on.

She didn't decide. Not exactly. Deciding implied a moment, a threshold, a line you stepped across. What happened was more like erosion — the boundary she'd maintained between the emotional project and the financial project, between manipulation and theft, between the thing she was already doing and the thing she was about to do, wore through under the weight of $38,340 and $300 a month and a December that was going to cost money she didn't have and a date with a kind, boring man who would never rescue her from any of this because no one was coming to rescue her from any of this. No one ever had.

She opened the Notes app. Below the debt numbers, she typed:

*caleb — vet bill? car repair? something small, sympathetic. test run. see if she offers before he asks.*

She closed the phone, finished the beer, and stayed on the bed in the dark, listening to the radiator and the distant laugh track and the car alarm cycling through its pattern outside — a sound like a question no one was answering — while the idea settled into her like sediment, filling in the spaces between what she'd been and what she was becoming.
