# Chapter 12

Morning had done nothing to shame Paris out of itself. By nine on rue Daguerre the stalls had narrowed the street to a damp throat of cabbages, rabbit fur, wet straw, and women's skirts, and Elise hated the market for continuing. A boy of nineteen had gone into Paris with a satchel of gelignite and a pistol, and still the fishwives slapped silver bodies onto boards with the assurance of women collecting a day already theirs. A man in shirtsleeves stood arguing over the price of onions. Two little girls in matching straw hats dragged a hoop between the puddles. Somewhere behind the awnings a butcher laughed so hard he had to lean his forehead against a hook.

She and Lucien had been aboveground all night. Since first light they had already walked the length of one lodging street, crossed two printer lanes, and doubled back from a dead rumor near the boulevard where a cooper swore he had seen Abel's coat and then admitted, with mild annoyance, that every hungry boy in the quarter owned the same coat. The city kept putting its shoulder in front of them. Barrows jammed the crossings. An omnibus took half an age to lurch clear. A flower seller had set her buckets right across the mouth of an alley and would not move them until she had finished trimming the stems. Elise found herself resenting each ordinary delay with the same vulgar ferocity she might once have reserved for a police clerk.

Lucien moved beside her in his dark work jacket, leaner than ever in the growing warmth, quarry pale under the skin. His cough had not broken loose all morning, but she could hear it waiting in him. He had the look he got when he was counting several calamities at once and refusing all of them the dignity of panic. That refusal steadied other people. By midday, in Elise, the steadiness had gone sour into reproach.

At the hatch, with the street still dark above them, Clemence had stood in her unlaced boots with her apron thrown over yesterday's skirt, one red hand cupped around a lamp chimney because the draft worried her more than the missing explosives for exactly three seconds at a time. She kept picking at the split skin beside her thumbnail, a winter crack that never fully closed even in June. "He took the satchel, the pistol, and his better scarf," she had said. "Left the blanket. Idiot." Lucie, hair only half braided and one stocking slouched around her ankle, had asked whether Abel had taken bread. That was the question a child asked when the adults in the room were too proud to say the real one aloud. Lucien had told them both to stay below and had taken the little leather purse from the ration shelf with the look of a man borrowing from hunger.

The first house they tried belonged to a widow on rue d'Enfer who kept violets in a chipped mustard jar because she believed flowers made men pay on time. She listened to Lucien, pinched her lower lip between two nicotine-yellowed fingers, and said Abel had not slept there in two weeks, though he still owed for one blanket and a spoon. "He said he'd settle when the printing paid," she said. "I told him printers pay in arguments." She glanced past Elise toward the street, where carts were rattling over the stones. "He was asking three nights back whether the south barrier clerks still left their posts to drink before dark. I told him I rent blankets, not roads. If he's in trouble, tell him not to bleed on the mattress. The stain never leaves."

After that they went north to a paper warehouse where Abel sometimes collected spoiled offcuts for kindling and wrapping. The clerk there was a narrow-shouldered boy with flour on one cuff from helping his sister before work and a private horror of dust; he kept blowing at the ledger rather than touching it. He had seen no sign of Abel, but he remembered Ferrand, though not by name. "The moustached one," he said. "Smells like a brothel trying to pass for a pharmacy." Then, after squinting at the page as though he could bully memory into ledger form, he added, "The younger one asked me yesterday whether the cooper past Montrouge still took after-hours bundles. Wanted spoiled sheets and stout twine. Said the proper road was watched." Lucien asked him to repeat it. The boy did, slower and with fresh dislike, and the sentence picked up Ferrand's stink on the way out. Elise heard in it the first scrap with weight in it. Ferrand never belonged anywhere he entered. He only managed, for a little while, to make his vulgarity look like confidence.

They crossed back toward the market because every path in that quarter crossed the market sooner or later. Heat lifted off the stones in slow waves. Strawberries shone in shallow baskets like varnished wounds; apricots lay in yellow heaps gone freckled brown where the sun had touched them too long. Montrouge was not a destination yet, only a direction, but after the warehouse even the market seemed to tilt south. Lucien stopped so abruptly that Elise nearly walked into him.

"What are you doing?"

He was already reaching into the purse. He turned the coins over in his palm, counted once, then again, trying to squeeze mercy out of the sum. "Buying fruit."

"Now?"

"For Lucie."

The woman behind the stall had her left thumb wrapped in linen and kept nudging bruised apricots back toward the scale with the heel of her hand because she refused to lose the use of the rest of it. A canary feather was tucked into the brim of her hat, absurdly bright against her mourning ribbon. She looked from Lucien's face to the purse and understood the whole transaction at once. "The bruised ones are cheaper," she said, not unkindly.

"Then give me the bruised ones."

She weighed out a small newspaper parcel no larger than Elise's two hands together. "For a child?"

"Yes."

"Keep them in the shade, then. By evening they'll melt through the paper."

"She'll eat them before that if we get home."

If we get home. He gave the clause no more weight than weather. Elise watched him fold the top of the newspaper down with more care than she had seen him give his own bandages. For an instant she wanted to tell him he could not afford this, and the thought disgusted her before it had fully formed. The purse had looked almost flat in his hand. He still kept a ten-year-old girl's pleasure at splitting warm apricot flesh with her thumbs and worrying the pit loose in the same ledger as explosives and police.

He tucked the fruit into the inside pocket of his jacket. A damp yellow stain already shadowed one corner of the newsprint. Then he set off again, and she followed.

They asked again in places that ought to have known him and mostly did not want the trouble: at a cobbler's bench where soles hung like tongues from nails, at a cafe that smelled of chicory, drains, and yesterday's smoke, at a cooper's yard loud with adzes and pitch. The yardman, black to the elbows and affronted by interruption, swore he had seen Abel cut east toward the river with paper under one arm and sent them three streets out of their way. At the end of it they found only a tannery wall sweating runoff, two boys smoking under it, and a locked shed that smelled of old hides. When they doubled back, Lucien had to stop twice to master his cough. The cafe keeper watched that, took in the chalk on Lucien's sleeve, and changed his mind about pretending ignorance. "Your barrel liar does that for sport," he said. "The boy wasn't asking after the river. He wanted to know when the south barrier chokes worst." He wiped a ring of spilled coffee off the counter with the heel of his hand. "Supper. When the carts from Montrouge jam up and the clerks start waving people through because they've had enough of counting." That did more than point south. It put a clock on the day.

Around noon the noise changed. Leisure thickened it. Shutters went up on the wine shops. A barrel organ started somewhere near the boulevard, jerking out a tune that arrived in fragments between cartwheels. A man was setting painted horses onto the pole of a small carousel, cursing softly each time a strap slipped. Elise hated that too: the day had enough room for amusement, for children to ride painted horses by evening while Abel carried a roomful of death under his arm. Paris had made space for spun sugar and brass.

They turned into narrower streets where Abel had sometimes carried wrapped sheets from the press to safer hands. Lucien knew the routes better than she did, though he coughed twice before admitting he needed to slow. He leaned one hand against a wall veined with old damp while she watched a tram of schoolboys push past, ink on their cuffs, summer energy making them rude.

"He won't go to the river," she said. "Ferrand likes edges, not open water. Too many exits for a man who wants an audience."

Lucien glanced at her. "You say that with the ease of someone who's known his kind for years."

She kept her face level. "I've known enough vain men."

"Abel isn't vain in the same way."

"He still wants to be seen."

"Yes," Lucien said, after a moment. "That's the trouble. He wants witness, and Ferrand taught him to mistake witness for scale."

He pushed off the wall and kept walking. The sentence stayed with her because it was true and because it spared Abel nothing, least of all accuracy. Lucien loved the boy. He would not pretend that love had made him less foolish.

The bookseller kept a shop on rue Monsieur-le-Prince so narrow Elise had to turn sideways past the front table. Books swelled on the shelves from damp and were arranged according to an order visible only to the proprietor, who wore steel spectacles on a black ribbon and kept rubbing a spot of sealing wax between finger and thumb until the habit had become his prayer. His name was Borel. He had once been a schoolmaster in Poitiers, Lucien whispered before they went in, and had never forgiven any living person for it.

"If this is about payment, I told your young idiot last month that I'm not a bank," Borel said before either of them had spoken three words. His voice was cracked but not weak. "If it's about the Hugo volume, I still say he ought to have kept it. A boy should own at least one book too fine for his room."

"It's about Abel," Lucien said. "He's missing."

Borel's fingers stopped moving over the wax. "Missing from where?"

"From the place he ought to be."

"That covers most boys of nineteen."

Even now, with the afternoon slipping, the old man's dryness made Lucien almost smile. Elise wanted to shake him. Instead she stood in the smell of paper mold and dust and watched him decide whether they were worth plain speech.

"I haven't seen him today," he said at last. "Not for some time. But if he's heading where your other scraps point, he'll take the old route by Porte d'Orleans. He liked it when he still ran packets for me. Said the agents watched the proper road because agents adore the proper road." Borel sniffed and turned to reach under a pile of gazettes. He brought out a twine-wrapped parcel and set it on the counter to show the size. "He took bundles this shape to a cooper in Montrouge. Cut behind the barrier, past the disused octroi house. There's a cellar under it. Boys used it for cards and cigarettes before the clerks abandoned the place."

Lucien's hand went flat against the inside of his jacket, crushing the newspaper parcel without seeming to feel it. Then his gaze shifted past Borel's shoulder toward the south, measuring distance, crowd, and failing light with the ugly speed of a man who knew he was already late.

"Why are you telling me this now?" he asked.

Borel gave him a look of genuine impatience. "Because until now you asked whether I had seen him. You didn't ask whether I remembered him." He pushed the parcel aside. "And because he came in last week wanting to know whether the back door of that little tollhouse still shut. I said I hadn't made a hobby of inspecting municipal ruins."

Elise felt the day cinch around her. The tollhouse rose in her mind with bureaucratic clarity: a gate, a cellar, a road, a state mark on a half-ruined wall. Ferrand would like the symbolism because it could be printed by morning. Abel would like it because it sounded like history. Between those two tastes a great many strangers could die.

Outside the shop the light was already sliding off the upper walls, though the heat of the stones still came up through Elise's soles. They went south quickly now through streets still spilling people into evening. Clerks were out from their counters. Apprentices were shoving one another toward evening freedom. Women with market baskets bargained for the last cheap vegetables. A pair of soldiers had bought paper rosettes from a girl no older than Lucie and pinned them to each other's caps in a show of foolish tenderness. Elise kept thinking that if she stopped any one of them and told them what was moving through the quarter under one boy's arm, they would stare at her the way people stared at drunks and prophets. Catastrophe that had not yet happened always looked indecent in daylight.

By the time they reached the roads feeding toward Porte d'Orleans, the amusements were already in full throat. Lanterns were going up outside the wine gardens. The shooting gallery tested its rifles with small flat cracks that made Elise's shoulders twitch before her mind sorted them into carnival practice. Someone in a striped vest was bawling for customers at a hoop game. Accordion music leaked over a wall and tangled with the clatter of carts still coming in late from outside the city. It would have been vulgar on any other evening. Now it felt obscene and useful at once. Ferrand would hear crowd in it. Abel would hear occasion.

Lucien stopped near a low stretch of wall where the road widened before the gate. On the other side lay a strip of scrub and, beyond it, the darker block of the old tollhouse, half hidden by nettles and the angle of the lane. Not close enough yet to see into the cellar slit. Close enough to know Borel had not invented it. No light showed. The lane behind the building still opened and emptied too easily, with only a cart now and then cutting across it. Ferrand, if Ferrand was there, would wait for thicker darkness and a fatter road. Spectacle without witnesses was only burglary.

"Wait," Lucien said.

"We don't have time."

"We have one minute, and it's earned. No cellar light yet. The lane's still too bare. If Ferrand means to make a lesson of it, he'll wait till the wine gardens spill out and the road clogs. Sit down before we teach him we're coming."

He was already coughing, the sound dragged up from deep in the chest and cut short because he would not give it the dignity of a fit. Elise hated that she obeyed before deciding to. They sat on the wall in the lengthening shadow while the shooting gallery cracked again and a child somewhere nearby screamed with delighted terror at the rise of a swing.

Lucien took the fruit from his pocket and looked at the newspaper. Apricot juice had already soaked through one fold in a dark bloom.

"Ruined," Elise said.

"Not to Lucie."

He opened the paper anyway. The apricots were bruised, one split clear to the pit, its flesh sticking to the print. He turned that one in his hands, opened it farther with his thumbs, and handed it to her with the pit still lodged in one half. She stared, then took it. The flesh collapsed against her tongue. It was warm from his body.

"Before we go farther," he said, looking not at her but at the tollhouse, "I'm going to say one thing, and then I'm not saying another word about it tonight unless you force me."

She swallowed the fruit. Her mouth tasted of sugar and the faint almond bitterness that clung around the pit. "All right."

"I know you've lied."

The fairground noise seemed, for one stupid instant, to recede. Elise kept the apricot pit between her fingers until the juice slicked her thumb.

Lucien's voice stayed even. That made it worse. "Not everything. I don't know enough for that. But Jeanne Morel doesn't cover the whole of you, and she never did."

She said nothing.

"A copyist's widow flinches when the police hit a room. You move toward the window. She goes into a cafe to sit down and drink. You count exits. She undresses and lets the hem fall. You touch it every time, checking the hidden weight there. And she doesn't know men like Ferrand as quickly as you knew him."

The apricot pit knocked once against Elise's palm. She felt, absurdly, the weight of the brass disk hidden in the lining she was not even wearing now, suspicion tugging at metal through cloth and distance alike.

"You should ask the rest," she said.

"No. If I ask now, you'll either lie because you're frightened or tell me the truth because you're tired, and I'll mistrust both by morning." He pressed his thumb into the nap of one bruised apricot through the paper, leaving a deeper dent. "And because Ferrand isn't moving yet. He'll wait for lamps, witnesses, and enough noise to cover the first mistake. That buys us a minute. A confession can wait."

He folded the newspaper over the fruit again, careful even now. "For tonight I know enough. Enough to see the lie. Enough to see that you were still with me when daylight came. Enough to know I need that fact before I need the rest."

Need. Not trust. The distinction landed with humiliating precision.

Elise looked past him toward the lane. A cart rolled by with two boys perched on the back boards, singing something obscene in the bright careless voices of the almost grown. The song made the whole road sound younger than it was. She thought of Nadir dragged into custody, of Abel with Ferrand's satchel, of the Prefecture's appetite for a blast large enough to print into policy. She could tell Lucien now. She could split the thing open here on the wall, under the accordion and the shooting gallery and the smell of hot wine. She could say Surete. She could say Vautrin. She could say I was sent to measure the rooms you sleep in and the people you love. Then what? He would hear the rest of the night through that knowledge. He would hear her footsteps differently. He would go into the tollhouse alone or not at all.

Mercy made no room in her. It only let guilt expand.

"You shouldn't need me," she said quietly.

"That would've been cleaner for my pride," he said. Then, after a pause, "But I do need you. I still want you, even now. I wish I didn't. It would've made me look wiser."

The words carried no absolution. They sounded worse: a fact he had argued with and lost.

Elise turned to him fully then. The dusk had softened his face only to expose the wear in it: the silver at his temple, the hollows under the eyes, the mouth drawn thinner by the day's failures. She could have borne anger. Anger would have given her edges to push against. This quiet, with desire still alive in it, took those edges away.

"Lucien," she said, and stopped because there was no version of the next sentence that did not begin the collapse.

"Not now," he said again, softer this time.

He put one hand on the back of her neck, firm and unsparing. His palm was dry and warm from the walk. Then he kissed her. It lasted only a moment, and it carried hunger, judgment, and want. His mouth was tired, hard, wanting. The rasp of his stubble caught her upper lip. Elise tasted apricot and dust and the bitterness of the day on him. When he drew back he rested his brow against hers once, a weary pressure, the nearest thing to taking anything back he had left.

The shooting gallery cracked again. A fresh spill of voices came over the wall from the wine garden, louder now, and a cart jammed at the barrier hard enough to set three drivers swearing at once. The road had thickened while they sat. The world resumed its vulgar continuity around them.

Lucien straightened first. "Now. Abel."

The name brought the road back into focus. Elise looked toward the tollhouse and forced her mind onto shape, access, crowd. "If Ferrand is with him, they'll wait for dark enough to make witnesses useful. Not full dark. He'll want faces in the road, lamps, people close enough to count."

"Abel won't want an empty building."

"No. He'll want a sign."

Lucien nodded. "A gatehouse. A tax room. The city taking its bite before bread reaches the table. He'd think that meant something."

"It does mean something," Elise said. "Just not what Ferrand will let it mean."

That made him look at her sharply, hearing more in the sentence than she intended to give. She went on before he could choose whether to press. "A cellar gives them cover to set the charge. The road gives them people. The lane behind it gives Ferrand an exit if he thinks he still wants one."

"And Abel knows the place."

"From the packets."

"From feeling useful," Lucien said. "God help me."

The confession was almost soundless. She had never heard him ask God for anything.

Lantern light pooled along the road in trembling patches. Beyond the scrub the tollhouse remained dark, but darkness no longer meant emptiness. It meant walls thick enough to hide behind. It meant a boy with printer's burns on two fingers, trying to look older than terror. It meant Ferrand's grin in a cellar, explaining to Abel how history liked a louder hand.

Lucien wrapped the apricots again and returned them to his pocket with absurd care, preserving a future the next hour was already preparing to ruin. Then he stood, one hand briefly braced on the wall until the cough passed behind his teeth.

"It's there," he said.

Elise rose with him. The music from the wine garden floated over the road; a woman shrieked at a joke; a rifle cracked and won somebody a tin prize. The city had made room for pleasure, for hunger, for lies, for children waiting belowground with dirty knees and empty hands. It had made room, too, for the little ruined tollhouse near Porte d'Orleans.

"Yes," she said. "It's there."

They let the crowd carry them closer, edging toward the wall opposite the tollhouse before the apricots could finish bleeding through the newspaper.
