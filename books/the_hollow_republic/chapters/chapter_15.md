# Chapter 15

By noon the quarry had turned close. Early summer aboveground meant the air downstairs stopped moving; lamp heat and human breath sat under the limestone and would not lift. Elise knew the brass marker was gone before Clemence said a word. The hem of her dress felt wrong against her shin, stitched edge rough where somebody with steadier hands than hers had opened it and sewn it shut again. Each step tugged the bandage tight around her left ribs. The blast had left that whole side raw and yellowing, and the pain kept drawing her palm back to the empty place in the cloth. Wanting made a fool of muscle; her hand kept searching for metal.

She found Clemence in the ration room with the door half shut against the gallery noise. Bread, onions, two wedges of hard cheese, and the week's lamp-oil ledger were spread over the table in the order Clemence liked: food first, then numbers, then the things that made the food visible after dark. A child's stocking with the heel worn thin hung from her apron string, waiting for evening and a needle. She was cutting the noon loaf into shares so even they looked accusatory.

"You came on your own," Clemence said without lifting her head. "Good. Shut it."

Elise closed the door. The latch sounded louder than it should have.

Clemence finished the slice in her hand, set the knife down, and drew the marker from her apron pocket. Brass on wood. Small enough to hide in a hem, heavy enough to settle a life. She placed it between them beside the bread crumbs.

"Name."

Elise's mouth dried at once. "Clemence, let me tell it properly."

"You can tell it any filthy way you like after. Name first."

The room smelled of onion skins, old soap, and the damp wool dress Clemence had hung too close to the stove. Elise watched her own reflection bend in the coin-bright brass.

"Elise Valette."

"Again."

"Elise Valette."

Clemence nodded once, small and accounting-precise. "And what are you, Elise Valette?"

Elise put both hands on the table because she did not trust them at her sides. The burn scar inside her left wrist caught against a crumb. "A confidential operative with the Surete."

"A paid liar, then."

"Yes."

Clemence breathed out through her nose, and the sound held neither shock nor full fury yet. It had narrowed into something harder, the kind of breath a woman used when a wash tub split and the water went over the floor after three hours' work.

"How long?"

"From before I came in."

"I know that. Don't waste my time pretending I'm stupid." Clemence tapped the marker with one nail. Her knuckles were rough and red from lye. "What were you sent for?"

"The refuge. The press. Names. Routes. Whoever Vautrin could make into a case."

"Lucien?"

Elise felt the answer before she said it, a fresh pull under the bandage. "Yes."

Clemence looked at her then. Her face did not open into outrage. That would have been simpler. It stayed flat with work, the way it did when a pot boiled over and there were still three more things on the fire.

"He was the point."

"Yes."

"He wasn't one name among many."

"No."

Clemence slid the marker back toward herself and closed her hand over it. "My girl slept with her head against your arm after the raid on the yard."

Elise said nothing.

"I boiled your bloody shift in my own pot yesterday. I cut the cloth off your side because you were shaking too hard to hold still. I thought I was tending a woman who had been stupid in one direction and brave in another." Clemence's voice stayed brisk, almost dry. That was worse than if she had shouted. "Turns out I was rinsing state brass out of my own wash water."

"I never put Lucie on paper," Elise said. "I didn't put you there either. I left you both out from the beginning."

"Don't hold that out like it washes you."

"I know it doesn't."

"You walked in here to price us. I opened your hem with my own hands this morning and found state brass stitched where the thread ought to have been."

The words landed clean because they were clean. Elise had no counter to them that was not another arrangement of guilt. She could feel the old training trying to rise anyway, the one that took damage and sorted it into order before it became panic. Facts. Sequence. Leverage. She hated herself most in the moments when that part still worked.

"Ferrand was police too," she said. "He wasn't like me. He was worse. He was paid to make something bloody enough to print. I learned it after the raid. I warned Lucien what I could without saying what I was."

"And Abel is still dead."

"Yes."

"And Nadir is still dead."

"Yes."

Clemence picked up the knife again, not to threaten, only because her hand wanted work. She shaved a strip from the cheese rind and set it aside for soup. "Did you know what they were doing to Nadir?"

"No." Elise heard how fast that came and forced herself not to say more until the truth found its own shape. "I didn't know while he was alive. I knew Vautrin wanted a case. I didn't know how far they'd gone with him until the papers lied."

"The papers always lie."

"Yes."

The quarry held its sounds the way a chest held a cough. Somewhere deeper in the passage somebody dropped a pan. The metal rang once and died. Elise could hear Lucie reciting letters in the schoolroom beyond the wall, too loud on the ones she liked, softer on the ones that still felt foreign in her mouth.

Clemence said, "How close are they now?"

"I don't know for certain."

"Then why am I giving you daylight?"

That stung because it was practical. Clemence did not care whether Elise suffered. She cared whether any of this filth might still turn boots away from the passage for one more hour.

"I can still learn enough to matter," Elise said. "He'll want something after the tollhouse. He doesn't have the current map or the entrances as Lucien keeps them now. If I can hear what he thinks he knows, I can bring it back before he moves."

"If."

"If."

Clemence looked at the bread, the ledger, the stocking, then at the woman across from her, measuring what one more danger would cost in a room already full of mouths and blankets.

"You tell Lucien before evening bread," Clemence said. "In your own name. In your own mouth. I'm not doing that labor for you."

"I will."

"You tell him everything that concerns this place. If I hear you left out one fact that mattered because it made you look less foul, I will finish the work the blast started."

Elise believed her.

"Before that," Clemence went on, "you go see your inspector and you find out what sort of ruin he is buying. Engineers, smoke, a cordon, a plain grab in the street, I don't care. Bring back something solid I can put in front of Lucien before evening bread."

"Why give me the hours at all?"

Clemence's face changed then, not softer, just more tired. "Because if he's already moving on us, I would rather know by noon than by the sound of boots." She stripped off the child's stocking from her apron string, folded it once, and shoved it into her pocket. "And because Lucien has buried enough boys this week. I'm letting you hand him one lie less than I had to."

Elise swallowed. The room tasted of limestone dust and onion.

"Take the brown shawl," Clemence said. "Your sleeve is burned through. You look like trouble from half a street away."

She threw it across without looking. Elise caught it one-handed against her sore side.

"Clemence."

"Don't say thank you to me. Go."

Elise wrapped the shawl around the scorched shoulder of her dress and left with the brass marker still in Clemence's fist.

***

Aboveground the heat came at her all at once, not clean warmth but the rank Paris mix of horse sweat, drains, cabbage water, and carriage grease cooking in the sun. She had spent enough weeks in the quarry that the light felt excessive now. Every surface reflected. Window glass, shop signs, a knife on a butcher's board, the bare forearm of a woman leaning from a wash line to shake crumbs into the gutter. The city was full of people buying fruit and arguing over fish while the papers had already turned two dead men into a public warning and a third into an erased body by breakfast.

Elise took the long route north because habit still made her check reflections and crossings even with her side on fire. She passed a newspaper board with Ferrand's name big as theater type and Abel's smaller beneath it, the two of them flattened into one public warning for respectable readers. Delatour's name had not made the headline yet. That omission felt temporary. The state liked sequence. First the dead boys, then the plot, then the schoolmaster at the center explaining it all with the wrong face in the dock.

The stationer's on rue Saint-Jacques kept copybooks in the window, black mourning borders on the top shelf and school primers below; grief and instruction shared the same glass. Elise had used the back room twice before for exchanges Vautrin did not want entered at the Prefecture desk. The owner never asked questions. He only rang the bell once when she stepped in, then busied himself with sealing wax as though women came in bleeding through their stays every day.

Vautrin was in the rear office by the window, which he had opened half an inch and no more. Dust still managed to get in. He had laid his gloves on the desk with the fingers matched precisely. Even empty, they kept parade order. His cuffs were cleaner than the room deserved.

He rose when she entered, not out of courtesy so much as inspection.

"Mlle Valette," he said. "You look as though the Republic has been making waste of you."

"That was always part of the arrangement."

"Sit down."

She stayed standing. The chair would have forced her ribs against the wood.

Vautrin noticed, filed it away, and sat alone. A narrow envelope lay beside his blotter. On top of it rested a blue Charenton account sheet, folded so the amount due showed above the crease. Elise saw the figure before she could stop herself.

"I expected you sooner after the tollhouse," he said. "But you are here, which suggests you still understand the difference between injury and folly."

"Say what you want."

"I should think that has become obvious." He touched the envelope with two fingers. "Your stipend for the month. Ninety francs. An additional twenty because the incident produced visible damage. I can also have the Charenton balance taken off today's stack before close of business. Your mother need not pay for your recent instability."

The words did what they were meant to do, but not in the heart. They struck lower, in the stomach, where hunger and money and fear of letters from institutions lived in the same wet place. Ninety francs, and twenty more for the visible damage. The monthly twenty-eight francs kept Charenton from writing again in that ugly blue hand. It was enough to buy linen that did not smell of damp strangers, enough to keep a clerk from moving her mother to a dirtier ward out of spite or routine or because the right signature had not come in time.

She said, "And in return?"

Vautrin's heavy lids lowered slightly, amused by the necessity of asking. "The quarry map on linen backing. The current entrances. The names of whoever remains. Most importantly, a signed statement that Lucien Delatour housed Ferrand, retained Saint-Clair after the theft, and directed the conspiracy that culminated at the tollhouse."

"Directed." She could not keep the contempt out of it.

"Do not be childish. I am not asking you to invent a dragon. Only to place the existing bodies in their proper administrative order." He steepled his fingers. "Ferrand lodged among them. Saint-Clair acted from within their circle. Delatour maintained the refuge and exercised authority there. The court does not require lyric truth. It requires a chain."

"A clean statement," Elise said.

"Exactly."

"A lie fit for a docket."

"A statement the court can process. You have always understood the distinction."

She had. That was what made the room dangerous. Nothing in his voice sounded monstrous. He made it sound reasonable in the old office language, the one that made starvation into arrears and beatings into necessary pressure and men like Nadir disappear into corrective procedure. She had spoken it herself for years. More than once she had survived by speaking it better than the men in the hall.

Vautrin watched her face and went on in the same patient tone. "The alternative is not noble ruin, Elise. Let us leave novels aside. The alternative is that you become a deniable woman with a damaged body and no sponsor, attached to a circle that now knows what you are. That is not tragedy. It is merely bad administration. I am offering you re-entry while you can still take it."

"Re-entry to what?"

"To salary. To use. To being protected by the office that made you worth paying in the first place." He slid the sealed paper half an inch toward her. "There could be clerical work later if you have finally lost your taste for sentiment. There could even be less field exposure. I do reward correction when it arrives in time."

Elise looked at the wax seal and thought of her mother combing the same strip of hair fifty times because the attendants only noticed quiet patients. She thought of the last time she had visited Charenton, the iron smell under the wash water, the way one old woman had gnawed the edge of her own blanket into strings because nobody had given her enough to do with her hands. Twenty-eight francs a month bought the difference between a cleaner corner and a bucket set closer to the bed. It bought a nurse remembering a name. It bought extra soap. It bought the right not to have a woman left in her own filth until afternoon.

Vautrin knew where to press. The blue Charenton sheet sat between them like a tray of instruments.

"You have something for me," he said.

Elise had, in fact, brought something. Since the raid, when the milk-ink slips went into the drain, one habit from the old life had survived all the same. When fear grew too large to carry whole, she turned it into headings. She had written by lamplight at dawn because pain and dread had pushed her back into order: survivors, injuries, newspaper lies, the wine-stair entrance, how long Lucien's cough had lasted in the dark before he mastered it. She had folded the page and tucked it behind her stays before climbing aboveground, because some foul little chamber inside her still believed a woman ought to arrive at a bargain carrying merchandise.

Now she reached into her bodice and drew out the paper.

Vautrin's gaze sharpened. "There. You see? We are not dealing with melodrama after all."

The sheet was damp with sweat from her skin. Her own hand stared back at her in small, disciplined lines, the ugly efficient script he had paid for and improved. Abel dead. Ferrand dead. Belkacem officially dead. Delatour: coughing, diminished, still central. One entrance possibly compromised after the blast. One child omitted again. It was all there, almost tender in its accuracy. That was the obscenity of it. The state liked a page it could file more than truth, and she had been trained to supply one.

The office lamp stood ready on the desk because Vautrin disliked shadows on documents. Elise held the corner of the sheet to the chimney.

For the first second he did not understand what she was doing. Then the edge browned, curled, and caught. Milk-pale fibers darkened. Her neat hand began to blister away.

"Elise."

She held the page until the flame bit her fingers. Lucien's name blackened first, then split. The line about the entrance went next, then the count of blankets in the sleeping gallery, then all of it collapsed into a twisted strip that dropped ash across Vautrin's blotter.

He remained seated. He picked up one glove, brushed the ash into a narrow gray strip, and nudged the Charenton sheet back into line with the desk edge before he spoke.

"That was the last thing I had for you," she said.

"No." Vautrin folded the glove once, neat as a clerk closing a file. "The last thing you have is the map."

"You'll have to find it without me."

"And Delatour's statement."

"Without me."

He looked at her burned fingertips, at the singed paper on his desk, at the woman who had just scorched her fingers to keep one page out of his file.

"This is a sentimental choice," he said.

"No." Elise shook her stinging hand once. "It's an expensive one. That's different."

"Expense is not virtue."

"I know."

For the first time something almost like fatigue touched his mouth. It was not pity but annoyance at waste. "You have been among them too long if you think refusal cleans you."

"I don't think it cleans anything."

"Good. Then we remain in the vicinity of reality." He folded his gloves one over the other and set them in his hat. "Listen carefully. The papers can be made to wait a little while longer for Delatour if I wish it. Engineers cannot be made to ignore stone forever. Old surveys exist. Street reports exist. Blind searches also exist, and blind searches break more than a guided one. You are declining the guided version."

The warning landed because it was almost generous in form.

"How long?" she asked.

"Long enough that you should use the afternoon well. Not so long that you should mistake it for mercy." He stood. "As for the asylum account, when the office writes next, do not imagine feeling will answer the letter."

She believed that too.

At the door he added, "If you change your mind, you know where to find me. If you do not, someone else will eventually find the quarry. You are not indispensable, Mlle Valette. You are only early."

She stepped back into the stationer's shop with the smell of burned report on her fingers and the blue Charenton figures still crawling under her skin.

Outside, the newspaper board had not changed. Ferrand still grinned in print. Abel was still reduced to a cautionary youth. Nadir was still dead in a sentence nobody had earned. Elise bought nothing, took nothing, and walked south hard enough that the wound under her bandage began to seep again.

***

By the time she came down the wine stair the quarry had entered its evening hour, when bodies tried to pretend routine could still hold. Broth steamed from the cook fire. A child laughed too loud and then stopped because the room remembered itself. Men came in dusty from odd work aboveground. Near the ration room, somebody shook limestone grit from a blanket. The familiarity of it struck her harder now that it was almost over. She had learned the shelter the way a thief learned a house: first for exits, then for habits, then unwillingly for the people breathing in it.

Clemence was waiting near the schoolroom arch with her arms folded under her apron. The brass marker had disappeared again. So had any room for postponement.

"He's in there," she said.

"Did you tell him?"

"Not my job." Clemence moved aside. "Don't take all night."

Lucien was alone except for Lucie's cracked slate on the bench and the white quarry wall behind him smudged with the day's lesson. He had looped thread around one split corner of the slate and was drawing it tight, trying to keep the wood from peeling farther. The work made his long fingers look older. A paper fox, softened by damp, leaned near the lamp on the ledge above his shoulder. Nadir had folded it weeks ago for Lucie out of a rejected broadside; one ear was starting to collapse.

Lucien glanced up when Elise entered. His face had gone quarry-pale again in the last two days, not from dust alone. The cough had thinned him. Grief had done the rest.

"Clemence said you needed to speak to me before supper," he said.

Elise shut the schoolroom door, and for a stupid second the movement reminded her of nights when shutting a door meant privacy instead of judgment. The memory was bodily, treacherous. His shirt open at the throat. Chalk still on his fingers when he put his hand between her shoulder blades. The rasp in his breath against her neck. She pushed it down before it could become begging.

"My name isn't Jeanne Morel," she said.

Lucien set the slate on the bench and let the silence stand.

"It's Elise Valette."

Still he waited.

"I came into the quarry under orders from Inspecteur Vautrin, anti-anarchist section. I was meant to map the refuge, identify the press, report names, habits, entrances, anything that could be turned into arrests."

"This refuge in general," Lucien said, and even now his voice held that teacher's steadiness, the one that kept panic from getting its own grammar.

She could have lied a little longer. She could have said yes and left the sharpest blade wrapped. Clemence had already warned her off that instinct.

"You in particular," she said.

The stillness after that altered the room's balance. Something load-bearing had cracked.

Lucien looked at her a long moment. Then he said, "From the first day."

"Yes."

"From the schoolroom."

"Yes."

He turned his head aside and coughed once into his fist. The cough deepened, caught, came again. By the time it loosened, his eyes were wet with the force of it. He wiped the heel of his hand across his mouth and looked at the smear as though offended by his own body for choosing this moment.

"I knew parts of your story were false," he said. The sentences had shortened, just as pain always forced them to. "I knew there was something trained in you. I didn't think the Prefecture had sent it to sleep in our beds."

Elise took the words without moving.

"How much did you send?"

"At first? Enough to matter. I didn't send everything. I never sent everything." She forced herself to stay plain. "I left Clemence and Lucie out from my first report. I left out whole chambers when I could. After the raid I burned my cipher slips."

"But before that."

"Before that, yes. I sent names. Movements. What I believed was relevant."

"Relevant to whom?"

"To him."

Lucien nodded once. That was all. It was worse than if he had struck the wall.

"And Ferrand?" he asked.

"I learned in May that he was being paid to produce a bomb case. I warned you to move the press because of him. I should have told you the whole truth then. I didn't."

"No."

"Today Vautrin offered me the salary back. My mother's account at Charenton, money, protection. He wants the map and a statement that you directed Ferrand and Abel both."

"Did you give him either?"

"No."

"Why should I believe that now?"

She held up the hand with the reddened fingers. The burn was small, ugly, and immediate. "Because I burned the last report I had on his lamp."

Lucien looked at the fingers, then back at her face. His expression stayed closed.

"That proves you burned one page."

"Yes."

"That proves you burned one page, not your soul."

"No."

She had not expected mercy, but hearing the refusal named so plainly made something inside her pull hard enough that the room blurred for a second. She steadied herself against the bench with the cracked slate.

"If you need details, ask them," she said. "I'll give you all of it now."

"All of it." He gave a short breath that was not laughter. "That would take longer than we have."

The paper fox on the ledge tipped further toward collapse in the quarry damp. Elise wanted stupidly to fix it. Her hands stayed where they were.

"Did you ever put Lucie in danger by what you sent?" Lucien asked.

"No."

"Clemence?"

"No."

"Nadir?"

That was harder, because the answer was not a clean sentence. "I don't think any direct report led them to him. But I wrote things that fed the machine that took him. I can't sort the percentages for you."

"Don't ask me to."

"I'm not."

He came away from the wall then, one step only, enough that the lamplight found the hollows grief had cut under his cheekbones. "What about me, Elise?"

He had not called her that in the quarry before, not once. Hearing her own name in his mouth should have been relief. It felt like a blade entering where a bandage had been holding.

"What about you?" she said, because there was no safe version.

"Was I only work until it became inconvenient to say so?"

The bed came back in particulars: the open throat of his shirt, chalk dust ground into his fingers, the rasp of his breath at her neck.

"No," she said.

"No which part?"

"No, you weren't only work."

"But you were work."

She made herself answer in one piece. "Yes."

His eyes shut for a moment. When they opened again the steadiness had changed texture. Less patient. More final.

"I can understand being watched," he said. "I've been watched by the state since before Abel had whiskers. I can understand being studied, followed, written down. It's an old French habit. What I can't understand is lying down beside me with that in your pocket and wanting me to call it separate."

"It wasn't separate enough," she said. "That's the truest thing I can say."

"It wasn't separate at all."

She could have fought the wording. She did not. The quarry made liars of everyone eventually, but some sentences did not improve under handling.

"You saved lives," Lucien said after a moment, and the fairness of it made her throat hurt worse than accusation had. "I know that too. You kept Clemence and Lucie off your reports. You warned us about Ferrand, though not enough. You went into the tollhouse instead of leaving a boy to become a headline. I know what I saw."

Elise had not realized until then how badly she wanted at least that much kept intact. She looked down before gratitude could shame her.

"But a shelter isn't only walls," he went on. "It's who knows where a child sleeps, who knows which cough means blood and which one means damp, who knows where the map is wrapped and where the extra oil is kept, who gets listened to when the room is frightened. You came inside that knowledge under orders from the men who have already killed enough of us. You don't stay in the middle of it now."

The words were quiet. They hit harder because of that. This was not a public denunciation made for courage. It was an inventory of removal.

Clemence opened the schoolroom door then and stayed in the frame, not interrupting, only present to hear the terms.

Lucien spoke as much to her as to Elise now. "She sleeps in the narrow alcove by the ration room, alone and apart from the women and children."

Clemence gave one short nod.

"She doesn't touch the map," Lucien said. "She doesn't sit in council. She doesn't hear routes, names, reserves, or the places we send anybody aboveground. If there's hauling to do, she hauls. If there are buckets to carry, she carries them. If the police come before dawn, she'll do what she's told like any other pair of hands, and not one thing more."

Elise heard her own future narrowed chamber by chamber.

"Lucien," she said.

He looked at her without cruelty. That would have been easier. He looked at her with the exhausted plainness he gave bad lessons and deaths and budgets that did not balance.

"No," he said. "You don't ask me for a second ruling in the same hour."

She had meant to say the police would move soon, that Vautrin had mentioned engineers, blind searches, stone broken without care. The facts still mattered. She forced the words out plain.

"He said old surveys exist. If he can't get the current map, he may come in blind. He mentioned engineers."

That reached him. She saw the teacher disappear and the organizer take one hard step forward inside the same body.

"How soon?"

"He would not give me an hour. Only enough to tell me not to waste the afternoon."

Lucien nodded, accepted it, and stored it with the things that could still keep people alive.

"Anything else?"

"He wants you alive for the papers if he can manage it."

"Of course he does."

Silence again. Elise knew she should stop there, while the room still belonged to necessity and not to whatever was left between them. Instead she said the thing that kept scraping at her throat.

"What we had wasn't false."

Clemence's mouth hardened at once, but Lucien only looked tired beyond speech.

"Don't ask me to split the same night in two," he said. "Tenderness on one side, the trap on the other. I won't do it. I can't."

The answer was so completely his that it ended the question better than anger could have. Elise felt it settle.

From the gallery came the rattle of bowls and Lucie's voice asking somebody whether there was more salt. Ordinary life, still insisting. For one savage moment Elise wanted to go out there, take the bowls, pour broth, do some common task so the chapter of her life might look less singularly ruined. Then she remembered she had just been assigned a new place in the room, and it was not among the common things anymore.

Lucien put the repaired slate down on the bench and turned toward Clemence.

"Wake the two men who know the lower passage after they eat," he said. "I want the south cut checked tonight. If the old survey lines are already in police hands, it matters more than the wine stair. And bring me the map."

He asked Clemence for the map and went on speaking to the others, leaving Elise outside work she could still have done. The exclusion was complete in its practicality.

Clemence jerked her head toward the door. Elise moved.

At the threshold she stopped once, because stopping hurt less than leaving cleanly.

"Lucien."

His back stayed to her.

"I am sorry" would have been obscene in that room. "I loved you" would have sounded like theft. "Trust me now" was impossible. No sentence she owned could cross the distance without insulting it.

So she left with nothing said.

Clemence took her down the side passage to the narrow alcove cut beside the ration room, barely more than a notch in the limestone with room for one pallet and a pair of boots. Somebody had already laid spare blankets there with an old coat rolled under one end for a pillow. It was not a pallet cast into the street, and it was not one kept inside. It was the plain measure of what remained possible. No one in the passage asked what it was doing there. That meant the story had already started moving without her.

"You heard him," Clemence said.

"Yes."

"If you bring them here before night, I won't wait for Lucien next time."

"I know."

Clemence studied her a second longer. Up close the lye had split the skin beside her thumbnails. There was flour on her sleeve from slicing bread and a new gray thread caught in the hair she had pinned up too fast. Little domestic accidents of a woman still keeping a world going while others broke it.

"Lucie asks after you," Clemence said. "I told her you were sleeping by the ration room because the inner chamber cough was keeping people awake. That lie is for her, not for you. Don't build anything on it."

Then she went back toward the schoolroom.

Elise sat on the pallet carefully, teeth set against the pull in her side. The alcove held ration-room air: cold bread, onion skin, lamp oil, the blunt soap Clemence shaved into the wash water, and blanket wool that never dried all the way in summer closeness. Voices carried straight through the narrow opening, close enough for her to hear bowls set down and too far to catch the words once they dropped low.

After a minute Lucien's voice reached her anyway, practical now, already moving beyond the wound she had made. He asked for the map. He asked who among the adults could still carry weight in a flood passage. He asked which children had shoes fit for water and which would need rags bound round their feet. Her name never entered it again.

Much later, after the bowls were washed and Lucien's low questions about flood passages and children's shoes had gone thin in the gallery, Elise eased off her boots and lay down fully dressed with one blanket folded under her cheek. The quarry never went fully still, but exhaustion took her before the voices did.
