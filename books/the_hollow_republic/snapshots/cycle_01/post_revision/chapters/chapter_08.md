# Chapter 8

Clemence turned the bread sack upside down over the ration table and shook out flour dust, limestone dust, and two black beetles stiff with cold. Nothing else fell. The table was an old door laid across trestles, scrubbed so often the grain stood up like whiskers. Elise watched the empty sack keep its shape for a second in Clemence's fists, a poor body's last lie.

There were four loaves. One had gone green at the heel. There were eleven potatoes with pale shoots budding from the eyes, three onions soft at the neck, half a crock of herring clouding in its own brine, a rind of goat cheese wrapped in newspaper, and enough flour for two more suppers if nobody minded grit. Clemence lined the things in rows as if shame improved under order. Beside the food she set what had survived the night intact: one good lantern, one lantern with a cracked chimney, two cakes of soap cut down to mean slivers, a kettle leaking at the seam, six needles, a spool of black thread, and the little tin of chicory that only Lucien pretended was coffee.

What had not survived went on the other side of the table: the handcart, Nadir's composing stick, two bundles of the prison appeal broadside, a good blue basin from the laundry yard, the brass key to Charvet's print room, Marcel Bisset, Etienne Sorel, and Ferrand.

Lucie sat cross-legged on a folded blanket and counted dried beans into groups of five, lips moving with the numbers. Clemence had replaited one side of the child's hair and left the other side to spring loose; the braid stopped neat behind one ear and the rest of her head had given up. Each time Lucie reached five she clicked a bean against the lid of the tin as if it were a coin.

"Say it proper," Clemence said without looking at her. "If you're counting, count for use."

"Five, ten, fifteen, twenty-three," Lucie said.

"Twenty-five."

Lucie frowned at the beans as if they had insulted the numbers. "Then two are hiding."

"They'll fit the room."

Nadir gave a short breath that was not quite a laugh. He had washed his face, but the blood remained under his nails and in the seam of one ear. A spoiled proof sheet had somehow become a paper fish in his fingers while he listened; he flattened it when he noticed Elise looking. "The room's full of excellent examples today."

He stood a little sideways, as he always did, his nicked cheek pale against the limestone. The sleeve of his coat had dried stiff where Colombe's blood had soaked into it. Colombe Bisset lay on two mattresses pushed together by the wall, one boot cut away, her right ankle wrapped in towels gone pink and then brown. She kept turning the silver thimble on the black ribbon at her throat with her thumb and forefinger, as if a hem still waited for her somewhere outside this chamber. Every time someone said Marcel's name, the thimble clicked lightly against her collarbone.

Abel paced behind the trestles, too young for the look of stubble he was trying to wear. His jacket sleeves still stopped short of the wrists no matter how often he tugged them down. When he wanted to interrupt and thought better of it, he rubbed the two burned fingertips of his right hand together until the skin whitened. He had been doing it since Clemence began laying out the loaves.

Lucien sat at the end of the table with a pencil and the marbled school copybook Elise had seen in the lessons room. The cover had been stitched back together with blue thread, neat as a surgeon's work. He did not use it for speeches. He wrote full names in it, spelled the way people spelled themselves when the police were not helping. He had already made Elise give the room twice since they came back below, every stair and shout of it, and now he was setting Nadir to the same wreckage. Beside Marcel Bisset he had already put last seen on the landing. Beside Etienne Sorel he had written courtyard, separated in the crush. Ferrand's name he had left without a note, the pencil hovering above the paper for so long that Elise could feel Clemence's temper building from across the table.

"Cross him off," Clemence said at last. "He ran before the room even broke."

Lucien did not look up. "I can mark that he vanished. I can't mark him dead or gone for good because you want him gone."

"I want bread more than I want him gone."

"That's not the same question."

"It's exactly the same question if he comes back hungry."

Elise kept her face empty and still. The quarry smelled of wet wool, old chalk, lamp smoke, and the herring brine Clemence had uncorked. It was warmer than it had been in April, but not truly warm. The cold had only moved inward, into the blankets, into the stone bench, into the place in her skirt where the cipher slips had once ridden flat against her thigh. She felt their absence like a missing tooth. It made every silence harder to hide behind.

Lucien wrote vanished after Ferrand's name. His cough caught him on the second syllable of something he had meant to say next. He turned his head and pressed a handkerchief to his mouth before the cough could open wider. When he straightened again, the schoolmaster's gentleness had returned to his face, but thinner now, stretched over a harsher reckoning.

"Tell me again," he said to Nadir. "From the courtyard."

Nadir's expression shut another notch. "You heard it the first time."

"I know. Tell me again."

Nadir set the ruined paper fish on the table beside the cheese rind. "We had the bundle, the money, and Charvet's key. Marcel was ahead. Etienne was on the stairs with the cart. I heard boots below before I heard shouting above, which meant they had both ends. Ferrand went left so quickly I thought he'd been yanked by a wire. Colombe slipped on the second landing and the stone for the handbills came down on her foot. Marcel came back for her. That was the last clean sight I had of him."

Colombe's fingers tightened on the thimble. "He'd come back for a dropped spoon. Don't make him sound noble."

"I wasn't," Nadir said.

"Then don't make him sound dead either."

No one answered that. Water ticked somewhere in the deeper passage, slow and steady as a clock the quarry had made for itself. Overhead, faint and broad, a carriage went by in the street. Elise thought of the city moving above them with butcher's errands and laundry wagons and clerks late to offices, all of it balanced on this bad white stone.

Clemence pushed the chicory tin aside and took up the pencil herself for a moment, not to write but to point. "Nine mouths here if Ferrand keeps running. Ten if Marcel crawls back. Eleven if Etienne does. Twelve if Colombe can keep broth down. I've got enough for two days decent, maybe three if we water the soup until it tastes of the pan and not the food. Oil for two nights if Lucie doesn't read and nobody sews. Soap enough to keep the wounds from turning black if nobody wants clean shirts. After that we can eat Abel's verses."

"I'd be honored," Abel said, too quickly.

"You won't be when I start with your better adjectives."

Lucie looked up from the beans. "If Marcel's arrested, do we still save him some?"

The question landed more cleanly than the adult speech had. Lucie's fear always went for the practical part first, as if facts were small handles cut into a dark wall. Elise saw Lucien hear it and fail, just for an instant, to answer his pupil.

"He still belongs to us," he said.

"That isn't what she asked," Clemence said.

Lucie did not let either of them off. "Does he get our dinner if he's in prison?"

"No," Clemence said. "Prison gives you other slop."

Lucie nodded once, unsatisfied but informed. Then she asked, "Does Etienne still have the cart?"

"If he does," Nadir said, "he's had a very bad morning with it."

Abel stopped pacing. "We can't sit on our arses staring at potatoes while they're taking people. We go back out. We find the stations. We ask at the hospitals. We ask the concierge in the next house. Somebody saw something."

"You don't ask a concierge anything with your face looking like a proclamation," Clemence said.

"My face looks normal."

"Your face looks like it wants witnesses."

Abel colored. Elise had seen him take insult with more grace when the insult came from older men. From Clemence it hit the place where hunger and boyish pride still lived together. He glanced to Lucien for rescue, and Lucien, because rescue came easier to him than refusal, gave him a version of it.

"The impulse isn't wrong," Lucien said. "The order may be."

"Then give the order," Clemence said. "Who's first?"

That was the moment Elise understood what the morning had been building toward. Until then they had still been pretending the laying out was the work. Line up the loaves, say the missing names, spread the coins flat, and some decent answer might rise from the table. But that was only what one did before the knife went in. After that came choice.

Lucien looked at the notebook, then at Colombe's swollen foot under the towels, then at Lucie and the loose braid down one side of her head, then at the two missing names on the page that were not Ferrand's. He had moral authority underground because he could make a room feel larger than the fear inside it. He could teach children letters on a quarry wall and make the chalk seem more solid than the police. He could tell a starving man he still belonged to history and nearly make it sound generous. But now the room wanted a butcher's order, not a teacher's mercy.

"Colombe needs a bone-setter," he said. "If the foot dies, it won't matter whether Marcel returns."

Colombe pushed herself up on one elbow with a curse that came out thin. "Marcel first. Set the foot after. My brother turns stupid when frightened. He can't hold his water when men shout at him. You know that."

Lucien flinched, not from the vulgarity but from the fact. He had known it. Elise could tell by the way his eyes lowered. He wrote something in the notebook instead of answering.

"The papers first," Nadir said. "If they take the front room or the wine shop gets watched, they'll come looking for names next. Bread matters. Names matter longer."

"Food first," Clemence said. "You can't eat a clean alias."

"No," Nadir said, "but you can rent one."

Abel struck his fist into his palm. "Ferrand first. If that bastard sold the room, he'll sell the rest."

Clemence did not even turn. "You're assuming he had anything to sell besides his own hide."

"He ran."

"So did sensible men in other centuries. That proves nothing except legs."

"Enough." Lucien's voice did not rise much, but the word carried. Then it thinned again. "Enough. Colombe's right to ask for Marcel. Nadir's right about the papers. Clemence's right about the food. Abel isn't wrong that Ferrand's a danger now whether he betrayed us or only proved himself worthless. I'm not pretending any of that is smaller than it is."

"So who's first?" Clemence asked again.

He took too long.

The pause sagged under its own real weight. Theatricality would have been easier to forgive. Lucien had simply frozen there, trying to keep from cutting one life away from another and discovering that all his principles had been built for rooms with a little more bread in them. Elise knew the type of delay. She had committed it in offices with different stationery. Put a line between two necessities and the hand learned suddenly to hover.

Lucien made the mistake of answering honestly. "I don't know if there's a just first."

Clemence let out a breath through her nose. "Then we'd better pick an unjust one before the day picks it for us."

The silence after that had weight. Abel looked stricken on Lucien's behalf, which was worse than anger. Nadir looked away altogether. Colombe lay back down and turned the thimble until it clicked against bone. Elise kept her eyes on the beans in Lucie's tin because looking directly at Lucien in that minute would have felt indecent, like watching a man stripped.

He closed the notebook without slamming it. He never did anything hard unless it had been forced on him. "We'll do what we can in parallel," he said, and even he heard the weakness in it.

"With what?" Clemence asked. "Parallel with which hands?"

He had no answer that did not sound borrowed. Colombe answered instead. She shoved herself half upright, face gone the color of candle grease. "Then stop jawing and set it. Or leave it to rot. But stop making me listen while Marcel's out there."

"Lie back," Lucien said.

"No."

Clemence moved first. She swept the bread, onions, and chicory tin to one corner until the old door of the table lay bare. "Elise, lamp. Abel, her shoulders. Nadir, the soap." When Clemence pulled the towels away, the foot looked less like a foot than something a cartwheel had argued with: ankle blown sideways under skin stretched shiny and pale over purple, stocking dried into the split above the heel, grit ground into the blood. Lucie made a small sound and pressed her fist to her mouth.

Clemence slit the stocking with her little shears. Elise held the lantern so close the heat bit her knuckles. As the wool peeled back, two wet crescents of skin came with it and Colombe's scream struck the quarry wall and came back smaller. Nadir shaved one soap sliver down to nothing in a bowl of water gone gray at once. Lucien stood useless for a beat, then put both hands on Colombe's shin when Clemence said, "Now." They tried to draw the foot straighter. Something shifted inside with a thick, wet click. Abel gagged into his shoulder. Lucie crouched by the wall, one fist closed around three beans, her forearms hard against her ears.

Colombe's head rolled back. Her breath sawed once, twice, then disappeared so completely Elise thought she had gone. Lucien let go first. "Enough," he said. "Bind it. No one goes up for Marcel or Etienne before dark. Not with last night's faces still remembered on every stair in the quarter."

Colombe spat the rag from her mouth. "He'll be colder by dark."

"I know," Lucien said, and that was the whole of his answer.

Clemence tore the empty bread sack where the cloth was least foul and wrapped the foot anyway. By the time she was done, the ration table had ceased to be a ration table. It was gray water, bloody wool, the used soap gone to paste, and Colombe shaking so hard the mattress ropes rattled under her. Abel made for the passage once, wild to go in spite of the order. Nadir caught him by the collar and walked him back like a dog that had seen a gate open. Only then did Lucie begin sorting the beans again from the floor, slower now, lips dry with the effort not to cry.

By afternoon the damage had spread from the ration room through the whole of the refuge. Elise helped where she could because work was the only innocent posture still available to her. She carried boiled cloths to Colombe and held the lamp while Nadir rewound the loose screws on the deeper press with a tool too small for the work. She found the second cracked lantern hanging behind the schoolroom bench and discovered that the wick was gone, cut clean, perhaps months ago, perhaps last night; scarcity had a way of erasing dates. In the sleeping gallery she turned blankets and sorted what could still be called dry. Six whole, three sour with old damp, two stiff with blood from the raid. Clemence said nothing when Elise gave her the number, only nodded and marked it on the back of a handbill with a bit of carpenter's chalk.

The schoolroom wall still held the previous week's sums. Lucie had written her name there in an uncertain, proud hand, and beneath it Lucien had drawn a straight line to show her where letters ought to stand if they wished to behave. Beside the slate tray lay his notebook. Elise should not have touched it. She did not. She only looked. The page he had left open held columns of names, dates, addresses gone stale, who had been taken where, who had used which alias in which lodging house, whose mother still believed him in Lyon, whose child ought not to be told until the body was certain. A police file would have shaved all that down to leverage and category; Lucien's notebook seemed built to smuggle their full weight through a century that would prefer labels. Elise felt the old professional reflex stir in her at the sight of good information, then sour. There were forms of exactness that did not deserve the same name.

When Lucien came in behind her she stepped away from the bench at once.

"I wasn't reading," she said.

"You were looking," he said. His cough had left his voice roughened, but not unkind. "That's different."

"Only if the room forgives it."

He followed her glance to the notebook. "It's easier to lose people on paper than in a corridor. Paper gives you permission."

Elise thought of arrest sheets, cross-indexed aliases, the clean bureaucratic pleasure of reducing a body to a usable line. "Sometimes paper is all that's left of the corridor."

He looked at her then, properly, the way he had not managed in the ration room. Exhaustion had rubbed him almost plain. "Were you always this practical, Jeanne, or only when the plaster starts falling?"

Her alias fitted better when he asked nothing personal of it. "I was practical when there was rent due. I stayed practical."

"That sounds expensive."

"Anything that keeps a roof over you is."

The corner of his mouth moved, not into a smile. "Clemence's right that I failed them this morning."

Elise could have lied and called it generosity. It would have sounded kind and he would have heard the false note anyway. "You wanted a clean answer."

"There wasn't one."

"No."

"That didn't stop the room from needing it."

He rested his knuckles on the bench, close to the notebook but not touching it. The healed scar high in his shoulder had pulled the fabric of his shirt a little crooked there; Elise noticed because the quarry taught attention through inches. "If I send for Marcel, Colombe may lose the foot. If I hide the papers first, I'm admitting we'll need to run. If I buy food before I trace Etienne, I choose the ones who are already under my eye over the ones who vanished serving us. Instead I told them dark would decide for me, as if dusk were a principle. I know all of that. Knowing isn't the same as choosing."

It was the nearest he had come, with her, to asking not for absolution but for witness. That was more dangerous than if he had reached for comfort. Comfort could be managed. Witness got into the blood.

"Then stop waiting to deserve the choice," Elise said.

His eyes came back to her, quick and sharp despite the fatigue. For an instant she thought she had spoken too close to her real training, too near the language of orders and acceptable losses. But what he heard in it seemed otherwise.

"You say that as if practice has made it easier."

"Practice makes almost everything dirtier."

Before he could answer, Clemence called from the passage for both of them. Her voice had the cut of a woman who had finished sorting the damage and disliked what remained.

They gathered again in the schoolroom because the ration table had become a bandage station around Colombe. Dusk had not yet reached the surface, but underground the lamps already mattered. Clemence shut the door cloth against the draft and set a round biscuit tin on Lucien's bench. The tin had once held butter cookies for a family that could afford ornament; now the painted lid was scratched white at the edges. She kept one hand on it a second before opening it, as if the warmth of her palm might change its contents.

Inside lay the reserve in ugly little dignities: one fifty-franc note folded down and softened by too many hidings, a five-franc piece, eight single francs, and a spill of centimes wrapped in rag so they would not talk. Beneath the money sat papers in oilcloth: two residence certificates, a widow's booklet with the name not yet inked in, three baptism extracts, a blank labor carnet with a municipal stamp good enough at dusk, and the rubber seal Nadir had once said looked true only if you did not insult it by staring.

"Sixty-three francs and forty centimes," Clemence said. "All told, unless somebody's learned to mint in his sleep. This and the papers can't stay under my bedding now. If they come through here next, they lift the blankets first. Men always think women's beds are purses."

Abel leaned in despite himself. "Give them to me."

Clemence put the lid back on with one flat motion. "I'd sooner tie them to Lucie's ankle."

"I'm serious."

"So am I. You couldn't carry soup across the street without turning it into a cause."

His mouth opened on anger. Nadir cut across it before the speech could swell. "She's right about the need, if not the courier. The room at Charvet's is gone. The old flour niche is too near the front entrance. We need a new hole aboveground by tonight."

"I can take them," Abel said. "Nobody knows me."

"Plenty of people know a nineteen-year-old boy trying to look thirty," Nadir said. "It's a common species."

Lucie, curled on the bench with her chin on her knees, studied the biscuit tin instead of the adults. "If the papers get wet, do the names come off?"

"Sometimes," Elise said before anyone else could answer.

Lucie took that seriously. "Then don't let them get wet."

Clemence looked at Elise for a beat too long. Suspicion had moved beyond intuition now. It had settled into method. Elise could feel it measuring the speed with which she had dropped in the raid, the steadiness of her hands afterward, the way she listened when routes were named. Clemence did not know what Jeanne Morel was, but she knew what she was not.

"Not you," Clemence said.

Elise kept her voice level. "Because you don't trust me or because I'm wrong for the street?"

"Both would do."

Lucien lifted a hand, perhaps to soften it, perhaps to worsen it by trying. "Clemence."

"No, let her ask." Clemence's red hands stayed on the tin. Soap had eaten the skin beside her nails into small white crescents that never healed properly. "You heard me."

Elise could have taken the safer road and stepped back. There had been months of it, months spent watching, reporting, omitting, nudging, and keeping her own body clear of the load whenever possible. That had already led them here: to Colombe's foot, to Marcel's name with no station beside it, to Lucien forced into public helplessness while she stood clean enough to judge him. The thought came without nobility. She did not want to keep her hands empty while other people bled for the room.

"I'm probably the best shape for it," she said. "Widow's clothes, no child with me, no cough, no printer's ink on my cuffs. I know how to carry a bundle and look like the bundle is the whole of me."

Abel stared at her. "Since when?"

"Since before you needed shaving."

Nadir's mouth twitched. Clemence did not.

"And if you decide not to come back?" Clemence asked.

There it was, cleanly spoken. Suspicion had edges now, squared off and ready for a name.

Elise looked at the tin, not at Lucien. "Then you've lost sixty-three francs and forty centimes, a seal, and some lies on paper. If you send Lucien, you lose the route knowledge with legs on it. If you send Nadir, you risk the best compositor you have and a face the street remembers. If you send Abel, you lose the packet to the first policeman who asks him a dull question. If you go yourself, Lucie goes with a neighbor by night because she'll never stay put while you're out. I'm the body this room can spare, if it has to spare one."

Lucien's head came up sharply. "Don't call yourself that."

"I'm telling you what breaks," Elise said.

"Losses aren't neutral just because you keep your voice calm."

"No. But they're still there."

The room shifted around that under pressure rather than agreement. Lucie reached out and put one finger on the biscuit tin's painted lid, tracing the worn gold edge of a flower no one could fully see anymore. Abel looked furious that logic sounded better in Elise's mouth than in his. Nadir watched everyone except Lucien, which was how he watched hardest.

Clemence asked, "How would you carry it?"

That was the first concession. Elise answered at once, because hesitation here would read as invention. "Sewn into the inner hem or under the skirt facing, split in two packets if you want. Papers flat under the stays, money lower where it moves like weight and not like paper. Put laundry over the rest. Let me smell of soap and old water instead of fear."

"Route?"

"Not the wine shop straight through. Out by the yard. Across the Bievre where the mud ruins everybody's boots alike. After that you tell me."

Nadir gave the smallest nod. "That's not stupid."

"Nothing she's said today has been stupid," Clemence said. "That's part of the trouble."

Lucien's expression changed at that, not to surprise. He had been feeling the same thing and disliked himself for it. Elise saw the knowledge pass between them and remain unspoken. A few weeks ago she might have found satisfaction in that. Now it only made the stone seem closer to her face.

"The reserve goes to the woman over the mourning-ribbon shop," Clemence said. "The old flour niche rotted through yesterday and I don't fancy losing the rest of us to damp before the police manage it. She'll put it where it lasts. You go at first light. Not tonight."

Abel made an incredulous noise. "And that's it? We hand her the whole future because she talks like a clerk?"

"No," Clemence said. "Because the future fits in a biscuit tin and a skirt hem now, and I don't have the luxury of pretending otherwise."

Lucien closed his eyes briefly, then opened them on Elise. "If you take it and anything turns wrong, the papers first. Hide them, burn them, throw them in the river if you must. The money matters. The names matter more."

A softer room might have mistaken that for sentiment. Here it came bare as wire: strategy cut to the bone. Elise respected him more for the order than she had for any speech he had made that morning, and the respect made everything worse.

"I'll get them there," she said.

Clemence was already taking out the needle. She did not trust promises when thread would do better. "Stand still, then."

Elise stepped forward. Clemence opened the oilcloth packet and separated the contents with the speed of a laundress sorting stains. The labor carnet and the blank widow's booklet went flat against Elise's stays. The folded note and coins she wrapped in a scrap of chamois and pinned inside the lower facing of Elise's skirt, where the drag would read as bad dressmaking if anyone noticed at all. Her fingers were brisk and unceremonious, tugging cloth, biting thread, testing each stitch with the same ruthless competence she brought to ripped cuffs and burst seams.

"If you run," Clemence said around the pin held between her teeth, "you'll do it dragging my sewing."

"Fair enough."

"If you're searched?"

"I'll cry about modesty and say my dead husband taught me nothing that pays."

Abel snorted.

"What?" Elise asked without turning.

"You don't sound like a widow."

"No one does until they're one."

That shut him. Lucie watched the sewing with grave attention, then said, "My name won't fit in there."

Lucien answered before Elise could. "Your name is staying with us."

Lucie considered that, then slid off the bench and came to stand by Clemence's elbow. "Make the stitches small," she told her mother. "Big ones catch."

"I know my trade."

"I know skirts."

Nadir, almost under his breath, said, "God preserve the police from competent women."

Clemence gave the hem one last hard tug and let it fall. The new weight dragged at Elise's left side, a downward insistence that altered her gait by less than an inch and more than that. Money had its own way of announcing itself to the body. So did paper when paper could decide whether a room was emptied as criminals or dispersed as widows and laborers and cousins with decent parish records.

Lucien picked up the notebook from the bench and tucked it into his coat. For a moment Elise thought he might say something private to her, something about trust or the lack of it, something that would try to clean the uglier part of what had just happened. He did not. He had learned a little in the course of the day.

"Sleep if you can," he said instead. "You leave before the carts start."

It was almost full dark by the time Elise lay down in the sleeping gallery. She did not sleep. The packet altered every turn of her body. When she shifted, the coins gave one dull, intimate knock against her thigh, like a hand from inside the skirt asking whether she understood at last what she had offered. Around her the quarry settled into its night sounds: Colombe's breath catching when the pain climbed, Lucie's brief talking in sleep, Abel still awake somewhere deeper in the passage and reciting under his breath to keep from thinking, Nadir tearing a proof sheet cleanly down the middle for tomorrow's kindling, Lucien coughing once and then smothering the rest for the sake of the others.

Elise stared into the dark until the white of the wall began to suggest itself beyond the lamp's reach. She had entered the refuge to turn lives into reportable matter. Then she had begun leaving names off pages, hiding the cache list, burning slips. Those had still been forms of observation, however compromised. This was something meaner and more binding. Beneath her hem rode bread, rent, aliases, the right to cross a checkpoint without vanishing into an office. If she failed aboveground, the loss would not be information. It would be supper. It would be the next room. It would be the thin legal fiction by which Lucie might pass for somebody the Republic had no immediate wish to lock away.

Before dawn, when the stone sweated colder and the city above had not yet started its wheels, Elise rose and dressed. Clemence was already waiting by the passage with a bundle of washing over one arm. She pressed it into Elise's hands without greeting. Trust had not grown in the night. It had only put on work clothes.

Elise took the damp weight of the laundry. Under it, under the skirt, under the discipline of her own careful face, the hidden packet pulled at her with every breath. For the first time since she had come down under the hill, she was carrying one piece of their continued existence. Then she turned toward the narrow climb to the wine cellar and the morning above it.
