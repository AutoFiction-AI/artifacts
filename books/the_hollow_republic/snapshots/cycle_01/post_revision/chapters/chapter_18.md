# Chapter 18

Two days after she left La Sante with Lucien's notebook hidden in her bodice, Elise came back to find the visitor line already cinched tight in the heat. The first week of July had gone hot enough to make the prison smell of limewash and old piss before noon. Stone on the outer wall sweated under a film of dust, and every basket handle and shawled shoulder in the queue kept brushing her sleeves. A woman ahead of her carried two hard eggs in a handkerchief. Another had a loaf under her arm and a bruise yellowing along the edge of her jaw. Nobody spoke above the level required by the walls. The prison took sound and flattened it.

The registry window stood open to a room no larger than a pantry. The clerk behind it had one ear puffed and folded from an old blow, and he kept scratching at it with the feathered end of his pen between names. He took visiting slips, glanced once, stamped them, and pushed them back with the same indifferent wrist. When Elise gave him hers, his eyes dropped to the line, then moved sideways to a ledger thick enough to stop a bullet. He found the page, wetted his thumb, and turned another.

"Delatour," he said. He pronounced it cleanly, as if accuracy cost him nothing. "No visit."

"I was told today."

"You were told badly, then."

He drew a small printed form from beneath the ledger and wrote on it without hurrying. The nib scratched. A fly kept touching the inkstone and lifting away again. At Elise's back, the line compressed by another inch.

"What does that mean?" she asked.

He sanded the line, tipped the excess onto the floor, and slid the form through the gap. "It means the prisoner expired in the infirmary at four-ten this morning. Pulmonary congestion, according to the doctor. Effects withheld pending review. Next."

The word expired sat on the page like a tradesman's bill settled and filed. Elise looked at the hour first. Four-ten. He had still been alive when the bell struck the quarter before dawn somewhere over the city. She had been asleep in her room on rue du Cherche-Midi with the notebook under her pillow and a chair shoved under the latch because there was nobody left she trusted not to come in.

"I want to see him."

The clerk had already reached for the next basket. "That isn't done."

"I saw him two days ago."

"You don't see him now."

"I want the doctor."

"You want a petition." He tapped the printed form with the pen. "If you're family, state so in writing. If you're not, don't waste the paper."

The woman behind Elise sucked air through her teeth. Somebody farther back asked whether the beef in a parcel would spoil before the turn came. The clerk lifted his head a fraction and called, "Next," over Elise's shoulder with the steady patience of a man who believed that every grief came in the same size once it reached his window.

She stepped aside because the line made her step. There was no ceremony to refuse. A big woman with red knuckles planted her basket where Elise's hands had been and began asking after a son in the remand wing. The clerk scratched at his broken ear and opened the ledger again.

Outside the gate, the heat hit with more force than it had in the queue. The street had the white glare of afternoon coming early, and a cart full of cabbages was backing badly around a watering trough while the driver cursed the horse with flat professional hatred. A boy ran past shouting headlines about a ministerial quarrel. Another was selling a racing sheet. Nobody shouted about the man who had coughed blood in a cell until the prison doctor wrote congestion on a line and the clerk passed the day forward.

Elise stood with the paper in her glove until the sweat from her palm softened it. The prison had left her a time of death, a cause, and a clerk with an old damaged ear to certify the transaction. Lucien had died under inventory. She could almost see the room anyway: the narrow iron bed, the basin furred with lime, the blanket that never dried properly once the lungs started giving way. He had spent months teaching children to read on quarry stone, and the Republic had found a way to reduce him to a time of death and a cause written in four quick words.

She folded the paper once and walked because standing still would have made her visible.

Her room was under the roof of a widow who rented by the week and watered the wine she sold downstairs more shamelessly with each rise in temperature. The stairwell smelled of boiled onions and dust from the carpet runner. In the room itself, the air had not moved since dawn. Elise shut the door, drew the bolt, and put the printed death notice beside the washbasin. Then she took Lucien's notebook from under the pillow and the other paper from the hem she had stitched inside her valise weeks ago, the one she had copied in police shorthand after slipping through Vautrin's office in May.

That second paper was thin as onion skin and filled with numbers nobody outside a bureau was supposed to care about. Three cash disbursements to Jules Ferrand. Thirty-five francs marked Advance for cultivation expenses, routed through a tobacconist on rue Saint-Jacques. Forty francs marked Supplementary indemnity upon successful introduction. Sixty francs marked Final payment upon delivery of material proof. Elise had copied the dates, the sums, and the initials with the old obedient hand of her trade, then hidden the slip and told herself she was waiting for the right use. There would be no righter use now.

She opened Lucien's notebook.

His entries had never tried to sound noble. That was part of what made them unbearable. He corrected official lies the way other men corrected school exercises: patiently, with exact dates and no appetite for thunder. Nadir Belkacem, compositor, arrested on a delivery run, not foreign unknown. Abel Saint-Clair, nineteen, printer's apprentice, not mastermind. Henri Vasseur, cooper's assistant, arrested in error during a street handoff, not courier chief. Names, wages, kin where known, burial where knowable, and, in the margins, the small stubborn facts that kept a body from being filed under a category and forgotten. Nadir made paper animals for Lucie from spoiled sheets. Abel burned two fingertips on hot type and quoted poems to sound older than hunger. Ferrand had dirty money in him from the day he arrived and treated other men's deaths as a route to supper. Lucien's own name appeared only once, in someone else's cramped later addition, and he had crossed out agitator and written schoolmaster first, then organizer second, as if order still mattered.

Elise sat on the bed with the notebook open over her knees until the ticking from the hall clock reached her through the wall. Publishing any of it would do nothing to the dead. Nadir would stay dead. Abel would stay nineteen forever. Lucien would not sit up because some compositor set his name properly in lead and some newsboy bawled it in the street. Worse, if she let the quarry's whole history out in a single generous gush, the police would follow every name that still breathed and finish what the smoke had started underground. Witness was not confession. Witness required selection. It required her to decide, line by line, who would be abandoned to silence so the living did not get dragged back into daylight and catalogued.

She found a carpenter's pencil in the washstand drawer and turned to the page that had made Lucien crowd his hand into the margin. Clemence Rigal still at the laundry yard on the Bievre. Lucie favoring the left heel since the drainage cut. Ask no questions where steam can carry a voice. The note was half instruction, half care, meant for comrades who might need to find broth, soap, or a blanket, and for nobody else. If she left it in, a plainclothes man could spend one afternoon by the wash lines, watch for a woman with lye splitting the skin across her knuckles and a child limping on one heel, and deliver both of them back into the state's mouth before supper. Elise pressed so hard the graphite snapped. She sharpened the stump with her penknife and scored through Clemence's name, the laundry yard, the child's bad heel, every mark that could teach the police where to stand. The black line furred the paper. On the reverse side the pressure left a bruise she could feel with her thumb. She crossed out two front-room references after that, then a widow's surname, then a ration list that would have mapped the survivors better than any informer. When she was done, the pages she could use were mostly the dead and the paid.

By the time she left the room again, the prison notice and Ferrand's payment slip were in her pocket, and the notebook sat under her arm wrapped in a dish towel to keep the sweat from soaking through the cover.

The print shop on rue des Cinq-Diamants had no sign larger than its own grime. Elise knew it from an imprint at the foot of a mutual-aid circular Nadir had once corrected in the quarry, a modest line in small type that said Vignot et Fils though there had not been a son at the cases for years. The front room sold envelopes, prayer cards, and cheap school copybooks. The press worked in back, where the air turned metallic from ink and hot lead. A canary in a wire cage hung near the window and kicked seed husks onto a pile of damp proofs.

Madame Vignot looked up from a stone table with a composing stick in her hand. Her left thumb had been flattened broad at the tip by years of locking formes, and she wore a man's wedding ring on a black ribbon under her blouse because, in the heat, her fingers swelled too much to take metal. When she moved, the ring tapped lightly against her breastbone. She took one look at Elise's black dress, the wrapped notebook, and the expression she had not managed to school back into usefulness, and she set the stick down without welcome.

"We're not taking memorial verses," she said. "The church can have those."

"I haven't brought verses."

"That's a mercy. What have you brought?"

Elise put the towel-wrapped notebook on the table and laid the prison notice beside it. "Names. Dates. A police agent the Prefecture paid and then called an anarchist when he had served his purpose."

Madame Vignot did not reach for the papers. "They call half the city anarchist now. The paid part is the line that matters. Sit down before you fall over and stain something."

Elise remained standing. "Belkacem worked here sometimes."

The printer's face altered by a degree so small another woman might have missed it. "When he was short of bread. He set faster than any man I ever had, and he never stopped complaining about my commas. If you're using him to buy tenderness, you'll leave poorer than you arrived."

"I'm using his name because they wrote foreign agitator over him after they killed him."

Madame Vignot wiped her hands on a rag already black at the center and finally took the notebook. She turned pages with the caution of someone who respected paper more than sentiment. The canary shuffled once, clicked its beak, and fell quiet. After the first two entries she lifted the prison notice, read the time of death, then took the thin shorthand slip and held it close to the window.

"This is your hand?" she asked.

"Yes."

"You learned it in an office."

"I learned it where I learned it."

Madame Vignot snorted once. "That's not an answer, but it'll do for now." She read the figures again. "If these dates are true, Ferrand was on police money before the tollhouse."

"He was on police money from the start."

"Can you prove the signature?"

"I can tell you which tobacconist's back room they used, which drawer held the cash box, and which inspector signed with initials because he preferred not to leave the whole of himself on a page."

The printer set the slip down carefully. "That's better. Proof is what survives fear. Stories are what fear leaves behind."

Elise heard the dryness in her own voice before she answered. "Then set the proof."

Madame Vignot looked back into the notebook. "How many living names are buried in here?"

"Enough that I broke the pencil getting them out."

"Show me."

Elise turned the bruised page toward her. "Clemence Rigal. Laundry yard on the Bievre. Lucie limping from the drainage passage. Lucien wrote it so someone could find them with broth, not so the police could wait by the wash rails."

Madame Vignot read the scarred-over line and clicked her tongue. "Leave that in and the child lasts till evening."

"If that."

"So there are still people worth hunting."

"There are children worth not delivering."

Madame Vignot met her eyes then, properly, with no more pretense that this was a simple commercial errand. "If I set this, my shop gets searched."

"If you don't, they remain what the Prefecture called them."

"Don't try to bribe me with decency, madame. Ink has never paid rent."

Elise felt the first crack in her own discipline open. "No," she said. "It's only kept some men from being called dogs after death."

The printer's gaze stayed on her a beat longer. Then she called through the back partition, "Lucette, mind the front and stop feeding the bird sugar." A girl answered indistinctly from beyond the shelves. Madame Vignot tugged the ring on its ribbon once, an absent habit, and pulled a galley tray toward her.

"I can give you a column on the second page of tomorrow's issue and a broadside if the formes aren't seized before dawn," she said. "But if this shop prints the death notice and Ferrand's payments alone, the Prefecture will come through my door before the paste is dry. A daily has to force them into public denial first. While they're lying for respectable readers, I can get the names onto walls."

Elise pulled up the stool opposite her. "And if the daily refuses?"

"Then I print anyway and pray my landlord enjoys politics. But don't make me do that if you can help it." Madame Vignot handed her a copy pencil. "You'll sit there, you'll read proofs, and you'll tell me every place the police lied with their nouns."

She began setting the title aloud as she chose letters. "Dead of the Ferrand Affair," she muttered. "No. Too neat. The dead named after Ferrand again." She spat the word to one side. "Names the Prefecture Used Badly. That'll do."

Type clicked against metal. The sounds had a steadier mercy than prayer. Elise read from the notebook while Madame Vignot composed, not every detail, only the ones a printed line could carry without making a legend out of people who had already paid too much for being made into symbols by other hands.

"Nadir Belkacem," Elise said. "Compositor and translator. Twenty-eight. Arrested the third of June during a delivery run. Reported dead in custody after an alleged escape attempt. Body not returned."

Madame Vignot's flattened thumb set each word into place. "Keep going."

"Abel Saint-Clair. Nineteen. Printer's apprentice. Wasn't an organizer. Burned two fingertips on hot type and couldn't stop quoting verse when he was frightened."

The printer's mouth twitched despite itself. "That sounds like half the boys I have trained."

"It sounded like him."

"Then it goes in."

Line by line, the dead came back to the page at the scale they had actually lived: work, age, kin, one habit, one correction. The copy kept Abel's burned fingertips and Nadir's ink-darkened hands, the stubborn human detail that refused to behave like legend. When they reached Ferrand, Elise gave the dates and sums only. Thirty-five francs. Forty. Sixty. Advance for cultivation expenses. Supplementary indemnity upon successful introduction. Final payment upon delivery of material proof. Madame Vignot whistled low through her nose.

"Cultivation expenses," she said. "That reads like instructions for growing a corpse."

"It won't be enough."

"No," the printer replied. "It never is."

She locked the first column, pulled a proof, and shoved it to Elise with fingers still black at the creases. The sheet came out damp and warm. Elise corrected spellings, restored an accent the type had lost, and struck the word fanatic where Madame Vignot had used it ironically in a subheading.

"I won't help them do it from our side," she said.

Madame Vignot glanced at the correction mark and gave a short approving grunt. "Good. We have enough men who turn the poor into theater without charging admission."

When the first page was clean enough to live, the printer lifted the prison notice again. "Take this to a daily."

"A daily won't print the dead properly."

"A daily doesn't need to. A daily needs to print the payments and the death before hearing. Respectable men enjoy discovering that officials are dirty. They enjoy the dead less. Use their appetite where it exists." She named the paper without looking up. "Le Courrier du Soir. Ask for Saunier. He hates the Prefecture because they feed their scandals elsewhere first."

Elise folded the proof into the dish towel with the notebook. "Will he print it?"

"He'll trim it, oil it, and make it fit beside horse racing, but he may print enough. More to the point, if he runs it tonight, I keep my formes till morning." Madame Vignot pulled another sheet from the press and checked the pressure with her thumb. "Go now. If you wait for justice to be ready, the edition closes."

Le Courrier du Soir occupied three noisy floors near the boulevard, and every one of them smelled of dust, wet wool, tobacco, and the electrical panic of men who thought deadlines made them important. A clerk with sleeve garters tried to stop her at the stairs until she set the prison notice in front of him and said the matter concerned Ferrand. Ferrand's name still opened some doors because it had been in every police-fed paper since the tollhouse. The clerk frowned, then sent her up with reluctance and curiosity fighting visibly in his face.

Saunier's office had glass on one wall and no privacy worth naming. He was a narrow man with a pale forelock that refused pomade and a silver fruit knife in his hand. A saucer of soaked raisins sat by his elbow; he ate them one at a time while he read, as if the doctor who had forbidden wine had not yet managed to forbid appetite. He did not offer her a chair until after he had glanced at the death notice and the shorthand slip.

"You're late," he said.

"He died this morning."

"Men usually do choose their moments badly." He pointed with the knife. "Sit."

Elise sat because the room had become small in a way that reminded her of interview cells and office corners, spaces arranged so a person would give more away than she intended. Below the glass partition, copy boys ran with galleys, and someone in the next room was dictating a fire in Batignolles with the delighted speed disasters gave journalists.

Saunier read the payment dates first. "Where did you get this?"

"From the same place Ferrand got his money."

"That answer is cleverer than useful."

"It's the answer you've got."

He looked up at her properly then, taking in the plain black dress, the careful stillness, the hands that had been scrubbed and still carried ink under the nails. "You've got office habits," he said. "Not clerical. Police or law."

Elise said nothing.

"That's close enough to confirmation." He tapped the death notice. "And Delatour. The schoolmaster from Montrouge. They've been preparing him for court all month."

"They prepared him too long."

Saunier grunted. "Perhaps. Or too efficiently. The public likes either version depending on the weather." He opened the notebook to the marked pages. "What is this, exactly?"

"The names and lives of the people they turned into labels to build the case. The ones who are already dead."

He turned a few pages. "Not the survivors."

"No."

"Then you haven't gone fully mad."

"I'm trying not to finish what they started."

Saunier set the notebook down with more care than he had shown the first papers. "If I print every history here, the column becomes an appeal, and appeals are for funerals and opposition weeklies. My readers want the dirt and the mechanism. They want to know whether a police agent was paid to stand near a bomb and whether the accused schoolmaster died before the examining judge could put him in a neat paragraph."

"They can have those things."

"They can have some of them." He selected another raisin, rolled it once between finger and thumb, then ate it. "Don't mistake me. I'm not turning my paper into an anarchist memorial sheet because a woman with a good hand arrives at three in the afternoon with a wound she won't name. I can give you six inches below the fold, perhaps eight if the Batignolles fire stops at one warehouse. I can ask why Ferrand was drawing unofficial money before the outrage and why Delatour was permitted to die without a hearing. I can mention Belkacem by name if the line stays short. I can't convert my subscribers into honest people by edition time."

Elise heard, with unwelcome clarity, how much she had once counted on men like him: cynical enough to use, vain enough to predict. "I didn't come for honest people."

"What did you come for?"

"A stain," she said. "Something that doesn't wash out by tomorrow's police briefing."

Saunier laughed once, not kindly. "Tomorrow's briefing washes out plenty. By next week they'll call this an unfortunate irregularity and print a promotion beside it. But a stain is a realistic ambition." He picked up the fruit knife again and used the tip to nudge the prison notice square with the edge of his desk. "Give me the names of the dead you most need unflattened. One line each. Work, age, the lie corrected. Keep the adjectives out of it. Adjectives let officials recover."

Elise opened the notebook to the marked pages. "Belkacem first."

"Because you knew him best?"

"Because they thought nobody would care if they called him foreign and left it there."

Saunier nodded once and pulled a pad toward him. "Good. That has shape."

She dictated. He cut. She put a fact back in when he cut too close to the bone. He let one line survive about Abel's printer's burns because, he said, readers understood burnt fingertips better than revolutionary theories. He would not print every date. He would print Ferrand's payments in a list because lists looked objective even when they accused: thirty-five francs, Advance for cultivation expenses; forty francs, Supplementary indemnity upon successful introduction; sixty francs, Final payment upon delivery of material proof. He changed paid provocateur to police informant and then, when Elise said that word made Ferrand sound smaller and cleaner than he had been, changed it again to agent under protection.

"Better," she said.

"For whom?"

"For the dead."

Saunier glanced at her over the edge of the pad. "You don't talk like a lover."

"I'm not here as one."

"No. You're here as something worse for business and better for copy."

When he had the column blocked out, he rang for a copy boy and handed the pages off with instructions written in the cramped brutal hand of the overworked. Then he stacked the death notice and the shorthand slip into a clean pile instead of returning them.

"I need these until the edition closes," he said.

"They're the only papers in my hand."

"Then you've waited too long to use them." He slid the pile toward his in-tray. "Listen carefully. Once this runs, the Prefecture won't only deny it. They'll ask which of their clerks learned shorthand, which widow on rue Saint-Jacques talks too much, which woman close enough to Delatour knew he died before hearing. If you sleep tonight where you slept last night, you're volunteering."

It was the first line he had given her all afternoon that wasn't chiefly about copy. Elise felt it land anyway, with the same cold practical weight as a lock turning.

"I don't pay for unsigned allegations carried in by hand," he went on. "I do sometimes tell a source when the room is already filling with smoke."

"And is it?"

"You're standing in it." He leaned back in his chair. "If the Prefecture sends a denial, I'll print that too. If they sue, I'll ask where Ferrand's money came from under oath and see who becomes uncomfortable. If nothing else, it may improve a dull Thursday."

He meant it as candor, perhaps even as respect. Elise stood with the notebook under her arm again and felt only the precise size of what could not be won. Vautrin would still go into his office tomorrow. The clerk at the prison window would still scratch at his ruined ear and turn pages. But Ferrand's sums would now exist in more than one hand, Lucien's death would not belong entirely to the prison register, and staying in Paris another night had just become a different kind of obedience.

"Thank you," she said, because measured politeness was easier than anything truer.

Saunier was already reaching for another raisin. "Don't thank newspapers. Buy one early, then get gone before the police do arithmetic."

By the time she got back to her room, the light had gone thick and yellow over the roofs. The hall clock downstairs struck six, then seven. Elise lit the lamp despite the heat because there was no good light left and spread the notebook, a blank school copybook, and Madame Vignot's proof across the bed. Saunier had kept the death notice and payment slip for the composing room. That should have made her feel barer than it did. Instead it felt like having finally spent what could not be hoarded.

Lucien's hand had been steadier than hers even in prison. She could see where the cough had taken him in two later entries, the letters smaller, the crossings-out harsher, as if the pen had needed more persuasion than usual. He had not written for effect. That had once annoyed her. Men who wanted to change the world usually liked themselves too much in ink. Lucien had liked precision better. He had given her no speech to carry, only names, dates, work, and the refusal to let the dead be filed incorrectly because the state found vagueness convenient.

She copied first what was already moving into type: Nadir, Abel, Henri, the prison hour, the lies corrected. Then she copied Ferrand's ledger in full onto a clean page of the schoolbook. Thirty-five francs, Advance for cultivation expenses. Forty francs, Supplementary indemnity upon successful introduction. Sixty francs, Final payment upon delivery of material proof. She wrote the labels twice, once in plain script and once in the tight office hand she had once used for Vautrin, as if doubling the record might make it harder to kill.

When she reached the bruised page naming Clemence and Lucie, she did not copy it. She eased the leaf out of the sewn binding slowly enough not to tear the next one, and the small dry sound of paper giving way felt uglier than the prison clerk's voice had. She folded the page into four, laid it in the basin, and set a match to one corner. The flame took reluctantly, then with appetite. When it reached Lucie's name, the ink rose in a wet blister before it vanished. Elise watched until the last black curl went gray and collapsed into itself. Then she ground the ash flat with the handle of her toothbrush so no word survived larger than a flea's leg.

From the purse in her valise she counted what remained of her money onto the bedspread: two twenty-franc notes gone soft at the folds, a five-franc piece, seven francs in silver, a scatter of sous. Forty-two francs. One month at Charenton cost twenty-eight. The room would want its week. The train would take what it could get. She made three piles anyway, because arithmetic was still the one mercy left that answered honestly. One pile for her mother. One for leaving. One so small it did not deserve a purpose.

She copied until the lamp guttered, then slept in her dress for less than two hours with the schoolbook under her ribs and the remainder of Lucien's notebook wrapped in the dish towel beside her face.

At dawn the city was already carrying the story badly. A boy outside was shouting Ferrand's name and agent under protection in the same breath, flattening both into sales pitch. Elise bought a damp copy of Le Courrier du Soir from him and read while she walked. Saunier had trimmed exactly where he had promised to trim. Ferrand's payments were there in a neat list. Delatour's death before hearing was there. Belkacem's name survived. The column was only damp paper and printer's ink, but it forced the lie to stand in public beside a corrected line.

Two streets later she saw Madame Vignot's broadside pasted crooked to a wall by a tobacco shop, the paste still wet enough to shine. Names the Prefecture Used Badly. Below it Nadir, Abel, Henri, and the others stood in black type with their work, their ages, their corrected fates. Somebody had already drawn a phallus in the margin beside one column. Somebody else had underlined Belkacem twice. The paper looked flimsy enough to tear in one hand. Elise stood there until a carter shouldered past and swore at her, then moved on. The dead had made it to the wall before the police could finish swallowing them. That was all.

The Charenton accounts office smelled of starch, damp wool, and institutional soap. The clerk behind the grille took the twenty-eight francs, counted them twice, wrote a receipt, and stamped the ledger without once asking whether the woman at the window could manage next month. One month more of her mother's bed, broth, locked door, and whatever mercy the attendants still had in them. After that, the page went blank again. Elise folded the receipt and put it away without reading it twice. The office gave her a stamped square of paper, one more month of bed and broth, and the grille still between her and the clerk. The payment bought time, narrow as the receipt and just as easy to lose.

By the time she reached the station, the remaining money was enough for a third-class ticket, bread, and almost nothing after. The clerk took her coins and slid the pasteboard over without asking a name. That was the only part of the morning that felt generous. Elise boarded with her valise, the school copybook, and what remained of Lucien's notebook wrapped in the dish towel. Paris slid past in factory walls, coal yards, and laundries steaming near the Bievre. She did not feel freed. She felt deducted: one month bought for her mother, one stain laid across the case, one city closing behind her under names she could no longer use.
