# Chapter 4

The print chamber sat deeper than the sleeping gallery, where the chalk walls held the smell of ink and lamp oil the way old wool held rain. Elise came down the passage with a basket of rag paper braced against her hip and the same question working under everything else: where, precisely, did the bombs begin. In Vautrin's office the answer had seemed indecently simple. A quarry. Printers. Wanted men. Put those nouns in the right order and the Prefecture could call the rest conspiracy before anyone had lit a fuse. She wanted the proof now. She wanted something harder than the memory of Henri Vasseur taken at the curb while she kept walking under another name.

Nadir stood at the nearest case slightly side-on to the room, as if one shoulder expected a door to open behind it. Ink had gone so far into the creases of his hands it looked permanent. He had tucked a strip of spoiled proof into his cuff and, between one line and the next, was folding it with absent precision into a paper bird for Lucie, who waited in the passage with one stocking at her ankle and chalk dust caught in her lashes. Opposite him Abel worked the composing stick fast enough to make the metal click. He was narrow and unfinished, his cuffs too short, two fingertips glazed with old printer's burns. A violet carpenter's pencil rode behind one ear. Every few moments he pushed it back as though the gesture helped him think.

Clemence took the basket from Elise and dropped it on the table. Lye had roughened her knuckles into a red map, and one button on her skirt had been replaced with a loop of string. "If you're going to be useful, be useful with both hands," she said. "Nadir, give her the pied mess. Abel, stop courting the cases."

Nadir tipped a tray toward Elise. It held a ruin of letters, bent rules, wandering commas, and the little lead blanks that disappeared faster than bread. "Common vowels first," he said. "Otherwise you'll spend the hour blessing God over ampersands."

"Does anyone bless God over ampersands?"

"Only Catholics and bad printers."

Lucie laughed because she liked the sound of adults pretending not to answer one another. Nadir handed her the paper bird without looking. She accepted it solemnly, checked that both wings matched, and went off toward the schoolroom with the bird balanced on her wrist.

The tray chilled Elise's palms through the metal. She began sorting by nick and weight, feeling for the notch with her thumb the way Abel had shown her the day before. The room answered itself in noises that did not belong in a police report: the throat of the press as it took pressure, Lucien's voice through the wall sounding children through syllables, Clemence counting sheets aloud because paper lost by one hand had to be paid for by another. A drip from the ceiling hit the same stone every few breaths. The lamp smoked whenever the draft shifted.

Instead of formulas or coded lists, the copy waiting beside the cases offered the humiliations of ordinary need. One handbill announced a subscription for the wives of men taken after a sweep near the river. Another listed two addresses willing to take in a seamstress and child if the rent came each Saturday in coins, not promises. There were notices for a communal soup pot, a request for boots with soles not yet split through, a page of prison visiting hours copied in letters large enough for old men with failing eyes, and four broad sheets of reading drills for the schoolroom: la, le, li, lo, lu; pain, lampe, porte. Elise had expected a hidden inventory of powder, a cache list, a date written in a hand too steady to be innocent. The quarry kept giving her bread, socks, and syllables.

She checked every margin anyway. Habits had their own appetite. She held damp proofs to the lamp, lifted blotters, turned over the scrap basket, slid her fingers under the drawer where spare sorts lay wrapped in paper. Nothing hid there except two onions, a pawn ticket for a winter coat, and a slate on which Clemence had set down the week's arithmetic: bread, eighteen loaves; lamp oil, three bottles; soap, seven bars if the price held. Beside Abel's name a ration line had been scratched out, written again, scratched out once more. He had missed suppers, then been restored to them. Clemence's mercy had the shape of revisions.

"You're not meant to search it with your whole body," Abel said.

He was close enough that she could smell metal on his fingers. He took a piece of type from between her thumb and forefinger, turned it, and dropped it into its square without glancing down. "Feel the nick. If you look first, you're late."

"I thought lateness was a sin only in schoolrooms."

"It's a sin anywhere other people are waiting on you." He nudged the case toward her. The pencil left a ghost mark on his temple when he scratched there. "And that comma doesn't live with the apostrophes. It only visits."

He slid a composing stick off the table, thumbed the measure smaller by a hair, and rebuilt in seconds a line Elise had taken a minute to spoil. A thin space here, a thicker one there, the whole thing suddenly even and inevitable under his hands. He had the unearned authority of nineteen and the earned authority of a boy who knew metal better than manners.

"What are you making?" Elise asked.

"A prison appeal. Two hundred copies if the press behaves."

"And when it doesn't?"

"Then we flatter it and curse it and flatter it again." He smiled before he could help it, quick and bright. The smile made him younger, almost painfully so. "You can make anything sing if the spacing's right."

Nadir did not look up. "He means anything can sound like a funeral if you leave him alone with it."

Abel's ears went pink. "I mean a page ought to have some blood in it."

"There," Nadir said. "He's started."

Lucie slipped back in for more spoiled sheets and stood on one scraped knee against the bench, reading the proof upside down with the grave concentration of a child testing whether letters stayed themselves when mistreated. One braid had loosened and was sticking out above her collar. "That's not pain," she said. "You've set plain."

"It's pain," Abel said.

"It has an l."

"It doesn't."

She thrust the sheet at Elise for judgment. Elise had to laugh, which still felt like spending money she had not meant to spend. "Lucie's right. You've given the bread an extra leg."

Abel snatched the proof, saw it, and swore softly enough that Clemence only clicked her tongue from across the room.

"Mind the child," Clemence said. "And make me thirty more of the laundry notices before noon. Mme Brissot says the old men by the river can't read anything smaller than a coffin lid."

"I'm not a child," Lucie said.

"Then act like an underpaid adult and fetch M. Delatour's chalk."

Lucie considered this, decided the exchange was fair, and went.

Elise took the spoiled proof from Abel and, because she wanted him talking, said, "You hate being corrected."

"I hate being corrected by ten-year-olds."

"I'm nearly eleven," Lucie called from the passage.

"You've been nearly eleven for months," Abel said back.

"That's because it takes time."

Nadir's mouth twitched. "Give her the point. It's the best argument you've heard all morning."

Abel tried not to smile. He failed. It altered him less than the first smile had. That one had been unguarded. This one knew it was observed.

Elise held the damaged sheet to the lamp. "Does the Republic require much saving from soup lists?"

Abel gave her a sideways look. "You say that as if soup lists are harmless."

"Aren't they?"

"To people who stay fed, perhaps." He tipped a fresh line into the galley, every movement exact. "You think a printed page only counts if it kills somebody. That's police thinking."

He said police without accusation, more with young contempt for any mind that sorted force into respectable and vulgar forms. Elise kept her face where she had trained it to stay: attentive, mildly abashed, useful. "I think a printed page counts according to who bothers to seize it."

Nadir looked down the stick and trimmed a line with his thumbnail. "Then you'll have noticed they seize recipes when poor people copy them in groups."

The answer ought to have pleased her. It did not. A room like this could be translated in three lines of administrative French until the onions under the drawer became supply storage and the reading cards a circulation network. Vautrin would never need to lie outright. He only needed nouns stripped of the hands that used them.

Nadir went to fetch more oil from the ration room, and Abel, relieved of the witness he respected, loosened into talk. He showed Elise the map of the case, how the common letters sat where the hand could fall to them quickest, how a hair space changed the whole temper of a line. When he forgot himself he was beautiful at it, and she disliked the thought as soon as it formed. Not because he was handsome; he was too raw for beauty and too hungry for it besides. But his work had the vanity of grace. He wanted the page to remember him.

From his apron pocket he drew a narrow strip of proof, already smudged by rereading. "This was better before Delatour got pious about it."

Elise took it. The appeal asked for coal, boots, and witness money for a cooper arrested in a sweep. Under the practical lines, in a sharper hand and darker ink, Abel had added: THE DEAD OF SEVENTY-ONE STILL WAIT IN THE STONE.

The words were not childish. That made them worse. They were the kind of line a boy wrote when he wanted grief to look back at him and choose him.

"He made you cut it?" she asked.

"He said it turned need into theater." Abel made Lucien sound older than forty-four, older than the quarry itself. "As if theater hasn't moved men since the first fool climbed a cart."

"Did it need the line?"

He bristled. "Everything needs the line if you want anybody to remember it."

Lucien came in before Elise answered. Chalk dust marked one sleeve; damp had curled the silver at his temples. He carried Lucie's primer under one arm and coughed once into his fist before he spoke. "What needs a line?"

Abel folded the strip too late to hide it.

Lucien held out his hand. "If you're going to sulk over a correction, at least let the rest of us admire it."

Abel gave over the proof with the bad grace of someone already composing his own defense. Lucien read it, and for a moment his face was unreadable except for the small tightening around the eyes that came with the cough he was refusing. Then he looked at Abel.

"It's not a bad sentence," he said.

Abel straightened despite himself.

"It's also not the cooper's wife asking for coal."

"People read what bites."

"People also freeze while boys practice sounding historic." Lucien laid the strip on the table between them. His voice stayed mild, which only made Abel's flushed silence look younger. "If you want to write something that survives, begin by letting the living take up their proper space."

Abel stared at the proof as if it had betrayed him by lying flat.

Lucien turned to Elise. "Has he taught you the difference between a thin space and a thick one yet, or only the pleasures of censorship?"

"I've learned that bread can accidentally grow an extra letter."

Lucien glanced at Abel. "Then the city is doomed."

That won him half a laugh from Clemence in the next room and a full one from Lucie, who had reappeared with chalk on her nose. Even Abel's mouth threatened mutiny for a second before pride dragged it straight again.

Lucien set the primer down beside the proofs. On the top page Lucie had written pain, lampe, mur in cramped, careful letters. "Set the appeal clean," he said to Abel. "The cooper's wife needs coal, not an anniversary."

"The anniversary's already in the walls," Abel muttered.

"Yes," Lucien said. "That doesn't mean you get to spend it."

He took Lucie back toward the schoolroom. She paused long enough to whisper to Elise, "He only gets ugly when the line's his," then ran after Lucien before Abel could hear the whole of it.

Abel worked in silence for a while, each piece of type put down a little harder than needed. Twice he touched the pocket where he had hidden the rejected strip, as if checking a pulse. Elise kept sorting and let the room settle around the little wound. She had found no formula, no target list, no timetable. The press yielded relief sheets, primers, ration notices, addresses for spare beds, and a hundred small acts of keeping one another alive through laws written to make such keeping criminal. The only thing in the chamber that leaned toward explosion was Abel himself. Not yet in deed. In appetite.

By afternoon her fingertips had taken the cold shape of metal. Ink sat in the half-moons under her nails and would stay there no matter how long she rubbed with sand soap. It was work she knew how to respect: exacting, repetitive, badly paid, ruinous to the back. It offended her, on a level deeper than politics, that Vautrin's kind of men could look at such labor and see only evidence waiting to be arranged.

But she had not forgotten why she was there. Respect did not absolve. She kept listening for the wrong emphasis, the sentence too eager for destruction, the practical discussion that would turn, at last, toward an actual target. Nothing came. Clemence wanted larger letters for the laundry notices because one old man by the Bièvre had begun pretending he could still read to save face. Nadir wanted the prison visiting sheet reset because a date had shifted. Lucien wanted fewer adjectives on the appeal because adjectives cost space and space cost money. Abel wanted the world.

There, at least, was something she could use.

Toward evening the damp thickened. Supper was cabbage water with a heel of cheese shaved through it and bread already a day nearer staleness than anyone admitted. Clemence had just begun dividing the loaf when footsteps came from the entrance passage: one set careful from habit, Lucien's, and another too loud for anyone born to hidden rooms.

The stranger ducked into the lamplight with rain beading on his moustache and cheap cologne fighting a losing war against stale sweat. He was broad across the chest in the way of men who let other people carry the patient work. Two knuckles on his right hand had healed crooked and made a ridge under the skin. Before he said a word he looked around the gallery the way some men looked into a wine shop, measuring not danger but comfort.

"God, that's cold," he said, stamping one boot and wincing. "I've had river water in my heel since noon. Tell me you don't drink chicory on purpose down here."

"Tell me your name first," Clemence said.

He took off his cap with a flourish too neat for the room. "Jules Ferrand. From Belleville by way of too many patrols and one bastard of a cabman."

Lucien came in behind him, rain dark on his shoulders. "He was sent with a name only two people here would know."

Nadir tore bread without offering any. "That's a sentence, not a guarantee."

Ferrand grinned as if he liked resistance because it gave him more surface to slide over. "I don't ask guarantees. I ask soup and somewhere to dry my socks before they rot off me."

"You'll get soup," Clemence said. "The socks depend on whether Belleville still remembers your face tomorrow."

Abel had already risen half out of his seat. Men who came in from above with rain on them carried the city's rumor along their sleeves. At nineteen he still believed rumor might select him. Ferrand saw it at once and turned enough of his grin in Abel's direction to count as an invitation.

Elise kept her bowl in both hands and watched Ferrand watch the room. No ink in the creases of his fingers. No chalk dust. No paper cuts. He had the quick readiness of a man used to entering places under names that only half belonged to him. That alone meant nothing. Half the quarry lived that way. But there was another familiarity in him, one she knew from police corridors and back rooms where informers waited to be paid: men whose courage began in other people's bodies.

Clemence gave him soup in a cracked bowl and a piece of bread smaller than the one Abel had hoped to hand over. Ferrand ate fast and talked while chewing, too easily at home. Two rooms gone in Belleville. A bookseller searched in Charonne. Patrols thick near the barriers. The police frisking carts for printed matter and taking down addresses from laundry tickets if they smelled the wrong politics on the woman carrying them. This was useful, precise enough to sound earned. Lucien listened. Even Nadir stopped glaring long enough to file it.

Then Lucien said, "What do you need besides a night's shelter?"

Ferrand wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "Depends what sort of season you mean to have."

"I mean this week."

"This week, next week, May after that." He shrugged. "They're winding the screws for the marches already. Once a policeman starts imagining sparks, he sees them in flour sacks and hymn books. If you mean to answer, answer hard."

Clemence snapped the loaf in two. "Answer what?"

Ferrand waved the bread toward the room, making hunger itself into evidence. "The season. The dragnets. All this. Paper's decent work, I know. But if you want the boulevard to hear you, there's quarry powder to be had cheap outside Gentilly. Fuse too. Proper fuse, tarred against damp. Not that rag-wick rubbish boys buy and lose fingers to."

The room did not freeze; it narrowed. Elise felt it in the way sound changed, the lamp hiss suddenly louder than the bowls.

Nadir set down his spoon. "Interesting."

Ferrand looked at him. "What's interesting?"

"Lucien asked what you needed. You answered with powder."

Ferrand laughed, easy as spit. "I answered with what the police are preparing for. You don't answer a boot with a sermon."

"Sometimes you answer it with a better pair of boots," Clemence said. "If you know where anything is cheap, bring soap. Then coal. Then I might listen to the rest."

"Soap never made a deputy tremble."

"No," she said. "But dirty shirts keep children lousy, and that troubles me more."

Abel's eyes had not left Ferrand's face. "Proper fuse in damp matters."

Nadir turned on him. "To whom?"

Abel lifted one shoulder, already defensive. "To anyone who dislikes blowing off his own hand."

"Everyone dislikes it," Nadir said. "That isn't the same as planning it."

Ferrand leaned back, bowl balanced on his knee. "The boy has sense. That's all I said."

"And you say plenty," Nadir replied.

Elise broke her bread instead of eating it. "You name fuse quickly for a man who hasn't been told whether he's here for one night or ten."

Ferrand turned to her with the instantaneous familiarity of a man who treated every room as already won. "I name whatever the room pretends not to want."

"Mlle Morel," Clemence said without warmth.

Ferrand touched two fingers to his temple. "My apologies, Mlle Morel. I was only saying the city listens better when something cracks."

Lucien set down his bowl. "The city listens long enough to send carts."

"Then make the carts expensive."

Lucien's voice stayed quiet. "There are children in these rooms. Remember that before you start admiring cracks."

Ferrand raised his hands. "Who said anything about these rooms? Lord, schoolmaster, you hear a fuse where I only mention a market."

"A market in what?" Elise asked.

He smiled at her as if she had offered him flirtation instead of scrutiny. "In what's available."

"You said powder, fuse, and lost fingers. That's a tidy inventory for a man discussing the weather."

That made Abel glance from one to the other, suddenly aware that the exchange had edges sharper than the words. Ferrand's grin thinned but did not disappear.

"Paper makes me sneeze," he said. "Chemistry doesn't. Everyone has to excel somewhere."

Nadir barked a short laugh that held no amusement. "There. At last, a vocation."

Lucien rubbed the heel of one hand over his mouth, thinking. The cough wanted him; she could hear it waiting in the breath he was saving. He disliked paranoia because underground life produced enough of it without cultivation. Accuse the wrong man once and the room paid for it in ways no police file ever measured. Ferrand knew that too. He had chosen his first overstep carefully, large enough to be heard, small enough to be denied as mere talk.

"You'll sleep in the outer gallery tonight," Lucien said at last. "We'll discuss markets when there's daylight enough to look at one another properly."

Ferrand dipped his head as if accepting a gentleman's courtesy. "As you like."

"And if you want my attention tomorrow," Clemence said, "bring news of soap before news of heroics."

"I'll search the city for both."

"Search for soap first."

That won him the corner of a smile from Lucien and nothing from anyone else.

Supper resumed, though not at its former size. Ferrand told one story about a patrolman slipping in gutter mud and another about a cabman who thought every laborer in Paris was an anarchist if he tipped badly. He told them well. That was part of the trouble. His anecdotes had the loose fast quality of tavern speech, all elbows and confidence, skimming over the places where a careful listener might ask who had been present, when exactly, by whose invitation. Abel laughed first. Lucie did not laugh at all. She had come back in for her bread and now sat tucked against Clemence's side, watching Ferrand with the direct animal caution children sometimes brought to rooms that adults overexplained.

When the bowls were empty and the bread cloth folded away, Ferrand followed Abel toward the print chamber with the excuse that he wanted to see the press. "I've always liked machines," he said. "More honest than committees."

"Paper makes you sneeze," Nadir reminded him.

"Presses don't."

Nadir started to rise. Lucien stopped him with a slight movement of the hand, not because he trusted Ferrand but because he refused to stage a brawl out of intuition. Elise understood the choice. She hated it immediately.

The print chamber felt smaller with Ferrand in it. He stood too close to Abel's shoulder, smelling of wet wool, street tobacco, and the sweet cheap cologne that never quite hid a day's sweat. The press, still tacky with ink, gleamed dully in the lamplight. Ferrand ran a finger along the frame without touching the ink itself, like a man admiring a horse he had no intention of grooming.

"Good stock," he said. "Better than half the shops above."

"Nadir keeps it from dying," Abel said.

"And you?"

Abel lifted his chin. "I keep it from looking dead."

Ferrand laughed low in his throat. "That's the boy."

Elise took up the tray of damaged sorts and began resorting it again, though there was no need. She wanted an excuse to stay. Through the open door she could hear Lucien in the gallery, speaking softly to Clemence about bedrolls and morning routes. The ordinary sounds of hiding. Inside the print room Ferrand's attention moved over Abel with a patience he had not given the rest of them. That, more than the bomb talk, made Elise's skin feel rubbed the wrong way.

Abel, eager under the gaze and ashamed of eagerness, made a show of straightening proofs. The rejected strip slid from his pocket and landed on the table. Ferrand picked it up before Abel could snatch it back.

"Nothing," Abel said too fast. "Only a line Delatour thought too pretty."

Ferrand read it once with his lips moving slightly, as if he respected the act of reading enough to slow for it. Then he looked at Abel.

"The dead of seventy-one still wait in the stone," he said. "That's not for a soup appeal."

Abel's shrug carried too much force to count as indifference. "That's what I said."

"Delatour said it was theater," Elise said.

Ferrand turned the strip over between finger and thumb. "Maybe. But theater fills a square faster than arithmetic."

From the passage Lucien said, "Arithmetic keeps people alive long enough to reach the square."

Ferrand smiled without surprise. "I wasn't insulting arithmetic."

"No," Lucien said. "Only the people doing it."

For the first time Ferrand's expression showed a seam. Not anger. Annoyance, quickly papered. He set the strip down and spread his hands. "You're touchy for a schoolmaster."

"I'm tired," Lucien said. "There's a difference."

Abel looked from one man to the other, wanting the scene larger than it was, wanting someone to strike a table or name a doctrine or at least choose a side loud enough for history to hear. Nobody did. Clemence called from the gallery for Lucie to stop pretending sleep exempted her from washing her hands. Nadir banged a spoon against a bowl hard enough to register as opinion. The quarry returned, stubbornly, to its own scale.

Ferrand gave Abel back the proof strip with two fingers, privately, as if handing over a contraband pleasure. "It's a good line," he said. "It deserves a wall, not a basket."

Abel closed his burnt fingertips over the paper.

That was all. No pact. No whispered target. No map opened on the table. But Elise saw the current complete itself anyway. She had spent the day looking for violence in the press, in the drawers, in the copy, in the old chalk tunnels that the Prefecture already treated as an arsenal in waiting. The press had given her relief sheets, prison hours, children's lessons, and laundry notices. Ferrand had brought the first real odor of catastrophe with him, and Abel had turned toward it the way poor boys sometimes turned toward candles, knowing perfectly well what fire did and loving the promise of light all the same.

Later, when the lamp was turned down and the chamber went back to chalk, damp, and breathing, Elise lay awake on her blanket with the taste of cabbage still sour on her tongue. If she wrote the quarry cleanly for Vautrin, he would have enough names to break a shelter. If she wrote it falsely, she might save these rooms long enough for something worse to rip through them from the inside. Abel did not want merely revenge or justice. He wanted scale, witness, a sentence big enough to step into and disappear. Ferrand had heard that hunger within an hour.

It was the first thing all day that felt like evidence.
