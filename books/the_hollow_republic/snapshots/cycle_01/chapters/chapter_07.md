# Chapter 7

The packet of notes rode flat and hot inside Clemence's stays. Elise could tell where it sat by the way Clemence kept one forearm folded over her middle as they walked, not modest and not delicate, simply protective in the same practical way she shielded bread from rain. The paper bundle in Nadir's arms had taken the shape of his body already. He carried it turned a little sideways, as if he were making room for a blow that had not yet arrived, and every few streets he checked the inside pocket of his coat with the back of his ink-black fingers. A corner of folded proof stuck there. Lucie had begged him for a fox before they left the quarry, and he had promised one with a seriousness he would have mocked in anybody else.

Ferrand had come because Ferrand came where movement and money met. He said it was because he knew the lane and because a man looked less suspicious than two women and a compositor carrying wrapped stock. Elise had not corrected the count. Ferrand always spoke as if the world were already arranged to flatter him. Cheap cologne leaked off him in warm bursts when the wind shifted, trying and failing to cover a night's sweat. He had shaved in a hurry. One patch under his jaw still showed the dark rasp, and the knuckles on his right hand were swollen old white where they had once broken and healed badly.

Late spring had softened the city without cleaning it. Cart ruts held old rain and horse piss. Laundry snapped from upper windows over a butcher's blue flies. They were south enough now that the streets widened and thinned in uncertain patches, Paris fraying into Montrouge with yards, workshops, and little plots where stubborn people kept onions in dirt the color of dishwater. Elise felt the hour in her body with the same trained certainty with which she felt the knife hidden in her left boot. Vautrin's slip had browned over a candle the night before and given her the courtesy of a time: after lamps, after the district was half dark, after the runner had had room to move the goods. It was why she had agreed to come. It was why she had not warned Lucien off the errand. There had been filth enough in that choice already. She had not expected Vautrin to take even the lie back.

"If this packet gets damp," Clemence said, "you can all line your shoes with it and call it a political victory."

Ferrand grinned at her. "You've a hymn ready for everything, haven't you?"

"It's not a hymn. It's arithmetic."

Nadir did not look at either of them. "Mind the curb," he said. "The bottom bundle is already bowing."

Ferrand spread one hand. "I said I'd carry it."

"You said it three streets ago," Nadir replied. "Talking isn't lifting."

Elise kept her eyes on the lane ahead. She was Jeanne Morel here, the useful widow with careful hands and no politics worth naming. Jeanne did not hurry when there was no reason to hurry. Jeanne noticed doorways because poor women noticed doorways. Jeanne did not count the men at the far corner by their boots and the way they had made themselves look occupied with nothing. Elise saw them anyway: one with a newspaper held too still, another pretending to study harness leather in a shop window, a third whose cuffs were cleaner than his cap. She told herself she was borrowing danger from habit. If Vautrin had moved the hour up, he would have had to move more than the hour. He would have had to risk her.

Then Ferrand lifted two fingers at a cooper's boy and the boy looked away too fast.

Elise felt her stomach go small and mean inside her.

The runner's room was on the second floor of a passage house behind a shutter-maker's yard. Reine Talbot opened the door with three dressmaker's pins between her lips and a tape measure still around her neck, as if the years since she had sewn for a living had not persuaded her to stop expecting cloth worth measuring. A child's jacket lay turned inside out on her table among drying broadsides. She had chalked one cuff for shortening. The room smelled of paste, lamp oil, and boiled chicory. Reine took the pins from her mouth and squinted at Clemence first, then at the bundle, then at Ferrand with the kind of smile women used when they had long ago given up mistaking appetite for charm.

"You took your time," she said. "I've got the stove lit for glue and three neighbors who think that means I'm taking in washing. If anybody asks, you're here to collect a shirt."

"Then show me a shirt," Clemence said.

Reine snorted and moved aside. "In the chair. Don't steal it. It's the only one in the room without holes."

Nadir brought the paper in and set it down with more care than some men used laying down a child. Reine's face changed when she saw the stock. Hunger came into it, not for food but for workable things. Good paper was not abstract in rooms like this. It was time, reach, possible voices, a week's worth of circulation that would not blur into pulp at the first touch of wet fingers.

"Well," she said softly, and then with briskness again, "Lucien's getting extravagant. What's he after, canonization?"

"He's after legible type," Nadir said. "Which is harder."

He slipped the folded fox from his pocket and set it on the mantel near Reine's tin cup. "For Lucie. I forgot."

Reine picked it up with ink-thick thumb and forefinger. "Her or me?"

"Her. You've already got enough vices."

"Unkind. I've got very few. I keep them in order."

Ferrand laughed too readily, as if he had said something clever himself. He moved to the window and looked down through the slat in the curtain. Elise watched the back of his neck. Men who risked arrest with conviction in them stood a certain way when they entered a room. They spread into it. They put some part of themselves between the room and harm without looking as if they meant to. Ferrand never did. He occupied a place only until the place asked anything of him.

Clemence drew the notes from inside her dress. The packet had left a damp square on her chemise. She flattened it on the table beside the chalked jacket and began counting in the low blunt school hand of her voice, every amount spoken as if it had been scrubbed first. Reine counted with her, lips moving. Elise kept half an eye on the money and half on the door. She had hidden her own slips deeper than the notes, stitched into the inner seam of her petticoat where a casual hand would find nothing but linen. Blank to any ordinary eye. Legible with heat. Damning if understood.

Too quiet.

The yard outside should have carried a saw's whine from the shutter-maker. A woman with a basket had been quarreling in the passage when they came up. Somebody's baby had been crying through the wall. Now the house held itself in. Elise heard only the glue in Reine's pot giving a small wet burp as it simmered.

Then the first blow landed downstairs.

Not a knock. Wood gave with the flat cracking report of a shoulder or a pry bar, and at once after it came the sound Elise knew too well: men on a stair trying not to sound like men on a stair. Weight kept to the edges of the boards. Breath saved. No wasted speech until they had the room.

After lamps, Vautrin had written. The bastard.

Ferrand turned from the window before anybody else moved. For one clean second his face lost its social expression and became only calculation. He did not look surprised. He looked inconvenienced.

"Back way," he said, already stepping toward the scullery door.

Reine stared at him. "What back way? That stair jams."

"Then unjam it."

He caught the door latch, shoved it once to see if it would give, and when it did he slipped through with his own hat in hand and nothing else. Not the money. Not the paper. Not the woman whose room he had just delivered into danger. Elise caught one last breath of his cologne and then he was gone down the service stair like grease poured down a crack.

Clemence's mouth opened, closed. "That son of a bitch."

The second blow struck their own landing. Someone shouted, "Police. Open."

Reine did the stupid brave thing people did in rooms that held their whole life. She reached for the notes first. Nadir reached for the paper. Clemence reached for the stove. Elise heard herself say, too fast and too certain, "Leave the front. They'll stack there first."

Clemence's head snapped toward her.

There was no time to fix it.

The door burst inward on the next hit, splinters spitting across the room. A plainclothes sergeant came through with one sleeve half torn from the shoulder seam and a wedding band worn pale into the flesh of his hand. Two men crowded behind him so close they jammed themselves in the frame for a beat, cursing and shoving. The room was too small for authority to look elegant in. Boots slid on paste. One man's club caught the edge of the table instead of a body and sent the counted notes skidding through Reine's chalk dust.

Reine snatched the child's jacket by mistake, realized it, and lunged again. The sergeant drove forward. His shoulder took her in the chest and flung her back against the stove. The glue pot came over. Thick paste hit the floor and her skirt together with a wet slap. She screamed once, more fury than pain, then the stove door sprang open and a length of iron kissed her calf red through the wool.

Nadir dropped to one knee to save the paper bundle. Elise saw the choice come into him. The stock was on the floor by his hands. Reine was pinned against the stove with the sergeant half over her and the second man raising his club. Nadir looked at the bundle, at Reine, and then at nothing at all. He went for the body.

"Move," he said, quiet as ever, and drove his shoulder into the policeman's waist.

The three of them went down in glue and ash. The club came down anyway. It caught Reine along the temple instead of on the arm she had thrown up to shield herself. Blood opened at her hairline in a sudden dark ribbon. Elise smelled hot iron, lamp smoke, and something rank from the kicked-over chamber pot under the bed. A sheet of broadside paper slid into it and bloomed brown.

Clemence seized the fallen stove door with the hem of her apron and slammed it shut before the coals could roll. One of the detectives grabbed at her wrist. She swung the iron hook she had snatched from beside the grate and cracked his knuckles so hard he yelped like a boy. That was Clemence entire: fear translated at once into a household tool.

The third policeman came in from the landing with his hat gone and his hair pasted to his forehead. "Take the papers first," he barked.

Elise moved before she had thought of moving. She caught the chair with the holed shirt on it and hurled it at the door to jam the frame a half second more. Then she went low, under the swinging club, and got her hands under Reine's shoulders beside Nadir.

"Scullery," she said. "Now. Yard last."

"How do you know that?" Clemence shouted.

"Because I can hear them."

It was not enough of an answer, and Clemence knew it, but the floor had become a paste slick and the first sergeant was trying to rise with Nadir on top of him, so doubt had to wait its turn. Reine's blood ran warm over Elise's fingers and made the tape measure at Reine's neck slippery as eel skin. The runner was not heavy until they had to lift her over the lip where the kitchen boards swelled. Then she became all dead weight and pain.

The scullery door banged off the wall. The service stair beyond was narrower than Elise had hoped and steeper than Reine's limp leg could bear. Somebody below shouted that the yard gate was manned. Somebody above shouted to search the roof. Elise had known they would split the stair and the yard, but not yet, not this quickly. Vautrin had not only moved the hour. He had fed the room full.

Ferrand had known.

Nadir wedged himself under Reine's left arm and hauled. "On three," he said.

"There isn't a three," Clemence said.

They dragged Reine down anyway. Her boot heel struck every step. Elise heard each hit in her teeth. Halfway to the yard, a constable shouldered up from below, face flushed, truncheon lifted in both hands because there was no room to swing it properly. Elise saw where he meant to hit before he did. She let go of Reine for one terrible beat, caught the rail, and drove the heel of her boot into his knee. Bone did not break. It gave enough. He screamed and sat down hard on the step, jamming the stair with his own body.

Reine gasped, "Jesus Christ."

Clemence stared at Elise as if a seam had split open in daylight.

Elise had no breath to dress the moment. She got both hands back under Reine and dragged. The constable's cap rolled past them to the yard below where rainwater, soot, and laundry soap had made a black skin over the puddles.

The yard was smaller than it had looked from the room, a square of brick and slime with privies down one side and a broken cart against the wall. A man in shirtsleeves was already at the gate, pounding to be let through from the alley. Another policeman answered from outside. They had the mouth, then. Elise's mind laid the pattern down whether she wanted it or not. Front stair, street door, yard mouth, then neighbors, then roof if the house was worth the manpower. If they could get over the cart and through the washhouse on the left, they might still spill into the next passage before the outer ring tightened. Not because she was brave. Because Vautrin had taught her the order and because surviving men like him meant remembering what they preferred.

"There," she said, jerking her chin at the washhouse wall.

"Not the gate?" Nadir asked.

"Do you want cuffs?"

He did not waste breath arguing. Reine was slipping in his grip. Blood had run down behind one ear and soaked the collar of her blouse. The paper bundle still lay upstairs under police boots. The notes were either in evidence already or stuck in somebody's glue. The errand had gone from clandestine labor to ruin in less than a minute.

Nadir looked once up toward the room they had left, where voices were overturning drawers and furniture in the official hunger of a search. Then he hitched Reine higher and turned his back on the lost stock.

"All right," he said. "We save the woman."

It was not a grand sentence. He said it like a man choosing which pan to pull off a fire first. Elise would remember it anyway.

The broken cart came apart under them. Clemence kicked a wheel spoke loose, laid it across the trough beside the washhouse, and went first with the agility of a woman who had spent half her life moving weight through spaces built too meanly for it. Nadir shoved Reine upward from below. Elise got hold of the back of Reine's skirt and the tape measure together and nearly tore both. Lime-wet brick scraped the skin from her palms. A neighbor on the other side screamed at the sight of them and dropped a basin. Water splashed over the wall into Elise's hair.

Then they were through the washhouse, out by the tubs, and into an alley that smelled of lye, urine, and cabbage leaves turning sweet in rot.

For ten paces no one followed.

They stopped only because Reine made a sound that was less a cry than a choke and her knees buckled completely. Nadir hauled her upright again with hard economy. Blood from her temple had tracked into the corner of her mouth. She spat pink on the stones and blinked, furious and dazed.

"My jacket," she muttered. "The blue one. The cuff's not done."

Clemence touched her face once, quick and almost gentle. "To hell with the cuff."

"The client already waits like a widow," Reine said, and then winced as if her own attempt at wit had jostled the wound.

There it was, the detail of her that had nothing to do with pamphlets or raids: a woman half senseless and still annoyed about a hem she had promised to finish. Elise felt the shame of finding that moving.

Nadir shifted Reine's arm more securely over his shoulders. "There's a bone-setter on rue du Pere-Corentin who doesn't ask questions if the money's up front."

"You've got money?" Clemence said.

"Not enough. But she's bleeding now, so arithmetic can come after."

His folded fox, soaked from the splash over the wall, had worked itself loose from his pocket and hung by one softened ear. He did not notice. He started down the alley with Reine against him, walking the same side-on angle as before only now every step cost him twice. Clemence moved to go with him.

Elise touched her sleeve. "If three of us walk with blood between us, we're a procession. Let me come later."

Clemence pulled free, then looked at Reine, then back at Elise. Suspicion and necessity crossed in her face so plainly that Elise could have laid a finger between them.

"Five minutes," Clemence said. "No more."

"Go."

Nadir did not turn. "If you're not there," he said to no one in particular, "I'll tell Lucien you stopped to join the police."

"He'll know you're lying," Clemence said.

"I know. That's why it's funny."

Even with a bleeding woman on him, his wit came out dry as old type. Then he was gone around the bend, Reine's boot dragging, Clemence behind them with her shoulders already set for the next cost.

Elise waited until their footfalls had thinned. Her hands shook only then, once, not with fear exactly but with the body's delayed resentment at being used. She put two fingers into the inner seam of her petticoat and ripped the stitches she had laid there by candlelight. The slips came free in a little packet no bigger than a prayer book's leaves.

Blank paper. That was what anybody else would see.

Milk ink had its own smell when it burned, sweet first and then ugly. Elise knew that because Vautrin had trained her with household things. Invisible writing for a woman who could not officially exist in his office. Blank scraps passed hand to hand like shopping lists, then held to heat and made suddenly legible: names, hours, corners, an address that might mean a raid or a payment or the difference between her mother eating broth and not. The slips in her hand held the habits of the last weeks. Where to leave reports. How to mark urgency. Which carrier took Prefecture money and which one sold messages to whoever paid second.

If Clemence searched her later and found them, Jeanne Morel would die before morning.

If the police searched her later and found them, Elise Valette would live, perhaps, but only in Vautrin's terms.

She crouched by the alley drain. Greasy water moved there in a skin the color of pewter. Somebody had emptied soup into it earlier; cabbage leaves clung to the grate. Elise struck a match against the wall. The sulfur flared yellow and stung her nose. She cupped it deep in both hands and touched it to the first slip. The paper browned, then curled inward as the hidden writing surfaced for a second like bruises coming up under skin. One date. One initial. The square that had promised after lamps. She fed it to the drain flame before it could finish darkening.

The second one fought her. Damp from her sweat, it wanted to smolder instead of catch. She held it closer, too close, and fire nipped the side of her thumb. Pain steadied her. She used it. One by one she burned them all, pinning the blackened curls under the sole of her boot until the grease took them and carried the ash away in gray strings.

Ninety francs a month, when Vautrin was pleased. Twenty-eight due monthly at Charenton. Three hundred and twelve already hanging over her mother like a gate waiting to fall. The numbers came to her exactly because numbers always did. She saw the account office in her head with its ink bottle, its polished corner worn by generations of forearms, the clerk who never lifted his eyes higher than a woman's gloves unless payment had failed. These scraps had been the clean road back to that office. Not safety. Nothing so grand. Only routine obedience, plausible service, the practical fiction that she could still do the work and live in two ledgers at once.

Ash stuck to the wet on her thumb. She rubbed it off against the stones and left a dark smear.

"What was that?"

Clemence's voice came from the mouth of the alley, not loud. Elise had not heard her return. That frightened her more than the stair had. Clemence stood with her hands empty now, apron dark where Reine's blood had dried into it. Her hairpins had started to slip, and one lock clung damply to the side of her face. She was not breathing hard. She had had time, then, not only to walk back but to stop and watch.

Elise rose too quickly and had to hide the stiffness in her legs by brushing grime from her skirt. "Nothing useful."

"I could see that."

Clemence came closer by a step. Not close enough to smell the burned milk in the air, perhaps, but close enough to see the little black curls still lodged by the grate.

"Receipts?" she asked.

"No."

"Addresses?"

Elise could have lied more fully. She could have said sewing patterns, letters, a saint's card gone soggy in her hem. The old training pressed at her, neat and fast. But Clemence had seen too much already: the instant at the stair, the kick at the constable's knee, the way Elise had known the yard would be taken. A bad lie now would not mend anything. It would only cheapen the damage.

"Instructions," Elise said.

Clemence's face altered by a degree so small another person might not have noticed. Elise noticed because Clemence always did her feeling through countable units. A half loaf. Two bars of soap. One blanket missing. One woman who should not have known what she knew.

"Whose instructions?" Clemence asked.

The right answer would have been nobody's. The useful answer would have been mine. Elise said nothing.

Clemence looked at the smear of ash on Elise's thumb. "Widows who copy invoices don't put a man down on a stair like that."

"Widows who stay alive learn things."

"Not that fast."

There it was. No shouted accusation, no theatrical revelation. Clemence was too tired and too practical for either. Suspicion sat in her voice the same way money sat in her household books: entered once, then kept until proved otherwise.

Elise made herself meet her eyes. "If I'd wanted you taken, there were better chances than today."

"That's not much comfort."

The answer landed clean. Elise almost smiled, not from humor but from respect. Clemence did not ask questions for the pleasure of hearing herself ask them. Each one was a hook laid only where she meant to pull.

From the street beyond the alley came the thud of running feet and somebody calling for a surgeon. The raid was spreading through the neighborhood now, outward into all the ordinary lives around it. Landlords would swear they had known nothing. Children would be dragged back from windows by the scruff. By nightfall there would be three versions in circulation already: the police version, the street version, the version the quarry would tell itself to keep breathing. Elise had just burned the only route by which she could feed the first one tidily.

Clemence followed her glance toward the street. "Can you walk?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Good. Then walk."

She turned first. Elise fell into step beside her. The alley narrowed before opening to the lane, and for a few yards they had to go shoulder to shoulder. Clemence did not edge away. That almost made it worse. It was not pardon. It was proximity under revised terms.

When they reached the corner, Clemence stopped and looked both ways before crossing. The motion was ordinary. So was what she said next.

"Lucie asked if you'd be back for supper."

Elise kept her face still with effort. "What did you tell her?"

"I told her not to ask me questions I couldn't price."

It was the sort of dry line Clemence allowed herself only when the work had already gone bad enough that wit could not worsen it. Elise heard, inside it, the thing Clemence would not yet say aloud: that every person in the quarry cost something to feed, hide, trust, and bury, and Jeanne Morel had just become more expensive.

They crossed into the next street where the evening had not yet fallen and therefore exposed everything. A greengrocer was still shouting over wilted carrots. Two girls in aprons laughed outside a doorway, not having heard the noise beyond the passage house. Laundry water ran in a bright thread along the gutter. The city went on with the indecency of all cities, using the same stones for panic and commerce and appetite, never bothering to choose.

Elise felt the absence at her waist where the packet had been hidden. It was a physical emptiness, not symbolic, a strip of loosened seam against her skin. She had cut herself off with household fire and gutter water. No dead-drop note could undo that now. No tidy report could place her again in Vautrin's careful columns. If she wanted back into his good graces she would have to go in person, with all the risk of that, and offer him something larger than paper. A name. A map. A clean betrayal.

She knew, even before the thought had finished, that she did not have one clean enough left to offer.

Clemence did not speak again for the length of the street. She only kept walking with that square practical stride, red hands bare at her sides, blood drying in the cracks around her knuckles. On another day Elise might have asked how bad Reine's head wound looked, whether Nadir had enough money for the bone-setter, whether the notes had all been lost upstairs. The questions crowded her and found nowhere to go.

At the far end of the block Clemence slowed just enough to make Elise look at her.

"When we get back," she said, "you'll tell Lucien exactly how that room went bad."

Not who sent you. Not what you burned. Not why you moved before the door. Exactly how. Clemence was already setting terms, narrowing the ground on which lies would have to stand.

"All right," Elise said.

"And if you try to dress it up, I'll stop you."

"I heard you."

"I know."

They went on toward the road that would take them below again, carrying nothing from the errand except blood on their cuffs, the memory of paper left under boots, and a silence that had changed its shape. Clemence's suspicion did not blaze. It settled. Elise could feel it there between them, no wider than a ledger line and just as permanent once inked.
