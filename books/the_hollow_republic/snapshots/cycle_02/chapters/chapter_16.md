# Chapter 16

The lamps had been burning badly in the summer closeness, but this was not lamp smoke. Lamp smoke stayed greasy around the chimney glass. This came under the stone like a patient hand, tarry and deliberate, and found Elise in the narrow alcove near the ration room where Clemence had put her after the truth came out, after Lucien's low questions about flood passages and children's shoes had finally blurred into sleep. By the time she opened her eyes it had already settled into the blanket under her cheek and roughened the back of her throat.

Limestone grit sifted from the roof. Someone was running in the gallery, and it was not just one person but several, moving with something more deliberate than the blunt pounding panic of a raid already in the room. These steps stopped, turned, and started again as people counted, carried, and decided.

Lucie stood in the mouth of the alcove with one stocking fallen to her ankle and a paper swallow crushed in her fist so hard that Nadir's careful folds had gone soft at the beak. Her hair had been braided for sleep and had come half loose. She looked not at Elise's face but at the smoke flattening itself along the ceiling.

"They're making the air bad at the wine stairs," she said. "Is it the wine stairs?"

Elise was up before the question finished. Her dress was still stiff where the hem had been resewn after Clemence ripped out the brass marker. She shoved her feet into her boots without lacing them, the old pull in her side catching when she bent, and stepped into the gallery.

The quarry had a dozen ordinary smells that fought one another every day: lamp oil, wet wool, cold bread, the sour iron of printer's ink that never quite left the deeper chambers, soap from Clemence's buckets, the mineral damp that sweated out of the walls no matter the season. This smoke sat over all of them. It smelled of wet straw and pitch, with something sharper underneath, almost chemical. It had purpose. A raid with pistols had speed in it; this smoke promised masonry.

People were already dragging bundles into the sleeping gallery and then abandoning them when they realized what their own hands had chosen: blankets, a kettle with no lid, a heel of bread, Therese Bertaud's sewing basket. Therese herself had the basket hooked over her arm and the wooden darning mushroom she always kept tucked at her waistband knocked against her hip as she moved. She had once spent an entire supper explaining how she would mend a gentleman's coat so neatly he would never know the elbow had gone, and now she stood in her night jacket, coughing into one wrist, trying to decide whether thread counted as life.

Clemence came out of the ration room with Lucie's other stocking still looped through the apron string at her waist, as if she had meant to finish mending it before breakfast and the morning had split open first. Her hands were wet to the wrists. She had been at the water bucket already.

When she saw Elise she did not waste time on surprise or accusation. The accusation had been settled the night before and the night before that. She only said, "If you've still got anything hidden, now is the hour to name it."

"Not this," Elise said.

"No," Clemence said. "I suppose not."

At the turn toward the schoolroom Lucien was listening to the stone as if it were a pupil refusing to read clearly. He still had his tobacco tin of chalk ends in one hand. He saved the blunt white stubs because waste offended him more than inconvenience. A handkerchief was bunched in the other. Chalk dust had settled into the dark hairs at his wrist and along one cuff. Somebody must have pulled him out of a lesson or he had tried to begin one anyway, out of habit, out of defiance, out of the old schoolmaster's superstition that routine could keep a room human.

He looked at Elise once and then past her, toward the cellar entrance no one could see from here.

"Listen," he said.

She did. One muffled thud from the direction of the wine stairs. Another, farther north and lower, where an old slit passage had once taken air before a cave-in cut it almost useless. Then the scrape of stone against stone, men shifting weight together, and a shouted order blurred by distance and smoke.

"Three mouths," Elise said.

"At least," Lucien answered. His voice was plain already, stripped by the first coughing fit. "The cellar, the north slit, and maybe the cart shaft if they found it."

One of the older men from the gallery, a cooper named Marius who slept with dried mint in his vest because he believed it kept the damp out of his chest, spat into the chalk dust and said, "You'd know how they'd do it."

The words landed where they meant to. Half the room looked at Elise. The other half kept looking toward the smoke.

She said, "If they wanted bodies quickly, they'd come down and lose some of their own in the dark. This is cheaper. They'll bank smoke at every opening they can find on the survey, brick what they can brick, and wait for us to cough our way toward daylight."

"You say that like kitchen work," Therese Bertaud muttered.

"Because to them it's kitchen work," Elise said.

Another thud shivered through the wall. White dust came down in a fine sift over Lucie's hair. She wiped it away with the same hand that held the paper swallow and only blurred it into paste.

Lucien closed his eyes for one breath, not to steady himself but to count. When he opened them, the room had made its choice for him simply by watching. Nobody else here knew the old survey lines well enough. Nobody else had heard the roof for years the way he had. Nobody else had lived long enough underground to know which draft belonged to air and which belonged to collapse.

"Clemence," he said, "the sump."

Her mouth tightened. "That run's half drowned."

"Better drowned than smoked."

"The first squeeze takes the shoulder off anybody broad as me."

"I know."

She looked at Elise before she looked back at him. The glance measured cost instead of asking mercy.

Lucien turned to Elise. The smoke had already begun to scrape at his lungs. She could hear the damp drag in the center of each breath before the cough took it. "You remember the linen map."

She had seen it twice laid open, once by accident and once because he had wanted her to. Linen backing, survey hand beneath his corrections. Ink faded in the older cuts, newer pencil marks on top. She had studied it then as a professional instrument. Now the memory came back under pressure in sections: the sleeping gallery, the deeper print chamber, a ragged branch marked with Lucien's cramped note about floodwater and rotten footing.

"The drain below the ration room," she said.

"Yes. It was cut to take seepage toward the Bievre before the north fall changed the level. Most of it's still passable if the spring runoff didn't choke it."

"Most of it," Clemence said.

"That's the kindest answer I've got."

He coughed then, hard enough to bend him. The handkerchief came away dark at the center when he pressed it back into his palm. Lucie saw it. Elise saw Lucie see it.

"How far?" Lucie asked. Fear always made her ask for the measurable part. "Is it farther than the schoolroom? Is it farther than the print room?"

Lucien put the chalk tin down on a ledge so carefully that the tiny pieces did not rattle. "Farther than you want and shorter than staying here."

He gestured for Clemence to open the ration room niche. She shoved aside two sacks of flour gone gray at the seams and levered up a stone slab Elise had always taken for part of the floor. Cold air breathed from below, wetter and fouler than the quarry's usual damp. The hole was not a stair. It was a throat.

People recoiled from the smell.

The cooper said, "I'm not sending my lungs into that drain after a police whore."

"Then keep them here," Clemence snapped. "See how much tar they hold."

The man's mouth worked, but he did not move toward the exit either.

Lucien said, "Children and anyone who can't run go first. Lucie. Therese. Pons. The two boys. Marius. Clemence, you take the rear. Elise takes the front."

Clemence did not raise her voice. She did not need to. "You can ask me to crawl. You can't ask me to hand my girl over to her."

Elise felt every face come back to her.

Lucien said, "The first bend narrows. I'll jam there. You'll jam there. Elise won't."

"You don't know what she'll do once we're in it."

"I know what the smoke will do if you stay out of it."

That shut nobody up because nobody had anything useful left to say. The silence went thick with hatred and calculation.

Elise stepped past them to the open slab. The stone lip was slick under her palm. She hitched her skirt, put one boot down, then the other, and descended until the black water took her mid-calf. It was colder than the air above, and the cold bit straight through the half-healed pull in her side. The passage pinched at once, limestone on one side, old brick on the other where someone had once lined a runoff channel in a more hopeful decade. She crouched with one forearm braced tight against her ribs, moved forward three body lengths, then five. The roof stooped lower. Mud sucked at her boots. Ahead, where the passage forked, a thread of air touched the wet skin below her ear from the right-hand arm.

She backed out before the smoke behind her thickened further. When she rose through the floor hole the water ran black off her hem and pattered on the ration stones.

"It goes," she said. "The right branch still pulls air. The left is dead water."

The cooper looked at her skirt, at the muck streaking her stockings, and then away. He still did not trust her, but the mud proved the drain went somewhere.

Lucien crouched by the ration ledge and pulled a small oilcloth packet from behind the flour sack where Clemence kept the meaner necessities: extra lamp wick, the last hard soap, money nobody admitted aloud. He opened it. One fold held notes and coins. The other held papers: labor books, rent receipts, letters with names that could open one poor door and close another.

"The money goes with you," he said to Clemence.

"And the papers?"

"The wrong ones stay with me."

Elise understood before anyone asked. The decent forgeries had already been used or moved. What remained were the dirty copies, the emptied addresses, and the names of men who had crossed the river or gone to ground months ago. They were enough to make police feel rewarded and enough to bend a search the wrong way for a few hours.

"Come with us," she said. It came out flatter than pleading. She did not know how to plead with him now without sounding like she meant to purchase something.

He met her eyes only long enough to refuse cleanly. "They know my face. They know the cough. Let them spend the morning on both."

"You'll be taken."

"Yes."

He said it like a number he had already carried once and knew the weight of.

Smoke rolled thicker along the ceiling. Somewhere back toward the cellar entrance a child began crying with the hiccuping outrage of a chest that could not fill properly. Clemence turned at once, swore under her breath, and dragged the smaller of the two boys toward the ration room by the wrist. Lucie did not need pulling. She had gone very still, which frightened Elise more than tears would have.

Lucien put the false papers into his coat. Then, as if remembering himself too late, he touched the chalk tin on the ledge with two fingers and left it there.

"Keep the right wall until the brick ends," he said to Elise. "Then duck. There's a root through the roof where the earth comes near the top. After that you'll hear the water split around a stone lip. Take the lower sound, not the louder one. The louder branch falls back into the old workings."

She repeated it once. He nodded.

"If the grate at the far end sticks, the third brick on the left is loose enough to use as a lever."

Clemence was already loading bodies toward the opening with the savage efficiency of a woman who had spent too many years making a household move on less than enough. "No bundles bigger than your own chest," she said. "Drop the pot. Drop it, I said. Therese, keep the needle roll if you must, but the basket stays. Lucie, where's your scarf? On. Now."

Therese Bertaud made one last small noise over the sewing basket before she dumped it. Spools hit the stone and rolled. One bright green thread ran a little way toward the floor hole like it meant to sew the quarry closed.

Elise held out her hand to Lucie.

Clemence saw it and gave a short, humorless breath through her nose. Then she took Lucie's hand herself, put it around Elise's scarred wrist instead of her palm, and said, "Hold there. Wrists don't slip."

Lucie tightened her fingers at once. "How far?" she asked again.

"Far enough to hate me," Elise said, "and not far enough to stop. Come on."

She went down into the throat of the drain and took the child with her.

The first stretch had to be done bent almost double. The water reached Elise's knees, then dropped, then rose again where the floor had sunk under years of runoff. Each step tugged at the half-healed damage in her side until she had to breathe shallowly to keep it from seizing. Behind her she could hear the order of bodies by sound alone: Lucie's short breaths trying not to become panic, the wet drag of Therese's bad foot, Pons testing each foothold with the care his bad eyes had taught him, the small smothered coughs of the boys, Marius swallowing his own until one tore loose, Clemence's voice behind everyone else cutting clean practical commands through the dark.

"Lower your head."

"Not there. My left, your left."

"If you vomit, vomit and move."

The roof came down farther after ten yards. Elise had to turn the way that hurt least and slide with one forearm clamped over her side. The old brick lining gave way to raw limestone that felt greasy under her fingertips, rubbed smooth by water rather than use. Lucie's grip on her wrist tightened so hard it hurt.

"Is this under the bread room?" Lucie whispered.

A shudder passed through the stone overhead, not a full collapse but something heavy shifting where it had no right to. Silt thickened in the water around Elise's calves.

"Keep moving," Elise said.

"If it's under the bread room, Mother's jars are gone."

Elise pictured the ration ledge without wanting to: the sacks, the hard soap, the chipped blue bowl Clemence used for flour, Lucien's notebook once hidden there, the floor slab standing open like a mouth. She did not answer because an answer would have required either hope or precision, and she had neither to spare.

The passage opened just enough for her to straighten one vertebra at a time. She lifted the lamp she had borrowed from the ration room. The flame was low and mean inside its dirty chimney, but it caught the fork ahead. The left arm was wider. The right was narrower and darker. No air came from the wider branch.

Behind her the cooper said, "Left climbs. I worked cuts like this before you were born."

Elise put the lamp down by the right wall and held her wet fingers up to the dark. The faint draft touched them again. On the limestone just beyond the right-hand bend, she found the edge of old brick under her palm, then lost it where the lining had broken away.

"Left climbs into a sump," she said. "Smell it."

The left branch stank of standing water and stone that never saw daylight. The right smelled worse in one sense and better in another: rot, mud, current, air from somewhere that had touched weeds and heat before it came back underground.

"You smell the outside?" Lucie asked, voice small and intent.

"A little."

"Then go."

Elise took the right arm. Therese Bertaud muttered something about saints she no longer believed in. One of the boys lost control of his stomach and vomited bright yellow into the black water, then began crying because his shoe floated off in the same moment. Elise bent, caught the shoe against the wall, and shoved it back into his hands without stopping.

The passage sank lower. Mud rose nearly to her thighs. Each high step wrenched at the healing damage under her stays. Once the roof pressed so close she had to turn her face sideways to keep the lamp chimney from striking stone. Her shoulder blades rubbed rock. The lamp glass collected soot, then moisture. The air changed with each body added behind her, warmed by breath and fear until the drain seemed to be swallowing them and digesting them at once.

She counted without speaking: herself, Lucie, Therese, Pons with the bad eyes, the smaller boy, the older boy, Marius with the mint smell, and Clemence at the back. Eight living weights were connected by the dark.

This was what her reports had never contained: not only names, entrances, the press, false papers, or the price of coal, but the time it took a frightened child to lower herself into water she could not see, the room an old woman's hip required at a turn, and the fact that one wrong pair of shoulders could stop all the rest.

Something struck stone behind them with a crack like a plate broken underfoot. The drain breathed inward hard. Lucie gave one sharp gasp and flattened against Elise.

"Was that the room?" she said. "Was that our room?"

Before Elise could answer, Clemence's voice came thin through the bend. "Forward, Lucie. Ask me outside."

The reply was so like her that the child obeyed it on instinct.

The next choke point took Therese Bertaud almost to pieces. Her skirt snagged on a protruding shard of brick and the remaining needle roll in her pocket slipped down into the water. She twisted after it with a cry of genuine grief, not because the needles were sentimental but because metal cost money and money cost labor and labor cost years.

"Leave it," Elise said.

"I paid for those."

"Then curse them later. Turn sideways."

Therese did not. Panic made her stupid and strong together. She shoved back against the bodies behind her. The whole line locked.

Elise wedged the lamp on a ledge, reached into the black water up to her forearm, found the sodden cloth roll by feel, and slapped it into Therese's hand.

"Now move."

Therese began to cry then, not elegantly and with no old-woman dignity left, but with wet, furious sobs that turned to coughing and then to obedience. She turned. Elise dragged her by the sleeve through the brick teeth until the snag tore free and the passage took her.

The boys behind started crying in answer. Pons cursed them with a tenderness he tried to hide.

"Keep them low," Clemence said. "If you can talk, you can crawl."

They kept going.

The root Lucien had named came down through the ceiling twenty yards later, pale and blind in the lamp glow, thick as a child's wrist. Real earth smell clung to it. Elise ducked under, scraping her back, and felt the space change again. The water quickened around her ankles. Somewhere ahead it divided itself around stone, exactly as Lucien had said, with one sound broad and showy and another narrow and lower, almost hidden by the first.

She paused only long enough to hear properly. Behind her the line breathed, waited, hated her, needed her. She chose the lower sound and bent into it.

The drain narrowed so severely after that that Lucie had to let go and crawl with both hands. Elise moved backward for a moment, guiding the child by elbows and shoulders, pushing her feet toward the right purchase in the mud.

"Don't look up," she said.

"I wasn't."

"Good."

"How much more?"

"More."

Lucie swallowed and nodded once, as if the answer had at least belonged to a world with measurements in it.

When the child got through, Elise sent the first boy after her, then the second. Pons came next and stuck halfway because terror made him arch his back against the stone. Elise put both palms flat on his ribs and pushed while Clemence cursed him from behind with kitchen precision so filthy it almost made the smaller boy laugh through his crying.

Then it was the cooper's turn. Halfway through the squeeze he said, very quietly, "If you drop us here, there won't be enough room to come back and finish it."

Elise knew what he meant. He was talking about betrayal, not about the stone.

"Then don't make yourself heavy," she said, and shoved until he came free.

The lamp went out two turns later, not with a dramatic gutter but with a small surrendering hiss as dirty water slopped across the base. Darkness shut over them at once, total and physical. Lucie made a sound that was not yet a scream.

Elise put one hand on the wall and the other back until she found Lucie's hair, damp braid half undone.

"Here," she said.

Clemence's voice came from the rear, thinner now, farther than Elise liked. "Talk, all of you."

So they did, because silence in the dark would have made the drain too wide for the mind to hold.

Pons said, "I'm here."

Therese said, "My foot's in the wrong of it."

One boy said, "I lost the shoe again."

Lucie said, "I've got the bird."

The child had held onto the paper swallow through mud and panic and vomit and the squeeze where adults nearly got trapped alive. Elise felt something in her own chest give way from usefulness into pain and then harden again because pain was no use here.

"Keep your hand on my skirt," she said. "When it pulls, move."

The air freshened by degrees so slight she first mistrusted them. Then it came colder, not with quarry damp but with the outside world's filthy range in it: weed rot, horse piss, river slime, dye vats somewhere distant, hot stone touched by sun. The roof lifted. Mud gave way to gravel. Her fingers found iron ahead: the grate.

It had been meant once to keep rubbish out of the drain mouth. Rust had eaten it nearly through at the lower bars, but the upper hinge still held. Elise felt along the wall to the third brick on the left, exactly as Lucien had promised. It shifted under her thumb.

She worked it free, used it as a lever, and drove the corner between iron and stone. Each shove jarred her side hard enough to light her vision at the edges, so she planted one knee in the gravel and put her weight through the brick until the hinge gave with a shriek loud enough to sound like the police themselves had found them.

Daylight knifed through the gap. It opened first onto a ditch, nettles, a wall beyond, and then a slice of white-hot summer over the top of it. It was enough.

Elise shoved the grate outward, crawled into black ditch water and weed stalks, then turned and hauled Lucie through by the forearms. The child came up coated to the elbows in mud, hair hanging in ropes, face streaked white where chalk dust had turned to paste on her skin.

"Outside," Lucie said, not as joy but as fact checking.

"Yes."

They dragged the others out one by one. Therese emerged weeping and clutching the salvaged needle roll like relics. Pons blinked in the light as if it insulted him personally. The smaller boy collapsed onto the bank and retched river water and bile into the nettles. The older boy came after him on one bare foot, clutching the rescued shoe to his chest. Marius lay on his back for three breaths with his mint leaves plastered to his chest.

Clemence came last, shoulder and hip scraping hard enough against the stone lip to leave skin on it. She rolled clear, got to her knees at once, and counted heads aloud, tapping each one with two fingers as if she were checking linen at the wash.

"Lucie. Therese. Pons. The small one. The older one. Marius. Me."

Her eyes cut to Elise.

"You count yourself."

Elise counted herself and made eight.

The ditch ran behind a dyer's boundary wall where stripped willow branches leaned in a heap and somebody had dumped broken crockery years ago. The Bievre lay somewhere beyond the next row of yards. The air was foul in the ordinary aboveground way, broad and layered and alive. After the drain it felt obscene in its abundance.

From the direction of the quarry came the dull, continuous labor sound of men sealing something they intended to own afterward.

Lucie twisted to look back toward the wall, toward nothing she could actually see from here. "Where's Lucien?"

Clemence wiped mud off her mouth with the back of one hand. "Buying us time."

"How much?"

Clemence did not answer because no answer would have kept the child quiet.

Elise stood. Her legs were shaking, not with generalized fear but with the cold still locked into her muscles from the drain water. Mud sucked at her hem. Her scarred wrist throbbed where Lucie had gripped it, and when she straightened fully the damage in her side answered with a hot pull under her ribs. Beyond the wall another shout carried, then another. They were not close yet.

"If they take the papers and believe them," she said, "they'll push east first. They think in addresses once they think they've won."

Clemence's mouth hardened. "And if they don't?"

"Then they sweep the yards."

"You sound cheerful."

"I sound exact."

For a moment Clemence only stared at her. Then she nodded once toward the children huddled in the ditch bank shadow.

"So be exact usefully. See what the cordon's doing."

It was neither forgiveness nor trust. It was labor assigned where labor might help. Elise took it for what it was.

"Keep them low," she said. "If I don't come back in ten minutes, you move west and don't wait."

"We were moving west before you were born," Clemence said.

Elise left them under the nettles and climbed the ditch with both hands, favoring the hurt side even though it slowed her.

The ground above the wall was a ragged strip of scrub between yards, hot already under the noon sun. The sudden brightness after the drain made her eyes water. She moved bent at the waist through waist-high weeds and broken plaster, partly for cover and partly because standing upright pulled too hard at the half-healed tear in her side, until she had a view over a stack of rotting barrels toward the slope where the wine-shop entrance lay hidden beneath ordinary Paris business on other days.

There was nothing ordinary there now.

Smoke drifted out low and brown from the cellar stair mouth and from a second opening farther north where old quarry stone had once been disguised by rubbish and scrub. Police stood back from both, not charging, not brave, cloths tied over nose and mouth, sleeves rolled, more work gang than assault party. Beside them municipal laborers and hired masons were already setting rubble and mortar at the wine stairs. A cart of broken stone waited uphill. Another had tipped its load beside the north slit. This was how the state did heroism when it expected no witnesses worth respecting: with shovels, contractors, lime.

At the edge of the cordon stood Vautrin in a summer coat the color of old bone. Even here, even with smoke drifting across his polished boots, he kept his hat level and his cuffs cleaner than the men who obeyed him. One sleeve carried a streak of mortar dust where he had brushed against the unfinished wall. He had not noticed it yet.

Elise flattened herself behind the barrels and looked for Lucien.

She found him where the mapped north slit opened into light between two heaps of spoil. He did not burst out. He climbed. One hand on the stone, one on the bank, coat blackened at the front, his body pausing halfway for a cough that looked big enough to tear him loose from himself. When he reached air properly he got one breath. Then the police were on him.

He did not fight them. Fighting would have spent the wrong currency. He let them wrench his arms back. The false papers came half out of his coat in the struggle, a fan of damp labor books and receipts and folded letters dropping into the dust.

Vautrin crossed the last yards at a walk, as if arriving late to a meeting he nevertheless controlled. One officer held Lucien upright by the elbows while another kicked the scattered papers together with his boot.

Even at this distance Elise could read Lucien's exhaustion in the set of his shoulders. He had spent what lungs he had left underground and saved the rest for this.

Vautrin stooped, picked up a labor book, opened it, then another. His posture changed almost invisibly, not with surprise but with appetite.

He said something Elise could not hear over the scrape of the masons at the wine stairs. Whatever it was sent two men and a sergeant running east at once, down toward streets where the rooms named in those papers no longer held the people attached to them. That much was good. Lucien had bought them something after all.

Then Vautrin said another sentence, quieter. The officer nearest him nodded and pointed at the cellar mouth. The masons resumed, stone knocking on stone while mortar slapped wet between the courses. A man with a shovel began throwing loose rubble into the north slit behind Lucien as if filling a rut in the road.

Lucien turned his head once, not far enough to search the yards, only far enough to look toward the quarry hill itself while they tied his wrists. Smoke streaked his cheek. White limestone dust clung to the silver in his hair. Elise could not tell whether he was listening for collapse, for voices below, for nothing at all.

Vautrin took hold of Lucien's chin and lifted it in a gesture so controlled it was worse than a blow.

This time Elise heard him.

"You have been expensive," he said.

Lucien answered, and the answer came back to her only in fragments because a cart wheel hit stone and drowned the middle of it.

"... cheaper than your lie ..."

The officer behind him struck him in the kidneys for that, efficient as closing a drawer. Lucien folded at the waist, coughed hard, and nearly went to his knees. Vautrin stepped back so the mud from the boots would not touch him.

The quarry gave a low interior thunder then, some partial fall or deliberate dump of rubble into an already weakened shaft. The ground under Elise's palms trembled. One of the laborers swore and jumped back from the north slit. Dust breathed out around the stones they were shoveling in, white and sudden as flour from a burst sack.

There it was: no dramatic annihilation, no clean ending the newspapers would prefer, only a home being made uninhabitable by inches. That meant the schoolroom wall where Lucien had written sums in chalk, Clemence's ration ledge, the sleeping gallery with its patched blankets and bad dreams and bodies packed close through winter, the copper hand press left in the deeper chamber because metal had become less urgent than breath, the floor slab over the drain, and the tobacco tin of chalk stubs on the ledge. All of it remained under the hill while men above argued over paperwork and mixed mortar.

Lucie had once asked Elise whether caves remembered voices. Elise had almost laughed then at the childishness of it. Now, watching the wall go up across the wine stairs and the rubble choke the slit, she understood the better question was what happened when the voices had nowhere left to go.

Vautrin handed the damp false papers to a sergeant. Lucien's wrists were tied. A wagon had been brought up, plain and municipal, the sort used to move confiscated furniture or sacks of spoiled grain. They put him in it with the same economy.

Elise stayed where she was until the cart jolted once and started toward the city. Only then did she back down from the barrels and return through the weeds.

Clemence had the children moving already, west along the ditch where willow shadow broke the line of sight from the yards. Therese limped and cried at intervals without admitting she was crying. One of the boys had Lucie's paper swallow now because the child's hands were needed to keep hold of her mother's skirt. The swallow had gone soft enough to tear at the next hard touch.

Clemence looked once at Elise's face and read the answer there.

"Taken?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Did he turn them?"

"East first."

Clemence nodded. That was all either of them could afford from the news.

Lucie, still clutching the back of her mother's skirt, said, "Are we going home after dark?"

No one corrected the word. No one said there was no home left under the hill, only chambers filling with smoke and dust and the slow state craft of walling up what poor people had made useful.

Elise looked back once through the gap between two yards. Above the rubble line the summer sky had gone brilliantly empty. Below it the quarry mouth vanished course by course under a mason's trowel. The men worked steadily, as if closing a pantry. Smoke still lived in the cloth at Elise's shoulder, warmed back to life each time she moved.

Then Clemence hissed for the line to move, and Elise moved with them because the living were still on this side of the wall.
