# Chapter 14

Fine white grit kept turning up between Elise's teeth. Each time she swallowed, she found another grain of the tollhouse cellar and worked it loose with her tongue. The blast had begun to sort itself into separate claims on her body: pain along her left side, where stone and splintered wood had come through wool and skin together; the sting in her scalp under a strip of linen that smelled of boiled soap.

The quarry had gone back to its ordinary sounds around her. Water dripped somewhere deeper in the tunnels. A man coughed in the print chamber and spat into a pail. Carriages rolled overhead in a dull wooden thunder that felt farther away than the sky. The enclosed air held lamp oil, wet wool, printer's ink, old soap, and a faint sweet rotten edge from the singed timber that still clung to her hair.

Clemence had done the night's work already. She had cut Elise out of the worst of the dress in the dark, packed the torn place at her ribs with folded lint, and bound it tight enough that breathing required attention. The bandage had dried a little to the skin. When Elise shifted on the pallet, the linen tugged at her side and brought the whole cellar back in a white hot instant: Abel's shoulder under her hands, Ferrand shouting above them, the kick of the satchel as it went off, the wall arriving where the air should have been.

"Don't move like that," Clemence said from the brazier. "If you tear it open again, I'm not wasting another strip this morning."

Her voice was brisk with lost sleep. She stood in shirtsleeves with her apron tied on over her skirt and a dented cocoa tin open beside her knee, full of saved buttons, clean pins, a needle stuck through a scrap of felt, and short lengths of thread wound around cardboard. Nothing in the tin had been bought new. Clemence kept such things the way another woman might keep relics.

Lucie sat on an upturned crate near the lamp, one stocking rolled at the ankle and the other properly pulled, as if she had begun to dress and then forgotten the second half of the task. A paper fox, softened from pocket sweat and handling, kept appearing and disappearing in her fist. Nadir had folded it for her weeks ago from a spoiled handbill. She had flattened one ear by worrying it with her thumb. Now she leaned over a newspaper almost as broad as her chest and mouthed the letters under her breath.

"Con... spi..." she whispered. "Conspi..."

"You can wait for bread before you eat the ink," Clemence said.

"I wasn't eating it."

"You were licking your finger."

Lucie tucked the finger into her palm, chastened for exactly one breath, then bent over the page again. Elise watched her mouth shape the syllables and felt the peculiar shame of surviving into daylight. Abel was dead. Ferrand was dead. Nadir might be dead as well. The child still had a paper fox in her hand and a headline under it.

Elise pushed herself up on one elbow and had to stop halfway. The bandage bit. Black dots moved through her vision and thinned slowly.

"Water," she said.

Clemence crossed without hurry and held the cup while Elise drank. The tin taste of boiled water gave way to limestone. Elise could not tell whether her hands were shaking or Clemence's were simply more solid.

"Where's Lucien?"

"Up," Clemence said. "He went for the morning sheets and to see what else the city is selling with them."

At that, Lucie looked up. "He said he'd bring the one with pictures if there was one."

"He said he'd bring himself back in one piece," Clemence said. "The pictures are extra."

The irony of that nearly made Elise laugh, and laughter would have split something. She swallowed it down. Her mouth still tasted of burned wool.

Lucien came in a few minutes later with street dust on his cuffs and daylight on his shoulders, carrying three folded newspapers under one arm and a heel of bread wrapped in yesterday's sheet under the other. When he bent to set them on the crate, something bruised and orange rolled from his coat pocket and landed against Lucie's shoe.

It was an apricot. It had split down one side and stained the lining of his pocket.

Lucie stared at it, then at him.

He looked at the fruit as if he had found a message from another life. "I bought those yesterday," he said. His voice was hoarse, plain with fatigue. "I forgot one."

Lucie picked it up carefully. "It's still good."

"The clean half's good." He rubbed a thumb over the newsprint before unfolding it, a schoolmaster's old habit, flattening the creases as if tidier paper might produce tidier facts. "Eat it before your mother sees you waste the sun."

"I already see it," Clemence said.

"Then I stand corrected."

Lucie scraped the burst side off against the edge of the crate and sucked the rest down in two intent bites, apricot juice on her wrist, fox tucked under her elbow. The room watched her eat. It was not hunger alone that made them quiet. The ordinary act had become indecently dear.

Lucien laid the newspapers across Elise's blanket. The black ink had already come off on his fingertips.

"What are they saying?" she asked.

"Enough," he said. "More than enough."

He opened the first paper. The headline took half the front page.

ANARCHIST PLOT AT PORTE D'ORLEANS  
TWO FANATICS DESTROYED BY THEIR OWN MACHINE

The second had chosen a different arrangement of the same appetite. SECRET CELLAR OF NIHILISTS. The third preferred civic triumph: THE POLICE TRACE A VAST CONSPIRACY.

Lucie sounded out the last word, slower this time. "Con... spir... a... cy."

"Yes," Lucien said. "That one."

He read standing up, because there was nowhere in the chamber that did not already belong to someone's pain or labor. Ferrand had become a "known extremist courier." Abel was a "young disciple corrupted by foreign doctrines and criminal propaganda." A schoolmaster of notorious sympathies was said to have directed the circle but remained at large. The tollhouse explosion, which Elise remembered as a cellar, a body in her grip, and one terrified second of useless prayer to a God she did not believe in, had become in print an attempted massacre narrowly averted by the vigilance of the authorities.

The piece handled Ferrand with a clerk's special care, as though the copy had been warned not to smear a useful corpse. It placed him among the dead without a smear of doubt, as though he had spent his last breath in service to the cause instead of trying to feed it bodies. Vautrin would have arranged that. A dead provocateur was more useful as evidence than as payroll.

"Read the rest," Clemence said.

Lucien did not answer at once. He turned the page with the side of his hand. Elise saw his cough gathering before it came. He looked away, coughed into his fist, then went on.

"There's a note lower down," he said. "On Belkacem."

Lucie straightened. The sucked apricot pit sat bright in her palm like a small wet bone.

Lucien read the paragraph once to himself before he read it aloud. "'The named associate Belkacem, arrested earlier this week in connection with the same anti-social milieu, died yesterday while attempting escape during transfer after a violent struggle with his escort. In view of ongoing inquiries, the body has not yet been returned to relations.'"

No one in the room moved.

The sentence had the finished polish Elise knew too well. The phrases not returned, ongoing inquiries, and violent struggle were language built to make a corpse do clerical work after the heart had stopped. She could hear the office in it: a pen scraped clean, a blotter pressed down, a man saying that this wording would do because nobody worth counting would ask what Belkacem's face looked like afterward.

"They killed him," Lucie said.

Clemence drew in a breath through her nose. "Eat the rest of your bread when we have it."

"I know they killed him." Lucie looked from one adult to the other with furious child patience. "I didn't ask that part."

She set the apricot pit on the newspaper, right on the word transfer. "How do you run if they took your belt and your laces? They always take those."

Lucien lowered the page. For a moment his face was older than the silver in his hair. "You don't, most likely," he said.

Elise put her fingers on the column and drew the sheet closer. The paper crackled under her hand. There it was in smaller print, buried as if burial inside the page mattered: attempted escape. She had heard policemen say it at tavern doors over men who could barely stand, over women dragged by the wrists, over bodies still warm enough to steam in winter. An escape attempt could mean a boot, a truncheon, a staircase, a bucket of water thrown too late. It could mean they had wanted words from him and had taken too much flesh while getting them.

"They didn't return the body because they don't want it counted," she said.

The room took that in.

Clemence's gaze shifted to her with a quick sharpness that felt more surgical than surprise. "You know their phrases well."

Elise kept looking at the print. "I've heard enough of them."

"That's not what I said."

Lucien cut across before the line could harden. "The paper says what it was written to say. That's the part we know."

"We know Ferrand's in there clean as baptism," Clemence said. "That bastard never looked that decent alive."

Lucie's mouth tightened around the child effort not to cry. "Will Nadir have a grave?"

No one answered quickly enough.

Lucien folded the top of the page back with deliberate care. "Not yet," he said. "But a man doesn't stop belonging to the earth because the Prefecture mislays him."

Lucie nodded as if filing the sentence for later argument with God, teachers, or officials. She put the apricot pit in her pocket beside the paper fox.

Elise read the article again while Lucien moved through the other pages. The details disagreed from column to column. One paper said two bombs. Another said a cellar arsenal. A third hinted at foreign funds and secret maps. The lies did not need to match. They only needed to accumulate until Ferrand's name, Abel's name, and Belkacem's name all lay under the word conspiracy like flies under a net. By afternoon the city would know the shape it had been instructed to see.

And somewhere in the same city Vautrin would be drinking coffee under clean windows, turning dead men into proof.

Lucien laid the second paper on top of the first. "My name isn't printed," he said.

"Yet," Clemence answered.

"Yet."

"Then he thinks he still has use for the hunt."

"Or for the witness," Elise said before she could stop herself.

Lucien looked at her. The silence that followed was not Clemence's practical silence or Lucie's baffled one. It held recognition, old and not yet fully spoken.

"Yes," he said at last. "Possibly that too."

His eyes dropped to the bandage under her blouse, to the line where dried blood had browned the linen. He came and crouched beside the pallet. The closeness brought street air and tobacco that had not been lit, only carried. His hand hovered once over her shoulder as if asking permission from flesh instead of from her mouth, then settled lightly on the blanket near her knee.

"How bad?" he asked.

"Bad enough to make Clemence insulting."

"That means you'll live," Clemence said.

"You saved a room full of strangers who will spend the day reading that you meant to blow them apart," Lucien said.

"A poor use of labor."

It should have been dry. Instead it came out ragged. The laugh that followed scraped her side and turned into a hiss.

Lucien's hand tightened on the blanket. "Don't do that again."

"What, save them?"

"Pretend you're made of hinges and string. We can quarrel when you can stand."

The word quarrel hung between them like a future both near and expensive. Elise looked at his hand and knew exactly how little she had earned the right to want it there.

From deeper in the quarry someone called Lucien's name. The caller arrived a breath later: a cooper from the upper gallery, broad in the shoulders and powdered to the elbows with limestone, with a strip of sacking tied around one wrist where the staves had rubbed him raw. He did not look long at Elise on the pallet, because wounded bodies had become part of the morning's furniture, but his breath came fast enough to show what the papers had done to the rest of the refuge.

"Delatour. The women by the cistern have started rolling the children's bedding. They say conspiracy in print means a raid before dark, and the press men want to go up for Belkacem before the police put him somewhere nobody can find. Do you want the school slates moved now or later?"

Lucien stood, coughed again into his fist, and gathered two of the papers. "No one goes aboveground in a pack for a body the Prefecture might be using as bait," he said. "Move the children and the slates to the lime gallery. Leave the front cots looking slept in till noon."

"They won't like it."

"I know."

Clemence glanced at Elise's blanket, then at the dress stiff with blood by the pallet. "This bed stays till I'm done sewing her."

Lucien's mouth tightened. "Then be done fast. If the police come, they'll smell last night on the cloth before they hear a word out of anyone."

"Take the bread," Clemence said.

"Cut it first."

"I can do both."

"I know."

He bent and touched Lucie's hair with the backs of his fingers as he passed. She leaned into it for the span of a breath and then pretended she had not. He went out with the papers under his arm, shoulders narrowed against a ceiling too low for any useful kind of pride.

When he was gone, the chamber seemed smaller. The headline stayed on Elise's blanket. The ink had rubbed off where Lucie's apricot pit had sat and made a dark crescent.

Clemence took up the empty cup and basin. "Now," she said. "Before the cloth sticks harder and before half the quarry tramples through here looking for a place to hide a child."

Elise knew what she meant before she reached for the torn dress at the foot of the pallet. In the night's confusion Clemence had pulled most of it off her, but the skirt and one side seam remained tangled under her hip, stiff with blood and quarry dust. Elise had hidden the brass marker in that hem on her first week below ground, three flat stitches inside the fold where no search had found it because no searcher had thought like a woman mending clothes she could not afford to replace.

She had not thought of it in weeks. Pain had driven every smaller secret out of the room.

Now it came back entire.

"Burn that," she said too quickly.

Clemence looked at the dress, then at her. "Burn good wool because you bled on it?"

"It's torn."

"So are you. I'm not feeding cloth to the stove for sentiment."

Lucie slid off the crate with the newspaper under her arm. "Should I go?"

"You should sit in the schoolroom doorway where I can see you," Clemence said. "Take your paper. Read me the big words if you trip over them. It will keep your mouth busy."

"That's not what mouths are for."

"Today that's what they're for."

Lucie went, muttering the headline to herself with offended dignity, fox and apricot pit making little shapes in her pocket. From the doorway she settled cross-legged just beyond the lamp spill, close enough to watch, far enough to pretend she was not, while two older boys carried the school slates past her toward the lime gallery with both hands under the frames to keep them from knocking.

Clemence set the basin on a crate and poured in fresh hot water from the kettle. Steam rose, carrying soap and metal and the wet mineral smell of the quarry. She put the cocoa tin by her knee, drew out the needle and thread, and then reached for the cloth at Elise's side.

"Hold the blanket between your teeth if you need to," she said. "I need both my hands."

"Your manner improves every day."

"And still you stay."

The first peel of linen from skin made the chamber tilt. Elise took the blanket after all. The cloth had glued itself to the torn place under her ribs. Clemence worked slowly, wetting, lifting, wetting again, until the bandage came away pink and then red. Air touched the wound and turned into pain so exact it had corners.

It was not large enough to look theatrical, which almost offended her. The tear ran ragged above the hip, with stone grit still in two places and skin charred brown at one edge where heat had kissed through wool. The body was vulgar in its economies. Ferrand and Abel had died for a blast that had not even given her a handsome scar.

Clemence did not waste pity on it. She wiped the blood away, frowned, and leaned closer. Her knuckles were rough from soap and cold water and labor. She smelled of lye, wool, and the onion she must have cut before dawn.

"You're lucky," she said.

"I had noticed."

"No. You hadn't." Clemence used the clean cloth to press at the deeper side of the tear. "An inch higher and this turns into prayers and piss."

From the doorway Lucie said, "I can hear you, Maman."

"Then hear useful things. Sound out the line under conspiracy."

Lucie obeyed with poor grace. "'The... author... ities...'"

Elise shut her eyes. The words came from two places at once: the child in the doorway and the column on the blanket, both of them laboring under the same language.

Clemence cleaned the wound again. Grit clicked into the basin. Once she had to use the tip of the needle to tease out a splinter buried shallow in the skin, and Elise swore around the blanket before she could stop herself.

Clemence glanced up. "There. Now you sound like someone with blood in her."

Elise spat the blanket out. "Fuck you."

"Better."

Lucie looked over from the doorway, startled enough to forget the newspaper for a second. "You said she ought to swear if it helped."

"I didn't say she ought to teach you new words."

"I already knew that one."

"Of course you did."

Clemence threaded the needle and took two small stitches where the skin would not lie shut by itself. The pull of thread through flesh felt unnaturally domestic, as if sewing and injury had decided to become one profession. Elise watched Clemence's face rather than the work. Fear made Clemence more exact, never softer. Each movement was measured. Each pause had an account behind it.

"How long have you known papers lie like that?" Clemence asked.

The question came as neatly as the stitch.

Elise looked at the ceiling. White dust furred the beams. "Since before this morning."

"That's not an answer either."

"It's the one I've got."

Clemence cut the thread with her teeth. "You always have one."

In the doorway Lucie began again. "'The authorities continue their vigilance against anti-social elements...'"

Her small voice flattened on vigilance and then recovered. Elise felt each syllable like another stitch. There had been nights, before the quarry, when she had copied police dictation till her wrist cramped, taking down the soft phrases men used when they wanted clubs to sound administrative. The Republic loved a clean euphemism. It could push a face into the floorboards and call the gesture vigilance before the blood dried.

Clemence reached for the dress at Elise's hip. "Lift."

Elise did not move quickly enough.

Clemence's hand stilled on the cloth. "Lift," she said again.

Elise pushed herself a little to one side, breath held, and Clemence dragged the remaining skirt free. The hem came with a tearing sound where blast and blood had joined it to the wound. Elise had to bite the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste iron.

The dress looked less like clothing than evidence. One panel was scorched through. The side seam had burst. White quarry dust had caked into the folds. And along the lower hem, where Elise herself had once worked tiny hidden stitches by lamplight, the thread had loosened under the strain.

Clemence saw the loosened seam before she saw what it held. A laundress's eye went there before a spy's fear did.

"This was resewn by someone who didn't expect another pair of hands in it," Clemence said.

"I mend my own things."

"You mend like a clerk."

"There are worse crimes."

"There are also plainer answers."

She slid a fingernail under the half-torn fold to free the cloth from a clot. Elise knew the marker was no bigger than a two-sous piece, brass gone dull from months against wool. She had hidden it because Vautrin had told her never to let it leave her person. It was proof of approval, proof of access, and proof that if another officer took her up she could be claimed instead of jailed. She had carried it against her body the way a poorer woman might carry a saint's medal.

Now it was only weight in the wrong seam.

"Leave that," Elise said.

Clemence did not even look at her. "Don't tell me how to save cloth."

"It's filthy."

"So I'll wash it. We're not rich enough for your disgust."

She tugged the hem open another inch. A few grains of grit pattered into the basin. Then the brass marker came loose and dropped with a clear, mean sound against the enamel.

Lucie looked up at once. "What was that?"

No one answered her.

The disc rocked once in the bottom of the basin, wet now, brighter than it had any right to be. On one side the stamped shield caught the lamp. On the other, under the smear of blood and soap, the word SURETE showed plainly enough for anyone who could read slowly and accurately.

Clemence did not gasp. She did not curse. Her face, which usually moved faster than most people's hands, went still for one second and no longer. Then she put two fingers into the basin, lifted the disc out, and turned it in her palm.

From the doorway Lucie said, quieter, "Maman?"

Clemence closed her hand. "A bit of brass," she said. "Mind your line."

Lucie looked unconvinced, but the newspaper still owned part of her concentration. She bent back over it. "'Against... anti-social...'"

The room did not feel the same after that. Elise saw the change first in Clemence's hands. They were not rougher, exactly, but more deliberate than roughness could ever be. The woman set the marker on the folded edge of her apron for the briefest instant, wiped the basin water from her fingers on her skirt, and tucked the disc into the deep pocket at her hip as if pocketing a coin owed.

Elise could have spoken then. She could have lied, or confessed enough to spoil the lie, or said Vautrin's name out loud and shattered the hour. Pain left her too slow and pride left her too late. More than either of those, Lucie was in the doorway with Nadir's paper fox in her pocket and the newspaper in her lap, working her mouth carefully around the word authorities. There was no way to drag the truth into the room that would not land on the child first.

Clemence dipped a clean strip of linen in the water and wrung it out. "Roll a little," she said.

Elise obeyed.

The bandage went on with infuriating competence. Around them the quarry no longer sounded indifferent. Someone dragged pallets over stone toward the lime gallery. A woman in the schoolroom told the older children to carry the slates flat so they would not clack. The press, usually steady as breathing, had stopped altogether. Aboveground the city was already widening the blast into a story large enough to trample any body that contradicted it. Belowground another story had narrowed to the size of a brass disc warming in Clemence's pocket.

"Does anti-social mean thief?" Lucie asked from the doorway.

"Sometimes," Clemence said.

"Does it mean murderer?"

Clemence tied the knot under Elise's ribs, small and exact. "It means they want everyone frightened in the same direction."

It was the closest thing to tenderness she had offered all morning, and it was not meant for Elise.

She gathered the ruined dress into her lap, not to return it, not yet. Her thumb rested once over the place in the hem where the marker had hidden. When she rose, the apron pocket gave a small weighted pull against her skirt.

Elise looked up at her. Clemence looked back. Nothing was said. That silence had more shape than speech. It had timing in it. It had labor. It had the clean patience of a woman who had found the exact stain and chosen not to wash it in front of the child.

From the doorway Lucie caught on the same word again and again. "Es... cape. Escape. Escape."

Clemence did not correct her.
