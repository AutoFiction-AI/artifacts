# Chapter 1

The schoolhouse still smelled of chalk under the wet-paper stink Lucille Holt had brought in with the county forms. Somebody had shoved the little desks against the walls and laid two trestle tables under the blackboard, so the room no longer looked fit for sums or penmanship. It looked fit for sorting. Della stood just inside the door with red clay drying on her shoes and listened to the downriver concussion of a blast from the Marlowe Dam works roll through the window glass. Even that sound had begun to seem like government, the way it reached the valley first and explanation after.

Lucille Holt looked up from the table nearest the stove. Her hair was pinned so cleanly it seemed nailed in place, but a thread of it had worked loose at one temple and glued itself there with sweat. Mud had darkened her sensible shoes to the ankles, and above the left one Della caught the blue darning in her stocking where she had mended it instead of buying another pair. On the table in front of her sat church books, county sheets, a coffee cup gone cold, and a cigar box full of yellow tags with wire ties already cut to length.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," Lucille said. Her voice had the clerk's balance to it, all the parts set in order, but she was moving too fast through the papers for true calm. "I'm obliged you came early."

"You wanted me before your room filled up and started correcting you."

Lucille let out a breath through her nose that might have been the beginning of a laugh if she had more time for laughing. "That too. Please come here."

Della crossed the room. The boards underfoot still remembered children, thin old pine gone smooth in the middle and splintered at the edges. She set her sack on a desk scarred with knife initials and looked where Lucille pointed. It was a county form with Della's name written on it in a neat hand, occupation left blank.

"Temporary cemetery relocation assistant," Lucille said. "Four dollars a day while removals last. Sign there, and I'll fill the rest."

The four looked mean and welcome at once. Four dollars would buy coffee, lard, a spool of thread, kerosene if she stretched it. Four dollars a day would not touch the truck note or the feed advance sitting on Rafe's neck like a boot, but it would keep smaller hungers from stacking up behind the larger one. Della did not like seeing her own need written into county ink. She took the fountain pen anyway.

Her left thumb caught halfway around the barrel, stiff as a rusted hinge this early in the morning. Lucille noticed and pretended not to. Della bent over the line and wrote her name carefully, each letter thickening where the nib hit old callus in her fingers.

"You'll be working with Silas Wynn and the labor crew," Lucille said. "The state sent me men who can lift and tag, but they don't know this hillside. Half of them read stone worse than first-graders, and the other half trust whatever somebody shouts loudest. I need the graves taken up right. I need the tags tied to the right people. And if the right people aren't plain from the stone or the book, I need somebody who can say so before I make a record I can't take back."

Della set the pen down. "I'm here to keep your boys from chopping through coffin lids."

"Today that may amount to the same thing."

Lucille slid one of the church ledgers over. The leather had gone shiny from handling, and damp had swollen the pages until the book would not shut flat. A finger marked a line in brown ink: Sadie Kincaid, row B, plot 7. Next to it Lucille had clipped a receipt from the undertaker in Larkin and a small note in pencil.

"Her daughters came by before sunup," Lucille said. "One says the book is wrong. The other says the coffin plaque will prove it. I haven't seen the plaque yet because she's still in the ground. I thought it best not to choose between them before we had the box in daylight."

"Best for who?"

"For me. For the county. Possibly for everybody." Lucille met her eyes then, and Della saw the intelligence under the office manner, not soft and not foolish. "If the federal office decides the wrong next of kin was notified, payment can be suspended until a hearing. If the wrong surname goes onto the relocation papers, it can alter who has standing later. I don't mean just grave money. I mean land, allowances, probate, all of it."

The money sat on top of the paperwork, not buried in it at all. Della had known money would cling to the cemetery move the way clay clung to a hem, but she had still been fool enough to imagine the county mostly wanted muscle and local directions. Lucille was not hiring her to point at rows and keep boys from breaking the dead. She was hiring the part of Della that remembered who had slept in what bed, who had come home under whose roof after a bad winter, whose name the preacher wrote because it was lawful, and whose name the household used because law had never chopped wood for it.

Della shut the ledger. "You can leave a surname blank until somebody proves it."

"Sometimes. Immediate competing claims change that, especially if the families start the dispute on cemetery ground in front of witnesses." Lucille reached into the cigar box and lifted a yellow tag between two fingers. "I would prefer not to have that happen in the first hour."

Voices were gathering outside already, low and rough on the porch boards. A woman coughed. Somebody hawked and spat into the yard. Through the open door Della could see hats, shoulders, the dark shape of a mule tied under the chinaberry. Nobody had come here because they respected process. They had come because the county was about to lay hands on their dead.

"Then keep them back from the hole," Della said.

"Will they mind me if I tell them?"

"No."

Lucille nodded once. The answer only narrowed the problem. "Will they mind you?"

Della thought of Old Sion with its cedar roots and leaning limestone, its child graves sunk flat and its adult stones listing downhill year by year. She thought of strangers with clipboards and pry bars walking over the fever plot, boots set where other people had buried their children and fever dead. She thought of water taking the valley whether any of them cooperated or not. There were moments when refusing a thing only decided who got to do it badly.

"Some of them will," she said. "The rest will mind less."

Lucille gathered the church ledger, the blank forms, and a bundle of tags tied with string. "That will have to serve."

They stepped out into a morning already warm enough to promise trouble by noon. The path down to the cemetery had been chewed into slick ridges by boots and wagon wheels. Red clay glazed the ruts. Past the schoolyard, beyond the sycamores, the valley opened in long folds of green still trying to look permanent. Here and there the white condemnation stakes showed through like bones in a bad field.

By the gate at Old Sion stood Silas Wynn with the tripod pins over one shoulder and a coil of rope in his good hand. He was tall enough to look all joints now, the years bent into him, but he still had the steadiness of a man who had spent a lifetime setting weight where it ought to go. A strip of red flannel was tied around his right wrist for the ache he never named. When he coughed into his handkerchief, the sound came up from deep in him with the dry scrape of quarry dust.

"Morning, Della," he said.

"You sound worse."

"I sound like a man they keep waking too early."

From Silas, it counted as complaint. Two county laborers were already at the gate with shovels, young enough to be careless and old enough to think carelessness looked like speed. One had Knoxville written all over him in the cut of his trousers and the shine on his belt buckle. The other was local, Luther Sipe's middle boy, jaw working a plug of tobacco he was too green to handle without swallowing half the juice.

Past them, the cemetery climbed the hillside in crooked rows. Limestone markers leaned among broom sedge and last year's cedar cones. The newest graves sat higher up where the ground was firmer; the old ones had sunk toward the lower edge where fever years had filled the soil too quickly and sometimes without names. Della did not look there yet. She kept her eyes on the upper row where Sadie Kincaid lay, or where the church book said she ought to lie.

"Row B, plot 7," the Knoxville man said, consulting Lucille's sheet and trusting it over the stone in front of him. He started toward a stone whose mossed face read SARAH KIRKLAND if a person stood near enough to know a K from an N.

"Not there." Della caught his sleeve before the shovel bit. "Sadie is uphill of the Angel child and downhill of Virgil Tate. That marker there belonged to Sarah Kirkland before your mother was old enough to braid her hair."

The boy flushed under his tan. Luther's boy smirked, then stopped when Della turned that same look on him.

"Take sod clean," she said. "No chopping once you hear wood. She's deeper on the downhill side because the slope slipped after the burial. And mind that cedar root. If you come in from the west you'll split the box."

Silas grunted agreement and set the tripod where she pointed without asking a second question. Lucille stood just beyond the row with her papers hugged close to her chest. The wind lifted the corner of a form and slapped it back against her sleeve.

Sadie's daughters were easy to pick out even before they opened their mouths. The older one, Lena Kincaid, held a sun-faded parasol over herself though the light was still thin, and the ferrule had been wrapped in black tape where it had split. Her cardigan smelled sharply of mothballs when she passed near Della, the scent of winter trunks and shut-up rooms not yet gone from it. The younger, Bess Holt, had flour dried in the fine hair on one wrist, having left some kitchen work half-finished to come up the hill, and one heel on her black pump clicked with a tin tack every time she shifted weight. Sadie had borne them twenty-two years apart to two different men and had spent the whole of that interval refusing to smooth herself into a story fit for church minutes.

Della remembered other things about her too. Sadie had liked her tomatoes salted hard enough to draw water. She had sewn with a copper thimble hanging from a ribbon at her neck because her fingers swelled in damp weather and rings made her curse. When she laughed, which was not often but was not rare either, it came out through her nose first, one sharp note before the rest of it arrived. None of that lived in the church book.

Lena saw Della and came at once. "Mrs. Bledsoe, you tell her," she said, jerking her chin toward Lucille. "Mama was Kincaid when she went in. The church wrote it right."

Bess snapped back before Della answered. "The church wrote what Reverend Puckett liked to hear. Holt paid for the coffin. Holt kept her. Holt buried her."

"He buried the name on the box, that is all."

"He bought the box, Lena."

"Girls," Della said.

They did not stop.

"You didn't wash her when she was laid out," Lena said. "I did."

"And who kept her through the stroke? Who turned her when she wet the bed? Not you, with your husband saying there wasn't room for old women in his house."

"My husband at least married me in front of witnesses."

Bess's face changed at that, her features drawing to the hard edge she had likely used all her life when somebody wanted to divide lawful from lived. "If we're counting witnesses, you ask the valley who fed her the last ten years."

The first shovelfuls of earth came out with the wet, sucking sound of clay letting go. Della stepped between the daughters and the grave before either of them had the bad sense to take one more pace forward. She could smell camphor from Lena's cardigan and biscuit flour from Bess's sleeve, both of them human smells, kitchen and closet, not the sort people imagined clung to an inheritance fight.

"You can say every mean thing you've saved up," Della told them, keeping her voice level, "but you'll say it back from the hole. If one of these boys slips because you wanted the last word over your mama's boards, you can live with that instead."

Lena's parasol ferrule quit ticking against her shoe. Bess took one step back from the hole and pressed the flour-dusted hand to her mouth. Behind them, the mutter along the path pinched off so abruptly that the next shovelful sounded loud as an ax blow.

The digging took on the rhythm grave work always did once the first impatience wore off: slice, lift, throw; slice, lift, throw. Clay landed heavy on the canvas spread beside the plot. A cedar root came up pale and wet where the Knoxville boy hacked too close; Silas took the shovel from him without ceremony and showed him how to work around it with the narrow spade, shaving soil instead of stabbing. Lucille kept to the high side, writing when she could, waiting when she had to, her ruined shoes sinking deeper each minute.

When the shovel finally struck wood, the sound changed everything. Talk thinned. Even the children at the back of the crowd quieted, because the ear knew that knock before the mind admitted what it was hearing.

"Hands now," Della said.

They knelt and cleared the coffin by hand, scraping mud away from planks blackened to the color of old liver. The downhill end had slumped, just as she had told them it would, and one corner of the lid gave under Luther's boy's fingers with a soft inward sigh. A smell rose out of the opened seam, damp wood and old cloth and the sour-sweet taint that no grave lost completely. It was not fresh or strong. It was the plain truth of a grave opened to daylight.

Bess made a noise in her throat and looked away. Lena, stubborn as fence wire, stared harder instead, trying to hold her mother arranged by force of will.

"Easy," Silas said. "She's not going anywhere but where we carry her."

He said it almost kindly, which from him was a whole sermon.

Della brushed the mud from the lid with her palm. The wet grit grained under her skin. Near the middle, where hardware had once shone, a brass plaque sat greened and half-hidden. She scraped it clean with her thumbnail. Letters emerged by degrees.

SADIE HOLT

Bess let out a breath so fierce it might have been victory if victory did not sound so close to grief. Lena stepped forward two paces before Della's arm stopped her.

"That don't mean legal," Lena said at once. "That means what Bess paid the undertaker to screw on."

"It means what our mother was called in my house for twenty years."

"Your house was sin, not law."

"My house was warm."

Lucille had already flipped open the church book again. Her pencil hovered over the margin, then punched a dark dot clean through the paper when her eyes moved from the page to the plaque and back.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," she said. "Do you know whether Sadie and Mr. Holt were ever legally married?"

Every head in earshot turned toward Della. Even the boys at the ropes let the straps sag against their shins and stared over the coffin lid.

Della kept her hand on the lid. Beneath the softened plank there was a hollow give where the lining had fallen in and whatever remained of Sadie had settled into herself. Years ago Della had washed the old woman after the stroke, lifting one arm at a time, working soap into the sour fold beneath each breast while Sadie cursed the indignity with perfect accuracy. That body had once been heavy and argumentative and alive enough to bite if you caught her in the wrong temper. Now two daughters stood over the softened plank with their mouths already shaping two different names.

"I know what house she lived in," Della said.

"That is not quite my question."

"It's the part that mattered to the woman in the box."

Lena made a small triumphant sound, thinking perhaps that Della had moved in her direction. Bess heard the caution in it and did not.

Lucille's jaw tightened. "The county still needs a tag."

Della looked at the yellow rectangles in Lucille's hand. The breeze worried the top one loose and laid it across the greened plaque. Bess snatched it off before the damp paper could stick.

"Then tag her Sadie and stop there," Della said. "If you put Kincaid on her now, this one says you have cheated her. If you put Holt, that one says the same. Tie a number. Write Sadie. Let them fight the rest where talking won't spill her."

"There is a procedure for disputed claims," Lucille said.

"Good. Use it."

Bess's grief flared back into anger. "You know more than that. Everybody here knows you know more than that."

"I know enough not to shout it over open boards."

That landed with the crowd better than anything legal would have. The mutter dropped to throat-noise. Nobody crowded the bier. Lena planted the parasol ferrule a full step farther back from the hole.

Lucille studied Della for a long beat, her fingers resting on the blank line where the surname belonged. Then she pulled the page free from the stack and wrote in a separate, careful hand:

Sadie - surname disputed pending hearing.

Lucille blotted the ink, tore a duplicate free, and clipped both sheets together before the breeze could peel them apart. When she looked up again, she looked at Della instead of at the coffin.

"All right," Lucille said. "We will proceed under dispute."

"Proceed where?" Lena demanded.

"To the removal," Lucille said. Her office voice had returned, firmer now that it had found the shape of the trouble. "And then to a claim hearing. I will notify both households. No funds will be released on this burial until the identity question is settled in the record."

"Identity question," Bess repeated, looking at the coffin instead of at Lucille. "That is my mother's body."

"And it will remain your mother's body whatever a clerk writes beside it," Della said before Lucille could answer with some thinner truth.

Silas lowered the straps. "Let's lift her before the sun gets hot enough to sour all of us."

They threaded the straps under the coffin where the box would still bear weight. The wood was spongy at the foot, and when the men eased upward the whole thing sagged in the middle with a faint tearing from inside, fabric or flesh or both. The smell thickened. Old black water ran from a seam and darkened the clay at Della's boots. She did not flinch from it. There was no honor in helping people into the world and then pretending the body became clean business afterward.

"Steady," she said. "Head high. Don't jerk."

Silas took most of the uphill strain without making a show of it. His face had gone the color of lye soap, and the cough was building again in his chest, but his hands stayed exact. Luther's boy swallowed and adjusted his grip. The Knoxville man looked greener than the clay. Together they brought the coffin clear and settled it onto the temporary bier.

Bess touched the lid once with the backs of her fingers. The boards were wet, and she did not trust her own hand not to press too hard. Lena opened her parasol and shut it again, a nervous little machinery she could control because nothing larger would obey her.

"She hated peonies," Bess said suddenly, to no one and to the coffin both. "Everybody keeps saying we ought to have put peonies on her. She said they smelled like a dentist's rag."

Lena looked at her sister, startled right out of argument. "She did say that."

For the first time all morning, the grave gave them peace, and even that lasted less than ten seconds. Then Lucille asked one of the laborers for the relocation number, and the spell broke under pencil scratch.

They loaded Sadie onto the wagon slated for the temporary receiving shed. Behind Della, talk broke open in low hard bursts.

"My aunt's stone hasn't got a plaque."

"The baby in row D's only in the margin."

"What happens if the book's wrong?"

Lucille had another form half-filled before the wagon cleared the gate, and two women were already following her uphill.

Lucille came to Della while the wagon rolled out.

"I need you Thursday," she said quietly.

"For what?"

"For the first claim hearing. This one. There will be more if this goes the way I think it will."

"You think quickly."

"I have to." Lucille glanced toward the departing wagon. "Do you know how many entries in that book I cannot trust now that I have seen one plaque contradict it in the first grave?"

"One."

That got another almost-laugh out of her, brief and tired. "Then let me put it a different way. I know how many entries I can trust without somebody like you standing beside them. Fewer than I had an hour ago."

Della looked over the rows. Wind moved the broom sedge in little runs. Below the upper graves the ground dipped toward the older section where markers thinned and then failed. She knew every shift in that slope. So did Silas. No paper in Lucille's hand knew it. No federal man would ever know it by looking.

"I came to work a shovel," Della said.

"You came because strangers would do damage you could not abide watching."

There was no use denying that to a woman who had already read her this cleanly.

"Thursday," Lucille said. "Nine o'clock. Here first, before we go through the files."

Della did not answer yes. Lucille took her silence for something near enough and turned away to chase a signature from one of Sadie's sons-in-law.

By midday the cemetery had the used look of a battlefield nobody would admit calling that. Fresh holes lay raw in the clay, and canvas mounds sat beside them while men worked with dirt up to their knees. Women dabbed sweat and fury from their upper lips with handkerchiefs. The dam sounded again, a far blunt punch in the air. Nobody mistook it for thunder anymore.

When Della finally went to the hand pump by the schoolhouse to wash the grave from her arms, she found Rafe waiting there with one boot heel braced against the trough. He must have come up from Cedar Bench in a hurry. A snapped tomato stake was tucked through his belt where he'd forgotten to take it out, and green leaf smell clung to him under the sweat. He had bits of red clay on his trouser knees and the old scar at his chin had gone white with the way he was holding his mouth. In one pocket something metallic clicked when he shifted, a handful of nails or bolts he had not bothered to set down.

"So they did hire you," he said.

Della pumped water over her wrists. Cold iron taste came up from the well. Mud ribboned off her skin and swirled down the cracked concrete into weeds. "You can see they did."

"Four dollars?"

She looked at him then. "Who told you that?"

"Nobody had to tell me. You think this valley can keep a number in its mouth?"

He laughed without any pleasure in it and bent to spit into the grass. Rafe always did that when the shame was close, spitting first and trying to clear his mouth of it before it came out as words. Della had seen the habit in him since he was small enough to come in from the yard crying blood over a loose baby tooth.

"Well," he said, "I'm glad somebody in this house is drawing cash."

"It's this house drawing cash."

"Is it?" He pushed off the trough and came nearer. His voice did not rise much. It roughened instead, which was worse. "Because it looks to me like the county can find money quick enough when they need graves moved, but when our place sits tied up because Eli walked off and never had the courtesy to die on paper, everybody goes deaf. The bank isn't deaf. McRae sure as hell isn't deaf."

He was finally talking about the frozen Bledsoe claim, not the wage. Waiting all morning had only made it meaner. Della dried her hands on her apron though they were not clean enough for drying yet.

"This wage will buy food," she said.

"Food for how long? Two sacks of flour and some coffee? You know what July looks like for us." He held up one hand and started ticking the numbers off on filthy fingertips. "Four hundred eighty-six at First State on the truck and feed. Seventy-three and change at McRae. Seed bought on faith because the land was still ours in March and condemned in April. If Eli was declared dead, we'd at least have the claim opened. But no, now we're hauling other people's kin uphill for day wages."

The figures hit because they were true and because he carried them in his body the way another man might carry fever, always present, flaring under exertion. Della had her own count of lack, but Rafe's was harder, public, male, rubbed raw by the fact of failing to keep his house clear of debt.

"I can't swear a man dead because you need him dead," she said.

"I didn't ask you to swear. I asked you to help." He shook his head. "That's the part gets me. You'll help the county sort out whether Sadie was a Kincaid or a Holt, but when it comes to our own business there's always one more reason to wait."

Della felt her temper tighten down into the calm that came just before it showed its teeth. "Our own business has not changed because you said it louder."

"No. It changed because they hired you to remember for everybody but us."

She could have told him memory was not proof, that the law only liked witness when witness could be made to serve paper. She could have told him four dollars a day was not betrayal but groceries. She could have told him he was a thirty-nine-year-old man angry at a sixty-three-year-old woman for accepting work because the world had made both of them small. None of it would have helped. The trouble with need was how quickly it made every explanation sound like contempt.

Before she answered, Lucille came out of the schoolhouse with the Kincaid file under her arm and stopped when she saw Rafe's face. The dry part of her expression sharpened. Quarrels announced themselves to her the instant they opened.

"Mr. Bledsoe," she said.

"Miss Holt."

He did not take his eyes off Della.

Lucille shifted the file to her other arm. "Mrs. Bledsoe, I need you Thursday at nine for the Kincaid hearing. We can go over the particulars before the daughters arrive."

Rafe turned his head then. "Hearing already?"

"Disputed surname tied to compensation," Lucille said. "It has to be docketed."

"Doesn't it," he said. "Fast work when money is in sight."

"Trouble moves fast," Lucille replied. "The work doesn't."

That made him look at her properly for the first time. Lucille was not a woman valley men usually read right away. She looked too tidy for mud, too young for authority, too thin in the shoulders to be worth arguing with until they discovered she could freeze a payment with one sheet of paper. Della watched Rafe measure all that and decide, rightly, that the clerk was not his true enemy but might prove useful as one.

"You'll have my mother up there giving witness for Kincaids and Holts before the week is out," he said. "Maybe after that you can spare a minute for Bledsoes."

"If the Bledsoe file reaches my desk with what it requires, it will be handled the same as any other," Lucille said. The sentence was balanced and official. The irritation under it was private and hot. "Until then, I can only process what exists."

"That's a nice way to say poor people ought to produce better paperwork."

"No," Lucille said. "It's a plain way to say the state likes dead men documented."

Rafe barked a laugh at that, short and ugly enough to count as agreement. "Well, there is wisdom for the church bulletin."

"Rafe," Della said.

He rounded on her again. "What? Tell me I'm wrong. Tell me this will fix anything worth fixing."

"It will keep strangers from scattering the dead."

"The dead won't feel it."

"The living will."

He wiped the back of his wrist across his mouth. The snapped tomato stake knocked lightly against the buckle at his waist. Della could see the field still on him, the interrupted row, the plants waiting to be tied before the next wind. He had come up the hill carrying his real work with him, and here she was with grave stink under her nails telling him the living would feel a thing he could not eat.

"Yes," he said at last. "That's exactly the problem."

For one second she thought he might say more. Something less sharp, maybe. Something about Ruthie, or June, or how many times he had counted the same debts by lantern light after everybody else slept. Instead he bent, picked a loose bolt or nail from his pocket and tossed it from one hand to the other, a little trick of motion to keep from striking at the wrong object.

"Take the hearing," he said. "Take their pay. But don't come home and tell me there was no way to help us while you're spending your days deciding which name belongs to other people's bones."

He walked off before she answered, shoulders set against further waste, boots throwing clay from the path, the way tired men went when they still had a row to finish. At the turn he stooped and pulled the tomato stake free from his belt, then carried it in his hand the rest of the way downhill, his stride already shortening into the hard field pace he used at home.

Lucille stood beside the pump in silence for a moment. The schoolhouse behind them had begun swallowing voices again, one argument ended and three more starting up in its place.

"I can come by the house if Thursday seems easier," she said finally.

"No."

Lucille inclined her head. She knew when not to offer softness. She tore a few damp notices and duplicates from the file and held them out. "Nine o'clock, then."

Della took the packet and folded it once before tucking it under her apron. Lucille went back inside. Della remained at the pump with water dripping from her fingertips and drying red on the concrete below. Across the cemetery, yellow tags had begun to show here and there against stone and dark wood, small squares kicking in the breeze. A gust turned three of them at once. One slapped wet against a child's marker; another twisted on its wire until the knot bit into soft cedar.

Below the cedar shade, where the older ground sloped toward the fever plot and the markers thinned into absence, no yellow square stirred because there was nothing down there to tie one to. Della pressed the folded packet flat against her belly and kept looking downhill until the dam boomed again. When the sound thinned out across the valley, she started the half mile uphill toward Cedar Bench with Lucille Holt's papers under her apron, already heavier than paper had any right to be.
