# Chapter 12

Rain had started before daylight and never quite grown into a storm. It came down in the mean, steady way that soaked everything without giving anybody the decency of thunder. By the time Della reached the lower gate of Old Sion, the half-mile path from Cedar Bench had turned to a dragged strip of red grease, and the hems of her dress were heavy as feed sacks. Water ran in little brown threads between the graves. The cedar roots held where they could and let go where they could not. Down in the fever plot the slope looked wrong in a way she felt first in her knees, before she could have named it with words. The earth there had lost its patience. It bellied outward under the grass as if something under it had taken a long breath.

She stood a moment with one hand on the gatepost and made herself take air slowly. Her chest had been shorter with her lately. Not sick shorter, not the hot tightness of winter cough, but a workman's shortage, as if every hill had grown steeper in the night and meant to stay that way. Her left thumb had locked around the tin pail handle on the climb. She had to pry it open with the fingers of the other hand before she could lift the latch.

Silas Wynn was already inside the gate, setting a plank down near the wagon track with the care of a man lowering something breakable though the plank was only wet pine. He had tied a strip of worn leather around the handle of his shovel where his palm rubbed raw, and he still had his cough rag tucked neat into his breast pocket instead of balled damp in a trouser leg like most men would. He glanced at her and then at the hill below them.

"Morning," he said.

"If you want to call it that."

He gave the corner of his mouth a movement that was not a smile. "Hill's moving."

"I can see it."

"Can you stop it?"

"No."

"Then we'd better get paid before it does what it came to do."

Lucille Holt came up behind them carrying her files inside an oilcloth satchel and her good sense on her face like a burden she had long since stopped resenting. She had tied flour-sack strips around the tops of her sensible shoes to keep the mud from sucking them off, but the sacks were already brown to the ankle and one had come loose and was slapping her stocking with each step. Her pinned hair had softened at the temples. She looked like a woman who had spent the walk rehearsing how to say something nobody would take kindly.

"I need both of you before the hands start," she said.

Silas leaned on the shovel. "That sounds official."

"It's official. That's why it sounds so ugly."

Della waited. Lucille talked fastest when she wanted feeling to stay out of it.

"The Marlowe office changed the lower-road schedule at first light," Lucille said. "The engineers advanced the concrete runs because the river came up more than they expected and the east cut's slipping. Beginning tomorrow, the haul trucks take the lower road from six in the morning until near four. After Friday they're closing it outright except for project traffic and emergency county use. Mr. Hensley says if we haven't cleared the unstable rows before then, he won't keep men on this slope until after the hearing. He may not keep them on it at all."

Della looked down toward the river though she could not see it for the wet trees. She could hear the dam works even in rain, the blunt metal noises, the backing trucks, the low concussion that came late and traveled through wood before it reached flesh. "They told you next week."

"They told me a week many times," Lucille said. "This morning they told me Friday."

"And what counts as unstable?"

Lucille's mouth thinned. "Everything below the third cedar line. The fever plot included."

Silas spat rainwater to the side. "Everything below the third cedar line was unstable when Polk was president."

"The engineers have only now discovered the fact," Lucille said. "Which means it becomes true in paperwork today."

Della pressed the heel of her right hand into the heel of her left until the thumb joint gave a little hot click. "How many men do we keep?"

"The same four county hands for now. Two of the day laborers left for the dam yesterday afternoon. I couldn't replace them. They're paying cash every Friday and sometimes midweek if the foreman thinks it'll keep men from walking."

The words were ordinary enough. Cash. Friday. Men. Yet Della felt Rafe inside them before Lucille spoke his name, the way a woman could smell a pan starting to scorch before she saw smoke.

Lucille's eyes shifted to her and then away again, not out of gentleness but because she disliked making private things public when public things were already bad enough. "Your son signed on with the downstream crew three days ago. I thought you knew."

Della let the sentence sit where it landed. She had known and not known. Ruthie had said the night before that he might try for a shift, might ask a cousin who knew a foreman, might go down only until the bank got off his neck. But might was a word people used when they still wanted shame to have some room in it. Signed on meant the shame had punched its own hole and climbed through.

"He said he was going to look," Della said.

"He looked hard enough to draw a number badge."

Silas scratched the side of his jaw with a muddy thumbnail. "Everybody's looking hard enough for that."

Lucille nodded once. "I'm not saying this for gossip. I'm saying it because the dam men now know this hillside from both ends. They know what road they're taking from us and on what day."

Della said, "How many days before the affidavit hearing?"

"Three, if they leave it where it stands. Tuesday morning." Lucille shifted the satchel higher on her shoulder. "I can delay argument. I can't delay gravity. If that lower ground opens any further before then, every person with eyes becomes a witness before the county does."

No one answered her. Rain tapped on stones. Somewhere downriver a truck gear ground low and long, and then a blast rolled under the valley, not loud exactly but deep enough that the gate chain shivered against the post. Della felt it in her gums.

Silas said, "That's one."

"One what?"

"One reminder."

The men arrived in twos, heads down under hats gone soft at the brim. By the time the wagon came with fresh tags and burlap, the morning had brightened only enough to show how wet everything truly was. Della put Lucille's schedule in the same part of her mind where she kept a laboring woman's cervix and a near-empty flour tin: not as words, but as narrowing space. Third cedar line. Friday. Six to four. Fever plot included.

They started on the row just above the lower cedar roots, where the markers leaned because the ground beneath them had been washing thin for years. Della wired tin tags to stone bases, checked names against Lucille's list, then crouched to scrape mud from lettering with the blade of a broken spoon she kept in her apron pocket for that purpose. The spoon slipped twice from her stiff thumb. On the third marker her hand cramped closed so hard the handle bit her palm. She had to set the spoon between her knees and pry her fingers back one by one. Rain dripped from the tip of her nose onto the limestone and made the carved surname swim.

Lucille saw it. "Do you need somebody else on the tags?"

"I need somebody else on my hands."

"I can't requisition those."

"Then quit asking foolish questions."

Lucille almost smiled at that but did not. She crouched instead, skirt tucked out of the mud with the absent decency of habit, and held the list while Della rewired the tag. Even in wet weather her office voice wanted the world in sequence.

"Miller, Jonas. Section C lower. Marker and remains both present."

"I know how to read, Miss Holt."

"Then read faster."

Della looked at her. Lucille kept her eyes on the page, but the dry edge was there. She was angrier than she sounded, not at Della, not even at the work, but at the whole chain of men and departments that could make mud into law by stamping it with the right seal.

They took up two coffins before dinner and broke one handle clean off another because the wood had gone to dark sponge under the straps. Della bent with the rest and put her shoulder under weight she had no business carrying now that the ground itself had begun to slide. Wet pine pressed through her dress into the meat of her upper arm. The strain ran from her wrists through her elbows and into the middle of her back, where it stayed after the coffin was laid down, a hard board of hurt lodged crosswise under her ribs. When she straightened, breath did not come all at once. It came in torn pieces, as if her chest had to fetch it from somewhere uphill.

Silas heard the catch in her breathing and said nothing for a while. That was one mercy in him. Then, after they had eased the coffin onto the wagon skids and the county hand nearest them went off to piss behind the cedars, Silas took a peppermint drop from the little tobacco tin he used for them and held it out.

"For your mouth," he said. "You sound dry."

"I'm wet through."

"Your lungs don't know that."

She took the peppermint because refusing it would have wasted more strength than accepting it. The sugar hit her tongue sharp and medicinal. Silas put one in his own mouth, coughed around it twice, and spat into the leaves. The phlegm came up gray from old quarry years and pink at the edge from the work of now.

"You oughtn't be hauling weight today," she said.

"You either."

"I asked you first."

He looked downhill toward the fever plot, where rainwater had cut new narrow seams through the clay since dawn. "That hole down there doesn't care which one of us ought."

At noon they stood under the wagon's canvas tilt and ate without much appetite. Della had brought cold biscuit and a heel of salt pork wrapped in wax paper gone translucent with grease. Lucille drank coffee from a jar and made a face at it anyway, though she had brewed it herself in the office before daylight. One of the county men said the dam had half the valley on payroll now and the other half cussing them for it, and Silas answered that this was what payroll usually looked like in the end.

Lucille wiped rain off her upper lip with the back of her wrist. "The project clerk told me this morning they're paying one dollar thirty-five an hour for mucking and truck guidance. More for powder crew if a man can keep his nerve."

"That enough to make hell smell like bread?" Silas asked.

"It's enough to make a bank patient for one week," Lucille said.

Della did not say Rafe's name. She did not need to. The figure sat between them with its wet hat on. One dollar thirty-five. Four dollars a day for her if the cemetery kept her. Seventy-three dollars and forty cents at McRae's. Four hundred eighty-six at the bank. Numbers had their own meanness. They lined up better than people ever did.

She ate half the biscuit and put the rest away. The salt pork turned in her mouth to something she could not swallow for a moment, not because of sickness but because she saw too plainly Ruthie's careful hand cutting it thin at dawn to make it seem like more.

"We move lower after this," Lucille said. "If we're going to keep ahead of the trucks, we have to."

Della answered the literal question and not the real one. "The wagon wheels will sink to the axle if we angle too close to the cedar roots."

"I know."

"Then put the planks down first."

Lucille let it pass. So did Silas. That was the trouble with people who knew you well. They recognized avoidance and sometimes were tired enough to allow it.

The afternoon went meaner. Rain quit and left steam rising off the clay, which was worse because the ground stayed slick while the air turned thick as wash water. Cicadas started up in the trees with a pressure sound that seemed to shove at the skull. The damp in Della's dress warmed against her body and then cooled again whenever the wind came. Her thumb swelled further until the joint looked packed with lard. She stopped trying to close wire with that hand and began pinching everything between forefinger and middle finger instead, which made the tendons in her wrist sting clear to the elbow.

When they shifted the next marker, her lower back seized so suddenly she had to sit down on a flat grave curb before she disgraced herself by dropping in the mud. The limestone was warm from the brief brightening and slick with algae near the base. She sat straight, because slumping made the pain worse, and kept her face blank while the county hands argued over chain length two yards away.

Lucille came over carrying a coil of tag wire. "Tell me the truth."

"I've been trying to avoid that all summer."

"Not the large truth. The ordinary one."

Della looked at the coil in Lucille's hands. Thin wire, brown at the bends from damp. "My back's making a fool of me."

"Sit five minutes."

"We don't have five minutes to hand out like fans at a revival."

"Then sit four."

Della did. While she sat, she listened to the hill. That was not foolishness. Earth spoke if a person had spent enough time on it. Water found new ways down. Roots gave a little tug when the clay around them loosened. A stone that had held quiet for fifty years made a different sound when the soil under it turned slick and shallow. She could hear all of it now under the insects and the distant project noise: the little settled ticks, the slow whispered sifts. The cemetery was not resting. It was adjusting its body the way a sick woman did in bed when she knew sleep was lost for the night.

Silas came to stand beside her. He did not ask if she was all right. He looked where she was looking.

"That lower corner's gone softer," he said.

"I know."

"We ought to shore it before dark."

"With what?"

He nodded toward the wagon. "Planks. Rope. Men who still have legs."

"We don't have enough planks."

"Then we'll use what we have and cuss the rest."

Another blast came up from the dam. This one was sharper, close enough in timing that Della knew the charge line had moved. The sound reached the cemetery and then a second thing reached it after, a tremor that passed under the soles of her shoes and through the stone she sat on. Small dirt trickled from the edge of the path below.

Lucille muttered, "God."

Silas said, "That wasn't weather."

"No," Lucille said. "That was schedule."

Late in the day, while they were laying planks toward the lower section, Della saw a man coming up the footpath from the river road with the gait of somebody whose legs had spent ten hours learning a machine's pace and not yet remembered how to belong to ground again. For an instant she did not know him because the project had got at him first. His shirt was dark with sweat and stippled over with pale gray where cement slurry had dried on the cloth. Mud from the dam was not the same as grave mud. Grave mud stayed earth-colored. This had oil in it, and powder smoke, and the bitter chalk smell of fresh concrete. The front of his work badge had worn a clean square on his chest where rain had not quite touched it. He carried a dented lunch pail in one hand and a coil of signal flagging in the other as if he had forgotten to put it down before climbing.

Rafe.

He came through the gate without looking at anybody first, which told her more than if he had walked in cursing. Shame made him avoid a room before anger ever did. His chin scar was bright with rain. The skin at one wrist had gone red and raw where wet cement had sat too long under his cuff. He had not touched the biscuit in his pail; she could see the cloth-wrapped lump shoved against one side, flattened and damp.

"You need off this hill," he said, breathing hard from the climb.

Silas answered before Della could. "That's true every day. Why today in particular?"

Rafe wiped his mouth with the back of his hand and looked downslope. "They fired the west cut twice and they're setting another before dark if the powder holds dry enough. Lower road's already rutted near the culvert. Foreman says if we get one more hard rain tonight that whole shoulder starts walking clear to the church path."

Lucille said, "The engineers gave me Friday."

"Engineers say plenty when they're standing in a clean coat. Men on the haul road are saying tonight."

"And which are you now?" Lucille asked. "Engineer or haul road?"

Rafe gave a humorless breath that might have been a laugh if there had been any pleasure in it. "I'm the fool with a flag in his hand and a whistle in his ear. That's me now."

Della looked at the flagging tape looped in his fist. Bright project red against the cemetery green. "You came straight from shift."

"Where else was I going to go first?"

She could have said home. She could have said to wash. She could have said nowhere near me. Instead she said, "How much are they paying you to drown your own dead?"

His face changed but not into anger yet. Anger would have been easier. "Enough to put flour in the bin. Enough to keep McRae from sending a boy with a note another week. Enough that Ruthie didn't have to cut last night's bacon in half and call it planning. You want the number? One thirty-five an hour. They paid us half-week in cash because men keep quitting. I spent half mine before dinner."

The words came fast and turned sharp on themselves, just as his always did when shame got too near. He shoved the lunch pail at the ground and let it rest by a stone. "You think I don't know? I stand there and wave trucks over rock they're tearing out of our own river. I hear them talk about this valley like it's already under water and the people in it are too stupid to climb. I know what they got me doing. It still spends."

Silas looked away. Lucille did not. Della felt the sting of truth in the last two words because they were ugly enough to be honest.

"You should've told me before now," she said.

"Told you what? That debt has better hearing than pride? You knew the bank paper same as I did."

"I knew your need. I didn't know you'd put their mud on your clothes."

"Their mud and ours are mixed by now."

That was the cruelest sentence in the chapter and maybe the truest. Della heard it, and because she heard it, she did not answer at once. Downhill a plank shifted under one of the county men's boots with a slap. The hill gave a little settling sigh.

Lucille said, very even, "If what you heard on shift is right, we need rope on the lower corner now."

Rafe nodded. "Use the wagon chain too if you've got sense. That cedar on the left still holds."

"You giving orders here?" Silas asked.

"I'm telling you what the foreman's eye is on. Water cut under the road bank by dinner. We filled it with stone and it bled through anyhow."

Silas's cough roughened in his chest. "Then stop talking and put your hands to something."

Rafe did. He set down the flagging, stripped off his badge on its cord as if it burned him, and went for the wagon chain. Della watched his fingers fight the hook once before catching it clean. Those were still his father's hands and not his father's hands; hers had raised them and the valley had finished the job. Powder grit sat in the whorls of his knuckles. Under it the skin was split from ordinary farm work, old damage now wearing new dirt.

They worked the lower edge together without calling it together. Lucille tied off rope with neat clerk's hands that had learned from necessity what offices never taught. Silas set the planks and tested each with his weight before letting anybody else step there. Rafe braced the chain around the cedar and hauled until the muscles in his forearms stood up rope-hard under wet skin. Della went down farther than anybody wanted her to, because she was the only one who knew exactly where the fever plot line ran once the markers gave out and the child graves thinned and the hidden adult box began. She planted tag stakes and marked the edge by memory: not that stone, lower; not the fern patch, nearer the wash; Gabriel there, though she did not let the name into her face.

The earth under her shoes felt slick as soap. Once her left foot skidded and sent her knee into the clay hard enough to bark skin through her stocking. She pushed herself up with the flat of her hand and felt the heel sink deeper than it should have. Water had been working underneath. Not just on top. Underneath.

"Della," Rafe called. "Come up two feet."

She did not turn. "I'm not deaf."

"I didn't say you were. I said come up."

"If I come up, you'll put that stake wrong."

"Then tell me where."

She pointed without looking back. "By the root split. No, the other split. Christ, leave it. I'll do it."

She bent again. The motion sent a strip of pain across her back so bright it made the edges of the cemetery go white for an instant. She breathed through her teeth and drove the stake with the heel of the mallet anyway. When she straightened, she heard the sound before anybody shouted. Not a crack exactly. More like a long wet rip, cloth tearing under a heavy hand.

The hillside moved.

It did not leap. It slumped. A sheet of red clay just below the third cedar line gave way and went down in one thick slide, carrying grass, stones, and a short marker with it. The plank path jerked sideways. Rope snapped taut. One county hand fell to his backside and scrambled upward cursing. Lucille threw herself against the chain-wrapped cedar as if her own small weight could persuade the tree to keep doing its job. Silas shouted once, hoarse and flat: "Back!"

Della tried to climb and the hill answered by taking half a step away from under her. Rafe was suddenly beside her, not saying her name, only gripping her upper arm hard enough to bruise and hauling as the clay slithered past their shoes. The pull lit her shoulder and lower back together. She hated needing it and used it anyway because there was no space left for pride.

Mud ran over the lip of the fever plot in a thick, slow pour. A child marker disappeared. Roots showed white where the earth tore from them. Then the slide thinned, and beneath it something long and straight took shape where no straight thing belonged in open ground.

Wood.

Not fresh wood. Not even honest rotten wood. It lay black-brown and slick under the red smear, the corner of a coffin lid peeled half clear by the moving clay. Two square nails showed at the edge. A rusted side handle jutted out of the bank crooked as a broken finger. More dirt slipped away and bared another length of board. The box was canted now, shoulder-high in the torn earth, no longer hidden in the old fever trench but not yet free of it either.

Nobody spoke for a full breath.

Then Lucille said, very quietly, "Dear God."

Silas came down as far as he dared, coughed once into his rag, and looked without blinking. "That's it."

Rafe's hand was still on Della's arm. He let go as if he had remembered himself too late. His face had gone blank in the dangerous way, all his fast talk burned off. Wet concrete grit streaked one cheek. He looked from the exposed coffin to the rest of the slope above it, measuring what still might come down.

"There's more under the left side," he said. Even now he spoke practical first. "If that bank slips again it'll tear the lid clean."

Lucille's voice came back into her body. "Get the men off the lower track. Now. Rope the path. Nobody steps in without my say."

"Your say won't hold that hill," Silas said.

"It will hold the county long enough to keep gawkers out of it."

Della could not stop looking. She knew the coffin by position before she knew it by shape, and by shape before she let memory finish the job. The length of it. The poor-grade pine darkened to almost black. The way the slide had opened the shoulder end first, just as the ground had been shallower there on the night she and Silas brought him in on a door and lowered him by lantern light because no bell could be rung for that death and no preacher asked. Rain ran over the lid now in narrow red lines. One line found a crack and vanished inside.

Her breath came short again, but not only from the climb. Forty years had sat under that hill and never once forgiven her enough to stay still.

Lucille was saying something about tarps, about morning, about sending for Mr. Hensley and locking the office file. Della heard the words as if through a wall. Rafe bent to snatch up the dropped flagging and stared at the red strip in his muddy hand like he had not known until that moment what color it was.

"No," Della said.

Lucille turned. "No what?"

Della looked at the coffin and then at the slope above it, already shining with fresh seepage. Another blast rolled up from the dam, farther off this time but coming all the same, and a little shower of wet dirt pattered down over the exposed lid.

"No tarp fixes this," she said. "No paper fixes it neither."

Rafe did not argue. That frightened her more than if he had started in shouting. He only looked at the opened bank, his jaw working once before going still.

Silas tucked the cough rag away. "Hill's done the deciding."

Della stood in mud to her ankles with her back afire and her thumb swollen like a club, and knew he was right. The ground had cut past hearing dates and affidavits and all the small delaying tricks by which people pretended time might still be theirs. The fever plot had opened its mouth another handspan. By morning it would want more.
