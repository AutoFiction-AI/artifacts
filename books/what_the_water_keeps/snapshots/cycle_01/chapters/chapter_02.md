# Chapter 2

By the time Della climbed the half mile from Old Sion to Cedar Bench, the red clay on her shoes had dried hard enough to knock against the porch step like crockery. Downriver, the dam gave one of its late booms, not loud so much as deep, a concussion that rose through the hill and settled in her knees. She kept a hand over the papers under her apron as if the sound could shake them loose. Lucille Holt's duplicates and notices were only a few sheets, but paper had a way of growing heavier once it crossed a threshold. Outside the schoolhouse office it was clerking. In a poor kitchen it became flour, or loss, or one more reason a man could not look his family in the eye.

The screen door rasped in its frame. Ruthie had patched the torn corner with a square cut from an old feed sack, white print turned inward so the hens on the cloth would not show. The patch held, but the door still leaned wrong. Everything in the house leaned wrong now except Ruthie. She stood at the stove with her sleeves rolled above her elbows, forearms reddened from lye and wash water, lifting the lid on the bean pot with the same folded dish towel she always used when it was too scorched to be called clean anymore. June sat at the table with one leg tucked under her, copybook shut, fountain pen taken apart into three neat pieces beside her plate so it would not leak again. The girl had a blue-black stain along the side of her middle finger that no amount of soap ever seemed to take. Rafe was by the window, head bent over an envelope, using the last inch of a pencil to write figures on the back.

Ruthie turned without taking her eyes off the stove. "Wash first. I don't want grave dirt in the beans."

"It's clay, not grave dirt." Della untied her apron one-handed and hung it on the nail by the door instead of laying it over a chair where Rafe's eyes might go. The papers stayed folded in the bib. "And I wasn't in the ground today."

"You were near enough." Ruthie nodded toward the basin. "Same rule."

June looked up, watching the apron hang. "Did they move more today?"

"Not today," Della said. "They tagged, measured, argued, and pretended arguing wasn't what they were doing."

Rafe snorted through his nose without looking up from the envelope. "Sounds like county work all right."

Della washed at the basin. The water had gone gray around the edges from June's hands and Ruthie's potato peels. She scrubbed the clay from the creases beside her nails, dried on the towel by the door, then crossed to her chair. One of the back slats had split in February and Rafe had mended it with wire instead of a new piece of wood. The wire bit through her dress if she leaned wrong.

Ruthie set out supper: beans stretched thin with onion and a strip of salt pork that had given up most of itself two meals back, skillet cornbread, a dish of turnip greens cooked down so far they were more shine than leaf, and buttermilk watered enough that the white of it looked tired. She laid the plates carefully, as if care could enlarge them. On the sill over the sink, three cleaned jars waited for whatever summer might still put by for them. The shelves in the cellar were not empty yet, but they had begun to show wood between the jars, and that was its own kind of weather.

Della reached into her skirt pocket and laid four folded dollar bills beside Ruthie's plate. "Lucille paid through today."

Ruthie flattened the bills with a damp thumb before the steam could curl them. "That gets flour and lamp oil."

"It gets McRae to stop asking after his seventy-three dollars for one more week," Rafe said. He pushed away from the window and came to the table with the envelope still in hand. The scar in his chin went white when he set his jaw. "If he feels charitable."

Ruthie sat. "Sit down and eat before you start cussing over arithmetic."

"It ain't arithmetic if the answer stays the same." He dropped into his chair. One rung had come loose, so the chair tipped before it caught itself. "First State still wants four hundred eighty-six dollars by July fifteenth. Condemnation or no condemnation. The truck note don't go holy because the government's flooding us."

Della broke her cornbread. It steamed at the center and went dry at the edges in the same breath. "I know what the bank wants."

Rafe gave a short laugh with nothing in it. "Then maybe tell me what the county wants. They can tag half the valley's dead in tin and wire but they can't loose our claim because one man walked off twenty years ago."

Ruthie passed him the beans. "If you can let one meal be a meal, do that."

"The government already ate with us," he said. "It came in with those four dollars."

June had been watching the apron on the wall more than the beans in her bowl. She looked at Della instead. "What do the tags say?"

Rafe muttered, "Value, mostly."

Della shook her head. "A number. Name if there is one. Age if somebody knows it. Church row. Family section. Enough to keep the box from becoming nobody's once it comes up."

"Do the dead mind being moved?" June asked.

"The dead don't mind much of anything." Ruthie took the pork strip from the pot and cut it into four neat pieces, smaller than a thumb. "It's the living that fight."

Della nodded once. "A body doesn't care what hill it's on. A daughter cares if her mother gets carried under the wrong husband's name and the money goes someplace else."

June absorbed that with the grave attention she gave sums. "So the name is for the money."

"It's for the grave too," Della said. "But money is what makes people learn to spell."

That made Ruthie's mouth twitch, though she did not smile. Rafe did, but meanly. "There. That's more truth than the courthouse gets most days."

He tore off a piece of cornbread, dragged it through the beans, then said, as if he had held it in his teeth long enough, "Lucille say anything about Bledsoe land?"

Della ate first. Rafe hated that about her, the way she could put chewing between a question and its answer without seeming to delay at all. "Not to me."

"She say it to somebody else?"

"I don't know."

"You could ask."

"I could."

Rafe waited. When nothing followed, he slapped the envelope against his palm. "Jesus, Ma."

Ruthie set down her spoon. "Leave off 'Jesus' at the table."

He ignored her. "You know people over there are walking home with something clearer than they had when they came. You stood in front of half the graveyard and told that registrar whose people were whose. But when it comes to this house everything turns to fog."

Della looked at the envelope. "What's that?"

"As if you don't know." He shoved it toward her. The bank seal was smudged where his thumb had worked at it. "Final notice. They underlined 'final' this time. Maybe that means they bought a better pen."

June's spoon stopped halfway to her mouth. Ruthie said, "Eat."

Rafe said, "We keep eating, it'll be on somebody else's porch."

No one answered that. The silence in the room had shape to it. It pressed at the patched window, at the pot on the stove, at the line of jars waiting on fruit they had not yet been able to gather. Outside, a truck changed gears on the road below the bench, working uphill under too much weight.

June's gaze shifted back to the apron. When Della reached for the salt, the cloth pulled and one corner of the folded papers showed at the bib, county stamp and all. June saw it at once. The girl saw what had moved on a shelf, what had been mended twice instead of once, which sentence in a room had been left unfinished. That was her gift and the worst of it.

"What's that?" June asked.

"Work paper." Della tucked the corner back without hurry.

"Why keep it under your apron?"

"So it wouldn't blow off the seat walking home."

"There wasn't any wind."

Ruthie made a sound in her throat that might have become a laugh another year. "June."

But the girl kept her eyes on Della. Not insolent. Exact. "Was it about Sadie Kincaid?"

Rafe turned at that. "Let's see it."

"It's not for supper."

"If it's county paper and it touches this valley, it's for supper, breakfast, and whatever comes after."

Della left her spoon in the bowl. "It's hearing notice and duplicate tags. Not magic."

"Paper looks like magic when you ain't got enough of it." He held out his hand. "Let me look."

Ruthie said, quieter now, "Rafe."

He did not take his eyes off Della. "Let me look, Ma."

Della met him with the calm she knew he hated most, the one that narrowed a room instead of raising her voice in it. "No."

The answer sat there. June glanced from one to the other. Ruthie pinched the bridge of her nose with wet fingers, as if she could keep the whole evening from splitting by force.

Rafe leaned back. The chair creaked under him. "Fine. Then tell me this. How many more papers you got put away that know more than the county does?"

Ruthie closed her eyes once. "Lord."

Della did not move. "About what?"

"About everything." His words came faster now, outrunning themselves the way they did when shame got close enough to smell. "Births, deaths, who married who without a preacher. You always did know more than the courthouse and more than the church book. If you wrote half of it down, then maybe there's something in your keeping that might stop us losing this place like beggars."

June lowered her spoon. The beans in her bowl had skinned over.

Della kept her voice level. "What I kept wasn't for property men."

"What was it for then?"

He asked it with a dare in it, expecting perhaps that she would go silent and leave him the victory of naming her stubborn. Instead she said, "For girls who would have got the strap if the date on a baby's birth sat where the truth put it. For women whose husbands were dead in all but paperwork and needed a witness before the law would let them have a name that matched their own table. For children who needed somebody written down as father so they could stay in a house instead of being turned out of it. For burying the poor decent when respectable kin wouldn't come claim what was theirs."

Rafe blinked, not because the words were strange but because she had laid them out plain. Della went on before he could shove them back at her.

"I didn't keep a little gold mine in a box, if that's what you mean. I kept harm from landing where it always lands easiest."

Rafe's lip curled. "Easy for who?"

"Not for me."

"No. For the people you picked."

Ruthie said, "That's enough."

But Della had already crossed into it and knew better than to pretend she had not. "If I had written clean legal truth every time, I could name you three women would have been put out of their houses, one boy sent to the county home, and a body left unburied till it swelled. That's what clean truth buys the poor sometimes."

"The poor?" Rafe said. "We're the poor."

"I know that without you telling me."

"Then act like it."

Ruthie put down her spoon hard enough to ring the bowl. She was not loud, but when she stripped a sentence down there was nothing soft left in it. "Both of you, hush."

Rafe turned on her because he could not turn any farther on Della. "You want hush, you can sew the roof to it. The note's still due."

"I know when the note's due. I've got to look at the pantry same as you do." Ruthie wiped her fingers and pointed at the shelves with the dish towel. "I know exactly how much flour that four dollars buys, and I know June's school shoes are taking water at the toes, and I know the porch post by the steps is rotting at the foot. I'd still like one plate in this house to be a plate and not a courthouse bench."

June looked down fast at the mention of her shoes. One sole had indeed gaped open at the side this week. She had stuffed newspaper into it that morning, thinking nobody saw.

Rafe let out breath through his teeth. "Ordinary don't hold back the bank."

"No," Ruthie said. "But it keeps a girl from learning every bite she takes has to taste like panic."

That landed where she meant it to. Rafe's eyes flicked to June. His shoulders lost a little height. He picked up his spoon again and said nothing for the space of three bites.

June, still staring at her bowl, asked, "If the paper's only tags, why'd you hide it?"

There it was, the exact question, and not even Ruthie could be angry at the shape of it. Della looked at the girl's bent head, at the ink mark on her finger, at the way she had lined the spoon exactly parallel to the plank seam before picking it up again. June was fifteen and already knew that grown people only hid what could move a wall or a life.

"Because," Della said, "once paper comes through a door, folks start thinking it has authority over supper."

Rafe laughed once, tired and ugly. "It does."

Della said, "Only if you let it."

He pushed back from the table. "That sounds fine from somebody earning four dollars a day off it."

His bowl was still half full. Ruthie said, "Sit back down."

"I'm done."

"Then take the kindling in before you go brood on the porch."

He stood there a second, measuring whether disobedience would buy him anything. Then he took the kindling crate from beside the hearth and set it down harder than needed. Splinters jumped from one broken slat. He grabbed his tobacco pouch, found it too light, cursed under his breath, and went out anyway. The door hit the frame and bounced because even anger could not make it fit better than it did.

After that the room went to sounds that belonged to a house again: spoon against crock, stove tick, June swallowing, Ruthie pouring the last of the buttermilk. But the quiet had been opened. It did not mend just because nobody spoke.

Ruthie rose first and began clearing before Della could stop her. She always moved fastest when she was tired enough to shake. Up close the backs of her hands looked older than thirty-five, a network of little cracks gone white with soap. There was a safety pin through the cuff of her dress where the hem had torn that morning and she had not taken time to mend it proper. Della reached for the plates.

"I can do that," Ruthie said.

"I've got hands yet."

"Use them on the cornpot then. The soot's thick on the bottom."

June stood too. "I'll dry."

Ruthie handed her the least clean towel. "You will, and you'll keep the chipped cups for last because I don't trust your mind when it gets off wandering."

June did not answer, but her glance slid once more to the apron on the wall. Della saw it. Ruthie saw Della seeing it. Nothing was said.

At the sink, Ruthie kept her voice low enough that only Della could hear it over the water. "If there's trouble in those papers, I'd rather know before it comes stomping over my clean floor."

"There's trouble in the valley," Della said. "The papers are only where it sits down."

"That's an answer for church women."

"That's the answer I've got."

Ruthie scrubbed harder at the bean pot. "June noticed."

"June notices a flea on a moving dog."

"I know. Doesn't help."

Della dried a plate with a towel already damp from three uses. "I'll handle her."

Ruthie snorted softly. "You'll try."

That was all. Ruthie was too tired to pick a fresh quarrel and too honest to pretend the old one was settled. When the dishes were done she took the four dollars and folded them into the blue flour tin on the shelf above the stove, under a receipt from McRae Mercantile and a stub of pencil. She did it in front of Della, not furtive, merely factual. Here is where the money lives. Here is how small it looks.

June went to her schoolwork after. Rafe stayed on the porch until full dark, the boards speaking under his boots each time he crossed from one end to the other and back again. Twice Della thought he might come in and start another round. Twice he did not. When he finally entered, he smelled of tobacco crumbs and night air and the sour helplessness of a man who has counted himself through too many futures and liked none of them. He spoke only to say good night to June. He did not say it to Della.

The house settled by degrees. Ruthie banked the fire. June rinsed her pen and wrapped her copybook in oilcloth against damp. Rafe coughed behind the back room door. Della took the papers from the apron, unfolded them, and read them once by lamplight. Hearing date for Sadie Kincaid's daughters. Duplicate removal tags. Lucille's careful hand reminding her to bring any family knowledge that might clarify kinship claims. As if knowledge were something a woman could lift by the handles and set on a clerk's desk without the rest of the room smelling what had clung to it.

She refolded the papers smaller than before. The lamp flame made the county seal shine and dull, shine and dull.

The midwife ledger had been on her mind since the first coffin came up wrong on Monday. She had tried to tell herself that the dead at Old Sion could be walked through from memory alone. Most of them could. She knew whose babies lay under lamb stones, whose husbands had come home from war with one lung or none, which row held the fever spring and which held the respectable church dead. Memory had carried her fine until Lucille began pinning names to compensation and Rafe started looking at every grave like a locked feed store.

The trouble with memory was that it could stay in a woman's head and still ruin her. The trouble with writing was that it could outlive her and ruin people she had once kept breathing.

By the time Della undressed, she knew she would not sleep without settling it one way or the other. She lay down anyway. The mattress was thin in the middle from years of bodies and worry. Her left thumb ached from gripping shovels and coffin boards; the joint had thickened so much these last few seasons that a jar lid could humble her in front of a child. Somewhere below the bench a dog barked. Farther off, the dam gave another dull report. The quilt over her legs smelled faintly of cedar and old smoke. None of it helped.

She got up before the clock on the mantel had worked its way to ten.

The kitchen boards were cold through her stockings. She did not light the big lamp again, only the small one with the tin reflector, enough to throw a cone of yellow over the table and not much beyond. June's bed rope gave a single protesting squeak from behind the hanging quilt in the side alcove. Della stood still. No second squeak came. If the girl was awake, she knew better than to advertise it.

The cellar door opened with a pull upward before it would swing. The smell rose at once: damp earth, onion skin, old apples going sweet at the core, paraffin, mouse droppings. Della went down carefully, lamp held away from her skirt. The stone steps had worn hollow at the center. She could have walked them blind and once had, coming down in the night for fever cloths or canned peaches or a jar of lard when some labor ran longer than her stores upstairs.

At the back wall, behind the shelves where Ruthie kept beans and beets and the last of the peaches from last August, a limestone block sat looser than the rest. Della had loosened it herself forty years ago with a pry bar Eli never noticed missing. Since then she had slid it out only when she had to add, correct, or remember more than was safe to remember in daylight. Not often, these last years. Less often than the house itself seemed to breathe.

She set the lamp on the shelf and moved three jars aside. One lid pinged softly under her hand. The loose block resisted, then gave with a scrape that sent grit under her nails. Behind it, wrapped in oilcloth gone stiff at the folds, lay the ledger.

For a moment she only looked at it.

It was not much to see: a thick school copybook gone blunt at the corners, once marbled black and now the color of dishwater through the oilcloth, tied round with a strip torn from one of Eli's old work shirts. No county clerk would have taken it serious at first glance. That had been part of the point. The most dangerous things in a poor house were rarely the handsome ones.

Della lifted it out. The weight of it surprised her even though it should not have. Names weighed almost nothing until enough of them lived together. Then they could pull a whole hillside after them.

She carried the ledger upstairs and shut the cellar door with her hip. The house held still around her. On the table lay Lucille's papers, neat and bright and official. Beside them, the oilcloth bundle looked like contraband, which in a plain sense it was.

She untied the shirt strip. The knot had set hard over the years. Her thumb failed twice before it came free, and the little burst of pain in the joint brought sudden, dirty anger to her mouth.

"Shit," she said softly to the empty kitchen.

Then she opened the book.

The pages had yellowed unevenly. Some were buckled from damp, some smoothed flat by being pressed under weight long ago. Her own hand moved through them in pencil, in ink, occasionally in the purple of the copying lead she had bought cheap once and hated ever after. Dates. Places. Names given, names withheld, names shifted by a day or a season or a whole husband if that was what a woman needed to stay alive under the law she had been handed. Not every page held a lie. Most held only more truth than the county wanted to bother with: first breath, breach birth, cord round neck, child buried before dawn, witness present though not kin, couple living as man and wife these seven years and better than many licensed ones. The church books kept marriage or did not. The county kept tax names and death certificates if somebody paid attention in time. This book kept the rest, the part that happened in beds and kitchens and back rooms before respectability arrived to name it.

She turned slowly.

A widow on North Fork who needed her dead husband's surname to cover a child the world would have counted too late. A mountain girl no more than fourteen, thighs bruised yellow-green where her father had held her down while shouting scripture, begging Della not to let the birth month sit where it truly sat. Two brothers who stood up in a tool shed and swore themselves witness to a marriage because the preacher would not come till the man sobered, and by then he had been dead two days in the quarry. Three fever burials in one August week, each carried at dusk because no one wanted children to see the skin slipping at the wrists.

No public office in Hale County had those facts. No clerk in Larkin wanted them unless money suddenly depended on them. Even then what the office wanted was the useful edge trimmed clean, not the human mess that gave it shape. They would take a corrected surname. They would not take the reason the surname had to be corrected, because the reason usually involved a man with standing or a girl without any.

Rafe had called it a gold mine in a box without saying the words. Della laid her palm flat over a page until the paper warmed.

She had never taken money for changing what needed changing. Eggs, sometimes. A Sunday chicken. Jam. One woman had pressed a pair of child's shoes on her because shame had been all she had ready to hand. Della had accepted things because refusing them made people feel more naked, not because a date could be bought. Fraud for profit was what a bank man would call it if he ever saw the pages. He would not smell the blood, the milk gone sour on a nightgown, the kerosene on a shed floor where a couple had spoken vows because no church would seat them yet, the stink of August rot when a buried baby needed one name in public and another one whispered over the box. He would only see that truth had been bent.

Truth had been bent because straight truth would have broken the weakest neck in the room.

She knew that. She also knew that knowledge had a sour vanity in it if a woman was not careful. She had told herself protection, and mostly it had been protection. Mostly. But there had been pride too, the hard private pride of being the one person who could set a page against the world's cruelty and make it hold, at least till morning. Pride did not make the pages false. It only meant she was not as pure in them as she might like to remember.

She turned farther in, past births and confinements, to the shorter section of burials she had never meant anybody else to see. There the writing tightened. Death wanted fewer words. Fever. Stillbirth. Quarry crush. White child. Colored child. Unknown father. Husband absent. Preacher refused. Grave marked by cedar, no stone. She had written what would let her find a place again when memory thinned, and what would let the child or widow left behind tell a better story if better was the same thing as survivable.

Between two pages near the middle lay a pressed sweetgum leaf, brittle now. She did not remember putting it there. When she lifted it out, a grain of dirt fell onto the table, dark and fine as snuff. Her breath caught in a way that had nothing to do with age.

Gabriel's page was farther on. She knew exactly how many turns away. She did not go to it. Not tonight. Knowing where it waited was enough to put a taste of metal behind her teeth, not blood exactly, not memory exactly either, but the thing both left when they had dried.

She shut the book halfway and listened.

The house gave only its normal sounds: Ruthie turning once in bed, the settling pop of the stove iron, June's measured breath from the alcove, Rafe coughing into his pillow as if he begrudged even sleep the noise. Della sat with the ledger under both hands and thought of Lucille's practical mouth, of Sadie Kincaid's daughters counting widowhood and legality by different names, of her own son looking at every county form as though paper itself had decided to single him out for hunger.

If she burned the ledger now, the kitchen would smell sharp and greasy from the old ink and the oilcloth, and by morning one whole kind of danger would be gone. No thief could take it. No registrar could demand it. No grandson or granddaughter, someday, could open a drawer and learn what had been done in mercy and what in fear. The women in it would stay covered. The men too, the ones worth protecting and the ones not. Gabriel would go on being only her burden.

And the county would go on doing what counties did: trusting church books written by men who had not stayed to mop the floor after the labor, trusting deed names over fed children, trusting stone over witness even when the stone had been carved by someone half drunk and half paid. The wrong daughter would lose. The wrong widow would stand up in Larkin and be told her whole marriage had left no legal print. The dead moved this spring would go up the ridge cleaner on paper than they had ever been in life, which meant the poor would pay again for having lived messily in front of respectable people.

Della opened the book again.

"If I use you," she whispered, not to the book itself so much as to the years packed inside it, "I use you myself."

The sentence made her feel foolish. She was too old to bargain with paper. Still, she said more.

"No clerk gets the whole of it. No court either. Page by page. Grave by grave. Only what the dead at Sion force loose."

That was not safety. It was only a fence she could try to hold. But fences were what most honest work came down to: not perfect protection, only the line you kept mended between danger and whatever smaller thing had been entrusted to you.

She pulled Lucille's hearing notice closer and set the ledger beside it. The neat county paper and the swollen private book looked like enemies made to share a bench. Della found a stub of pencil, licked the tip without thinking, and on a blank rear page of the notice wrote three names she might need to check before Sadie's daughters went to fighting in public again. Then she stopped. She would not start turning the ledger into office copy at midnight like some clerk in her own kitchen. That was drift, and drift was how a woman lost the edge of what she meant to do.

Instead she opened the ledger to the front and read the first page properly for the first time in years. Her own younger hand named her purpose plain enough: births attended, marriages witnessed where the law lagged the body, burials made without proper record. There it was. Not holy. Not innocent. Exact.

She smiled once, without amusement, at the old arrogance of that hand. Then she closed the book and wrapped it again, not in the stiff oilcloth but in a clean dish towel from the drawer. Oilcloth belonged to hiding. A dish towel meant use.

That choice bothered her more than the opening had.

The safest thing would have been to carry it back below, behind the loose limestone and the canned beans, where damp and dark and habit had kept it hers. Instead she rose, took the lamp, and carried the rewrapped ledger to her room. In the bottom drawer of her bureau, beneath winter stockings and the black dress she kept for funerals, there was space enough. She laid the book there and shut the drawer softly.

Not hidden well enough. Hidden near.

On her way back through the kitchen she saw that the hanging quilt over June's alcove had shifted half an inch. The girl had turned in bed or had put a careful hand to the cloth to make herself a crack. Della stood looking at it.

"June," she said quietly.

A pause. Then, from behind the quilt, awake as a bell though trying to sound drowsy, "Yes ma'am?"

"What are you doing up?"

"I heard the cellar door."

Della let the silence sit long enough for June to feel it. "And?"

"I wondered what you needed."

"Something old."

June was quiet a moment. "From the cemetery?"

"No."

That answer was true and not. It would do for now.

After another second June said, "All right," in the tone she used when she knew an adult had fenced a field and meant to make note of where the gate might be later.

"Go to sleep."

"Yes ma'am."

Della blew out the lamp in the kitchen and lay down again in darkness thick enough to feel. The drawer across the room seemed to shine in her mind though no light touched it. She had taken the ledger out of the earth and put it inside the house among the living. That was all. That was enough.

From the alcove came no more sound, but Della knew June was lying there with her eyes open, arranging facts into a shape. In the back room Rafe coughed once and turned over. Beside him Ruthie would be measuring tomorrow's bread in her sleep. Downriver, beyond the bench and the emptied houses and the tagged stones at Old Sion, the dam kept on with its slow blunt labor.

Della closed her hand over the ache in her thumb and waited for morning, knowing she had not solved a thing. She had only chosen which danger would sleep in her house.
