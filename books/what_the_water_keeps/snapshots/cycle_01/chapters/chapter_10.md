# Chapter 10

By the time Della carried Ruthie's potato dish through the side door of Old Sion, the fellowship room had already gone slick with steam. August heat sat low under the pressed-tin ceiling. The windows were shoved up with hymnals, and flies worried the rims of the sweet tea jars. Someone had laid brown paper over the long tables to save the cloth beneath from ham grease, though everybody in the valley knew grease would find its way through if it had the mind to. The room smelled of baked onions, coffee boiled too long, banana pudding, damp shirt collars, and the river's rank summer breath pushing uphill from below the cemetery.

People looked up when she came in. They tried to do it like neighbors noticing a dish, not like a room counting the woman who would have to speak next week if anybody meant to stop Rafe's paper from hardening into law. Della felt the weight of their looking the way she felt the potato pan through the folded towel in her palms: not sharp enough to burn clean through, only steady and mean.

Ruthie had already turned the kitchen end of the room into something workable. She stood near the stove in a checked apron gone thin at the ties, her reddened hands moving pans into rows that made sense to her and nobody else until they did. She had a safety pin holding one apron strap where the cloth had torn, and she kept shifting it off her shoulder with the back of her wrist so she would not grease the fabric worse. "Set yours away from the window," she said without pausing. "Sun's mean on cream."

Della slid the potatoes between Miss Cora Kincaid's chow-chow and a platter of deviled eggs dusted so red with paprika they looked rouged. Miss Cora, who never trusted a church supper to return a full set of jars, had brought her relish in three washed jelly glasses and kept checking that all three still stood together. Her new place on Limestone Road had a west-facing kitchen, and she had already told six people that anything pickled would have to be moved clear across the room by noon or it would soften in the heat. She told Della again anyway.

"That window catches sun like a grudge," Miss Cora said, pushing at one of the jelly glasses until it lined up square with the others. "I told my son if he wants crisp beans come January he better build me shelves on the north wall and stop talking about it."

"He'll talk till frost if you let him," Della said.

Miss Cora snorted. "He gets that from his daddy. Dead twelve years and still taking up room in a conversation."

That got a real laugh out of Della before she could guard it. Around them the room worked itself into the old patterns that had kept valley people fed, married, buried, and informed for longer than any county office had cared to remember. Olan Mercer, a widower who salted every plate before he tasted it because his sinuses had gone bad in the quarry dust, was already worrying aloud over the new school route. If the bus came by the state road, his youngest would get sick on the curves and shame himself before breakfast. Mrs. Fairley was telling anybody who would listen that the new graveyard on Limestone Ridge had not one honest patch of shade after two o'clock and that her mother had spent fifty years avoiding hard afternoon sun only to be planted in it for Judgment Day. Pearline Doss, the church clerk, had tucked two sharpened pencils into the knot of her gray hair and was making a list on the back of a seed catalog of every dish that needed to go home with somebody other than the person who had brought it.

Land was not what they talked about first, and neither was money. They talked about what money could not keep from going missing once the valley emptied out. They talked about which porch on Cedar Bench took breeze enough for shelling peas in August, which cow could be trusted not to kick when a child milked her, and how far frost laid itself along the low road before it reached the benches. They talked about which persimmon tree held fruit late enough for Christmas pudding if the birds did not strip it first, whether the new cemetery would take cedar cuttings, and whether church ladies in town would know to send soup to a house where a baby had come wrong and the sheets were still soaking in lye water out back.

That was what the room had come to say good-bye to, though no one put it that clean. Della heard it in the way people said road and shade and jars and school as if they meant kin. She heard it in the caution under each practical question, because every ordinary thing had become a thing a person might have to relearn among strangers.

June came through carrying a stack of plates to the sideboard, long arms full and careful. She had a blue-black smear of ink on the side of her finger where Pearline had set her to marking borrowed serving spoons with scraps of paper, and there was still one church bulletin folded into a narrow strip in her sleeve cuff because she forgot to empty pockets when given a job. She moved quiet, but her eyes missed little. When a woman lowered her voice at Della's approach, June noticed. When a man nodded to Rafe and not to Della, June noticed that too. Fifteen had not taken the child out of her face yet, but there were moments when she looked up from under that hair and some older exactness showed through hard enough to startle.

"Miss Doss says the county office has metal cabinets clear to the ceiling," June said, setting the plates down.

Ruthie, still moving, said, "Then Miss Doss can tell it to me after she salts the beans. Take those forks out, June."

June obeyed, but she looked back toward Pearline and the list in her hand the way other girls looked at catalog dresses.

Rafe came in last among the family, hat in his hand, chin scar pale against the dark of his face where he had shaved close for church. His good shirt had been mended at one cuff with thread a shade too light because that was the thread Ruthie had. He stood just inside the door a second as if the room's heat had hit him like a wall. Then he crossed to the coffee urn and set to carrying chairs from one side over to the long tables because doing something with weight in it always suited him better than standing among talk. He did not look at Della. He did not have to. The air between them had been measured in public now, with courthouse miles and filing dates inside it.

Noreen Acker arrived carrying a chess pie in a bent tin she had wrapped in a dish towel with a burned corner. The green dress she wore had been let down and hemmed again; you could see where the old fold had paled the cloth. She had one of those cheap peppermint drops tucked in her cheek to settle an empty stomach, and she kept nudging it from one side of her mouth to the other while she looked for a place to set the pie.

"Landlady says if I bring one more dish home smelling of onions she'll start charging me kitchen rent," she said to Della as she put the tin down. "As if I came to town for the pleasure of listening through a wall to a man cough up his lungs every morning."

"You still taking that room?" Della asked.

"Till the widow over the drugstore dies and frees up the better one." Noreen shrugged. "This one's got a window on the alley. I can tell what the cafe's frying before dawn, which is not a gift I asked the Lord for."

It was said dry, not fishing for pity, and Della liked her better for it than she always meant to. Noreen cut the pie into careful wedges with a butter knife she had brought from home because church knives went dull and could not be trusted. When she leaned close to say something lower, Della smelled starch, old soap, and the peppermint trying to cover hunger.

"I miss the dark here," Noreen said. "Town dark's never dark. It hums and leaks. This place, when it went quiet, it went all the way."

Della looked past her to the open window where the graveyard slope dropped away under cedar and limestone. Downriver, too far off to see, a dull concussion rolled from the dam works and came up through the floorboards like somebody striking the underside of the room with a padded hammer.

"It doesn't go all the way anymore," Della said.

"No." Noreen set the knife down. "But I still know what it used to sound like."

For a little while the room held to that gentler current. Children ran between tables until somebody hissed them down. Pearline put June to turning pie plates over and reading the names scratched or penciled on their bottoms. Miss Cora and Mrs. Fairley began arguing amiably over whether tomatoes ought to be canned with or without a spoon of sugar if the fruit had grown in new soil. Olan Mercer told a long, unnecessary story about a mule that had kicked its own trace free and walked home alone from the lower road in 1939, and three men corrected details while laughing because all of them had been there when it happened and all four versions could not be true. Ruthie moved among them with a coffee pot, keeping cups full and the room from snagging too long on any one discomfort. There were people in Brier Valley who thought the preacher held a church together. Della had lived long enough to know it was more often a woman carrying hot liquid between quarrels.

She might almost have forgotten the hearing if not for the way conversation bent when it neared the words record, name, county, claim. Those words sent a caution through the room, quick as a draft under a door. People did not stop talking. They changed the shape of how they said things. A pause grew where a name might have been. A glance crossed the table instead of a sentence. Somebody said, "when all this is settled," and did not say what this was. Everybody understood anyway.

After the first plates were filled and the worst hunger taken off, Vernon Wolfe rose from his chair near the head of the room with his napkin folded exact in one hand. He had not sweat through his collar, though half the men present looked boiled in their own clothes. A wintergreen lozenge bulged in the pocket of his vest, waiting for after ham, and even from where she sat Della could see that he had wiped his fingertips clean before standing. He had the look he always had in public, broad and brushed and fitted neatly inside his own authority, like a man who expected any room with prayer in it to carry his weight for him.

"Before folks scatter to pie," he said, voice pitched warm enough to pass for generosity, "I'd like to say one thing while we're all here under the same roof."

No one told him no. That was part of his standing too.

"We've lived a long while in this valley by one another's memory," Vernon said. "That has been a blessing, and in some cases a mercy. But memory is not made cleaner by confusion. If anything, a hard season asks more of us. It asks us to keep faith with what is rightful. Rightful land. Rightful graves. Rightful names. A lawful name is not some cold office trick. It's what keeps a family from dissolving into rumor. It's what lets the dead lie as who they were and the living stand where they belong without shame."

He paused then, not because he needed breath, but because he knew how to leave room for his words to choose their target in other people's heads.

"We're all being tested some," he went on. "By government men, by deadlines, by the temptation to answer disorder with more disorder. I hope we won't give in to that temptation. I hope we'll remember that decency begins with calling things by their proper name."

The room did not go silent all at once. It tightened. Forks slowed. A child was hushed too late. Della sat still with her hand around a spoon she had forgotten to lift. He had not said her name. He had done something meaner. He had set the whole room looking at the empty place where her name belonged.

Noreen did not let the silence settle into him.

"Lawful's done a fair amount of choosing in this valley," she said from three chairs down, not loud, the peppermint making one corner of her cheek rise. "Some folks got names written straight and some got left carrying theirs loose in their mouth. I wouldn't dress that up as decency."

Heads turned toward her so sharply a chair leg scraped. Vernon looked at her with the patient face he wore before he chose whether patience would do.

"No one's speaking against hardship, Noreen," he said. "I'm speaking for order."

"Order." Noreen gave the word back flat. "That's a nice one. When a man keeps the deed and the church book, he gets to call himself orderly. When the rest of us say the counting was crooked, we're troublesome."

"This is neither the time nor the place to air grievances."

"A church supper before the valley gets drowned seems exactly the time to say lawful never meant moral. It meant whoever had a pen and a lockbox."

Miss Cora stopped with her fork halfway up. Olan Mercer looked hard into his plate as if mashed potatoes had become scripture. June, standing at the sideboard with three pie servers nested in her hand, had gone so motionless she might have been holding her breath.

Vernon's smile thinned but did not crack. "There are women in this county," he said, "who have been wronged by circumstance, and men as well. That doesn't change the fact that once we start treating names as optional, everything else comes loose behind them."

Noreen pushed her empty coffee cup aside with one finger. "Names are not what's optional. Mercy is. Fair dealing is. Plenty of men kept those optional and still got called respectable every Sunday of their life."

Ruthie set the coffee pot down hard enough to ring the table. It was not a slam. It was just enough iron in the gesture to make everybody hear.

"If y'all are set on preaching, do it after the plates are cleared," she said. "The ham's drying out while decency gets discussed to death."

That broke the held breath in the room, not because the matter was over but because Ruthie had handed everybody a smaller task to hide in. Miss Cora muttered, "She's right about the ham," and reached for the platter. Somebody laughed once, thin and grateful. The sound of serving spoons started up again.

But the room had sorted while the coffee steamed between them. Della could feel it as plainly as she felt the bench under her thighs. Mrs. Fairley turned her whole body toward Noreen when she asked for pickles, a little act of alignment so small only a watcher would have marked it. Olan took Vernon's side of the table when he went back for tea, then seemed ashamed of how visible the choice was. Pearline Doss kept her face bent over the list and did not join anybody, which was its own kind of choice for a woman who lived by written names. Rafe ate with his head down and jaw working too hard, as if the biscuit had insulted him personally and had to be punished for it.

No one said hearing. No one said affidavit. No one said fever plot. The words sat outside the room like wet boots. Everybody knew whose boots they were.

Della took a spoonful of potatoes and tasted nothing but salt. Across from her, June had edged down beside Pearline and was looking at the church clerk's stack of minute books with naked interest.

"Do you have to copy all of that by hand?" June asked.

Pearline, whose left eye watered whenever she copied for too long, blinked at her and dabbed the corner dry with the cuff of her cardigan. "Every blessed line. Quarterly reports too, when conference wants them."

"Can a person get paid for that somewhere besides church?"

Pearline looked amused. "County pays Miss Holt to drown in paper all day, if that counts."

June's fingers tightened a little on the pie server. "I think I'd like that."

Ruthie heard but did not interrupt. Della did, and the words sat with her stranger than any prayer Vernon had offered. She had spent so long thinking of what June needed to be protected from that she had not given equal measure to what the girl might want to move toward. She pictured a desk and metal cabinets. She pictured paper that came to a person as work and not as threat. She pictured a life where silence was not the main inheritance.

At the far end of the table, Miss Cora had started in on graveyard shade again. "I don't care how new and dry Limestone Ridge is," she said. "If they plant my Harry where there's not one cedar to break the sun, I'm liable to haunt the county myself."

Mrs. Fairley said, "You don't haunt. You nag from beyond, that's different."

Even Vernon had to smile at that, though it did not soften him. The room found another small pocket of ordinary life and crawled into it for shelter. People talked about where to move peonies so the roots would take. They talked about whether the school in Larkin still paddled boys for cussing. They talked about the preacher in town who wore store-bought shirts with pearl snaps and called everybody brother like he meant salesman. Somebody asked Della if babies came quicker in storm weather, and she answered with a real answer, not a symbol: sometimes yes, if the mother had already been close and the air turned heavy enough to make her restless.

That was the part the county could not list on any compensation form. Not the acreage, not the market value of the woodlot, not the truck note hanging over Rafe like weather. The texture of what one person knew to ask another. The exact voice a woman used when she wanted advice but would not say she was frightened. The fact that half the room already trusted Della with the shape of their dead and the other half distrusted her for the same reason.

When the first wave of plates emptied and people began carrying things back toward the kitchen, Ruthie caught Della's eye and nodded toward the pantry. "There's room on the shelf now. Set the leftovers in there before they go soft."

Della took up two bowls and the potato dish and shouldered through the swinging door. The church pantry was scarcely more than a narrow room lined with shelves, but it held the thick practical smell of any place built to keep a congregation going: onions sprouting at the neck, old flour in the wood grain, coffee tins, bleach, mouse poison, paraffin wax, dust. Somebody had stacked clean pie plates against the wall, each one turned bottom-up so the name or initials written there could be seen and sent home right. The single bulb overhead gave off more heat than light.

She set her dish down and flexed her left thumb where the pan had pressed it. The joint had stiffened in the last weeks until certain grips cost her later. She stood a moment with her palm against the cool rim of a pie plate and listened to the muffled room beyond the wall: chair legs, low talk, a child's quick laugh cut short.

The pantry door pushed in behind her.

Rafe came through and let it swing almost shut, not enough to latch and enough to narrow the light.

"You heard him," he said.

Della did not turn at once. She reached for the dish towel hanging from a nail and wiped a streak of potato from her knuckle. "The whole county heard him. He's got lungs."

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Answer the edge and skip the point."

There it was, the exactness she had handed him against her own interest over the years: the ability to hear what a person was avoiding and feel insulted by the shape of it.

She turned then. He stood with one hand on the shelf post, long fingers splayed against the rough wood. Up close she could smell sweat dried under church soap and a trace of machine oil that never seemed fully to leave him now, no matter what labor he had come from. The mended cuff at his wrist had already frayed where a nail or splinter had worried it open again.

"If you've come in here to fight at a supper," she said, "say what you came to say and let me by."

Rafe laughed once, with no pleasure in it. "A supper. That's what you call that?"

"It was till Vernon opened his mouth."

"He only said out loud what they'll all be thinking when you take that stand."

"Then let them think."

"I don't have that kind of money."

The words hit harder because he said them plain, not dressed. He pushed off the shelf and took one step nearer.

"First State doesn't take dignity in place of four hundred eighty-six dollars," he said. "McRae doesn't hand over flour because a man chose the finer truth. Ruthie's turning collars and pillowcases till the cloth goes white in her hands, and June is near grown in dresses cut down from old ones. You want me to stand there next week and watch you throw away the only paper that might keep us above water because Vernon Wolfe deserves correcting?"

"This isn't about Vernon deserving anything."

"Hell it isn't. Everything with names on it in this valley turns into his kind of business sooner or later."

Della kept her voice level because she could feel anger pushing up and knew if she let it break loose too quick she would waste it. "You walked to Larkin and lied in ink. Don't bring it to me like weather."

He flinched, small and hot. "I brought it because weather don't ask a damn thing from me. Paper does. Paper wants a name on the line. It wants one right now. If I don't give it one, the county'll take the easiest one anybody richer than me can live with."

"Eli isn't the man in that grave."

"You know that. I know you know that. What I don't know is what sacred thing you're saving that's worth more than a roof."

Della looked at him, at the sweat dark under the arms of the good shirt, at the jaw he had from Eli and not from Gabriel, at the forty years of labor already starting to write themselves in a body not yet forty. On the shelf beside him sat three pie plates with names on their bottoms in grease pencil. Fairley. Kincaid. Mercer. They were easy names, the kind that could be returned to the right houses.

"You're not asking me for uncertainty," she said. "You're asking me to swear against what I know."

"I'm asking you to understand cost."

"I know cost."

"No. You know memory. That's not the same thing."

The cruelty in it was not accidental. He knew where to put a blade when he meant one. Della felt the old temptation to strike back with the full truth, to split him open on the spot and be done with half-lies forever. Gabriel's name rose in her like a bad taste. She swallowed it.

"What you filed could make a county record out of a false man," she said.

"And what you tell could make a public ruin out of me."

The pantry had gone hotter, or maybe it was only the bulb. Somewhere in the kitchen a spoon fell and rolled. Neither of them moved toward the sound.

"You heard Vernon," Rafe said. His voice had dropped now, rougher for it. "Lawful names. Rightful lines. He gets to say that because his people are wrote down where he wants them wrote down. If I walk in there with you against me and whatever secret blood thing you've been nursing all these years comes spilling out, they won't hear witness. They won't hear careful. They'll hear I filed false, that my house runs on lies, that my daddy's bones ain't even his if that's where this is going. They'll hear Bledsoe and spit."

He had come close enough now that she could see the pale half-moon where he had once taken a hook or blade to the chin in boyhood. He raked his thumb over it when shame got too near. He did it now.

"Rafe," she said, softer than before because softness could cut too, "if you had not filed that paper, we would not be standing here with the county at our throat."

"If you had told one straight thing at any point in my life, maybe I wouldn't have needed to."

That landed because he knew there was a shape to the truth even if he did not know the shape itself.

He saw it land and pressed harder.

"You want the honest answer?" he said. "Truth's a luxury now. That's what it comes to. It's a rich man's thing. Folks with full cupboards and deed books and a second pair of Sunday shoes get to love truth. They can afford the hit. People like us use what'll pass inspection and keep the roof one more month."

He hit the shelf post with the heel of his hand, not hard enough to break anything, hard enough to set the pie plates clinking against one another like small alarm bells.

"I can't feed June on whatever noble feeling you've got tied up in that grave," he said. "I can't walk into the bank with righteousness. I can't tell Ruthie the county froze us out because my mother preferred the finer wound."

Della's thumb found the crack in the edge of the potato dish. She pressed there until the sharp glaze bit skin. It kept her from saying the first thing, which would have been too much and not enough at once.

"And I can't bury one man under another man's name because your note's delinquent," she said.

"It's not just the note."

"I know it isn't."

"Do you?"

He said it without shouting. That made it worse. His anger had gone past performance into something stripped. She could see the boy in him then, the one who had come home from school bloodied for somebody else's insult and learned early that need turned a person into spectacle if he let it. She could see, too, the man he had built over that boy: proud in the wrong places, humiliated in the right ones, tired clear to the bone.

"You think this is about paperwork because paperwork is what showed up first," he said. "It isn't. It's about not being the family everybody points at while pretending they're sorry. It's about not letting him in there make me less clean than he is when half his decency is made of things people swallowed for him."

Della answered the literal question because the larger one had teeth. "He doesn't get cleaner because he says it in church."

"Then don't help him."

"By lying for you?"

"By understanding that poor people don't get truth the same way rich people do." He stepped back just enough to let the sentence have room. "Rich people get truth as history. Poor people get it as punishment."

For a moment all Della could hear was the dull hymn of voices through the wall and, underneath that, the river project's far machinery holding its own note down in the valley. The pantry shelves smelled of onion skin and flour and hot paint. Rafe stood waiting, chest lifting hard under the good shirt Ruthie had made presentable for one more Sunday. He was not wrong about cost. That was what made him dangerous.

"Move," she said.

He stared at her.

"Move out of the doorway."

"So that's all?"

"It's enough."

"For you, maybe."

This time anger showed clean in him. He stepped aside, but not graciously, one shoulder scraping the shelf as if the room itself had crossed him. Before she passed, he said, low and forced through his teeth, "When they start choosing what I'll be next week, don't act surprised that they use your mouth for it."

Della went by him without answering. In the kitchen, Ruthie stood at the sink with both hands in gray dishwater, her face set in the blank economy she used when she had too much to do for feeling. She did not ask what had happened. She looked once at Rafe coming out behind Della and knew enough.

"Dry those platters," Ruthie said.

It was not absolution. It was work, which was sometimes the nearest thing.

Della took the towel and obeyed. Through the pantry wall she could still hear the room shifting itself toward departure, cups emptied, chairs scraped back, the low Sunday murmur of people beginning to carry portions of one another's lives home in tins and jars. June was by the sideboard with Pearline, copying names from the bottoms of two cake plates onto a scrap of paper because the pencil marks had smeared.

"Miss Doss says Larkin High's got commercial classes," June was saying. "Typing and bookkeeping both."

Pearline, stacking minute books into a box that smelled of cedar and mold, said, "If your folks can spare you the time."

June glanced up then and saw Della watching. She did not look ashamed of what she had asked. She looked resolved in a new, careful way, as if a room full of adults failing one another had taught her something she meant to make use of.

Beyond her, Vernon stood by the church door shaking hands with men who wanted his approval more than they wanted peace. Noreen had wrapped two pieces of chess pie in wax paper for later and was talking to Mrs. Fairley about rent in town and where a body might still find sorrel close enough to gather. Miss Cora was arguing with Olan over whose son had promised to bring cedar saplings to Limestone Ridge first. Ruthie was wringing out a cloth. Rafe had gone to hauling chairs again, working hard enough at it to make labor look like a verdict.

Della dried the platter until the painted roses on its rim came clear under the film of grease. Around her the room had gone back to ordinary voices, but she could hear what sat under them now. It was not only worry. It was selection and sorting. The hearing would not be a legal errand in Larkin. It would be this room carried into county light. Whatever name she put to the fever-plot dead would not stay on the dead alone. It would fasten itself to the living: thief, bastard, victim, witness. The water was still downriver. The naming had already begun.
