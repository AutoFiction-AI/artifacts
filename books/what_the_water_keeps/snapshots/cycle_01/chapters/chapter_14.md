# Chapter 14

The kitchen had been scrubbed so often the pine boards had gone pale around the hearth, but Della still knew the brick Gabriel Wolfe had struck with the back of his head. It sat just left of the crane hook, darker after rain than the rest, as if the room kept one stain for itself no matter how much lye she spent on it.

She stood in the doorway with cemetery mud drying in the seams of her shoes and listened to the house hold its breath. Ruthie had the coffee pot shoved to the cooler edge of the stove so it would not boil bitter. In the pantry June was penciling dates on a row of peach jars because she could not abide a shelf where the labels leaned crooked. Outside at the pump, Rafe worked the handle hard enough to ring the yard iron, each stroke answered from downriver by the dull concussion of the dam.

Ruthie did not look up from the towel she was folding into exact squares. Her laundress hands were red to the wrists from other people's soap and her own washwater. "If you're fixing to tell it," she said, "do it before the road tells it ugly."

Della set her palm on the back of the nearest chair. The day had left her left thumb thick and mean. "I know."

"Do you?"

Della looked past Ruthie to the dark mouth of the pantry. A peach jar clicked against the shelf, then another. June was trying to be quiet and failing by a glass edge.

"Let her finish in there," Della said.

Ruthie's mouth moved once, not quite a smile. "She has ears either way."

The pump handle stopped. A moment later Rafe came in, face and hair wet from the yard basin, shirt collar dark where water had run down into it. Grave dirt still held in the half-moons of his nails. He had changed nothing else. His work pants were caked to the knee and the scar in his chin looked whiter than the rest of him. He shut the door harder than it needed shut and stood with one hand on the latch as if the room might try to turn him back out.

"Well?" he said.

The word was for Della, but he cast it at the whole kitchen, at Ruthie's coffee pot, at the pantry, at the place in the floor where mud from the grave had dried into fine red scales.

Della said, "Sit down."

"I stood up out there while half the valley watched you hand me over. I can stand here."

Ruthie reached for the coffee pot. "You want some?"

"No."

She poured herself half a cup anyway and leaned against the stove with it, eyes on the black enamel as if heat marks there deserved more attention than any of them.

Della took the chair at the table because she knew if she stayed on her feet she would start with chores and weather and every other coward's delay she had practiced for thirty-nine years. Rafe did not sit. He planted himself across from her with his wet hands braced on the chair back.

"Lucille says the hearing still stands," he said. "Vernon says he's bringing somebody from Knoxville, like the county's not mean enough on its own. Men I don't know were already talking my name before I got off the hill. So say it plain. What does your truth buy?"

The dam sounded again, far off and felt more than heard, a low shove through the crockery shelf. One spoon rattled against a jar. Della waited for the sound to pass.

"Nothing," she said. "Not a thing you can spend."

Rafe gave a short laugh that did not warm into anything. "That's what I figured."

"That's what I should have told you first. I know it."

"You should have told me first a good many years ago."

He was still looking at her as her son then, wanting an answer from the woman who had mended him, fed him, buried his dog, slapped mud off his Sunday shoes when he was too small to reach the pump. Della could see the wanting under the anger, and because she could see it she knew she was about to kill that too.

She laid both hands flat on the table to keep them from going to the stove rag, the coffee pot, the dish stack, any task that might save her from speech.

"Gabriel Wolfe was your father," she said. "Not Eli."

The pantry went still. Even the jar glass stopped clicking.

Rafe did not move for so long Della heard the kettle beginning to tick at the back of the stove. Then he said, almost gently, "No."

"Yes."

"No."

He said it again with more force, not because force would change it, but because there was nowhere else for him to put the blow. He straightened from the chair back. Water from his hair ran down his neck and vanished into his shirt. "That's the kind of thing people say to hurt a man when the hurting's already done."

"I didn't say it to hurt you."

"Then what the hell did you say it for now?"

Della looked at the hearth instead of him. The brick had a hairline chip in one corner where the poker had struck it years later, some ordinary winter accident trying and failing to overwrite the older thing. "Because everybody else has enough of it now to make their own version. Because by tomorrow it will be half-laughed over at McRae's and turned into church talk by Sunday. Because if you were going to hear the rest, you were going to hear it from me."

"The rest."

He dragged the chair out then, not to sit but to have something in his hands. The legs grated over the boards Gabriel had kicked with his boots the night he died. Della heard both sounds together.

"You named him out there," Rafe said. "I heard that part same as everybody else. The grave was his. Fine. He was Vernon Wolfe's brother. Fine. That still leaves Eli. That still leaves me. So don't hand me one rotten apple and call it supper. Tell it."

Ruthie took one swallow of coffee and set the cup down. She was not drinking for taste. She was giving her hands somewhere to go.

Della said, "I was married to Eli. You know that much."

"I know what the Bible says and what the county says."

"I was married to him and I lay with Gabriel anyway."

The words came easier for being ugly. Once they were out, they took the air with them.

Rafe turned his face a little, not away from her exactly, but as if what he needed to hear might sound different from the side. "How long you been saving that clean little sentence."

"It never was clean."

He laughed again. This time the laugh snagged in his throat and came out rough. "No. I reckon not."

In the pantry June shifted her weight. The old floorboard under the flour bin answered with a small, traitorous pop. Ruthie glanced toward the sound and then back to Della. She did not tell the girl to move. She did not tell her to stay.

Della drew a breath that tasted of old coffee, damp wood, and the sour earth still trapped in her own sleeves from the grave. "Gabriel came around when I was still foolish enough to mistake wanting for rescue. He knew how to make an offer sound like a favor. He would lean on a gatepost and talk about weather, about apples, about how a man ought to keep a woman from wearing herself out for poor wages. He never said plain that he meant me until he had already made sure I'd hear it."

Rafe's mouth twisted. "So I came from a gentleman."

"You came from me letting a pretty-voiced man use his pretty voice on me when I was lonelier than I had any right to be."

That shut him for a beat. Not softened him. Shut him.

Della went on because stopping now would turn the whole thing false by mercy. "By the time I knew I was carrying you, Gabriel was already smoothing it down into something temporary. He said he'd see me right. He said we'd settle something after harvest. He said Vernon would make a fuss if he moved too fast and I ought to let him handle his own people. He had a phrase for every month of that year except the month a paper might have had to be signed."

"And Eli?"

"Eli knew enough."

Rafe's eyes snapped to hers. "When?"

"Not the first day. Maybe not the first month. But a man can count. A man can look at a child and count years after he says he's not counting. He knew enough."

"And he stayed."

"He stayed."

"Why?"

Della almost said because pride can chain a man as hard as love, because poor men will live inside a lie if the truth makes them smaller in public, because Eli could not bear Vernon Wolfe's laughter any more than he could bear Gabriel's hand on what he called his house. All of that was likely true. None of it was hers to swear to.

"Because leaving wasn't simple," she said. "Because we were poor and still had a roof. Because your cradle was in the next room and mornings still came. Because people don't always walk the first time they know they should."

Rafe wiped his wet hand over his mouth. "You mean he stayed because he could not afford pride without supper under it."

"I mean most things in this valley come tied to money whether we say so or not."

"That part I know."

He shoved the chair aside and began walking the narrow strip between stove and table, three paces one way, two the other, because the kitchen would not give him room for a bigger anger. His boots left damp half-moons on the boards. Della watched him refuse to look at the hearth, watched the room herd him toward it anyway.

"Did he call me his?" Rafe asked.

"Most times."

"Most times."

"Don't make me lie now in the small places because I lied in the large one."

That stopped him harder than the rest had. Della heard Ruthie set her cup down again, soft this time.

Rafe said, "Then tell the large one. Tell the night."

The room shortened around Della. The stove face gave off its dry old heat. A fly buzzed once against the window and blundered away. In the pantry June had gone so still Della could picture her without looking: long arms folded close so she would not knock another jar, pencil caught above one ear, ink dry on the side of her finger. Fifteen and already listening like a clerk.

Della rubbed the pad of her right thumb along the seam in the tabletop where a crack had been filled with wax twenty winters ago. "I had started asking Gabriel for paper by then. Not love. I was cured of that. Paper. Something with his name and yours where a clerk might one day have to look at it. He never liked me better than when I asked for less than I deserved. Once I asked for more, he started speaking careful."

Rafe said nothing. She could hear him listening through his teeth.

"That night he came after supper. Late enough the road was empty. He had on a white shirt too good for weekday and that gray coat he wore when he wanted to look like he was not doing anything dirty. He smelled of horse sweat and orchard rot and the cologne he bought in town when he meant to pass for a finer man than the valley already took him for."

The room in front of her stayed the room in front of her: Ruthie's folded towel, the blue crock of spoons, the chipped enamel basin on the side shelf. Over it came the older room, not different in shape, only fuller of danger.

"I told him I was done being put off. I told him if he was going to keep using my back door and my patience, he could put something in writing for the boy he'd made. Not a promise. Writing. He smiled like I'd asked for a dance. He said there were easier ways to keep me and the child comfortable. A cow. Cash by installments. A little ground maybe, later on, after he had smoothed Vernon. He kept saying sensible. He always used that word when he meant cheaper for him."

Rafe muttered, "That sounds like him, from all I heard."

"It sounded like him in this kitchen too."

She could see Gabriel's hand flat on the table, good cuff already picking up flour because he never noticed what his sleeves touched. She could see herself standing by the stove with the dipper still in her hand, trying not to hear desire in a bargain because desire would have made her more foolish than she'd already been.

"I told him no. I told him I was done being sensible for his sake. I said your name was going to cost him more than market talk and hidden visits. He said if I pushed, Vernon would shut the whole thing so hard I'd end up with less than I had now. He was still talking when Eli came in."

Rafe's pacing stopped.

"From the mill?" he asked.

"From hauling at the mill that week, yes. He had his dinner pail and red dust on his cuffs. One of the bucket latches was loose and it clicked every time he walked. I can still hear it."

The sound had reached her before Eli did: that thin metal click and the heavier drag of his left foot when he was over-tired. Then the door had opened and the night air had come in behind him, wet and rank from the river.

"He saw Gabriel before he saw me," Della said. "Or maybe he saw us both at once. I don't know. Some things happen too fast for proper order. Gabriel turned and started talking at him in that same easy voice, like this was still a room where he could negotiate. Eli set the pail down hard enough to spill bean liquor across the boards. Gabriel took one step toward him with his hand half up, maybe to calm him, maybe to keep speaking. Eli told him to get out."

Della's left thumb cramped against the table edge. She pried it loose with the other hand.

"Gabriel laughed," she said.

Rafe closed his eyes once.

"That was the last stupid thing he did in this house. Eli hit him across the mouth. Not with a stick. Not with the poker. His hand. Hard enough to turn Gabriel but not, I think, hard enough to kill him by itself. Gabriel stepped back into the bean liquor. His heel went out from under him. The back of his head struck the hearth edge right there."

She pointed to the darker brick. No one followed the finger. No one needed to.

"He did not even get a curse out. Air left him like a bellows kicked through. Then there was blood at his ear and the back of his hair opened and his boots drummed once on the boards. I was on my knees before he quit moving. There was a little pink froth at the corner of his mouth. I wiped it once because hands do what they know in front of damage. Then I put my fingers against his throat and knew."

Ruthie looked down into her coffee. The skin on it had gone dull and brown.

Rafe said, "Eli killed him."

"Yes."

"And you buried Gabriel like fever dead."

"Yes."

"And told everybody Eli ran off."

"Yes."

The plainness of it made each answer sound like a blow laid down flat.

Rafe put both hands on the table and bent over it until his face was level with hers. "Why."

Della met him because she had forfeited the right to look away. "Because if Vernon Wolfe had found his brother dead in this kitchen with me standing over him and you asleep under my roof, he would have owned every word said after that. He would have made Gabriel into a churchgoing martyr and Eli into a brute before dawn. He would have had men looking for Eli with shotguns before the sheriff found his pants. He would have called me a whore and he would have called you bastard and there would not have been enough truth left in the room to feed on."

Rafe did not straighten.

"So I sent Eli off," she said. "I gave him the pay envelope in the sugar tin and two shirts tied in a feed sack and told him to take the south road before light. He stood right where you're standing now, blood on his knuckles and that loose bucket latch still clicking, and kept saying he had not meant it. I told him meaning had gone past. I told him if he loved his own hide he'd move it."

The memory came in pieces that had never stopped being sharp: Eli's broad face gone gray under sweat, the two stiff fingers on his right hand not closing all the way around the sack she'd thrust at him, the smell of blood under the smell of beans, the way the lamp glass smoked because no one had trimmed the wick.

"When he was gone," Della said, "I sat here with Gabriel till near dawn because I could not lift him alone and because every wagon noise on the road sounded like judgment. Then I got word to Silas. He came with his mule before sunup and did not ask me one question that would have forced me to lie worse. We wrapped Gabriel in a quilt from the chest you were born under and took him to the fever plot because the fever rows already held the county's forgetting. I thought if the grave stayed nameless, the rest of us might."

The dam sounded again. This time the kitchen windows gave a small answering shake. June sucked in a breath in the pantry, quick and involuntary.

Rafe heard it. His head turned toward the sound. "You may as well come out if you're listening."

June did not.

Ruthie said, very quiet, "Let her be."

Rafe stared at the pantry door a second longer, humiliation and fury crossing his face in such quick turns Della could not tell which one had won. When he looked back at her, the son was farther gone.

"So that's it," he said. "That's what I'm meant to make out of a day like today. My dead father is a Wolfe who never signed me. My living father was a man you shoved down the road with blood on him. And the name I carried all my life was the one part bought with panic."

"It kept you fed, named, and out of their hands."

"Fed."

He stood up so fast the chair tipped. It hit the floor and stayed there. "Fed? Mama, First State doesn't take blood in payment. They take money. They take four hundred eighty-six dollars on a truck note and a feed advance that should have been cleared before summer. McRae wants his seventy-three forty and writes it big enough my wife can see it from the counter. The dam pays me a dollar thirty-five an hour to help drown the only ground I ever worked, and now you hand me a father with orchard blood and not one scrap of orchard paper. Fed."

His voice had gone louder than the room liked. June made a small sound in the pantry, fear or surprise. Ruthie pushed herself off the stove and moved one step that way, not to shut the girl out but to put a body between doorways if she had to.

Della said, "I know what you owe."

"Do you know what I counted on?"

"Yes."

"No, you don't. You know what you stopped. That's not the same thing."

She might have argued if there had been any point in it. He was right. She knew the shape of the lie she had pulled from his hands. She did not know what private scaffolding he had built inside it just to keep upright.

Rafe dragged his hand down his face. Mud from his wrist streaked across his cheek. "If that grave had been Eli on paper, we had a chance. Maybe not a fine chance. Maybe not enough. But a chance. Condemnation money. The tract loose. Something to take to the bank besides work sores and apologies. Now what've I got? A story. A dirty story with a dead rich man in it, and every part that matters to money died before it reached a clerk."

"Yes," Della said.

He stared at her.

She kept her voice level because if she let pity into it he would hear insult. "Yes. That's what the truth amounts to in cash. Nothing. Gabriel never wrote you in. He never legitimated you. Eli is still not proved dead. The law doesn't grow a conscience because blood turns up late."

Rafe's lip curled. "Then why in God's name would you choose it now?"

"Because I wasn't going to let you bury one man under another man's name for a payout."

"A payout."

The word came out like spit.

"That's what this is," Della said. "Call it aid, call it compensation, call it the county taking with one hand and tipping pennies back with the other. It's still money under a dead man's name. And that dead man was Gabriel. I put him in the ground myself. I wasn't going to damn him again because the bank likes cleaner lies than families do."

Rafe took that in with a look she had seen on him only a few times: when the mule kicked through the barn wall, when June's fever broke too late to spare her hearing for three days, when the truck first coughed and died in town traffic and every man behind him leaned on his horn. A look not of surprise but of being cornered by fact.

"So you chose him over me."

"No."

"You chose his bones over my house."

"I chose not to make you live off a lie that would eat through everything it touched."

"We're already living off lies."

It was the nearest he had come to speaking the whole house at once. Ruthie's face changed a little at that. Not because he was wrong. Because he had finally said it aloud.

Della said, "Yes."

He barked another laugh. "You have one good use left for truth, don't you. You can always swing it late."

"Then hear the rest of it while it's still mine to say."

He made a bitter motion with one hand. "There's more."

"There always was."

Her mouth had gone dry. She reached for the coffee cup Ruthie had left nearest and found only the cold ring it had made on the wood. Ruthie handed her the cup without a word. Della drank coffee that had gone thick and burnt and let it scrape the back of her throat.

"Eli wasn't only the man who hit Gabriel," she said. "He was the man who knew for years and stayed anyway. He put your shoes on some mornings. He taught you to hold an ax away from your shin. He could be hard, and harder than that, and still he did those things. The blood doesn't unsay them. It doesn't clean him either. I won't clean him for you now."

Rafe's eyes narrowed. "Did he ever say it to me? Did he ever look at me and see it?"

Della thought of Eli sitting in the yard doorway after supper, whittling nothing, Rafe small at his boots with a bent nail and a string, both of them silent because silence had been the one language Eli always trusted. She thought of the rawer days too, the look that could come over Eli when Rafe laughed with his head back and showed a row of Wolfe teeth no one had named as such.

"He saw what he saw," she said. "Some days he loved you in the only ways he had. Some days he hated that he couldn't stop seeing Gabriel in your face. Both were true."

Rafe flinched like something had struck him from behind. It was a small movement and Della might have missed it if she had not delivered it herself.

"Christ," he said, and pressed the heel of his hand against his eye.

The kitchen went quiet except for the fly at the window and the wet settle of wood in the stove.

Ruthie said, still quiet, "Sit down before you fall down."

He turned on her. "Don't."

She did not retreat. Her voice lost what little softness it had carried. "Then quit swaying like a calf in spring mud. I'm not saying it to comfort you."

For one wild second Della thought he might snap at Ruthie harder than that, might choose the nearest body that loved him and spend himself there. Instead he gripped the table edge until his knuckles stood out pale beneath the grave dirt.

"What am I supposed to tell people?" he asked Della, but the question was smaller now, more dangerous for that. "What am I supposed to tell June when they start it? That I'm not my own father's son and the better one died on your floor trying to buy us off proper?"

"Tell them Gabriel Wolfe fathered you and died here."

"Like that helps."

"It doesn't help."

"Then tell me the part that does."

Della looked up at him fully. The room had reached the place where mercy would only be one more form of lying. "There isn't one."

Rafe stood very still.

Ruthie shut her eyes once. Not in prayer. In the weary acknowledgement of a nail driven all the way through.

Della said, "The only useful thing here is that it's true, and truth isn't always useful before it's necessary."

"That sounds like something people with food say."

"Then hear it plainer. This won't save the truck. It won't settle the bank. It won't bring Eli's death onto paper and it won't turn Vernon generous. I know that. I'm still saying it because I wouldn't have you build your house on a dead man I knew by touch."

Rafe pushed away from the table. He stood with both hands at his hips, breathing through his mouth as if the room had gone too small to air him. Then he reached into his shirt pocket and took out a folded yellow paper, soft at the creases from being worried between his fingers.

Della knew it before he opened it. The duplicate of the affidavit. His careful signature sat near the bottom in cramped, deliberate letters because he wrote slowly and hated to have his name look ignorant on county stock.

"I carried this all day," he said. "You know that? Since Larkin. Since before. I kept going over the lines because I wanted them straight if anybody ever saw my copy. Eli Bledsoe. Identified by son. That was the one thing I had that looked like a way through."

The paper shook once in his hand. Not much. Enough.

"Rafe," Della said.

"No. You don't get to say my name gentle now."

He went to the stove, jerked the iron door open, and fed one corner of the yellow sheet into the red seam where the wood had burned down. It smoked but did not catch at once. He snatched the paper back, struck a match off the box with more force than skill, and held the flame under his own signature until it blackened and curled.

The smell hit the room sharp and chemical, ink and paper and county sizing gone to scorch. Rafe watched until the fire bit deep enough to take from him and then dropped the sheet onto the coals. It flared blue for a blink, then orange. His name went first. Eli's held longer. The witness line shrank into a black ribbon and folded in on itself.

"There," he said. "The bank won't take smoke either, but that's that."

Della did not rise. If she had, he would have thought she meant to stop him, and she had no right to stop even this.

He shut the stove door hard. The latch snapped. For a second the kitchen smelled exactly as it had that night long ago: heat, iron, something burnt that had been meant for record and was now only ash.

Ruthie said, "Rafe."

He turned toward the door.

"Rafe," she said again, and there was a whole marriage in the single word, all the mending and the late bills and the bed they still climbed into side by side despite the world. "Don't go taking this out where men can use it against you worse."

"It's already out."

"I mean tonight."

He got his hand on the latch. Della could see the muscles working in his jaw, could see he was holding himself together by anger alone and that anger was running thin at the edges.

"If you want me in this house by morning," he said without looking back, "don't ask me to bless any of it before then."

The latch lifted. Night air slipped in, damp and green from the slope below. Cicadas rasped in the dark. Somewhere farther down Cedar Bench a dog barked once and stopped.

Della said his name one more time.

He did not answer. He went out and shut the door with care so sharp it hurt more than a slam would have.

For a while nobody moved. The stove gave one little settling thud as the burning paper collapsed in on itself.

Then the pantry door opened.

June came out holding the pencil she had meant to use on the peach jars. The side of her middle finger was smudged blue where the lead had rubbed. She had taken off her shoes at some point so the boards would not tell on her. Her face had gone past childish fear into something harder to name and older to wear.

"I know I wasn't meant to hear," she said.

Ruthie let out a breath through her nose. "Well, you did."

June looked at Della, not at Ruthie. She always did that when she wanted the fact and not the smoothing. "Was the man in the fever plot there because he died in this room?"

Della felt the question land in the same place the older blows had landed. Not because it was cruel. Because it was exact.

"Yes," she said.

June nodded once. "And Papa tried to call him Eli so we could get the land money."

"Yes."

June's grip tightened on the pencil. The wood gave a soft crack where she'd worried it too hard with her thumb. "And Gabriel Wolfe was Papa's father."

"Yes."

Ruthie moved to the table and righted the fallen chair. She set it back on all four legs with the same care she'd use on a broken plate if there was any chance the break could be hidden from daily use. "That's enough for tonight."

June did not move. "Did you hide Papa too?"

Ruthie looked up sharply. Della did not.

The stove still held the yellow paper in fire. Through the iron you could smell the last of it turning over into ash.

"Yes," Della said after a moment. "I hid a dead man in the fever plot, and I hid your father inside another man's name. That's what you heard."

June's eyes shone once in the lamplight but no tears fell. She was too intent on the shape of the thing to spend herself crying yet.

"Can a name do that?" she asked.

Della looked at the darkened hearth brick, then at the stove door where Rafe had burned his way out of one lie and into a hurt that would not feed him either. "It can if everybody around it agrees to carry it."

Ruthie touched June's shoulder. "Bed."

June let herself be turned this time, though halfway to the doorway she stopped and looked back at the stove. "Will Papa still be Papa?"

There was no comfort in the truthful answer, only scale.

"Yes," Della said. "And he'll be hurt."

June took that in and went with Ruthie at last, barefoot, pencil still in hand, as if she had not yet decided whether what she knew now wanted writing down or burying.

When they were gone, Della opened the stove and nudged the paper's curled remains with the poker. The affidavit had gone to a frail black lace. Her son's careful signature broke apart at the first touch and floated up the flue.

She shut the door and sat back down at the table where Gabriel had bargained, where Eli had struck, where Rafe had stood in his wet shirt and counted what the truth would not buy. The kitchen had not kept any of them. It had only held the sound.

Outside, Rafe's steps had long since disappeared into the dark, but Della could still feel where the house had given him up.
