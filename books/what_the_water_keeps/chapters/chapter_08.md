# Chapter 8

Only after June took her school reader to the porch and the supper plates were stacked did Della hold herself to what she had said at the cemetery, and by then she could feel how completely the place had followed them home. Red clay dried on the floorboards in the shape of their boots, and the air inside carried lye soap, lamp smoke, and the faint sweet-sour rot that clung to cloth after a day among opened graves. Ruthie had scrubbed the kitchen table twice before supper and once again after, but the place still knew where they had been.

Then Della took the lamp and went down the cellar steps, feeling with her bad thumb for the loose edge of the third tread from the bottom. The board came up with a little dry complaint. Under it, wrapped in oilcloth gone slick with old kitchen grease, lay the ledger.

It looked too ordinary every time she touched it. A brown-backed notebook no wider than her hand, corners blunted, spine repaired with strip after strip of flour-paste paper. Nothing about it warned what it could do in a room where money had got shorter than mercy.

She brought it up and set it on the table where the lamp made the cover shine. Outside, cicadas had started their hard metal racket in the trees below the bench. Farther downriver, a dull concussion rolled up from the dam works and moved through the dishes on the shelf in a small clatter.

Della opened the book.

The first pages were older and written in the tighter hand she had used when she still trusted herself to record a thing once and leave it there. Births in kitchens, births in smokehouses, births in the back room of McRae's when a woman had not made it home in time. Boys born blue and rubbed until they pinked. Girls who lived three minutes and were wrapped in flour sacking before older children woke. A marriage carried in a widow's front room with two cousins standing witness because the first husband had run off to Carolina, and the law had not bothered to lose him fast enough. A woman delivered of her fourth child with one eye purple and shutting, the husband named nowhere because naming him would have sent him to jail for a week and sent her home to starve after. Fever burials in the lower plot, dusk work, no preacher, Silas with the shovel and her with the notebook because somebody had to know where the bodies went if the markers rotted off.

Some pages had corrections written over corrections, a life trying on names until one would hold. Some had none at all, only a date and a mother's first name and the weight of what had been asked of Della before dawn. The book held girls saved from disgrace and girls handed back to it with a prettier lie. It held children placed with aunts, cousins, older sisters, women who could lactate and keep their mouths shut. It held men dead in all but paperwork. It held the price of a county that preferred clean ledgers to clean hands.

And it held Gabriel.

His name sat in a page she had not turned to yet, but she felt it waiting there the way a bad tooth waited under the tongue. It was not only Gabriel she felt waiting there. Eli waited too, in the blank left behind when one man died on her kitchen floor and another ran before morning. Rafe's whole life lay in the seam between those two names, and if she split it now there was no telling what would come spilling out besides truth.

She heard Ruthie at the doorway before she looked up. Ruthie did not drift through a room. She entered it the way she did everything else, direct and already carrying work. Tonight it was one of Rafe's shirts over her wrist and the smooth wooden darning egg she kept tucked in the pocket of her apron. She saved pearl buttons from ruined blouses in a Mason jar above the stove, and one of them shone wrong and pretty on the cuff she had just repaired.

"If you're going to sit up with that thing," Ruthie said, "trim the wick first. It's smoking."

Della pinched the lamp knob and cut the flame down. "June outside?"

"For now."

Ruthie came in and laid the shirt flat over the chair back. She did not ask what the book was. She had known there was a book for years, if not what all of it carried. A woman who ran a house on twelve different kinds of not-enough did not miss a hidden object simply because nobody named it for her.

"They talked ugly today," Della said.

"They did."

Ruthie stood with one hand on the chair and looked at the open pages. Her face was not hot with anger. That would have been simpler. She had the pinched-in look she wore over the wash ledger on Saturday nights, mouth pressed thin while she decided which want would miss another week.

"At the spring," she said, "one of the Pike girls asked June whether she ought to answer when somebody called Bledsoe, or if you had another name folded up for her under the floorboards."

Della shut the ledger halfway, too late to make a difference.

"What'd June say?"

"She said her name same as she always has, and then she came home and scrubbed the dipper with sand like the thing had dirtied itself touching that girl's hand."

Della pictured June on the flat stone by the pump, skinny knees, school shoes gone white at the toes, grinding the tin lip of the dipper with sand until the metal squealed. Shame had a sound to it if you listened close enough. She had heard it from girls in labor, from widows at the courthouse, from boys told whose blood they were and whose they were not. Now it had reached her own porch.

"Children get their meanness borrowed," Della said.

"Borrowed from where?" Ruthie asked. "From the graveyard? From church? From my own kitchen? I don't much care whose mouth started it. It's in my girl's ears now."

Della slid the book away from the edge of the table. "I know that."

"Do you."

The words were plain enough, but they came down with the same thrift Ruthie used at the cloth table, cutting right up to the selvage and leaving no strip to hide in. Della had seen women shout and mean less than Ruthie did with four bare syllables.

"You think because you meant well once," Ruthie said, "that the meaning keeps doing the work. It doesn't. What you kept to spare people back then is what folks are using on us now."

"Us," Della repeated.

"Yes, us. Me. June. Rafe, whether he deserves ease or not." Ruthie took the shirt back up and worried the patched cuff through her fingers. "You get to decide alone, and the rest of us get the talk after."

Della leaned back and felt the chair complain under her. "Alone was what there was most nights. No judge. No clerk. No man worth asking. A woman bleeding and me with a basin and whatever name might keep the child fed."

"I know what the nights were," Ruthie said. "I'm not calling you cruel."

She said it in a way that let Della hear the rest without its being spoken. Ruthie was not calling her cruel. Ruthie was not calling her clean either.

Ruthie set the shirt down again. "But this house isn't one of those nights. You don't get to keep doing the whole thing by yourself because it suits the way you've got used to being necessary."

The sentence hit harder than a louder one would have. Della fixed on the ledger's flour-paste spine so she would not have to meet Ruthie standing there with the darning egg in her hand like a small polished bone.

"If I hand all this to Miss Holt," Della said, "I don't just settle graves. I turn loose every shame a woman ever trusted me to hold. Half the county would use it like a corn knife. Some of the names in here belong to living people who have built thirty years on top of what I wrote."

"Then don't hand her all of it."

Della's thumb stalled on the rough page edge.

Ruthie met her square. "That's what I've been trying to tell you."

"Selective truth still leaves teeth marks."

"So does secrecy. We've been chewing on that one for years."

Outside, June turned a page on the porch. The thin paper made a dry insect sound. Della had the sudden, sharp memory of June at seven with chicken pox scabbed over her ribs, solemnly asking whether scratching made the spots deeper. Some harms did. Some only drew blood where it itched already.

"You think there's a middle path," Della said.

"I think you've been pretending there isn't because if there is, you'd have to let somebody else stand on it with you." Ruthie pulled out the chair opposite and sat at last. "Give the county what touches the dead and what settles the present claims. Keep the rest in your own stove if you want. But stop acting like your choices are lock it up or burn it."

Della ran a finger down the edge of the paper. Her left thumb would not bend proper after long days with shovels and coffin handles, so the nail scraped a faint rough track. "You don't know what all is in here."

"No," Ruthie said. "I don't. That's the point."

The lamp hissed low between them. Heat sat heavy in the kitchen even with the back door propped open, and a smell of wet earth kept coming off the dark. Lucille's dry official fingers seemed already in the room, lifting pages by their corners one by one. The stove mouth offered its own answer: paper curling black among potato peelings and busted chair legs. Under the stair lay the third answer, waiting in the dark while Rafe strained harder at her and the valley kept making stories meaner than the truth.

Ruthie watched her a moment longer, thumb stroking the worn place on the darning egg, and then said, "This isn't only about the fever-plot man, is it."

Della said nothing.

Ruthie's jaw shifted once. "It's blood."

Still Della said nothing.

"And naming," Ruthie went on. "Blood and naming both. I don't need the whole tale to know that much."

Della felt the room draw in around that sentence. Ruthie had not guessed the shape of it before tonight, not all the way. Now she had enough to carry her forward into the damage, which made Della want to slam the book shut and hide it again, because knowledge moved like creek water once it found a cut bank.

"Some things can't be untold cleanly," she said.

"Nothing in this house is cleanly anything," Ruthie said. "Look at my hands."

She held them up. The knuckles were reddened from lye and line water, one finger nicked where she'd slipped trimming a frayed collar, the nails kept short because long nails tore cloth. Those hands had washed their dead grandmothers' sheets and scrubbed June's blood from her first menses rags and patched Rafe's work shirts until the original cloth lived mostly as memory between seams.

"I know exactly what living with a mess looks like," Ruthie said. "What I don't know is why I ought to keep living blind inside it because you decided once, forty years ago maybe, that nobody else could bear what you were bearing."

Della opened her mouth and shut it again. There were answers. There had always been answers. Men with deeds. Men with fists. Preachers who called one child legitimate and another a warning. A county that found a bastard useful as labor but not as heir. All of that was true. None of it changed the fact that Ruthie was sitting across from her now with June sleeping one room over and Rafe somewhere down at the shed or the road, and the old way of holding silence had started costing the wrong people.

June appeared at the doorway with her reader hugged to her stomach before either of them heard the porch board creak. She had her tobacco tin of pencil stubs in the other hand, the one she lined with cotton batting so the sharpened points would not snap against each other. Her hair had worked loose in the damp and curled at the temples.

"I can come back," she said.

"No," Ruthie said.

Della and June looked at her.

Ruthie pushed back from the table and stood. "No. You can both stop sending each other out of rooms as if that fixes anything. If this house is going to get judged for a book, then the least the house can do is know what kind of book it is."

June's eyes went to Della, not pleading, not defiant, but measuring. "I don't want the gossip version," she said. "I want the real version."

"You're fifteen," Della said.

"I'm old enough to get laughed at for it."

That landed because it was true and because June had not raised her voice to say it. She crossed to the table and set her reader aside. The pencil tin clicked softly against the wood.

"I don't mean tell me everybody's sins," she said. "I mean teach me what makes a record true. Teach me what you're looking for when you turn a page. Everybody keeps saying stay out of it, but I'm already in it."

Across the table Ruthie stayed put, giving back no nod, no rescue, only the fact of her being there.

"Sit down," Della said at last.

June sat.

Della pulled a stack of scrap paper from the shelf, the backs of old feed circulars and a few clean sheets Ruthie had saved from a church flyer misprint. She laid them beside the ledger. "If we're doing this, we do it narrow. Grave matters. Current claims. Nothing that gives the county a free peek into living people's beds."

June nodded once, serious as a clerk.

"And you write it the way I say it," Della told her. "Not prettier. Not plainer. Word for word."

"Yes, ma'am."

Ruthie moved to the stove and set water to heating though nobody had asked for coffee. It was what she did when a room needed another task in it to keep from turning theatrical. A moth battered itself stupid against the lamp chimney. June set a pencil point to paper and waited.

Della opened the ledger to Sadie Kincaid's page. "First line: Sadie Kincaid. Buried under second husband's marker, but first lawful marriage never dissolved in county book. Witnesses to household use of Kincaid name: Alma Kincaid, Boyd sister, and Reverend Teague's wife."

June wrote in a neat, upright hand. "Do I put household use before or after lawful marriage?"

"Before. That's what put food in the cupboard."

June wrote that too, then glanced up. "Not those words."

Della almost smiled. "No. Not those words."

They worked by category first, then by name. Burials where the stone and the church record did not match. Infant graves whose parents had used one name in public and another inside the house. A widow's brother listed as next of kin because the man she'd lived with for eleven years had never got round to making it legal before a mule kicked him in the chest. Fever-row burials where Della and Silas were the only two still living who knew which board covered which bones.

Some entries she copied whole. Some she cut to the part the grave required and no more. She left out the fourteen-year-old delivered in a tobacco barn with her own father drunk against the outer wall and cussing the weather. She left out the girl sent north to Knoxville with milk still leaking through her dress because her baby had gone to an aunt who could pass him off as late-born. She left out the wife who'd asked Della to write her husband's name false because the true man was a deputy with church standing and a fondness for dark sheds. Truth might have been truth there too, but it was not grave truth, and the dead did not need those women dragged up beside them to satisfy a courthouse.

June noticed what got skipped.

"Why not that one?" she asked once, tapping a page Della had turned past.

"Because the body in question is still washing dishes in her own kitchen," Della said.

June absorbed that. "So not every true thing belongs to a file."

"Most of them don't."

"Then why write them at all?"

Della laid two fingers on the page where the pencil had once bitten hard. "Because sometimes a woman needed one other person on earth to know what happened to her."

June's pencil hovered a beat above the paper, then went back down. That was enough. Della was grateful for the girl's restraint and ashamed of needing it.

When Rafe came in, it was close to ten and full dark outside. He had looped baler twine around his wrist again, two short lengths saved because no usable cord ever quite counted as trash to him. His shirt stuck to his back in the damp and his chin scar looked white against the rest of his face.

He stopped in the doorway when he saw the spread on the table. "Well."

Nobody answered right off.

Rafe gave a little laugh with no amusement in it. "Now it's a school lesson."

"It's work," Ruthie said from the stove.

"Looks like more paper to me."

Della kept her hand on the ledger. "We're sorting grave facts."

"You found a sudden taste for sorting."

June bent lower over her page, but Della saw the set of her shoulders change.

"Enough," Ruthie said.

Rafe's eyes flicked to June, then back to Della. "You trusting the child with it now?"

"I'm teaching her how to copy what concerns the dead," Della said.

"That's generous."

There it was again, that laugh with hunger under it. Everything in him came back to hunger now if you left it long enough. Money hunger, respect hunger, the old child's hunger of wanting the one thing in the room his mother would not hand over.

"If you have something to say, say it plain," Della told him.

Rafe thumbed the twine on his wrist until it tightened and cut the skin. "Plain is this: every day this mess sits here, somebody else gets to the courthouse first."

"Courthouse for what?" June asked before she could stop herself.

Rafe looked at her, then away. "Nothing you need."

"That's enough from both of you," Ruthie said, sharper now.

He put his cap on the peg by the door. "No, go on. She's learning, isn't she."

Della let silence answer him. It was the only answer she trusted not to blow wider. After a moment he spat into the sink, ran water over his face and neck, and went through to the back room without asking what was on the pages. The restraint meant less comfort than curiosity would have. She listened to his boots hit the floor once, twice, then stop.

June breathed out slowly. "Should I keep writing?"

"Yes," Della said.

So they did.

By midnight the lamp had burned low enough to show every mote in the room. Ruthie took over trimming the wick and brought them reheated coffee thin as ditch water but hot. June's script stayed neat longer than Della's own hands could have managed. Della dictated the settled parts and held back the rest. When they came near the page she dreaded, she closed the book and rubbed her thumb into the heel of her other hand until the joint gave a small hot pulse.

"Not that one?" June asked.

"Not tonight."

Ruthie, darning now by habit more than necessity, did not look up. "Because it isn't grave business, or because it's too much your own?"

Della hated how precisely Ruthie could lay the blade in. "Both."

"Then at least you know the difference."

They went on. The copy pages filled. June sanded the last line because she hated a wet smear, then blew the grit off too hard and got it on her own cheek. Della reached over without thinking and brushed it away with the side of her finger. June was still all bone and growth under the skin, but the child's face kept slipping from her a little more every month. Soon enough somebody would hand the girl a marriage line and expect her to step inside it. Della felt a cold dislike of the future so cleanly it might have been a taste.

When the church clock from down the road struck one, Ruthie stood and rolled her shoulders. "That's all for tonight."

June blinked at the stack of copied sheets. "There are more."

"There are always more," Ruthie said. "Go wash your face."

June gathered her pencil stubs back into the tin one by one. She never dropped them loose. Then she looked at Della. "Am I helping for true, or just being kept busy?"

It was the kind of question only June asked, the one that stripped the dodge off a thing and left its bones showing.

"For true," Della said.

June nodded, satisfied only because she had chosen to be. She took her reader and the tin and went to bed, bare feet whispering over the boards.

Ruthie stacked the copied pages and squared them against the table. "Put those somewhere separate."

"I know."

"Do you."

Della gave her a look. Ruthie gave one back that said she had earned the right tonight. After a moment Della slid the copied pages into a clean flour sack and tied the mouth with string. Those could go under the mattress tick till morning. They were not safe outright, but they were safe enough for one night if the house held.

Ruthie watched. "And the original?"

"Back where it was."

"If somebody's been watching you use it, that won't matter."

"If somebody's been watching this house that close, we have more trouble than a hiding place."

Ruthie did not argue further. She took the shirt she'd been mending and the darning egg and paused at the doorway.

"I meant what I said," she told Della. "You don't get to be the whole committee anymore."

"There never was a committee."

"Then make one out of whoever's still standing in this kitchen."

After she had gone, Della sat a minute longer alone with the ledger open beneath the lamp. The pages had swollen a little from years of kitchen damp. Names rose under her fingertips where the pencil had bitten hard. She turned at last to Gabriel's page.

There he was, plain enough in her own hand to damn her for having called any of it uncertainty. Gabriel Wolfe. Lower fever row, east of the broken cedar, man about thirty-eight years, old healed break at left collar, upper left premolar missing, buried without church word. She had written it the morning after because she had known memory could fail, and because a body deserved at least one place where it was not lost.

Her pen hovered over the clean paper beside the copy stack. If she made Gabriel into a second page, the thing would not stay a grave note for long. It would go from her hand to Lucille's desk or Rafe's fist, and once it did the Bledsoe claim would shrivel where it stood. No death proof for Eli. No county money worked loose from the knot of missing paper. Just Gabriel Wolfe set down in black marks, with all the rest of it crowding after close behind: whose blood Rafe carried, why Eli had run, which side of the valley had eaten well on one man's disappearance. Truth, yes. Also a foreclosure notice written slower.

She lowered the nib till it touched the sheet. A dark bead spread into the paper and sat there. Della watched it widen, then turned the blank page facedown under the stack before the ink could dry enough to read as a letter. Gabriel stayed where he had been all these years, in the old book and in her hand alone.

She wrapped the ledger back in its oilcloth, carried it down the dark cellar stairs, and knelt to slide it under the loose tread again. Potato dirt cooled the air below. A jar of beans knocked lightly against another when she shifted her weight. For a second she had the ridiculous thought that the book looked small enough there to be harmless. Then she set the board back in place and knew better.

Upstairs, the house had gone still in layers. Ruthie's breathing through the wall, light and wakeful even in sleep. June turning once on the rope bed in the front room. Rafe not making any sound at all from the shed room off the porch. Della slid the flour-sack bundle of copied pages under her mattress tick and lay down on top of them.

Sleep came in scraps.

Once she woke because a truck changed gears down on the river road and the sound climbed the valley like a groan. Once because the dam gave one of its far blows before dawn and the window glass ticked in the frame. Once because she was certain she had heard the kitchen floor speak under a careful foot, but by the time she held her breath hard enough to listen, all she had was cicadas and the roof settling out heat. A house full of family made every night noise half innocent. That was its protection and its danger both.

Gray light had just started worrying at the window when she gave up on sleep. Her back felt as if somebody had wedged a shovel handle crosswise between the bones. She checked the copy bundle first by habit, hand sliding under the mattress tick. Still there. Paper, string, the square edges she had counted on.

Then she went to the cellar.

The third tread came up too easy.

Della stared at the dark slot under it without understanding for one full second, because the mind liked to offer small excuses before it accepted a larger wound. She thought she had the wrong step. She thought the bundle had shifted backward. She thought the oilcloth had darkened and blended into shadow.

Then she put her whole hand into the space and felt only splinters, dust, and one cold nail head.

The ledger was gone.

She searched the rest of the stairs anyway, then the shelves, then behind the crocks where no sensible person would have moved it. Her breath grew loud in the low cellar. Potatoes rolled in their bin when she struck it with her shin. She knew better than to search like a fool, but a fool's search was what came first when something irreplaceable had just been lifted out of the world. Nothing in that cellar gave it back to her.

By the time she climbed back up, the kitchen had light enough to show every mark the night had left. A damp crescent from the coffee pot on the table. Pencil grit glittering near the lamp base where June had missed a pinch. One of Rafe's boots absent from the door mat, the other kicked under the bench. The back door shut but not latched.

Ruthie was already outside at the line in her slip and work shoes, pinning out a rinsed sheet with the same severe little thumb-and-forefinger pinch she used on every clothespin. June still slept, or pretended to. The shed room door stood open to the porch, empty to Della's glance from where she stood.

Any of it might have meant something. Any of it might have meant nothing. That was the misery of a house. Ordinary movements came furnished with their own excuses.

Ruthie turned at the sight of her. "You're up early."

Della heard how flat her own voice came out and knew Ruthie would hear it too. "The book's gone."

Ruthie's hand stopped on the line. The wet sheet sagged between two pins. "Gone where?"

"It's gone."

Ruthie came in without another word, leaving the sheet to drip on the yard. Together they looked through the kitchen, the flour bin, the pie safe, the warming closet, places the ledger had never lived and had no reason to be. June came to the doorway in her nightdress, hair flattened on one side, and understood from the air before anybody told her.

"What happened?" she asked.

"Go put some clothes on," Ruthie said.

June did not move. "Was it the pages?"

"No," Della said. "Not the copies."

June looked from her to Ruthie, tallying what had been said and what hadn't. "The other one."

Della nodded.

June swallowed. "Did Daddy know where you put it?"

Ruthie cut her eyes toward the girl. "That's enough."

But the question stayed in the room after she said it, as real as the lamp, as ugly as the damp footprints drying by the door.

Della went back to the cellar once more and pressed her palm into the empty slot under the step as if the wood itself might confess. Forty years she had carried that book as a private authority, a little paper kingdom nobody could raid without going through her first. Now some other hand had it. Some other eye could turn those pages at a table, in a truck cab, in the schoolhouse office, wherever people went when they meant to make use of a secret.

Up in the yard the day was beginning. Chickens scratched under the currant bush. Another low blast rolled from the dam and shivered the jars on the cellar shelf. Before breakfast she would have half the cellar and pantry turned out in the kitchen, flour bin dumped into a washpan, peach jars lined on the step, still pretending a hard enough search could call the ledger back. By breakfast time the valley would be fully awake, and somewhere inside it her book would be awake too, opening under hands that had not earned what it knew.
