# Chapter 13

The rain had quit before dawn, but the hillside kept moving after it, giving little sighs underfoot and settling the whole graveyard deeper into the mountain. By the time Della came back up the path from Cedar Bench, the lower end of Old Sion was already ringed with people who had no work there. They stood under cloud-torn light with their collars up and their Sunday curiosity on their faces, waiting to see whether the exposed coffin in the fever plot belonged to a Bledsoe, a Wolfe, or just another patch of old trouble the valley could worry to death before noon.

The dam sounded once downriver, a low concussion that came through the wet air more than through the ear. It shook water from the cedar branches and sent a thin rill of clay sliding across the path. Della put one hand against the fencepost until the tremor finished moving through the ground. Her left thumb had swollen stiff in the night. It would not bend cleanly around the shovel handle now. Her lower back felt packed with broken crockery. She had slept three hours at most, and none of them deeply enough to make her forget what the rain had done.

The landslip had bitten farther into the bank. Last night only the lid had shown through the clay. This morning half the box lay naked to the air, pitched crooked from the hill with one end buried and the other hanging out over the wash in a half-risen lurch. The lid was sprung along one seam. A cedar root as thick as Della's wrist had shouldered through the clay just above it and left the whole slope looking pried open by something patient and stronger than any of them.

Lucille Holt stood near the gap with her skirt pinned two inches off the mud by black office clips she must have stolen from her own desk. She had wrapped bread sacks over her sensible shoes and tied them at the ankles with string, a losing trick she kept trying anyway every wet morning of the move. Her hair had started to come loose at one temple. She kept smoothing it back with the knuckle of the hand that held her pencil, trying to pin composure back in place by habit.

Rafe was already there, shoulders dark with dam mud, his work shirt dried to his back in tide lines of sweat and red clay. He had come straight from a dawn shift or never gone home from the night before; Della could not tell which. Either way, the blast dirt of the project was on him. It sat in the seams of his neck and the scar at his chin. The river works had already started claiming him in their own color. He was talking too fast at Lucille, one hand out toward the exposed coffin, the other closing and opening on nothing.

Vernon Wolfe stood a little apart from the mud, not because there was less of it under his polished shoes but because he carried himself with the expectation that mud ought to stop short of him. He had come in a black coat too heavy for the season, with a clean white handkerchief folded like a church program in his breast pocket. Every time a shovel splashed nearby, his fingers went to that pocket, checking that the square of linen still existed. Noreen Acker had planted herself on the other side of the opening in a dress whose cuffs had been turned twice to hide old wear. She held a peppermint in one cheek and bit it now and then, not for sweetness, Della thought, but to keep from speaking before she chose her word.

Silas was not yet in sight. That worried Della more than she liked.

Lucille saw her first. Relief crossed her face so quickly it looked like irritation. "Mrs. Bledsoe. I was about to send for you."

"You don't need to," Della said. "I'm here."

Rafe turned. Whatever he had meant to say to Lucille broke off and came at Della instead.

"Good. Then say it and let's be done. Everybody can see what this is now. It's washed half out already."

His voice had the scraped edge it took when he had been awake too long and thinking about money. Della could hear the bank note in it, the mercantile tab, the gas for the truck he could barely keep running, the fresh dam cash burning a hole through the shame of earning it. There were days lately when everything he said seemed to come through a layer of figures.

Vernon lifted a hand before she answered, palm outward in the old deacon's gesture that expected the whole scene to obey.

"What everybody can see," he said, "is that a grave has been disturbed by weather and ought to be secured until the hearing can examine the matter properly. This crowd is not a court. We do not need a spectacle."

Noreen rolled the peppermint against her teeth. "Decency always comes on strong when a Wolfe wants a lid kept shut."

He did not look at her. "This is not about my family."

"Then you won't mind if it opens."

Lucille cleared her throat. She had her pencil ready but had not yet found the sentence she wanted to live inside. "The exposed remains do have to be addressed today, Mr. Wolfe. I can't leave a coffin hanging in a slide until Thursday. The slope isn't stable, and county instruction is to relocate opened graves as they surface."

"Relocate, yes," Vernon said. "Identify, no. There is a difference between preserving order and indulging rumor."

Rafe gave a hard little laugh with nothing in it but fatigue. "Rumor? My father vanished when I was still a boy. You know damn well what it means if he's here."

"Your affidavit says what you hope it means," Noreen said. "That doesn't make bone read."

He swung toward her. "And you don't get to talk like this ain't your business. You want a Wolfe under there because you think it'll shake money loose."

"I want the right dead man named," she said. "That would be a start."

The crowd made a pressure sound, the way chickens do when they shift together in a roost. Nobody wanted peace. They wanted a name to carry downhill. Della could feel it in the way people leaned without seeming to move, in the hush they kept letting fall and then picking back up. The air smelled of split clay, wet cedar bark, and the sweet rotten breath coming off old coffin wood. If they waited for Thursday, the hearing room would take that smell out of it. Men like Vernon did better once the body was gone and only nouns were left.

Lucille turned toward Della with official caution arranged over plain need. "Mrs. Bledsoe, before I take any step here, I need to ask whether you believe this grave can be identified on site by lawful witness."

Lucille was not offering comfort. She was opening the only lawful minute left that still belonged to Della.

Della looked down the slope at the box she had helped hide all those years before. The cedar above it had been no thicker than a broom handle then. The dirt had been cold enough to burn her fingers, though the hard weather had only just begun. Gabriel had still been warm in the hollows where the blanket held his body close. Eli's boot marks had gone black in the lantern light where he ran downhill and did not come back. Silas had dug without talking because if either of them started then, the whole night would have split.

If county hands tarped the opening and hauled the coffin away with the lid shut, Rafe's affidavit would walk into Thursday's hearing first. Vernon would have his voice and his name and the room full of people who liked a legal story better than a dirty one. Lucille would do what she could, but county procedure had a way of turning hesitation into fact. Delay had stopped being mercy. Delay was just another man choosing for her.

She stepped past Lucille and started down the soft cut of the slope.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," Vernon said sharply. "I object to this."

"You can object while you hand me that pry bar," Della said without turning. "I'm not leaving him half out of the bank for crows and rain to finish."

The words went through the crowd so hard and clean that even Rafe stopped moving for a second. Della felt every eye land on the back of her neck. She kept going, planting her boots where the roots held and not where the clay shone slick. The bank gave a little under her weight. Mud slid into her shoes cold as creek water. When she reached the coffin she crouched slowly, because fast would have put her flat on her face, and set her palm against the lid.

The wood was soft enough to dent under two fingers.

Lucille called down, "Do you need the county men?"

"I need hands that'll listen," Della said. "Not boys showing off their backs. Rafe. Get down here. And if Mr. Wolfe's got objections left over, he can bring a plank and make himself useful."

A few people laughed before they caught themselves. Vernon colored under the clean shave of his cheeks and did not move. Rafe came because not coming would have looked worse. He slid the last few feet and landed in the muck beside her with a curse he did not bother to lower. Up close the dam had put a different wear on him already. The tendons in his forearms stood out hard as baling wire. There was cement dust at one cuff, pale as old flour, mixed with the valley's clay.

"You don't have to put on a show," he muttered.

"Then stop making one."

He looked at her, breathing through his mouth, anger and want and fatigue all mashed together until none of them sat clean. She held his gaze long enough to make him take the plank from a worker above and wedge it under the coffin's exposed corner.

By then Silas Wynn was coming down the upper path with his long frame bent against the grade. He carried his narrow spade in one hand and stopped twice to cough into a folded red handkerchief before he reached them. Quarry dust had lived in his lungs so long it seemed part of his voice now. He wore the same felt hat he always wore in weather, brim gone soft enough to sag over one eye when it rained. He straightened only when he got a clear look at the grave.

"Well," he said. "Hill's done the choosing for us."

"About time you got here," Della said.

"I move slower when I plan on leaving with the same breath I brought."

That earned another nervous stir from the crowd. Silas set the spade down, knelt with more care than pride, and touched the soil just above the coffin line. His fingers were gray in the creases from stone work and old dust no scrubbing ever fully removed. He did not look at Della when he said, "You want it up, we do it now. Next rain takes the rest of that bank."

Lucille had come close enough to hear. "I need witness names if this proceeds."

Silas glanced at her. "You'll have mine."

Rafe shoved both hands into the muck under the plank and levered upward. The coffin shifted with a damp sucking sound that made half the people on the ridge step back at once. Della heard somebody whisper Lord have mercy. Somebody else said hush, but not because they were praying. They did not want to miss anything.

"Easy," Della said. "Don't break what's still holding."

They cut around the buried end with spade and trowel and hands. Della used her pocketknife where the root had hooked into the bank, sawing fibers slick with rain and grave water. Her thumb screamed each time she pressed. She kept pressing. Clay packed under her nails and worked into the cracked skin of her knuckles. Once the slope shifted and sent a wash of cold muck down the back of her skirt. She did not look up to see who had flinched. Above them Lucille kept naming what she saw in the clerk's manner that steadied her when feeling tried to come through: exposed adult coffin, lower fever plot, witnesses present, weather damage, immediate relocation necessity. The pencil moved fast. Procedure, Della thought, was just one more kind of shovel. It could bury or uncover depending on who held it.

When they had enough of the box free to work it, Silas slid the pry bar under the sprung seam and nodded for Rafe to brace the side. The wood answered with the long wet groan of something giving up after years of patient collapse.

Della smelled it before the lid came loose: not flesh anymore, that was too long gone, but the trapped cellar smell of cloth rot, damp wood, mineral water, and the sickly sweetness of old varnish melting back into earth. It hit the hot back of her throat and pulled the past up with it. For one ugly second she was in her kitchen again with lamp smoke on the rafters and Gabriel on the floor under the table edge, blood gone black where it soaked into the grain around a chair leg. She saw his shirt stuck to the wound each time he tried to breathe. She heard Eli's breathing louder than Gabriel's, louder than hers, dragging the whole room into the hard cage of his chest. Then Silas had been at the door, and there had not been room in the night for grief or law, only for what a body weighed once it had stopped helping.

She swallowed the taste back down and lifted her chin.

"Take it slow," she said. "Let it open where it wants."

The lid peeled upward in sections. One board split through the middle and fell inward. Another came away whole in Silas's hands, leaving the coffin interior exposed to the washed gray morning.

No one in the crowd made a sound then. The stillness had a body to it.

The blanket had gone to black strings and brown pulp. The skull had rolled slightly toward the open side where the slide pulled everything downhill. Ribs lay caved into the chest space. One arm had slipped free of whatever shape it once kept and rested crooked across the boards in a gesture so human it made Della hate the watchers for seeing it. Mud had seeped through the coffin break and painted half the bones red. The other half held a cleaner pale under the damp, like crockery kept in a cellar.

Rafe breathed, "Christ."

Della did not answer him. She leaned closer until her knees complained and her breath warmed the loosened clay. It was not enough to know. She had known from the place, from the root, from the pull in her own body when she first saw the washout. But knowing was no longer the hard part. Saying it so the wrong people could not unsay it afterward, that was the work now.

She touched the left shoulder first because her hand went there before her mind did. The clavicle lay mostly clear, mud washed off by the angle of the open lid. Even in the bad light she could see the thickened ridge halfway along it, a heavy, ugly knitting where the bone had once broken and healed crooked. It was not fresh damage. It was old life written into the frame.

She had wrapped that shoulder herself one summer after Gabriel came off a sorrel mare drunker than he admitted, laughing through pain while she bound him tight enough to make him swear. He had complained more about not being able to button his good shirt than about the bone. She had wanted to hit him and kiss him in the same minute. Seeing that same knot of healing now, naked and colorless under decades of dirt, collapsed the years. Time was no road. It kept turning the same break up in her hands.

"Don't anybody touch the left collarbone," she said. "Look at it from where you are."

Lucille stepped closer, careful not to crowd the grave. "I see an old healed fracture."

Vernon remained above, refusing the mud, but Della heard his voice come thin with distance. "That proves nothing. Plenty of men have broken bones."

"Not in this grave," Della said.

She set two fingers at the jaw, gentler than the work required, and turned the skull just enough to open the upper gumline to the light. One front tooth was chipped. Farther back, on the left side, the premolar was missing clean from life, the ridge smoothed over long before death. It had not been knocked out by a spade, and it had not been lost in the ground. It had been gone for years before the coffin.

"There," she said.

Rafe bent despite himself. Mud dropped from his sleeve onto the coffin edge. "What?"

"Upper left premolar. Missing before he was buried."

He looked where she pointed and then at her face, blaming her for the gap all the same.

Silas wiped his mouth on the back of his wrist. "That's old loss, all right."

Della sat back a little and let herself look at the whole grave again, not only the body. The placement mattered too. It had always mattered. Gabriel was in the lower fever strip, south edge, tucked below the cedar even then because the regular rows would have put him under names and questions by daylight. Two infant stones had once stood uphill from him, small enough to vanish under grass by spring. The runoff ditch cut close to his feet because that was where the ground yielded fastest with a hurried spade. Eli had never lain there. Eli had run.

She rose as far as her knees would allow and turned so the people above could hear without making her shout.

"This grave is Gabriel Wolfe's," she said. "It sits where Silas Wynn and I put him. South side of the fever plot, below the cedar root, under the two child markers that washed away years ago. The left collarbone healed thick from an old break. The upper left premolar was gone before he ever entered this box. This is Gabriel."

The name struck the air like a rock thrown into water. The whole ring of people changed shape around it.

Noreen made a sound Della would not have called a sob because there was too much anger in it. She took two steps down before Lucille put a hand out to stop her from the unstable edge. Her face had gone bare and sharp all at once.

"Say it again," she said to Della, not pleading, just refusing any room for backtracking.

Della looked at her. "Gabriel Wolfe."

Vernon came forward at last then, not into the grave but near enough that mud streaked his trouser hem. His control did not break all at once. It tightened first.

"Mrs. Bledsoe is making an assertion from memory," he said. "A dramatic one. We are talking about old bones in a collapsed coffin after a slide. No prudent authority would settle inheritance or identity on that."

"I didn't say settled," Lucille replied, faster than usual. "I said identified for record and hearing purposes by direct witness."

"Direct witness to a burial nobody reported at the time."

That landed where he meant it to. Heads turned toward Della. The question was no longer only whether it could be Gabriel now. The question was whether she had known all along, whether she had kept a named man hidden under the fever dead while the county and the families and her own son stumbled around him blind. The shift in the crowd was almost visible. Curiosity had wanted scandal. This was better. This had blame attached.

Lucille did not save her from it. To her credit, she did not try.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," she said, pencil ready again, voice level. "For the record before witnesses: when the grave first surfaced, did you already suspect the remains were Gabriel Wolfe's?"

Della felt the whole hillside waiting to hear whether she would wriggle or stand.

"Yes," she said.

A woman up on the ridge sucked breath through her teeth. Somebody muttered, She knew. Rafe's face changed not toward surprise but toward the bitter satisfaction of being right in the worst possible way.

"You knew," he said. "All this time. You let me file. You let me go to Larkin and sign my name to that thing while you stood there knowing."

"I told you it wasn't Eli."

"That ain't the same and you know it."

His voice cracked on the last word, not from tears but from the kind of fury that scraped the throat raw on its way out. He took one step toward the coffin, then stopped because the dead man in it had become inconveniently real. His last easy road had a face now, or as much of one as bone could keep.

"You could've ended it," he said. "Weeks back. One word."

"No," Della said. "One word was never going to end it."

"It would've kept food in my house."

"It would've made a lie county fact."

"County fact buys meal. Truth doesn't."

The line came out so fast and flat it sounded used, something he had been telling himself on dam scaffolds and in truck cabs and walking home in the dark. Della felt it strike her with the dull force of a tool handle. She did not let herself answer with comfort. Comfort would have been another lie, and he was beyond taking it from her anyway.

Above them Vernon seized the opening.

"Mr. Bledsoe's distress is exactly why this should not be done by public theater," he said. "We have a hearing in two days. Let the county weigh evidence there. Let tempers cool. Let sober process do what a muddy crowd cannot."

Noreen laughed once, sharp as a snapped bean. "Sober process is what let you sit on Gabriel's trees twenty years."

"Mind yourself."

"You mind me for a change. If that's Gabe under there, you've been eating his apples and mine besides."

Color climbed Vernon's neck. He corrected her because correction was the last railing he had left. "Your brother's name was Gabriel. Not Gabe. There is no need to turn this into common gossip."

"You hear him?" Noreen said to nobody and everybody. "He's losing the body and still wants the syllables."

That got them. It moved through the crowd in a rough low murmur, not laughter exactly, but the sound of people recognizing a man's weakness at the same moment he showed it. Vernon heard it too. For the first time since Della had known him, his face lost its church-made arrangement and showed the mean thinner thing under it.

Della felt that murmur crawl across her shoulders under the damp cloth of her dress. She had wanted Vernon cornered often enough, but a crowd smelling blood never made anything cleaner. It only made the hill feel smaller.

"You people are eager for filth," he said. "That is all this is. Old filth dragged out because some of you cannot bear order unless you are standing above it throwing mud."

Silas rose from his crouch with both hands on his knees. It took him longer than it once would have. When he got upright he had to stop and gather breath, mouth open, eyes narrowed against the cough climbing his chest. He spat to the side into the weeds, wiped his lips, and looked straight at Vernon.

"That's your brother," he said.

Vernon snapped toward him. "You cannot possibly know that."

"I can because I helped put him there."

Nothing on the hillside moved.

Silas did not raise his voice. He never needed to. The gravel in it did the carrying for him. Hearing him say Gabriel's name aloud after all those buried years hit Della low in the gut. For a blink she hated herself for needing his witness at all.

"Me and Della buried Gabriel Wolfe in this ground before dawn," he said. "South edge of the fever strip because it was the only patch we could open fast and hidden. I seen that collarbone broke years before when he took that fall off the sorrel at the creek road. I seen that tooth missing every time he laughed with his mouth too wide. I dug this hole. I remember what I laid in it. That's Gabriel."

Rafe took the words like a blow. Noreen closed her eyes once, hard, then opened them on Vernon. Lucille's pencil was flying now. Vernon looked not at the grave but at the people around it, counting and losing at the same time.

"You're confessing to an unlawful burial," he said finally.

"Looks like I am."

"And you expect the county to trust a confession of wrongdoing as proof?"

"I expect the county to trust a man that knows the ground he cut and the body he lowered better than you trust the story that's fed you."

Lucille lifted her head from her notes. The office clips were slipping in her skirt hem. She ignored them. Della saw the pencil pause and knew the next sentence would harden the whole filthy morning into county language.

"For the relocation record," she said, "the exposed adult remains in the lower fever plot are identified on site as Gabriel Wolfe by Della Bledsoe and corroborated by Silas Wynn, both claiming direct knowledge of burial location and distinguishing bodily features. Further legal disposition will be addressed at hearing. But the county will not tag these remains as Eli Bledsoe."

The scratch of pencil did it. One bureaucratic sentence, and Rafe's affidavit was dying right in front of him with mud on its mouth.

He swore then, not at Lucille alone and not at Della either. He said it to the whole slope, the county, the rain, the dead man, the bank note at First State, the three miles of mud between Old Sion and the new cemetery, the dam payroll in his pocket that bought too little and cost too much. "Goddamn it."

No one answered him. That silence was worse than argument. It made room for what he had lost.

He scrubbed both hands down his face, leaving red streaks from forehead to jaw. "So what am I supposed to do now?"

Della heard the question underneath the one he had asked. It was about money, yes. It was about name too, though neither of them would touch that yet. It was about the shape of a life built on one absence and now handed another in its place.

"You do what comes next," she said, and hated how thin it sounded even while it was the only honest thing she had.

"That all?" he said. "That's what you've got?"

She could have told him to hold his temper until they were off the hill. She could have said she had done what she thought would keep him alive when he was small and what she had thought then might not still look like sense now. She could have said poverty made beggars out of truth and liars out of everybody else. All of it would have been real enough and useless as wet flour.

"No," she said. "But it's what I've got for here."

Rafe laughed once, a torn-off sound. Then he climbed out of the grave in two angry motions and nearly slipped on the bank because his knees were shaking with effort and rage both. He did not look back when he reached the ridge. He stood there breathing hard, broad shoulders pulled in until his shirt looked too tight across them.

Below him the coffin still lay open. Gabriel had more force in the valley now as bone than he had been allowed in life at the end. Della hated that too.

Noreen spoke down into the space between them all. "I want it written plain. I don't want him put back under maybe and perhaps and likely. My mother wore her hands to knots over those orchard books while your brother went missing and your family went on eating. I want his name where anybody can read it."

Lucille nodded once. "It will be written."

Vernon found his civility again by force, the way a man buttons a coat over a wound that has started showing through. "You are all moving far too quickly from rumor to appetite. An accusation against the disposition of family land is not established by hillside drama."

"Family land," Noreen said. "You mean land you've been calling family so long you forgot the rest of us were kin."

He gave her a look Della had seen men give women in labor when the pain made them speak too plainly: offended not by the content but by the breach of order. "I will answer in the proper venue."

"You answer every time by asking for another room."

The murmur rose again. His hold slipped again with it.

Della let them have each other for a moment while she turned back to the grave. The work still needed doing. Revelation did not lift coffins. Argument did not move bones out of a bank before another rain. She asked Silas for the clean sheet of canvas from the relocation crate. He handed it down without comment, though his cough had started again and would not quite settle. He kept one hand braced against his ribs when he thought nobody was looking.

Together they began the small respectful ugliness of transferring Gabriel from broken box to county canvas. Della supported the skull in both hands and felt how little weight a head had when speech, appetite, and trouble were gone from it. Mud came away on her palms and dried there red. One rib lifted loose where the coffin bottom had rotted through. Silas gathered the arm bones in order, exact and workmanlike. Lucille turned aside for one part of it, not from squeamishness but to give them privacy where she could, though privacy on that hill had already been spent. Vernon did not come close enough to dirty his cuffs. Noreen stayed where she was and watched every motion with a face that had not yet decided whether finding a lost brother counted as mercy this late.

When Della raised the skull again to settle it into the canvas cradle, the jaw fell slightly open. The gap where the premolar had been looked almost insolent. Gabriel had always smiled with the easy confidence of a man accustomed to bargaining with the world. Even the bone remembered something of that ease, or Della was foolish enough to imagine it.

She tucked the sheet around him. The gesture came from muscle older than thought, the same care with which she had wrapped wet newborns, the same fold she used on the dead when families had only feed sacks and shame to offer. Her hands knew how to make a body carryable. They had been doing it so long they no longer asked whether the work was mercy or theft.

Lucille crouched near enough to attach the provisional tag to the canvas loop. She wrote slowly this time, making the letters legible for future hands. Gabriel Wolfe. She blotted the card with the side of her thumb and then looked at Della.

"Will you confirm the spelling?"

Della almost laughed at the obscenity of it, that after blood and lies and all those buried years the world still wanted spelling. But that, too, was how it worked. A name could be murdered by one wrong letter as cleanly as by a shovel.

"G-a-b-r-i-e-l," she said. "W-o-l-f-e."

Lucille repeated it under her breath while she checked the card, a habit of hers Della had noticed before. She trusted the shape of words more if she heard them once in air before handing them to paper.

Vernon watched that card harder than he had watched the bones. "You are putting a provisional label on without court order."

"On an identified exhumation subject to hearing," Lucille said. "Yes."

"You'll regret overreach."

She met his eyes with the flat patience of a woman who had spent too many mornings being warned by respectable men. "You can write the county if you like. Use your best paper."

Silas barked a laugh that turned at once into coughing. He bent double with it, one hand on the shovel handle, the other pressed to his chest. Della looked up sharply.

"Sit down," she said.

"In this muck? I'd rather die standing."

"You talk too easy about that."

"At my age it's just weather."

But he did sink onto a cedar root after the spell passed, hat tipped back, face gone the dusty gray it took when the quarry cough dug deep. Della felt a fresh unease move through her. There was too much left to do in the coming weeks for any one body on that hill to fail, much less his. The cemetery had started charging in advance.

Rafe had not gone. He still stood at the ridge with his hands on his hips, staring down at the tagged canvas and the written name laid out for public viewing. Some of the crowd were already peeling off toward the road, hungry to be first with the news. Others lingered because leaving too soon might mean missing the next collapse. Della could almost hear the next round of talk forming in their mouths: not only Gabriel Wolfe found, but Della Bledsoe knew; not only the grave named, but the midwife had been sitting on a dead Wolfe while her own boy filed papers on another man. By supper the valley would have shaved all tenderness off it and left the hard parts exposed.

She straightened with effort and climbed out of the grave one root and one boot print at a time. By the top her back felt hammered flat. Red clay had pasted itself to the hem of her skirt and dried there in scales. She faced the people who remained because if she let them look at her bent over now, they would mistake pain for retreat.

"The grave's opened and the dead man named," she said. "If you've come to help, stay and help. If you've come to stare, you've had your turn."

That cleared several of them. Shame still worked on some valley people if you handed it to them plain. A few men came down with shovels at last, suddenly eager to appear useful after the fact. Della set them to cutting a safer path for the stretcher without thanking them for the conversion.

Rafe did not move.

She went to him because if he carried that rage downhill untended, the road would get to his house before she did.

Up close he smelled of wet wool, blast smoke, and the sour iron scent of a man who had run on coffee and anger too long. He kept his eyes on the grave.

"Don't," he said before she spoke. "Don't stand there with that grave voice like you done something clean."

"He couldn't stay half-named in that bank."

"What he can't do now is keep his mouth shut," Rafe said. "By the time I hit my yard, Ruthie'll have it from the road and June'll hear the scraps through the wall. They'll know I filed on the wrong dead man, and they'll know you knew better."

"I'll tell them before the road finishes with it."

He turned then. Hurt made him look younger, not softer. Just younger, closer to the boy who used to come in from fields too hungry to wash first and still pretend he was full so June could eat more later. Della saw the child under the grown laborer for one terrible blink, and that was worse than his anger.

"Tell them what?" he asked. "That you let me make a fool of myself in Larkin? Or the rest of it too?"

Her mouth closed on the first answer that tried to come. On this hill, with Lucille still writing and Noreen still watching and Vernon not yet gone from the fence, the truth would not land clean anywhere it touched.

"In the kitchen," she said.

He gave that torn-off laugh again, smaller now and meaner for it. "Course there is."

Then he went past her toward the road, boots striking mud hard enough to throw it behind him. Nobody stopped him. Della watched until the trees took him, because she knew with a certainty that made her stomach feel packed with river stones that if she did not get to that kitchen before the valley did, the truth would arrive there already skinned and spoiled.

When she turned back, Vernon was leaving too, though not quickly. He paused once at the fence, hand on the post, and looked over his shoulder at the canvas bundle tagged with his brother's name. The look was brief and mean and frightened all together, gone so fast another woman might have missed it. Della did not. Men like Vernon spent years mistaking possession for peace. The first time the world corrected them, they looked offended that dirt had taken a side.

Noreen saw it too. She put the peppermint back in her mouth, bit down, and said, not loudly but loud enough, "I'll see you in Larkin."

Vernon did not answer. That was answer enough.

By noon the grave was empty except for wet impressions where the coffin had rested and a few black scraps of wood too rotten to carry. The fever plot looked violated and ordinary at once, as all opened ground did after the work passed through it. Lucille had her forms damp at the edges. Silas had stopped coughing only because he had gone quiet. The canvas-wrapped remains lay on the stretcher ready for the wagon, Gabriel at last among the named dead and no safer for it.

Della stood over the stretcher a moment before they lifted.

All those years she had kept him in the one place no Wolfe would willingly search and no Bledsoe could profit from. She had told herself secrecy was the only blanket left to pull over what happened in her kitchen. Maybe once it had kept one man alive in his leaving and one child fed under a cheaper lie. Standing there with mud drying on her wrists and wagon boards creaking under Gabriel's tagged weight, she knew the secret had spoiled long before this morning. The body had not gone nameless under the hill. It had only waited for the hill to speak first.

"Lift him careful," she said.

They took the stretcher at shoulders and feet. The weight was not much. The meaning of it nearly buckled her all the same. Beyond the cemetery gate, the road to her kitchen was already carrying the news ahead of her.
