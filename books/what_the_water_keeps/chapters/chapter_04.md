# Chapter 4

By the time Lucille Holt called Sadie Kincaid's matter, the schoolhouse room had already spent two hours making poor people explain themselves.

The county had shoved three pupil desks together where the teacher's table used to sit. Behind them Lucille kept the files squared in two neat stacks, a pad of claim forms, an ink bottle, and the brass hand stamp that made every sheet look heavier once it came down. The probate deputy from Larkin sat beside her in his vest with his collar gone soft at the points, reading names through half-moon spectacles and asking questions that stripped the skin off a life faster than any undertaker ever could. The blackboard still held a ghost of multiplication tables. Flies worried the window screen. Every truck that hit the road outside sent a tremor through the room and lifted a taste of chalk into the air.

Della had listened to one woman made to say three times that the father of her sons had drowned before the preacher could come, as if saying it cleaner might bring him back dry. A bent old man from Red Branch had been asked to spell his own mother's name while two clerks argued over whether an extra letter on a tax receipt changed which strip of creek bank had been hers. Nobody raised a voice. The meanness lived in how mild they stayed. The county could flay a household in a tone fit for reading weather.

She sat on the second bench with her straw hat in her lap and her left thumb tucked under the brim where nobody could see how stiff it had gone. The room had the crowded heat of quilting day without any of the mercy. Sweat worked down her spine under her dress. Lucille's ruined shoes clicked from desk to filing crate and back again, brisk as a metronome, mud dried pale around the edges from the walk over. When she was near losing patience she capped her pen too hard, and Della had heard that hard little click seven or eight times already.

Sadie's oldest girl, Hester, sat two places down with her good brown skirt brushed thin at the knees and a loose black thread caught on one cuff. She kept rubbing that thread flat with a spit-wet thumb as if the whole morning might mend if she worried one bad place long enough. Pearl, the younger one, held herself straighter and worse. She had powdered her face until the freckles at her nose showed through in islands, and every few minutes she opened her handbag just enough to touch the neck of a small bottle of smelling salts, though she never lifted it all the way out. It was not faintness so much as preparedness. Pearl liked to meet shame with supplies.

Vernon Wolfe had taken the back chair by the wall in his dark coat though the day had already turned warm. He sat broad and clean and church-respectable, one ankle across the other, tapping every page he touched against his knee before he laid it down, even if it was not his paper to straighten. He looked like a man who had come to observe order being kept. Noreen Acker stood under the county map near the windows instead of sitting anywhere at all. The strap of her black pocketbook had been stitched back together with blue darning thread that showed in a hard bright scar against the leather. She had the flat waiting stillness of somebody who had learned chairs could turn into traps if you sank into them too soon.

Lucille glanced up from the desk and found Della's face. "You'll answer what you know firsthand."

"That's the only sort I've got."

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

Lucille's mouth gave one brief crooked twitch. She came around the desks and passed Della a paper cup half full of pump water. "When he asks more than you can swear to, say so. Don't let people borrow your certainty for their own uses."

Della drank. The water tasted of tin and old pipe. "That warning belongs to this whole room."

Lucille did not argue. She just took the cup back and called the names that mattered.

The probate deputy read from the top file in a voice so dry it seemed to powder the paper. "Claim dispute on relocation allowance and condemnation residue attached to the decedent entered as Sadie Kincaid in the church burial register and Sadie Dunn on coffin plaque filed at removal. Interested parties, step forward."

Hester rose first, not because she was bolder but because fatigue had worn the hesitation out of her. Pearl stood an instant later and set her hat brim with two quick fingers before she moved. They came to the front of the room and turned half sideways under everybody's eyes. Della had delivered Hester's first son in the Kincaid cabin and stitched Pearl after Pearl's second girl tore her deep on the way out. She knew exactly how both women looked when they were in pain. This room had made each of them choose a face for being watched instead.

Lucille swore them in. The deputy asked full names, place of residence, relation to the deceased. It was the relation line that stung. Both women answered daughter, and then the deputy asked, "By which husband?"

Pearl said, "By Clyde Dunn."

Hester said, "By Hiram Kincaid."

The room took that in with a thin stir of skirts and breath. Della felt the benches tightening around her. Rafe would have called the whole thing foolishness if he had been there, but he would have understood the money of it. The county held not only the burial payment for Sadie's grave but the small condemnation residue on the Kincaid cabin lot, the last rag of value left after weather and men had done their work. There was not enough in it for rich people to bother over and more than enough for poor ones to bleed over.

The deputy looked at Pearl first. "State the basis of your claim."

Pearl swallowed, uncapped the smelling salts bottle, breathed once near it without touching it to her nose, and put it back. "Mama lived with Clyde Dunn seventeen year. Everybody in church called her Sadie Dunn. That's the name Mr. Bell cut into the metal card because I paid him to do it, and I paid him because Hester didn't have the money then and hasn't got it now."

The sharp medicinal sweetness reached Della a breath later and brought Pearl's second labor back whole: Pearl on a tick mattress with her hair pasted to her cheeks, cussing through her teeth while blood dried brown at the quilt seam, too proud to cry till the baby was out and bawling. She had always preferred a bottle, a rag, a joke, anything that let her put one thin thing between herself and being watched.

Hester's face went set and dull. "I never said I had the money."

"No," Pearl said. "You let me say it for you."

Lucille lifted a hand before the deputy could. "Speak to the file, Mrs. Dunn. Not over each other."

Pearl took breath through her nose and tried again. "My mother kept house with Clyde till he died. After that she stayed with me some and with Hester some because she couldn't manage alone. But the name she went by in town was Dunn. It was Dunn at the store. Dunn at church. Dunn on the coffin. I don't see what else there is to rake through."

The deputy wrote a line. "Mrs. Kincaid?"

Hester did not correct the name though it wasn't hers anymore, if it had ever been truly hers. She had married a Harmon boy, buried him young, and gone back to being mostly what work called her. "What there is to rake through," she said, "is who had her at the end and what she asked for."

Pearl made a sound in her throat. "You had her because you were nearer the privy."

"I had her because she came back to the Kincaid place when her legs wouldn't carry her up your steps."

"That's not why."

"That's why."

Lucille said, sharper now, "One at a time."

Hester turned her face toward the desks instead of toward Pearl, which was the only reason she stayed steady. "Mama was sleeping in my front room the last six months. Before that she was in the side chamber off the old Kincaid cabin. I washed her. I emptied the pan. I turned her when the sores broke at her hips. She told me she meant to be put down under Kincaid because that was the name that belonged to the place and the children from it."

Della felt her left thumb lock harder under the straw brim. Hester had not prettied a thing, and that was why the words landed as hard as they did. Anybody who had ever lifted a wasting body knew the wet drag of sheets under ruined skin.

Pearl said, "She said that to hurt me and you know it."

Hester's mouth twitched once. "No. She said it because she'd got too sick to lie polite."

The deputy let a beat pass. "Are either of you able to produce marriage licenses or probate determinations for both unions in question?"

Pearl stared at him as though he had asked for a moonbeam in a jar. Hester did not even bother pretending to search her pocketbook. That, more than any tears would have, showed the room what sort of valley this was. Nine miles to the county seat might as well have been three states for women who had spent forty years keeping children fed on borrowed flour and whatever man had not yet gone missing.

Della bit the inside of her cheek and kept still. She could have answered for half the benches in that room, named exactly how a woman wound up married in practice and nobody's wife on paper. Easy was not the same as useful. If she started translating all of Brier Valley's crooked living for the county, the deputy would call it opinion and lose the one narrow fact he had dragged her here to give.

Vernon shifted in his chair. "If I may."

Lucille did not look pleased. "You may state your reason for speaking, Mr. Wolfe."

"Only the obvious one. If the county begins preferring recollection to record, you will have no end of these disputes. The church book and the plaque are at least documents. They are something more durable than a hard winter and a deathbed mood."

Noreen let out one dry huff of contempt. Nobody looked toward her, but everybody heard it.

Lucille said, "This is not your claim."

"No," Vernon said, with that careful civility of his that always sounded like a man dusting off a weapon before use. "It is only the first claim that establishes what standard the county means to use. That concerns all deed holders in the valley."

Della felt the words deed holders settle in the room like a second set of bodies. The county called it standard. Vernon called it order. She had spent too many nights in women's kitchens not to know what it mostly meant. A license cost money. A divorce cost more. A death certificate for a man who had wandered off could take years and two mules' worth of travel if it came at all. Men with orchard acreage and store credit kept cleaner histories because they could afford to. Everybody else lived crooked to the paper and got punished for the shape necessity had made of them.

The deputy cleared his throat and turned a page. "The office has requested Mrs. Della Bledsoe as witness on decedent identity and burial instruction. Mrs. Bledsoe, will you come forward."

She rose with the bench wood warm against the backs of her legs and walked to the front while the whole room watched the way people watched a midwife carrying hot water: not because the water itself was holy, but because something in the next room depended on whether she spilled it.

Lucille held out the Bible. Della put her hand on it, swore, and sat where they pointed. The deputy's pencil hovered.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," he said, "did you attend the deceased in her last illness?"

"I did."

"Did you prepare the body for burial?"

"Yes."

"Did the deceased identify herself to you by one surname or another?"

Della looked past him a second, not at the window but into the small hard place behind her eyes where certain nights kept living. Sadie on the rope bed in Hester's front room, not much left to her but bone under stretched skin and a stubbornness that had outlasted teeth. The room had smelled of boiled sheets, chamber slop, and the sweet bad edge of a body beginning to fail from the middle first. Della had slid a folded cloth under her hips because nothing Sadie swallowed stayed where it ought to. Sadie had caught her wrist with surprising strength.

Don't let them lend me that Dunn name underground, she had said. I took shelter there. I did not belong there.

Della came back to the schoolhouse and answered the question the room had asked, not the many others hiding behind it. "She did."

"Which surname?"

"Kincaid."

Pearl's hand flew to her handbag. Hester did not move at all.

The deputy wrote. "On what basis do you state that?"

"On the basis that I heard it from her own mouth while I was tending her and that I laid her out after."

"Did you witness any lawful marriage between the deceased and Clyde Dunn?"

"I did not."

"Did you witness any lawful marriage between the deceased and Hiram Kincaid?"

"I did not witness it. That was before my time with her."

Vernon leaned forward. "Then with respect, the witness cannot speak to which marriage counted in law."

Della turned her head enough to look at him. Her voice came out level, almost gentle. The cut always came cleaner from that pitch. "You asked law. The county hired me because the paper could not tell one grave from another. I'm here to tell what I saw with my own eyes and touched with my own hands."

The deputy said, "Then limit yourself to that, please."

"Gladly."

She rested her palms on her skirt to keep them still. "Sadie was in the Kincaid house when she died. Hester had her there. I washed the body there. When I tied her jaw and pinned the shroud, the name she had given me was Kincaid. I passed that name to Reverend Mays for the burial register. If the coffin card said Dunn later, it was because somebody ordered it after my part was done."

Pearl said, too quick, "I ordered it because that was the name folks knew."

"That may be," Della said, and kept her face toward the desks. "It is not what Sadie asked of me."

The deputy's pencil paused. "Mrs. Bledsoe, did the decedent state any reason for preferring Kincaid?"

This was where the line lay. Della could feel it like a sill under her foot in the dark. On one side was the grave, one body, one set of instructions given close enough to death that nobody could pretend the words were ornamental. On the other side was the whole soaked quilt of valley life: who had lain with whom when a husband vanished, which man had put meat on a table and which man had put fear there, who had worn a borrowed surname because winter was longer than pride. Once she stepped onto that side there would be no clean stopping place.

"She said that was the name she wanted to be buried under," Della said.

"For what reason?"

"You would have to ask her."

A few people in the room shifted. The deputy frowned. "I can't ask her."

"No," Della said. "You cannot."

Lucille looked down, and Della saw the corner of her mouth tighten with the effort of holding back a smile.

Vernon spoke again, more clipped now. "This is exactly the difficulty. We are being asked to move property on kitchen recollection."

That pulled Della's eyes back to him. She had not meant to say anything more than the narrow fact. Then his tone put Hester and Pearl and every other woman in the benches under the same bootheel, and anger came up in her with an old cold steadiness. "Then the county ought not to ask women who washed the dead to come here at all."

The deputy's pencil stopped above the page. A back bench gave one hard scrape on the floorboards and then quit. Lucille let the pause stand until even the flies at the screen sounded loud.

Lucille counted off one beat, two, before she said, "The witness will answer the office's questions. Mr. Wolfe, you will save yours unless I ask for them."

Vernon settled back. He did not redden. That made him more dangerous, not less. He folded his hands over one knee and looked at Della with a new kind of attention, as if some household tool he had always taken for harmless had just shown an edge.

The deputy cleared his throat and tried a different road. "Was the deceased residing in the Kincaid cabin or on the Kincaid lot at time of death?"

"Yes."

"For how long?"

"Near six months in Hester's front room. Before that, on and off in the side chamber of the old cabin whenever her bowels and legs got bad enough she needed watching."

He looked at Hester. "Can that be corroborated?"

Hester said, "By the mattress stain if you carry it in here."

Pearl flinched, whether from the image or the meanness of truth Della could not tell. Hester kept going. "Or by the chamber pot I scrubbed till the glaze went rough. Or by my youngest boy, who slept in the kitchen all spring because Mama took his bed."

Pearl said, "And I brought flour and lard both. You make it sound like I left her to dogs."

"I didn't say that."

"You didn't have to."

Lucille put down her pen. "Nobody here is deciding who loved her best. We are deciding what the county can defend in a file."

No one had said a truer thing all morning, and it landed uglier than any insult. Pearl's face changed under the powder. Love had nothing to do with it, not in a room with forms laid flat and a hand stamp waiting. Della saw it pass across both daughters at once: the knowledge that grief had been brought here, weighed, and priced by what it could attach to.

The deputy consulted the file again. "The office notes that no formal death registration was made at the time of burial. The burial register shows Kincaid. The coffin plaque filed at removal shows Dunn. Witness testimony establishes verbal burial instruction and residence at death under Kincaid household. In absence of stronger documentary proof to the contrary..." He looked at Lucille. She had already drafted the line he meant to say.

Lucille took over in her steadier voice. "For purposes of relocation payment and provisional standing on the condemned cabin residue, this office will enter the decedent as Sadie Kincaid and recognize Hester's line through Hiram Kincaid as first claimant, subject to appeal in Larkin within ten days."

Pearl's fingers closed so hard around the smelling salts bottle that Della thought the glass might crack. "Ten days?" she said. "With what money am I meant to appeal to Larkin?"

Lucille's face did not soften, but her tone did by a degree. "That's the county period. I didn't set it."

"No," Pearl said. "I reckon none of y'all set anything."

Hester did not look victorious. She looked sick and relieved and ashamed of the relief all at once. She sank back a little on her heels as if the room had taken a blow at her and handed her a parcel in the same motion. Della knew that feeling. Pearl stared at her sister, then at Della, and there was accusation there but not only accusation. There was also the raw astonishment of somebody learning that the words spoken over a body could matter more than the years spent hauling groceries to it.

Vernon said, to the room and not to either daughter, "This valley will flood in claims if recollection outranks documentation."

Noreen answered from the wall before Lucille could stop her. "The claims were here already. Paper just didn't hear them till money showed up."

Vernon's head turned. "Mrs. Acker, this is not a public debate."

"No," Noreen said. "It's a public sorting."

Lucille rose. The hearing was over whether any of them liked the taste left by it or not. "The matter is entered. Next file after recess."

People stood too fast. Bench legs scraped. The room swelled with breath and cloth and quiet muttering. Hester went back to her seat with both hands empty, the way some people carried delicate things. Pearl stayed where she was a moment longer, staring at the front desks as if she might still see another answer hidden under the forms. Then she put the smelling salts back in her bag with great care and walked out without looking at anybody.

Della remained seated until the first rush passed. Her knees ached when she stood. Across the room, two women she barely knew were already bent together over a family Bible, whispering names. An old man near the door had a folded tax notice in his fist. Nobody spoke to her yet. It was worse than that. They watched her in a fresh way, calculating.

Lucille came around the desk with Sadie's file under one arm. "Step outside a minute, if you're able."

Della said, "I'm able," though the truth of it felt partial. She followed Lucille into the schoolhouse hall, then out onto the front steps where the heat hit cleaner without the press of bodies in it. The yard grass had gone patchy under too many boots. A truck rattled past on the road carrying empty lumber crates toward Old Sion. The river smell came up thin and metallic from below.

Lucille set the file against the porch rail and loosened her collar by one button before she seemed to remember she was in public. "You did what I asked and what I was afraid you'd do at the same time."

"That sounds like county work."

"Don't start being funny now."

Della looked across the yard. Pearl was at the far gate, talking hard with a man in overalls who kept taking off his cap and putting it back on. Hester stood alone under the chinaberry tree, pressing her fingers into her eyes as if tears were another thing she could postpone by force. "I wasn't being funny."

Lucille tapped the file edge against her palm. "How many more cases in this valley have got that shape?"

Della knew the safer answer was not many. The truer answer was most, if you knew where to push. A grave was one body, one box, one night's hard fact. A marriage opened into bed linens, missing men, preacher visits that never got written down, ring marks, bruises, winter coal, babies conceived while somebody else's surname still clung by law. She did not intend to hand all that over because the county had at last discovered an appetite for precision.

"More than one," she said.

Lucille shut her eyes once, briefly. "That's not a number."

"You asked the wrong sort of question."

"I asked the only sort that helps me schedule hearings."

"Then schedule all week and you'll still come up short."

Lucille gave her a look dry enough to crack. "You enjoy being difficult more than your public face suggests."

"Only when cornered."

"That I believe." Lucille glanced back through the schoolhouse door where voices had begun to rise again around the next file. Lower now, she said, "Mr. Wolfe came because he wanted precedent. Mrs. Acker came because she smelled one. If today's ruling stands, this office turns into a place where every buried arrangement in Brier Valley may come asking to be named."

Della rested one hand on the porch post. The wood was hot and splintered smooth where children's palms had worn it years before. "You heard the room when she said Kincaid."

"That's not comfort."

"Didn't offer it."

The screen door pushed open behind them with a slap. Noreen stepped out alone. Up close her dress showed two different blacks where the sleeves had been pieced with newer cloth, and there was kitchen grease dark under one thumbnail she had not scrubbed all the way out. She came straight to Della, chin up, purpose plain on her face.

"You did right by Hester," she said.

Della watched her. "I told what belonged to Sadie's grave."

"Same thing today." Noreen hitched the blue-stitched strap higher on her shoulder. "Pearl will curse you for a month, maybe a year. That doesn't make you wrong."

Lucille said, "Mrs. Acker, if you're here on a new matter, you need to file first."

Noreen did not even turn her head. "If I had money enough to file every wrong on me, Miss Holt, your whole desk would split down the middle."

Lucille accepted that without apology, which Della gave her credit for.

Noreen fixed back on Della. "I wanted to see whether you'd tell it straight if a straight telling cut the wrong person."

"The wrong person to who?"

"To the ones sitting on top." Noreen jerked her chin toward the schoolhouse, meaning Vernon without wasting the name yet. "Men like that always love records till a record starts leaning the other way."

Della could not deny it. She also would not let Noreen pull a confession by laying a trap baited with truth. "You came for more than Sadie."

"I've spent twenty years being told my memory is a poor woman's hobby." Noreen's mouth thinned. "My daddy had three children. Folks talk now like he had two sons and a shadow. The land shrank every time a paper crossed a man's desk. Funny how acreage can do that."

Lucille looked between them, and Della knew that look from the hearing room: it was the look Lucille got when an argument started dressing itself up as a file. "If you're raising a Wolfe tract dispute, that isn't before this office today."

"No," Noreen said. "I came to see whether it'd be worth scraping a filing fee out of room wages."

Vernon emerged onto the porch as if summoned by the sound of his own name unspoken. He had his hat in hand now, and Della saw that he pinched the brim each time before speaking, a small careful crushing he probably did not know he made. "If Mrs. Acker means to revisit family grievance, she may do it in proper venue. The county cannot run on insinuation."

Noreen gave him a look that could have shaved bark. "The county runs on it fine. It just stamps the insinuation and calls it settled."

Lucille said, "Enough."

Vernon ignored her, still looking at Della. "Mrs. Bledsoe, the county asked you about one burial. It did not ask you to encourage grievance against settled title."

His voice was mild. His eyes were not. Della felt the threat in the difference.

She kept her own tone measured. "Then folks with old claims had better hope the records deserve all the faith you put in them."

Something moved behind his face then, quick and mean: his mouth pinched, his eyes narrowing as if he had just counted her over again and disliked the sum. Della had the strong impression he'd wanted her useful where the county needed local knowledge and harmless where property began. The hearing had spoiled that arithmetic.

"Memory," he said, "can become slander in a hurry."

"So can paperwork," Noreen said.

Lucille rubbed her temple. "I won't have this porch turned into the afternoon docket."

Della could have stepped back. She could have soothed it down, let Vernon go off believing she meant no trouble beyond Sadie's grave. That would have been the prudent thing, maybe even the merciful one. But the smell of the room still clung to her, all that orderly humiliation, and something in her had gone past politeness for the day.

"I didn't speak to hurt a powerful family in there," she said. "I spoke because a dead woman told me what name to carry for her, and the county asked. If another grave asks, I'll answer that one too. No further unless I must."

Lucille looked at her sharply on the last sentence. Noreen did too. Vernon's expression told Della he'd heard challenge before he'd heard limit.

"That's a dangerous way to conduct public business," he said.

"Public business got dangerous before I touched it," Della said.

They stood with that between them. Down in the yard Hester had started walking home alone, shoulders squared as if she expected the ruling to strike her from behind. Pearl was gone. The truck noise on the road thinned, then came back from farther off, heading cemetery-ward. Somewhere in the trees below the schoolhouse a chainsaw started up and kept whining.

Noreen broke the stillness first. "If it comes to the Wolfe tract," she said to Della, "I don't need you saintly. I need you exact."

Della's answer came before she had time to dress it for anybody. "Saintly's never been the trouble."

Noreen's mouth eased a fraction into grim approval. "Good."

Lucille picked up the file again, pulling herself back into office shape. "If there is a Wolfe filing, it will come through the desk, not through ambush on schoolhouse steps. Do we understand one another?"

Noreen said, "I understand you like a paper first."

"I like a process that survives appeal."

"Those are not the same thing."

Lucille gave her a level look. "No. They are not."

Vernon put his hat on, finally. "Mrs. Bledsoe, I hope you'll remember that every answer given under oath ripples farther than the room intends."

Della met his eyes. "You noticed that today?"

He held her gaze a second too long and then went down the steps, each heel landing with care that made the boards sound thinner than they were. He did not look back at Noreen. He did not need to. The set of his shoulders looked to Della like he'd already begun sorting which paper, which elder, which warning he might bring to bear next.

Lucille exhaled through her nose. "There. That's the rest of my week ruined."

"It was never your week to keep tidy," Noreen said.

"Mrs. Acker."

"I heard you the first time." Noreen shifted the pocketbook strap again, then nodded once to Della. "You know where the boardinghouse is if you decide exactness wants coffee."

"I know where it is."

"Good." Noreen went down the steps after Vernon had long enough start not to make it look like pursuit. She walked with the quick forward pitch of a woman who had spent years carrying trays and did not believe in wasting a trip.

Lucille watched her go. "Tell me plainly whether I should expect her back with a filing."

Della looked out over the yard. Two men had come up from the road while they were talking. One held a deed envelope gone soft from sweat. The other had a family Bible under his arm with a ribbon hanging from the middle. Farther back, the women from the bench were no longer whispering to each other. They had spread papers over an upturned crate in the shade and were tracing names with spit-wet fingertips.

"Don't cork the ink yet," Della said.

Lucille followed her gaze and went quiet.

The man with the Bible opened it before he reached the porch, finger already tucked among the births. A woman came up behind him with a tax card folded into quarters and smoothed flat against her palm. They all had that pantry-door look Della knew too well: shame, hope, and calculation jammed together because none of them could afford to leave any one of the three behind.

Lucille said, more to herself than to Della, "I need another box of forms."

Della might have laughed if she had not been so tired. "Get two."

She put on her hat.

Lucille said, "Will you stay for the next file?"

Della settled the brim and felt the sweat-cool band against her forehead. "If there's a grave in it, send for me."

"There will be."

"Then send."

She went down the side steps before the men reached the porch front, taking the narrower path past the rain barrel and the weeds gone to seed by the fence. Even so she could feel them turn after her. By the time she hit the road, three more people had risen from the shade under the chinaberry tree with papers in their hands, and none of them looked at her as the midwife anymore. They looked at her the way people looked at a key.

She did not turn home. Sadie was entered, not yet settled, and the cemetery still waited downhill.
