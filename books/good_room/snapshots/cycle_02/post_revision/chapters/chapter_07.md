# Chapter 7

At 9:59 Tuesday morning, the buzzer rang. When Doug opened the door at 10:01, Simone Barr was standing in the hallway with a laptop bag over one shoulder and a paper cup of coffee that wasn't from anywhere he recognized. The fact that she'd arrived when they'd agreed already distinguished her from every person Doug had dealt with since the video. Layla was always twenty minutes late or forty minutes early. Charlie Falk had called twice at 6 a.m. Pacific, which was 9 a.m. in Columbus and therefore, in his mind, reasonable.

She was shorter than he'd expected, though he didn't know what he'd expected. He'd Googled her the night before and found a headshot on a management site — at least five years old, with different hair — and a few credits listed on IMDb without photos. Three late-night shows. He didn't know which ones. He didn't watch late-night.

"Doug," she said. Not a question.

"Yeah. Come in."

She stepped inside and surveyed the apartment with the same attention Doug gave a water-damaged house before opening the claim file: measuring, not judging, though the measuring carried its own judgment. She didn't comment on the Ikea bookshelf or the art print Megan had left behind or the cereal bowl he'd forgotten to wash that morning. She set her bag on the kitchen table, sat down, pulled the lid off her coffee, and blew on it once.

"You want some coffee?" Doug asked. "I've got a —"

"I'm good." She took a sip. Her nails were short and uneven, trimmed without a mirror, and her left thumb had a callus along the side from years of pressing a pen too hard. No rings. Her hair was pulled up with a clip that was handling maybe sixty percent of its job. She wore a gray sweater with a small hole near the cuff of the left sleeve that she hadn't fixed or hidden, just kept wearing, past the point of seeing the hole at all. Doug noticed it.

He sat across from her. The kitchen table was small enough that their knees nearly touched, and he moved his chair back an inch. Simone didn't register this.

"So," Doug said.

"So." She opened her bag and took out not a laptop but a yellow legal pad, the kind you could buy at any office supply store for three dollars. She also took out a pen — black, nothing special — and placed them on the table beside the coffee. She didn't write anything. She looked at Doug.

"How does this work?" he asked.

"We talk. I listen. Sometimes I write things down."

"What things?"

"Things that work."

"How will you know?"

"I'll know."

Doug didn't know what to do with his hands. He put them on the table, then in his lap, then back on the table. The refrigerator motor kicked on behind him, louder than it needed to be. Outside, a car passed with a bad muffler — the scrape of metal on asphalt — and he thought, automatically, that if the muffler detached and struck another vehicle the liability would fall to the city for road maintenance, not the driver, unless the driver had been previously cited for the exhaust condition. This was not a useful thought. He had it anyway.

"Be funny," Simone said.

Doug waited for her to smile. She didn't.

"Funny," he said.

"Yeah."

He thought about it. He was aware of himself thinking about it, which made it worse — the same problem as trying to fall asleep by concentrating on falling asleep. He thought about jokes he knew. He didn't know many. Roy had told him one last month about a horse walking into a bar, and Doug couldn't remember the punchline, only that it involved the horse ordering a drink Doug couldn't remember and the bartender either commenting or not commenting on the horse being a horse. He couldn't remember which version was the joke.

The silence went on.

Simone didn't fill it. She sat with her coffee and her legal pad and her pen and she waited. She had the patience of someone who had watched a lot of people sit in exactly this chair and produce exactly this silence. The quiet passed through uncomfortable into something worse — a zone where the silence itself had become the subject, where every second that passed without Doug saying something funny was another second of evidence that he could not say something funny, evidence that was accumulating in the record like unanswered interrogatories.

He looked at the kitchen window. The glass needed cleaning. There was a smudge at eye level that had been there since he'd leaned his forehead against it during a phone call with Layla three weeks ago, and he kept meaning to wipe it and kept not wiping it, and by now the smudge had become a feature of the window rather than a failure of maintenance.

He cleared his throat.

"I don't really — I don't have anything."

"I didn't ask you to have anything. I said be funny."

"That's the same thing."

"It's not."

Doug looked at the blank legal pad. The blankness felt personal, like a claim form with no entries — not absence of proof, but proof of absence.

"Okay," Simone said. She shifted in the chair. "Tell me about your day yesterday."

"My day."

"Yeah. What happened."

"I went to work."

"So tell me about work."

Doug had been telling people about work for twenty years and nobody had seemed interested, including, on many days, Doug himself. But Simone was sitting there with her pen and her pad and the clip in her hair slowly losing its grip, and she was waiting, and he didn't have anything else to offer.

"There's a claim I've been working on," he said. "Homeowner in Westerville. He had an above-ground pool in his backyard. Intex, twenty-four feet, the ones with the metal frame. They retail around seven hundred dollars. He'd had it three summers."

Simone didn't write anything. She held her coffee.

"The wall failed. Middle of the night, nobody outside. September, so the pool was still full — about fifteen thousand gallons. All of it went sideways into the neighbor's yard."

"Okay."

"The neighbor had roses. Hybrid teas. Seventeen plants. She'd been growing them for eleven years. She had receipts for the original plants, the fertilizer, the trellises, the pruning equipment, the soil amendments, the pH testing kits. She had photographs from every June going back to 2013. She had a log of every bloom cycle."

Doug paused. He'd said "log" and the word had a weight he didn't expect, sitting in his kitchen with a woman he'd known for twelve minutes. Everything he was describing — the documentation, the receipts, the careful recording of something that mattered to the person who kept the records — sounded like something else when he heard it out loud. He moved on.

"Total documented loss, forty-two hundred dollars. That's the plants, the infrastructure, and the lost growing season. She filed a claim."

"On whose policy?"

Doug sat back an inch. Nobody asked that. People always asked what happened to the roses.

"That's exactly the problem," he said. "She filed on her own homeowner's policy. But her policy covers weather events, fire, vandalism, accidental damage from covered perils. A neighbor's pool wall failing isn't weather. It's not vandalism. It's a third-party structural failure from a product with a known defect in the frame connectors."

"So she's not covered."

"Not under her own policy. The pool owner — his liability coverage has a sublimit on property damage that caps at two thousand, and even that's arguable because the policy language says 'sudden and accidental discharge.' You could make a case that a wall failing from a known manufacturing defect isn't accidental, it's negligence. And once you're in negligence territory, his insurer might deny the whole thing."

Simone's coffee had stopped moving. She was watching him with an expression he couldn't read — not boredom, not the polite attention people gave when Doug talked about insurance at parties, which was part of why he'd stopped going to parties.

"So you've got a woman with forty-two hundred dollars in dead roses and no coverage path. His insurance might deny, hers definitely denies, the manufacturer has a class action pending but it won't resolve for three years, and this woman is standing in her yard looking at seventeen dead hybrid teas and she calls me — she calls me personally, because I handled her kitchen fire claim in 2019 and she trusts me — and she says, 'Doug, someone owes me for my roses.'"

He stopped. He didn't know why he'd told this story. It wasn't a bit. It wasn't material. It was the kind of problem that made his job interesting — a jurisdictional tangle most people found deadly boring and Doug found genuinely absorbing. All the pieces were there. None of them fit the same picture.

Simone laughed.

Not a big laugh. It started as a breath through her nose and then her mouth opened slightly and her shoulders moved and there was a sound that was, unmistakably, a person being amused.

Doug stared at her.

"Why are you laughing? It's a real claim. She lost eleven years of roses."

"I know."

"There's no coverage path. I spent three days on the phone with two adjusters and a claims attorney trying to find one. It's a genuine jurisdictional problem."

"I know it is."

"So I don't understand what's funny."

Simone was still almost smiling — not broadly, just the residual expression of someone who had found something she'd been looking for in a place she hadn't expected. She picked up her pen for the first time and wrote something on the legal pad. Doug couldn't read it upside down. He thought he saw the word "roses" but it might have been something else.

"What are you writing?"

"Notes."

"About what?"

"About you."

Doug sat with that. A pipe knocked once in the bathroom wall. The smudge on the window caught the thin spring light and held it.

"I still wasn't trying to be funny," he said.

"I know," Simone said. "That's the whole thing."

---

They met four times that first week. Tuesday through Friday, same table, same time. Simone brought her own coffee every morning — always a paper cup, always black, always from somewhere different, as if she were conducting a private survey of every coffee shop in Clintonville. Doug offered to make some on Wednesday and she said, "I don't trust anyone else's coffee," and Doug understood this immediately because he didn't trust other people's claims documentation either, not until he'd verified the source.

Wednesday, after she sat down, she said, "Do the pool one again."

Doug had spent part of Tuesday night replaying the conversation in bed. Something had worked. He'd made a person laugh. The mistake, he learned at 10:03 Wednesday morning, was thinking he'd understood why.

"Okay," he said. He cleared his throat. "So this guy in Westerville buys one of those above-ground pools, the kind that already looks half broken when you take it out of the box, and in the middle of the night the wall blows and wipes out the neighbor's roses. Seventeen of them. Which is too many roses, if we're being honest, and then she's got receipts for everything because apparently the rose people are —"

Simone lifted a hand.

Doug stopped. "What."

"You don't say 'the rose people.'"

"I just did."

"In life, you don't."

Doug felt the back of his neck go warm. A bus went by on High Street hard enough that the spoon in his cereal bowl from breakfast rattled in the sink.

"I was trying to make it sound more like a bit," he said.

"I know."

"That's the job, isn't it?"

"No." She pointed at him with the pen. "The sentence before that one worked."

"Which sentence."

"'Seventeen of them.' Then you started translating."

"Translating what."

"You're telling me what attitude to have about it instead of telling me what happened." She put the pen down on the table between them. "Do it again. Start with the size of the pool."

Doug started again.

"Twenty-four feet," he said. "Intex. Metal frame. Three summers old. The wall failed in the middle of the night. About fifteen thousand gallons went into the next yard."

"Keep going."

"The neighbor had hybrid teas. Seventeen plants. Eleven years. She had receipts for the original plants, the fertilizer, the trellises, the pruning equipment, the soil amendments, the pH testing kits. She had photographs from every June going back to 2013."

"Keep going."

"Total documented loss, forty-two hundred dollars. She filed on her own policy first, which was the problem, because there was no coverage path there. Her carrier said no. His carrier had a sublimit. The manufacturer had a class action. She kept saying somebody owed her for the roses."

Simone laughed again, shorter this time but actual.

Doug stopped talking. "Same place you laughed yesterday."

"Yeah."

"But not when I tried to help it."

She didn't answer that. She didn't pick up the pen either. It sat on the table between them like a tool neither of them was going to use.

"Tuesday I didn't know the story was funny," he said. "Today I knew. And I tried to do what I thought you wanted and it died."

"Yeah."

"That's going to be a problem."

"That's going to be the whole problem," Simone said.

They sat with it. The legal pad was still blank.

Thursday she didn't ask about insurance.

"Describe the apartment," she said.

"What about it."

"Start anywhere."

Doug looked past her at the living room. "Two bedrooms. Kitchen. Bathroom. Ikea bookshelf. One art print Megan left when she moved out."

"What's on the shelf."

"A book about the history of the insurance industry I got at a library sale for two dollars. A novel Roy lent me in July that I haven't started. The user manual for the dishwasher."

"Why is the dishwasher manual on the bookshelf."

"The drawer where I keep manuals is full."

"Show me."

Doug opened the drawer beside the stove. Inside were manila folders labeled KITCHEN, MEDIA, and SMALL APPLIANCES. The labels came from a label maker he'd bought at OfficeMax in 2014 and kept refilling ever since. Simone looked down. Doug felt suddenly indecent, like she'd found a room in him that wasn't for visitors. She slid out the blender manual. Black-and-Decker. Cover bent.

"You donated this."

"Yes."

"And kept this."

"It was filed."

Simone slid the manual back and closed the drawer.

"What's the print."

Doug looked into the living room. "Museum poster. Woman in blue dress. I don't remember the painter."

"Why's it still up."

"It would leave pale rectangles on the wall."

"Is that the only reason."

"It was also already hanging."

"What'd you have for dinner last night," she said.

He looked at her. "Why."

"Because I asked."

"Cereal."

"What kind."

"Raisin Bran."

"Big night."

He looked at the table. "It's fast."

"How often."

He did the math before answering because accuracy had been requested. "Four nights a week, probably. Sometimes five if work's bad."

"At the table."

"At the counter."

"Why."

Doug felt the question in his shoulders before he felt it anywhere else. "Because if you sit down with cereal at the table it starts being dinner."

Simone held the pen but didn't write. She was quiet long enough that Doug could hear the water heater cycling in the closet behind the bathroom wall.

She glanced toward the hall. "What's in the second bedroom."

"Boxes."

"From when."

"The move. Twelve years ago."

"You haven't opened them."

"I know what I use."

"Do you."

He looked at the hallway. "Mostly."

Simone didn't say anything. The silence sat there between them until Doug stood up, which he had not planned to do, and walked down the hall with her following two steps behind. The carpet there had flattened into a darker strip from years of the same path to the bathroom and back. He stopped at the second bedroom door. His hand went to the knob. He did not turn it.

"What's in the boxes," Simone asked.

Doug kept his hand where it was. "Tax files. Lamps. A set of plates we got as a wedding gift that Megan said were too formal to use every day. A space heater that smells like dust for the first twenty minutes. Cords for electronics that no longer exist. Framed pictures I took off the wall and never put back up anywhere else."

"Can I see it."

"No."

She stood behind him. The hallway was narrow enough that he could feel her there without turning around.

"Okay," she said.

He let go of the knob. Something cold had collected in his palm from the metal. They walked back to the kitchen without speaking. Simone sat down, opened her pad, and wrote for a long time — not notes from a conversation but something else, something that took up most of the page. Doug sat across from her and watched the pen and didn't ask.

Friday he tried to get ahead of her.

He bought a yellow legal pad on his lunch break because it seemed possible that the pad itself was part of the method, the same way a clipboard made a stranger look official in a parking lot. He sat at the kitchen table for forty minutes Thursday night and wrote twelve things he thought might count as material. The first four were about insurance. Two were about airport bathrooms from the flight back from Los Angeles. One was about how nobody ever knew what "full coverage" meant. One was just the words ROY'S SANDWICH underlined twice.

When Simone arrived Friday, he slid the pad across the table before she'd even taken her coat off.

She looked at it. "What's this."

"Things I wrote down."

She sat. "Read one."

Doug flipped to the first page. His handwriting on paper always looked more officious than he'd intended, like he was documenting a witness statement.

"Okay. So an umbrella policy, despite the name, has nothing to do with weather. Which I think is confusing. For people."

Simone waited.

Doug looked up. "That's the first one."

"I know."

"It sounded better last night."

She tapped the pad. "Read another."

"When people say they have full coverage, what they mean is they have not read their policy."

Nothing.

Doug turned the page. "There's one about airport soap dispensers."

"No."

She pushed the pad back toward him. She still had not written anything on her own.

Doug turned it facedown.

"Can't you just write me ten minutes that work," he said. "Actual jokes. Something I can rehearse."

"I could write you ten minutes," Simone said. "They wouldn't sound like you."

"Maybe that's fine."

"It isn't."

"You don't know that."

She leaned back. "Tuesday you told me about roses and you didn't know you were being funny. Wednesday you tried to help it and it died. Today you wrote twelve things and they sound like a claims brochure."

"The pattern is that I can't do this on purpose."

"The pattern is you can't do it as somebody else."

Doug looked at his yellow pad lying facedown on the table. He could feel the twelve lines underneath it like embarrassing medical results in a sealed envelope.

"The special is supposed to be an hour," he said.

"I know."

"And people are buying tickets because of the toast."

"I know."

"The toast had actual stakes, though. My daughter was getting married. I was drunk. I said things I wasn't supposed to say in front of people I had to keep seeing. That's different from a blender manual."

"Yeah, it is." She sat forward. "That's the part you keep skipping."

"I'm not skipping anything. I'm saying you can't build sixty minutes out of cereal and dead roses."

"Then don't," Simone said.

He stared at her. "That's the opposite of a plan."

"You want a plan because a plan is something you can make by yourself."

"The problem is people bought tickets."

"Yeah."

"So what am I supposed to do."

Simone looked at him for a long time. The pen was on the table between them and neither of them touched it.

"I don't know yet," she said.

Doug hadn't expected that. He'd expected a method, a framework — the professional version of what he did when he built a claim file from insufficient documentation. Not knowing was supposed to be his problem. It was strange to hear it from the other side of the table.

"You don't know," he said.

"Not yet." She stood and pulled her bag over her shoulder. "But I know your twelve things aren't it."

When she was at the door he said, "Wait."

She turned.

"The woman with the roses. Did she ever get her money?"

"Did she?"

"Sixteen hundred. I found an umbrella policy on the pool owner that everybody'd missed. Not full recovery, but it was something."

"How'd she take it."

"She said I was the only person who took it seriously."

"The claim."

Doug put his hand on the doorknob. "The roses." He was quiet for a second. "She told me there was a difference. Between taking the claim seriously and taking the roses seriously. I didn't understand the distinction at the time."

Simone shifted her bag higher on her shoulder. "And now?"

"I still don't," he said. "But I think about it."

"Same time Monday?"

"Monday."

After she left, Doug went back to the kitchen and washed the cereal bowl he'd left in the sink. The faucet clicked and dripped against the basin. He looked out at the smudged window and pictured the June photographs from the claim file, one for each year, the same patch of yard getting fuller.

He dried his hands and went down the hall to the second bedroom. He stood with his hand on the knob for a moment, not opening it, just standing there with the boxes on the other side and the apartment quiet around him.
