# Chapter 9

Doug had practiced the set fourteen times. He knew because he'd kept a log — hash marks on the back of a Mutual Fidelity envelope that sat on his kitchen counter between the toaster and the jar of pens he never used. Fourteen trips between the bathroom and the kitchen, roughly eleven steps, about the amount of stage Simone said a small room would give him. The first five times he'd read from the pages she'd printed out and annotated in her small, exact handwriting — *pause here*, *this is where they laugh*, *don't rush the transition*. The next five from memory, checking the pages only twice. The last four he hadn't needed the pages at all. Twenty-five minutes of material, organized into three sections with callbacks and what Simone called a "button" at the end, a comedy term for a closing line that left the audience feeling finished.

By Thursday afternoon the foil package was gone from the counter, but the Mutual Fidelity envelope was still there.

Doug could deliver all twenty-five minutes to his refrigerator with confidence.

The refrigerator had never been to an afternoon session on pharmaceutical sales incentives. The refrigerator had never consumed four drinks at an open bar. The refrigerator had not come to the keynote expecting to see the guy from the wedding toast video, the one the internet had declared a comic genius, the one eighty million people had watched and decided was saying something important about fatherhood or marriage or American masculinity or whatever the latest thinkpiece had settled on. The refrigerator was a 2019 Kenmore with a dent on the lower left panel where Doug had hit it with a suitcase, and it had been an excellent audience.

Layla had called the night before. "Okay, so, quick rundown. It's Astelline Pharmaceuticals, they do like anti-inflammatories or something, I don't know, it doesn't matter. Regional sales conference, about four hundred people, they've been in sessions all day and they want to be entertained. You're the after-dinner keynote. They've seen the video, they love you, they're primed. Thirty minutes, in and out. Fifteen K. You're gonna be great."

"What if I'm not great," Doug had said.

"Then you're still getting paid fifteen thousand dollars, babe. Think about that."

Doug had thought about it. Fifteen thousand dollars for thirty minutes. He made sixty-one thousand four hundred dollars a year before taxes at Mutual Fidelity, which came to $29.52 an hour if you divided by the standard two-thousand-and-eighty-hour work year, which Doug had once done on a slow Tuesday. Fifteen thousand dollars for thirty minutes was five hundred dollars a minute. It was $8.33 per second. Every second he stood on that stage, whether he was funny or silent or dying in front of four hundred strangers, was worth what his turkey club at the sandwich place cost. This did not make him less nervous. It made him nervous and aware that his nervousness was expensive.

He drove to Indianapolis on a Thursday afternoon in late April, two and a half hours on I-70 through landscape that transitioned from Ohio's version of flat to Indiana's version of the same flat with different highway signs. He'd left at three, telling Janet he had a personal appointment. That was technically true by insurance standards: accurate statement, misleading picture. He listened to the radio for the first hour and turned it off because every song either had too much energy or reminded him of something and he couldn't afford either.

The Indianapolis Convention Center was a building that looked like it had been designed by a committee whose only instruction was "large." Doug parked in a garage that charged eighteen dollars. Not unreasonable for downtown parking. He noted it anyway because he noted the cost of things. It was how his mind organized the world. Some people noticed architecture or other human beings. Doug noticed that the parking garage charged by the half-hour for the first three hours and switched to a flat fee after. The flat fee was a worse deal if you stayed less than four and a half hours. Doug planned to.

Inside, the convention center smelled like carpet cleaner and the particular staleness of air that had been circulated through the same building for three days of corporate programming. Banners for Astelline Pharmaceuticals hung from the ceiling — a teal logo, a tagline he couldn't read from this distance, and a photo of a woman smiling while doing yoga, which he assumed had something to do with the anti-inflammatory product line. People in lanyards moved through the hallways with the purposeful drift of the professionally conferenced, carrying tote bags filled with branded items they would never use.

A woman met him at the backstage entrance. Her name was Denise. She was the events coordinator, and she had a clipboard, a headset looped around her neck, and a blister on her left heel that she was trying not to favor. Doug could tell because she shifted her weight to the right every few seconds and winced slightly when she forgot about it. She walked him down a corridor with exposed pipe runs along the ceiling and gray industrial carpet that was a different, worse gray than the carpet in the public areas.

"So we've got you on at eight," Denise said. "Dinner wraps around seven-thirty, then there's a little bit of remarks from our VP of sales — Brian, he'll be quick, he's been told to be quick — and then he introduces you. Sound good?"

"Sure," Doug said.

"The video screens will have your name up. You want any slides or anything?"

"No."

"Great. There's water on the podium and we've got a handheld mic or a lavalier, whatever you prefer."

"Handheld," Doug said, because Simone had told him to use a handheld. She'd said it gave you something to do with one of your hands.

"Perfect." Denise checked her clipboard, flipping to a page she'd already looked at. "Is there anything you need? Green room's got some snacks, water, sodas. I think there's a fruit plate that's been sitting there since this morning, so maybe skip the fruit plate."

"I'm fine," Doug said.

"Okay great. I'll come get you about five minutes before." She paused. Her expression shifted into something less professional and more person-shaped. "My daughter watches your video like once a week. She's fourteen. She thinks you're hilarious."

Doug didn't know what to say to this. "That's nice of her."

"She made her stepdad watch it and he didn't think it was that funny, which she took as a personal attack on her judgment." Denise gave him the tight smile people used when they were mentioning family without really getting into family. "Anyway. Five minutes before. I'll come grab you."

She took three steps, stopped, and looked back at her clipboard. "Actually, before I park you, Brian wants to say hi. Two minutes. He likes to meet the speaker."

"I am not speaking yet," Doug said.

"Tell him that," Denise said, and turned toward the ballroom.

Dinner was in Ballroom C, which meant a room big enough to be divided but not improved by the division. Black curtains cut off the far third of it. The useful section held round tables in white cloths, teal uplighting at the walls, and two projection screens with his name on them in a font that managed to look energetic and mandatory at the same time. There was a photo of him too, clearly pulled from the wedding video. He was holding a microphone, tie half loose, eyes bright with the false competence of bourbon. Someone had cropped the frame close enough that the drink in his other hand was gone. The drink had been part of the explanation.

Brian was standing near the stage before anyone else at his table had stood, as though sitting through his own conference required a kind of courage he did not possess. Up close he was a large man with a golf tan above the collar and a watch so heavy it seemed orthopedic. He took Doug's hand in both of his.

"Doug. Great to have you. Huge fan. My wife sent me your clip at one-thirty in the morning and I watched it in bed with the sound off because she was asleep, which tells you how committed I was."

"I don't think the sound-off version would help much," Doug said.

Brian bent at the waist laughing. Two people at the table laughed a moment later because he had. "No, that's good. That's good," Brian said. "This is gonna be great."

A woman with a silver Astelline pin was already holding up her phone. "Can I grab one? My husband loves you. He says you said what everybody's thinking."

"I doubt that," Doug said, but she was already beside him.

He stood while she angled the phone high. In the screen he looked older than he expected and more sober than he deserved. The woman pressed in close enough that the side of her blazer touched his sleeve.

"Please tell me you're gonna do the marriage stuff," she said while she framed the picture.

"It's mostly insurance," Doug said.

Her smile held for one extra beat while the sentence found somewhere to go. "Honestly," she said, "same difference."

Denise steered him to a place card two rows from the stage. DOUG BRIDGE, in all caps on heavy cream stock. He sat because the card made it seem like not sitting would create paperwork. On his right was a regional manager from Cincinnati named Carla. On his left was a district rep from St. Louis named Brent. Across from him sat two more people whose names he missed because they were already telling him how glad they were he was here.

"We've needed this all day," Carla said. She was in her forties, wearing a green sheath dress and conference flats and the exhausted brightness of someone nearing the end of a professional obligation. "The afternoon sessions were brutal."

"What were they about?" Doug asked.

"Compliance. Incentives. Resilient selling." She drank half her water. "One of them was somehow about mindset."

Brent said, "That one felt illegal."

The table laughed. Doug laughed half a second late, because it was an accurate statement and accuracy frequently arrived after timing.

Dinner came under silver domes: chicken breast with brown sauce, rice pilaf, green beans laid beside each other as if alignment improved flavor. Doug cut the chicken into small exact pieces. He ate two and one green bean. After that the food expanded in his mouth and would not go down until he pushed water after it.

"So are you gonna do the toast?" Brent asked.

"No," Doug said.

"Not the actual toast," Brent said. "I just mean that version of you."

Doug set down his fork. "There wasn't really a version. There was an event."

Brent looked at him for a second, then laughed because he thought maybe that was the joke. Carla joined him out of courtesy. Doug did not.

"I love that," Carla said. "You're already in it."

"No," Doug said again.

Across the table, a man with a loosened tie told a story about a doctor in Terre Haute who kept spelling ibuprofen with two Bs on every lunch-order form. He did the doctor's voice in a way that was not especially good, and the whole table laughed anyway, not because the story was better than the pool-and-roses claim or because the voice was better than the story deserved, but because they had already agreed to receive him. Doug listened with the attention of a man watching someone assemble a device he had been told was simple.

More people stopped by the table to say hello. A woman from Louisville touched his shoulder and said, "My sister made me watch your clip in the airport and I had mascara running down my face by boarding." A man from Toledo told him, "You don't even need material, man. Just be yourself." Another asked if he could get a selfie for his wife and then, once the photo was taken, said, "You're gonna kill," in the tone people used at funerals when telling a family the deceased looked peaceful.

By the time dessert arrived, Doug had become a small station in the room's pre-show circuitry. People came by, spoke at him, took what they wanted from the exchange, and moved on. He saw the screens every time he looked up. The still image from the wedding stayed above the stage while people scraped cheesecake with dessert forks and checked email under the table and nodded along to conversations about market share. He could not stop looking at the photograph. Whoever had chosen it had frozen him at the exact moment he seemed most certain of himself.

Then Brian went onstage to hand out sales awards. One winner thanked caffeine and fear. Another thanked her district for not quitting on her in Q1. The room laughed hard at both of them. They were bad remarks, Doug thought, but bad in a way the room already knew how to love. Every laugh came quickly, with no negotiation. Doug sat with his hands in his lap and watched what ease looked like at scale.

Denise appeared beside his chair while someone at the podium was still saying "circle back." "Five minutes," she said quietly.

Carla touched Doug's forearm. "Go kill."

Doug stood. "I don't think that's the metric," he said, but nobody heard him because the room was applauding another plaque.

He followed Denise back through the service corridor to the green room.

Doug sat in the green room. It was beige and barely a room, just a partitioned section of backstage with a folding table, four chairs, a mirror ringed with bulbs, and the fruit plate Denise had warned him about. Three of the bulbs were dead. The grapes had moved through ripe and past resigned into something approaching geological. He didn't touch them.

He could hear the ballroom through the wall. Four hundred people, their collective sound arriving as a generalized hum — the same frequency as any crowd in any large room, many conversations producing a single indistinct noise. It was like standing next to a machine he couldn't see. He tried to pick out individual voices and couldn't. They were all one thing. In a few minutes he would stand in front of that thing and try to be funny, and it would either laugh or it would produce the other sound, the one that was patience disguised as silence.

He took the pages out of his jacket pocket. The material. Twenty-five minutes, printed on three sheets in twelve-point type because Simone had told him he shouldn't need to squint. He looked at the first line. He knew the first line. He knew every line. The problem was not the lines.

His phone buzzed. A text from Simone: *Don't rush the opening. Let the room come to you.*

He typed: *OK.*

A second text: *You know this material. Trust it.*

He put the phone facedown on the table. Through the wall, the crowd hummed. Someone laughed — a big laugh that carried through the partition and arrived in the green room as a muffled bark. It was the sound of someone being funny in front of those four hundred people, and that someone was Brian the VP of sales getting one last laugh before the introduction, or someone at a table telling a story about their regional numbers, or a man doing an impression of his boss, or any of the thousands of small human interactions that produced laughter without effort or preparation or fourteen rehearsals between a bathroom and a kitchen.

The fruit plate sat under the fluorescent light, the melon edges glossed over. Doug picked up a grape and squeezed it between his thumb and forefinger. It did not give.

---

When Brian went back onstage for the introduction, he had the energy of someone who had been told to keep it brief and resented the instruction. He wore a suit jacket over a polo shirt, which Doug noticed because it was a choice that committed fully to neither formality nor casualness and achieved a third thing that was worse than both. Brian did six minutes of remarks about regional performance metrics and market penetration trends and then pivoted to the introduction with the practiced ease of a man reading from a teleprompter while pretending he wasn't.

"So, uh, we've got a really special treat tonight," Brian said, and Doug, standing behind the stage-left partition, could hear the speech through a monitor speaker mounted on the wall. "I think most of you have probably seen the video — I mean, who hasn't, right? Eighty million views, the whole thing. And I just wanna say, as someone who gave a best man speech at my brother's wedding that, uh, did NOT go viral — I've got nothing but respect." Laughter. Brian was getting more laughs in his introduction than Doug was going to get in his entire set, and Doug knew it before Brian finished the sentence. The evidence was already organized. He just hadn't written it up yet.

"Please welcome, the man, the legend, the guy your wife has been showing you on her phone — Doug Bridge!"

Doug walked out.

The applause was genuine and warm, the sound of four hundred people who had already decided they liked him. The lights were brighter than the green room or his apartment or anywhere he had ever practiced, and they turned the audience into a dark, applauding mass with individual faces visible only in the first three rows. The mic was heavier than the one Simone had borrowed for their sessions at his kitchen table. It was warm from Brian's hand. Doug held it and walked to the center of the stage and the applause faded and the room waited.

"Thank you," Doug said. "Thanks for, uh. Thanks."

Good start.

The first three rows came into focus. Front row: a table of eight, conference badges still on, water glasses and wine glasses in various states of empty. A man with a red tie who was already smiling, committed to the evening. A woman with reading glasses pushed up on her forehead who looked ready to laugh. These were friendly faces. Doug picked the man in the red tie and aimed the next line at him. Simone's instruction had been simple: find one person and talk to them.

"So Layla — that's my manager, which is still a weird sentence — told me this was a pharmaceutical sales conference." Pause. The room waited. "I work in insurance. So we're both in the business of meeting people right before or right after disappointment."

A laugh. Small. More relief than pleasure. It was enough to let Doug take one more step.

"You people get them at hope," he said. "I get them in a damp basement with a ruined carpet and a Shop-Vac going. You say improved outcomes. I say mildew. My ex-wife used to say I approached every conversation like I was taking a recorded statement."

He waited because there was supposed to be a little turn there, just enough time for them to find it.

"If you've ever had an adjuster tell you he'll circle back after reviewing the policy," Doug said, "that means he's going to disappoint you in a more organized way."

Nothing. A fork clicked against a plate somewhere off to Doug's right. The man in the red tie kept smiling. The woman with the reading glasses slid them down onto her nose.

Doug moved the mic to his left hand and reached for the water on the podium. The rim of the glass hit his front teeth. He set it down too hard, and the thunk came back through the speakers larger than the mistake deserved.

He moved into the claims section, the one Simone had liked best.

"I had a file in Westerville," he said. "Above-ground pool wall split at two in the morning. Six thousand gallons into the neighbor's yard. The neighbor came outside in boxers and dress shoes. That's how fast it was. First thing I had to do was assess the damage. Fence. Patio furniture. Seventeen rosebushes. These men had been friends for eleven years. The flood didn't end it. The rosebushes ended it. Because weather would've covered the roses. If rain kills them, everybody stays friends. If your buddy's pool does it, now you're hearing the phrase third-party structural failure."

A few people laughed at the phrase. A table in the middle gave him a little more than that. Then a woman in the third row — younger, blazer, conference lanyard — looked down at her phone. She didn't pick it up. She just checked it once, like she was verifying an exit. It lasted a second. Doug saw it and it was louder than anything else happening.

He was rushing now. He could hear the set narrowing. The pauses Simone had built into it collapsed first. Then the transitions. He skipped the section about depreciation, his bridge out of the claims material and into the personal stuff, and the whole set lurched with him.

"I live alone in a two-bedroom apartment," he said too fast. "That is an optimistic amount of apartment. The second bedroom has been full of unopened boxes for twelve years. I eat cereal for dinner because there's no cleanup. You pour it, you stand at the counter, you assess the damage, and then the night is over."

A little air moved through the room on "optimistic amount of apartment." Then silverware started again. A server in a black vest slipped between two tables with a tray of coffee cups, using Doug's silence like cover.

"I bought curtains for the second bedroom in 2018," Doug said. "They're still in the Target bag. Apparently the boxes didn't need privacy as bad as I thought."

No one laughed. His free hand had gone to the folded pages in his jacket pocket. He kept smoothing the same crease with his thumb as if the paper were something he could flatten back into usefulness.

"My friend Roy uses the word adequate the way other people use excellent," Doug said. "He bought a lawn mower ten years ago and still talks about it with mild respect."

Nothing there either.

"That's most of adulthood past fifty anyway," Doug said. "You wake up, you assess the damage, you see what's still standing."

A man near the back coughed. Real cough, throat-clearing, probably just convention-center air. In the silence after Doug's line it took over the room. The sound carried the same warning Doug heard behind wet drywall. Small signal. Bigger problem underneath.

At a table near the doors, two men bent toward each other and started talking in voices they must have believed were discreet. A woman by the center aisle gathered her tote bag and eased her chair back while Doug was still speaking. She ducked her head in apology to no one specific and kept going.

He heard himself repeat "assess the damage" and the second use came back dead, already used up. He had six minutes left, including the Roy section Simone liked and the closer built around a coverage gap. The man in the red tie had stopped smiling. The woman with the reading glasses had her arms crossed now. The phone woman had put her phone away. The ballroom held still.

"I think that's probably enough," Doug said.

Someone in the second row started clapping. The rest joined in, one after another, until the whole ballroom was doing what manners required. By the time Doug reached the stage steps, the applause was already breaking apart into chair scrape, glass clink, and the first relieved rise of table talk.

Doug said, "Thank you," and walked offstage.

Backstage, Denise was waiting with a water bottle. "Great job," she said too fast and too bright, the verdict already written before the evidence was in.

"Thanks," Doug said.

From the ballroom, Brian's voice came back over the speakers, already thanking everyone for a record quarter and reminding them about the morning shuttle.

"Brian's gonna do a couple minutes of wrap-up and then we're done for the night," Denise said. "There's a shuttle to the hotel at nine-fifteen if you need it."

Doug did not need the shuttle. He needed to be in his car, alone, driving east on I-70 toward Columbus, but it was nearly nine and he had a room at the Holiday Inn Express on West Washington Street and a pizza he'd ordered on his phone during Brian's introduction, which meant the pizza would arrive before he did, which meant it would be sitting in a hallway getting cold. He gave that problem his full attention because the real one was still unmanageable.

---

The Holiday Inn Express on West Washington Street was four stories of beige siding with a parking lot that was three-quarters empty. Doug's room was on the third floor. The hallway smelled of cleaning solution, carpet adhesive, and the faint residual presence of everyone who had ever walked it with a keycard and a reason for not being home.

The pizza was outside his door, in a box on the carpet. He brought it in. It was from a place called Tony's Original that he'd found on his phone while Brian was talking about Q2 sales targets. The pizza was cold. The cheese had set into one sheet. The crust bent only at the tip. Doug ate two slices standing at the bolted desk. Adequate. Roy's word.

The room had a king bed, a desk built into the wall, a television mounted at an angle that suggested theft prevention, and a bedspread with overlapping brown diamonds and a green the manufacturer probably called sage. The pattern was inoffensive. Doug sat on the bed. The mattress gave exactly enough.

He sat there. The clock on the nightstand said 9:47. A lamp was bolted to the nightstand. Reasonable policy.

His phone had two texts from Layla. The first, from 8:41, said *How'd it go???* The second, from 9:18, said *Call me when you've decompressed. We'll adjust.* There was nothing from Simone. The absence of Simone's name felt more accurate than either text.

In Columbus, Roy would be getting ready for bed. Roy went to bed at 9:45 every night. He'd mentioned it once and left it there.

Doug took out his phone and called Roy. It rang four times.

"Hello."

"It's Doug."

"I know. Your name comes up."

"Right." Doug paused. "Did I wake you up?"

"No. I was reading. There's a book about the Dust Bowl that's better than you'd expect."

"Huh."

"How'd it go?"

He pressed his thumb into one of the brown diamonds on the bedspread. The pizza box sat on the desk. The lamp and television were bolted down, and the desk itself was part of the wall. Everything in the room had been fastened except him.

"Bad," he said.

"Bad how?"

"Bad like everyone was polite about it after."

Roy was quiet for a moment. Doug heard a page turn. Then the house went still again.

"Polite is worse than bad," Roy said.

"Yeah."

"You know where you are with bad."

Doug didn't say anything.

"I stopped at nineteen minutes," he said, after a while. "The set was twenty-five. I got to nineteen and told them that was probably enough."

"Was it?"

"Was it enough? I don't know. It was enough for me. I don't think they would've fought for six more minutes of what I was giving them."

"Then nineteen was the right number."

Doug lay back on the bed. The pillow had been flattened by years of other heads. He stared at the ceiling. It was stippled. A water stain sat in the far corner, brown at the edges. Old damage. Painted over. No active leak. Someone had fixed the sightline and left the structure alone. The most common mistake in residential repair.

"I got paid fifteen thousand dollars tonight," Doug said.

"For nineteen minutes."

"The contract was for thirty. The set was twenty-five. I did nineteen."

"That's still a lot of money for nineteen minutes."

"Seven hundred and eighty-nine dollars a minute. I did the math in the parking garage."

"Of course you did."

They were quiet for a few seconds. Doug could hear Roy breathing, steady and unhurried.

"Are you driving back tonight?"

"No. I've got a hotel room. Holiday Inn."

"How is it?"

The air conditioner rattled once and kept going. The lamp was bolted down. The television was bolted down. The desk was fixed to the wall. The pizza box lid had gone translucent with grease. The bedspread pattern looked like it had surrendered during its own design process.

"It's a Holiday Inn in Indianapolis," Doug said.

"Right."

They sat on the phone. At Mutual Fidelity they could work side by side for ten minutes without speaking and neither man would mistake it for strain. This felt familiar. It was adequate.

"Roy."

"Yeah."

"Simone and I built twenty-five minutes that make sense on paper. Three sections. Callbacks. A closer. I can do all of it in my kitchen. Then I put four hundred people in front of it and the whole thing comes back denied."

"What thing?"

"The thing from the video. The thing they came for. They came to see the guy from the toast and I can't — " Doug stopped. He picked up the cold pizza crust from the nightstand where he'd set it and looked at it and put it down again. "I can't do it on purpose. The video was — I was drunk. I was angry about Brendan's toast being so goddamn perfect and I said things I never meant to say out loud. You can't rehearse that. You can't put it in a set list."

"No."

"Simone knows that too. She knows the sections are in the right order and the room still doesn't want them. But she doesn't know what else to try, and I don't know what else to try, and tonight I stood up in front of four hundred people and did the material and it was like — " In the far corner, a water stain sat under a layer of paint that fixed the sightline and nothing else. "It was like filing a claim you know is going to be denied. All the documentation is right, and the forms are filled out, and the loss is real, and you submit it and you know it's coming back rejected because the underlying thing isn't covered. The structure is sound and the answer is still no."

"You should get some sleep," Roy said.

"Yeah."

"Drive back in the morning. Don't drive tonight."

"I won't."

A pause. Then Roy said, "Doug."

"Yeah."

"Nineteen minutes is nineteen more than most people manage."

Doug wasn't sure if this was comfort or observation or just Roy's way of placing a fact in its correct location before closing the file. With Roy, the distinction was often academic.

"Good night, Roy."

"Night."

Doug hung up and set his phone on the nightstand next to the bolted lamp. He took off his shoes and dress shirt and lay on the bedspread in his undershirt and pants. The remote was under his hip. He left it there.

He thought about the woman checking her phone. The cough. Brian saying *the man, the legend*. He thought about the still image from the wedding on the projection screens, frozen into certainty he had never possessed sober. At the wedding, nobody had been braced for him, including him. Tonight everybody had.

Outside the window, traffic moved along West Washington Street. The air conditioner rattled and caught. The fifteen thousand dollars would arrive by direct deposit within fourteen business days.

After a minute he pulled back the covers and got under them.

Down the hall, the ice machine dumped a load into its bin. No one came for it.
