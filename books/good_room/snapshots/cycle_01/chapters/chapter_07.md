# Chapter 7

Simone Barr arrived on a Tuesday at ten in the morning, which was the time they'd agreed on, and this fact alone distinguished her from every person Doug had dealt with since the video. Layla was always twenty minutes late or forty minutes early. Charlie Falk had called twice at 6 a.m. Pacific, which was 9 a.m. in Columbus and therefore, in his mind, reasonable. Simone rang the buzzer at 9:59 and was standing in the hallway when Doug opened the door at 10:01, holding a laptop bag over one shoulder and a paper cup of coffee that wasn't from anywhere he recognized.

She was shorter than he'd expected, though he didn't know what he'd expected. He'd Googled her the night before and found a headshot on a management site — at least five years old, different hair — and a few credits listed on IMDb without photos. Three late-night shows. He didn't know which ones. He didn't watch late-night.

"Doug," she said. Not a question.

"Yeah. Come in."

She stepped inside and looked around the apartment the way Doug looked at a water-damaged house before he opened the claim file — measuring, not judging, but in a way where the measurement itself was a kind of judgment. She didn't comment on the Ikea bookshelf or the art print Megan had left behind or the cereal bowl he'd forgotten to wash that morning. She set her bag on the kitchen table, sat down, pulled the lid off her coffee, and blew on it once.

"You want some coffee?" Doug asked. "I've got a —"

"I'm good." She took a sip. Her nails were short and uneven, trimmed without a mirror, and her left thumb had a callus along the side from years of pressing a pen too hard. No rings. Her hair was pulled up with a clip that was handling maybe sixty percent of its job. She wore a gray sweater with a small hole near the cuff of the left sleeve that she hadn't fixed or hidden, just wore, the way you wear something when you've stopped noticing it. Doug noticed it.

He sat across from her. The kitchen table was small enough that their knees nearly touched, and he moved his chair back an inch. Simone didn't register this.

"So," Doug said.

"So." She opened her bag and took out not a laptop but a yellow legal pad, the kind you could buy at any office supply store for three dollars. She also took out a pen — black, nothing special — and placed them on the table beside the coffee. She didn't write anything. She looked at Doug.

"How does this work?" he asked.

"We talk. I listen. Sometimes I write things down."

"What things?"

"Things that work."

"How will you know?"

"I'll know."

Doug didn't know what to do with his hands. He put them on the table, then in his lap, then back on the table. The refrigerator hummed. Outside, a car passed with a bad muffler — the scrape of metal on asphalt — and he thought, automatically, that if the muffler detached and struck another vehicle the liability would fall to the city for road maintenance, not the driver, unless the driver had been previously cited for the exhaust condition. This was not a useful thought. He had it anyway.

"Tell me something funny," Simone said.

Doug looked at her.

"Funny," he said.

"Yeah."

He thought about it. He was aware of himself thinking about it, which made it worse — the same problem as trying to fall asleep by concentrating on falling asleep. He thought about jokes he knew. He didn't know many. Roy had told him one last month about a horse walking into a bar, and Doug couldn't remember the punchline, only that it involved the horse ordering a specific drink and the bartender either commenting or not commenting on the horse being a horse. He couldn't remember which version was the joke.

The silence went on.

Simone didn't fill it. She sat with her coffee and her legal pad and her pen and she waited. She had the patience of someone who had watched a lot of people sit in exactly this chair and produce exactly this silence. The quiet passed through uncomfortable into something worse — a zone where the silence itself had become the subject, where every second that passed without Doug saying something funny was another second of evidence that he could not say something funny, evidence that was accumulating in the record like unanswered interrogatories.

He looked at the kitchen window. The glass needed cleaning. There was a smudge at eye level that had been there since he'd leaned his forehead against it during a phone call with Layla three weeks ago, and he kept meaning to wipe it and kept not wiping it, and by now the smudge had become a feature of the window rather than a failure of maintenance.

He cleared his throat.

"I don't really — I don't have anything."

"I didn't ask you to prepare anything. I asked you to tell me something funny."

"That's the same thing."

"It's not."

Doug looked at the blank legal pad. The blankness felt personal, like a claim form with no entries — not absence of proof, but proof of absence.

"Okay," Simone said. She shifted in the chair. "Tell me about your day yesterday."

"My day."

"Yeah. What happened."

"I went to work."

"So tell me about work."

Doug had been telling people about work for twenty years and nobody had seemed interested, including, on many days, Doug himself. But Simone was sitting there with her pen and her pad and the clip in her hair slowly losing its grip, and she was waiting, and he didn't have anything else to offer.

"There's a claim I've been working on," he said. "Homeowner in Westerville. He had an above-ground pool in his backyard. Intex, twenty-four feet, the ones with the metal frame. They retail around seven hundred dollars. He'd had it three summers."

Simone didn't write anything. She held her coffee.

"The wall failed. Middle of the night, nobody outside. September, so the pool was still full — about fifteen thousand gallons. All of it went sideways into the neighbor's yard."

"Okay."

"The neighbor had roses. Hybrid teas. Seventeen plants. She'd been growing them for eleven years. She had receipts for the original plants, the fertilizer, the trellises, the pruning equipment, the soil amendments, the pH testing kits. She had photographs from every June going back to 2013. She had a log of every bloom cycle."

Doug paused. He'd said "log" and the word had a weight he didn't expect, sitting in his kitchen with a woman he'd known for twelve minutes. Everything he was describing — the documentation, the receipts, the careful recording of something that mattered to the person who kept the records — sounded like something else when he heard it out loud. He moved on.

"Total documented loss, forty-two hundred dollars. That's the plants, the infrastructure, and the lost growing season. She filed a claim."

"On whose policy?"

Doug looked at her. Nobody asked that. People always asked what happened to the roses.

"That's exactly the problem," he said. "She filed on her own homeowner's policy. But her policy covers weather events, fire, vandalism, accidental damage from covered perils. A neighbor's pool wall failing isn't weather. It's not vandalism. It's a third-party structural failure from a product with a known defect in the frame connectors."

"So she's not covered."

"Not under her own policy. The pool owner — his liability coverage has a sublimit on property damage that caps at two thousand, and even that's arguable because the policy language says 'sudden and accidental discharge.' You could make a case that a wall failing from a known manufacturing defect isn't accidental, it's negligence. And once you're in negligence territory, his insurer might deny the whole thing."

Simone's coffee had stopped moving. She was watching him with an expression he couldn't read — not boredom, not the polite attention people gave when Doug talked about insurance at parties, which was part of why he'd stopped going to parties.

"So you've got a woman with forty-two hundred dollars in dead roses and no coverage path. His insurance might deny, hers definitely denies, the manufacturer has a class action pending but it won't resolve for three years, and this woman is standing in her yard looking at seventeen dead hybrid teas and she calls me — she calls me personally, because I handled her kitchen fire claim in 2019 and she trusts me — and she says, 'Doug, someone owes me for my roses.'"

He stopped. He didn't know why he'd told this story. It wasn't a bit. It wasn't material. It was the kind of problem that made his job interesting — the jurisdictional tangle that most people found deadly boring and Doug found genuinely absorbing, the way a puzzle is absorbing when all the pieces exist but none of them fit the same picture.

Simone laughed.

Not a big laugh. It started as a breath through her nose and then her mouth opened slightly and her shoulders moved and there was a sound that was, unmistakably, a person being amused.

Doug stared at her.

"Why are you laughing? It's a real claim. She lost eleven years of roses."

"I know."

"There's no coverage path. I spent three days on the phone with two adjusters and a claims attorney trying to find one. It's a genuine jurisdictional problem."

"I know it is."

"So I don't understand what's funny."

Simone was still almost smiling — not broadly, just the residual expression of someone who had found something she'd been looking for in a place she hadn't expected. She picked up her pen for the first time and wrote something on the legal pad. Doug couldn't read it upside down. He thought he saw the word "roses" but it might have been something else.

"What are you writing?"

"Notes."

"About what?"

"About you."

Doug sat with that. The refrigerator hummed. The smudge on the window caught the thin spring light and held it.

"I still wasn't trying to be funny," he said.

"I know," Simone said. "That's the whole thing."

---

They met four times that first week. Tuesday through Friday, same table, same time. Simone brought her own coffee every morning — always a paper cup, always black, always from somewhere different, as if she were conducting a private survey of every coffee shop in Clintonville. Doug offered to make some the second day and she said, "I don't trust anyone else's coffee," and Doug understood this immediately because he didn't trust other people's claims documentation either, not until he'd verified the source.

The sessions had a pattern, though it took Doug until Thursday to see it. Simone would ask him something. Doug would try to answer the way he thought a comedian should — broader, louder in some unspecifiable way, with a setup and a turn and something at the end that was supposed to make a person laugh. And it would die. Not spectacularly. Not in a way that felt like failure so much as absence — the energy would leave the room the way air leaves a tire, slow and total, and Simone would sit there watching it go with the expression of someone who'd known it would happen and had let it happen anyway because the letting was part of the process.

Then she'd ask something else, or wait, or look out the window at the street, and Doug would forget that he was supposed to be performing and start talking like a person — like Doug — and something would happen. Not every time. Not on command. But enough that Simone's legal pad was filling up, page by page, with things he'd said that she considered worth recording and he considered just talking.

Wednesday she asked about his apartment.

"What about it?"

"Describe it for me."

"Two bedrooms. Kitchen. Bathroom. There's a bookshelf in the living room, the Ikea one."

"What's on it?"

Doug looked at the bookshelf from the table. He could see the spines from where he sat. "A book about the history of the insurance industry that I got at a library sale. I think I paid two dollars. A novel Roy lent me in July that I haven't started. The user manual for my dishwasher."

"Why's the dishwasher manual on the bookshelf?"

"The drawer where I keep manuals is full."

"What's in the drawer?"

"The manual for the microwave. The manual for the TV. The manual for a blender I don't own anymore. I donated the blender to Goodwill in 2018 but I kept the manual because it was filed with the others and removing it would've left a gap in the folder."

Simone wrote something down. Doug watched her write it and felt the specific discomfort of someone finding the ordinary facts of his life notable. He kept the manual for a blender he'd given away eight years ago. That was filing. That was consistency of records. He didn't see what else it was.

"What's the second bedroom?" she asked.

"Office."

"You work in there?"

"No."

"Computer?"

"No."

"What's in it?"

"Boxes."

Simone waited. Doug recognized the technique — the silence that people fill because people can't stand silence, the interviewer's move of leaving a space the subject will step into. He'd done it himself, sitting across from policyholders who'd left something out of the paperwork, waiting for the omitted detail to surface on its own. He knew what Simone was doing. He walked into it anyway.

"They're from the move," he said. "Twelve years ago. I unpacked most of the apartment but the second bedroom — I put the boxes in there and shut the door. I haven't opened them."

"You don't know what's in them?"

"I know what I use. I haven't needed whatever's in those boxes in twelve years. If you don't file a claim within the policy period, you lose — " He stopped. He'd done it again. He'd answered a question about his life in insurance language, as if the boxes in the second bedroom were subject to a statute of limitations and twelve years of not opening them constituted an expired filing window.

Simone didn't laugh this time. She wrote something long — two lines, maybe three — and Doug had the feeling that whatever she'd written mattered more than the roses.

Thursday she asked about Roy.

"He's my friend. We work together."

"Tell me about him."

"He's been at Mutual Fidelity longer than me. I don't know exactly how long because he doesn't talk about it. He eats the same sandwich every day at lunch. Turkey, mustard, no tomato. He's gotten that sandwich for the entire nine years I've known him. I asked him once if he'd ever thought about trying something else on the menu and he said he'd found what he liked and didn't see any reason to keep looking."

"And?"

"And nothing. He said it and ate his sandwich."

"But you remembered it."

Doug shifted in the chair. "I remember things people say. It's — you have to, in claims work. Policyholders tell you things and you need to know which details matter later."

"So Roy's sandwich order matters later."

"I didn't say it matters. I said I remembered it."

But it did matter, and Simone either knew this or guessed it, because she wrote it down and then looked at him with the expression she'd been wearing all week — diagnostic, appraising, the look of someone identifying a pattern in a data set rather than sharing a moment with a person. It bothered Doug. Not because it was unkind but because it was accurate. She was treating him the way he treated a claim file with documentation that didn't quite add up — looking for the discrepancy, the thing present in the evidence but missing from the narrative, the gap that would explain why the numbers didn't reconcile.

Friday, something shifted.

"I've been thinking about this," Doug said. He'd been thinking about it all week, sitting across from Simone, failing to be funny on purpose, watching her write down the things he said when he wasn't trying. "I should watch some comedy. Study it. How people build a set, the structure, the rhythm. I should learn the form."

Simone put her pen down. She did this when she was about to say something she'd been holding. The pen going flat on the table was a signal Doug had already learned to read, like Roy's long pause before answering — the tell that the next thing would carry weight.

"Don't," she said.

"Why not?"

"Because you'll try to do what they do."

"I know I can't do what they do. I'm talking about the mechanics. The structure."

"The mechanics aren't your problem."

"Then what is?"

Simone leaned back. The hair clip had come loose on one side again and she reached up and fixed it without looking, the automatic gesture of someone who'd fixed the same clip a thousand times. Through the kitchen window, the street was gray and damp, the trees along the sidewalk showing the first cautious suggestion of leaves — not quite committed, the way Columbus handled the transition out of winter, a slow negotiation between seasons where neither side fully conceded until sometime in May.

"You think funny is something people do," she said. "A skill. A performance. You watch someone do a tight set on TV and you think, that's what funny looks like, and I don't look like that. And you're right, you don't look like that."

"Right, so —"

"I'm not done. The toast worked because you weren't doing anything. You were standing up drunk saying true things. No form. No structure. Just a man with too much whiskey saying what he'd been carrying for twenty years, and the room could tell. That's why eighty million people watched it."

"So what, I need to drink before every show?"

"You need to not be trying." She picked up her coffee cup, looked into it, found it empty. "Which is the hardest thing I can ask you, because every time you sit at this table and try to be funny for me it dies. And every time you forget you're trying and just talk about your life — the roses, the boxes, Roy's sandwich — something happens. And you don't believe me."

She was right. He didn't believe her. The things she was calling material — a coverage dispute, a blender manual, a man who'd eaten the same sandwich for nine years — weren't funny. They were his life. They were the ordinary mechanics of a man who lived alone and worked in insurance and had organized his existence around the principle that things should be documented, filed, and kept in order. That wasn't comedy. That was procedure.

"The special is supposed to be sixty minutes," Doug said. "I can't go up there and talk about filing systems for an hour."

"Why not?"

"Because that's not what they're paying for. They want the toast."

"The toast was seven minutes of you talking about your life. Not a bit, not a routine — your marriage, your daughter, the table, twenty years of things you'd never said out loud. Your life. The filing systems, the roses, the sandwich — it's the same material. Different room."

"It's not the same."

"Why?"

"Because the toast was — " Doug stopped. He didn't know how to finish the sentence. The toast was what? Real? He was real right now, sitting at his kitchen table with a comedy writer and a dripping faucet and a legal pad full of things he'd said that she thought were worth something and he thought were just how he talked. But it wasn't the same. The toast had been reckless. A man with no guardrails saying things that cost him something — his dignity, his privacy, his daughter's wedding reception. This was careful. This was Doug being Doug, and Doug being Doug had never, in fifty-two years, been enough to hold anyone's attention for more than the time it took to process their claim and send them the check.

"Can't you just write me material?" he said. "Jokes. Bits. Something I can rehearse."

Simone set the empty cup on the table. "I could write you ten minutes of solid, professional stuff by Monday. Setups, tags, callbacks. Tight, structured, the works."

"Okay. Good."

"And it would be dead. Because it wouldn't sound like you. It'd sound like a writer's version of you, and the audience would feel the gap the way you feel a crack in a windshield — you can't say exactly why it bothers you, but something's wrong and you can't stop looking at it."

She paused. She flipped the legal pad back a few pages, read something she'd written on Tuesday. Her lips moved slightly as she read — a habit, Doug thought, of someone who'd spent twenty years turning speech into text and text back into speech, the constant translation between what people say and what works on paper and whether those ever quite line up.

"You're not a comedian, Doug."

"Yeah. I know."

"No — you know it as a failing. You hear 'you're not a comedian' and you hear 'I can't do this thing everyone wants me to do.' I'm saying something different. You're not a comedian the way a documentary isn't a feature film. It's not a lesser thing. It's a different thing. The way you talk about roses like they're a tort case, the way you keep a manual for a blender you gave to Goodwill — that's a voice. It's not a comedy voice. It's your voice. And it happens to be funny, and you can't see it because you've spent your whole life watching other people be funny and none of them sounded like you."

Doug looked at his hands on the table. He had the hands of a man who typed claim reports and occasionally tightened the bolts on a kitchen faucet and did nothing else that required physical skill or dexterity. The faucet, in fact, had been dripping for two weeks — a forty-cent washer, a fifteen-minute fix. He hadn't done it because the drip had become part of the apartment's operating noise, like the refrigerator hum and the radiator's occasional tick and the specific silence of a room where one person lives and does not expect visitors.

He wanted to believe her. He wanted to believe that the way he described coverage gaps and blender manuals and boxes he hadn't opened in twelve years was material — that the specific way he moved through the world, documenting and filing and assessing, was itself the thing that would fill sixty minutes on a stage. But he'd watched the toast video dozens of times now, and the man in that video was not this man. That man was out of control. This man — sitting at the kitchen table with a dripping faucet and a clean window and a legal pad full of someone else's observations about his personality — was the opposite of out of control. This man had spent his entire adult life making sure nothing got out of control, and it had worked, mostly, in the sense that nothing had, and also in the sense that nothing had.

"I'll think about it," he said.

"Sure." Simone closed the legal pad. She put the pen in her bag. She stood and put on her jacket — olive-green canvas, worn soft at the elbows, the kind of jacket that had gone in and out of style enough times that it had given up and just become a jacket. "Same time Monday?"

"Monday."

At the door she stopped and turned back. "The woman with the roses. Did she ever get her money?"

"Sixteen hundred. I found an umbrella policy on the pool owner that everybody'd missed. Not full recovery, but it was something."

"How'd she take it?"

"She said I was the only person who took it seriously."

"The claim?"

"The roses. She said I was the only person who took the roses seriously. There's a difference, she said, between taking the claim seriously and taking the roses seriously." He paused. "I didn't understand the distinction at the time."

"And now?"

Doug looked at Simone. She was standing in his doorway with her bag and her jacket and the clip in her hair that had held up better today than yesterday, and she was asking him a question that wasn't about comedy or material or the special, or maybe it was about all of those things and also none of them.

"I still don't," he said. "But I think about it."

Simone looked at him for a moment — not the diagnostic look, not the assessment. Something else, something he didn't have a filing category for. Then she left.

Doug sat at the table. The apartment returned to its default state — quiet, still, the sound of a space resuming the shape it held when nobody was watching it. He looked at the chair where Simone had been sitting. There was a faint impression in the cushion that would be gone by the time he got home from the office.

He washed the cereal bowl from that morning. He stood at the sink and looked out the window — the smudge was still there; he'd only imagined wiping it — and he thought about the woman in Westerville and her roses. How she'd made a distinction between the claim and the roses as though they were different things, and how, standing in his kitchen with the faucet dripping and the legal pad gone and the week's sessions sitting in him like something he hadn't figured out how to process yet, he thought maybe they were. The claim was the money. The roses were the eleven years she'd spent on her knees in the dirt, and the photographs every June, and the pH testing kits, and the fact that she'd built something alive and specific and irreplaceable and then fifteen thousand gallons of water had destroyed it overnight, and what she'd wanted from Doug wasn't sixteen hundred dollars. She'd wanted someone to understand what had actually been lost, and sixteen hundred dollars was as close as the system could get.

He picked up his keys. He drove to Mutual Fidelity. He had claims to process and Roy was expecting him for lunch and the sandwich place wouldn't change and neither would Roy's order and that was, for the moment, the only piece of his life that didn't seem to require him to be something he wasn't.
