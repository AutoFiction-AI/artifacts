# Chapter 15

The bar had high ceilings and the kind of exposed brick that meant it had been something else first. Doug counted fourteen beers on the chalkboard behind the counter while Simone ordered a bourbon neat. He picked one of the fourteen that sounded like a beer and not a statement, and the bartender, who had dark red nails and a silver ring on her thumb, poured it from a tap with a wooden handle.

Nine dollars. Doug did the math on what that would be annually if he came here twice a week, which was a thing he did with any purchase over six dollars. It was not a useful calculation. It was just what his brain did.

They sat at a high-top near the windows on tall stools that didn't have backs, which meant Doug had to decide what to do with his posture. He chose upright. Simone settled onto hers the way she settled into anything — as if she'd already assessed the structural integrity and found it adequate.

She didn't have her notebook.

Doug had been watching Simone write in that notebook for almost three months. She wrote in pencil, quickly, in handwriting that tilted so far left it looked like the words were leaning away from a strong wind. She wrote things Doug said when he didn't know he was saying anything worth writing. She underlined things twice. She crossed things out with single precise lines. The notebook had been on his kitchen table at every session, and now it wasn't here, and the absence registered the way a missing page in a claim file registered — not alarming, but indicative of a different kind of meeting.

It was the last Tuesday in May. The light through the tall windows was long and warm and the sidewalk outside was full of people who didn't seem to be going anywhere in particular, which Doug supposed was what people did in the Short North on a warm evening. He'd been to this neighborhood four times in his life, counting tonight. Two of those times were to drive through it on the way to somewhere else.

"You look like someone who's never been to a bar," Simone said.

"I've been to bars."

"You look like you're appraising the property."

Doug looked at the windows, the tin ceiling, the long counter with its row of stools. "The ductwork's been painted but not insulated," he said. "You'd get condensation issues in winter."

Simone drank her bourbon. "That's exactly what I mean."

They ordered food. Simone got a salad with something on it Doug couldn't identify from the menu description — it said "shaved fennel" and he took this on faith. Doug got a burger. The waiter was a tall kid, early twenties, with the careful posture of someone who'd recently been told to stand up straighter, and he said "awesome" when Doug ordered, which was not a word Doug associated with a hamburger.

"How's your week been," Simone said, and it came out flat, more statement than question, the way things came out when two people had spent enough time together that the formal structures of conversation had worn smooth.

"Fine," Doug said. "Processed a hail claim. A woman in Reynoldsburg — her roof's twenty-three years old and she wants full replacement cost. The policy caps it at actual cash value for roofs over twenty."

"How does that work?"

Doug explained. He explained the depreciation schedule, the inspection requirements, the specific way you calculated the remaining useful life of a twenty-three-year-old asphalt shingle roof. Simone listened with the expression she wore when she was listening professionally, except this wasn't professional, which made Doug aware that he was talking about roof depreciation on what was either a dinner between colleagues or a dinner between two people who no longer had a reason to be colleagues.

He stopped. "Sorry."

"Don't apologize. That's the first interesting thing anyone's said to me today."

"You need to meet more people."

"I've met plenty of people. Most of them are funnier than you on purpose and less interesting."

Their food arrived. The burger was fine. Simone's salad had the fennel on it, which looked like translucent strips of something that didn't want to be eaten. She ate it with the steady, unbothered pace of a person who ate salads at bars and didn't consider this remarkable.

For a while they didn't talk. The bar was filling in around them — a Tuesday crowd, which in the Short North meant young people in clothes that appeared to have been chosen with intention, and a few older couples who had the comfortable silence of people who'd been married long enough that dinner was a proximity arrangement, not an event. Doug could hear the music now, something with a guitar and a woman's voice that was probably famous and that he would never be able to identify.

"I keep thinking about Detroit," Doug said.

"The heckler?"

"Not the heckler. The eight minutes after. When I was just talking about the drive."

Simone tilted her head. "What about it?"

"I wasn't doing anything. I wasn't performing. I was just describing what the drive was like. The coffee, the rain, the Holiday Inn. And the room got quiet."

"I know."

"But I can't do that for sixty minutes. I can do it for eight minutes when I've run out of everything else and a man's been shouting at me and I've got nothing left. That's not a plan."

"No," Simone said. "It's not."

She swirled the bourbon in her glass and looked at it like there was something she could read in the way the liquid settled. Doug watched her do this. He'd been watching Simone do things — small things, the way she held a pencil, the way she listened with her whole body tilted slightly forward, the way she pushed her hair behind her ear with her ring finger specifically — for three months, and the watching had shifted at some point from professional attention to something else. He didn't know when the change had happened. He suspected it was gradual, like depreciation — you didn't notice the value shifting until one day the numbers didn't match what you expected.

"Can I ask you something?" Doug said.

"Sure."

"Why'd you stop performing?"

Simone set her glass down. She didn't answer right away, which was unusual — Simone answered things quickly, the way someone who'd spent two decades in writers' rooms had learned to, where silence meant you agreed with whatever had just been said and your joke was going in the trash.

"I didn't know you knew I performed," she said.

"Layla mentioned it. When she was telling me about you. She said you'd done the circuit."

"Layla said that?"

"Those were her words."

Simone picked up her bourbon again but didn't drink it. She held it near her chin, and Doug could smell the whiskey from across the table, sweet and sharp, and he thought about how his own relationship with alcohol had become a negotiation since the toast — one beer, always one, always counted, always the awareness of what happened the last time he'd stopped counting.

"I did stand-up for eleven years," Simone said. "Improv for three of those. Some sketch. Chicago first, then New York."

"Were you good?"

"Technically, yeah. I could write a set. I could work a room. I understood structure — where to put the turn, how to build a callback, how to use silence. All the craft stuff."

"But."

"I was miserable." She pushed a piece of fennel to the edge of her plate with her fork. "And I don't mean nervous — nervous is fine, nervous goes away after the first minute if you know what you're doing. I mean miserable. Like the whole time I was up there, I could feel myself standing next to myself, watching. This person at the microphone doing an impression of a comedian, hitting the marks, getting the laughs, and the real me was three feet to the left thinking, this isn't you, none of this is you."

Doug said nothing. He was still on his stool, the way he went still when a claimant started telling him the real story instead of the one they'd prepared.

"The laughs were the worst part," Simone said. "Because they were real. The audience was laughing at material that worked, and the material worked because I knew how to build it, and I still felt like I was stealing something. Like the laughs belonged to whoever I was pretending to be up there, and I'd go home and it would be quiet and I'd be just me again and the funny was gone." She said this looking at her glass, not at Doug. There was a small crease between her eyebrows that he'd seen before — she got it when she was concentrating on a note she was writing — but he'd never seen it aimed at her own speech. She was concentrating on being honest, and the effort was visible. "So I started writing for other people. And it was a relief. I could hear what was funny about someone else and put it in their mouth, and when it worked it was theirs. Not mine. I didn't have to stand there and own it."

"How long have you been writing?"

"Twenty years, give or take. Three late-night shows. A pilot that never aired. A bunch of corporate stuff I'd rather not get into. Two podcasts." She paused. "Your material."

"My material doesn't work."

"It doesn't, no."

They sat with that for a moment. Outside, the light was finally softening, the sun dropping behind the buildings on the west side of High Street. The bar had gotten louder — someone had turned the music up or the people had gotten drunker or both. The bartender's silver ring tapped against a glass as she poured.

"I'm funnier on paper," Simone said. "You're funnier in person." She paused. "I don't know which is worse."

Doug thought about this. He thought about Simone in her twenties, in a comedy club in Chicago, delivering material that worked while some essential part of her stood three feet to the left, and he thought about himself on stage in Pittsburgh with his mind empty and eleven seconds of silence and the pool story that worked for ninety seconds before he lost the room, and the two images sat next to each other in his head like claims from the same weather event — different addresses, different damage, same storm.

"Probably the same amount of worse," he said.

Simone laughed. It was short, more breath than voice, and she covered her mouth with her hand and then took her hand away. "See, that's what I mean. You don't even know you did that."

"Did what?"

"Never mind."

The burger was gone and Simone was on her second bourbon and Doug was still on his one beer, now warm, which he drank anyway because leaving a beer unfinished was wasteful in a way he couldn't articulate but felt strongly about.

Simone was talking about a comedian she'd written for years ago — a woman whose timing was so precise that Simone could write a joke and know, within a beat, where the laugh would come. "She'd hit the word and the room would respond like she'd pressed a button. Every time. It was mechanical. It was perfect." She tore a piece of bread from the basket that had come with Doug's burger and that neither of them had touched until now. "And it was dead."

"Dead how?"

"Dead like — the laughs came in the right places but they didn't mean anything. Nobody left her shows different than they came in. They laughed and then they went home and it evaporated. Because she was asking for them. Every single joke was a request. Laugh here. Now here. Now here. And the audience complied because the craft was flawless, but compliance isn't the same thing as —" She stopped. She set the bread down. She picked up her glass and put it down without drinking.

"As what?" Doug said.

"Recognition." She was quiet for a second. "I think that's what it is. The laughs that actually matter — the ones that change the room — those aren't requested. They're involuntary. Something true gets said that nobody's supposed to say, and the laugh is the body's way of catching up to it. Like the body knows before the brain does. The social contract says don't say that, and somebody says it, and the laugh is the sound of everyone in the room recognizing it at the same time."

Doug felt something shift, the way the numbers in a claim sometimes shifted when you put the documentation in a different order and the whole sequence suddenly made sense. It wasn't new information. It was a new arrangement of information he'd already had.

"That's what the toast was," he said.

"Yeah."

"I was drunk enough that I didn't — the filter was gone. I wasn't asking anyone to laugh. I wasn't asking for anything. I was just saying things."

"Things you meant."

"Things I'd been not saying for twenty years. And everyone laughed because — because it was true, and you're not supposed to say those things at a wedding, and I said them anyway." He was talking slowly because he could feel the shape of the idea and didn't want to lose it. "And the reason the material doesn't work is that material is asking. The whole structure of a set is: here's a joke, please laugh. Even if the jokes are good. Even if they're honest. The structure itself is a request. And the toast never requested anything."

Simone was looking at him with the expression she'd worn the first day at his kitchen table when he'd told her the pool story — alert, still, the look she got when something was working. "That's it," she said.

"So I need to not ask."

"You need to not need."

Doug turned this over. It sounded right. It also sounded impossible, like telling someone to fall asleep — the trying was the obstacle. He thought about the eight minutes in Detroit, how he'd stumbled into it by running out of everything else, how the room had leaned in at the exact moment he had nothing left to offer them.

"That's the same thing my mother would say," Doug said. "Except she'd say it meaner and then ask if I was eating enough."

Simone laughed, the real kind, the kind that came from her chest and not her craft, and Doug watched it happen the way he watched weather data — not controlling it, not causing it, just observing the phenomenon and noting that it occurred.

"Your mother," Simone said, and shook her head.

"She told me the toast was the only real thing I'd said since I was twelve. She was eating a dinner roll when she said it."

"That's a good detail. The dinner roll."

"It's not a detail. She was eating a dinner roll."

"I know. That's why it's good."

They sat there. The bar was loud now, the Tuesday night crowd settled in, and Doug could hear someone at a table behind them talking about a television show with the passionate specificity that people reserved for things that didn't matter, and he could hear the bartender's ring against glass again, and he could feel the high-top's edge against his forearm and the stool's absence of a back and the warm air that came in every time someone opened the door to the sidewalk.

"The taping's in twelve days," he said.

"I know."

"I don't know what to do."

Simone didn't say anything for a while. She looked at the bread she'd torn and not eaten. Then she said, "I don't either. And I've been doing this for twenty years." She finished her bourbon. "At least you know what the problem is now. That's more than you knew in Cleveland."

"In Cleveland I was wearing a costume."

"You were. You were doing Mulaney-meets-insurance-adjuster. It was terrible."

"Thanks."

"You're welcome."

Doug signaled for the check. It came to forty-seven dollars. He put his card down. Simone reached for her wallet and he said, "I've got it," and she said, "You sure?" and he said, "You came all the way from New York to teach me to be funny and I'm not funny. Least I can do is buy you dinner."

"You're doing it again," she said.

"Doing what?"

"Being funny without knowing it."

"I'm telling you the truth."

"Exactly."

They stepped outside. The air was warm — the kind of warm where you realized your body had been bracing against cold for months and had finally stopped. High Street was lit up, storefronts and streetlights and the headlights of cars moving slowly through the foot traffic, people going to bars and restaurants and wherever people in the Short North went on a Tuesday in late May. Two women passed them going the other direction, one of them laughing hard at something the other had said, and the sound of it — unforced, involuntary — hung in the air for a moment like a demonstration of the thing they'd just been talking about.

They walked south. Simone had parked two blocks down, on a side street off High. Doug had parked on a meter around the corner from that. The walk was short and they didn't talk for most of it, and the silence between them had weight — not uncomfortable, not empty, but present. A thing they were both inside and aware of.

Doug could smell something blooming. He didn't know what it was. He wasn't a plant person. But it was there, sweet and out of nowhere, the way things showed up in late spring in Ohio when the temperature finally committed.

At Simone's car — a gray Subaru with New York plates and a coffee cup visible on the passenger seat — they stopped. Simone got her keys out. She held them in her hand without unlocking the door.

"Thank you for dinner," she said.

"Yeah."

"And for the roof depreciation lecture. That was genuinely interesting."

"It's a twenty-three-year-old roof. There's a conversation to be had."

Simone stood there. Doug stood there. The distance between them was about the width of the car's side mirror. Doug could hear the bar noise from the block behind them, and somewhere a car door closing, and his own breathing, which he noticed because he didn't usually notice it. He was aware of Simone's hand on her keys and the way she was looking at him — not the professional look, not the diagnostic look she used in sessions, but something he didn't have a category for, something that fell between the established coverage areas the way the pool and roses claim had fallen between the pool owner's liability and the neighbor's weather provision. A gap. Not a failure of the system. Just a place the policies hadn't anticipated.

It was there. Whatever it was, it was there, in the two feet of sidewalk between the side mirror and where Doug stood, and they both knew it the way you know a storm is coming before the rain starts — the pressure in the air, the shift. The everything that comes before the thing itself.

Neither of them moved, not toward each other and not away. They stood in it for a moment, and the moment was long enough to be real and short enough to not require a decision, and then Simone said, "Goodnight, Doug," and there was nothing extra in how she said it except that she said his name, which she didn't always do, and the name carried it.

"Goodnight," Doug said.

She unlocked the car. The lights flashed once, orange in the dim of the side street. She got in. She moved the coffee cup from the passenger seat to somewhere he couldn't see. She started the engine, and Doug stepped back onto the curb, and she pulled out and turned left on High and the Subaru's taillights joined the others heading north and then were gone.

Doug walked the half block to his Civic. He sat in the driver's seat and didn't start the car. The meter had expired — he'd fed it two hours and dinner had been closer to two and a half — but when he checked, there was no ticket. The windshield was clean. Small mercies, or Columbus parking enforcement, which was inconsistent on weeknights.

He sat with his hands on the steering wheel and thought about the thing Simone had said. You need to not need. He thought about the taping in twelve days and the space where a plan should be and wasn't. He thought about the toast — the original toast, the seven minutes that started all of this — and how the conditions that produced it were as unreplicable as a weather event. You couldn't schedule a thunderstorm. You couldn't prepare for the specific collision of whiskey and silence and twenty years of things unsaid and Brendan's perfect speech that preceded his wreck of one. You could only be outside when it happened and hope you survived what came through.

Be unintentional on purpose. Try to not try. The problem was clear and the solution was its own impossibility and the taping was in twelve days and Doug Bridge, insurance adjuster, fifty-two years old, sitting in a Civic with 114,000 miles on it on a side street in the Short North, did not have a plan.

He started the car. The engine turned over with the reliable, unbeautiful sound it always made. He drove home to Clintonville, eleven minutes, the route he could do without thinking. He parked in his usual spot. He went inside. He stood in his kitchen.

The kitchen table where he and Simone had worked for three months — her notebook, his voice, the recorder she set between them like a referee — was bare. He'd wiped it down after their last session. It was just a table now. A flat surface in a quiet apartment in a building where nobody knew about the taping or the tour or the eight minutes in Detroit or the two feet of sidewalk outside a gray Subaru that neither of them had crossed.

He opened a beer and drank it standing at the counter. He thought about Simone holding her keys and the way she'd said his name and the thing they'd left where it was. Then he rinsed the glass, put it on the rack, and went to bed. The taping was in twelve days. He knew what the problem was now, which was either progress or a more precise way of being stuck.
