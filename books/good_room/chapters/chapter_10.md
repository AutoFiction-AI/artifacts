# Chapter 10

Doug drove to Dayton on a Saturday because Saturdays were when Gloria expected him and because after Indianapolis he had no interest in finding out what excuse Gloria would reject. The drive was seventy miles on I-70, flat and straight, the fields on either side still brown but showing the first green suggestion of spring, as though Ohio were considering it but hadn't committed. Doug's Civic needed an oil change. The indicator light had been on for eleven days and he'd been meaning to deal with it but hadn't, and the orange glow on the dashboard was the kind of small failure he understood — quantifiable, fixable, ignorable for a while longer.

He was specifically and deliberately not thinking about the Holiday Inn bedspread or the nineteen minutes that should have been twenty-five or the polite applause that was worse than silence. He was thinking about oil changes and exit numbers and whether he should have brought Gloria flowers, which he hadn't, because the last time he'd brought Gloria flowers she had said they were the wrong kind and set them on the counter and never mentioned them again.

Maple Glen Senior Living looked like what it was — a one-story complex off Route 35 with a parking lot that was always half-empty and landscaping that someone maintained without enthusiasm. The sign out front had a maple leaf in the logo. There were no maple trees on the property. Doug had pointed this out to Gloria once. She had said, "There's a motel on Route 40 called the Paradise. There is no paradise on Route 40."

The woman at the front desk was named Angela. She wore a lanyard with a photo ID that showed a version of herself smiling more broadly than the one behind the desk. She gave him the standard "Here to see your mom?" and Doug said, "Yes, thank you," and signed the visitor log. The hallway smelled like institutional carpet and something that was supposed to be lavender but had given up halfway.

Gloria was in her room, sitting in the recliner that she'd brought from the house in Kettering when she moved in three years ago. The recliner was brown. The room was beige. Gloria was wearing a blue cardigan that Doug remembered from at least 2018 and a pair of white sneakers that were so clean they looked ceremonial. She was watching the local news with the closed captions on because the volume on her television had been set to a level that satisfied her and did not change regardless of programming.

"You're late," she said.

Doug looked at the clock on her nightstand. It was 11:07. He had said he'd be there at eleven.

"Seven minutes," he said.

"Your father was never late."

"I hit traffic on 35."

"Harold drove on 35 for thirty years and was never late."

Doug sat on the edge of her bed because the only other chair had a stack of crossword puzzle books on it that had achieved structural integrity and he did not want to be responsible for dismantling them. The bed was made tight enough to suggest military training that Gloria had never received.

"Your belt's on the last hole," she said. "What's in your refrigerator?"

"Food."

"List it."

"Eggs. Mustard. Half a gallon of milk."

"That's three items. That's not a refrigerator. Name one thing you've eaten off a plate this week."

"I had a sandwich yesterday."

Gloria considered this. "What kind?"

"Turkey."

"From where?"

"The place near work."

"The place with Roy."

"Yes."

"He's a strange man, Roy. Not bad. Strange."

Doug waited. He knew why she'd wanted him to come this weekend specifically, and he knew she'd get there, and he also knew that rushing Gloria was like trying to rush an insurance claim through underwriting — it did not speed the process and it annoyed everyone involved.

"I watched it," Gloria said.

She didn't say what. She didn't need to.

"How many times?" Doug asked.

"Four. The first time to see what the fuss was about. The second time because I missed part of it when Ruth called me in the middle of it. The third time because Patricia wanted to see it on my television because hers is too small. The fourth time because I wanted to make sure I was right about what I thought the first time."

"Were you?"

"I'm always right about what I think the first time. That's why I think it."

She picked up a notepad from the arm of the recliner. It was a lined notepad from the Dollar General, the kind with a spiral binding at the top, and Doug could see her handwriting on it — small, precise, tilted slightly to the right. Gloria had taken notes. She had watched her son's drunken wedding toast on the internet and she had taken written notes.

"First note," she said. "You spent the whole time folded over, tugging at that microphone cord and looking at the carpet. If you're going to stand in front of people, then stand. You looked like someone had called the wrong name and you were too polite to say so."

Doug said nothing.

"What you said about Brendan. About his toast being" — she glanced at the notepad — "'the kind of speech you give when you've been practicing in the mirror.' That was mean. He didn't deserve that." She paused. "But you weren't wrong. I watched his toast too. He cried at exactly the right moment. Nobody cries at the right moment. He's a surgeon. Surgeons rehearse. That's the whole job."

"He didn't rehearse his toast."

"He rehearsed. Trust me."

She turned the notepad over. There was more on the back.

"The part about the table," she said. Her voice slowed enough that Doug noticed each word separately. "About nobody sitting across from you again. About making your peace with it and not being at peace."

Doug's hands were on his knees. The bedspread under his palms was thin and pilled and he pressed into it.

"That part was yours," Gloria said. "Everything else — the whiskey, the laughing, the microphone trouble — that was racket. The last time you said anything that plain you were still in seventh grade. Then you got careful. Now you say the version people can stand. That line came out before you could fix it."

She put the notepad down on the arm of the recliner and squared it against the edge, because Gloria squared everything.

"Are they paying you?" she asked.

"For what?"

"For whatever it is you're doing now. The speeches. The television."

"Yes."

"How much?"

Doug considered not answering. He considered it for approximately one second, which was the maximum amount of time deception survived in Gloria's presence.

"Fifteen thousand for the one in Indianapolis."

The number sounded different in Gloria's room. It broke at once into rent, gas, groceries, whole months at Mutual Fidelity. Nineteen minutes for that amount still felt like a processing error somebody would catch and reverse.

Gloria's eyes moved slightly — the calculation was nearly visible, the arithmetic of a woman who had balanced household budgets for forty years on Harold's GM salary and could tell you what a gallon of milk cost in any year you named.

"For how long?"

"Thirty minutes. It was supposed to be thirty minutes."

"And how long was it actually?"

"About nineteen."

"So they paid you fifteen thousand dollars for nineteen minutes of talking."

"Yes."

"How much do you make at the insurance company in a month?"

Doug told her.

She nodded. The nod held a complete financial assessment. Her mouth settled into the expression Gloria wore when a number came out better than expected.

"That's decent money," she said. "For standing up there looking like you wanted to be somewhere else." She studied him. "Did it go well?"

"No."

"How bad?"

"People clapped because they felt sorry for me."

"That is bad."

"Yes."

"But they still paid you."

"They still paid me."

Gloria folded the notepad and set it on the arm of the recliner. "We should go to lunch," she said. "It's pork chop day. The pork chops are terrible but Ruth gets upset if we're late and she's saving seats."

---

The Maple Glen dining room had the acoustic quality of a room where sounds didn't land properly — forks on plates, the hum of a heating system that was still running in late April even though the weather outside had turned mild, conversations held at the volume of people who had lived long enough to stop worrying about being overheard. The carpet was green. The tables were round, six chairs each, and every table had a small vase with a plastic flower in it — the same plastic flowers that had been there every time Doug visited, slightly dusty, aggressively cheerful in a room that was neither.

Ruth was already at the table. She had saved four seats by placing her handbag on one chair and a folded newspaper on two others, and she waved at Gloria with the urgency of a person directing an aircraft onto a carrier deck. Ruth was eighty-one, wore clip-on earrings — gold circles that she adjusted every thirty seconds whether they needed it or not — and had been married three times, which gave her opinions about relationships the weight of field research.

Doug set his tray down and kept one hand on it longer than necessary. Two women at the next table gave him the quick, practiced look he'd been seeing for weeks now — recognition trying to pass itself off as manners.

The tray flexed under his hand. Recognition had its own sequence now: glance, second glance, the quick rearrangement of the face into ordinary politeness. He never knew which part he hated until it was already on him.

"He's here," Ruth said to Gloria, as though Doug were a delivery she'd been tracking.

"He's here," Gloria confirmed.

"I saw you on the computer," Ruth said to Doug. "My grandson Nathan showed me. He's in Cincinnati. He put it on his phone and then we watched it on the computer because the phone is too small and I can't see anything on those phones, I don't know how anyone does."

"Did you," Doug said. He sat down.

"You were funny. I didn't know you were funny. Gloria never said you were funny."

"Gloria doesn't think I'm funny."

"Gloria doesn't think anyone is funny. I thought you were funny. You should do one of those dancing shows. The one where they put regular people with the professionals. You have a nice presence."

Gloria made a short sound in her throat, the one that meant the subject had reached its limit.

Doug felt the back of his neck go hot. Being discussed by strangers in a ballroom had been one thing. Being discussed over pork chops by women who would still be sitting here after he drove home was another.

Patricia arrived with her lunch tray already loaded — pork chop, mashed potatoes, green beans, a roll, a cup of coffee that she held with both hands even when the tray was on the table and there was no practical reason for it. She held coffee in both hands as if it were a posture, her version of crossing her arms. She was seventy-nine, a retired English teacher from Dayton Public Schools, and she still corrected people's grammar reflexively — a habit she either didn't notice or had decided was a public service. She wore a red turtleneck and her reading glasses on a beaded chain around her neck, and she looked at Doug with the measuring expression of a woman who had spent thirty years grading.

"I watched the video," Patricia said. "Twice."

"Everyone watched it twice," Gloria said. "Or four times."

"It's a cry for help," Patricia said. She sat down and placed her napkin on her lap with the precision of a woman setting a table for the dean. "The entire performance is a cry for help. The drinking, the public confession, the humor used as a deflection mechanism for genuine emotional distress. It's textbook."

Doug cut into the pork chop and watched the breading drag loose from the meat. A cry for help implied intention, a flare gun, a plan. What had happened at the wedding and then in Indianapolis had felt less organized than that.

"It's not textbook," Gloria said. "There's no textbook."

"There are many textbooks. I read one last month about emotional avoidance in adult men. The patterns are quite clear—"

"Doug doesn't need a textbook. Doug needs to eat." Gloria looked at Doug's tray. "Is that all you're eating?"

Doug had taken a pork chop, potatoes, and a dinner roll. "This is plenty."

"Take more potatoes. They put butter in them. It's the only thing they do right here."

Deirdre was the last to sit down. She moved carefully but not uncertainly — a tall woman, thin, with white hair that she wore pinned back and the posture of someone who had spent her working life sitting up straight and had never stopped. She was eighty-three. She had been a bookkeeper for a car dealership in Springfield for thirty-one years and had moved to Maple Glen after her husband Ernie died and her house became, in her words, too many rooms for one person. She wore a green wool sweater with a small moth hole near the left cuff that she either hadn't noticed or had decided wasn't the sweater's fault. Her eyes were pale blue and they didn't miss things.

"Hello, Doug," she said.

"Hello, Deirdre."

She unfolded her napkin. The table settled into the rhythm of eating, which at Maple Glen meant the specific pattern of forks and talk and the pauses between talk that were comfortable rather than empty, the silences of people who had known each other long enough that silence wasn't a thing to be filled.

"Ruth wants to put you on one of those television dance programs," Gloria said to Doug.

"I heard."

"Ruth is in a fantasy. At your own wedding you kept your elbows glued to your sides and stared at Megan's shoulder."

"That's true."

"Harold was no better. Music came on and he looked cornered."

Doug could remember the exact feel of Megan's hand between his shoulder blades that night, steering him through a slow song in a rented hall while he counted under his breath so he would not step on her feet. He took a bite of potatoes so he wouldn't have to say any of that.

Ruth adjusted her earrings. "I'm just saying he has a nice presence. On the video. People like him."

"People liked him because he was drunk," Patricia said, taking a precise bite of her pork chop. "That's not the same as presence. That's disinhibition."

"I wasn't that drunk," Doug said. He was that drunk. Everyone at the table knew he was that drunk. Gloria didn't even bother to look at him.

"Eight whiskeys," Gloria said. "You said it in the video. Eight. Your father drank bourbon and he never had more than two. Three at Christmas."

"I don't usually drink that much."

"You don't usually do anything. That's your whole situation." Gloria cut her pork chop into precise squares. She ate pork chops the way she did everything — methodically, completely, without apology. "You do the same things every day in the same order and then one night you do something completely different in front of people with cameras."

"It was about a hundred and forty people."

"It was on the internet. It was eighty million."

Doug ate his potatoes. The mashed potatoes at Maple Glen were actually decent — Gloria was right about the butter — and he focused on them with the attention of a man who preferred potatoes to conversation. Ruth talked about her grandson Nathan's job in Cincinnati, which involved something with computers that she described as "he does the internet, I think he makes the websites." Patricia mentioned that her daughter in Chicago had called twice last month, which Patricia reported with a specific count and no commentary, though the count itself was the commentary — twice in a month was not enough and everyone at the table knew it and nobody said it. Deirdre ate quietly and nodded at things other people said, which was her version of participating and which carried more agreement than most people's whole sentences.

It was Ruth who brought the conversation back. "What did your daughter think?" she asked Doug. "About the video."

Doug put his fork down. "She wasn't thrilled."

"Of course she wasn't thrilled," Patricia said. "It was her wedding. Her father got inebriated and—"

"Patricia," Gloria said.

Patricia stopped. She picked up her coffee cup with both hands and held it like a verdict she was keeping to herself.

"Kelsey is fine," Gloria said. She said it to the table but she was looking at Doug. "She's her father's daughter. She'll be mad and she won't tell anybody. She'll wait. Then she'll say it in front of the wrong people. Bridges do that."

Doug didn't answer. He ate his roll. It was dry and needed butter but he wasn't going to get up to get some because leaving the table meant the conversation would continue without him and he didn't trust what Gloria would say in his absence. He could hear the dining room around them — a man two tables over laughing at something, a woman asking for more water, the particular ambient hum of a building where fifty people lived and ate and sat and waited.

"I thought it was sad," Deirdre said.

The table went quiet. The dining room kept up its low-grade murmur, but at their table every fork stopped for a beat. When Deirdre spoke, people listened, because Deirdre speaking was an event. She could go an entire meal saying nothing and her presence still registered more than Ruth's commentary or Patricia's diagnoses.

"I thought it was the saddest thing I've ever seen," Deirdre said. She was looking at her plate, not at Doug. Her fork was resting on the edge. "And also funny. Both at the same time. I didn't know those could be the same thing, but they were."

Doug looked at his plate. The breading had slid off one side of the pork chop. The plastic flower leaned in its vase. The green carpet showed between the chair legs. Deirdre had put the sentence on the table and left it there.

"Deirdre's right," Gloria said. And that was the end of it. She returned to her pork chop. Ruth started talking about a program she'd watched about the royal family. Patricia corrected Ruth's pronunciation of "Buckingham." The table moved on. What Deirdre had said stayed underneath the ordinary business of eating and talking and being alive on a Saturday in April.

---

After lunch, Doug walked Gloria back to her room. The hallway was wide enough for two people and a wheelchair, which was the architectural statement of Maple Glen — everything built for the body's eventual limitations, every doorway a little wider than necessary, every handrail bolted to the wall at exactly the right height. Someone had put Easter decorations on the bulletin board near the nurses' station — paper eggs in pastel colors and a construction-paper rabbit that looked like it had been made by someone who had a general idea of rabbits but hadn't committed to the details.

Gloria sat in her recliner. Doug sat on the bed again. The crossword books hadn't moved. Through the window he could see the parking lot and beyond it a strip of grass that ended at a chain-link fence, and beyond the fence the back of a Wendy's.

"Do you want coffee?" Gloria asked.

"I'm fine."

"I have instant. It's terrible but it's what I have. Harold used to make real coffee. After he died I switched to instant because there was no point in making a whole pot for one person, and I've been drinking terrible coffee for fifteen years and I don't see any reason to change now."

"You could get one of those single-cup machines."

"I don't want a machine. I want terrible coffee or no coffee. Those are my options and I've chosen."

Doug leaned back against the wall. The wall was cool through his shirt. Gloria's room was small — bed, recliner, dresser, a closet with a sliding door that stuck halfway. On the dresser were three framed photographs: Doug's parents' wedding photo, a picture of Kelsey at her high school graduation, and Harold in his backyard, standing next to a row of tomato plants, squinting into the sun with the expression of a man who was satisfied with his tomatoes and did not require the photograph to validate this.

"Your father," Gloria said.

She said it without transition or preamble, as though the previous conversation had ended and this new one had already started without needing anyone's permission.

"I want to tell you something about your father. Not because it's a secret. It was never a secret. It just never came up, and now it's coming up."

Doug waited.

"The Novaks had us over for dinner. This was — I don't know the exact year — 1989 or 1990. You were in high school. Frank and Jean Novak. They lived on Ackerman, the yellow house with the fence that was always leaning. Frank had just retired from GM. Early retirement. He'd been talking about it for two years — all the things he was going to do. Travel, learn to paint, fix the fence, plant a garden. He had a list. He brought up the list at every dinner party we went to for two years."

Gloria paused. Not for effect. To remember.

"So we're at dinner. Jean made a ham. It was always ham at the Novaks'. There was a salad and dinner rolls and Frank had bought wine, which was unusual because Frank didn't usually buy wine, but he was retired now and he was trying things. Frank starts talking about the list again. He was going to go to Spain. He and Jean were going to go to Spain. Everyone at the table is nodding and smiling and asking questions about Spain, and Harold is sitting there eating his ham and not saying anything. Which wasn't unusual. Harold didn't talk at dinner parties unless he had something to say, and he usually didn't."

She looked at the photograph on the dresser. Harold and his tomatoes.

"And then Harold says, right in the middle of the ham, 'Frank, you're not going to Spain.' Frank laughed a little. Harold said, 'No. You're not. You're going to stay home and keep talking about Spain. That's what you're going to do.'"

Gloria's hands were still in her lap. Her voice hadn't changed register. She told it as if she were recounting breakfast — flat, factual, without performance.

"Jean put her fork down. Donna Miller looked at the tablecloth. Jim Miller took a drink of water. Frank stopped chewing. Nobody said Harold was wrong."

Doug didn't move. The bedspread was thin under his hands. He could hear the television murmuring next door, someone else's afternoon, someone else's room.

"We left early. Jean walked us to the door. She said good night and held the knob so hard I noticed."

Gloria looked at her hands. Her fingers were thinner than Doug remembered them being, the knuckles more pronounced. She wore the wedding ring she'd worn for forty-three years. She had not taken it off after Harold died and Doug had never asked her about it.

"In the car, Harold didn't say a word. He drove the whole way home — eighteen minutes, I know because I watched the clock — with both hands at ten and two. He looked at the road. He didn't say he was sorry. He didn't say he was right."

She stopped again. Doug could hear his own breathing.

"I reached over and held his hand. On the steering wheel. He didn't look at me. I didn't say anything. I held his hand for the rest of the drive and we pulled into the driveway and he turned off the car and we sat there for a minute and then he went inside and went to bed. We never talked about it. Not that night. Not the next morning. Not ever."

She picked at the arm of the recliner where the fabric had worn down to a smoother surface underneath, the spot where her left hand rested every evening.

"Frank never went to Spain," she said. "He sat in that house for fourteen years and then he died."

She looked at the clock on her nightstand.

"It's almost two. You should go. The traffic on 70 gets bad if you wait."

Doug stood up. His legs had stiffened from sitting on the low bed with his back against the wall. Gloria stood too, slower, using the armrest, and for a moment he saw her body's negotiation with standing — how she braced, steadied, settled into her feet — and he looked away because she would not have wanted him to see it.

"Thanks for lunch," he said.

"The pork chops were terrible."

"They weren't bad."

"They were terrible. Don't patronize me about pork chops." She straightened her cardigan. "Bring me those butter cookies next time. The ones from the German bakery on Far Hills. Not the ones from the grocery store. The grocery store ones taste like dust."

"I'll bring the cookies."

"From the German bakery."

"Yes."

"And get your oil changed. I could hear your car from the window when you pulled in."

Doug didn't think that was possible. He also didn't think Gloria was wrong about much. He leaned down and kissed her on the cheek. Her skin was dry and cool and smelled like the soap she'd used his entire life, a specific pharmacy brand in a green wrapper that he could never remember the name of and that he would recognize anywhere.

"Bye, Mom."

"Drive safe. Not fast. Safe. There's a difference and your father never learned it."

He walked down the hallway past the Easter decorations and the nurses' station where Angela was eating a yogurt and reading something on her phone. He signed out in the visitor log. The entry above his was from two days ago — someone named Hernandez, visiting room 14. The parking lot was warmer than when he'd arrived, the afternoon sun on the asphalt, and his Civic ticked with heat when he opened the door.

He sat in the car for a while before starting it. The oil change light was on, orange and patient. Through the windshield he could see the front of Maple Glen — the sign with the maple leaf, the parking lot, the landscaping, the building where his mother lived and ate terrible pork chops and told the truth to anyone who sat still long enough.

He started the car. The engine caught on the first try, which was more than his father's Buick had ever managed, though Doug didn't know why he thought of that now. He pulled out of the lot and turned east toward 70.

The drive home was seventy miles. Exit 38, exit 44, exit 52. The fields were greener now, the dark ground showing spring in uneven patches. He did not turn on the radio. He thought about his father driving home from the Novaks' in silence, both hands at ten and two, and his mother reaching across the seat. Eighteen minutes. He thought about the toast, and the table, and the part Gloria had said was his, and four hundred pharmaceutical reps in Indianapolis clapping because they felt bad for him. He thought about the Novaks' ham cooling on the plates and Frank's list and Jean holding the front door at the edge of her anger.

He drove. The oil change light glowed. At exit 52 he noticed his hands were fixed at ten and two and left them there. The highway was flat and straight and exactly long enough.
