# Chapter 3

Ferrymouth was already trying the word in different mouths.

Two boys on the slip were leaning over the pilings, taking turns spitting into the tide-black water and saying bridelight in different voices to see whether the pink on the slime would answer better to mockery or praise. It kept its own counsel, a faint blush along the barnacled wood. The ferry chain rattled. Somebody cursed over a dropped crate. The whole landing smelled of wet rope, eel blood, hot algae mash, and the thick sweet rot the river sweated up once the day had gone warm enough.

Silas stopped a moment under Junie's lintel with the damp still on his coat and Inez Cordero still in his ears. He had spent the walk down telling himself that a kitchen was a different country from Lantern Steps, that stew and family would take the edge off being told, in front of half a stair district, what his mouth had done to other people's lamps. Then Junie shouted from inside, without looking up, "If that's you, wash first. I can smell Patent Row through the broth."

He went in grinning because that was the shape his face knew best.

Junie's place was only half a room larger than the stove that ruled it. Bench tables crowded the front, already slick with the next rush's elbows. The back shelf bowed under crockery, onions, a jar of pepper gone soft with steam, and the ledger Junie kept for God's eventual inspection. She moved between pot and board with her sleeves rolled and her hair pinned up by two bone pins and a carpenter's pencil. Flour rode one cheek. The old split in her left eyebrow flashed pale whenever the steam licked it clean.

"You look peeled," she said. "Somebody finally take a knife to the varnish?"

"Only a civic-minded woman with a tray and excellent aim."

"Good. Basin's there."

He washed in the tin bowl by the door. The water went cloudy at once, paint stain and street grit making a skin on top. Junie slapped a heel of bread into his hand before he had dried properly, then pointed at the stool by the stove with the same spoon she used to move dumplings, scold Otis, and threaten drunks.

"Sit. Eat while it's hot, then you can explain why boys are making kissing noises at my pilings."

"That's commerce," Silas said, taking the bowl she set down.

"That's infection."

The eel stew was thick enough to stand the spoon nearly upright. Junie cooked it with potatoes, onions, and enough black pepper to bully the river mud back into something a Christian could love. He had not realized how hungry he was until the first mouthful burned him. It made his eyes water. Junie saw and took satisfaction from it.

"There," she said. "Now you're honest for one second."

He ate. The room pressed warm around him. Outside, a ferry bell clanged twice and men answered it with boots on plank. Inside, Junie took the ledger down, wiped her thumb on her apron, and opened it in front of him like a priest opening a book of sins.

"Before you flirt with that stew," she said, "we'll do the ledger version of love."

"That sounds indecent already."

"Everything with you does. Here."

Her finger, square and flour-dusted, tapped the page.

"Six evening meals you said you'd settle after the varnish job on Cork Lane. Four breakfasts. Two crowns eight for linseed and rag because you came whining about a client who wanted a sign to look expensive and clean at the same time. One bottle of lamp wash you swore you'd replace. And Havelock sent a boy by with a note asking whether his forty-three crowns had taken up residence under my roof."

Silas kept his spoon moving because if he stopped he would have to look at her too directly. "Havelock has a poetic turn."

"Havelock has a knife for a face and only writes when he's close enough to blood to smell it." She turned a page. "Then there is me."

"I had hoped I'd been rolled into the family rate."

"You have. That's why you're eating first."

The joke did not move her much. Junie, tired enough, got faster instead of louder. He knew that speed. It meant she was past temper and into tally.

"Pell came this morning," she said. "Not to eat. To smile. I distrust a landlord who smiles in daylight."

Silas looked up. "What does he want now?"

"What he always wants. More for less, with a compliment tied around it." She reached under the ledger and pulled out two folded papers, both gone soft at the corners from being handled. "He's calling the landing desirable after dark. Says people are coming from up-ridge to look at the pink water and take supper by the slips, so Ferrymouth has entered a brighter market. Those were his words. Brighter market. One of them was a woman in gloves who left half her dumplings to go stare at the pilings like the river had shown her its ankles."

Silas took the paper. Pell's clerk hand had laid the words down neat and mean. Revised terms. Basement storage review. Fuel hookup surcharge pending. He could almost hear the clerk taking pleasure in the flourishes.

"That's extortion in decent boots."

"It's rent," Junie said. "Which is extortion taught to keep its nails clean."

She set the second paper over the first. The second hit harder because he knew that species at a glance: fuel credit, numbers, dates, little polite hooks that ended in a choke.

"Aram Pell and his friends are carrying twenty-nine crowns on the kitchen now, with the arrears still growing because mash went up again this week. Half a crown more on each barrel, and the man telling me so had the nerve to say I might make it back selling to sightseers."

"Mash went up because the digester men are thieves."

"Mash went up because every silk fool in Gilt Crescent wants a pink jar on the mantel and the suppliers would rather strain fuel for parlors than send ugly barrels to kitchens." She took the papers back before the damp from his fingers ruined them. "The city gets one pretty light and suddenly stew is luxury."

He tried a shrug. "It'll pass."

Junie gave him a look so flat it could have leveled lumber.

"No. A rainstorm passes. A ferry passes. A fit passes. What this does is sit down at your table and ask for seconds."

From the front room came a burst of laughter, then someone calling for three bowls and more bread. Junie answered automatically, "Coming," and did not move yet.

"I'm not saying this so you'll pull that wounded face and promise the moon," she said. "I know what your promises are worth before you've eaten. I'm saying it because whatever you called that slime on Patent Row didn't stay on Patent Row. It's got into rent paper and fuel paper, and if you think that's still a joke because a crowd clapped at you, then you're dumber than you dress."

Silas opened his mouth with something slick and easy ready to go, some line about civic miracles and landlords breeding faster than rats. Junie waited. He could see the fatigue at the root of her, not theatrical, not noble, just a woman measuring whether this week's pot would stay hot enough to make next week's. The easy line turned to ash.

"How close?" he asked.

Junie nodded once. That was all the reward truth got.

"Close enough that I watered the stew at noon and told the men the river eels were lean this season. Close enough that Otis doesn't get boots again until autumn unless a saint learns sums. Close enough that if Pell touches the basement hooks, I cook slower and serve less and then I stop being a kitchen and become a room that used to feed people."

She took his empty bowl before he had quite finished and tipped the last inch into the pot. She wasted nothing.

"Now," she said, already turning back to the stove, "if you've got any cleverness left in you, use it to carry trays."

***

The supper rush came in damp and hungry.

Ferrymouth always ate hard after the late boats. Men with rope burns on their palms and river silt drying pale on their cuffs crowded shoulder to shoulder with warehouse girls, porters, a washerwoman who brought her own hot sauce in a medicine bottle, and three clerks who had started coming down from Patent Row since the pink on the pilings made the district look dangerous in a way they could pay to admire and leave. Junie did not care where a mouth came from so long as it paid or had a tab honest enough to insult to its face.

Silas carried bowls because there was no graceful way not to. He moved between benches with stew heat slicking his neck and the smell of wet wool turning the room animal. Every time the door opened, the river shoved in a draft full of salt iron and lamp smoke. Every time it shut, the stove took the room back.

Otis arrived in the middle of it all, narrow-shouldered and freckled, one trouser knee carrying the landing's signature in dock mud. He came through the back with a slate under one arm and two chalk stubs in his pocket, kissed Junie on the sleeve because her hands were busy, and then saw Silas with the tray.

"Ma," he said, delighted, "Uncle Sile got demoted."

"He got hungry," Junie said.

"Same thing mostly," Silas told the boy.

Otis dropped onto the bench by the stove and started tracing letters into the steam on the tabletop with one blunt finger. He had copied sign hands since before he could read them straight, loving the curl of a flourish long before the word inside it meant anything. Silas watched him draw a crooked B, wipe it away, then try again.

The sight put a small ugly shame under Silas's ribs. Otis loved the loop and tail of a letter the way other boys loved knives or marbles, without yet knowing how often a pretty word was only a better-dressed debt.

"They were saying bridelight at school," Otis announced.

Junie snorted. "You're at school to learn numbers, not foolishness."

"I can learn both."

"That's the spirit of modern education," Silas said.

Otis ignored him. He was working the word in his mouth now, trying the corners of it. "Bride-light. Bride-blight. Bright-light. If everybody says a name wrong, does it answer wrong?"

Silas set down a bowl in front of a deckhand and nearly slopped broth onto the man's cuff.

The question hit the same bruise Inez had left on him that afternoon. He had named a nuisance for sport, and now the sport was sitting in a schoolroom, trying on consequences with chalky fingers.

"Sometimes," he said.

"What if you call it something mean?"

"Then it might get a bad temper."

"Like Ma?"

Junie did not look around from the stove. "Like the hand currently holding this spoon, yes."

The room laughed. Otis grinned, pleased with himself, then lowered his voice into a theatrical growl and addressed the damp windowpane. "Hear that, you nasty brideweed. Mind your manners."

From a front bench somebody called, "Brideweed's no good. My landlord's calling the alley behind our rooms Bridal Walk and wants another six bits a week for the privilege."

"You should charge him six bits each time he says it," Junie shot back.

Another voice said, "My little girl won't go to bed now unless the lamp's called bridelight first. Swears it burns prettier if you ask nice."

"Pretty doesn't boil potatoes," Junie said.

"Tell that to Gilt Crescent," Silas muttered.

But that was the thing. He heard the word moving through the room the way steam moved, taking shape against every colder surface it met. The word had gone common as steam, beading on everything it touched: a deckhand's complaint, a mother's bedtime trick, Otis testing syllables with chalk on his thumb. He had always loved that part, the way a phrase could jump the track you laid for it and go breeding in strangers. He had built a living on that leap. But here, with Junie knocking the ash down in the stove because she was trying to make fuel behave itself, it did not feel like applause. It felt like weather inside the house.

One of the Patent Row clerks, a nervous young man with a collar already wilted into surrender, lifted his spoon toward Silas in a toast better suited to a performer than a man carrying stew.

"Mr. Vale, is it true the moss prefers romance?"

"It prefers damp masonry and a public with no discipline," Silas said.

"That sounds like romance to me," the clerk said, and got enough laughter to encourage him. "You ought to publish instructions. Ladies are buying cuttings off the back steps."

Junie swung around so fast the ladle clicked the pot.

"If any lady brings a live cutting in here for fashion, I will feed it to her. Eat your stew."

The clerk ducked his head and obeyed. The washerwoman with the medicine-bottle sauce laughed so hard she coughed into her kerchief and waved away concern with an irritated flick; pity offended her more than choking.

Otis had abandoned the tabletop and moved on to his slate. He was writing names in a column now, mouthing them as he went.

"Bridelight," he said.

Scratch.

"Brideweed."

Scratch.

"Kissmoss."

Junie pointed with the ladle. "No."

"That one was off the ferry," he said defensively. "They were saying it by the railing."

"Then the ferry can raise you."

Silas took the slate from him, more sharply than he meant to. Otis blinked.

"Easy," Junie said.

The chalk dust came off on Silas's thumb. The letters were childish, all stems and panic, but they were letters. The boy was not performing for a crowd. He was sorting the city into marks he could keep. For one ridiculous second Silas saw the future of it, words handed around from bench to ferry to schoolroom to lamp hook until no one remembered which mouth had first sent them out. He had always counted on that forgetting. It was the way a man like him survived being borrowed by richer ones.

Otis watched his face and went quieter, not sulking yet, just uncertain.

"Did I do it wrong?"

Silas handed the slate back. "No. You did it exactly right."

"Then why are you looking like that?"

"Because your uncle's face has poor manners."

Otis accepted this as likely. He bent again to the slate, slower now. Junie shoved a bowl toward Silas without looking at him.

"Take table three before they mutiny," she said. Then, softer, only for him, "And stop acting surprised. Children put everything in their mouths. That's how the world gets inside."

He served the last of the rush with that lodged under his ribs like a fish bone. By the time the benches started emptying, the kitchen had thinned out, the heat broken each time the door opened and shut. Outside, more people were strolling the landing than the hour deserved, pausing by the rail to watch the blush on the pilings with the proprietary attention of people who thought the river had put on a show for them personally. Junie watched them through the front pane and made a face like she'd tasted soap.

"There," she said, wiping the lip of a bowl with her thumb. "Your admirers. Maybe they'll pay Pell for us."

"I'd sooner ask gulls."

"Gulls at least arrive hungry and honest."

***

Damaris Pike came in after the worst of it, carrying the damp evening in the hem of her widow's black and a stack of folded sheets under one arm. The black had gone shiny at the elbows and seams from hard wear and too much leaning over proofs. Ink stained the side of her right hand where she rested it when she wrote fast. She smiled at Silas with the chipped front tooth fully in view, turning the damage into another kind of typeface.

"If there's any coffee left that hasn't already confessed to murder," she said, "I'll take it."

"You'll take what the pot thinks of you," Junie said.

"That's all any editor ever gets."

She set the papers on the driest corner of the back shelf. Junie hooked a foot under a burlap sack and shoved it toward her.

"If you're going to poach my brother after hours, sort tomorrow's potatoes," Junie said. "Bench is for things with skins to lose."

"Extortion from a hostess. I respect it."

Damaris hitched widow's black clear of the floor and crouched by the sack without another word, flicking bad eyes from potatoes with an ink-stained thumbnail while Junie poured the coffee. Damaris seldom asked permission for entry into any scene she could profit by. She looked up at Silas. He'd plainly been occupying a reserved place in her head all day.

She always gave him the rankling sense that tomorrow's paper might already contain a better-arranged version of whatever he was about to say. Tonight that feeling prickled under his collar, half vanity and half the fear of waking up turned into public property again.

"You have had an excellent week for ruining other people's certainty," she said. "Don't sit. I need you vain enough to pace."

"You say that like it's a salaried post."

"Everything becomes one if I can print it often enough."

Junie set down a cup. The coffee looked punitive. Damaris drank, winced, and approved of it with a little nod.

Silas could smell the proposition coming before she named it: ink, coffee, damp wool, and the first clean edge of money. Relief had begun to stink suspiciously to him. It still made his mouth water.

"I've got a proposition," she said. "Not the respectable kind. If I wanted respectability I'd marry badly again."

"I thought widowhood was agreeing with you."

"It does. That's why I protect it."

She pulled one of the folded sheets free and held it up from her crouch. The headline was his own trouble turned blocky and enthusiastic. The sheet told the truth in the most ruinous way possible, the sort of truth that had learned to dance in public.

"First installment sold out by noon," she said. "Newsboys were making up extra scandal in the street because I hadn't given them enough copy to mispronounce. The town wants the moss, but more than that it wants the argument around the moss. It wants to know what to call the damned thing next and whether the calling will stain somebody's curtains or marriage prospects. You're attached to the question now, unfortunately for your peace."

Silas folded the sheet shut before he had to read his own name again.

"I'm touched to hear it."

"You should be paid to hear it." She leaned one shoulder against the shelf, coffee in one hand and potato in the other, her voice quickening when she smelled circulation. "Give me a weekly series. Your rounds, your guesses, your failures, the district nonsense, the respectable panic, the way every grocer in town is suddenly pretending he discovered luminescent romance in a drainage problem. We make a serial of it. You're the man chasing a contrary bloom through a city too stupid and too greedy to agree with itself. I print six weeks and if the thing is still changing by then, I print six more."

"And what do I get besides libel performed in installments?"

"Money. Enough to matter before the week turns rotten again. Twelve crowns against the first three pieces if you give me your word before I get back uphill. Four of those go straight into Junie's till at dawn if she'll feed my carriers for a fortnight. Those little bastards already eat half the slips bare. I may as well formalize the vice. I keep your own name on the copy, and I don't ask exclusivity because I'm not an idiot. Exclusivity is for men who think ownership and understanding are the same appetite."

Junie, wiping down the next table, snorted without turning. Damaris flicked two fingers at her in acknowledgment.

"Four crowns buys mash and a barrel's breathing," Junie said. "Don't dress it prettier than that."

"I wasn't going to," Damaris said. "Pretty is what got us here."

"How generous of you to admit your limits," Silas said.

"I know my limits exactly. My presses can't force a city to obedience. They can only make disobedience impossible to ignore." She wrapped both hands around the coffee cup. For one brief second, the theatrics thinned. "Take the wrong money and you won't even have your own lies left."

That brought him fully to attention.

"Who's shopping?"

Damaris gave him a look of false innocence and real impatience.

"Who do you think? Wren has been buying wall space like a churchman buying candles ahead of a storm. He's not waiting to see whether this becomes useful. He already knows it's useful. He'll want one stable name with cuffs on it, a packet design, maybe a parlor story for the better rooms and some practical lie for the kitchens. He'll want you because the public has already put your mouth on the thing."

"You seem cheerful about that."

"I'm cheerful about anything that sells. That's not the same as blind."

A boy in a printer's cap skidded into the doorway before Silas could answer, breath steaming and both cuffs freckled black with ink.

"Mrs. Pike. Harrow wants to know whether to hold the forme or strike another hundred. The second run's nearly gone."

Damaris did not look away from Silas. "Paid gone or shouted gone?"

"Paid."

She gave one short nod, quick arithmetic sharpening her face.

"Then make it fourteen," she said to Silas. "The city's being vulgar with my money tonight and I'd rather spend some of it before Wren does."

To the boy she said, "Tell Harrow yes. And if the hawkers invent adultery, they spell it correctly."

The boy vanished.

"That's an ugly way to negotiate," Silas said.

"It's an honest one." She took another swallow of coffee and shivered at the bitterness. "Listen to me closely, Silas. If you won't let me print you, then at least understand the condition you're in. You're not outside this story anymore. You're the part people point at with dirty fingers while the rich men measure shelf space."

He looked over at Junie. She had stopped wiping the table. Otis, on the bench by the stove, had fallen half asleep over his slate, his cheek marked white with chalk and the last unfinished word smudged under his hand.

"Fourteen and carrier suppers is not nothing," Junie said.

"No," Silas said. "It isn't."

"What it is," Damaris said, "is loose. Loose has value."

"Loose gets hungry."

"So does tied up. One of those conditions only fools respectable people."

He rubbed his thumb over the newsprint edge. It came away gray.

"You want sensation."

"Of course I do." She bared the chipped tooth again. "I'm in the sensation trade. But I also prefer a city with more than one throat. Wren prefers choir practice."

"And Pell?"

"Pell prefers any miracle that raises rent." She set the cup down. "If he keeps calling this landing desirable after dark, I can make his clerk hand famous for the wrong reasons. That will buy your sister a little room to spit."

"You've always had a gift for civic virtue," Silas said.

"Never accuse me of virtue in front of witnesses."

Junie came over then and lifted the empty cup before Damaris had quite finished it.

"If you're done pricing my brother by the pound," she said, "I need the shelf back for tomorrow's potatoes."

"I'm trying to buy him in installments," Damaris said. "Entirely different sin."

"Same knife, smaller cuts."

Damaris rose, brushing potato dirt from her skirt and gathering the papers under one arm. At the door she paused and glanced once toward Otis asleep by the stove. Something in her face altered, not softer exactly, but less amused.

"If Wren comes," she said without ornament, "he won't come for a story. He'll come for a leash. Price that properly."

Then she was gone into the landing dusk, black hem catching a little of the damp, already halfway back to print in her mind.

Silas watched through the pane as she turned up toward Patent Row, quick even in widow's skirts, one hand holding her papers to her side like she was carrying a loaf that mattered.

Junie said, "Well?"

"Well what?"

"Don't play stupid indoors. It spreads. Fourteen crowns and carrier suppers is not theory."

"No."

"Then don't answer her like she's begging a favor."

"I wasn't planning to."

"You hate deadlines."

"I hate mornings. Deadlines merely accompany them."

Junie scraped Otis's slate clean with the side of her thumbnail and held it up so he could see the wet chalk paste. "I hate being fashionable. Looks like we all have our crosses."

***

They closed late because the gawkers at the rail kept drifting in for one last bowl, one last beer, one last excuse to sit near the pink water. By the time Junie shot the back bolt, the kitchen smelled of old steam, cabbage, dishwater gone gray with grease, and the coppery tang the fuel tank gave off when it was low enough to breathe air between gulps. Otis slept all the way through the washing-up, curled on the bench with his feet tucked under him and his hand still blotched white from chalk.

Silas was drying bowls when the knock came.

Three measured taps came through the door, each one placed. Any dockman would have hammered. Any after-hours beggar would have scratched.

Junie and Silas looked at each other.

"If that's Pell, I'll throw the wash water on him," she said.

Silas opened the door.

Gideon Wren stood under the lintel untouched, the damp somehow stopping short of him. His coat was dark and fine without showiness. His collar had survived the whole day unrumpled. Silver had begun to take the hair at his temples, and his gloves were the pale kid kind a sensible man would never wear within half a mile of Ferrymouth unless he trusted that dirt belonged to other people.

"Mr. Vale," he said. "Miss Vale. I am sorry for the hour."

Junie, elbow-deep in the wash basin, said, "No you're not, but come in if you're paying for the floor after."

If he minded the insult, he did not show it. Gideon removed one glove finger by finger before accepting the chair Silas pulled out. He laid a leather folder on the table and looked once around the kitchen, not with disgust, which would have been easier to dismiss, but with the assessing calm of a man who counted rooms by what leverage they held.

"Mrs. Pike has likely been here already," he said.

"Your spies are quick."

"Your routes are obvious."

Silas sat opposite him. Junie kept washing, metal clinking softly in the basin. The sound made the pause feel longer.

"If you've come to buy a bowl," Silas said, "we're shut."

"I have come to buy an answer."

"That sounds expensive."

"It is. Which is why I am here after dark rather than sending a clerk."

He opened the folder. The paper inside was thick, good rag stock, the sort of page meant to make a man feel he had already improved by touching it. An envelope sat on top, unsealed but heavy enough that the table noticed when Gideon set it down.

"The city is filling with provisional names," Gideon said. "Some comic, some sentimental, some merely stupid. That was tolerable for two days. It is less tolerable now. Households cannot cook by novelty. Merchants cannot stack it on a shelf. Carters cannot send it across the city under six different names."

Junie barked a humorless laugh from the basin. "Funny. Households were cooking by one of his novelties before your investors smelled it."

Gideon inclined his head to her without turning. "Precisely. Which is why it must be settled before the damage spreads."

Silas rested his forearms on the table. The wood was still tacky from being wiped in haste.

"You say stabilize. I hear collar."

"Collars are for dogs. Trade requires repetition." Gideon slid the top sheet across. "Verdancy House proposes a retainer. Exclusive. You will investigate, test, and devise the trade name under which the spill-born chorus strain, now wandering the streets under a dozen titles, may be brought to market without confusion. Every packet label, counter card, poster, and wall notice bearing that name runs through my office for the duration of the agreement."

Silas scanned the page. The language had teeth hidden in velvet. Exclusive consultation. Exclusive derivation rights. Exclusive use of names discovered, devised, or made fit for sale in the course of investigation. There it was. Not just what he would invent. What he would hear.

"You don't even know what it is yet," he said.

"I know what it needs to become."

"And what is that?"

"A thing a customer can ask for twice. A thing a clerk can enter in the order book. Dull enough to survive success."

Silas laughed despite himself. "Christ, that's a eulogy."

"It is commerce." Gideon folded his hands. "You mistake liveliness for durability, Mr. Vale. A city may gossip in twenty tongues at breakfast. By supper it still needs one label on the packet."

Junie set a dripping pot upside down on the board with more force than the pot deserved.

"My customers need the barrel to come full and the flame to keep steady. The packet can go drown."

Gideon looked at her then. "And when the barrel fails because every corner of the city is pulling the strain in a different direction with nursery rhymes and alley jokes, who pays for that failure? Not the boys at the railing. Not Mrs. Pike. Women like you do."

Junie knocked the dripping pot into line with the others, once, twice, then stripped the dishcloth hard through her wet fingers.

"One, two, three," she said under her breath, fast and level.

Silas knew that count. It meant she had gone past shouting and into the cleaner part of anger, the part that cut to use.

"Don't tell me what women like me pay for," she said. "You're in the room because we already know."

Gideon accepted the hit as confirmation of a premise.

"Then we are spared the sentimental portion of the discussion."

He pushed the envelope forward.

"Sixty crowns on signature. Further payment when the name holds and the packets go out. You clear your pigment debt. You breathe. Miss Vale gets the benefit of a brother not currently one insult away from having his credit shuttered across town. In return, I buy priority and discipline. You do not feed the matter to the papers. You do not loose fresh names in public for applause. You do the work, privately, until there is something fit for the shelf."

The number was sixty.

The number landed in Silas like a fist to the sternum, not because it was impossible, but because it was exact enough to start spending itself at once. Havelock's forty-three crowns gone. Something toward Junie's paper. Coal for the stove. Boots for Otis when autumn came cold again. A week without inventing nonsense for men who called their own greed innovation.

He covered that first leap with a smirk.

"And if the thing refuses your discipline?"

"Everything refuses at first." Gideon made patience sound like a cleaner form of force. "That does not make refusal sovereign."

"Suppose the neighborhoods keep their own names."

"They will. In kitchens, on stoops, in the little domestic republics where people insist on being local. I am not at war with private habit. I am concerned with open trade. Shelves. Posters. Orders shouted across counters. The words repeated by people who need to buy the same thing twice and find it waiting for them." He paused. "A curiosity draws a crowd. A staple keeps the books. Your gift, I think, lies in helping the first cross over into the second."

That was very nearly flattering. Gideon knew it. Junie knew it too; Silas could feel her attention sharpen behind him.

He said, "Damaris offered a weekly series. Fourteen crowns against the start and carrier money for Junie's stove."

"Of course she did."

"Without exclusivity."

"Because she sells heat. I sell persistence." Gideon's voice stayed even, almost devotional in its civility. "Mrs. Pike wants the city excited. Excitement is brief. I want the city trained. Training lasts."

"That's an ugly word."

"Only to men who profit from improvisation."

From the landing came a paper boy's cry, shrill as a gull and already dirtier than anything Damaris herself would have set in type.

"Pike extra. Bridelight at Ferrymouth. Come see where Vale eats and the pink creeps in."

Laughter answered it. Someone rapped the front pane with a coin. Otis jerked awake under the blanket, blinking hard and clutching the edge of it in one chalk-streaked fist.

Junie crossed the room in three wet steps and slapped the shutter closed.

"Get off my window," she shouted. "You want a show, kiss the river."

The footsteps scattered, not far enough to count as shame. Junie turned back, one hand still on Otis's shoulder until the boy sagged down again.

"I need fuel," she said, "not a gallery."

Gideon waited through it all, hands folded.

Silas said, "And your persistence keeps that off her glass?"

"It keeps the work private until there is something to place," Gideon said. "I am buying a product, Mr. Vale, not a family anecdote."

"Comforting," Junie said.

Silas leaned back. The chair complained under him. The stove ticked as it cooled. Somewhere below the floorboards, the fuel line gave a hollow glug, a sound like a throat trying not to embarrass itself.

He thought, absurdly and unwillingly, of Inez in the blue lamp light on Lantern Steps, the severe patience in her face when she had held out those damaged trays like evidence from a murder no one else had yet admitted was one. He thought of Junie reading Pell's pretty extortion in a room that smelled of pepper and dishwater. He thought of Otis writing the city into columns he could carry to school.

He said, "That clause there. Names discovered in the course of investigation. That's broad as a wharf."

"It is meant to be broad."

"Meaning if I hear something in a kitchen, it becomes yours."

"Meaning if you intend to use something in the campaign I am financing, I will not pay to be second in line to your conscience."

"There you are," Silas said softly. "That's the part with the knife."

Gideon did not deny it.

"Mr. Vale, if this were a moral matter, I would have sent a parson. It is a market matter. The city is already naming the strain whether you sign or not. The only question is whether the process remains wasteful, dangerous, and available to every fool within earshot, or whether someone competent imposes sequence on it."

"Competent," Junie said. "He means paid."

"Often the two conditions are not enemies," Gideon said.

Silas looked down at the contract again. The ink held steady, black and impeccable, while the room around it breathed stew and soap and poverty. The paper seemed almost indecent on Junie's scarred table. That, more than the number, made the choice feel dirty. Money always looked clean when richer men brought it into poorer rooms.

"You'd have me work in private," he said.

"Until the public act matters."

"And the stable name. You already have one in mind?"

"Several possibilities. I am paying you because none yet has both grace and obedience."

"Obedience," Silas repeated.

"From the market, not the moss."

"I don't think you know the difference."

For the first time, something a shade colder entered Gideon's face.

"I know the only difference that survives distribution."

Junie dried her hands and came to stand beside the stove. She did not tell him yes. She did not tell him no. That would have been mercy, and they had passed the point where mercy came cheap.

"Read it all before you sign," she said. "If you sign."

Silas almost laughed at the if. Not because it was false. Because it was still being offered.

He read. He read the clauses twice. They smelled wrong the way some rooms smelled wrong before you spotted the mildew, all sweetness first and rot underneath. Exclusivity. Retainer. Materials. Trade name. Packet labels. Wall notices. Discovery rights. A man's mouth turned into an intake valve.

Pike's fourteen would carry mash, carrier suppers, maybe a week's peace with Pell if the laughter bit deep enough. It would also keep boys at the pane and Junie's stove in the morning papers. Gideon's sixty would clear Havelock and throw a heavier door across the kitchen, but only by turning every useful word he heard behind that door into Verdancy feed. One bargain spent the room in public. The other spent him in private.

He looked at Otis, half under again, Junie's wet hand still spread between the boy's shoulders. That tipped it. Or gave him a cleaner lie about what had tipped it.

When he took up the pen Gideon offered, the nib scratched on the page because the air had swelled the fibers. His signature looked too decorative for the occasion, all the old flourish still clinging to the S of his name like vanity refusing to die when called.

He signed anyway.

Gideon slid the envelope across at once, efficient as a banker closing a drawer. He rose, replaced his glove, and collected the contract with a care that made it plain he already considered the thing filed inside the future.

"You have done wisely," he said.

"I've done expensively," Silas said.

"Wisdom often presents that way at first."

Junie opened the door for him with a politeness so bare it functioned as insult. Gideon stepped back into the damp lane, his shoes avoiding the worst of the puddled grit without seeming to look down. Then he was gone, a dark clean stroke moving up toward the better-lit streets.

Silas stayed at the table with the envelope in his hand. The paper was thick, warm from Gideon's pocket. Junie did not ask to see inside it. She knew the weight of money without counting by touch. Otis slept on, breathing milk and chalk and child sweat into the cooling room.

"Well," Junie said at last.

Silas thumbed the flap open and looked at the notes. Real notes. Enough to turn several kinds of disaster slightly sideways.

"Yeah," he said.

That was all. Nothing noble fit in the kitchen after a choice like that. The stove ticked. The basin water slowly lost its last heat. Outside, somebody on the landing laughed and said bridelight, making it sound like nothing more than a pretty thing to have in the mouth.

Silas sat with Gideon's money in his hand and knew at least this much clearly: whatever the moss answered to next, his need would be in the word, and by morning need would send him back toward the wards that knew more than he did.
