# Chapter 5

By noon Gideon had dressed Patent Row for a wedding between commerce and obedience.

The old cooperage wall had been skinned in fresh lime and damp plaster, smooth as a rich man's lie. Verdancy House bunting drooped from the scaffold in strips of pale green silk that looked refined from the middle of the street and fungus-sick when a person got close enough to see the water marks. Two hired boys in white gloves stood guard over trays of handbills and paper crowns. A string quartet, paid well enough to hate itself, kept sawing out something solemn while the whole block sweated under them. Even the gutters had been sluiced. They still smelled of eel rot, brewer's mash, and the sweet dead breath of warm pond water, but now they smelled of those things in public clothes.

Silas stood on the scaffold plank with a mahlstick under one arm and his brush roll at his hip, staring at the wall that was supposed to save him.

Celadon Crown had sounded expensive the first time Silas said it across Gideon's desk and watched the man smile like a till springing open. It sounded more expensive now, printed in soft green on the handbills, set in looping Roman letters above the promise of regular color, regular heat, regular supply. It was the sort of name that knew which fork to use. It belonged in Gilt Crescent under cut glass and folded napkins. It did not belong in a cellar where a woman needed to know if her soup would scorch or if the lamp by the stairs would stay cool enough for a child not to blister two fingers on the cage. Inez had shown him too many kitchens, lamp cages, and wet steps for the word to sit clean in his mouth anymore. Silas knew that now. He knew too much now. None of it had stopped him from wearing his best coat and turning up early with his brushes cleaned and his hair tied back.

He needed the launch to work. Need sat in him with the vulgar simplicity of rent. Gideon's advance had already gone into meals, varnish, and the sort of immediate necessities that vanished so fast a man looked down and wondered whether money had ever been a material thing at all. There was more to be had if the campaign took. More work, more visibility, more proof that his mouth and hand were still worth a crowd stopping for. A failed private job stank for a week. A failed public christening got into the brick.

"You look like you're waiting to be hanged," Damaris Pike said.

She had come in widow's black gone shiny at the elbows, with ink smudged into the lines beside her thumb and a peppermint turning slowly between her teeth. The chipped edge of her front tooth showed when she smiled. She had pinned her hat with two ordinary steel pins and one little brass fishhook, because she never wore anything without making it answer two jobs.

Silas glanced down at her. "Only if the rope's paying."

"If the rope's paying, you'll flirt with it first."

"You're here early for a hanging."

"I'm here for a christening. A good hanging comes free if the Almighty is kind."

She looked up at the wall, the silk, the quartet, the trays of crowns, and her mouth curled around the peppermint.

"He's laid this on thick," she said.

"That's because he's selling thin goods."

"Don't be vain. You're thin goods too."

She said it pleasantly, as if remarking on the weather. Silas preferred her to nicer people for that. Nicer people put a napkin over the knife before they set it in.

Across the street, buyers from Gilt Crescent were arriving under parasols the color of milk and old pistachio. One man wore a waistcoat bright enough to announce an inheritance. Two sisters from a ribbon house picked their way between puddles with the furious precision of women who had never once in life been forgiven for dirty hems. Behind them came the real body of Patent Row: apprentices with size on their cuffs, newsboys, clerks, laundry women on dinner break, ferry men in shirts gone dark between the shoulder blades, errand girls, and children who had followed the handbills because a printed crown still looked like a prize if you were eight and barefoot.

And beyond them, where Patent Row tilted down toward the drain cut, Silas saw Inez.

He picked her out because she was not trying to be seen. Everybody else on the street shifted, called, bargained, displayed. Inez stood with one boot on the curb and one on the slick plank thrown over the gutter, her weight easy as if the city had been built for her balance alone. Her dark hair was wrapped in a work scarf the color of old brick. A strip had come loose at the wrist and showed the pale crescent burn scar he had noticed in the grow houses when she reached past him for a hot pipe without flinching. She was not dressed for spectacle. She was dressed for weather and bad surfaces and something that might need lifting.

Two women stood with her. One had red forearms from lye work and a green comb stabbed through her hair like a threat. The other had a basket hook looped over one elbow and kept scratching the side of her neck with it, slow and absent, as if crowds always made her itch. They watched the wall the way practical people watched a man who had promised too much.

Silas wished, suddenly and stupidly, that the whole thing looked less foolish from where she stood.

Gideon emerged from under the awning at exactly the moment a lesser man would have hurried. He never hurried. He moved as if time had been instructed in advance. His coat was pearl gray, his gloves cream, his silvering hair so neat that the damp seemed to refuse him on principle. A little mist of river humidity hung on everybody else. Gideon appeared to have negotiated separate terms.

"Mr. Vale." He tipped his head up toward the scaffold. "When I begin, I want the first line in place before the second refrain."

"You want miracles to count bars now?"

"I want timing. Miracles are a language for people who dislike invoices."

His eyes moved once over the crowd. He had the habit of looking at human beings the way other men looked at shelving.

"There is appetite here," he said quietly. "That is useful. We will give it shape."

Silas almost laughed. Appetite here was sweat, debt, boredom, curiosity, and the city's incurable taste for anything that might embarrass its betters before supper. But he nodded. It was easier than arguing, and harder to explain why he had begun to feel, after the morning in Sump Ward, that a shaped appetite could still bite the hand that arranged it.

Gideon stepped onto the little platform built before the wall. One of the white-gloved boys struck a brass triangle for silence. The quartet stopped on a held note and the street, by some miracle of appetite and heat and curiosity, actually quieted.

Gideon did not raise his voice. He never needed to. He spoke like a man who expected the room, the ledger, and the weather to meet him halfway.

"Neighbors, patrons, householders," he said. "Our city has lately enjoyed more novelty than discipline. Novelty has its amusements. Discipline keeps a stove steady, a lamp dependable, a purchase respectable. Verdancy House is pleased today to present a strain under a name equal to its quality and fit for repeated trade."

Gideon was at his most dangerous when he made greed sound like civic housekeeping.

The handbills lifted across the crowd. Buyers leaned. Children put on the paper crowns and instantly began ruining them. One little boy with dock dirt ground black into one trouser knee turned his crown into a telescope before the speech was halfway done. His mother smacked his shoulder without looking at him.

Gideon went on.

"A city may chatter. Commerce must answer clearly. The name is Celadon Crown."

On cue, the quartet shifted into something lighter. The boys with crowns began the refrain first, trained and shrill and a little frightened.

"Celadon Crown," they sang.

The hired callers by the curb repeated it. Then the crowd did, because crowds would repeat almost anything once if enough bodies said it first.

"Celadon Crown."

The handbills shook. The paper crowns bobbed. A line of sample trays on the platform, each holding a fed patch of the runaway moss under glass, went from unsettled green to a pale, courtly shade that could have sat nicely beside silk ribbon and expensive soap.

The cooperage wall answered too.

The seeded wash beneath the damp lime breathed out a soft glazed green, almost blue at the edges, the color of cool porcelain in a rich dining room. A little murmur moved through the block. One of the ribbon sisters actually smiled. The man in the inheritance waistcoat said, "There now," to nobody in particular, with the relieved vanity of a person whose taste had just been endorsed by biology.

Silas felt it in his own body, that terrible sweet lift when a thing might work after all.

For one minute the wall did look obedient. The green held. The handbills made sense in the light. Even Patent Row seemed briefly willing to pretend it had not been built on scams, spills, and people shouting from one bad floor to another. Silas hated how badly he wanted that minute to lengthen. He wanted Inez to see that he had not learned nothing from her. He wanted Gideon to be right just long enough to pay out. He wanted the city to sit for him like wet paint under a good brush.

Gideon turned without looking up. "Now, Mr. Vale."

***

Silas set his mahlstick, dipped the long lettering brush into the celadon pot, and touched the wall.

The first stroke took beautifully.

The brush slid through damp heat and lime the way a skiff knife slid through river skin, easy and sure. He pulled the high curve of the C with his whole shoulder, then thinned the tail with a practiced turn of the fingers. The green against the brighter green beneath had just enough shadow to lift. He heard the little public sigh a crowd made when labor looked effortless. That sound had paid his supper more than once.

Below him, the trained children sang again.

"Celadon Crown."

He began the E.

Before he could finish the E, a woman above the cooper's balcony rail barked, "Cooklow."

The voice had lye in it. Silas looked up without meaning to and saw the laundry woman with the green comb in her hair. She had climbed the stairs with her wet arms bare and her apron still on, and she leaned out as if the whole performance had been staged for her correction alone.

"Cooklow," she said again, louder. "That's what it is if you've got a pot to mind."

Her little girl, who had soap flakes stuck to one shin, echoed her at once because children trusted repetition more than prestige.

"Cooklow!"

Three women near Inez laughed and took it up. It wasn't mockery exactly, not at first. It was more the reflex of people hearing a false instruction and answering with the right one before it got somebody burned.

"Cooklow."

The lower third of the wall cooled toward a denser kitchen green, darker and steadier than the elegant wash Gideon had bought. The tail of Silas's E blurred.

He heard it happen before he fully saw it: a moist little hiss under the paint, as if the plaster had put its tongue through the letter.

"Keep going," Gideon said.

Silas kept going. Of course he did.

He laid in the L fast, then the A. The crowd's official refrain tried to regain the street.

"Celadon Crown."

But a ferry hauler by the curb, broad as a packing crate and still wearing one leather shoulder pad from the morning's loads, cupped his hands and shouted, "Ropegreen."

"Ropegreen," somebody from the docks answered at once.

"Ropegreen!"

Ropegreen moved through the middle distance of the crowd with the rough comfort of a thing said a hundred dawns running. Silas had heard something like it at Ferrymouth without marking it. Missing it there felt worse than hearing it fresh.

The center of the wall darkened to a dockside green with black in it, the color of wet rope and old pilings. His pale lettering rose against it for half a breath and then began to sink, as if the wall were swallowing his hand a fraction behind his hand.

The G from Celadon puckered at the top. A bubble swelled beneath the fresh stroke.

Silas flattened it with the brush.

The bubble spat.

A fan of celadon slurry shot out from the brick and peppered the nearest buyers. One of the ribbon sisters took three wet flecks across her bodice. They did not sit there politely. They brightened. One turned a cheerful bridal pink against the cream silk, another darkened to rope green, and the third went a flat kitchen white that made the stain look at once expensive and diseased.

The sister looked down in horror.

"Oh, for Christ's sake."

The street laughed. It was not the whole street, but it was enough.

Silas heard Damaris laugh with her whole chest. He did not look at her. Looking at Damaris when she had just found money was never wise.

"Silence, please," Gideon said.

He did not shout. His quietness made it funnier.

Silas wiped the brush on the pot rim and reached for more pigment. His fingers had started to slip in the damp. Heat came off the wall in pulses now, cool and warm by turns, as different names struck it from different mouths. Sound was painting faster than his hand.

A thin old man under the awning, the sort of clerk who had kept his cuffs clean by despising everybody, called up in a nasal fury, "Use the proper term."

From the other balcony a woman Silas had not noticed before leaned out with a mourning ribbon tied around one wrist and said, very clear, "Wakeblue."

Wakeblue did it, not because she shouted but because three people on the landing below her repeated it with the grave immediate obedience of people who knew exactly what room that color belonged in.

"Wakeblue."

The upper edge of the wall slid toward a deep funeral blue that made the pretty green beneath look cheap and frightened. It was beautiful. The beauty was part of the humiliation. The wall was not merely failing him. It was showing off.

Somebody near the drain cut yelled "Bridelight," half as a joke, half from old habit, and pink lightning veined through the O he had just started in Crown. The crowd answered that one with scandalized delight, because they all remembered where it had begun and because people preferred the filthy accidental name to the decent purchased one.

"Bridelight!"

"Ropegreen!"

"Cooklow!"

"Wakeblue!"

The trained boys kept trying to hold their line.

"Celadon Crown," they sang, and now they sounded as if the song were happening to them against their will.

Inez had not moved. Silas looked down and saw her watching the wall, not him. He hated that more than if she had laughed. Her mouth had flattened to a straight line. She barely blinked as she tracked the color spread, the way she had watched a cracked lamp tray in the grow houses when she was already following the leak to whatever it would ruin next.

"Mr. Vale," Gideon said, still quiet. "Finish it."

"You hearing the same city I'm hearing?"

"Finish it."

Shame crawled up under Silas's collar, and his mouth reached for a joke before the feeling could finish landing.

He leaned down off the plank. "You lot want to pick one before I grow old up here?"

"Pick your own stove, darling," the lye-armed woman called back, and the street barked.

"You paint what the wall says," the ferry hauler added. "That's your trade, isn't it?"

The worst part was that Silas could not say no with a clean conscience.

He turned back to the wall and painted harder, as if force had ever once in history improved lettering.

Celadon Crown came out across the cooperage bricks in immense capitals, but each letter lived in different weather by the time it was done. CELA lay over mottled green with pink veins running through the counters. DON sat in a patch gone almost white at the edges, fever-pale and wrong. CROWN broke under bands of rope green and wake blue. The wet lime kept sweating through the pigment, lifting it in translucent blisters. Every fresh correction birthed a new leak. By the time he outlined the final N, the first C had already begun to fray at the shoulders.

The paper crowns in the crowd started changing color to match whatever name their owners shouted. Children discovered this at once and turned it into sport.

"Wakeblue!" one boy hollered, and his crown darkened.

"Bridelight!" screamed another, and three crowns blushed pink.

"Cooklow!" cried the girl with the soap on her shin, and the band of her paper crown turned a domestic dull green that looked ridiculous above her solemn face.

Laughter rolled outward in hot, bright waves.

Gideon's hired callers tried to restore order by giving the official name more volume. More volume only widened the channels. More mouths, more answer. Patent Row took the opportunity the way a hungry man took dropped fruit.

Now the names were not only correction. They were wager, territory, class irritation, neighborhood pride, old joke, small revenge. People from Lantern Steps wanted the upper houses to hear they still named their own light. Dock men wanted the ridge buyers to know a rope had touched the thing before silk had. Women who cooked by it refused to have upholstery vocabulary dropped into their pots. Boys shouted whatever got the prettiest reaction. Old men muttered the name of a dead wife's lamp habit just to see if the wall remembered grief.

The wall remembered everything at once.

It brightened until the letters seemed backlit from inside the brick. Then it shifted in islands and bands so fast Silas's eyes watered trying to follow it. A violetless white flashed under the middle of Crown. A pink run burst through the R and crawled down like a tongue. Blue filmed over the top line. Green thickened at the base. The effect was not elegant, not stable, not sellable.

It was magnificent.

Its magnificence made him furious.

He slapped one palm to the brick to steady himself and felt the living temperature of the thing kick between cool and hot under his skin. It was no metaphor. He felt actual heat, then no heat, as if a dozen households were arguing through the wall about what they needed from it.

"Fuck," he said.

He did not say it under his breath or to himself. He said it to the wall, the crowd, the contract, the silk bunting, and the exact shape of his own stupidity.

The wall answered with another spit.

This time the slurry caught the man in the inheritance waistcoat full across the front. It hit him in a broad starburst that turned first celadon, then bruised blue at the edges. He made a small shocked sound and took a blind step backward into one of the tray boys, who dropped a stack of sample cards into the gutter. The cards stuck, drank street water, and came up on the soles of passing boots like a flock of drowned leaves.

Now even the ribbon sisters laughed, because once expensive people were visibly sticky the moral universe felt briefly restored.

***

Silas climbed down before he broke his neck. The ladder rungs were slick with mist and paint, and for one hard second he imagined exactly how the street would sound if he slipped and came down backward into his own branding launch. The image got him to the ground more carefully than dignity did.

Heat closed over him at street level. Perfume, pond rot, lamp smoke, wet brick, human armpit, and the mineral stink of torn plaster all crowded the same breath. Somebody's elbow hit his ribs. Somebody else pushed a handbill against his chest like a joke gift. The crowd had lost its shape. A crowd gone shapeless was always the danger point. A crowd with a program could be steered. A crowd that had smelled blood wanted participation.

"Ask the wall!" a boy yelled.

"Ask the cook!" somebody else answered.

"Ask your damned laundry!"

"Wakeblue!"

"Bridelight!"

"Celadon Clown," said a voice from somewhere he could not place, and that one nearly took the block apart.

Even Silas laughed once, a sharp involuntary bark, because there were moments when the city's vulgarity was too exact not to admire.

Gideon did not laugh.

He had stepped down from the platform and removed one glove finger by finger, as if bare skin might somehow negotiate better terms with disaster. There was a wet fleck of pale green on his cuff. It was the most naked Silas had ever seen him.

"Mr. Vale," Gideon said.

The two words were enough. Gideon used no volume and made no scene, which meant he was already entering the whole disaster into some private ledger for later use.

"It was holding," Silas said. "You heard it. Another half minute and we'd have had the line fixed."

"We had the line fixed. We did not have the street."

"That's because the street lives here."

Gideon's gaze settled on him, cool as ledger metal.

"That is not news."

"Then next time don't stage a coronation on a drain."

Silas knew at once he had gone too far. Gideon's expression barely changed, but the little remaining politeness in it folded up like a paper card.

"There will be a next time," Gideon said. "You will be less theatrical in it."

Before Silas could answer, Damaris was beside them, notebook already open. She wrote in a fast cramped hand with the pencil braced against her chipped tooth between bursts, as if she kept the point sharp by spite alone.

"Was that 'less theatrical' before or after the gentleman in the blue waistcoat got christened in public?" she asked.

"Mrs. Pike," Gideon said, and if contempt could have been notarized it would have sounded like that.

"Mr. Wren. Marvelous event. Strong colors. Fine disorder."

"You are obstructing business."

"No, sir. Business obstructed business. I only arrived with paper."

She tipped her chin toward the wall where Celadon Crown still stood in huge letters, every capital now crosshatched by the city's disobedience.

"I wouldn't rush to call it a failure," she said. "You've got argument. Argument sells better than agreement unless the agreement is attached to funerals or sugar."

"I am not selling newspapers."

"That's why I'm."

She snapped the notebook shut and turned to the crowd like an actress hitting her mark.

"What do you call it in Lantern Steps?" she shouted.

The answer came at once, overlapping and delighted now that somebody respectable had finally asked the useful question.

"Cooklow!"

"Wakeblue!"

"Ropegreen!" from the dock men, louder now because nothing encouraged a ferry crew like seeing upper-street commerce lose its hat.

Damaris pointed her pencil without choosing.

"And in Sump?"

The question produced a beat of silence, not because no one knew, but because some names were not for her column and some people on that street still understood the difference.

Then Inez said, "Depends what you're doing."

She had stepped forward just enough for Silas to hear her without strain. She did not raise her voice to a public pitch. She never gave more air than a sentence needed. The crowd near her quieted around the fact of being answered by somebody who worked for a living with the thing under dispute.

"If you're cooking, one name," she said. "If you're crossing stairs with a baby and no daylight, another. If you're sitting up with the dead, another. That's the trouble with your pretty crown. It doesn't tell anybody what their hands are in."

Damaris's eyes lit like press lamps.

"There," she said softly, mostly to herself. "There you are."

Silas saw the exact second the story left Gideon's platform and entered Damaris's hands. It was not mystical. It was trade. She had the line that carried class, joke, injury, and consequence all at once. By breakfast it would be on half the ward tables with grease on the margins.

Gideon understood it too. His jaw tightened once.

"Mrs. Pike," he said, "if you print household misinformation, you will answer to counsel."

"If I print what your own wall did, counsel can sue the brick."

She whistled to a runner boy in a cap too large for him. He came skidding through the gutter water, one shoe half unlaced and a smear of printer's black already along his cuff.

"Tell Nall to hold the second side for this," she said. "Make it a big head. Keep it ugly. I want verbs that make a racket."

"What's the head?"

She looked at the wall, at Silas striped in accidental color, at the ruined waistcoat, at the paper crowns going pink and blue over a crowd now cheerfully arguing itself into neighborhoods.

"Patent Row Crowns a Moss and Loses the Street," she said. "No. Better. Crown Refused by Kitchens, Docks, and Dead. Run both if there's room."

The boy grinned and bolted.

Silas felt the blood drain through him in a slow ugly pull. It was not only embarrassment. Embarrassment he knew. This was the larger, colder thing underneath it: the sense of a door swinging open behind him onto consequences he could not charm shut again. Damaris would make him comic by morning. Gideon would make him useful or punishable by afternoon. And Inez, who had walked him through rooms where names kept people safe, had now watched him stand on a public scaffold and try to lay one expensive word over all of it like wallpaper.

He turned to her.

"I didn't think it would go like this."

It was a stupid sentence. He knew it while it was still warm in his mouth.

Inez looked at him at last. Up close the air had freckled damp along her temple. There was a bit of plaster dust on her scarf where somebody in the crowd had brushed past. Her expression had not gone soft, but it had shifted out of pure anger into something worse for him: exact appraisal.

"No," she said. "You thought the wall would mind manners if the paper was nice enough."

The lye-armed woman behind her snorted. The basket-hook woman scratched her neck again and said, not unkindly, "That's Patent Row sickness."

Silas could have lied. He could have told Inez he had tried to split the difference. He could have said he meant to get money out of Gideon and then steer the rest better later. All of that had some truth in it. None of it improved the scene in front of them.

Instead he said, "I needed it to take."

"Of course you did."

Her flat certainty hurt because she did not say it like a moral victory. She said it like a maintenance fact. Damp rose. Pipes burst. Men needed money and called it judgment.

She nodded once toward the gutter where celadon run-off was already threading downhill through the brick channels in bright diluted ribbons.

"That slime's heading south," she said. "If it gets into cook stoves or lamp trays, you'll hear about it from people who don't give speeches first."

Then she stepped back into the crowd and was gone, not vanished, just absorbed by bodies that had better reasons than Silas to be in the street.

The quartet had given up. One of the violinists sat on the platform edge wiping his strings with a handkerchief that would never come clean. The ribbon sisters were leaving with their damaged hems held up. The inheritance waistcoat man kept touching his chest as if hoping the stain would prove imaginary. Children were still testing their paper crowns for color, turning in circles and shouting names at one another with the shrill ecstatic seriousness of a game they would carry into schoolyards, kitchens, ferries, and alleys before the day was done.

"Wakeblue!"

"Bridelight!"

"Cooklow!"

"Celadon Clown!"

"Celadon Clown" came back again and again because mockery had the best legs in Nacre Bend.

Silas looked up at the wall.

Celadon Crown was still there, huge and undeniable. He had painted it well. His own good lettering was the insult. The capitals had weight, curve, proportion, a professional confidence no crowd could deny him. But the city had written through him. Pink burned in the counters. Rope green thickened at the bases. Blue held the upper brick like evening in a mourning room. White flashed where the heat had gone wrong. The letters looked less like a decree than like something caught in crossfire.

Gideon followed his gaze.

"This can still be placed," he said.

Silas turned. "Placed where?"

"Everywhere repetition is better managed."

The answer chilled him more than rage would have. Gideon was not surrendering. He was taking notes.

The lone wet fleck on Gideon's cuff had turned, during all this, from pale green to a sharp accusing pink.

Silas almost told him. Instead he watched Gideon notice it, and watched the man's face harden by one careful degree.

Down the slope, Damaris's runner vanished into press alley. The crowd was already carrying the argument outward in clumps, each cluster with its own preferred word. Handbills stuck to boots. Paper crowns drifted in the gutter. Celadon wash ran through the channels toward Ferrymouth and the lower wards in bright infected threads.

Silas stood in the middle of the emptied ceremony with paint drying on his knuckles and other people's names drying on his work, and understood that what had broken was not only the launch. Damaris would have it in print by breakfast. The run-off would reach the lower wards sooner than that.

Patent Row had seen him lose the street.
