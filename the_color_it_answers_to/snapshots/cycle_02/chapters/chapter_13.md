# Chapter 13

The landing reserved for honored guests had been scrubbed until it smelled expensive. Someone up on Gilt Crescent had dumped soap, lemon peel, and brass polish over the usual breath of warm mud and algae mash, and the whole waterfront wore the effort badly. Under it all the Sallow still stank of sweet rot. The launches rocked against their ropes with garlands of cut glass and paper pennants, and every pennant carried the same pale green letters: CELADON CROWN. The words were printed so many times they looked less like language than wallpaper for a rich man's delusion.

Silas stood there and felt his collar drinking him. Gideon's tailor had sent over a cream summer coat that fit across the shoulders and nowhere else, and the cloth held heat like spite. His right wrist, still not right since the trough, ached inside the cuff whenever he flexed his hand. Below the rail the quay churned with ferry crews, shop girls, buyers from the ridge, newsboys with bundles under their arms, and the sort of men who came to any public arrangement in hopes that free liquor might fall out of it. Brass from the municipal band skimmed over the water. Somewhere a barge choir was testing the first lines of the regatta hymn. Even the gulls sounded instructed.

He ought to have laughed. A month ago he would have laughed until his ribs hurt. Gideon had turned a living civic crisis into a floating advertisement and dressed it up like a bride from a warehouse catalogue. But laughter needed looseness, and Silas had come tight with purpose. He had a folded scrap in his inner pocket with three names and three uses in Inez's hand, not because he meant to steal them again, but because they reminded him what a name was for when it was not being sold by the yard. If he got the horn, if he got even half a clean breath inside Gideon's ceremony, he meant to spoil the neatness of it: muddy the rhythm, make the crowd answer back, give the city room to remember its own mouth.

He went down the plank to the lower quay where the working vendors had been shoved under the ornamental bunting. Junie's scull sat wedged between a champagne boat for the ridge people and a fish seller who had tried to disguise his smell with mint. She had rigged a brass kettle over a little dock stove and was moving like six hands belonged to her by right: ladling broth, slicing bread, taking coins, knocking away a gull with the same spoon she was serving from. Flour streaked her forearm. The old split in her left eyebrow showed white whenever she frowned, which was most of the time today.

"You look like a corpse in pastry," she said without looking up.

"Good noon to you too."

"Lift that basket, then talk."

He lifted the basket of eel pies with his left hand. The rope handle bit into his palm. The stove under the kettle burned a dogged workman's green, but the flame sat lower than it ought to, breathing thinly around the iron plate.

"Pell shorted you," he said.

Junie snorted. "Pell sent half a barrel and a boy who kept squinting at my ledger like he meant to marry it. I've got enough mash till midafternoon if I stretch the broth and tell the crowd they're tasting elegance." She took the basket from him, counted under her breath, then looked up. "You eat?"

"Later."

"That's stupid. Eat now or you'll say something clever on an empty belly and get us all killed."

He nearly told her then, Tell me exactly how short, tell me what he's said, tell me if anybody's been sniffing round the stove room. But the quay swelled with customers and she had no spare surface on her body for his panic. A deckhand was already shoving coppers at her. She bent to him with a grin that was half hospitality, half threat.

Silas leaned closer. "If it turns mean up there, pack early."

"Turns mean? It's a river full of investors. It started mean." She tore a heel of bread off a loaf and jammed it into his hand. "And keep your mouth dirty. Clean speech is how those bastards win."

That was Junie: affection delivered as kitchen doctrine. He took the bread. It was still warm in the middle. He chewed and watched her feed the next customer. Each ladle came out a little more careful than the one before; she was doing fuel arithmetic with a soup ladle.

When he straightened, he found Inez on the plank walk above him, one hand on the wet rail. Her dark scarf had been tied back tight against the river wind, but damp had already darkened the cloth at her temple. The crescent burn on the inside of her right wrist flashed pale where she gripped the rail. She had put on a clean blouse for the day and ruined its neatness within minutes by leaning against tarred wood. That did something low and stupid in him even now.

She came down only far enough to keep clear of the crowd pressing Junie's scull. "How's the flame?"

"Hungry," Junie said, before he could answer. "Like everybody else in this city."

Inez looked at the stove, then at Silas. Her voice stayed public and spare. "If the regatta catches, every kitchen in Ferrymouth will look like that by tomorrow."

"It won't catch if I can help it," he said.

She watched him another moment. In trust or bed she let warmth into the sentence. Here she did not. "Don't let him have a clean line. They've built this thing on rhythm. Break the rhythm and it stumbles."

He touched the inside pocket where her folded scrap sat against his ribs. "I know what to do."

"Do you?" There was no sneer in it, only workman's doubt. "Silas, if you get the horn, don't perform at it. Make it ugly. Make them argue."

Junie shoved a bowl at a customer and muttered, "For once in his life, that's sound advice."

The corner of Inez's mouth moved, not quite a smile. It hurt him more than if she had given him the real one. They had not had a private room since the supper. They had had strategy, quick touches in doorways, her knee against his under Junie's table when people were shouting over one another. It was enough to keep him lit, not enough to quiet him. He wanted five minutes alone with her. He wanted a month. He got the river, the crowd, and Gideon's banners.

A widow-black figure elbowed through the newsboys and stopped under the bunting. Damaris Pike had come dressed for respectable mourning and profitable scandal in the same outfit as usual; her sleeves shone at the seams, and the chipped edge of her front tooth caught when she smiled up at him.

"Patent Row's saint of bad decisions," she said. "You do scrub up like a fraud promoted against nature."

"Mrs. Pike, if you've come to pray, aim elsewhere."

"I never pray before press time." She flicked a thumb at the barges. "He's got song sheets, Vale. Printed refrains. He's paying children to rhyme for him. If you mean to ruin the day, do it before the second chorus. After that it'll belong to the ferries and the schoolyards."

Inez's gaze sharpened. "How many barges?"

"Three I saw, maybe four if he's hidden one round the bend. The one with the choir master has a walrus mustache and a left boot that squeaks on every downbeat. He is trying to look important enough to ignore the rash chewing his neck." Damaris lowered her voice. "And Gideon's got a private tent up top. He sent for you two minutes ago."

"Me?" Silas said.

"You unless there's another overpromised sign painter in a cream coat I haven't been told about."

Junie clicked her tongue. "Go on then. And if he feeds you sugar water, spit in it."

Silas started up the plank. Inez caught his sleeve before he could climb past her. Her fingers closed just above his wrist, careful of the bad joint, and for half a second the whole quay narrowed to that pressure.

"Dirty," she said.

"Dirty," he echoed.

He went because he had been summoned like staff and because not going would have given Gideon the pleasure of sending two men to fetch him. The private tent stood behind the main reviewing dais, its striped canvas pegged above a stretch of dock that had been scrubbed so hard it smelled of wet hemp and lye. Inside, everything had been arranged to convince money it was attending nature without touching mud: polished crates draped in linen, a silver bucket sunk in river ice, bowls of sugared peel sweating into their own shine.

Gideon Wren stood at the folding table with his gloves off, which somehow made him look more clothed. His cuffs stayed unnaturally white in a city that stained anything it could reach. He was aligning a small stack of papers with the side of his thumb, tapping them flush as if their obedience mattered. When he looked up, his face held the public version of pleasure, all courtesy and no warmth.

"Mr. Vale. Thank you."

Silas shut the tent flap behind him. "If this is about the coat, it pinches."

"I can bear that disappointment." Gideon rested his fingertips on the papers. "We're late enough that I should be direct."

"You manage direct the way other men manage embalming."

Gideon let the line fall. That was one of his nastier talents, deciding in real time which insult deserved oxygen. "There are twenty-three vendors on the lower quay, seven hired song leads, four river launches, and a paid chorus of children waiting for a cue. I need one thing from you. The delivery must be clean."

Silas leaned one hip against the table because standing straight made him feel like a defendant. "I'm under no illusion about what you need from me."

"No," Gideon said. "You are under several more childish illusions, but not that one."

He slid the paper stack across the table with two fingers. They were not regatta programs. They were stained account notes, folded and refolded until the creases looked greasy. One carried the half-moon print of a thumb browned by broth. Another had a streak of flour ground into the corner. Junie's hand was all through them, blunt numbers marching down cheap paper, dates cramped to save space. At the top of the oldest note, Aram Pell's office stamp bled crooked blue ink through the fibers.

Silas did not pick them up. He knew them anyway. He had seen enough of Junie's scraps over the years to know the pressure of her pencil, the little hard kick on every seven. His mouth filled with the taste of the bread she had just handed him.

"What's this?" he said, though he knew.

"Your hinge," Gideon said.

Silas looked at him. "You bought her fuel paper."

"I bought arrears attached to a dependable appetite. Ferrymouth cooks regardless of fashion. It is one of the district's better habits."

Silas's hand closed around the edge of the table. His wrist answered with a hot, warning pull. "You couldn't make me dance, so you went after my sister's stove."

"I went after the mechanism that reliably moves you." Gideon's voice stayed almost gentle. "Please do not flatter either of us by calling it melodrama. This is accountancy."

He separated one note from the stack and set it forward. "Twenty-nine crowns, plus continuation on delivery. Pell was prepared to tighten her line this week even without me. I merely simplified the question. If you step to that dais and give the name clearly, her credit stands through the end of regatta season. The next barrels are released. Her kitchen keeps heat."

"And if I don't?"

Gideon met his eyes. "Then by evening the stove room at Ferrymouth is padlocked on arrears, her current mash is seized against the note, and every supplier I have touched this month declines further extension. She will not go under because she lacks courage, Mr. Vale. She will go under because soup cools."

For a moment all Silas could hear was the choir outside running scales on some bright civic lie. Soup cools. The bastard had found the ugliest possible phrase because it was true. It was not rent or bankruptcy or a number on a page. It was soup cooling in a dock kitchen while men with twelve-minute dinner breaks swore and walked elsewhere, Junie explaining to Otis why the stove stood dark.

"You're filth."

Gideon's expression altered by less than a teaspoon. "I am efficient. Had you been a solitary man, I would have no use for this."

Silas looked back down at the notes. One line item had been marked overdue in somebody else's neat clerk hand. Lamp mash, second delivery. Kitchen fuel. Emergency barrel. Every stroke smelled faintly of Junie's life, broth steam and cheap soap and the damp cupboard she kept her papers in above the spice tin. Domesticity laid out for leverage like exposed wiring.

"You could've come to me," Silas said. "You could've threatened my face, my hand, my contract. You didn't need to put your glove in her stock pot."

"I already came to you," Gideon said. "You embarrassed me in public, delayed me in private, and let yourself be educated into moral complication. That avenue has been tested."

Silas wanted to hit him. The desire arrived clean and stupid and almost relieving. He pictured Gideon's jaw breaking under the silver bucket. He pictured those perfect cuffs spotted red at last. But even if his wrist had been whole, which it was not, and even if the tent held no witnesses, which it did in all but flesh, hitting him would only have made the problem more physical and therefore simpler for Gideon. Men like Gideon loved simple.

"What exactly do you want said?"

Now Gideon smiled, very slightly. "There he is."

Silas felt the smile like spit.

"You will wait through the first hymn," Gideon said. "You will take the speaking horn from me and say, 'Nacre Bend, behold Celadon Crown.' Then you will repeat the name once more, without ornament, and hand the horn back. The choir has its answer. The vendors have their cry. The boys have their sheets. Clarity will do the rest."

Silas laughed once. It came out ugly. "You hired me for my mouth and now you want a clerk."

"I want authority, not style. Today even your vulgarity is only useful if it points in one direction."

Silas stared at the paper until the numbers blurred. In his inner pocket Inez's folded scrap pressed hot against his ribs. Dirty, she had said; he had to make it ugly. They had a plan thin as wire and maybe as useful. He could still do it, perhaps. He could step up and give the crowd five names instead of one, let the barges fight over echoes, turn Gideon's own saturation against him.

And Junie's stove room would be locked by dusk.

He heard himself ask, because a cornered man always wanted one last useless measurement, "If I do it, you keep your hands off her notes after?"

"I keep them solvent through the season," Gideon said. "I am not in the charity trade. But I remove the immediate blade. That is what you are buying."

And what was he buying it with? Half the city's light, the ward names already buried under print, Inez's face when she understood. He knew the price so well he could have painted it on a wall.

Gideon picked up his gloves, tugged them on finger by finger, and that was somehow worse than the papers had been. "We are done."

Silas did not move.

Gideon adjusted one cuff. "Mr. Vale."

Silas slid the notes back across the table because if he kept them in his hand he might tear them, and that would help no one. "If your boat sinks today," he said, "I'll watch."

"I am sure you will."

Outside, the band struck the opening march. The canvas walls shivered with applause.

Silas stepped back into the daylight like a man walking out of a confession booth to discover the church was built over a slaughterhouse. The quay had thickened. Buyers in summer linen leaned over the rails of hired launches. Ferry crews jammed shoulder to shoulder with wives in their better hats. Newsboys were already chanting the printed chorus under their breath to learn it fast. On the lower scull Junie had tied her sleeves higher and was moving even quicker now, the kettle burbling thinly. She glanced up at him once, saw something bad in his face, and set her jaw without asking in front of customers.

Inez waited at the foot of the dais steps. She had made herself a still point in the shove of bodies, which only made him feel his own shake more keenly.

"What did he want?" she asked.

He could have told her. That was the moment. He could have put the whole rotten thing in her hands as well as his own and let them decide it together, even if together meant running to Junie and then to Pell's office and then nowhere clean. He could have chosen not to choose alone. Instead he saw Junie's fuel notes on Gideon's table, flour-streaked and helpless, and he did what he had done too often in his life when shame came close: he tightened around it and called that control.

"He wanted the speech neat," he said.

Inez watched him long enough for the lie to become a physical thing between them. "And?"

"And I told him he'd have to work for it."

"That's not an answer."

"No," he said. "It's what I've got."

Her eyes went flatter, not cold but working, and that was worse. "If you've changed the plan, tell me now."

He looked past her to the water. A barge of schoolchildren in white collars was taking position, each one holding a paper lantern cup with a nursery patch glowing faint green inside it. One little girl at the end of the row kept smoothing the ribbon on her own cuff with the serious concentration of a child trying to be elegant on command. A river hand on the next launch was picking black grit from under his nails with a bait hook while reading the printed refrain upside down. The whole city was built from little human habits ripe for theft.

"I haven't changed it," he said.

It became true only in the meanest sense. He had not changed the plan. He had simply already begun betraying it.

The dais boards thudded under the weight of officials taking their places. Gideon mounted the center platform between two municipal men who looked relieved not to be the most hated figures in sight. Behind them, the regatta launches spread over the water in a fan: polished hulls, moss lanterns caged in cut glass, white streamers dragging their damp fringes across the river breeze. Every visible surface had words on it. Programs, pennants, crate labels, placards hung from oars. The printed phrase existed everywhere already, a city waiting with its mouth open. All it lacked was the moment somebody authoritative gave it breath.

Damaris had found a perch on a piling stub near the newsboys. She had her notebook open but was not writing. He noticed that because Damaris always wrote when frightened until the fear got too near the bone, and then the pencil stopped.

Gideon raised both hands. The band cut cleanly. The sudden absence of brass made the river noises come up hard: rope creak, gull cry, the slap of wash against pilings, Junie's ladle ringing the kettle rim below.

"Ladies and gentlemen," Gideon said, and his voice carried without strain. Of course it did. Everything about him had been designed to carry. "Nacre Bend has spent too many months at the mercy of accident. A city's light should not depend on rumor, whim, or neighborhood quarrel."

A few boos came from the dockside. A few laughs came from the ridge boats. Gideon let them pass through him like weather measured and priced.

"Today," he said, "we offer not novelty, but steadiness. Not confusion, but placement. A name you can ask for and receive. A name that travels from quay to kitchen, from parlor to ferry, without degradation."

Silas almost barked at that. Without degradation, he thought. The whole damned city was built on degradation properly distributed. But the crowd wanted to hear what came next. He could feel want moving through them like heat over marsh water. Buyers wanted certainty. Workers wanted the fuss to mean something besides another rich man's party. Children wanted a phrase bright enough to take home. Even people who hated Gideon wanted to know what shape the trap had this time.

The first hymn began from the choir barges. High voices, trained to a public sweetness that made the words feel clean enough to swallow. Silas caught fragments: crown of evening, crown of tide, crown of every window. He could have torn the lyricist's throat with his teeth. On the lower quay vendors took up the printed cry when the refrain marked it. "Celadon..." one called experimentally, not yet committed. The boys echoed from habit, practice, greed. It was a name half born, waiting for a midwife.

Below the hymn, Junie's scull had acquired a witness. One of Pell's clerks stood planted beside her stove with a leather satchel under his arm and a folded notice gone damp in his fist. A little brass padlock hung from two fingers and tapped his knee whenever the wash rocked the hull. Junie served around him as if he were a piling somebody had misplaced. Once Inez turned at the knock of brass against wood. She saw the satchel, the boy, the way he kept one eye on the dais, and her mouth thinned.

Silas stood three men down from Gideon, among aldermen, patrons, and one woman from the river commission whose feathered hat made her look like a furious heron. He should not have been the shabbiest soul on the platform in that coat, but he was by virtue of knowing what it had cost to put him there. His wrist pulsed. His inner pocket burned with Inez's folded scrap. Twice the crowd below spotted him and shouted something between a cheer and a heckle.

"Name us a better one, Vale!"

"Don't fall in the trough this time!"

"Paint us a miracle!"

He gave them his old grin once, purely by reflex, and heard how wrong it sounded coming out of him today. The grin got a laugh anyway. He had built too much of his life on those reflexes. That, more than Gideon's papers, might have been the real machinery.

The hymn ended. Applause moved across the water in staggered sheets. Gideon turned to him with the speaking horn in his gloved hand. It was not polished silver as Silas had expected. It was tin painted cream, practical under the ceremony, and the lip was wet from the last man's breath.

"Mr. Vale," Gideon said, just loudly enough for the near rows to hear. "If you would."

Humiliation had a way of clarifying edges. Silas saw the crease beside Gideon's mouth where satisfaction lived. He saw the council heron's annoyance that a tradesman would share the platform. He saw Junie below, arm-deep in bread crate, with Pell's clerk moored to her scull and the brass padlock glinting against his knuckles. He saw Inez by the rail, not moving at all now. He saw the schoolchild with the ribbon cuff mouthing along with the printed blank where the chosen name belonged. He saw Damaris holding still enough to count as prayer.

He could still wreck it.

That thought came with such force he nearly smiled for real. Of course he could. He was Silas Vale. He had made and unmade crowds for less than this. He could take the horn, turn Gideon's clean river pageant into a market brawl, crack the rhythm open, hand the thing back in pieces, and let Junie's stove room pay the bill.

That was the problem. Junie's stove room was not an abstraction or a noble sacrifice performed over somebody else's supper. It was Junie's kettle, Junie's hand on the ladle, Otis coming home to a dark kitchen and asking what broke. He had spent weeks learning that names landed on bodies and budgets, on women with blisters and men with twelve-minute meal breaks. Now the lesson had turned and bared its teeth at his own door.

He took the horn.

The tin was warm, slick with old spit and river damp. It pulled at his wrist. He stepped to the front rail. The crowd leaned in with one body. Even the launches seemed to hinge toward him.

He began where he had meant to begin. "Nacre Bend," he said, and the old public ease came back enough to carry the name of the city without a crack. "You've called this poor shining nuisance half the filthiest, prettiest things I've ever heard."

The laugh came where he wanted it, and for one beat he had them.

"Patent Row called it one thing to sell pomade. Ferrymouth called it another when it fouled a stove. Sump Ward's had names for what it does when a child touches the hot side and when an old woman needs the cool side and when the wash line wants light but not blaze."

A murmur moved through them, less easy now. He felt movement at the edge of the crowd where Inez's people had scattered themselves. They were ready. If he kept going, if he made the next sentence dirty enough, maybe the barges would miss their cue and the crowd would start answering in pieces. He could feel the opening, an actual live seam.

Then he looked down, not at Gideon first but at the lower quay, where the clerk had not budged. He was young, maybe nineteen, with ears that stuck out and a fresh shaving nick still holding a bead of blood under his jaw. He was not evil. That was part of the obscenity. He looked like a boy sent to fetch butter. The folded notice had gone soft in his hand. The little brass padlock kept tapping his knee. Junie, still holding her ladle, was talking at him through her teeth while serving with the other hand because custom did not stop for blackmail. The clerk glanced up toward the dais when he felt the crowd shift, waiting on the word he had been sent to obey.

Silas could have kept wrecking the sentence. He knew that. Nobody forced his jaw. Nobody bent his spine. He made the calculation himself with full sight of the sewer it ran through. If he tore the ceremony open now, Junie paid first, before dark, before her kettle cooled.

He hated that clarity. He hated himself for wanting it. A blur would have let him call it fate. This did not.

His next breath hurt on the way in. He heard Inez say dirty in the back of his skull. He heard Junie say keep your mouth dirty. He heard Gideon say soup cools. All the voices in the city reduced to three and one of them owned paper.

He made his mouth clean.

"The name," he said, and the river held still enough to hear it, "is Celadon Crown."

For one holy fraction of a second nothing happened. Silence sat there, shocked by its own obedience.

Then the choir answered exactly as paid. "Celadon Crown," the children sang, bright as cut glass.

The vendors caught it next because money trained faster than conscience. "Celadon Crown! Fresh cuttings! Celadon Crown!"

The newsboys shouted it because shouting was their trade and a new two-beat cry was meat. Ferrymen took it up to mock the ridge accents and found it sat just as well in a cracked mouth as a polished one. The little girl with the ribbon cuff lifted her chin to shape the second word. Three drunks on the public steps bawled it wrong, then corrected themselves because the crowd was already teaching them.

Silas could have dropped the horn. Instead, because cowardice liked completion, because half a betrayal still left room for hope, because Gideon's instruction had been precise and he had agreed to it in the tent, he said it again.

"Celadon Crown."

This time the name went through the regatta like a hook pulled under skin.

You could hear the fit of it. Cel-a-don made the tongue work against the teeth, a small show of refinement even from men without any. Crown made the chin lift on the round. The phrase adjusted bodies as it moved. Backs straightened. Mouths pursed pretty for the first half and opened royal for the second. Housewives on the quay tried it with broth ladles in hand. A coal heaver grinned because it made him sound expensive. A child said it purely for the pleasure of how the last word sat on air. The barges repeated. The printed sheets suddenly matched what people were already saying. Every channel Gideon had paid for locked at once.

Across the water the moss answered.

The lantern cups on the school barge blushed out of their work green into a pale, lacquered gold that looked rich enough to hang round a duchess's throat and useful enough to betray a kitchen. Ropes furred with living glow took the same color in streaks, then full. The garland on the lead launch brightened and thinned, shedding its old earthy heat for a prettier, colder burn. People gasped because beauty always got there first. That was another of the city's bad habits.

Down below, Junie's stove flame changed.

It happened at the edge first: the green going blond, the steady blue belly of heat lifting out and away. Then the whole fire sharpened into a pale, expensive gold that threw a lovely light and a mean boil. The broth stopped its heavy dockside roll. Junie saw it in the same instant Inez did. She swore, snatched the kettle half aside, and the ladle clanged overboard.

Inez moved before the splash finished.

She did not look up at him first. She vaulted the lower rail, boots finding the mooring post and then the scull deck with the easy exactness of somebody born to wet wood. Her clean blouse took a stripe of soot at once. She grabbed the stove valve bare-handed, flinched only when the crescent scar on her wrist kissed the hot metal, and snapped, "Not that air. Starve it. Junie, feed the dark mash, not the bright."

Junie was already hauling up the backup tin from under the bench, cursing so fast the words ran together. "I know what a starving flame looks like, woman."

"I know. Do it anyway."

The boy clerk with Pell's satchel had backed off two steps from the sudden labor, the notice still pinched in his hand, the little brass padlock knocking against his knee. He looked as if he had expected paperwork and found a kitchen emergency instead. Junie saw the padlock, saw Inez see it, and then all three of them looked up to the dais together.

Only then did Inez lift her head to the dais.

He had wanted anger. Anger had room in it for the body, for a quarrel, for later. What she gave him was recognition so complete it felt like the removing of a bandage. She saw the clean line he had given them. She saw the clerk. She saw the stove turning decorative under her hands. Whatever still lived between them had to share air with that.

He tried to speak, maybe her name, maybe an explanation, maybe the truth at last now that it was too late and public.

She did not let him waste the breath. "Don't," she said, and even across the waterlogged noise of the quay the word came to him hard and flat. "I haven't got time to hear how you meant it."

Then, because Junie was wrestling the mash tin open and the crowd kept crying the new name above them like gulls over offal, Inez turned from him to the people who could still be helped. She shoved the clerk back by the chest with her soot-blackened forearm and said, "If you're waiting to close a stove, wait till she's fed the line you're profiting from." Then to Junie: "Keep your own books dry. I'll get the ward women off the free cuttings before they bring this shit home."

Junie, flushed and furious, looked from the padlock to Silas and understood enough to go white under the heat. She did not accuse him. That might have been mercy or simple lack of time. "If you've bought me dear," she shouted up, "you'd better pray I can spend it."

Then Inez was gone into the crowd, moving low and fast along the vendor line, knocking branded nursery cups out of hands, shouting practical warnings at any woman who looked as if she might actually listen. He heard pieces.

"Not for stoves."

"Keep the old trays separate."

"Don't mix it with kitchen stock."

Aunt Vero had to be somewhere farther back because suddenly another older voice was answering in rougher terms about pretty light and useless soup. A ripple of argument started at the edge of the quay where the working people stood. The catch was too far along to stop, but maybe the burn could still be slowed. Inez had already gone where the work still was.

Gideon's gloved hand settled on Silas's shoulder with the intimacy of a hand on a coffin lid. The applause had begun, ridiculous and sincere in equal measure. People loved to clap for the completion of a thing, even if the thing was their own collar being fitted.

"Well done," Gideon said.

Silas shrugged him off so hard his bad wrist flashed white pain to the elbow. The horn nearly slipped. He shoved it back into Gideon's chest and came away with cream paint on his palm. Somebody on the launch below called for the name again, wanting to hear it repeated as if repetition itself were a blessing. The choir obliged. The newsboys obliged. The vendors obliged because they could smell which way the crowd's hunger had turned.

Celadon Crown. Celadon Crown.

The words went outward over water and inward through the city at the same time. He could hear them landing in different mouths and changing shape only slightly, not enough to break. That was the real victory Gideon had bought: not purity, but resilience. A dockhand with broth on his cuff said it with mock grandeur and then again because he liked the feel. Two girls from Lantern Steps whispered it into each other's bonnets and laughed. A man on the ridge barge tried it in French, failed, and came back to the common version. The name had legs already.

On the lower quay Junie got her stove breathing darker again by force and foul language. The flame held, but sullenly, and not before the first row of paying customers had watched her fight it. The boy clerk stood useless as a nailed bird. Junie kicked her day's cash box farther under the bench where no clerk could lay a hand on it without losing one, then set about serving with a face that promised murder in several practical forms. She had been saved exactly as Gideon promised: not from pressure, only from immediate extinction. It was almost enough to make Silas retch.

Damaris finally moved her pencil. He saw her write one line, stop, tear the page out, and crush it in her fist. That, more than her notebook, told him what sort of day it had become.

The regatta launches had begun their ceremonial pass. Oars dipped. Lantern garlands threw pale gold across the chop. The river took the new color greedily, broke it, returned it in trembling lengths against the pilings. Everywhere the light touched skin, people admired themselves in it first. That too was human. That too was part of the problem.

Silas stood on the dais in the coat Gideon had hired for him and listened to his own betrayal go common. He had thought there might be some theatrical instant afterward when the world marked him, some gasp or blow or curse from heaven. What came instead was traffic, repetition, commerce: the real machinery of disaster, ordinary as a lunch rush.

When he looked for Inez again, he found only the space she had left in the crowd and the movement spreading from it: women turning their branded cups over, a vendor being shouted down for claiming the new glow burned hotter, Junie barking for somebody to fetch more dark mash from the crate under the bench. Work had taken her where love could not afford to stand.

Below, one little boy no older than Otis tugged at his mother's sleeve and asked to say the name again because it made him feel grand. She let him. Why would she not? In his mouth it was only a word. In a hundred mouths it was still only a word crossing from paper to throat to lamp to stove to rent.

Celadon Crown.

By the time the second launch cleared the quay, the name had already learned the city better than he ever had. It rode the band brass, the sales cries, the school chorus, the mutter between two women comparing the new color to cheap jewelry, the private try of a laborer shaping it against broken teeth. It sat in ears and changed how people held their chins. It got into the shoulders. It turned the moss on the mooring ropes pale gold and made the light look rich while the heat went mean. Standing there with the river throwing that pretty useless shine back at him, Silas could feel the whole town beginning to wear the word on its body.
