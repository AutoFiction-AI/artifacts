# Chapter 16

Silas spent the morning prying boat nails out of a dead ferry plank with his bad wrist wrapped in one of Junie's oldest apron ties. The cloth had once been blue, maybe. Now it was the color of dishwater left overnight, and it smelled like onion skins and old broth. Every time the hammer claw bit under a nail head, a line of pain ran up the inside of his forearm and sat in the elbow like a lodged fishbone. He kept going. The plank had river in it still, old tide and tar and passengers' sweat, and if this scheme was going to live anywhere it could live in that.

Junie had taken over the back stretch between her kitchen door and the alley wall as if she meant to feed a siege. A cauldron that had held eel stew two nights ago now held flour paste gone gray with salt and cheap glue. She stirred it with a paddle and counted under her breath at the same time, not trusting either numbers or starch unless both were kept under pressure. A fleck of bay leaf clung to the sweat on her forearm. She did not seem to know it was there.

"If you split that board, you can eat it," she said without looking at him. "I'm short on fire and patient enough already."

"I know how to lift a nail."

"You know how to name polish. Lifting a nail is newer."

He worked the claw deeper and got the rusted thing out in one pull. "There. A miracle."

"Sell it to somebody else."

The alley smelled of paste, river mud, coal smoke, and the thick green sweetness of cut moss warming in baskets by the kitchen wall. Somebody on the dock was gutting fish. The scales flashed in a pail like dropped coins. Beyond the sheds the Sallow kept hitting the pilings with that hollow knock that made Ferrymouth sound half built and half sinking. Silas had always liked the noise because it made the city seem busy even when he was broke. Today it sounded like a timer.

Inez came through the alley gate carrying a brace of narrow slats on one shoulder and a tin of lampblack in her left hand. She wore her scarf tied high, dark hair all but swallowed under it, and she had tucked a carpenter's pencil behind one ear as if she had been born with it there. The crescent scar inside her right wrist flashed pale when she shifted the tin. She set the slats down, tested the ferry plank under Silas's hand with one thumbnail, then leaned to smell the wet wood. It was a habit of hers he had only seen in grow houses and kitchens, people checking whether a thing was rotten by giving it the dignity of a close breath.

"This one holds," she said.

"I'm relieved to have passed inspection."

"You haven't." She took the hammer from him, drove the last bent nail back through from the opposite side with three clean hits, then handed the hammer back. "No borders."

"I wasn't thinking borders."

She looked at the brush bundle near his knee.

"I was thinking a small header."

"No flourishes either."

"Then why am I here at all?"

"Because you can letter plain without whining once you get tired enough."

That stung because it was exact. He had not heard warmth in her voice in days, but he had heard attention, which was worse in one way and better in another. He could not tell whether the difference kept him hungry or merely upright.

Behind her Aunt Vero came in with a roll of spoiled newsprint under one arm and a pair of dressmaker's shears shoved through her apron sash. Her thick white braid was looped twice around her head. She had tied a saint medal to the braid with red thread, either for devotion or because she kept misplacing it. Her wandering eye drifted toward Silas, amused already, then came back hard.

"Painter," she said, "if I catch one pretty curve on these boards, I'll break your fingers and save the city the trouble."

"You people know all the sweet ways to encourage a man."

"Sweet costs extra."

She dropped the roll on the table Junie used for chopping onions. The papers unfurled damp and showed the blurred corpse of one of Damaris's headlines beneath a wash of ruined ink. Some alderman's shame or some merchant's mistress had bled into gray clouds. Silas touched the page and felt the cheap wood pulp rise in wet pills under his fingertips.

Damaris herself arrived a minute later, pushing a handcart that squealed so sharply every turn sounded like a small accusation. Her widow's black had gone shiny at the elbows and hips from too much use. One boot was tied at the ankle with printer's twine where the buckle had given up. She had a peppermint tucked in one cheek, which made the chipped front tooth show when she smiled.

"Mind your gratitude," she said, seeing the papers. "That's three days of unsold scandal and one entirely libelous extra. I'm making a patriotic sacrifice."

Junie snorted. "You're making room in the pressroom."

"Both things can be true."

Damaris slapped the cart handle. More ruined bundles lay stacked there, along with two jars of paste and a crate of small tacks. "The back room roof leaked on the midday edition. Ruined the columns, blurred the names, sent half my subscribers into moral disappointment. Perfect for us."

Silas looked from the buckled papers to her. "Perfect how?"

She took one sheet, held it up to the light, and read from it with exaggerated delicacy. "'Society widow found in a compromising-'" She lowered the page and let the ruin hang.

"Position," Aunt Vero said.

"Balcony," Junie said at the same time.

Damaris waggled the blurred sheet between them. "There. Rain leaves a hole and everybody stuffs a word in it. That's all I need from the city. They stop, they stare, they finish the line for me."

Inez set the lampblack tin on the table. "We're not asking the paper to work by itself."

"Of course not," Damaris said. "If paper could save us, I'd have ruled this city years ago. I just need it to make them lean in long enough for the next mouth."

Silas almost smiled. It felt like theft, taking comfort from her trade even now, but he took it. He had spent half his life trying to make words stick faster than thought. The notion that delay could serve them had the vulgar charm of a dirty trick, which meant it might actually work.

Junie ladled paste into chipped bowls. "Talk less and spread more. Bellglass won't wait for your theories."

They set to it. Junie brushed paste in thick wet strokes over the plank faces. Damaris slapped ruined newsprint onto the paste and smoothed it with the flat of her palm, grinding yesterday's gossip into today's backing. Aunt Vero cut narrow strips from drier pages and sorted them in piles on the table edge, muttering as she worked. Some strips had names already copied in different hands from supper two nights before. Some were blank. Some carried use notes in abbreviated scrawls that looked more like kitchen orders than doctrine.

Silas took up a broad brush, held it in his left hand first, swore at the wobble, then shifted it back to his right and found he could manage if he kept the strokes blunt and short. He gave himself no filigree, no hooks, no chance to show off. He painted the board header the way Inez wanted, heavy and plain enough for a dockman to read at a glance.

CALL IT HOW YOU USE IT

The letters sat there without one ounce of charm. He looked at them and felt, absurdly, a little bereaved. Then Inez nodded once, and the grief changed shape.

"Lower," she said, tapping the empty space beneath. "Leave room for additions."

"There's room enough for six."

"Leave room for ten. Nobody ever stops at six once they begin arguing."

That, too, was exact.

On the first board they pasted strips in two staggered rows:

STEP-BLUE on Lantern Steps, cool on wet stone

POTFAST in Ferrymouth kitchens, holds heat steady

DOCK-GREEN on gangplanks, clear in river fog

WAKE-BLUE in fever rooms, low light, no scorch

One from the lower cut that said BARGE-EYE, same use as dock-green, according to the cramped note beside it, but no one seemed interested in reconciling the difference. Another from Sump Ward read PAN-GREEN, which Junie said was close enough to potfast if you wanted to start a fight for sport. Aunt Vero insisted on adding a strip that read BRIDELIGHT for kisses and foolishness, then laughed so hard at Silas's face that her drifting eye nearly disappeared into the crinkles beside it.

"You object?" she asked.

"Only to accuracy."

"Too late for that."

By the time they had five large boards and a stack of narrower hand-copies, the alley had turned into a paste yard. Newsprint scraps clung to the soles of their boots. The kitchen door stood open behind Junie, belching steam and onion and pepper into the lane. Twice customers came to the threshold, took one look at the table full of planks and ruined papers and neighborhood matrons, and decided their business could wait until the stew was ready. Silas could feel the hour moving by the cooler edge coming into the air. Late summer was beginning to tire. Bellglass would answer more slowly tonight than it would have a month ago. That was why Gideon had chosen a blessing, no doubt. A ceremony could do what heat no longer did for free.

Damaris counted the smaller sheets and swore. "Not enough."

"Enough to start," Inez said.

"Starting is what respectable failures say before breakfast."

Junie wiped paste from her hands onto her apron and jerked her chin toward the cart. "What else did the leak ruin?"

Damaris considered the question like a merchant weighing whether truth ought to cost extra. Then she grinned around her peppermint. "A crate of half-set type forms, a stack of weather notices, and one sermon supplement no one was going to pay for anyhow. Give me twenty minutes."

"You'll be back?" Silas said.

"Darling, I own a newspaper. Running toward trouble is the only faithful habit I've got left."

She went out squealing toward Patent Row. Junie started another pot of paste without complaint, which in her case counted as optimism. Inez gathered the finished boards against the wall to dry, each one ugly as a patched door and twice as useful. Silas reached automatically for the corner of the first board, meaning to mark it, not with a signature exactly, but with something a little more his. A thicker tail on the last T, perhaps. A sly curl hidden in the brushwork.

Inez's hand came down on his wrist before the brush touched.

Her grip was neither hard nor intimate. It was simply certain.

"Don't," she said.

He looked at her hand on the apron tie and the old scar on the inside of her wrist, white against the heat-dark skin around it. "I wasn't going to sign it."

"You were going to make it remember you."

He might have lied a week ago. He did not now. "Yes."

She let go. "Let it remember the city instead."

The line landed clean. He set the brush down.

By noon Damaris came back with more spoiled stock, two newsboys, and a smell of wet ink so sharp it could have scoured a pan. After that the work sped up. The boards leaned in a damp row by the alley wall, drying under the back-window heat of Junie's kitchen. They looked too homely to sell anything. Silas took that as the first decent omen he had had in weeks.

* * *

By the time the boards were dry enough to carry, Ferrymouth was in full lunch ugliness, gulls screaming over fish guts, ferries bumping the slips crooked, men eating standing up because sitting wasted daylight. Junie cut a loaf into thick doorstops and shoved them at whoever had agreed to carry. She moved as if the whole campaign were merely another meal rush that had arrived badly timed. Her split eyebrow shone damp with sweat. She tucked a sugar lump into Otis's palm without pausing in her work and pointed at him with the bread knife.

"You don't stop to watch any fighting. You don't go near the rail. If a man in a green sash tries to take that board, you bite him and run."

Otis closed his fist over the sugar as if he had been handed a gold sovereign. His missing front tooth made his serious face look counterfeit, but the rest of him was all nerve and intent. One trouser knee was black with old dock dirt no scrubbing had ever beat out. He had two small boards slung across his back with onion twine and a pocket full of chalk stubs for copying when paper ran out.

"Do I bite first or after he reaches?" he asked.

"Use your judgment."

"That's a poor gift to give a child," Damaris said.

Otis ignored her and looked at Silas instead. "If they say two names at once, does the moss listen with both ears?"

"Moss hasn't got ears."

"Then how's it so nosy?"

Silas laughed before he could help it. The sound felt rusty. "Go ask it after we're done."

The children took the first wave because children crossed the city the way flies crossed a table, uninvited and impossible to sort. Otis led three others out of the alley, not because he was the oldest but because he already understood the value of moving before an adult could improve the plan. One was a wash-house girl from Lantern Steps with raw red knuckles and two onions tied into the corner of her apron because her mother had sent her for them and she refused to come home empty. Another was a ferry deckhand's son with arithmetic tables penciled up his cuff and a habit of clicking his tongue each time he changed direction. The third, a tiny dark-faced boy Silas knew only as Little Nuno, carried nothing but tacks and a hammer much too large for him, which did not keep him from looking pleased.

They scattered toward the slips, the stairs, the boarding houses, the alley mouths where people stopped to spit and gossip. Damaris's two newsboys followed with bundles of damp hand-copies under their arms, already shouting the header because she had told them to practice until it stuck.

"Call it how you use it. Call it how you use it."

The phrase made heads turn, which was good enough.

Silas took a pair of larger boards and went with Inez toward the first ferry. He had to turn sideways to get between a crate of live eels and a woman carrying two basins of laundry. The board edge knocked his thigh with each step. The paper backing smelled of paste and ink and something faintly sour from the ruined sermon pages Damaris had sacrificed. He hoped a little blasphemy improved adhesion.

At the ferry shelter a deckwoman in a patched oilskin nailed one board to the wall beside the fare table before anyone could object. She held the nail between her lips while she worked and removed it only to spit into the river.

"My brother says dock-green," she said, reading the strip with her head tilted. "My mother says rope-light. They'll argue till winter."

Inez handed her a blank strip and a stump pencil. "Then give them room."

The woman grinned, showing a gold tooth filed flat on one corner. "Now that I understand."

By the time the ferry shoved off, three passengers were already arguing over the board aloud because the rain-ruined backing blurred the strip edges. An old man tapped POTFAST with two fingers and said it twice before nodding to himself. Two girls laughed at BRIDELIGHT, then tried it again into each other's sleeves until the lamp over the fare table flushed pink enough to make them squeal and look away.

At the next landing a woman shelling peas wiped her hands on her skirt, crossed the lane, and penciled STAIR-BLUE in the margin because, she announced to a cousin on the opposite stoop, Lantern Steps did not own every stair in Nacre Bend. A cook at a kitchen hatch read the hand-copy while ladling stew, slapped WAKE-BLUE with her spoon when a customer called it widow-light, and sent him off still muttering the correction. Outside a schoolyard gate, four children chanted wake-blue at a crooked poster until the moss in the drain grate beside it cooled from yellowish gold to a bruised blue and a teacher came out to hush them. By the time Silas crossed the third street, the header was coming back to him in dock bark, schoolroom singsong, and boarding-house scold.

Then Gideon's runners found the first board on Patent Row.

They came in pairs, neat as clerks, green sashes over their coats and Verdancy labels stacked under one arm. One had pomade in his hair and little silver scissors on his watch chain, as if he might trim the world to shape given a quiet room and enough permission. The other smelled of violet water strong enough to make Silas's eyes sting. They stopped before the board nailed to a tobacconist's wall and laughed.

"What is this, then," the violet one said, "charity instruction for the semi-literate?"

Silas had expected anger. The mockery pricked worse, maybe because he knew how often he had once used the same tone on men selling practical things.

The scissors man peeled at the edge of the damp paper. "No source. No authority. No quality guarantee."

"No charge either," Silas said.

They turned. Recognition hit first, then a mean little pleasure. "Mr. Vale. Reduced to handbills."

"Reduced to walking, apparently," Silas said. "Hard luck for us both."

The violet one smiled. "Mr. Wren says confusion is a luxury the poor can't afford."

Inez stepped up before Silas could answer. "He's right. That's why one pretty lie is more expensive than ten useful names."

The man looked at her work scarf, the lampblack on her knuckles, and filed her instantly in whatever drawer he kept for women who ought not interrupt commerce. "Madam, naming for market is not your province."

"Neither are stairs till you break your neck on them."

He had no reply ready to that. He settled for tearing the board down.

For one bad second Silas felt the whole plan go soft in his hands. All morning's work, all that paste and stubbornness, and here it was coming away from the wall with one peel and a wet ripping sound. Patent Row had always been quicker with removal than memory. He knew this. He had made a living from it.

Then from across the street a fruit seller called to a customer, "Step-blue if you're taking Lantern Steps home, hear me?" and the customer called back, "Thought it was stair-blue."

"Lantern lot say step-blue," another woman said from her boarding-house stoop. "My aunt calls it shin-saver."

The runner still had the board in his hands when three names had already escaped it.

Otis shot past at that moment, barefoot for speed, onion twine slapping his calves. Little Nuno was behind him with the oversized hammer, and the wash-house girl had somehow acquired two more copies under her arm and a heel of bread in her mouth. They did not even look at the torn board. They were already past, down the alley toward the upper stairs, carrying duplicates.

"You can't strip every wall in the city," Silas said.

The violet runner lifted a shoulder. "We don't need to. We only need to keep people from trusting what they see."

Damaris, appearing from nowhere the way trouble always seemed to peel her out of the air, answered for him. "Then you're a half day too late."

She had stationed a newsboy atop a crate by the tobacconist and another on the cab stand railing opposite. At her signal both began reading the use strips aloud, loudly and badly, like auctioneers with fevers.

"Dock-green on gangplanks. Potfast in Ferrymouth kitchens. Wake-blue in fever rooms."

The tobacconist's wife leaned out her door and shouted, "Gangplanks, you little idiot. Gangways are for churches."

A porter on the curb answered with potfast in a mock sermon voice, and two draymen took him up for sport. By the time the runners had reduced the board to a wet curl of paper and bare plank, one of those draymen was still rolling potfast around his mouth as he drove toward Bellglass.

Damaris touched two fingers to the brim of her hat at the runners. "Pull faster, gentlemen. We're helping one another's circulation."

The scissors man swore under his breath and they moved on, hunting cleaner targets.

Silas stood in the little public mess they had left behind and felt the idea settle deeper than cleverness. It was not inspiration or triumph. It was something rougher and more dependable, the knowledge that Gideon could buy printers and choir singers and clean walls and still fail to police the walk between a kitchen hatch and a ferry slip, the schoolyard mouths that liked saying the wrong thing on purpose, the women on stoops who corrected one another for pleasure.

By midafternoon he heard the bridge before he saw it. A cook balancing two baskets said potfast to a fishmonger at the corner and got pan-green back. Ferry passengers stepping onto the road argued over dock-green and rope-light before their boots even cleared the gangplank. A knot of children passed him chanting wake-blue without the least idea what they were blessing, and a teamster two carts behind them kept testing stair-blue against step-blue as if one of them might fit his tongue better. Bellglass was already seeded by the time the iron came into view.

When they reached the south approach, three chorus boards were already up, one tied to the iron fence with washline cord, one nailed to a signal post, one propped in the mouth of a side lane by a woman selling fried river cakes. She read from it between customers, stumbled over BARGE-EYE, started again, and sold two cakes while a clerk beside her mouthed the strip with a greasy thumb. A boy on the ridge side shouted barge-eye down at the bridge and got dock-green and rope-light shouted back. Traffic snagged around the quarrel: a woman with a market parcel stopped on the first stair, two clerks backed into a cart horse, and a man who had halted only to swear wound up reading the whole left column aloud to his wife. By the time Silas reached the rail, three more blank strips had gone up beneath the originals.

* * *

Bellglass Bridge took a public blessing every year once the first cool edge came into the evenings. Ward crews checked lamp cages, merchants made speeches about safety and prosperity, and anybody with a product to tie to civic necessity tried to slip a sales pitch under the prayer. Silas had worked the edges of the ritual before, lettering banners or gilding donation boards, and he knew its smell: hot metal cooling, damp iron, river wind undercut by perfume from the ridge and frying oil from the lower stalls. It was piety with receipts under it.

Gideon had bought half the bridge.

Pale gold bunting draped the lamp posts. Verdancy men had set up a dais near the center arch with a lectern, a brass speaking horn, and crates of branded cuttings arranged like offerings. Even the city inspector's ribbon looked cleaner than the rest of Nacre Bend had any right to allow. Gideon stood near the lectern gloved as usual, silver at the temples, coat untouched by any weather lower than Gilt Crescent. He had a habit, when impatient, of smoothing each cuff once with the thumb of the opposite hand. Silas saw him do it twice while the crowd thickened and knew he was feeling the strain already.

The bridge was visible from three directions at once. Ridge people crowded the north entry in cleaner coats, sniffing at the drain stink and pretending they did not. Ferrymouth workers came up from the west with supper grease still on their hands. Southward, along the Sump side, women stood on the plank approaches with shawls and market baskets and the flat stillness of people who had not shown up to be dazzled. Bellglass itself held all of them in one iron throat.

Silas carried the first full chorus board under one arm and felt the old appetite begin to move in him as the crowd sound thickened. He hated that it still could. Put him in front of enough strangers waiting to be told what a thing was, and some mean bright part of him always lifted its head. The brass horn on Gideon's lectern shone like a bottle mouth in lamplight. One sentence into that horn and the bridge would lean his way. He knew exactly what kind of sentence would do it. Filthy enough to wake the ferries, sharp enough to draw laughter, practical enough to seem reformed. He could feel the shape of it in his teeth.

He tightened his grip on the board until the tack points bit his palm through the paper.

Inez came up beside him with a coil of line over one shoulder and a pouch of nails at her belt. She had changed nothing for the ceremony except washing the worst of the paste off her hands. Even clean, she looked like work. A smear of lampblack remained near her thumb knuckle. He found it comforting, which was ridiculous.

"When they start," she said, eyes on the dais, "you hold the ladder."

"That's all?"

"If you're lucky."

"And if I'm not?"

She glanced at him then with an expression that was neither soft nor cruel. "Then you remember this isn't your mouth's evening."

He might have made a joke once. Instead he said, "All right."

Gideon saw them. Of course he did. He moved through the opening crowd with the smoothness of a man who believed passage was his natural condition and stopped just far enough away to avoid the paste on the board.

"Mr. Vale," he said. "You do choose interesting moments to embrace amateurism."

Silas shifted the board higher. "And you do choose expensive cloth for a damp bridge."

"Damp is weather. Your associates are strategy."

He gave Inez a brief, measurable look, as if assessing whether she counted as person or obstacle in the ledger. "Miss Cordero. I admire persistence when it remains local."

"Funny," she said. "I admire your silence under the same condition."

One corner of Gideon's mouth moved, but the shift was too small to call a smile. "This city is tired. It wants settlement."

"No," Silas said before he could stop himself. "You want settlement. The city wants light that works."

Gideon turned the full weight of his quiet onto him. "And yet one name gave people exactly that until interference made appetite look like principle."

Silas nearly took the bait and nearly stepped into the old game of who could phrase theft more elegantly. The horn on the lectern seemed nearer than it had a moment before. He could win a line against Gideon. He had done it before. Winning a line was how he'd helped lose the city.

Inez reached and took the board out of his hands.

She did not ask or even glance at him. She simply removed the object and handed him the folded ladder.

"Hold this," she said.

It was the cleanest humiliation he had ever received, and because it was deserved it steadied him better than praise would have.

The ward clerk began the blessing with a bell and a page of municipal righteousness nobody listened to. The crowd shifted, coughed, muttered. Gideon's hired singers arranged themselves at the south rail, pale gold ribbons at their throats. When the clerk got to the line about prosperous continuity, Gideon stepped toward the lectern and laid one glove on the horn.

"Citizens of Nacre Bend," he said, his voice carried by money and training more than force, "you have seen what uncertainty costs. Tonight we bless a season of steadier light."

A murmur moved through the ridge side. Ferrymouth answered with less reverence and more curiosity. Silas planted the ladder feet against the iron rail where Inez pointed and held it while she climbed. Wet metal vibrated under his palms from all the bodies on the bridge.

Gideon lifted the horn.

Inez spoke first.

She did not have a horn. She did not need one. Her voice was not huge, but it was cut for working rooms where if you did not hear the first time you got burned.

"On wet steps," she said, tying the top corners of the board to the rail, "it's step-blue."

The line hit the bridge like a thrown tool. Heads snapped around from both rails. A carter on the north approach stopped mid-chew with grease on his chin, and a woman buying river cakes turned with her coins still in her hand.

Inez fixed the second tie and kept going. "On a crossing where iron sweats under your boots and your mother's carrying supper in both hands, it's step-blue. Call it pretty later if you've got spare bones."

A laugh broke from the west rail. Then half a dozen voices answered at once, each trying to plant its own authority in the same patch of air.

"Lantern lot say it exactly so."

"We call it stair-blue on Clay Rise."

"My aunt says shin-saver."

Junie came up through the west-side crowd with a ladle still tucked through her apron string and shouted without ceremony, "At my stove it's potfast, unless anybody here enjoys raw eel."

That pulled a bark of laughter from the Ferrymouth side. A pair of kitchen women below her took it up at once, potfast, potfast, and a cluster of customers repeated it with their mouths still full of river cakes.

Aunt Vero, who had somehow planted herself just off the dais steps without being removed, cupped her hands and bellowed toward the lower ward side, "In a fever room it's wake-blue, and any silk bastard who says different can come wipe a sweating child with me till dawn."

The singers lost the beat. One of them, a narrow boy with an honest cowlick he had not managed to pomade flat, looked as if he might rather be anywhere else on earth. Someone on the ridge called dock-green from pure contrariness. Someone under the bridge shouted barge-eye back. Three more voices jumped in before the echo died, each one louder for having company.

Gideon put the horn to his mouth. "Celadon Crown," he said, clipped and clear. "A stable light for every district."

A few voices answered him out of habit, out of loyalty, out of wanting the evening to remain legible. Pale gold ribbons fluttered. The bridge held its breath between two uses of language.

Damaris solved that by setting her newsboys loose.

"Read the boards," she shouted from somewhere near the north approach. "Read them wrong if you must, but read them."

The boys obeyed with hideous enthusiasm. One stood atop a bench and mangled step-blue into step-bloo before being corrected by five strangers. Another read every use note as if auctioning livestock. The corrections flew thicker than the original cries. A woman from Sump Ward added PAN-GREEN to the board with a pencil stub while Inez was still on the ladder. A ferryman tied a strip beneath DOCK-GREEN that read ROPE-LIGHT and insisted both were proper depending on weather. Nobody paused to settle it. The board began acquiring names the way a wharf piling acquired posters and saints and warnings, by pressure and proximity.

Silas held the ladder and kept his mouth shut through the hardest thirty seconds of his adult life.

He could feel the crowd noticing him anyway. Patent Row knew his shoulders, his hair, the lazy slouch he had once sold like a patent cure. A few voices called for him by name, wanting the entertainment of his rescue or his further disgrace. The horn still gleamed at the lectern. He could have taken it. He knew he could have taken it. Even now some part of the bridge wanted one man to explain what all this meant.

Instead he handed a second board up to Inez.

"Left side," she said.

"Too high for you."

"Then stop talking and climb."

He climbed two rungs, held the weight, and let her tie the second board above the first. From that height he could see the city's channels converging. A ferry below them was docking with passengers still reading a hand-copy aloud. On the ridge approach three women had stopped under a lamp and were arguing over whether wake-blue belonged in a sickroom or only beside the dead. Ferrymouth boys were pasting smaller slips to the fence uprights. Southward, along the plank route, children carried duplicates like contraband prayer cards.

It was already too many mouths for a horn to master cleanly.

* * *

The first change showed low, where wet iron met foot traffic.

Silas saw it because Inez had just climbed down and he was still crouched by the ladder feet, breathing paste and river damp and crowd sweat. Along the edge of the third stair below the bridge deck, the old Celadon pale gold shivered, thinned, and took on a wash of blue cold enough to look useful. It was not theatrical blue or bridelight's blushing nonsense. It was the color of shade under a water barrel, of clean steel, of evening before the lamps were all the way up.

"There," somebody shouted.

That was all it took.

The cry moved outward. People leaned, pointed, repeated whatever name had most recently pleased or convinced them. Step-blue gained one rail section, then another. Under the hands of dock men shouting dock-green from the west side, the moss on the lower truss thickened into a denser green that cut through mist and river spray like bottle glass held to sun. Near the food sellers where Junie's crowd kept chanting potfast between jokes, the lamp-cage growth warmed toward a hard cooking green with amber at its core, a color that promised a steady boil rather than fashionable shine. Someone on the south side kept saying wake-blue in a rough grandmother's rhythm, and cool blue islands gathered in the sheltered inner braces where sickroom quiet might plausibly belong.

Celadon Crown did not vanish. That would have been too neat and too kind. Gideon's singers and runners and ridge customers kept the gold alive in broad expensive patches along the center arch and the dais rail. It sat there like plated fat, elegant from a distance, thin and a little sickly up close. Around it, other colors held.

Gideon saw it happen. His face changed less than most men's would have, but the change was there if you knew where vanity lived. He took one step toward the nearest blue rail, stopped, and smoothed his cuff once, then again. When he raised the horn this time the polish was gone from his voice.

"Repeat one name," he said. "One. Or you'll have chaos in every kitchen and stair."

Junie barked a laugh so rude it cut straight across the bridge. "We've already had chaos in our kitchens, you expensive fool. That's how we learned the difference."

Ferrymouth answered with barking laughter that made the north-side ladies flinch. Two deckhands slapped the rail and shouted potfast hard enough to spray spittle. On the ridge side, where a few well-dressed women had been trying to keep Celadon Crown pure by sheer diction, a maid with her cap askew tried wake-blue under her breath and then louder when the sheltered brace cooled blue above her mistress's shoulder.

Damaris was spinning in place, taking notes on her cuff with a pencil almost worn to skin. "Don't say rainbow," she shouted at one of the boys. "If you say rainbow I'll dock your supper. Say bands. Say islands. Say the damned thing as it stands."

"It stands ugly," the boy yelled back.

"Good. Print ugly."

Another runner from Verdancy lunged for the first board and tore away half the strip that read POTFAST. Before he could rip farther, Little Nuno darted between legs and hammered a duplicate to the fence post beside him with three wild, glorious blows. The oversized hammer almost took the runner's thumb off. The man swore, yanked his hand back, and the crowd laughed him smaller. Otis slid under the rail with paste and stuck two hand-copies lower where adults had not thought to guard.

"Mind the edge," Silas shouted, too late to be useful.

Otis twisted around, hair in his eyes, and called back, "I'm minding four edges."

He sounded delighted. That frightened Silas more than fear would have. Children loved contagion because they did not yet know how much labor it took to clean it afterward. Then again, children were how contagion got beaten sometimes too. The bookish little holy picture of that did not interest him. The practical fact did.

Names were coming in now from farther off than the bridge itself. Ferries passing beneath picked up whatever the passengers had heard and threw it up at the girders in work-thick voices. From the west embankment somebody shouted pan-green from a kitchen doorway. From a stoop on the north rise came stair-blue, then shin-saver, then laughter as if the name itself were less important than refusing to let one district's tongue rule the rest. Even bridelight came back, shrill and teasing from a pack of girls near the ridge rail, and pink freckles flashed in the moss by the lamp crowns where old flirtation still had customers.

Silas felt the whole bridge become legible in layers, and he knew it would never be simple again. Use by use, pressure by pressure, neighborhood by neighborhood, the growth answered where it was being asked from. Blue veined the steps. Green thickened on the handrails and lower truss. Gold still clung in marketed bands where Gideon's saturation had money behind it. Pink blushed up in corners whenever memory or mischief revived the first stupid name he had ever given it.

If there was a victory in that mess, it was not purity or the clean replacement of one banner by another. The victory was the public ruin of the claim that one polished phrase could own all uses at once.

Gideon came off the dais at last. The crowd opened for him reluctantly, more from surprise than respect. He stopped before the nearest board and put one gloved hand against it, maybe to pull it down, maybe to steady himself, Silas could not tell. The paste gave under the pressure. Newsprint shifted. A streak of wet lampblack and flour glue smeared over the white kid leather.

Gideon looked at his hand as if the city itself had spat on him.

Inez saw it too. She was standing by the rail now, not looking at Silas, not needing to. Her face was set in the hard clean stillness she got when anger had already chosen usefulness and had no need to flare. She said, to Gideon and to anybody within hearing, "You'll have to keep buying every wall, every ferry, every stair, every cook's mouth in Nacre Bend if you want that gold to rule now."

He met her eyes. "Then perhaps I will."

Aunt Vero cackled from behind him. "You'll die broke and hoarse first."

A hard laugh cracked across the bridge. A teamster near the north end barked "Broke and hoarse" to his mate, and by the time the phrase reached the lower stairs two ferry boys were already chanting it at a Verdancy runner.

Silas did not move toward Gideon. That, too, felt like labor. There had been a time when the whole meaning of a public evening lay in whether he could cut the rich man prettiest. Tonight the cut had already been made and it did not need his signature under it.

Instead he picked up a fallen strip, found a dry patch on the second board, and held it steady while a woman from Clay Rise wrote STAIR-BLUE in square stubborn letters beneath STEP-BLUE. Beside them the ferry deckwoman added ROPE-LIGHT under DOCK-GREEN and underlined it twice for weather. A boarding-house keeper near the north end squeezed in NIGHT-KIND beside WAKE-BLUE, muttering that sickroom and mourning were not always separate things in cheap houses. The board became a city wall in miniature, layered, contradictory, useful because of the contradiction and not despite it.

Dark came up slow and public. One by one the lamp cages along Bellglass caught the new divided colors and threw them down onto hats, cheeks, sleeves, wet iron, river chop. People on the far bank stopped because they could see the bridge change from a distance. A ferryman below whistled, long and low, when the whole south rail banded blue-green-gold in separate holds instead of blending. Even the ridge watchers, who had come for a blessing and a spectacle they could purchase later, had to admit what their own eyes were doing to them.

No one stopped arguing as the colors climbed their sleeves and cheeks.

"Potfast and pan-green are close enough," one man insisted.

"Close enough burns babies," a woman answered.

"Dock-green on fog days, rope-light on clear."

"Stair-blue's for stone. Step-blue's for iron."

"Bridelight if you want kissing, you liar."

"Celadon Crown if you've got more money than sense."

The names flew, crossed, collided, and held in separate pockets wherever enough mouths pressed them into use. Bellglass looked diseased and magnificent, like a civic bruise finally showing all the colors that had been gathering under the skin for months. Silas stood in the middle of it with paste drying on his fingers and his wrist throbbing under the old apron tie and understood, with a clarity that felt almost indecent, that he would never again be able to pretend authorship was the same as use.

By full dark the bridge carried six colors and more arguments than a council chamber. People kept stopping midspan to point out which rail had gone step-blue and which lamp brace held wake-blue, until carts stalled and a pair of clerks had to duck between axle and crowd. Even the city inspector, ribbon askew now, stood staring at a gold band cut clean through by cold blue. No one crossing Bellglass could honestly call the moss one thing anymore.

Silas stayed until Damaris's boys had pasted the last blessing bills in wet curls along the south approach, then went back to Ferrymouth with the bridge still arguing in his skull.
