# Chapter 9

By morning his right hand had the color and shape of a bad pear. Junie had bound it the night before with a strip torn off an old apron and two thin laths from a fish crate, and the whole thing had gone damp in his sleep. When Silas loosened the knots in the gray light of the shop, the wrist looked almost ordinary until he tried to turn it. Then pain ran from the heel of his hand to the elbow, bright and private as a nail pushed under the skin.

He sat on the stool by the front window and breathed through it until the room stopped narrowing. The shop smelled as it always did: oil, river damp, chalk dust, old varnish, the sweet rot of rags that had cleaned too many colors. Usually the smell steadied him. This morning it only made him aware of every place his body had been handled yesterday. Mud had dried in the seam of one boot. There was a faint green line under his thumbnail where the trough scum had lived through soap and scrubbing and another round of Junie calling him filthy as she tied up his hand.

He told himself he needed one clean board, one paid board, something square and ordinary enough to put the whole feast behind him. Men got laughed at in this city every day and still had to eat. A trough dunking was only a trough dunking if he could get paint to obey him by noon.

He had promised a fresh hanging sign to a collar-and-notions stall off Patent Row, a narrow place run by Mrs. Valey, who wore mourning for a husband dead six years and still pressed the black cuffs of her sleeves flat whenever she had to discuss money. She arrived ten minutes after opening with the old board under one arm and a canary-yellow tape measure looped around her neck like church trim.

"You look like hell," she said, which in her mouth counted as concern.

"That's kind of you, Vera."

"Don't flirt with me while you've got pond slime in your ear."

He put a hand to it at once. She snorted and set the board on the trestles.

"Just the same as before," she said. "Cream ground, blue letters. I don't want any of your patent flourishes. Women buying hook closures don't need to feel courted by a sign."

"You wound my trade."

"Your trade can survive it. Can your hand?"

"Course it can."

She looked at the splint, then at his face, and did him the favor of pretending not to see the lie settle there. Mrs. Valey worried the edge of one cuff between finger and thumb while he marked out the lines with charcoal in his left hand. The left could measure, no trouble. The left could even draw a passable guide. It was only when he picked up the sable liner with the right and tried to roll the stroke down into the first V that the morning went bad for real.

The brush should have turned under his fingers like a thing bred to it. Instead the wrist gave a tiny dead hitch. The line thickened where it ought to have narrowed, then dragged crooked across the cream field and finished in a little black hook that looked less like a letter than a fishbone.

Silas stood very still.

Outside on the lane a wagon hit a rut and somebody swore at a mule. From farther up Patent Row came the sing-song cry of a broadside boy selling midday scandal before midday existed. The city kept its own rhythm. The bad stroke sat on the board and did not improve.

"Well," Mrs. Valey said after a moment, quiet enough to save his pride if he wanted the saving. "That's not my V."

He wiped the brush. "No. It isn't."

He took a rag dipped in turpentine and scrubbed the wet line off before it could bite into the ground. Black smeared gray. The smell rose sharp enough to sting his eyes. He waited for his hand to stop trembling, reset the brush, and tried again.

The second V looked like a man trying not to limp.

Mrs. Valey did not sigh. That made it worse. She only reached into her sleeve pocket, brought out the little purse she carried pinned inside her bodice, and counted the deposit coins back into her palm one by one. She always counted with her lips moving. Her dead husband had kept the books before his lungs gave out; now she moved her mouth for both of them.

"I'll take it to the stencil man near Gallow Court," she said. "I hate his work. He makes every shop look like a pharmacy. But he doesn't wobble."

"Give me two days."

"I need it before the excursion ferry comes through tomorrow."

"By tomorrow, then."

She folded the coins back into the purse. "Silas."

He hated pity from people without imagination. It felt too much like being wrapped for storage. "I'm fine."

"You're many things. Fine isn't the one that concerns me." She took the board back under her arm, the tape measure knocking lightly against her breastbone as she lifted. "I liked your old lettering because it looked as if a hand had done it. That was the virtue. But a hand still has to function."

After she went, he shut the shop door though the room was already hot. He tried two more boards from smaller jobs, one for a boat chandlery and one for a washhouse stair arrow, and ruined both in different ways. On the block letters the wrist failed at the end of each downstroke so the line bellied out as if the wood itself had softened. On the arrow he could not keep the point true. It bent drunkenly toward the gutter. He scraped, sanded, started over. By eleven the floor around the easel was furred with curled paint skins and his shirt stuck to his back.

The worst part was not the pain. The pain at least made sense. The worst part was how much of the work lived in a turn too small for anybody else to notice, in a fraction of pressure, a slight roll of finger and wrist, the tiny private grace that made an O sit proud instead of stupid. He had spent years teaching his hand to lie beautifully for money. Now the lie kept breaking open halfway through the sentence.

He wrapped the wrist again and went to the window for air. Across the street a Verdancy House runner was pasting fresh placards over last week's mess. The man had ears that stuck out through his wet hair and a habit of smoothing every corner with the side of his thumb before he moved on, as if he could not bear a wrinkle left alive behind him. CELADON CROWN, the posters said in pale green capitals bordered with gilt. ONE CLEAN NAME FOR A STEADY FLAME.

Silas had written that line three nights ago by Gideon's lamp, half drunk on relief because the advance had cleared Havelock Pigments and bought him one evening without creditors in his throat. He had thought it a neat piece of trade, with a bit of internal chiming. It was easy to remember, easy to mouth, the kind of phrase that slipped under a door before a man could set his boot against it.

Now it looked uglier in public than any insult painted on a privy wall.

The runner finished papering the post and stepped back to admire his rank little harvest. He noticed Silas in the window then, blinked once, and looked away too quickly. That was the new thing. Yesterday strangers had laughed in his face while he came up shining from the trough. Today they did not laugh. They refused him the common courtesy of pretending not to know.

Silas turned from the glass, counted what money remained in the till, and found less than he had been bracing for. There were a few loose crowns, some coppers, a half-used block of leaf, and the bent nib of a ruling pen. Paint he could not spread was only colored debt. He lowered the shade, locked the shop, and carried his bad hand down toward Ferrymouth because if he could not earn, he could at least lift things.

The river announced itself before he saw it. Ferrymouth always did: rope slap, gull noise, paddle churn, men calling distances they did not trust each other to judge, the steady iron complaint of docking hardware taking another blow. Junie's kitchen sat in the middle of it with both front shutters propped open, leaking steam and onion and eel broth into the dock stink. The sign over her door still read VALE'S BROTH & BREAD in his own hand, a bit faded on the downwind edge. He stood under it a second too long, looking at the clean certainty of the letters, then ducked inside.

Junie had three pots going and six lies arriving at once. She was broad across the shoulders and moving so fast the steam seemed to follow her rather than the other way round. A streak of flour ran from one thumb knuckle to her wrist where she'd swatted at sweat without washing first. She looked at the wrapped hand, then at his face, and pointed with the ladle toward a crate of onions.

"Sit there and peel till I tell you different."

"Good morning to you too."

"Morning was an hour ago. Peel."

He sat. The bench rocked on an uneven leg. He braced the onion against his stomach and worked the skin loose one-handed while ferry crews came in hungry and left louder. Junie moved among them with her usual brisk blasphemy, sliding bowls down the counter, keeping tabs in her head, correcting a boatman's arithmetic without looking at him. She had not asked for the story of the feast. She had seen enough when he came in last night shining green and humiliated from eyebrow to boot sole. Her restraint had felt almost tender then. In daylight it felt like a bill being held for later.

By the time he had a basin full of onion skins and a watering left eye, the lunch rush was packed tight enough that bodies warmed the windows. Dock hands in algae-stiff aprons, a crate porter who always took mustard with a spoon as if he were daring it to fight him, two ferry clerks from the up-river line, and one schoolteacher with wet hems and no patience for men who lingered over change jammed the room. The room sounded like cutlery on crockery and water slapping pilings and the quick repetitive thud of Junie's knife through stale bread.

What he had not expected was to hear his own trade come in with the customers.

"Saw a new board up by Lantern Steps," one of the ferry clerks said, wiping broth off his mustache. "Pretty pale thing. Crown on the top. They say the bed burns cooler under the proper name."

"Proper for who?" Junie said.

"For whoever can pay, I suppose."

The crate porter laughed through his spoonful of mustard. "My wife says if it answers to one name it'll probably stop listening to ours."

"Your wife says that about dogs, priests, and engines alike," said the schoolteacher, and the whole counter snorted.

Then the outer door banged and Otis came in at a run from the slip, narrow shoulders damp with fog-sweat, one trouser knee dark with the same old dock dirt that never fully left it, slate tucked under his arm. He had lost the front tooth and could not stop tonguing the gap between words. He went first to the bread stack because he always checked whether the crust heels had been saved for him.

"Wash," Junie said without turning.

"I did at school."

"Then it'll only improve you to do it twice."

He gave Silas a quick grin on the way to the basin, the kind children used when they had clocked adult damage and meant to be kind without quite knowing how. "You still look swampy, Uncle Sile."

"You look undereducated."

"Miss Bell says that's not a proper word."

"Miss Bell can write me a letter."

Otis giggled, splashed his hands, and came back with his slate and a folded scrap of paper gone soft in his pocket. He climbed onto the end stool by the stove and watched Junie plate bread and eel. His eyes moved greedily over everything in the room: bowls, steam, strangers' boots, the ferry bells beyond the shutters. He lived as if the city were a toy set always one turn from revealing a new hidden door.

"We had copy practice," he announced.

"Did you," Junie said.

"Miss Bell had those free sheets from the supply man with the nice loops. We had to write the line six times."

He unfolded the paper and smoothed it flat on the counter with both hands. At the top, in pale green print gone fuzzy from pocket sweat, stood a tiny gilt crown and the same phrase Silas had watched pasted on the post outside his shop.

ONE CLEAN NAME FOR A STEADY FLAME.

Below it, in schoolroom pencil and a child's fierce concentration, Otis had copied the line again and again. The first version leaned uphill. The second broke the S in steady into two snakes. By the sixth he had the rhythm of it.

"Look," he said, pleased with himself. "I can do the C proper now."

Junie's ladle stopped in the air.

The room did not go silent. Ferrymouth never granted anybody that sort of stage. Someone at the back wanted more bread. A ferry bell rang twice. Steam kept lifting off the pots. But within the space directly around the counter something changed so hard Silas felt it like a chair leg striking his shin.

Junie set the ladle down with great care.

"Where'd she get that?"

Otis blinked. "The supply man. He had a whole stack. He said the letters were good practice."

"What supply man?"

"The nice one with the green cuffs. He gives boiled sweets if you say the whole line first."

One of the ferry clerks snorted. "Efficient fellow."

Junie turned her head and looked at him. She did not raise her voice. She did not have to. The clerk bent over his bowl at once as if it had become a matter of religious study.

She looked back at Otis. "And what did Miss Bell say about it?"

"That it's important to ask for goods by their proper trade names or shops cheat you." He frowned, feeling trouble now without knowing its edges. "I only copied it. Everybody did."

Silas knew the exact place in the sentence where the hook had been set. Proper trade names. That was not his line, but it lay snug against it, both of them built to do the same work. Make one word feel clean and everyone else's feel grubby, backward, uncertain. He could hear Gideon praising the usefulness of the turn. He could hear himself accepting the praise because praise from men with money always came wrapped around something he needed.

Junie took the paper between two fingers as if it had grease on it.

"Listen to me, Oat. Goods don't become honest because some bastard paid to print a crown over them. A lamp burns how it burns. Broth feeds you or it doesn't. A thing's use comes first."

"I know," Otis said, but too quickly.

"Do you? Then say it back."

He looked from her to Silas and back. He had inherited Junie's fast little weather sense. He knew when the room around him had changed, even if he did not yet know why. "A thing's use comes first."

"Again."

"A thing's use comes first."

"Good. Now take that sheet and feed it to the stove."

Otis flinched. "But my writing."

"You can write it better on clean paper that isn't selling you while you learn."

That was when Junie got colder than she had been over the trough, colder than she had been when he staggered into her back room stinking like a cut ditch and trying to make a joke before he fell onto the chair. Silas had expected anger at his disgrace. Anger he understood. This was something flatter and worse. This was the look of a woman seeing a stranger's hand inside her cupboard.

Otis slid off the stool and hesitated by the stove, paper in hand.

"Ma?"

"Do it."

He fed one corner of the sheet to the open grate. The fire licked it, then took the rest. The gilt crown curled black first. The slogan puckered, flashed, and was gone. Otis watched it with a sorrow out of all proportion to the scrap itself, because children hated wasting effort and could not yet distinguish between labor and obedience.

Junie saw that too. Her face changed, though not much.

"After service I'll help you make a fresh copy line," she said. "Something worth practicing."

"What line?"

She picked up the ladle again. "We'll think of one that doesn't belong to a patent office."

Otis nodded, subdued, and went to collect bowls.

Silas said, low, "Junie."

"Don't," she said.

"I know."

"You don't know enough yet. That's the issue."

She moved past him with the broth pot, using her hip to shove his knee aside because her hands were full. "I can live through you making a public ass of yourself. God knows I've done it. Men get hauled through their own nonsense every week on this dock and the tide still comes in. What I can't stomach is them teaching my boy to ask permission before he asks for light."

He looked at Otis carrying bowls toward the wash tub, careful despite the missing tooth, careful despite the way he'd been wounded by the burning of his own copy work. "It was a line. Trade trash."

"Yes," she said. "And now it's in the schoolroom. That's how trash learns table manners."

The lunch crowd kept arriving. A deckhand came in humming the slogan without knowing he was humming it. Two boys on the slip took turns shouting the words at the ferry windows to hear the echo off the glass. A woman buying bread to carry home asked if Junie had thought of repainting her sign in a lighter color because the excursion people trusted pale goods. She meant it kindly. That was part of the obscenity. The city was absorbing the sales pitch as advice.

Silas peeled potatoes after that, because onions were done and apology was useless. The bad hand rested useless in his lap, heavy as borrowed meat. Every few minutes he caught the phrase returning from some other mouth outside, thinner or louder or half mangled, and each version did its work anyway. Words crossed water fast in Nacre Bend. A joke on Patent Row could be a school exercise by noon and kitchen doctrine by supper. Ferries carried more than bodies. They carried the laziness by which a city decided what counted as proper.

When the rush finally thinned and the room could breathe again, Damaris Pike appeared in the doorway with a parcel of damp papers hugged to her ribs. Her widow's black had gone shiny at the seams from years of elbows and worktables, and the chipped front tooth flashed when she smiled. She always looked as if she had slept three hours in a chair and spent two of them arguing with the chair.

"You print vultures quick," Junie said.

"Only on days ending in y." Damaris thumped the bundle onto the counter and fanned herself with a folded proof. "You look murderous, which improves the room. Silas, you look like a saint dragged through algae and denied the promotion."

"If that's this week's headline, spare me."

"The headline's better than that. Unfortunately for you, the city is already getting bored with your suffering." She took a bowl without asking and sniffed it approvingly. "Which means the interesting harm is elsewhere."

Junie snorted. "Take him, then. He's been sitting here like a damp curtain when he isn't peeling things badly."

"I heard that," Silas said.

"I aimed it so you would."

Damaris tore bread with inked fingers. "Come ride the three-forty crossing with me."

"For what?"

"Because you're still reading this as a scandal. It isn't one anymore. It's habit. Habit pays better and lasts longer."

He should have refused. He was tired, his hand throbbed, and he had no appetite for being instructed by the woman who'd made a serial amusement out of his every wrong turn since spring. But Junie had already turned away to count coins into stacks by the till, lips moving quick as knife work, and he knew the difference between being dismissed and being sent.

"Back by dark," she said without looking up. "Otis still needs that copy line."

"What's it going to be?" Otis asked from the wash tub.

Junie glanced at the river, then at the boy. "We'll start with this. Ask what a thing does."

Otis repeated it softly to see how it sat in his mouth. "Ask what a thing does."

Silas followed Damaris out to the slip.

The ferry deck was slick with old mist and shoe polish and the greenish paste that grew wherever rope stayed wet too long. Damaris knew everybody who worked the line, though not always by their right names. She flicked a paper at the ticket girl, who wore spectacles tied behind her head with blue thread and kept scratching the same ink stain on her cuff as if she could erase it by irritation alone.

"You still owe me two fares from last month," the girl said.

"Then carry me in debt, my dear. It becomes me."

"Nothing becomes you."

"Cruel and accurate."

They boarded with the laborers and errand boys and two women carrying lamp trays wrapped in sacking. Silas kept to the rail because his stomach wanted air and his wrist wanted a place to rest. The ferry shuddered free of the slip. Water opened under them in a chop the color of spoiled bottles. Nacre Bend rose on either side, terraces and plank runs and damp brick faces layered with signs like scales on a fish no one had ever finished gutting.

Damaris handed him a paper. He did not open it.

"Cowardly," she said.

"Practical."

"Fair."

She leaned her elbows on the rail beside him. Up close she smelled of printer's ink and some cheap violet water trying bravely to cover it. Her hat was pinned at an angle that would have looked rakish on a man and looked merely overworked on her.

"Do you know why I like ferries better than presses?" she asked.

"Because the ferries float."

"Because presses only print what I put in them. Ferries print whatever anybody repeats by the time they dock."

She nodded toward the cabin, where a pair of office clerks were examining a handbill together.

"Morning headline, noon joke, evening purchase. That's the whole sermon. A paper starts it if it's lucky. A ferry makes it common."

He looked where she was looking. The handbill in the clerks' hands bore the same pale border he'd seen on the school copy sheet. One clerk mouthed the slogan while reading and the other answered the rhythm under his breath without seeming to know he had done it.

"You're pleased with yourself," Silas said.

"I'm never pleased with myself. At best, I'm professionally interested." She bit into the bread she'd stolen from Junie's and chewed. "You've been very good copy, yes. Man of words hoist by wet neighborhood. Public loves that. But while everyone was laughing at you, somebody figured out how to move the campaign out of the papers and into the useful places."

"Schools."

"Schools, ferries, supply invoices, chalk boards in lodging houses, little cards tucked into lamp shipments, copy slips, prayer raffle prizes, all the humble holes where habit nests." She swallowed. "Look."

They were passing the lower edge of Lantern Steps. From the water he could see the landings stacked up the hill, little platforms of life between stair runs. Shirts hung on lines and buckets sat by doors. A woman shook a rug hard enough to mean it. Along the retaining wall below, three men in paint aprons were working their way over a row of older signs.

One board had advertised repairs for blue-burn lamp cages in lettering so weathered only the pressure of the strokes remained. Another, nailed crooked over a cellar door, still carried half a warning about hot trays and children. The crew had already sanded the faces pale. Now they rolled a single soft green over all of it, methodical as undertakers washing a body before display.

Above the wet ground color came gilt stencils: a crown, a flourish, the neat certified capital letters of Verdancy House.

"Those weren't even shop signs," Silas said.

"No. They were instructions." Damaris tapped ash from an unlit cigarette she kept mostly for gesture. "Which is inconvenient if your business model requires the city to stop speaking in instructions and start speaking in purchases."

The ferry moved on. He twisted for another look and his wrist punished him for it. One of the paint men reached the old warning board's lower edge and covered the word HOT last. For a second the letters showed through the celadon skin, dark and ghostly. Then the roller went back over them and the wall became decor.

Silas knew that pressure. He knew exactly how many coats it took to kill a stubborn old letter on cheap wood. He knew the tricks for making a replacement feel inevitable: soft border, centered wording, enough breathing room around the phrase that people mistook expense for truth. Gideon had not bought only wall space. He had bought legibility. He was whitening the city's teeth while the mouth was still in it.

"Keep looking," Damaris said.

At the next bend the ferry gave them Patent Row's flank and then the cheaper stretch below Gilt Crescent where aspiring shopkeepers copied upper-district taste badly and charged extra for the attempt. Here too fresh celadon had begun to bloom. A boarding house he'd once lettered BRACKEN ROOMS in russet and cream was now half hidden beneath a new panel: CROWN VIEW LODGING. The old name still showed at the bottom where the carpenter had measured wrong, the K peeking out like a broken nail.

On the levee below it, a fruit seller's awning had been repainted the same pale green. Even the ferrymen's directional arrows were being redone, not to point toward people or functions but toward approved sellers. AUTHORIZED FUEL THIS WAY. CERTIFIED CUTTINGS AHEAD.

"It's everywhere," he said.

"That's what campaigns are for."

"No. I mean the kind of it."

Damaris glanced sideways at him but let him take his time. That was one of the reasons he trusted her more than was wise. For all her appetite for spectacle, she understood that a person sometimes required a few beats to become smarter than his own vanity.

Silas watched a woman on the quay below bend to pick up the old plank sign somebody had thrown down after replacing it. She was middle-aged, red-armed, wearing a man's cap with the brim cracked through. She tested the board's weight, rubbed the surviving paint with her thumb, and then tucked it under her basket rather than leave it for scrap. He did not know her. He only knew the motion. It was the motion of somebody salvaging a name before strangers could decide it had never mattered.

"He's not only selling fuel," Silas said.

"Congratulations."

"Shut up."

She actually did, which was rarer than kindness from her and therefore counted more.

He kept his eyes on the city. From the water the changes lined up in a way he had not seen from the street. He saw the school copy sheet, the ferry placard, and the pale respectable color washing over a hand-lettered warning, a market arrow, and a rooming-house board with someone's older bargain still faint beneath it. Separate, each thing was trivial enough to laugh at. Together they made a pattern as plain as a net.

The net did not have to drag a man into the river to take his place from him. It only had to rename the block until the people already living there sounded incorrect.

He thought of Junie being advised to repaint lighter for better customers, as if broth had a class so long as the sign did. He thought of Otis copying the line because the loops were pretty and the teacher said proper names prevented cheating. He thought of the women in Sump Ward speaking to the moss by use and risk and heat while he and Gideon had trimmed those living rough instructions into something purchasable. It was not a product, not really. It was a border, a way of telling the city which mouths counted as civilized enough to define a thing and which ones would henceforth sound provincial, suspect, low.

It was eviction without marshals, without even the vulgar honesty of a locked door.

You washed the wall. You called the old name confusion. You taught the children a cleaner sentence. By the time rent rose or a supplier changed terms or a woman stopped asking for the local tray by its right rough word because the clerk made a face, the work had already been done. The room had been taken before anyone admitted a taking.

The ferry engine thumped under his boots. Across from them, one of the women with the wrapped lamp trays had opened her bundle to check the stock. The moss inside gave off a muted practical green, the color of work left alone. She covered it again the instant a passing clerk leaned too close, not out of melodrama but out of habit. Guard the useful thing. Guard the name attached to it. Guard whatever a richer man might decide looked better after translation.

"I thought the shame was the point," Silas said at last.

"Your shame is always a point," Damaris said. "Just rarely the point."

He let that pass.

"I helped him build the rhythm for it," he said. "Not just the names. The way the lines close. The way they stick."

"Yes."

He turned to her. "You don't have to enjoy that quite so much."

"I'm not enjoying it." The wind flattened her veil against one cheek and showed the tired skin under her eye. "I'm telling you not to make a religion out of feeling rotten. It's boring and it wastes daylight. If you have finally noticed the thing is bigger than your humiliation, then do something useful with the notice."

They were nearing the far slip. Bell metal rang. Deckhands shouted. The city pressed in again with its wet planks and stacked goods and impatient bodies. Damaris folded the rest of her bread into her paper, tucked it into her pocket, and straightened her hat.

"I've got evening editions to lie in," she said. "You can stay on for the return if you're thinking."

"I'm thinking."

"Try not to sound so injured about it."

She stepped off with the crowd, black skirts brushing fish scales and coal dust alike, already calling after a runner about column space before both feet had found the dock. Silas stayed where he was as the ferry took on new passengers and shoved back into the channel.

The return crossing gave him the same city with less excuse not to understand it. A pair of boys on the upper bench sang the slogan to the tune of a hymn and then dissolved into laughter when they forgot the middle. A drayman asked another where to find certified cuttings. An old woman corrected him with a local name anyway, then muttered the official one after it because experience had taught her which word would get the sack loaded. Near Ferrymouth two workmen were taking down a sign for a cheap lodging house and leaning a fresh celadon panel against the wall to replace it. The new board had been centered beautifully. He knew the hand that had spaced it, if not the man. It was decent work. That was the filthiest part, good work in service of a smaller mouth.

His wrist ached in time with the ferry engine. He rested it on the rail and looked until the ache stopped being the chapter heading of his own disgrace and became only one hurt among others. He thought of Junie's kitchen, Otis's copy sheet, the warning board painted over, the woman rescuing a discarded plank as if it had family in it, and Inez's people hearing their rough practical speech come back to them in clean purchased form and being told the cleaned version was proper.

He had thought theft was the moment a man pocketed a word that was not his.

It was also the slower labor after: the sanding down, the whitening, the teaching, the part where a city learned to repeat the stranger's phrase until the people already living there sounded wrong in their own mouths.

When the ferry slid back against Ferrymouth and the ropes flew, he got off without hurrying. Junie's sign still hung over the kitchen door, dark letters on honest board, broth and bread promised plain. Evening damp had deepened the blue strokes he had laid there years ago. For the first time all day he did not look at it as proof of what his hand used to do.

He looked at it as something still alive enough to be taken. He had spent years teaching walls to flatter strangers. He had helped give eviction a prettier mouth.
