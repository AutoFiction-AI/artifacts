# Chapter 7

The room Gideon kept above Verdancy House sat high enough over Patent Row to miss the gutter stink for three breaths and no more. After that the city got in anyway: yeast, wet paper, lamp mash warming in cellar pipes, the sweet edge of rot that all rich men in Nacre Bend pretended was someone else's climate. Gideon had arranged his office as a rebuttal. The curtains were cream instead of greened white. The desk was walnut instead of pine. Even the ink smelled dearer, iron and clove instead of the ordinary sour black used by printers and creditors.

The clerk who admitted Silas wore a mourning brooch shaped like a tiny anchor and kept cracking fennel seeds between her back teeth as if the office itself gave her heartburn. She set a folded paper beside Gideon's hand and said, "Mr. Vale," with a look that suggested she had been told what kind of man he was and had improved the picture privately.

Gideon waited until the door shut before he touched the paper. He was gloved as usual, pearl gray kid, clean enough to insult half the city on sight.

"You look tired," he said.

"I got laughed at in three wards on my way here. It takes a certain energy."

"Public amusement is not fatal. It is often preparatory."

Silas dropped into the chair opposite him without being asked. The leather gave a little squeak under his damp coat. "If that's your sermon on the launch, save it. I was there for the collection."

Gideon slid the folded paper across the desk. It was a receipted statement from Havelock Pigments. Forty-three crowns, settled in full. The owner's greasy thumbprint sat at the bottom like a bruise.

Relief hit first, clean and ugly. Havelock would stop sending boys past his shop to stare in at the empty shelf space. Havelock would stop saying Mr. Vale in the tone usually reserved for men found asleep in drains. Silas ran his thumb over the number once, then hated himself for letting the pleasure show anywhere near his face.

"You asked for time," Gideon said. "I have purchased it."

"For me."

"For the work."

Silas folded the receipt and tucked it into his waistcoat. "And what work is that now? Celadon Crown got itself chewed to gristle."

"No." Gideon sat back. "Celadon Crown remains useful. What failed on the wall was exclusivity delivered too early and too theatrically. That is an error of sequence, not of principle."

There were sample labels laid out in neat fans across the desk, cream stock and dark green borders. Silas had noticed them when he came in and pretended not to. Gideon drew one closer with a fingertip.

"People do not adopt a name because it is true," he said. "Most products have no truth to speak of. People adopt a name because it feels already worn by someone they trust. A wife at a wash line. A mother on a stair. A ferryman with a bad knee who has needed the thing in winter. Habit, Mr. Vale. The market rewards habit."

Silas laughed once, short through the nose. "You want home words."

"I want tested words."

"From where?"

Gideon looked at him as if the answer ought to have been obvious enough to shame him. "From the people who have already done the testing for free."

The clerk's fennel crack came faintly through the door. Somewhere below, a press thudded three times and stopped. Silas stared at the labels. The blanks had headings penciled at the top in Gideon's elegant square hand: kitchen, stairs, nursery, sickroom, yard.

"Those are neighborhood names," Silas said. "Use names."

"Yes."

"You want to strip them."

Gideon lifted one shoulder. "I want to place them. Strip is a word for men who break doors at midnight. I am speaking of circulation."

"Circulation that gets billed."

"Naturally."

Silas rubbed his forefinger against the nicotine stain on his thumb. He could feel the little ridge of dried paint under the nail. He thought of Junie's coal bin. He thought of the Havelock receipt warm already against his chest. He thought, unwillingly, of Inez in Junie's kitchen two nights before, sleeves shoved up, explaining in that flat steady voice why a name that cooked too hot could blister a child's hand before the rice softened.

"You said we were looking for the stable public name," he said.

"We are looking for whatever will make the public ask twice. One banner name, yes. Several household shades beneath it. Shades breed loyalty. People like to think their purchase has intimacy."

"So truth can go to hell."

Gideon's mouth changed by half an inch, which in him counted as candor. "Truth is a scholar's hobby. I am trying to keep hold of a city."

Silas leaned back and let the chair take his weight. "You can't get those names from me by asking pretty. Most of them won't tell me where they piss, let alone what they call the glow in the nursery."

"Then do not ask pretty."

"You make theft sound like a table manner."

"You make too much of ownership." Gideon folded his hands. "A phrase spoken in a kitchen does not become sacred simply because the ceiling is low. If a useful name exists, leaving it buried is waste. We will improve distribution. Households benefit. We benefit. You are paid."

There it was, the whole creed in one dry mouthful. Folk knowledge as ore in wet ground. Somebody else's labor, already proven under heat and hunger, waiting for a man with better stationery.

Silas ought to have walked out then. He knew that. The knowledge did not move his body an inch.

Part of the trouble was that Gideon was not wholly wrong about the names themselves. Silas had heard enough now, in wash yards and stairwells and over Junie's sink, to know the best ones came out of use the way color came out of a bruised plum. Nobody invented them from nowhere. They arrived with burnt fingers and dark landings and old women refusing to break their hips in the privy dark. They had work on them. They clung.

And he loved that. He loved it even while he hated the hand reaching for it.

"Suppose I bring you trash," he said. "Jokes. Decoys. Half the city has decided it's funnier to lie to me than to pay their tabs."

"Then bring me enough of it to sort." Gideon reached for a small morocco notebook and pushed it across the desk. "You know how words wear. You know when a mouth is trying on a hat and when it is naming the pan it uses every morning. That is why I retained you and not a stenographer."

The notebook was better than anything Silas carried for himself, soft leather, thick paper that would take ink without feathering. He opened it. Gideon had ruled faint columns on the first page. Sound, use, district, repeatability.

Silas barked a laugh in spite of himself. "Repeatability."

"That is the only category that matters."

"That is the only category that matters to you."

"Which is why I am solvent."

Silas flipped the notebook shut. He could feel the trap with both hands. He could also feel the craft in it, and that was worse. There was a game inside the theft, a difficult and delicate one: separating live language from the city's deliberate nonsense, hearing what had been worn smooth by need, choosing what would travel without dying in transit. Gideon knew it. Gideon had built a ledger around that knowledge before Silas had admitted it to himself.

"If I do this," Silas said, "I'm not promising you the final name."

"You are promising me useful raw stock."

"You have a hell of a gift for making ugly work sound like a refinery."

"And you have a gift for making ugly work sound like art. This is why we suit each other, Mr. Vale."

Silas stood because if he stayed seated he might throw the notebook back across the desk and watch whether Gideon flinched. Gideon would not. That was part of what made him intolerable.

"You will want quick returns," Silas said.

"I always do."

"And if I decide some of it's too private?"

Gideon looked up at him. The gray gloves rested motionless on the blotter. "You will decide according to your need for money and according to the very evident standard by which you have lived so far."

Silas felt the line land. It was not loud. Gideon never wasted volume where humiliation would do better.

"That's a cheap shot from a man whose cuffs cost my rent."

"Cheap shots are for crowds. I am only taking inventory."

Silas tucked the notebook under his arm and headed for the door.

"Mr. Vale," Gideon said behind him.

Silas stopped.

"Do not waste time looking for authenticity as if it were a saint's relic. I do not need the truest word. I need the word a stranger will believe has already been used by someone poorer and wiser than himself."

Silas turned enough to show half his face. "That may be the foulest sentence I hear this week."

"Then let us hope the week is professionally fruitful."

On the stair down, the air got hotter with each landing. By the time he reached the street the Havelock receipt was sticking to his chest, damp through the cloth, and the little notebook in his hand felt less like paper than a fresh-made tool somebody had put there for burglary.

* * *

Junie did not thank him for the sack of potatoes or the coil of smoked eel or the jar of lamp mash he set near the stove. Gratitude was not the house currency. She lifted the mash, judged the weight with one hand, and called to the pot boy to keep his filthy thumb out of it. Then she squinted at Silas across the steam.

Flour had pasted itself to the split in her left eyebrow and gone gray with sweat. "Havelock's runner came by smiling this morning," she said. "I liked him better mean."

"You'll live."

"That's usually the plan."

She went back to skimming eel fat. "Whatever your patent man bought, he had better not have bought your last clean thought with it."

Silas took a heel of bread from the board and tore it apart with both hands. "What makes you think I had one."

"Because you're still vain enough to keep one in reserve."

That was all she said. Lunch was coming on. Ferry bells knocked against the damp air outside, and the room had already shifted from family to trade. Men stamped in with rope burn on their palms and river mud drying high on their boots. Otis darted through once with a stack of spoons and a trouser knee gone black from the dock, then vanished again when Junie snapped his name. Nobody in the room had time for a confession, which suited Silas better than mercy would have.

He left by the side door with Gideon's notebook in his pocket and the city already talking around him.

At Ferrymouth the first lie came from a ferryman with two wedding rings on one hand and none on the other, a broad pink man who hummed hymn tunes while he patched net floats between runs.

"What are folks calling that glow now?" Silas asked, leaning on the rail.

The ferryman did not even look up. "Taxman's Mercy."

"That sounds invented."

"Everything sounds invented till the third week."

The next came from a woman gutting silver eel behind a shed, her sleeves rolled to the shoulder and one ear stuffed with wool against the gull noise. She said the proper ward name was "Bishop's Spit" and smiled without teeth. Three boys on overturned crates snorted so hard one of them nearly fell backward into the fish scales.

By the fourth and fifth Silas had the measure of it. The lies came too fast, too polished, or with too much waiting after them. A real use name had weight in the mouth. It carried a task with it. These came dressed for the stage: dock bride, saint-piss, bellglass milk, widow wink, prayer mold, green fever lace. They were excellent, some of them. That was the infuriating part. The city could lie beautifully when it had a target.

And the target was him.

Word had gone ahead. He could tell by the way people sharpened when he drifted near, by the little nudges and glances before somebody offered up a phrase. A teamster with a cauliflower ear said "wharf virgin" and got a cuff on the arm from his partner for trying too hard. A girl selling ribbon scraps from a tray at Patent Row, no more than fourteen and already keeping her own accounts in blue pencil on the inside of her cuff, told him it was called "Mother's Lie" in three districts and held his gaze until he wrote it down. Then she burst out laughing and said, "Now he has it in his book."

Silas laughed too. He could not help it. The whole thing had the shape of a card hustle run against him by a city that had finally decided to learn his favorite trick and improve it. He spent an hour following the lies anyway, partly because sometimes a false trail crossed a true one, partly because he was good at hearing how people chose their nonsense, and partly because the chase still pleased him. It pleased the part of him that liked pigments lined in jars and letters balanced on a brush point and the first hot second before a crowd decided whether to hate him or cheer.

He sat on an upturned cask outside a chandlery and sorted the notebook pages with his thumb. Gideon's columns were beginning to fill.

Taxman's Mercy: repeated by men who had been watching each other too closely. Joke.

Mother's Lie: better. Bitter enough to feel used. Could be decoy. Could be old. Worth another listen.

Bellglass Milk: nonsense, but elegant nonsense.

Dock Bride: spread by children too quickly to trust.

He hated himself for enjoying the discrimination. Good lies had their own brushwork. He could hear where a word had been fattened to tempt him, where a little smear of obscenity had been laid on for his benefit because everyone in Nacre Bend knew he liked his language with a little gutter in it. The neighborhoods were not merely refusing him. They were feeding him counterfeit according to his taste.

By midafternoon the notebook looked busy and told him almost nothing. He crossed Patent Row under walls still ghosted from the failed Celadon launch and watched two newsboys arguing over whether bride-rot would make a better headline than crown-slime. Even the children were jamming the line on purpose now. A grocer's wife with violets pinned to a hat gone shiny at the brim leaned out to call after him, "Ask Sump Ward for angel sweat, Mr. Vale. They keep barrels of it."

Her friend inside the shop laughed so hard she had to brace a hand against the flour bin.

Self-defense, Silas thought. Not just mockery. The city had understood the exchange. If names had value, then value could be spoiled at the mouth before it reached the buyer. Feed the patent man noise. Make him pay to sort sand from sugar. Make him work for what he meant to take for free.

He admired that too.

By the time the light started bending toward evening, the honest part of the day had made itself plain. Nobody with sense was going to hand him anything useful. Not in Ferrymouth, not on Patent Row, not while his mouth still had Gideon's money on it.

Which meant the asking part was over.

He stood under a poster half scraped from brick and looked south where the street fell into the wetter districts. Sump Ward lay there in its own haze, all plank cuts and low roofs and lamp vapor. He told himself he was going because their names would be easier to recognize against the trash. He told himself he would only listen. He told himself half a dozen things a man told himself when he had already chosen the uglier road and wanted it paved before he walked it.

Then he put the notebook back in his pocket and went down toward the water.

* * *

Sump Ward always felt lower than it was, as if the whole district had learned over years of flood to tuck its shoulders against the sky. The heat stayed trapped between the houses. Wet shirts on lines dragged cool against Silas's cheek when the breeze shifted. Planks flexed over black runoff ditches that smelled of soap, algae mash, and old river weed. Somewhere close, fermentation pipes gave their slow underwater bubbling like a giant stomach trying not to complain.

Inez was on the catwalk outside one of the grow houses, trimming pale frill from the edge of a mother tray with a thin hooked knife. She had her hair wrapped in the dark work scarf again. The burn scar inside her right wrist flashed once when she turned the tray. She saw him before he had both boots on the landing and her face went still in the precise way she had when she was angry enough not to spend extra movement.

"What do you want?" she said.

"Brought back the warning tin from Ferrymouth. The one I repainted."

He lifted it. The sign still smelled of fresh size and lampblack. LOW HEAT ONLY in hard clear letters, arrow beneath, no flourish. He had done it plain on purpose.

Inez wiped the knife on a rag. "Leave it by the door."

"That's all?"

"What else were you hoping for."

Before he could answer, Aunt Vero called from inside, "If he's hoping for praise, tell him to take off his boots first. I don't want gratitude tracked over the clean boards."

She emerged carrying a basket of cuttings lined with damp burlap. Her thick white braid had come loose at the nape, and one eye drifted a little as she sized him up, which only made the look meaner because it felt like being judged twice from slightly different angles.

"Afternoon, Vero," Silas said.

"That depends who is having it."

"I fixed the sign."

"Then you know how nails work. Hang it and be useful for one full minute before you turn back into yourself."

Inez stepped aside just far enough to let him pass, which was not welcome so much as the absence of a barricade. He took the hammer from the hook by the door, set the tin where she pointed, and drove the nails in while three women inside the grow house pretended not to watch him. The boards sweated under his palm. The last nail bent crooked, and he had to pull it and do it again.

When he stepped down, Inez was already carrying trays toward the back room. "Since you're here," she said without looking at him, "take that stack to the wash bench. Not the blue-tagged ones."

There was the discipline in her: if a man insisted on occupying space, turn him into labor.

Silas lifted the stack. Wet heat came through the wood into his hands. The moss on top glowed a queasy green under the burlap, sulking after a trim. He followed the narrow passage between hanging sacks and shelves of jars, shoulders turned to avoid brushing the live edges.

Voices carried more cleanly inside than out. He heard Aunt Vero muttering at somebody to stop breathing directly over the starter beds as if youth itself were a contaminant. He heard a younger woman's answer, quick and tired.

"I'm not breathing on them, I'm asking which trays go to Belen first."

The young woman came into view only by slivers between hanging cloth. She had spectacles mended with wire at one temple and little fish drawn in chalk all over the hem of her apron, as if she had been amusing a child while the starch dried. She was balancing two trays against her hip and waiting for instruction.

Inez answered from the room beyond, all business. "Hushgreen to her bed. Potback to the broth shelf. And put step-blue by the privy door before dusk, or she will miss the plank again."

Silas stopped so abruptly the tray edges bit his palm.

Nobody had said the words to him. Nobody had even lowered a voice because of him; that was the point. The names were moving inside a task the way they had always moved, fast and useful and attached to a body that needed something before night. Sickroom. Cooking shelf. Safe footing in the dark. Three names and what they did, all at once.

He could have made noise then. Shifted his boot. Let the stack thump. Given the room a chance to clot around the words and keep them.

Instead he stood still.

Hushgreen. Potback. Step-blue.

He set them in order in his mind the way he set letters before brush met board. Not by sound first but by placement: bed, broth, plank. Then by sound, because he could not help that either. Hushgreen with the breath buried in it. Potback blunt and work-worn. Step-blue quick on the tongue and impossible to misunderstand.

The tray in his hands felt suddenly heavier, not because it weighed more but because he knew what he was doing and kept doing it.

When he moved again he did it carefully, as if gentleness altered the nature of the act. He laid the stack on the wash bench. His fingers had left wet crescents on the wood.

Inez came through the hanging cloth a moment later with her own arms full of cuttings. She looked at him once. Maybe she saw something in his face, maybe only sweat. "You can go," she said.

"That's the warmest dismissal I've had all day."

"Then your day is earning its character."

Aunt Vero barked a laugh from the next room. "Don't flirt at my tray beds unless you're planning to fertilize them with gold."

Silas gave them the grin his body knew how to produce without help. "If I ever shit coins, Vero, I will start with your doorstep."

"No, you won't. You'd spend them showing off halfway down the lane."

The exchange landed the way such exchanges always did, half comic, half blade. Inez did not smile. That was what finally made him leave.

Outside, dusk had thickened the ward into blue-green layers. He crossed two plank bridges and ducked under the eave of an abandoned wash shed when the first warm spit of rain came on. Mosquitoes combed the air over the ditch. From somewhere behind him, Belen or some other old woman started coughing, the wet chest-deep kind that made a whole house sound short of air.

Silas pulled the Havelock receipt from his waistcoat because it was the first paper his hand found. Forty-three crowns, settled in full. On the blank back he wrote:

hushgreen - bed/sickroom

potback - broth, kitchen

step-blue - privy/stairs

The nib scratched harder than he meant it to. He stared at the words until the ink took hold and lost the wobble of his hand.

There. That was the moment. Not the overhearing. Men overheard things every day. Not even the remembering, because memory could still fail if a man was decent enough to let it. This. The paper, the pinning down, the choice to make somebody else's working speech into stock.

Rain drummed harder on the shed roof. Silas folded the receipt along the old crease so the paid debt sat inside the stolen names and put it back over his heart.

Then he walked uphill to deliver it.

* * *

Verdancy House never truly slept. It only changed machinery. By day the showroom windows glittered and clerks moved about with samples on velvet pads. By evening the back floors woke: paste heated in kettles, proofs hung from lines, and men with ink-black cuffs argued over type sizes as if salvation depended on twelve-point versus fourteen.

Gideon had moved downstairs to the proofing room by the time Silas arrived. He stood at a long table under three shaded lamps, gloves off at last, sleeves unmarked. Sheets lay all around him, some handbills for patent tonics, some bills for a summer recital on Gilt Crescent, some blank stock waiting for whatever fresh appetite the city could be taught by dawn.

A pressman with a flattened left ear was setting type in a composing stick nearby and singing to himself under his breath. Every few lines he paused to tuck a peppermint clove into one cheek, which gave the whole room a medicinal sweetness over the ink.

Gideon looked up. "You have something."

Silas laid the folded receipt on the table. "I've got possibilities."

Gideon opened it and read in silence. His eyes moved once, then back, slower. The pressman stopped singing without being told to.

"And the rest?" Gideon said.

"Mostly jokes. Waste. People have grown strategic."

"Good. Resistance confirms value."

Silas pulled Gideon's notebook from his pocket and flipped it open. "You wanted enough to sort. Here. If you like Bellglass Milk for a nursery line, by all means damn yourself with it."

Gideon took the notebook, skimmed a page, and almost immediately set his finger on three entries. Not the funniest ones. Not the flashiest. The useful ones.

"These are false," he said, tapping dock bride and taxman's mercy away. "These are vulgar decoys. These" - his finger came back to the receipt - "have wear."

Silas's jaw set. "You can hear wear, can you."

"So can you."

That was the worst part again: no need for pretense between them. Gideon understood the theft exactly and admired it more for that.

He handed the notebook to the pressman. "Mr. Herron. Pull me a household series under the Celadon umbrella. Half-sheet first, wall bills by district after."

Herron wiped his fingers on his apron and took the book. The flattened ear made his head sit slightly askew, and when he read he moved his lips, a habit from childhood or injury or both. "Text, sir?"

Gideon was already composing aloud. "Celadon Crown Household Shades. Hushgreen for nurseries and convalescent rooms. Potback for kettle, galley, and cooking shelf. Step-Blue for stairs, privies, and safe night crossings."

Silas heard the transformation happen in real time: not only the theft of the names but the theft of the labor inside them. Broth shelf became kettle, galley, and cooking shelf because richer buyers would not think of broth first. Privy door became safe night crossings because embarrassment sold worse than reassurance. Gideon was not merely taking the words. He was polishing the fingerprints off them while keeping enough grime to look trustworthy.

"You can't print those by supper," Silas said.

"Why not."

"Because they're not yours."

Gideon glanced at him. "They are not anyone's in the only sense that matters."

"That's not a sentence, that's a burglary tool."

Herron coughed into his fist and kept setting type. The tiny metal clicks sounded like teeth.

Silas leaned both hands on the table. "I brought them for assessment. Not immediate issue."

"Do not be childish. If they work, delay helps your opponents." Gideon sorted through paper weights until he found the green border he preferred. "You have done your part. Let me do mine."

"My opponents."

"Do not go sentimental now, Mr. Vale. It makes you vague."

Silas wanted to snatch the receipt back. He wanted to grind the wet ink under his palm and leave the whole room smeared. But he had brought the words here himself, folded in with the proof of his paid debt like a joke too exact to laugh at. Gideon had only unfolded what Silas delivered.

Herron set the first proof on the roller bed and looked to Gideon. "Caps on the shades, sir?"

"Yes. Let them read at distance."

The press took the sheet with a sucking kiss and spit it out wet and sharp. HUSHGREEN sat there in black block letters, clean as a magistrate. The paper shone where the ink had not yet dried.

Silas stared at it long enough for the letters to stop being letters and become what they would do once boys shouted them at corners and wives asked for them over counters and some idiot on Patent Row explained them to a room he had never cooked in.

"You could still choose not to," he said, though now it sounded weak even to him.

Gideon signed the order slip. "No. You could have chosen not to."

The bill-stickers went out before midnight with buckets of paste and rolled sheets under their arms. Silas did not mean to follow them. He found himself doing it anyway, trailing a half block back through streets gone slick under a thin rain. Patent Row first, then the turn toward Ferrymouth, then the lower lanes where people woke early enough to catch the first boat and would read with their mouths still tasting sleep.

Paste slapped brick. Brushes rasped. The men worked quickly, covering old advertisements and hand-painted warnings alike. On a warehouse wall near the slips, a fresh half-sheet went up over a faded notice for tide levels. On the side of a coal yard, another covered a chalked menu. By the time the first bakery fire was being coaxed alive, CELADON CROWN HOUSEHOLD SHADES had begun marching down the city in even green borders.

At the corner by Junie's lane, one of the bill-stickers papered over a hand-lettered board Silas had done years back for a widow who sold boiled coffee before dawn. He watched HUSHGREEN go across her old price list like a coffin lid.

A woman with her hair tied in a rag came out carrying a chamber pail and stopped to squint at the new bill. She had a cut on her knuckle wrapped in blue thread and sleep still stamped along one cheek.

"Step-Blue," she read aloud, tasting it. "For stairs."

From inside the house behind her, somebody answered, "That sounds useful."

Useful. That was how theft crossed the threshold. Not with a scream. With recognition. With a tired stranger hearing a true thing stolen into a new uniform and reaching for it because dawn was cold and the steps were slick and usefulness had never had the luxury of purity.

Silas stood in the rain and watched the paper shine.

By six bells, three ward names were already in mouths that had never earned them, and the paste on the walls was still wet.
