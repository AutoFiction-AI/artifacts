# Chapter 2

By noon the city was answering him with his joke in its teeth.

Silas climbed Lantern Steps through air that smelled of wet lime, lamp smoke, and the sweet warm rot the Sallow River breathed into every lower street after rain. He had no clean reason to be there. A sensible man would have stayed on Patent Row, taken another lap past the windows where people were already pointing at last night's pink bloom, and let the word he'd flung off a signboard do what words did in Nacre Bend, which was travel farther than their author deserved and pick up worse company on the way. But sense had never kept him in tobacco or paid Havelock Pigments on time. Curiosity had done better. Vanity had done best of all. He wanted to see whether bridelight had truly climbed into the stair district or whether the morning's talk had only painted rumor over damp stone.

It had climbed.

Every third public lamp along the rail showed some stain of it. Not a steady color, not yet. A blush under the green, a watered rose caught in the glass, the sort of tint a man could call charming if he had no intention of carrying soup, babies, or his own half-drunk body down slick steps after dark. A fishwoman in a tarred apron laughed the word to her sister, and the lamp over their heads flushed a shade brighter as if it liked applause. Two boys half his nephew's age had pink chalk on their cuffs and were arguing over who had first heard him say it. A clerk from Gilt Crescent, clean collar already losing the fight with humidity, had come down only to stare and say the name with the reverence some men reserved for a new racehorse.

Silas ought to have felt pleased. He did, for about half a landing at a time. Then the feeling kept slipping on something he could not name. The pink did not sit on the lamps the way paint sat on a board. It lived too near the surface. It seemed to gather and thin with the breath of whoever was nearest. He knew color. He knew fraud in color better than most honest men knew prayer. This was neither.

He saw the woman before he understood she was waiting for him.

She stood in the middle of the stair where the steps widened enough for two tenants to quarrel without tumbling into the drainage cut, compact and planted and impossible to edge around. A dark work scarf was wrapped hard over her hair. The hem of her skirt was wet to mid-calf. Two lamp trays hung from her hands by their bent wire handles, one in each fist, as if she had brought him a pair of arguments too heavy for language alone. The inside of her right wrist showed a pale crescent scar when she shifted her grip, old heat laid down clean and deep. Behind her, crowding the landing rail, waited four women and a girl with a cracked chimney glass hugged to her apron. They had the patient appetite of people who had come to watch a man be corrected.

"Silas Vale," the woman said.

He touched two fingers to his hat brim. "When the law insists on particulars."

"Good. Then you won't pretend to be somebody less useful."

Her voice did not rise. It landed flat and exact, the kind of voice used to calling problems by the right size before somebody made them expensive. She stepped down one stair and set one tray on the stone between them. The metal hit with a blunt wet knock. A ribbon of pink slime shivered over the lip and dropped onto his boot.

Up close, the tray had gone bad in ways no lamp keeper could fake for sport. The shallow belly had warped inward on one side as if heat had licked it too long. The wire cage over the moss bed had buckled. One rivet had sweated itself loose and left a rusty tear in the tin. The growth inside was alive, if that was the word for something that glowed while smelling faintly of pond scum and hot pennies. It held the same flushed pink that had charmed Patent Row the night before, only here it looked indecent, cosmetic, a cheap rouge streaked across work metal.

Silas crouched before he could stop himself. Trade habit. Damage spoke in materials before it spoke anywhere else.

"That tray was steady yesterday," the woman said. "Cool enough for a landing lamp. This morning it bowed."

One of the women at the rail, a narrow laundress with one lens missing from her spectacles and blueing powder dusted up both forearms, sucked her teeth and added, "Took the skin off my kettle thumb too. If you're collecting compliments, have that one."

Silas looked from the bent tray to the woman's thumb. A fresh blister shone there, high and angry. He pushed at the tray rim with one paint-stained nail. The metal gave under his touch where it should have held.

"Might be bad mash," he said. "Or a cheap plate sold by a liar with softer tin than sense."

The woman in the scarf did not blink. The laundress with the blistered thumb only watched him. No rescue laugh came off the rail. "I grow beds for six houses and the public lamps on this run. Do not explain my own trays to me."

Still crouched, he had to look up at her while the rail behind her held its breath.

He rose. "Then I'll skip theory and move straight to apology for the unknown tinner."

"Don't dress it." She nudged the tray with her boot until the warped belly rang again on stone. "I know your kind of joke when it walks indoors."

He smiled because people expected him to smile, and because once he stopped smiling in public a great deal of his life would have to be re-negotiated from the mud up. "My kind of joke usually buys drinks before it ruins cookware."

No one laughed. The girl with the cracked chimney glass shifted it higher against her chest as if afraid he might charm that away too.

The woman said, "Inez Cordero."

It landed like a tray tag tied on for the shift, clear enough that nobody could mistake it for welcome.

He gave her a tiny nod. "Miss Cordero."

"Miss will do until you learn something. Look there."

She pointed past him, not at the tray but at the public lamp bolted to the landing post. Its glass belly had gone pink in streaks, color snagged inside green like blood working through river weed. Beneath it, tied to the post with a strip of wet cloth, sat a hand-sized spare cutting in a shallow tin saucer. Silas had not noticed it because he had been looking at the room of faces instead of the test laid out for him.

Inez bent, lifted the saucer, and held it level between them. The moss in it was greener than the tray growth, with a pink creep already nibbling in from one edge.

"This piece never left the landing," she said. "No Patent Row wall, no crowd around it, no drunks kissing it for luck. Just here. Last night the stair boys came up yelling your word because they'd heard it below and thought pink was a grand joke. By midnight this piece matched the rail lamps."

Silas heard himself say, "You make it sound jealous."

"I make it sound busy."

She turned her head toward the women behind her. "Say it."

They did not sing. They did not sway or perform a little ward rite for his benefit. They said bridelight the way neighbors called for more coal, or asked whose child was loose on the steps, or warned one another about a rotten plank. Flat, repeated, public. The laundress said it around the clench of pain in her thumb. The girl said it with unhappy dutifulness. Even a woman in a patched men's coat, who had been picking fish scales out of her cuff with the point of a hatpin, gave it one dry repetition.

Bridelight. Bridelight. Bridelight.

The saucer in Inez's hand answered at once. Pink ran across the green in a wet fast blush and the heat off it touched Silas's knuckles. Overhead the public lamp brightened with the same false warmth, a flattering color fit for a ballroom painting and useless on a stair slick with runoff.

Silas did not say anything. His mouth was suddenly occupied with staying shut.

Inez watched him watch it. Then she tipped her chin toward the women again.

"Now the house name."

The answer came back in a different rhythm, more settled because it belonged to use and everyone there already knew where it fit.

"Stepblue."

They said it twice, then a third time in the same low rhythm.

The pink withdrew as if drawn through the flesh of the moss by an unseen thread. Blue spread in its place, not sky blue or the hard enamel of rich parlor tiles, but the laundry blue women cut with water to bully yellow out of shirts. Cool color. Working color. In the saucer it deepened until the whole patch held a sober mourning cast that made the landing stones look suddenly farther apart and more honest about their wetness. The lamp over them followed, its blush draining into the same grave blue. Even the heat came off differently. A moment before Silas had felt it on the back of his fingers like a flirt. Now it laid itself flat and broad, useful rather than insistent.

The woman in the patched coat said, "That's what keeps old Mr. Dane from pissing off the edge on his way down."

That got one bitter snort of laughter from the rail. It broke just enough tension for Silas to hear the river below and the hiss of damp gas off the lamp bed.

"Stepblue," he repeated.

Inez shifted the saucer to her other hand. "For stairs. Broad throw, low burn. Doesn't cook sleeves when a drunk leans too near. Doesn't make shadows jump. Doesn't draw every fool moth in the cut."

"And bridelight does."

"Bridelight preens." Her lip moved by the smallest degree, not a smile and not near one. "Pretty thing for a signboard. Rotten thing for a landing."

The words hit him harder because she had not raised her voice to throw them. He wanted to answer with something easy and knife-bright, something that would turn the whole little tribunal back into spectacle where he knew his footing. Three fine lines rose ready in him, all of them variations on the city wanting entertainment and names never staying where a man put them. All of them died before they reached his tongue. The blue in the lamp was still settling. He could smell cooled tin, damp stone, the rank sweetness of live growth. Nothing about it felt like theater except him.

"You taught it a trick," he said at last, and hated how thin it sounded.

"No." Inez set the saucer back beneath the lamp. "We taught it a job. You gave it a courtship name and now every idiot with lungs is making it blush."

One of the women behind her, the laundress with the missing lens, let out a short ugly laugh. "And while it blushes, I get to boil washing by luck."

Silas looked at her blister again. Then at the warped tray. Then back at Inez. He had spent years turning words into surfaces men could profit from. A polish. A gleam. A little difference between one thing for sale and the next thing for sale. He knew the thrill of hearing a crowd pick up a phrase he'd made and carry it forward as if they had invented it themselves. That thrill was still in him, stupidly alive. So was something uglier now, like the instant after a brush slips and drags a whole loaded stripe across finished lettering, when the damage is already on the board and the stomach has not caught up yet.

"All right," he said. "You've shown me a moody lamp."

The women at the rail made small sounds of disgust. Inez did not.

"No," she said again, cleaner than before. "I've shown you that your mouth can reach a stair from Patent Row. Now pick that tray up."

He blinked. "Why?"

"Because it answers to you, and because you're coming inside."

She put the bent tray's handle into his hand before he could object. The wire bit his palm. The thing was heavier than it looked, wet and badly balanced. Heat still lived in one warped flank. Pink slime smeared his cuff. The smell rose stronger from this close, a stink of wet copper and pond weed and something singed underneath.

He did not drop it. The whole landing was waiting to see whether he would.

Inez took the second tray, turned without checking whether he followed, and started down the next run of steps with the quick sure footing of somebody who trusted slick wood and wet stone because they had spent years teaching her their terms. Silas adjusted his grip and went after her, feeling all at once like a man carrying his own cleverness after it had spoiled in the pan.

The stair district narrowed as she led him downward. Landings gave way to short plank bridges flung over runnels of gray water. Laundry lines crossed above like low pennants of surrender. The houses were packed close enough that windows argued with one another. Somewhere below, hidden by walls, a ferry bell knocked twice. Above, farther up the steps, someone was still trying out bridelight for amusement. The word came down thin through the damp and made the tray in Silas's hand flush at the edges.

Inez saw him notice. "Keep moving."

"I'm moving."

She glanced back. "You can contract if you're tired, Mr. Vale. The tray won't think less of you."

He barked one laugh in spite of himself. "That's crueler than it needed to be."

"No. Crueler is later if you still think this is a novelty."

She took him first into a one-room kitchen on a middle landing where the door swelled in its frame and had to be shoulder-butted open. The woman inside had a watch chain clipped to her apron though the watch itself was long gone; the empty chain swung against her belly every time she stirred the pot on the lamp stand. She did not look up when they entered. She was too busy scraping the bottom of a kettle with a spoon and swearing at it under her breath.

"Mrs. Rusk," Inez said.

Mrs. Rusk yanked the lid off the kettle and showed Silas supper in two different moods. One side of the stew had thickened into a paste blackening at the rim. The other side still sloshed pale and underdone, turnip cubes floating hard as stones in broth that had not decided whether it meant to warm or sulk.

"There," Mrs. Rusk said. "You can letter that if you like. Call it half-starved, half-burnt."

Under the kettle, the lamp bed shone with a muddy battle between blue and pink. The heat at the stand came in little shoves, enough to sting the back of Silas's hand when he tested the air, then fall away so suddenly the spoon in Mrs. Rusk's pot stopped rattling.

"It used to hold steady?" he asked.

Mrs. Rusk gave him a look sharp enough to skin eels with. "I don't cook by prayer, Mr. Vale."

Inez said, "Stepblue under a kettle gives you slow even heat. Not quick, not fashionable, but you can leave a widow's dinner on it while you mend a stocking and it won't take revenge. Since this morning it keeps answering to whatever's loudest outside."

Silas looked from the lamp to the window, where damp voices moved past in the lane. He could not blame draft or bad tin here. The kettle stand had not warped. The trouble was in the bed itself, in the way the color kept trying to climb into blush whenever a shout came down from the higher steps. Mrs. Rusk caught the spoon against the side of the pot and showed him the bottom. A black crescent of scorch ran across it like a grin.

"My boarders work the early ferry," she said. "If I feed them mush and cinder they'll eat it and still come back because men will chew anything with salt. But I lose the broth and I lose the lamp bed, and then I lose the next day's fuel because it takes more to set another tray right. So when people tell me a pretty name is good for the district, I hope they choke on it before supper."

She turned back to the kettle. That was all the attention he was going to get from her, and more than he deserved. Inez did not gild it with commentary. She waited until he had seen enough of the room's damp walls, the patched sleeve hanging to dry over the chair back, the narrow shelf with three potatoes on it and no fourth, and then she led him out again.

On the landing, he shifted the warped tray to his other hand. The wire handle had printed itself into his palm. Pink slime was drying tacky on his cuff.

"How many house names?" he asked.

Inez kept walking. "Depends what you're asking the bed to do."

"That many?"

"More."

"Patent Row would go blind trying to sell more."

"Patent Row doesn't carry boiling water down stairs."

It was not a clever line. That was why it stayed with him. She was never trying to win the exchange the way he won exchanges. She was moving through facts and leaving him either to keep up or to look foolish in public. It was a nastier kind of authority than charm. More expensive too.

The next landing held no room at all but a worktable jammed under the public rail lamp, half under the eave and half in the weather. A woman sat there with a clove bud tucked into one cheek, mending cuffs by public light. She had short practical fingers and a way of biting thread only at the side of her mouth, as if she distrusted the center. On the table before her lay two cuffs, one trimmed for mourning and one for ordinary work, and under the spoiled cast of the lamp both ribbons had sunk into nearly the same bruised dark.

"Bess Quill," Inez said.

Bess lifted the cuffs between finger and thumb. "Pick the widow."

Silas stepped nearer. In clean light it would have been a child's errand. Here the black and the deep river blue had both gone to plum. He pointed to the cuff in her right hand.

"That's the clerk," Bess said. "Widow sent the black one back with spit on the note and asked whether I meant to mourn her husband in warehouse trim."

The clove shifted from one cheek to the other. Bess jerked her chin at the lamp. "Stand between that and my table."

He looked at Inez. She only watched him. On the run above them two girls tried bridelight again for sport, and the rail lamp warmed another dirty shade. Bess tapped the tabletop once with her thimble. "There, Mr. Vale. Unless you'd rather keep me blind."

He moved. His coat and shoulders cut the rosy spill. In the weak daylight slipping under the eave, the colors pulled apart at once: funeral black and river blue, no kin at all. Bess retied the paper tags, then held the widow's cuff up near his face so he could see what he'd missed.

"That's two coppers docked," she said. "And a widow thinking I'm careless with the dead. Stay there till I finish the seam."

He stayed. The warped tray dragged at his arm. Pink slime dried tacky on his cuff. By the time Bess flicked two fingers for dismissal, the heat in his shoulder had worked down to his palm, and he had stood where she put him without a joke to soften it.

Below them the houses grew closer to Sump Ward in their smell, more ferment in the walls, more wet burlap, more living green where brick ought to have been clean. The tray in his hand had cooled under the eave, then twitched warm again as a group of boys somewhere above started testing his word for sport. The city was taking the name up too fast and worrying it for amusement. That morning he would have called that learning. The distinction was losing ground now.

"Do you know what angers me?" Inez said, not looking at him.

He almost said the list could not be short enough for a landing. Instead he said, "Tell me."

"I'm not angry because you named a thing in public. The city does that all day. Patent Row greed doesn't shock me either; if I waited for that, I'd never sleep." She took the last three steps of a run without touching the rail. "It's that you people talk like use is a stain. Like the best thing a word can do is shine."

He tightened his grip on the tray handle. "You don't know what I talk like."

"I heard you last night from two districts away."

That shut him up more effectively than a slap might have. The insult was not that she had heard him. It was that of course she had. Of course the performance he thought belonged to Patent Row had crossed the city already, entered kitchens, ridden ferry bells, gone under doors. He had always known words traveled. He had not had to smell where they slept once they got there.

The last house sat half a level below the rail, reached by two wet boards and a threshold worn hollow at the center. A child had chalked a line of uneven stars beside the door, and someone older had tried to wash them off without the heart to finish it. Inside, a woman with two copper nails holding her hair in place was bent over a school satchel spread on the table. Beside her sat a girl of about nine sorting buttons by size into a cough-drop tin, serious as a clerk at inventory. One knee of her stocking had been darned in green thread so bright it might have come off a flag.

"Nora," Inez said.

The woman straightened. One of the copper nails slipped and she pushed it back through her hair with the side of her wrist. "If you've brought me another tray I haven't got room for it."

"I've brought the man whose mouth is in them."

Nora's eyes moved to Silas and stayed there a beat longer than was comfortable. Then she snorted once, not with amusement but recognition of a nuisance she had previously only met at a distance.

"Fine," she said. "He can watch, then. Ettie, keep sorting."

The girl did not look up. "They go odd then even if I do the holes first."

"Then do the holes first."

On the table, next to the satchel, a squat kettle sat over a low lamp bed whose light should have been blue and was not quite anything. Blue at the core, pink feathering through the edges, enough to make the room look fevered. A strip of leather for the satchel strap softened near the kettle in a dish of tallow.

Nora touched the kettle handle with one quick knuckle and hissed. "It ran cool all breakfast. Now it keeps jumping. I can't tell whether I'm warming glue or branding the table."

Inez moved closer, studying the bed. "Who has been saying what on this landing?"

"Everybody." Nora gave a sharp laugh. "A paper boy came through twice. One woman from Gilt Crescent came down just to gawp and ask if she looked washed out in the pink. Then the boys started parroting her."

Ettie finally looked up at Silas. She had a freckle at the tip of her nose and an expression of grave suspicion that would have suited a magistrate. "Did you make the bad name?"

Silas had been called worse by better people. The question still caught under his ribs.

"I made one name," he said.

"It's a bad one." She dropped a button in the tin and returned to her sorting. "It lies about heat."

He had no answer ready for that. This was the true obscenity of children. They could sometimes say the exact thing and leave a grown man's fine language standing outside in the rain.

From the stair beyond the window came the slap of quick feet and then a stretched street cry, raw with ambition and too loud for the close walls.

"Bridelight takes the upper lamps! Bridelight, latest sheet, bridelight!"

The boy outside repeated it because selling required repetition. Two voices behind him mocked it back. Then another. Public enough. More than enough.

The lamp on Nora's table answered before anyone in the room did. Pink rushed through the bed in a hot wet seam. The kettle gave a little metallic tick as the heat climbed. Nora swore. Inez moved. Silas saw both motions and did not understand either in time.

Ettie, still intent on her buttons, reached for the kettle ear by habit, meaning only to slide it aside so she could set the tin closer to the light. Under stepblue the ear would have been merely warm. Under the sudden blush it was a branded strip of metal.

She yelped high and hard and snatched her hand back. Two fingers, first and middle, flared red at once where the skin had caught. The cough-drop tin hit the floor and buttons scattered under the table like teeth.

Nora grabbed the child around the waist. Silas caught the wobbling kettle with the edge of his sleeve before it could overturn and pour over the satchel, his own palm kissing enough heat through the cloth to make him swear. Inez had already seized the basin from beside the washstand and thrust Ettie's hand into the cool water.

"Hold her," she snapped.

Nora did. Ettie screamed once more, then bit it down into sobbing breaths. The nails in Nora's hair shook with the force of her grip. Inez kept the child's fingers under water, speaking now in that same exact public voice she had used on the landing, only lower.

"Stay there. That's right. Don't pull it out yet. Nora, you have goosefat?"

"A scrape."

"Fetch it. Clean rag too."

Nora lurched toward the shelf. Silas stood stupidly with the kettle still in his hand, the heat of it coming through sleeve and skin, and understood that he was holding a household object like a bomb he'd laid himself.

Ettie's face had gone blotchy with pain. She stared at her own hand in the basin as if insulted by it. Buttons glittered across the floorboards. One had rolled into the chalk stars by the door. Silas set the kettle down before he dropped it and crouched, not because he knew what to do but because standing over a hurt child in a room this small felt obscene.

"I didn't know," he said, and even to himself it sounded thin, a line spoken after the audience had already gone home.

Inez did not look at him. "No. You didn't."

Nora came back with a crock and a rag torn from some cleaner garment than anything else in the room. Inez lifted Ettie's hand from the basin. Water ran down the child's wrist in bright beads. The pads of the first two fingers were already whitening at the center where the burn would blister worst. It was the household kind of hurt: too small for the papers, mean enough to foul the rest of the day and the next one too. Inez smeared the fat gently over the red skin while Ettie sucked air between her teeth and tried not to cry again.

"Looks ugly," Inez said. "It'll heal if she leaves it alone."

"She won't," Nora said automatically.

"I know."

That, more than the burn itself, turned something in the room. They were women who already knew the shape of one another's next complaint. This was not incident. It was maintenance, pain folded into the day's work and handled because no one was coming with medals for it. Silas had sold names into rooms like this without ever having to sit in one while they bled.

Ettie sniffed. "I hate that name."

Nora brushed the damp hair off her forehead. "You're allowed."

Inez tied the rag lightly around the two fingers and only then looked at Silas. Nothing in her face had softened. It had simply become more precise.

"Tell me again it's publicity," she said.

He looked at the bandage. At the scattered buttons. At the kettle ear that had done exactly what metal did when heat lied. His own palm throbbed where the sleeve had failed him, a small mean pulse he was embarrassed to feel in the same room as the child's pain.

"It isn't," he said.

The words sat there. Bare. Useless for repair, but at least not false.

Inez waited, perhaps for the joke that usually followed his honesty like a dog after a butcher. None came. He found he was too tired of hearing himself from a distance.

"What do you call it," he asked, "when it burns steady and doesn't lie?"

Nora gave him a baffled look, as if this were the first sensible question he had offered all day. Ettie, already calmer now that the first bite of pain had passed, resumed glaring at the lamp as if she meant to discipline it by eye.

Inez wiped her fingers on her wet apron. Goosefat and river water shone on her knuckles. "That depends," she said.

"On what?"

She held his gaze long enough for him to understand that the answer would cost him more than curiosity.

"On the room," she said. "On the hands using it. On whether a body needs light, heat, warning, or all three. That's the part you people never bother to hear. You want one good word you can print big. We use the one that gets us through the task in front of us."

Outside, farther up the landing now, the paper boy's cry thinned into distance. The lamp on Nora's table cooled by degrees. Blue worked back into the pink, cautious and resentful, like water reclaiming dye from cloth.

Silas looked at it until the colors stopped fighting long enough for him to see the room by it: the damp patched wall, the satchel strap, the child's bandaged fingers held away from her body with offended care. Then he looked at Inez, at the old burn scar on her wrist, at the steadiness with which she stood in a house not hers and was nonetheless obeyed before anyone thought to ask why.

He would have liked to call her furious and be done with it. That would have been easy, only a mood he could have weathered. It would have asked nothing of him but endurance. What stood in front of him was worse: the kind of competence that could indict a man without raising its voice and make his whole trade look like a disease of decorative rich fools.

He set the warped tray down by the door. Pink slime had dried in a crescent on his cuff. His palm carried the hot memory of the kettle through the cloth. Somewhere above them, on the steps between Patent Row and the river, people were still trying his word in their mouths because it amused them.

When he spoke again, there was no performance left in it.

"All right," he said. "I hear you."

Inez's expression did not change. It did not need to. She had not dragged him from the steps to win an argument. She had dragged him to put him in the same room as consequence and make him breathe it.

"Good," she said. "Now try learning faster than the city talks."

He went back into the wet air with the stink of goosefat and hot tin on him and Inez's last line following him harder than the tray handle had.

Lantern Steps emptied him toward Ferrymouth, and the word had already gotten there ahead of him.
