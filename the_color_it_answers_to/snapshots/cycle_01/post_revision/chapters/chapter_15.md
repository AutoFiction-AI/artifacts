# Chapter 15

The kitchen hit Silas before Junie did. Broth, damp burlap, onion skins, and that sweet-metal stink the Celadon phase left on every hot surface in town met him at the door as the first ferry bell struck the dark apart. Junie had three kettles going on a fire that should have had no business holding them. The branded fuel burned pretty and thin. It gave off a pale aristocratic glow that looked costly on a hotel balcony and behaved like an insult under a soup pot. She had wedged the cheapest coal she could still bully on credit under the grate.

He came in from the yard with his sleeves rolled and his hair still wet from the pump. He had scrubbed at himself hard enough to raise a rash along his throat and still carried the city's slime in the seams of his hands. Paint used to live there, fine blue, vermilion, lampblack, the honest dirt of his trade. Now the creases held pale gold gum and the gray fuzz of dead moss roots.

Junie did not look up at him right away. She was shaving burnt mash from the underside of a kettle with the back of a spoon, counting under her breath the way she did when numbers were all that kept rage from turning theatrical.

"Three sacks of onions," she said. "Two pounds of eel ends. Half a brick of coal I had to kiss Pell's clerk for, and that was with my mouth shut. Morning."

"Morning to you too."

"Take the bread before Otis wakes and sees it."

He took the heel from the table. It was yesterday's rye, the crust gone rubbery in the damp. He ate it standing. Across the room the chalkboard tab list had been wiped and rewritten so often the wood looked furred. Half the names on it were ferry hands who used to pay on Fridays and now paid whenever the light held steady enough for night loading. Junie had started adding little marks beside the worst of them, not hearts and not crosses, just a slanted knife-stroke that meant she was keeping score with the future.

She turned then, her hair pinned up with two skewers because she could never find a clean hairpin at this hour, the old split in her eyebrow silver in the stoveglow. Flour dust lived in that brow no matter how hard the city tried to coat everybody in moss.

"You're going to Lantern Steps," she said.

He swallowed. "Am I."

"You are if you plan to keep eating here."

"I thought I'd help with service."

"You thought wrong. I can use a pot boy. I can't use you haunting my stove like a bad debt with boots."

Junie left the order plain and brisk, without a sermon, and that made it hit harder. He set the bread down, suddenly too dry to finish.

"Who asked for me?"

"Nobody." She scraped the kettle once more, hard enough to ring metal. "That's the point. They asked for backs. Hands. Somebody who can haul bad trays and not die on the planks. Aunt Vero said she was short two bodies. I said I had one body I was tired of looking at."

"She said yes to that?"

"She said, 'If he talks like Patent Row, drown him shallow.' I took that for consent."

Despite himself, the corner of his mouth twitched. Junie saw it and cut it off with a look sharp enough to pare apples.

"Don't you start acting grateful because somebody found a funny way to insult you," she said. "Go useful. That's all."

He reached automatically for the rag hung by the sink, then remembered the weakness in his right wrist only when the rag twisted and pain ran up the forearm like a hot wire under skin. The sprain had settled into him, not dramatic anymore, just mean. It made fine brushwork stutter. It made a heavy pail slip at the wrong second. It had become one more small fact about his ruin, too boring to earn sympathy.

Junie saw that too. She saw everything in a room where food moved.

"Wrap it after the first load, not before," she said. "If you baby it, they'll smell the excuse on you."

"I wasn't offering one."

"Good. Save your breath. The Steps don't run on speeches."

She set a cloth packet on the table. It held bread, an onion, and a wedge of hard cheese that had gone slick at the corners. Beside it she dropped a pair of eel-hide gloves, stiff with dried salt.

"Those were Tomas Brigg's. He left them after a storm and then left this earth without coming back for them. They still fit a liar."

"Junie."

"What."

He had not known, walking in, what shape the morning might take. Some part of him had kept a fool's little shelf inside, a dry place where apology might be enough for one room, where suffering visibly could count as amendment, where the loss of the shop might begin earning him a softer face from someone. Junie did not even let him unpack the hope.

"If I go down there," he said, "I'm not asking anybody to forgive me."

"You'd better not. Forgiveness doesn't scrub a tray or cool a lamp. Keep up, keep your feet under you, and if one of the girls tells you to shut your mouth, shut it before she has to repeat herself."

He picked up the packet and the gloves. They smelled faintly of river fish and tobacco. As he turned for the door, she called after him.

"Silas."

He stopped.

She was already back at the kettles, shoulders broad under a washed-thin work dress, one hand on the ladle as if it were a tiller.

"If you see Inez, don't go giving her your face like it's a job she forgot to finish."

He stood with the cold morning off the yard pressing against his back.

"I wasn't planning to."

"Good," Junie said. "Then today might stay about work."

Outside, Ferrymouth was lifting itself awake in coughs and knocks and rope slap. The river smelled cooler than it had a week ago, though the city still sweated. A fish cart went by with its boards wet red. Two newsboys were arguing over a bundle of old sheets whose headlines had already gone damp and useless. Across the lane, a branded Celadon poster had bubbled off the wall in the night, leaving a white-faced lady with one eye missing and a strip of paste full of flies.

Silas tucked the food under his arm and walked south.

Patent Row would have been waking too, clerks unlatching shutters, paste boys smoothing fresh lies over yesterday's lies, men who still had shops standing arranging their brushes as if the world admired care. He did not turn that way. He went down toward the low planks and the rank sweet breath of the ward, where the city kept the work that did not count as display even when it saved everybody's supper.

***

At the base of Lantern Steps a handcart had been turned into a tool table. Hooks and scrapers lay beside gloves, rags boiled to colorless strings, buckets of ash, and baskets lined with wet sacking. People were already taking what they needed. Nobody looked at him with surprise. If Junie had sent word ahead, it had been in the plainest possible language, and the lack of ceremony stung worse than an introduction would have.

Aunt Vero stood on the cart shaft, making herself taller by three inches and therefore impossible. Her thick white braid had been looped over one shoulder. Her broad hands were already stained green-brown up to the wrists, and the eye that drifted when she was tired had gone lazy with the hour. She wore a saint's medal tucked into the front of her dress and a man's old leather belt with two knives and a lump hammer hanging from it. The hammer handle had been wrapped in yellow prayer ribbon, dirty enough that only the knots still looked holy.

She glanced at Silas once, taking his measure in the same sweep she used for tools.

"You're late by useful standards and early by vanity standards," she said. "Take a hook."

"Morning, Aunt Vero."

"If you call me that before noon, I will put you in the cut up to your ears."

He took a hook.

Beside the cart a woman about Junie's age but harder in the face was rolling her sleeves over forearms thick as bread loaves. A dead pocket watch hung pinned to the front of her apron, the crystal cracked into a silver spiderweb. She caught Silas looking and rapped the watch twice with a thumbnail. He knew the warning inside the gesture: look once, then get your hands moving.

"It quit the day my husband had the decency to drown," she said. "I keep it because I liked the chain. Cora Nix. You're with me till I get sick of seeing you."

Before he could answer, a girl slid down the handcart wheel and landed in the mud like she had been born with knees made for impact. She was sixteen, maybe. Her face was narrow, her black braid tucked through the back of a boy's cap, and one boot sole was cinched on with copper wire. A school primer stuck from her waistband, pages swelling from damp. She plucked it free, shoved it deeper, and squinted up at Silas with frank delight that had nothing kind in it. He had once been good at turning that kind of stare into a joke. This morning he let it sit.

"This him?" she asked Cora. "The crown bastard?"

"Pru," Cora said.

"What. He knows what he did."

Pru looked at Silas another second, then shrugged as if he were less interesting than advertised. "If he falls in, I've got first laugh."

On the other side of the cart a boy of fourteen or so was sorting nails from a coffee tin and rolling them over his knuckles like cards. His lashes were so fair they vanished unless he blinked. A strip of bright green scarf had been tied around his head to keep his hair out of his eyes, and the cloth had once been finer than anything that ought to end up on a labor crew. He noticed Silas noticing.

"My sister wore it for church," he said. "Now I wear it for filth. That's economy. Nico Perret."

"Enough courtship," Aunt Vero said. "We are not here to make friends with the man. We are here because every fool with a mouth helped turn the beds gold and now the city can't boil water. Steps first. Drain after. Cora, take him on the rail lamps. Nico, with Pru on the wash carts. If he talks too much, give him the heavy bucket." Every fool with a mouth included him, and he let the words stay where they landed.

"I can hear you all right here," Silas said.

Pru grinned. "Then save us the trouble."

They moved.

The Steps had never liked him. Even in better days, when he came through with money in his pocket and a line ready for every face, the place had always made a city man of his sort feel clumsy. The stair flights rose sharp and damp between houses pressed so close their wash lines crossed like arguments overhead. Mosslight clung to rails and landings in sick pale veils, pretty from a distance, treacherous under the hand. Local feet knew where to place themselves. Silas had to watch every board.

Cora walked up with a crate on one shoulder as if it were folded linen. "Rail cages first," she said. "The gold crust is keeping heat where hands go. Kids are blistering palms."

She showed him once, not slowly. He had to slide the scraper under the skin of growth, lift before it tore, shake the gummy pale fronds into the ash bucket, then wipe the iron with salt water so the old bed beneath could breathe. The trick was the angle. Too shallow and the scraper skated. Too deep and he gouged the older growth that still had a chance of answering to something useful.

He gouged the first one.

Cora slapped the back of his wrist, not hard but exact. "You shave a cheek like that, fine. You shave a lamp like that, somebody eats dark."

"Right."

"Don't say right. Do it right."

He tried again. The second cut held. Gold slime folded back off the iron with a sound like wet paper peeled from a wall.

Above them Pru was on the next landing, boots planted wide on slick boards, popping open cage lids and singing spelling words under her breath as she worked.

"Commerce," she said, scraping. "Com-mer-ce. Insolent. In-sol-ent. Catastrophe." She looked down at Silas. "That one's for you. School says say the hard ones twice."

"School says a lot of things."

"Yes," she said. "That's why I go."

Nico came past with a yoke over his shoulders and two slopping buckets of spent mash hanging from it. He had a smoker's cough without having earned it yet. "Don't mix the hot scrap with the cool scrap," he called. "Cora'll chew you."

"It all looks like hell to me."

"That's because you're looking with your face," Cora said. She pointed with her scraper. "Cool scrap there. See the flat blue under the gold? That's step-blue once we get the Celadon scum off it. Railings, landings, school stairs. Burns cool, holds a while."

"And the stringy green?" Silas asked before he could stop himself.

Pru answered from above, because of course she did. "Pot-green if you've got sense. Ferry kitchens, wash boilers, anybody who needs heat more than show. It smokes if you crowd it."

Nico thumped his buckets down and jerked his chin toward a narrow crate lined with burlap. "And that thin sad stuff goes wakelight. Mrs. Basco's sitting with her mother tonight on Marrow Street. Wakelight stays humble. Doesn't spit. Doesn't flare when people cry near it."

Silas looked from one bucket to the next. Under the pale rot the fronds were kin to one another, with the same slick flesh and the same fine hair at the edges, but Cora had already built three futures out of them and Pru was barking a fourth from the landing.

"You can tell that by looking?"

Cora snorted. "No. We tell it by using. Looking's what rich men do before they buy the wrong thing."

He bent back to work.

The morning turned into an argument between his body and every object in reach. The scraper handle raised a soft blister at the base of his thumb. The bad wrist lit up each time a cage stuck and had to be yanked free. Pale slime coated the knees of his trousers and dried there like old glue. Twice he planted his boot too flat on the plank and felt himself sliding toward the open side before some stripped-down animal part of him found balance. Cora never grabbed him. She only said, "Toes first," in the tone a person used for weather facts and stubborn children.

People crossed around them all morning carrying the city's practical life in pieces. A woman with a basket of eels and curl papers in her hair stopped long enough to complain that her back room still smelled like expensive lamp oil and gave Cora two lemons in exchange for a handful of cleaned step-blue. An old man in a cooper's apron brought over cracked wick cages and spent ten minutes arguing about whether his daughter's shop needed pot-green or dock green, because the barrels were stored half inside, half out, and the moss behaved differently if the river breeze got ideas. Nobody called any of this magic. Nobody even called it strange. They called it work and kept moving.

By the fourth landing Silas had quit trying to be entertaining, and once his mouth stopped scrabbling for room, he began to hear properly.

Pru did not just sort moss. She sorted words with the same quick contempt for waste. When a younger girl from down the alley brought up a tray and called all of it cooklight, Pru corrected her without looking up.

"No. Cooklight's what hotel fools say when they want the color and not the stove. Pot-green if you mean the kitchen. Say the thing you need."

The little girl nodded once, repeated it, and took the tray on.

Nico, meanwhile, had a whole private devotion to ferry bells. Every hour he tried to whistle the note sequence from the upriver boats and failed on the last turn, which irritated him enough to keep practicing. Between loads he used chalk to sketch little iron bridges on the side of the ash bucket and then rubbed them out with his thumb before anyone could accuse him of loafing.

At one point Cora paused to cough into a rag scented with orange peel. When the fit passed, she tucked the rag back into her apron and said to no one in particular, "If my lodger steals one more pickled pear, I'm marrying him off to a widow with seven children."

"Which widow?" Pru asked.

"The loud one with the bad feet."

"That doesn't narrow it."

Silas laughed before he remembered he was not here to belong. Cora looked at him sidelong.

"Careful," she said. "You'll think yourself company."

By midday they had cleared the upper rail lamps and moved down to the wash court below the Steps, where contaminated trays had been stacked in ranks against a wall. Here the heat got mean. A nearby ferment room pumped out a steady breath of yeast, hot copper, and pond rot. Flies clothed the rim of a runoff barrel. The court bricks sweated underfoot. On one side women were boiling rags in lye. On the other, three boys younger than Nico were shaving dead gold growth off pipe joints with oyster knives.

Aunt Vero sat on an overturned crate eating bread and raw onion, her belt knives dark with slime. When Silas came for his packet, she did not offer him a seat or tell him to leave. It was the closest thing to neutrality he had earned from her in months.

He leaned against the wall and opened Junie's cloth. The cheese had gone softer in the heat, and the smell of it turned his stomach with a quick clean shame because she had cut it out of her own margin. He ate it anyway.

Aunt Vero said, "You drag the scraper like a priest blessing a sick goat."

"I've improved."

"From disgraceful to merely slow."

He chewed. "You're in a generous mood."

She took a bite of onion, eyes watering instantly, and smiled with all the warmth of a blade laid in sun. "No. I'm in a hungry one."

Across the court, Nico was showing Pru a trick with two bent nails and a washer, making the washer jump from one nail to the other with a flick of the wrist. Pru pretended not to be impressed, which fooled nobody. Silas watched them with the stupid envy of a man who had spent years mistaking patter for company.

"You teach him that?" Silas asked Vero.

"What, foolishness? The river teaches that free."

She watched the pair a moment longer. "His sister used to sing in chapel. Voice like a clean dish. Fever took her in June. He kept the scarf because he can't keep a song."

Silas looked away before pity made a spectacle of itself on his face.

From the lye pots came the voice of another older woman he had not yet worked beside, a heavyset laundress with two silver hair combs and a laugh that started low and climbed until it broke. She was wringing out rags with hands red from soap.

"Pru," she called, "if your mother asks, tell her I'm keeping the blue for her stairs and not for that idiot boarder she lets smoke indoors."

"She can ask it herself, Miss Dela."

"I would, but then I'd have to hear her answer."

The court laughed, not because the line was exquisite but because labor needed a place to put its breath.

Silas drank from the pump and let the iron taste sit on his tongue. The old itch arrived then, mean and familiar. Listening like this, hearing the names move hand to hand, he caught himself sorting them the way he used to sort a client's hunger. Step-blue landed flat and safe in the mouth, like a stair under a boot. Wakelight sank low and stayed there. Pot-green came off the teeth ugly and useful. He could have built a whole new campaign from that if he'd still been the man who thought theft improved when it was artful.

He put the heel of the bread between his teeth and bit down until the thought lost some of its shine.

Aunt Vero did not look at him when she said, "If you're collecting again, I can still aim a hammer."

"I'm not."

"Good. Because if you were, I'd break the other wrist and save us all time."

He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "You always this tender after lunch?"

"Only with family and fools."

When the break ended, the real ugly work started.

The trays had to be stripped, boiled, resanded, and reset. None of it looked dramatic, but the work kept coming in the same blunt order: lift, scrape, dump, rinse, stack. Then they did it again. The pale-gold Celadon skin came off in translucent curls that clung to arms and necks and made everybody shine sickly. Underneath, surviving beds showed their old mixed colors in bruised patches, blue at one edge, green at the root, a wet blush where the city still remembered the earlier name and hated itself for it. Silas worked beside Pru and one of the younger boys from the pipe crew, passing tray after tray to the boiling vats.

"Not that pile," Pru said for the tenth time. "That's dock green."

"Why is that one dock green and not pot-green? It came off the same fucking wall."

She did not stop working. "Because it's going to a rope shed. Smoke, tar, open boards. Dock green doesn't mind a draft and won't die if gull shit lands on it. Pot-green throws hotter. You put pot-green in a rope shed, it'll sweat itself stupid."

The boy beside her, ears sticking out under wet hair, added, "And step-blue goes to stairs because drunks fall cleaner when they can see."

"Thank you, theologian," Pru said.

He grinned. "My ma says I've got a gift."

Silas took the next tray and held it until Pru jabbed her scraper toward the correct pile. He set dock green by the rope-shed stack, pot-green by the kitchen carts, step-blue beside the school rail crate, and wakelight under wet burlap for Marrow Street. After a while the names stopped jostling in his head like rivals for a headline. They behaved more like routes, the shortest way to get the right heat or light into the right hands before dark.

***

Late in the afternoon, Cora sent him and Nico down to the drain cut with a wheelbarrow full of spoiled posters, rotten slats, and dead tray backing. The runoff there had gone thick with paste from ruined advertisements. Whole faces drifted in the muck: a lady's mouth, a gloved hand, the golden corner of the word Crown floating like a fish belly in brown water.

Nico pitched a bundle into the barrow and said, "You painted some of these, didn't you."

Silas did not bother lying. "Some."

"You were better at letters than the fellows now."

That hurt more than mockery would have. "Thank you, I think."

"I didn't say you were any good at judgment."

Nico shoved the barrow into motion. The green scarf at his head had come half loose. He retied it one-handed while walking, a trick that made Silas think again of sisters and graves and the cheap way grief kept dressing itself as ordinary habit.

At the cut they unloaded the rotten paper and sorted the wood. Most of it was too soaked to save. Some had enough sound grain left for patching catwalk edges or lining new trays. Silas picked up one long ferry-plank from the side of an old schedule board. The painted times had blurred to ghosts. Beneath them, older layers showed through where damp had peeled the top sheet away: a flour notice, an election slogan, half a sermon, chalk sums, somebody's obscene drawing of a landlord with a rat's tail. The board had spent years taking whatever a city wanted from it and had not yet fallen apart.

He turned it in his hands.

"Leave that if it splinters," Nico said. "Pru stuck a nail through her palm on one last week."

"It's sound."

"Everything's sound till you lean on it."

Silas set the plank aside with the keepers.

When they got back, the court was in the rich dirty hour before quitting, when fatigue stripped everybody to fact. Cora's dead watch swung against her apron as she hauled the last crate. Pru had gone hoarse from reciting and now mouthed the spellings silently while she worked. Aunt Vero was crouched over a tray with a bit of charcoal, marking its edge before passing it on.

Silas came closer. She had written three words in a rough practical hand: STAIRS, BASCO WAKE, KITCHEN SOUTH.

"You label every tray?" he asked.

"Only the ones I don't want idiots switching," she said.

"That's most of them."

"You're learning."

She passed the charcoal to Cora, who added NIX LANDING in letters broad enough for a drunk to read. Pru took the next bit of wood and scrawled SCHOOL RAIL. Nico, seeing a free surface, put BRIDGE SHED with a little bell mark next to it before Aunt Vero smacked the back of his head for decoration.

Silas stood there with the charcoal dust in the air and the heat standing off the vats and watched one piece of scrap carry five different hands without complaint.

The old hunger rushed him then, sudden and vivid. He could see Bellglass Bridge in his head, iron lit against dusk, a board slung up where everybody crossing had to look. He could see himself painting one perfect title across it in a sweep so clean the whole city would have to follow. He even felt, for one ugly bright second, the old delight of imagining the crowd turning because his hand had told it how.

The feeling was so familiar it nearly took him by the throat.

Then Pru said, "Don't put Basco Wake by the kitchen lot, for God's sake," and Cora answered, "I'm old, not blind," while Nico rubbed space clear on the tray edge to sneak his bell mark back in. Their voices crossed over STAIRS and BASCO WAKE and SCHOOL RAIL, and the whole vision of one beautiful commanding word turned rotten in his head. A single name would do what Celadon had done, only with better brushwork. It would take this mess of use and grief and errands and turn it into another stage.

He did not want a stage. He wanted a surface that could hold the mess and keep holding it. He wanted bad handwriting and crowded additions and names that could sit beside one another without killing each other for room. It would have to be a board, maybe several boards cut from ferry plank, with stout nails, paper backing where paper helped, blank strips where chalk could take over, and room in the middle and the margins both.

Infrastructure, Junie would call it if he brought her the idea dressed in an apron instead of vanity.

Aunt Vero was looking at him now, not kindly, not unkindly either. She was measuring him.

"What," she said.

He realized he had been staring at the marked tray as if it had started singing.

"Nothing," he said first, because the thought was still fragile and he did not trust his mouth around fresh things.

Vero waited.

He wiped his hand on his trouser thigh and tried again without performance. "Not nothing. I was thinking about a board."

"A coffin lid has the shape."

"Not that kind." He pointed at the tray edge, the layered labels, the charcoal shared hand to hand. "I mean a public one, wide enough for more than one name. It has to change. It has to keep anybody from winning the middle of it alone."

Pru had stopped scraping. Nico had not, though his ears had tilted toward them.

Aunt Vero's drifting eye came straight for a moment, the way it did when something surprised her into full attention.

"And whose pretty signature goes at the bottom?" she asked.

Aunt Vero laid the cut where it belonged. If he flinched from it, he deserved it.

He looked at the plank by the drain pile in his mind, all those dead notices and live chalks over one another.

"Nobody's," he said. "Or mine can go on the back where the woodworms can admire it."

Nico barked a laugh.

Pru said, "Make it broad, then. People in this ward spell sideways when they're tired."

Cora, still hauling, added, "And use a face cheap enough nobody cries when it gets written over."

Aunt Vero took the charcoal from the tray edge and pressed it into Silas's hand. Her palm was hot and rough as kiln brick.

"Bring me a scrap tomorrow and show me you're talking about a board, not redemption," she said. "If I smell redemption, I piss on it."

"Fair."

"Nothing fair in this city," she said. "Quit standing there and finish the last stack."

They worked until the light thinned and the air over the cut turned almost cool. By then his shoulders felt scooped out. The blister at his thumb had burst and gone flat. His back was slick with old sweat under clean sweat. He had not been thanked. He had not deserved to be. He had done one day's labor among people who would be back tomorrow whether or not a man like him learned anything from them.

On the way down from Lantern Steps he carried the salvaged ferry-plank on one shoulder. It was awkward as sin and smelled of river mud, paste, and old iron nails. Nobody offered to carry it for him. That suited him.

Ferrymouth was closing into evening by the time he reached Junie's yard. She was out back skimming fat into a crock for tomorrow, apron streaked, eyes mean with fatigue. Otis sat on an upturned crate nearby, tongue out over a copybook, printing letters with painful care while the last of the kitchen steam climbed around his ears.

Junie saw the plank first.

"You stealing lumber now?" she asked.

"Salvage."

"That's a longer word for stealing."

Otis looked up. "Can I draw on it?"

"Not yet," Silas said, then heard himself and amended it. "Soon, maybe."

He leaned the plank against the wall and stood there another second, feeling the day in his knees.

Junie dipped the spoon, blew across it, tasted, and frowned at the fuel's weak burn as if she could insult it back into usefulness.

"Well?" she said.

He could have told her he had kept up. He could have said they did not throw him in the cut. He could even, in a cheaper mood, have offered the board like a triumphant reveal, as if he were saying, Watch me matter again. Watch me come back useful through design. The shape of that performance was still in him. He knew all its joints.

Instead he went to the pump, washed his hands as clean as they would go, and came back for a bit of charcoal from Otis's copy slate.

On the plank's least-rotted face he did not write a name.

He marked out spaces.

The spaces were not elegant. He left room for a broad hand at the top, smaller strips down the sides, a patch left blank where paper could be pasted and scraped away, and another where chalk could take quick changes from stoop to ferry to kitchen. He measured with his thumb, not for beauty but for legibility at a run. He left margins big enough for arguments. He left the center unclaimed.

Otis crept closer, pencil forgotten. "What's it for?"

Silas kept working. The charcoal dragged over old paint ghosts and snagged on damp grain.

"For ferry changes, stove trouble, wake houses, busted stairs, whatever has to reach the next pair of hands before dark," he answered.

Junie watched him a long moment. The yard was full of evening sounds, pans cooling, gulls fighting over offal, a ferry rope slapping wood somewhere out in the dark. At last she nodded toward the board.

"Then make it stout," she said. "The city won't use it gently."

He looked at the empty middle, at the black ribs of space he had left for other hands, and for once did not imagine signing the corner.
