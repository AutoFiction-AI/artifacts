# Chapter 18

Junie's first lid-rattle and hatch argument was already rattling up through the floorboards when Silas opened his eyes. The window above her kitchen stood open on a slice of pear-colored morning; by early autumn the damp had stopped trying to smother the city and settled for living in its pockets, and cool air moved over his bare chest as he lay for a moment with one arm over his eyes and felt the ache in his right wrist before he felt anything else. Cold always found the old sprain first. That was one of the small jokes the body kept for itself.

Inez was already up. She stood in her shift and petticoat with one stocking on, tying her dark hair into the work scarf she favored when there was no reason to impress anyone and no point pretending there might be. Her oilskin ledger sat on the chair beside his rolled brushes. His tobacco tin rested on top of it as if the two objects had been born to share a surface. Her boots were under the bed with his, toes touching.

"You awake," she said, not looking around, "or are you planning to bill the morning for your thoughts?"

"Depends who the client is."

"The south ferry line. They pay in bad coffee and complaints."

"Then I'm fully alert."

That got him half a smile. She bent to pull on the second stocking, and the crescent burn scar inside her right wrist flashed pale against her skin before the scarf fell over it. He had learned the scar the way a man learned a favorite bend in a stair rail, by use rather than announcement. The knowledge sat deeper in him than half the names he had once sold for money.

"Captain Lusk wants the new board up before the noon traffic," Inez said. "Some fool scraped off the warning slat at Lantern Steps and now people are asking for pretty light where they need cook heat."

"A fool or a salesman."

"Same breed."

Silas pushed himself up. The blanket slid off his hips. The room was narrow enough that when he stood he had to turn sideways to reach his trousers hanging from a nail. It had started as a storage room for sacks of meal and had become, through a month of use and very little ceremony, a place where his shirts hung beside her spare scarf and his extra brushes dried in a jar next to her hairpins. Their life had gathered there in small practical heaps. He was grateful for that in ways he still did not say cleanly.

He crossed behind her to reach the washbasin and put his mouth against the back of her neck on the way. Her skin was cool from the window air. She answered with a low hum in her throat, enough to welcome the kiss at her neck and fence off everything lower, and caught his wrist before he could let his hand wander lower.

"Not with your fingers full of sleep grit," she said.

"Cruel woman."

"Wash."

He did. The water in the basin had gone cold enough to sting. When he straightened, he saw his own face in the little spotted mirror Junie had nailed crooked to the wall: dark hair flattened on one side, scar under the left ribs pale in the glass where the blanket had dragged low, the look of a man who had once made a profession of entering rooms at an angle and now mostly entered them carrying equipment. He liked this version less some days. He trusted him more.

From below came the ferry bell at the landing and Junie shouting at somebody to either sit down or stop leaning on her hatch. In Junie's voice the two commands carried equal affection.

Inez was wrapping the ledger in its oilskin. "Bring the thick brushes. The river's been chewing paint this week."

"If the river wants lettering, it can pay my old rates."

"Your old rates are dead."

"They are."

He said it without flinching and found he meant it. That was newer than the room, newer even than the way her scarf ended up over his chair most nights they worked late enough to stop lying about going separate ways.

Downstairs, Junie's kitchen was already loud. Steam filmed the front panes. The long counter wore its usual dents and knife scars, but the room had changed the way a face changed after illness: the false bloom burned off, the bones plain, every useful mark showing. The board that had waited on a chair the night before now hung over the pot shelf. It no longer offered a single proud lamp source in tidy script. It carried three columns in three hands. `Dock-green` in Junie's blunt chalk. `Pan-hold` in Inez's narrower block letters. `Tourists may call it crown if they enjoy eating in the dark` in Silas's own slanted paint, smaller than the rest because Junie had threatened to box his ear if he turned her breakfast wall into a performance.

Under the chalkboard a row of mismatched jars held moss cuttings with rag tags tied around their necks. Some tags named color. Some named use. One just said `Not for frying, ask first.` Another said `Lantern Steps calls this widow-blue in fog.` None of it was elegant. All of it was alive.

Junie stood at the stove with her sleeves rolled and her hair pinned up in a hurry that had let one dark coil escape against the healed split in her left eyebrow. She stirred the oat pot with one hand and, with the other, flicked a bit of fried onion from the cutting board onto the floor for the three-legged dock cat she denied feeding. The cat took it under a bench with priestly dignity. Junie caught Silas seeing it.

"You mention him and you're washing pans for a week," she said.

"I wouldn't dream of exposing your criminal softness."

"Eat."

She shoved two bowls across the counter, thick oat mash loosened with broth and topped with yesterday's eel scraps and pepper. The smell came up hot and rough and absolutely sufficient. At the far end of the room two ferry hands in wet caps were arguing over a bundle of glow cuttings one of them had bought cheap off a Patent Row cart.

"He said it was bridge-pearl," one of them complained, prodding the rag-wrapped bundle with obvious distrust.

"He said that because your face told him you'd pay extra for anything with a pearl in it," Junie said. "What do you need it for?"

"My girl wants a cleaner light in the room."

"Cleaner than what?"

"Than the green."

Junie shrugged. "Then call it cleaner if you like, but don't cook over it and don't trust a man selling lamp stock from a hatbox."

Silas blew on his spoon. The rag bundle had that slick nacre sheen Gideon had once paid good money to make fashionable, and for one ugly instant he could feel how easy the old sale would have been: give it some lady name, take the bit, let somebody else's potatoes char black in the pot. The mash tasted saltier after that.

The second ferry hand snorted into his bowl. "Told you."

"If your girl wants to sew by it, that's one thing," Inez said as she took her spoon. "If she wants to boil potatoes, tell her not to be stupid for the sake of color."

"She's not stupid," the first man said, wounded on behalf of somebody absent.

"Then she'll listen."

That settled it better than any kindness would have. The man nodded. Silas watched Inez eat standing up, one hip against the counter, speaking the way she always did when the matter was use and there was no profit in prettying it. He had spent half his life trying to make language glitter. She made it hold weight.

Otis was at the back table with his school satchel open and a stub of blue chalk in his hand. He had a permanent smear of dock dirt ground into one knee of his trousers, and he had threaded three punched brass ferry tokens onto a string that hung from his vest button like a little saint's charm for bad travel. He was copying letters off a broken warning slat, tongue caught between his teeth in concentration.

`KEEP DRY / DON'T STACK HOT`

His version had become `KEEP TRY / DON'T STARK HAT`.

Silas set his bowl down beside the boy's elbow. "That'll save lives in a way no one expects."

Otis grinned, the gap from his lost front tooth now mostly replaced by a new one that had come in crooked enough to look argumentative. "I can fix the hat."

"Leave it," Junie said. "Might improve the city."

Otis looked from one adult to the next, weighing the chance of indulgence. "Can I ride out with you?"

"You've got lessons," Junie said.

"Miss Berrick's got the chest cough again. We only do sums when she can breathe."

"That sounds like a lie."

"It sounds like Tuesday," Silas said.

Junie cut him a look that admitted he might be right and resented it. She ladled more mash into a waiting bowl for a carter at the hatch. "You'll ride if you keep clear of the winch and don't repeat any dock language in front of God or your teacher."

"Which God?"

"Any of them feeling sensitive."

The room laughed. Otis looked pleased with himself and then, because he was a child and could not leave a live wire alone, pointed with the blue chalk toward the window where a new handbill had been pasted over an old Verdancy poster.

"That man out there says the proper name list costs one bit and keeps you from embarrassment."

Silas turned. Outside on the landing, a neat little clerk in a glazed collar stood under the ferry notice board with a sheaf of folded pamphlets under his arm. `APPROVED NAMES OF THE AUTUMN STRAINS`, the cover declared in damp blue ink. Below it, in smaller print, `For Visitors, Investors, and Serious Householders.` Someone had already written `and fools` across the bottom in charcoal.

The clerk was doing brisk enough trade among two women dressed too cleanly for Ferrymouth and one warehouse runner who had probably mistaken the pamphlet for authority. The runner paid his bit, opened straight to the middle, and smoothed the damp page with a reverent thumb as if paper might keep him from sounding foolish on the upper stair. The sight put a sour little smile in Silas's mouth. One monopoly drowned and every tidy bastard with a clean collar came rowing after it with a bucket.

"You should buy one," Junie said. "Would save me hearing your guesses."

"If I spend a bit on proper names, I want a receipt fit to wipe with."

"You always did."

She slid a paper packet across the counter toward Inez without looking at her. Fried crusts for the ferry, wrapped in yesterday's scandal sheet.

"Bring the paper back if Pike wants her lies boiled into stock again," Junie said.

Inez tucked the packet under the oilskin ledger and crooked one finger toward the chalk tabs over the stove. "Mara Dace still owes you two jars and six coppers."

"She owes me teeth," Junie said. "The jars I'll take."

Silas finished his bowl and set it in the wash pan. On the shelf above it lay his working kit stripped to use: an oilcloth roll of brushes, two tins of paint, a bundle of rag, charcoal, hooks, and the little spirit level he had once used for window commissions in districts too rich to tolerate a crooked line. He took the roll down. The brush handles clicked softly together inside the cloth, familiar as knucklebones in a cup, and for once the day ahead felt sized to his hands.

When they stepped out into the morning the boards underfoot were slick, but not with summer's greasy sweat. Autumn had sharpened the damp instead of thickening it. The Sallow moved gray-green under the ferry slips, carrying strips of leaf and an orange peel and one stubborn floating handbill with Gideon Wren's old pale-gold crest bleeding into the water until it looked diseased.

Across Ferrymouth and up the first rise toward Lantern Steps, signs had begun to layer rather than replace. A boarding house board offered `ROOMS / NIGHT-BLUE LAMP / ask for stair-safe cuttings.` A chandler had left his official patent name in gold but allowed somebody to add, in red under it, `burns hot as a liar after midnight.` At the fish shed a practical stencil warned `don't stack dry moss by the boiler`, and some wit had added `unless you miss your eyebrows.` A carter hauling empty crates up the rise jabbed two fingers at the red addendum and shouted toward the chandler window, "You sell my cousin liar light again and you'll buy him new brows." The apprentice inside leaned out with a rag in one hand and yelled back, "Tell your cousin to stop boiling fish over parlor stock, then." The answer carried all the way to the slips.

Silas had once heard that kind of cross-shouting as rot in the brand. This morning it sounded like the city with its boots on.

Captain Lusk was waiting on the ferry with one foot on the ramp chain, smoking a clove stub down almost to her fingers. She was a narrow woman in a tar-black coat, already wearing hand-knit red ear flaps though the season had barely earned them, and she kept a lemon peel tucked into her collar for the river stink. She spat overboard, saw the board under Silas's arm, and nodded.

"About time," she said. "If one more fool asks me whether lower Lantern wants crownlight or widow-blue I'm throwing him in and letting the water sort his vocabulary."

"Morning to you too," Silas said.

"Morning's got nothing to do with it." Her eyes cut to Otis. "You keep your hands away from the paddle housing, boy, or I'll send your mother one finger at a time."

"Yes, Captain."

"And stop looking pleased. That wasn't a joke."

Otis immediately looked even more pleased.

The ferry was half loaded: market women with baskets under shawls, a carpenter with a door balanced on his shoulder, two upper-ridge ladies pretending not to smell the place, a barrel of lamp mash lashed by the rail, and the pamphlet clerk from the landing, who had apparently decided the best way to sell certainty was to chase it from stop to stop. He had his packet of proper names tucked under his arm like scripture.

Silas and Inez set the new board on two crates near the wheel post. It was cut from salvaged plank, the wood planed but not disguised. Along the top Silas had already laid out the landing name in charcoal: `LOWER LANTERN STEPS`. Under that, smaller, room for local additions. Under that, a blank strip for warnings and weather notes that could be changed without losing the whole board.

He uncapped the white paint. His wrist gave a brief hot complaint as he dipped the brush. Inez noticed because she noticed everything that mattered and too much that did not.

"Let me pull the long line," she said.

"I can manage a straight stroke."

"You can. You're just going to pay for it at supper."

"Then I'll take the left side and save the pain for your company."

"That's the ugliest flirtation you've tried this week."

"This week is young."

She took the mahl stick from him anyway. Their fingers slid against each other on the polished wood, paint tacky at the edges. He leaned close enough to smell the soap from her collar and the colder smell of the river trapped in the wool of her scarf. There was no audience here worth playing to, which made the work sweeter.

The ferry shoved off. The deck shuddered under them. Bells knocked once, ropes loosened, and Ferrymouth began to slide backward in a wet clatter of dishes, gulls, and dock curses.

Silas set his hand to the first letter and felt the old pleasure rise despite everything, that small sovereign thrill when line met surface clean and the world accepted correction. He still loved that. Losing the shop had not beaten it out of him. What had changed was the hunger behind it. He no longer needed the line to prove he was singular. He needed it to hold long enough for other hands to answer.

`LOWER LANTERN STEPS`

The letters went up in a durable cream meant to show in fog. Below them Inez penciled guide marks for the names people actually used.

`Needle Run in dry weather`

`Soapstep after wash overflow`

`Widow Stairs in fog`

Silas glanced down. "You'll make it look crowded."

"It's crowded."

"Aesthetically, I mean."

"That sickness again."

He laughed and painted the next line. She was right. The board looked busy already, and when she marked out another empty band at the bottom for warnings it threatened downright ungainliness. Ten months ago he would have fought for symmetry, for breathing room, for the arrogant beauty of a single answer clean in the eye. Now he only asked, "How much space do the wash women need?"

"More than you think. They change it by tide."

"Savages."

"You'll survive them."

The pamphlet clerk had edged nearer with that air certain men carried when they believed tidiness itself was a public service. He was younger up close than Silas had thought, with little damp curls plastered at his temples and a dragonfly stickpin in his collar that told on him harder than his shoes did. He watched the board fill up and failed to keep the wince off his face.

"Is that meant to help people?" he asked.

Inez kept marking lines. "That's the hope."

"Three names and a weather note?"

"Better that than one bad answer," she said.

The clerk frowned. "A stranger still won't know what to ask for."

That was old music. Patent Row, Gilt Crescent, investors with clean cuffs, Gideon in every respectable throat. Improve the mess. Standardize the appetite. Put one label where life kept breeding six. Silas felt the old clever answer stir, sharp and eager, and let Inez beat him to it.

"If widow-blue fails, somebody nearby has another name for the same damn thing," she said. "That's enough."

The carpenter balancing his door on one shoulder grunted agreement. One of the market women said, "My sister asked for widow-blue last week and got sold tourist pearl. Couldn't see her own doorstep by it."

"That's because your sister shops like she's in love," the other woman said.

"She's been married three times. Same skill."

The ferry laughed, including Captain Lusk at the wheel. Even the clerk smiled against his will, then caught himself. Otis, who had been reading over the man's elbow upside down, said, "You spelled Ferrymouth wrong on page three."

The clerk blanched and snatched the pamphlet shut. Silas looked over in time to see the truth of it. `Ferrymauth.` Incredible. He bit the inside of his cheek to keep from barking outright.

"Did I?" the clerk said thinly.

"Yes," Otis said. "Also you call this line Lower Lantern all through, but my mother says no one says that unless they're lost or charging extra."

"Your mother isn't an editor."

"Thank God," Junie's voice said from the ramp chain behind them.

She had stepped aboard at the last second with a slop bucket in one hand and a folded supply sheet in the other, because of course she had. Her kitchen never let her entirely leave it. A smear of flour rode one forearm and the dock cat's white hair clung to the hem of her skirt like evidence.

"He's right, though," she said. "If you're selling help, try spelling the place before you improve it."

The market women cackled. The clerk turned the color of boiled ham and retreated toward the upper-ridge ladies, who pretended they had not seen him. Silas let himself enjoy the man's discomfort for one beat and no more. The city would keep manufacturing him. The best you got was to spread the embarrassment around.

Junie passed Inez the supply sheet. "Six jars to Lantern, four to Soapstep if the wash house asks, and tell Mara Dace I won't extend her tab unless she stops feeding ferrymen on my biscuits."

"She says yours are too hard."

"Then she can chew slower."

She turned to Silas. "And don't get grand with the lettering. This isn't a theater bill."

"Nobody in my own family respects art."

"Not once it needs carrying."

She was off again almost before the sentence landed, stepping back onto the wet ramp while the ferry still moved near enough for it to be a terrible idea. Captain Lusk swore at her for form's sake and did not touch the wheel. Junie lifted one hand without looking back and vanished into the steam and hatch noise of the landing.

Silas watched the place where she had gone a moment longer than necessary. Through the gap between passengers he saw Junie hit the landing, catch the hatch rail with the slop bucket, and hand the supply sheet straight to a wash woman waiting there with two sacks of coal mash and a string of leeks. Junie thumbed the leeks, rejected one, bit it anyway, and shoved an extra heel of bread into the woman's apron before either of them could call the bargain kindness. It was breakfast bought half in coin and half in mutual bad temper.

The board took shape under his hands. He laid the cream letters clean, then the brick-red arrows, then the small black warning at the bottom:

`Fog name shifts by ward. Ask before buying home stock.`

Inez studied it, head tilted. Wind lifted the end of her scarf and slapped it once against his shoulder.

"Add one more line," she said.

"There's barely room."

"Make room."

"What am I adding?"

"`Not for nursery lamps.` Last week a woman at the lower stair singed a cradle quilt because some bastard told her pale light meant cool light."

He took up the smaller brush without argument. The cream letters were still handsome enough to tempt a fool, the brick-red arrows prettier than they had any right to be. He pinned the hard black warning under them anyway, plain enough that even a man in a hurry could not miss it unless he meant to.

Inez leaned in to blow a drip from the tail of the `y`. "You always make that letter vain."

"It's a handsome letter."

"It's a warning."

"Even warnings deserve beauty."

She gave him a look that would have gutted a weaker vanity. Then she put her thumb lightly against the side of his jaw, where he had missed a streak of cream near his ear, and wiped it away with the corner of her apron. The touch was brief, domestic as a match strike, and it altered the morning more thoroughly than any declaration could have. Silas turned his head enough to kiss the inside of her wrist where the burn scar curved.

"You're getting paint on me," she said, too even.

"You're already carrying worse."

The corner of her mouth twitched. Three strangers kept arguing over lamp stock ten feet away and neither of them moved. With them, that counted as ease.

They hung the finished board at the forward shelter on iron hooks. Captain Lusk came down from the wheel long enough to inspect it, clove smoke and lemon and river on her coat.

"Needs one thing," she said.

"Here we go," Silas murmured.

"You forgot the tide mark. When it floods the bottom stair disappears and half the idiots from the ridge try stepping onto water."

Inez nodded. "She's right."

"Of course she's right," Silas said. "This city breeds correctness like mildew."

He took the black paint again and added, on the last free sliver at the bottom:

`At high water the third plank lies. Step wide.`

The cramped black sentence spoiled the composition and improved the board. He could see the old version of himself arguing for cleaner lines while some ridge fool in good boots stepped straight into floodwater because the sign had been too pretty to tell the truth.

Captain Lusk read it once, satisfied. "That'll keep at least two men alive through November."

"Only two?" Otis said.

"I don't aim greedy."

By the time they reached the middle of the crossing the sun had climbed enough to catch the patched moss growth on the ferry rail. It had taken root where damp rope rubbed the wood and where too many hands had rested while waiting to land. The strip had gone banded since summer: old bridge-green at the base, a paler gold left over from Gideon's season of forced beauty, then little runs of blue and rose where the chorus boards had done their patient damage to certainty. In the slant autumn light it looked less like one thing than a quarrel held together by slime.

One market woman rapped the rail with a knuckle. "Wasn't that stripe gold a minute ago?"

The carpenter bent around his door to squint. Even one of the upper-ridge ladies leaned closer before she remembered the tar and recoiled from it.

The pamphlet clerk, chastened but not cured, had opened one of his copies again. He stared from the rail moss to the new board and back with the strained expression of a man discovering that reality had poor respect for indexes. The upper-ridge lady nearest him said, "What is it now?"

The question ran across the deck and through Silas like a memory of appetite. Once he would have stepped into that half-second before everybody else and sold them the answer. Now he wanted the answer to arrive crowded.

Answers came at once.

"Bridge-green."

"Not on a ferry."

"Rope bloom."

"That's what my uncle says when he's drunk."

"Widow spit."

"Only in fog."

The names crossed, mocked, corrected each other. No one won. The old instinct still twitched in him, eager to seize the loose bundle and twist it into something marketable, but it no longer felt like genius. It felt like theft with better posture. He kept both hands in his coat pockets and let the city do its own bad work.

Otis had climbed onto a coiled hawser for a better look. The wind put color in his face. He squinted at the moss with grave schoolroom attention, as if preparing to recite a lesson he had not actually been assigned.

"It looks like when pumpkin skin gets on your cuff and you wipe at it with spit," he announced.

There was a beat of silence. Then Captain Lusk barked a laugh sharp enough to startle the gulls.

"Pumpkin spit," she said.

One market woman snapped her fingers at once. "Pumpkin spit. That's it."

The second said, "Ugly little thing. It does."

The carpenter, door still on his shoulder, tried it in his mouth like a coin. "Pumpkin-spit light won't sell in the parlor."

Captain Lusk cupped her hands toward the bow. "Hoy, deck boy. Pumpkin spit on the rail."

The boy looked up from his coil. "Looks more copper than pumpkin."

"Then say copper-spit and keep working."

"Copper-spit sounds mean."

"Everything sounds mean in your mouth."

The upper-ridge lady tried both under her breath as if either might stain her gloves. Silas felt the old clean click when a name stopped belonging to the mouth that first let it out. Otis had tossed the thing into the air; the ferry had already bitten pieces off it.

The laughter went around again, looser this time, and while it did the little rail patch answered. The answer came modestly; the city had had enough theater for one season. Along the pale-gold band there came, before all of them and plain as breath on glass, a thin wet seam of orange deepening toward rust where the morning sun hit hardest. The color carried rust in it. Nacre Bend had learned better than purity. Pumpkin skin under spit was near enough.

Otis saw it first and nearly fell off the hawser.

"It moved."

"Hold still," Inez said automatically, one hand already at the back of his coat though the shift had nothing to do with his balance. She looked at the rail, then at Silas, and recognition crossed her face edged with tired amusement, the look of a woman seeing tomorrow's maintenance arrive while today's work was still wet.

"Well," she said. "Now we'll have fools asking for that by supper."

"If they read the board first, they'll at least ruin supper on purpose," Silas said.

Complaint lived in it, braided tight with interest. He knew her well enough now to hear the life inside the irritation. The clerk looked down at his printed list, found no help there, and put the pamphlet away.

Silas rested his forearms on the rail beside Inez's. The ferry kept up its heavy practical shudder beneath them, each knock traveling through wet timber into his bones. Damp tar chilled through his sleeves, and Junie's grease packet pressed warm against his pocket.

He rubbed his thumb against the callus where the brush always sat and felt dried cream paint flake there. Once that spot had glittered with gilt dust from shop windows, proof of work meant to flatter rich men. Now it held cheap paint and the grit of honest use. He stayed at the rail and knew which trade he'd rather answer for.

Otis was still staring at the rail as if he had discovered weather and been personally vindicated by it. "Do I get to keep it?" he asked.

"No," Inez said.

"Absolutely not," Silas said.

"What if everybody says it?"

"Then it's everybody's mistake," Silas said.

Otis considered this and, because he was his mother's child and not Silas's despite long evidence to the contrary, seemed satisfied by the fairness of it.

Silas felt a mean little pride at that. Ten months ago he might have taught the boy to plant a flag in the word and charge admission. The thought made him shift the brush roll higher on his shoulder.

They came in toward Lantern Steps. The lower landing showed itself in layers: steep stone rising out of damp, laundry flapping over alleys, one old official sign half scraped away, two chorus boards nailed over it at different heights, a widow in a red shawl standing on the second stair with a basket of cuttings and shouting contradictory prices depending on who looked richest. Above her, on a wall still carrying the ghost of `CELADON CROWN` under newer paint, somebody had added in green:

`ASK WHAT IT'S FOR`

Under that, in another hand:

`AND ASK WHO TOLD YOU`

The ferry bumped the pilings. Ropes flew. Captain Lusk barked orders. Passengers gathered their baskets and door and dignity. One of the market women was already leaning over the rail toward the widow in the red shawl.

"You selling pumpkin spit or copper-spit today?"

"For stairs or for soup?" the widow shouted back.

"Soup."

"Then not copper, and if you scorch the pot don't come whining at me."

The upper-ridge lady repeated that exchange under her breath as if studying for an examination. The clerk started to unfold a pamphlet, heard the deck boy yell `copper-spit` at a hauler on the pier, and stuffed the papers back under his arm. The orange seam on the rail brightened once in the angle of the sun and then blurred back into the quarrel of colors that had made it.

Inez picked up the paint tins. Silas took the brush roll and the board hook. Their shoulders knocked as they moved toward the ramp, neither of them giving way because both knew exactly how much room the other needed.

"You left space on the bottom," she said.

"For the tide mark?"

"For the next argument."

He looked at the board hanging fresh against the shelter, cream letters drying above the empty sliver still waiting for whatever Lantern Steps would insist on calling itself in rain, in fog, in grief, in market weather, in somebody else's joke by nightfall.

"Good," he said.

Otis hopped down after them, tokens clinking against his vest, already testing variants under his breath. "Pumpkin spit. Copper spit. Pumpkin starve. No, that's bad."

"They're all bad," Inez said.

"Leave them bad," Silas said.

She snorted and took his free hand for three steps on the wet ramp before letting go to save a stranger from carrying nursery light home for supper. By then three more people had the new name in their mouths and two of them were improving on it, and the little strip of moss on the ferry rail, sly as any city worth living in, was trying to answer to all of them at once.
