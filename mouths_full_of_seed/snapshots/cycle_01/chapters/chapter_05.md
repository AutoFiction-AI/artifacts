# Chapter 5

By the time they poled down to Hymn Cut, the singing Daro had heard under Tovin's floorboards had worked itself into his teeth. Every knock of the ferry against a half-hidden post set the note off again. It was not a clean melody. It had grit in it, silt under the tongue, the drag of something caught where water should have run free. He could not tell whether the sound was coming up from the cut ahead or from the shame the last night had cracked open in him.

Hymn Cut sat on a low mud rise hardly bigger than a yard, with the shrine built up on pilings no thicker than wrists. Small bronze bells hung from the eaves and from two leaning posts that marked the narrow channel mouth. Cots had been shoved under an awning on the inland side where pilgrims waited out the flood: a woman with a funeral jar in her lap and her hair coming down from its pins, an old man in a coat shiny with old rain, a boy whose left foot was wrapped in oilcloth and twine. Smoke from a cook fire kept failing in the wet air. The whole place smelled of mud, lamp fat, boiled reeds, and the sweeter high note of oranges somebody stubborn had refused to surrender to monsoon rot.

That last smell belonged to Ilyan Moor. Daro knew it before he saw him.

Ilyan stood under the awning with one hand on a bell post worn smooth by years of finding it in the dark. He was tall enough that the patched shoulder of his shrine robe brushed the hanging bells. His blind eyes were the same unsettling milk as always, but his face had thinned since Daro had last sung a funeral here three years ago. He held a strip of dried orange peel between finger and thumb and kept worrying the rind until the oil came up fresh. A novice girl with bitten nails and a sash she would not stop retying hovered beside him with a kettle.

"Less water in the broth this time, Fen," Ilyan said to the girl before the boat even touched the landing. His voice crossed the cut like warm iron. "Pilgrims aren't geese."

"It was thin once," the girl muttered.

"Once is enough to become a habit."

Riva set the pole and brought them in harder than she needed to. Mud sucked at the hull. Iven jumped first and caught the post with a flourish that would have been comic if his eyes had not kept flicking to the cots, counting witnesses. Lale stepped off with the ledger wrapped in oilskin under one arm. Nami stayed crouched in the bow for a moment, staring at the bells as if she expected one to turn toward her like a bird.

Daro rose last. The note in his teeth sharpened. He could feel the channel beneath the shrine before he saw it: a seam in the floodwater where the brown surface kept dimpling inward, taking tiny breaths.

Ilyan turned his blind face toward the sound of Daro's boots on the planks.

"Come up, son," he said.

No one moved. Even the novice stopped tying and retying her sash.

Daro's first stupid thought was that Ilyan had mistaken him for somebody else's grief. Shrine keepers called strangers son, daughter, love, poor thing, whatever got them over the threshold. But Ilyan did not talk that way. He chose words the way some people set hooks.

Riva said, "Say that again."

Ilyan's mouth curved, not kindly. "I don't repeat myself for effect."

The old man on the cot raised his head. The woman with the funeral jar looked openly from Daro to Ilyan and back again, already smelling blood or miracle, maybe both.

Iven let out a breath through his nose. "Well. That's one way to welcome people."

Lale's gaze landed on Daro's face with ledger-quick calculation. Daro hated that she was always good at chronology when he himself felt suddenly made of noise.

"We're here for the rite," Riva said. "If you've got theatrics to spend, spend them after."

"You're here for the rite because you need me," Ilyan said. He tipped his head toward the novice. "Wash basin."

Fen brought out a shallow copper pan cloudy with herb water. Ilyan remained where he was, fingers still on the orange peel, as if he had all day and they had stepped into it rather than the other way around.

"Hymn Cut doesn't answer muddy mouths," he said. "Hands too. Your sister should approve of that."

Riva's jaw worked once. She hated being managed by anybody who smelled of incense. But she shoved up her sleeves and plunged her hands into the pan. Reed grit floated off her knuckles in gray curls. One by one the rest of them washed. When Daro bent over the basin, the bitter water smelled of mint gone rank in the rain. He lifted his head and found Ilyan listening to the drops slide off his fingers.

"You came hearing it already," Ilyan said quietly.

Daro should have denied it. Instead he said, "It's loud enough to hear from Tovin's floor."

"Good."

The single word sat in him like a swallowed coin.

Riva was done waiting. "Explain the cut."

Ilyan passed the orange peel from one hand to the other. "Reed Gate was labor. Iron, mud, debt of the back and shoulder. Hymn Cut is want. The part your mother made profitable."

Nami's head came up. The bells gave a faint dry chatter overhead though there was almost no wind.

"We can talk inside," Lale said. "Without an audience."

"No," said Ilyan.

That got Daro's eyes on him again. Ilyan's blind gaze remained fixed somewhere just left of Daro's throat, unnervingly precise.

"The lie had witnesses," Ilyan said. "The truth can have them too."

He turned away then, expecting to be followed. They followed. Of course they did. The shrine had only one room open to the cut and one smaller back chamber curtained off with a reed mat. Along the walls stood votive shelves blackened by lamp smoke, a row of prayer bowls, a heap of rolled sleeping mats, and hooks for bell ropes. The cots outside put every sound in common. There was no such thing as private speech here unless you wanted to go whisper in the mud with the eels.

* * *

Fen moved to bank the cook fire and kept glancing in through the doorway. Daro noticed her left shoe had been patched with a strip of waxed sailcloth. The woman with the jar shifted closer on her cot. Pilgrims had spent generations training themselves to hear through walls when shrines started telling the truth.

Ilyan took his place by the channel-side opening where the cut breathed below. "The second rite is simple enough to say plain," he said. "Somebody speaks aloud how Sereya used desire as leverage instead of blessing. Then the one the cut answers to sings."

Riva laughed once, without humor. "That's your plain version?"

"It is."

"And if we refuse the public confession piece?"

"Then you keep dragging your dead backward."

Iven looked toward the cots. "You've got mourners out there."

"Yes." Ilyan folded the orange peel in half until it cracked and the smell brightened again. "They've paid to be here. Let them learn what they kneel to."

Daro should have hated him then. Instead he felt something ugly and eager uncoil in his stomach. The shrine was full. The bells were listening. Outside, rain made a soft hard curtain over the awning. Every person waiting to hear their dead move on would hear his family broken open first. Shame and stage had always lived in adjoining rooms for him. His mother had taught him that before he'd had enough beard to resent it.

Riva looked as if she'd rather take an oar to the bell posts and chance the flood. But she had the whole route pressing between her shoulder blades now, and Daro could see her measuring outrage against drowned bodies and doing the ugly arithmetic.

"Fine," she said. "You want it said, it gets said. Then we move."

Ilyan inclined his head toward her. "Begin."

She did not. She looked at Lale instead, because when something needed cutting clean, Lale's hand usually reached the knife first.

Lale tucked the wrapped ledger tighter under her arm as if paperwork could keep her dry here. "Sereya called it blessing when people gave her labor after she touched them," she said. Her voice was even, almost inland, until the last two words roughened. "That was the bargain she liked best. Not coin. Access. She found out what somebody wanted from her and made the wanting do work."

From the cots outside came the scrape of somebody shifting closer.

Riva picked it up, blunt as a mallet. "If she wanted a gate dredged, she'd spend a night making a man feel singled out, then ask him for six days in the mud. If she wanted a ferry mended, she'd lean on the right rail with the right woman and make the job sound like proof of devotion. People hauled and patched and watched flood nights because they wanted another turn in her room."

The woman with the funeral jar made a sound halfway between a laugh and a spit. Nobody hushed her.

Iven said, "Let's stop polishing it. She fucked people into service."

Riva's head snapped toward him, but he was already going on, fast now that the blood was in it.

"Not every time. Sometimes a look did it. Sometimes a hand on the neck. Sometimes she'd let them think they were the only one she'd ever let through the door when there were three more waiting on the walk. She liked people holy and stupid at the same time. Made them easier to aim."

"Iven," Nami said.

"What? You want me to lie prettier?"

Nami hugged her own elbows. Rainlight flattened the bones of her face. "No. I just." She swallowed and looked toward the cots, then back to the floor. "I remember the smell after. Smoke and river and skin. People would come out of her room crying, but not like something bad had happened. Like they'd been scraped open where they wanted it. I thought that's what saints did. I thought holy meant somebody could make you feel chosen and ashamed together and you'd still come back."

Silence took the room for a moment. Fen, outside by the fire, had stopped moving entirely.

Daro felt the note in his teeth change. It deepened, not louder but nearer.

Ilyan said, "And you?"

The words went straight to Daro, no place to duck. He tried to laugh them off and could not. He was too old for the old escape routes and too young in the wrong parts of himself for anything wiser.

"She used my voice before I knew what it was worth," he said.

There. The room had it. He had not meant to start so naked.

"At funerals," he went on, hearing the velvet come into his own speech and hating that he was dressing the wound even now, "if a widow wouldn't open her purse, my mother would push me forward first. Pretty little song, pretty little grief, let the boy make her cry and then she'll pay for a second hymn. When pilgrims came hot for blessing, she'd sit me by the lamp and tell me to sing the room softer so they'd mistake their own hunger for revelation."

He tasted old lamp smoke, old cheap wine on Sereya's mouth, the grease from roasted eel on the fingers she'd once put under his chin and used to angle his face toward paying eyes.

"If I sang well," he said, lower now, "she kissed my forehead with somebody else's perfume still wet on her. If I sang badly, she said I was sulking the river closed."

Outside, the old man on the cot said, very clearly, "Fuck."

The word should have broken the charge. Instead it made the room more true.

Ilyan let the silence sit. He was good at that, good enough that Daro understood all at once why people had once crossed floodwater for the man's voice alone.

Then Ilyan said, "She did not invent the trade alone."

Riva stared at him. "That's your contribution?"

"My contribution," Ilyan said, and now there was something colder in the sonorous rhythm, "is that I wrote hunger into doctrine because I liked hearing it sung back to me. Sereya and I made a rite from appetite and called it discernment. She used desire as leverage. I used sanctity to hide how much I enjoyed being used by her."

No one in the room had anything to say to that. Not for a few heartbeats.

The bells outside shook once, all of them together.

It was not wind. Daro knew wind. This was the cut taking in breath through its own throat.

The woman with the jar rose from her cot and came to the doorway. She had a wart on one thumb and a mourning scarf pinned crooked under one ear. "Is that enough truth for your river?" she asked. Her voice was rough from crying too long or smoking too much. "Because I've got a husband souring by the hour and I'd rather not spend the day learning saints are common."

Ilyan turned his blind face toward her and gave the faintest bow. "Stay, Mera. You'll hear the answer."

So he knew them all, Daro thought. Even here. Even blind.

He should have felt stripped raw beyond repair. Instead he felt the room narrow toward him. The humiliation had not emptied him. It had made a shape. The cut was listening differently now. He could hear the brown floodwater worrying at the pilings, the minute clinks of the bells settling, the breath of the people outside holding and holding.

Ilyan lifted one hand toward the channel mouth. "Now sing it open."

Riva said, "Tell him what he needs."

"He already knows."

Daro almost snapped back at that. He did not know. He knew funerals, river cant, the way to lean a room toward grief or up out of it. He knew how to make widowers feel briefly less alone and how to take too much pleasure in being the thing they looked at while they broke. That was not the same as knowing how to sing a whole cut open under his father's ear. If that word even belonged to him. Son.

But the bells had gone still again, waiting like held breath, and every face in and outside the shrine had turned toward him.

There were worse drugs than attention. There were holier ones. Sometimes they were the same thing.

He stepped to the channel-side opening.

* * *

Hymn Cut was no grand sluice like Reed Gate. It was narrower and meaner. Two short rows of posts marked the edges where the old channel had been lined once, and below the mud rise the floodwater kept worrying at the half-sealed seam, trying to remember where to go. Bell ropes ran from the shrine hooks down to the posts so the bronze could answer current or hand or song, depending on what woke first.

Riva and Iven went out onto the wet walk to catch the mooring lines. Practical hands, necessary hands. Lale took the ledger from under her arm and set it on the shelf farthest from spray, as if even now she could not quite trust the river not to revise them. Nami brought a lamp to the opening and crouched with it, eyes too bright, face set in that still way she got when she was one push from volunteering herself to anything that looked like meaning.

The pilgrims stood if they could stand. Mera hugged her jar to her ribs. Fen came in from the fire with broth forgotten entirely, her lower lip bloodless between her teeth. Outside, the rain thinned to a hiss.

Ilyan moved until he stood close enough for Daro to smell the orange oil and old wool in his robe. He did not touch him at first.

"Don't imitate me," he said.

Daro laughed once, startled into it. "That was the plan."

"Then it's a bad one." Ilyan's mouth barely shifted. "My voice belongs to this bank because I gave it forty years and both eyes. The cut doesn't want a copy. It wants the wound you brought."

There it was again, the maddening ease with which the man said things that sounded like doctrine and insult at once.

"You could be clearer," Daro said.

"No. I could be more decorative."

Iven barked a short laugh from the walk. "He's definitely kin."

That landed harder than the joke deserved.

Ilyan raised his hand then and laid two fingers against Daro's throat, just above the notch of bone. The contact was light. It still felt indecent. Daro had been touched in a hundred public ways over the years by mourners, lovers, drunk patrons, his own siblings dragging him by the sleeve. None of them did what this did. Ilyan's fingertips were callused at the pads from instrument strings long gone. He rested them there and listened.

"Don't push from the mouth," he said softly. "Let the note lean up from lower. You've got the shelf for it."

Daro's own hand almost came up to cover Ilyan's. He stopped himself before the movement became visible.

Shelf. The word was absurdly intimate. Not lungs, not chest, not throat. Something shaped. Something inherited.

Riva said from outside, "If we're blessing him, do it fast."

Daro looked out at her. She had braced one boot against the slippery walk and both hands on the wet rope, forearms roped hard with effort. She was built for weight, for strain, for the kinds of proof that bit into palm and shoulder. None of that could help him now. He loved her for it and resented her for the same reason.

"I don't need blessing," he said.

"Good," said Ilyan. "Sing."

Daro drew breath. Mud smell. Lamp smoke. Orange oil. Wet reed. The cut's under-song rubbed along the inside of his ribs. He let the first note out low enough that it barely seemed like singing at all, more the body remembering a door before the hand found the latch.

The bells did nothing.

Heat climbed his neck. He heard Iven shift on the walk, heard Mera's jar knock once against her bangles. He could already imagine Riva's face if he failed: not mockery, worse, the quick practical recalculation of how to do without him.

He closed his eyes, not for mysticism, just to get the crowd off him for one breath.

Sereya came back at once. Not as saint. As appetite in a wet dress, laughing with eel grease on her wrist, shoving him forward toward a room full of mourners and telling him, sweeter, darling, sweeter, they're not open yet. The memory should have sickened him. It did and did not. That was the trouble. There had been love in her use of him sometimes. Or enough skillful counterfeit that a child could not separate the two without years to ruin it.

He opened his mouth again and gave the cut the thing he had tried not to hand it all morning: not clean hymn, not trained funeral smoothness, but the raw note under the performance, the one that wanted to be singular because singular things got kept.

This time the nearest bell answered.

Not a ring. A small struck tremor. Then the post on the opposite side shivered and its bell answered lower. The sound climbed the ropes like something alive feeling along a fence.

Daro did not stop. He took the opening phrase of the old Hymn Cut descent and bent it, roughened it, let the river-cant syllables scrape where Ilyan would have polished. He sang of passage, yes, but also of unsealing, of mouths opening not to praise but to release what had lodged and festered. On the third line his voice found the seam in the water's own breath and slipped into it.

The flood below the shrine changed shape.

The half-sealed surface that had kept dimpling inward all morning suddenly dropped a hand's breadth between the posts. Brown water drew hard toward the seam. Old petals, ash, a scrap of blue mourning cloth, three drowned beetles, and a skin of lamp oil all spun once and vanished down the narrow opening. The bells began ringing in no pattern human hands would choose, answering note with current with note.

Someone cried out. Fen, Daro thought, but he could not look.

He sang louder.

Now the channel took the sound and threw it back at him altered, deeper under the bank. The shrine boards thrummed under his feet. Mera started sobbing without trying not to. The old man who had said fuck whispered the refrain on the wrong pitch, then found it on the next pass. Nami joined softly, not to lead, just because the sound had caught her by the sternum and pulled. Even Iven, out on the walk, gave the last three words back under his breath like somebody testing whether mockery and prayer could share a mouth.

And the people were looking at Daro.

Not past him to Ilyan. Not around him to the moving water. At him.

He felt it with an accuracy more bodily than sight: the way a room leans when its center changes. It came up his shins through the boards. It settled hot along the back of his tongue. He pushed into the refrain again and the bells answered so hard the smallest one snapped its rusted loop and dropped, clanging along the wet planks before rolling against Nami's knee.

Riva swore and hauled the mooring line as the boat jerked with the new pull. "Hold it there!"

Iven laughed, wild with effort. "It's holding us, captain."

The cut had opened. Not wide, not yet settled, but open enough that the backward pressure eased under the shrine with an audible sucking groan. Water that had been bunching inland side now drew seaward through the seam in a hungry brown rush. Somewhere downstream something heavy thudded free.

Daro should have stopped. The rite had answered. Instead he let himself take one more phrase, then another, because the answering did not feel like completion. It felt like appetite meeting appetite and calling it covenant.

When he finally let the last note go, the room stayed full of it. Bells rang smaller and smaller until only the far post still chimed at intervals, as if a hand below water kept flicking it.

No one spoke at first.

Then Mera stepped up to him with the funeral jar clutched to her chest and, before Daro could move, pressed two wet fingers to the sleeve over his forearm and touched the same mud to her own lips.

"For luck," she said hoarsely.

It was not a shrine rite he knew. It was simply something she wanted from him.

Others moved after that. The old man with the shiny coat reached as if to grab Daro's wrist, thought better of it, and instead bowed his head so low his nose nearly hit Daro's knuckles. Fen stared openly, broth forgotten on the shelf, her face young enough that awe looked almost indecent there.

Behind them, Ilyan stood very still.

Riva came in from the walk wiping mud down the side of her trousers. She had water up to mid-calf and a rope burn fresh across one palm. She looked from the now-breathing cut to the cluster of bodies around Daro, and something in her face changed so slightly anybody else might have missed it. It was not surrender. Riva did not surrender. It was the cold arrival of a fact she did not know how to get leverage on.

She could outwork a gate. She could not outwork a room turning toward her brother.

"Move back," she said to the pilgrims, not harsh, not gentle. "The bank's still soft."

They moved because the bank was soft. They did not move because she had said it. Daro knew the difference. So did she.

Ilyan spoke over the scrape of boots and bells. "The cut will settle if no fool overloads it by sunset. One ferry at a time till morning. Keep weight centered."

Practical again. Useful again. Daro ought to have been grateful for the return to terms he understood. Instead the practical instructions felt like lesser music after what had just happened.

Riva was already resetting into task. "Iven, check the stern lash. Nami, get that fallen bell off the boards before somebody busts a toe. Lale, mark the time."

Lale did, though she was still looking at Daro with an expression he could not read cleanly. Not envy. Not pity. Possibly the two had rubbed together into something meaner.

Daro stepped back from the opening at last. His legs felt wrong, as if the channel had replaced some private fluid in him with its own colder rush. Ilyan touched his elbow, just once.

"Back room," he said.

Riva turned. "No."

Ilyan did not raise his voice. He did not need to. "Your brother's throat is raw. I've got willow bark and better sense than whatever stew you people call aftercare."

"I'm fine," Daro said automatically.

He was not fine. He was lit in places that should have stayed hidden.

Ilyan tilted his head. "Even worse. Come."

The command should have soured him. It did not. Daro glanced once at Riva. She looked like she wanted to block the doorway with her body. But the pilgrims were still inching closer, the boat needed resetting, and the cut they had just opened could still punish stupidity before nightfall.

"Two minutes," she said.

"You don't own my throat."

"No," Riva said. "But I'm still the one getting you to the next bank alive."

Daro almost smiled. There she was. Rope, weight, consequence. Blessedly familiar.

"Two minutes," he said, and followed Ilyan behind the reed mat.

* * *

The back chamber was smaller than Daro expected. Shrines always sounded larger than they were because people brought their dead into them. Here there was only a narrow cot, a shelf of wrapped books, a cracked green bowl with orange peels drying in it, and a peg with Ilyan's spare robe hanging from it, one elbow darned in black thread neat enough to shame most wives. The air smelled of damp wool, citrus, and the bitter medicine tang of willow bark steeping.

Ilyan felt for the stool and sat. He did it without groping, counting the room from muscle memory. Then he reached for a cup on the shelf, found it first try, and poured from a small kettle into two clay mugs.

"Fen overboils everything," he said. "She's convinced heat fixes weakness. It doesn't."

Daro took the mug because his hand needed occupation. The willow tea was too hot. It tasted of bark and old pennies.

"If this is where you tell me you call everybody son when the weather turns holy," he said, "save it."

Ilyan's face did something almost like weariness. "I call plenty of people son. Not you."

The mug clicked against Daro's tooth.

Outside the mat, he could hear Riva's boots, Iven's quicker lighter tread, a bell being rehung, the low murmur of pilgrims already converting what they'd heard into future versions. Privacy here was only a thinner kind of public.

"Then say it clean," Daro said.

Ilyan folded his long hands around his own mug. "I fathered you."

There it was. No incense around it. No hymn.

Daro looked at the man's face as if sight could do what sound already had. Tall spare body. Rich voice. Blind scarred eyes. Nothing in it resembled the cheap easy fantasies boys built when they wanted a father to arrive late and make sense of them. Ilyan was too old, too proud, too implicated. Which made the sentence harder to dismiss.

"My mother had lovers like flood years have mosquitoes," Daro said. "You'll forgive me if I ask for more than a line and a mood."

"You should ask." Ilyan took a drink. "She came here the year the east bank cracked. Before the shrine was finished. She'd spent the day dragging a corpse ferry through water up to her thighs. She had mud drying behind her knees and enough river in her hair to salt a pan. She said the hymn I kept for this cut was too pure, too cautious, and that the dead would never trust a road that sounded ashamed of the body."

Despite himself, Daro smiled. It was a cruelly recognizable thing for Sereya to have said.

"We argued for two nights," Ilyan went on. "On the third she stopped arguing. A month later she told me she'd quickened. Another month and she told me the child belonged to the river, not to me, because men grow proprietorial when given nouns like father."

"She wasn't wrong."

"No." Ilyan's mouth tightened. "She often wasn't. That didn't make her merciful."

Rain ticked against the roof harder for a moment and softened again.

"Why now?" Daro asked.

"Because you're thirty-five and the dead are climbing back upstream and I'm tired of keeping faith with silences that served only her." Ilyan set the mug down. "Because when you sang today, the cut answered in the same break your voice had at sixteen, at twenty, at twenty-nine. Do you think I didn't know? You've sung funerals here often enough. I knew before I had the courage to insult us both with certainty."

Daro felt a strange ugly relief at the insult in that. Not romance, then. Not clean yearning. Recognition arriving by increments, hampered by pride. More believable. More dangerous.

"You could've said something before."

"To what end?" Ilyan asked. "Sereya would've denied it. Then she'd have sung the denial prettier than I could argue. Later you stayed away. Later still I let myself enjoy being only the shrine keeper when you came through, because fathers are judged by a more pitiless choir than singers."

That landed. Daro had not come here often because Ilyan made him feel measured in registers he could not control. Now he had to wonder what exactly had been measuring him all along.

"And today changed that?"

"Today changed the cost of silence." Ilyan leaned back, fingers finding the seam in the mug's cracked glaze. "Listen to the bank."

Daro did. Outside, the cut had settled into a different sound than before: no longer the clogged muttering he had heard on approach, but a narrower running draw, purposeful, almost elegant. He could hear people still moving around it with the caution reserved for something that had just proved itself.

"Your sister can move iron and people with the same pair of hands," Ilyan said. "Useful gift. Necessary. But when floodwater climbs into a town's kitchen and puts its mouth on the sleeping, labor is not the only thing people go looking for. They look for a voice. A body to point at. Somebody who seems answered."

Daro should have thrown the tea in his face for that. He heard the lure in it exactly because it was pitched to the rot he hid best.

"Tovin said the route wasn't meant for one heir."

"Tovin believes maintenance is a moral system." There was fond contempt in Ilyan's tone, which made Daro think it was old and earned. "She isn't wholly wrong. Sereya spread the work. She spread everything. Work, beds, children, blame. Distribution is not the same as center."

He let that sit.

"Crowds don't kneel to a committee," he said at last.

The sentence passed through Daro like a blade that had been warmed before cutting.

Outside the mat, somebody asked for the singer. Not Ilyan. The singer.

Daro's skin prickled under his damp shirt where Mera's mud had dried in two fingertip marks.

"What do you want from me?" he asked.

Ilyan gave a short sound that might once have been a laugh. "More honesty than your mother got. Less than priests demand. I want you not to pretend the river answered you by accident. I want you to know what blood you came from before others explain you to yourself in meaner words. And yes," he added, too precise now to be casual, "I would like Hymn Cut remembered through something other than my own old mouth when I am under mud."

There. Pride. Loneliness. Relevance hunger. It made the offer uglier and therefore easier to trust.

Daro ran his thumb around the clay mug's rim. "And if the route belongs to all of us?"

"Belongs is a trade word. Bad one." Ilyan turned his face slightly as if listening to Daro's breathing. "Ask a better question. Ask who the river makes legible when it needs a face."

Riva slapped the doorpost outside hard enough to rattle the hanging spoon. "Time."

Ilyan did not move. "Of course it is."

Daro set the mug down. His hand was shaking a little, not with sentiment but with the effort of holding several incompatible hungers upright at once. He wanted to laugh. He wanted to walk back out and tell Riva everything just to watch the clean practical misery of her response. He wanted to keep it wholly to himself until it had ripened into power.

"Does anyone else know?" he asked.

"Not from me."

"Did you ever tell her you'd claim me?"

Ilyan's blind eyes remained unreadable, but the answer came fast enough. "Yes."

"And?"

"She said I liked inheritance because I was too vain for service."

Daro let out a breath that might have been amusement if there had been less ache in it. "Also not wrong."

"No." Ilyan's mouth curved then, the first expression all day that felt stripped of performance. "You do see how we're kin."

That nearly undid him.

Riva knocked again. "Daro."

He pushed back the reed mat and stepped out.

The shrine room had changed while he was inside. Not physically. Socially. That was the more volatile architecture. Mera stood waiting by the opening with her jar hugged close and two other pilgrims near her. Fen had finally remembered the broth and was ladling it into cups, but she kept glancing up at Daro with the stunned concentration of somebody who had just discovered a story in the flesh and had not yet learned how to distrust it. Nami sat on her heels rewinding the broken bell's cord around the post, humming under her breath what Daro had sung. Iven had found a way to look amused and watchful at once, which meant he was scared. Lale was rewrapping the ledger, movements exact to the point of violence.

Riva stood by the doorway with one hand on the pole. Mud had dried in a fan across her jaw. She looked from Daro to the pilgrims and back again. No one had to explain the geometry of the room to her. She saw it instantly.

Mera stepped forward. "Singer. If my husband catches the road by nightfall, will the cut hold him?"

Riva opened her mouth, probably to answer on schedule and weight and current. Daro heard himself speak first.

"If they don't crowd it and nobody gets foolish with freight, yes," he said. "By tomorrow it'll take him clean."

Mera nodded as if he had blessed her. She touched the jar to her forehead and stepped back.

Another mourner started asking something about fees, about order, about whether the saint's son would be here tomorrow. Daro did not catch the whole question because the phrase saint's son had already gone through him and lodged.

Riva's face gave him the smallest flinch. Not surprise. Recognition. She had heard it too.

He knew exactly what she was seeing: not just a successful rite, not just a channel opened, but a room full of people willing to build meaning around him faster than she could nail planks over it. Labor could answer floodwater. It could not stop mouths once they had found a shape they liked.

"We're leaving," she said.

Not loud. Final.

Iven hopped onto the stern and muttered, "Before he starts charging for touch."

"Shut up," Lale said, with enough edge to mean she was not remotely amused.

Nami looked between Daro and Riva with naked curiosity, smelling a change she could not yet name.

Daro followed them out to the boat. The bank had already begun to soften where too many feet had gathered. Pilgrims called thanks after him, or his name, or nothing coherent at all. One woman bowed. Fen, from the fire, lifted a cup of broth in awkward salute and immediately looked embarrassed by herself.

Ilyan remained under the awning, one hand on the bell post, orange peel bright in his fingers. He did not call after Daro. He did not need to. The truth he'd handed over was already aboard.

Riva shoved off hard. The hull dragged free of the mud with a wet sucking sound and swung into the newly obedient pull of the cut. For a while nobody said much. That was the family at its most dangerous, when speech had not yet decided what shape to take.

Behind them Hymn Cut kept ringing in smaller intervals as the current settled. Daro stood in the stern and let the opened channel's pull run up the pole into his palms. The marks Mera had left on his sleeve had dried dark. He rubbed them once with his thumb and did not wipe them away.

Riva was the first to speak. She kept her eyes on the water.

"Whatever he told you in there," she said, "don't let it slow your hands."

Not don't believe him. Not what did he tell you. She had chosen her ground already: work, movement, survival.

Daro looked at her broad back, the scar on her chin pale under drying mud, the way she set her weight against the pole as if the world could still be argued into obedience through correct force.

"My hands are fine," he said.

"Good."

That was all. But he heard the strain in the word. Not fear exactly. Something more offended than that. As if the river had introduced a kind of authority into the family economy that refused to be audited.

He turned and looked back once.

The shrine had already shrunk to bells and smoke and a patch of awning on the mud rise. Yet he could still feel where the room had leaned toward him. He could still hear Ilyan saying crowds don't kneel to a committee in that maddening low formal voice. He could still feel the light touch of two string-callused fingers on his throat, naming a shelf there as if inheritance could live in cartilage and resonance.

Tovin's warning from Reed Gate had not vanished. Shared burden. No single heir. He knew it, and he knew too that truth was not the same thing as what people reached for when they were frightened. The river amplified desire as readily as duty. Everyone had said that in one form or another. But what if desire was the only language people ever obeyed cleanly? What if Sereya had been wrong in her damage and right in her method? What if the route needed all five of them to work and only one of them to be believed?

The question was rotten. He knew it was rotten. It still opened in him like a flower fed on ditch water.

As the boat cleared the last bell post, the far bell rang once behind them, sharp and solitary.

Daro did not mistake it for proof.

He only let it mean what he wanted.
