# Chapter 3

Rain had beaten the eel racks flat enough to make Tovin Reed's yard look combed by a giant black hand. The racks ran in crooked lines from the mud bank to the sheds, each one strung with empty hooks and split reeds that clicked together whenever the wind found them. Beyond them, Reed Gate sat in the channel like a broken jaw. Its iron teeth rose from the water in a rust-furred curve, half buried in silt and old offerings, with the winch house leaning over it on pilings that had gone green and slick with flood slime.

Riva brought the Bitter Thread in nose first and hated how slow she had to do it. The current kept worrying the hull from two directions at once, the upstream flood pressing at her left shoulder while some blocked-downriver force shoved back through the gate and made the barge yaw on her moorings. She had the tiller rope wet in both hands and the old towing-hook scar on her chin itching under the rain by the time the bow kissed mud.

Behind the barge, lashed on a longer line so the dead did not thump against her stern, the little corpse-ferry tapped and swung. Tovin had made them bring it from Knot Bend before dawn. The drowned girl lay under an oiled cloth that did not quite cover one blue-white foot. Beside her, the swollen man they had pried from under the ferry house pilings was wrapped in reed matting that had gone dark as liver in the rain. If the cut was truly opened, the current should take them seaward. If it was not, the dead would keep waiting in the same water as the living. Riva did not like tests she could watch fail.

Tovin stood at the top of the bank in an eel-skin apron under her oilcloth, already wet through at the shoulders. She had lined a row of bent nails on the sill of her shed window as neatly as prayer sticks. Riva had seen her do that since childhood, collecting every crooked piece of iron the yard spat up and pocketing it for later hammering because waste offended her more than fatigue ever had. Even in flood season, with the river trying to eat the district, she had found time to sort ruined nails by size.

"You're late," Tovin called.

"We're here before full light."

"That's late if the water's already mean."

Riva stepped onto the bank. Mud tried to keep one boot. She yanked free and looked straight at the gate. "We can have it moving inside an hour if you let me rig the Thread to the upper rack."

Tovin spat rain. "You can strip the teeth first."

"That's what the winch is for."

"The winch is for a barge. That gate's a cut. Different temper."

Riva had spent half the night listening to dead voices move under the ferry house floorboards and half the morning getting her siblings and a corpse-ferry onto the water without anybody starting a fresh war. Her patience was running on rope fibers. "The route needs to open. We don't have three days for reverence."

"Good thing I didn't ask for reverence." Tovin jerked her chin at the gate. "I asked for hands."

The others came off the barge in the order they always seemed to choose when nobody named it. Daro first, graceful even while slipping in mud, with his instrument case wrapped in waxed cloth under one arm as if the channel might ask him for song before it asked him for work. Lale followed more carefully, skirts pinned up, indigo stains dark in the beds of her nails even through the rain. Iven jumped the last gap and nearly went on his ass, then grinned as if falling had been his own idea. Nami came down with a basket of tools against her hip, wide-eyed and alert to everything, smelling faintly of smoke and brine under the river rot.

Iven looked from the gate to the Bitter Thread and back to Tovin. "Tell me she's not making us do this like pilgrims. I didn't bring my pious face."

"You didn't bring your useful face either," Tovin said.

"That one costs extra."

"Then keep it shut. Get the shovels."

Daro planted the instrument case in the driest strip under the shed eave. "If the cut answers to bond, maybe it wants witness before it wants scraping."

Tovin did not bother looking at him. "If the cut wanted your mouth, I'd have sent for your mouth. I sent for the lot of you."

Riva almost smiled at that. Almost. Then Tovin picked up a rake, threw it into Riva's hands, and pointed at the black ridge packed against the lower teeth.

"Strip it to iron," she said. "No engine yet. I want to hear what the channel does when you touch it."

The first step off the planks put cold floodwater over Riva's boots. The second buried her to the calf in silt. By the fourth she had to plant the rake crosswise to stop herself pitching forward into the gate housing. The mud had layers to it: fresh brown wash from the monsoon on top, old black slime underneath, and under that a packed heaviness that held fish bones, reed stubble, rust flakes, and the sweet-sick stink of trapped rot. Every pull of the rake brought up something that had once belonged elsewhere.

The siblings spread out because Tovin told them to and because Riva glared until they did. Iven took the right side with a shovel and a running complaint about leeches. Lale worked with two baskets, scooping the loosened muck and passing it up to the planks with quick economical motions. Nami went in knee-deep without being asked and started pulling weed knots barehanded from the lower grate. Daro lingered one beat too long at the edge, looking down into the water as if it had said something only he had heard, then waded in and set his shoulder to a jammed reed mat.

Rain drummed on the winch house roof. The gate answered in smaller sounds. Under the slap of weather and the scrape of metal, Riva could hear the sealed channel breathing wrong. It did not rush or sing. It sucked in short angry draws and pushed back with a wet choke, like something with mud packed in its throat.

She hated that Tovin was right enough to be useful.

If she had been working an ordinary obstruction, she would have rigged a line, taken tension slow, let the Bitter Thread's engine do the uglier part, and called it a day's honest labor. Every hour mattered. Oren Pike's debt sat in the back of her skull like a nail head, four hundred eighty silver marks plus the repair interest that had started breeding the moment she signed for the barge. The post-monsoon fair was six weeks away if the rains behaved. The rains had not behaved since she crossed back into Reedmouth.

Iven dug up the first iron tooth with a shout. "Found the bastard."

Riva turned. A wedge of rust-dark metal showed through the muck, jagged like a broken molar. Nami ran her palm over it before Riva could stop her.

"Careful," Riva said.

"It isn't sharp there." Nami kept feeling along the tooth as if reading it. "It's warm."

"Everything's warm in floodwater."

Nami glanced up. "Not iron."

Tovin heard and grunted, but she did not explain. She had gone up to the shed and come back with a pry bar, two oak wedges, and a length of chain that left old grease on her wrists. Her arthritic fingers did not move fast, but they moved with a certainty Riva still trusted in spite of herself.

"Again," Tovin said. "Clear the next two. Get your hands in around the hinge-box. Shovel work only gets you the lie on top."

Riva wanted to tell her she knew the difference between surface wash and packed obstruction. Instead she bent and drove both hands into the black seam where the tooth disappeared under the gate. Mud went under her nails, slick and granular. Something soft gave way against her knuckles, maybe river weed, maybe fish flesh, maybe worse. When she pulled back, her wrists were striped with snail eggs.

By the time they had the second tooth clear enough to show shape, nobody was talking except to swear or ask for a tool. The rain had eased into a denser, straighter fall that turned the yard into bead curtains. Lale's pinned-up hem had come loose and was black to the thigh. Iven's gold tooth flashed every time he cursed at the suction taking his boot. Daro had taken off his outer shirt and wrapped it around the handle of a hook because the iron was skinning his palm. Nami's hair had worked free from its knot and stuck in wet ropes to her neck.

Riva levered up a clump of roots and old shrine cloth and saw, under it, the square mouth of the key housing.

"There," she said. "We can take the rack now."

Tovin did not move.

Riva straightened enough to look at her. "You hear that? Housing's clear."

"Mostly."

"Enough."

Tovin's mouth flattened. "No."

Riva let the rake fall against the gate. "What now?"

Tovin came down off the planks and into the water with the chain over one shoulder. The old woman's oilcloth opened at the knee, showing a scar the color of wax running from shin to ankle. She planted herself between the siblings and the half-cleared teeth.

"Now," she said, "you tell the cut what she took."

Nobody answered. Even the rain seemed to stop for a beat and listen.

Riva said, "What?"

Tovin looked at each of them in turn. "This gate held for Sereya because she built it with me and bled in it and loved on both sides of it. It doesn't answer to muscle by itself. It answers to labor and bond. You can clear the mud all morning and still get nothing but broken wrists if you come here lying."

Iven barked a laugh that landed wrong. "Old Reed, if this is your way of making us apologize to the weather-"

"Not apologize." Tovin cut across him. "Name the theft. One each. Plain. No saint talk, no pretty grievance. What did Sereya take from you?"

Riva stared at her. Time was running off the roof in sheets. The dead rocked in their little ferry behind the Bitter Thread. The gate breathed wet and stubborn at her knees.

"This is nonsense," she said.

"Then walk away and see if nonsense keeps the corpses upriver."

Tovin handed the chain to Nami, the pry bar to Lale, the rake back to Riva as if assigning places in a rite she refused to call a rite. Then she pointed at the mucked iron and said, "Keep working. Talk while you work. That's the only way the cut listens."

Riva had never wanted to hit an old woman more in her life.

She bent back into the silt instead.

***

By midmorning they had three teeth clean, both hinge-boxes exposed, and every one of them dirtier than if Tovin had simply dropped them in the river whole.

The labor changed once the demand had been made. It did not get easier. It got quieter in the wrong places. Every scrape of shovel and suck of mud sounded like a pause stretched thin waiting for the first mouth to open.

Riva kept hoping Tovin would break and name some practical threshold instead. One more basket. One more hinge. One more foot of chain. Instead the old woman stood on the planks with rain dripping off her nose and said nothing until the silence itself turned heavy.

It was Iven who cracked first, because of course it was.

He had both arms sunk to the elbow in the left hinge-box, feeling for the buried pin while the water shoved at his ribs. "Fine," he said. "She took a clean hand."

No one moved. Even Iven seemed annoyed with himself for having started.

He spat mud and kept working. "Happy? She taught me to palm a card before I was tall enough to see over the table. Sat me on her knee, showed me how to keep the ace warm under my thumb, and then laughed when the men started calling me her pretty little cheat. After that I couldn't win a coin clean if I wanted to. Even when I was honest, people were looking for her in my wrist."

He found the pin then and yanked it up with a curse, a rusted length of iron slick with black slime.

"There," he said. "Now the gate knows I'm spoiled stock. Everyone satisfied?"

"Not everyone," Lale said.

She did not look up from her basket. She was kneeling on the planks, skirt plastered to her shins, sorting out the heavier lumps of rust and bone from the muck so the drain trench would not choke again. It was such a Lale thing to do, tidying catastrophe while she spoke, that Riva almost missed the damage under the dry tone.

"She took respectability," Lale said. "Before I even knew I wanted it."

The basket in her hands tipped. Black water streamed through the slats.

"I spent years sewing myself into other people's standards," she went on. "Invoices straight. Cuffs clean. Speech clean. Nails hidden. It never mattered. She had already walked into every room first. Half the time people were polite to me only because they were measuring how much of her body still showed through mine."

Iven opened his mouth, maybe for a joke, maybe for defense. Lale glanced at him once and he shut it again.

The gate made a new sound. Not movement exactly. More like a throat clearing under water.

Riva heard it. Tovin heard it too. The old woman nodded once but did not let them stop to admire anything.

"Again," Tovin said. "Keep the teeth bare."

Daro had gone very still. Water sheened over his bare forearms and dripped from the sharp line of his jaw. His throat worked once. Twice. When he finally spoke, he did it softly enough that everyone leaned without meaning to.

"She took prayer," he said, "and turned it into applause."

The words sat there between the rain strokes.

Daro looked down at the gate instead of at any of them. "She'd sing or bless or touch somebody's grief, and the whole bank would hold its breath waiting for the next thing. I was a child and I thought that was devotion. By the time I knew the difference, I was already hungry for the same noise. I still don't always know when I'm serving the dead and when I'm asking the living to look at me."

There was no performance in that last sentence. That was what made Riva believe it.

Tovin believed it too. She tapped the plank once with her boot heel. "Good. Keep going."

Iven snorted to cover whatever had landed in him. "No blessing? No old woman's wisdom? He bleeds a secret and all he gets is more shoveling."

"What did you expect?" Tovin said. "A sweet bun?"

Nami had been working with her whole body in the job, hauling weed mats free with both hands and bracing her feet against the lower rail. She straightened now and pushed wet hair out of her eyes. Mud ran down the inside of her forearms in black strings.

"She took getting to know her plain," Nami said.

The sentence was so simple it split Riva cleaner than Daro's had.

Nami kept one hand on the gate tooth while she talked, as if she needed the iron to stay upright.

"By the time I was old enough to ask what she liked for breakfast, people were already telling me what saint blood wanted. Everybody had a story. Everybody had a rule. They'd touch my face in the market and tell me not to waste what she left in me. I didn't get a mother first. I got everybody else's version of one."

Her voice shook only on the last word. She looked furious about that.

Riva became aware of the little corpse-ferry knocking gently at its line behind them. She did not turn around. She knew which body Nami had helped wash before dawn. The drowned girl had been younger than Nami had been when Sereya died.

The gate shuddered.

It was small but unmistakable. The exposed tooth under Riva's hand knocked once against its housing. Mud belched out of the right hinge-box in a black bloom that stained the water around their knees.

Iven sucked air through his teeth. "Oh, fuck me."

"No," Tovin said. "Fuck the next hinge. Move."

Riva did move. So did all of them. But the thing that had been keeping her upright until then, her hard contempt for the exercise, had loosened by a notch she did not like. The cut was answering. Not in a storybook way. Not in a clean miracle. It was answering the same way a seized engine answered patient oil and the right pressure. It made her angrier precisely because it worked.

They cleared another arm's width in silence. Daro and Iven shifted the chain under the middle rack. Lale wedged one plank under the exposed lower rail so the backwash would not bury it again. Nami climbed onto the beam to knife away a mat of old prayer reeds that had twisted around the rack like hair. Riva kept her eyes on the iron and told herself that was why her chest hurt.

Tovin said, "Yours, Riva."

Riva kept scraping. "Later."

"Now."

"When the housing's clear."

"It's clear enough."

Riva jammed the rake deeper. "Then you say something. You're the one who loved her."

Tovin's laugh was brief and filthy. "Everybody here says something because I loved her. That's how this works."

Riva looked up hard enough to splash water from her lashes. "I said later."

Daro leaned on the chain. "You heard old Reed. Maybe the gate knows command voice when it hears it and has grown tired of yours."

Riva turned on him at once. "Shut up and pull."

"There she is."

"You want to sing at the metal, go ahead. See if it opens for vanity."

Daro's face changed. Not much. Just enough for Riva to know she had hit the true rib under the skin. He straightened slowly. "You think labor keeps you clean of hunger. It doesn't. It just gives you better excuses."

"At least my excuses float."

"Children," Tovin said, and there was murder in it.

Lale slammed a basket down on the planks. "Can you two postpone the heir war until the gate isn't trying to drown us?"

The word hung there. Heir.

Daro smiled without humor. "Who said anything about heirs?"

"You keep saying it without the noun," Lale said.

Nami had gone still. Iven was watching all of them with the watchfulness he used at card tables when the room was one insult from broken glass. Riva climbed out of the water, meaning only to get leverage on the beam, and found herself halfway to the bank as if her body had decided to walk out of the demand altogether.

Tovin stepped into her path.

Riva was taller. It did not help.

"Move," Riva said.

"No."

"Then say your piece and let me do mine my way."

Tovin's flattened nose shone with rain. "My piece is plain enough. Sereya never meant this route to belong to one heir. Not one of you. She split it on purpose."

Daro opened his mouth.

Tovin cut him off with a raised hand. "No. Listen while somebody older talks."

It was almost enough to make Iven laugh. He wisely did not.

"She knew what happened when a whole town wanted one body to carry their need," Tovin said. "She made use of that, aye. More than she should have. But later, when she had more sense than vanity, she said to me plain: if any child of mine tries to wear the whole road alone, stop them. Break the pole if I have to. Break their hand if I have to. She didn't trust blood with singular holiness. She barely trusted herself."

The rain had gone harder again. It pinged off the exposed iron, off the chain, off the roof of the winch house. Riva could not tell whether the roaring in her ears was weather or fury.

Daro said, very quietly, "Convenient, hearing that from the keeper whose gate comes first."

Tovin shrugged. "Truth got here first too. You want to wrestle that, be my guest."

Riva should have liked hearing Daro cut down. Instead the words hit her sideways. Not one heir. Not one of you. She had spent the last day telling herself she was only here because nobody else knew how to move heavy things without making the damage worse. That was a practical claim. An ugly one, maybe, but practical. Hearing it named alongside Daro's hunger made it feel less clean.

The corpse-ferry knocked the stern of the Bitter Thread again. A dull, hollow sound. Riva turned and saw the drowned girl's uncovered foot, small and pale against the wet wood, jerking with the tug of the line.

Something in her went flat.

She stepped back into the water.

"Fine," she said.

Nobody spoke.

Riva set both hands on the exposed tooth. Rust gritted under her palms. "She took my girlhood and called it training."

The words came out before she had arranged them. That was probably why they were true.

"She'd hand me the pole and tell me keep the ferry straight. Then she'd go bless somebody or fuck somebody or drag some mourner into her plans, and if I failed to hold us off the pilings I was the one who got hit for it. I was big enough to ballast the boat, so she used me for ballast. That was the first thing she ever trusted me to be."

Her siblings were silent in a way she hated. She kept going because stopping would have been worse.

"I left Reedmouth and still took her with me. Every deck I've worked since, every stupid repair, every person who needed saving, I heard her in it. Do this. Hold that. Stay put. Be useful. She took the part of me that might've learned another shape."

When she finished, her hands were shaking on the iron, not from revelation but from the effort of not dropping them.

The gate answered with a wrench so sudden it nearly pulled her forward. All three exposed teeth jumped. Water punched out from under the rack in a filthy surge. The chain Daro held snapped taut and sang.

Nami cried out, not in fear. In recognition.

Tovin smiled for the first time that day, a quick ugly slash of satisfaction. "There you are," she said. "Now we can work."

***

By afternoon the rain had thinned to a hard silver sheet and the whole yard smelled of torn weed, iron mud, and the old trapped stink released from under the gate.

They had the teeth bare to the lower rack, the hinge-pins greased as well as anything buried that long could be greased, and a trench cut behind the housing so the worst of the backwash had somewhere to spit its filth. It still was not enough. The gate would shift a finger's width and then jam again as the river leaned against it from the wrong side.

That was where ordinary work re-entered the matter.

Riva stood on the Bitter Thread's deck and looked from the upper rack to the winch drum, then to the chain run, then back to the gate. The answer arrived in parts. Take tension high, not low. Use the barge to hold the rack open once the key turned. Wait for the backward pulse to slacken. Do not fight the whole river. Slip past the moment it loosened.

She started issuing orders before anyone had formally asked her to.

"Iven, bring that chain up to the bow roller. No, not that side. You'll twist the teeth and shear them."

"I was going to do it the good way," he said.

"Then start today."

He grinned once at her over his shoulder, all gold tooth and rainwater, and obeyed.

"Lale, I need the trench deeper by the housing. If the mud packs back in when the rack lifts, we're done."

"I know what packed mud does," Lale said, but she took the shovel.

"Nami, wedges by the second and third teeth. Keep them dry till I call."

Nami hugged the oak blocks to her chest and nodded hard enough to fling water. She looked lit from inside, not happy exactly, but charged.

"Daro," Riva said.

He was on the bank with the corpse-ferry line in one hand, hair slicked back, face unreadable. "Yes, captain?"

There was enough acid in the title to clean a knife.

Riva ignored it. "Keep that ferry straight when the pull comes. If she swings broadside she'll jam the mouth."

"And if the cut wants a song after all?"

"Then it can wait until Hymn Cut and talk to Moor for one."

That landed. So did the fact that he still moved to the line without another word.

Tovin came out of the winch house holding the key.

It was longer than Riva remembered, a bar of black iron with a square jaw at one end and a crosspiece wrapped in old eel leather at the other. The leather had gone slick with years of palm sweat and rain. Tovin carried it carefully, not ceremonially. The way you carried a tool that could break your wrist if the mechanism bit back.

"Housing's ready," Tovin said.

Riva climbed down from the deck. "I can take the upper strain with the barge, but somebody has to turn as soon as the back-push drops."

"Aye."

Tovin held the key out.

For one stupid second Riva thought the old woman was showing it to her, not handing it over. Then the iron rested across her palms, heavier than it looked, cold through the mud on her skin.

"Why me?" she said before she could stop herself.

Tovin's expression made it clear she found the question offensive. "Because your hands listen."

That could have been tenderness in another mouth. In Tovin's it came wrapped in contempt so nobody had to blush.

Riva took the key.

The siblings watched. She felt it without looking at them. Daro's sharpened quiet. Lale's measuring. Iven's quick curiosity. Nami's naked relief. It would have been easier if one of them had laughed.

Instead Daro said, "So that's that, then."

Riva did look at him then. "That's a tool."

"Everything turns into one in your grip."

"Better than turning it into a mirror."

Tovin slapped the side of the winch house with her open palm. The sound cracked across the yard. "Enough. Count the pulse."

That was for Riva, and Riva obeyed because the water had already begun its ugly breathing again. She moved to the housing, slid the square jaw of the key into the socket, and set her feet wide in the slime.

There were patterns in bad current if you did not insist on treating water like a mood. The back-push came in long loaded surges. Between them there was a thinner moment, not calm, never calm, but a lessening. A tiny theft of pressure. On a salvage job you listened for cable song. Here she listened for the choke in the gate throat.

One push. Two. On the third she felt the rack shiver half a hair under her fingers.

"Ready," she said.

Iven braced at the winch. "Always."

Lale drove the shovel deeper into the trench wall and held. Nami crouched at the teeth with one wedge and both hands. Daro planted his feet by the corpse-ferry line. Tovin stood beside Riva, not helping yet, simply present with the kind of solidarity that looked almost identical to threat.

"Now," Riva said.

Iven kicked the winch brake loose. Chain screamed over the roller. The Bitter Thread took strain high and left, her hull rocking hard enough to slap the bank. Riva threw her weight on the key. Nothing happened.

Then something very old inside the housing gave with a crack that shot pain to her elbow.

"Hold," she barked.

The upper rack lifted half an inch.

Backwash hit at once and tried to slam it shut again. Mud fountained up from the trench. Nami shoved the wedge under the second tooth and almost lost two fingers doing it. Lale caught the girl's wrist and dragged her hand free just before iron bit oak with a sound like a snapped shin bone.

"Again," Tovin said.

Riva reset her grip. The eel leather turned in her palms. Her shoulders had gone hot and unreliable. She could smell the old oil in the housing, the river rot boiling up from below, her own wet shirt souring against her skin.

One push. Two.

"Now."

The barge strained. Iven swore at the winch like it owed him rent. Daro hauled the corpse-ferry line hand over hand to keep the little craft from slewing. Nami jammed the second wedge as the teeth rose. Lale shoved muck out of the trench with both forearms. Tovin counted under her breath. "Three teeth. Four. Keep it. Keep it. Do not get sentimental."

Riva turned the key until the socket chewed her palms raw through the leather. The mechanism resisted at every quarter turn, then yielded with shocking suddenness. Water struck her thighs with new force. Not the backward shove. Something underneath it, older and faster, waking in the cut.

"It's taking," Nami said.

"Not yet," Riva snapped, because saying yes too soon was how jobs killed you.

The fifth tooth cleared.

Then the rack jammed.

Not gently. With murderous finality. The whole gate locked and shuddered. The winch chain jumped. The Bitter Thread lurched broadside. Daro lost ground at the corpse-ferry line and skidded to one knee. The little ferry swung out, nose turning toward the half-open mouth where a snag there could ruin everything.

Riva let go of the key and grabbed the side brace before the kickback broke her wrist. Iron rang through the yard.

"Line!" she yelled.

Iven tried to take more strain and got nothing but a howl from the drum. "We're pinned."

Lale's face had gone white under the rain. "The lower hinge packed again."

Of course it had. The released pressure had shoved a whole belly of old silt down into the one place they could not afford it. Riva saw it in one glance: black slurry jamming the bottom rail, teeth lifted but not free, all that downward weight waiting to bite as soon as the wedges slipped.

There was no time for argument. The lessened pulse was already filling again.

"Nami, back off," Riva said.

"I can get it."

"Back."

Riva dropped to her knees in the trench. The mud there was colder, dense as meat. She drove both arms under the lower rail up to the biceps, hunting blind for the packed clot while the gate groaned above her. Something sharp cut the heel of her hand. She did not have room to care.

Tovin was beside her at once. "When it breaks, it will break hard."

"Then hold the second wedge."

"I was planning to."

The old woman shoved her own scarred forearm under the brace and took weight. Riva found the obstruction at last, not one clot but a knot of root, wire, prayer metal, and grave cloth compacted by years into something tougher than rope. She tore at it. It held.

Above them Daro said, not mockingly now, "Riva."

"Not now."

"Riva, the pulse."

She heard it then. The gate throat changing. The back-push drawing in for another hit.

There was only one move left. The stupid one. The right one.

Riva braced a boot against the trench wall and ripped with everything she had.

The knot came free in a gush of black water and smell so foul it seemed to have color. She saw, in the same instant, bits of shrine foil, fish spine, a child's red thread bracelet turned brown, and something like the stitched edge of old mourning cloth. All the years the channel had been fed and neglected at once in her hands.

"Now!" she shouted.

Tovin heaved up on the brace. Iven threw his weight on the winch. Lale drove the shovel blade under the lower rail as a skidding support. Nami screamed with effort and rammed the last wedge home. Daro hauled the corpse-ferry line so hard the rope burned his palms. Riva slammed her shoulder into the key and turned.

The gate broke open with a sound like a building exhaling.

Not a crash. Not a song. A deep ugly release from the river's chest.

Water punched through the teeth and out the cut in one black-green surge studded with reeds, old petals, dead fish, and the long trapped filth of seven closed years. The wedges shot loose and spun away. The Bitter Thread leaped at her lines, then settled as the strain changed direction. Every loose thing in the yard rattled at once.

The corpse-ferry jerked.

For one sick beat it looked as though it would turn over. The drowned man rolled under his matting. The girl's uncovered foot flashed pale. Then the current found the little craft cleanly under the bow, straightened it, and pulled.

Daro let line run through his hands. Rope hissed. Nami made a sound Riva had heard from people at burials when they finally saw the boat push off.

The corpse-ferry slid through Reed Gate and out into the reopened cut, fast now, stern wobbling once before the current took command of it. It did not stall. It did not circle back. It went downstream.

All six of them stood where they were and watched.

Rain feathered the surface. The little ferry passed the last eel rack, then the leaning marker post, then the bend where the channel narrowed and the reed beds swallowed sight. It carried the dead girl and the man toward water that had finally remembered its work.

Riva became aware of her own body in pieces. Her palms were skinned. Her right shoulder had gone numb from neck to elbow. Mud had worked into her shirt, her braids, the waistband of her trousers. She tasted iron where she had bitten the inside of her cheek without noticing. None of it mattered as much as the fact of moving water where there had been refusal.

Tovin took the key out of the housing and pressed it back into Riva's hand.

"Keep it," she said.

Riva looked at the iron, then at the old woman. "You might need it."

"I just saw who needs it more."

That was as close to blessing as Tovin Reed was ever likely to come.

Behind them, Iven laughed once, breathless and disbelieving. "Well. I'll be fucked. The old gate actually had manners left."

Lale wiped mud from her face with the back of one wrist, leaving a darker stripe. "It had labor. That's not the same thing."

"You going to write that in a ledger?" Iven asked.

"If I do, I'll spell your part small."

Nami was still staring downstream. "They went," she said. "They really went."

"Aye," Tovin said. Her voice had roughened. "That's the job."

Daro came up the bank slowly, rope burns fresh across both palms. He looked at the cut, then at the key in Riva's hand. His expression had settled into something smooth enough to be dangerous later.

"So now you're captain of saints and sluices both," he said.

Riva tucked the key into her belt because if she held it any longer the gesture would start meaning too much. "I'm captain of a barge and a family of idiots standing in floodwater. That's enough."

"For you, maybe."

There it was. The resentment arriving almost before the proof had stopped moving in front of them.

Riva was too tired to soften. "If you've got a better plan for Hymn Cut, save it till we're dry enough to hear it."

Daro's mouth tilted. "You make command sound like maintenance. It's almost admirable."

"And you make need sound holy," she said. "That trick is getting old."

Tovin stepped between them with the easy authority of someone who had spent her life separating dangerous equipment from stupid hands. "Save it. The cut's open, not settled. We give it an hour, then we lash the yard and move downstream."

Nobody argued. That was what Riva noticed most. Not the obedience exactly, but the small horrible pause before each of them looked at her to see if she would add to Tovin's order, alter it, or own it.

She did not want the pause. She knew that as cleanly as she knew a fouled prop when she heard one.

She looked at the reopened water, at the black mouth of Reed Gate breathing the right way at last, at the place where the corpse-ferry had vanished around the bend. Then she looked back at her siblings, mud-striped and rain-beaten and newly impossible.

"Lale, check the trench in half an hour and see what settles back," she said. "Iven, dry that winch line and rewrap the drum before it birds-nests. Nami, eat something before you pitch into the cut yourself. Daro, get the ferry clear of the main pull and make sure the dead line is stowed right this time."

Iven gave her a mocking little salute but went. Lale bent to the trench without comment. Nami nodded as if receiving a charge. Daro held her eyes for one beat longer than the others and then turned away with the rope.

Only Tovin stayed.

The old woman looked downriver once, toward the vanished dead, and then at the key under Riva's hand.

"You don't have to like the burden," she said. "You just have to carry it without lying about why."

Riva let out a breath she had not known she was holding. The reopened cut made a low steady draw beside them, workmanlike now, almost calm.

"That supposed to comfort me?" she asked.

"No," Tovin said. "Comfort is for dry houses."

Then she went to gather up her bent nails before the water could steal them.
