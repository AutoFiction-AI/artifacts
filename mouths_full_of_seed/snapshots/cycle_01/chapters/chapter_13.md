# Chapter 13

By the time Sereya's old funeral ferry nosed into Halcote's loading basin, the town had lost the smell Lale remembered. Flour and brick dust were still in it somewhere, but the flood had worked both into a wet paste that coated the wharf planks and climbed the walls in pale streaks. The basin itself was fuller than it should have been. Water had backed up through the grain drains and the old quarry run, and every culvert mouth along the quay made the same thick breathing sound, as if the town had grown lungs in its foundations.

Halcote's people stood back from the edge. They did not want to seem frightened, which only made the fear look expensive. Men in mill aprons held their own elbows. Women with market shawls tucked their hands under their arms and stared at the ferry as if rot might jump the gap. A mule cart had been left crooked in the lane with one wheel sunk to the axle in mud. The driver had apparently gone off in a hurry and forgotten the carrots still tied in a bunch behind the seat. One of them had slipped loose and floated in the gutter water like a severed orange finger.

Lale saw Oren Pike before he saw her. He had his waxed hat on, though the brim had gone soft in the rain, and he was biting a clove the way he always did when he wanted to smell wealthier than the room allowed. He kept one hand under his ledger satchel, protecting it from the weather with more tenderness than most men here gave their children. Two years ago he had haggled her down over shroud cloth and then praised her accounts in the same breath, a neat inland trick that had almost made him tolerable. Now he was looking at the five of them as if Reedmouth had sent a disease upriver wearing a family face.

Riva braced the ferry pole against the basin wall and held the boat steady. Her shoulders looked even broader on the stripped-down funeral craft than they had on the Bitter Thread. Losing the dredge had taken iron and engine from her, not command. Rain ran down the scar on her chin and darkened the rope-burn seams in her hands. "Who's walking?" she called.

No one answered at first. They looked instead at Daro, at the brass bells tied under the ferry eave, at Nami wrapped in a wool blanket though it was not cold. They looked at Iven's gold tooth when he spat basin water off the side. They looked at the family and kept performing disbelief as long as performance still cost less than surrender.

Lale stepped onto the quay before Riva could do it for her. The mud took her shoe to the ankle. Halcote always did that in flood weather: pretended to be brick until it remembered it had been cut out of river dirt like everywhere else.

"You sent for us," she said in inland standard, because that mattered here more than truth. "If you're going to stand there and make faces at the boat, we can leave."

That got them moving, not forward exactly, but into language.

"We didn't send for all of you," said a woman in a brown oilskin cap. She was older than Lale had first taken her for, the skin at her throat folded fine as wet paper, and she kept rubbing flour paste out of the groove where her wedding ring had once sat. It was Cel Beren, grain hall keeper. Lale knew her from invoices and funeral cloth. Cel could tell barley by smell and once cried over a spoiled bin as if it had been a child. "We sent because three people walked out of their beds and down to the quarry. One of them was my nephew. He came back with half his feet skinned. That doesn't make it sorcery."

"No," said Daro softly behind Lale. "It makes it worse."

His voice landed wrong on the quay. It was too smooth for these brick people, too close to prayer even when he did not mean it. Several of them shifted back a pace.

Oren Pike finally came down the slick boards, careful of his boots. "Riva," he said, and Lale heard the debt in the name. "You were meant to come back with a barge. Preferably intact."

"You were meant to stop lending against weather," Riva said. "Looks like both of us missed our mark."

Iven barked a laugh. It was brief and ugly and carried no joy in it. Nami, sitting under the ferry canopy with the blanket tight around her shoulders, stared past Oren toward the uphill lanes.

"There's more than three," she said. Her voice came out thin from the fever that had not fully left her, but the words were clear. "The pull's up in the stone. Like when crabs hide under a pot and you can hear them scratching before you lift it."

One of the mill men made a warding sign against his chest. Another muttered, "Christ preserve us," which was funny in Reedmouth and useless here.

Lale looked up the lane. Halcote rose in shelves from the basin: warehouses first, then the dye yards and mule lots, then the brick terraces where clerks and cutters slept with their shutters latched against the rain. At the top of the town, beyond the chapel roof and the kiln chimneys, the old quarry held a black sheet of water in its stone bowl. She could not see the water itself from here, only the raw white cut of quarry wall and the gullies feeding it. But she could see how the rain was running. Every lane, every broken drain, every overflow gutter turned uphill for a stretch and then bent toward that cut in the stone. If a body under seed-pull followed shine and sound and easy grade, Halcote had built a polished invitation.

Under the damp sleeve of her coat, the tide marks Paska had put into her forearm lifted darkly against her skin. They were only ink, only scar, and yet in weather like this they always seemed to wake before she touched them. Paska had laughed while she worked and said that water did not need much persuading. It took the first decent lie offered to it.

Riva followed Lale's gaze. "How far to the quarry?"

"Ten minutes if you know where you're going," Cel said. "Longer if you don't."

"And the sea road?" Riva asked.

That was the problem. Lale felt the whole shape of it settle at once, clean and unwelcome. Halcote was inland from the route proper. The flood pulse had thrown the sickness this far because the lower cuts were not yet settled, because the whole broken chain was still shoving pressure where it did not belong. If they all stayed here and played shepherd to sleepwalkers, the downstream work stalled. If they ignored Halcote, the town would bleed people into quarry water while Reedmouth called them saints and failures in the same breath.

Riva knew it too. Lale could see the arithmetic click behind her eyes, brutal because it had to be. Downriver the last pull waited. Here the living were wandering toward stone water like obedient cattle.

Oren Pike watched the sisters in the silence and mistook it for uncertainty. "You don't know what this is either," he said. "That is useful to know."

Lale turned to him. "I know enough to see your drains are serving the quarry instead of your people."

"My drains aren't the point."

"Everything is the point when people start walking in their sleep."

Cel Beren made an impatient sound. "Can you stop it?"

Lale opened her mouth with the civilized answer on it and threw it away. Precision would not buy anything here unless it ended in work. "Yes," she said. "Not cleanly."

That at least sounded expensive enough to be true.

Riva planted the pole again and looked at the siblings one by one. "I've got to keep moving," she said. "The next push is toward the sea, not up a grain lane. Daro comes with me. I need his mouth if the water turns stubborn. Iven too. Nami-"

"I'm not staying in a warehouse," Nami said. She pushed the blanket down and sat straighter. Her face had gone a little hollow under the fever, which only made her eyes look larger. "If the water's wrong downriver, I'll hear it before you do."

Riva's mouth tightened. She was still in the habit of wanting to protect Nami after the fact, which was a habit like trying to stop a pot from cracking once it had gone in the kiln. "Can you stand?"

"Can you stop asking that every time I answer you?" Nami said.

Iven grinned at that, quick and involuntary. "She's alive, then."

Daro came off the bench where he had been hunched under the eave. Rain had flattened his hair against his skull and sharpened the bones of his face. He looked like something drawn with a single black line and then left in the wet. "The quarry's loud," he said quietly. "So is the lower water. Split us or don't split us, but choose fast. It doesn't feel patient."

Lale was already choosing. She hated that Riva could probably see it happen before she spoke. Halcote was her town more than it was any of theirs. She had spent years learning where to stand in it, how to price herself so the respectable wives would call her useful instead of river-bred. She knew which alleys clogged first, which hall keys hung on whose belt, which men got devout when frightened and which only got mean. Riva could force a gate open with her body and Daro could sing a cut awake, but neither of them knew how Halcote lied to itself.

"I'll stay," Lale said.

Riva looked at her hard, assessing rather than surprised. The old version of that look would have meant Don't make work for me. This one meant Can you carry it. There was more respect in it and less comfort.

"With who?" Riva asked.

"No one from the family. You need all the hands you have." Lale nodded toward the upper streets. "What I need is lanterns, hall space, and people who know their own neighbors by the sound of a shutter."

"They don't trust us," Iven said.

"They don't trust me," Lale corrected. "They distrust you. There is a difference, and I can use it."

That got a brief glance from Riva, almost a smile and not warm. "Do it, then."

Oren Pike started to object. "You're not leaving one woman-"

Lale cut across him. "One woman who kept your books accurate when your own clerk wrote figures like chewed straw. One woman who knows your grain hall has three side doors and a loading ramp wide enough for litters. One woman who is standing here instead of rowing away. If you want to waste time arguing over my sex, do it while someone else's husband walks into the quarry."

Oren shut his mouth because Cel Beren laughed at him, a short hard bark that showed a back molar capped in tin.

"Hall key's with me," Cel said, already untangling it from the cord under her collar. "If we let damp feet in there and the grain spoils, I'll hold you answerable till one of us dies."

"Reasonable," Lale said.

Riva stepped off the ferry long enough to press something into Lale's hand: a short boat hook with the wood worn smooth by years of palms. It was not a weapon exactly, though it could become one in a room that had lost its manners. "Keep them turning," she said. "Don't let anyone block them head-on if they're under. They'll drive through the chest like cattle through wicker."

"I know."

Riva's eyes dropped to Lale's forearm where the tide marks had shown through the damp cloth. "Use what Paska gave you."

"I'm using it."

The line came out tighter than Lale meant, too corrected for rain and mud. Riva heard that too. She only nodded once.

Then the ferry shoved off. Daro touched two fingers to his throat before he took up the spare pole. Iven gave Lale a look that tried for insolence and landed nearer worry. Nami watched her with the strange still attentiveness that always made Lale feel both seen and accused.

"Don't let them cover the windows," Nami said. "If the room goes dark, they'll think the water moved."

Lale did not ask how she knew. She only tucked the boat hook under her arm and turned uphill as the ferry slid away from the basin.

Behind her, Halcote's people did what frightened towns always did first. They waited to see if one person would tell them where to stand.

Lale pointed with the hook. "You. Get every lamp that can survive rain. You. Bell the mule lots and wake anyone sleeping near the quarry lane. Cel, open the grain hall. Oren, if you own a chain, leave it home. Bring rope, blankets, and every empty water barrel you have."

He frowned. "Water barrels?"

"Do you want explanation or do you want your town kept out of the quarry?"

His clove shifted in his cheek. "You always did answer a question as if it had annoyed you."

"And you always did need three answers where one would do. Move."

He moved.

Halcote had never made room for Lale out of affection. It had made room because she was neat, because her sums balanced, because she could make mourning cloth come out black enough for respectable grief. She had thought that counted as belonging for longer than she liked to admit. Marching uphill ahead of them now, with the rain needling the back of her neck and the quarry breathing over the roofs, she felt the truth of it in a colder place. Belonging had never been the offer. Utility had been. She could work with that. Utility, at least, could be counted.

The first sleepwalker she saw was a boy of about twelve in a linen nightshirt gone transparent in the rain. He was coming down Marker Lane with both hands held slightly out from his sides, not reaching, not protecting himself, simply making room for the body's forward need. His feet were bare. He stepped on a scatter of oyster shell someone had used to weight a drain and did not even flinch, though blood marked each pale fragment after him.

"Fenn," Cel Beren said sharply. "Fenn, boy."

He did not answer. His eyes were open and wrong. They were not rolled back or blank; they were fixed past the world in front of him as if the wet black quarry water had been poured behind them and he was walking inside it already.

Two women came stumbling after him, one pulling a shawl over her hair with both hands, the other still buttoning her dress crooked. The second one was Fenn's mother, Anja Rusk, who had bought winter burial cloth off Lale the year her own mother died. She had a habit of pinning her hem up on one side even when no mud threatened, from an old leg injury she never explained.

"He's overtired," Anja said at once. People under true fear always reached for the smallest lie first. "He worked the loft till midnight. He walked as a child. He-"

Fenn stepped directly into the side of a cart wheel and rebounded without complaint, then kept going toward the uphill street.

The lie snapped in the rain.

Anja made a sound like cloth tearing. Cel Beren caught her by the elbow before she went down.

Lale moved in front of the boy and did not touch him yet. His attention was not on obstacles. It was on the line the water had drawn through the town. She followed the angle of his body, the way his feet kept seeking the wetter side of the stones. Down the gutter, around the brewer's wall, across the little rise toward the quarry lane. He was reading the town as water would read it.

She would too.

The tide marks on her forearm prickled. Paska had not given her prophecy. She had given her habits: the memory of how floodwater cut corners, how it preferred shine to shade, how a channel did not have to be true if it was easier than the original.

Lale looked toward the grain hall. It sat three streets over, built long and high with double doors on the loading side and clerestory windows under the eaves. The ramp sloped up from the mule lane, and a drainage run already cut under the threshold to keep grain from sitting in seep water. If she widened that wet, lit it low, and made the hall sound alive with dripping, it might take the walkers for a safer mouth than the quarry.

"Don't grab him," she said as Anja lunged. "Turn him."

"Turn him where?" Cel demanded.

Lale pointed. "There. The hall."

Oren Pike came trotting up with two warehouse boys and a coil of rope over one shoulder, furious at the pace and wet through to his cuffs. One of the boys was his nephew Jori, an earnest young man with a white patch of skin at the temple where hair never grew back after a mule kick. Jori always chewed fennel seed to hide the tobacco his aunt forbade. Lale smelled the sweet sharp scent on him even in the rain.

"Barrels are coming," Oren said. "And if I find out you've sent me all over town for nonsense-"

"Jori," Lale said.

The boy blinked. "Miss An?"

"Take that lamp and put it on the grain-hall ramp, low. Not chest height. Boot height. Then another inside the doors. Then another past the first row of bins."

"Why?"

"Because bodies under pull follow the nearest shine that looks like wet." She was already moving as she spoke. "Cel, get the side doors open too. I want air and noise moving through the place. Oren, your barrels go to the threshold. Tip them slowly so the run under the hall starts singing."

He stared at her. "Singing."

"If I use smaller words, will you work faster?"

Cel Beren snorted again and set off at a near run. Oren swore under his breath and followed.

Lale took the hook in both hands and stepped to Fenn's left side. "Anja," she said. "Walk on his right. Not touching unless he turns away. When I say, you step in and make the lane narrower."

Anja's mouth was shaking. "If he falls-"

"Then we catch him. If you stop him outright, he'll fight you and not know your name while he does it."

That landed. It was not comfort. Comfort had gone missing before dawn.

They began. Lale edged left, the hook low against the stones, creating a little hard line where the lane should have continued uphill. Anja stepped closer on the other side, crying without sound. Fenn adjusted almost at once, not because he recognized them, but because the easier path had shifted.

He turned toward the grain hall.

For one bright second Lale felt the whole town look at her as if a seam had just held under strain and they had not expected it to.

Then three more walkers appeared at the top of Marker Lane.

One was an old woman with her braid half loose and her apron soaked black to the knees. One was a cooper still in his leather work jerkin, his left hand bleeding because he had apparently punched through a window to get out. The third was Jori Pike.

Oren saw his nephew and stopped dead. "No."

That single syllable carried more belief than anything he had said since the ferry arrived.

Jori came on at a steady pace, fennel seed still under his lip, eyes fixed on the upper cut in the stone. He stepped through the spilled contents of a fruit crate without bending. Pears rolled against his ankles. He did not notice.

"Get his aunt," Oren said, already moving after the boy and forgetting entirely that he was meant to distrust Lale. "Get Nessa now."

"Later," Lale snapped. "Now you work."

He looked as if he might strike her for the tone. Instead he caught Jori by the shoulders, and Jori drove straight through him with such force that both of them hit the wall. Oren's hat dropped into the gutter. Jori did not know he had a name, an uncle, a body in front of him. He only knew the quarry.

Riva had been right. Block them head-on and they treated the living like furniture.

"Sideways," Lale said. "Not the chest. Sideways."

She shoved Oren out of the line and jammed the hook against the stones. Cel Beren, coming back with keys bouncing against her ribs, saw at once what Lale meant and swung in on Jori's left. Together they made a narrowing, a channel rather than a wall. Jori faltered, corrected, and took the hall ramp instead of the quarry lane.

The old woman followed. Then the cooper did.

Rain hissed on the lamp glass. Inside the grain hall, the first barrel began emptying under the threshold. Water ran into the drain with a long throat sound. Another lamp glowed low against the boards. The hall did not look like safety. It looked like a lie dressed as a mouth. For the next hour, that was close enough.

Word ran faster than the walkers. By the time Lale had six people stationed at the hall doors and three more carrying lit lamps down the lanes, whole families were dragging half-dressed relatives from houses while still insisting there had to be another explanation. A mother with one stocking on said her daughter was only feverish until the girl walked out of her own retching and tried to crawl under a cart toward the quarry slope. A butcher kept saying his wife was devout, as if devotion and enchantment could not share a body, until she stepped barefoot onto a dropped cleaver and kept going with the steel edge standing up between her toes like a coin.

Lale did not argue theology. She assigned hands.

"Blankets under the arms, not around the throat. If they bend for the gutter, let them bend and turn them. Don't slap them awake. They're not asleep the way you mean it."

"What are they, then?" Cel Beren demanded while they hauled a miller between them. Flour had turned to glue on Cel's sleeves and made her look dusted in old bones.

Lale tightened the blanket under the miller's chest and felt him buck against it with blind strength. "Busy," she said. "Ask me later."

The answer satisfied no one and therefore helped.

Inside the grain hall the sound changed by the minute. At first it had been dripping, barrels, boots, commands. Then came the human noise. It was not screaming, or not much of it. Most of the walkers made only the little effort sounds of bodies denied a destination: breath shoved hard through the nose, throat clicks, heels drumming on planks when someone pulled them back from a door. One woman sang three bars of a cradle song over and over while her daughter tied her wrists to a grain post with a strip torn from the family tablecloth. A man in a clerk's shirt gnawed the wet cuff of his own sleeve until Cel smacked his hand away and stuffed a roll of sacking between his teeth.

Lale kept moving because if she stopped, the room would become only bodies and kin and pleas. Moving let it remain a problem of lines and pressure. She opened the side doors for cross-draft. She stationed lamps where the reflected shine on the wet boards drew inward instead of out. She had boys pour two more barrels under the ramp and then another three in the back drain so the whole hall kept talking in water sounds. She dragged empty bins clear of the wall to create lanes. She turned the sleepers as dye turned in a vat: not by wrestling the whole mass, but by working the current where it wanted to go anyway.

Still, families kept trying to reclaim innocence.

"He would never leave us," said a woman with wet curlers hanging down one cheek while her father strained toward the loading door.

"She only wants air," said a baker as his sister bit clean through the web of his thumb trying to reach the rain.

"He hears his wife," whispered Anja Rusk, who had finally brought Fenn down onto a blanket pile but kept smoothing his hair as if neatness might call him back.

That one hit somewhere Lale did not have time to inspect. Sereya's children had spent years listening to people rename appetite as holiness and damage as devotion. Halcote was doing the same cheap labor with cleaner hands.

Oren Pike came in carrying a fresh lamp and wearing no hat now. Rain had slicked his thinning hair to his skull. He looked older without the hat, and smaller, and more like the boy Jori had just nearly knocked senseless into a wall. "The lower terrace has two more," he said. "Maybe three. My sister's with the nephew."

"Did she see him?" Lale asked.

Oren's jaw worked once around the taste of the answer. "Yes."

"Good. Then she won't slow us down."

He stared at her, offended and almost admiring in the same instant. "You always did make kindness sound like bookkeeping."

"That was kindness."

He glanced across the hall at Jori, who was now tied by one wrist to a bin cleat while his mother sat cross-legged in the wet straw beside him and rubbed fennel seed between her fingers because she had taken the rest from his pocket and did not know what else to do with her hands. Oren swallowed and said, "Yes. I suppose it was."

The quarry bells started then.

They were not chapel bells. They were quarry bells, iron on iron, hit too fast and with no pattern, the sound workers used when stone shifted unexpectedly and anyone below needed to clear the cut. The whole hall lifted with it. Half the sleepers jerked toward the upper doors in one animal motion.

"Shit," Cel Beren said.

The bells stopped. Then they started again, farther off.

Someone was up there, not warning of falling stone, but calling to the water, or to the people who wanted it.

Lale did not waste time inventing who. The quarry lane had been the main pull from the start. If one frightened fool had run ahead to fetch his own kin or drown his own fear, he could pull half the town after him.

"Oren, with me," she said. "Cel, shut the upper side door and keep the lamps low. If anyone comes in, turn them straight to the middle aisles. Don't let the hall darken."

"You don't order my hall," Cel said automatically.

"Then take the sentence as a suggestion from a woman with less sentimental interest in your nephew drowning."

Cel's mouth tightened. "Go on, then."

The lane to the quarry had become a brown slide. Mud sucked at their boots and pebbles clicked loose underfoot. Oren panted almost at once, which Lale found irritating and then unfair. He was a broker, not a boatwoman. His lungs had been built for chairs.

The bells were coming from the old weigh station near the quarry lip. Lale knew the place: a stone hut with a scale beam and a cracked slate roof, where cutters used to tally cart loads before the main pit flooded. When Halcote was dry the quarry was ugly and practical. In flood, with the lower benches drowned and the upper walls sweating, it became ceremonial without asking anyone's permission. The black water sat too still. The white stone faces around it threw back every lamp and every scrap of sky until depth lost its edges.

There were six people on the slope when Lale reached it. Two were family members trying and failing to hold a sleepwalker between them. One was a boy ringing the warning bell with both hands because panic had simplified him to noise. One was Thom Vesk.

Lale knew him by sight before she remembered his name. He was a quarry cutter, a widower, and the father of two girls who sold river shells painted silver at market. He wore the same yellow scarf every wet season because the younger daughter had knitted it with one edge shorter than the other and would not forgive him if he left it home. Now the scarf was hanging half loose down his chest, streaked with lime mud. White quarry grit had pasted itself into his eyebrows and the creases beside his nose.

He was not fully under. That was the trouble.

He stood at the lip above the flooded lower bench, swaying toward the water while his sister clutched his arm from behind. Tears and rain ran together over his face. "Let me go," he was saying with terrible politeness. "Please. For God's sake, let me go. She's there. I can hear her clear as market bells."

His sister, Mara, was barefoot and bareheaded and still wearing one quilted oven mitt because she must have come straight from her cookstove. "Thom, no. Thom, no, you bastard, you'll leave the girls with me and I swear I'll hate you for it."

He shook her off so hard she fell to her knees in the mud.

Lale saw the exact measure of the danger then. He was not a blank sleeper to be turned by easier shine. He was a man split between grief and enchantment, conscious enough to beg and strong enough to obey the wrong thing anyway. The quarry water below him reflected the bell boy's lamp in a broken gold line. Every time the light shivered, Thom's body leaned after it.

Oren grabbed Lale's sleeve. "We need more men."

"We need him away from the edge."

"How?"

Thom looked at Lale as if he had only just noticed she existed. There was recognition in it and contempt and pleading, all mixed. "Ledger girl," he said, breath hitching. "You know me. Tell them. I only want one minute."

Mara dragged herself up by the bell post. "Don't listen to him."

Lale took one step forward. Mud slid under her heel. She stopped. Everything in front of her had become ugly in a way she understood too well. There was no clean argument that would carry over the sound the water was making in the cut, no rightful authority, and no decent version of restraint. Thom's daughter had knitted the scarf hanging off his throat. His sister was on her knees in quarry slime. The bell boy was crying while he rang, each strike an apology to someone he could not save.

Lale kept her voice level because if she let it fray, all of them would hear fear instead of instruction. "Thom. Look at me."

He did, obedient as a child for exactly one heartbeat.

"Your girls are down the hill," she said. "If you step in there, they get your body and the debt on your tools. That's what you're offering. Nothing finer."

His mouth twisted. "You don't hear her."

"No."

"Then fuck you for talking like you know the sum."

There it was. It was not ceremony or tragic nobility. It was an angry man wet to the bone, half seduced by water, cursing a woman he had probably once haggled over memorial stone thickness. It might almost have been easier if he had turned monstrous. Men like Thom never did. They stayed themselves just long enough to make the necessary thing feel like treachery.

He took another step backward toward the lip.

Mara screamed.

Lale moved before the thought finished. She flung the boat hook aside because it was too long for the distance and because some animal part of her refused to let wood make the decision for her. She hit the last stretch of mud at a run, slipped, caught herself on one hand, and came up into Thom's chest hard enough to jolt him half around. He smelled of wet stone and old sweat and the sour edge of panic. He grabbed at her shoulder as if she had come to help him jump.

"Please," he said right in her face now. "Please just let-"

She punched him.

It was not a good punch. Lale knew that even while it was happening. Her hand was wrong for it, too clever, used to needles and ledgers and stirring thick dye with a stick instead of meeting bone directly. She hit him across the mouth because that was where the plea was coming from and because his head was bent toward hers and there was no time to aim elsewhere. His teeth split the skin over her knuckles. Pain flashed bright and stupid all the way to her wrist.

But it turned him.

His body rocked sideways instead of back. Oren lunged from the left, all broker panic and surprising weight, and Mara caught Thom's coat from behind. The three of them went down in the mud in a knot of limbs and scarf and quarry grit. Thom made a sound Lale had never heard from an adult man at that pitch, more shocked than hurt. Then he started sobbing so violently he could not get breath between the sounds.

Blood ran off Lale's hand in thin diluted threads. Some of it was hers. Some of it was his. She could not tell which until she saw his lower lip hanging open and red.

The bell boy stopped ringing. Silence slammed into the quarry harder than the noise had.

Mara looked up at Lale from where she knelt over her brother's back. Her hair was plastered to her cheeks. The oven mitt was still on one hand. "You hit him."

"Yes," Lale said.

It was not apology. It was not defense. There was nothing to narrate away. Her fist hurt. Thom was alive. The quarry water had not taken him in the minute required to keep her hands clean.

Thom twisted under Mara and Oren, trying once more for the edge. Lale crouched, caught the yellow scarf, and yanked it free before it could strangle him. "Tie his wrists," she said.

Mara hesitated. Lale could see every decent teaching she had ever been given fighting the order.

"Tie them," Lale said again, and this time the plainness in her own voice startled her. There was no explanation and no measured clause in it, only the line.

Mara pulled the scarf through Thom's wrists and knotted it with fingers that shook so badly she had to do it twice.

When they dragged him downhill, the bell boy finally spoke. He could not have been more than fourteen. Freckles stood out raw on his wet face and one lens of his spectacles had cracked all the way through. "I thought if I rang loud enough they'd stop," he said.

Lale picked up the dropped lamp and gave it back to him. "Then ring when stone moves," she said. "Not when grief does."

He nodded as if she had named a trade rule and not merely survival.

Back at the grain hall the ugliness spread usefully. That was the part no one admitted later when they told the story as if trust had arrived out of nobility. Word went ahead of Lale that she had struck Thom Vesk bloody to keep him from the quarry. The tale traveled with the speed gossip always borrowed from fear. By the time she came through the hall doors with split knuckles and Thom bound in his own daughter's scarf, nobody was still pretending the work could stay gentle.

Families stopped saying only. They stopped saying tired, feverish, devout. They said hold him. They said use the blanket. They said if she runs, trip her. A cooper asked for rope without whispering. A woman tore up her own wedding linen for ties because the good sheets were closer than the old grain sacks. Cel Beren set a piss bucket in each aisle and told anyone scandalized to take it up with the river. Jori Pike's mother slapped her son twice when he lunged for the door, then cried into his hair, then tightened the knot on his wrist herself.

Lale hated how quickly the room improved once shame had somewhere concrete to go. She hated that the mark of authority on her now was blood drying tacky over her fingers. She hated, too, that part of her found relief in it. The hall had stopped testing her and started obeying. No one asked whether a woman should order men larger than herself. No one called the work sorcery. They had seen what hesitation cost at the quarry lip. They had chosen results.

She went to the wash barrel by the side door and dipped her hand in. The water pinked instantly. Her knuckles had opened in a neat row where Thom's teeth had caught them. Indigo stain still lived in the moons beside her nails, impossible to scour fully out even after years inland. Blood spread through the blue and made a color she had once mixed for mourning sashes after a child's drowning: not purple, not brown, something river-black in between. She remembered, with an abruptness that made the room tilt, how her mother used to test dye with bare fingers and say skin told the truth before cloth did.

Lale pulled her hand out of the barrel and wrapped it in a strip of clean sacking so she would not have to look at it.

Cel Beren came up beside her carrying a tray of lamp chimneys like severed throats. "Your nose is bleeding too," she said.

Lale touched her face. Thom must have clipped her on the back swing. Her fingers came away red under the rainwater. "Not badly."

"No." Cel set the chimneys down. "He'll hate you for a week."

"Only a week?"

Cel's mouth twitched. "After that he'll decide you saved him and hate the feeling more."

They stood in the hall stink together: wet grain, lamp oil, muddy wool, urine, blood, and the raw green smell of rainwater pooled too long on old boards. Bodies shifted and muttered around them. Someone began reciting numbers under his breath to keep from listening to his own wife pull at the ropes. Someone else laughed in one startled burst and then covered his mouth as if laughter itself were indecent here.

"They're asking whether you'll stay the night," Cel said.

"I'm staying the night."

Even contracted, the line sounded stiffer than she meant it to. Lale almost laughed at herself. Pressure tidied her diction first and stripped it later. She could feel the second stage waiting.

"No," Cel said. "I mean after. Till this passes."

Lale looked down the central aisle of the hall. Thirty people now, maybe more, counting the family members bedding down beside them rather than going home. Lamps glowed low along the boards. The false water she had built into the room kept the sleepers turning inward, restless but contained. It would not hold forever. Nothing on this route had. But it would hold long enough for the town to learn the shape of the work.

"You don't need me for forever," Lale said.

"We might."

That landed far harder than gratitude would have. Halcote had spent years wanting her legible, solvent, decorous. Useful in ways that kept her clean. Now Cel Beren, who had once rejected a whole bolt of black because the selvedge smelled too much like river reeds, was asking for a different version of utility altogether.

Lale did not answer because Oren Pike came striding down the aisle with rain on his shoulders and a set of decisions already irritating him.

"The lower basin has word from your sister," he said.

Lale straightened too fast and felt her bruised nose complain. "Riva?"

"A carter met them at South Sluice. They forced a partial run and doubled back for you. She wants poles if the town has them, long ones. Lamp oil too. Says the lower water's choking on debris and their ferry's too light for the next push without better leverage."

That was Riva all through: affection translated into requirements. Even a plea arrived disguised as a list of materials.

Lale asked, "How long?"

"An hour, maybe less, if the basin stays navigable."

Cel set the lamp chimneys aside. "If you go, I lose the only person in town who can read what these bastards are going to do before they do it."

"No," Lale said. "You lose the only person who came in already knowing. That isn't the same thing."

She looked around the hall at Anja Rusk, who had finally learned to step into Fenn's line instead of clutching at him; at Mara Vesk, who sat with Thom's bound wrists in her lap and did not soften when he pleaded now; at Jori's mother, who had begun setting fennel seed under her son's tongue only when he quieted, not before; and at the bell boy, spectacles cracked, moving lamps from door to aisle without being told.

Work had taught them faster than belief ever would.

"Can you keep the water talking in here?" Lale asked Cel.

Cel glanced toward the barrels and the drains. "Yes."

"Can you widen the lanes if more come in?"

"Yes."

"Can you stop relatives from calling it a miracle when it's only restraint?"

Cel barked her rough laugh again. "That one I can do all season."

Lale nodded. "Then you don't need me for knowing. You need me for beginning. That's done."

The grief on Cel's face was brief and practical, as grief often was in capable women with ten tasks behind it. "Then I'll need payment."

"Add it to the invoice."

"With interest."

"Obviously."

That got a real smile, small and unwilling.

When Lale walked back down to the basin, the rain had softened into a fine needling mist and Halcote had changed shape around her. The town had not transformed. She did not insult herself with that. It had changed. People moved when she told them to move. Doors opened before she reached them. A butcher's apprentice ran past carrying three lamp tins and called, "Miss An, the back drain's starting to clog," in the tone of someone reporting to a foreman rather than accusing a curse. A mule driver she had known for years only as a man who spat sunflower husks onto her invoices touched his cap and asked where the spare poles should go.

At the quay she found half the town already there.

They were not gathered to stare this time. They were gathered to load.

Quarry crews had brought six long stone poles, heavier than boat poles and rough with old hand grease, used for sounding flooded cuts along the quarry wall. Mill boys were rolling oil casks down planks with rags under the rims to stop sparks. Two carters had hitched flatbeds with spare yokes and bundles of woven blankets. Cel's nephew Fenn, feet bandaged, slept under an awning while his mother argued with a cooper over which rope knots came loose fastest under wet strain. The bell boy from the quarry stood beside a stack of lamp tins hugging them to his chest like books.

And Oren Pike was in the middle of it, coat off, ledger satchel abandoned on a crate, shouting counts over the noise with a voice Lale had never heard outside a contract dispute.

"No, the long poles, you idiot, unless you want them grounding out in the basin mud. Put the short ones on the second cart. And if anyone punctures those oil skins before they reach the ferry, I'll make you drink the loss."

Lale stopped on the boards and simply watched for a moment. The sensation was not triumph. It was stranger and more painful than that. She had spent years wanting Halcote to take her seriously in the tidy ways: as a woman whose ink did not blot, whose cloth weights were honest, whose mother could be omitted from the room. Now the town was taking her seriously with mud to the knees and blood under one fingernail, after she had broken a man's lip in public and ordered families to tie one another up. Respectability had never had this much muscle in it. Respectability had never moved this fast.

Riva's ferry came through the mist a minute later, black hull low in the water, Daro and Iven poling together off the stern while Nami crouched under the middle eave with her chin on her knees. The boat looked smaller against Halcote's brick basin than it had at Reedmouth, as if stripped-down things always did when set beside inland certainty. But it came straight, and Riva did not waste the landing.

"What have you got?" she called before the hull even kissed wood.

"A town," Lale said.

Iven barked a laugh from sheer exhaustion. Daro looked past Lale at the loading crews and then at her wrapped hand. Something unreadable moved across his face, half pride and half the old irritation he reserved for anyone else proving necessary. Nami sat up slowly and smiled with only one corner of her mouth.

"You smell like grain rot," she said.

"You smell like fever broth," Lale answered.

"Good. Then we're still ourselves."

Riva stepped off the ferry, took in the poles, the oil, the flatbeds, the laborers already waiting for instruction. Her gaze settled last on Lale's hand. She did not ask what had happened. That was another form of respect between them now, harsher and cleaner than comfort.

"How many can you spare?" she asked Oren Pike.

The broker sucked clove and looked as if the answer offended his own instincts. "Twelve at least. Maybe fifteen if I start voluntelling men with weak excuses. Three carts. Lamp oil enough for two nights if you stop using it like festival light. And the poles are on loan, mind."

Riva glanced at Lale. "He always this generous?"

"Only under duress," Lale said.

Oren sniffed. "Only when the alternative is the town drowning because you lot couldn't carry enough wood."

It was as close to allegiance as a man like him would ever speak in public. Lale heard the shift anyway. They were not cursing the family anymore, not quite. The siblings were necessary.

Cel Beren came down the slope with flour still on her sleeves and a bundle of rolled blankets over one shoulder. "You'll bring her back," she said to Riva, meaning Lale and not pretending otherwise.

Riva looked at Lale before she answered. The choice was still hers, which was the final measure of the change. Halcote wanted her. The family needed her. For years those two claims had pointed opposite directions. Now they ran into each other like floodwater meeting floodwater, neither clean, both real.

Lale climbed onto the ferry and felt the boards shift under her weight. "You'll manage the hall," she said to Cel.

"I'll hate every minute of it."

"Yes."

Cel adjusted the blanket bundle on her shoulder. "Thom Vesk's girls sent this for you."

She held out the yellow quarry scarf, washed badly and still pink at one edge where blood would not quite leave.

Lale stared at it. "That is an odd gift."

"Children are often odd." Cel pushed it into her good hand. "Take it. If he asks for it later, I'll tell him the river stole it."

For once Lale had no measured answer ready. She only nodded and tucked the scarf beside the boat hook.

Loading began in earnest. Quarry poles were laid along the gunwales, and oil skins went under tarred cloth. Two of the flatbeds would follow the river road where the bank held; the third could not, and its crew began shifting their load straight onto the ferry. The bell boy brought the lamp tins himself and set them down with ridiculous care. Oren Pike sent Jori's older cousin in his place when Lale objected to the number of workers he was stripping out of the town. "The cousin can pole," he said. "The nephew still walks in his sleep if the room goes too quiet. I'm not stupid."

No, Lale thought. He was only late.

As the basin filled with purposeful noise, she wrapped the yellow scarf once around her bleeding hand and once around the ferry rail to keep it close. The wool smelled faintly of smoke and wet stone and children's soap. A man she had punched in the mouth to keep him from drowning had sent her the thing his daughter had made. Nothing in it was tidy. Nothing could be entered cleanly into a ledger. Yet it weighed more, in the moment, than all the respectable years Halcote had given her.

Riva put a boot against the quay and shoved them off. "South road!" she shouted to the cart crews. "Keep pace where you can, cut across where you can't, and don't get precious about the wheels if the bank starts going. We need the oil more than we need the wood."

One of the quarry men grinned through the rain. He had a missing ear and a habit of rolling his shoulders before any hard lift, as if he were politely warning his own bones. "Hear that? We hired on under a cheerful captain."

"You hired on under necessity," Riva said. "Cheer is your own bad judgment."

That got the right kind of laughter: brief, workmanlike, no illusion that fear had left with it.

The ferry slid out of Halcote basin heavier than it had arrived, the long poles humming against the gunwale when they struck one another. On the quay, Cel Beren raised one arm without smiling. Oren Pike did not wave at all. He only stood with his coat open and his clove forgotten, watching the load-out as if memorizing a number he would later pretend had never frightened him.

Lale looked back until the grain warehouses blurred into mist and brick and then into a wet smear of inland light. Her hand throbbed under Thom Vesk's scarf. The quarry still breathed above the town, but now the hall would answer it with its own false mouth, its low lamps, its taught human cruelty made useful. Halcote would not call the An children blessed after this. Halcote was too sensible for that, even in fear. But sense had shifted under their feet. The town had seen the family arrive in a relic ferry with floodwater on their hems and leave carrying half its labor on the decks.

Necessary, Lale thought, and hated the pride in it less than she expected.

Riva handed her a pole without looking. "Can you still work?"

Lale slid the scarf tighter around her hand, set both feet, and took the weight. "Yes," she said. Then, because the day had taken enough polish out of her for one stretch of water: "Let's go."
