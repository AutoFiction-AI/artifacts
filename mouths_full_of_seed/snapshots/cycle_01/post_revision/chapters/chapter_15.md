# Chapter 15

By the time Daro brought Sereya's brass-ferruled pole back from Salt Room to Knot Bend, the pole had printed a green half-moon into the heel of his hand and the rumor had outrun his skiff.

The House of Oaths stood above the flood on its old pilings, half shrine, half rotten family carcass, with eel-smoke and wet mildew breathing out of the gaps in the boards. People had already crowded the landing and the walkways, pilgrims with prayer bowls tucked under their arms, Reedmouth fishers with rain capes pasted to their backs, Halcote families who had come downriver in grain carts because their sons and daughters had started rising from bed with water on the brain. The house had always drawn need the way a sore tooth drew the tongue. Today it had drawn panic too.

A girl of about twelve sat on an overturned net basket with her ankles tied loosely together in washline. Her mother had padded the rope with strips torn from a blue apron, but the skin above the knots was still rubbed raw. An old man in a hat patched with gull feathers held a covered birdcage in his lap, though there was no bird inside it. A cooper from Halcote kept turning his wedding ring around and around with a thumb thickened by hammer use until the skin went white. Nobody spoke above a murmur. They were all listening to the water under the house.

Daro heard more in it than the suck and slap against pilings. He heard breath where no lung ought to have been. He heard the drag of names.

Bato's voice from Salt Room was still in him, plain as a nail driven into green wood. The river never chose her. She chose the lie because people obey a chosen body faster than a shared burden.

If that was true, then why had the dead been in Daro's ear since boyhood? Why had the bells at Hymn Cut answered his throat and not Riva's hard hands or Lale's ledgers or anybody else's clean unbelief? Why had he spent half his life walking around with songs arriving in him whole, as if some wet black mouth below the planks had selected him for delivery? He could call it hunger. He could call it damage. He could even call it vanity if he wanted to cut himself cleanly enough. The voices still came.

He stepped up onto the porch rail above the landing. The pole made a hard, beloved weight in his palm. Brass gleamed at the tip under a skin of old salt. For one ugly second he felt as if he had stolen not only a relic but a posture, the exact rake of Sereya's shoulder when a room turned toward her. The shame of that went through him hot and low. So did the pleasure.

He planted the ferrule against the porch boards. The metal rang once.

Heads lifted.

"You've all heard five versions already," he said, and because he had not slept and the sea air from Salt Room had roughened his throat, the first line came out lower than usual, close enough to make them lean in. "Maybe ten. Work crews, route order, sleepwalkers, the dead coming back upstream with fruit stones in their mouths like the world's ugliest blessing. You've heard how careful we all need to be. You've heard how everyone has to do their part."

He let the pole tilt, caught it, and heard a woman gasp when the brass scraped the plank.

"I'm not here to tell you careful will save you."

The silence tightened. Somebody on the landing began to cry in the slow, embarrassed way of a person trying not to.

"A ferry doesn't move because everybody loves one another all at once," Daro said. "A ferry moves because one body takes the shove and the rest trust it enough not to capsize the damn thing. Somebody has to stand where the current hits first. Somebody has to answer."

The cooper from Halcote looked up. "And that's you?"

He asked it without mockery. That was the part that nearly undid Daro. The man looked like he wanted permission to believe.

Daro looked past him at the rooming house windows across the water, at the saint rags tied to the pilings, at the dark mouths of the returning channels. He did not have to fake the weight in his chest. He only had to choose what to name it.

"I'm Sereya An's son," he said. "I carry Ilyan Moor's throat. I've heard the drowned since I was small enough to sleep under a bench. If this flood wants a ferryman, it already knows my voice."

The old man with the empty birdcage made a sign against his chest. The girl with the tied ankles stared open-mouthed. Her mother whispered, "Please."

Daro felt the word land on him like a wet cloth. Please. It was what they had always brought him, even before this. Not money, not much of it. Not respect clean enough to keep. Just grief and hope knotted together badly and shoved toward his mouth for singing.

"I can take the last crossing," he said. "You don't need five heirs snapping at each other while the water climbs your stairs. You need one answer."

Movement ran through the crowd. Not assent yet. Recognition of shape.

Behind them a skiff slammed the landing hard enough to jar the whole walkway. Daro knew the rhythm of that impact before he turned. Riva came up first, wet to the thigh, chin scar white as fishbone, one hand still on the mooring post as if she might rip it out of the mud. Lale climbed after her with her skirt hitched clear and indigo under her nails. Iven came next, all loose posture and no looseness in the face. Nami's hair was damp and smoked-sour from too many fever nights. Tovin hauled herself up one-handed, arthritic fingers crooked around the rail, with Bato behind her, broad and salt-scarred, his bad shoulder making his coat hang wrong.

The porch seemed to narrow under Daro's feet.

Riva took one look at the crowd and understood everything. He saw it happen. Her mouth flattened, and the fury in her face sharpened into something colder because people were watching.

"Get down," she said.

He smiled before he meant to. He hated that she could still pull that reflex out of him, the old youngest-son impulse to turn command into performance. "No."

"Give me the pole."

"Also no."

Somebody laughed, frightened and eager. The sound moved through the landing like a spark through lint.

Riva came up one step onto the porch. "This isn't house business anymore. You're choking the yard. We need boats turned and lines sorted if we're getting back down clean."

Daro raised his voice just enough for the people on the lower walkway to hear. "You hear that? That's my sister's mercy. Everybody gets a task. Everybody gets wet. Everybody gets to die feeling included."

A few people laughed, because panic would always take a joke if it smelled like permission.

Riva's eyes fixed on him. "You're making them worse."

"I'm making it plain."

"Plain?" Bato said from the landing, rough sea-trader consonants blunted by half-deafness. "Plain is the river didn't choose shit."

The words struck the crowd crookedly. Several people turned to stare at him. A living Bato Serin was rumor enough to break a day on its own, and here he was, nearly white hair plastered to his temples, salt scars webbed over his cheeks, looking less like a ghost than like the kind of man a storm had chewed and forgotten to spit back.

Daro could feel his own heartbeat in the hollow above his collarbone. This was the edge. Step wrong and he would lose not only the room but the shape he had come here to cast.

"He told us what he had to tell us at Salt Room," Daro said. "I heard him. I heard every dead inch of it: shared burden, no chosen body, all of us muddy together until the flood decides to be fair." He let contempt roughen the last word. "Fine. Beautiful. Maybe that's even true in the long shape of things. But look at them."

He pointed the pole toward the landing, toward the tied-ankle girl and the man turning his ring, toward a woman clutching a sleepwalker son whose wrists were bound in dishcloth strips printed with pears.

"Look at what's standing here now. They don't need philosophy. They need somebody who'll step where it hurts first."

Riva came another step up. "That somebody won't be you alone."

"Why, because you can't measure it?" he asked. "Because if one body can answer, then all your hard useful grief isn't the only coin in the room?"

He saw it hit. He saw the old injury wake in her face, the one that always came when labor was not enough to settle a thing. He had aimed for it because he knew where it lived. A murmur went through the landing, hungry and mean, the sound people made when private damage finally gave them a public answer.

"Listen to me," Riva said, and now she was not talking to him at all but to the crowd. "Storm Mouth isn't a shrine door. It's a broken cut under crest water. We opened every other channel in order for a reason. If he goes down there with one pole and a room full of prayers, the last mouth will turn him into bait and take the rest of you after."

"Or," Daro said softly, and the softness did more than a shout would have done, "it will know the blood and the voice built for it."

Lale spoke before Riva could. "Built?" Her diction came out tidied flat, which meant anger. "Nothing about this was built for one heir. That was the whole point of the route."

"According to whom?" Daro asked. "A dead mapkeeper's guilt? A storm diver who hid in a wall for seventeen years? You want them to trust that over what they can actually see?"

He lifted the pole. Brass flashed dull gold in the damp light. The house, their mother's house, breathed rot and old prayers behind him. The shine ran through the crowd faster than any argument. Chins lifted. Hands went to mouths, to prayer cords, to the raw knots on bound wrists. By the time the brass dulled again, he had them looking where he wanted.

The woman with the pear-print dishcloths said, "If you take the crossing, will my boy stop hearing the water?"

Daro looked at the boy. He was perhaps nine, with one front tooth chipped and the knees of his trousers patched in a fabric that had once been curtains. His eyes were open but fixed somewhere below the planks.

"If the last cut opens right," Daro said, "the pull slackens. For him and the rest."

Riva snapped, "He can't promise that."

"Neither can you," the woman shot back, already turning toward Daro again because certainty, even borrowed certainty, had more heat in it than truth.

Tovin spat into the flood. "He's selling you throat music."

The cooper from Halcote shook his head once, miserable and stubborn. "Maybe throat music is what we've got."

That did it. Daro felt the room of them tip.

The old man with the empty birdcage stood with a grunt and lifted the cage as if making an offering. "One saint, one body," he said, not triumphant, only pleading for the world to become simpler than it was. "That's how a crossing works."

The phrase moved through the crowd with the speed of contagion. Not everyone repeated it. Enough did.

One saint, one body.

The tied-ankle girl whispered it to herself first, testing the taste. Then her mother said it louder. Then somebody on the rear walkway took it up with the cadence of prayer. Daro should have felt victory. What he felt was the cold instant after a hook set properly, when the line goes taut and you know the fish is yours or you are its.

Riva looked at him as if she had watched him put a knife into his own throat and keep smiling around the blade.

"Get out of my way, then," he said to her, because he could not back out now and stay whole in front of them. "Or come watch."

She did not move. Neither did he. The house held its breath between them while below the planks the dark water knocked and knocked.

***

Riva could have hit him.

The thought came simple as weather. One fist to the mouth, drag him down off the porch by the collar, get the pole out of his hands before the crowd could decide they'd witnessed martyrdom instead of necessary family violence. Her shoulder even twitched with it. Then she saw the landing again, all those faces stretched thin with flood fear, and knew the punch would consecrate him.

So she climbed onto the fish gutting table beside the net shed instead, where everybody could see she had chosen wood and work over theater, and hated him harder for forcing that choice into shape.

By then the yard had already begun sorting itself. Daro's people did not look like a proper congregation yet. They looked worse, because they looked useful to panic. A woman was tying saint rags to the porch rails. Two boys were hanging cracked prayer bells from the lintel. Somebody had propped Sereya's pole against the front post as if the house had grown a spine back. Every few breaths another hand went up to touch the brass.

Riva made herself look away.

The table under her boots was slick with old scales and rain. Tovin's iron gate key knocked against her thigh where she had hooked it through her belt rope. Lale stood below with her forearm bare, Paska's tide marks dark against damp skin. Iven had already dragged a coil of line and three punting poles into the yard. Bato leaned on the shed corner, one shoulder low, watching the channel mouth with the narrowed, insulted attention of a man who trusted water only when he could insult it first. Nami hovered between house and shed, fingers flexing against the bandage on her palm.

Riva raised both hands for quiet. The crowd gave her some of it, though not the good part. The good part was with Daro.

"Storm Mouth still needs boats, anchors, poles, and people who can keep their feet under them," she said. No preamble. No blessing. "If you want the dead moving seaward instead of back into your wells, I need six skiffs stripped light, every usable lantern in this yard, and anybody who can stand in waist water without turning foolish."

A murmur. Not refusal yet. Calculation.

"You heard Bato," she went on. "You heard me. The route was split on purpose. That wasn't poetry. That was engineering. Labor plus bond, keeper by keeper, or the cut bucks everything backward. We have four channels settled behind us and one last mouth ahead. We don't fix that with a single pretty death."

Someone from near the porch called, "What if it isn't death?"

Riva looked and saw the cooper, ring still turning on his thumb. Sweat had pasted his shirt to his chest. He looked like a man who had already spent every better version of hope.

"Then good," she said. "I'd like him alive. But hoping won't trim the current."

Daro's voice came over the yard warm as eel smoke. "Listen how she sells it. Doesn't that make your heart leap."

Laughter, thin and guilty. Riva didn't look at him. If she did, she'd lose the thread.

"I'm not selling anything," she said. "I'm telling you what the water charges."

That landed with the people who had already paid. She saw it in their faces before they moved. A Lantern Quay widow with a red wool flower pinned absurdly to her rain hood stepped away from the porch first. Riva remembered her from the communal supper at Lantern Quay not because of her name, which she had forgotten, but because she had salted fish with one hand while holding her grandson on the other hip as if the body simply had more grips than most bodies did. A mill hand from Halcote followed, flour still clotted pale in the cuffs of his trousers. Then the mother with the pear-print dishcloths around her son's wrists. Not because she trusted Riva more. Because the lie had stopped comforting her as soon as she heard it used on her child.

Others held back.

"If we do your work," said a man with eel tattoos faded blue over both knuckles, "what do we get that he isn't already offering?"

Riva almost said the truth, which was no spectacle, less delusion, and a smaller chance of being dragged under. It would have sounded like punishment.

"A chance that isn't built on feeding one of us to the cut," she said instead.

The man's face did not change. Desperate people could hear chance and translate it straight into maybe not.

Lale climbed up beside the table without asking. Her careful posture made the table look dirtier by contrast. "The route was distributed because singular inheritance turned service into rule," she said. Her voice was dry enough to survive scrutiny even now. "You've already seen what bad order does. Marrow Market got bodies in the reeds because we rushed what was supposed to settle. If you hand the last mouth to one heir because fear likes a single face, you're not solving the flood. You're repeating the disease."

Some heads turned toward her. More toward Daro.

Iven snorted and pitched a pole from one hand to the other. "If any of you were hoping holiness meant no hauling, I should tell you the water doesn't give discounts. Pick whoever you like, you'll still need boats."

That won a few grim sounds that might have been laughter. He saw them and pushed harder. "I've watched my brother make a room cry from ten paces since we were children. Good for him. Still can't pole three skiffs at once with one pretty throat."

Riva caught the flash of pain that went over Daro's face across the yard, quick as a fish turning under muddy water. She knew Iven had seen it too and hated him a little for using it, though he was on her side. Everybody here was using damage like rope.

Tovin did not bother climbing anywhere. She just barked from the mud, "Anybody who wants a miracle can drown waiting for one. Anybody who wants the cut open, over here. Full names if you know them, so I know who I'm cursing when you drop a line."

That got more movement than Riva's speech had. Tovin always sounded like labor already underway.

Bato added only, "Storm Mouth won't marry him. It'll eat him."

Plain nouns. He had that gift.

Nami was the one Riva watched hardest. The girl had gone wax-pale listening to Daro on the porch, as if some part of her still wanted the world to narrow kindly around one marked body and let everybody else go home. Riva understood the temptation well enough to despise it. Shared burden was uglier. Shared burden meant you got wet too.

Nami came closer to the table, eyes on Riva, not the crowd. "If we do it your way," she asked, "does that mean nobody has to disappear?"

It was the wrong question for comfort and the right one for the moment. Riva answered before she could sand the edges off it.

"No," she said. "It means nobody gets to disappear clean and call that mercy."

Nami took that in. Her mouth worked once. Then she nodded and picked up a lantern crate with her good hand under the base and her bandaged palm braced against the side.

That mattered more than half the yard.

Once the first dozen crossed, the shape of the split showed itself. Daro kept most of the watchers, the ones who still believed the right voice could spare them from needing their own bodies. He got pilgrims, saint-hungry townspeople, and the merely terrified. Riva got the ones with proof already under their fingernails. Families who had tied down sleepwalkers. Reedmouth fishers who had poled corpse-ferries backward for a week and knew what prayer weighed when it went waterlogged. Halcote men and women who had stood in the grain hall all night while their kin walked into the lantern lines and cried with somebody else's voice. People who did not ask whether the work would be beautiful.

It was still not enough.

Riva counted twice anyway. Four skiffs, not six. Nine solid pole-hands if she included herself and Tovin. Three children old enough to carry lamp oil without dropping it. Lale for maps and sequence. Iven for anything reckless and fast. Bato for the water's mood, if he agreed to stay near enough to hear. Nami, fever-thin but stubborn, loading lanterns. Enough to keep trying. Not enough to make trying glamorous.

Across the yard, Daro had come down off the porch. People moved toward him in a pressure she recognized with perfect unwilling clarity: the exact communal lean Sereya had once pulled from rooms, that animal wish to be near the person who seemed most likely to turn fear into meaning. A man knelt to ask for a blessing on a ferry hook. A woman held up a sleeping baby so Daro could touch its forehead. He kept his face soft, solemn, almost unwilling. That was the part he had from their mother, not the singing. The ability to make appetite look like service from the outside.

Riva tied down that thought before it got sentimental. She knew his hunger too well to pretty it up.

"Move," she told her side. "We strip the skiffs now. No benches we don't need. Oil the line. Tie down anything that'll bang in a cross-current. Lale, I want the sequence said aloud every time we load or cast off, I don't care if we all sound like fools. Bato, you see the approach shift, you tell me before the water does. Iven, stop grinning at people and get me anchor stones."

"I wasn't grinning," Iven said.

"Your face was thinking about it."

That got a real laugh from two of the Halcote women, quick and raw. Good. She would take any sound that came from the body instead of the myth.

Still, when she jumped down from the table and the old wood shuddered under her boots, she knew she had lost the yard. Not all of it. Enough of it. Daro had the house, the relic, the phrase people were already repeating like something old and holy instead of something born here in fear less than half an hour ago.

One saint, one body.

She heard it drift from the porch while her people hauled lanterns and pole-hooks through the mud. It made her want to put her fist through the wall. Instead she shouldered a crate of oil jars and carried it to the skiffs herself, because carrying was the only register left to her that nobody could steal.

***

By nightfall the House of Oaths smelled like a shrine again, and Daro knew he was in trouble.

Not because the room loved him. That had happened before in little ways, at wakes and river burials and chapel vigils when grief wanted a mouth shaped like his to move through. Trouble began when love turned industrious.

They had lit every lamp the house could hold. Rain tapped the roof. Floodwater boomed under the planks with a slower, heavier breath than it had in the morning. Saint rags hung dripping from the rafters. Somebody had found Sereya's old brass bowl and filled it with blackwater, fennel leaves, and three coins that had gone green around the edges. The pole stood upright near the long family table, lashed to a support beam with prayer cloths and one child's red bead bracelet. Offerings kept appearing at its base: a tin crucible spoon, a braid of hair tied with fishing line, a bone whistle, a ferry token rubbed blank by decades of fingers.

Daro sat on the bench by the east wall because standing much longer would have made his knees show their tremor. People came to him one after another and he let them, because turning them away now would have looked like doubt and because some part of him had wanted this so long that denying it would have felt like lying in the wrong direction.

A woman with one eyebrow singed off from an old stove flare rubbed eel oil into his throat with two careful fingers and said it was what her grandmother had done for ferrymen before flood crossings. Her hands smelled of onion and lampblack. An inland clerk with spectacles tied together by string pressed a list of the drowned into Daro's palm and asked him to keep it dry in his coat if he went out. The old man with the empty birdcage had set the cage under the table and was now carving tiny notches into the cane handle with a bait knife, one for each person he wanted remembered. Every so often he lifted his head as if listening for the bird anyway.

Each touch went through Daro in a different register. Some of it was tenderness. Some of it was power. Some of it was plain body, ugly and alive, the heat of attention settling low in him because devotion and desire had never stayed politely separate in the house Sereya built. When the woman with the eel oil let her fingers rest at the hollow of his throat, he did not move them. The line of petitioners only tightened.

Nami found him there after full dark with a cup of boiled reed tea and her hair braided badly enough that he knew she had done it herself. She held the cup out without smiling.

"Drink before you go holy and forget you've got a body," she said.

He took it. The tea tasted of mud and bitter leaves and the soot of a pan that had not been scrubbed right. "That's rich from you."

"I know."

She stayed standing, shoulder against the wall post. In the lamplight the old river rash scars at her ankles looked silver. Somebody in the front room had started a prayer chant on the phrase the old man had given them, and each repetition made the floorboards vibrate.

One saint, one body.

Nami rolled her eyes toward the ceiling. "I liked it better before they started harmonizing."

Daro laughed into the cup and immediately wished he hadn't. Laughter made the whole thing feel thinner, and he needed thickness tonight.

"You came to talk me out of it?" he asked.

"No." She picked at a splinter in the post. "I came to see if you knew what you were doing or if you were just being you in public."

That was mean enough to be sibling speech and honest enough to hurt. He appreciated both.

"What's the difference?" he asked.

"Usually about six bodies and a broken boat."

He drank the rest of the tea. "Bato gave you his sermon, then."

"He gave me facts." Her eyes lifted to him. Open, dark, embarrassing in how little they hid. "Facts don't stop wanting. That's the trouble."

Daro looked toward the front room where three women were knotting saint cloth around the pole. One of them had brought a sleepwalking husband down from Halcote with a harness strap round his waist because ordinary rope had cut him bloody. Another was one of the Reedmouth crabbers who used to flirt with Daro at the eel market by correcting his hymn lyrics on purpose. They were not stupid people. They just wanted the world to choose a shape before it drowned them.

"Shared burden is true," he said. "It's just too large for a frightened room to hold."

"So you'll give them something smaller and wrong."

"I'll give them something they can walk toward."

Nami's mouth moved like she wanted to spit and kiss him at once, a family trait and a miserable one. "You sound like her."

"Maybe she sounded like a ferryman because ferries need one."

"Or maybe she sounded like that because people touch the chosen body before they touch each other, and it's easier." Nami pushed off the wall. "I wanted it to be one person too. I still do, some of the time. That doesn't make it right."

She left before he could answer. He watched the bad braid swing down her back and felt the smallest possible crack of uncertainty move through him. Then the front door banged open and brought the weather in with it.

Riva, Tovin, Lale, Bato, and Iven came through soaked to the elbows, smelling of salt and cut reed. Mud striped Riva from knee to hip. Tovin's forearm burns shone slick in the lamplight. Bato carried a snapped reed pole under one arm like evidence. Iven had a fresh scrape along his jaw and three anchor hooks banging from one fist.

The room quieted by degrees. Daro stood because everybody else looked ready to.

Riva didn't waste breath. "Outer reed chain's breaking," she said. "We took a scout run as far as the split below Lantern Quay. Half the marker posts are already under. The sea pull's started chewing the back channels."

People stared. Tovin stepped forward and slapped the snapped pole onto the table hard enough to splash blackwater out of Sereya's bowl.

"There won't be another patient night after tomorrow," she said. "You have one daylight and one dark worth naming. After that the approach to Salt Room goes bad for anything but madness and driftwood."

Bato nodded once. "Less than two nights."

Lale stripped the scarf off her forearm and laid the tattooed skin flat under the lamp. The tide marks ran glossy and dark, a map written into flesh. "She's right," she said. "The bar is already pulling crooked. If we wait through another full cycle, larger skiffs won't clear the side channel, and the ones that do will come in wrong to the mouth."

The words should have made the room less theatrical. They did the opposite. Deadline loved a doctrine.

Voices rose at once.

"Then he has to go tomorrow."

"No, tonight."

"What about the sleepers?"

"What about the children?"

"Can the boats make it?"

"Can all five of them make it?"

Riva turned in a full circle and bellowed, "Quiet." The room obeyed because her command still had work in it. "If we move before first light, we might still get ahead of the worst break. That means no shrine games. We load now. We leave with crew discipline. Nobody touches Storm Mouth before we set the line and reset the chain together."

Riva's last word hit the rafters and came back thin. The women knotting saint cloth around the pole let the cloth sag between their hands. Two men by the door stopped pretending to study the tide and turned outright toward the brass.

Daro watched their eyes leave Riva and collect at the beam where the pole stood. Together meant delay. Together meant shared exposure. Together meant there was no blessed throat in the center taking the first blow for them. Bato's facts had sharpened the need that had filled the house all day, not cured it.

The old man with the birdcage stood again, shaking a little. "If there's less than two nights, then what are we doing still arguing? Let the heir go."

Not the ferryman anymore. The heir. Public doctrine with blood under it.

Riva rounded on him. "There's no heir."

"There's one if the room needs one," Daro said.

He hadn't meant to say it aloud. Once said, it sounded more honest than anything else he had offered them all day.

Lale looked at him as if he had finally admitted to theft in proper terms. "Need doesn't square a bad count. A lie's still a lie, and it still tips the boat."

"No," Daro said. "But it gets them into the boat before dawn."

Iven gave a bark of laughter with no joy in it. "Listen to him. Went and found himself a doctrine word."

"Shut up, Iven."

"Gladly, if you'd shut up first."

The exchange cut some of the room's fear with sibling ugliness, but not enough. People were already turning back toward Daro. A mother in a netted cap pushed her way forward carrying a little boy so asleep he hung loose as laundry from her arms. His socks were mismatched, one yellow, one gray. "If he goes as heir," she asked Daro, not Riva, "do I come with the prayer boats or stay behind?"

Heads turned from Riva to the door, to the pole, to the bundles at their feet. Hands went to sacks, prayer bowls, sleeping children. A man near the stove started counting boats under his breath.

Daro looked at the child, at the damp hair pasted to his temple, at the shadow of pulse in the throat. He could not tell her the truth, which was that he did not know whether any of them were heading toward miracle or a more ornate form of drowning. He could tell her what action fit the shape he had built.

"Those who come with me come light," he said. "No heavy cargo. No children near the edge boats. We move on the falling tide and keep the center clear."

Riva made a sound like metal failing.

"You hear yourself?" she said. "You're not the tide master. You're not the route. You're a singer with a pole you stole."

He met her eyes. "I'm the one they can move behind."

"And when the cut tears you open?"

"Then at least it'll be doing it to the right body."

That was the line they wanted. He felt the intake around the room as if every lung had become one lung.

He also felt Nami's stare from the wall, horrified and pitying at once. Good. Somebody should be.

Bato spoke into the hush. "Boy."

Daro turned.

Bato's nearly white hair was dripping on his collar. He did not raise his voice. He never had to. "A mouth doesn't care who thinks he belongs in it."

Daro answered before he could choose better. "Then why have I heard it all my life?"

Bato's face changed, not much. Enough to show old guilt under the salt scars. "Because damage echoes," he said. "That isn't the same thing as being named."

The room heard him. The room rejected him. Daro watched it happen and understood, with a clarity so ugly it almost felt holy, that Bato's truth had no handles on it. It could not be carried by a frightened crowd. It asked too much imagination from people whose floors were already under water.

So Daro gave them the handle he had been carving all day.

"Falling tide before dawn," he said. "Prayer boats in front, work skiffs behind if they'll come. We make for Storm Mouth before the second dark takes the choice away."

The room broke into motion. Not orderly motion. Better than that. Believing motion. People reached for lamp oil, saint cloths, ferry hooks, dry bread wrapped in rags. Someone started the chant again and this time the timing had purpose in it, footfall and loading rhythm and the hot relief of no longer having to stand still.

One saint, one body.

Riva stepped toward him as if she might still stop it by force. Tovin caught her elbow hard enough to make the bench scrape.

"Not here," the old woman muttered. "You swing on him now, they carry him out like relic bone."

Riva's chest rose and fell once. She looked around the room, at the believers and the workers and the people trying to be both. When she spoke, it was not to Daro.

"Strip the fast skiffs," she told her own side. "We chase them clean. No extra cargo. Lantern covers tied down. If they want a procession, they can have one with us at their stern."

Tovin let Riva go and shoved past the nearest believers toward the side door. Iven followed with the anchor hooks clanging against his leg, and the room split its traffic around them, one current surging toward Daro and the other toward the skiffs.

Daro took the pole from its lashings. The brass ferrule was warm now from all the hands that had touched it. Outside, under the house, floodwater drove hard against the pilings and carried up the first clean salt smell from the sea. It should have smelled like warning. Tonight it smelled like an answer given too late to take back.

When he stepped onto the porch, the yard below had already split. His prayer boats were loading by the saint rags and the front landing. Riva's people were levering the old funeral ferry into the side channel under Tovin's curses and Lale's exact count. Between them the house stood up on its rotten legs as if it had always meant to become a courthouse for bad doctrine.

Less than two nights.

Less than that, probably.

Daro lifted the pole and the crowd below answered him with their whole frightened throats. Then the first stolen skiff shoved off into the black channel, pilgrim punts nosing after it, and the rest of them began to follow.
