# Chapter 10

At first light, after guarding the recovered page under her palm all night, Lale spread it on a fish-scaling table at Lantern Quay and weighted its corners with oyster stones because the paper kept trying to curl back into whatever small hiding place had held it for years. The night before had left the whole village tacky. Fish scales clung to the plank grain in silver commas. Rain had damped the ash out of the cookfires and pasted it into the seams. The old reed ledger sweated under her palms as if it recognized the missing piece and disliked being made whole.

The torn edge matched exactly.

That was the first obscenity of it. Jun's name would wound her in a moment, and the thought of what the page might say already sat in her throat, but the fit struck first. Seventeen years of silence and mold and pocket grease, and the fibers still knew where they belonged.

She held the ledger flat with her left hand. Paska's fresh tide marks crossed her forearm in dark wet arcs, their skin still a little raised when the air went damp. The missing page smelled faintly of fish oil, old smoke, and a sweeter thing underneath, clove or pomade, whatever life it had lived after leaving the book and before Iven carried it back from Sena in his shirt. Lale did not ask for those details. She had enough of other people's handling on the paper already.

The family had crowded around without speaking. Riva stood in the doorway of the fish shed with one shoulder against the jamb, broad as a gatepost, her forearms still wet from checking lines at the quay. Daro had come too close, of course, leaning over the table with his singer's throat bare to the damp air, as if beauty might get him admitted to the page before the rest of them. Iven sat on an upturned bait bucket with his elbows on his knees, his gold tooth gone dull in the morning light. He had the used face of a man who had been awake all night for reasons he would not share if he could help it. Nami hovered beside Lale's right shoulder, her bandaged hand tucked against her stomach, dark eyes fixed not on the page but on the tear where it would join the ledger. Tovin was farther back at the open side of the shed, wrapping fresh eel hide around the handle of a gaff hook because she could not sit near a loose tool without fixing it.

No one told Lale to begin. That would have been easier.

She said, "If any of you speak over the words, I will stop."

Iven rubbed his jaw. "Then read fast."

"That's not the same thing," she said.

Riva did not look away from the rain-gray water outside. "Read it clean."

Daro laid two fingers on the tabletop, close enough that Lale had to resist the urge to move the ledger farther from him. "Jun's name has waited long enough."

Nami said, very softly, "Was it large in the book before?"

Riva kept her eyes on the water. Iven scraped a thumbnail along the lip of the bait bucket. Daro only pressed his fingers harder into the tabletop. Lale wished one of them would answer. The question sat in the shed like another wet garment, something intimate left hanging where strangers could see it.

She fitted the page to the ledger's gap. The reed-thread binding had been cut so neatly years ago that it still shamed her to remember her own hand doing it. She had not torn blind with panic. She had lifted the stitch with a needle, eased the page free, folded it once, then twice, as carefully as if she were protecting something delicate instead of burying it. Lale had always wanted her sins to look like order.

Sereya's hand slanted across the page in two different moods. The upper half carried the mother's usual braided script, every stroke too lush for bookkeeping, as if even a route note had to arrive dressed for company. Lower down the letters tightened. The loops closed. The words lost appetite and became account. Lale knew that change in a ledger hand. It was what came after shortage, after death, after a trader stopped pretending there would be enough and began counting what was gone.

She read the first lines aloud.

"Storm Mouth is no common cut. It shuts on strangers and chews every rope we give it. Bato says wait till the dry pull. He thinks patience and fear are the same muscle."

Daro exhaled through his nose, a sound with too much meaning packed into it. Lale ignored him and kept going.

"The dead are already backing in the reeds. The sea road needs a keeper of our own flesh if it is to stay. Jun sleeps when the pull begins. He does not startle. He smiles into spray. The mouth knows him."

Nami's nails bit lightly into Lale's sleeve. The bandage around her palm smelled of old vinegar and medicinal leaf.

Lale read the next sentence because that was what evidence demanded, because stopping before the worst line did not make the worst line less true.

"Tonight I carry him between us and bind our blood to the ferry nails. If the sixth mouth takes hold, no child of mine will ever row the dead by hand again."

The shed stayed silent except for gulls fussing behind the racks and the soft pull of tide under the pilings. Even the village dogs had gone quiet.

Riva pushed off the doorway. "Read the rest."

Lale looked down. Sereya's ink had feathered where water had hit it. A smear crossed two lines, but grief had sharpened the writing underneath. She could still make most of it out.

"It failed before dawn. The stern dropped. The cradle line tore wet. Jun went under where no lantern held. Bato dove till his ears bled and came up with weed, one sock, and my own screaming in his face. Storm Mouth takes what is offered and what is only loved."

Lale stopped reading because her mouth had dried out. She swallowed, tasted fish salt and ash, and continued anyway.

"I have written his name so I cannot lie later."

The last section had no ornament left in it at all.

"Jun An. Sixth mouth. Lost in the failed holding at Storm Mouth."

Iven shut his eyes briefly. It was not theatrical. That made it worse. His hands hung loose between his knees, those quick dishonest hands that had sold the page once for money and pleasure or drink or one more night's escape, whatever the price had been then. Lale wondered if he had remembered the exact weight of the paper all these years. She hoped he had.

Daro said, "That's not confession."

Riva turned on him. "She took a baby to Storm Mouth."

"She tried to hold the sea road open," Daro said. His voice had gone low and velvety, the register he used when he wanted a room to come nearer instead of retreating. "Don't flatten that because you're frightened by what it cost."

"I'm frightened by what she called necessary."

"That isn't the same thing."

Lale said, "It's a record of method followed by loss. The order matters."

Daro looked at her as if precision itself were a kind of blasphemy. "No. What matters is that she wrote his name so she wouldn't let the town turn him into weather. She didn't hide him."

Lale nearly laughed at that, and the laugh would not have been kind. "One page was hidden quite thoroughly."

The muscle in Iven's cheek moved. "You don't have to make the joke worse."

"I'm not joking."

Nami leaned closer to the page. "What does it mean, the sixth mouth?"

Lale could have answered as a bookkeeper: a count, five living children and a sixth. She could have answered as the girl who had once stared at the same words until the room tipped and the urge to erase became stronger than the urge to understand. Instead she looked at the script again and found that her mother's phrasing had already made the answer poisonous. Mouth could mean channel, child, or an opening that could be fed and made to hold. Sereya had written the family and the route in the same grammar.

Riva said, "It means she stopped calling him her son long enough to use him as part of the work."

"No." Daro's hand came down flat on the table. "It means she believed blood could keep the passage. It means the river already knew him."

Iven gave a small broken sound that might have become a laugh in a different chapter of his life. "There. That's the hymn starting."

Daro's eyes cut to him. "Say what you mean."

"I mean you've had the page less than ten minutes and you're already polishing it into a hymn."

"You're the one who sold it."

Lale's thumb pressed hard enough into the margin to bow the damp paper. Riva's head turned toward Iven. He took it without flinching, which was almost more alarming than any defense would have been. "Yes," he said. "I did."

Tovin's eel-hide strip tightened under her fingers until it creaked. She had not looked up once while Lale read, but at Jun's name her flattened nose had gone white around the bridge, and when Lale reached the line about the sock, the old woman's jaw had worked as if she were chewing river mud.

Nami asked, "Was he little little, or just little?"

Daro stared at her. Riva did too, though with less surprise. Lale understood the question at once and hated that she did. There were children one could imagine becoming people before memory set around them, and there were children who stayed all softness and dependency, impossible to turn into symbol without first committing an obscenity.

Daro looked at Nami as if she had opened a gut on the table. Riva's jaw set. Iven dropped his eyes to the space between his boots.

Nami looked from face to face, cheeks coloring. "I mean, did he walk."

"No," Tovin said from the open side of the shed.

The single word split the whole room more cleanly than the page tear had.

Tovin set the gaff hook down. The fresh eel hide shone dark around the handle where she had worked it tight. "He didn't walk," she said. "He kicked. Different thing."

Riva's gaze shifted to her at once. Lale felt the change in the room like a tray tilting. They had all heard it. Knowledge that did not come from the page. Knowledge with hands on it.

Daro straightened. "You knew."

Tovin picked up the hook again, tested the wrap with her thumb, and only then met their eyes. Her forearms smelled of smoke and old burns even across the shed. "I knew enough to hate hearing the rest come out loud."

Riva said, "Enough for what?"

Tovin's mouth hardened. "Enough for the next question, and I know you well enough to hear it forming."

The tide moved under the pilings with a breathy sucking sound. Somewhere up the quay a child shouted and was hushed at once. Lale looked down at the page again. Jun's name sat there in the blunt lower script, ink sunk hard into reed paper. She remembered the night she had cut the binding. Not the whole thing yet. Only the sensation of her own hands moving with unnatural calm, the way one packed away stained linen before guests could see it.

Evidence did not care whether the room wanted consolation, accusation, or myth. It went on being true under all of them. That was one reason Lale trusted it more than memory. Memory dressed for the weather. Paper kept the damage in its original shape.

Riva said, "Bring that to the boat."

Lale lifted the page before the damp could warp it further. Tovin did not object. That frightened her more than if the old woman had.

They left the fish shed with Jun back in the ledger and no one any steadier for it.

* * *

The Bitter Thread shoved off from Lantern Quay under a sky the color of scraped tin. Rain had not started again, but the whole morning felt braced for it. Riva took the tiller because steering gave her hands one job at a time and because if she let Daro or Iven near the wheel in the state they were in, she would end the day up on a mudbank explaining the angle of disaster to a salvage broker.

The dredge barge hated Reedmouth water in high monsoon. The Bitter Thread had been built for Halcote salvage runs, for weight and hooks and steadier channels than these breathing delta cuts. Its hull muttered at the reed islands when the current shifted underneath. The patched intake coughed weed stink into the pilothouse. Every submerged post sounded slightly different when the barge slipped past it, and Riva had learned to listen hard enough that sound became shape.

The ledger lay on the shelf behind her, wrapped now in a square of oilcloth and pinned under her starboard wrench. Iron made her feel better about paper. It did not make her safer. It only gave the feeling somewhere solid to sit.

Tovin stood just outside the pilothouse under the awning, rubbing lampblack grease into her arthritic knuckles before the damp seized them completely. She had finished the gaff hook wrap and moved on to a split handrail no one else had noticed. Riva had seen that habit since childhood. Tovin grieved by maintenance. If the world hurt her, she tightened a fitting, rewound a handle, patched a gate tooth, salted an eel. It was one of the few languages Riva trusted.

That meant the silence from her felt less like ignorance than refusal.

Riva kept the bow between two leaning posts and said, "You were there after."

Tovin did not look up from the rail. "After is a wide country."

"You know what I mean."

"I do. I'm deciding whether you do."

The barge brushed a drift of reeds. Their wet leaves whispered down the side like fingers on a washboard. Riva corrected half a point to port.

"Say it straight," she said.

Daro appeared in the pilothouse door behind her, one hand on the frame. He had changed shirts, but not into anything plainer. Even exhausted, he dressed as if a witness might matter. "Straight is not the same as true."

Riva did not turn. "I didn't ask for a song."

"And I didn't offer one."

Lale came up from below with the oilcloth-wrapped ledger against her ribs. Her face had set into the expression she used when people threatened to smear order with feeling. Iven trailed her carrying a coil of wet line he did not seem aware he was carrying. Nami remained out on deck, sitting where the winch housing gave off a little trapped warmth against the chill in her bones, but Riva could feel her listening through the boards.

Tovin capped the grease tin with her teeth, spat the lid clean into her palm, and tucked it away. "Fine. You want fact. Here's the fact I had."

She leaned one hip against the rail. The old wood groaned under her weight and the river answered from below.

"Sereya came back from the sea edge after sunrise with milk dried through the front of her shirt and salt caked in her eyebrows. She had torn both palms open on ferry nails. There was a piece of brass ferrule lodged in her thigh deep enough I could see the shine every time she stepped. She had Jun's blanket. She did not have Jun."

The pilothouse seemed to get smaller around Riva. Diesel, wet rope, the sour copper of her own mouth, all of it crowded together.

Tovin went on because once she had entered a task she rarely stopped halfway.

"Bato had gone under after the boy. Folk said he was dead by nightfall. Maybe he was, maybe he wasn't. I never saw him come back. Sereya did not cry the way other people cry. She kept wringing that blanket as if there was still water in it to get out. There wasn't any. She'd wrung it dry already, and she just kept doing it."

Nami made a small sound from the deck, not speech yet.

Riva said, "And then?"

Tovin's gaze sharpened on her. "And then she sat in my eel shed for three hours without blinking at me, the gate, or the smoke under the rafters. Then she said she would not keep the rest of you in one house."

Iven laughed once, a mean little bark at himself more than at Tovin. "A fine family policy."

Tovin ignored him. "She said she'd made a mouth of the whole damned family in her head, not just Jun. The next week she watched Riva walk the ferry rail with that long pole like the current was holding her up, and she went white as salt. Two days after that she caught Lale tracing route marks in fish blood on my table and near tore the rag out of her hand. After that every odd thing any of you did looked like the river reaching. She said if she kept you together, she'd start sorting you for signs. She'd start choosing."

The Bitter Thread entered a narrower cut. Riva laid more weight on the tiller. On either side, reeds pressed close enough that their seedheads dragged the awning.

Lale said, "Scattered where?"

Tovin's face did something ugly and tender at once. "Anywhere that broke the pattern: work boats, shrine cots, inland rooms with women who liked ledgers better than families, kin who could be bribed for a season, men who owed favors. She wanted you too scattered to make one bright line she could stare at and call fate."

Lale's thumb tightened on the ledger through the oilcloth.

Riva heard those words as hardware rather than grief or confession. Tovin had described a pattern, a bright line, a thing sighted and aligned. She thought of years spent cursing fathers, currents, money, chance. She had spent years believing the family had splintered because floodwater and appetite took whatever was loose. But someone had laid hands on the separation itself. Someone had decided distance was safer than risk and carried that decision out child by child.

She remembered whole seasons of sleeping in salvage lofts that smelled of wet rust and men she'd never call uncle. She remembered coming back to Knot Bend older and broader and finding new absences arranged as if absence were a tidy house custom. Daro had been sent somewhere holy. Lale was inland and careful. The younger ones moved through borrowed rooms until adults answered questions with a shrug and a new name. Riva had always filed those memories under aftermath rather than design.

"She thought she was sparing us," Riva said.

"Partly."

"And the rest?"

Tovin scraped a thumbnail over the repaired rail, testing for snags. "She was scared of her own wanting."

Daro stepped fully into the pilothouse. "Or she was the only one looking at the scale of the work."

Riva turned then, fast enough that the tiller banged her thigh. "What?"

His mouth was beautiful even when it made her tired. Especially then. "She was trying to hold Storm Mouth open permanently. You heard the page. If she'd done it, the dead would have had a sea road without all this scraping and pleading and lover-by-lover maintenance. No more backed channels. No more corpses in wells. No more children learning current before letters. You can call that hunger if it helps you hate her cleanly. I call it willingness."

"Willingness to do what?" Riva said. "To drag an infant into the worst pull in the delta and write him down as if blood were tackle?"

Daro's eyes flashed. Under pressure he could go either way, hush or sermon. He chose sermon. "You're hearing only the ugliest noun because ugly is easy. She was trying to end the burden. She was trying to make sure none of us had to spend our lives ferrying rot because the town wanted miracles on the cheap."

Iven leaned his shoulder to the wall. "And yet look at us, doing exactly that, plus the extra service of finding out she bought the miracle on credit."

Lale said, "It's not martyrdom or monstrosity by itself. It's a page. It records intention, action, consequence."

"A page written by a woman who had just lost her son," Daro said. "You don't get to pretend that's neutral."

"I didn't say neutral."

"You implied it."

"I implied you're already using him."

Daro looked as if she had slapped him with a wet cloth.

Riva put a hand on the tiller again before the barge clipped a post. The under-song in the cut had changed. Deeper. The current was turning under them.

From out on deck, Nami said, "What did he look like?"

Daro's mouth opened and shut again. Lale dropped her eyes to the oilcloth. Riva hated every one of them for that hesitation, herself included.

Tovin did.

"Black hair," she said. "Enough of it to lay down with spit when it stood up from sleep. He had Bato's feet, wide through the toes, like he expected to brace against a deck his whole life. He got storm rash on his chest if the weather turned. He kicked off every sock anyone put on him."

Nami's bandaged hand slid to her own ankle scars.

Tovin's voice roughened, not softer, just rougher. "He laughed when the ferry boards knocked under him. That was all. It wasn't omen. Babies laugh at stupid things."

Daro said, "Or at true ones."

"Daro An," Tovin snapped, full name and all, and there was the stress tell plain enough for anyone with sense to hear. "Don't make a shrine out of a child who still pissed through cloth."

The words hit the pilothouse like thrown tools.

Riva saw Daro take the blow and refuse to step back from it. That frightened her more than if he had yielded. He was already converting insult into heat, wound into proof. That was his gift and his danger: everything fed him if he let it.

She said, because someone had to nail one truth down before the whole thing floated off into song, "This is warning, then. That's the whole thing. This happens when one person starts hearing destiny every time the river makes a noise."

Daro stared at her. "No. This is what happens when one woman tries to carry communal labor in her own body because the rest of Reedmouth would rather worship than work."

Riva did not disagree with that last part. That was the trouble. Sereya had built something real, and the reality of it did not clean the blood off Jun's name. She could feel the split inside the argument like a misaligned seam. Truth on both sides, none of it merciful.

Lale looked down at the wrapped ledger. "She scattered us because she feared repetition."

"Yes," Tovin said.

"And you helped her."

Tovin gave Lale a flat look. "I fed whoever came through my door. I held Reed Gate. I told her she was out of her mind. Then I did what I could with the wreckage she left because there were children in it."

Iven's mouth twisted. "That's the family motto, isn't it."

Riva wanted to say something useful. Instead she heard the edge of the current hit the hull wrong, hauled the tiller hard, and missed a submerged piling by less than a foot. The Bitter Thread shuddered. Everyone grabbed wood or rail or each other.

When the barge steadied, no one resumed speaking at once.

Riva kept her eyes on the cut ahead. Her hands smelled of tar, wet brass, and the cold iron edge of comprehension. Their estrangement was not weather. It was carpentry. Someone had taken the house apart board by board and called it caution.

Behind her, the ledger made no sound at all.

* * *

Rain finally came by afternoon, thin white cords slanting across the deck without enough force to wash anything clean. Nami sat on the winch housing because the metal gave off trapped warmth from the morning's run and because keeping her injured hand above the spray made the bandage itch less. The Bitter Thread moved slower now through a broad reach where Lantern Quay still showed behind them between reed shoulders: smoke lines, prayer bowls hung to dry, children too close to the tide steps until someone shouted them back.

Her fever had gone down but had not left. It lived in her now as a second climate, rising when the air cooled, easing when she was busy, leaving everything touched by river water half a degree too bright. Jun's name had settled into that brightness and would not blur.

She kept seeing the black hair, Bato's feet, the storm rash on his chest, and the kicked-off socks.

Nami could not stop at the ritual language even if she tried. The adults kept saying sixth mouth, keeper, holding, offering. Those were their words. They made the boy feel like part of the route, part of the machinery that had ground through the family before she knew what the grinding was. The sock undid all that. One sock coming back up in Bato's hand while the rest of him stayed gone. She kept seeing that instead of sainthood.

Below her, the deck held everyone's work in separate patches as if the argument had taken physical space and forced them apart. Riva checked the intake grate with more violence than the task required. Every tool landed where it belonged, but landed hard. Lale sat under the awning with a sewing needle and waxed thread, stitching the page back into the reed ledger one careful pass at a time. She had spread a drying cloth under it and changed that cloth twice already when damp crept in from the air. Iven coiled and recoiled the same length of rope until the tar blackened his fingers. Daro stood at the bow where the rain beaded on his lashes and stared into the channel ahead as if the water might still volunteer to defend his mother better than any of them had.

Tovin had gone to gut a bucket of eels at the stern. She slit, thumbed, rinsed, and salted with the same expression she'd worn at Reed Gate when the sluice had nearly taken Riva's wrist: contempt covering care because care alone gave too much away.

Nami slipped her good hand into her pocket and felt the salt-fig seed there through the cloth. It was small, hard, and ridged like a tiny heart gone mean. She had kept it for proof. What it proved changed shape whenever she named it: chosenness, connection, a private line into the river. Lately it felt more like evidence in the other sense, something that would condemn her if anyone found it.

Daro came back from the bow and stopped beside her. He smelled of rain, old incense from Hymn Cut still clinging to one shirt no matter how often it got soaked, and the warm human sourness of a man who had not slept enough. He did not ask if she wanted company. Nobody in the family asked for room until too late.

"You've been quiet," he said.

Nami looked at the reach behind them. "Everybody else has been loud enough."

His mouth twitched. Not a smile, not quite. "Did you feel it when Lale read?"

She knew what he meant. He was not asking about understanding or grief. He meant the other thing, the possibility that the name landing in the air had changed the water around them.

"I felt bad," she said.

"That's not all you felt."

She hated how quickly her body betrayed her around him. It was not because he frightened her. It was because part of her always wanted him to be right about signs. A sign was cleaner than a life, cleaner than work, cleaner than having to decide things with tired hands.

"I keep thinking about the sock," she said.

That checked him for a moment.

"The sock," he repeated.

"Tovin said he kicked them off."

"Nami."

"What?"

Rain pinged off the winch casing between them. Daro lowered his voice into that near-intimacy he used when he wanted his words to feel like rescue. "You know the river notices some people differently."

She looked at him then. "That isn't what I said."

"Maybe it's still true."

Across the deck, Riva let the intake grate slam shut and came toward them before Nami could answer, weed slime shining on her wrists. "If you're preaching, do it where I can hear it."

Daro did not move. "I'm talking to my sister."

"Then talk like a brother."

He opened his mouth, and the Bitter Thread took a cross-current broadside. The hull gave one ugly roll. Lale's drying cloth shot out from under the ledger.

"Shit," Lale said.

The book skidded toward the scupper. Nami caught it with the bandaged hand because it was the near hand and because thinking came too slow. The needle buried in Lale's fresh stitch drove through wet linen and into the half-healed cut in her palm before tearing loose. Pain went white clear to her shoulder. The waxed thread lashed across her wrist.

Riva swore. Daro reached. Lale was already on her feet.

"Don't pull," Lale said. "You'll rip the page."

Iven dropped the rope and closed in fast, all the easy mouth gone out of him. At the stern, Tovin straightened over the eel bucket with her knife still wet in her hand.

Nami looked at her own bandage. Blood welled through it, hot and immediate. The stupid part was how natural the bargain felt. The book slid. Her hand caught it. The pain arrived after. In this family the work never asked twice before it took flesh.

Daro's face had lost the sermon. He looked young for one ugly second, hungry and scared and nothing like a man the river might choose for anything. "Nami," he said.

She looked at him because pain had stripped the room down to the barest things.

"I wasn't asking you to offer anything," he said. "I meant he wasn't only a warning."

Riva said, "Give Lale your hand."

Nami straightened before either of them could touch her. Touch would have broken something. "Stop saying what he meant."

Daro blinked. "What?"

She heard her own voice go flat in that eerie way it did when she got too close to whatever hurt most. "Stop saying what Jun meant. Stop saying what he proved. Stop saying what he cost. He had socks."

Riva sucked in one hard breath. Iven's rope slid from his hand in a wet slap. Daro only stared at her, blank for once, as if he'd missed a step in the dark.

Nami swallowed and kept going because now that the sentence had opened, the rest of it insisted. "He had black hair that stood up. He laughed at ferry boards. He pissed through cloth. Tovin said all that before any of you started making him useful. I can't make a saint out of somebody who had socks."

Riva looked as if someone had hit her in the chest with a wet plank. Iven went still with the loose rope at his feet. Lale stood with both hands out, one for the ledger and one for Nami, as if she could not decide which damage had priority.

Daro's face changed, not into surrender, worse luck, but into hurt. Real hurt. "Kinship doesn't erase meaning."

"Maybe meaning is the thing that erases him," Nami said.

Tovin let out a short harsh breath from the stern. Approval from her always sounded close to insult.

Only then did Lale peel the ledger out of Nami's grip and pack the drying cloth over the reopened cut. Nami hissed but did not pull away.

Before anyone could answer, a sound came over the water from the village behind them: bells, small and frantic, not shrine bells or prayer bowls. They were ankle bells. Lantern Quay had started tying them on sleepwalkers after the first bad week, anything that rang when a body moved where it shouldn't.

All of them turned.

At the tide steps, a man Nami recognized from the supper the night before was walking toward the water with his eyes shut and his mouth slack in a smile too peaceful for daylight. He had spent half the meal balancing prayer bowls on his head for the children. Now his two daughters were dragging him back by either arm, heels skidding in the slime. One girl had her father's oversized oilskin hanging off one shoulder. The younger still had fish scales stuck in one eyebrow from morning gutting. Their father kept leaning toward the steps as if the river were calling him somewhere pleasant.

More bells clattered from a house farther inland.

Riva did not wait for consensus. She crossed the deck in three strides and yanked the engine signal. "Enough. We go."

Daro turned back to her, furious. "In the middle of this?"

"There's no middle. There's just more of it if we stop."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one we've got."

Iven was already moving, because on his best days speed was the one virtue he could perform without lying. He kicked the stern line free, hauled in slack, and swore at the knot when wet hemp fought him. "If anybody wants to keep arguing, do it while working."

Lale wrapped the ledger in fresh oilcloth and tied it shut with the same waxed thread she'd used on the binding. "We're already doing both."

Tovin dumped eel guts overboard, wiped her knife on her apron, and came forward without a word. Her face looked older in the rain, the burn scars on her forearms silvering. Nami thought of her in the eel shed seventeen years before, taking in a woman with milk on her shirt and seawater in her eyes, then taking in the ruin that followed because somebody had to.

Daro stayed where he was one beat too long, hands open at his sides as if he could still summon a room back around himself and continue. Then the Bitter Thread shifted under him, committing to the cut, and he had to catch the rail like everyone else.

Lantern Quay slid backward. The father at the steps was still struggling gently in his daughters' grip, not fighting, just trying to continue the walk his sleeping body believed in. The bells on his ankles rang with every pull.

Behind the barge, Sereya's old funeral ferry tapped softly at the stern, narrow and black in the churn.

Nami kept her hand in her pocket around the seed until it hurt. Jun had gone back into the ledger, but he stayed stubbornly ordinary in her head: black hair standing up from sleep, wide feet under a damp blanket, one sock worked halfway off by kicking. The adults could keep sawing meaning out of him if they wanted. She could not stop seeing a brother small enough to need holding and dead enough to have become an argument.

Ahead, the channel bent toward Coil Reach where tar, rope, and another lover waited. Behind them the ankle bells kept ringing for someone the river had not taken yet.
