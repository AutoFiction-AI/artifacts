# Chapter 11

The Bitter Thread nosed around the reed point, and Coil Reach came at Iven in pieces: hot rain off the open water, then the cooked smell of hemp, wet ash, old tar, and the iron stink of a yard that had spent too many years fixing other people's damage for money that never arrived clean. It got into his mouth before the ropewalk came into view. By the time the bank unreeled through the reeds, he could already feel the joke everybody in Reedmouth had been carrying for years: of course Sereya's slickest son belonged to the rope man.

The dredge moved heavy in the flood, low and stubborn, her deck cluttered with the things Riva had kept alive through debt and bad weather: dredge chain, hooks, fuel tins, a hand winch, two iron-shod poles, the tool chest she trusted more than most people. Behind her, tied short by the stern, Sereya's old funeral ferry knocked against the barge with the patience of a thing waiting to be needed. The narrow black hull looked wrong among Riva's working gear, too lean, too intimate, built for bodies laid flat and covered, not for six living people with wet tempers and nowhere cheaper to fail.

Riva stood at the sweep with both hands set hard on it, chin scar pale in the damp. She had not spoken much since Lantern Quay, and she had spoken less after the page. Jun had moved through the family like a tool dropped into dark water. Nobody had heard it hit bottom yet. Lale sat on the forward crate with the reed ledger under oilcloth on her lap, keeping one hand on Nami's blanket every time the barge bumped. Daro leaned under the awning as if he could hear the fourth cut before the rest of them, his mouth gone soft and private. Nami's face shone with fever. She kept opening her eyes at odd moments and asking what that smell was, as if tar might turn out to be flowers if somebody answered carefully enough.

Iven kept moving because stillness had started feeling like honesty. He checked knots nobody had asked him to check. He wiped rain off the same brass fitting twice. He flashed the gold tooth at Lale when she looked too long in his direction, and she did not give him even the charity of annoyance.

"Don't start," Riva said without looking back.

"That wide, was it?"

"I could see it from your shoulders."

"That's cruel. I keep those elegant on purpose."

She spat rain. "Keep your mouth useful instead."

Useful. He nearly laughed. The last useful thing his mouth had done was say Jun's name in Sena Dey's bed because she had climbed on top of him and refused to let him slide past it with charm. Since then the word had been following him around like a rat in the bilge, alive whether he looked at it or not.

Coil Reach came clear through the rain. Enver's ropewalk ran long and low over the bank, a black timber spine lifted above mud and floodwash. The yard around it was all mooring posts, open sheds, tar kettles gone cold for the storm, and hung coils under oilskins. The fourth cut waited beyond the far end, where the bank narrowed and the floodwater sucked against a row of drowned pilings. Even from the barge Iven could hear it breathing wrong. The earlier channels had changed the sound of the delta. Here the water dragged at itself like a sleeper caught between dream and waking.

Enver stood halfway down the ropewalk with one boot up on the rail, testing a laid hawser with both palms. He was broader than Iven remembered and older in a meaner way, thick through the chest, beard smoke-dark, left ear bitten short where the lobe should have been. He dragged his bad leg a little when he turned. Between thumb and forefinger he held a fish-bone pick, and every few breaths he scraped tar from under one nail with patient disgust, as if dirt offended him more than people did. When he looked up, Iven had the ugly shock of seeing his own hands in a stranger's wrists.

Riva brought the Bitter Thread in along the east pilings. Ropewalk hands came out from the sheds to catch lines. None of them smiled. They looked from Riva's barge to Daro's face to Nami's sweat-bright skin to Iven, and that was worse than if they had stared at him first. He stepped onto the bank with a loose grin already in place.

"Afternoon, Coil," he called. "You keep the place ugly on purpose, or is that a service you charge extra for?"

Enver planted the fish-bone pick behind one ear. "I'd ask what drowned and washed you in, but I know your mother had a type."

One of the ropewalk hands barked a laugh and swallowed it when Riva came off the barge. She landed heavy, all business.

"We need dry line, lamp oil, and a pull on the fourth cut before dark," she said. "We'll pay what we can now and the rest after the passage settles."

Enver looked at the Bitter Thread first, not at her. Iven watched his gaze weigh the barge as iron, debt, resale, and risk before it ever became help.

"What you've got now won't cover the line you'll burn through opening it," Enver said. "And that deck stove of yours eats better than most children."

"Then name a number."

"I just did. More than you've got."

Lale came down after Riva, one careful step at a time because the boards were slick and because she did nothing carelessly in front of strangers. "We're not asking for a favor," she said. "We're asking for trade under flood conditions, which still counts as trade."

"You always did sound like a receipt," Enver said.

Daro smiled without humor. "You say that as if receipts don't outlast hymns."

Enver's eyes passed over him and moved on. "I'm not holding supper for saints' children. If you want the yard, the yard gets used my way."

Riva's jaw shifted once. "Fine. Your way."

Only then did Enver look straight at Iven.

"My son's going to work it with me."

The yard went very still, though not silent. Rain still hit the shed roofs, the fourth cut still sucked at the pilings, and Nami coughed under the awning on the barge. But the stillness sat inside the human part of the place. Iven had spent half his life making sure rumors stayed rumors because rumor gave you room to laugh. Enver took that room and stamped it flat in one line.

Iven spread his hands. "You trying to impress me in public, or is this how you sell rope to everybody?"

Enver did not blink. "If I was trying to impress you, I'd have done it before you owed Kes Varn sixty-two silver and two favors."

Riva turned fast. Lale closed her eyes once, brief as a stitch pulled tight. Daro's head tilted, curious despite himself.

"That's old market music," Iven said. "You listening to bookmakers now?"

"I broker half the smugglers who pretend they don't know my dock. I hear things. I heard about the page, too." Enver gave him a little shrug. "You sell light enough, boy. You'll move paper, promises, your own hide, anything that leaves a little change after."

He had called him boy, not son this time, and that landed worse somehow.

"I said my way," Enver went on. "The fourth cut doesn't care about your mother's legend anymore. It sits on yard tension and debt. Sereya's dead. What's left of her here is you. So you can take a line with me, son, or you can watch the rest of them go under because you'd rather keep your swagger dry."

Nami pushed herself up under the awning. Her blanket slid off one shoulder. "He walks like Iven," she said hoarsely to nobody. "When the boards are wet. Same hitch on the left."

"Nami," Lale said.

"I know what I saw."

Iven wished she had said anything else in the world.

Riva looked at him once, hard, measuring whether he was about to make trouble or collapse into it. "Can you do the work?"

"Depends. Is he planning to claim me before or after he sends the bill?"

Enver's mouth twitched in something more contemptuous than a smile. "You think this is claiming. That's rich."

He came down the ropewalk a few steps, dragging the bad leg, and the closer he got the less room there was for rumor. Iven could see the rain beading in his beard, the missing ear lobe, the ridge of old rope burn across both palms. He could also see the line of Enver's mouth, which he had watched years in a mirror without ever letting himself name it.

"Your mother took credit here," Enver said quietly, but not quietly enough to spare him. "She took rope and tar and men and time. She took my back in winter and my yard in spring, and she didn't leave me any saint's romance for it. She left me a son who learned every wrong lesson clean. Don't mistake accuracy for tenderness."

Riva cut in before Iven could answer. "We don't need tenderness. We need the cut."

"Then tie the Bitter Thread on my east posts and keep her steady when I tell you."

"If the line parts?"

"Then your barge goes where broken things go."

That earned him the first real flash in her face. "Watch your mouth."

"Watch your knots."

For one stupid second Iven loved him for saying it. The feeling curdled the moment it hit. It was not the resemblance or the public naming that got him. It was the precision, the way Enver put a sentence down like a hook and expected weight from it. Iven had spent years making language slick enough to slide out of other people's grip, while Enver built his to hold.

Daro stepped off the barge then, rain flattening his shirt to his chest. "If the cut wants blood relation named, say that. Don't dress it up as bookkeeping."

Enver snorted. "Everything important is bookkeeping. Your mother just knew how to moan over it until people called it religion."

Daro's face changed, not much, but enough that Iven could see a sermon trying to decide whether to climb into the singer's mouth. Lale spared them all by speaking first.

"If you want the work done before dark, stop admiring your own bitterness and tell us positions."

Enver glanced at her. "There. That's one of hers who'd have kept a decent yard."

"I've had that misfortune noted before," Lale said.

He pointed with two fingers. "Riva on the deck winch and stern lines. Ledger girl, you keep the fever girl out of the water and ready to cut loose whatever I say. Singer, you can use those pretty hands on the west brake or stay decorative. My son comes with me."

There was nothing in the yard for Iven to grab except the insult, and the insult had already grabbed him. He looked toward the Bitter Thread. The old funeral ferry bumped the stern again, narrow and black and patient. Riva had tied it short to keep it from swinging. He had laughed at that boat the first day she brought it out from under the ferry house, called it a coffin with opinions. Now it tapped the barge like a bad prediction.

Enver waited.

Iven gave him the grin he used when the cards were poison and he had to touch them anyway. "Lead on, then. If I'm such a valuable family asset, I'd hate to waste your afternoon."

Enver turned before the line was finished, as if trust had nothing to do with it. Iven followed him up the wet boards while the yard hands looked carefully elsewhere. Reedmouth had refined humiliation to this: nobody laughed, nobody pitied him aloud, and everybody gave him enough privacy to feel the knife all the way in.

At the ropehouse door Enver paused and tossed him a pair of split-palm work gloves gone stiff with old tar.

"You'll burn less skin with those," he said.

Iven caught them. "You always outfit strangers this nicely?"

Enver shoved the door open. "Only my own mistakes."

***

Inside the ropehouse the air was close, hot with old pitch and damp fiber. Hanging coils turned the rafters into a low brown forest. Tarred line, dry line, salvage hawsers, funeral cord, net repair bundles, towrope thick as a man's thigh, all of it gave off its own rank smell. A storm lamp burned on an iron hook over the central capstan, yellow light wobbling across the floorboards where rainwater had tracked in black from boots.

Beyond the far wall a slot window looked out on the fourth cut. The opening itself was almost all underwater, marked only by the way floodwater folded around two submerged gate posts and sucked hard at the gap between them. Enver's yard had been built around the mechanism. Three main hawsers ran from the house through iron sheaves and out across the cut to drowned anchors on the opposite bank. A fourth line, older and blacker than the rest, disappeared through the floor. Every few breaths it gave a little live shiver, as if something below was testing its patience.

Iven stopped just inside. "That's your miracle, then."

"It's a tension mouth," Enver said. He moved to the capstan with the familiarity of a man touching his own table. "Sereya liked song and spectacle. I liked things that held. So this one opens on balance. Pull east too hard and you shear the bank. Pull west too slow and the anchors walk. Let go altogether and the cut takes whatever boat is idiot enough to be tied nearest."

"Comforting."

"You don't need comfort. You need instruction."

Enver put both palms on the black line that came through the floor. His expression changed there, almost despite him. "Feel that," he said.

Iven hesitated. Enver's eyes flicked up. "What, you think rope's catching?"

Iven crouched and put his gloved hand on the line. The vibration in it was not magical. That would have been easier to laugh at. It felt mechanical and alive at once, a buried strain running under the boards, current translated into fiber and twist and pressure against his skin.

"It's taking from the earlier cuts," Enver said. "They've sent enough pull downstream to wake this one, but not enough to settle it. So we do the rest. Your sister keeps the Bitter Thread heavy on the east posts. I ease the floor line. You hold the west brake with me when it bites. If it answers clean, the mouth opens and the current straightens. If you show off, it kills one of us and maybe half your family with the rebound."

"You sell this very well."

Enver gave him a look that had no room for charm in it. "Put the gloves on right. Your thumb's wrong."

Iven looked down. He had already shoved one glove half crooked because that was what he did with instructions that landed too close to care. Enver stepped in, caught his wrist, and yanked the cuff straight.

"Rope burns where it slides, not where it sits," Enver said. "Keep the seam off the inside or you'll skin yourself to the knuckle."

The touch lasted maybe two seconds. It had more force in it than any of Sereya's stories about fathers had ever managed. There was no perfume or legend in it, only a man correcting his hand because a wrong hand on a loaded line got peeled open.

Iven pulled back. "You rehearse this? Bit late for teaching me trades."

"Bit late for learning one too, from what I hear."

Outside, Riva shouted that the east lines were fast. Daro answered from the west side, his voice carrying under the storm in that annoying smooth way he had even when he was only saying practical things. Lale called that Nami was awake and staying put. Enver did not raise his voice. He simply stepped to the open door and said, "Hold till I call."

The yard obeyed.

The obedience bit deeper than the paternity. Ilyan had held a room because people leaned toward him. Enver held one because nobody wanted the work to go wrong on his watch.

He came back to the capstan. "Take that brake bar."

Iven did. The wood was polished by older palms and tacky with rain.

"When the floor line jumps, lean with your hip. Not your shoulders. Your shoulders are for men who want witnesses."

"You practice insults while you're splicing, or do they come to you wet?"

Enver's mouth edged thin. "I had twenty-nine years."

There it was, clean as a knife honed on stone. It was not denial, and it was not apology either. It was arithmetic.

He set the capstan turning one notch at a time, easing load from the black floor line. The whole house answered. Boards creaked. Sheaves whined. Out on the cut something deep underwater gave a heavy iron groan.

"Now," Enver said.

Iven leaned into the brake bar. The line took at once. Force ran up through the wood into his hips, his spine, his teeth. It felt like bracing against a door with a flood on the far side of it.

Enver worked beside him, broad and steady, bad leg planted, hands exact on the wrap. "Don't choke it. Let it run the width of a finger, then catch again."

The instruction landed through the work before Iven could hate it. He adjusted. The line sang harder. Outside, Riva's winch clicked under strain. Somewhere to the west Daro swore, low and startled, because the current had changed position under his feet.

Enver nodded once. "Again."

They fed and caught the line again and again. Rain rattled the roof. The house darkened and brightened with the storm lamp's swing. The fourth cut began to sound less like suction and more like breath taken through clenched teeth.

For six, seven, maybe ten pulls there was nothing in the world but weight. Iven forgot his face, then the yard, then the people listening for failure outside. The line told him more truth than the family had managed in days; it told him to hold here, give there, not fight the lay, and let the strain travel. Enver, without looking at him, said, "Right hand lower. That's it. Feel where it's trying to roll."

And Iven did.

Iven's jaw locked so hard the bad tooth sang. He had taken Enver's correction cleanly, dropped his hand where the man told him, and felt the line answer. The fit of it was obscene. Some piece of him knew this work too quickly, as if it had been crouching under his skin for years, and he wanted to sink his teeth into the brake bar for it.

"So what was the plan?" he asked over the strain. "Watch me from the dock till I got old enough to look like you in bad light?"

Enver kept his eyes on the line. "The plan was for your mother not to make a boy in the middle of an unpayable season."

"Strong start."

"The next plan was for her not to vanish every time I said the word future."

The line jumped. Both men caught it.

"You could've said my name," Iven said.

"I knew your name."

"Then you could've used it."

Enver let the line run a finger's breadth. "For what, so I could drag you down here sooner?"

"Fuck you."

"There's the family speech."

Outside, Riva shouted that the east posts were slipping a little. Enver called for another wrap on the stern line. Lale answered this time, clipped and steady. He listened with half an ear, calculating, then shifted the capstan again.

"You think not speaking was the coward part," he said. "It wasn't. The coward part was watching Sereya take rope, time, and room till there was none left for sense, and deciding I'd sooner keep a yard than go under with her. Then years later here comes a boy with my hands and her mouth, already working every dockside fool who'll laugh, shaving supper off gossip and card smoke. What was I meant to do with that."

Iven laughed once. It came out raw. "Claim it, maybe."

Enver finally looked at him. "Claim what, then? By the time I got a look at you, you were already bad debt with boots on."

The word went through him so hard his grip changed. The brake bar slipped half an inch. The black line screamed against the floor slot.

"Hold," Enver snapped.

Iven hauled back. "Don't call me bad debt and ask for balance in the same breath."

"Then don't behave like it."

"You don't know a fucking thing about me."

"I know you sold a page that wasn't yours. I know you owe market scum money you can't cover. I know when Reedmouth goes quiet and looks your way, you show the gold tooth, climb your voice a notch, and act like a crowd is credit. That's enough for a first meeting."

It was too exact. Too close to things Iven had only admitted in bed and not all the way there. He shoved the brake bar into place and turned on Enver fully.

"A first meeting," he said. "That's what this is. You're generous."

Enver bent to set another wrap, and Iven caught the front of his coat before he quite knew he had moved. Wet canvas bunched in his fist. Enver's head came up, slow, dangerous.

"Take your hand off me," Enver said.

"Say my name, then."

"You want names now."

"I want one thing out of your mouth that isn't trade or blame."

The line strained between them, loaded and waiting. Enver's gaze dropped once to Iven's fist and came back up.

"Iven," he said, flat as a board. "Take your hand off me and do the work."

For half a breath that should have been enough. It might even have become something if the day had been kinder, if Jun had not already cracked the family open, if Iven had not been standing in a room full of ropes and inheritance and years. Instead the name only proved how easily Enver might have said it any time and chosen not to.

He shoved him.

Enver slammed back into the capstan and hit him twice as fast as a larger man ought to move. First a forearm across the throat, then the rope-burned palm cracking against Iven's cheek. Pain lit bright and stupid. Iven came back with his shoulder, driving them both into the brake bar as the loaded line gave another violent leap.

The house roared. Wood complained. The capstan spun half a turn wild under Enver's hand.

"You little shit," Enver said, and now the resemblance in their voices made Iven want to tear his own skin off.

They grappled in the exact worst place in the world for it, boots sliding on wet boards, elbows banging line and post and crank. Iven got one hand in Enver's beard and felt water and tar and age there. Enver kneed him in the thigh. The brake bar kicked free. A wrap slithered wrong on the drum.

Then the storm lamp went.

Iven never knew whether his shoulder hit the hook or the line snapped it loose. One second the lamp swung over them. The next it clanged off the capstan, burst against the floor, and threw oil under a nest of hemp fuzz and old tow. Flame took faster than reason. It ran blue for less than a blink, then yellow, then hungry orange up the nearest hanging coil.

Both men froze long enough to understand the size of the mistake.

Outside the fourth cut opened.

The sound was monstrous and intimate at once, like a locked jaw forced apart underwater. The black floor line dropped a whole handspan. Through the slot window Iven saw the flood fold sideways and then straighten, sucking hard through the gate posts as if the delta had found a missing tooth and yanked it free.

Fire climbed the ropehouse wall.

Enver moved first. He lunged for the line to keep the capstan from spinning itself murderous. Iven grabbed for the bucket under the bench and found it half sand, half stale rain. He threw it anyway. The water died hissing on the floor while the flames took the tar in the hanging ropes above with a delighted sound.

"Out," Enver barked.

But the loaded hawser running east had already caught. Fire raced along its tarred hair toward the door, thin and bright and fast as spite.

From outside Riva yelled, "Iven!"

He hit the doorway in time to see the flame run the length of the line straight toward the Bitter Thread's stern where the hawser was fast to her cleat, just above the stacked fuel tins and coil rack.

Hot tar hit the back of his throat. Burning hemp went sweet and foul. Three ropewalk hands on the bank had stopped long enough to watch the flame sprint down the hawser toward Riva's stern, and not one of them laughed. That mercy cut deeper than laughter would have.

***

The yard came apart in ugly sections. Ropewalk hands ran for hooks, knives, buckets. Rain hammered down harder as if the sky had finally decided to participate. Fire still beat it. Flame crawled the east line to the Bitter Thread and flowered under the stern rack. Riva was already on deck with an axe, chopping at the burning hawser before it could take the whole line path across her cargo.

"Cut the tender loose!" she shouted when she saw Iven. "Now. Then get the fuel off if you can."

The old funeral ferry banged the stern again, close enough now that sparks were landing on her wet gunwale. Iven jumped from dock to barge, knees jarring. Heat slapped his face. The first fuel tin on deck had started to smoke around the lid. Lale was there too, passing up what she could from the bank: the ledger wrapped in oilcloth, a bundle of blankets, the medical satchel, the food sack already split and leaking rice.

"Take the book," she said.

"You take it."

"I already did. Cut the ferry."

Even under fire she sounded like a clerk correcting an invoice. It nearly steadied him.

Nami stood under the awning with one hand over her bandaged palm, staring at the opened cut beyond the yard as if somebody had called her real name from inside it. Daro had both hands on her shoulders, not singing, not soothing, just anchoring.

"Nami," he was saying close to her ear. "Look at me. Not there. Here."

She blinked, slow and frightened. "It sounds hungry."

"Everything does right now."

Iven hacked through the painter line on one side, then the other. The funeral ferry dropped back free and knocked once against the piling. A ropewalk hand snagged it with a boat hook and dragged it clear. Good. One thing.

The hawser Riva had been chopping parted with a crack. Fire snapped up its loose end like a whip. She kicked the burning length overboard and turned at once for the fuel tins.

"Leave those," Iven shouted.

"No."

Of course not. Those tins were distance, heat, cooked food, and dry light. Every extra mile they still had a chance of buying. Oren Pike's debt did not care whether she burned with the collateral.

He grabbed one tin and heaved it toward the dock. It landed badly and rolled, sloshing. Another he could not move because the rope rack had already collapsed half over it, flaming. He swore and went for the rack with the axe.

Something in the ropehouse boomed. It was not an explosion but a beam giving way, heavy and final, and sparks lifted under the rain.

Enver was still in there.

Iven stopped with the axe in both hands and knew, all at once, what he hated most. He did not hate the man, or even the years. He hated that if Enver died in there now, the shape of it would belong to him forever. He would have to carry the line from public naming to dropped lamp to burning father in one unbroken sequence, and that was too clean a punishment for the river to resist.

Riva saw the look on his face. "No."

"He's still inside."

"Then he should've come out."

"Riva."

She came close enough to say it low. "If you go in there, I lose deck and brother in the same minute."

Behind her the Bitter Thread's stern was fully lit, flames running under the coil rack, smoke pumping up through rain. The open fourth cut pulled harder against the pilings now that the line path had gone wild. The whole barge strained on the remaining moorings.

Iven put the axe in her hands.

"Don't let her drift under the house," he said.

It was as close to an apology as he had time for. Then he jumped back to the dock and ran for the ropehouse.

Heat hit in layers. The doorway was open but half curtained with smoke. Inside, flame had eaten through two hanging banks of dry line and dropped them to the floor where they writhed in their own tar. The lamp oil stink was thick enough to chew. Iven pulled his shirt over his mouth and went low.

"Enver!"

He got no answer until a cough came from the far side near the floor slot.

He found him by the bad leg. A fallen crossbeam had taken one end of the capstan frame down and trapped Enver from the hip to the ankle. Worse, the black floor line had kicked loose and wrapped twice around his lower leg, pinning him against the beam. Enver still had a knife in one hand. He had cut through one loaded wrap and sliced his own palm open doing it.

"Took you," he coughed, then spat black, "long enough."

He was still needling him, even now.

"You could say thank you and make this memorable."

"Lift the beam first. Then cut the floor line on the dead side. Not the loaded side, unless you want it to take your face off."

There. Instruction again. More natural to him than pleading.

Iven got both hands under the beam and heaved. It barely shifted. His burned cheek throbbed. Sweat ran into his eyes though the room was full of rain leaks and smoke. Enver planted both palms and pushed with him. The beam rose a handspan, enough for pain to hit the other man hard. Enver made a sound like an animal choking on iron.

"Again," he said.

Together they got just enough room for Iven to jam his shoulder in and free the trapped hip. Then he dropped, found the line wrap by touch, and sawed at the dead side where Enver had pointed. The fiber parted in stages, tough and greasy. The loaded half snapped past his ear with such force it shaved splinters off the post behind him.

"Stand," Iven said.

Enver tried and nearly went back down. The bad leg had twisted under the beam. Iven got an arm under him. The man's weight came heavy and unwilling, but he moved.

Flame rolled across the rafters overhead. A burning coil fell and struck the floor where they had been kneeling.

They staggered into rain together, one limping, one dragging. Somebody on the dock shouted. Buckets came. Hands caught Enver from the other side. Iven let go only when he was sure the man would stay upright without him.

For one second Enver's whole weight hung on him anyway, chest to shoulder, breath foul with smoke.

"Easy, son," Enver muttered, not performative now, not for the yard. "My leg."

Son. Simple as thirst. He might not even have known he said it.

Iven set him on a piling stump and stepped back too fast.

The Bitter Thread answered for him.

One of the west moorings had finally torn loose. The barge swung broadside under the pull of the opened cut, stern burning hot and bow still tied. Riva sprinted for the foreline, trying to free it before the hull got pinned under the ropewalk. Daro jumped to help. Lale had Nami by the waist now because the younger woman's knees kept giving in strange directions, toward water rather than away.

"Riva!" Iven shouted.

She got the foreline loose with one cut, but that only gave the current permission. The Bitter Thread surged out from the dock half on fire, half under command, and for an instant it looked as if she might clear the pilings. Then the burning stern rack collapsed, spilling flaming coil and one smoking fuel tin against the deck winch. Fire blew forward under the awning. Riva had to jump for the dock or go with her.

She jumped. Daro caught her wrist. They both hit the mud hard.

The barge slid sideways into the throat of the fourth cut.

Iven had watched Riva pilot that hull through eel stakes, corpse jams, and water so narrow the reeds brushed both rails. He had never seen her look at it the way she looked now, as if a piece of her own work had chosen to die where she could not reach it. The Bitter Thread's name board, carved white once and now river-gray, flashed past through rain and smoke. The winch ripped free of its fastenings with a metal howl. Dredge chain spilled overboard link after link after link, black iron vanishing into blacker water. The tool chest tipped, struck the rail, and went under without floating once.

One of the fuel tins burst its lid and threw a sheet of flame up the mast stub. It was not a grand explosion, just a hard breath of fire that told everybody the salvage part was over.

Riva took one step after the barge. Enver, sitting crooked with his ruined leg out in front of him, caught the back of her coat with a fist and held.

"Don't," he said.

She turned on him with murder in her face. "That's my boat."

"That's ash with debt on it."

"Let go."

"Look at the pull."

Because he was right, because she was Riva and did in fact look, because the fourth cut had opened and was taking everything offered to it, she did not tear free. She stood there in rain and smoke with Enver's fist in her coat and watched the Bitter Thread strike one of the drowned posts. The hull rolled. Fire hissed where floodwater hit it and came up again in the seams. Then the stern sank first, dragged by chain and ruined iron. The rest followed slow enough to hurt. The name board disappeared last.

Nothing on the dock moved for a few breaths after.

Rain beat the river flat where the barge had gone down. Then the cut took the surface shape apart as if it had never held a boat there at all. Bits came back up: one hooked pole, a split crate, charred planks, a floating slick of lamp oil rainbowing under rain. Most of the heavy gear stayed gone; the winch, chain, spare anchors, fuel, iron teeth, and the chest were all taken under fast water or cooking in the part of the hull still burning below.

Lale was the first one back in motion. "What do we have," she said, already counting. "Say it clean."

Riva did not answer.

Daro did, breathless, mud on his knees. "Ledger. Food sack, though half of it's muck now. Blankets. Medicine. Three dry coils, if that ropehouse hand got them clear. The funeral ferry."

"Two poles," Lale said. "One boat hook. Half a cask of lamp oil if it didn't crack when it rolled."

Nami blinked rain out of her lashes. "The black little boat."

"Yes," Lale said.

"For dead people."

"For us now, apparently."

The sentence settled over them heavier than the rain.

The funeral ferry sat tied off against the lee side of the dock, black hull wet and untouched except for a scatter of sparks dying on the gunwale. It was narrow and hand-poled, built for shallows and stillness and the weight of grief distributed carefully. It looked even smaller with the Bitter Thread gone.

Iven bent over, hands on thighs, and finally felt what the fire had done to him. His palms were blistering under the gloves. One cheek was swelling where Enver had hit him. Half his left eyebrow had curled away in the heat. Smoke sat in his chest like a fist. Worse than any of that was the knowledge moving into place with obscene steadiness: he had knocked the lamp loose. He had gone into a ropehouse ready to be humiliated and found a man who could have taught him something, and he had answered by turning the lesson into flame.

He straightened because Enver was looking at him.

The older man's beard dripped black with soot. His cut palm leaked through a rag somebody had tied around it. His bad leg was already swelling at the ankle. He looked wrecked and infuriatingly unsurprised.

"You're burned," Enver said.

"You're welcome," Iven said.

One corner of Enver's mouth twitched toward a smile and stopped.

"I said what I said in the yard because the cut needed truth in the room," Enver said. "Not because I woke up hungry to be your father before noon."

Riva barked a laugh with no humor in it. "Good. I'd hate to think this was the warm version."

Enver ignored her. He kept his gaze on Iven. "But you did pull me out."

Iven wanted a sharper line than the one that came. "Bad judgment runs in the blood, apparently."

This time Enver did smile, one corner only, tired and ugly and real. "That, at least, sounds like family."

No one moved toward anyone else. Riva stood with river mud on her knees, watching black scraps of her deck knock against the pilings. Lale kept counting under her breath. Behind them the ropehouse popped and shed sparks into the rain. Enver's word stayed in the middle of the dock like a dropped hook: family.

Riva finally moved. She walked to the dock edge and crouched, not to pray, just to pick up the one iron-shod pole the river had spat back. She weighed it in both hands, then looked over at the funeral ferry.

"That hull won't take the whole lot of our old habits," she said. Her voice had gone flint-hard. "So listen now. We travel stripped. We take what fits by hand. No extra cargo. No vanity. If it isn't food, medicine, rope, or what opens the next water, it stays."

Daro rubbed smoke from his throat. "You just buried half our capacity."

"I noticed."

Lale tucked wet hair behind one ear. "The ferry will move where the dredge wouldn't. That's the gain."

"That's a very bookkeeper way to describe catastrophe," Iven said.

"I'm trying not to say we're fucked in front of Nami."

"I'm right here," Nami said. She had gone white under the fever flush. "And we're fucked."

Enver gave a cough that turned into something harsher. Iven moved before deciding to, steadying him by the elbow while the fit passed. The grip surprised them both. Enver's sleeve was wet and hot under the soot.

When the cough eased, Enver said, still looking at the river, "You'll need line once the fire dies down, whatever I can spare. Not on credit, though. You'll pay for it by helping me rebuild what's left of my house when the flood breaks."

Iven almost told him to choke on it. Then he looked at the cut, at the barge gone under, at Riva holding one salvaged pole as if it had insulted her personally, at the ferry meant for corpses waiting to carry the living. Trade and blood had been tied together now whether he liked the knot or not.

"Fine," he said.

Enver grunted. "Fine what."

Iven turned his head and met his eyes. The next word felt like swallowing wire.

"Fine, father."

Rain ran between them. Enver did not soften. He did not reach. He only nodded once, like a bargain entered in the only ledger he trusted.

By full dark the ropehouse fire had burned down to steaming black ribs. The yard stank of wet ash and cooked hemp. The opened fourth cut pulled clean now between the drowned posts, its surface flattened to a hard dark slick. Char from Riva's deck bumped a piling once, turned, and slipped under.

They loaded the funeral ferry in silence with one coil, two poles, blankets, the ledger, food, and medicine. The little that was left of the mission fit too easily.

When Iven stepped into the narrow hull at last, it rocked under him with the intimate balance of a craft built for the dead. Enver stood on the dock with his leg bound rough, one soot-black hand on a mooring post. Riva took the stern. Daro and Lale settled Nami low in the middle, her bandaged hand tucked against her belly. No one looked bigger in that boat than grief.

Iven set the salvaged pole against the gunwale and glanced once toward the place where the Bitter Thread had gone down. He could not see the wreck now, only rain stippling the cut and the occasional drift of black char moving seaward.

Nobody on the dock looked straight at him. They watched the ferry, the cut, Enver's bad leg, anything but his face. Under Iven's feet the little hull rocked coffin-narrow. His scorched eyebrow prickled, his palms throbbed inside the gloves, and Riva's boat lay somewhere below the black water because he had put his hands on a man before he put them on the work.

Riva shoved them off.

The funeral ferry slipped into the opened water like a blade entering old scar tissue. Behind them Coil Reach smoked in the rain. Ahead lay less boat, less gear, less room for performance, and no decent place yet to put Nami down out of the weather. Iven put both blistered hands on the pole anyway and pushed.
