# Chapter 4

The eel house hit Lale first as heat and pepper, then as shelter. Her skirts were stiff at the hem with river mud and her hands smelled of iron, weed slime, and the old coins of the dead. She had meant to keep hold of the ledger the whole walk back. She had meant to get it dry, turn the next route notes into a list, and go to sleep with something orderly in her head for once. Instead Tovin snatched the bundle from under her arm, set it on the highest shelf by the stove, and pointed the spoon at all five of them like a dock hook.

"If anybody says cut, saint, corpse, shrine, gate, or fate before I put food in you, I'll knock teeth loose. Boots off."

Wet leather hit the door plank before any of them answered. A chair leg scraped. Rain ticked on the patched tin over the smoke vent. Tovin's house had never been pretty. It was a low hot box nailed over the eel races, with smoke-black rafters, hooks full of drying nets, and an old market awning cut up to make curtains that never quite met in the middle. The air carried eel fat, pepper, old ashes, and the sweet rot from the troughs below. It smelled like work that had kept winning by one day at a time.

Tovin moved inside it, and the room yielded around her shoulders. She had wound a strip of blue cloth around the spoon handle so her burned fingers could grip it without swearing at the heat every few breaths. The cloth had once been part of a good shirt, and she had patched it three times instead of throwing it away. That was the kind of piety she believed in.

Riva opened her mouth anyway. "At first light we ought to-"

Tovin snapped the spoon against the pot rim. "You ought to sit down before you fall over and crack my floor."

Iven laughed and dropped onto the bench by the table with all the grace of wet rope. "Hear that? We've been demoted from holy laborers to hazards."

"You were always a hazard," Tovin said. "Holy hasn't touched you yet."

Nami was already standing too close to the stove, eyes gone soft at the steam. "What's in it?"

"Eel, onion, barley, dock leaves, and the last of my patience. Move your face back unless you want it boiled."

Daro, who usually managed to look composed even when he was soaked to the bone, had rainwater running off the end of his nose and down his throat. "That smells indecent."

"Good," Tovin said. "That means you'll shut up and eat it."

Lale sat because her knees had started doing the small dangerous tremor that came right before her body stopped asking permission. The bench was warm where it had been nearest the stove. She put both hands flat on the table and felt the grain swell under the varnish. This house had taken in floodwater more than once; the wood had dried crooked and stayed useful anyway. Lale could have made a lesson of that on another night. Not this one.

Tovin set out bowls chipped at the lip and thick spoons with their handles bent from years of stirring heavy pots. She shoved a plate of fried eel skin into the middle of the table, the strips blistered and salted hard enough to scrape a mouth raw in a pleasing way. Nami made a sound so nakedly hungry that Riva glanced at her and then back down into her own bowl.

"Take one," Tovin said. "Not all of them, Iven. I can see your hand moving before you move it."

"You wound me."

"I should have done it years ago."

He took two strips instead of one and hid one under his palm for Nami like he thought nobody noticed. Lale noticed because she always noticed small theft, small mercy, small changes in count. Nami noticed because she was meant to. She grinned at him and bit into the skin so the salt cracked across her lower lip.

The pot came down. Tovin ladled thick stew into bowls until the room filled with peppered steam and the greasy shine of eel broth. Onion threads clung to the spoon. Barley swelled like river pearls gone honest. She set down a loaf of coarse brown bread and a crock of pickled green plums that had gone soft enough to split under a fingernail.

"Eat while it's hot," she said. "If you start talking nonsense, I'll put you back in the rain."

The first few minutes held only the sounds of use: spoons knocking ceramic, bread tearing, somebody hissing because the stew had burned a tongue that had spent the day swallowing river spray. Lale had not realized how much hunger could feel like grievance until the hot broth hit her stomach and the grievance thinned enough to show its weave. She had been angry at everyone since dawn. She might still be. It was simply harder to maintain while sucking marrow-thick eel from a spoon with pepper oil running down her thumb.

Riva ate standing for three mouthfuls before Tovin shoved the back of her knee with one broad scarred hand and forced her onto the bench. "Sit like a person. The gate's open whether you crouch at it or not."

Riva sat. The surprise of it almost made Lale laugh. Their eldest sister always looked built for the next necessary thing, never the present one. Even now the iron gate key lay on the table by her elbow like an accusation made of metal. She had cleaned it, though. Mud still sat in the creases of her knuckles, but the key itself had been rubbed down with lamp oil until its teeth showed dull red through the rust.

Nami was tearing bread and dropping it into her stew until the pieces sank and came back slick. "You used to put too much pepper in everything," she said to Tovin.

"I still do."

"No, worse then. When I was little my lips went numb and I cried."

Tovin snorted. "You cried because Sereya laughed at you for spitting into my yard."

"She laughed first," Nami said, affronted by the sequence more than the fact.

"She did," Daro said. "Then she kissed your forehead and told you pepper was a form of schooling."

That was her voice. He had not even tried to imitate their mother at first, only fallen into the broad amused warmth she used when she wanted a child comforted without being allowed the luxury of dignity. Every head at the table turned a little.

Iven leaned back against the wall. "Do the rest. Go on."

Daro lifted one shoulder, false-modest and pleased with himself. "'If it doesn't sting, little tide, it isn't teaching you anything worth knowing.'"

Nami barked a laugh so sudden stew nearly came back out of her nose. Riva choked on bread. Tovin's mouth went mean in the way it did when she was trying not to smile and losing.

"That's a foul trick," Lale said, but her own voice had bent around the edge of laughter.

"It's accurate," Daro said. He tipped his chin, narrowed his eyes, and went broader, crueler, funnier. "'Tovin, don't cut the belly that thick unless you mean to marry me. Pilgrims can't pray on a full gut and lust at the same time.'"

Tovin slapped the table hard enough to jolt the spoons. "She said that once."

"Three times," Riva said before she could stop herself.

Iven pointed at her with a dripping crust. "You remember."

"Everybody remembers," Riva muttered, but there was color high in her cheeks now, the kind labor never put there.

Daro kept going because Nami was bent over laughing, because Iven had forgotten to hide his grin, because even Riva had stopped guarding her face. He widened his mouth into that impossible generous mockery Sereya had used when she wanted a crowd to think it had invented her. "'Then again, maybe they can. Give them another bowl. If devotion won't stand up under grease, it isn't devotion, it's weather.'"

That one broke them. Even Lale laughed, truly laughed, with stew halfway to her mouth and the spoon knocking her teeth. It hurt. The sound felt rusty coming out of her, like something retrieved from a drawer and forced open. Nami leaned against her shoulder, shaking. Iven put his head down against the table and slapped once at the wood. Riva laughed with her face turned away, mouth hidden against her wrist. Tovin did not laugh at all. She barked one short ugly sound and reached blindly for the salt crock because she had tears in her eyes and would rather have been caught bleeding.

Lale sat inside it and watched shoulders come down from ears, hands stay on spoons instead of tools, and nobody move toward the door.

It was not forgiveness. It was not even safety. It was only the old table trick, the one they had known before Sereya died and before Reedmouth learned to look at them as relics or warnings instead of hungry children with wet hems and bad tempers. It was a hot room with enough food to make greed visible. Somebody was performing. Somebody was insulting back. Their shoulders kept touching because there was not space enough to make distance look principled.

She remembered, against her will, a monsoon when all of them had still fit in this house without stooping. Riva was younger and already broad as a doorframe, coming in with two snapped eel stakes over her shoulder like trophies. Daro was singing some rude dock rhyme until Sereya told him if he was going to be obscene he ought to be musical about it. Iven was hiding sugared river nuts inside the flour bin because he thought no one who baked was capable of counting. Nami was half asleep under the table, clutching a spoon. Lale herself sat at the end of the bench, sorting split lentils from whole because Tovin hated waste and Sereya hated any labor that could not flirt back. The memory had the hard clean edges of crockery taken from a shelf after years. She distrusted it at once.

"What?" Iven said, catching her look.

"You still hide things like nobody in the room can count."

He grinned, gold tooth flashing. "There she is. I thought the river had rinsed you into clean books and regular hours."

"A clerk would have inventoried the theft before you sat down."

"You still can."

"I don't extend credit to goods already halfway out the door."

Riva groaned. "Can you two eat one bowl without turning supper into a market stall?"

"Unclear," Iven said. "This stew argues for it."

Tovin pointed at him with her spoon. "If you start talking about my stew like you're buying shrine favor, I'll hand you the pot and a scrub brush."

"I'm saying I'd let you overcharge me for the bowl if you kept filling it."

"You'd pay double if somebody brushed your wrist on the way past."

"Not anybody. I do inspect the goods."

"Then inspect faster and eat before it skins over."

Nami licked plum brine from her finger. "Do you still make the sweet cakes with eel fat and orange rind?"

"When somebody brings me oranges."

Nami looked around the table with the solemn hope of a child assigning future weather. "We could bring oranges back."

No one answered at first. The sentence was too ordinary. It presumed return, appetite, and a future specific enough to include fruit. Lale felt it move through the room and find a place to sit.

Riva tore off another piece of bread. "If Marrow Market hasn't drowned itself stupid, I'll bring you a whole sack and make somebody haul it home."

"Buy?" Iven said. "Look at her, getting grand."

"Shut up."

"What, no haggling? No stolen crate, no bad apology, no knife in the scale?"

"Say one more thing and you'll drag the sack on the towline with your own back."

"There she is," Daro said softly, smiling into his bowl.

Tovin rose to fetch more stew. Her left hand had gone stiff around the ladle, and she stopped to knock her knuckles twice against the mantel before forcing them open. She caught Lale watching and bared her teeth.

"Don't start offering pity. I can still outwork all of you."

"Nobody offered."

"Good."

She filled their bowls anyway. That was how care worked in this house. It arrived disguised as contempt and left grease on the table.

The rain slackened outside until they could hear the lesser sounds again: the drip from their clothes by the door, the slide of water under the floorboards, an eel trough lid shifting in the yard. Nami started asking useless questions for the pleasure of answers. Had Old Menna really married the cooper from Flood Row. Did pilgrims still leave coins nailed into Tovin's gatepost. Had the red bitch from the next yard ever learned not to steal fish heads. Tovin answered all of it with relish. The cooper had turned out bowlegged and vain. Pilgrims still nailed coins and Tovin still pried them off with a knife because the river had no use for amateur decoration. The bitch had learned nothing and recently whelped under the smoking shed with a face so guilty it could have been elected to office.

Daro embellished every story. Iven interrupted them and made them worse. Even Riva contributed, now and then, a flat practical line that landed harder because she never set one up. When Tovin described kicking a drunk boatman into the trough after he called Sereya a sanctimonious whore, Riva said, "Waste of a clean trough," and the table fell apart again.

Lale ate far past fullness. She ate until her skin warmed from inside and her eyelids dragged a little. She ate because everybody else was eating and because nobody was asking her to interpret the dead or preserve the living or explain the missing page. The relief of it was so sharp she disliked it on principle. Relief loosened the knots she depended on. It let her miscount the knives in the drawer.

Still, when Nami reached without looking and stole the last fried strip from Iven's plate, and Iven made an offended sound but let her keep it, and Daro went on with his terrible Sereya voice until even Tovin threatened to throw him through the wall, Lale felt something loosen she had mistaken for structure.

It left her exposed.

She understood then why these quiet hours hurt as much as the wrecked ones. Only once the room settled into its old pattern could she see the missing piece cleanly.

By the time the bowls were scraped and the bread reduced to heel ends and crumbs, shoulders had dropped and voices had gone lazy. Nami was barefoot, one foot tucked under her on the bench. Riva had taken off her coat and rolled her sleeves, showing the pale old hook scar at her chin as she leaned into the stove heat. Iven sat backward on a chair, arms folded over the back, talking about a card dealer in Halcote who could palm a coin with three fingers and still lose fairly. Daro hummed between sentences, not performance now, only contentment finding a road out. Tovin passed around a tin of bitter fennel seeds to chew after grease, and when Lale took a pinch she tasted smoke, anise, and the iron dust at the bottom of the tin.

She could have stayed there another hour and mistaken the bare feet, fennel bitterness, and slow talk for something durable.

Instead she stood, wiped her hands on a towel, and reached for the ledger on the shelf.

No chair scraped back yet. Nobody set a bowl down hard. Riva only looked over with a small line returning between her brows.

"Leave it till morning," Tovin said.

"If I leave damp stitching till morning, I deserve what happens to it."

"That sentence alone should have kept suitors away."

"It did."

Iven lifted his head. "That wasn't the stitching."

Lale ignored him. She took the ledger down and felt at once where the moisture had settled and where it had dried. Reed paper did not swell evenly. It took water along the grain and held the memory of it. She set the book on the table, away from the grease, and opened it with the careful pressure she used on dyers' accounts and burial tallies, fingers at the corners, thumbs keeping the spine from cracking further.

For another few minutes bowls clinked, the whetstone hissed, and rain fretted at the roof tin. Tovin and Nami cleared bowls. Iven carried the pot to the wash basin with a flourish so exaggerated it counted as mockery of labor and therefore, in him, a kind of participation. Riva stood by the door with a whetstone and ran it down the edge of a boat knife in slow even strokes. Daro stretched his legs under the table and tipped his chair back against the wall, eyes half closed, listening for doctrine in the rain.

Lale turned pages. There were shrine notes, lover-order, depth marks, and Sereya's ugly hurried hand crowding the margins whenever she had thought herself important enough to be brief. Near the back, where the notes thickened toward the final reaches, the absence opened again: two pages facing each other with a gap that had become familiar enough to start pretending it was natural.

It was not natural.

Perhaps the stew had steadied her. Perhaps the lamp was better here than it had been in the ferry house. Perhaps she had simply stopped looking at the wound as a fact and started looking at it as a piece of work. Whatever the reason, she saw the binding this time.

The ledger had been sewn in small folded gatherings, reed thread run through the spine and tied in knots that sat just under the cover wrap. One of those knots near the missing place had been lifted and retied. It had not been retied well enough to fool a bookkeeper, but it would pass a tired eye and wet weather blame. Two stitch holes had been widened by an awl or needle. The torn edge that had seemed ragged in poor light was only what remained after a page had been worked free from its sewing and then tugged at the last fibers.

Lale bent closer. Her thumbnail found the exact place where a patient hand had worried the thread loose.

No flood did that. No accident had done it either.

She said it aloud before she had decided to. "This was taken."

Riva stopped sharpening. "What?"

Tovin set down the bowl she was drying. "Of course it was taken. There's a hole in it."

"No." Lale kept her eyes on the spine. The lamplight showed the opened stitch like a tooth socket, neat and ugly. "Not torn in panic. Not rotted through. Somebody lifted the sewing, pulled the sheet free, and tied it back down."

Tovin's drying cloth stopped moving. Riva's whetstone went quiet. The stove still threw heat, but nobody near the table seemed to catch any of it.

Riva crossed over first, knife still in hand though not threatening anybody with it yet. She leaned over Lale's shoulder, smelling of wet wool and lamp oil. "Show me."

Lale pointed with the end of a fennel stem she had not realized she was still holding. "Here. See how the thread sits flatter on this gathering than the others? It was loosened. And these holes." She touched the paper lightly. "Something metal widened them. Whoever did it knew where to work without splitting the whole spine."

Iven came over slower. "That sounds insultingly competent."

"Take it any way you like."

Tovin wiped her hands on her apron and peered down with the flat concentration of a woman evaluating rot in a beam. "Could Sereya have done it herself?"

Lale shook her head before she could think. "Not after the cover wrap dried. This was later."

It had happened later.

The word sat there, thick as gristle.

Nami had gone still with a bowl against her stomach. "So whoever took it wanted us not to know something."

"Brilliant," Iven said softly. "At last, a family of thinkers."

Riva looked up at him. The knife in her hand went still. "You had hands on everything in that house."

His grin did not vanish. It only tightened at one side. "So did all of you."

"You steal for breath."

"And you don't?"

"Not from our own dead."

"No," he said. "You just work them hard."

Tovin clicked her tongue once. "Don't."

But Riva had already committed. Lale knew that look in her elder sister. Once it came down it behaved like a dropped anchor, all weight and no elegance.

"If there was anything in that ledger worth selling, you'd have smelled it."

Iven spread his hands. They were quick hands, rope-burnless compared to Riva's, beautiful in a used-up way, a mechanic's hands that still tried to pass for a gambler's. "Selling requires a buyer. What do you think the market is for half a saint's notebook? Men stroking themselves over route notes?"

"You sell scraps. You sell nails. You'd sell the piss from a shrine lamp if someone offered enough."

"Only if it was blessed."

"Iven."

He cut his eyes to Lale. "What? She likes me funniest when she's furious."

Lale hated them both for asking her to referee by standing near the evidence. She kept her fingers on the page edges because otherwise she might start smoothing the air between them like cloth that had bunched under bad cutting. Something in her own hand shape at the spine bothered her. A familiarity lived in the right amount of pressure. She pulled back at once.

"I didn't say it was him," she said.

Riva did not look at her. "Who else?"

That should have been an easy question. Iven was the family answer to damage with fingerprints. Iven lied small and often. Iven took things. Iven made appetite look like weather so nobody would ask what was starving underneath. Lale knew all that. She had said versions of it herself.

And yet the opened stitch refused him. This had been careful. This had been done by someone who knew the value was not in the paper as paper but in the absence it would make.

She could not say that either. Not with the taste of eel fat still in her mouth and a pressure starting behind her eyes like a finger pushed hard against old glass.

"Whoever did it could read enough to know what mattered," she said at last.

"That narrows us to half the delta and all our misfortunes," Iven said.

Riva rounded on him. "Don't get slick with me."

"Then don't start with a verdict."

"You think I don't know your angles?"

"I think you like having them ready before I even open my mouth."

"Because you keep proving me right."

The fight might have widened from there into old territory, paternity and debt and every other crude inheritance Sereya had left like knives in a drawer, but Daro made a small sound that was not speech.

It stopped them harder than shouting would have.

He had gone rigid in the chair, head tipped, one hand lifted a few inches from the table, palm angled toward the roof. The lamplight sharpened his throat. Whatever softness the meal had put back in his face was gone.

"What," Riva said, still hot.

Daro did not answer her. He stood, slow enough to make the motion seem involuntary, and crossed to the center of the floor. The house gave one of its ordinary settling creaks under his weight. He raised a finger.

"Shut up."

Nobody argued, not because he had authority, but because he sounded like a man listening at a deathbed for the exact second a breath failed.

He knelt and put his palm flat to the plank seams.

Below them the water moved through the eel races and the open gate channels beyond, carrying weed, runoff, dead insect husks, every loosened thing the flood could stomach. Lale heard that much: a mutter of current against posts, the hollow cluck under the boards where trapped air broke and reformed, and, somewhere outside, a loose lid tapping wood in the yard.

Daro lowered his ear to the floor.

Nami whispered, "Daro?"

His answer came just above breath. "There's a hitch in it."

Tovin swore. "That's water."

"I know."

"It's under my house every night."

"Not with that catch."

He stayed down there, eyes closed, face turned to the plank, holding still for the next knock under the boards. Lale watched his expression change by increments too small to call dramatic. Concentration came first. Then came recognition, or the performance of it. Then came the awful private hunger he wore whenever stray noise or chance lined up with his need to be addressed.

Riva saw it too. Her anger lost none of its force, only changed tools.

"Get up," she said.

Daro opened his eyes but did not lift his head. "Something came through the gate wrong. The current keeps catching on it."

"It's runoff under floorboards."

"Then why's it keeping time?"

That landed in the room like a hook.

Riva's mouth flattened. "There it is."

Tovin took one step forward, wiping damp hands on her apron with a violence that suggested she would happily strike revelation itself if it dripped on her clean floor. "Nothing under my house gets to be a sign because you're in a mood. Not eels, not rot, not whatever hitch you think the water has. Up."

Daro obeyed her, but reluctantly, as though he were tearing cloth along a seam he had hoped to preserve. He stood with his palm still damp from the floor. "I'm telling you the pull changed."

"You're telling me what you want it to be," Riva said.

Iven gave a short, ugly laugh. "Good. We've moved from accusing me to accusing the river. That's efficient."

Riva cut back toward him at once. She hadn't finished the earlier work. "Don't flatter yourself. You're still on the list."

"What list? The one where every missing thing ends up in my pocket before you count the spoons?"

"If the spoon fits."

"Fuck off, Riva."

Nami flinched at the sharpness, bowl still hugged to her ribs. Tovin took it from her and set it down before it slipped. Lale closed the ledger carefully, not from calm but because she could not bear for the pages to sit open while Riva and Iven sharpened themselves over them. The missing place had weight now. It was not memory yet, and it was not truth, but it already dragged at everyone in the room.

"Enough," she said, and was irritated to hear how schoolroom her own command sounded.

Nobody listened. They never had, not in family weather.

Daro looked at Riva with that half-bright shame-drunk fury he got when mocked out of sincerity. "You can call it runoff if you need everything to be a hinge and a rope and a thing you can haul. It was singing."

"Then let it sing till morning. We leave at first light either way."

"You think that's the point?"

"The point is getting to the next cut before the river changes its mind."

"It already changed."

"Good," Iven said. "Maybe it wrote a confession and tucked it under my pillow."

Riva took one step toward him. Tovin moved faster than either of them, planting herself between table and door with the ladle back in her fist like a cudgel.

"No," she said. "You're not turning my kitchen into a dock fight. Not after I fed you. Sit down or get out, and if you go out you'll do it barefoot."

That, absurdly, nearly held.

Riva stopped because Tovin had been ordering her around since she was fifteen and some reflexes survived any amount of age. Iven stopped because he did not want splinters in his soles and because Tovin, unlike Riva, would hit first and explain later. Daro stopped because the rain had thinned to roof ticks again, the trough lid had resumed its small tap in the yard, and he was dividing himself between those noises and whatever lay below the floorboards.

Lale stood with the ledger against her chest. Reed paper, damp board, old grease. The cover edge pressed into the soft part under her collarbone hard enough to leave a mark. She welcomed the point of it. It gave her body one plain fact to keep while the rest moved.

"I'm taking this upstairs," she said.

"There's no upstairs," Tovin said automatically.

"Then I'm taking it away from all of you."

Nami gave a breath of laughter that died quickly. Iven's grin twitched up on habit, Tovin's hand tightened on the ladle, and nobody let the sound live. That might have been the cruelest part.

Lale carried the ledger to the shelf by the stove and slid it back into the warm dry shadow above the hanging nets. Behind her, voices lowered without settling. Riva still breathed like a woman hauling load uphill. Iven muttered something too soft to catch and then louder, because softness never suited him for long, "If you're going to search my pockets, ask nicely first." Tovin told him she would search his skull for loose nails if he kept talking. Daro stood in the middle of the floor with his head turned slightly, still listening below the planks. Nami watched them all, gaze flicking from Riva's jaw to Iven's mouth to Daro's tilted head, waiting for the next crack.

The stove still ticked. Rain still moved across the roof in thin patient runs. The troughs below still carried black water under planks gone slick from years of boots. But Riva's stance had gone square again, Iven's voice had thinned to a blade, and Nami was watching them all over the rim of her own silence. An hour earlier the same boards had held laughter, plum pits, grease prints, Daro's filthy imitation of their mother, the shock of Riva smiling with her whole mouth. Ordinary life had been there in plain sight, shabby and real enough to touch.

Now Lale knew how little it took to puncture.

She stood by the shelf with the warmth of the stove on one side of her face and the colder draft from the floor on the other. The ledger sat above her shoulder, lighter by a page than it had any right to be. Below, the water went on under the house, carrying its noise through wood and nail and old smoke. The planks did not sing for her. Even so, she could feel them give very slightly under everyone's shifting weight, and across the room Daro kept listening, hungry for the next hitch to single him out cleanly.

The evening had only laid a table over moving water. The water was still there.
