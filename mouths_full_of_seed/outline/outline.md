# Mouths Full of Seed

## Primary Narrative Engine
The novel's engine is a combination of fantasy, speculative fiction, literary fiction, and character study. The speculative pressure comes from Reedmouth's rule-bound river ecology: when the pilgrimage channels clog, the drowned come back upstream with salt-fig seeds in their mouths and the living begin sleepwalking toward water. The literary and character-study pressure comes from five estranged siblings fighting over memory, sainthood, and the right to interpret their dead mother's life. Chapters should therefore turn on relational, spiritual, communal, and perceptual state shifts as often as on physical action.

## Story Spine
Seven years after the burial of ferryman-saint Sereya An, the monsoon begins returning corpses to Reedmouth. To stop the dead from drawing inland towns underwater with them, Sereya's five grown children must reopen the old pilgrimage channels in the order of her lovers: Tovin Reed at Reed Gate, Ilyan Moor at Hymn Cut, Paska Dey at Inkwater, Enver Coil at Coil Reach, and Bato Serin at Storm Mouth. Each reopened cut sends more of the dead seaward, but each stop also exposes another lie inside the saint's legend. The siblings' real enemy is not only the flood. It is the fantasy that one body can inherit holiness alone.

## Core Cast
- Riva An, 38, eldest, salvage pilot, practical to the point of cruelty when frightened.
- Daro An, 35, funeral singer, hungry for spiritual centrality and terrified that hunger makes him ridiculous.
- Lale An, 32, dyer and bookkeeper, the sibling who tried hardest to become legible to inland respectability.
- Iven An, 29, smuggler and cardsharp, unserious on purpose until the cost lands in his own flesh.
- Nami An, 24, cannery worker and crabber, youngest and most susceptible to the river's promises.
- Sereya An, dead before page one, ferryman-saint whose charisma, lust, and appetite for devotion built Reedmouth's channels and broke her family.

## World Logic
- Reedmouth's dead are supposed to move seaward along pilgrimage cuts maintained by labor, offerings, and remembered human bonds.
- Returned corpses with salt-fig seeds in their mouths indicate a broken downstream passage; once those seeds root in flooded ground, nearby living people begin drifting toward water in trances.
- The route can only be restored upstream to sea. Opening later cuts before earlier ones settle causes backwash, flooding, and spiritual confusion.
- The river's signs are real but not pure. It amplifies desire, grief, shame, and hunger, so chosenness is always suspect.
- Ferryman-sainthood is communal recognition, not official doctrine. Sereya turned it into a personal myth; the book's ending must undo that singularity without flattening the cost of what she built.

## Structural Promise
The first act reunites the siblings and proves the river's rules. The middle act moves lover by lover, alternating ritual chapters, labor chapters, humiliation chapters, and genuine quiet. The final act converts the inheritance struggle into a public schism, then resolves it through a collective rite that costs the siblings money, flesh, status, and illusion. Quiet chapters are not decorative; they establish what survival would mean if the family manages it.

## Chapter Outline

### Chapter 1: The Ferry House Takes Them Back
POV center: Riva.
The novel opens with a drowned girl returned to Sereya's abandoned ferry house, a black salt-fig seed rolling from her mouth into the wet boards. Riva comes back to Reedmouth expecting superstition and finds Tovin Reed already waiting, along with the siblings she has spent years avoiding. A second corpse lodges under the pilings before dawn, and the first seed sprouts in the house. State shift: the five siblings reluctantly accept that the pilgrimage channels must be reopened in lover-order or the dead will keep coming back upstream.

### Chapter 2: House of Oaths
POV center: Nami, with the family seen through her dangerous openness.
Inside the ferry house, the siblings sort Sereya's relics and re-enter their oldest roles: Riva commanding, Daro performing, Lale withholding, Iven deflecting, Nami absorbing everything as omen. They find Sereya's reed ledger of channel order, but a page near the final cut has been torn out. That night the drowned man's voice moves through the pilings and calls each sibling by name in a different register. State shift: the mission stops being logistical and becomes a contest over signs, memory, and hidden damage.

### Chapter 3: First Cut
POV center: Riva.
At Reed Gate, Tovin refuses speed and makes them clear the silt by hand. The gate will not move until each sibling names something Sereya took from them; the labor chapter is also the book's first honesty chapter. Tovin admits Sereya promised that none of them would inherit the route alone. When the iron teeth finally release, the current catches the first corpse-ferry and carries it downstream. State shift: the family gets proof that restoration is possible, and Riva becomes the practical center of the mission, which immediately threatens the others.

### Chapter 4: The Meal After the Dead Move
POV center: Lale.
The pressure drops for a single evening at Tovin's eel house. The siblings eat, joke, remember lovers and recipes, and briefly occupy a version of family not built from emergency. Then Lale notices the torn binding in the ledger and realizes the missing page was removed by someone who knew exactly what mattered. Daro hears singing under the floorboards, and Riva turns suspicion toward Iven. State shift: a domestic baseline is established and then punctured, sharpening both tenderness and distrust.

### Chapter 5: Shrine of the Blind Singer
POV center: Daro.
Ilyan Moor, the blind keeper of Hymn Cut, greets Daro as a son before he explains the rite. The channel only opens once someone speaks aloud how Sereya used desire as leverage rather than blessing. In front of listening pilgrims, the siblings name the humiliating ways their mother's charisma organized other people's devotion. Daro then sings the cut open and feels public holiness answer him. State shift: Daro learns Ilyan is his father and starts believing the river has marked him as Sereya's singular heir.

### Chapter 6: Bad Order
POV center: Iven.
Crossing Marrow Market, Iven cannot resist converting the family's first success into money and attention. He sells passage promises, jokes the crowd onto the ferry, and treats the route like a trick he can surf. Because the second cut has not settled, the current spits half-floating corpses into the market reeds and creates a grotesque jam of stalls, prayer boats, and bodies. The chapter is darkly comic until it stops being funny at all. State shift: the siblings' work becomes a public emergency, Iven accrues new debt, and Riva's authority is no longer uncontested.

### Chapter 7: Paska's Ink
POV center: Lale.
Paska Dey, the third lover and tattoo-cartographer of Reedmouth, refuses to help until Lale sits for new tide marks on her forearm. The bodily act of being inked reopens Lale's memory of the night Sereya died. Paska explains that Sereya distributed the route among lovers because any single heir would turn service into rule. Lale remembers tearing the missing page from the ledger after seeing the words "sixth mouth." State shift: the erased catastrophe moves from rumor to fact, and Lale loses the protective numbness that has defined her since Sereya's burial.

### Chapter 8: Sleepwalkers
POV center: Riva, with Nami's feverish visions pressing at the edges.
While the third channel is only half-open, living townspeople begin sleepwalking into floodwater. The siblings must rescue them while keeping the ferry and route intact. Nami cuts her hand on reed glass and develops a fever, but in that fever she sees a drowned infant curled in roots. Riva uses Daro's voice like a tool and worsens their hidden power struggle. State shift: the danger reaches the living in unmistakable form, and delay now carries bodily cost instead of just shame.

### Chapter 9: Lantern Supper
POV center: Iven.
At Lantern Quay, the novel takes a necessary breath. Fisher families feed the siblings, children play with prayer bowls, and the chapter lets conversation drift away from immediate crisis without abandoning its pressure. Iven slips off with Sena Dey, and their sex is not decorative: it lets shame, hunger, and recognition speak in the body before they speak aloud. Afterward Sena returns the torn ledger page Iven sold years earlier and makes him say Jun's name. State shift: Iven recovers the missing page and can no longer pretend his unseriousness has been harmless.

### Chapter 10: The Sixth Mouth
POV center: Lale, then the group in collision.
The recovered page names Jun, the drowned infant lost in Sereya's failed attempt to open Storm Mouth permanently with Bato Serin. Tovin confirms that the surviving children were scattered after Jun's death because Sereya feared they would repeat her hunger for singular inheritance. Daro reads the page as martyrdom, Riva as warning, Nami as kinship, Lale as evidence, and Iven as the bill arriving late. State shift: the saintly legend collapses into culpable grief, but the family has to keep moving anyway.

### Chapter 11: Ropehouse Fire
POV center: Iven.
At Coil Reach, Enver Coil names Iven his son and treats the entire family as wreckage Sereya left for others to clean up. The argument between father and son turns physical while they try to force the fourth cut. A lamp goes over; the ropehouse catches. They save Enver, but Riva's dredge barge burns and most of their heavy gear is lost to flood and fire. State shift: the mission becomes materially crippled, and Iven's appetite for spectacle is replaced by shame with an address and a face.

### Chapter 12: Reed Fever
POV center: Nami.
This is a care chapter. In an abandoned pilgrim hut, Nami burns with fever while the others improvise bandages, soup, and old forms of love they have not practiced in years. Lale and Riva finally speak honestly about how leaving Reedmouth after Sereya's death abandoned the youngest sister to other people's versions of the story. Daro confesses he has heard the drowned since childhood. State shift: the family chooses to continue without the dredge, on Sereya's old hand-poled funeral ferry, bound now by care rather than competence.

### Chapter 13: Inland Drag
POV center: Lale.
The flood pulse reaches Halcote, where families who mistrusted the An children begin losing relatives to trance-walking. The siblings split their labor for the first time: Riva drives toward the sea while Lale organizes lantern lines, grain-hall shelter, and forced restraint for the sleepwalkers. The chapter makes Lale bodily rather than merely observant; she has to strike a man to keep him from walking into quarry water. State shift: Lale proves she can lead outside the family, and inland communities start treating the siblings as necessary instead of cursed.

### Chapter 14: Salt Room
POV center: Nami, with Daro as countercurrent.
At the ruined sea chapel, Bato Serin appears alive: scarred, half deaf, and hidden in the back of the last cut since the failed rite. He refuses sainthood language entirely and tells Nami the river chooses no heirs; Sereya chose the lie because people obey a chosen body faster than a shared burden. Nami nearly offers herself anyway because being special still feels simpler than being responsible. Daro cannot survive the demystification and steals Sereya's brass-ferruled pole. State shift: the family's founding myth is rewritten in plain speech, and the private inheritance struggle becomes active schism.

### Chapter 15: One Saint, One Body
POV center: Daro.
Daro gathers pilgrims and frightened townspeople by offering what floodtime always wants most: one voice, one body, one answer. Riva tries to sell collective duty in the face of panic and looks weak beside him. The chapter should feel ceremonial, almost politically theatrical, without turning procedural. By the end, the flood crest is less than two nights away and the siblings are divided into rival plans for the final rite. State shift: the family conflict becomes public doctrine.

### Chapter 16: The River Taking Names
POV center: Riva and Iven in alternation of labor and damage.
The chase through unstable channels is the book's most kinetic chapter, but it remains grounded in bodies and decisions rather than action-movie abstraction. Nami nearly follows the dead overboard, and Iven mangles his left hand diving a mooring line across the cut so Riva's ferry can swing in. Daro opens Storm Mouth prematurely; pilgrims and corpses begin spinning in the same suction. State shift: catastrophe becomes immediate, and the siblings finally understand that the last cut can only be reset if all five of them act together.

### Chapter 17: Mouths Full of Seed
POV center: collective, but still anchored scene by scene in one sibling at a time.
At the sea breach, the family must perform the inverse of Sereya's lie. Each sibling surrenders a token from one lover and names an ugly truth the saint left in them: used desire, neglected children, hunger for exception, relief in being underestimated, the wish to be chosen even at lethal cost. Daro breaks his own instrument to redirect the rite and loses his singing voice rather than his life. The dead finally take the sea road. State shift: sainthood is remade as shared labor instead of singular inheritance, and the seeds go slack in the mud.

### Chapter 18: Low Water Feast
POV center: Riva, with the others in earned relation.
Weeks later, low water exposes the damage. The debts still exist, Iven's hand is permanently changed, Daro's voice is gone, and no one gets to turn the flood into a clean legend. Reedmouth's ferry house is rebuilt as a shared crossing rather than a shrine to one heir. Riva stays. Lale maps. Iven apprentices one-handed under Enver. Nami plants clean seed in a nursery bed and watches nothing monstrous come of it. The novel closes on a communal meal: not strategic, not symbolic in any cheap way, just the difficult beginning of a life.
