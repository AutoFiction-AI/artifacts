# Chapter 6

Outside the student union, the sidewalks still held the night's damp in their cracks, but the sky had already gone that hard white-blue Florida liked to put up before noon, a color with no mercy in it. Tiana had come up from Miami on the early bus, before the heat got rude. Now she crossed campus with her document folder tucked under her arm and the strap of her bag burning a line into her shoulder. Families moved around her in small, practiced clusters, carrying the kind of ordinary equipment that had started to look luxurious to her: refillable water bottles, envelopes clipped together, printed campus maps, a parent who knew where to stand next.

Nobody was cartoon rich. That made it worse.

One girl walked with a father in a pest-control polo and a mother still wearing hospital clogs, both of them arguing softly over whether the student union was the brick building or the glass one. A Haitian grandmother in a white skirt kept slapping her grandson's wrist away from his phone and telling him to look up at the campus God had provided. A white boy with acne scars and a Publix tan pushed a rolling suitcase while his mother read from a stapled checklist with the serious face people usually saved for medicine. They all looked tired. They all looked as if taking a day or two off work had cost them something. They had still come together. They still had a somebody to say you go get your ID while I hold the place in line.

For one stupid minute she hated all of them for having infrastructure.

Then she hated herself for that too, because she knew exactly what her mother had done to get her here at all. Marcia had ironed her blouse on the kitchen counter with the little travel iron that hissed and spat rust at the edges, then folded two ten-dollar bills into the back sleeve of the folder and told her to eat something that involved protein instead of just sugar and panic. Delroy, pulling on his boots in the half-dark, had kissed the side of her head and said, "Everything line up, baby girl. Go make them people know your name." He had said it the same way he said a tire only needed air when the tread was already showing wire. She had chosen, because she wanted the day, to hear promise instead of style.

At the check-in tables outside the student union, a student in an orange polo held out a name tag and a lanyard that said ORIENTATION in block letters. He had a shaved line through one eyebrow and the impatient, underfed look of somebody who had been up since five and had already decided every family in the state would ask him where the bathrooms were.

"Parents and guests are heading into the auditorium on the right," he said, not looking up yet. "Students go left."

"It's just me," Tiana said.

He looked up then, quick and embarrassed. "Right. Sorry. You're good. Sign here."

The pity never lasted more than a second. That was part of what made it cut. Nobody lingered over her lack long enough to feel ashamed of it. The whole place had been built for a version of family she could walk through but not trigger, like one of those grocery-store automatic doors that stayed shut if you were too light.

She signed her name, pinned on the badge, and followed the arrow for students. The lanyard ribbon dragged at the back of her neck. In the ballroom, the AC ran hard enough to make the damp at her temples go cold. Round tables had been set with plastic folders, campus maps, handouts about housing, dining, financial aid, honors, first-year success. She sat between a girl who had already bought a pale blue sweatshirt with the school logo on it and a boy whose mother had packed him cut mango in a glass container and did not care who saw.

The girl offered her a mint. Her acrylic nails were the soft pink of expensive restraint.

"You came up from Miami too?" she asked.

"Little River."

"Damn." The girl smiled. "My mom nearly cried about the drive from Orlando."

Tiana smiled back because the line invited it. "Then she would've died on the Turnpike."

The girl laughed, and for a few minutes it was easy. The dean on stage welcomed them. A student panel answered questions. People laughed in the right places. Tiana wrote notes she did not need because the writing itself calmed her down: office hours, add/drop, move-in, meal plans, book grants, honors advising. Facts sat still on paper even when the life under them did not.

When the panelists talked about residence halls, the room shifted. Students sat up. Parents started whispering to one another and circling dates in their packets. Tiana felt the word dorm move through her again, that private electric thing it had become since graduation night. The electric part was not the cinder-block room, the twin bed, or the shared bathroom with girls from Tampa and Broward and somewhere in New Jersey. What she wanted was the key and the sentence it implied: This is your room. Shut the door.

Outside the ballroom, campus kept offering itself in clean, expensive pieces. Shade trees. Brick paths. Bulletin boards announcing language clubs and undergraduate research and volunteering in Costa Rica as if going elsewhere were a thing people did to round out a life instead of save it. At the bookstore register a girl ahead of her added a planner, a school T-shirt, and a clear poncho to her stack without once asking the total. The cash register drawer chimed open. Tiana heard, inside that ordinary sound, how much of college depended on money moving without drama.

She let herself have the morning anyway.

At honors advising she got a sample first-semester schedule printed for her, fresh paper warm from the machine. The adviser, a woman with a silver braid and sneakers she had decorated with little green alligators, told her she had done very well and would do well here if she kept asking for what she needed before things got late. Tiana nodded as if that sentence did not have a whole second meaning pressing under it. She folded the schedule neatly into her folder and ran her thumb over her own name at the top. For one bright, reckless stretch of an hour she could almost mistake progress for arrival. She was on campus. She had a badge. People kept calling her an incoming student. The word incoming had direction in it.

By lunchtime the concrete was throwing heat back up at everybody's knees. Families lined up at food trucks and under striped umbrellas. Tiana bought a chicken wrap that sweated through the paper and a bottle of water that cost more than it should have. She sat on a low wall and ate fast, watching students flatten billing sheets against their knees and start doing the kind of arithmetic that could turn a welcome packet mean. She looked at her phone. Marcia had texted three words and three red hearts: You eat yet?

Tiana sent back a picture of the campus lawn and wrote, Ate. It's nice here.

Marcia replied almost immediately. Good. Ask every question.

Delroy had sent a thumbs-up and a fist earlier that morning. Nothing since.

Her afternoon slot was Financial Aid and Scholarship Verification, room 214, according to the schedule she had nearly started to trust.

* * *

The aid office waiting area smelled like toner, cold coffee, and wet clothes drying too slowly under AC. Students sat in molded chairs beneath posters about access, excellence, and deadlines. A television in the corner ran a silent campus slideshow while captions crawled along the bottom about summer disbursement dates. Tiana checked in at the front desk, gave her student ID number, and got handed a slip of paper with 63 on it. The woman behind the glass had lashes thick enough to cast a shadow on her cheeks and a tiny silver screwdriver tucked behind one ear, as if she had been fixing her glasses between students.

"Take a seat, baby," she said. "They're moving."

They were moving the way a wound moved when somebody kept prodding it to see if it still hurt.

Numbers jumped on the screen. Fifty-seven. Fifty-eight. Sixty-one. A father in cargo shorts kept tapping a highlighter against his knee while his daughter practiced saying "subsidized" under her breath like a new vocabulary word. A girl with a perfect low bun cried soundlessly beside a mother who had already stopped pretending not to be irritated. Nobody made a scene. That was the office's genius. It kept everybody in the same fluorescent key where panic looked like paperwork and shame sounded like printers.

When 63 flashed, Tiana stood so fast the folder edge sliced the web of skin between her thumb and index finger. A bright little line rose there. She licked it once, tasting paper dust, then followed the sign for cubicles.

The counselor in cubicle four wore lilac reading glasses on a beaded chain and a beige wrist brace under her cardigan. A half-eaten clementine sat on a napkin beside her keyboard, peeled with the neatness of somebody who liked to keep bitterness out from under the nails.

"Tiana Campbell?" the woman said.

"Yes."

"I'm Ms. Herrera. Have a seat."

Her voice was not unkind. It was worse than unkind. It was practiced. She typed with two fast fingers, clicked through screens, nodded once at whatever rose up in front of her.

"Okay," she said. "I'm seeing two issues here. One is residency verification. The permanent resident card we have on file expired in March."

Tiana had already taken the photocopy out of the folder. She slid it across. The copy looked old and grayer than she remembered, the lamination glare caught forever in the machine's bad light.

"My father said the renewal was submitted," she said. "He handled it."

Ms. Herrera glanced at the copy, then back to the screen. "Do you have the receipt notice for that? The I-797, if it was filed?"

"No. I thought the updated card would be coming."

"Okay." Ms. Herrera clicked again. "Right now I can't see a pending update in the system. If the renewal's in process, we can usually work with the receipt notice while immigration takes its time. But I need proof that the renewal was actually filed."

Actually filed: the phrase fell into Tiana's lap and stayed there.

"What's the second issue?" she asked, because the first one had already started spreading through her body and she wanted all the bad news at once if possible, the way you ripped off tape.

Ms. Herrera turned the monitor slightly. On the screen was Tiana's name, her student number, and under Requirements a red list:

Proof of current permanent resident status or receipt notice pending renewal.
Parent 2017 IRS tax transcript or verification of nonfiling.
2017 parent W-2s.
Signed parent verification worksheet.

The bullets were so tidy. The damage in them had no smell.

"Your FAFSA was selected for verification," Ms. Herrera said. "That happens all the time. For the 2019-2020 aid year, we need your parent's 2017 tax information and the signed worksheet before we can finalize aid packaging. Until both issues clear, your file stays on hold."

"Hold for what exactly?"

"For federal aid, need-based institutional aid, and final disbursement of your scholarship package."

"I already got the scholarship."

"You got the offer." Ms. Herrera looked at her over the tops of the lilac frames, not stern, just exact. "An offer isn't a disbursement. Right now it's provisional because your file isn't complete."

Tiana stared at the red list. She could feel the room narrowing around the plastic chair, the AC vent overhead blowing straight at the damp place between her shoulder blades.

"If I get these today," she said, "does that fix it today?"

Ms. Herrera gave her a look that contained no contempt and, because of that, landed harder. "If you upload everything cleanly and if the residency piece checks out, the hold can be reviewed. But no, not today. We're in July. Nothing moves today."

Nothing moves today.

Of course that was how the ruin came. It did not announce itself with a slammed door. It arrived as a queue number on a screen.

Tiana forced herself to ask the questions in order, because she could hear Marcia in her head saying if you want help from an office, leave with a list and a name.

"What happens if it doesn't clear before August?"

"Then your aid won't disburse on time." Ms. Herrera tapped the screen once with a capped pen. "Housing sees that. Billing sees that. You stay in pending status until the file's complete."

"Pending means what?"

"Pending means pending."

"For the school it does." Tiana heard her voice sharpening and hated it and kept going anyway. "For me, what does it mean?"

Something changed in Ms. Herrera's face. It wasn't softness. It was recognition of the gap between office language and rent language.

"It means you're not done yet," she said. "It doesn't mean canceled. But you need to move fast, and you need original, usable documents. Not stories about documents."

The humiliation of that almost made Tiana flinch.

She swallowed. "Can I print this?"

"I'm printing it now."

The printer coughed to life behind the cubicle wall. Ms. Herrera took the page when it came through, checked it, then slid it across. Her nails were clipped square and bare. On her ring finger was a white line where a ring had either been removed for work or removed for life.

"Call whoever handled your paperwork," she said. "Ask for the receipt number first. Then ask for the 2017 tax transcript and W-2s. The worksheet's online. If your parents didn't file taxes, that's a different route, but it's still paperwork. Don't let anybody tell you the school can just see it somehow."

The sheet in Tiana's hand was warm, newly born and already hostile. At the top, in block letters, it said UNSATISFIED REQUIREMENTS. Her name under it looked less like ownership than accusation.

"Thank you," she said, because she had been raised correctly.

"I know," Ms. Herrera said, and for the first time she sounded tired instead of professional. "I know, honey."

Outside the cubicle, the waiting room carried on in its low electric hum. Somebody laughed too loud near the front desk and then stopped. The girl with the perfect bun was still crying, but now she was talking too, words coming fast in angry little bursts while her mother stared straight ahead. Tiana stood beside a potted plant with dusty leaves and called Delroy.

He answered on the fourth ring with traffic and machinery roaring around him.

"Baby girl."

"I need the receipt number for the resident-card renewal."

"The what?"

She closed her eyes. "The receipt number. From when you filed it."

There was a beat in which she could hear him move the phone, maybe from one ear to the other, maybe away from his mouth so he could buy himself half a second.

"Why you need that right this minute?"

"Because the aid office can't verify anything without it, and they don't have any 2017 parent tax documents either. They have nothing, Daddy. They have a photocopy of an expired card and that's it."

"All right, all right. Slow down."

"No."

He made a small sound, almost a laugh, the one he used when he wanted a room to get friendlier than it was. "You up there sounding like police."

"I'm sounding like a person whose file just got put on hold."

"Hold don't mean dead."

"It means they won't finalize my aid."

"Them offices always say dramatic things. I sent what needed sending."

Tiana opened her eyes and stared through the office window at the parking lot beyond, heat already wobbling over the asphalt. Parents moved between buildings with campus folders tucked under their arms. Nobody out there knew her life had just shifted shape in a cubicle with a clementine on the desk.

"Then give me the receipt number."

"I don't have it on me. I'm working."

"Where is it?"

"Home. Somewhere safe."

The lie was not in the words. It was in how quickly he reached for safe, that pretty useless word, the same way he said soon when he meant not yet and handled when he meant stop asking me in front of people.

"And the 2017 tax transcript and W-2s?" she said.

"We'll print whatever they need."

"From where?"

"From online."

"With what account? Who set it up?"

He went quiet. The noise around him kept going: reversing truck, somebody shouting in Spanish, metal knocking metal. Under it she heard what she had not wanted to hear all day. It was not confusion. It was emptiness. There was no paper in his hand, no number, no plan, only delay wearing his voice.

"Daddy."

"Tiana, listen to me. Don't get yourself worked up in front of them people. Come home. Tonight we sort it out."

"Did you file it?"

Another beat followed, longer this time.

"Come home," he said again, and now the humor was gone. Shame had shortened him. "We'll talk home."

She hung up before she could say something that would break open in the wrong place.

Her thumb had started bleeding again where the folder had cut her. She pressed the warm requirement sheet against it and watched a faint pink crescent bloom through the corner by Parent Verification Worksheet. She almost laughed at that. Her blood knew where to land.

At the front desk she asked, in the most even voice she could assemble, if there was anywhere on campus she could upload documents if her family sent them later tonight. The woman with the screwdriver behind her ear told her yes, there were computer labs, but if the receipt notice did not exist then no amount of emailing would change the situation. She said it kindly. Tiana wanted to throw the potted plant through the television.

Instead she walked out into the heat.

The afternoon had gone full Florida now, thick and blunt and wet enough to wear. Her blouse stuck under her bra. The lanyard felt like a punishment ribbon. She sat on a bench outside the building because standing took more performance than she had left. The requirement sheet was already damp at the edges. It said aid hold, pending review, no disbursement until the file was complete. Housing would see that. Billing would see that. Every clock between July and move-in had started ticking in a language she hated.

Nothing moves today, Ms. Herrera had said.

The cruelty of offices was that they could be truthful and still miss the body. To the office she was a pending file, maybe a follow-up in five business days if the queue moved. To Tiana the problem had teeth. It bit through timing first. Orientation had been the day college was supposed to harden into matter. Instead the whole thing had gone filmy again, as if Gainesville itself were only a screensaver the school could shut off the minute her documents failed to load.

She looked down at her badge. INCOMING STUDENT. The plastic sleeve had already fogged from sweat.

What was she actually incoming to?

Marcia texted again: How aid go?

Tiana stared at the words until they blurred, then locked the phone instead of answering.

The bus back to Miami did not leave for another hour and a half.

* * *

By the time the bus hissed into the station that evening, the paper in her folder had gone soft at the corners from being handled too much. She had spent the ride south not crying, which took more work than the morning had. The man next to her had slept with his mouth open all the way to Fort Pierce. At one stop a woman got on carrying a foil pan that filled the whole bus with baked macaroni and onion. Two rows up, somebody watched fight videos without headphones until the driver threatened to pull over. The world went on with its same cheap mess, which felt insulting. She had wanted, just for that day, some sign that a hold on her future should alter traffic patterns.

Near Wildwood she had texted Malik because there were some humiliations that could not be carried all the way home untouched.

You around later?

He had taken twelve minutes to answer.

Class at seven. What time you get in?

She told him. He wrote back, I can catch you first.

Now, stepping down from the bus into diesel air and old gum heat, she saw him leaning against his silver Accord under a flickering light. He had changed out of his work shirt but not his pants, navy scrub bottoms with one knee gone paler than the other from too many washes. His backpack sat by the tire. In one hand he held a bottle of water, and with the other he was stripping the label off it in a clean white spiral, the way he always did when he was thinking or waiting or trying not to call somebody again.

He straightened when he saw her. He did not wave big. That was one of the things she liked about him. He never behaved as if public space needed more witnesses than it already had.

"You look cooked," he said when she got close enough.

"That's flattering."

"It's accurate."

His voice came out flat when he was worried, not cold but leveled. He picked up her bag before she could say not to, took one look at her face, and shut the trunk again without putting it in. "You want to sit first or move?"

She almost said move. Motion still passed for control if you didn't look too close. Instead she said, "Sit."

They got into the car with the doors open. The seat held the day's heat. Malik handed her the water. It was still cold enough to hurt her teeth.

"Thanks."

"I was already down here." He jerked his chin toward his backpack. "I got class in a little bit. So don't make me miss it for some vague nonsense. What happened?"

That was why she had texted him. He did not give her a soft Are you okay for the record. He went straight to the thing under the performance.

Tiana took the requirement sheet out of the folder and handed it across. He read faster than people expected him to. His eyes moved once down the page, then back to the top.

"Resident card expired," he said. "2017 parent tax transcript. 2017 W-2s. Verification worksheet. That's the whole fight?"

"That's enough fight."

"I'm asking."

She watched the station lights reflect in the windshield. Her own face hovered there faintly, badge still hanging off her neck like proof from a day that had revoked itself.

"I thought the resident-card renewal was already done," she said. "Daddy told me he handled it. He kept saying he handled it. And the tax stuff, he said we'd print whatever the school needed. I get there and there's nothing there. Nothing's late. Nothing's misplaced. There's just nothing."

Malik nodded once, still looking at the page.

"You called him?"

"From outside the office."

"And?"

She laughed, short and ugly. "And apparently safe somewhere in the house is now a legal category."

That lifted one corner of his mouth. It wasn't a smile. It was recognition.

"So he doesn't have the receipt."

"He doesn't have anything."

"You know that or you think that?"

She turned to him. "Why do you do that?"

"Do what?"

"Cross-examine me when I'm already bleeding."

He looked at the tiny paper cut on her hand. The dry blood had crusted into the crease. "You aren't bleeding much."

"You know what I mean."

"Yeah." He folded the page once, careful not to crease the printed boxes too badly, then opened it again. "I do. I also know there's a difference between a man lying because he's ashamed and a man lying because there was never a thing to lie about. Which one is it?"

The brutal part was that she did not know. Or maybe she did and hated herself for how quickly her mind had filled in the answer. Delroy's pause on the phone had not sounded like a file cabinet. It had sounded like a pocket turned inside out.

"I think," she said slowly, "if there was a receipt, he would've said the number wrong before he admitted he didn't have it."

"That's more like it."

"I don't need you grading my grief."

"Good. Because this isn't grief yet."

She let her head fall back against the seat. The station loudspeaker crackled something nobody could understand. A woman in pink slides dragged two little boys past the car, each boy holding one handle of a grocery bag between them and fighting anyway. Malik waited. He was good at that too, better than she was. He did not rush silences to prove he could hold them.

"They put a hold on my file," she said.

"I saw."

"No, I mean..." She groped for it, angry at language for not coming fast enough. "I mean I sat there all day being an incoming student. They had the badge and the campus map and the little honors schedule with my name on it and people saying welcome, and then one woman clicked around on a screen and all of a sudden I was not a person who was coming anywhere. I was a list. I was a maybe. I was a problem waiting in a queue."

Malik's thumb kept worrying the peeled strip of bottle label until it snapped.

"Yeah," he said quietly. "That part is real."

The agreement hurt worse than comfort would have. It made the humiliation solid.

"What keeps scraping at me is I used my own mouth to sell his lie," she said. "I kept telling that woman he handled it. Like I had proof. Like I wasn't the one sitting there with an expired card and a hold notice."

"You didn't know."

"Now I know enough."

She looked down at the scrub pants, the backpack, the spiral of label in his hand. "You were really going to class."

"I still am, unless your father makes me commit a felony on the drive over."

That pulled a sound out of her, rough and alive enough to count as a laugh.

"What class?"

"Human growth and development." He made a face. "Which sounds fake and then they hit you with fifty pages on attachment theory."

"That tracks."

"I knew you'd be rude."

He tossed the label strip into the cup holder, then finally looked at her full on. He had a tiny nick under his chin from shaving too fast and a line of wear at the bridge of his nose where his cheap sunglasses sat every morning on the drive to the rehab clinic. She liked that line. It made him look less polished than the care in him actually was.

"What are you doing when you get home?" he asked.

"Asking him once."

"Asking what?"

"What he did. Where the paperwork is. When he plans to stop talking to me like I'm a child he can entertain past the hard part."

"Good." He reached into the back seat and found a pen under a workbook, clicked it, and tapped the requirement sheet. "Ask for the receipt number first. If he changes the subject, ask again. Then 2017 tax transcript. Then 2017 W-2s. Then who signed what. Don't go in there with ten speeches. Go in there with nouns."

She took the pen from him and wrote on the margin of the page:

Receipt number
2017 tax transcript
2017 W-2s
Worksheet

The handwriting looked too clean for the feeling under it.

"You always this bossy?" she asked.

"Only when people act like fury is a filing strategy."

"Maybe that's how it works at my house."

"At your house it might be foreplay for yelling. Not the same thing."

She cut him a look. He lifted a shoulder.

"You called me," he said.

"I know."

"So either you wanted a witness or you wanted me to lie to you. I can do one of those."

That landed somewhere low and hot in her, not because it was romantic, though some part of it was, but because witness was the word. He had named the thing she had not wanted to admit. She had not texted him for rescue. She had texted him because somebody besides her needed to know the day had happened exactly as it had happened. That the office had reduced her and she had still sat upright in the chair. That her father's voice had emptied out in her ear.

"A witness," she said.

"All right."

Neither of them moved for a while. The station lot emptied and refilled around them. Somewhere a bus hissed, knelt, rose. Malik checked the clock on the dash, grimaced, and rubbed at the line between his brows.

"You hungry?" he asked.

"Not in a way food helps."

"That wasn't the question."

She thought about the chicken wrap at noon, the stale pretzels at three, the coffee smell on the bus that had turned her stomach. "A little."

He reached into his backpack and came up with a crumpled brown bag from the Haitian bakery near his job. Inside was a beef patty, flattened at one corner but still warm in the middle.

"I forgot I had this," he said. "Eat before you go start war."

She took it. The pastry left grease on the requirement sheet when she set the paper on her lap. Somehow that felt right. Documents should have to live in the same world as appetite.

"What if he says he'll fix it?" she asked after a few bites.

"Then ask how."

"What if he lies?"

"He will."

The answer came so clean she turned to stare at him.

He shrugged. "Maybe not all the way. Maybe just enough to get through tonight. Men like that hate the first truth because after the first one every other truth starts lining up behind it."

"You sound like an old woman."

"My grandmother raised me till I was nine. You want these skills or not?"

She smiled despite herself, flaky pastry sticking to her thumb. He looked relieved but did not make a thing of it. He never tried to collect gratitude while it was still wet.

"You need to go," she said.

"In a minute."

"You'll miss attachment theory."

"Tragic."

She finished the patty and folded the empty bag small. Outside the car, Miami had gone the color it got just before full dark, everything outlined but not yet softened. She could feel home waiting at the other end of the drive, the garage apartment with its thin walls and one overhead light that made every argument look cheaper than it already was.

Malik started the engine. The AC coughed awake.

"I'm taking you there," he said.

"I didn't ask."

"No. You looked like bus stations should've been outlawed. Close enough."

On the drive north he did not fill the space. Traffic on the expressway moved in thick red streams. Billboards rose and went past: personal injury, urgent care, Jesus, debt relief, a beauty school promising transformation in eight months or less. Tiana held the page of requirements in both hands like it might still change wording if she watched it hard enough. It did not. At a light near Little River, she saw two girls in fast-food visors smoking behind a dumpster, their laughter bending around the cars. The neighborhood came back piece by piece: botanica signs, chain-link fences, the fried smell from the jerk stand on the corner, men at the bus stop standing farther apart than the shelter required because heat turned other people's skin into extra weather.

When Malik turned onto her block, he lowered the music that had barely been there to begin with.

"Call me after," he said.

"After what?"

"After you find out whether this is a mess or a betrayal."

"That sounds dramatic."

"It sounds specific."

He pulled up half a house down from the stucco place so he would not be in the exact line of sight from the main house windows. Another thing he understood without having to be taught.

The light was on in the apartment. Through the thin curtain she could see movement, one shadow crossing and then crossing back. Delroy's shape, tall and loose even when tired. Micah's voice rose once, laughing at something. Ordinary sound. The house had the nerve to be ordinary.

Tiana kept her hand on the folder a second longer.

"You got the list?" Malik asked.

"Yeah."

"Then go in there with the list."

She opened the car door. Night air hit her damp face. The cut on her hand had reopened in the drive and left a small rust mark on the corner of the page, just beside receipt number. She stepped out with the folder tucked hard under her arm, the day still clinging to her in sweat and printer ink and diesel. Behind the curtain, Delroy laughed again, easy as if paper had not been eating through her all day. The apartment was still moving to a Saturday rhythm she had missed, and the ordinary noise of it made her angrier.

She shut the door and walked toward the apartment with the list in her hand, ready to stop him from laughing.
