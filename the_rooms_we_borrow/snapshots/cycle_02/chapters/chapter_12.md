# Chapter 12

The trophy case outside the school office held dead football seasons under glass, and the front doors nearby kept breathing out weak air-conditioning that smelled like copier heat and old floor wax. Micah stood beside it with the back of his T-shirt stuck to him from shoulder blade to waistband, pretending to read the names etched into little brass plates from years before he was old enough to care. August had that particular Miami meanness where the air did not move unless it was being paid to. It was easier to look at dead football seasons than at the office window where Ms. Francis could see him waiting.

Marcia had sent him with bus fare, one extra dollar for a cold drink if the heat got at him, and exact instructions. Go to the office, pick up the packet, come straight back. Don't stop by the docks. Don't stop by anybody's house. Don't let Dre carry you nowhere stupid. At the sink she had counted the bills twice before folding them into his palm, her fingers cold from rinsing out somebody else's old yogurt container to save for leftovers. Behind her, Kerene had leaned over the hot plate and curled one eyelash with the back of a teaspoon because she still had not replaced the metal curler she swore TSA had stolen. Tiana had been digging through the dresser drawer that was not really hers anymore, moving her document sleeve from one stack of shirts to another like the papers might get legs and leave without her. The room had been full before breakfast. Micah had understood what Marcia meant without her saying it plain. The house did not have room for one more problem wearing his face.

The office window slid open with a tired scrape. Ms. Francis looked at him over half-moon reading glasses that had been repaired at one hinge with clear tape. Her desk fan was turned straight at her chest, making the laminated saint card pinned beside her monitor flutter. "Micah Campbell."

He pushed off the wall and went up to the opening. "Yeah."

"Your mother didn't come?"

"She had somewhere to be."

Ms. Francis gave him the look adults used when they wanted a child to understand that his people had failed before she said it out loud. Then she reached below the counter and produced a fat yellow envelope with his name scrawled across the front in black marker. Through the paper he could already see the hard rectangles of forms, the kind that wanted initials in six places and a signature where the school had highlighted a lie in fluorescent pink.

"Behavior contract, reentry acknowledgment, probation conditions, class issue report." She tapped the stack with one long acrylic nail painted pale blue, tiny silver cross glued at the tip. "Every highlighted line needs a parent signature. Every page. If she misses one, they'll send it back and you'll sit in the dean's office till somebody can come."

He took the envelope. It was damp at the corners from somebody else's hands. On the front, under his name, a red stamp said DISCIPLINARY REVIEW.

"I know how to carry papers," he said.

Ms. Francis's mouth pinched. "Then carry them home, baby. That would be a start."

He walked out before he said something that would make the envelope thicker next time.

Outside, the heat hit like a hand over the mouth. The lunch bell had already rung for somebody, and the courtyard was noisy with first-week bragging, girls with fresh knotless braids taking photos against the mural wall, boys in clean sneakers walking the long way around puddles so nothing got on the white. Micah kept the envelope folded against his thigh so nobody could read the stamp. He still felt them reading him anyway. The dean's office had a way of leaking. You could walk out with a packet in your hand and half the school already knew you had been turned into paperwork.

Dre was leaning by the bike rack in a faded navy Saints cap so soft the brim bent in the middle. He had his house key hanging on the same bright-green shoelace his grandmother had made him wear all summer after he lost three copies in one month and had to sleep on the porch once. He saw the envelope and whistled low.

"Damn," he said. "That thing thick. They gave you homework for being Black."

Micah snorted before he could help it. "Shut up."

"I mean it. That packet look like they want your blood type, your first sin, and your mama's signature in triplicate."

"They want her to sign where they already decided I'm a problem."

"Same thing."

Dre straightened from the rack and fell in beside him. He always moved like he was arriving one second before the joke. Today there was sweat darkening the collar of his gray shirt, and his laugh landed a little too fast, like he was trying to get in front of something.

"You going straight home?" Dre asked.

"That's literally the mission."

"Mission from who."

"From the woman who pays rent in my house."

"That's crazy. I thought maybe some other woman had stepped up."

"Man."

Dre grinned. "All right. Straight home. I was only asking because if we cut behind the old laundry lot, it's faster than the bus stop, and I'm not standing in that sun to smell them boys' armpits."

Micah looked toward the avenue. A line was already forming at the bus stop, kids shoulder to shoulder in the weak strip of shade, everybody loud because silence at school always sounded like weakness. He could take the bus like Marcia told him. He could hold the packet under his arm and sweat through the yellow paper while people shoved and laughed and tried to look over his shoulder. Or he could walk the back route and be home sooner. He had maybe twenty dollars in cash hidden in a sock for clippers oil and a phone recharge, and he did not want to spend bus fare twice if the transfer failed. The family had gotten so tight around money that even Marcia's extra dollar felt hot against his thigh.

"It's faster?" he said.

"By like ten, fifteen minutes."

"You said five yesterday."

"Heat changes distance."

"That's not a real thing."

"It does if your skin works."

Micah shifted the envelope to his other hand. Across the street, a patrol SUV crawled past the corner store slow enough to count faces. Nobody reacted. In Little River the cops moved through like weather: not surprise, exactly, just something you noticed before deciding whether you needed to hide from it.

Dre saw him looking and clicked his tongue. "Come on. We cut through, grab a drink at Mr. Vega's if he's open, and you're home before your mother starts calling your sister to ask where you at."

"My mother doesn't need Tiana to find me."

"Everybody needs Tiana to find something."

That one landed a little wrong. Tiana's folder flashed through his head, traveling all morning from drawer to bed to grocery bag to nowhere, and then the sight of Kerene's folding cot opening where Tiana used to stack her dorm things. He shoved both images down before they could turn him soft.

"Fine," he said. "We cut through and then I'm gone."

"Look at that. Youth making positive choices."

"You should be a guidance counselor."

"I'd be great. I already know all the bad routes."

They headed along the side street, away from the front of the school and the buses and the girls taking pictures. The narrow strip of shade beside the chain-link fence was crowded with bottle caps, wet leaves, and the chalky white droppings of birds that liked the power line. Somewhere behind the auto shop a bass line thumped from an open trunk, the same eight seconds of dancehall looping because somebody was testing speakers or patience. Micah loosened his grip on the yellow envelope and let himself believe, for half a block, that the day might stay the size Marcia had asked for.

The lot behind the old laundry used to have a cinder-block building on it. Now it was broken concrete, shoulder-high weeds, and a mural half-scraped off a wall that still showed one blue wing and the edge of a halo. People cut through there all the time. Men from the barber shop smoked there, kids ducked through it to avoid the avenue, and old women with grocery carts crossed it because the sidewalk buckled worse than the dirt path through the middle.

Dre hopped the shallow curb and kept talking. He talked when he was happy and when he was scared, and with Dre those two moods wore a lot of the same clothes.

"My grandma says if I fail algebra again she's putting me in church every Wednesday till Jesus himself comes grade my papers."

"Jesus would fail you too."

"That's hate. That's exactly why your packet so thick."

"You better stop looking at it."

"I can't. It keeps calling to me. Open me, Dre. Read how the system loves your friend."

Micah shoved him lightly with the back of his wrist so the papers would not bend. Dre laughed. Then the laugh cut off.

A cruiser swung broadside at the mouth of the lot, tires spitting grit. Another came in from the alley behind them before Micah fully understood what he was seeing. Doors opened. Boots hit concrete. The first command came before either boy had said a word.

"Hands. Let me see your hands. Right now."

The older officer had one hand on his holster and the other out flat like he was stopping traffic. Sweat had darkened the neck of his vest, and a nicotine patch was peeling up from the skin below his ear. The younger one moved fast to the rear, mirrored sunglasses hiding his eyes, jaw shiny with fresh razor bumps.

Dre's shoulders jerked up. "We ain't doing nothing."

"Hands where I can see them," the younger cop said. "On the wall. Now."

Micah lifted both hands. The yellow envelope slid against his palm. For a second his brain noticed everything stupid and small at once: the grit under his slides, the sour smell of old detergent baked into the lot, a fly walking the wet rim of a crushed soda cup. Then the bigger understanding came in after it. Two cruisers. Two Black boys. No room to explain the order those facts had arrived in.

"Turn around," the older officer said.

Micah did it. He did it right away. He put both palms on the hot stucco wall at the edge of the lot and spread his fingers. Beside him Dre muttered, "Man, my grandma's key gonna brand me through my shirt," and tried to laugh. The laugh came out thin.

"Matching description from a strong-arm two blocks over," the younger officer said. "One of you reaches, one of you runs, this gets worse. You understand me?"

"Yes, sir," Dre said quickly.

Micah kept his mouth shut. He had learned something in the dean's office, and then unlearned it on the bus, and then learned a different thing in the neighborhood. Sometimes the first answer out of your mouth became the part they hated most.

The older cop stepped in close enough that Micah could smell mint gum fighting old coffee. "Empty your pockets."

"My hands are on the wall."

"Then tell me what's in them."

"Phone. Bus card. Gum."

"Any weapons?"

"No."

"Drugs?"

"No."

Beside him, Dre said, "All I got is my phone and lint. You can keep the lint if you want it."

"Shut up," the younger officer snapped.

Dre shut up so fast his teeth clicked.

Micah heard a radio crackle from one of the cruisers. Male juvenile. Black. White shirt. Gray shirt. Last seen heading south. The description was so broad it could have netted half the avenue. He stared at the wall and felt the heat pressing through it into his palms. A lizard darted out from a crack near the base and disappeared under a beer can.

"Step back one at a time," the older officer said. "Slow."

Micah stepped back. The envelope slipped from under his arm. It hit the ground, burst at one corner, and three forms slid out over the dust. One page turned faceup. DISCIPLINARY REVIEW. PARENT ACKNOWLEDGMENT. His own name, flat under the sun like something pinned for display.

He bent his head to look. Only that.

"Eyes front," the younger officer barked, and the back of a hand hit between Micah's shoulder blades hard enough to pitch him forward.

His cheek slammed the wall and skipped off the rough stucco. Pain flashed white and immediate. By the time he caught himself, he could taste blood. The younger officer grabbed both wrists, yanked them down, and ratcheted a cuff onto one hand.

"I didn't do anything," Micah said, because the words came out before he could stop them.

"Then stop resisting."

"I'm not resisting."

The officer drove him sideways toward the hood of the cruiser anyway. The metal was so hot it felt alive. Micah's face hit the edge where hood met grill and something opened under his eye with a sharp wet sting. He heard Dre say, "Yo, he said he ain't-" and then stop when the older cop swung toward him.

"You want to go down too?" the older one said.

"I'm already here, man." Dre was trying to smile. He sounded twelve years old. "I'm not going nowhere."

The older officer patted him down in quick, punishing slaps. The green key lace bounced against Dre's chest with every shove. "Keep talking," he said. "See how that works for you."

Micah's free hand was flattened across the hood. Blood from his cheek had started to slick the silver paint. He could see it in the warped reflection below his fingers, a red smear cutting through the ghost of his own face. He tried to turn his head away from the heat and the metal and the officer pushed him harder.

"Other hand."

"It's under me."

"Then move it."

Micah moved it. The second cuff closed. Tight. The paper on the ground stirred in a weak breeze and one sheet skated under the front tire.

"Where's the bag?" the younger officer asked.

"What bag?"

The pressure on the back of his neck increased. "Don't play with me."

"I don't know what bag you're talking about."

"Woman got her purse snatched outside the check-cashing place. Two males. Your size. Your clothes. Where is it?"

"I came from school." The words came out mashed against hot metal. "That's school papers."

"Everybody comes from school when I stop them at three o'clock."

The lot had gone strangely quiet around the noise. Micah realized it was not actually quiet; it was the kind of attention that felt like quiet because every other sound had arranged itself around it. The bass from the auto shop was still thumping, but farther away now. A bus hissed somewhere on the avenue. Somebody said, "Lord." Somebody else said, "That's Marcia's son." He did not know which hurt worse, being made into a suspect or being recognized while it happened.

He twisted just enough to see the edge of the sidewalk beyond the weeds. People had stopped there. Mr. Baptiste from the barber shop was standing with a broom in his hand, black apron still tied over his shorts, hair clippings stuck to the hair on his shin. Miss Pauline from church had one pink foam roller still clipped in her bangs and a plastic Family Dollar bag cutting into the crook of her arm. A little boy in a Spider-Man backpack was staring openmouthed until the woman with him turned his shoulders away. Nobody stepped into the lot.

Miss Pauline took out her phone.

The officer saw Micah looking and shoved his head back down. Fresh pain tore across his cheek. This time blood ran warm along the side of his nose and dripped from his jaw to the hood.

"Keep your face down."

Micah wanted, with a force that scared him, to jerk free just to prove that his body still belonged to him. He did not do it. He fixed his eyes on the curve of the hood where the blood was spreading and thought about Marcia counting bills at the sink, about the small mean dignity of following one instruction exactly. He had almost made the day that small. That was the part that made him feel sick.

Dre was still talking. He could never help himself. Only now the jokes had gone strange and brittle, coming apart at the edges.

"Officer, for real, you can check my messages. I was literally trying not to die in summer school all week."

"You think this is funny?"

"No, sir. I think my grandma is gonna think it's funny when I come home late and she locks me out, though."

The older officer spun him, put him against the second cruiser, and cuffed him too. Dre sucked air through his teeth. "Damn. Damn, all right. That's tight."

"Good."

Micah watched the reflected crowd grow in the cruiser window. Two girls from school had paused on the far sidewalk with their phones held at chest height, screens dark and ready. Their mouths were shaped with that hungry little shock people wore when something bad was happening to somebody they knew but not to them. Tomorrow the story would come back whether anybody posted or not: his split cheek, the yellow envelope by the tire, those girls pretending their screens were nothing.

The younger officer dug into Micah's pockets, pulled out his phone, the bus card, the gum, and held up the folded one-dollar bill Marcia had sent for a drink if he got dizzy in the heat.

"Big score," Dre said automatically.

The younger officer looked at him. "You really want to keep performing?"

Dre stared straight ahead. "No, sir."

That was when Micah knew Dre was scared for real. The sir had dropped out of him earlier like something learned. Now it came back from deeper than pride.

The radio crackled again. A different description this time. Red jersey. Bicycle. Older. The older officer put a hand to his shoulder mic and listened without expression. The younger one stayed where he was, palm flat between Micah's shoulders like he did not trust gravity to do the job.

"Victim says no bike?" the older cop said.

Static. Then something Micah could not make out.

The officer looked at the boys, at the envelope on the ground, at the tiny pile of useless things from Micah's pockets on the hood. For one second Micah thought embarrassment might cross the man's face. It did not. What crossed was annoyance, thinner and meaner.

"How old are you?" he asked.

"Fifteen," Micah said.

"You?"

"Sixteen," Dre said.

"Either of you on probation?"

Micah stared at the papers by the tire. "School probation."

The younger cop gave a short laugh through his nose. "That tracks."

The older officer ignored him. He pressed a finger to the mic again, listened, then said, "Turn around."

Micah did not move.

"Turn around," the officer repeated. "You're being released."

Released. Like they had been holding something they owned.

The younger cop uncuffed him first. The metal peeled off skin already ridged red. Blood had dried sticky along Micah's cheek and into the corner of his mouth. When he straightened, the world tipped sideways for a second from heat and adrenaline and the hard clamp he had kept on everything inside himself. He steadied a hand on the hood. The older officer uncuffed Dre, then stepped back like proximity itself had become inconvenient.

"Next time when officers give commands, you move faster," he said.

Dre laughed once, high and unbelieving, then turned it into a cough.

Micah said, "I moved."

The younger officer slid his sunglasses down just enough for Micah to see his eyes. They were pale brown, flat, and tired in a way that made the whole thing uglier. Not rage. Not panic. Just another stop under bad sun.

"You kept talking," he said.

"You asked questions."

The older officer cut in. "Take your things and go home."

Mr. Baptiste had already stepped into the lot by then, not close, but closer than before. He bent and picked up the yellow envelope with two fingers, careful of the blood that had spotted the front. One of the forms was wet at the corner where it had landed in something dark and sticky near the curb. Miss Pauline was saying into her phone, "Yes, Marcia, right by the old laundry lot. No, baby, come. Come now." Her voice carried because she had never learned how to have private worry.

Micah wanted the ground to split and take him with the weeds and the broken bottle glass.

Mr. Baptiste held the envelope out. His pinky nail was still grown long on one hand from the old barber habit, lacquered clear. "Take your papers, youth."

Micah reached for them. The younger officer flinched toward his belt before remembering the scene was over. That tiny movement burned almost as bad as the cut.

"You all right?" Mr. Baptiste asked.

It was a stupid question, and it was the only one available.

"I'm good," Micah said.

He was not good. Blood had dried tight from cheek to jaw, and one side of his face had started pulsing in time with the heat. His wrists felt peeled. The word good still came out because people were listening.

Dre wiped his upper lip with the back of his hand. "Man, that's some bullshit."

"Language," Miss Pauline said automatically, then looked at the blood on Micah's face and covered her mouth with her fingers.

The cops got back in their cruisers. No apology. No explanation anybody could take home and put in a sentence that made sense. Tires ground against the broken concrete. Then they were gone, leaving the lot to its regular trash and sun as if nothing had happened there that would still be happening later.

Marcia arrived three minutes after the cruisers left, half-running from the corner in nursing shoes she still wore even though she was between jobs. Her blouse was damp down the spine. She must have come straight off a bus because she still had her tote bag crossbody, the zipper open, papers and a plastic water bottle knocking against her hip.

She took one look at Micah and went very still.

Micah knew that look. This was the kind of stillness she put on when anger had to pass through math before it was allowed to become noise.

"Who touched him?" she asked.

Miss Pauline started in at once. "The younger one slam him on the hood and then the wall and I called you same minute because I said no-"

"Which officer?" Marcia asked, eyes still on Micah.

Mr. Baptiste lifted his chin toward the avenue where the cruisers had gone. "Badge number maybe on the call log if somebody got it. It happened fast."

Marcia stepped up to Micah and put two fingers under his chin. He smelled the outside on her, hot fabric and bus metal and the bleach she used to clean Celia's bathroom still living in her skin weeks after the job was gone. When she tilted his face toward the light, the skin under the cut pulled so hard he hissed.

"Look at me properly," she said.

He did. Her pupils flicked over the swelling, the blood, the grit stuck in the scrape. Then to his wrists. Then down to the envelope in his hand. That last part made something uglier pass through her mouth and disappear.

"Did they charge you?"

"No."

"Did they say why?"

"Matching description."

She let out one breath through her nose. "Of course."

Dre shifted beside him, suddenly smaller without the jokes doing their work. "Miss Marcia, we was just cutting through. We wasn't even doing nothing. They rolled up and-"

"I know you weren't," she said, not unkindly. She looked at the green lace with the house key bouncing against his chest, then at the sweat pasted to his cap line. "Go call your grandmother before she hears it from somebody else."

"Yes, ma'am."

He hesitated, like he wanted to say more, then touched Micah's shoulder with two fingers and headed for the sidewalk, already digging for his phone. Halfway there he looked back and tried on one more weak grin. "Don't let them put alcohol on that. You gonna cuss in front of your mother."

Micah almost smiled. It hurt too much.

Marcia turned to Miss Pauline. "Thank you for calling me."

"You know I had to."

"I know."

That was all. No scene. No shouting after the cops who were already gone. Marcia locked her face smooth again, but the narrow muscle in her cheek jumped once and kept jumping.

"Can you walk?" she asked him.

"Yeah."

"Don't lie."

"I can walk."

"Fine. We're going to the clinic."

He looked at her then, really looked, and saw the calculation begin behind her eyes before she said it. Bus fare there. Bus fare back. The fee at the window. Maybe medicine. Maybe stitches. Maybe an X-ray if his face was broken in some way they could not see yet. All of it moving behind her expression while the neighborhood watched. She reached into her tote, took out the small bottle of water, wet a corner of a paper towel from the envelope Mr. Baptiste had handed back, and cleaned the blood from under his eye enough to see the cut better. The towel came away pink, then red.

"It's not deep enough for emergency," she said, mostly to herself. "Walk-in first."

"Mummy, it's fine."

"Don't tell me what fine is with your face open."

The clinic was across town on a strip with a tax office, a botanica, and a storefront church whose windows were painted to look like cloud light. The air-conditioning inside hit Micah so hard his skin pebbled. The waiting room smelled like disinfectant trying to cover mildew, and a television bolted high in the corner played a daytime court show with the volume turned low. Under the plexiglass at the front desk a laminated sign listed fees in neat black print. New patient visit: $90. Wound care extra. Imaging separate.

Micah looked away from the sign fast, but not before the number fixed itself in him.

The receptionist had red reading glasses on a beaded chain and one heel tap worn white at the back of her shoe. She passed over a clipboard without looking up. "No insurance card?"

Marcia's voice went church-polite. "No insurance."

"Payment is due before the provider sees him."

"How much for just the visit?"

"Ninety."

There it was again. A whole bag of groceries. Most of a light bill when the light bill still got paid on time. More than half a week of whatever haircut money Micah had been scraping together two heads at a time.

Marcia took out her wallet. She kept her cash in different folds, and Micah knew those folds the way other boys knew game stats. Grocery fold. Bus fold. Rent fold, thin as tissue now. She peeled bills out one by one under the receptionist's patient eyes. Two twenties. Two more. A ten. Another ten from a side pocket where she hid emergency money so small it barely qualified. The woman counted it twice, stamped the intake sheet, and slid the clipboard back.

"Fill this out and take a seat."

Micah stared at the line asking for reason for visit. Three answers rose at once: matched description, police, because my face cost ninety dollars now. Each looked worse in writing than it already felt. Marcia took the clipboard from him and wrote for him in her tight practical print: facial injury after fall.

He looked at her.

She did not look back. "Sit down."

"That isn't what happened."

"I know exactly what happened." She kept writing. "Sit."

He sat. The vinyl chair squeaked under him. Across the room a toddler in only one shoe was asleep with his head in his auntie's lap, mouth open, while she ate salt crackers from her purse and watched the court show like it mattered. Somewhere in the back a printer started, stopped, started again. Micah pressed the folded gauze packet the nurse had handed him at intake against his cheek and felt the sting answer up into his eye.

Sorry sat in his throat with all the rest of it: that he had only taken the shortcut because it was faster, because the bus line was long, because he had been trying to make the errand smaller, not bigger. When the cop said matching description, the phrase had sounded almost casual, the way people said afternoon shower, like a thing that just happened in the weather of certain bodies. None of it came out. In the waiting room his shame felt less like speech and more like something packed under the skin with the gravel dust.

Marcia finished the forms and sat beside him. Up close he could see sweat dried white in the seam of her blouse. Her tote bag rested between her feet, one hand looped through the strap as if even here something might get taken.

"Does your head hurt or just the cheek?" she asked.

"Mostly the cheek."

"You feel sick?"

"No."

"You see double?"

"No."

She nodded once. Practical questions first. Feeling later if there was any room left.

"Miss Pauline said they had you on the hood."

"Yeah."

"And everybody saw."

It was not a question. He stared at the courtroom TV where a man in a cheap suit was lying badly about a car note.

"Yeah," he said.

Marcia sat with that a second. Then she said, quieter, "Lift the gauze."

He did. Blood had soaked a rusty moon into it. She took a fresh square from the little paper sleeve and pressed it to his cheek herself, gentler than he expected.

"Hold it there."

He held it. Her thumb had a tiny nick near the nail from some house job or kitchen knife or cheap plastic package that had fought back. He stared at that instead of her face.

"Mummy."

"What."

"You put fall."

She looked straight ahead. "The man at the desk didn't ask for truth. He asked what got us through the window fastest."

"So that's it?"

"For now, that's it."

For now was grown-up code for leave it ugly and call it handled. Everybody on the block had already seen his face on the hood, so what was a lie on a clipboard supposed to do, un-happen it? He bit the inside of his cheek, tasted fresh metal, and let the gauze drink more blood while the clinic printer spit out somebody else's bill in the back.

After a while the television changed segments and the air-conditioning clicked into a louder gear. Marcia reached into the side pocket of her tote and found the yellow envelope. She flattened the bent forms on her knee, smoothing each page with the side of her hand. His blood had dotted the corner of the parent acknowledgment form and feathered the ink on the red disciplinary stamp.

"They still want this back Monday?" she asked.

"Yeah."

"Then they'll get it back Monday."

He watched her read the highlighted lines. Behavior expectations. Reentry conditions. Parent cooperation. Student accountability. All the clean words institutions used when they wanted to describe pressure without naming who got to apply it. She did not make a speech. She only kept smoothing the pages flat, even the one tire had nearly taken, until the paper stopped trying to curl.

At the desk, the receptionist called another name. A woman rose, gathered up her purse and pharmacy bag, and went through the door. The fee sheet under the plexiglass caught the fluorescent light so hard it looked wet.

Micah pressed the gauze tighter. His cheek throbbed. His wrists burned. Behind all of it was the bright fixed image that would not leave: his own face in the silver hood, flattened and red, while people he knew watched from the sidewalk and decided how to carry it home. The officers had uncuffed him and driven off, and the neighborhood had exhaled, and still the day kept charging them for what it had done.

When the nurse finally opened the door and called his name, Marcia stood first. She tucked the forms back into the envelope, slipped the receipt into her wallet, and lifted her tote. Ninety dollars gone before anyone had even touched the wound clean. Micah rose with the gauze pressed to his face and followed her down the bright hallway while Marcia kept her hand looped through the tote strap, the clinic receipt folded inside her wallet with the bus money.
