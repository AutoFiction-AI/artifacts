# Chapter 15

From the patch of floor he'd slept on, Micah watched Marcia write a storm list on the back of a church envelope as if the pressure of her pen could stretch what they had. Her handwriting got tighter when she was angry; this morning it looked like she was trying to cut the paper. Cheap screens buzzed with the weather alert on the dresser, on the milk crate, beside Kerene's folding cot. On the TV balanced crooked on top of the little fridge, a woman with hurricane hair and perfect teeth stood in front of a map of South Florida and talked about watches, feeder bands, storm surge, all the ways water could stop being ordinary. The morning light had gone the color of dishwater. The air wasn't moving so much as holding itself back.

"Water, bread, batteries," she said, mostly to herself. "Candles if the price isn't stupid."

Tiana stood by the bed they called hers though it had stopped being only hers weeks ago, pulling her document folder up from the floor where she'd kept it within reach all night and stacking it on top of her jeans. She had on one of Malik's old T-shirts, hanging off her in a way that made her look younger until she lifted her face and the room remembered not to mistake her for a child.

Kerene sat on her cot in bicycle shorts and a bleach-white tank top, tying a satin scarf over her hair with the speed of somebody who had learned to make herself presentable in bus-stop glass. One hoop earring was missing, so the other one flashed by itself when she turned. "Miami people love a panic," she said. "You'll see. Same people who never buy canned food in daylight suddenly turning survivalist."

"We don't have money to critique culture," Tiana said.

"I can critique for free."

When Micah pushed himself up, the skin along his bruised cheek pulled tight. The police had left him that souvenir a week ago, and the swelling had eased to the point that everybody stopped looking at it as soon as he walked into a room, which was worse in its own way. He went to the sink and splashed water on his face. The faucet coughed warm before it turned cool.

It had been three nights since Delroy walked out. For three nights Marcia had moved around the apartment like she had tightened every screw in her own body. For three nights Tiana had talked in that trimmed-down way that meant she was spending whole paragraphs privately. Micah had expected shouting to keep echoing after the fight. Instead the place had gone meaner than shouting. It had gone careful.

Marcia counted bills onto the counter: fives flattened by old sweat, singles, a little hill of quarters from the bowl over the fridge.

"Thirty-two dollars and seventy cents," she said. "Bus fare comes out of that too, so don't let anybody sweet-talk you into nonsense."

"Who is 'you'?" Micah asked.

"You if you ask foolish questions. Tiana if she decides plain water is beneath her. Kerry if she starts shopping like a woman with options."

Kerene pressed a hand to her chest. "Me? I'm a daughter of strict economy."

"You say that in lipstick."

That got the smallest crack at the corner of Marcia's mouth. Micah saw it and felt something in him unclench before he could stop it.

Marcia looked at him. "Put on a shirt. You're coming with your sister."

"I know how to buy bread."

"And I know how fast you can turn one errand into a disappearance."

"That was one time."

"Try not to make me nostalgic for simpler failures," Tiana said.

He almost snapped back, but the TV woman was now standing beside a spinning cone in the Atlantic, and something about all that bright official color made him want to prove he could do one ordinary useful thing without being escorted like a little boy. He found a clean-enough T-shirt under the cot, pulled it on, and took the envelope from Marcia.

"What if the batteries are gone?" he asked.

"Then the batteries are gone." She handed him two canvas grocery bags. "Bring back what the money can carry. Not what your pride wants to carry."

Outside, Little River looked like it always looked before weather people on TV started sounding excited: low stucco houses sweating under the sky, chain-link fences, a rooster shouting like he had not read the forecast, bass from somebody's car trunk making the puddles in the cracked street skin over and shake. But there was a second layer to it now. Men dragged patio chairs under eaves. A woman in pink rollers hauled cases of bottled water into the back of a Lexus with dealer plates. The whole block was trying not to admit who had prepared yesterday and who was pretending today still counted as early.

Tiana walked at a pace that made conversation expensive. Micah kept pace beside her and swung the empty bags at his knee.

"You think it'll hit?" he asked.

"I think if it doesn't, people will be mad they spent money." She adjusted the strap of her purse. "If it does, they'll pretend they knew all along."

"You always talk like a guidance counselor when you're stressed."

"You always get funnier right before you say something stupid."

He grinned despite himself. That was almost normal. He'd take almost.

The Family Dollar on Twenty-Ninth had already been picked thin. Aisles looked chewed. The battery rack held nothing but odd sizes for hearing aids and one dented flashlight hanging there naked without its packaging. The canned-food shelf had gaps like missing teeth. Two old men argued in Spanish over the last case of water while a little girl sat in a shopping cart chewing on the cardboard corner of a cracker box. Somebody had broken open a pack of candles and taken half.

Micah went straight to the bread and found four loaves left, all of them the kind that flattened if you looked at them wrong.

"Get two," Tiana said.

"That leaves no money for everything else."

"There's no everything else."

She was right. He hated that she was right in the brisk, adult way she had gotten lately, like reality had decided she got to be rude first. They found one gallon of water on the bottom shelf behind a pyramid of sports drinks nobody in their apartment could justify, a jar of peanut butter, two cans of black beans, and a bag of rice so small Micah almost put it back out of insult.

At the register he kept waiting for the total to climb past what they had. Thirty-one twelve. He put the coins on the counter fast, before the cashier could look bored in a way that felt personal. Outside, Tiana stood under the narrow awning and looked at the plastic bags like they were evidence from a crime scene.

"No batteries," Micah said.

"No shit."

"No candles either."

"No, I noticed the darkness coming."

Rain hadn't started yet, but the wind had found the flags on the used-car lot and was whipping sharp snaps out of them. Micah wanted to say they should've done this yesterday, should've had a father who stayed home, should've had money for stupid things like extra food before TV women started drawing cones over the map. He said none of it. He took the heavier bag and started back.

The apartment sat behind the main house, one low converted garage with its thin little strip of yard and its concrete step darkened by old mildew. From the gate Micah saw the plywood first, two mismatched sheets leaning against the wall like oversized cards. Then he saw Delroy.

His father had on the faded Dolphins cap he wore when he wanted to look busy instead of guilty. Sweat had pasted his T-shirt to the middle of his back. The right shoulder sat a little lower than the left, as if the body had gotten tired of pretending symmetry. In one hand he held a grocery bag. In the other, a loose handful of nails. He looked up when the gate scraped.

"Took long enough," Delroy said, too light, too easy.

Micah stopped. Tiana stopped harder.

Marcia came to the doorway with a dish towel in her hand. The threshold locked up around her. The TV was still talking inside, that cheerful weather voice floating out through the screen door like it belonged to another family.

Delroy lifted the grocery bag a little. "I got batteries. D-cell. A couple candles too, though one pack bust open. A brother from the church had scrap plywood from a kitchen job. I told him I'd help him haul tomorrow if the world's still here."

Marcia's face did not change. "Tomorrow is ambitious."

"I like ambition. It gives the day shape."

Tiana laughed once through her nose, not because anything was funny. "You would show up with jokes."

He looked at her, and for a beat the charm slipped. "I showed up with this first."

Micah watched the truth of that settle heavier than any apology Delroy had left unsaid. A grocery bag with batteries in it could beat a speech every time. Marcia stepped back from the doorway.

"Bring them in before rain catches them," she said. "Lean the plywood by the wall, not on the bed."

The space she left was narrow as a blade, but it was space.

Delroy nodded once and bent for the plywood. The motion caught in his shoulder halfway down. He tried to hide it by turning it into a stretch, but Micah saw the mouth tighten. He saw Tiana see it too and look away.

Micah put down the grocery bags and grabbed the lower edge of one sheet before anybody asked him.

"Watch your hand," Delroy said automatically.

"I know where my hand is."

"Good. Keep it."

They carried the plywood inside together, awkward at first because neither of them wanted to adjust to the other's pace. The board bumped the doorframe and shaved off a little curl of white paint. The apartment seemed even smaller with Delroy back in it, as if his absence had been a space of its own and now that space had been taken back by his body, his smell of sun and cut wood and whatever borrowed couch or borrowed room he had been sleeping in.

Kerene looked into the grocery bags, then into Delroy's. "Only a storm could make all of you men bring home provisions in daylight."

"Put my name in the book of miracles," Delroy said.

"Not till I see if the batteries work."

Micah set the rice on the counter and felt the mood of the place rearranging itself around practical things. There was bread on the table, water in the sink to chill what little it could, plywood against the wall, and weather outside getting grayer by the minute. Nobody had forgiven anybody. That would've been corny and impossible. But there was work now, and work made lying harder. You either had the thing in your hand or you didn't.

He could live with that for one day.

* * *

The power went out just after three, not with drama but with a tired little blink. The fan died first. The TV lost the weather woman mid-warning and turned the room black in a way that showed how much noise the screen had been making even when nobody listened. The hush pinned everybody where they stood. In it they could hear the neighborhood react: one child cheering, somebody cursing, a car alarm doing two offended chirps and quitting.

"Well," Kerene said. "Now the fridge becomes a countdown."

Marcia opened it and stared into the weak cold. "We cook what can spoil."

"We?" Kerene moved her gently aside with her hip. "No, make way. If we're about to sweat through the apocalypse, we're not doing it on sad food."

She tied her scarf tighter and started pulling things out with brisk little sounds of disapproval. There was half a cabbage, one onion soft on one side, three eggs, a half stick of butter wrapped back in paper, a can of mackerel, leftover rice in a pot, two plantains with skins gone more black than yellow, and a scotch bonnet pepper sleeping in a margarine tub. She lined them on the counter like she was judging a competition no one else had wanted to enter.

"This is enough," she said. "It's not abundance, but it's enough."

"You say that about everything," Tiana said from the bed, where she was plugging her phone into a dead wall socket out of habit and then catching herself.

"Because everything wants to pretend it's less than it is. Hand me that knife."

Micah took the knife first. "What I get?"

"You get to cut onion and not take off your thumb. That's a serious assignment."

"Why do I always get vegetables?"

"Because if I put you near hot oil you'll flirt with the flames."

Delroy, sitting on the overturned bucket by the door and testing the batteries in the old lantern, snorted. "She's learning the boy."

"Please," Kerene said. "The boy introduced himself."

The match scratch sounded loud in the dark. Marcia lit one of the broken candles and jammed it into a chipped coffee mug. The flame did not do much for the room except prove how close everybody was standing to everybody else. Sweat gathered fast under Micah's shirt. The apartment held heat like a grudge. Outside, the wind was stronger now, carrying somebody's radio in pieces through the walls.

Kerene set the plantains to fry first. The smell of hot oil changed the whole place. Hot oil and browning plantain put a hard bright edge on the air, something alive that shoved back at the blackout. She moved around the kitchenette with quick elbows and small authority, tapping Micah's wrist with the flat of the knife when he cut the onion too big, stealing Tiana's church program to fan the burner when the flame sulked low, telling Marcia not to hover unless she intended to season something.

"Don't waste all the gas," Marcia said anyway.

"If the storm takes the line tonight, you'll regret caution more than flavor."

"That sounds like something a woman says right before she empties my cupboard."

"Your cupboard was already in mourning."

Micah laughed, then regretted it because laughter in that apartment lately felt like siding with somebody. But Marcia only shook her head and reached for the salt. The candle showed the shine on her forearm, the calluses in her palm. She looked ready to snap and too practical to bother.

Kerene dumped the rice into the pan with the mackerel and let the grains pop and hiss in the butter. Then onion, cabbage, pepper, all of it turning fragrant so fast Micah's stomach cramped at the smell. She mashed the soft side off the onion against the cutting board with her thumbnail and flicked it away.

"At the salon yesterday," she said, "this lady wanted me to do knotless braids down to her backside while she sat there watching storm coverage and drinking a frappuccino. I said, 'Miss, if a tree falls on your head, at least the braid part will stay neat.' She never laughed. Women with money hate a joke unless they bought it."

Tiana smiled despite herself. "How much was she paying?"

"She wasn't paying enough to hear my private comedy."

"You still did the braids, though," Micah said.

"Of course I did the braids. Poverty is not an artistic movement."

That one got Delroy. He laughed himself into a cough. Even Marcia let out a sound that could've become a laugh if she trusted it. The lantern flicked on in Delroy's hand then, weak but working, and he lifted it in triumph.

"Light," he announced. "Behold the modern age."

"Don't blind us with luxury," Tiana said.

He set the lantern on the table. The battery light was uglier than candlelight, more honest about faces. It showed the yellow-green fade of Micah's bruise, the tired shadows under Tiana's eyes, the new gray at Marcia's temples he was not sure had been there in June. It showed Delroy thinner than usual, chin more hollow, and that made Micah angry in a direction he couldn't name. A man could disappear for three nights and come back looking hungry for sympathy. That hardly seemed fair.

Kerene slapped his hand away from the plantains with the spoon before he touched one.

"You'll live."

"I might not."

"Then die after serving."

"Kerry, stop bullying the help," Delroy said.

"The help is stealing."

"That's a family business," Micah muttered.

The words left a mark as soon as he said them. Tiny, but there. Tiana looked down. Marcia's face closed one notch. Delroy rubbed his jaw and let the joke around his mouth sag.

Kerene moved fast, quicker than the silence. "Then the family business says plates," she announced. "Nobody is standing up in this heat to have emotional revelations. Sit."

She fed them out of the little pot like she had cooked for a full house all her life and intended to keep doing it until the house respected her for it. The rice and cabbage and salted fish stretched under butter and pepper farther than Micah would've thought possible. The plantains were fried at the edges so dark they tasted almost smoky. She gave each of them half a boiled egg, cut neat so it looked like more. Micah ate too fast and burned the roof of his mouth. He did not care. The food had weight. It made his hands stop shaking in that small embarrassed way they had been doing all afternoon.

Kerene sat down with her own plate, crossed one leg under herself, and ate with appetite instead of performance. That mattered more than Micah would've expected. She didn't play martyr. She didn't do that fake auntie thing where the woman cooking kept saying she was full already while watching everybody else clear the pan. She took the softest center piece of plantain for herself and added a sliver more pepper to her rice.

"This needs avocado," she said around a mouthful. "American people don't respect avocado. They refrigerate it till it tastes like furniture."

"Every storm chapter of your life has a menu critique?" Tiana asked.

"Obviously. Otherwise what is memory for?"

"To remember who ruined it," Marcia said.

Delroy glanced up. Micah's fork stalled halfway to his mouth, waiting for the room to split back open with grease on the plates and storm light at the window. Marcia only pointed with her fork at the cabbage.

"This is good," she told Kerene. "You overcooked the onion, but the rest is good."

"From you, that's almost a bouquet."

"Don't get greedy."

Micah watched the exchange the way he'd watched adults examine a tire before a long drive, testing it with little kicks, deciding if it would hold. The apartment still ran so hot his shirt clung to him. The power was still gone. Rain hadn't even started in earnest yet. Nothing was fixed. But everybody was chewing instead of accusing, and the noise of forks against plates, of Delroy asking for a little more pepper, of Tiana actually answering him without ice in her voice, changed the air more than the fan ever had.

When the plates were empty, Delroy got up and took them to the sink without asking if anyone wanted the gesture. Marcia did not thank him. She also did not tell him to leave them there. She passed him the dish soap by sliding it across the counter with two fingers.

Micah saw that and looked away before it could turn into anything tender. He wasn't ready for tenderness from either of them. He wanted facts. The soap bottle in Delroy's hand. The hot pan cooling on the stove. The lantern holding its weak circle over the table. Outside, the first spit of rain hit the plywood by the wall with the sound of fingers drumming for patience.

* * *

By dusk the storm had moved from television to property.

The sky pressed low over the neighborhood, green-gray and swollen, and the palms beyond the fence had turned their undersides out like frightened animals. Rain came in quick slants, then stopped, then came again harder. The main house had not opened up once all day; its shutters were already closed, and the car in the drive was gone. That felt about right.

Delroy stood the first sheet of plywood under the front window and squinted up along the frame. "Not a perfect fit," he said, "but perfect is for people with budgets."

"You say that about a lot of ugly work," Marcia said from inside.

"And still the ugly work stands."

Micah picked up the hammer before his father could ask him to. The handle was slick with old use. Delroy had brought a coffee can half full of nails, bent ones mixed in with straight. He sorted through them with his left hand because the right kept flexing open and shut at his side.

"We need a stud here and here," Delroy said, tapping the wall. "Don't waste the nails in the thin part."

"I know what a wall is."

"That's not the same as knowing how one lies."

It was the kind of line Delroy liked, halfway between instruction and a joke about life. Micah should've rolled his eyes. Instead he watched his father tap the frame again, listening for the deeper knock. Delroy had always known where things would hold. In other men that would've made him seem reliable. In Delroy it only made the failures hurt more. If you knew where to place weight and still put it wrong, what did that say?

They lifted the plywood together. Rain speckled Micah's forearms. The board was heavier than it looked, one corner swollen from having lived some former life in somebody else's kitchen. Delroy braced it with his shoulder and made a face before he could stop himself.

"Leave it," Micah said.

"Leave what?"

"The hero business. Just hold the bottom."

Delroy's face tightened with the old reflex, pride reaching for a line. Then the wind shoved a wet breath through the yard and he gave a short nod.

"Fine. Hold it level."

Micah planted his sneaker against the concrete lip and raised the hammer. The first nail bent. The second went in crooked.

"Don't beat it like it insult your mother," Delroy said. "Hit straight."

"Maybe the nail started it."

"Again."

Micah hit straighter. The board answered with a hard good sound. Delroy moved his hand an inch, pointing with two fingers instead of lifting the whole arm. He had gone quiet now, no stories, no extra charm. Micah had forgotten that version of him existed. The one who knew the work and had stopped decorating it.

From behind them Tiana held the lantern out through the doorway so they could see the nail heads. Its yellow beam shook when the wind caught her arm.

"If that board flies off," she said, "I'm blaming both of you equally."

"That sounds democratic," Delroy said.

"That sounds accurate."

Kerene appeared behind Tiana with a bath towel draped over her shoulder like a corner woman at a fight. "Don't let the rain in after all my cooking. I refuse to feed water."

Marcia set a second mug candle on the windowsill inside. The little flame bent every time the air found a crack. "Micah, watch your thumb."

"Everybody in here obsessed with my hands."

"Because your hands still have some use left," she said.

He drove the next nail and felt the sting jump up his arm. Something about the sting helped. It kept him in his own body instead of in the bad places his mind wanted to run: the police car hood hot under his cheek, the sight of Claude in their doorway, Delroy's voice going loud and then gone. Right now there was only wet wood, hammer weight, his father's low corrections.

They did the front window, then the side one by the bed. The second sheet barely covered it. Delroy found an old shelf board behind the kitchenette and used that to patch the gap.

"That's going to hold?" Micah asked.

"Long enough if the storm has manners."

"And if it doesn't?"

Delroy looked out at the darkening yard. "Then we learn something."

That was almost a joke, but not enough. Micah slid the shelf board into place and Delroy passed him the nails. Their hands touched, brief and damp. Delroy's palm was rougher than Micah remembered, and hotter.

The bathroom window was worst. Narrow louvers, ridiculous for weather, impossible to board from the inside without climbing half into the sink. Delroy tried to reach above it, hissed under his breath when the shoulder caught, and dropped his arm.

Micah pretended to study the coffee can of nails until Delroy said, flatly, "Take it."

There was no charm in it, no performance, just that.

Micah climbed onto the toilet lid, one sneaker on porcelain, the other wedged against the tub. He held the scrap board with his left hand and hammered with his right while Delroy steadied his calf so he wouldn't slip. It should've felt childish, getting balanced by his father like that. Instead it felt exact. Necessary. The man's hand on his leg said: don't fall, not right now, not for something stupid.

"A little higher," Delroy said.

"My arm ain't telescoping."

"You talk too much for a workman."

"You hire me anyway."

Delroy's mouth twitched. "Cheap labor."

"Family business," Micah said, and this time the words landed differently.

They got the last nail in just as the rain changed from scattered to serious. It came hard, flattening the hibiscus by the fence and sending a burst of cold spray through the gap under the door. Tiana swore and yanked the lantern back. Kerene grabbed the towels. Marcia was already rolling up the mat.

Micah jumped down from the toilet and nearly slid. Delroy caught his elbow with the good hand.

"Careful."

"I said I know where I am."

"You keep proving that's negotiable."

Micah should've pulled away. Instead he let Delroy's hand stay on his arm for one extra second before stepping back. The apartment smelled of wet concrete now, and cut wood, and the hot iron tang of a storm finally making good on its threat. His father looked tired as shit. He didn't look noble. He didn't look tragic. He just looked used up in a way Micah recognized from mirrors.

Delroy flexed the bad shoulder once, testing the damage like a man counting money he already knew was short. Micah saw him do it and said nothing. The silence between them had changed shape, slack now instead of cocked, built around a task finished together.

For one night, maybe that was enough.

* * *

The real weather came after dark.

By then the apartment had been remade into a smaller version of itself. The mattress had been dragged away from the windows. Towels were stacked by the door. The lantern sat on the table and the emergency flashlight on the milk crate, with one candle in the bathroom and one on the counter. The little fridge was sealed shut to preserve its last bit of cold like a secret. Every phone was dimmed low. The boarded windows took away what little outside view they had, so the room felt less like shelter than the inside of a shut box set gently in a floodplain and forgotten.

Rain hammered the plywood in uneven bursts. The wind hit between those bursts, then piled in with the rain and bullied conversation out of the room. Micah sat on the floor with his back against the bed and one knee up, listening to the boards report on every hit. Across from him, Tiana had her folder inside two plastic shopping bags and tucked under her pillow as if paperwork could sleep safer than people. She was supposed to take the originals to the office handling her renewal in the morning, and the thought of weather knocking that appointment sideways had her jaw set hard. Kerene had changed into an oversized T-shirt with a dancehall singer's faded face on the front and rubbed coconut oil into her knees because, she said, bad weather did not excuse ash. Marcia stood over the stove without cooking anything, just needing a place to put her hands.

Delroy stayed near the door on the overturned bucket. He was in a spot where he could help if the latch failed and still pretend this was not him coming home, only a man taking cover where his family happened to live.

The first leak started over the sink. A dark bead on the ceiling, then another, then a steady tap into the dish drainer.

"Pot," Marcia said.

Delroy was already up. He moved the drainer, slid the soup pot under the drip, adjusted it half an inch so the water hit center. He did it without asking. Marcia watched his hands and not his face.

"That seam was bad from June," she said.

"I know."

"Apparently."

He took the shot and let it stay there. Outside, something metal banged loose and kept banging. Micah felt the noise in his teeth.

Kerene looked around at all of them and clapped once. "Nobody is going to sit in separate corners like murder suspects. Come closer. If this roof plans to embarrass us, it can do it to a group."

"You really think you run this house because you fried two plantains," Tiana said.

"Three. And you're all depressing."

Still, she dragged her cot nearer the table. Tiana slid down off the bed and sat cross-legged on a folded blanket. Marcia wiped her hands on her skirt and lowered herself onto the edge of the mattress with the careful exhale of somebody whose back had been working since dawn. Delroy hesitated at the bucket until Micah heard himself say, "The lantern doesn't reach over there."

It came out like logistics, but Delroy took it for what it was. He moved closer and set the bucket by the wall.

For a while they only listened. The neighborhood had gone strange under weather. There was no bass and no roosters. There was just rain, wind, and now and then the human sounds that survived both: a baby crying in the main house, somebody shouting for a dog, a burst of laughter from down the block that sounded almost insane and then, because of that, useful.

Micah picked at the label on the lantern battery pack lying on the table. The adhesive came off under his nail in damp gray curls. He wanted to say something funny. He wanted to say something that would prove he wasn't sitting there scared. But the fear had weight tonight, heavy and crowding, like an extra person in the room nobody had made space for.

Delroy cleared his throat. "When Gilbert come through Clarendon," he said, "my mother make us all sit under the table because she swore the table was the strongest thing in the house."

"Was it?" Kerene asked.

"No. The table broke one leg before midnight. We spent six hours holding up righteousness with one hand."

Micah laughed before he could help it. "That sounds like your side."

"It absolutely is," Marcia said.

Delroy pointed at her with one finger. "Yet you married into it."

"Everybody makes one bad investment."

The line could've cut. Instead it sat there with a little heat and then cooled, because the rain thudded harder just then, because the soup pot rang sharp with each new drop from the ceiling, because nobody in that room had the energy left to turn every truth into a weapon.

Kerene leaned back on her palms. "In my grandmother's yard, when a storm was coming, she used to kill the fattest rooster first. Not for safety. For soup. She said if the roof went, at least people should go with a proper meal in them."

"That's practical theology," Delroy said.

"That's island logic," Kerene corrected. "Very different."

"You say that like Miami doesn't have its own foolishness," Tiana said.

"Miami has no storm culture. Miami has retail."

"That's true," Marcia said. "I saw one woman buying twelve loaves of raisin bread like cinnamon was structural support."

Even Tiana laughed at that, folding forward with it. Micah stared at her. It had been days since he'd heard that sound from her without an edge hiding inside it. The lantern put yellow half-moons in her eyes. In that light she looked like what she was: eighteen, tired, funny, still so young that laughter could take years off her in one hit.

"What'd you buy?" Micah asked her. "Before we met up. You went out yesterday."

"Shampoo."

"For a storm."

"My hair still exists in weather, Micah."

"You would've saved us if the roof flew off looking moisturized."

"Somebody in this family should have standards."

Kerene pointed at her. "See? Survival."

The wind hit the plywood broadside then, a long shove that made the whole wall complain. The candle flame went flat. Marcia's hand found the mattress beside her, fingers spread, not dramatic, just bracing. Delroy looked toward the window automatically, already calculating whether the nails would hold, whether the frame would go first, whether a man with one bad shoulder could do anything about it if it did.

Micah felt the same calculation move through him, faster and dumber. Could he get the trunk high if water came? Could he hold the door? Could he carry anything useful if Marcia started naming what mattered? He hated that fear came dressed like homework.

Kerene saw his face. "Don't make that expression," she said.

"What expression?"

"The one that says you're auditioning for man of the house. The house doesn't need audition tonight. It needs bodies with sense."

"She's right," Marcia said, before Micah could get defensive. "Nobody in here is proving anything to weather."

Delroy gave a small sound that might've been agreement or might've been the start of apology. It died before becoming either.

Rainwater had started running under the back threshold in a line thin as string. Tiana was first to spot it.

"Micah."

He got up with the towel before she finished the word and pressed it to the gap. The concrete under his bare feet felt slick and cooler than the room. Delroy joined him, kneeling badly, and together they shoved the towel down against the crack with a yardstick from the corner.

"We need another one," Delroy said.

Micah held the first towel in place with the stick while Kerene tossed a second from across the room, accurate as a pitcher. Delroy caught it left-handed.

"Good arm," he said.

"That arm pays rent in tips," she said.

They packed the second towel against the door. The water slowed, though it didn't stop. That felt like half of life lately: it wasn't fixed, only slowed.

When Micah sat again, Delroy stayed on the floor this time, back to the wall, knees up. Close enough that Micah could smell the rain in his clothes. Closer than they had been in weeks without somebody yelling. Delroy rubbed his shoulder once and looked over.

"You swung that hammer decent," he said.

Micah shrugged. "It's a nail."

"No. It's a wall. Different thing."

That was the kind of sentence Delroy could ruin by talking past it. Tonight he let it stand there by itself. Micah looked down at his own hands. There was a tiny crescent of dirt under the thumbnail, a splinter in the side of his index finger. It was proof.

Tiana leaned her head back against the mattress. "If they close that office tomorrow and the power's still out, I'm going to lose my mind."

"You can lose it after breakfast," Marcia said.

"Very caring."

"I'm full of care. That's why I schedule your breakdown."

Micah barked a laugh. Delroy did too, surprised into it. Marcia rolled her eyes as if the line had slipped out by mistake and she refused responsibility for any pleasure it caused.

Kerene stretched her legs and studied the leaking ceiling. "First thing I'm doing when I get a room with real money is buying a lamp for every corner."

"A lamp?" Tiana said.

"Many lamps. Wasteful lamps. Lamps that don't match. I want to come home and turn on light just because I can. Not because I've got to choose which part of my life deserves electricity."

Nobody mocked that. Maybe because the storm had stripped the sentence of any chance to sound silly. Maybe because everybody in the room understood wanting something small and excessive so badly it became almost holy.

Micah let himself picture it, not the lamp but the room it belonged in. There would be room to turn around without touching somebody else's grief, outlets you could waste one. He did not let the imagining get too big. Big was dangerous.

The rain shifted again, harder now, and the yard drain outside made a choking, gulping sound. Micah had heard that drain all summer and never listened. Tonight it sounded like a throat trying to swallow too much.

Marcia stood and checked the window boards with her palm, then the plastic bags around Tiana's folder, then the candle stubs, making one last inventory of a life too small for inventory. Delroy watched her do it. He did not offer instruction. He only reached for the soup pot when it got near full and carried it to the sink.

When he set the pot back down, Marcia shoved the straight-backed chair toward the door with her foot. "Sit there," she said. "If the towel slips or the water starts coming in harder, you wake everybody first. You don't open that door. You don't go outside trying to be a hero."

Delroy looked at the chair, then at her. Rain thudded on the plywood, rattling the mug candle. The soup pot kept ticking under the leak, and Marcia stood with one hand braced on her hip, listening toward the door as if she could hear the towels failing before anybody saw them.

"All right," he said.

He dragged the chair into place without another joke and sat facing the threshold. The scrape of its legs across the concrete ran through the room like a line being drawn. Micah followed the lantern light across all their faces as it moved: Kerene bright-eyed and shiny with heat, still somehow looking game for tomorrow if tomorrow came ugly; Tiana tired and sharp and young in the same breath; Marcia compact and unsurrendered; Delroy damp at the temples, quieter than Micah had ever known how to ask him to be. The storm kept thumping at the plywood, testing every cheap fix in the place. Still nobody drifted off alone. Nobody picked back up the fight and fed it.

When the next gust hit, they all looked up together. Delroy stayed in the chair by the door, one hand resting on the yardstick pinning down the towel, exactly where Marcia had put him.

Later, when the candle by the sink had gone to a blue nub and the lantern had been traded for the emergency flashlight on the milk crate, its beam thinned to the color of weak tea, Micah stayed awake on his blanket near the boarded window listening to rain on wood, water in the pot, Marcia's breathing from the mattress edge, Tiana lying still beside her with one arm over her face, Kerene turning once on the cot in the satin bonnet she'd tied on before sleep and muttering at somebody in a dream, Delroy shifting carefully in the chair by the door so the bad shoulder would not wake him all the way, and Tiana's phone buzzing once under the pillow with some useless alert nobody bothered to read.

Outside, the drain kept trying to swallow the yard and failing. For now the boards held, the towels held, the roof mostly held. Micah lay there with the wet towel smell in his nose and the hammer sting still faint in his palm, listening to Delroy breathe from the chair by the threshold and waiting for him to be the one who'd wake them if the water won.
