# Chapter 4

The dean's office was cold in the kind of mean way only school buildings managed in June, as if the air-conditioning existed less to cool anybody than to remind kids which rooms belonged to adults. Micah sat under a row of faded college pennants with his knees open and his jaw loose like none of this mattered. He kept his backpack hooked around one sneaker so the shaking in his leg did not travel into the whole chair.

Across from him, the trophy case shone with old football helmets and dust trapped behind glass. Every time the outer door opened, a rectangle of heat fell across the linoleum, then got swallowed by the office chill. Kids passed the doorway in clumps, loud and alive and headed somewhere that was not here. A couple of them looked in. Micah lifted his chin at them like he had chosen the chair and the wait and the white envelope clipped under his mother's thumb.

Marcia sat beside him in a navy blouse already darkened under the arms from the walk over. She had changed out of her church skirt and into work pants, but she still looked arranged, every button where it belonged, every edge under control. Her handbag sat upright on her lap. She was not digging through it, not checking her phone, not asking him again what happened. She had gone past talking.

That was worse than the talking would have been.

If she had wanted to cuss him out, he knew the shape of that weather. She would have gotten exact, then sharp, then loud enough to make the room understand she had not raised some wild little idiot for school people to pin to a bulletin board. Instead she sat with her back straight and her mouth fixed, as if whatever she needed from him would be collected later, in private, with interest.

Micah scratched at the healing split in his knuckle and stopped when she turned her head.

"Don't pick at it," she said.

Her voice was level, with no heat in it and no shelter either.

"I wasn't."

She gave him one look that said lie cleaner than that and turned back toward the closed door.

The office secretary worked behind a counter wrapped in fake wood grain, humming under her breath while she stabbed a stack of attendance printouts with a three-hole punch. The sound came down in hard little chunks, punching, lifting, punching again. Her nails were painted with tiny mangoes on a white base, bright enough to feel rude in this room. She called two students to the window, handled a pass, answered a phone, and never once looked at Micah like he was a child who had simply had a bad morning. He was already sorted, sitting there and waiting to hear the number attached to his name.

He rolled his shoulders like he was getting ready for practice, though the fight had not happened on any field where rules lived. It had happened between the science hall and the cafeteria, in that stretch where the floor always sweated and people moved slow because lunch had already started and everybody thought the day had made up its mind. The boy from world history had been on him for weeks, not every day, just enough to keep it hot.

Say something in Jamaican.

Where your boat parked, bro?

Ask your moms if she cleaned my auntie house in Gables too.

The first few times Micah had laughed because boys watched laughs closer than fists. Then the boy started doing the accent. Then he got bold enough to reach and pinch one of Micah's twists between two fingers and ask what kind of sponge he scrubbed his head with.

Micah had swung before he even felt himself decide.

He could still feel the slick snap of skin across his knuckles, the ugly satisfaction of not swallowing it this time, and the worse thing after that, teachers rushing in while the other boy shouted like he had been jumped for no reason at all. By Sunday evening the dean's voicemail had turned it into an incident. By Monday morning it was a hearing.

Marcia had listened to the message with one hand on the pot, spoon standing up in rice and peas, and said only, "You'll come with me in the morning." She had not asked whether the boy deserved it. She already knew the answer and knew it would not help.

Micah leaned back farther until the chair complained under him. "You think they gon' drag this all day?"

Marcia did not look at him. "Sit proper."

"I'm sitting."

"Don't try me in this place."

There it was, finally, a little edge. He should have felt relieved. Instead the words made the sweat gather at the base of his neck. He sat up and pressed his palms between his knees.

Outside in the hallway, a burst of laughter broke open and died. Somebody slapped a locker. A bell rang from somewhere deeper in the building, shrill and metallic, and the office swallowed that too. The secretary glanced up at the clock. Micah followed her eyes and wished he had not. They had only been there eighteen minutes.

Marcia pulled a folded tissue from her bag, blotted the shine off her upper lip, and put the tissue away so neatly it made him want to start something. She had called Miss Elena that morning, standing by the apartment sink with the faucet running to cover the conversation, and said she needed an hour, maybe two. Her voice had gone extra polite in that white-lady way it sometimes did, smooth enough to skate on. Micah had stood in the doorway tying his shoes and wanted to say he could go alone. He had not said it because even he knew that was bullshit.

The inner door opened.

The school cop stepped out first.

Officer Baines was broad through the chest and carried his radio high on his shoulder, a strip of silver unicorn glitter stuck to the side of it like one of his kids had decorated him and he had not bothered peeling it off all the way. He had a lunch cooler tucked under one arm and a damp half-moon at the collar from the heat outside. He looked at Micah, then at Marcia, then back at Micah with the calm face adults wore when they wanted a boy to do the math for himself.

Micah's skin pulled tight across his back.

The dean came behind him with a manila folder and a mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST AUNTIE in cracked gold letters. Ms. Valdez was one of those women who always looked like she had stepped out of a car fifteen seconds ago: lipstick still there, blouse smooth, chain at her neck resting exactly where it should. She kept peppermints in a cut-glass dish on her desk for kids with shaky hands before exams, which did not make her soft. It just meant she liked order in more than one flavor.

"Mrs. Campbell," she said. "Micah. Come on in."

Micah stood too fast and banged his knee on the chair arm. The pain flashed white and stupid. He grinned like it was nothing and slung his backpack over one shoulder.

Marcia was already up, handbag in place, face arranged for company.

By the time Micah followed them through the door, his body had understood something his mouth was still refusing to say. If the cop was in the room, school had already decided what kind of story this was.

***

Ms. Valdez's office smelled like toner, peppermint, and the wet roots of the plant dying behind her file cabinet. A framed photo of two girls in matching soccer uniforms sat near the edge of her desk beside the district handbook. The girls were missing both front teeth and grinning so hard it looked painful. Micah hated that he saw them. He did not want any picture in here making anybody feel more human.

He sat in the chair across from her desk. Marcia took the one beside him. Officer Baines stayed standing for a moment, thumbs hooked in his belt, then eased himself into the chair by the window with a little grunt like his knees were bothering him. Outside, the basketball courts wavered in the heat.

Ms. Valdez opened the folder. Paper slid against paper. "All right. We need to establish what happened Friday during fifth lunch and what needs to happen going forward."

Micah shrugged. "I already told the AP what happened."

"You're going to tell me now."

It came out of him fast because if he slowed down he would hear how bad it sounded in this room: the hallway, the comments, the hand in his hair, the swing, the other boy coming back at him, the teacher trying to get between them, the way everybody only really arrived once Micah had hit first and that became the whole movie.

He did not do the accent the boy used. He was not giving them that performance too.

When he finished, Ms. Valdez tapped the corner of one witness statement against the desk until all the pages aligned.

"So to be clear," she said, "the other student made comments you found offensive."

"I didn't find them offensive. They were offensive."

Her eyes lifted. "And your response was to hit him."

"My response was telling him stop for like three weeks."

"That's not what I asked."

Officer Baines leaned back, chair creaking. "You could've walked away, son."

Micah turned his head. "Yeah? That's what y'all say every time somebody decides I'm supposed to be cool with whatever."

"Micah," Marcia said quietly.

It was the first time she had used his name in the room. It did not help him. It sounded less like defense than warning.

Ms. Valdez folded her hands. Her nails were short and bare. "We have two staff statements saying you were verbally aggressive before the physical contact. We also have one teacher reporting that when she intervened, you jerked away and said, quote, don't touch me."

"Because she grabbed me."

"She was trying to stop a fight."

"After it started."

"Because it had already escalated."

"Because nobody stops shit when it's just talking."

Ms. Valdez let that sit. Not because she was considering it, Micah thought, but because silence looked official on her. "Did you report the comments before Friday?"

He laughed once.

Marcia's eyes cut to him.

"No, ma'am," he said, and hated how the words tasted. "I didn't report it."

"Why not?"

Because boys who reported things got looked at different after, and not by the people who could help them. Because the last time he told a teacher somebody kept calling him African booty scratcher in seventh grade, the teacher had smiled like she was all tired out already and said middle school boys could be unkind, then asked whether maybe he was giving as good as he got. Because every version of telling required him to stand there and explain that yes, he was Black and yes, his family was Jamaican and yes, those were different things to some people and no, he wasn't going to teach a lesson while somebody laughed.

He picked at the frayed seam of his backpack strap instead.

"Didn't think it mattered," he said.

Officer Baines blew air through his nose. It was not a laugh, not quite. "It matters now."

Micah looked at the glitter sticker on the radio by the officer's shoulder, the silver horn half peeled, and wanted to rip it clean off. "Yeah. Now I got paperwork."

Marcia shifted beside him. The vinyl chair whispered.

Ms. Valdez reached for the district handbook and turned it so the page faced them. The paragraph had a yellow sticky note on it, already marked. "Under the code of student conduct, physical aggression resulting in injury and disruption of instruction carries a range from five to ten days out of school suspension, followed by behavioral probation."

Micah stared at the highlighted lines without seeing them.

"He got injured?" he said. "He had a busted lip."

"That is an injury."

"So my hand don't count?"

"Micah," Marcia said again.

He wanted her to say more than his name. Wanted her to tell them the boy had been begging for it in that slick safe way kids did when they knew adults would call it teasing. Wanted her to say that a hand in a Black boy's hair was not horseplay just because it happened at school under fluorescent lights. Wanted her to speak in that exact, cutting tone that could turn a whole kitchen quiet.

She sat with her hands folded so tight he could see the white at her knuckles, and said nothing.

That hurt him so cleanly he almost missed the next question.

"Is there anything you'd like to add, Mrs. Campbell?" Ms. Valdez asked.

Marcia looked at Micah then, really looked, and he saw what she was doing. Counting. If she said the wrong thing, if she came in hot, if she gave them even one sentence they could call combative, the number would land hard and final. She was trying to keep the floor from dropping through. He knew that. He knew it and still wanted blood from her.

She turned back to the dean. "I'd like to know how he'll make up the work."

For a second Micah thought he had heard wrong.

Ms. Valdez blinked once, maybe surprised, maybe not. "Teachers will provide assignments through the portal. He'll also need to meet with our intervention specialist before returning."

"How many days?" Marcia asked.

"Given the physical altercation, the language toward staff, and the fact that Micah has previous referrals for defiance and classroom disruption, I'm assigning ten days out of school."

Micah swung toward her. "Previous referrals?"

Ms. Valdez opened another page. "Tardies. Refusal to surrender phone. Walking out of class without permission. Dress-code noncompliance."

"Man, you calling my hoodie and being late the same thing as this?"

"It's a pattern of oppositional behavior."

There it was: pattern. The word sat in the air with its shoes on the desk.

Micah laughed again, too loud. "Just write Black boy and stop wasting toner."

Officer Baines straightened. "Watch yourself."

"I'm watching. That's the problem."

"Micah." Marcia's voice sharpened, but only a little, like she was trying not to let it slip.

Ms. Valdez's mouth flattened. "And that right there is exactly why we're here. You keep deciding every adult correction is an attack."

"Because half the time it's that."

"No," she said. "Half the time it's somebody asking you to follow the same rules as everyone else."

He could feel the room narrowing. The plant smell. The cold air. The peel of sweat drying under his arms. He knew the smart thing now was to shut up. Let Marcia finish her questions. Let the number stay at ten and not somehow become more than ten in the story people told about him later.

But the word pattern kept burning.

"Everyone else?" he said. "You mean the dude who gets to say boat-boy shit in the hall till I put my hands on him? That's everyone else?"

Ms. Valdez's voice thinned. "We are not discussing the other student's discipline."

"Exactly."

Officer Baines leaned forward. The chair legs squealed. "Stop talking over her."

"Then stop acting like I'm the only person that was there."

"You're making this worse."

Micah looked at him. Really looked. The sweat stain at the collar. The wedding band worn dull. The glitter unicorn still stuck to the radio like home had followed him into this room and hardened there. And still he sat in school colors with a gun on his hip, helping turn hallway stupidity into something official enough to stain the year.

"It was worse when I walked in," Micah said.

The office went still.

It was not movie stillness or thunder stillness, just the small real stillness of adults deciding a boy had given them what they needed.

Ms. Valdez drew a form from the folder and began filling boxes. "Ten days out of school suspension, effective immediately. Behavioral probation through the end of the semester. Any further physical or verbally aggressive conduct will trigger recommendation for alternative placement."

The words kept coming, but Micah only caught pieces: portal, assignments, return conference. Zero tolerance was not the phrase she used because nobody used that anymore when they could say restorative and mean paper.

She slid the form across the desk to Marcia with a pen.

Marcia read every line before she signed. Of course she did. Her face gave nothing away. She signed once for receipt, once for acknowledgment, and slid the form back without letting the pen scratch.

"Can I get a copy?" she asked.

"Of course."

Micah stared at her hand until the secretary's printer outside started up again and the room remembered itself.

When Ms. Valdez handed him the packet, it was thick as a phone book and warm from the copier. His own name sat on the first page in block letters. MICAH CAMPBELL. He had seen his name on honor roll certificates before, on report cards, on the basketball roster. It had never looked this much like an accusation.

Marcia stood. "Thank you," she said, which was such a clean public lie it almost made him dizzy.

He got up slower. Officer Baines moved aside to let them pass. As Micah reached the door, the officer said, not unkindly, "Take the days and come back right."

Micah did not answer because every answer in him was ugly.

Outside in the office, the secretary lifted the copied pages from the printer and clipped them together. She handed them to Marcia with her mango nails and a sympathetic mouth she had probably practiced on parents all year.

Micah kept his face empty until they were back in the hallway.

Then he kicked the cinder-block wall once, hard enough to sting his toe through the shoe.

Marcia did not flinch. "Do that again and you'll buy me the paint."

He laughed because the alternative was worse. "With what money?"

The question sat between them.

Marcia tucked the suspension packet under her arm. "Come."

She did not touch him. She did not say one useless word about lessons or consequences or disappointed mothers. That blank space where the speech should have been walked beside him all the way to the front gate.

***

Outside, Miami hit him in the face full force. Heat, bus exhaust, wet grass, the metallic smell that rose off the chain-link fence when the sun had been on it too long. After the office air the day felt obscene. Boys in mesh shorts cut across the parking lot toward summer workouts. A teacher smoked beside her Corolla with her lanyard still on, staring into the distance like she had misplaced her life and expected it back by lunch.

Marcia stopped under the thin strip of shade by the bus stop sign and checked the time on her phone.

"I've got to go," she said.

Micah looked at her. "That's it?"

Her eyes came to him, tired and clear. "What you want me to say here?"

He wanted anything, something hot, something that sounded like he was still hers before he was the school's.

Instead he shrugged. "Nothing."

"Go straight home. You hear me?"

"Yeah."

"You're not on vacation. That packet better be open when I come in."

He nodded.

She looked at him one second longer, and there was something under the control then, something like fear or fury packed so tight it had stopped having edges. She could not spend it here. Not with buses groaning past and strangers half listening.

"Don't make me shame you twice in one day," she said.

Then she turned and walked toward the other stop, the one that would take her south. Her steps were fast and clipped. She did not look back. Micah watched till she got swallowed by a cluster of people and a county bus exhaling at the curb.

His own bus came ten minutes later, roaring hot, then cooling as soon as the doors folded shut. He tapped his Easy Card and went to the back, packet shoved in his bag like it might burn through the fabric if he held it. The seats were cracked blue plastic. The air smelled like old coins and somebody's coconut body spray. He took the window and watched the school slide away behind graffiti-tagged walls and palms bent ugly by the heat.

He told himself he did not care who saw him.

Then two girls from algebra got on at the next stop. One glanced his way, saw the envelope corner sticking from his backpack, and looked too fast in the other direction. That was worse than staring. It meant she knew and wanted him to know she knew and was generous enough not to enjoy it in front of him.

Micah leaned his head against the vibrating window. The glass was hotter than skin. In the reflection he looked older and dumber at once. His twist near the right temple still stuck out where that boy had grabbed it. He tried to flatten it and made it worse.

"Damn," a voice said from the aisle. "They cooked you for real."

Dre dropped into the seat across from him, legs wide, grin already halfway out. He had one white lace and one length of bright yellow extension cord threaded through his left sneaker, and he kept a little black rat-tail comb tucked behind one ear like he was too busy being broke to let his lineup collapse. The inside of his lower lip was rubbed raw where his braces had been cutting him. He worried it with his tongue between words.

"Who said they cooked me?" Micah asked.

"Your face said it. Bag said it. Whole vibe said it." Dre whistled low. "How many?"

Micah looked out the window. "Ten."

Dre slapped the back of the seat. "Ten days? Boy, you basically graduated."

Micah snorted before he could stop himself.

"See?" Dre said. "That's not even bad. That's freedom with homework stapled to it."

"Yeah, freedom. My mother's gon' kill me slow."

"Only if you sit in the house and let her." Dre leaned back, one arm over the seat top. "Come work."

Micah turned to him.

"Work where?"

"Dock with my cousin's people. Little river side, not the nice-boat shit. Real early. We unload coolers, stack bait, wipe down decks if the charter captains feeling fancy. Sometimes boxes come in off a truck and nobody asks too many questions. You show up, you move what they point at, you leave with cash."

He said cash the way other people said scholarship, like the word deserved room around it.

Micah stared. "You do that?"

"Sometimes." Dre shrugged. "Enough."

"Since when?"

"Since my grandma decided lights are more important than my phone bill, which apparently they are." He scratched his jaw. "Also my sister's birthday this weekend and I'm not buying her no gas-station teddy bear. She wants that mermaid cake from Publix with the blue icing that stains your teeth for two days. So. Men must labor."

It came out jokey, but Micah could picture it at once: Dre carrying some ridiculous blue cake into an apartment where everybody pretended life was not chewing through money fast as termites. That was the problem with Dre. He kept the joke on top, but there was always something heavier under it and you could hear it if you were not busy performing.

"What they paying?" Micah asked.

Dre's grin changed shape. There was less clowning in it now, more business. "Depends what boat, what truck, who owes my cousin a favor. Sixty if it's light. Eighty, ninety if it's nasty. More if you stay for the second run and don't whine about smell."

Micah tried not to show how hard the numbers hit him.

Ninety dollars was school shoes. Ninety dollars was groceries and maybe enough left for a fresh cut and a Metro reload. Ninety dollars was not having to ask Delroy for shit and hear a story instead of an answer.

"Smell like what?"

"Fish. Diesel. Bleach. Rich-people bait. Sometimes dead shrimp. Sometimes rotten plantain if produce came through first." Dre made a face. "Banana boats can go to hell."

Micah laughed again, smaller this time.

Dre watched him. "I'm serious, though. You got ten days. School already told you what they think. Might as well get paid while they meditate on your growth."

That was the first smart thing anybody had said to him since Friday.

The bus lurched through a light. Somebody up front started arguing with the driver about a transfer. A baby cried two seats over and got shushed with a bottle. Outside, auto shops blurred by, then a church with its marquee missing letters, then a stretch of warehouses where the sun glared off roll-up doors hard enough to make Micah squint.

"My moms ain't letting me just disappear before sunrise," he said.

"Then don't call it disappear." Dre shrugged. "Call it helping a man who tips cash. Call it character building. Y'all adults love that one."

"You stupid."

"And paid." Dre tugged the comb from behind his ear, ran it once through the front of his hair, and tucked it back. "Tomorrow. Four forty-five under the bridge by the river. If you don't come, I text the next boy."

Micah looked at him.

He should have said no on principle. He should have acted like fish guts and dawn were beneath him. He should have clowned Dre for smelling like bait and bilge water before first period half the time. That was the old script, the one where school still had enough of a hold on him that cash work looked temporary and embarrassing instead of clean.

Instead he said, "What I need?"

Dre's eyes brightened, but he kept his face cool. "Shoes you don't mind ruining. Gloves if you got them. If not, your hands belong to Jesus till payday."

"That's not a plan."

"It's Miami. It counts."

Micah nodded once.

Dre bumped his fist lightly against Micah's uninjured hand. "There he is."

The gesture was nothing, almost stupid, but it steadied something in him. It was not pride exactly. It was something meaner and more useful, a sense that maybe there were ways to move through a week besides sitting at a table under adult disappointment clicking through an online portal.

When Dre got off three stops later, he tapped the window twice and pointed two fingers at his own eyes, then toward Micah. Be there.

Micah watched him hop down to the sidewalk, yellow lace flashing, head already turned toward some other part of the day.

By the time the bus crossed into Little River, the packet in Micah's bag felt lighter than the thought of four forty-five under the bridge.

***

The apartment was hotter than outside. The little wall unit hummed and blew air that smelled faintly of bleach and damp towels, but it could not reach the corners. Tiana sat at the table with a folder open in front of her, highlighter cap between her teeth, one leg folded under her. She had papers spread in careful stacks weighted with a can of black beans.

She looked up when Micah came in. Her eyes went straight to the white packet under his arm.

"Well?" she asked.

He dropped his backpack by the couch-bed and shrugged. "They gave me some bullshit."

"How much bullshit?"

"Ten."

Her mouth tightened. It was not surprise, more like confirmation of something she already expected from the world.

"Damn," she said.

He hated that from her more than he would have hated a lecture. "You could at least pretend they're idiots."

"I know they're idiots," she said. "That doesn't mean you didn't help them."

He opened the fridge, stared at a pot, a bottle of ketchup, half an onion in a baggie, and shut it again. "You always got some smart shit ready."

"And you always act shocked."

He almost snapped back. Then he saw the papers on the table, the underlined dates, the clipped seriousness on her face, and understood she was managing her own disaster over there. Different office, same fluorescent knife. He did not have the energy to compete.

"Whatever," he said.

Tiana pulled the highlighter from her mouth. "Mummy said open the packet."

"Mummy says a lot."

"She also works harder than everybody in here."

He gave her the finger without turning around. She clicked her tongue and went back to the folder.

For an hour he moved around the apartment like there had to be a place in it where he could sit without being visible. There wasn't. The couch-bed was also the living room. The kitchenette was also the hallway. The bathroom was the only room with a door that shut all the way, and even that door had a swollen bottom edge that scraped the floor and left a gap wide enough for sound to leak through.

He took the packet into there and sat on the closed toilet lid. The paper was already curling from humidity. It laid out ten school days, probation, a parent conference required for reentry, and work posted to the portal. It was all written in that district language that pretended the page had no blood in it. Micah Campbell demonstrated aggressive and noncompliant behaviors. Micah Campbell will be expected to reflect. Micah Campbell may access support services.

He dropped the packet into the sink.

In the mirror he looked exactly fifteen. He did not look dangerous. He did not look misunderstood in some beautiful movie way either. He looked young and pissed off and caught. There was a faint bruise along his cheek from where the other boy's watch had clipped him when teachers pulled them apart. One twist still stuck out at the temple. He wet his hand and smoothed it down. It sprang back up.

He pressed a paper towel to the scab on his knuckle and watched the rust-colored dot bloom through. The sink smelled like old toothpaste and the lemon cleaner Marcia watered down to make it last. Through the wall he could hear Tiana's highlighter tapping the table in a rhythm that said she was irritated and trying not to be.

By late afternoon the neighborhood sounds changed. Children were outside. Somebody's speaker coughed up bass two houses over. Roosters carried on like rent was free for them. A bus braked at the corner. Delroy still wasn't back. Micah stretched on top of the made bed without taking off his shoes and stared at the ceiling stain above the fan while the heat pushed on his eyelids.

He must have slept for a little while because when he opened his eyes the room was dimmer and the apartment smelled like onions hitting hot oil.

Voices moved on the other side of the half wall.

"Ten days is ten days," Marcia was saying, low and tight. "Probation after that. One more foolishness and they start talking alternative placement."

Delroy made a sound in his throat. "For one fight?"

"Not one fight. One file. That is how they count these children."

Micah stayed still.

Pots shifted. Cabinet door. The scrape of Delroy's chair. He pictured his father leaning back like trouble was a thing he could charm if he gave it enough air.

"Boy have to just keep his head down," Delroy said.

Marcia laughed once, no humor in it. "You tell him that. You, who think every rule is a suggestion till somebody nails it to your forehead."

There was silence. Then Tiana asked carefully from the table, "Can he make up the assignments?"

"They say he can," Marcia said. "Portal, packet, meeting. Always another set of steps like time belongs to poor people in bundles."

"I can help him with algebra," Tiana said.

Micah shut his eyes.

He did not want help. He did not want their voices moving around his life like furniture. He did not want to be discussed while the rice steamed and oil popped and Delroy sighed like any of this was interesting instead of humiliating.

The wall unit kicked higher for one bright second, then settled back into its usual losing battle.

"And who watching him all day?" Marcia said. "I can't be here. You can't promise what you don't have. I'm not coming home to find he gone in the street because school decided to send him to me like extra laundry."

"I'm right here," Micah said before he meant to.

The room beyond the wall went quiet.

Then Marcia answered, not loud, not startled. "Yes. You are."

There was nothing else.

She did not tell him to come eat, come talk, or explain himself. The sentence landed and stayed there. Yes. You are. Like presence was all she had energy to verify.

Micah rolled off the bed and crossed to the plastic drawer unit by the TV stand. The top drawer stuck halfway, same as always. He jerked it open harder and rooted past charger cords, old mail, a remote with no back, domino score sheets curled at the edges.

The clippers were under a faded barbershop cape Delroy had picked up years ago from some cousin who had started cutting hair in a garage and then vanished to Broward. Silver body, black guard snapped on crooked, cord wrapped with electrical tape near the base. Heavy enough to mean something in the hand.

Micah had used them on Dre once, just to clean the neck. Then on two little boys down the block before church. Then on himself, badly. Marcia had called the back of his head a crime scene and fixed it with kitchen scissors while cussing under her breath. But people paid for a clean line if the neighborhood barbershop was booked and payday hadn't hit right.

He slid the clippers into the front pocket of his backpack.

From the kitchenette, Delroy started saying something about not making a bad week worse. Marcia cut him off. Oil hissed. A spoon hit the sink. Tiana's chair scraped back. The whole apartment kept happening three feet from every other part of itself.

Micah pulled his phone from the charger.

He stared at Dre's number for a moment, thumb hovering.

Then he typed: `What time again`

The reply came almost before he had locked the screen.

`4:45. Don't dress scary.`

Micah laughed under his breath. It was the first real laugh of the day. It felt wrong in his mouth and good anyway.

He shoved the phone into his pocket, zipped the bag, and pushed the suspension packet under the bed with the side of his foot until it disappeared into the dust and lost pens.

The clippers made a solid shape in the backpack now, more convincing than the stack of assignments, more useful than any explanation waiting on the other side of the wall.

When Marcia called that dinner was ready, he said, "Coming," and meant it only in the smallest possible way.
