# Chapter 10

By four-thirty the heat had not left the garage apartment. It sat in the block walls with the bleach sting from yesterday's mop water and the old frying oil that never quite washed out of the curtains. Delroy dressed in the dark until the shoulder made that impossible. Then he clicked on the stove light and worked his right arm into a clean shirt one inch at a time, jaw locked, the cuff brushing the enamel sink because he could not lift higher without the whole side of him going hot and mean.

On the table, under the edge of the rice tin so Micah would not knock it down, lay Tiana's document folder. Blue plastic, split at one corner, tabbed with index-card strips cut so straight they looked measured. Birth certificate copy. School letters. Aid printouts. The stack had the neat, desperate look of something that had already been asked to prove too much. Delroy kept his eyes on it long enough for the pain in his shoulder to get mixed up with the other thing, the older thing, the one that had not stopped working on him since the night he admitted where her document money had gone and tore the house open. He had taken money from that future and turned it into rent, light, groceries, bus fare, days bought one at a time. Necessary at the time. Shameful after. Both things could sit in the same chair and stare at him.

Marcia spoke before he heard her move. "You leaving now?"

Her voice came from the bed, flat with tiredness rather than sleep. She had not been resting anyway. Celia had been dead less than two days. Grief and money had crowded the room so completely there was no good corner left to lie down in.

"Claude said five o'clock pickup," Delroy said.

Marcia turned over. In the stove light her face looked smaller and harder, the skin under her eyes a little darker than yesterday. She had slept in the church T-shirt she used for housework, one hand still under the pillow like she had been searching there for a number that might save them. "Claude says plenty."

"It's one day."

"One day can still mash up a man."

He reached for the boots by the door. The motion dragged a line of pain from the back of his neck to the point of the shoulder, but he kept his mouth easy. Joking first. That was the old habit. That was the only habit he still trusted. "If one day could mash me up, Miami would've finish with me long time."

Marcia pushed herself up on one elbow. "Don't sweet-mouth me this morning. If that arm isn't right, say it plain."

He sat on the chair to lace his boots because standing on one leg had become a thing his body sometimes argued with now. "It's right enough."

"Right enough for what?"

He almost said for rent. For food. For your pride. For Tiana to stop looking at me like I broke something in her with my bare hands. Instead he shrugged with the left shoulder and kept his eyes on the knot in the lace. "For a day."

Marcia followed his glance to the blue folder on the table. She did not look at him when she answered. "Bring back money, Delroy. Don't bring back story."

That landed cleaner than if she had shouted. He nodded once, because anything else would have sounded like pleading, and stepped out into the dark little yard where the air felt like wet cloth laid over his face.

Little River was awake in pieces. A rooster somewhere behind the stucco wall carried on like it had a stage. Bass leaked thinly from a car turning the corner. Somebody had already started frying something heavy in old oil. Delroy locked the door softly, not because the neighborhood was quiet but because softness was the only kindness he had handy, and walked to the avenue with the shoulder held still inside the shirt as if stillness itself could be a brace.

Claude's pickup waited in the gravel lot behind a closed tire shop, tailgate chained with blue rope where the latch had gone bad. Claude stood beside it in a pale green fishing shirt too clean for the hour, shaking clove lozenges out of an old film canister into his palm. He had the same laugh lines as always and the same habit of making every arrangement feel casual until money came into it and the edges sharpened.

"Look who decide to be a working man again," Claude said. He tipped two lozenges into his mouth, crunched them, and held the canister out. "Want one? Helps with dust."

"Dust isn't here yet."

"Dust is always here. It just changes address."

Delroy took one. The clove hit sweet first, then medicinal. Around the truck, six other men waited with lunches in grocery bags and shirts already damp at the spine. A compact Honduran man with a gold wedding band looped on his bootlace instead of his finger kept tightening the knot as if he did not trust his own neck to hold it. A kid in a Buc-ee's cap, too young for the gray at his temples but wearing it anyway, checked his phone between every sentence because his girl's water had broken sometime after midnight and he was waiting for an auntie to text. Nobody here looked built for spare dignity. Everybody had packed enough of it to get through dawn.

Claude clapped Delroy on the left shoulder, mindful enough to miss the right. "You good for machine today?"

The real question sat under everything. Not whether Delroy had come. Not whether he wanted the work. Whether the body still qualified as a body worth loading into a truck.

He made his face insulted. "What kind of question is that?"

"The kind I ask because if I tell Manny I brought him a man for the skid and the concrete breaker, I need that man to still be a man at ten o'clock."

Delroy rolled the bad shoulder a quarter inch and stopped before it showed on his face. "I can run machine."

Claude watched him a second too long. In the labor yard months ago he had seen enough to know the lie's shape. But Claude was a middleman because he knew when to hear bad truth and when to leave it alone. He looked down at his phone, thumbed a message, and said, "Then don't make me look stupid."

"You ever look stupid, is by your own effort."

That got a laugh out of two men. Claude liked a room better once it had given him something back. He jerked his head toward the bed of the truck. "Come on then. We're gutting an old fourplex off Seventy-First. Bathrooms, kitchen walls, little back slab. Owner wants it ready for whoever's coming behind us to make it luxury for people who call mold character."

The men hauled themselves into the truck bed or squeezed into the cab and the bench seat somebody had bolted along the side. Delroy climbed more carefully than he wanted anybody to notice. By the time the truck pulled out, his shoulder had already started its deep, familiar muttering, the warning noise it made when a day was going to cost more than it paid.

Miami before sunrise had a brief softness to it that Jamaica mornings never needed. Here the roads had to cool off from yesterday's punishment before the city could pretend to forgive itself. They crossed under a rail line, passed a bodega where a woman in a pink bonnet hosed the sidewalk in the dark, and then the light came up mean and quick, silver on the warehouse roofs, exposing everything half-finished.

Claude kept one elbow out the window and talked over the engine about the job. Manny wanted speed. The owner wanted no neighbors calling the city before the walls were down. There were hard hats in a crate, but nobody should expect one that matched his head. If a pipe still had water in it, cap it with your thumb and call for help. If the little Bobcat started sticking in reverse, slap the right lever, not the left, unless you wanted to kiss the chain-link fence.

"And wet the cut before you saw," Delroy said.

Claude glanced over. "That's if anybody remembered a hose."

"You telling me they didn't?"

Claude clicked his tongue. "I'm telling you what kind of morning it is."

By the time they turned into the site, Delroy could feel the lie settling into him. Not a big theatrical lie. The everyday one poor men carried to work sites and interviews and landlord offices. Yes, I can lift that. Yes, I can stay late. Yes, I can make it on what you're paying. Yes, this body will hold because I have told it to.

The fourplex had once tried to look cheerful. Mint paint clung to the cinder block in sheltered squares. A sign over the front walk still said PALM COURT APARTMENTS, though only PALM C URT remained. The back slab was cracked into islands around an old rusted barbecue pit. Somebody had already punched out most of the windows. Concrete dust coated the hibiscus leaves at the edge of the lot gray as flour.

Manny came out of the shadow of the dumpster carrying a Spider-Man lunch box whose red had faded almost orange. He wore mirrored safety glasses and had a clean line of sunscreen rubbed only halfway into the ears, as if his daughter or his wife had told him to put it on and he had obeyed just enough to say he listened. He did not look like a man who owned anything here except the clipboard in his hand and the authority to make other men's bodies hurry.

"Claude," he said. "You brought me seven?"

"Six and Delroy." Claude grinned like the joke was too obvious to explain.

Manny snorted once. "Which one is Delroy?"

Delroy raised his left hand.

Manny's eyes went straight to the set of his shoulders, old-site appraisal. Age. Mileage. Risk. "You run machine?"

"Yeah."

"Concrete breaker too?"

The smart answer was something smaller. Drywall. Cabinets. Debris sorting. The smart answer was not what had dragged him out of bed before dawn.

"Yeah," Delroy said again.

Manny looked back at Claude. Claude's mouth barely moved, but the movement meant what it needed to mean: he'll do. In that small movement, Delroy sold the part of himself he could not actually spare.

Manny handed out dust masks from a torn box. There were four for seven men. The straps on two were snapped and tied back together with blue painter's tape. "Use these till they die," he said. "Hard hats in the crate. Watch the live wire in unit three till Tito kills it. Keep the debris moving. Owner wants that slab gone before lunch."

No harnesses. No proper respirators. No water line run out. Just the kind of instructions that assumed everybody present had already agreed to be worth less than the property once it was improved.

Delroy took a cracked yellow hard hat and a mask that smelled faintly of somebody else's sweat. Claude saw him looking at the breaker, the heavy electric jackhammer leaning against a wall like a threat with a cord attached.

"Use your left where you can," Claude said quietly.

"You getting tender on me?"

"I'm getting practical. Different disease."

Then Manny shouted for the first load and the morning became noise.

* * *

By eight o'clock the inside of Delroy's nose tasted like wet cement and old paint. They had started in the back units, ripping out vanities swollen from leaks, prying tile off bathroom walls, feeding busted cabinet doors and lengths of pipe down a plywood ramp into the dumpster. The dust mask turned damp and useless almost immediately. Sweat ran from his scalp into his eyebrows and carved pale lines through the powder on his face. Each time the breaker bit into the slab, the vibration ran up both arms and lodged under the right shoulder blade like a bolt being tightened there.

He learned the job's shortcuts in the order they threatened him. Manny did not shut power fully before sending men into the walls. The cut water line in unit two was plugged with a pair of vise grips and hope. The plywood ramp flexed under loaded wheelbarrows because nobody had braced it right. The Bobcat's reverse stuck exactly the way Claude had said, with a short ugly jump before it obeyed. When the saw came out to score the patio, they had no hose, so the blade screamed through dry concrete and threw a gray cloud over the whole yard thick enough to erase a man's mouth while he was still talking.

Demolition had its own specific cruelty. Not one dramatic danger. A hundred small permissions given to disaster because speed had won the argument before anybody arrived.

Delroy worked the breaker first, knees bent, weight spread, the machine punching through the slab in stuttering bursts. He had used better ones on better crews. Back in Jamaica, road jobs had their own foolishness, but at least the foolishness did not pretend to be American professionalism. Miami liked a vest, a clipboard, a contractor's sticker on a truck, and then the same old gamble underneath. Somebody else's timeline on your bones.

The first time the shoulder slipped, it was almost nothing. A hot skip under the skin when the bit caught rebar and kicked sideways. He let go with the right hand for a breath, shook it out low where nobody would see, then set back in.

The Honduran man with the wedding band on the bootlace came up with a wheelbarrow of busted block. Up close Delroy saw that the man had written two little girls' names in marker on the inside brim of his hard hat. Sofi and Alma. The sweat had blurred the letters but not enough to lose them.

"You want switch?" the man asked.

Delroy wiped his mouth with the back of his glove. "You saying I look bad?"

"I'm saying I know that face."

"Name?"

"Fermin."

"Delroy."

Fermin tipped his chin at the breaker. "Five minutes each. Better than hospital."

That was the closest thing to kindness most job sites offered: not pity, not comfort, just somebody adjusting the work so maybe you got to go home with one more useful piece still attached.

They switched. Delroy took the wheelbarrow and immediately regretted being proud. The ramp up to the dumpster was slick with dust and broken tile grit. The load wanted to drag left. He compensated with the right arm and felt the shoulder light up again, a warning electric and thin. He kept walking. In the dumpster Manny wanted the cinder block stacked to one side, metal to the other, because salvage brought back a little money and owners always respected a man more when he could make profit out of ruin twice.

The kid in the Buc-ee's cap worked beside the Bobcat, tossing splintered studs clear of the bucket. Between loads he checked his phone with gloves on, awkwardly, then tucked it back into a zip bag inside his shirt. Waiting for the baby. Waiting for news while breathing dust under a cracked hard hat. Delroy liked him for that and resented him a little too, the way older men sometimes resented youth for still having emergencies that sounded like beginnings.

By ten the shirt on Delroy's back was salt-stiff. His hands had gone numb from the breaker. His right arm no longer lifted cleanly above his waist without a lag, like it had to be convinced each time. Claude drifted in and out of the yard, checking another crew across the street, answering calls, hauling a bundle of contractor bags from one site to the next. He was not the boss. He was the man who made the boss's bad arrangements possible and survivable enough that men kept accepting them.

Manny came around the side of the building with the clipboard and no patience left. "Need this patio opened up now. Inspector might come by after lunch."

Claude pointed at the score lines. "That cut's too shallow."

"Then break it deeper."

"With what time?"

"With the time we're wasting talking."

Manny walked off before the sentence finished. Claude looked at Delroy, at Fermin, at the kid with the phone. For half a second something almost like apology crossed his face. Then he clapped his hands once. "All right. We break, load, clear. Delroy, take the breaker. Fermin, barrow. Junior, keep clear of the edge when the slab starts to move."

There it was. Not mastermind evil. Just a man inside the same machine choosing motion over refusal because refusal never paid him either.

Delroy took the breaker back.

The patio slab ran behind the units in one cracked strip, maybe eight feet wide, poured years ago without enough steel in it and patched twice since. Roots had pushed up one corner near the barbecue pit. The saw cuts looked pale and shallow, more suggestion than plan. Delroy set the bit in the line and leaned.

The machine hammered his wrists, his elbows, the sockets of his teeth. Dust came up so hard it coated his tongue. He tasted concrete, rust, yesterday's coffee turning sour in his stomach. Fermin rolled the loosened chunks back in loads, boots slipping, one hand always lifting to touch the bootlace at his throat where the ring sat. Junior worked the Bobcat now, following Manny's shouted directions with the frightened concentration of somebody driving expensive damage in a lot full of cheap men.

Delroy kept waiting for the shoulder to fail cleanly, with a clear announcement. Bodies almost never gave you that courtesy. They gave you a dozen warnings and then made you act surprised.

The first section broke loose near the barbecue pit. Good. They loaded it. The second section broke jagged and left rebar teeth sticking up. Delroy had to use both hands to lever the breaker clear when it jammed. Something shifted in the shoulder then, a wet little click, and the whole arm went light for half a breath. He dropped the tool, flexed the fingers, picked it back up before Manny could turn around and see.

Claude caught that much. He crossed the lot fast, wiping sweat with the hem of his shirt. "Leave it."

"One more pass."

"I said leave it."

But Manny was already yelling from the porch of unit three, "Need that path open. Dumpster swap's at noon."

Claude looked toward him, then back at Delroy. Every bad system loved that moment, the tiny hinge where one man's caution got folded back under another man's schedule. Claude swore under his breath. "Fine. Two minutes. Then off it."

Two minutes was enough.

Junior had the Bobcat too close to the patio edge. Anybody looking straight at the slab could see it. The bucket nudged a section Manny wanted loosened and the cracked strip shivered all the way down its length. Fermin shouted something in Spanish. Delroy turned, breaker still running, and saw the section under Junior's near tire crumble faster than the machine could back off.

Everything after that happened in pieces that refused to line up into one smooth memory. The Bobcat jerked. A barrow half-loaded with broken concrete tipped toward the kid. Fermin jumped clear and lost one boot in the dust. Delroy let go of the breaker with his left hand and threw his right arm out to catch the wheelbarrow handle before the whole load went over onto Junior's legs.

The shoulder tore with a deep, sickening pop, not loud in the air but loud inside him, like a heavy staple ripped through wet cloth. Heat flooded his neck. The arm stopped belonging to him. The wheelbarrow slammed sideways anyway, concrete chunks exploding over his shin and the patio. Delroy went to one knee, then both. The breaker hit the slab and rattled in a wild circle until Fermin kicked the cord free.

For a second there was no pain, only the fact of wrongness. His right hand hung at an angle it had no business hanging. Dust settled into the sweat on his chest. Then the pain arrived whole. Not sharp exactly. Huge. Bright. A pressure so total it made the yard narrow to one rectangle of white sky over the broken patio.

"Fuck," Junior said. He was standing, both legs under him, one shin cut open and gray with dust. "Fuck, fuck."

Claude crouched in front of Delroy. Up close he smelled of clove and diesel and the expensive deodorant he used when he wanted to remind people he was not just another labor-yard man. "Look at me."

Delroy did not want to. Looking at another man's face in that moment was too close to asking for help. But he did.

"Can you move the fingers?"

He tried. The hand twitched.

"Arm?"

Delroy tried that too and almost blacked out. The world flashed thin and metallic at the edges.

"No," he said.

That one word came out stripped bare. Shame had taken the story out of him.

Manny came down off the porch with the clipboard still in his hand. Junior started talking all at once, about the slab, the bucket, the wheelbarrow, everybody's names tumbling. Manny looked at Delroy's shoulder once, at Junior's shin, at the half-broken patio, and his eyes did arithmetic.

"Get them out the way," he said to Claude.

Claude stared at him. "He needs a hospital."

"Then take him."

"You calling it in?"

Manny's jaw shifted. "You want the county here? You want this whole site shut? I said take him if he can't work."

The abandonment came exactly like that. Not dramatic. Not movie villain stuff. Just a man with a lunch box and a schedule deciding which losses counted. Manny turned back toward the porch before Delroy had even gotten fully upright.

Claude swore at his back, low and venomous. Fermin spat gray mud into the weeds and sat hard on the curb, clutching his ankle where the wheelbarrow had clipped him. Junior kept pressing his shirt against the cut on his shin and staring at Delroy as if he might have to remember this later for the child being born into his life.

Claude got Delroy to the strip of shade behind the dumpster. Somebody passed over a half-cold bottle of water. Delroy could not unscrew it one-handed. Fermin took the cap off and handed it back without comment. His own ankle was swelling over the boot.

"You hearing me?" Claude asked.

Delroy took a swallow. Concrete grit scraped his teeth. "I hear."

"I can drive you to Jackson."

That meant registration, waiting, bills, questions, maybe X-rays that named the damage in a language he could not pay to fix. It also meant Marcia learning about Claude before he had even figured out how to bring the morning home.

"No hospital yet."

"This isn't a yet shoulder."

"No."

Claude looked like he wanted to argue and knew argument would only be another thing Delroy had to withstand. He scrubbed both hands down his face, then stood and walked back toward Manny with the stiff, controlled stride of a man trying not to make his own position worse while somebody else's body proved exactly what the job was. Delroy watched them from the shade. Manny shook his head before Claude finished speaking. Claude said something sharper. Manny held out both hands, palms up: what do you want me to do. The universal gesture of every little man inside a bigger theft.

Fermin leaned his head against the dumpster. "They'll pay half if we're lucky."

Delroy turned to look at him. "You know that?"

"I know them." Fermin gave a short laugh and immediately regretted it, hand going to his ankle. "Half if lucky. Nothing if they feel offended."

The pain kept changing shape. It throbbed deep in the joint, then sent little burning wires down to the elbow, then settled heavy at the top of his arm like somebody had driven a hot wedge into him. He held the forearm close to his belly because any swing of it made nausea rise. Under the pain sat the colder knowledge: no ladders for a while. No paint buckets. No carrying AC units up back stairs. No tile boxes two at a time to impress a contractor into calling again. A bad shoulder did not only hurt. It edited your menu. It told the future what to cross off.

By the time Manny finally came over with an envelope folded twice, the sun had reached the shade line and pushed them all hotter. Claude took the envelope, opened it, and his mouth tightened.

"What?" Delroy asked.

Claude did not answer right away.

"What?" Fermin said this time, flat and unsurprised.

Claude crouched again. His film canister had left a ring of clove dust in his shirt pocket. "Boss is saying you both left the site early."

Delroy almost laughed. The sound came out as air. "I left?"

"I'm telling you what he's saying."

"And what are you saying?" Delroy asked.

Claude met his eyes. That was the decent part of him, the part Delroy had trusted when he took the four hundred months ago. Claude would at least look at you while explaining the shape of the knife.

"I'm saying I pushed him for something. This is what he released today."

He slid the money into Delroy's left hand. Three twenties and a five, soft with sweat. Sixty-five dollars. Concrete dust stuck to the edge of the bills. Delroy stared at them until the numbers stopped feeling like a joke and settled into insult.

Sixty-five dollars for the morning's pain. Sixty-five dollars for the rent he had once stolen from Tiana's future and was still trying to carry back one ragged bill at a time. Sixty-five dollars, and the shoulder gone worse than it had been at dawn.

"The rest?" he said.

Claude's face closed a little. "There is no rest today."

"You mean there is no rest ever."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Make me into the whole system because I'm the one standing here."

The truth of it irritated him. Claude had not poured the slab crooked years ago. Claude had not built a labor market on bodies cheap enough to waste. Claude had not snapped Delroy's shoulder with his own hands. But he had brought him here. He had heard the lie and accepted it because the job needed a man more than it needed honesty.

Claude lowered his voice. "I can settle with you on the four later."

Pity. That was worse than collection.

Delroy curled his fingers around the money. The shoulder answered with a bolt so bright he saw white spots. "Keep your later."

Claude exhaled through his nose. "At least let me run you home."

That, Delroy accepted. Pride had already spent too much of him.

They rode in silence most of the way. Claude drove with both windows down because the truck's air-conditioning had died the previous summer and he had never fixed it, preferring to tell people a breeze made a man honest. At a red light he pulled the Spider-Man lunch box from the floorboard where Manny must have left it, looked at it, and set it in the back without comment. Evidence of another man's ordinary life among all this breakage.

When they reached Little River, Claude stopped one block short of the apartment.

"You need a clinic," he said.

"I need more than clinic."

"You know what I mean."

Delroy opened the door with his left hand. Getting down out of the truck nearly folded him. Claude watched without pretending not to.

"Text me tonight," Claude said.

Delroy laughed once, not kindly. "With what hand?"

Claude winced at that and let him go.

The walk from the corner to the garage apartment took longer than it should have. Children were yelling two yards over with the wild happiness of summer boredom. Somebody had draped wet jeans over the fence. The smell of bleach hit him before he got to the door, then frying onions, then mildew from the bathroom vent. Home did not look like relief. It looked like one more room where he would have to account for himself.

Marcia opened the door before he knocked twice. She took in the dust, the way he was holding the arm, the face he could not arrange in time, and stepped back without wasting one syllable on surprise.

"Sit," she said.

He sat on the chair by the table. Tiana was there with the folder open beside a legal pad, hair tied up in a scarf that made her look younger until she turned and he saw the set of her mouth. On the stove a pot of rice had been left covered to steam. The fan in the window clicked at the end of every turn.

Marcia fetched scissors from the drawer. "Can you get this shirt off?"

"Not pretty."

"I didn't ask if it was pretty."

Tiana was already on her feet, crossing to the freezer. She moved quickly when there was a task, more like Marcia than she would have enjoyed hearing. "There's one ice tray left."

"Wrap it in a towel," Marcia said.

She cut the sleeve seam from cuff to shoulder because peeling the shirt off whole made Delroy curse into his teeth. Concrete dust dropped onto the floor in gray flakes. When the fabric fell away, the top of the shoulder had begun to swell forward, a hard ugly rise where his body had gone out of order.

Marcia did not gasp. She leaned close, eyes narrowing the way they did over forms and bills and any other thing trying to cheat her. Her fingers, callused and cool, pressed lightly around the joint. Delroy nearly rose out of the chair.

"Don't touch it there," he said.

"I need to know what it's saying."

"It's saying leave me alone."

"It's saying you mash it proper."

Tiana handed over the towel with ice knotted inside. The cold landed and for one stupid second Delroy thought maybe it would fix something by force of cold alone. Then the pain settled back around it.

Tiana stood by the table, arms folded under the legal pad she had picked up without realizing it. "Did you fall?"

"Sort of."

"That's not an answer."

Her voice was controlled, the precise public one she used on people behind counters. Under it he could hear the strain, the effort not to let this become about him in the old way, with story and excuse taking all the air in the room.

"Slab shifted," he said. "Machine too close. I caught a barrow wrong."

Marcia's head snapped up. "Machine? What machine?"

"Little Bobcat."

"On a broken slab." She stepped back and laughed once, all disgust. "Of course."

He reached into his pocket with the left hand and brought out the money. That small act hurt more than it should have because it made the point so plain. A man came home half-broken and still the thing in his hand had to be counted first.

Marcia held out her palm. He dropped the bills into it. Concrete dust marked her skin. She looked down.

"That's all?"

"That's what came."

"For a whole day?"

"For a morning and a wrecked shoulder."

She flattened the bills against the table one by one, as if pressing them might produce more. Sixty-five dollars looked obscene on top of Tiana's papers, not because it was no money but because it was such a small, dirty amount to have asked this much body for.

Tiana stared at the bills, then at the shoulder. "You need imaging," she said quietly. "If that's a tear, ice isn't going to do anything except make tonight different from tomorrow."

"Tonight and tomorrow already different," Marcia said.

Delroy watched her do the math in real time. No Celia wages. Tiana's shifts not enough to stretch. Rent coming like weather. Claude somewhere behind all this with the four hundred still alive between them. Now clinic money if they went. Maybe medication. Maybe not. And after all that, a shoulder that might not lift a paint bucket next week or a toilet the week after or a box of tile at the end of the month. Injury had a long tail. Poor people felt it first in the future.

"We can go to Jackson," Tiana said. "They have to take you."

Marcia did not answer right away. She was still looking at the money, which was the more honest part of the problem. Being taken was not the same as being fixed, and it sure was not the same as paying for what came after.

"We can sit there twelve hours and come back with a bill and a sling," Marcia said. "Maybe that's still what we do. Maybe not. But don't talk like 'have to take you' means solved."

Tiana's mouth tightened. "I didn't say solved."

"No," Marcia said. Softer now. "You didn't."

Delroy wanted to rescue the room from that turn. That had always been his trick, throw a joke across the gap, make people step around it. But the pain kept him mean and the money kept him honest.

"No need to fight over me," he said.

Marcia looked at him then, really looked, and whatever she saw made her tired rather than angry. "Everything is over you and under you at the same time, Delroy. That's the problem."

He had no answer for that.

Tiana picked up the blue folder and moved it away from the dust the bills had left behind. The gesture was careful, almost tender in spite of herself. She took one of the cleaner sheets from her legal pad and laid it over the money before the grit could grind into the paper underneath.

"Don't put that on the documents," she said.

Not cruel. Not forgiving. Just exact.

Delroy looked at the hidden bills, at the white paper between his failed earnings and the thing he had once raided to keep the house going, and finally saw it more clearly than he had while the shoulder tore. Sixty-five dollars did not begin to restore anything. It did not repair the folder, or the lie, or the job future that had narrowed with one bad reach on a broken slab. It barely covered the shape of what he had lost today.

Marcia tore an old pillowcase into a strip and made a sling because there was nothing else in the room meant for this kind of emergency. When she lifted his forearm into place, Delroy bit the inside of his cheek hard enough to taste blood. The fan clicked. Outside, a bus sighed to a stop and moved on. Tiana sat back down with the folder and the legal pad, but she did not start writing again. She kept glancing at the shoulder the way people checked a cracked window during storm season, not trusting it to stay as it was.

Marcia tucked the money into an envelope that had once held the light bill and wrote nothing on the front. No category matched. Not wages. Not savings. Not help.

"Tomorrow," she said, tightening the knot at the back of his neck, "you don't go nowhere lifting anything. You hear me?"

He let his head rest against the chair for one second. The apartment smelled of rice, bleach, onions, and concrete dust now, home and job ground together so fine there was no separating them.

"I hear."

The shoulder throbbed against the sling. On the table, under the white sheet of pad paper, the sixty-five dollars made a low untidy shape. It looked smaller every time he glanced at it, and the arm in his lap felt heavier.
