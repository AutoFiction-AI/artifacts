# Chapter 14

The phone started its crawling again while Marcia had both hands in the sink.

It jittered somewhere under the couch cushion where Delroy had shoved it, buzzing against the cracked vinyl with the same stubborn nerve a trapped roach had when bleach hit it and did not kill it fast enough. Marcia stood at the basin with Micah's white school polo sunk in gray water, working soap into the brown half-moon under the collar where his blood had dried after the clinic. The cheap lemon detergent had already roughened the skin across her knuckles. Every time the phone shook, the water in the sink gave a faint answer off the pipe.

"Answer it," she said.

Delroy lay lengthwise on the narrow bed in his undershirt, one forearm over his eyes, his right shoulder propped on a pillow that had gone flat months ago. "In a minute."

"You said that two calls ago."

"Then maybe I mean it serious now."

He smiled when he said it, not enough to show teeth, just enough to make it sound like weather instead of warning. That smile had bought him more time in this world than wages ever had. Marcia wrung out the shirt hard enough to hear stitches complain and hung it over the faucet for another soak.

The apartment held everybody's evening at once. Tiana sat at the little table under the window with her document folder open in square anxious piles: originals, copies, things still needed. She had clipped her hair up with a black claw clip and was reading a form so closely Marcia could see her mouth move on the instructions without sound. Micah sprawled on the floor with one sneaker off, one on, picking at the frayed lace tip with his thumbnail like the thing had personally offended him. The yellow gone loose under his cheekbone made him look older in some angles and stupidly young in others. On the folding cot, Kerene had one gold sandal in her lap and a spool of clear fishing line between her knees, mending the torn strap with the concentration of a surgeon because that pair still had to pass for good shoes on Saturday nights.

The room smelled of bleach, hot cloth, and the onions Marcia had sweated down for rice and tinned mackerel before the phone began its complaining. Through the thin wall came the usual Little River chorus: a radio bass line from the front house, somebody laughing too hard in the alley, a rooster with no respect for clocks. Heat stayed pressed against the low ceiling even after sunset started thinking about them.

The phone buzzed again.

Tiana's eyes flicked up, then back down.

Micah said, "Whoever that is, they got commitment."

"Mind your business," Marcia said.

"It's my business if it keeps vibrating like some cheap sex toy."

Kerene barked out a laugh before she could stop it. "Boy, everything in your mouth have to come out dirty?"

"Not everything."

Marcia shot him a look. He shrugged and leaned back on his elbows, playing bored so hard it made the room smaller. That was how fear worked in him now. It did not make him quiet. It made him stupid on purpose.

The phone went still.

Marcia turned back to the sink. For three seconds the apartment had peace.

Then someone knocked.

Not a neighbor knock. Not the quick double tap of somebody borrowing pepper or asking if the laundry line was free. This one had patience in it. Heavy knuckles. No hurry because the person on the other side had already decided he would stand there until the door opened.

Delroy took his arm off his eyes.

Marcia looked at him. He looked at the turned-off fan, at the ceiling stain over the kitchenette, at anything but her.

"Who is it?" Micah asked.

"Nobody for you," Marcia said, wiping her hands on a dish towel already striped brown from onion skins.

She got to the door before the second knock and pulled it open only as wide as her body.

Claude Brown stood on the little patch of concrete between the apartment and the leaning chain-link gate, dressed too neat for the heat in a blue work shirt with the sleeves rolled exact at the same point on each forearm. He held a baseball cap in one hand and kept worrying the cheap purple bead bracelet riding tight on his other wrist, the kind little girls made at summer camp and forced on the adults they loved. Sweat had darkened the line down his spine. He smelled faintly of tire shine and bay rum.

"Evening, Miss Marcia," he said.

"Evening."

Claude's eyes moved once, quick, over the inside of the room: Tiana at the table, Kerene on the cot, Micah on the floor, Delroy half-risen from the bed. Whatever he had prepared to say changed shape in his throat.

"Delroy there?" he asked, though he had obviously already seen him.

"He there. What you need?"

Claude rubbed the bracelet again. "I need a word with him. Outside if possible."

Marcia felt Delroy behind her before she heard him. The mattress springs had their own language by now. She planted her hand flat against the doorframe, wanting one clean minute, one private breath to understand the danger before it stepped all the way in.

"Anything you have to say can say through me first," she said.

Claude gave her a look that was not disrespect and not comfort either. "I'd rather not, sister."

Behind her Delroy said, too easy, "Claude. Look at you. You find my place now? I thought you only knew job sites and domino tables."

He came up beside Marcia with his left shoulder leading, his right arm hanging careful. He had put on charm the way other men put on cologne. Marcia could smell the effort from him.

Claude did not smile back. "Brother, I've been calling you all day."

"Phone was in the room."

"You live in the room."

Micah snorted from inside. Marcia wanted to slap the sound out of the air before it spread.

"Take it round back," she said low to Delroy. "Now."

But the path round back was gravel, old buckets, a busted lawn chair, and one window that did not shut right. There was no private room left in this house. There had not been for months. Privacy had gone the same way as full paychecks and fresh fruit: a thing they still talked about as if it might walk back through the gate.

Claude lowered his voice anyway. "I need my money, Delroy."

Marcia felt rather than saw Tiana go still at the table.

Delroy laughed once. Too quick. "You come to my gate for that?"

"I came to your gate because you stopped answering."

"I said next week."

"And the week before that you said Friday. Before that you said after the job clear. Brother, school starting Monday. I already promised my granddaughter shoes. I can't keep floating you because you don't like shame."

The word money had not hit the room yet. Shame did.

Micah pushed himself upright. "What money?"

Marcia kept her eyes on Claude. "How much?"

Delroy said, "Marcia."

"How much?" she repeated.

Claude looked almost angry at her for making him do arithmetic at her own door, though she knew the anger was at the whole damn position, not her. "Four hundred."

The number landed flat and heavy.

It was too much for a joke, too little for a bank, exactly the size of a secret that could survive only if nobody spoke plain around it.

Tiana set her pen down. Kerene's fishing line flashed between her fingers and went slack.

"Four hundred what?" Micah said.

"Micah," Marcia said.

"No, because everybody's acting like we got private offices in here. Four hundred what?"

Delroy's voice hardened. "Watch your tone."

"Then answer the question."

Claude exhaled through his nose and looked toward the alley as if maybe another life was available over there. "Cash advance. Before the demolition."

Marcia turned to Delroy slowly. The towel in her hand was still wet from Micah's shirt. Bleach water touched the inside of her wrist and ran cold to the elbow.

"You borrowed from him," she said.

"I handled it."

"No. You hid it. That's different."

Claude said, "Miss Marcia, I didn't come to pull your house apart. If he'd just call me back, I wouldn't be here."

Marcia believed him enough to hate the whole thing more. There was no real enemy standing on her step, no clean villain she could send away. Just another man short on breath and money, too proud to beg and too squeezed not to collect.

Delroy took a step onto the concrete. "Claude, come on, man. You know how this look."

"I know exactly how it look. That's why I gave you all day first."

"And I said I would deal with it."

"With what? Story?" Claude's voice cracked sharper than he meant it to. He checked himself, glanced at the room, lowered it again. "Brother, I watched you last Friday at the money counter on Twenty-Seventh filling out a slip for Devon. Don't stand here like cash hasn't passed through your hand."

The whole apartment changed shape.

Marcia did not move. Behind her, something small and metal clicked against the table leg. Tiana's binder clip, maybe. Kerene made a soft sound through her teeth that could have been pain or prayer.

"For Devon?" Marcia said.

Claude shut his eyes for one beat. "That part wasn't for the step."

Delroy swung toward him. "Mind your mouth."

"Then mind your debt."

Marcia heard Micah say, from somewhere behind her left shoulder, "Daddy been sending money to Jamaica?"

Nobody answered him.

Claude dragged his hand over his face. The purple bracelet flashed again, bright and childish against the dark hair of his wrist. "Look. Delroy. Call me by morning. That's all I'm asking now. Don't make me keep coming back here."

He put his cap on, then took it off again immediately, as if even that small piece of shade felt disrespectful in the middle of what he had done. His eyes met Marcia's for a second.

"I really didn't come for this," he said.

She believed that too.

"Morning," she said.

Claude nodded once to the room he had already cut open, turned, and walked back toward the gate. Outside, a car rolled by slow with windows down and dancehall leaking from it in a hiss of bass. Claude got into a faded silver truck with a pink child's umbrella hooked behind the passenger seat and sat there a second before starting it, both hands locked at ten and two like he was steadying something bigger than the steering wheel. Then he went.

Marcia shut the door.

No one spoke while the truck noise thinned away.

The onions on the stove had gone too far. She could smell the bitter edge.

She crossed the room, turned off the burner, and came back. Delroy had not moved from the middle of the floor. His shoulder pulled one side of him lower than the other when he got tired. Tonight the sag looked less like injury than confession.

"Tiana," Marcia said without taking her eyes off her husband, "take your brother outside."

Micah said, "I'm not five."

"Then act older than five and move."

But Tiana did not stand. Her hands stayed flat on the table, one over a sheaf of photocopies, one over the corner of the folder. Her face had closed into the careful blank she used in offices when she did not intend to give anyone the satisfaction of seeing confusion.

"No," she said quietly. "If you're doing this with the whole door open, don't start pretending we're children now."

Marcia felt the sentence strike, not because it was disrespectful but because it was true.

Kerene eased her sandal onto the cot and rose with both palms out, salon-soft, trying to charm the room smaller. "Micah, come help me shake the bath mat, nuh. I wash it and the thing still smelling rank."

"I'm good."

"You don't want fresh air?"

"I'm good."

Kerene looked at Marcia, asking whether to push. Marcia gave the tiniest shake of her head. There were moments when forcing children out only taught them to listen harder.

So they stayed. The room stayed. The heat stayed.

"How long?" Marcia asked.

Delroy's mouth flattened. "Marcia, lower your voice."

She laughed then, a short ugly sound she did not recognize as hers until it was gone. "You want softness now?"

"I want us not to do this in front of everybody."

"That ship sail when Claude call out four hundred dollar on my step." She pointed toward the door. "How long?"

He looked at the floor. At the stove. At Micah. At Tiana's folder. Everywhere but her.

"It wasn't every week," he said.

"That wasn't the question."

"I sent a little when I could."

"How long."

"Since June."

June. Before the school office. Before Tiana's aid file stalled. Before the partner hand broke. Before Micah came home with his face opened into somebody else's story. June, when there had still been a version of the summer Marcia let herself touch in secret: deposit paid, partner payout in hand, maybe another rental if they moved fast and begged right. June, while she had been walking other people's polished floors and telling herself endurance was an investment.

She crossed to the bed, dropped to one knee, and reached under it.

Delroy straightened. "What you doing?"

She dragged the cedar trunk out by its handle. The metal feet scraped across the concrete with a noise like a blade being sharpened.

Tiana inhaled. Not surprise exactly. Something closer to recognition. Marcia had no room in her just then to sort that out.

"If you want exact," she said, "we can have exact."

The trunk sat squat and dark in the middle of the floor, its brass corners dulled by years of hands and damp. It held birth papers, church programs, old school photographs, envelopes with dead return addresses, all the evidence a family kept because losing paper in America was like losing blood. Marcia flipped the latches and lifted the lid.

Delroy said, "Leave that alone."

"Why? You left enough in here."

She went past the nursing-school letter she had not touched in years, past the old immunization card, past the passport copies, until her fingers found the thin stiff slips Tiana must have disturbed and then reburied. Yellow remittance stubs. White receipt copies. Names, dates, transfer numbers. Money made visible by carbon paper and bureaucracy, neat where the life behind it had not been.

She laid them out on the table one by one.

"Forty-five." She slapped the first down. "Sixty." Another. "Fifty." "Forty." "Seventy-five." The last one she held up between two fingers. "This one from last Friday, same Friday Claude watched you send it. Two hundred seventy dollar."

Micah whistled under his breath.

Kerene said, "Jesus," so low it barely made sound.

Tiana's eyes were on the receipts, not Marcia. Not Delroy either. The paper itself. As if the numbers could still alter if she stared hard enough.

Marcia turned back to Delroy. "Two hundred seventy dollars in this house. Do you know what two hundred seventy dollars looks like in here? It looks like groceries for two weeks. It looks like the clinic and the bus and the light bill not getting cut ugly. It looks like me not stretching mackerel with onion skin and pepper water. It looks like not having a creditor at my door."

Delroy's face had gone still in the dangerous way his did, too still, like charm had stepped out the back and left pride to finish the conversation. "He's my son."

"And these?" Marcia swept her hand at Tiana, Micah, then herself. "These are what? Lodgers?"

"Don't do that."

"Do what. Count?"

"Make it like I sent away rent money in sacks."

"What should I make it like?"

He came to the table, favoring the shoulder though trying not to show it. With his left hand he touched one receipt, straightened it, crooked it again. A useless gesture. Something to do besides defend himself with open palms.

"It wasn't all one thing," he said. "One time the boy needed help for his course fee. One time his mother roof lift and water was coming through the kitchen. One time he called because he was short, just short, and I had something in my hand. I sent it."

"The boy," Marcia said. "You mean Devon. Use the name if you're going to spend our food on it."

At that, Delroy looked up. Hurt moved across his face so fast it almost passed for anger.

"Our food?" he said. "Marcia, every dollar that came in here, I fought for too."

"And every dollar that stayed in here, I accounted for. That's the part you skip because arithmetic embarrass you."

"No, the part you skip is blood."

"Don't use blood with me tonight."

"Why not? Because it make the room inconvenient?"

Marcia felt the bleach on her hands start to burn where the skin had split. She welcomed it. It gave her somewhere to put the heat.

"Don't play big philosophy now," she said. "Not after you hid it in papers under my bed."

"I hid it because if I told you, you'd say no."

"Yes. I would've. Because no was the right answer."

"Easy for you."

She stared at him. "Easy."

"Easy to cut off a son you don't hear on the phone."

"You think I don't know what obligation is?" Her voice rose despite herself, then sharpened finer. "I packed barrel with you when we had enough to pack barrel. I sent shoes and powdered milk and school socks. I did not object to your child existing. I object to you running a second household out of the bones of this one and leaving me to smile like a fool while you do it."

Micah muttered, "Damn."

Marcia did not look at him. "You," she said, and the boy shut his mouth.

Delroy dragged a hand over his jaw. "Second household. Listen to you."

"What should I call it? Because from where I stand, money left this room under your name for months while your daughter carry expired paper in a folder and your son cough blood into a clinic rag and I go out every day trying to make dignity out of pennies. What word fit that proper?"

Tiana flinched, not visibly if someone did not know her, but Marcia knew the little tightening at the corner of her mouth. Shame on that girl always tried to dress itself up as patience first.

Delroy heard it too. "Don't bring the children in it like I don't know what the children need."

"They're already in it. That's what you still refuse to learn."

He turned toward Tiana then, maybe for rescue, maybe for witness. "Ti. Tell your mother every man don't get to be father the same way. Some of us get left with phone calls and whatever cash make it through."

Tiana looked at him for so long Marcia thought she might say nothing.

Then she said, in that careful clean voice she used when she wanted the words to stand without her, "You don't get to use us to explain why you lied to us."

Delroy blinked.

Micah sat up straighter. The bruise on his cheek caught the yellow light and made him look punched fresh again. "Facts," he said.

Marcia heard the edge in the boy and knew it was fear wearing somebody else's jacket.

Delroy pointed at him. "Watch your little commentary."

"Why? You got whole secret payment plans and I can't talk in my own house?"

"Micah," Kerene said.

"No, because every week it's some new thing. Some old thing. Some thing we weren't supposed to know till after it already mash up."

The room throbbed once with that truth. Not just this truth. All of them. The way bad news in this family almost never arrived as news. It arrived as aftermath. A notice already posted. Money already gone. A suspension already signed. Bruises already blooming. Marcia stood inside that pattern so long she sometimes mistook it for weather. Hearing Micah say it out loud made her want to sit down and hit somebody at the same time.

Delroy's voice dropped. That was how anger came in him when embarrassment had been worked hard enough. "I said watch your mouth."

"Or what?" Micah shot back. "You gone send my bus pass to Kingston?"

The silence after that was vicious.

Kerene moved fast, quicker than her bright easy talk ever suggested. She caught Micah by the shoulder and yanked him half behind her before Delroy even took a breath. The fishing line still clung to one finger, glinting.

"No," she snapped. The charm had dropped clean out of her voice. "No more."

Delroy had not raised a hand. Marcia knew that. He had only stepped forward, stung, shoulder jerking high with the reflex of it. But the whole room had read the possibility at once, and once a possibility like that showed its face you could not tell everybody they had imagined it.

Delroy looked from Kerene's body in front of Micah to Marcia's to Tiana's, and Marcia saw it hit him plain: not a single soul in the room had assumed safety first.

Shame took the color out of him.

"I wasn't going to touch the boy," he said.

No one answered.

Outside, somebody's bass rattled a bottle in the alley. A bus sighed at the corner. From the front house came a burst of laughter from a television sitcom, canned and cruelly timed.

Delroy turned back to Marcia because she was the only fight he could still enter without losing completely.

"You hear one name and you make me monster," he said.

"No. The lie make you liar. The debt make you reckless. The rest you earning line by line."

"Everything with you have to sound like bookkeeping."

"Because somebody in this place has to keep the book."

He slapped the nearest receipt with the back of his fingers. "You want the full story? Fine. Devon calls me at six in the morning Miami time because he never keeps the difference straight. Half the time he starts with evening like the boy still thinks I live down the road. He don't ask pretty. He don't even ask smart. He circles it. Says he can manage. Says he's only checking in. Then he says the real thing at the end because pride does not disappear just because a person need. You know what it feel like hearing your own habits come back in your child's voice from another country?"

Marcia almost hated him most when he got close to the truth. He could make pain sound so human you had to work not to hand it absolution.

"Yes," she said. "I do. I hear your habits in Micah every day and it terrifies me."

Micah gave a tiny wounded laugh and then swallowed it.

Delroy went on as if he had not heard. Maybe he had not. Maybe once a man started defending himself, language narrowed to the tunnel ahead.

"The boy missed enough of me. I missed enough of him. Every time I tell him no, I hear the years I wasn't there getting bigger. So I sent what I had when I had it. Not to show off. Not to disrespect you. Because I can't be absent twice."

For one ugly second, Marcia understood him completely. Not agreed. Understood. Understood the guilt like a second spine. Understood how a voice on a phone from Jamaica could make a man feel like he still owed the sea itself. Understood too that understanding changed nothing in the cupboard.

She took the top receipt and pushed it at his chest. It fluttered down because his right arm did not rise fast enough to catch it.

"You were absent here while you were doing charity for guilt," she said. "You were absent when Tiana came back from that office and found out her father had used the money and lied. You were absent when Micah sat in that clinic with his face swollen. You were absent when Claude called and called and you shoved the phone under a cushion like if cloth could pay debt. Don't tell me about being absent twice. You found a way to be absent in stereo."

Kerene sucked air through her teeth. Even Micah looked at Marcia then like he had not known she kept knives that sharp.

Good, a part of her thought savagely. Let them all know.

And another part, smaller and meaner because it was frightened, knew the children were learning her too.

Delroy stared at her as if she had slapped him. Maybe she had, in the only currency left.

"So that's who I am now," he said.

"Who were you before? Tell me. Because Claude know your debts, Western Union know your business, the aid office know your negligence, and somehow I'm always the last damn person to get informed in my own life."

That was it. The line under the line. The old wound inside the new one. Marcia heard it as she said it and could not take it back. She was tired, yes. Angry, yes. But beneath both sat the humiliation she had been carrying for months like a basin on her head: men, offices, daughters, accidents, everybody delivering facts after they had already changed her choices. She was the planner only in the narrow window before reality arrived with a stamp and made planning into comedy.

Delroy understood that too. She saw it in the way his mouth opened, then shut. If he had apologized right then, clean and ordinary, maybe the night would have found a softer failure.

Instead he said, "You think you own pain because you inventory it."

Marcia laughed again, harsher this time. "And you think naming love excuses theft."

"Theft."

"Yes, theft. What else you call taking from this table without telling the people who eat off it?"

"I'm not a thief in my own house."

"Then stop moving like one."

Micah made a little startled noise, not quite a word. Tiana's face had gone pale under her brown. Kerene looked toward the door like she was measuring whether this was the moment to make everybody step outside and let the neighbors have the show.

Delroy leaned both hands on the table. The right one shook when the weight landed wrong and he pulled it back fast. Marcia watched him do it. Watched pain crack through the performance and vanish again.

"You know what," he said softly. "You want me standing here so everybody can count out my sins one by one. Fine. Claude four hundred. Devon two-seventy. Tiana's renewal money before that. Anything else? Put it on the table. Let's finish the auction."

Tiana stood so suddenly the chair legs screamed.

"Stop," she said.

Everybody turned.

She had one hand pressed to the document folder so hard the cardboard bent. Her eyes moved between them, not pleading, not even really angry anymore. Worse than angry. Exhausted in a way eighteen-year-olds should not know.

"This isn't helping," she said, then corrected herself with a bitter little shake of the head. "No. That's too soft. This is making everything truer in the worst way."

Marcia almost said something to comfort her and found nothing that did not sound fake.

Micah looked from his sister to his father. "You leaving?" he asked Delroy, too fast, as if by saying it first he could turn it into a joke before anybody else could make it a fact.

Delroy straightened.

He looked at Marcia one more time, and what she saw then was not rage. Not really. It was the point after shame when a person would rather step out into night with nowhere arranged than remain under the eyes that had measured him correctly.

"Yeah," he said. "Probably best."

Marcia did not tell him to stay.

That was the failure that would keep its teeth in her later.

He went to the dresser and yanked open the top drawer with his left hand. Socks, an old church tie, pain pills in an orange bottle, his charger, two work shirts. He packed badly because he was angry and because the shoulder made speed clumsy. The duffel bag slipped once when he tried to swing it up and Micah half moved, instinct more than forgiveness, then stopped himself so hard his whole body jerked backward.

Delroy saw that too.

He put the bag down, slower the second time, and zipped it.

"Where you going?" Kerene asked before she could stop herself.

He gave a laugh with no humor in it. "Miami full of places a man can sleep when he don't fit where he paying rent."

Marcia said, "You paying rent?"

It came out reflexive, crueler than she had even meant. The room winced.

Delroy nodded once like that line settled something for him. "Right. Forgot."

He bent for his boots. Wincing did not stop him. He shoved his feet in without untying them properly and nearly lost balance on the second one. Again Micah moved and again did not finish moving.

Tiana stood by the table with her folder clutched to her ribs, watching the man who had failed her in so many ordinary administrative ways become suddenly, absurdly, just a man trying not to grunt while he put on work boots with one bad shoulder. Marcia could not know all of what moved through her daughter right then, but she could see it was not simple hatred. Simplicity had gone out of the room with Claude's truck.

Delroy slung the bag across his left shoulder, picked up his phone from under the couch cushion, and looked at Marcia.

"You could've talked to me different," he said.

The sentence was so small compared to the wreck around it that Marcia almost choked.

"You could've talked to me at all," she said.

He flinched. Not because the words were loud. Because they were exact.

Then he opened the door and stepped into the damp night.

No one followed him. No one called him back. They listened to his boots on the concrete path, then the gate, then nothing.

For a long while after, the apartment kept the shape of a person who had just left it. The bed looked wider and poorer. The air seemed to move wrong around the empty space in front of the dresser. On the table, the remittance receipts fluttered under the fan's weak chop beside Tiana's college forms, transfer numbers and checklist boxes sharing the same patch of fake wood as if that had always been the story.

Kerene was the first to move. She sat back down on the folding cot, picked up her sandal, and stared at the clear line threaded through the broken strap without seeming to remember what came next.

Micah said, "So that's it?"

No one answered him.

Marcia gathered the receipts into one stack, then put them back down because touching them made her feel dirtier, not cleaner. The bleach had dried on her hands. When she flexed her fingers, the skin pulled.

Tiana slowly sat again. She opened her folder, closed it, opened it once more. Her eyes were bright but dry. "I still need the originals for tomorrow," she said.

That nearly broke Marcia. Not the argument. Not Delroy leaving. That sentence. The simple forward motion of bureaucracy refusing to care what had just detonated in their one-room house.

"We'll find them," Marcia said.

Her voice sounded normal. She hated that it did.

Micah leaned back against the wall and stared at the door. "He really gone."

"For tonight," Kerene said, though she could not know that.

Marcia looked at her children, at her cousin with the ruined sandal in her lap, at the trunk still open with old papers exposed like organs, at the stove with dinner cooling into a film no one wanted. The room wanted instruction from her. It always did. It wanted next steps, portions, order, some sentence sturdy enough to nail everybody back into family.

What she had instead was the sound of Claude saying school starting Monday, and Delroy saying he couldn't be absent twice, and Micah asking if his bus pass was going to Kingston, and Tiana refusing childhood at the table.

The economy of the house had broken open where everybody could see the wires now. There was no honest way to cover them back up tonight.

Outside, thunder rolled somewhere far enough off to be harmless for the moment and close enough to remind her it was coming anyway.
