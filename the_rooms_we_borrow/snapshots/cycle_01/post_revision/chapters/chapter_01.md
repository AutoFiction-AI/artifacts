# Chapter 1

The auditorium was cold enough to raise a rash of gooseflesh along Tiana's forearms, which would have been a mercy if the rest of her had not been sweating through the lining of her dress. Graduation always pretended to be about youth and futures and a clean ceremonial crossing. In practice it was also about evidence: who came for you, who had flowers, and who knew how to dress for a room like this without looking as if they had spent the bus ride praying nobody would stare too long.

She sat in the third row of graduates with the cap pins biting her scalp and three gold cords warming the back of her neck. The folding chair dug a ridge into her thigh. On the other side of the aisle a girl from student government kept smoothing the skirt under her gown as if the fabric could still matter now that everybody was dressed in the same blue polyester tent. Up in the stands, families leaned over the rail with phones already out. Men wore jackets the color of sand. Women wore church hats and white linen and perfume that had made it from parking lot to bleacher without dying in the heat. Little cousins swung their legs and dropped program booklets to the floor.

Four seats in the family section, two rows up from the center aisle, stayed empty.

Tiana looked at them, then looked away. She had folded the programs on those seats into sharp rectangles so the usher would know somebody was coming. Now the paper just looked stranded, four white flags of a claim nobody had shown up to defend.

Her phone hummed once against her thigh.

`On d way. Traffic dumb.` from Delroy.

Nothing from Marcia was worse. Delroy could text anything and mean none of it. Marcia only texted when she was forced into a machine she did not trust.

Onstage the principal adjusted the microphone and smiled the wide exhausted smile of a man who had been saying the same good-luck sentences all week. His face gleamed under the lights. Beside the podium stood the guidance counselor, a narrow woman in a mint suit whose left sneaker gave a tiny rubber squeak every third step because the sole had come half loose. She held a milk crate full of scholarship packets and donor envelopes. She kept knocking the stack square with the side of her hand, not from nerves exactly but from habit, as if every piece of paper in a school had to be made to behave before it could do damage.

Tiana had marked the program at home. Her own name appeared four times: English Department Award, Social Studies Medal, Principal's Scholar, state university scholarship recognition. She had pretended the black pen circles were just organization, not appetite. If the family had gotten there on time they would have seen it when they opened the booklet. They had not gotten there on time. So the wanting stayed private for another ten minutes.

The principal began the dean's roll call. The gym smelled like hot extension cords and old varnish and the sweet damp of too many people pressed into one room. Somewhere behind the bleachers a baby started crying with the offended, insulted howl of a creature brought against its will into fluorescent ceremony. Everybody laughed politely. Tiana did not. Her eyes kept lifting to the side doors.

She knew the script already. If Delroy came in first he would enter like he had been invited to improve the room. If Marcia came in first she would have apology on her face before she even found the seats, though she had probably taken two buses from Coral Gables and changed shoes in the back of one. Micah, if he had been dragged along at all, would perform indifference so hard it circled into theater.

Her row stood for the processional of honors graduates. Caps shifted. Tassels brushed cheeks. Tiana moved when the others moved, walked when the row in front of her walked, smiled when the photographer stationed by the fake ficus tree lifted his hand. She knew how to perform competence even when nobody had given her enough room to make it comfortable.

When the vice principal said her name the first time, the room offered a clean clap and nothing else.

Tiana Campbell, English Department Award.

She crossed the stage with the small fixed smile she had practiced in mirrors and dark bus windows. The English teacher waiting by the certificate table wore her reading glasses on a bead chain and smelled faintly of lemon oil, the way old classrooms smelled after somebody had tried to scrub ambition into the desks. "I told you Baldwin would take you somewhere," she whispered as she handed over the cream paper.

Tiana smiled harder. "Thank you, Ms. Prado."

Offstage again, she sat, set the certificate on her lap, and kept her face still while the applause drained away. Ms. Prado had meant well. Teachers always thought they were giving you a ladder when sometimes they were only giving you a prettier name for distance.

The doors at the back of the gym banged open during the science awards.

Sound arrived before Tiana turned. Delroy's stage whisper carried like a church tambourine. "Excuse me, excuse me, this section? No, man, I've got it." Metal seats knocked. Somebody muttered. A few heads twisted in a single, practiced motion that told Tiana they did not usually have to wonder which family the disruption belonged to.

Her family came down the bleacher steps in a compressed, embarrassed cluster. Marcia first this time, one hand on the rail, one hand clutching the strap of a black purse so hard the tendons stood out in the back of her wrist. She had changed out of her work shoes but not out of the day; the lilac blouse she saved for church had a damp crescent between her shoulder blades, and her lipstick had worn off in the center of her mouth. Delroy followed in dark slacks and the white button-down he called his good shirt, though the cuffs had gone shiny with years and one collar point never sat right. Micah came last, lanky and irritated in a tie he had plainly knotted from YouTube and already loosened, his dress shoes unlaced because they were blistering him and he did not care who knew it.

Tiana felt the heat rise from her collar all the way to her scalp.

She also felt relief, which was the insulting part.

Delroy spotted her immediately and lifted one hand in triumph. It was not a discreet wave. It was an announcement. His grin flashed so fast and bright she nearly laughed, stupidly, before the woman in the pearl drop earrings in front of him turned, saw the whole family at once, and performed a tiny startled smile that might have been kindness and might have been social mercy. Tiana hated her for it and hated herself more for noticing.

The principal kept talking through the interruption. The room settled around the Campbells by force rather than grace. Marcia found Tiana's eyes across the distance and pressed her lips together in a look that said later, later, I'm sorry, I came.

The vice principal reached the social studies medal, then the principal's scholar recognition, then the state scholarship acknowledgments in quick succession, and Tiana went up and back and up and back until the carpet runner on the stage felt familiar under her heels. By the third trip Delroy had lost any respect for auditorium scale.

"That's my daughter," he said, not shouting exactly, but not far enough from it to save her.

Several people laughed. One of the donor women near the front smiled toward the bleachers. Micah, seeing the whole thing could not be undone, leaned into it with the ease of a boy who always preferred the wound he chose himself. He put two fingers in his mouth and whistled once, sharp and indecently loud. An usher half rose. Delroy clapped Micah on the shoulder, proud of a crime he had not committed. Marcia closed her eyes long enough to swallow it, opened them, and started clapping anyway.

Tiana accepted the scholarship certificate from the guidance counselor and shook hands with a man from the local alumni association whose watch looked more expensive than her mother's monthly electric bill. The envelope had the University of Florida logo in blue and orange on the corner. It should have felt like a door opening. In her hand it felt only heavier than paper had any right to be.

Back in her seat, she could hear the family now that they were in place.

"You missed the first one," Micah whispered, still breathing a little hard from the bleacher steps.

"We missed two," Marcia said under her breath. "If you had put your shoes on when I told you to, maybe we would have missed only one."

"Nobody was looking at my shoes."

"They're now."

Delroy leaned forward between them. "Look how she's collecting them, though. Like she owns the place."

Tiana kept her eyes on the stage. Pride from him always came wrapped in performance, but it still reached her, and that reach was part of what made him dangerous. He could make love feel like a crowd had witnessed it.

When commencement finally ended and the gym broke into scraping chairs and shouting and flashes of phone light, Tiana tried to move fast, the way she always moved after events like this, before people could braid congratulations to pity. Families flooded the floor. A woman with a camera bag shouted for her nephew to stop blinking. Somebody's grandmother in a yellow suit sat down right there in the aisle and fanned herself with the program because her feet had swollen past patience. Teachers got trapped in circles of gratitude. The air-conditioning lost the fight it had been pretending to win all evening.

"Graduate!" Delroy spread his arms as if he needed a larger body in order to contain her success. He kissed Tiana's forehead, leaving a trace of aftershave and sweat. "Come, let me see all of them."

She handed him the certificates because refusing would have been ruder than the room could afford. He fanned them out badly, mixing the pages, reading half the headings aloud and half inventing the rest.

"English, scholarship, principal scholar. You hear that?" He pitched the last line toward nobody and everybody. "Principal scholar. She was too bright for this house from the beginning."

"Don't say that in front of the child like it's a compliment," Marcia said. But she was smiling when she took the papers from him and stacked them properly. Her hands were callused and careful. She read every line. She always did.

Micah had found the basket of butter mints from the donor table and was emptying them into his pocket with a thief's solemn concentration. "If she gets one more certificate they have to let us park in Coral Gables."

"You don't drive," Tiana said.

"That's why I said us."

She laughed before she could stop herself. Micah grinned with the raw pleasure of somebody who had successfully dragged his sister down from her ledge, however briefly. He had a blister peeled open at the back of his heel. He kept lifting one foot and then the other, pretending it was dance rather than pain.

The guidance counselor appeared at Tiana's elbow carrying the UF packet and a clipboard. Up close, the mint suit had a loose thread at one cuff. "There you are. I was trying not to lose you in the stampede."

"Sorry." Tiana straightened without thinking. Around school adults who could alter her future, her spine always seemed to know its job before the rest of her did.

"Don't apologize for being popular on your own graduation day." The counselor smiled at the family and then at the packet. "This is the housing and final aid packet from Gainesville. Your spot is provisionally held, but the $225 dorm deposit has to be paid by August first or the reservation releases to the next student. Do not miss that date."

She tapped the orange card visible through the top of the folder. Two hundred and twenty-five dollars. August first landed in Tiana like something dropped into water, the ripple running wider than the room.

Marcia shifted closer. "What is that?"

The counselor, helpful and brisk, turned toward her. "Housing confirmation. The university just needs the $225 in on time and the file should keep moving."

The word should sat there between them, glossy and useless.

Tiana took the packet before the woman could say anything else. "It's fine. It's just the dorm paperwork."

"Just?" Micah said. "Listen to rich girl."

"Shut up."

The counselor had already moved on to another graduate, her left sneaker squeaking away through the crowd. Delroy touched the Gators logo with one finger and whistled low, softer than Micah's earlier blast, almost reverent. "University of Florida. You hear that, Marcia?"

Marcia heard everything; that had never been the issue. Her eyes rested on the edge of the packet a beat too long, then lifted to Tiana's face. There was pride there, yes. There was also the first click of arithmetic beginning behind it.

"We'll take a photo first," she said. "Then we can talk at home."

Tiana's stomach tightened. She hated that her first response to we can talk at home was not warmth but inventory. Home meant numbers. Home meant what things cost after people stopped congratulating you.

They took pictures under the painted school seal. Delroy insisted on one with all the certificates visible. Micah kept making ugly faces at the last second until Marcia pinched the soft skin under his arm hard enough to correct his spirit. An assistant principal with a comb-over and nicotine-colored fingers held Delroy's phone and took six crooked shots, muttering "One more" every time Delroy demanded better lighting from a building that had no interest in flattering poor people.

In the fifth photo Tiana looked happy enough to fool almost anybody. Her cap sat straight. Her smile did not show the clench behind it. Marcia stood beside her with one hand flattened against the small of her back, not possessive, not even especially tender, just steadying. Delroy leaned in too close, proud as brass. Micah, finally cooperating, held the bundle of certificates like he was the bodyguard of paper.

For a moment Tiana let herself stand inside that version of the family, loud and late and underprepared and hers.

Then she felt the stiff corner of the dorm packet against her palm again.

On the walk to the bus stop, she slipped it behind the certificates so her mother would not keep seeing the orange card.

***

By the time they reached Little River the night had thickened into its usual wet grip. Even the dark felt humid. Music leaked from a car idling too long at the corner. A rooster on the next block, confused by streetlight, offered one rusty half-crow and then thought better of it. Tiana carried the certificate bundle and the packet and her cap. Delroy carried nothing except his running commentary about how half the graduates on that stage had not looked half as ready for the world as his daughter.

"You see the one with the red shoes?" he said. "That boy looked frightened of a stapler."

"Everybody looks frightened of something," Marcia said.

"Not my girl."

Tiana almost answered. She did not.

Their path to the apartment ran along the side of the decaying stucco house the landlord rented out front. The side yard smelled of hot dirt, old frying oil, and the sour wetness from the garbage bins. Gravel shifted under their shoes. The motion light over the converted garage clicked on a second after they turned the corner.

Then Tiana saw the paper on the door.

It was stapled crooked to their door at eye level, a hard square of municipal red against the peeling cream paint. It was not pink or orange. It was red like a hand where somebody had been struck. Black block letters stamped across the top under a city seal. There was a case number and a folio number and the kind of language that did not need a raised voice because it had already decided who was smaller.

She stopped so abruptly Micah bumped into her shoulder.

"What now?" he said, and then saw it. "Damn."

Delroy stepped ahead of them. "What's this foolishness?"

He reached to tear it down.

Marcia caught his wrist before his fingers closed on the page. "Leave it."

"It's on my door."

"That's exactly why you leave it."

The motion light hummed overhead. In the front house a television blared laughter at a joke nobody near the Campbells could hear. Mosquitoes found the exposed skin at Tiana's ankles. She read what she could without moving closer.

NOTICE OF VIOLATION.

UNPERMITTED OCCUPANCY.

COMPLY OR VACATE WITHIN NINETY DAYS.

Below that, smaller paragraphs. References to unsafe electrical modifications, illegal conversion, inspection authority, daily fines. There was a blank line for owner of record and another for responsible party. No line at all for the people sleeping inside.

Delroy laughed once. It came out wrong, too dry. "This is for the landlord. They post paper on everything in this city."

Marcia moved close enough to the page that her shadow swallowed half of it. She read with her head tilted slightly, the way she read medicine labels and bus schedules and all the other small print that could punish you for assuming. Tiana watched her mouth move once over the phrases without sound. Her mother's hand still held Delroy's wrist, not tightly now, just there.

"It says the unit has to be vacated if the violation is not corrected in ninety days," Marcia said.

"By the owner."

"You think code enforcement is going to argue semantics with us while we stand here with our pots and shoes?"

"Marcia."

"I'm reading what's on the page."

That shut him up for three seconds. Tiana counted them because the quiet felt expensive.

Micah leaned sideways to read around Marcia's shoulder. "Unpermitted occupancy sounds like they caught us existing wrong."

Nobody answered. He tried a grin anyway. "Maybe we can tell them we're very permitted. Spiritually."

Tiana almost told him to stop. Then she looked at his face under the hard porch light and saw he had gone shiny with sweat above the lip. He was frightened. The joke was what he was using instead of saying that.

Marcia released Delroy's wrist and flattened the page against the door where the humidity had started to curl one corner. "Ninety days from when?"

Tiana found the date stamped near the bottom. "Three days ago."

Marcia inhaled through her nose. "So less than ninety."

"Still plenty of time," Delroy said quickly. "You know how these people do things. They send paper first. The landlord pays somebody. Maybe he fixes one wire, maybe he knows a man at the office. It doesn't mean men are coming tomorrow with a truck."

"It doesn't mean they aren't," Marcia said.

He shook his head, annoyed already, which was always how his fear dressed itself before it admitted anything. "You go straight to funeral."

"And you go straight to fairy tale."

The words did not come out loud, but they landed loud enough.

Tiana looked back at the red notice. The city seal sat at the top like a judge's face. All that crisp authority for a room that could barely fit their lives now, let alone after it had been converted into evidence. She thought of the auditorium, the donor envelopes, the guidance counselor's squeaking sneaker, the orange card inside the packet pressed under her arm. Two pieces of paper in one night, both demanding money by a date certain if she wanted the future to remain available.

Delroy yanked his keys from his pocket and shoved the door open. Warm air rolled out, thick with bleach, fried onion, and the mildew they kept trying to outrun with open windows and luck. The apartment was one long compromise: bed in one corner, Micah's mattress folded against the wall, kitchenette shoved under hanging cabinets that never closed properly, bathroom door swollen from old damp. A standing fan turned its head with a click at each end of the arc, distributing heat rather than fighting it.

On the table by the hot plate sat the little grocery-store cake Marcia had bought that morning and hidden in the fridge behind eggs. Congratulations Graduate, the icing read in blue loops already sweating at the edges. Tiana had forgotten it existed. The room looked ashamed to be caught trying to celebrate.

Marcia came in behind them with the red notice still in her hand. She set her purse down, found the one working pen by the sugar jar, and pulled over the back of an electric bill envelope to write on. "We need the landlord's number."

"I've got it."

"Then call him."

"Tonight?"

Marcia looked up. "You think tomorrow will make this prettier?"

Delroy spread his hands. "It's after ten. If you call a man at this hour over a city paper, all you do is let him know you're panicking."

"I'm not panicking." Her voice stayed level, which landed harder than a shout would have. "I'm counting. Ninety days means June, July, August. Tiana needs school money in August. Rent is eleven-fifty before that. If this fool puts us out to save himself fines, we don't have someplace ready to go. So I'm counting."

The room shifted at that, just a little. Tiana kept her face neutral. She did not know what school money Marcia was imagining. Books, probably. Supplies. Some basic mother estimate of the cost of a child leaving. Not the actual number: $225 due by August first, an amount small enough to sound manageable and vicious enough to turn ugly once it had to be produced all at once in a house already chasing $1,150 rent.

Micah had already cut himself a corner of the cake with a butter knife and was eating it standing by the sink. Blue frosting ringed his thumb. "If we get evicted before I finish summer, do I still have to go to school or is homelessness like a district exemption?"

"Don't play with your mouth," Marcia said automatically.

"I'm trying to innovate."

"Take a plate if you're going to eat."

He did not. He licked the frosting off his thumb and reached for another bite, hunger making him bold enough to risk her irritation. Tiana was suddenly grateful for the stupidity of cake, for the ordinary appetite of it. If Micah could still steal dessert while a red notice sat on the table, the world had not ended in front of her. It had only tilted.

Delroy took the paper from Marcia and scanned it more aggressively than carefully, as if force of personality could extract a different meaning from printed text. "This says owner of record right here. It isn't talking to us."

"Where do you think we live?" Marcia asked.

"On his property."

"In the unit they say is illegal."

"Half Miami is illegal if you ask the right office."

"And half Miami can still lose a room."

He slapped the notice lightly against his palm. "You want me to do what? Throw everybody into the street tonight because the city found red paper?"

"I want you to stop talking as if refusing to understand something is the same as fixing it."

That hit clean. Tiana heard it. Delroy heard it too. His mouth tightened at one corner.

"I understand enough," he said. "I understand that a man who can't pay code fines fast wants tenants to panic faster so he can clear the place cheap. I understand that landlords lie. I understand that city offices move like old people taking stairs. We have time."

"Time for what?"

"For me to talk to him. For him to sort it out."

"Sort it out with what money?"

Silence came again, and this time it was packed.

Delroy put the notice down. "You don't know what he has."

Marcia let out a small sound through her teeth, not quite a laugh. "No. And you don't know either. That's why I'm making a list."

She started writing on the envelope in her small exact print.

Landlord.

Church room?

Agency extra shifts.

Ask Sonia about cousin in Broward.

Boxes.

Tiana watched the list arrive. This was what her mother did when fear threatened to go soft around the edges and become useless. She gave it nouns. She gave it errands. She pinned it to paper before it could spread.

"Mummy," Tiana said, keeping her voice light, "it's one notice. Maybe Daddy is right and it takes a while."

Marcia did not look up. "Maybe. That would be nice."

It struck Tiana then that nice had become a word for fantasy in this house. It did not mean joy or comfort anymore. It meant fantasy.

Delroy went to the sink, ran water into a glass, and drank it too fast. A little escaped the corner of his mouth and ran into his beard. He wiped it with the back of his hand, irritated at the evidence of thirst. "I'm calling the landlord in the morning. Early. Before he starts ducking me."

"Call code enforcement too," Marcia said.

"For what? So they can put our names on another list?"

"We're already on one."

Micah pointed his butter knife at the red notice. "Maybe Tiana can write them a letter. She wins all these essay prizes. She can say, Dear City of Miami, congratulations, you have discovered poor people."

"Give me that knife before you stab the air to death," Marcia said.

He handed it over and finally took a plate. His face had gone slack with teenage boredom, but Tiana knew him too well. He was watching every sentence. He always acted like a room had to earn his attention right before proving he had not missed anything.

The fan clicked. Outside, somebody revved a motorcycle up the block and let it idle back down. The front-house television audience laughed again through the wall. Their lives kept happening, which felt like one more insult.

Marcia looked up from the envelope. "Tiana, what exactly did the counselor give you?"

The question came so neatly it almost sounded harmless.

Tiana felt the packet against her ribs as if it had grown corners sharper than cardboard. "Just housing stuff. Checklists."

"Anything with a date on it?"

Delroy turned at that. Not sharply. Just enough.

She made herself shrug. "It's summer. Everything has a date on it."

Marcia held her eyes for a beat longer than comfort allowed. Then she nodded once. "Bring it to me tomorrow. We'll see what is what."

Tomorrow bought her one night.

"Okay," Tiana said.

Micah, because he could smell pressure like rain, broke in before the silence could harden. "If she gets a dorm, can I have her side of the room? I can put up that poster you hate."

"You don't have a side of the room now," Tiana said.

"Exactly. I'm a man with a vision."

Delroy snorted despite himself. Marcia did not smile, but some of the set left her mouth. Micah saw that too and pushed his luck, lifting his paper plate like a toast. "To higher education and illegal occupancy."

"Boy," Marcia said.

"I'm saying there is range in this family."

This time even Tiana laughed, unwillingly, the sound leaving her before she could inspect it. It lasted only a second. The notice remained on the table. The list remained on the envelope. Nothing had changed. But the room remembered itself, briefly, as a place where cruelty and humor used the same cutlery.

Later, after the cake had been eaten in uneven strips and Micah had folded his mattress out and Delroy had made two more calls that went to voicemail, Tiana took her folder into the bathroom and locked the swollen door.

The overhead bulb buzzed. The mirror above the sink showed her graduation face half dismantled: lipstick faded, edges of makeup broken apart by sweat, one side of the cap-flattened hair already rebelling. She set the folder on the closed toilet lid and opened it carefully, as if the paperwork might startle and fly.

The certificates sat on top, then the commencement program, then a flyer for Bright Futures renewal instructions, and then the university packet.

Inside was the orange housing card, bright as hazard tape.

Housing reservation confirmation. Advance rent payment of $225 due by August 1 to secure dorm assignment.

The line below that was worse, because it was cheerful.

Failure to submit payment by the deadline may result in cancellation of your space.

That may was another bureaucratic lie of softness. It meant will, and everybody who had ever been poor enough to live by paperwork knew it.

Tiana ran her thumb over the embossed edge of the school seal until it left a pale dent in her skin. In the other room she could hear her parents through the thin wall, their voices low because Micah was trying to sleep. Low did not mean gentle.

"You should've told me earlier the landlord was getting inspected," Marcia said.

"I didn't know it reached this stage."

"That's not what I asked."

"I heard things. People hear things all the time."

"And you did nothing with them."

"I'm doing something now."

"Now the red paper is on my door."

The word my held more than property. It held all the labor she had poured into making one converted garage look less temporary than the city had always meant it to be.

Two hundred and twenty-five dollars was less than a quarter of the $1,150 rent and still the kind of number that could split the room open, because it had to arrive clean and on time. Tiana slid the orange card back into its sleeve. She could walk out there now. She could put it on the table beside the violation notice and let both truths sit in the same heat. Her August would meet their ninety days. Her mother's list would get another line. Her father would make another promise or another joke, maybe both. The room would shrink further around all of them.

She closed the packet instead.

The night's arithmetic was ugly. The family could survive celebration or dread. It could not hold both deadlines at once and still breathe.

She tucked the university papers beneath the certificates, then under her immunization records and test scores, deeper in the folder where official things went to wait their turn to ruin her. When she came out, the apartment was mostly dark. Delroy sat on the edge of the bed with his elbows on his knees, looking at nothing. Marcia lay on top of the covers in her slip and bra with the red notice on the pillow beside her, reading it again as if a second pass might reveal a mercy clause hidden in the code sections. Micah had one arm flung over his face and one bare foot sticking off the mattress, a butter-mint wrapper still trapped against his wrist.

Tiana slid her folder under the bed.

The fan turned. The notice rustled once under Marcia's hand, then again as she folded it smaller. Somewhere outside, a bus sighed at the corner and pulled away.

In the dark, her mother began counting days under her breath, not for drama, just to make the number real enough to work on.

Tiana kept August silent.
