# Chapter 9

By seven the apartment had its usual morning smell of bleach, old grease, damp towel, and heat beginning again. Tiana sat at the table with a mug of bush tea, her phone face down beside it, and the blue document folder Marcia had made her bring out at seven sharp. The legal pad beside it was already scarred with due dates and copied numbers. Marcia had on one of her house dresses and a scarf tied back from her face, and the sight of her wiping the counter made Delroy feel accused in a way shouting almost never managed. She had a way of cleaning when she was furious that made every object in the room look guilty.

Dawn was still in his ears. He had lain under the sheet and kept his eyes shut while Tiana slipped back through the side gate, one soft click under her hands and then her careful steps past the window unit, past Micah's sprawled legs, past the place on the floor where the linoleum bubbled up like old skin. Delroy knew the sound of each person in that room. Tiana, these days, moved like the house was a test she meant to pass without touching anything. He had not asked where she had been when she set her bag down without a word, and he was not going to ask now. He had no righteous questions left in him.

Micah was still under the fan with one foot out from the sheet. Tiana had changed clothes, but her eyes still looked used, and not from crying. Delroy saw the same shirt from yesterday balled in the laundry bucket. He looked away before his mind started arranging things he did not want to know about his child.

He had twenty-eight dollars in his wallet, a quarter tank of gas, and the old car still starting if he did not ask too much from it. He had been turning the idea over since midnight while Marcia slept stiff with her back to him and Micah breathed through his mouth and the room held everybody's shame like steam. If they stayed in that apartment all day, the house would go back to being what it had been since the partner broke and the paperwork lie came out: a place where every sentence carried a bill or an accusation behind it. He could not fix the money by breakfast. He could maybe buy them six hours where nobody had to look at the walls.

"We're going to the beach," he said.

Nobody answered. The fan clicked once on its loose screw.

Marcia kept wiping. "With which money?"

There it was. Not loud. Exact first.

"Not South Beach. Virginia Key." Delroy made his voice easy. "I've got gas. We have food in this house. The children need some air."

Micah pushed himself up on one elbow. "You said children like we're six."

"You're fifteen and ugly in the morning. That's still child enough for me."

Micah snorted despite himself, but his eyes cut toward Marcia to see if the room allowed laughter.

Marcia wrung the rag over the sink. "What you mean, we have food? You mean the chicken I was saving for tonight? You mean the bread that is already going dry?"

"I mean the same food we'd eat here," Delroy said. "Only we eat it where the wind can touch us."

Tiana lifted her mug and drank without looking at him. "This some kind of apology production?"

The line was flat and educated in the way she used when she wanted to shame him cleanly. Delroy took it. He had earned worse.

"If it was, I'd charge admission. I'm saying let's go before all of you turn into salt fish right here in the room."

"We're already salt fish," Marcia said. "Only dry and expensive."

That almost got him smiling. Almost. He moved around the kitchenette, opening cupboards so he would have something to do with his hands. Standing still made him feel like a defendant. He found the cooler with the cracked white lid, the one missing its side handle, and set it on the floor.

"I can get ice."

Marcia folded the rag over the faucet. "You can get ice with which money?"

"The same money I've got. Marcia, please. I know the arithmetic. I'm not asking you to forget it."

She turned then. Her face was not loud either. That was the trouble. Loud you could answer. This look had measuring in it.

"No," she said. "You're asking us to act like we can rest because you woke up with a plan."

He wanted to joke first, as he always did. He felt the joke come to the front of his mouth and stop there. That habit had carried him through enough rooms that he still trusted it a second longer than he should have.

"I'm asking for one day that doesn't smell like panic."

Tiana's gaze flicked up at that. Micah pulled the sheet off his legs and sat fully awake now.

Marcia looked back at the counter, then at the freezer, then at the front door. Her eyes worked over each one like she could bully a fourth choice into the room. Delroy knew that look too. It was the one she wore when she was lining up all the bad choices and checking whether one of them at least came with a breeze.

"I'm not cooking a feast for your performance," she said.

"Nobody asked for feast."

"Good. Because all I've got is chicken thigh, bread, and some mango that needs to get eaten today."

"That sounds like beach food to me," Micah said.

Tiana let out a small sound through her nose. "Everything sounds like beach food to you."

"Because I understand joy, Sis. Try it sometime."

She finally looked at him, and Delroy saw the old pair of them again, from before school offices and college folders and this month's betrayals sharpened everybody into their own corner. Micah grinned, already half defensive. Tiana tried to hold her mouth straight and could not quite.

Delroy pounced before the room changed its mind.

"Good. Micah, get up and find the folding chairs. Tiana, bring the towels in from the line if they don't smell like rain. Marcia, let me deal with the ice."

Marcia gave him a look that said she had not granted him command of anything. Still, she opened the freezer.

"If this turns stupid," she said, "we're coming straight back."

"Fair."

"And don't go spending gas like it grows in the glove compartment."

"Gas doesn't grow anywhere. That's why the country keeps acting insane."

"Daddy," Tiana said, already tired of him again.

But she stood up.

The work of going turned the room ordinary by force. Micah found one beach chair and one kitchen chair that had to serve as a beach chair because nothing in the apartment matched long enough to remain a set. Tiana brought in towels stiff from line-drying and shook a drift of lint onto the floor. Marcia salted the chicken and heated oil because she would not send her family to water with only bread and sliced fruit, no matter how angry she was. Delroy rinsed the cooler in the yard with the hose and watched a palmetto bug struggle toward shade. The morning was already heavy enough that his undershirt stuck to his back. When he bent to lift the cooler, his right shoulder gave him the old warning twinge, sharp then sulky. He kept moving.

At the gas station he bought two bags of ice, a loaf of cheap wheat bread, and one case of off-brand orange soda with the last decent bills in his wallet. The cashier, a Haitian woman with a silver tooth and reading glasses hanging on a bead chain, looked at the sweating cooler in his trunk and said, "Beach day?"

"Trying."

She gave him a look like she understood trying as a verb. "Go early. Sun wicked today."

When he got back, Marcia had packed the chicken in foil and paper towel and put the mango in a margarine tub. She had also, without announcing it, changed into a blue T-shirt over her swimsuit. Delroy noticed because for all the anger in the room, there was still that. There was still his wife's body under ordinary clothes, still the memory of her on beaches when they were younger and had less money but more nerve.

Tiana came out in shorts and a long white cover shirt over a black one-piece that made her look older than eighteen and younger at the same time. Micah wore trunks and a Heat jersey and carried the speaker like it mattered. Delroy took the keys before anybody could ask whether the toll was worth it. He needed momentum more than permission.

The car smelled faintly of old vinyl, Armor All, and the fries Micah had eaten in it three weeks earlier. The air-conditioning pushed out a sour breath for a minute and then decided to try. Marcia sat in front with her bag on her lap like she did not trust the day enough to put it down. Tiana and Micah got in the back without touching shoulders.

"Nobody brought cards?" Micah asked as Delroy pulled away from Little River.

"You're going to a beach, not prison," Tiana said.

"Same heat. More gulls."

Delroy kept his face on the road and let them talk. Houses gave way to wider streets, then to the causeway where the city opened like a fist. Water flashed on both sides, gray-blue first, then brighter where the sun struck clean. The air through the vents changed. Even inside the car he could smell salt and something green and tidal under it, the sour-sweet rot of sea grass baking back to life.

Micah rolled his window down the last inch. "Damn."

It was only that one word, but Delroy felt the room of the car loosen around it.

Tiana leaned her head against the glass and watched the sailboats out by the basin. Marcia did not say anything for nearly ten minutes, which from her felt as close to surrender as he was likely to get.

At the gate Delroy paid the fee before anybody could ask if he was sure. The ranger handed him a receipt limp with humidity. Virginia Key opened ahead in green and blue layers: sea grape, palms, a strip of white glare where the beach began, and beyond that water moving like something large and asleep. Even the parking lot felt different from home. At home, heat pressed down from walls and engine blocks and other people's breath. Here the wind kept passing over them. Nobody had earned it. Nobody could own it.

Micah was out of the car first. Tiana came slower, then stopped when the wind hit her bare legs under the cover shirt and lifted the hair off her neck. Delroy saw her shoulders loosen before her face did.

He took the cooler and hated the way his shoulder objected. The sand started hot and then gave way under his feet, loose and cooler a few inches down. He set up under a patch of shade cast by a crooked sea grape. Marcia spread the towels with the same capable irritation she brought to everything that still needed doing. Tiana shook out the chairs. Micah had already kicked off his slides and run to the waterline.

"Don't drown before we finish setting up," Marcia called.

"If I do, tell school I was absent for a legitimate reason."

"I'll tell them you were foolish in public."

Delroy laughed. Marcia did not quite, but the corner of her mouth gave a little.

He stripped down to his trunks and went toward the water, rolling his sore shoulder once before he pretended it did not matter. The first wash around his ankles was warm on top and cool below, and then another wave came high enough to slap his calves and bring a thank-you up behind his teeth. He let the salt take it. By the time he reached his waist, the sea had gone cool under the skin of heat. Salt held him up where gravity at home always seemed to remember exactly how much he weighed. He dove forward, came up slick and blinking, ears full, nose burning, sun hard on the back of his neck.

Micah splashed him from the left. "Old man, race you to the buoy."

"You want me to embarrass you in front of these tourists?"

"You already embarrassing me in front of everybody."

They went anyway, not to the buoy because it was farther than Micah had judged, but out to where the water turned from clear green to bottle-dark and the bottom went uncertain. Delroy let the boy win by half an arm and both of them knew it. Micah came up laughing and sputtering.

"You cheated."

"By being stronger? That's not cheating. That's genetics."

"That's delusion."

When they came back in, Tiana was at the edge of the surf with her shorts rolled and her phone held up uselessly against the glare. Micah lunged and flicked water at her knees. She yelped and jumped back.

"Micah!"

"What? Beach baptized you."

"I'll actually drown you."

"In twelve inches of water? You'd have to work harder than that."

He did it again and this time she went after him, half mad, half laughing before she remembered she was not supposed to laugh so easily in this family anymore. The sound came out anyway. She grabbed the speaker from the towel pile and held it overhead while he chased her, yelling about his music. Then she buried one of his slides ankle-deep in wet sand and stood there grinning when he swore.

Delroy stood chest-deep and watched his children become briefly, miraculously, themselves in a way that had nothing to do with crisis. Micah hopping on one foot, Tiana refusing to hand over the slide until he admitted she was smarter than him, both of them brown and sunlit and still young enough to play at war without any official paperwork behind it. It hurt him, how much he wanted to freeze that minute. It also made him ashamed. Wanting one good minute did not erase the things he had done to deserve the bad ones.

Marcia came down at last with her sandals in hand. She did not step all the way in, only stood where the water licked over her toes and around her ankles. The wind pushed her shirt against her body. She closed her eyes once when a cooler wave climbed her shins. Delroy saw the exact second the relief reached her. Her shoulders, always carrying more house than anyone thanked her for, dropped maybe half an inch.

"Cold?" he asked.

"Good," she said, and that one word had more peace in it than anything he had heard from her in days.

They ate late enough that the chicken had gone only warm, not hot, and nobody cared. Grease slicked the foil. Sand got into the mango and they ate it anyway, sweet and fibrous, sucking it from their fingers. Micah made a disgusted face at the orange soda and drank two cans. Tiana sat with her knees up and let the sun dry salt on her shins in pale maps. Marcia finally set her bag beside the cooler instead of in her lap.
Before she took her second piece of chicken, Delroy saw her count what was left with one quick glance. Tomorrow was already on the sand with them, sitting by the cooler with its hand out.

Delroy started telling some story about a beach in Saint Ann when he was a boy, the road there half pothole, half promise, and Micah said, "Every story from Jamaica sounds made up."

"That's because Miami doesn't know how to be beautiful without charging parking."

"They charged parking here too," Tiana said.

"There she go," Micah said. "Miss Fine Print."

"Somebody has to read the receipt."

"You read receipts for fun. That's your kink."

Tiana laughed so abruptly she had to cover it with her hand. "Please don't ever use that word around Mummy again."

"Around Mummy?" Micah sat up and widened his eyes. "Interesting phrasing."

Tiana threw a wad of napkin at him. He ducked and let out that foolish high cackle he used to have before his voice dropped. Delroy laughed too hard, partly because it was funny and partly because he had not heard them bounce off each other like this since before the school hearing.

Marcia looked from one to the other and shook her head. "All two of you need is water and bad sugar."

"And excellent parenting," Delroy said.

"Don't push your luck."

He held up both hands.

It was when the afternoon had thinned into that drowsy, expensive-feeling hour, everybody salted and slower and almost fooled into believing the day had taken, that Marcia's phone started buzzing inside her bag.

She frowned before she even saw the screen. Work never called with good news on a day off. Delroy watched her wipe chicken grease on a napkin, dig the phone out through towel sand, and tip the screen against the glare. Elena's name flashed white across the glass.

"It's Elena," she said.

Marcia's fingers cinched around the phone. Out in the shallows Micah stopped kicking at the foam and turned. Tiana set her soda down so carefully the can gave only one thin click against the cooler lid.

She got up with sand stuck to the back of one thigh and walked a little away from the towels out of habit. Work calls always pulled a person a few feet out of family life. Delroy saw her turn her back to the water and press two fingers into the free hand she kept at her side.

"Hello?"

He could hear only Marcia's side at first. The wind kept stealing half the words.

"Yes... Yes, this is a good time." A pause. "Oh."

Just that. Oh. Small enough that if he had not been watching her face he might have missed the way it emptied.

She listened. The children quieted without being told. Even Micah knew the difference between ordinary interruption and the kind that made grown people straighten.

Elena's voice crackled tinny from the phone when the wind shifted. It was clipped and controlled, the same kind of voice that wore linen in grief and still found the right envelope. Delroy had met her only twice, but he remembered the way she never wasted a syllable even when thanking somebody. He caught fragments now: "this morning," "hospice nurse," "very peaceful," "wanted to let you know myself."

Marcia said, "Was anybody with her?"

Another pause.

"I see."

Her eyes went to the water then, not looking at it so much as through it. Delroy got up and walked toward her, not too close. He did not want to arrive like a man claiming rights he had misplaced.

Elena kept speaking. Delroy heard the rustle of what might have been paper on the other end, or maybe one of those thin expensive bracelets women wore because they never washed dishes in them.

"No," Marcia said. "No, I understand. Thank you for calling me."

Then, after another beat, and quieter, "Yes. If you can do that, I'd appreciate it."

She listened one last time, said goodbye, and lowered the phone. A thin wash of water reached her feet and dragged back, leaving wet sand pasted over her toes before she put the phone in the bag.

"Marcia?"

She looked at him with the blank surprise of somebody remembering he was attached to the day.

"Celia dead," she said.

He had known before she said it. Still, the words landed heavy. The old woman had been dying for months, maybe longer. That did not make the sentence lighter. He pictured the little tight mouth, the dyed hair Marcia complained about fixing, the way Celia had once told him in her front yard that Cuban coffee in Miami was getting worse because everybody had become lazy. He had liked her for that, though liking was too big a word for what employers and husbands of workers mostly did with each other.

"Lord," he said, because there was nothing smarter ready.

Marcia laughed once, ugly and brief. "Don't start with Lord."

"I wasn't preaching."

"Good. Don't."

She moved past him toward a crooked sign near the dunes and sat on the low wooden border around it. Delroy stayed standing until she looked up like she would tolerate him there if he did not say anything foolish. Then he sat a little apart, sand grinding under his thighs.

Out by the towels, Tiana had one arm around her knees. Micah stood in the shallows, looking at them and then away, old enough now to know when to keep his distance but not old enough to hide how badly he wanted instructions.

Marcia took the phone out again, looked at the blank screen, locked it, unlocked it, then put it back.

"She went this morning," she said. "Elena said the nurse was there. Elena was on the way. By the time she reached, they had already cleaned up half the room."

Delroy waited.

"She hated people seeing her mouth open when she slept." Marcia stared at the water, trying to rebuild the room in her head with the bed already stripped halfway bare. "Always saying, Marcia, don't let me lie there looking common."

The imitation brought the old woman up whole: sharp, painted, annoyed even by her own body failing.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"I know."

She rubbed her thumb hard over the side of her forefinger, the way she did when soap or bleach had dried her skin raw. "You know what Elena said after? She said she'd make sure the agency processed my remaining hours and somebody would put my scrub jacket aside for me."

There was no self-pity in her voice. That made it worse.

"That woman," Delroy started.

"No." Marcia shook her head. "She wasn't cruel. That's the ugly part. She was trying to do correct. She said she knew how much I cared for her mother. She said Mama always asked if Marcia was coming. She said it all decent."

She looked down at her own hands. The knuckles were a little swollen from work and bleach and carrying everybody.

"But that's how it goes. You wash somebody's backside six days a week. You trim their nails when the daughter can't bear to look too long at the yellowing. You know which swallow means pills going down and which one means they tuck it under the tongue to spit out later. You know where the good bras are and which nightgown will make them feel less dead before the dead part arrives. Then they die, and by afternoon you're payroll again."

The wind pressed her shirt flat. He could smell salt and sunscreen and, faint under it, the fried chicken grease still on their fingers.

"Marcia."

"Celia used to cuss me if I put the pale lipstick on her. She wanted coral. Coral, in that bed, with the oxygen rubbing her cheek raw. Stubborn old woman." Her mouth trembled and hardened around it. "And she had money enough to be vain all the way down. Good for her. I mean that. Good for her. But don't tell me not to count the work in it too. I'm not one of those people who can afford grief with no arithmetic."

He put his hand near hers first, not on it. When she did not move away, he rested it lightly over her knuckles. Her skin felt hot from the sun.

"You don't have to split them apart," he said.

"Have to? I don't have to. It just comes together." She breathed in through her nose. "I liked her. That's the stupid thing rich people count on. They let you in near enough to care and not near enough to belong. That old woman knew my children's names. She knew when my shoulder was acting up before you knew. She knew if I had been crying because my face went shiny right here." Marcia touched the crease beside her nose. "She also paid me because I changed her diapers. Both are true."

Delroy looked out toward the water where Tiana had gotten up and was now pretending to rearrange the towels. The beach around them kept going. Somebody's speaker farther down was playing old dancehall. A little boy ran past with a plastic shovel. An airplane moved low over the bay, caught the sun silver, then went ordinary again. It offended him, how the world could keep its same volume.

"Do you want to go there?" he asked.

"Now? For what? To stand in the back with the staff and watch the family cry where the furniture is too expensive for my wet clothes?" She shook her head. "Maybe tomorrow. Maybe if Elena asks. Today I only know that tomorrow morning my bus route to Coral Gables belongs to nobody."

He heard the job loss inside that more clearly than if she had said wages. He also heard the part underneath it: the vanished routine, the woman whose body had structured Marcia's days, the house where she had been both invisible and necessary.

"We'll figure the work," he said.

Marcia turned to him then, not angry, which almost hurt more.

"With what?" she asked. "Tell me with what, Delroy. I'm not trying to insult you. I'm asking with what."

He had no answer that did not sound like his old tricks wearing a fresh shirt.

"All right," she said after a second, because she had seen the emptiness in his face. "Leave it."

That was worse than blame. Blame still expected something from him.

Back at the towels, Micah asked, "Mummy all right?" in a voice too casual to be real.

"Celia died," Tiana said before Delroy could answer, her voice flat and exact. "The woman she works with."

Micah's expression changed. Delroy watched the boy make the adjustment from not understanding to understanding enough. "Damn," he said softly.

Marcia picked up the cooler handle. "Pack up."

Nobody argued. The day folded fast after that, chairs collapsing, wet towels slapping sand, sunscreen bottle rolling under the seat. Delroy loaded the trunk while his shoulder burned cleanly now, no longer warning but statement. When he shut the trunk he looked once at the water. The relief was still there for other people. It had not lied. It had only been temporary, which was not the same thing.

The drive home narrowed to the blinker, Micah's sleep whistle against the window, and the hiss of tires over the bridge. Tiana watched the causeway go backward and kept pressing her thumbnail into the damp label on her soda can until it peeled up in tiny curls. Marcia held Celia's house key in her fist all the way to Little River. Delroy drove and felt the old appetite rise in him, the stupid masculine hunger to do something hard enough that maybe the room would look different afterward.

At home Marcia put the cooler in the sink and stood there longer than washing required. Then she changed clothes without shutting the bathroom fully and came out in a dry skirt and blouse, pulling on the version of herself that still knew how to think in straight lines.

"I'm going to call the agency in the morning," she said to no one and everyone. "And I need to see if Elena wants me there tomorrow."

Tiana asked, "Do you want me to come with you?"

Marcia shook her head. "Not unless I say so."

Micah leaned against the fridge. "That the old lady who used to send the Cuban crackers?"

Marcia's mouth moved. "Yes."

"She was funny."

"She was difficult."

"Funny too."

Marcia sat down at the table with the house key, a packet of tissues from her bag, and her phone. Delroy saw her looking not at the screen but at the nick in the brass key where years of being shoved into the same front lock had shaved it thin. He knew that look. She was not crying yet because crying took privacy and the apartment charged rent for every inch of it.

He could not help in there. Not with words. Not with touch. Not with promises, which had become a kind of debt in his mouth.

"I'm going out for a minute," he said.

Marcia did not ask where. That stung too.

Outside, the evening had fallen into that Miami half-dark where the air stayed warm and damp enough to wear. Somebody two houses over was frying fish. Bass thudded from a passing car and went away. Delroy stood by the fence first, hand on his hip, and stared at nothing until doing nothing started to feel like a sin.

He took out his phone and called Claude.

Claude picked up on the fourth ring with domino noise in the background, hard tiles kissing harder tile, men arguing over points as if numbers had mothers.

"Star," Claude said. "You remember I exist now?"

Claude always sounded like he had just bitten into a joke and expected the rest of the room to do its share. Delroy pictured him exactly: cap pushed back, eyes too knowing, that little crescent scar beside his mouth whitening when he grinned.

"I remember debt regular enough."

"Good. Debt likes attention. What you need?"

Delroy looked back at the lit square of his own window. Tiana's shadow crossed it, then Micah's.

"That demolition work," he said. "You still have space?"

Claude went quiet long enough to make the dominoes behind him seem louder.

"I got space," he said. "Question is if you have shoulder."

Delroy rolled the arm once and felt the catch like a rusted hinge.

"I got enough."

"Enough for a real site or enough for talk?"

"Claude."

"No, I'm asking proper. This is not patching drywall for some white woman with a yappy dog. This is dust, stairs, concrete, men young enough to believe their knees love them. You come, your four hundred starts paying back from that same day. If you break, you break on your own time."

There it was. Friendship itemized, same as always. Delroy almost loved him for the honesty.

"When?"

"Monday. Five-thirty. Old medical building off Twenty-Seventh. Bring gloves if you have them. Bring boots if you still own a pair worth anything. And don't come with no heroic foolishness. Foreman don't pay for style."

Monday. Too soon to heal anything. Soon enough to look like movement.

"I'll be there."

"You sure?"

Delroy listened to the house behind him, the muffled clink of Marcia setting a glass on the table, Micah laughing once at something Tiana said, the small sounds of people still living inside trouble because stopping was not on offer.

"I'm sure."

Claude exhaled through his teeth. "All right then. If you disappear, don't make me come collect in front of your woman. I hate domestic theater."

"You love domestic theater."

"Only when I'm not in it. Monday, star."

The line clicked off.

Delroy stayed by the fence a minute longer with the phone warm in his hand. His shoulder had started to throb from the day, from the cooler, from swimming harder than he should have because for half an hour the water had made him feel younger than the truth. He lifted the arm once to test it and pain ran from the joint down to the elbow bright as a wire. He let it drop.

Claude would start taking the four hundred back the minute Delroy showed up Monday. It would be dust, stairs, concrete, and men young enough to trust their knees. If the shoulder tore all the way, it would tear on Delroy's own time, for free. He stood there and took the full shape of that before he put the phone away.

Inside, Marcia had turned on the small lamp by the table. The apartment looked almost gentle from the yard, which was another lie buildings told. Delroy went back in.

Micah was in the shower. Tiana was drying the cooler with a dish towel. Tiana's blue document folder sat under the edge of the rice tin to keep it away from Micah's elbows, one split corner lifting in the fan. Marcia sat with Celia's key beside her hand and a notepad pulled close, already writing columns: agency, rent, landlord, church sister who knew somebody at Jackson, numbers turned into a ladder because she did not know any other way to climb. When she stopped, it was only to press the heel of her hand against her eyes before the pen went down again.

He filled a glass from the tap and set it by her elbow without interrupting her math. He did not tell her about Monday, or Claude taking his cut from the first hour, or the old medical building off Twenty-Seventh, or the way his shoulder had already begun to spit heat down his arm.

Delroy went to the closet and pulled out his old work boots. The leather was cracked white at the bend and one lace had been knotted back together three times. He set them by the door with the toes turned toward the wall.

Marcia glanced at the boots, then at him.

"You found something?"

He kept his face plain. "Maybe."

Her eyes held on him for a beat, measuring whether maybe was worth the space it occupied. Then she went back to the page.

"Wake me if you leave early," she said.

"I'll try."

That too might have been a lie. He had not decided yet.

He sat on the edge of the bed and listened to the shower run, to Tiana humming under her breath without knowing she was doing it, to Marcia turning pages in the notepad, hunting the first number that would finally behave. Salt dried on his skin. Sand scratched the backs of his knees. His shoulder pulsed with its own weather.

By the door the boots looked meaner than he remembered. He left them there, set the alarm for 4:18, and put the phone face down in the dark.
