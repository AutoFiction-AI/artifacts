# Chapter 7

The Grinlow Leaves did not change all at once. Sleep did not help. For three nights after Amycus Carrow had turned the punishment dais into a lesson in the Hall and Owen had answered with the condition of contact instead of any proper spell, the corridor working kept ringing in his palm whenever he shut his eyes. On the third, a page near the centre of the manuscript began to take ink. At first he thought it was soot from the common-room fire. Then the marks persisted when he shifted the book into candlelight, pale brown lines rising through the paper as if they had been waiting lower down and his stupidity in the corridor had driven them upward. By supper the page held a route under the kitchens, a cluster of founder marks he could now half follow, and one repeated instruction that his mind kept translating into the same plain phrase: ward scar sufficient. East Undercroft answering.

He shut the book twice and opened it again. His right palm, the one the coffer had branded in September, ached against the cloth cover. His wand had been unreliable since the greenhouse and worse since Martha, if he was honest, since Amycus had made her stand there and he had let the whole room watch him fail in public. The castle had answered too. For hours after the punishment the walls had seemed to ring through his feet. Even now, in certain corridors, a dull pressure climbed through the bones behind his face as if somebody far below was tapping stone with a metal spoon.

By half eight he gave up pretending he could wait for Prue or think sensibly alone. The page wanted a place, and the only person who had ever hinted that the kitchens ran deeper than pipes and coal was Tallow.

The kitchens were nearly finished for the night. Heat breathed out of them in thick wet gusts that smelled of onions, soap, singed pastry, and the heavier stew intended for tomorrow's lunch. Tallow stood at the scrub table beneath the hanging copper pans, polishing pudding spoons one by one with a square of old flannel and rejecting any that showed the smallest twist. He put the bent ones to his left in a separate little line, as if they had disappointed him personally.

Owen waited until the nearest pair of elves disappeared into the scullery with a cauldron between them. "Tallow."

Tallow did not look up. "If young sir wants treacle crusts, there are two under the blue cloth and he may take only one, because greed makes crumbs."

"I don't want crusts."

"That is already an alarming start."

Owen set the wrapped manuscript on the table between the straight spoons and the bent ones. "I need the east-side maintenance run. The older one. Under this level."

At that Tallow's hand stopped. The flannel stayed clamped round the spoon bowl. He lifted his eyes at last, and the skin around them tightened before anything else in his face did. "No," he said. "Young sir needs sleep, and perhaps better judgement if there is any left."

"It's in the Leaves." Owen pulled the cloth back just enough to show the new page. "This wasn't visible before. Not until after the corridor."

Tallow stared without touching. His damp hands hovered an inch above the paper and then retreated into the flannel. "Yes," he said softly. "The house heard about the corridor. Every knife in the wash-rack sang. The tureens knocked their lids off. That doesn't make it a place for students."

"It means the site answered."

"It means you rang something old and stupidly loud."

Owen felt the correction arrive in him like a click. "Not loud, exactly. Specific. I think the scar matched whatever threshold this uses. It says sufficient." He tapped the founder marks with one ink-stained finger. "Or near enough. I need to know whether the larger claim is real."

Tallow set the spoon down with deliberate care. "Young sir keeps saying larger claim as if that is tidier than saying what he means. The old work hurts my kind. The deeper routes hurt worse. There are passages house-elves use and passages we know better than to use. This is one of the second sort."

"I know."

"No, you don't. Knowing a word for pain isn't the same as having it behind the eyes."

Owen nearly answered too quickly and stopped himself. "Fair. Sorry. I know I don't know it the same way. But the page changed now, not in September, not after the greenhouse. Now. If the disturbance has opened the undercroft, then either the manuscript's true and there's something under the castle worth hiding, or I've burnt my hand and half broken my wand for a very elaborate lie. I need to see which."

Tallow made a small sound in his throat that might have been contempt or worry. He looked past Owen at the banked ovens, at the stacked plates, at the ceiling as if the school above them had become heavy enough to notice. "There are rules," he said.

Owen waited.

"There's no wandlight in the run. The old seams don't care for school spells. There's no touching side marks unless I say. If the house gives warning through the floor, we stop. If I say back, we go back. You won't argue just because boys who love books think pain is an opinion."

"I won't."

Tallow's mouth pulled sideways. "That answer would be prettier if I believed it." He folded the flannel, slid it into his apron, and swept the rejected spoons into a drawer with more irritation than force. "Come on, then. Before I remember sense."

He led Owen past the main ovens, through the buttery, and into a root store where sacks of potatoes leaned against the wall in soft collapsing towers. Tallow paused long enough to straighten a crate of onions whose corner had tipped into damp. Only after he had wedged a folded rag under it and nodded to himself did he tug aside an empty flour bin and uncover a squat iron door low in the stone.

The hinge made no sound when he opened it. Cold came out instead, thick and mineral. Owen crouched, then had to fold lower still as the passage dropped away under a barrel curve of stone no full-grown adult wizard would have chosen willingly. The grease lamp Tallow carried threw a short amber circle that moved over wet mortar, old pipe clamps, and occasional founder marks cut into the wall like maintenance instructions left for hands smaller than his. The air narrowed as they went. Kitchen noise sank into the stone behind them until all that remained was their own movement, Owen's sleeves brushing grit, Tallow's bare feet placing and replacing themselves with careful precision.

After twenty yards the pressure started in earnest. The roots of Owen's teeth hummed with the same thin metallic misery as biting hard on tinfoil and never being allowed to let go. His right palm grew hot through the bandage. He kept one hand on the floor for balance and felt, through cold slime and grit, a faint recurrent thud as if something far off were hitting the castle with the side of a fist.

Tallow slowed. Owen heard his breathing change.

"Are you all right?"

"Tallow is walking," he said, which was not an answer. Then, plainer, "Don't ask again unless you mean to turn round for the answer."

The passage bent east. Twice it narrowed enough that Owen had to put the manuscript ahead of him and worm after it on one shoulder. Dry heat ran through the wall here, locked in the stone and almost feverish under the damp. At one bend he saw old broom wear on the floor, centuries of slight curved scratches where something had once been dragged regularly through this route. Secrecy was only the by-product, he thought. The route existed for service and upkeep. Whatever lay below had needed mops and buckets and hands.

That was what steadied him more than any amount of diagram. Hidden things in stories were always altars, tombs, prophecies. This place smelled of wet lime and old soot and of things kept working by people no one had thanked properly in several hundred years.

The passage opened just enough for him to straighten at the waist. Ahead, the lamp found a wall that was too smooth to be natural and too featureless to be a door unless one already knew it was there. The founder marks on his page matched the shallow cuts round its edge exactly. At the center sat a thumb-deep hollow lined with the same dull white metal that threaded certain pages of the Leaves when the light caught them wrong.

Tallow had stopped several feet back. His singed ears were pressed flat. "Quickly, if it's going to open."

Owen wedged the grease lamp on a stone nub and peeled the cloth off his right hand. The old burn across his palm had faded from blister-red to a stubborn brown gloss, but near the life line a fresher mark pulsed in sensation alone, the place where the corridor working had rung back through him. When he pressed his hand into the hollow the metal went hot at once, a hard warning heat that stopped short of pain and still told him he had been expected.

Something shifted inside the wall. Pins withdrew one after another with small dense knocks. The seam breathed out dust and colder air. Then the slab rolled inward on hidden weight.

The East Undercroft met him as a service room built for weight. The chamber ran roughly circular and high enough to lose its ceiling in soot-dark shadow. Drains and service channels divided the floor into walkable ribs. Benches of black-green stone stood under two walls with bowls, clamps, and dead lamps left in deliberate rows. Iron rings had been sunk into one section of floor for hauling or bracing. Shelves, long emptied, still held clean tool-shapes in the dust. The place smelled of cold metal, old lime, and labour gone dormant rather than forgotten.

Owen moved toward the sloped gleam on the far wall.

Tallow caught his sleeve. "Dry stone only. Step where I step." He pointed with two cramped fingers toward the channels cut through the floor. "If those start silvering, we're finished. And stone first. The house tells on the stone before it tells on the chart."

Owen bit back the impatient answer and followed the line Tallow marked. At the centre rose the anchor stone.

It came out of the floor to just below Owen's chest, a faceted mass of green-black rock the size of a coal cart and too exact in its geometry to belong to any quarry he knew. Four broader bands entered the floor channels like buried conduits and ran under the walls toward the cardinal directions. Finger-width veins of silver and the same dull white metal threaded the block in ruled circuits, dipping below the surface and surfacing again. One face carried two generations of repair: old dark staples buried under a later skin of leaded sealant, founder cuts recarved over the top where a crack had split the first geometry. Apparatus, then. Damaged, patched, still carrying load.

Sweat had darkened the soot at Tallow's temples. "East face," he said. "There. That's where your corridor note came through."

Owen crouched. A section no bigger than a school slate had opened into hairline fractures across the white metal. Founder scoring veered round the damage with the ugly stubbornness of later repair. He put a finger out on instinct.

Tallow's hand snapped between him and the crack. "Look with your eyes."

"I'm only looking."

"Do more of it."

The rebuke steadied him better than agreement would have done. He circled the stone more carefully, reading what he could. Some facets carried conditions he recognized from the Leaves: masking load, transfer limits, thresholds for signal suppression. Others packed themselves too densely for him to parse, but even those read as work rather than ornament. At the damaged eastern facet the truth of the corridor settled in his stomach with a physical drop. He had struck an actual piece of the system.

The far wall carried the table the manuscript had failed to render until now. A shallow bronze slab jutted from the masonry on thick fixed supports, sloped for standing work. Silver points and threaded arcs crossed its surface around a pale central disc washed to the colour of bone seen through water. Tiny black inserts ringed the outer edge. Everything about it belonged to calibration: sight-lines, pressures, reference points, the marks left by people keeping a burden in trim.

Owen stepped toward the disc.

Tallow made a raw little sound. "Edge, young sir. Use the lip. The middle wakes hot."

When Owen laid his unbandaged hand against the bronze rim, the silver inlays sharpened under the skin of the metal. A cluster northwest of the central disc brightened into the same geometry as the cracked facet behind him. Hogwarts sat there by angle and repeated founder mark. More points answered in a broken ring, most burning low, two guttering almost out. For one foolish second he thought he was looking at Britain drawn badly. Then one silver arc ran cleanly off the right edge of the slab and picked itself up again on the lower curve with the same notch mark, the sort of cheat only round things needed when forced flat.

He dragged the Leaves half out of his pocket with his free hand and nearly dropped them. The newly surfaced gloss page carried the same paired sign at four quarter marks around a wash of ink the page had labelled world-cover rather than local load. He looked back to the table. Two faint scored bands crossed the pale disc north to south and east to west, rubbed almost out by old use.

He heard himself laugh once under his breath, not from amusement but because his body had chosen the wrong sound for shock. "It's true."

Tallow looked up sharply. "Which part?"

"Hogwarts," Owen said, touching the bright cluster with one knuckle. "And others. Not county by county. It's wrapping the whole thing."

"Whole what?"

Owen swallowed. "The planet, I think. Or near enough that arguing about the word would be stupid."

Tallow looked from the stone to the table with the expression of a cook discovering that the cracked range under his hands also held up the whole house. "Literal is often the rude option."

Along the lower lip ran three neat columns of marks, finer than the load notations and grouped in repeated sets. Owen knew some from the newly surfaced gloss pages: capacity, interval, transit, after-remnant load. He read them once and got stores. Crates, perhaps. Replacement stock. Tools meant for the benches and clamps behind him.

"No," Tallow said from the doorway, squinting. "Out-marks. Same thinking as stores and laundry lifts. Send, count what's left, seal what's empty."

Owen read them again and disliked how quickly that corrected him. One recurring sign he had taken for vessel sat beside the same tally stroke the kitchens used for heads at table. Not crates. Occupants. He tried to make the stepping numbers into rationing, plague loss, anything messier and less deliberate than departure. The entries refused him. Each reduction paired with the outward mark and a maintenance note about masking load after transfer. The last lines were cleaner than the rest: closure, decommission, planned departure. The kind of ugly practical record left when a household moved and meant the old rooms to stay shut behind it.

He flattened his fingertips to the metal and forced himself slower. "They left on purpose," he said. "The Meridian Commonwealth. They shifted the population and left the ward running. They meant to go."

"Left what behind?" Tallow asked.

Owen turned his head but not enough to lose the lines. "Us, apparently."

The lamp flame bent sideways though there was no draft. Along the nearest drain a pale thread appeared and crept through the cut stone bright as molten pewter. Tallow saw it and went still.

"Back," he said.

Owen lifted his hand from the bronze. Then the black inserts at the rim answered one by one, not all of them, only the ones aligned with the bright Hogwarts cluster and two guttering points farther round the disc. The manuscript page in his pocket gave a hot twitch against his ribs. He stopped halfway to the door and turned back.

"Young sir."

"Wait."

He dragged the Leaves out fully and laid them against the lip. The first margin line nearly sent him the wrong way. Recurrent convergence after high discharge could have meant weather, recoil, ordinary load seeking balance. Then the next cluster repeated a hunter's hook from a damaged gloss he had only half understood, used there for pursuit conditions rather than repair. Not convergence, then. Predatory convergence. Reacquisition risk.

The coldness of it frightened him more than any prophecy would have done. Beyond the world disc the silver arcs frayed and gathered at the same worn places, metal thinned almost white where attention kept finding brightness. Whatever noticed those flares did not need a face or a creed. Pattern was enough. Motion was enough.

"If I'm reading it right," Owen said, not looking away, "a flare doesn't just leak out. Something finds it again."

Above them, thickened by stone, something heavy struck in the castle proper. A door slammed, or Amycus hit a boy into a wall, or a suit of armour toppled in one of the corridors. The undercroft returned it as a body-blow through the floor. Owen flinched. Tallow closed his eyes for one second too long.

"School again," he said.

The word pulled the school back into Owen whole: Martha swallowing blood because Amycus was watching, Neville counting who still stood, boys learning to keep eating through a scream two passages away. None of that had eased. Above them somebody was still being hurt, and if the school ever lit up properly the table was saying hurt would not stay local.

The pale thread in the drain split and ran down a second channel. Tallow's free hand clamped the jamb so hard the knuckles blanched under soot.

"Back," he said again, harsher now.

This time Owen obeyed. He scooped the Leaves to his chest, but not before fixing the outer ring in place: three whitening marks, one of them Hogwarts, and the warning written like maintenance, not fear.

He stepped away from the table and back toward the centre stone. Up close, on the cracked eastern facet, he could see where some older repair had failed and been reworked by less skilled hands. A smear of founder-era tool scoring wandered off true by less than the width of a quill. The whole planet might presently depend on margins like that.

He rested his burned palm lightly against the anchor stone. Heat answered from deep inside it, stored strain climbing his arm to the shoulder socket. He didn't know how to repair it yet. He barely knew how to read it. But disbelief had ended here. The damage was real, the work older than ministers and Dark Lords, and somewhere above them Amycus was still teaching children with pain.

When he finally turned away, he took the image of the table with him so hard it hurt: the world at the centre of a maintenance chart, the dim nodes, the whitening marks outside the mask where notice gathered whenever magic flared too brightly. The castle overhead groaned once more, ordinary violence or ordinary panic carried down into the stone, and he carried both sounds with him.

Tallow sealed the door behind them with both hands and a muttered string of house marks Owen could not follow. By the time they had crawled halfway back to the kitchens, the pressure behind Owen's eyes had eased just enough for thought. He knew he should be sick with fear. Instead he felt the colder, steadier thing that had replaced fear whenever the manuscript stopped teasing and started proving.

At the flour-bin entrance Tallow paused to set the iron panel back into its slot and wipe his palms on his apron as if he could remove the undercroft by ordinary kitchen means. "You won't bring a crowd next time," he said.

"There won't be a crowd."

"No. I thought not."

"Young sir," Tallow said, quieter now, "whatever that stone is, the house has been carrying it a very long time. Mind how you use what it gives you."

Owen nodded. Beyond the root store the kitchen clocks ticked, pans cooled, and somebody in the scullery dropped a ladle with a familiar domestic clang. Then, from higher in the passage, came the blunt scrape of feet on stone and a boy's voice saying, too fast, "Hospital wing. Move."

Owen looked up. The sound held there for a second, plain as an opened door.

He set the wrapped manuscript on the flour-bin lid instead, pulled a nub of pencil from his pocket, and took the few seconds needed to sketch the outer ring before the whitening points could blur.
