# Chapter 4

In the little Hufflepuff study alcove off the cellar corridor, Owen spent most of Tuesday proving that the pages would not do it for his left hand.

They behaved themselves for the left hand. They stayed as they had been since the shelf-chase: mottled vellum, old watermarks, diagrams that began cleanly and then gave up halfway across the leaf as if the scribe had been dragged from the table by the collar. When he turned them with his unburned fingers, they were only damaged and maddening and older than anyone had a right to hide in a school.

With the right hand they stopped pretending.

The pipes knocked there and the sound covered page-turning. It smelled of coal dust, wet wool, and the onion tart someone had sneaked down from supper. The round window near the ceiling let in a disc of late afternoon light the colour of old brass. He had his ash-linen notebook open, three nibs laid out in a row, and the Grinlow Leaves propped on a folded towel so the damp from the stone table would not creep up into them.

He rested the heel of his burned palm on the lower margin of a page that had shown him nothing useful all week.

The mark in his skin heated at once. It was sharper than the ordinary smart of a healing burn and deeper than the tug it gave him when he gripped a door handle too hard: a hot, exact line as if someone on the other side of the page were tracing his palm with a blunt needle. The vellum darkened under his hand. Then pale notation rose through it in slow threads, not ink exactly but something like bruising surfacing under thin skin.

He took his hand away. The page went blank.

He put it back. The buried writing came up again, line after line, as patient as frost.

For a moment he only stared. What he felt first was not triumph. It was a sour, almost childish resentment. He had taken the thing to Neville, endured a roomful of people looking at him as if he were trying to dodge real courage by inventing larger, cleverer problems, and all the while the manuscript had been keeping its mouth shut because he had been touching it wrong.

Prue Hollis's voice came back to him whether he wanted it or not. Which edition had that footnote been in, then?

He pressed harder, because annoyance was as good a reason as any to keep moving. More text appeared in a cramped side hand around the diagram already faintly visible on the page: a dead stem laid across a ruled frame, three bracketed marks at either end, and a note in glossed runes he could only half parse. His reading of runes had never been special. He was decent at schoolwork because he hated getting things fractionally wrong in public, which was not the same as being gifted. But the margin notes were giving him more than the runes alone had. The burn did not merely reveal them. It seemed to supply a sort of pressure, a suggestion of how the marks leaned.

He copied as fast as he could before they sank back.

`least exercise for opened admission`, he wrote, then crossed out `opened` and tried `admissive opening`, then swore under his breath because neither phrase sounded like anything. Another gloss gave him `spent runner only`. Another, more useful, gave `fixed frame required`. A third resolved after several tries into `ask the frame, not the fibre`.

That one made him sit back.

The ordinary logic of school magic was target, intention, execution. You aimed at the thing you wanted altered. Even when the spell behaved badly, it behaved badly at the thing. The manuscript was not describing aim. It was describing terms.

He put his palm down again until the heat sharpened and the next line lifted out. Half of it remained obstinately absent, the lower right of the page blank no matter how long he held on. What he had was enough to be dangerous and not enough to be safe:

`For the least amendment use dead vegetal matter in a fixed frame. Let admission be asked by bound marks. Do not--`

Nothing after that. The line simply ended in clean vellum.

He tried another page. Blank. Another. A table of symbols rose, then a tiny sketch of hinges beside a note too rubbed to carry. Hinges again. He flipped back. The first exercise looked almost insulting in its smallness: dead vine, fixed frame, bound marks. Nothing about blood. Nothing about founder stones or planetary wards or the mad larger claim that magic itself was a kind of fencing round the world. If the book meant to seduce him into larger lunacy, it was doing so by way of something that looked very like a classroom practical gone peculiar.

He should have waited.

That was plain enough even as the thought came. The lower note was missing. The page answered only while his hand sat on it like a stamp. He did not know whether the revealed text was complete or whether the burn was forcing partial legibility out of something that expected another kind of access entirely. He should have shown nobody, of course; Neville had solved that question for him. But he should at least have waited another day, copied more, compared pages, found out whether the second diagram matched the first.

Instead he looked at `fixed frame required` and thought of the abandoned greenhouse beyond the working row, the one with the warped ventilators and the potting benches nobody used because the heating charm there ran mean and sour and all the glass wanted replacing. It had old stone footings. It had dead vines in plenty. It had doors and latches he considered, very briefly and very stupidly, part of the virtue of the place. If anything went wrong, what was there to break that did not already look halfway broken?

He shut the manuscript and sat with his hand over the cover until the heat in the burn eased down from pain to warning.

Then he opened his notebook to a clean page and began copying out the exercise properly, in the narrow exact script he used when he wanted a thing to behave.

***

By the time he slipped through the greenhouse door, dusk had already flattened the grounds into blue glass and soot.

The abandoned house sat at the far end of the Herbology row with two panes painted over from inside where something had once spat acid at them. The path was slick from an earlier shower. Owen's shoes left dark chevrons on the flags. He had his satchel under his robe, the Grinlow Leaves wrapped in a pillowcase inside it because he did not trust canvas against rain or himself against panic, and a pocket full of bits he had nicked or borrowed without asking: chalk, two lengths of copper wire, a plant label, a jar lid, and the stump of a candle.

He did not need the candle. The greenhouse held heat like a bad temper.

The moment he shut the door behind him he stepped into damp air thick with old compost and the sweet rot of leaves left too long in trays. The glass overhead had gone cloudy in patches, so the last of the light came through blurred and greenish. One of the side benches had collapsed months ago and been left where it fell. Earthenware pots nested under it like dull skulls. At the back, a run of dead vine still clung to a trellis nailed to the brick pier between two ventilation cranks, all of it crisped brown and silver with dust.

Hot, but neglected. Private, but not tidy. He found this reassuring.

He put his satchel on the least filthy section of bench and took out the notebook first, then the wrapped manuscript. Even alone he felt furtive handling it. The pages gave a faint drag against one another, heavier than paper ought to be. He opened to the copied exercise rather than the original text because he was not enough of an idiot to prop the manuscript in direct potting-shed damp if he could help it.

The procedure, as far as he had it, did not look theatrical. It looked irritating. He had to bind the marks to a fixed frame. He had to use dead vegetal matter, not living tissue and not loose litter. He had to make admission the asked quality rather than the named object. That last phrase still felt wrong in his head. Asked quality. It sounded like something Binns would mumble just before the class died of boredom.

He chose a vine about as thick as his little finger and snapped it free of the trellis. It broke with a dry tick. Under the dust it still held a faint twisted pattern where it had once tried to spiral round wire. He laid it on the flagstone sill under the trellis because the manuscript's drawing had shown a stem crossed by frame lines, and a sill at least had the virtue of being stone and fixed and already part of the greenhouse rather than something portable he might accidentally enchant into the lake.

He drew the first mark with chalk. A bracket, then a second, then three short lines crossing the space between. At either end he wound the copper lightly round the stem and pinned it under the chalk marks with bent bits of label wire. Bound marks. Fixed frame. He even used the jar lid to hold a spoonful of water because one note seemed to suggest the dead thing ought to be reminded what admission had once felt like.

That done, he stopped and looked at the arrangement with the kind of dissatisfaction he usually reserved for nearly-right Arithmancy diagrams. It looked flimsy. It looked like nonsense. It looked, if he were honest, like the sort of thing a desperate person would set up because he could not bear another day of being told to wait.

He almost packed it away.

Then he remembered Neville saying, very evenly, that if Owen had time for manuscripts he had time to carry messages.

That settled it in the worst possible way.

He opened the Grinlow Leaves to the exercise page and laid his right hand against the margin until the hidden gloss surfaced. The heat lanced through his palm hard enough that he hissed. He kept reading.

Ask the frame, not the fibre.

Let admission be asked by bound marks.

Do not--

Still blank.

"Fine," he muttered to the page. "Fine. Small, then."

He set the manuscript back on the bench, crouched over the vine, and rested two fingers of his burned hand lightly on the middle chalk line. No wand movement presented itself. No familiar shape of spell. The notes suggested phrasing mattered, but not the way Latin mattered in charms. More like pinning a question so the room could not wriggle out of it.

He spoke quietly, feeling foolish and not letting that stop him.

"Let what's shut in this open enough to take water."

Nothing.

Of course nothing. He bit down on his irritation, looked back at the copied gloss, and tried again, narrower.

"Ask admission here. In the bound thing. In the frame."

For a second he thought he had failed so completely that even humiliation would have to wait its turn.

Then the dead vine gave a papery sigh.

Its skin split cleanly from end to end. Four dry curls peeled back from the pith like ribbon from a parcel, every inch of it still dead.

Owen leaned closer despite himself.

"Oh," he said.

Behind him, a cupboard latch clicked.

He turned in time to see the potting cabinet under the far bench spring open by an inch. Then another sound came from above, a metal jerk and slam, and both roof ventilators kicked outward together with enough force to shower old paint flakes down the brick pier. Cold evening air plunged into the trapped heat. The candle stump rolled off the bench.

Owen stood up too fast and cracked his shoulder on the trellis.

The greenhouse began opening.

Not everywhere at once. That would almost have been easier to understand. The rule ran the room in a rushing sequence. Drawer. Hasp. Latch. Vent. Door to the little tool lean-to. Lid of the sand barrel. The glazed cold frame at the far wall snapped up hard enough that one of its hinges screamed. Shrivelled seed pods spilled from a split packet and skittered over the flags. His satchel clasp sprang. Then the greenhouse door behind him thudded wide and bounced off its stop.

"No, no, don't," he said, to the room, because there was no single thing left to address.

He lunged for the satchel first. The pillowcase-wrapped manuscript had started sliding out of it, tugged by nothing visible except the fact that the flap no longer believed in closure. He jammed it back with his forearm and reached for his wand.

"Colloportus."

The spell hit the greenhouse door and flashed a thin bright orange line across the latch. For one hopeful second the metal held. Then the latch shot upward of its own accord with a cheerful clack and the door banged wider than before.

Everywhere in the greenhouse, hardware answered.

The tool lean-to spit open. Trowels and a rusted pair of shears slid clattering across the boards. The potting cabinet dumped a drift of mouldy twine and a sack of bone meal at his ankles. Above him the ventilators jerked another inch and the crank handles on the wall started to turn by themselves in small eager jumps. The dead vine on the sill kept patiently opening, each strip curling farther back as if it had all the time in the world.

He tried a second spell, a plain locking charm so easy a first-year could manage it half asleep. The wand kicked in his grip and threw sparks into the damp air with a noise like fat spitting in a pan. One of the water-butt lids leapt off and struck the opposite bench.

Owen swore properly then.

The burn in his palm pulsed once, hot enough that he nearly dropped the wand, and he understood at last what he'd actually asked. He had not asked the vine. He had asked for admission in a room built almost entirely out of things with latches: vents, frames, doors, cupboards, lids, catches, valves.

The greenhouse did not care which answer he had meant.

***

He got the manuscript back into the satchel and tied the flap shut with copper wire because clasps had plainly ceased to deserve trust.

After that the problem became physical.

The outer door would not stay put. He shoved it closed with his hip, wedged a stack of seed trays against it, and turned to find both cold frames up again and the tool lean-to door slamming itself against the inside wall like someone trying to break in from the wrong side. The roof vents were the worst of it. Every time the gusting evening air caught them they yanked wider, and every yank tugged the wall cranks in a little enthusiastic circle, which seemed only to remind the rest of the greenhouse that opening was still the rule of the hour.

He went for the nearest crank and hauled it down.

The handle bit into the freshened burn through the cloth he had wrapped round his hand before coming out. Pain went white and immediate. He lost his grip, snatched it back, and smelled singed linen before he realised it was his own handkerchief scorching against skin that had no business being that hot. He set his feet, took the crank in both hands this time, and leaned his full weight on it until the ventilator overhead grudgingly shut with a bang that shook warm dust down into his hair.

At once the opposite vent flew wider.

He laughed once, short and ugly, because the choice was that or shout.

Bootsteps sounded on the path outside.

He froze with one hand on the crank and listened. Two sets, quick, with the skittering scuff of girls' shoes not bothering to keep in time. Then voices through the glass, blurred by the panes.

"I told you it wasn't this one."

"Well, I can't see the number if it's dark, can I?"

Third-years, maybe. Out after the bell and trying not to get caught somewhere they should not have been. The greenhouse door gave another traitorous thump against the seed trays. One of the girls yelped.

Owen swore under his breath, crossed the floor in three strides, and caught the handle before it could leap again. Through the glazed panel he could make out two shapes in cloaks, one small and square in the shoulders, the other tugging her sleeve over a hand as if she had a habit of chewing the cuff.

"Occupied," he said through the crack before they could peer in properly.

There was a startled silence. Then the cuff-chewer said, "What?"

"Sprout's put me on repairs," he lied. "Go the other way if you don't want it on your shoes."

The smaller girl, who had a nose flattened slightly to one side as though it had once been broken and poorly persuaded back, said, "On whose shoes?"

"Compost," Owen said. "Obviously."

This at least sounded plausible enough that they fled, the pair of them half-running, half-sliding on the wet path, muttering at one another. A moment later another sound came from farther off: adult boots on gravel, heavier, unhurried.

That would be the patrol.

He had no time left for elegant solutions, if there had ever been any. He seized the sack of bone meal, dumped half of it by accident over his shoes, and used the empty sack as a wedge under the cold frame lid. He tied the lean-to handle to the bench leg with mouldy twine. He shut the potting cabinet with both knees and jammed a broken terracotta pot through its handles. Every closure felt provisional. The rule still wanted the room open. He could feel it in the way latches trembled under his palms, in the constant tiny ticks from all around him as metal tested itself against restraint.

He made the mistake of trying another charm.

It was not even ambitious. He only wanted the spilled seed trays stacked out of the way before whoever was outside looked in. He pointed his wand and said, "Wingardium Leviosa."

The wand gave a nauseating sideways jerk. The seed trays twitched but did not rise. Instead every lid left unsecured in the room snapped up at once: the butt lid, the sand barrel, the biscuit tin some long-ago worker had forgotten on the top shelf and which now disgorged a drift of fossilised crumbs and dead blue mould onto Owen's shoulder.

He stared at the crumbs on his robe for one stupid second, then wiped them off with the back of his wrist.

All right. No more wand.

He tucked it away and trusted his hands instead, though one of them was rapidly becoming a liability. The burn had deepened from the neat old brand the coffer had left. New red lines had risen out of it, crossing the original mark at angles that made no sense as skin and far too much sense as notation. When he gripped the final roof crank and dragged it shut, the raw places in his palm stuck to the iron for a horrible instant before peeling free. His eyes watered. He kept pulling.

Outside, the patrol slowed.

Amycus Carrow's voice reached him through the glass, thick with boredom and stale amusement. "If that's more of you little shits hiding out there, I'd rather you just say so. Saves me the walk."

Another voice, lower and dutiful, said something Owen could not catch.

He had the outer door pressed shut with both shoulders now, the seed trays braced against his shins. Cold air came in through the badly mended panes and over the top of the door, carrying wet earth and the sharper smell of the lake. Inside, the greenhouse crackled softly around him, held for the moment but no nearer calm.

The patrol boots stopped just outside.

Owen stood very still and discovered he was breathing in short, hot draughts that tasted of bone meal and rust.

Amycus tried the handle.

It lifted at once under his hand. Only Owen's weight and the trays held the door. The amended rule thrilled through the latch like a plucked wire. Owen thought, with complete and useless clarity, that if Carrow yanked harder the whole arrangement would come apart and he'd be found in a disused greenhouse covered in mould, gripping a satchel that could not survive inspection.

Instead Amycus let the handle drop.

"Locked wrong," he said. "Sprout's lot never could keep a house."

The other man made a noise that might have been agreement. Boots moved away.

Owen stayed where he was until the sounds had gone completely and even then another count of fifty besides, because panic made fools of arithmetic and he wanted at least one thing in the evening to obey.

When he finally let go, the door trembled but held.

The greenhouse looked ridiculous.

That was nearly the worst part. If it had looked solemn or blasted or beautifully uncanny, he might have granted himself the dignity of terror. Instead it looked petty and interrupted. Twine crossed handles. Bone meal crusted the flags white. Dead crumbs spotted the bench. The vine on the sill had opened itself into four brittle ribbons and now lay there innocent and useless.

He picked up one strip and turned it over. The inside showed pale pith, dry and fibrous, exposed rather than revived.

That was the first exercise. So it wasn't mending after all. The vine lay harmless in his fingers while the rest of the greenhouse kept giving little restless ticks around him. The room still wanted more mouths open.

He stood there with the opened vine in his fingers and felt the shape of the thing settle in him, cold now rather than hot.

The work was real. His hand would not stop throbbing, and sweat had gone cold under his shirt despite the greenhouse heat. He put the strip down and went back over the nearest catches with his left hand, pressing each one shut and waiting to see whether it meant to argue.

By the time he left, he had the worst of the place lashed shut and the rest looking merely shabby rather than bewitched. Merely shabby was about the best standard Hogwarts offered anyone that year.

***

He scrubbed his hands in the cellar lavatory until the water in the basin ran the colour of weak tea from compost and dust. The right hand did not improve.

Under the tap the old burn showed itself more clearly than it had in the greenhouse. The original brand from the coffer ran across the palm in a dark curved score, but the new lines branching from it were redder, slightly raised, and too regular to be random splits. They crossed the base of his thumb and ran down towards the wrist in measured strokes, a half-finished diagram written into flesh. When the cold water hit them, the pain did not ease. It compacted. The heat sank inward and sat there.

He dried off, wrapped the palm in a clean strip torn from the lining of an old shirt, and went to the common room because not going would have invited questions.

Hufflepuff at evening always tried to carry on as though routine had moral force. The round room glowed gold from the lamps sunk in the walls. Wet cloaks steamed by the hearth. Someone had smuggled up a plate of rock buns from the kitchens and they were disappearing one blunt bite at a time. Homework sprawled over tables among bottles of ink, mended quills, and socks drying on the fender. The occupation had thinned people, sharpened them, made them quicker to glance over a shoulder before laughing, but habit was stubborn. It kept putting chairs in circles and sending students looking for hot tea.

Martha Sorrell had one foot tucked under her and the other boot off while she darned the heel of a sock with a strand of bright green embroidery silk that turned the repair into an insult. Her plait had mostly come loose. She looked up when Owen came in, took in his face, his filthy cuffs, and the state of his shoes, and clicked her tongue once.

"You smell like a plant funeral," she said.

"Thanks."

"Not a compliment." She bit the thread clean with her front teeth and held the sock up to squint at it. "What happened to your hand?"

He had forgotten the bandage showed below his cuff.

"Caught it on wire."

"Where?"

"Greenhouse."

She gave him a flat look over the top of the sock. "That would explain why you smell like greenhouse, yes."

He might have laughed on another night. Instead he shrugged and started pulling books from his satchel with his left hand. The copper wire he had used to tie it shut had bitten into the canvas hard enough that it took him a clumsy half minute to unwind. Martha watched without comment, which was the kindest thing anyone had done for him all day.

Around them the room went on in scraps of ordinary talk.

"Have you got the Charms notes?"

"No, Finch took them."

"Well, get them back. I've got detention."

"You always have detention."

"Yeah, and?"

The harmlessness of it made him feel briefly unsteady.

Martha set the sock aside and pushed the tin of thread toward him with two fingers. "You want that stitched properly, ask Hannah. If you put dittany on it now you'll only stick the cloth in."

"I wasn't going to."

"Right."

She said it without edge, only tired certainty. She reached for a rock bun, broke it cleanly in half, and ate the drier piece first as if saving the less disappointing half for later. That was so exactly the sort of thing she did that it nearly undid him.

He looked down before she could see anything in his face and found that his quill case had split along one seam in the greenhouse. Feathers and nibs were inching out through the gap. A simple repair would do it. A first-year repair. He wanted very badly for one thing to behave the way it had yesterday.

He drew his wand.

Martha's eyes flicked up. "You all right?"

"Fine."

He pointed the wand at the torn seam. "Reparo."

The magic left his hand wrong.

It wasn't a matter of strength. The whole thing had gone sideways. He felt it as a slip in the wrist and then in the room, the way you felt a stair that was not where your foot had expected it. The split seam in the quill case did not mend. Instead the latch on Martha's thread tin sprang open, the lid of the sugar bowl on the next table flipped back, three satchel clasps clicked loose in quick succession, and somewhere by the hearth a trunk gave a startled wooden cough as its lid lifted an inch and dropped again.

The common room went briefly still.

A second-year nearest him snatched her schoolbag off the floor and tucked it under both knees. By the hearth, the boy with the trunk put one hand flat on the lid and kept it there. Another girl gathered her parchment and her inkpot and carried them to the far armchair without a word.

"What was that?" the boy by the fire said.

Owen lowered the wand at once.

"Sorry," he said. "Bad cast."

"Bad how?" someone asked from behind the sofa.

"I don't know."

A first-year near the window said, not quite under her breath, "Can he not do more of it in here?"

No one shushed her. The room had already started protecting itself in small practical ways.

Martha did not laugh. She looked at the open thread tin, then at Owen's wand hand, then back at him.

"You're white as paper," she said.

"I'm tired."

"Everybody's tired."

"Yes. I know."

He heard the edge in his own voice and hated it at once. Martha heard it too. Her mouth tightened, but she only took the wand gently out of his hand and laid it on top of his books as if removing a kitchen knife from near somebody drunk.

"Don't do more of that near my homework," she said. "Or anybody else's. Look at them."

He did look then. Satchels had come up onto laps. The sugar bowl lid had been set aside rather than trusted. A freckled fourth-year at the next table was threading her scarf through the hasp of her trunk with quick angry fingers.

"It was thread."

"Today it was thread." Martha kept her hand on the wand when he reached for it. "No more spellwork in here. Not tonight."

"Martha, that's a bit much."

"Is it?" said the boy by the fire, still holding his trunk shut.

Owen shut his mouth.

Martha waited. "Say it back."

He looked at the room pretending not to listen and failed to miss how thoroughly they were listening.

"No spells in the house," he said at last. "Not till I know what's happening."

"Good."

Martha pushed half the rock bun across to him. "Eat something."

"I'm not hungry."

"Then hold it and look convincing."

He took the rock bun because refusing would have turned the moment into something he did not have the steadiness for. It was still faintly warm in the middle. He sat with it in one hand and the other wrapped under the table in cooling pain while homework resumed around them in soft scratchings and mutters.

It resumed differently. No satchel went back on the floor. The girl in the far armchair kept her inkpot on her knee. The thread tin stayed open between Martha's elbow and Owen's books as if nobody was inclined to test whether lids deserved trust tonight.

After a minute Martha picked up her sock again.

"Green silk was all I had," she said, not looking at him.

"It suits the sock."

"Shut up."

That, at least, came out right.

Later, in the dormitory, he kept the promise because even wanting to break it told him Martha had been right. He hid the manuscript under the false bottom he had made in his trunk from a cracked Charms text and two strips of packing wood. He tied the trunk shut by hand instead of trusting the latch. Then he sat on the edge of the bed with the wand across his knees, opened his notebook to the last page, and wrote three things in the narrow exact script he used when he wanted a thing to behave: no spellwork in Hufflepuff, no repair or locking charms, and as little trust in his wand as he could manage.

The rules looked absurd. They also looked, tonight, like the nearest thing he had to sense.

He unwrapped the bandage.

The new lines in his palm had darkened. They no longer looked like the random after-effects of damaged skin. They looked appended.

He turned the wand once between thumb and fingers. Ordinary magic had always felt, if not easy, then legible. You learned the angle, the emphasis, the bit of language that made the world agree to a small temporary impossibility. Tonight the wand gave him nothing back. It was only varnished wood in his hand and the mean suspicion that the next mistake would go sideways again.

He had wanted proof the Grinlow Leaves were more than a hidden old book full of lunatic marginalia.

He had it now in the lashed-up greenhouse with its tied doors and humiliated cupboards, in the common room with half the house quietly guarding their clasps from him, and in the rules on the back of his notebook. The hot added lines crossing his hand made it plain enough that the manuscript had begun making notes on him in return.
