# Chapter 3

The Room of Requirement had stopped pretending to be mysterious. By late September it had settled on the look of a school storeroom after a small war: camp beds shoved too close together, trunks underfoot, laundry strung between bookcases, steam from a kettle going nowhere because the windows it had invented were only a kindness and not a real route for air. It smelled of damp wool, onion soup, old socks, and the sharp medicinal green of dittany. Somebody had bled on one of the cushions and flipped it over. The blood had come through anyway.

After nearly two days of keeping the Grinlow Leaves to himself, Owen had finally brought them here wrapped under his jumper and had the immediate sense that he had brought the wrong object into a room that needed five dozen other things first.

Neville Longbottom held the centre of it with a scrap of parchment in one hand, boots planted on the stone, saying names in the flat voice of somebody who had already had to repeat himself too many times that day.

"If you're sleeping here tonight, you don't leave after lights-out unless somebody sends you," he said. "I don't care if you hear your own dormitory door. I don't care if you think it's your own dormitory door. Ginny, can you take the first-years on the left side? Dean, I still need two lookouts for the fourth-floor turn. No, not both of you, because if both of you get grabbed we're worse off than before."

A younger Hufflepuff on the nearest bed had her school tie tied around her wrist above a curse welt that shone wet and red. Seamus was trying to pry a splinter of broken frame out of his thumb with his teeth. At the back wall a tall Ravenclaw girl Owen knew only by sight was sorting salvaged library books into two stacks, dry and ruined, and muttering a separate apology to each ruined one before she set it down. Her wire-rimmed spectacles kept sliding to the end of her nose. She pushed them back with the back of her wrist because both hands were full of warped paper.

Martha saw him first.

"There you are," she said, low and exasperated, as if he had wandered off during washing-up rather than vanished into his own head for the better part of two days. Her dark plait was loosening down one shoulder and there was a smear of something grey on her cheek that might have been dust or soot. "If you've come to stand in the doorway, shift. You're making everyone squeeze round you."

He moved because three people immediately needed the space he had been occupying. One brushed past with a stack of blankets that caught his sleeve; another came through behind him with a basin and clipped his hip. By the time he'd found somewhere else to put his feet, the gap was already full of bodies he wasn't part of. His right palm pulsed under the handkerchief he had wound round it. The burn had quieted enough during the walk that he had nearly convinced himself it was ordinary. Here, in the crush and heat, it had started needling again, as if the manuscript disliked crowds.

"Sorry," he said.

Martha was tying a bandage round a third-year's forearm one-handed while she talked. "Take that kettle over if you're useful. If you're not useful, sit somewhere narrower."

He took the kettle. It was hot through the handle and slopped weak tea over his knuckles. Nobody thanked him, which was fair. The tea was not for him. It was for a first-year Gryffindor whose mouth still shook after every swallow. Owen carried it across and nearly trod on a pair of shoes drying by the fire, stuffed with newspaper. One of the soles had been patched with cardboard cut from a Potions essay.

By the time he got back to the edge of the room Neville was crouched over a rough floor plan of the fourth floor scratched in charcoal on the top of an old wardrobe laid flat.

"They've started checking the suits of armour again," Neville said. "Not all of them. The two by the tapestry of Barnabas are still blind if you pass close. Amycus knows about the broom cupboard now, so stop using the broom cupboard. If anyone is still hiding notes in the flour bins, stop doing that as well. Tallow nearly had my head off."

There was a tired ripple of laughter at that, real enough to count as relief.

Martha came up beside Owen with a basin that smelled of pennywort and burnt cloth. "What happened to your hand?"

He folded the burned one in against the manuscript under his jumper before she'd properly seen it. "Nothing. I caught it on a hinge."

She looked at him for a moment that was too long for the lie and too short for a fight. "Right."

He should have told her then. Or if not her, Neville, because Neville at least would know what to do with new danger if it had a shape and a corridor attached to it. That was why he had come. The manuscript had spent the afternoon on his bed like a thing that had chosen him badly. When he touched the resolved pages the diagrams had settled into legibility for a few seconds at a time, enough for him to understand that he did not understand them, and underneath that, worse, enough to understand that the claim was not a metaphor. Magic was not the machinery itself. It was only the bit people learned to touch. The manuscript kept pointing past that to an older arrangement written into the world like load-bearing grammar, and it treated Hogwarts as one local problem inside a much larger one.

He had tried, alone, to think what a sensible person did with that.

The sensible answer had appeared to be: tell other people immediately.

The flaw in that answer was the other people.

"Owen," Martha said, because Neville had paused to hear from a fifth-year Slytherin about patrol changes and Owen was still standing there like a badly placed chair. "Did you need something?"

Half the room did not look at him. The half that did looked in the way people looked when any small interruption threatened to become a larger one.

"I found something in the library," he said.

Neville looked up, waiting. He had a bruise yellowing along one cheekbone and the particular worn expression of somebody who had stopped expecting good news but still had to leave a space for it in case it arrived by accident.

"Is it a route?" Neville asked.

"Not exactly."

"Is it about patrols?"

"No."

Martha shut her eyes briefly. "Just say it, then."

Owen pulled the manuscript free enough to show its wrapping, then more because stopping halfway looked furtive and going on looked mad, and there was not, apparently, a useful degree between those two conditions. The vellum leaves slid against one another with a dry, insect-thin noise. Several of the pages facing outward were blank. One held a smear of diagram in rust-coloured ink that looked less like writing than a map of cracked riverbeds.

The younger students nearest him leaned away first and then, seeing it was only paper, leaned back.

"This was in a hidden shelf behind the north stacks," Owen said. "In a lead coffer. The footnote was in an old classroom copy of *A History of Magic*. Pre-1883, I think. I checked the shelf numerals after the blasting and they still match the old arrangement, or enough of it does, and the note said `North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement,' which shouldn't have still been there at all, and it wasn't just a book hidden there, it's something older than that, and I think it means the castle has-"

"Slow down," Neville said.

Owen stopped, swallowed, and discovered he had already lost the order of what mattered.

"It means," he said, smaller, "there's a kind of magic that tells the usual sort what it can do."

Nobody answered. The kettle clicked on the hearth. A first-year pulled her blanket higher round her shoulders. Seamus looked from the page to Neville and back again.

Seamus stared at the nearly blank page. "Tells it what it can do."

"Not bigger," Owen said quickly. "More like what the rest sits on. The bit that tells spells and wards what's allowed. I know how that sounds."

Neville straightened. "All right. Start again. What do you actually know?"

"That it's about the castle," Owen said. "At least partly. There are diagrams of Hogwarts and references to warding on a scale that doesn't make sense if they're only talking about one building."

The first-year Gryffindor with the shaking mouth clutched her mug in both hands. "Can it make them miss this room?"

The hope in it went through him like something thrown.

Neville answered before Owen could. "Not on a guess it can't. Nobody in here is sleeping easier because a page twitched under his hand. If he's got a route, or a way to keep a patrol off a corridor tonight, that's different."

"I do have a route," Owen said, too fast now because the room had gone taut in a new way. "The old service run by the kitchens. That's how I got to it. Behind the north stacks."

Martha's head came up. "Can we use the run?"

"One at a time, maybe two if they're small. Not with beds, not quickly, and I don't know if they've found the far end since the blast."

Neville's eyes narrowed. "You're sure it's still open."

"The wall still shifts if you go in low and shove past the cracked brace," Owen said. "I checked the old shelf numerals after the blasting. The route's real."

Neville jerked his chin at Dean. "Put it on the map. Nobody uses it till we know it's clean."

Dean nodded once.

It was a simple question. Owen should have had an answer ready before he'd opened the manuscript in public.

"Help," he said, and hated the word the moment it left his mouth because it sounded childish and because it was true. "I need another look at it. Or someone who knows old references. The book that pointed me there shouldn't have had that note in it at all. The citations are wrong for the editions we have, and some of the pages only clear if I-"

His palm gave a hard, bright stab under the handkerchief. He flinched and the wrapped leaves slid against his jumper with a dry scratch.

Martha looked at his hidden hand. "Did that thing do something to you?"

He folded the burned one tighter in against the manuscript. "Not exactly."

Dean said, with no grin left in it, "If it's the wards underneath things, does that mean this room could stop hiding us? Or that you can make it hide us better?"

Half the room seemed to lean without moving.

Neville cut in at once. "It means nobody in here is promising either one. If he can keep a door shut or a patrol blind tonight, he says that now. If he can't, we're not ripping up beds and watches because the castle might be stranger than we thought."

"It's not just that," Owen said. "It says the old magical civilisation didn't die out, it left, and what we've got now is only part of it. There were protections, or there are, and if Hogwarts is tied into them then what's happening here may be-"

"Owen," Martha said.

He could have lied. He could have said the room needed warning because the castle sat on something older and stranger than anyone knew. He could have said the manuscript might matter later. Both statements were true, and both would have sounded like a person trying to make his terror nobler than other people's terror.

Instead he said the truest and worst version.

"I think this is bigger than the Carrows."

No one shouted him down. It went worse than that. Shoulders angled away from him by fractions. Somebody at the fire gave a disbelieving little snort through the nose. A first-year actually looked afraid, not of the manuscript, but of him, of the possibility that one more older student had finally cracked and started speaking in useless grand statements while the rest of them still needed supper and lookout rotations.

Martha's voice dropped. "Don't say that in here."

"I don't mean they're harmless, I mean scale. I mean if the castle itself is part of-"

Neville cut across him for the first time. "No."

The word came out flat and final.

"Listen to me," Neville said. "If you've found something real, then good. Hide it properly and keep yourself alive long enough to work out what it is. But I can't have this room hanging off a promise you can't make, and I can't scare the first-years with half an answer either. Maybe tomorrow the world turns out stranger than we thought. Tonight I need corridors covered, children counted, and people asleep where the Carrows won't find them."

Nobody moved. Neville went on, plain and tired and absolutely right.

"If you want a bed, have one. If you want someone to look at your hand, we'll do that. If you want me to tell everyone here that their actual problem tonight is less important than your theory, I won't. I don't have that luxury, and neither do they."

Owen opened his mouth. Nothing came out that was fit to say.

Martha rubbed at the grey smear on her cheek with the heel of her hand and only spread it wider. "Next time say the route first."

The words landed harder because she had not raised her voice. She sounded fed up, embarrassed for him, embarrassed for herself for having expected better.

At the back, the Ravenclaw girl with the ruined books had stopped sorting. She was looking at Owen now with her head slightly to one side, measuring him against whatever she had just heard. The expression belonged to someone who had heard a shelf creak behind a wall and was trying to decide whether the building plan allowed it.

Dean let out a breath through his teeth. "Can we get back to not dying till breakfast?"

"Yes," Neville said at once, without taking his eyes off Owen. "We can."

Neville turned a shoulder away, enough to make room for everybody else and none for Owen. "Ginny," he said, "have the first-years on the left side eaten yet?"

The talk came back in separate scraps rather than all at once. A few glances still snagged on the manuscript and then slid off in a hurry, the way people looked away from vomiting. Martha went to the third-year's bandage. Seamus found his splinter again. Someone by the fire said they had one heel of bread left and another voice said cut it thinner, then. The ordinary stupidity of surviving stitched itself back together without him and left Owen standing outside it while still technically in the middle.

He wrapped the leaves up with fingers that had gone clumsy.

His burned palm had dampened the handkerchief through. When the cloth shifted, the skin beneath flashed up angry and ridged, the burn spread into a dark branching mark he had no name for. He covered it again at once. If anyone had seen, nobody said.

He did not know where to look. Anywhere in the room was wrong. At the beds made him feel theatrical. At Neville made him feel younger than eleven. At Martha made him feel false. He ended up looking at the floorboards, where somebody had spilled soup earlier and half wiped it, leaving a pale onion crescent in the grain.

For one shabby second he considered saying sorry to the whole room.

That would have made it about him again, which was apparently a skill he had discovered a fresh aptitude for.

He waited until nobody was directly watching and edged backwards toward the door. A narrow table now blocked the strip of floor he'd used on the way in. On it sat three labelled jars of salve, a stack of folded socks, and a chipped china dog with its head broken off. He had not seen the dog before. He had the absurd impression somebody had set it there simply to improve the quality of his humiliation.

"Phipps."

He stopped with his hand on the latch.

The Ravenclaw girl from the back wall had come after him. Up close she was older than he'd thought from a distance and more tired around the eyes. One cuff of her jumper was ink-black where she had clearly been using it as a handkerchief for weeks. She had tucked two bent quills behind one ear as if she'd forgotten they were there. In one hand she still carried a ruined book, held carefully away from her body so the warped pages could dry.

"Sorry," she said, brisk and purely functional. "What printing was the *History of Magic*?"

Owen stared at her.

The room noise carried on behind them. Neville was assigning another corridor pair. Somebody swore softly because a bandage had stuck.

"What?" Owen said.

"The edition." She pushed her spectacles up with one knuckle. "The footnote you quoted. `North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement.` Which printing had that? Because the classroom text drops the founder shelf marks before the 1883 clean issue, and after the 1894 recatalogue nobody writes `C-13` for anything in the north stacks. So either you've had a breakdown in a very specific bibliography, or somebody cut that note out of every copy that ordinary people can still find."

For a moment Owen only looked at her. He had spent two days with a discovery so large it made the school year feel like a bad local arrangement of furniture. It had not occurred to him until then that the first person who might believe him would do it because the citation style was wrong.

"The title page was half gone," he said. His voice came back rough. "But it was old. Binns's classroom shelf. Not library stock."

The girl's gaze sharpened into the grim concentration of someone seeing a filing error big enough to imply deliberate damage.

"Right," she said. "I thought so."

"Prue," Martha called from across the room, distracted and irritated. "If you're done courting mildew, I need the dry bandages."

The girl did not look away from Owen. "In a minute."

Then, quieter, to him: "Don't wave that about in here again."

Her eyes flicked to the bundle under his arm, then to Neville's map and the camp beds. Before he could answer, she crossed the room to Martha and took the dry bandages out of her hands.

Owen stayed by the latch a moment longer, feeling the burn in his palm pulse through the handkerchief.

By the time he left the room, the question pressing hardest was no longer how to explain the pages to anybody else. It was what, exactly, they wanted from his hand, and how to ask without an audience.
