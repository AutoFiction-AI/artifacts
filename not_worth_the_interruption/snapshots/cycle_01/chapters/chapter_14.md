# Chapter 14

By April the greenhouses had started sweating before breakfast. Condensation ran down the panes in fat, slow tracks, and the first breath inside Greenhouse Three always hit like a hand over the mouth: wet soil, tomato leaf, damp rope, old compost warming up. Professor Sprout had propped two broken panes with mismatched bits of glass and a strip of tin, and new growth was already climbing over the repair as if damage were only another sort of frame.

Nobody spoke much at first. They had trowels in one hand and essays in the other. Someone had abandoned a Transfiguration parchment on the potting bench and pressed a crescent of dirt straight through the title. A pair of third-years on the far side were arguing in undertones about whether the vanishment chart was due Thursday or Friday, with the low, desperate seriousness of people to whom the answer mattered. It still mattered. The castle had not stopped producing deadlines because it was occupied. There was a two-foot essay due on controlled vanishment, and if the school cracked open over their heads the essay would probably still have been due on controlled vanishment.

Owen worked a tangle of seedling roots free of their tray and tried not to think in systems. Spring made systems too easy. Every bench was a proof of persistence. Nasturtiums had gone reckless over one corner, bright enough to look rude. Shrivelfig cuttings that had sat all winter like dead fingers were furred silver-green at the tips. Someone had left the greenhouse door open a fraction, and cold air kept moving across the backs of his wrists in little draughts that smelled of thawed mud and the lake.

Sprout clapped once. "Mind the roots, not the leaves. The leaves are showing off."

A few people laughed. Not much. Enough.

Owen shifted another plug of soil into a pot and looked, against his better judgment, out through the sweating glass toward the lawns. The grounds were greening in uneven swathes. Black patches still held winter, but between them the first clean colour had come up in a hurry, so that the paths seemed to have been drawn through a place briefly willing to forget itself. Beyond the slope the lake moved under a hard white sky. Wind roughened it in dark strips. From here he could not see the chamber below it, the broken lens, the torn brightness in the old work. He could see only water, and the giant beech near the shore carrying buds at every branch tip like a promise made too early.

His right palm ached where the old burn crossed it, not from pain exactly but from recognition. Some days the hidden work sat in his body before he thought of it, a change in pressure behind the teeth, a hot awareness along scarred skin. Today even the little school magic around him seemed louder than it had a month ago. A girl at the next bench muttered a coaxing charm over a tray of knotgrass and the syllables rang against the greenhouse glass with a density that made him glance up. Nothing happened except the knotgrass straightening obediently in its damp earth. That was the problem. So much of the world still worked.

After Herbology the castle smelt of thawing stone and boiled cabbage. Students moved through the corridors with books clamped to jumpers, skirts damp at the hem from the grounds, ties loosened by degrees as the day warmed. Someone had opened a high window near the moving staircase and the whole landing was full of March-cold air, sharp enough to make noses run. A second-year went past carrying three library books and a loaf heel wrapped in a napkin. Two boys from Ravenclaw were having an earnest row over Arithmancy tables with such total focus that one of them walked into a suit of armour and apologised to it.

It would have been easier, Owen thought, if everything had flattened. If the school had become purely a machine for fear. But ordinary life kept insisting on itself in scraps and chores and stupid routine. Ink still dried on cuffs. People still nicked extra butter at breakfast. Peeves still tipped water out of a trophy cup onto anyone silly enough to run under him. The occupation sat over all of it like rust over metal, but the metal was still there.

At lunch he took his tray to the Hufflepuff table and found space halfway down the bench. The soup was green enough to suggest cress and thin enough to suggest otherwise. Bread came round in torn chunks rather than slices. Somebody had salted the radishes too hard. Over at the Gryffindor table Neville was bent over a bit of parchment with two younger students crowded in, talking with his finger, not his voice, moving from line to line as if the plan would hold together better if he could pin it to the wood. He looked tired in the compact, competent way he had looked all year: collar not quite straight, bruise yellowing at the jaw, hands too rough now for a boy who had once mostly handled plants.

Martha crossed the aisle carrying a second bowl she clearly had no business carrying on her bad leg. She favoured the left side when she thought nobody was looking and then over-corrected when she caught someone noticing. Today she had her dark plait wound up in a knot that had already begun to collapse, and when she sat she tore the crust off her bread by habit and slid it into her pocket instead of eating it. Later, for somebody who missed supper or got shut in somewhere. Or just later. Owen knew the movement before he let himself know he was watching.

She did not look at him. That was not new. It still landed fresh.

Prue arrived late enough that most of the decent bread had gone. She dropped onto the Ravenclaw bench with a stack of books under one arm and pushed her spectacles back up by the bridge without using a hand, an old little trick of her face. Her cuffs were inked blue again. She carried pencils loose in her pocket and they always worked free and jabbed through the lining; once, waiting in the library, Owen had watched her pull out three snapped points and curse at them with extraordinary quiet intensity. Today she set a folded scrap of parchment beside her bowl, glanced once down the hall as if checking whether anyone was watching the wrong thing, and then looked across at him.

She did not smile. She tipped her head very slightly toward the doors.

Later, that meant. Walk, or talk, or both.

He looked down at his soup and nodded once.

The afternoon went on being itself. A corridor smelled of floor polish and wet wool. Somebody in Charms had managed to set a quill smoking and got sent out with black fingers. There was homework from Binns as if Binns still inhabited the same century as the rest of them. Owen copied half a page of notes in the library and none of the words sat properly because he kept hearing, behind the ordinary scrape of chairs and turning paper, the pressure change he had felt under the lake when the star-lens flared. Battle-scale magic. Brightness through a tear. An event already waiting somewhere in front of them, coming on whether the school was ready or not.

When the dinner bell went he nearly did not go. Then he went because not going would be visible, and visibility had its own costs now. By the time he came back down toward the cellars the kitchen heat had started to breathe through the stones. Bread, onions, something yeasty and sweet. The scent caught under the ribs with unreasonable force. It was one of the castle's oldest lies, that if somewhere bread was being baked then things might still, with enough care, be kept from breaking altogether.

Prue was waiting at the end of the corridor where the cellar passage turned out toward the yard door. She had traded the stack of books for a single notebook tied shut with string. One curl had worked itself free and was lifting in the draught by her cheek. When he came up she looked at him for a second as if measuring whether he would try to be difficult from the first word.

"You ate?" she said.

"Some of it."

"Good. Come outside before I change my mind."

He went.

* * *

The path above the lake was still half winter. Mud sucked at the edges, and the reeds at the shore lay in flattened brown mats where the ice had crushed them. But primroses had started up in the shelter of the wall and the willow by the boathouse was beginning to haze green. The evening light was thin and bright enough to make the black water look metallic. Somewhere higher up the grounds a group of students were shouting over a game with all the desperate normality of people making use of an hour that might later be taken away.

Prue sat on the low stone wall, gathered her skirt under her, and untied the string around the notebook only to wind it round her fingers again. She always needed one thing in hand when she was thinking. If there were no book she used string, and if there were no string she picked the paper label off her own bottle of ink in strips and regretted it later when the bottle went tacky.

For a minute they said nothing important.

"Did Sprout still have you all elbow-deep in compost?" she asked.

"Mostly seedlings. One tomato section and some shrivelfig cuttings."

"How pastoral."

"You say that as if you'd have preferred Binns."

"I would have preferred a fire and two uninterrupted hours, but the school remains vulgar about my preferences."

The corner of his mouth moved. Not much. Enough that she saw it and looked away at the lake as if not to press too hard on the almost of a better evening.

"I had to spend forty minutes helping Idris Fawley understand why his chart kept slipping a column," she said after a while. "It was because he can't count, but delicately. There's only so gently you can phrase that."

"Did you phrase it gently?"

"No. I'm trying not to lie more than necessary."

The line sat between them longer than it should have. Owen felt the shift and hated himself for hearing it as structure before he heard it as warning.

He said, "Right."

Prue rubbed the notebook string between thumb and forefinger until it bit white into her skin. "I know something changed."

He kept looking at the lake. "A great deal keeps changing."

"Don't." Her voice did not rise. It thinned. "If I ask badly, answer the better version. I know something changed below."

Wind came off the water and found the gap at the back of his collar. He could have lied. Not cleanly, but enough to preserve the shape of the evening for ten more minutes. He had got good at answering the question nearest the question, at making timing sound like the problem when the problem was value.

"I learned some things about pressure," he said. "About what makes the anchor worse."

"And now you look as if you're already somewhere else even when you're sitting still."

"I'm here."

"You know that's not what I mean."

He dug his thumbnail into the old burn at his palm. The skin there had gone smooth and shiny months ago. It still felt, at certain moments, as if it had only just closed. "I'm trying to work out what's urgent."

Prue gave a short laugh with no amusement in it. "No, you aren't. That's the bit you're quite settled on."

He turned then. "That's not fair."

"It is, actually. I just don't think you like the arithmetic."

Her spectacles had slid down her nose again. She pushed them back up and looked at him over the rims with that dry, almost embarrassed exactness she used when she was coming closest to something personal. Behind her the lake took the light and broke it into hard pieces. A gull cried once, ugly and ordinary.

"I'm not asking you to give it up," she said. "I know you won't, and I'm past wasting breath on fantasies. I'm asking for a proportion."

He said nothing.

"Listen." She unwound the string from her fingers and rewound it more carefully, as if the thought needed tidier handling. "The school isn't going to stay like this. Something's coming. We both know that without you needing to start being coy. If it goes bad here, if it goes properly bad, then we help here first. The people in front of us. The living, obvious, inconvenient people. Then, if there's an after and if the lower work still needs doing, we go back to it. Together, if you want. I mean that. I'm not pretending the other thing doesn't matter. I'm saying there has to be an order large enough for human beings to fit in."

The tenderness of it made him feel briefly sick. Not because she was wrong. Because she had made the wrongness impossible to call stupidity.

He looked down at her hand and the string cutting her fingers. He wanted, with a force that felt almost childish, to take it away from her and say yes. He wanted the sentence, wanted the shape of himself inside it: a boy who could promise that when the castle cried out he would come upstairs, stand with everyone else, keep the scale human and local and bearable. He could see it whole. That was the worst part. He could see the life he was refusing.

"Prue," he said, and stopped.

She waited.

"It isn't that simple."

Something shuttered in her face, not surprise, not yet anger. Recognition. "No," she said. "Of course that's what you've got."

"I mean it literally. Timing matters. Conditions matter. If I leave it too late, late may be the point where there isn't anything left to fix."

"For the ward."

He said, after a moment, "Yes."

"And for us?"

He should have answered at once. The pause did it for him.

Prue looked away. When she spoke again her voice had gone quieter still, and more precise for the effort of not letting it shake. "That's what I was trying to make easy for you, by the way. I was giving you the smaller promise. I wasn't asking for declarations or forever or anything else idiotic. I was saying: when the floor gives way under actual people, stand with the people first. Afterwards you can go back to saving the abstract."

"It's not abstract."

"No. That's exactly the trouble. It's more real to you than we are."

"That's not what I said."

"You don't have to say it if you keep demonstrating it."

He opened his mouth and found only method waiting there, narrow and bloodless. "If the condition shifts before the anchor is stabilised, I may lose the only workable approach. It isn't a matter of preference. It's a matter of sequence."

Prue stared at him for a second, then made a short, unbelieving sound. "Do you hear yourself?"

"I'm trying to be accurate."

"I know. God, I know. You always get more accurate when the thing under the sentence is unbearable."

He had no answer that would not prove her right in a different register.

Below them a wave slapped against stone. Somewhere behind the greenhouses someone yelled with laughter and got shushed. The whole school kept making these small claims on existence. Noise. Dinner. Wet socks drying by illicit fires. Revision schedules. Splinters in the rowing sheds. All the ridiculous local weight of being alive in one place. Owen felt it pressing at him from every direction and knew, with the same exhausted clarity with which he had read the lens below the lake, that it would not be enough to move him.

Prue slipped off the wall and stood facing him. She was nearly his height on the path, the notebook tucked against her ribs like a shield she had not meant to use.

"All right," she said. "Then I need the honest version."

He forced himself to look at her.

"If it comes to it," she said, "if the school breaks open and you can't do both, are we still the first thing?"

The wind worried at the loose curl by her cheek. He could see the little smudge where ink had marked the side of her hand. There was a minute in which honesty and cruelty were, for practical purposes, the same object.

"I don't know," he said.

She breathed in once through her nose and shut her eyes. He saw the sentence land in her body not as shock but as impact, the way a heavy tray landed in a pair of hands that had expected the weight and still did not quite absorb it cleanly.

"Yes, you do," she said.

He said nothing.

"You know, and I know, and that's enough." She looked past him toward the castle. "I kept thinking this was partly about fear. About you deciding that if you cared too much for anything upstairs it would become leverage. Which would've been horrible, but at least it would've been ordinary. Instead you've built yourself some private measure and climbed onto it."

"That's not fair."

"Stop saying that as if fairness is what I owe you."

He flinched. She saw and almost softened. Almost. It made things worse.

"I'm not telling Neville," she said after a moment. "And I'm not telling Martha. Not because you deserve protecting. Because the Carrows don't need help and because panic wouldn't improve anybody's thinking. If you need a practical assurance, there it is."

"Prue, I didn't ask you to keep anything."

"No. You ask by standing there and making everybody else do the arithmetic."

The notebook string had cut a red line around her finger. She unwound it at last and let it hang.

"I can still help with text," she said. "If you bring me notation, I can still look at notation. I'm not suddenly stupid because I'm angry. But whatever this was meant to be with us, whatever nearly-shape it had, I can't do it while you keep arranging the world so that every person in reach comes second to a buried mechanism."

He could feel himself losing the only reply that mattered even as he reached for it. Sorry was true and useless. Trust me was impossible. It will be worth it sounded insane, and worse than insane, it sounded proud.

"I'm sorry," he said anyway.

Prue gave him a tired look. "I know you're sorry. That's almost the bleakest part."

She started back toward the castle. After three steps she stopped without turning.

"Help the living if you can bear it," she said. "That is the compromise. It was a real one."

Then she went on, fast enough that he would have had to call after her to stop her, and he did not.

He stood on the path until the cold got through his socks and the primroses at the wall lost what little colour they had held in the dusk.

* * *

The kitchens were louder than the rest of the castle because they had to be. Pans struck stone. Water ran. Someone laughed outright at a joke Owen could not hear. Steam took the low ceiling and came back damp. After the lake the heat felt almost indecent. It smelt of onions, yeast, dishwater, and the singed sweetness of something caught at the edge and rescued in time.

Tallow was at the long scrubbed table by the ovens, sorting bent spoons into three neat rows by damage rather than size. His hands were wet to the wrist. One ear, already singed at the tip, twitched each time a bigger cauldron lid came down somewhere behind him. He had tucked a dish towel into his belt and forgotten it there.

Owen stopped just inside the arch. He had been coming to the kitchens all year and still had not learned how not to enter as if he were asking a favour of a room.

Tallow did not look up. "If you're after hot bread, Mr Phipps, you're late. The good loaves went upstairs. The rest are cooling and thinking bitter thoughts."

"I'm not after bread."

"A pity. Bread is easier to improve than boys."

Owen might once have smiled. Tonight the line only made the inside of his chest feel scraped clean.

He came closer. "I need a route."

That got Tallow's eyes on him at last. They were dark and bright and entirely unamused. "You do very often."

"No, right, sorry. I mean I need to know if there's a lower way from here to the undercroft if the main corridors are blocked."

Tallow picked up a spoon with one twisted tine, pressed the metal against the table edge with both thumbs, and straightened it slowly before answering. "Blocked how?"

"Crowded. Watched. Not usable."

"That is not an answer, young sir. That is three anxieties in a stack."

Owen heard the old habit in himself before he could stop it. "If there was fighting. Or enough people moving at once that the stairwells jammed. Or if the Carrows had half the doors sealed. Or if I had to get below quickly without being seen by anyone who'd think the wrong thing of it."

"Ah," Tallow said. "So. Only the ordinary cheerful possibilities."

He set the repaired spoon aside and reached for another. Around them the kitchen kept going. A tray of peeled carrots thudded down. Two younger elves hurried past carrying soup tureens between them, not glancing at Owen at all. Somewhere near the far hearth a pot boiled over and somebody swore in a mild, concentrated way.

Tallow said, "Miss Sorrell was in earlier. She carried the potato sacks badly because of that leg and then had the manners to look ashamed about it, which was foolish because potatoes have no pride. Miss Hollis put away six catalog ledgers for the library and stopped to dry her spectacles over the pastry oven because she will not admit they fog. Everybody brings something through the kitchen when the house is under strain."

Owen stayed very still.

"I know what you think of me," he said.

"Do you?" Tallow's tone remained light, which only made the rebuke more exact. "Because Tallow thinks many things of you, and some of them do move around."

He took up a spoon so bent it was nearly useless, considered it, and dropped it into a separate bowl.

"The house has had clever boys before," he said. "Clever girls too. Clever is not rare. What the house gets short of, every so often, is children who know the weight of other children while they are being clever. You came asking for routes when there were books. You came asking for routes when there were old stones. Now you come asking for routes because you think the castle may split, and Tallow is meant to clap and say very good, young sir, excellent foresight. But foresight is not the same as care."

The words landed harder than Prue's had because they were smaller. Less generous. Less interested in his soul. Tallow was discussing him as a maintenance problem, a load borne badly.

Owen looked past him at the ovens, at the dough waiting under cloths, at the rack of drying bowls. "If the castle may split," he said quietly, "then the route matters whether or not I deserve it."

Tallow nodded once. "Yes. Better. That is the first sensible thing you've said since coming down those steps."

He wiped his hands on the towel at his belt and hopped down from the stool. Up close the damp in his cuffs had gone grey with flour. He smelled faintly of smoke and washing soda.

"There's a service stair behind the scullery rack," he said. "Not the first rack. Boys always pull the first rack because boys are committed to obviousness. The third. Left side. The hinge sticks in damp weather, so lift before you pull. It goes down past the hot-water mains and the old coal drop. At the bottom you will want the passage with the cracked blue tiles, not the one that smells of lye. If you smell soap too long, you've gone toward laundry and you are an idiot."

Despite himself, Owen said, "Cracked blue tiles. Not lye."

"Then a narrow turn with a drain underfoot. Mind it. The stone sweats there and feet go from under people in a way that embarrasses everybody. You follow that until the wall starts sounding thick on the left and hollow on the right. Count seven iron brackets. At the seventh there is a hatch no taller than your shoulder. The latch is hidden under a lip of mortar because the founders had no trust in anybody's fingers. That gets you into the older run."

"Toward the east side?"

"Toward where the house still remembers itself, yes."

Tallow's face altered a fraction on the last words. Not softer. More tired.

"I won't take you past the mains," he said. "The lower dark bites now. More than it did in winter. It has an appetite on it."

"I wasn't asking you to."

"No. You seldom ask that particular thing. You wait for other creatures to offer it and then behave surprised when they bleed."

Owen shut his eyes for a second. The kitchen noise moved around him, all the labour of feeding a school pretending not to hear. When he opened them Tallow was already at a shelf, reaching up for something.

What he brought down was not a lantern or a map but a heel of yesterday's loaf wrapped in cloth and a little paper twist of salt.

"For later," Tallow said. "Not because you've earned cosseting. Because boys below stairs forget they are animals and then fall over at important moments."

He put the bundle into Owen's hand without ceremony. It was still faintly warm at the centre where the loaf had held its heat.

"Thank you," Owen said.

Tallow gave him a look. "If you break crockery in that lower passage, leave it where it lies till morning. Tallow is not climbing after your guilt in the dark."

It was the nearest thing to kindness Owen had been offered in hours, and even that arrived shaped like a rule.

"Right," he said. "I won't."

Tallow turned back to the spoons. "See that you don't."

Owen stood there another moment, bread and salt in one hand, route beating itself into memory. Around him the kitchens carried on with their hot practical life, and he felt with a sudden, ugly clarity that he had been taking from rooms like this all year under the name of necessity. Food. Passage. Quiet. Interpretation. Warmth when it was offered. He had told himself use was not the same as betrayal because the danger was real. The danger was real. That did not make the ledger clean.

Nobody asked him to stay. Nobody told him to go.

When he left, the ovens were breathing and the spoons on the table made three tidy rows, each one bent to the degree the house could still justify keeping.

* * *

The Hufflepuff common room had gone soft with evening by the time he came back. Firelight, damp shoes by the hearth, the low continuous mutter of revision. Somebody near the far wall was reading notes aloud to himself in a hoarse whisper, getting stuck on the same line every time and beginning again with visible resentment. Two fourth-years were sharing a bowl of stolen treacle tart with the solemn concentration of priests. A pair of socks had been hung too near the fire and were beginning to smell singed.

Ordinary. Not harmless, but ordinary.

Owen stood just inside the round doorway with his notebook under one arm and Tallow's wrapped bread in his pocket and let the room press against him. This was the part Prue had been asking him to admit scale for. Not abstract resistance. Not noble suffering in bulk. This room. Eli Mercer asleep face-down over half-finished History of Magic notes. Mae Doran filing her broken nails with the edge of a ruler because she could not think with roughness catching on parchment. The Hufflepuff clock a minute slow as it had been since October, nobody fixing it because everybody had got used to leaning around the error together.

If he crossed the room and sat, someone would make space. If he asked after the revised watch rota, somebody would answer. If he went looking properly, he could still find the Room of Requirement before curfew. He knew how to get to the Ravenclaw stair without being seen from the main landing. He knew, down to the hinge-creak, the route to every ordinary loyalty still available to him.

He went to the dormitory instead.

The room smelled of wool blanket, soap gone flat in the dish, and the damp mineral note that came in from the lake through cellar stone. His trunk was under the bed. The boards complained when he knelt. He took out the ash-linen notebook, the spare pencil shaved with a knife because sharpeners were unreliable, two lengths of candle, the bit of chalk wrapped in rag, and the cleanest bandage he had left. He added Tallow's bread. Then he stopped with his hand on the latch and looked at what he had made.

It looked, offensively, like preparation. Not obsession. Not panic. A kit.

On the bed opposite, somebody had left a Charms textbook open face-down at a page on shielding practice. A sock hung from the bedpost by one damp toe. Beneath the window sat an abandoned mug with a skin forming over the tea. The room was full of boys expecting tomorrow. Even fear here kept the form of tomorrow: essays, punishments, breakfast, revision, maybe a better day if they were stupid enough or sane enough to keep imagining one.

He sat on the edge of his bed and took out a sheet of parchment. For a long time he did not write. Then, very small, he put down Prue's name.

He stared at it until the letters looked like a copied exercise.

What could he say that was not another demand dressed as disclosure? Warn them, but he could not explain. Trust me, when trust was exactly what he had failed to make possible. I am sorry, which she already knew. Martha's name came to him next without permission, along with the sight of her at lunch slipping bread crust into her pocket for later, saving for scarcity even in a room with food on the table. He thought of the way she had once, in winter, shoved a bowl at him and told him to stop acting like being hungry was a moral refinement. He thought of Prue winding string into her fingers on the lake wall until honesty had cut a mark into her skin.

He turned the parchment over. Blank on both sides now.

Down in the common room someone laughed hard enough to choke. Somebody else told him to shut up. The sound came through the floorboards warm and distorted, human beyond argument.

Owen put the parchment away unwritten.

Then he opened the notebook and copied the route exactly as Tallow had given it, notating the hinge, the tiles, the drain, the iron brackets. Once the directions were down, his hand kept going. He added timings from the cellar stairs, probable choke points if the upper corridors jammed, the likely interval between first alarm and full crowding if the school had to move students quickly. Method came to him with hideous relief. It always had edges. It did not ask whether the thing being done deserved doing. Only whether it would hold.

When the notes were finished he sat with the pencil lifted and listened to the castle.

Above him, footsteps crossed and recrossed. Somewhere deeper on the right a pipe knocked twice, then settled. The lake pressed at the walls in its old slow rhythm. Underneath all that, or in between it, he could feel the other structure he had spent months teaching himself to notice: tension in the older stone, a wrongness not yet motion but preparing for it. The anchor was not a voice. It had no intention he could claim to hear. But it was under strain, and strain had its own texture. He knew it now the way a carpenter knew warp in timber by touch. The school could go on setting essays and baking bread and revising defensive charms right up to the hour something larger used all of that life as load.

Prue had offered him the human bargain. Help here first, then below. It had been a real bargain, not a test. That was what made refusing it so clean. No one was forcing the scale onto him in this room. No one was asking him to prove loyalty by speech. Martha was elsewhere in the castle, angry enough not to want him near her. Prue had walked away on her own feet. Neville barely counted him now except as a problem at the edge of planning. There was still, even so, a route back into ordinary obligation if he wanted it. He could choose to stand upstairs when the thing came. He could choose the school as the whole of the world because it was the world in front of him.

He looked down at his hand. Ink along the side of the finger. Chalk dust in the cut at his knuckle. The old burn in the palm gone pale and shiny where skin had healed wrong. A schoolboy's hand, except for the parts that were not.

Then he closed the notebook, packed the pencil into its spine, and slid the bundle under his bed where he could reach it in the dark.

When he went back through the common room, Mae looked up from her ruler-filed nails and said, "You all right?"

It was the easiest question in the world to answer badly.

"Yeah," he said. "Just tired."

She nodded and went back to her parchment. The room accepted the lie because rooms like this had to.

At the round door he paused. Left would take him toward the corridor that eventually rose to the rest of the school, to watchrooms, whispered plans, other bodies, the whole miserable beloved scale of immediate things. Right would take him past the kitchens, past the heat and the bread smell and the third rack in the scullery, down toward the blue-cracked tiles and the old dark waiting under the house.

He stood long enough that the fire behind him shifted and somebody complained of the draught.

Then he turned right.

Later, if there was one, would judge him or not. The choice itself was smaller and uglier than judgment. It was only a boy in a cellar taking the path he had prepared with his own hands. He went past the warm kitchen wall without stopping, found the service door by touch, and slipped through before he could look back toward the light.
