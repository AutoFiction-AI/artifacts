# Chapter 1

Breakfast still arrived on silver platters. That was part of what made the year feel deranged.

The porridge steamed, the toast racks made their little domestic journeys up and down the Hufflepuff table, and somewhere above the smell of bacon fat and damp wool there floated the ordinary beeswax polish of the Hall itself, as if the castle meant to keep being a school by force of habit. Amycus Carrow stood in the middle aisle with a mug in one fist and a second-year by the back of the neck, and the house-elves still sent out kidneys and tea.

Owen sat three-quarters of the way down the table, where people could borrow the marmalade from him without needing to look at him for long. It was a useful position. He had been in it often enough to know which plates would come back with egg yolk drying on them and which people would pretend they were not still hungry. He shifted the butter dish toward a fourth-year before she had to ask. He moved his own cup out of the line of an elbow. He had become, without ever quite deciding to, competent at surviving rooms that wanted something larger out of everyone in them.

His bag sat between his shoes. The old copy of *A History of Magic* inside it dragged the leather down against his ankle like a brick wrapped in cloth.

Martha Sorrell dropped onto the bench opposite him hard enough to rattle spoons. Her dark plait had half come apart already, and she had one cuff rolled because the other still carried a brown stain from whatever she had been lifting before breakfast. She always ate the burnt side of the toast first, as if disappointment ought to be dealt with early and without ceremony. She tore a blackened edge off now, chewed it, and pointed the rest at him.

"You're coming tonight."

Owen kept his eyes on the marmalade jar. "To what."

"Don't do that."

"I'm asking."

"Neville needs people after supper. Not heroes. Just people with legs and a sense of direction." She reached across, took the knife out of his hand because he was not using it quickly enough, and buttered her toast in three brutal strokes. "We're moving two first-years out of the west stair dorms before the Carrows decide they've had enough sleep for one week."

Owen looked up then, not at her but past her, toward the high table. Snape sat in the middle under the black banners, one white hand laid next to his goblet, not eating. He had the particular stillness of a man who seemed to regard movement as an avoidable error in other people. Alecto was saying something to him. Amycus was still in the aisle with the boy.

"I've got detention," Owen said.

"Detention ends."

"Sometimes it doesn't, actually."

Martha snorted once through her nose. "Fine. Then after. We need runners."

"Martha."

"What."

"If they're watching the west stair dorms, they'll be watching the corridors off them. More bodies don't always improve a corridor."

She stared at him for a beat, then looked down at his bag. "You've brought Binns to breakfast."

He should not have. He had meant to return the book before anyone noticed he still had it. Yesterday he had taken it from the shelf in Binns's classroom because the newer editions were all missing the older index tables, and because Binns himself had drifted through the end of the lesson half a yard above the floor and had not noticed a live boy borrowing anything. It was a classroom copy from 1878, stout-backed and shedding at the hinges, with the title barely readable under a skin of old finger grease. Too old to be useful for the O.W.L. syllabus, really. Too old to be worth the trouble of carrying. He had brought it down intending to leave it on the return trolley outside History of Magic.

"I'm taking it back," he said.

"You said that yesterday."

"I was delayed."

"By what."

He considered, briefly, telling the truth, which was that he had sat on his bed and read a chapter on twelfth-century wand tariffs because it was easier than going where Neville had told half the school to go. "Homework."

Martha laughed without any amusement in it. "That isn't even a lie with proper shoes on."

Amycus jerked the boy in the aisle around to face the room. He was a small Ravenclaw, maybe twelve, with one lace loose and his fringe wet where tea or sweat had got into it. Amycus's fingers nearly disappeared around the back of the child's neck.

"Look at him," Amycus said. His voice carried easily. He had the sort of coarse cheer that made every sentence sound like it might end in a slap. "Go on. It's breakfast, not a funeral. Look."

Nobody looked quickly enough for him, so he shoved the boy's face down into the nearest bowl of porridge. The bowl jumped, tipped, and rolled in a white arc across the table runner. There was a wet choking sound. A few people flinched. Owen did not. Flinching where Amycus could see was just participation from the other side.

Amycus hauled the boy upright again. Porridge clung to his eyebrow and one ear. "Mr Penrose thinks muttering counts as courage. Mr Penrose thinks if he says filthy little things under his breath in my lesson, perhaps I won't hear them."

The boy's mouth worked. Oatmeal and spit hung in a thread from his lower lip. He was trying not to cough.

"Tell them," Amycus said.

"I didn't say anything," the boy whispered.

Amycus smiled. He had a chipped front tooth he worried with his tongue when he was pleased. "Wrong answer."

He slapped the side of the boy's head, not with enough force to floor him but enough to ring the sound through the Hall. The porridge on the boy's ear flecked onto the table and somebody's sleeve. Amycus turned him by the shoulder and marched him toward the staff dais with one hand still sunk into his robes as if he were carrying a sack that leaked.

There was a long moment after, a long soft scrape of cutlery starting again all over the room because the alternatives were worse.

Martha put her toast down. "This is what I mean."

Owen kept his voice low. "I know what you mean."

"Do you."

"Yes."

"Because from where I'm sitting it looks a lot like you mean to be very sensible till the year is over and then discover there wasn't one."

He should have said something better. Something human-sized, at least. What came out instead was, "If I get caught in a corridor with two first-years and Neville, that doesn't improve anything."

"It improves it for the first-years."

"Until it doesn't."

She closed her eyes briefly. When she opened them again the anger had gone flatter, which was worse. "You always do this. You answer the bit of the sentence that lets you get away."

Owen looked down at the table. Someone had left a ring of tea around the base of the milk jug. His own thumb moved toward it with the reflexive urge to wipe it clean.

"Tonight," Martha said, but there was less demand in it now and more disgust, as if she already knew what his answer was going to be and was testing whether he knew as well. "If you change your mind, seventh-floor tapestry. After supper. If you don't, then at least stop carrying that corpse of a textbook about like it can excuse you."

Amycus came back down the aisle still drinking from his mug. He paused near the Hufflepuff table long enough for the smell of stale drink under his breath to reach them. His little eyes moved over the plates, the faces, the hands. Owen slid one hand off the table and hooked it through the strap of his bag.

"Phipps," Amycus said.

Every part of Owen that could tighten did so quietly.

"Yes, Professor."

"Library again tonight. Pince says you stack straight and don't whine. Don't make a liar of the woman. She has one of those faces already."

A few people laughed because not laughing would have been visible. Amycus gave the table one satisfied glance, then moved on.

Martha watched him go. "There. Your exciting life with books."

"I didn't ask for detention."

"No. You ask for the sort of life that makes detention feel like a personality." She stood, gathered her satchel, and then, because she was still Martha and not only what the year kept making her into, pushed the butter dish back within his reach before she left. "Eat something."

He said, "I'm eating."

She pointed at his untouched eggs. "That isn't the same thing."

Then she was gone into the standing scrape of benches and robes.

Owen waited until the table had started to break apart around him. He pulled the old History of Magic volume half out of his bag and looked at the cracked board, the faded classroom stamp, the brittle threads showing at the spine. Returning it would have been the tidy thing. Returning it would have been what a boy did when he meant to keep the rest of his life containable.

He slid it instead into the deep inner pocket of his robe, where the weight of it sat against his ribs like concealed contraband, and finished his tea before it went cold.

* * *

Library detention would have been less hateful if the library had been allowed to remain itself.

It had not. The north stacks still smelled of the blast from four nights ago: hot stone, singed paste, the sour dust that came out of old bindings when they had been struck hard enough to remember they were made of hide and glue. One entire bay leaned two inches out of true. A cracked atlas case had been roped off with red cord that trembled whenever anyone walked past. The high windows on that side of the room showed a black September evening pressing itself flat against the glass.

Mrs Pince patrolled the damaged aisles like an insult given legs. Her bun had been pinned so tight that nothing about it admitted weather, and every few minutes she bit down on a clove pastille and made the sound of a drawer shutting. The blast had cost her three front ranks of shelving, seven catalogue slips, a ladder, and, in her own estimate, civilization.

"Not there, Mr Phipps," she snapped as he reached for a folio with a split board. "That is Balkan herbals. You are holding punitive edicts. Unless the Ministry has merged botany with stupidity in the last hour."

He shifted the armful to the shelf she wanted.

"Sorry."

"You are. Put them flush."

He put them flush.

Amycus had done the damage himself, according to the whisper that had gone round Hufflepuff, though nobody agreed on why. The version Owen liked best held that he had attempted to blast a hidden packet of seditious leaflets and instead pulverized a cabinet of seventeenth-century border charters because he had mistaken calfskin for treason. The version most likely to be true was simpler and stupider: he had been angry, and the books had been there.

Pince had set Owen to the repair edge of the mess because he was, as Amycus had advertised, good at stacking straight and not whining. He worked with his sleeves rolled to the forearms, sorting scorched bindings from warped ones, brushing plaster grit out of hinges, carrying anything truly ruined to a trolley that looked too small for the amount of damage it was being asked to dignify. The labor would have been almost peaceful if it had not taken place inside occupation and under a woman who regarded breathing as a form of theft when done too noisily near manuscripts.

The old Binns copy pressed against his side each time he bent.

He waited until Pince had gone three aisles over to hiss at a sixth-year from Slytherin about index cards. Then he set down his stack, crouched in the shadow of the leaning atlas case, and slipped the book out under cover of the shelf.

It was worse in this light. The brown cloth had gone silky from handling generations ago and then rough again where damp had once got in. Professor Binns's classroom stamp sat on the inside board above a date that made Owen blink and look twice: 1878, as he had remembered, though the print line below carried the sort of fussy publisher's address modern school texts had long since abandoned. Several pages had been mended with old translucent strips that had yellowed to the colour of weak broth. It smelled faintly of dust, lamp smoke, and the cold cupboards of dead classrooms.

He had been using it for the index tables, nothing more. The older editions still admitted that magic had once possessed local histories and administrative embarrassments, which the modern school version preferred to flatten into proper nouns and dates. Binns lectured from any edition as if all of them were equally dead, but Owen had found, not for the first time, that the margins of the older books contained the things teachers no longer wanted to look after.

He turned to the section he had left folded with a bit of string: late medieval consolidations, educational charters, the slow swallowing of dangerous private collections into institutional libraries. Boring on purpose. Safe. Perfect detention reading.

One of the footnote marks near the bottom of the page had bled slightly into the paper as if the type had once been set too hard. He followed it down.

At first he thought he was misreading the line because of the light and the dust in his eyes. He bent closer.

*One trunk of covenant-restricted leaves remained at Hogwarts library, North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement.*

He read it again.

Then a third time, slower, with his thumb braced in the gutter.

North Stacks C-13.

The present library did not have anything called North Stacks C-13. It had History Aisle Four, Restricted Alcove Two, Charms Overspill, Oversize Reference, and a maddening modern system of floating brass labels that drifted half an inch in front of the shelves like well-brought-up ghosts. Pince referred to areas by subject or by who was forbidden from them. Nobody in school spoke in lettered north-stack shelf marks unless they were quoting something older than their grandparents.

Which, Owen thought, he currently was.

"Mr Phipps."

He shut the book at once and nearly trapped his own finger in it.

Pince stood at the end of the aisle with two cracked periodicals in her hand. "If you have found a way to restore collapsed shelving by staring at a school text, you may publish the method after you finish the actual work."

"Sorry."

"You keep saying that as if repetition improves the article."

He rose, slid the Binns copy inside his robe again, and took the periodicals from her.

Pince's mouth pinched. "And stay out of the Restricted side. I am not spending my evening explaining to Professor Carrow why one of my detentions has lost track of his own hierarchy."

"I wasn't going there."

"No?" She glanced once at the pocket where the outline of the book still showed through the cloth. "Then it is fortunate for us both that I did not ask why you are reading before curfew instead of after it."

She moved on. Owen stood with the cracked journals in his hands until the blood returned properly to his fingers.

The footnote kept reopening in his head. Not because it promised treasure. He was fifteen, frightened often, and not stupid enough to assume that anything hidden in Hogwarts under the present administration would improve his life on contact. It was the concreteness of it that caught him. Not some vague founder legend. Not a mystical whisper about lost knowledge. A shelf mark. A direction. The dead flat tone of institutional storage.

North Stacks C-13.

The blast damage was in the north stacks.

He put the journals away wrong on the first attempt and had to pull them out again before Pince saw.

After that the detention changed shape around him. The work did not get harder, exactly, but it ceased to fill the available space inside his head. He noticed the angles of the damaged shelving, the patches where old stone showed through behind ripped backing boards, the way one bay had been shoved sideways far enough to expose a strip of wall that had not seen light in years. Once, carrying a stack of warped indexes, he caught sight of a cut mark behind the leaning case, something like a carved numeral under the drift of plaster dust. Pince barked his name before he could angle in close enough to be sure.

At half past eight the clock near the entrance gave its discreet silver cough. Pince dismissed the Slytherin sixth-year with a threat about adhesive paste. She kept Owen another quarter-hour because his trolley, she said, had been arranged by a person with a moral allergy to order.

By the time she finally snapped, "Go, then, before your House invents a need for you," the corridors beyond the library had already begun to empty into curfew.

Owen said thank you, because one could say thank you to Mrs Pince without danger and to very few other authorities in the school, shouldered his bag, and walked out at an even pace.

At the end of the main aisle he stopped.

The sensible thing was obvious. Curfew, cellar, bed. If Martha had meant what she said, Neville's people would already be shifting first-years or risking themselves or both. Owen could still choose to go. He knew the tapestry she had named. He knew, with painful exactness, how disappointed she would look when he did not appear.

He stood under the library arch with the old book heavy against his ribs and listened to the quiet of the north side of the room.

Then he turned back.

* * *

The damaged section lay beyond the rope and two warning placards, one of them new enough that the ink still smelled fresh. Owen ducked under both. His shoes made almost no sound on the carpet runner there; Pince preferred the soft hush because it let her hear page-turning from offensive distances. Tonight it let him hear other things too: the small continual settling of a wounded room, a splinter shifting somewhere high above, the faint chink of chain from the Restricted cases further in.

He kept his wand low and lit only enough of it to move.

Up close, the blast had been clumsier than it had looked from the safe aisles. A shelf support had cracked clean through and then held anyway, which gave the whole bay a crooked stubbornness Owen recognized from some of the boys in Hufflepuff when they had decided not to cry in public. Plaster had blown off the wall in pale scabs. The backboard behind one history run had torn loose at the nails and curled outward.

He set down his bag and leaned in.

There it was: not floating brass, not modern ink, but a number cut straight into the stone pier behind the shelves.

C-11.

The carving had been done deep, meant to outlast labels and librarians alike. Dust sat in the grooves. Owen rubbed it with the side of his thumb and came away white.

He moved sideways. The next pier showed C-12, half hidden behind a drooping row of dislodged shelf pegs. Beyond that the leaning atlas case blocked the wall entirely.

North Stacks C-13.

His mouth had gone dry. He glanced back toward the library doors. Nothing. Only darkness and the far green glimmer of banked lamps at the desk.

He squeezed into the wedge between the atlas case and the wall. The space smelled of cracked leather, stone chill, and the old mouse-dust scent of untouched places. The case had shifted on the blast and jammed itself skewed against the pier. He had to turn one shoulder, then the other, and suck in enough stomach to slide another six inches. Dust dragged at his robe. Something with gilt edges dug into his hip.

At the far side, where the case had pulled away from the next section of wall, the plaster had gone completely. Bare stone showed under a drift of powdered mortar and splintered board.

He lifted the wand a little.

C-13.

Not merely a mark. A seam ran down beside it, hair-thin but deliberate, the sort of line no modern library wall should have had. The shelf in front of it had been built later, fixed across older work. Even in the poor light he could see the difference in material: the external facing smoother, school-century carpentry nailed over older blocks cut closer and heavier. Modern stacks sitting on top of a previous arrangement. A hidden run, or the bricked mouth of one.

The footnote had not been decorative. It had been an address.

Somewhere out in the corridor a bell rang once for the late edge of curfew. Owen flinched then, unable not to. He killed the wandlight and stood in the dark gap listening, breath held high in his throat. No footsteps. No Pince. No Amycus lurching in to make a moral lesson out of him.

When he lit the wand again he crouched lower and touched the seam beside the carving. The stone was colder there than the exposed pier. A thread of air moved through it and over his knuckles, stale and mineral and faintly metallic, as if the wall behind it enclosed something that had not been asked to share itself in a very long time.

He should have gone back at once. He knew that. Not because it was forbidden, though it obviously was, and not because Martha was right, though she probably was about several things at once. He should have gone because this was exactly the sort of opening a person like him was meant to leave alone. Older boys with nerves or talent or a martyr's appetite found hidden places in wartime schools. They brought back contraband, gossip, injuries, a story worth telling somebody else in a whisper. Owen liked indexes. Owen liked predictable shelves. Owen had spent the whole term meaning, as hard as he could, not to become noticeable to anyone who mattered.

But the line in the book had existed. The carved marks had existed. And now the wall itself had answered with architecture.

He pulled the Binns copy from inside his robe, braced it on his knee, and checked the footnote one more time in the wandlight. The paper shook slightly, whether from the draught or his own hand he could not tell.

North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement.

Sealed, then. Not gone.

Some part of him, a part he usually kept fed on timetable and caution, made one last competent effort. He could tell Pince in the morning that the damaged wall exposed obsolete shelf marks. He could mention it to a prefect. He could even, absurdly, mention it to Martha and let the secret become communal at once, which would almost certainly save him from doing anything stupid alone.

Instead he memorized the spacing of the stones, the height of the seam, the way the atlas case had jammed and how much room there was behind it for a boy his size and not much larger. He put the book away. He picked up his bag.

On the walk back to Hufflepuff he did not think about Neville's tapestry or the first-years or even Amycus, though Amycus's laugh carried once from a distant stair and made the hair rise briefly on Owen's arms. He thought about service routes, old shelf plans, whether the library had ever kept retired maps, whether house-elves knew the underside of the stacks, whether a seal by founders' agreement would be made to stop charms or only hands.

By the time the barrel entrance admitted him and the smell of warm earth and supper scraps closed around him, he was no longer simply getting through the year.

He was trying to work out how to get back to North Stacks C-13 without being seen.
