# Chapter 12

Owen knelt in the black mouth of a service recess with his notebook open on his thigh and listened to Martha hit the wall again.

The wrecked corridor still smelled faintly of burst cisterns and wet plaster from the sabotage, though the house-elves had got the worst of the flood out days ago. Damp had dried in dirty crescents on the skirting. A cracked suit of armor lay in sections along the flagstones where it had been pulled apart and never properly put back: gauntlets by the first window, breastplate by the third door, one leg propped against a stack of boxed school records that had gone soft at the corners. The far stair should have led down toward the moving portraits and then the turn for the Trophy Room. Instead the damaged thresholds folded the passage back into itself. Anyone who took the last door arrived again by the broken armor and the white scuff of Amycus's boot.

"Up," Amycus said. His voice carried lazily in the empty hour after curfew. "Don't lie there and make me fetch you. If I have to fetch you, I'll do it badly."

Metal grated on stone. Martha got both hands under the breastplate and dragged it in against her knees. Even from the recess Owen could hear her breath catching. She had lost her plait halfway through the detention. Dark hair stuck to her face and neck with sweat. One sleeve was wet through at the cuff where she had gone down in the old toilet water on her second pass. There was blood on one shin now, not much in amount but bright enough to look separate from everything else in the corridor.

"It weighs the same every time," Amycus said. "Thought Hufflepuffs were sturdy."

"Thought teachers could count," Martha said, and her voice came out rougher than usual but still hers, practical and annoyed before it was frightened. "You've had me carry it six times."

Amycus laughed through his nose. He had a habit of working at the side of one back tooth with his thumbnail when he was enjoying himself. Owen could see the glint of the nail as he leaned in the middle of the corridor with his wand tucked under one arm like a cane. He smelled of sweat, old wine, and whatever over-spiced meat the staff had eaten for supper. "Then you ought to be getting better at it."

He flicked his wand. The breastplate jumped in Martha's hands and slammed hard into her thigh. She swore, bent double, nearly lost hold of it, then straightened because he was waiting for her not to.

Owen looked down at the page under his left thumb.

He had spent three nights preparing it in smaller and smaller claims until the line would hold. Not an escape charm. Not rescue. Nothing as clean as that. The First Art would not answer to a wish dressed up as certainty. This was a local amendment written over a copied threshold table from the lower castle, soot and chalk and a sting of his own blood worked into the linen surface until the notation darkened and sat still. If he pressed the page to the third lintel, where the corridor's broken return had the oldest stone under it, and let his palm burn open against the lower hinge, the passage should answer to its shortest true course for perhaps forty seconds. A minute if the castle was merciful. Long enough for a body already pointed toward the stair to go down it instead of being spat back into Amycus's hands.

That was all.

That was not all.

The same page, spent on the lower maintenance door off the kitchen runs, might finally persuade the sealed turn beneath the East Undercroft to answer as a door again instead of a wall. He had the route, the copied fractions, the mortar seam, the half-legible margin from the Leaves that only made sense when laid against founder stone. He had the corridor above empty because Amycus had cleared everyone off for this little show. He had, for once, enough quiet to try the deeper work without tripping over prefect patrols or other students wanting warmth or bread or honesty he had no intention of giving them.

If he fired the amendment here, the ward scar would ring through two floors of castle. Snape would feel it if he was awake. Owen's wand would go wrong for hours afterward. The lower door would keep being a wall. The route would wait, yes, but routes did not merely wait. They got noticed. They got bricked. They got cleaned, catalogued, assigned some tedious school use that made secret work impossible. Old opportunities died by inches.

Martha lurched past the second window with the breastplate clanging against her shins.

Amycus had turned the corridor into a lesson. That was the ugliest part of him. He never wanted pain by itself if he could help it. Pain was better when it taught the room what he wanted the room to learn. Two fourth-year Slytherins had been made to watch the first half hour from beyond the rope at the stair mouth. One of them, a sandy boy with a runny winter nose and a cardigan sleeve unraveling at the wrist, had tried not to look and been struck so hard round the ear he fell into the wall. Amycus had sent them off only after he got bored with their faces and decided Martha alone would do.

Now he was making her carry the armor from one end of the broken corridor to the other, through a door that kept returning her, and every time the space cheated he acted as if she had failed some simple instruction. When she slowed he hit her calves with short blunt jinxes that made the muscles jump under the skin. When she slipped he made her start again from the armor's scattered heap instead of where she had fallen. Once, twenty minutes before, he had kicked the helmet at her and split her lip when she did not catch it properly.

Owen knew all this because he had been there for twenty minutes and had not left.

He could keep the facts narrow if he kept the language narrow. The page would not save Martha for good. At best it would get her one clean run at the stair. Amycus might catch her on the landing. She might not understand quickly enough what had changed. Snape might come. The corridor might over-answer and unfasten the wrong turn. Any of those things could happen, and if one of them did the only result would be more pain, more scrutiny, less chance of reaching the anchor before the castle's damage worsened again.

All of that stood up well enough in the mind.

It did not change the smaller, meaner fact that the page under his thumb would have helped her tonight.

Martha got to the far door. She shifted the weight of the breastplate, boot sliding on grit, and tried to shoulder it through with the last of her balance. The door took her. For one second she was gone from the corridor. Then the broken threshold returned her neatly to the starting stretch by the first window.

The breastplate slipped from her hands and crushed down across her foot. She made a short animal sound and bit it off.

Amycus actually clapped once. "There she is. We were losing our rhythm."

She bent to lift the thing again. One of her hands failed. He had not seen that happen before. Her right hand opened and closed uselessly over the rim as if the fingers belonged to somebody standing next to her. She stared at them, annoyed, then tucked the bad hand against the metal and levered the weight up against her hip with her forearm instead.

He could end this pass. He could do it now, while Amycus was still talking.

His palm itched under the old burn. His teeth hurt with the nearness of worked stone. The page was ready. The copied line had settled. He even had the third lintel chosen already, had measured the cracks around it with his eye when Amycus first hauled Martha into the corridor and slammed the warded door behind them.

Martha dragged the breastplate forward, limping properly now.

"Come on," Amycus said. "You've still got the stairs to miss."

She reached the place in the corridor where the service recess ran behind the wall in a slit too narrow for anyone but a child or a starved older student to use comfortably. Owen had his body pulled back from the gap, but not far enough. Her head turned on the sound of the armor scraping. Her eyes snagged on the black seam in the panelling and found him at once.

She did not stop. She did not call out. Her expression changed less than he would have thought possible. There was a flicker of recognition, then something flatter and much colder than surprise. She shifted the breastplate higher, took one more limping step, and looked away from him so she could see where she was putting her feet.

Amycus had not noticed. He was digging again at his molar with the nail of his thumb.

Owen could still have used the page.

He waited for the heat of shame or panic or decency to make the choice for him. Nothing obliged. There was only the corridor, the notation, the weight of the founder seam below the kitchens like a second pulse under the stone. He thought, with the ugly exactness that came to him when he wanted to feel less instead of more, that Martha was conscious, still upright, still capable of taking the stair if given it, and that a leg could be mended better than a lost route.

He folded the page once, very carefully, so the wet-dark line in the corner would not crack, and slid it back under the oilcloth.

Amycus said, "Again," and Martha, without looking toward the recess this time, bent for the helmet.

Owen backed out of the slit, turned into the service run, and went down.

The kitchen-side passages were warm only by comparison with the rest of the castle. Steam from the scullery pipes slicked the stone. Farther in, past the last place house-elves stacked fireblackened pans, the warmth died and old pressure took over. He moved with his lamp hooded and low, counting the turns he had already marked in pencil on the inside cover of his notebook. Two left under the cask room. One descent by the split step. Keep his hand off the west wall where the mortar sweated in cold weather and left salt in the skin. The work steadied him because it always did. The work asked for sequence and weight and what answered to what. No part of it required that he return to the corridor above and act like a person whose first duty was to the nearest body.

At the maintenance door below the East Undercroft he knelt again.

The door had no handle left, only the memory of one, a long vertical discoloration in the stone where metal had once fixed into a slot. He laid the prepared page against that mark and put his burned palm over the center. Pain arrived cleanly, as if the old coffer had woken under his skin. Not heat this time. Pressure, then a sharp inward pull that seemed to take the hinges through his wrist and up into the roots of his teeth. The copied line in the page went slick and alive beneath his hand.

He said the condition exactly as he had narrowed it, no bigger than necessary. The linen darkened. The stone gave a little under his palm.

Above him, somewhere very far away and still far too near, there came a muffled clang that might have been armor or a body or both.

He did not stop.

The seam of the door shivered. Dust dropped in a thread from the lintel. A strip of black opened, no wider than two fingers, and colder air licked across the back of his hand carrying lake damp and the old mineral smell from deeper under the castle.

For one second relief was stronger than anything else in him.

It was working. The page had held. The lower turn had been answering as wall for months and now, under the copied line and founder pressure, it was willing to remember otherwise. Not open yet, not fully, but enough to prove the route real. Enough to keep going.

He breathed once, twice, feeling the ache race up his arm. The castle rang around the amendment in a pitch only half sound. It was softer than the contact working in November had been. Better controlled. Lower down. He could tell himself that mattered.

By the time the linen page went slack and dead under his hand he had widened the gap to the width of his fist. Beyond it lay a descending dark and the cold wet smell of stone that had not seen ordinary school air in centuries.

He sat back on his heels with blood from the reopened burn creeping into his lifelines and thought, not for the first time and not for the last, that this was what justified everything.

When he climbed up an hour later, the corridor above was empty. Amycus had gone. The rope had been taken down. The armor lay in a different pattern now, as if the pieces had been kicked apart for sport. There was a smear of blood low on the wall by the third door where a hand had caught and slipped.

Owen did not touch it.

***

The kitchens had gone quiet by the time he found Martha.

Most nights there was always some clatter left in them: spoons in sinks, pans stacked to dry, the low collective muttering of house-elves who worked as if silence itself were a spill needing mopping. Tonight the big ovens only hissed. A skin had formed on the pan of onion broth nearest the banked fire. Tallow stood at the central table sorting bent forks into two neat piles, his singed left ear tied up with a strip of checked pudding cloth so it would not brush his shoulder. He looked up once when Owen came in and then looked down again at the cutlery as if deciding whether Owen belonged with the salvageable things or the scrap.

Martha sat on a flour bin by the wall with one leg stretched out on an overturned crate. Madam Pomfrey had done what she could and no more. The knee was bandaged thickly under a borrowed pair of cut-off pajama bottoms, but the joint beneath the wrapping was still grotesquely swollen. There were green-brown bruises already climbing her calf where Amycus's jinxes had landed. Somebody had cleaned the blood off her lip. Her hair had been gathered into the first failed stages of a plait and then abandoned, so half of it hung loose over one shoulder. She held a bowl in her left hand because the right was strapped from knuckle to wrist and did not want to close properly.

She was peering into the broth with faint irritation.

"It's got skin on it," she said to Tallow. "I know that's not your fault. I'm still saying it."

Tallow flicked one bent fork into the good pile. "Miss Sorrell is welcome to complain after she drinks it. Complaining before drinking it is vanity."

"It's not vanity if I'm correct."

"It often is."

She gave a short laugh that caught in the middle and turned into a wince. That hurt Owen more than the sight of the knee had done. Not because the pain was worse. Because the laugh was ordinary. Because she was still doing it.

Tallow took a second bowl from the hob with a folded cloth and set it on the far edge of the table without looking at Owen. "If Mr. Phipps has come to stand in a doorway till the draft does the rest, Tallow asks him to choose a hobby less damp."

"Thanks," Owen said.

Tallow made a small noise that might have meant nothing. He went on sorting forks.

Owen took the bowl and sat on the bench opposite Martha because there was nowhere else to sit that would not make a point of itself. The broth smelled of onion and pepper and a little too much bay. Heat rose into his face from it. He had not realized how cold the undercroft had left him until then.

Martha sipped carefully, swallowed, and only then looked at him.

"So," she said.

"So."

"How far did you get?"

He did not pretend not to understand her. "Past the maintenance door."

She nodded once, as if confirming a sum she had already done. "Right."

Tallow's hands did not stop moving over the bent cutlery, but the sound of metal on metal got very exact.

Owen put his bowl down. "Pomfrey said your knee should mend."

"That's nice of it."

"She said the hand's mostly swelling and strain."

"Amazing. Have you got any more medical miracles, or was that the lot?"

"Martha."

"No, go on. You seem to have come with notes."

He had, in a sense. He could hear himself choosing smaller claims before he made them. "I had a line ready."

"Yes," she said. "I saw."

That should not have surprised him, but it did. The cold in him moved lower.

Martha shifted the bowl from one palm to the other because the heat was getting into the bad hand through the wrapping. "I wasn't sure at first if it was you or just a shape in the wall. Then you moved your thumb. I know your stupid hands."

He looked at his fingers. Ink in the cuticles. Dust ground into the creases. A little dried blood at the heel of the palm where the burn had split again.

"It wasn't certain," he said. "I could have straightened the corridor for a bit. Maybe. If the lintel answered properly. But if I'd done it there, with Amycus right on top of it, the scar would've rung half the floor. Snape's already watching. And the same line was the only stable version I had for the lower door. I needed-"

"I know what you needed."

"You don't know the whole-"

"I know the size of a corridor," she said, still very calm. "I know the size of my knee. I know you had something in your pocket that would've changed the room and you left it there because you wanted the other thing more. That's plenty, Owen."

He tried again because method was easier than silence. "If I'd spent it there, the route below would've stayed shut. I had the ratios from the table at last. I had the quiet for it. Amycus had cleared everyone off. I couldn't guarantee the corridor would've held long enough for you to get clean away, and if it went wrong you could've been hurt worse than-"

She laughed once, not because it was funny. "Worse than what, exactly?"

The question sat between them with the kitchen heat on it.

He looked at the swollen knee and failed to answer.

"No, really," she said. "I'm curious now. Worse than that? Worse than him dropping the breastplate on my foot because I couldn't feel my hand? Worse than being sent back through the same bloody door till I started knowing which patch of wall I was going to hit before I hit it? Give me the chart. I want to see where the red line is."

Tallow turned away to the oven, lifted the broth pan, and gave them privacy so pointedly that it was almost indecent.

Owen said, "I'm not saying it was fine."

"You are, a bit."

"I'm saying I couldn't do both."

"No." Martha put the bowl in her lap and held it there with both forearms because the bad hand would not trust itself. "See, that's where you start cheating. You keep saying both like the choice was me on one side and the whole world on the other. It wasn't. It was me in a corridor and the work you wanted to be doing instead. Those aren't the same thing."

"The work is bigger than-"

"Stop." The word was not loud, but it cut him off cleaner than shouting would have. "Don't hide in that. Bigger isn't magic. Bigger isn't holy. Bigger doesn't wash your hands for you."

He stared at the steam coming off his untouched broth. The skin on it had started to wrinkle.

"I know how it sounds," he said.

"I know how it is."

There was a long enough silence for the oven to give a settling tick and for a pan somewhere near the sinks to drip twice into another pan. Martha shifted her leg a fraction and shut her eyes against the pain before opening them again.

"If you'd frozen," she said, "that would have been one thing. If you'd run because you were scared and then hated yourself after, that would at least be ordinary. If you'd not been there at all, maybe I could tell myself you hadn't known. But you were there. You worked it through. You looked at me and kept the page for later."

"It wasn't later. It was immediately after."

"That's not helping."

He shut his mouth.

Martha drank more broth, because even now she was the sort of person who would finish hot food before it went to waste, and wiped her upper lip on the back of her wrist where the split had started bleeding again. "Do you know what I kept thinking in there?" she asked.

He shook his head.

"I kept thinking you'd do something annoyingly technical and then apologize for interrupting me while I was being punished." Her mouth twitched once. "That sounds stupid when I say it out loud, but there it is."

"It's not stupid."

"No. It was stupid, actually. That's on me." She adjusted the bowl again. "I've kept making you smaller than the thing you're turning into because I liked the old size of you. I thought if I dragged you to enough meetings and enough meals and enough daft jobs you might stay where people could reach you."

He felt the shape of the service recess again under his shoulders, the black slit in the wall, her eyes finding him and then going flat.

"Martha-"

"No, listen now. You don't get to tidy this up into fear or bad timing or you being secretly noble in a way I can't quite see. It isn't that. You've gone wrong in the scale of things. Ordinary-sized human pain doesn't weigh enough for you anymore if there's a diagram under your hand. That's what tonight was. Not cowardice. Not confusion. Just that."

The words were plain. That made them worse. If she had shouted, if she had called him monstrous or useless or cruel, he could have argued with the shape of it in his own head. But this was smaller and more exact. This he could fit against what had happened and find no slack in.

"I was trying to get to something that might matter for more than one corridor," he said, hearing as he spoke how little the sentence had in it besides himself.

"And I was in one corridor." Martha leaned back against the wall, suddenly pale under the kitchen heat. "People are always in one corridor, Owen. Or one bed, one room, one ditch, one queue for soup, whatever it is. That's where life happens. If your great important thing keeps asking you to step round the person in front of you, then it isn't making you better at seeing. It's teaching you not to look."

Tallow came back with a small plate of bread crusts rubbed with garlic and set it by Martha's good elbow. He did not set any by Owen. "Miss Sorrell needs salt," he said to the room at large. "Arguments make young creatures leak."

"Thanks," Martha muttered, and picked up a crust awkwardly between her left thumb and forefinger.

Owen almost said thank you too and stopped because the words would have sounded like theft.

Martha chewed, swallowed, and then said, more tiredly than before, "I can't keep doing this with you."

He knew what she meant and still asked, "Doing what?"

"Being the bit of ordinary life you visit between descents." She looked at him straight on. There were shadows under her eyes that had not been there in September. "I'm not your reminder to eat. I'm not your excuse for still being at our table. I'm not going to keep hauling you by the sleeve and pretending you'll come back if I say the right thing plain enough."

"You don't have to haul me anywhere."

"Good. Then we agree."

He felt the answer land before she spoke it.

"We're finished, then," she said. Not dramatic. Not vicious. The sort of sentence someone used when a chore had gone on too long and there was no point wasting more soap on it. "Properly. Sit somewhere else. Work with whoever still wants to work with you. If Neville asks why I don't send for you anymore, I'll tell him myself."

He sat very still. The bench under him had a wobble in one leg. He had never noticed that before.

"I'm sorry," he said, and because even now part of him wanted precision, he added, "I know that isn't the useful bit."

Martha nodded. "No. It isn't."

She turned her attention back to the bread crust in her hand, then to the bowl, blowing the skin aside with one tired breath before taking another sip. She did not dismiss him. She did not need to. The whole kitchen had already adjusted around a fact that no longer required discussion. Tallow moved behind the table with his careful cutlery and did not once ask Owen if he wanted more broth. The ovens hissed. Somewhere in the far pantry a stack of plates settled against itself.

Owen looked at Martha's bowl, at the smear of butter on the crust she had not quite managed to spread evenly with one hand, and thought absurdly of all the small things she used to notice first: whether the porridge had caught at the bottom, whether somebody had left a scarf on a damp peg, whether he'd gone a whole day on tea and crusts and called it enough. She had always dragged the world back down to touch and food and bruises before it could float off into talk. He had liked that in her because it saved him trouble. He saw now what else it had been.

He stood.

Neither Martha nor Tallow looked up.

His own bowl sat full on the table, film forming over the broth. He left it there and went out through the scullery door into the colder dark beyond the kitchens, and for the first time since he had found the footnote in the old Binns edition he understood that a piece of work could go forward exactly as planned and still leave something ruined in its wake that no later correctness would mend.
