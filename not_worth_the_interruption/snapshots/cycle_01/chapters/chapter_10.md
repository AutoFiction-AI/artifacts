# Chapter 10

By midnight the scullery off the kitchens smelled of wet stone, old onions, and lamp oil going sharp in the heat. Owen had the Grinlow Leaves spread on an upturned flour bin because it was flatter than the table, and flatter mattered. The page he wanted would hold for perhaps six breaths if his burned right palm pinned the lower margin and the lamp sat exactly level with the nick in the opposite wall. On the seventh breath the ink went pale and the lines slid back under the vellum as if the manuscript had thought better of showing him anything.

He had been stuck there three nights.

His ash-linen notebook lay open beside it, dense with cramped tries and cross-outs. He had written that the quarter mark repeated at ninety degrees, that the thing was not stars but indexed by stars, not a calendar. He had written the last sentence four times in four different places as if changing where it sat might make it truer.

The kitchens beyond the scullery still muttered at low volume: pan bottoms ringing softly, water thrown into a basin, one of the younger elves laughing and then stopping at once when an older voice shushed him. The ordinary work of the castle went on under the occupation because somebody had to scrub the roasting tins and bank the ovens even when Amycus Carrow was upstairs teaching children to scream properly.

Footsteps came along the service passage. They were not Tallow's quick soft slap but human footsteps, cautious and tired.

Prue came in sideways through the narrow door with a roll of transparencies under one arm and a folded bundle of charts clamped to her chest. She had not bothered with gloves. She never did when paper was involved; wool caught on thin pages and drove her mad. Her fingers were pink with cold and already marked with blue ink near the nails. Over her school jumper she wore an old bottle-green cardigan with one wooden replacement button at the throat, and she worried that button between two fingers while she looked at him and the book and did not yet put anything down.

"Neville said you wanted the clear ones," she said.

"I did."

"That was his charitable summary."

Owen nodded once. "I couldn't get the projection to stay put."

She set the bundle on the bin beside the manuscript without sitting. Her spectacles were smudged at one corner. "Martha's still limping."

The sentence landed in the room and stayed there. Owen looked at the page because the page at least obeyed some kind of rule.

"I know," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yes."

She let out a breath through her nose. "Right."

Neither of them said sorry. It would have been too early for a useful apology and too late for a good one.

Tallow slipped in behind her carrying a tray with a dented kettle, three thick cups, and a plate with two heels of bread that had been toasted too long on one side. He had wrapped kitchen string around the handle of the kettle because it had cracked in January and he refused to throw it away on principle. He turned each cup so its chip faced the wall before setting it down, a small private courtesy to damaged things.

"No ink on the bread," he said. "If there is ink on the bread, Tallow is not pretending not to notice."

"Wouldn't dream of it," Prue said.

Tallow gave her a look that suggested her dreams could not be trusted around paper. His singed ear twitched. "Old stone is sharp tonight. If you two are going down, you will step where the light steps, and if one of you falls and cracks a tooth Tallow is not fetching Madam Pomfrey to explain why."

Prue turned to Owen. "Going down where?"

He had meant to say it more carefully. He had meant to ease her to the edge of it, perhaps show her the anchor page first and only then admit that the page now wanted the undercroft more than it wanted the scullery. Instead he heard himself answer in the most literal shape available.

"Below," he said. "The page won't stabilize properly anywhere younger than founder stone."

She watched him for a moment. "And this is the first time you thought to mention there was a below?"

"It's not safe in the sense people usually mean."

"Mm. Helpful distinction."

He nearly started explaining what the distinction actually was, then stopped because that was exactly the sort of answer that made her face go flat like this. Tallow poured tea to spare them from themselves. It was over-brewed and black as old nails.

"Drink first," the elf said. "Thinking goes rotten faster in the cold."

Prue took the nearest cup and sipped. Her whole mouth tightened. "This is vile."

"It is hot," Tallow said. "Not every useful thing needs charm."

That almost got a smile out of her. Almost.

Owen drank because it was there. The tea scalded the back of his throat and made his empty stomach wake up. He had not realized until then that he was shaking a little. Lack of sleep or too much of the book, impossible to tell.

Prue unrolled her transparencies across the flour bin. Thin sheets of smoked glass and marked parchment clicked softly together. She had copied sky grids, declination lines, and an old equatorial projection in a hand so neat it looked engraved until one noticed the cramped corrections at the corners. On top of that she laid a folded tracing of the world, oceans and coastlines reduced to the minimum a pattern needed. She smoothed it flat with the side of her wrist to avoid leaving fingerprints.

"You said projection," she said. "Show me exactly where it slips."

He put his burned palm to the lower margin. The skin there was always hotter than the rest of him now, as if the coffer had left a coal under it. Lines came up through the vellum in a slow black seep: circles within circles, radial marks, four larger sigils that refused at first to mean anything except repetition. Prue leaned in until her shoulder brushed his and held one smoked sheet above the page. The ink steadied. He felt her freeze, not from fright exactly but from the sudden technical greed of seeing a system begin to answer.

"There," he said quietly. "If the third ring is treated as sky, not geometry."

"It isn't sky," she said at once. "Or not only sky. Hang on."

She shifted the transparency left, then back, then rotated it by the width of two fingernails. The marks on the page snapped into a different relation. What had looked like decorative hatching at the outer rim became latitude bands. The inner knots opened into four anchor points set around a sphere that had not been drawn as a sphere because the manuscript assumed the reader already knew how to unfold it.

Owen had known, roughly, that the table was large. He had not known it was world-sized until Prue's hand made the page admit it.

"Oh," she said.

It was not an exclamation. It was the sound of somebody stepping off a stair she had expected to be there.

The ink went pale again. Owen swore under his breath.

"No, wait," Prue said. "Again."

They did it again, and again. Each time the image held a little longer, enough for her to mark one relation on the tracing, enough for him to copy terms that he could half-parse and half only feel in his teeth: holdfast, veil hinge, southern return, school not by name but by category, schola, rendered through the manuscript's logic as a place where repetition and concentration had been built to stack.

By the fifth attempt his wrist ached and Prue's curls had escaped their tie and were catching lamp glow at the edge of his sight. She smelled of cold wool, library dust, and a cheap soap that left almost no scent at all. The closeness was not romance. It was concentration. It was what happened when there was only one warm patch of air in a room and both of them needed to occupy it.

"This isn't enough," Owen said. "It keeps degrading."

Prue did not look up from the page. "Then perhaps your mysterious below stops being mysterious now."

Tallow had been standing with his tray like a witness called to the wrong hearing. At that he clicked his tongue, gathered the kettle, and jerked his head toward the dark service slit behind the scullery shelves.

"Bring the lamp," he said. "And don't touch the wall on the narrow turn unless you enjoy blisters in odd shapes."

The passage was too small for dignity. Owen went first with the lamp, the Grinlow Leaves wrapped in oilcloth under his arm and his notebook tucked into his waistband. Prue came after, carrying the transparencies high and flat as if she were transporting surgical instruments through a drain. Tallow led them far enough to show the safe places and then stopped at the last low arch where the air changed.

It always changed there. The warmth of the kitchens fell off, and the old work began. Tooth-pressure gathered behind Owen's molars. The stone seemed to thicken the sound of their breathing until each inhale came back at them from the walls.

Tallow set the kettle down at his feet and rubbed his damp hands hard on his apron. "Further is yours," he said. The formality had come into his voice, which meant he was frightened enough to use manners as a shield. "Tallow will wait where the house still feels like itself."

Prue looked from him to the dark stair beyond. "Does it hurt you?"

He gave a little offended shrug. "Everything hurts somebody if it gets old enough."

Then, less tidily: "It is worse for elves. Mind the third step. It is hollow in the middle."

That, more than any warning, convinced her. She nodded once. "All right."

Owen took her down the stair.

The route curled through the castle's forgotten structure rather than its planned one: under a laundry flue warm as a throat, along a ledge where the wall sweated black, through a slit door whose hinges had fused long ago and now opened only because he had learned where the founder stone had cracked around the pin. Prue did not complain. She touched the places he told her to touch and ducked where he ducked. Once, when the ceiling dropped lower than he remembered, she caught the back of his robe to stop him from moving on too fast.

"You could have mentioned the crawling," she said.

"I forgot that bit."

"That's not reassuring."

The undercroft opened abruptly after all that constriction. The chamber was not large, but after the passage it felt like a lung. Damp mineral cold came off the floor. The anchor stone sat in its shallow setting at the center, not dramatic except to anyone who knew what it was: a broad disk of dark founder stone with lines incised so fine they vanished until light hit sideways. The star-table around it had been cut directly into the floor, rings and spokes and notation worn by age but not by use, because almost nobody had stood here for centuries.

Prue stopped just inside the threshold.

The old-work pressure reached her then. He saw it in the minute way her mouth changed shape behind her hand, in the swallow she had to force.

"Is it meant to feel like that?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Good. I hate surprises when they're physical."

He set the lamp on a dry shelf of stone and knelt by the anchor. The chamber answered the manuscript before he touched it. The oilcloth twitched once in his hands, eager and repellent at the same time.

"Don't stand directly on the inner ring," he said. "It isn't dangerous exactly, but you'll lose the thread."

"That sounds very much like danger in a cardigan."

"I know what I mean."

"I know you do."

This time the flatness in her voice had edges.

He unwrapped the Leaves and laid the page across the anchor stone. The effect was immediate. Ink rose black and sure through the vellum, not seeping now but taking its proper lines as if it had been waiting all evening to stop pretending. Prue let out a quiet breath and dropped to her knees opposite him, too interested to bother staying offended for one second longer than interest required.

They worked.

That was the chapter's real weather: not speech but labor. Owen held the page and translated what Meridian notation he could. Prue tested each relation against her grids, her world tracing, and the old astronomy tables she had copied so many times at school that she now trusted the shapes more than she trusted most people. When one approach failed she did not dramatize it. She simply pushed her spectacles up with the back of her wrist, crossed out the line, and tried the next premise. Owen kept narrowing his claims in the notebook and then crossing out the narrowing because the stone made it obvious when he was being cowardly with a conclusion.

The four anchor points came first as pattern, then as place.

One lay under Hogwarts, or rather under the region Hogwarts belonged to in the older system, the northwestern school holdfast built into the castle's foundations and disguised for centuries beneath all the smaller magic that had crusted over it. Opposite it, at a rough southern turn of the globe, another sat under a red inland waste so broad the manuscript marked it with heat bands instead of names. A third held in a high western spine where the air, according to the note Prue finally coaxed clear, bit water into stone. The last was pinned beneath an old salt plateau in the great interior of Asia, all dryness and glare even in the reduced language of the table.

Prue traced each point onto her world sheet with ink made thin by cold. "Four," she said, mostly to herself. "Not a web. More like a tent with the whole planet hanging in it."

"A quadrant hold," Owen said. "Or that's the nearest English to it."

"Nearest isn't the same as right."

"No."

"Still. Tent will do for tonight."

He liked that she could be exact without pretending exactness was free.

When they had the points, the stress marks began to make sense. The outer rings were not decorative. They were load records, not dates in any ordinary form but recurrence intervals, surge tolerances, places where the field had brightened and then failed to settle. Prue found the rhythm first because she had spent half her life making dots and arcs mean time.

"Look," she said, tapping a cluster of fine slashes around the Hogwarts point. "This isn't random deterioration. It jumps. Holds. Jumps again. Closer together later."

Owen bent over the page until his nose almost touched it. The chamber smelled of old mineral water and hot oil from the lamp. He could hear the castle above only as occasional thick thuds through many yards of stone, like furniture being shoved in another house.

"Those are local events," he said. "High-force loads."

"Meaning what, exactly?"

"Curses. Punishment magic. Anything heavy enough to strike the old structure rather than just the rooms built on top of it. Repeatedly."

Prue's finger moved to a darkened notch deeper than the rest. "And this."

He knew that one. It marked late November, the punishment scene when Amycus threw force across a row of students while Owen, desperate and half-ill, changed contact just enough to make curses skid. He had felt the castle answer then like a bell hit in the middle.

"That was me as well," he said.

Her head came up. "As well."

"Not the punishment. The amendment. It leaves a scar. Every successful one does."

"You have been cutting the same place you're trying to mend."

"Shallowly."

"That's not very comforting, Owen."

"I didn't say it to be comforting."

She looked at him for a long second, then went back to the page because the page was, for the moment, the only thing either of them could make move. They marked the other nodes. The western spine showed long old damage and recent brightening. The red inland anchor pulsed weakly but steady, a bad sign because weak and steady suggested attrition rather than a single wound. The salt plateau had a fracture pattern like dried glaze.

Hogwarts was worse than any of them.

It was not merely brightest. It was agitated. Each fresh load sent hairline spidering outward from the point, then drew back. It was a site being prodded awake by brutality, a school turned signal flare by a regime too stupid to know what its own violence was doing to the stone under its boots.

Prue sat back on her heels and scrubbed her hands over her face. There was ink on the side of her wrist now and a black half-moon near her jaw where she had touched herself without noticing.

"They're doing this," she said quietly.

"Yes."

"All year."

"Yes."

Another thud came through the chamber, heavier than the others. A shout followed, too muffled to make words of, only human shape and distress. On the page the Hogwarts point darkened and threw out one more fine line.

Prue stared at it. "That just answered something upstairs."

"Probably."

"Probably."

Owen looked at the mark. "Yes."

She laughed once, without humor. "You only go vague when it's awful."

He almost corrected the statement because it was not always true. Then he let it pass because it was true often enough to count.

They went on. He needed the full geometry now, not merely the fact of four anchors. The table around the Hogwarts point carried subsidiary notation, local clauses or route conditions or repair permissions; he could not yet tell which. Prue saw the same set of nested signs and shifted closer to catch the lamplight better. Their knees knocked. Neither moved away. Her left shoulder settled against his for balance while she copied a run of symbols into the margin of his notebook. The contact was practical and not practical. It warmed a strip of him he had not known was cold.

"This one repeats after every surge mark," she murmured. "Not all four nodes, only here."

Owen traced the phrase with the nail of his left thumb. "Hostile distinction. No, hang on. Directed force within bound institution."

"That's very school, however you translate it."

"Yes."

"Can it be used?"

He should have hedged. He should have said he did not know yet, which was true. He should have said perhaps, in theory, under conditions so narrow they might never occur, which was also true. Instead the stone and the sleeplessness and the relief of finally not working alone made him answer the question she had actually asked.

"Almost certainly," he said.

She stopped writing.

The silence after that was small but exact.

"Used for what?" she said.

Owen kept his eyes on the page. The notation sat there indifferent to human proportions. "Local redirection, maybe. Suppression. If the clause means what I think it means, the anchor can distinguish between ordinary spellwork and hostile wand force inside the school's bounds."

"Inside the school?"

"Yes."

"You mean detentions?"

"Not only."

"Corridors. Raids. Carrow turning a class into a butcher's yard."

"Potentially."

She took her shoulder off his. The warmth went with it.

"And if you can do that," she said, "if you get far enough into this to do that, you could stop them."

He heard the trap and disliked that he heard it as a trap when she was only following the line where it went. "Not cleanly."

"That was not my question."

"No working like that is clean. It would draw on the same node. It would scar it further unless the larger repair happened first or at the same time, and I can't do both blind."

"So if somebody is being hurt upstairs and this helps, you still might not use it."

There it was. Plain enough now that even he could not pretend the question meant something else.

He put his left hand flat on the stone to steady himself against a fresh pulse of tooth-pressure. "If using it locally risks the whole field, I can't promise that."

Prue did not speak.

He went on because once he had started he could feel the argument building in the only order that made sense to him, and sense had always been the thing he trusted when other people were looking at him as if he had gone wrong.

"There are four anchors left doing the work of whatever system used to cover the planet. One of them is under this school and it's already fraying from every bit of heavy magic thrown through it. If I spend what little purchase there is on one corridor, or one detention, or even ten of them, and the larger failure widens because of that, then I've solved the nearest thing and failed the actual problem."

He looked up then, because the sentence sounded colder aloud than it had in his head and he needed to see whether that was only tone.

It was not only tone.

Prue had gone very still. It was not dramatic stillness. It was the opposite: the kind that came when a person stopped wasting movement on any hope of hearing something better in a second attempt.

"The nearest thing," she repeated.

"I know how it sounds."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

"I don't think you do."

The chamber gave another blunt thud from somewhere above. Neither of them looked at the ceiling this time.

Prue took off her spectacles and pinched the bridge of her nose. Without them her face looked younger and more tired, the dark hollows under her eyes less disguised. "Let's not make this prettier than it is," she said. "You're saying there may come a point where you can stop what's being done here, to actual people, and you'll stand there weighing that against the larger repair as if we're figures in the margin."

"That's not fair."

"Isn't it?"

"No. Because I'm one of the figures in the margin. It's not contempt. It's scale."

She gave a short breath that might have been almost a laugh if anything in the room had been funny. "Those aren't different enough for comfort."

He wanted to tell her she was compressing the logic badly. He wanted to say that of course local suffering mattered, that only an idiot or a monster would claim otherwise. But the stone was under his hand and the page was open and the proof of the larger danger sat between them in black lines, and he could not make himself lie in either direction.

"I don't know what promise you want from me," he said at last.

"A human one."

He had no answer to that because the answer, if he gave the honest version, was that human promises were exactly what the work had started feeling too small to govern.

Prue put her spectacles back on. Her fingers shook once, from cold or anger or both. When she spoke again her voice had gone careful, which was worse than shouting.

"All right," she said. "Then I understand."

He knew enough to hear the damage in that.

She pulled the notebook back toward them. "This mark here. The subsidiary ring. It's still repeating whether or not I approve of you, so move your hand a fraction left and let's finish the page."

It was a brutal kindness, or perhaps only discipline. Either way he obeyed.

They finished the page.

The last hours before dawn thinned into routine: test, copy, verify, correct. Prue's distrust did not make her less exact. If anything it made her quieter and better. She cross-referenced the subsidiary ring to the load intervals and proved that Hogwarts was not only one anchor among four but the most immediately unstable because the school had become a machine for concentrated magical violence. Owen charted the stress sequence into his notebook until even he could see the relation at a glance. There were four anchors: three distant failures under ice-biting height, red desert heat, and salt glare, and one failing school node in Scotland, brightening every time the occupation leaned harder on children or he himself touched the First Art without enough control.

By the end they had something legible where there had been only the manuscript's spiteful half-disclosures: a world tracing pinned under Prue's transparencies, the four anchor points marked in black, the Hogwarts node webbed with fresh stress lines, the subsidiary local clause copied twice in case one version turned out to be wishful reading. The page on the stone had begun to fade again with the coming light. Perhaps dawn weakened it. Perhaps the chamber had merely given what it meant to give for one night.

Owen flexed his right hand and felt the burn pulse back at him.

Prue gathered the transparencies one by one. Her movements were neat from habit, not calm. She paused only once, at the threshold, and looked behind her toward the route they had come by as if fixing it in memory against a future need she did not yet want to name.

"I can find my own way back to the kitchens," she said.

"You haven't done it before."

"I do now."

He stood, slower than he meant to. "Prue."

She did look at him then. There was still care in her face, which made the rest of it worse.

"I know," she said. "The problem is that I know."

Then she took the lamp and went into the passage, leaving him with the anchor stone, the completed map, and the first method he had trusted enough to build on. The undercroft felt larger after she left, and emptier in a way that had nothing to do with sound. Above him the castle carried on being a school and a prison and, now that he had the page to prove it, one of four failing holds between the world and whatever hunted brightness in the dark. He put his hand over the marked Hogwarts point on the tracing until the ink warmed under his skin and wished, uselessly, that the true arithmetic had come with any smaller answer.
