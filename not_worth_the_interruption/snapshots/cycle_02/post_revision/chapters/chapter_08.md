# Chapter 8

Owen left the Great Hall as soon as he decently could. Christmas had thinned Hogwarts without making it kind. The portraits on the way out watched from their gilded frames like informers, and a laugh from two landings up came down the stone stripped of any warmth it might once have had. Even the Hall, with its bewitched candles and the paper chains somebody had bullied into service again, looked less like a feast than a room trying to remember what feast meant. After the East Undercroft and the shout for the hospital wing scraping down the passage above it, he had wanted one room in the castle that admitted, without theory, what people were for.

He took the service stair toward the kitchens. The air changed halfway down. Cold stone gave way to yeast, onions, damp wool steaming itself dry, and the sweet metallic tang of washed copper. By the time he ducked through the painting and into the kitchens proper his glasses would have fogged, if he had worn any. The room hit him with light and condensation. Huge pans hung over the ranges, every polished curve blotched white with steam. Holly had been jammed into cracks in the beams where it would do no structural harm. Someone had stuck cloves into the peels of two oranges and set them by the big proving drawer, so the whole place smelled faintly festive and mostly of soup.

Tallow stood on an upturned crate at the nearest stove, dipping a ladle, watching the soup sheet back into the pot, and muttering at an open cutlery drawer. He had tucked a blue pencil behind one singed ear and left a greasy thumbprint on the side of his own cheek. On the crate beside him lay three bent dessert spoons and two more in a neat accusing row, as if he meant to hold a hearing later.

"No, young sir," he said before turning around. "Don't stand there trailing corridor cold over the tiles. Sit down. Soup's right now and if it gets any thicker it'll have to be sliced."

He turned then, saw Owen properly, and clicked his tongue at the look of him.

"You're pale in a peevish way," Tallow said. "That means hungry. Sit."

Owen sat. The table was scrubbed pine with knife-nicks all along one side where years of hurried slicing had gone through bread and into wood. It looked ordinary enough to hurt a little. Somewhere behind him, out of sight, another elf laughed. Something went into a hot pan with a violent hiss.

"What's wrong with the spoons?" Owen asked.

Tallow shot the drawer a black look. "What's wrong with the spoons is that students think cutlery is portable by divine license. Five dessert spoons gone in three days and one soup ladle singed black. No house can keep standards if every pudding is attended by theft."

He stirred, tasted again, then added a cap of cream with the solemnity of a man correcting a legal injustice.

"If the school gets much quieter," he said, "the missing spoons will start accusing one another just to hear company."

"Then interrogate the ugly ones first," Prue said from the next table. "They're the likeliest to break."

Owen had not seen her at once. She was half-hidden behind a stack of plates, tall even sitting down, her dark curls pushed back with a ribbon that might once have been navy and now had the colourless defeat of too many washings. One hinge of her spectacles had been repaired with a twist of copper wire, and she kept nudging them up her nose with the heel of her hand so she would not get grease on the lenses. There was ink on both cuffs. There was always ink on both cuffs.

Tallow sniffed. "Miss Hollis speaks as if the handsome spoons are any more loyal."

"I don't trust pretty silver," Prue said. "It's vain metal."

That got a brief snort from Owen before the kitchen door banged again and Martha came in sideways, shoulder first, with her scarf half off and her plait loosening down her back. She had a heel of bread in one hand already, which meant Tallow had either given up on stopping her years ago or never tried in earnest. She shut the door with her boot and pointed the bread at the pot.

"If that's the parsnip one, I want the thick bit," she said.

"Miss Sorrell wants the thick bit of every situation," Tallow said, but there was more approval than complaint in it. "Sit before you eat the tablecloth as well."

Martha flung herself onto the bench opposite Owen. She was still compact even in all the winter layers, broad through the shoulders, face pink with cold and temper. One cuff had split and been mended in green thread that did not remotely match the jumper. She saw him looking at it and tucked her wrist under the table without comment.

"You escaped the Hall early," she said.

"So did you."

"Amycus was trying to sing. I drew a line."

"Sensibly," Prue said.

Tallow brought the pot over himself rather than trusting any of them with it. The soup came out thick and gold-brown, steaming hard enough to blur the scratched windows over the sink. He followed it with a plate of bread, a dish of butter gone soft at the edges, and a smaller plate on which sat four mince pies with badly pinched lids.

"Those are for later," he said at once, because Martha had looked at them.

"I know what 'later' means."

"You know what it means when someone else says it. That's not the same."

Martha rolled her eyes and tore bread anyway. Prue took one corner of a slice and set the rest back down with exactness. Owen wrapped both hands around his bowl. The heat went through his fingers and found the burn in his palm at once. The scar did not like sudden warmth. It brightened under the skin with a sensation that was not pain exactly, more a private wrongness, as if the hand remembered the coffer and mistrusted all containers since.

For a little while they ate like people who had not come to accomplish anything. Martha blew on each spoonful too hard and splashed the heel of her thumb, swore, licked it clean, and kept going. Prue scraped cream from the rim with a piece of bread because she said skin was only acceptable if attached to a person. Tallow, passing between counters, kept glancing over as if he expected one of them to insult the meal by turning solemn in its presence.

It was Martha who preserved the ordinariness first.

"Dad does bubble and squeak on Boxing Day whether anyone wants it or not," she said into her bowl. "He says if you've made it through Christmas you owe the leftovers proper respect."

"Your father sounds doctrinaire," Prue said.

"He's about frying. Not about anything useful. He does it in the big iron pan because he says the little one lies about heat. Burns the first side every year and calls it flavour."

Tallow made a scandalised noise from the sink.

"That's not flavour," he said. "That's impatience with grease attached."

"Tastes good, though." Martha tore another piece of bread. "He puts in too many sprouts on purpose because he knows my aunt will pick them out and complain and then he can complain back. Whole afternoon sorted."

"That does sound useful," Prue said.

Owen said, before he could stop himself, "Does it actually squeak, or is that just the name?"

Martha looked at him over the rim of her spoon, then laughed, properly laughed, the sound bright enough to make the room larger.

"You poor sheltered thing. Yes. A bit. If the potato's wrong."

"Or if the cook is wrong," Tallow muttered.

"The cook is always wrong, according to someone," Prue said.

"According to evidence," Tallow said.

The talk moved sideways the way talk did when nobody was trying to win it. Martha described her father's habit of cooking in one sock and one slipper because he never noticed when laundry baskets ate the other pair. Prue said her mother overboiled every green vegetable in existence and had therefore ruined saltless food for her on principle. Tallow said a school full of adolescents could empty six bread bins in an evening and still look as if it had been starved by policy. Martha asked if there was any crackling left from upstairs. Tallow said there had been and now there was not and stared at her until she stopped smiling quite so hard.

Owen let the voices pass over him. He answered when spoken to. He ate more than he had meant to. He watched steam run up the windows and vanish. The kitchen had its own weather and its own law. Under the table his shoes were damp from the thaw coming off them. Somewhere below the flags, and too near now that he knew the way, the anchor stone sat in its chamber.

Knowing that had altered the room. Every scrape of bench and clink of spoon seemed to happen over something buried and waiting. He kept having to drag his attention back up to the soup, the clove-stuck oranges, Tallow muttering over the cutlery as if that were the whole problem in the world.

Tallow came back with the mince pies after all, one on each chipped plate, and set them down without admitting that he had planned to do so.

"There," he said. "If anyone says the kitchens are ungenerous, Tallow will require names and dates."

Martha bit into hers at once and hissed through her teeth when the filling burned her mouth.

"Serves you right," Prue said.

"I'm hungry, not strategic."

"A distinction many movements fail to maintain."

"Listen to yourself," Martha said. "You've spent too long with catalogues."

Prue smiled into the pastry. The smile lasted half a second.

"Did Miri come down last week?" Martha asked Tallow abruptly. "The little Ravenclaw one. The one who used to nick the sugared orange peel from the tray when she thought you weren't looking."

The change in the room was small enough that no sound announced it. Tallow's hand stopped on the back of Martha's bench. Prue lowered her eyes to her plate.

"No," Tallow said after a moment. "Miss Kell had not been in the kitchens since the train came back."

"She's not in her dorm either," Prue said. Her voice went flat; she set each word down separately. "Her bed's stripped. Her trunk's gone."

Martha swallowed, too fast this time. "Did someone fetch her?"

Prue gave a little helpless lift of one shoulder. "If they did, they didn't sign the night ledger."

Tallow resumed moving. He picked up the empty soup bowls one by one though nobody had quite finished.

"The little miss left two gloves in the warming cupboard in November," he said. "Green ones with the thumbs stretched. Tallow kept them back in case. They're still there."

No one said anything to that. Across the room the other elves kept working. A pan clattered. Somebody laughed again, not having heard this end of the table or having heard and declined to carry it.

Martha worried at the crust of her pie until it broke apart under her fingers.

"Hester's brother didn't come back either," she said. "At least, not after the first week. They told the table he'd got dragon pox, which was stupid because Hester said her family doesn't even keep a kettle hot unless they have visitors. Nobody with dragon pox in that house would survive long enough to infect anyone."

Prue made a noise that might have been a laugh in another month. This time it snagged in her throat.

"That's very nearly medical reasoning," she said.

"It's kitchen reasoning. Better science."

Owen looked from one of them to the other. The names arrived without enough purchase. He could summon scraps: a small Ravenclaw girl with sugar on her mouth once, maybe Miri, and some boy Martha had pointed out in the corridor for keeping contraband Gobstones in his sock. Not enough to say anything that would not sound borrowed. Silence lengthened. He reached for the plainest fact he had and heard too late how badly it would fit.

"A lot of people go home over Christmas," he said, because silence had begun to seem like an accusation.

He heard the inadequacy of it before the others did. Or perhaps at the same time.

Martha stared. "Owen."

"I only mean we don't know. Some of that's ordinary. The trains, and families, and-"

Prue set her spoon down with care. "That wasn't the thing she meant. And no, before you start narrowing it, I heard you."

He looked at her.

"I know," he said. "I just meant it isn't necessarily all the same thing."

"You're still doing it," Prue said. "Taking the worst part and making it paperwork."

"People aren't categories," Martha said. It came out tired rather than fierce.

Tallow gathered the plates with rigid care, setting one atop the next so softly the china barely clicked. His ears had flattened back a little.

"Young sir," he said without looking at Owen, "there's room in a house for uncertainty and still no room at table."

That ought to have embarrassed Owen into silence. Instead it made him want to defend the principle, which was worse.

"I'm not saying it doesn't matter."

"All right," Martha said at once. "Don't."

She bent over her ruined pie and ate the rest in two practical bites as if the conversation had not happened. Prue took off her spectacles and wiped them with the least greasy corner of her napkin, though there was nothing on the lenses Owen could see. Tallow carried dishes away and began, with unnecessary energy, to inspect the cutlery drawer again.

The meal went on, which felt stranger than a row would have. Martha asked whether anyone had seen snow settle on the greenhouses. Prue said only on the north side and only badly. Tallow complained that students tracked salt over the flagstones and then blamed the floor for being gritty. Owen answered a question about whether the fifth-years were still being made to polish trophy cases with toothbrushes. He said yes. Martha said bastards. Prue said yes, that too.

After that, each new subject came with a beat's delay, as if somebody had to lift it over the thing he'd dropped in the middle of the table.

When they were done, or near enough, Prue folded her napkin around the remains of her pie crust and stood.

"I've got to get back before my dorm decides I've defected to domestic service," she said.

"Leave the plate," Tallow told her. "If anyone is defecting it won't be with crockery."

"I make no promises."

She slid her spectacles back on, gathered her scarf, and looked at Owen directly.

"If we talk tomorrow, don't do that again," she said. "Don't make it sound tidy just because it helps you think."

Owen opened his mouth.

"No," Prue said. "I know it helps you think. I'm saying I can't listen to it when it's people."

"Good night," she said.

"Night," Owen said.

Martha said, "See you tomorrow?"

"Probably. Unless the library collapses from sentiment."

It was a decent joke. On another evening Martha would have thrown something small at her for it. Tonight she only nodded. Prue left. Cold knifed in with the opening door and was gone again.

Martha stayed seated another half minute, rolling the stem of an empty spoon between her fingers.

"Walk up with me," she said at last.

Owen said yes before he considered whether he wanted to.

Tallow handed Martha a square of paper with a mince pie in it and, after a moment's thought, handed Owen one as well.

"Take your scarves," he said. "And if either of you pockets so much as a teaspoon, Tallow will know."

"You always say that."

"And I'm always right eventually."

* * *

They climbed out of the kitchens into the narrower passage where the heat gave up quickly. The stone there held yesterday's cold and intended to keep it. Orange peel and roasting fat fell away behind them. Ahead lay the dark service corridor toward the Hufflepuff cellar, lit every twenty feet by lamps with mean little blue flames. Martha wrapped the pie in both hands for warmth before taking a bite.

For a while they just walked. Their footsteps had a different sound out here, thinner and more solitary than the kitchen clatter. From somewhere above came a burst of song from the Great Hall, late and ragged and overforced. It sounded like people trying to prove to one another that the season still existed.

"He's right about the sprouts," Martha said with her mouth half full. "My dad puts too many in."

"Apparently your whole family cooks by provocation."

"Yes." She swallowed. "Works well till it doesn't."

They turned into the lower corridor where narrow windows looked out at black grounds dusted grey with old snow. The glass had filmed over at the corners. Someone had scratched a star into one pane and then tried to rub it out again.

Martha slowed. "I got a letter this morning," she said.

Owen looked at her. "From home?"

"Obviously from home. Nobody else sends letters that smell of paraffin and dog."

"Right."

She picked pastry off the paper with her thumbnail.

"Dad wrote about the weather for most of it," she said. "The canal freezing at the edges. Mrs Greeley's hens not laying. My cousin taking a shovel to the privy step because of the ice. Then two lines about me not to worry and to eat enough if the school was feeding us. Which sounds funny till you remember who's in charge of the school."

"Letters are being watched," Owen said. "He was probably being careful."

"I know he was being careful."

Martha let the words sit between them. Her jaw worked once. She kept her eyes on the window as they passed, her own reflection gliding beside them in warped black patches.

"That's not the difficult bit," she said. "The difficult bit is that I couldn't tell whether he was being careful because he's frightened or because he's always careful now. Those are different things and they look exactly the same on paper."

Owen looked down at the grease spotting the paper round his pie. His mind went at once to censors, delays, who handled which sack of post on what day. That was the sort of answer he knew how to build. It was no use to her at all, and he felt that before he opened his mouth.

He chose it anyway.

"You probably can't tell from one letter," he said. "Not on its own."

Martha laughed once, under her breath.

"See, there it is."

"What?"

"That." She turned to face him properly now, back against the cold wall. "I say I'm scared and you start trimming the edges off it till it'll fit in a notebook."

"I didn't mean-"

"I know you didn't mean anything by it. That's the problem."

The lamp nearest them guttered blue. Owen could hear the faint hiss from it. At the far end of the corridor the barrel-door entrance to Hufflepuff sat in shadow, iron hoops catching a little light.

"I'm only saying one letter isn't enough to conclude-" he started.

"Conclude?" Martha said. "For fuck's sake."

She pushed away from the wall, then stopped herself from walking off and came back a pace as if sheer fairness compelled her.

"I'm not asking you to conclude anything," she said. "I'm asking whether it gets to you. Properly. The missing people, the letters, the way everyone keeps talking lower every week like if we whisper enough none of it will be true."

Owen looked at her. There were flour smudges on her sleeve from the kitchen table. One bit of pastry clung to the scarf at her throat. Her plait had come half undone, strands sticking to her cheek where the cold had dampened them. She looked fifteen and frightened and furious at herself for letting the frightened part show.

Of course it got to him. Amycus got to him. Martha nearly taking curses in November got to him. The undercroft got to him. It all got to him so steadily that he had built a habit of sorting it: what could be answered, what could only be endured, what had to be put aside till he could function again. The habit had become quicker than speech.

"It does," he said, and heard how bloodless that sounded. He tried again. "I just can't, if I let every- sorry. That's not what I mean. I mean if I start treating every gap and rumour as the whole shape of things, I can't think straight enough to be any use."

Martha closed her eyes.

"Any use to what?"

He should have lied. Not cleanly and not well, just enough. To us, perhaps. To the school. To you. Instead he hesitated. The hesitation itself answered.

When she opened her eyes again, whatever softness had been left in them had gone flat.

"Right," she said.

"Martha-"

"No, I heard it."

"You didn't, not exactly."

"Then exactly what did I hear?"

He had nothing fit for air. The corridor waited with her. The blue lamp hissed. From very far away, or very deep below, the castle gave one of its settling groans.

"There's something I'm trying to hold onto," he said at last. "And if I lose the thread of it because I'm- because everything here is loud all the time-"

"Everything here," Martha said slowly, "is loud because people are getting hurt."

"I know."

"Do you?"

He flinched, not in body so much as in attention. It felt like missing a stair in the dark and only learning it from the jolt in the knee. She saw that too.

"You know what's stupid?" Martha said. She looked away, out through the filmy window where the grounds were only a deeper black. "I keep thinking if they came for me I'd want thirty seconds to make the bed. Just that. Pull it straight. Hide the socks. I know that's idiotic. I know if they come they're not going to stand there while I tuck corners. But I keep thinking it anyway."

She rubbed the side of her thumb over the paper that had held the pie until it tore. Tiny flakes drifted down to the flags.

"And when Hester's bed got stripped and Miri's trunk vanished and my dad writes about hens as if that's enough information for a person to live on, I think maybe the whole world is turning into that. Beds made for nobody. Meals for whoever's left. I just wanted-" She stopped and shook her head. "I wanted you not to answer it like a timetable."

She said it without looking at him. Owen stared at the shredded paper in her hand.

He could have said he was sorry. He was sorry. But even now the apology kept trying to become a clarification: sorry, but there are other pressures; sorry, but if you could see; sorry, but. Every version betrayed itself before it reached his mouth.

He managed, "I don't know how to do both."

Martha let out a breath that clouded in front of her.

"That's at least honest," she said. "Miserable, but honest."

They stood in silence. In the kitchens below, someone dragged a crate across stone. The sound came up through the floor faint and grating. Owen thought absurdly of Tallow lining up spoons in their drawer, counting what the room still possessed.

"That wasn't just the letter," Martha said.

He looked up. "What?"

"In there as well. You heard Prue." She made a small, irritated motion around his face, his shoulders, perhaps his whole person. "You stand there and you are here, technically, and then after about ten seconds you go somewhere else. Not physically. Worse."

He wanted to deny that. He wanted, more secretly, to ask whether Prue had said anything specific. Both impulses were mean.

"I've just been tired."

Martha gave him such a flat look that the lie died where it was.

"Fine," she said. "Be tired, then."

She folded the torn paper into a square so small it disappeared in her fist.

"I keep bringing the wrong things to you," she said. "That's my mistake."

"Martha, that's not-"

"No. Leave it."

She tucked her scarf up under her chin with two hard little jerks, the way she did when both hands wanted a job. Then she stepped around him and started down the corridor toward the barrel door.

Owen moved after her once, then stopped. "Will I see you in the morning?"

She did not turn round.

"You'll see me somewhere," she said.

She gave him only a small lift of the hand, already half on her way to the barrel. At the Hufflepuff door she knocked the right pattern and slipped through when the wood swung aside. The door shut behind her with its usual thick, stupid warmth.

Owen stood in the blue lamp light with the untouched mince pie cooling in his hand.

The corridor smelled of old stone, paraffin, and the spiced fruit Tallow had sent upstairs in paper. Grease bled through the wrapping into his palm until the burn began to throb. One word kept coming back in Martha's voice. Timetable. He looked at the shut barrel door and thought, stupidly, that he could still knock.

He stayed where he was. By the time he made himself turn away, the paper had gone translucent in his fist and the burn in his palm was throbbing under it, a hard little rhythm easier to follow than the thought of knocking.
