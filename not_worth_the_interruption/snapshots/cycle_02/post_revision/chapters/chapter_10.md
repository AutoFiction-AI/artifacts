# Chapter 10

The scullery off the kitchens smelled of wet stone, old onions, and lamp oil going sharp in the heat. Midnight had come and gone, and Owen still had the Grinlow Leaves spread on an upturned flour bin because it was flatter than the table, and flatter mattered. The page he wanted would hold for perhaps six breaths if his burned right palm pinned the lower margin and the lamp sat exactly level with the nick in the opposite wall. On the seventh breath the ink went pale and the lines slid back under the vellum as if the manuscript had thought better of showing him anything.

He had been stuck there three nights, which was another way of saying that since the press business nobody downstairs had found a better use for him.

His ash-linen notebook lay open beside it, dense with cramped tries and cross-outs. He had written that the quarter mark repeated at ninety degrees and that the thing answered to stars the way library shelves answered to numbers. He had written that much four times in four different places as if changing where it sat might make it truer.

The kitchens beyond the scullery still muttered at low volume: pan bottoms ringing softly, water thrown into a basin, one of the younger elves laughing and then stopping at once when an older voice shushed him. The ordinary work of the castle went on under the occupation because somebody had to scrub the roasting tins and bank the ovens even when Amycus Carrow was upstairs teaching children to scream properly.

Footsteps came along the service passage, cautious and tired, too heavy for Tallow's quick soft slap.

Prue came in sideways through the narrow door with a roll of transparencies under one arm and a folded bundle of charts clamped to her chest. She had not bothered with gloves. She never did when paper was involved; wool caught on thin pages and drove her mad. Her fingers were pink with cold and already marked with blue ink near the nails. Over her school jumper she wore an old bottle-green cardigan with one wooden replacement button at the throat, and she worried that button between two fingers while she looked at him and the book and did not yet put anything down.

"Neville said you wanted the clear ones," she said.

"I did."

"That was his charitable summary."

Owen nodded once. "I couldn't get the projection to stay put."

She set the bundle on the bin beside the manuscript without sitting. Her spectacles were smudged at one corner. "Martha's still limping."

Owen looked at the page because the page at least obeyed some kind of rule.

"I know," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yes."

She let out a breath through her nose. "Right."

Neither of them said sorry. It would have been too early for a useful apology and too late for a good one.

Tallow slipped in behind her carrying a tray with a dented kettle, three thick cups, and a plate with two heels of bread that had been toasted too long on one side. He had wrapped kitchen string around the handle of the kettle because it had cracked in January and he refused to throw it away on principle. He turned each cup so its chip faced the wall before setting it down, a small private courtesy to damaged things.

"No ink on the bread," he said. "If there is ink on the bread, Tallow is not pretending not to notice."

"Wouldn't dream of it," Prue said.

Tallow gave her a look that suggested her dreams could not be trusted around paper. His singed ear twitched. "Old stone is sharp tonight. If you two are going down, you will step where the light steps, and if one of you falls and cracks a tooth Tallow is not fetching Madam Pomfrey to explain why."

Prue turned to Owen. "Going down where?"

He had meant to say it more carefully. He had meant to ease her to the edge of it, perhaps show her the anchor page first and only then admit that the page now wanted the undercroft more than it wanted the scullery. Instead he heard himself answer in the most literal shape available.

"Below," he said. "The page won't stabilize properly anywhere younger than founder stone."

She watched him for a moment. "And this is the first time you thought to mention there was a below?"

"It's not safe in the sense people usually mean."

"Mm. Helpful distinction."

He nearly started explaining what the distinction actually was, then stopped because that was exactly the sort of answer that made her face go flat like this. Tallow poured tea to spare them from themselves. It was over-brewed and black as old nails.

"Drink first," the elf said. "Thinking goes rotten faster in the cold."

Prue took the nearest cup and sipped. Her whole mouth tightened. "This is vile."

"It is hot," Tallow said. "Not every useful thing needs charm."

That almost got a smile out of her. Almost.

Owen drank because it was there. The tea scalded the back of his throat and made his empty stomach wake up. He had not realized until then that he was shaking a little. Lack of sleep or too much of the book, impossible to tell.

Prue unrolled her transparencies across the flour bin. Thin sheets of smoked glass and marked parchment clicked softly together. She had copied sky grids, declination lines, and an old equatorial projection in a hand so neat it looked engraved until one noticed the cramped corrections at the corners. On top of that she laid a folded tracing of the world, oceans and coastlines reduced to the minimum a pattern needed. She smoothed it flat with the side of her wrist to avoid leaving fingerprints.

"You said projection," she said. "Show me exactly where it slips."

He put his burned palm to the lower margin. The skin there was always hotter than the rest of him now, as if the coffer had left a coal under it. Lines came up through the vellum in a slow black seep: circles within circles, radial marks, four larger sigils that refused at first to mean anything except repetition. Prue leaned in until her shoulder brushed his and held one smoked sheet above the page. The ink steadied. He felt her go still beside him, caught by the sudden technical greed of seeing a system begin to answer.

"There," he said quietly. "Treat the third ring as sky. The geometry starts making sense after that."

"It's sky laid over something else," she said at once. "Hang on."

She shifted the transparency left, then back, then rotated it by the width of two fingernails. The marks on the page snapped into a different relation. What had looked like decorative hatching at the outer rim became latitude bands. The inner knots opened into four anchor points set around a sphere that had not been drawn as a sphere because the manuscript assumed the reader already knew how to unfold it.

Owen had known, roughly, that the table was large. He had not known it was world-sized until Prue's hand made the page admit it.

"Oh," she said.

Her "Oh" came out thin and careful, like a foot finding air where a stair ought to have been.

The ink went pale again. Owen swore under his breath.

"No, wait," Prue said. "Again."

They did it again, and again. Each time the image held a little longer, enough for her to mark one relation on the tracing, enough for him to copy terms that he could half-parse and half only feel in the drag of his burned palm against the vellum: holdfast, veil hinge, southern return, school not by name but by category, schola, rendered through the manuscript's logic as a place where repetition and concentration had been built to stack.

By the fifth attempt his wrist ached and Prue's curls had escaped their tie and were catching lamp glow at the edge of his sight. She smelled of cold wool, library dust, and a cheap soap that left almost no scent at all. Their shoulders stayed pressed together over the bin. The lamp made one small warm pocket in the room, and the work kept drawing them back into it.

"This isn't enough," Owen said. "It keeps degrading."

Prue did not look up from the page. "Then perhaps your mysterious below stops being mysterious now."

Tallow had been standing with his tray like a witness called to the wrong hearing. At that he clicked his tongue, gathered the kettle, and jerked his head toward the dark service slit behind the scullery shelves.

"Bring the lamp," he said. "And don't touch the wall on the narrow turn unless you enjoy blisters in odd shapes."

The passage was too small for dignity. Owen went first with the lamp, the Grinlow Leaves wrapped in oilcloth under his arm and his notebook tucked into his waistband. Prue came after, carrying the transparencies high and flat as if she were transporting surgical instruments through a drain. Tallow led them far enough to show the safe places and then stopped at the last low arch where the air changed.

It always changed there. The warmth of the kitchens fell off, and the old work began. The burn across Owen's right palm tightened as if the skin were shrinking around the scar. The stone seemed to thicken the sound of their breathing until each inhale came back at them from the walls.

Tallow set the kettle down at his feet and rubbed his damp hands hard on his apron. "Further is yours," he said. The formality had come into his voice, which meant he was frightened enough to use manners as a shield. "Tallow will wait where the house still feels like itself."

Prue looked from him to the dark stair beyond. "Does it hurt you?"

He gave a little offended shrug. "Everything hurts somebody if it gets old enough."

Then, less tidily: "It is worse for elves. Mind the third step. It is hollow in the middle."

That, more than any warning, convinced her. She nodded once. "All right."

Owen took her down the stair.

The route curled through the castle's forgotten structure rather than its planned one: under a laundry flue warm as a throat, along a ledge where the wall sweated black, through a slit door whose hinges had fused long ago and now opened only because he had learned where the founder stone had cracked around the pin. Prue did not complain. She touched the places he told her to touch and ducked where he ducked. Once, when the ceiling dropped lower than he remembered, she caught the back of his robe to stop him from moving on too fast.

"You could have mentioned the crawling," she said.

"I forgot that bit."

"That's not reassuring."

The undercroft opened abruptly after all that constriction. After the passage, even this chamber felt like a lung. Damp mineral cold came off the floor. At the center, in its shallow setting, sat the anchor stone: a broad disk of dark founder stone with lines incised so fine they vanished until light hit sideways. The star-table around it had been cut directly into the floor, rings and spokes and notation dimmed by age alone, because almost nobody had stood here for centuries.

Prue stopped just inside the threshold.

The old-work pressure reached her then. He saw it in the minute way her mouth changed shape behind her hand, in the swallow she had to force.

"Is it meant to feel like that?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Good. I hate surprises when they're physical."

He set the lamp on a dry shelf of stone and knelt by the anchor. The chamber answered the manuscript before he touched it. The oilcloth twitched once in his hands, eager and repellent at the same time.

"Don't stand directly on the inner ring," he said. "Stand there and the work starts sliding out from under you."

"That sounds very much like danger in a cardigan."

"I know what I mean."

"I know you do."

This time the flatness in her voice had edges.

He unwrapped the Leaves and laid the page across the anchor stone. The effect was immediate. Ink rose black and sure through the vellum, not seeping now but taking its proper lines as if it had been waiting all evening to stop pretending. Prue let out a quiet breath and dropped to her knees opposite him, too interested to bother staying offended for one second longer than interest required.

They worked.

The room narrowed to nib-scratch, lamp hiss, and the steady ache building through Owen's palm. He held the page and translated what Meridian notation he could. Prue tested each relation against her grids, her world tracing, and the old astronomy tables she had copied so many times at school that she now trusted the shapes more than she trusted most people. When one approach failed, she pushed her spectacles up with the back of her wrist, crossed out the line, and tried the next premise. Owen kept narrowing his claims in the notebook, then crossing out the narrowing because the stone made it obvious when he was being cowardly with a conclusion.

The four anchor points came first as pattern, then as place, but not cleanly.

Prue laid the world sheet over the rings and caught the first knot under the wrong sea.

"Hang on," she said at once. "The chart came later than the prime line."

She slid the tracing west by less than the width of her nail and held one smoked sheet above it. The knot dropped under Britain. At the same moment a cramped marginal tag Owen had been treating as a unit mark opened into category instead: school holdfast, northwestern division. The burn in his right palm kicked so hard he nearly lost the page.

"That's us," Prue said. For once she had forgotten to keep her voice level. She looked up at the ceiling as if all that stone might turn transparent out of courtesy. "Not as a symbol. Literally us."

Owen had known the anchor under Hogwarts was real. Seeing it sit in relation to the rest of the world was different. It took the school out of its own story and nailed it into something older and much less interested in children.

The second knot refused longer. He called it ocean because the tracing put it in open blue. Prue said, "No, look at the bands," and swapped in the stripped chart, the one with only the meanest coastlines left on it. She rotated the glass a hair. The bands that had looked decorative deepened into heat marks around a vast inland red.

Prue sat back so abruptly her heel squealed on the stone. "That's half the planet away."

"More," Owen said, because the outer ring had finally stopped being coy about scale.

She laughed once under her breath, without any humor in it, and leaned in again. "Fine. Again."

The western point came from a misread. Owen took the note for tide bite and started drawing coast. Prue caught his wrist with two ink-cold fingers.

"Air," she said. "It says air bit water into stone. Not tide."

She dragged the lamp closer, bent until her curls brushed the vellum, and turned the projection on its side. The supposed coast reared up into a long mountain spine far to the west, high enough that even the manuscript bothered to mention water freezing where it fell.

After that the fourth anchor gave faster. By then they knew what sort of evasions the table liked. A salt plateau in the great interior of Asia, all dryness and glare even in the reduced language of the map.

Prue traced each point onto her world sheet with ink made thin by cold. Her hand steadied only once the last dot was down. "Four," she said, mostly to herself. "Not a web. More like a tent with the whole planet hanging in it."

"A quadrant hold," Owen said. "Or that's the nearest English to it."

"Nearest isn't the same as right."

"No."

"Still. Tent will do for tonight."

He liked that she could be exact without pretending exactness was free.

When they had the points, the stress marks began to make sense. The outer rings logged load: recurrence intervals, surge tolerances, places where the field had brightened and then failed to settle. Prue found the rhythm first because she had spent half her life making dots and arcs mean time.

"Look," she said, tapping a cluster of fine slashes around the Hogwarts point. "This isn't random deterioration. It jumps. Holds. Jumps again. Closer together later."

Owen bent over the page until his nose almost touched it. The chamber smelled of old mineral water and hot oil from the lamp. He could hear the castle above only as occasional thick thuds through many yards of stone, like furniture being shoved in another house.

"Those are local events," he said. "High-force loads."

"Meaning what, exactly?"

"Curses. Punishment magic. Anything heavy enough to strike the old structure rather than just the rooms built on top of it. Repeatedly."

Prue's finger moved to a darkened notch deeper than the rest. "And this."

He knew that one. It marked late November, the punishment scene when Amycus threw force across a row of students while Owen, desperate and half-ill, changed contact just enough to make curses skid. He had felt the castle answer then like a bell hit in the middle.

"That was me as well," he said.

Her head came up. "As well."

"Not the punishment. The amendment. It leaves a scar. Every successful one does."

"You have been cutting the same place you're trying to mend."

"Shallowly."

"That's not very comforting, Owen."

"I didn't say it to be comforting."

She looked at him for a long second, then went back to the page because the page was, for the moment, the only thing either of them could make move. They marked the other nodes. The western spine showed long old damage and recent brightening. The red inland anchor pulsed weakly but steady, a bad sign because weak and steady suggested attrition rather than a single wound. The salt plateau had a fracture pattern like dried glaze.

Hogwarts was worse than any of them.

The point burned hottest and shivered under the vellum. Each fresh load sent hairlines spidering outward from it, then drew back. It was a site being prodded awake by brutality, a school turned signal flare by a regime too stupid to know what its own violence was doing to the stone under its boots.

Prue sat back on her heels and scrubbed her hands over her face. There was ink on the side of her wrist now and a black half-moon near her jaw where she had touched herself without noticing.

"They're doing this," she said quietly.

"Yes."

"All year."

"Yes."

Another thud came through the chamber, heavier than the others. A shout followed, too muffled to make words of, only human shape and distress. On the page the Hogwarts point darkened and threw out one more fine line.

Prue stared at it. "That just answered something upstairs. Somebody got flung into a wall or a desk hard enough for this thing to feel it."

"Probably."

"Probably."

Owen looked at the mark. "Yes."

She laughed once, without humor. "You only go vague when it's awful."

He almost corrected the statement because it was not always true. Then he let it pass because it was true often enough to count.

They went on. He needed the local geometry now, not merely the fact of four anchors. The table around the Hogwarts point carried a secondary run of notation that did not recur at the other anchors. Prue saw it at the same moment and shifted closer to catch the lamplight better. Their knees knocked. Neither moved away. Her left shoulder settled against his for balance while she copied the signs into the margin of his notebook, quick and narrow and exact. The contact was practical and not practical. It warmed a strip of him he had not known was cold.

"This one again," she murmured. "Every time the load jumps. Only here."

Owen traced the phrase with his thumbnail. "Institutional distinction. No, that's too tidy. Directed force inside bound school."

"School, specifically?"

"Yes. The Hogwarts node keeps a local set the others don't."

"Meaning what, in practical terms?"

He should have said he needed another night with it. Instead the half-visible answer, the sleeplessness, and the relief of finally not working alone made him answer too soon.

"Meaning there may be ways to work on this place as a place," he said. "Not just the whole field. I can't tell yet if it's access or warning or some sort of local correction."

She stopped writing.

"Local enough to help people here."

"Maybe. Later. If I can read it properly."

"And if later turns up while Carrow's still upstairs making an example of somebody?"

He disliked how quickly the line had found its real shape. "That depends what it costs."

Her shoulder came off his. The warmth went with it.

"Right. So that's it, then."

"If it draws from the same purchase as the repair—"

"No. Don't start there. I asked whether you'd use it."

"I know, but it's the condition."

"It's always the condition with you. If this, if that."

He put his left hand flat on the stone. The burn across his right palm kicked once, hot enough to make the skin tighten. "There are four anchors left doing the work. Hogwarts is the one getting hit over and over. If I spend leverage here because it's nearer, and that opens the wider failure, then I've done something immediate and still made it worse."

"Nearer," she said. "You say that and skip the rest."

"You know what I mean."

"I know. I know, and I'm still arguing with you."

The chamber gave another blunt thud from somewhere above, thicker than sound. The Hogwarts line on the page darkened under Prue's hand.

She looked at it, then at him. "That moved because somebody upstairs got hit again. Hard."

He hated that she was right in the form she meant and wrong in the larger one, and that both truths sat in him at once. "If using a half-read local condition breaks the field, then no. I can't promise I'd use it."

Prue took off her spectacles and rubbed the mark they had left on the bridge of her nose. Without them her face looked younger and much more tired. "You hear yourself, don't you? No, that's wrong. You hear the version with the person rubbed out. Somebody upstairs just hit a wall hard enough for the stone to jump, and you go straight back to conditions."

"That's not fair. I'm in that room as well."

"That isn't the point."

"I'm telling you the scale of it," he said.

She let out a flat little laugh. "Stop saying scale. I know it's bigger than one room. I know. But that noise upstairs lands in somebody's ribs or their teeth."

He wanted to tell her she was flattening the logic into something sentimental and unusable. He wanted to tell her that if the school failed, the room upstairs did not get safer, only smaller. What came out instead was, "What do you want me to say?"

Prue set her spectacles back on. Her fingers shook once, from cold or anger or both. "Start there, for once. With whoever just hit that wall. Then tell me the price if you have to."

He had nothing he could say that did not either lie or make the same argument again.

After a moment Prue pulled the notebook back between them. "Move your hand a bit left. The mark's going."

He obeyed.

What followed never quite settled into routine. The castle kept interrupting. Three times something heavy hit or struck overhead hard enough to travel through the stone, and each time the Hogwarts line on the tracing thickened before settling back. Prue marked every change without comment. She had stopped giving him the benefit of broad claims. If he translated a sign too widely she said, "No, smaller," and waited until he narrowed it.

By the time the lamp was burning low they had one usable sheet: the four anchors fixed in black, the Hogwarts point furred with fresh stress, and the school-specific run copied into Owen's notebook with more question marks than certainty. The page on the stone had already started giving itself back to secrecy. Dawn or exhaustion; he could not tell.

Owen flexed his right hand. The burn answered at once.

Prue stacked the transparencies one by one. Her movements were neat from habit, not calm. At the threshold she glanced back toward the route they had come by, fixing it in memory.

"I can get back to the kitchens."

"You haven't done it before."

"I've just done half of it," she said. "I'll manage."

He stood, slower than he meant to. "Prue."

She waited with the lamp in her hand. He had three possible sentences and none of them were any use: that the danger was real, that he was not being cruel for sport, that he knew exactly how he had sounded. By the time he found breath for any of them, the moment had gone.

Prue turned and went into the passage.

He stayed only long enough to wrap the Leaves, slide the tracing and the ash-linen notebook into his satchel, and make sure the copied line would still be there in daylight. Then he followed the last of the lamp-smell back toward the kitchens and the service stair, where the first grey of dawn had started finding the upper cracks, with her flat silence ahead of him all the way up.
