# Chapter 15

The first sign was not the shouting. It was the floor.

Owen was crossing the Hufflepuff common room with his ash-linen notebook under his jumper, meaning to get back to the kitchens before curfew hardened into head count, when a low note went through the stone and up his legs. The nearest table rattled its teacups. A fourth-year girl revising Arithmancy put both hands flat on the page as if the castle might try to slide it away from her. Half a second later the old burn across Owen's right palm turned hot against the notebook's cover.

He stopped.

The room did not. Someone burst in from the cellar passage with his tie half off and shouted, "He's here."

That should have required a question. It did not. Every head came up at once. The common room, which had spent the year learning the downward shape of itself, altered in a single breath.

"Harry," the boy said, louder now because he had them. "Harry Potter. In the castle."

The name ran through the room like a match through dry lint. A first-year actually laughed, one quick bark of sound with terror under it. Two girls by the fireplace grabbed each other's sleeves. Someone said, "No, don't be thick," and someone else said, "Neville said it, I heard him," and chairs were already scraping back before any of them had decided what to do with their bodies.

Owen should have felt the same thing they did. He had spent months waiting for a break in the pattern, for some older and more dangerous person to kick a hole in the miserable ordinary of detentions, searches, and Amycus Carrow's breathing. Potter was exactly that kind of break. Potter in the castle meant the year had finally stopped pretending it could go on.

What Owen felt instead was the second pulse through the floor, harder than the first. The ward-scar in his hand tightened. Somewhere above them, high enough that the sound came down as a dull struck thud rather than a clear bang, old magic had begun taking new orders.

The anchor won't take a battle, he thought, and then, a beat behind it, Harry's here.

He was moving before he had decided to move. By the time he reached the cellar steps the passage was full of students pulling jumpers over their heads, dragging shoes on without laces, shouting contradictory versions of the same miracle.

"He got in through the portrait."

"No, he was in Ravenclaw Tower."

"Alecto Carrow's dead."

"Nobody said dead, don't say dead."

"Snape's gone up."

"Shut up and move."

Hope moved faster than instruction. It made everyone clumsy. It made them bright. A boy from the year below Owen, who had spent most of March speaking with one side of his mouth because Amycus had cracked a tooth, ran past grinning so hard the broken edge showed white in the torchlight. Owen had never seen him grin before. The sight of it landed in the chest like grief.

He climbed into the crush. The stone kept talking through the soles of his shoes. Every time a stair took a new load, every time some higher passage woke an old defensive charm, he felt the answer in his bad hand and behind his eyes. Hogwarts had room for punishing children, for corridor curses, for the Carrows' little public festivals of pain. It had been carrying those all year. This was different. This was the whole castle drawing breath.

At the first turn above the cellar, a portrait of a Tudor witch flung her own painted door open and cried, "They're rousing the statues," to nobody Owen could see. Three portraits away, a friar was ringing an invisible handbell with such conviction that the frame shook.

By the time Owen hit the wider corridor near the kitchens, the change had become visible. Armour that had spent the year standing decorative and stupid along the walls was no longer still. One suit rolled its shoulders with a metal groan. Another stepped down off its plinth and nearly trod on a Ravenclaw's foot. The torches had gone brighter without anyone touching them. Farther off, up toward the moving staircases, a woman's voice cracked through the air in clipped command and was answered by a thunder of feet.

The castle was choosing sides.

He got carried with the flow as far as the broad landing below the Entrance Hall before he could cut free. Bodies kept knocking him off line: girls in dressing gowns over trousers, boys with school ties hanging loose and wands gripped wrong from haste, a pair of sixth-years hauling a box of green-glass potion phials between them. Nobody looked like a soldier. That was the worst part. Nobody had turned noble or abstract on the strength of Potter's name. They were still the same underslept, half-fed schoolchildren they had been an hour earlier, only now they were moving with direction.

Neville stood three steps up from the landing, making direction by force.

He had lost a button from his shirt and not noticed. His sleeves were rolled unevenly. A broken watch with a frayed leather strap kept sliding out from under his cuff, and every few seconds, between one instruction and the next, he shoved it back with the heel of his hand as if time might start behaving if he bullied it hard enough. His face was bruised yellow and purple under one eye. He looked older than seventeen and much more tired, but there was nothing grand about him. He was simply the person who had been doing this all year and was now doing it with the walls awake around him.

"If you're leaving, leave now," he was saying. "If you're staying, get armed and get back. First-years go with the little ones from Ravenclaw. Herbology lot, west corridor. No, not the front doors, don't be stupid, they'll watch the front doors first. Move."

Martha was below him on the landing with a roll of bandages under one arm and a canvas satchel bumping her hip. Her dark plait had mostly come loose and kept catching at the corner of her mouth when she turned her head. She kept spitting hair out of the way without using her hands because both hands were busy: one holding the satchel open, the other shoving things into it as people passed, dittany, strips of linen, a squat jar of honey-colored salve, a packet of biscuits flattened nearly to crumbs.

A first-year Hufflepuff girl stood frozen at Martha's elbow with tears hanging off her chin. Martha pulled one of the crushed biscuits free, shoved it into the girl's hand, and said, "Eat. I know it tastes like wall, just eat it. Then go with Dennis, all right? Don't stand here looking at me."

The girl nodded and went.

Neville leaned down without breaking stride and caught Martha's sleeve. "Your leg."

"Still attached."

"That wasn't the question."

"It's my hip tonight, actually." She winced as she shifted her weight, then looked annoyed at her own face for doing it. "I'm fine. Keep shouting at people."

For half a second his hand stayed on her arm. It was not leadership or symbol. It was just Neville making sure Martha was still upright before he let the next demand have him. Then somebody yelled from above that the courtyard wanted more bodies and he turned away.

Martha saw Owen in the same instant.

Whatever she might have said to him a week ago was not available now. There was too much happening for the luxury of old anger. Her eyes hit him, assessed him, and moved on again in a single hard motion.

"Phipps," Neville said, seeing him because Neville was seeing everyone. "Good. Get to the kitchens. We need anything they can spare, bandages as well if Tallow's got sense enough not to hide them. Then come up to the courtyard."

The floor answered with another long subterranean shiver. Owen's burned palm flashed hot.

He knew, all at once and with the sick steadiness of a calculation finally resolving, that the first battle-spell had not even been thrown yet. This was only preparation: chairs being dragged, shields waking, professors pulling old protections back into use. The true load had not arrived. If he waited for the first curses to start crossing the grounds, if he waited for what everyone else meant by the battle, he would be too late.

He said, "I need to go below."

Neville was already looking past him to the next face. "Below, above, I don't care, just get through the kitchens faster. Move."

It was so nearly permission that Owen hated him for it.

Martha had heard. He saw that much in the small flattening of her mouth. But two boys came up with a crate between them and one had blood running into his sock, and she bent at once to deal with that instead. Owen stood there long enough to feel useless and seen, then slipped out of the stream and back toward the kitchen passage while the whole landing kept pouring upward.

On the turn down, he looked back once.

Neville was still on the stair, voice gone rough already, one hand lifted to stop a panic before it started. Martha had braced the satchel against her bad hip and was tying a bandage around the ankle of the bleeding boy with quick angry fingers because anger was what she had left for fear. Somebody farther up was laughing too hard. Somebody else was crying and pretending not to. Harry Potter's name kept erupting in scraps as if saying it were the same as loading a wand.

He fixed the landing in his head as if that might count for staying: Neville on the stair, Martha bent over the boy, the noise of children trying not to sound frightened. Then he took the turn for the kitchens anyway.

The kitchens were no refuge from it. They had become a lung.

Heat hit him first, thick with steam and yeast and wet wool. Then sound: pans, shouted orders, boots on stone, house-elves underfoot, the hollow iron noise of cauldrons being lifted too fast. The long worktables that usually held bread and pies now carried armfuls of torn linen, baskets of potatoes, stacks of plates nobody had bothered to clear, and one alarming mound of old silver cutlery that had apparently been collected because in a crisis everything became either food, bandage, or weapon.

Tallow stood on a flour bin near the central range so he could see over the human bodies blundering through his workspace. He had tied a strip of blue cloth around one singed ear to keep it folded back from the heat, and his apron was dusted white to the knees. One hand held a ladle. The other held a spoon blackened at the bowl where something had caught and burned. He was staring at a cauldron as if personal insult had risen off it in the steam.

"Lower that flame," he snapped at an older Slytherin who was trying to help and failing. "It's soup, not vengeance. If it splits now, you'll be feeding fighters greasy water and Tallow won't be blamed for your panic."

The Slytherin lowered the flame.

Tallow looked up and found Owen. Whatever warmth he had once reserved for him did not appear.

"Young sir," he said. "You are either late for carrying or early for hiding."

Owen pushed through to the edge of the bin. "I need the route."

Tallow's eyes flicked once toward the ceiling, where a deep impact had just rolled dust from the joints between stones. The house-elf flinched, almost too small to see unless you were already looking for it.

"Of course you do," he said. "Because there is a war in the dining rooms and a clever boy always wants the passage under the spoons."

"Tallow."

"Do not call me Tallow like that as if it shortens the sentence." He set the burned spoon down with exaggerated care. "Third flue beyond the scullery. Left of the coal bins. The house is tightening there, so mind your shoulders. And if a rung gives under you, you deserve the bruise."

Owen started to move. Tallow spoke again, quieter.

"The route is open. That is not the same as blessing it."

There was no answer to that which would not sound childish. Owen gave a brief useless nod and turned away.

Students kept forcing him sideways as he cut across the kitchen. A pair of Gryffindors wrestled a cauldron of boiling water toward the service stair, red-faced with steam and fright. A house-elf ran under Owen's elbow with a sack of crusts nearly as big as his own body. Somebody had started slicing bread on the nearest table and left the knife standing upright in the loaf because there had not been time to lay it down properly. Every person in the room seemed to be hurrying toward the fight or hurrying so that somebody else could. Owen kept cutting across the grain toward the flue behind the coal bins.

A Hufflepuff third-year he half recognized shoved a stack of folded cloth into his chest, assuming he was part of the same traffic. Owen caught it on reflex.

"Upstairs, please," she said, breathless. She had tied her dressing gown shut with green twine. "Hospital wing's already full."

"Right," he said.

The cloth stayed in his arms for a beat too long. Then he laid it on the nearest table and kept moving.

The scullery passage was narrower and less bright. Steam filmed the stone. The floor had gone slick with old water and fresh ash tracked in from above. Owen ducked past the coal bins, found the third flue behind a stack of dented tureens, and got both hands on the iron ladder bolted into the throat of the wall.

Another impact ran through the castle.

It was not close yet, but it was stronger. The iron bit his palms. In his right hand the old burn answered so hard he had to press his shoulder against the brick until the wave passed.

He put one foot on the first rung.

"No," Prue said from behind him. "No. Stop there."

He turned too fast and cracked the back of his head on the flue wall. For a stupid second all he saw was black brick and orange sparks.

Prue stood at the mouth of the passage with one hand braced on the coal bin as if she had run the whole way from somewhere higher and only just bullied her lungs into silence. Her spectacles were in place but badly smeared, one lens grey with kitchen steam and fingerprint. Her curls had come loose from whatever she had tried to do to them and were frizzing damply around her face. She had an old book satchel across her body, the strap twisted, and three stiff index cards were still stuck under the buckle from sheer habit or accident. In one hand she held her wand. In the other, absurdly, a stub of carpenter's pencil.

He said, "What are you doing here?"

"Following the obvious," she said, still breathing harder than she wanted him to notice. "You vanished the second Neville gave you a job. There are only so many varieties of awful."

Above them, feet hammered the service stair. Someone shouted for more buckets. The sound came through the stone blurred and bodied, as if the castle were swallowing the words before handing them down.

Owen stepped off the rung but did not come away from the flue. "I don't have time for this."

"That isn't a serious sentence."

"It's true."

"Yes, and so is half the rubbish in your notebook. That doesn't make it the point."

He might have laughed on another night. He did not have it in him. "Prue."

"No. You don't get to use my name like a pause." She pushed her glasses up with the back of her wrist. The hand holding the pencil shook once and steadied. "Tell me properly. Are you actually going below now?"

"Yes."

"Tell me why."

Another shudder moved through the wall. This one came with grit. Dust loosened from the ceiling and caught in Prue's hair. Owen felt it in his teeth before he felt it in his feet.

"If the fighting starts properly," he said, "the anchor lights. Once it lights, the seam below goes with it. I've got a window now. After the first real exchange, I may not."

A pair of sixth-years came pounding down the service stair carrying a crate of bottles between them. Prue had to flatten against the wall to let them pass. Owen turned sideways as well, and for an instant they were pressed almost shoulder to shoulder, not touching, both forced to face the damp stone while the crate knocked by and one of the boys muttered, "Sorry, sorry, sorry," without meaning any one apology more than the others.

When the passage cleared, Prue looked at him again over the smear on her spectacles.

"Neville's upstairs," she said. "Martha's upstairs, and she can barely stand straight. The stairs are full of kids carrying water and sand and trying not to be sick. I'm not saying forget it. No, that's not fair. I'm saying not yet. Come upstairs first and do one thing for the people actually here. Carry, fight, hold a shield, anything. Then, if we're alive in ten minutes, I'll go below with you."

Above them, from somewhere out beyond the kitchens, a surge of sound rose and broke into many separate noises at once: cheering, screaming, a command Owen could not make out, then the unmistakable crack of curses hitting shield or stone. Dust fell in a soft rush from the flue throat onto his shoulder.

That was it. The first proper answer from the grounds.

The heat in his hand turned savage.

Owen caught the ladder before the sudden weakness in his knees could show on his face. Below that shaking iron, below the kitchen, below the service stone, he could feel the anchor responding like a live thing under a knife.

"It's begun," he said.

He scrubbed the back of his wrist under his nose. It came away dark.

Prue's voice went raw. "I know it's begun. That's why I'm here."

A line of older students forced down into the passage carrying sacks of sand and two shields between them. Owen and Prue had to flatten apart to let them through. One of the shields clipped Owen's shoulder hard enough to spin him half back toward the ladder. A girl at the rear saw where he was facing and did a double take that was almost comic in its plainness.

"Are you going down?" she asked, because people under pressure said obvious things.

Prue answered for him without taking her eyes off his face. "Not if he's got any sense."

The girl nodded as if that settled it and kept moving.

When they were alone again, Prue said, much quieter, "You can still turn round."

He could. Neville was three corridors away. Martha was somewhere above the kitchen traffic, probably swearing at anyone who tried to take the satchel off her. He could go back into the noise with both hands and whatever courage he possessed.

Below them the deeper seam kept opening, and every fresh strike upstairs tugged at it.

He heard himself say, very calmly because panic would only have sounded like a lie, "No. I'm not turning back."

Prue's face did not change at once. Then it did, subtly and much worse than tears would have been. She became more exact.

"Right," she said. "Fine. I wanted you to say it plain."

His mouth opened on nothing. Instead he put his foot back on the rung.

Prue did not grab him. That would have been mercy. She moved one step aside so he could pass cleanly if he meant it.

"Owen."

He looked down.

"When you hear them up there," she said, "don't do that thing. Don't turn it into pressure and stone. That's Neville on the stairs. That's Martha. Those are kids hauling buckets."

Then she held the passage and the kitchen light behind her, and he climbed down into the wall.
