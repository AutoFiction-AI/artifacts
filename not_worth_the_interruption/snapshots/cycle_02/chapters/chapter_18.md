# Chapter 18

Owen came back up from the undercroft to a kitchen stair filmed with soot. Dawn had got into the castle the way smoke did, through cracks that had not been there yesterday and under doors that no longer shut square. Somebody had slopped broth or potion or blood over the fourth step from the top and it had dried tacky in the grit. He kept one hand on the wall because the stone still moved fractionally under him, not in truth, not enough to throw him, but enough that his body had not yet accepted the battle was over above his head.

His right palm felt boiled inside the old burn. His nails had split at the white crescents. Blood had dried from his nose down the front of his jumper in a rusty fan, and each time he swallowed he could taste the chalk dust he had breathed below. The satchel strap bit into his shoulder. Inside it the Grinlow Leaves lay wrapped in a tea towel gone stiff with damp, and his ash-linen notebook pressed against his ribs like a second, smaller body.

The passage opened near the kitchens, but the smell from the Great Hall drew through the corridor and made a shape in the air before he reached it: smoke, old stone, singed wool, broth, blood, and the sweet wrong stink of spells burnt into masonry. He followed it because there was nowhere else to go that would count as coming back.

The Hall had stopped pretending to be a dining room.

The house tables had been shoved crooked against the walls to clear floor space. One trestle had lost a leg and leaned into Ravenclaw's bench at an angle that looked comic until Owen saw the dead laid in the gap beside it. Blankets, cloaks, table linen, a ripped curtain from somewhere above; anything that could cover a face had been pressed into use and none of it matched. Under the enchanted ceiling dawn showed itself as a dirty pearl, smoke dragging grey ribbons across the high charred blue. The windows on the courtyard side had blown inward. Glass crunched under shoes. The flagstones were gritty with shattered stone and the fine plaster dust that got into the back of the throat and stayed there.

People moved in the half-order that came after panic rather than before it. A pair of seventh-years carried a first-year between them, her feet trailing because none of them had strength left to lift properly. Somebody at the staff table was making lists in a voice too hoarse to rise above the room. Cauldrons of hot water smoked along the wall. A Hufflepuff boy from two years below Owen sat with both hands wrapped round an empty mug, blinking at nothing while a cut in his scalp dripped slowly behind one ear. Near the front, Colin Creevey had been covered with a blanket that stopped short of his shoes. The laces on one trainer had melted together into a black bead.

No one looked up when Owen entered. The room was full of people who had earned being seen.

He stood there too long. Then Martha's voice reached him from halfway down the Hall, brisk with fatigue.

"If you're upright, stop blocking the bloody doorway."

She was kneeling beside a makeshift pallet with her bad leg shoved straight out in front of her so she did not have to trust it bent. Someone had wrapped her left calf from ankle to knee in uneven white strips already gone grey at the edges. Her dark plait had come mostly loose and she had caught the tail of it under the collar of her jumper to keep it off the blood on her shoulder. She was tying off a bandage around a girl's upper arm with the quick domestic efficiency of somebody securing a parcel that had split open in transit.

Owen crossed the Hall because she had told him to do a thing and that was simpler than deciding what to say.

"Sorry," he said.

"Yeah." She jerked her chin toward a bucket by her knee. "That needs filling. Not from the lav by the stairs, that one's got plaster in it. Courtyard pump."

He picked up the bucket. The handle was sticky. "Are you all right?"

Martha looked up then, only once. Her face was ash-grey with tiredness under the grime, and there was a crescent bite mark on her lower lip where she had been worrying it raw.

"No," she said. "But the leg still works when I swear at it, so off you go."

He took the bucket to the courtyard pump because she had given him a route and a tool and he could still obey those. Outside, dawn made the broken cloisters look flatter than night had. Part of the outer wall had come down in a heap of pale blocks and splintered beams. The lawn beyond was blackened in streaks. A suit of armour lay in pieces near the archway, one gauntlet still twitching now and then as leftover enchantment ran out of itself. There were bodies out here too, though fewer, and a line of the living sitting against the wall with blankets round their shoulders, all facing the same direction as if waiting for a lesson to begin.

The pump handle was cold enough to hurt. He worked it until rusty water gave way to clear. When he bent to lift the bucket his vision pinched black at the edges. He waited, breathing through his mouth. Somewhere in the grounds a cheer started, broke, and turned into something that might have been crying from a distance. Victory, Owen thought, and the word felt like hearing about a meal from the bottom of a well.

When he brought the water back, Martha had shifted from the pallet to the floor and was rolling torn sheets into narrower strips. She did not thank him. She took the bucket and set it beside her.

"There," she said. "Useful."

He stayed standing because sitting down near her felt like presumption. "I didn't know if you were—"

"Alive?" She wet a strip of linen and wrung it hard. "Yeah. Same as you."

"Martha."

"Don't." Her hands kept moving. "Not while I'm doing this."

He looked at the girl on the pallet. Fourth year, maybe. Freckles across the bridge of her nose under the soot. Martha had tucked the blanket round her feet with absurd care, making sure the heels were covered. That detail hurt more than anger would have.

"I was below," he said, and heard at once how useless it was.

Martha gave one flat laugh with no humour in it. "I know you were somewhere. Everybody was somewhere."

"It mattered."

"Maybe." She yanked the knot tight with her teeth and spat a thread. "Maybe it mattered a lot. I haven't got the spare parts in me right now to care at your scale."

He could have told her about the crack brightening under battle magic, about the clause in the anchor he had not used, about the way the stone had nearly gone liquid under his hands. None of it would have answered the room they were in. None of it would have moved the blanket higher over Colin's shoes or put sleep back under Martha's skin.

"You look awful," she said after a moment, still not looking at him. "There's tea near the staff table if you're going to fall over. Don't do it on my bandages."

The kindness in it was so small and so stubborn that it landed like a refusal.

"Right," he said. "Yes."

She set another rolled strip in a neat pile. "And Owen."

He waited.

This time she did look at him. Her eyes were bloodshot, ordinary brown made strange by the night's length.

"Don't come asking me for the right shape of it," she said. "I haven't got one for you. I've got this and that girl and my leg and whoever they bring in next. That's what I've got."

"I wasn't going to."

"Good."

Somebody behind him called for clean water. Martha had already turned away, one hand braced on the floorboards as she hauled herself toward the next body. The conversation had not ended cleanly because nothing in the Hall did. It just stopped needing him.

He moved aside. Two girls from Gryffindor came through carrying a transfigured door with a dead boy on it. One of them had tucked a sprig of greenhouse rosemary behind her ear and kept absently touching it with the back of her wrist, as if checking it was still there. Neither spared Owen a glance.

Prue found him near the wreck of the Hufflepuff table, where he had stood too long staring at a scorch mark shaped like a thrown map.

She had tied her hair back with a strip torn from blue graph paper. The knot had softened in the damp and one corner of the paper kept lifting against her temple. Her spectacles were smeared white at the edges, and the left lens had a crack through the bottom corner that made the flagstones buckle if he looked directly at it. She was holding a stack of folded blankets against her chest with the rigid care of someone afraid that if she loosened her arms at all, they would start shaking.

"You've come up, then," she said.

"Apparently."

She looked him over from the dried blood at his mouth to the satchel on his shoulder. "You should sit down."

"I'm fine."

"You're not. I didn't say collapse dramatically. I said sit."

He did not sit. The Hall was too public for it. "Are you hurt?"

"A bit." She shifted the blankets higher. "Nothing worth inventorying."

The crack in her lens flashed as she turned her head. Beyond her, at the far end of the Hall, a knot of people closed and opened round somebody important enough to change the air of the room merely by being alive. Owen did not need to see who it was. The castle had already tilted toward its legible heroes and its legible dead.

Prue followed his glance, then looked back at him. "I haven't said anything."

He answered the smallest question inside that sentence because it was the one he could bear. "You don't need to."

"No." She pressed her thumb into the blanket stack until the top one creased. "That isn't what I said."

He shut his mouth.

"I haven't said anything," she repeated, quieter now, "because if I do it now it'll go stupid at once. It'll turn into one sentence. Two, if we're lucky. You had a way to stop it and you didn't. People will hear that and fill in the rest with whatever makes them least sick."

"It's true enough."

"No." Her voice sharpened. "It isn't enough."

He could feel the anchor still in his bones, the patched line below the castle giving a faint sick tug every few breaths. He wanted, absurdly, to show her the exact condition as if the stone itself could speak for him. But she had seen enough. That was the point.

"It wouldn't have held the way they would've meant," he said. "The local clause would've collapsed hostile wandwork in the castle, yes, but only for the fight in front of us, and the breach condition would've kept widening because all the larger load was still there and I was already losing the edge on the seam and if I'd tried to split attention at that stage—"

"I know," she said, cutting across him before he could build a machine out of explanation. "I know that part. I was there."

He stopped.

For a moment they listened to the room together. The scrape of benches. A kettle lid rattling. Somebody saying a name twice because the first time had not brought an answer.

Prue let out a breath through her nose. "You keep doing this thing where you tell the truth in units too small to be argued with."

"Do I?"

"Yes." She adjusted her spectacles with the side of one wrist because her hands were full. "You say the clause wouldn't have solved the larger problem. Fine. It wouldn't have. You say the breach is still there. Fine. I believe you. You also left us upstairs with the smaller problem while it was still killing people one body at a time. That part doesn't shrink because the other part is planetary."

The word sounded ridiculous in the ruined Hall. Planetary. As if the world could be made of anything grander than broken benches and damp blankets and Martha dragging herself on a bad leg from one body to the next.

"I know," he said.

"Do you?"

"Yes."

Prue studied him. He knew what she saw: the blood, the ash, the spent look of somebody who had not been idle. Proof had never been the issue with her. Only scale.

"What happens now?" she asked.

He could have lied, or made it smaller, or offered a technical answer so narrow it sounded almost manageable. He was too tired.

"The patch holds," he said. "For now. The crack isn't gone. It's closed enough that nothing outside notices immediately, I think. But the node is damaged and it'll need tending, re-inscribing, watching. If the castle takes another battering like tonight before I settle it properly..."

"It opens again."

"Yes."

"How long have we got?"

"I don't know." He hated how honest that sounded, how much like begging. "Longer than last night, but well short of safe. That's the best I can do."

She nodded once, classifying the answer rather than agreeing with it.

"All right," she said.

He waited for more and hated himself for it.

What came was softer, and worse for that. "I can't go down there with you again."

His fingers tightened on the satchel strap. "I didn't ask."

"No. You don't ask for the expensive bits. You just keep moving as if other people will sort themselves around the work." Her mouth twitched, not with humour. "I'm not helping you disappear into it. If you go back, call it what it is."

He said nothing.

Prue looked past him toward the smashed windows where the dawn had brightened from pearl to a thin exhausted silver. "They're going to remember this as an ending," she said. "Maybe they have to. I can't even say they're wrong. But don't stand here wanting it from me."

"I'm not."

She gave him a tired, incredulous look that would have been almost ordinary in the library and was not ordinary here at all. "Right."

Someone called her name from the far end of the Hall. She flinched before she answered, as if the sound had reached a bruise.

"I'm coming," she called back, and then to Owen, "Wash your face. You look frightening."

That, too, was not absolution. It was housekeeping from a person who still preferred precision to spectacle. She shifted the blankets in her arms and went, pausing only once to hitch the torn graph-paper ribbon tighter at the back of her head.

Owen stayed where he was until the room began to reorganize around breakfast for the living and names for the dead. The cauldrons along the wall changed from hot water to tea. A tray of bread appeared from the kitchens and was descended on without ceremony. People found one another or failed to. House loyalties and battle loyalties and family names began to pull the Hall into comprehensible shapes again. None of them had room for him. He could have taken a mug, sat on a bench, waited for someone to tell him what happened in the battle he had not seen finish. The thought made his skin crawl.

He turned back toward the kitchen corridor before anyone asked him where he had been.

The kitchens looked less ruined than the Hall because labour had already begun to erase the ruin.

Bread had been sliced directly on the scrubbed tables, leaving long pale furrows in the wood where the knife had bitten too deep in a hurry. A copper pan hung off one hook by its handle because the rim had buckled from heat. The big black range was going hard enough to sweat the stones. Steam and soot pasted themselves to the ceiling in greasy layers. Three house-elves slept sitting up against the flour bins with their chins on their chests, hands still powdered white to the wrists. Every flat surface held something halfway back to usefulness: cracked mugs sorted by size, damp towels, onions, bowls of broth skinning over while somebody shouted for more salt.

Tallow stood at the scullery end with a dishcloth over one shoulder and a row of chipped serving platters spread before him like a set of difficult medical cases. He was fitting broken edges together to see which ones could still be trusted with weight. One of his singed ears had been wrapped in a narrow strip of linen and pinned up with a bent fork tine. He did not look up until Owen was close enough to drip soot on the clean boards.

"Shoes off the flagged bit if you're bleeding," he said. "That side's for bread."

Owen stepped where indicated without argument. His legs were starting to shake in earnest now that he was under a lower ceiling.

"Sorry."

"Mm." Tallow set two platter halves together, decided against them, and laid one in the discard basket. "Sorry is what children say when the milk's gone over. This is a longer job than that."

The satchel seemed suddenly obvious on Owen's shoulder. Tallow's eyes flicked to it and away.

"House is still standing," the elf said. "Not gracefully. But then neither are half the students, and here we all are."

"You felt it."

"Tallow felt every saucepan in the west kitchen rattle itself stupid and one pudding collapse clean in the middle, yes. The stone settled with a complaint from one end to the other." He reached for a fresh platter. "Young sir has got the look of a wall that has taken weight and not decided whether it resents it yet."

Owen lowered the satchel to the table. "I need to put these away."

That made Tallow's mouth flatten. "Away where?"

"Not the shelf-chase. Too exposed now. The north stacks are half-broken and everyone will be rummaging for weeks."

"So."

"At the node." He touched the tea towel bundle through the satchel flap. "It started answering harder every time I worked at the seam. After last night it feels warm straight through."

Tallow stared at the bag for a long second, then picked up the dishcloth and wiped his hands with greater care than the dirt required.

"Of course it does," he said. "Young sir patched a house-wound with the thing in his head and his blood in the scoring. Once records have been read against live stone, they stop fancying shelves."

He hopped down from the bench and crooked two fingers. "Come along, then. Before somebody decides they need this table for pastry and we all lose what little reason remains."

The route back to the undercroft felt shorter in daylight, though there was no daylight in it. Owen followed Tallow through the hot wet dark behind the ovens, down the passage where the stone always smelled faintly of onions and cold iron, through the warped service door that opened only if lifted a quarter inch before the latch turned. The castle changed underfoot as they descended. Kitchen vibration gave way to the dull mineral hush of the older levels. Sound lost its edges. Owen's own breathing came back to him off the walls like somebody else's.

By the time they reached the anchor chamber, his headache had narrowed to a hard point behind one eye.

Nothing down here cared that the war had been won upstairs. The chamber was still damp, still half-carved and half-grown out of the bedrock, with water beading in the same seams and running down the same grooves it had yesterday and all the yesterdays before that. But the night's work had marked it. Chalk and ash were ground into the floor in overlapping arcs around the anchor stone. Dark handprints smeared one flank where he had caught himself from falling. Blood had dried in the fine inscription channels and turned almost black. The repaired seam through the stone's face showed as a pale tight line like a healed cut that would split again if smiled too hard.

Owen set the satchel down with both hands. The motion sent a flare through his palm sharp enough that he had to close his eyes for a moment.

Tallow, without comment, pushed a three-legged stool toward him with one foot. Owen sat because refusing would have been performance.

"You made a filth of it," Tallow said, surveying the floor.

"Yes."

"Well. A battle isn't a tea tray." He moved nearer the anchor and winced, not theatrically, simply because the old work hurt him here. "Is it holding?"

"Yes."

"That wasn't a courtesy question."

"Yes," Owen said again, more exactly. "It's holding under present load. The outer brightness has dropped. The seam isn't travelling."

"Mm."

Tallow looked at him sideways. "And if the castle has another night like that one by supper."

"Then we're in trouble."

"There we are."

Owen pulled the tea towel bundle from the satchel and laid it on the stool across his knees. It was damp clear through. The cloth had taken an outline from the pages inside, a flattened uneven rectangle with corners that no longer met, and the lower edge had gone gritty with the same chalk-salt residue that crusted his burned palm after hard work at the seam. His notebook he set aside on the floor, safely out of the drip line from the ceiling. When he unwrapped the manuscript, the chamber seemed to draw a fraction tighter around them.

The Grinlow Leaves had never looked like one proper book. They were a packet of unequal sheets, some thick as card, some thin enough to show the shadow of writing through from the other side, all of them darkened at the edges from age and lamp smoke and his handling. The scripts on the upper pages shifted when he glanced across them, not changing exactly, but refusing to stay in the category of ordinary ink. Near the anchor they were more restless. Fine mineral brightness began to show in the fibres, not randomly but along the repaired tables he had leaned on in the night, as if the stone beneath the chamber floor were finding its own handwriting there.

Tallow muttered something under his breath about ugly paperwork and stepped away from the direct line of it.

Owen had thought on the stairs that he would choose where to hide it, examine the seams, test the shelves cut into the founder stone, and decide on a placement and a covering layer the way a sensible person hid a dangerous object. The undercroft removed that illusion from him at once.

When he lifted the top leaf, the anchor answered as if he had brought a missing fitting back to a machine that was still shaking itself apart. The seam had only been stitched shut for a few hours, and the work was still raw enough to recognize its own instructions.

The pale repaired line on the stone darkened. A hairline split opened beside it, no wider than the edge of a fingernail. Cold damp air came out of it, carrying the lead-and-stone smell of the old shelf-chase. The uppermost page bowed downward toward the seam. Owen held on instinctively, and the sheet resisted him with the terrible softness of soaked paper. Under his thumb the edge roughened. Grey mineral thread showed through the tear before he let go.

"Leave it," Tallow snapped, sharper than he had spoken all morning. "Do not pull against the grain when the house is taking back its service papers."

Owen released the page.

One by one the Leaves bent and settled. They did not fall or drag. They simply stopped insisting that they were separate objects. The pages he had leaned on most during the night's repair went first, sinking flat into the seam as if the node meant to keep whatever had already been spent on its behalf. What stayed nearest the surface was later work: thickened service marks, interval notations, margin corrections cut by hands that expected damp stone, bad light, and no patience for deciphering. The tea towel wrapper slumped empty in Owen's lap.

He sat very still.

There was no question of carrying the manuscript back upstairs now. He had not rehidden it in a niche or box. The undercroft had taken it into the node itself and sealed the question under mineral glaze. If he wanted the departure tables or the buried lore again, he would have to coax them out here on the stone's terms or scratch through the new skin and worry open the very seam he had spent the night holding shut. The chamber had not given him storage. It had taken away portability.

Tallow breathed out slowly through his nose. "There. That's told us."

"I thought there might be a choice."

"There might have been last year," Tallow said. "Before young sir woke the pages with his burn, read them against the open node, and then spent a battle stitching the house back together. Once the stone is live and hurt, it keeps the service papers close."

Owen gave a laugh that scraped his throat. It hurt too much to count as humour.

Through the thin stone skin, one page remained more legible than the others. It was not the opening, and it was not the broad tables of departure. It was a cramped later hand full of maintenance intervals, threshold symbols, and corrections written into the margins at different dates. The strokes were broader than the scholar's script elsewhere in the Leaves, widened for rereading under strain. Somebody had written this part to be usable when the node was sweating and the reader was tired.

"Service hand," Tallow said. "Written to stay up when the load is bad. Clever lore can sink if it likes. Maintenance doesn't get to."

"Of course it is," Owen said.

"Yes," Tallow said. "That's the trouble with them. Houses are work."

Owen reached the wall shelf before Tallow did. He came back with a bucket, a stiff brush worn to a fan at the end, a packet of chalk, and a squat jar of something that smelled of lime and wet ash. He set them down beside the anchor with more care than he had managed with his own body all night.

Then he dragged the notebook onto the stool and turned past months of cramped copies and corrections until he found the spread with the north-stacks route and the departure figures he had been stupid enough to think might remain useful at a desk. He tore both leaves out along the stitching. The sound was small, ugly, final.

Tallow watched from beside the wall, saying nothing.

Owen ripped the pages into strips and kneaded them into the lime-and-ash paste with the butt of the brush handle until the paper gave up its shape and the ink bled grey through the slurry.

"Loose copies walk," he said, mostly to the jar. "This doesn't get to walk anymore."

He packed the fibrous paste into the fresh hairline split beside the absorbed Leaves, opened the notebook to a clean spread, and copied the visible intervals into a blunt column: first light, last light, seep, brightness, load. Then, on the bare strip of stone next to the seam, he wrote Phipps in chalk and pressed the heel of his burned palm over the name to fix the dust into the damp.

The stone took the print. Cold ran through the scar and up his wrist in a narrow line, not pain exactly, but recognition hard enough to make his teeth set.

"Well," Tallow said at last. "Young sir needn't wait to be asked."

Owen looked from the mark to the tools and back. "No."

Tallow's expression did not soften, but neither was it cruel. It was workman's irritation at a fantasy of finished labour, with no further room left for it. "Listen properly. Since you've gone and signed for the filthy thing, the seam will need watching at first light and last light. The channels will need keeping clear. If the stone sweats black, you wake me. If the line travels, you wake me. If a duel, a feast, a Ministry inspection, or some fool with a commemorative plaque starts hammering on this bit of floor from above, you notice. That is the arrangement you've just written yourself into."

Owen put his good hand round the brush handle. The wood was swollen from age and damp. Somebody, long ago, had carved a shallow thumb-rest into it.

"All right," he said.

Tallow tilted his bandaged ear as if listening to whether the chamber believed him. "You'll miss breakfast."

Owen looked up toward the layers of stone and hall and smoke above them, where the living would be drinking tea and counting losses and beginning, already, to turn horror into story.

"Yes," he said.

"You'll miss the speeches as well, when they come."

"Fine."

"Mm." Tallow nudged the bucket nearer with one foot. "Tallow thought so."

The first stroke of the brush through the inscription channel loosened blackened blood and chalk into a paste the colour of dishwater after roasting tins. Owen had to work the bristles into the groove carefully, following the old marks without widening them. It was not noble. It was not mysterious. It was scrubbing filth out of a damaged line before the residue dried hard enough to distort the next reading.

After a minute the chamber settled around the rhythm of it. He dipped the brush, scrubbed, wiped the stone with the rag, checked the seam, and added fresh chalk where the line had thinned. Tallow moved beside him, sorting tools, muttering over a crack in the side shelf, stopping now and then with a small pained shake of his head when the old work pressed too close.

Owen's knees began to ache on the damp floor. His face tightened with each pass of the brush when the movement pulled at the burn in his palm. He kept going. Under the thin skin of stone the trapped Leaves waited, no longer his in any ordinary sense. His notebook lay open by the bucket, ready for intervals, seep, brightness, load. Above, faint through yards of rock, the castle started ringing its bells for the dead.

He marked the first time in the notebook. Then he bent back to the seam and kept working.
