# Chapter 16

At the bottom of the ladder, smoke had got down even here.

It came under the kitchen doors, along the service flues, through the crooked throat of the passage Tallow had shown him in winter, until the East Undercroft smelled less of wet mineral and old lime and more of a hearth gone wrong. Owen reached the last squeeze of stone with his satchel banging his hip and his lungs already full of soot. Every curse cast above travelled through the castle first as a noise and then as a pressure. The noise came thin and distorted, a scatter of muffled shouts and breaking timber. The pressure arrived in the old burn across his palm, in the split beds of his nails, in the way the wall against his shoulder seemed to jump a fraction and settle again.

He turned sideways through the final slit and almost stepped into Tallow.

The house-elf had stopped at the threshold where the old work began to hurt in earnest. He was holding a dented pudding basin wrapped in a tea towel, as if he had simply taken a wrong turn while carrying something out of the scullery. His singed ears lay flat. Damp curls of soot stuck to his cheeks. There was still flour dried in the crease of one wrist.

"You're late," Tallow said.

"I know. Sorry."

"No need to tell Tallow what he knows." The elf looked past him into the passage Owen had come down, as if he expected the battle itself to push through after him. "The potatoes went to mush and then caught. One pan cracked. Nobody cared. That's how bad it is upstairs."

The basin in his hands held what Owen had asked for hours ago and had not expected to receive now that the school was at war: chalk snapped down to stubs, a skin of water grey with stone dust, two folded rags, a narrow kitchen knife with the handle dark from years of washing, and a stoppered jar of coarse salt. Tallow had laid them out neatly anyway. Of course he had.

"You shouldn't have come this far," Owen said.

Tallow made a face that might have been irritation and might have been pain. "And you shouldn't drip blood all over a flag joint older than your Headmaster. We're both being unwise tonight."

Another impact travelled down through the vault. Dust sifted from the curve of the ceiling and stippled the elf's tea towel. Tallow flinched, then smoothed the cloth flat with the side of his hand, fussy even now.

"Is it worse?" Owen asked.

"The house is too bright." Tallow's eyes did not leave Owen's face. "The moving staircases are sulking, half the second-floor lamps burst together, and the north passage by the Charms corridor has started humming like a kettle. There are children running where there ought not to be children. There are grown wizards running where there ought not to be grown wizards. The ovens keep drawing smoke down instead of up." He pushed the basin into Owen's hands. "If you can mend the lower thing, mend it. But mind yourself while you do it. The old stones are hungry tonight."

That was as close to pleading as Tallow had ever sounded.

Owen took the basin. The metal knocked lightly against his cracked nails. "All right."

Tallow hesitated. The old-work pressure had already made his breathing shallow. Even so, he reached out and pinched soot from Owen's sleeve as if Owen were about to embarrass him in front of guests.

"You've got ash on the cuff," he said. "It smears the pages."

Then he backed away up the passage, one hand against the wall, his expression set into the formal blankness he used when pain had outrun manners. Owen watched him go until the dark swallowed the twitch of the tea towel over his shoulder. After that there was only the undercroft and the battle above it.

The East Undercroft was not a chamber made for comfort, secrecy, or worship. It had none of the theatrics school legends would have given it. It was a built cavity under the castle, circular and low-vaulted, with founder-era ribs in the ceiling and damp shining at the joints. The floor had been cut into rings and channels so old that even the damage looked ancient. At the centre the anchor stone rose from the ground in a blunt canted mass of dark mineral, shoulder-high on Owen, too large to be an object and too specific to be natural. When he had first found it, months ago, it had seemed dead in the way machinery looks dead when nobody remembers the switch. The channels had been there. The star-table had been there. The pressure in the body had been there too: hot stone, ringing ears, the faint nausea of being somewhere reality had been persuaded into a stricter shape than the human body liked. But the anchor itself had stayed stubbornly blank.

Tonight it was lit from within by the castle's distress.

It was not bright, which would have made it easier. The grooves across its face held a dim metal wash, as if quicksilver had been rubbed into them and mostly wiped away. The floor-rings around it came and went with every heavy impact overhead. A hairline crack Owen had only inferred before now lay plain across one angle of the stone, no wider than a thread at first glance and then, when the next concussion struck somewhere up in the school, opening to the width of a fingernail before drawing tight again.

There you are, he thought, with the hard ugly satisfaction of finally seeing a thing he had been right about for too long.

He set the basin down on the outer ring and unshouldered his satchel. The Grinlow Leaves came out wrapped in his spare shirt. His ash-linen notebook came after, swollen with inserted pages and singed at two corners. He laid both on the driest stretch of floor he could find. When he tried for wandlight on instinct, the spell broke in his grip into a red spit of sparks and a smell like singed copper. He swore softly, shook the sting out of his fingers, and dragged one of Tallow's rags through the grey water instead.

The work did not want wizard theatre. It wanted surfaces.

He knelt on damp stone and scrubbed at the ring nearest the anchor until grit came up under the rag and the old cut lines showed cleaner. He wiped the face of the stone where the seam pulsed. He cleaned a flat patch for the manuscript. Dust kept falling. Once, a distant detonation above made a fresh veil of white sift over the place he had just cleared. He scrubbed it again. By the time the ring was usable, soot had printed the heel of his hand black and his knees ached from the pressure of the floor.

He opened the Leaves with his burned palm.

The pages that had stayed blind in the greenhouse and only half-yielded in the Departure Chamber now came up dense at once. Ink darkened out of the fibres in layered tables, old notation crossing itself, marginal hooks answering the geometry in the stone at his knees. The active node had given the manuscript teeth. Whole columns that had been pallid three days ago were suddenly legible enough to hurt. He found the sequence he had prepared in his notebook and then found more under it, conditional branches that had not existed until the castle met whatever threshold the battle had satisfied.

It was siege, he thought, or something close enough for founder work.

A thud passed through the ribs of the ceiling so hard that the water in the pudding basin jumped. A second followed before the first had finished travelling through the stone. The seam flashed wider and did not entirely close this time. Owen felt the old work gather under his hands, not welcoming, not exactly, but available. Hogwarts was close enough to breaking that the lower machinery had stopped pretending it did not need him.

He let out one breath, then another, and rolled his sleeves to the elbow.

He had his window because children and murderers were hitting the castle hard enough to wake the foundations.

* * *

The first difficulty was blood.

The Leaves had been clear enough about that much from the start. The work did not always require blood, it did not require it for every amendment, and it did not require it in the theatrical sense boys in dormitories whispered about when they wanted to feel wicked without understanding anything. The work wanted claim. It wanted a body to answer for the sentence being laid into the world. Founder stone could carry more of the burden than bare wood or open air, but not all of it. For anything beyond the botched little changes he had made in the greenhouse and corridors, the cost had to stick somewhere living first.

Owen dried the kitchen knife on his sleeve and set the edge against the thickened scar across his right palm.

He had expected the old burn to split reluctantly. Instead it opened almost at once, as if the scar had been waiting for a better excuse than adolescence and homework. Dark blood welled into the white seam and ran down to his wrist. He hissed, stupidly more from surprise than pain, and held his hand over the basin while the first drops hit the chalk-streaked water and unclouded it into threads of pink.

That, at least, was legible.

He stirred chalk and blood together with the knife tip until it made a thin slurry, not paint and not quite paste. The outer ring wanted width more than intricacy. He dipped two fingers into the mixture and began laying it into the cut channel circling the anchor. The line had to be unbroken. Twice the floor shuddered hard enough to make him skid outside the groove, and twice he wiped the mistake away with the heel of his hand and went over it again. By the time he completed the ring his fingertips were white and red together, the chalk drying his skin to paper.

He moved inward.

Nothing about the amendment resembled spellcasting once it was actually happening. There was no sensation of power gathering cleanly at the wand arm. There was no obedient wait in the air for the right syllables. It felt more like aligning a cracked weight-bearing wall while the rest of the building was being shelled. He had to persuade the categories to meet. That was the only language his mind ever found for it, however little it resembled school magic.

He could not try to mend the ward. That was too large and too vain and would have killed him inside a minute.

He could not try to save the castle. The castle did not require saving in the sentimental sense, and sentiment was poison in this work.

He went smaller until the terms fitted.

He aimed at this seam, this local failure of counted enclosure, this refusal at one edge of one node to go on meaning hidden when pressured to brightness from outside and violence from within.

He wrote the narrowed claim down in the notation he actually possessed: Meridian hooks where he trusted them, translated English in his notebook margin where he did not, old school Latin only when the Leaves and the anchor table both supported the same inference. He spoke as he wrote, quietly at first and then louder when the chamber kept trying to drown him out.

"As bounded, remain bounded. As sheltered in stone, remain counted under shelter. Let the broken edge answer to the held edge."

The words were not the amendment. They were the handhold that let his mind keep the shape from blowing apart.

He drew a second line from the outer ring to the seam's lower fork, then a third toward the star-cut nearest the anchor's western face. The moment the third line connected, the stone answered with pressure rather than light.

Something pushed up through his hands and into his forearms, a low hard surge that made the joints in his wrists feel threaded too tight. The chamber hummed. A crack raced in one of the damp wall-joints and sealed again. Somewhere far above, several spells failed at once. He knew that not because he could see them but because the backlash came through the castle like a missed step through rotten stairs. Conventional magic did not like old work. Conventional magic never had.

He nearly lost the line there. The wording in his head blurred. For a few ugly seconds the symbols on the page looked as arbitrary as scratches in frost. Then another impact overhead widened the seam again and the geometry became obvious in the brutal way only panic ever made things obvious to him. He bent lower, blood dripping off his hand onto the stone, and corrected the branch he had misread.

"Not the whole join," he muttered. "Just the edge. Just this edge. Don't be stupid."

He was speaking to himself. He was speaking to the castle. The distinction had stopped mattering.

The next ten minutes, or forty, or three, had no sensible shape. Time in the undercroft broke down into labour units: redraw, check, phrase, feel for answer, repair the place where the answer had come through too violently, start again before the whole thing sloughed off. Smoke kept descending in erratic breaths from the passage behind him. Dust gathered in the wet channels and had to be blown clear. Once his nose started bleeding and would not stop, hot threads down over his upper lip and into his mouth. He wiped it away on his shoulder without looking up. The blood helped and that made him hate it.

The battle above never resolved into scenes. It only kept producing evidence. A run of impacts on the same side of the castle sent a long groan through the ceiling ribs and shook loose chips of mortar that pattered into the chalk like hail. A sound reached him down one service flue so cleanly that for a second it might have been in the room itself: someone screaming a wordless full-throated scream and then cutting off in the middle. The next concussion came half a breath later, heavy enough to ring the anchor like struck iron.

Owen did not look upward. There was no upward to look at.

He kept working until one section of the seam stopped flickering under pressure and held the same width through three impacts in a row.

That was the first real success.

He knew it bodily before he knew it in words. The nausea eased by a degree. The ringing in his ears changed pitch. The anchor's western face, which had been spitting dim metallic pulses at random, settled into a steadier wash. The repaired edge did not heal exactly, because healing was a human metaphor and the stone had no use for it. But one side of the failure stopped behaving like an open mouth.

And because that side held, something else in the table declared itself.

It rose first on the manuscript page as a set of marginal bars he had taken for damage. Then the same pattern came up in the stone to his left, under a film of dust he had not bothered to wipe because it had seemed irrelevant. Owen bent over it, breathing through his mouth. The first pass gave him wardwork, which was useless, obvious, and probably wrong.

Another impact ran through the anchor. One of the bars opened into a hooked stroke he had only seen in house-law clauses about instruments and use. His first reading sloughed off at once. This branch was about wands, and it carried the local syntax of Hogwarts rather than the colder Meridian hand: rooms, bodies, and what a school roof was willing to host. A founder clause nested inside the broader anchor relation, dormant until the castle was driven near enough to open siege and somebody at the node had already taken direct claim.

He wiped the dust off with his sleeve and leaned close enough that blood from his nose freckled the stone.

The clause read: hostile wandwork within the held bounds may be suspended by the house under direct claim.

He checked it against the manuscript, then against the star-table, because wishful reading here was just stupidity in academic dress. The clause bit down as he followed it: hostile meant intent already moving toward a body; wandwork meant wood, wielder, and aimed harm in one live line; suspended meant the house would choke that line off while the claim held. A Killing Curse would gutter green and die in the wand. So would Neville's next Stunning Spell if he was already throwing it at a man on a staircase. So would the jinx Martha might be slinging back over her shoulder while she dragged some bleeding child by the robes. It would not stop a knife, a kicked body, or masonry already coming down. And nobody upstairs had ever used it because nobody upstairs could see it. The branch only surfaced once the house was already under siege and a claimant was physically holding the live node. That claimant was him.

Owen sat back on his heels so abruptly that the chamber tilted.

Something shouted down one of the service flues, clear enough to skin the abstraction off it. "Longbottom!" somebody yelled, voice cracking with smoke. Then Neville himself, hoarse and furious: "Get them down. Now." A crash answered him, then a thinner cry cut short hard enough to leave the stone listening after it.

He closed his eyes for one blink, opened them, and tried to prove the reading false. He looked for the negation mark that would turn hostile into hostile-from-without. He looked for an exception that would spare defenders, or children, or anybody the house liked better than a sentence liked them. The branch kept offering the same answer, clean and hateful. With it open, with the authority he already had half-built under his hands, he could reach through the castle and cancel hostile wand relation inside the bounds.

Another impact struck somewhere close enough to shed grit over the page. The new clause flared silver-grey in the stone.

Grit skipped across the line and the line kept shining. Theory had already run out. The branch sat there waiting for his mouth.

* * *

He had one live working and two possible sentences.

That was the shape of it once he forced himself to stop staring.

The seam he had caught on the western face was still only a caught edge. If he took both hands away from it, if he let the phrasing blur for long enough to retune the relation entirely, the repair would not wait politely in place. It would sag back open. He had never held this much old work before. His forearms already trembled with it. The cracked beds of his nails throbbed in time with the anchor hum. The taste in his mouth had gone from blood to chalk to something stranger, a flat mineral bitterness as if the chamber air were ground from old mortar and swallowed directly.

He turned the choice over anyway, because not to do so would have been a lie so childish even he could not manage it.

There were ways to fake ignorance. He had used them all year. Narrow the question. Answer the smaller thing. Pretend timing was the only issue. Pretend a task had no moral surface because it had technical depth. None of that worked here. The branch sat in the stone beside him, exact and bright. Upstairs, people were already dying. The clause would help them. Not cleanly. It would kill the curses and the counter-curses alike. Neville would lose whatever spell he was about to throw as surely as the man facing him. Martha would lose the spell she was using to keep somebody off her as surely as the curse aimed at her. The school would still be full of knives, shattered banisters, loose stone, and frightened bodies with momentum. But the Killing Curses would stop being possible here. A few more minutes of the battle at its present pitch and the seam under his left hand would start failing faster than he could correct.

He could not have both.

That was the cruel mechanical fact of it, and because it was mechanical he trusted it. He could either spend this authority locally, on the school war everybody else understood, or he could keep the sentence narrow and force one edge of the larger breach to remain answerable. There might be a world in which a stronger practitioner could have split attention and done both. There was not a world in which Owen Phipps, fifteen, half-bleeding into a chalk ring under a collapsing school, could do it.

The clause for hostile wandwork opened under his fingers like a second handle.

He touched it.

The response was immediate and monstrous in its intimacy. What the anchor offered was relation, not faces, but relation gathered itself around people he knew whether he wanted it or not. One line held with Neville's plain forward pressure: wand up, command already in the chest behind it. Another snagged and dragged on bad time, Martha's limp made visible as magical rhythm. The rest were knots of murderous intention, frightened defence, wood, cores, and aimed harm. If he finished the sentence, the lethal lines would gutter. So would Neville's next curse. So would the next spell Martha threw over her shoulder to keep someone off her. The house would not sort the worthy from the murderous. It would only refuse hostile wandwork and leave the rest to bone, stone, and whoever could close the distance fastest.

Something came down the flue then, not words at first but a body hitting masonry hard enough that even the undercroft felt it. After it came Neville, unmistakable despite the distortion. "Move. Keep them moving." Another voice answered too blurred to place. Then someone screamed in pain, and farther off somebody shouted for a healer who might already be dead.

A distant boom travelled through the floor so heavily that the new branch brightened to white for an instant.

He saw, in the ugly cold shorthand the First Art always preferred, what the clause would do to that moment: Neville's wand going dead in his fist while the curse aimed past him spat out into sparks instead; Martha half-carrying a first-year on the bad leg, the spell she threw behind her dying with the attack she was outrunning; Prue, if she was still anywhere near the kitchen mouth, left with nothing but corridor stone, breath, and whatever ordinary courage a body had left. The thought did not soften him. It only made the terms worse.

If he turned the authority sideways now, more of them might live.

If he let the seam go, the larger breach remained what it was: Earth flashing brighter to the thing outside, the old masking structure one hard battle closer to tearing where no corridor heroics could reach it.

A scream carried down the flue again. This one did not cut off cleanly. It thinned into the kind of ragged shouting that meant several people at once and no room left for dignity.

Owen put his bleeding hand flat over the hostile-wand branch and began, under his breath, to shape the opening claim.

The anchor lurched toward him.

Nothing in the room shifted by an inch, but the live seam under his other hand loosened at once, the repaired edge slackening like stitching pulled from wet cloth. He felt the outer relation shift, the held margin giving way because he had stopped asking it to remain held and had offered the stone a different priority. It would take so little. That was the most terrible part. It would take one more line into the branch, one full sentence of direct claim. Neville's wand would be dead wood in his hand. The green curse meant for somebody behind him would fail. The spell Martha threw back would fail with it. The battle above would turn at once into dropped wands, running bodies, and empty hands while the older wound widened quietly below.

He stopped.

For a second he stayed bent over both possibilities, not moving, the opening words of the clause sour in his mouth.

Then he took his hand off the new branch.

The slack in the repaired seam turned instantly savage, as if the stone were punishing indecision. Owen swore, shoved two fingers back into the chalk-and-blood slurry, and rammed the line deeper into the western fork to keep the edge from unravelling altogether. The correction cost him a fresh burst of pain up both arms. His nose bled harder. One of his thumbnail beds split wide enough to wet the stone.

He knew what he had just done.

Above him the battle went on at full pitch. The clause beside his elbow remained legible, ready, entirely valid. Its opening words were still in his mouth. The seam had gone slack the moment he leaned toward it. He could still feel the pull of the other claim in his wrist and shoulder. He could have finished it. Instead he had hauled the repair back into place and fed the older wound his blood. He had chosen the other sentence.

The recognition passed through him cleanly and left no room for self-pity.

Another blow landed somewhere in the school and sent a wave of dust across the floor. It thickened the edge of the hostile-wand branch until the symbols looked almost frosted. Owen did not wipe them clear this time. He bent back over the seam, shoulders hunched, and narrowed his attention again to this edge, this bound, this held relation under stress.

He spoke the repair phrasing louder now because the chamber had become difficult to hear inside. Each word scraped his throat raw with soot. He fed the next line from the outer ring into the western face. He pressed his reopened palm against the crack until blood slicked into the cut channel and the stone accepted it. The anchor answered with a hard descending hum that ran through the floor and up the walls. Somewhere above, conventional magic buckled in reply. He felt it in the undercroft as a long shiver of failed compatibility moving through stair, corridor, classroom, hall.

Good, he thought with a viciousness that surprised him. Answer, then.

He kept it in the only category he could bear: work. Chalk dried in the cuts, soot scraped his throat, and he forced line after line into the stone. Anything grander would have stopped him.

He built the next sentence smaller than the last, precise enough to survive his exhaustion. The repaired edge tightened. The hairline crack across the anchor's face drew down from a fingernail width to a thread-fine stubbornness, damage left visible even while the stone agreed to behave. The western side now held through impact after impact without opening all the way to brightness.

By the time he trusted that much, both sleeves were stiff with chalk and blood. The kitchen knife had fallen somewhere into the outer dark. The basin water was almost black. His hands no longer felt attached at the wrists in an entirely ordinary way. When he flexed his wand fingers, the air around them fuzzed faintly, as if the old work had left an afterimage in his magical signature and not yet decided whether to depart.

The hostile-wand branch stayed lit on the anchor's left face.

Once, as he shifted his weight, his sleeve brushed it and came away silver-grey with stone dust. He looked at the mark, then looked back to the seam.

Above him people kept killing and being killed by means he could have interrupted.

He pressed his bleeding palm back to the older wound and kept speaking.
