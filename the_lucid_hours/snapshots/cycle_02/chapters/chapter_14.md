# Chapter 14

The resident in Room 110 had gotten stool clear through the pad again by seven-thirty, third time that shift. Tammy rolled her onto her side, cleaned the mess out of the folds of her skin, worked a dry sheet under her hips, and snapped a fresh brief into place while the woman stared at the wall and made that low radiator sound she always made. Barrier cream. New linens. Dirty bundle into the hamper. The smell followed Tammy into the hall and stayed in her scrubs.

When she came back with a fresh towel, Carmen was still in Agnes Chen's room. Med pass had ended forty minutes ago. The cart was locked at the station, but Carmen had the door to 108A pulled nearly shut and was talking low enough that Tammy couldn't make out words. Agnes sat in her chair by the window with her chin tucked down like always. Her eyes stayed on Carmen's mouth. When Tammy passed with the linen bag, Agnes's eyes cut to her, then back to Carmen. Quick. Exact.

At ten-fifty-five Priya came on and Carmen gave count. Tammy was wiping Mr. Delaney's hands after his pudding cup and could hear the numbers from the station. Oxy balanced. Alprazolam balanced. Priya signed. Tammy dropped the wrapper in the trash by the station. Carmen was locking the cart.

"What's going on with Chen?" Tammy said.

Carmen didn't look up from the lock, but her hands slowed for a beat before the answer came. "She's declining fluids again. I was documenting her intake."

"That's forty minutes to document fluid intake."

"I had charting to catch up on, too." Carmen adjusted her badge. "You know how it gets."

"Sure," Tammy said.

Carmen said, "I've got one note left," and headed back down Wing A without her coat. Ten minutes later, when Tammy clocked out, the spot Carmen used by the side entrance was still full.

Home was twelve minutes away: a studio in Dunellen over Grace and her Christian radio, four hundred seventy-five a month because the carpet pad still held the previous tenant's cats. Kevin had the house, the better furniture, and the blond Verizon girl from Bridgewater Tammy had once looked up on Facebook after too much Yellow Tail. Tammy had the stained couch, the Walmart TV, and sixteen dollars and fifty cents an hour. She had Friday off for once. She sat in her car instead of starting it.

She backed into the far row facing Wing A, the row she'd used for fifteen years when she wanted ten minutes alone with a vending-machine coffee or, back when she still smoked, a cigarette. From there you could see the far windows if the lights were on, including the TV room around the bend. Inside the building, that room sat in a blind spot. The sub-station couldn't see it. Devon never got up enough to try.

Something was off. Tammy had learned to wait on the bigger words until she could back them up. Carmen had been different since the state survey in September. Before that she did her med passes, charted, and went home. After, she'd started lingering after count, closing herself into certain rooms. Harry Zielinski's. Agnes Chen's. Lola Fernandez's. Three oxy missing one week, five alprazolam another, always followed by the kind of explanation that sounded fine until you had heard six of them in a month.

Agnes bothered Tammy more than the counts. Counts could be bad math. Agnes was different. Tammy remembered her on admission three years ago, pissed off enough to fill legal pads with complaints to the ombudsman and her daughter and anybody else with a mailbox. Then one week that all stopped. The chart called it decline. Tammy knew decline. Mrs. Okafor had lost language a little at a time, over months, like coins slipping out of a torn pocket. Agnes had gone still in a week flat. Only her eyes kept giving her away. They tracked conversation, tracked chart talk, tracked Carmen.

Once Tammy let herself say that in her own head, other things lined up behind it. Harry missed the month and the president when Dr. Prasad asked. But last Tuesday she'd been doing water pitchers on Wing A after the dinner trays went back. Harry's door was open. She knocked and leaned in.

"You need anything, Mr. Z?"

He was on the edge of the bed with both feet on the floor. He looked up clear.

"No, I'm fine. Thank you, Tammy."

Same man usually asked for Eleanor four times a shift. Tonight he gave her a short, finished sentence with her name in it, and the hand that reached over and pulled his own door shut was steady. Next morning he was back to asking Priya where Eleanor was.

Lola could chatter herself halfway to Cuba and forget what day it was, then move through a fire drill cleaner than half the staff. Carmen staying late joined the pile. Taken together, it was enough to keep Tammy in the parking lot on a cold Thursday instead of going upstairs to cat stink and silence.

In fifteen years she'd seen a man turn blue on a piece of chicken while she tried the Heimlich on the dining-room floor. She'd seen a night nurse snoring in the med room with vodka on her breath. She'd seen Cliff hang fresh posters in the lobby while hearing-aid batteries went unordered for a month. Strange shit showed up every shift. Carmen behind 108A, 112A, and 114A after count had pattern in it.

Cliff would get nothing until she had something he couldn't shrug off. Marcus Webb had complained about understaffing two years ago and got himself fired for the trouble. The understaffing stayed. Forty-two residents still got handed to one overnight aide with a phone glow on his face and a nap coming by midnight. Reporting was how you ended up written up for your tone while the actual problem kept breathing. Tammy decided to watch.

At eleven-twelve Priya crossed the station with a chart. At eleven-twenty Devon was already sunk at the sub-station, shoulders rounded, phone lighting his chin blue. At eleven-thirty Carmen's car was still there. Tammy killed the engine and let the heater tick into silence so she'd hear if a side door opened.

After fifteen years in one building Tammy knew useless things. Which hall had the bad fluorescent tube. Which rooms smelled like GI bleed before the labs caught up. Which fire door stuck in August and which window on Wing A lined up with the parking lot if you sat two rows back. Tammy had spent enough unpaid breaks out here to know the angle. If anybody used the TV room after midnight and turned on the light, she'd see it from where she sat.

She watched the blank dark glass and did wage math because that was what her brain did when it had nothing better. Twenty-two hundred clear on a decent month. Four seventy-five rent. Two-forty car payment. Gas. Phone. Groceries. Enough left for cereal and ibuprofen and not a lot else. Kevin's Post-it from the divorce had said Sorry, T. She still thought about that some nights because twelve years of carrying somebody's lunches to the fridge should've bought more than a cheap little note.

At twelve-thirty the TV room light came on.

Tammy sat forward. The room was still too far away for faces, and one light by itself meant almost nothing. An insomniac resident could've wandered. Priya could've parked somebody in there with late-night cable to stop the yelling. Staff used empty common rooms for phone calls sometimes, especially the travel nurses who didn't want to cry where anybody knew them. But the TV room was the farthest common area from the station, around the L-turn where Devon couldn't see it, and the care plans said residents were supposed to be in bed. The light stayed on. After a few seconds shapes moved across the window. One of them looked low and wide, maybe a wheelchair. Another crossed in front of it and back again.

Tammy stayed another ten minutes. The light stayed on. Nobody came out the front. Nobody came out the side door she could see. The whole thing sat in her chest the way a bad skin spot did when you found it on a resident's heel and knew you weren't looking at a one-day problem.

She drove home a little before one. The apartment smelled like old cats the second she opened the door. She hung her jacket on the hook Kevin had installed back when there had been a hallway worth hanging things in, pulled a junk-mail envelope off the counter, and found a pen.

On the back she wrote: 10:55 p.m. count. 11:30 p.m. Carmen's car still there. 12:30 a.m. TV room light. She added the date and slid the envelope into her purse.

Friday was supposed to be her day off. She set her phone alarm for 11:15 p.m. anyway.
