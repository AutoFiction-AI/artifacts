# Chapter 1

The air at Lakeview Gardens didn't move in August. The building's central AC had been serviced in June — Carmen had seen the work order on Cliff Moorehead's desk, signed and filed, problem solved on paper — but the vents in Wing A pushed out a thin, tepid breath that did nothing against the heat stored in the walls and the linoleum and the bodies of sixty-three residents who hadn't been outside since the Fourth of July barbecue in the courtyard, the one with the grocery-store sheet cake and the aide who burned the hot dogs. The smell was the first thing about Lakeview Gardens and the last thing. Pine disinfectant laid over urine — that was the base note, the constant, the thing visitors noticed and staff stopped noticing after a week. On top of it: the yeasty warmth of skin that didn't get enough air, institutional chicken from the dinner trays stacked on the cart outside the kitchen, hand sanitizer from the dispensers mounted every fifteen feet, and under all of it, close and sweet and impossible to name precisely, the smell of people who lived in a building. Whose skin and hair and breath and the lotions rubbed into their bedsores and the talcum powder shaken into their skin folds had become part of the building's own air.

Carmen Reyes pulled on a pair of nitrile gloves and knocked on the doorframe of Room 110.

"Mr. Delvecchio, I'm going to help you turn now, OK?"

The moaning didn't change. It never did. Mr. Delvecchio was ninety-one and contracted into a permanent C-shape, a contracture that had set in years before Carmen started — his knees drawn to his chest, his hands curled against his sternum, his spine a fixed curve that no amount of physical therapy was going to straighten. He moaned on every exhale. A low, steady sound, not distressed exactly, more like the sound a building makes when the pipes are running — constant, mechanical, part of the room. Carmen had been at Lakeview thirteen months. She didn't hear it anymore unless she stopped and listened on purpose.

She put her hands on his shoulder and hip, braced her feet on the linoleum, and rolled him toward her. He weighed sixty-eight pounds according to the care plan and she could believe it — he was terrifyingly light, a body that had shed everything it didn't absolutely need. She repositioned the pillow between his knees, tugged the draw sheet smooth under his back, and checked his sacrum. She pressed two fingers against the skin at the base of his spine and watched for blanching, for the redness that meant pressure was building toward a wound. The skin was intact. She noted the time on the positioning log clipped to the wall: 4:15 PM. He needed to be turned every two hours. The six o'clock turn was supposed to be the aide's job, but Devon had called out sick for the second time this week, and the evening was short-staffed, which meant Carmen would either do it herself between med pass and dinner trays or find Tammy, who was already covering both wings alone.

She stripped the gloves, dropped them in the bin by the door, pumped sanitizer from the wall dispenser into her palms, and rubbed them together as she walked. The sanitizer was cold for half a second, then warm, then gone. Her hands were dry and cracked at the knuckles from it. Thirteen months of hand sanitizer and handwashing and nitrile gloves, and her hands looked ten years older than the rest of her.

Room 112. Lola Fernandez was sitting up in bed with a game show on, the volume turned low. Her roommate, Mrs. Kim, was asleep behind the half-drawn privacy curtain — a thin woman who weighed less than Mr. Delvecchio and slept twenty hours a day. Lola's silver hair was loose on her shoulders. She hadn't been put to bed yet. She held a coffee cup in both hands, and the tremor was visible from the doorway — a fine, constant shake that made the surface of the coffee ripple under the overhead fluorescent. The gold crucifix at her throat caught the light from the television.

"Mija," Lola said. She smiled — warm, loose, the kind of smile that was present but not entirely tracking. "Come keep me company for one minute."

"How are you feeling tonight, Mrs. Fernandez?"

"Oh, these hands are being rude." Lola lifted the saucer a fraction and watched the coffee shiver. "Tammy'll fuss if I spill on the blanket again. She fusses nice, though. Is this the one with the doors? No, no, that's the other one. My daughter sends those church cards with the saints in blue robes. I keep meaning to write back." She trailed off, still smiling. The tremor made the cup rattle faintly against the saucer on her lap.

"Tammy can handle a little coffee."

"Mm. She handles everybody."

Carmen wrapped the blood pressure cuff around Lola's left arm, pumped, watched the gauge, released. She clipped the pulse oximeter onto Lola's index finger and waited for the number. She wrote the vitals on her clipboard. Lola's Parkinson's was in the chart — the neurologist's notes from six months ago described early-stage disease, resting tremor predominant, cogwheel rigidity in the right wrist. It was real. The tremor made Lola's handwriting a kind of seismograph, turned her signature on the meal-preference form into a jittery line that barely resembled the version from her admission paperwork a year and a half ago.

"Tammy's going to come by and help you get ready for bed, OK?"

Lola nodded, already turning back to the television. A woman from somewhere in the Midwest was trying to guess the price of a washer-dryer set. The host's voice was bright and urgent and had nothing to do with anything in the room.

---

The medication pass was supposed to take sixty minutes. It took ninety when the evening was fully staffed and longer when it wasn't. Carmen pushed the med cart down the hall of Wing A, room by room, the cart's bad wheel catching on every rotation with a click that followed her like a metronome. She'd put in a maintenance request for the wheel three weeks ago and again last week. The wheel still clicked.

Each resident had a labeled slot in the cart's drawers. Each dose was cross-referenced against the medication administration record on her clipboard — drug name, dosage, route, time, her initials, her license number. She poured pills into paper cups, handed them over with water, watched them swallow, checked mouths, documented. The routine was the same every shift. The routine was the job.

Agnes Chen, Room 108A. The room was dim. Agnes's roommate, Mrs. Nowak, was asleep on her back with her mouth open, her dementia so advanced that she occupied the bed the way furniture occupied a room — present, unaware, requiring maintenance. Agnes sat in her wheelchair by the window, her back straight, facing the wall. She was small and thin, gray hair cut short, wire-rimmed glasses reflecting the slant of hallway light. She didn't turn when Carmen came in. She didn't acknowledge the cart or the pill cup or the water or Carmen.

"Mrs. Chen? I have your evening medications."

Nothing. The wall held Agnes's attention with an intensity that suggested it was showing her something no one else could see.

"You can take them when you're ready, or I can come back before end of shift."

Agnes didn't move. Carmen set the cup and water on the bedside table and documented the refusal on the MAR — *Patient declined, medications left at bedside per patient preference.* It was Agnes's right to refuse. Some nights she took them. Some nights she sat in the dark like this, silent and upright, and Carmen would find the pills untouched at the next check. The physicians had noted it. Nobody had changed anything about it.

Room 116A. Earl Washington was in the recliner by the window, a magazine open on his lap — something from the facility's waiting-room stack, months old, a copy of *Reader's Digest* with a cover about Mediterranean diets. Earl was tall even folded into the chair, with hands large enough to make the magazine look like a pamphlet. He had the settled, still expression of a man who had spent decades in rooms where other people talked and he listened and when he spoke the room paid attention because it happened so rarely. He looked up when Carmen came in. He nodded once.

"Evening medications, Mr. Washington."

He took the cup, swallowed everything with one pull of water, and handed the cup back. He didn't speak. Carmen checked the time, documented, and moved on.

Burt Kovacs occupied the other bed in 116A. He was in it, which was where Burt usually was by this hour — propped up on pillows, his wispy white hair pressed flat on one side, the blanket pulled up to his chest. His hands lay on top of the blanket, long and thin, with the fine bones of someone who'd done precise, delicate work. They shook — not the rhythmic tremor of Parkinson's disease but something looser, a general trembling that came from everywhere at once, as though his body couldn't quite hold itself steady. Carmen checked the fentanyl patch on his left shoulder, pressed down an edge that was starting to curl, and offered his evening oxycodone in the paper cup.

"How's the pain tonight, Mr. Kovacs?"

He blinked at her. His eyes moved across her face without settling. Something tried to organize itself behind them — a recognition, a thought — and didn't make it.

"Erzsébet?" The name came out with a soft Hungarian vowel on the first syllable. His wife. She'd been dead five years.

"It's Carmen. Your nurse tonight."

He kept looking at her, or past her, at something the room contained that she couldn't see. His mouth worked for a moment without producing sound. Then he took the pills with the mechanical compliance of a man who no longer tracked what he was swallowing or why. Carmen gave him water, checked his mouth, adjusted his blanket. She touched his shoulder briefly — not procedure, just contact — and he didn't seem to register it.

She moved on. The cart's wheel clicked behind her down the hall.

---

At seven o'clock, Tammy Briggs was in the supply closet restocking her cart with disposable briefs and bed pads. Her scrubs carried the damp-laundry smell of someone who'd been lifting and turning and cleaning for four hours in a building where the air conditioning was a polite fiction. Her ponytail had loosened to the point where it was less a hairstyle than a reminder that one had existed earlier. She looked tired, but the tiredness on Tammy had settled into the grooves beside her mouth, the set of her shoulders, the flat efficiency of her movements. Carmen couldn't remember ever seeing her look rested.

"Devon called out," she said, not looking up from the supply shelf.

"I know."

"So I'm running both wings by myself, which, yeah." She pulled a package of underpads from the shelf and set it on her cart with the economy of someone who'd done this motion so many times her hands knew the weight and trajectory without her brain's involvement. "Mrs. Ramos was in the same brief she was in at three o'clock when I got to her. I changed her right after, but that's what it is. That's what happens."

"I'll take the turns on Wing A if you can handle the bed routines."

"Already done. Mrs. Fernandez is set — brushed her hair, got her teeth soaking, water on the nightstand. Two sugars and milk for the morning coffee. Not cream, milk. She'll tell you it doesn't matter but it matters." Tammy said this the way she said everything — flat, factual, the information delivered without warmth or complaint, just the accumulated knowledge of fifteen years of showing up. She knew Lola Fernandez's coffee order the way she knew which call bells were real emergencies and which were loneliness and which were the button getting pressed by accident when a resident shifted in bed. "Mrs. Kim's done. Mr. Patel's done. I'll get the rest of B wing after I restock."

Carmen's badge had twisted on its lanyard. She straightened it without thinking — the LPN credential, her photo, the Lakeview Gardens logo, all facing forward. "Did Cliff come by?"

"Five o'clock. Wanted the fall-risk numbers for the quarter."

"What'd you say?"

"I told him they were in the system. He said he couldn't find them. I told him they were in the system." She paused, looked at Carmen with an expression that contained an entire performance review of Cliff Moorehead's administrative capabilities. "He went away."

Carmen had seen Cliff earlier, briefly, passing through the front hallway between the lobby and his office at around 4:30. He'd been moving with the soft, hurried posture of a man shaped by his desk — fifty-two, round in the way that suggested long hours sitting and lunch eaten in front of a computer, wearing a purple polo that meant he'd come from some kind of meeting. He'd nodded at Carmen without stopping. He cared about survey scores and census numbers and documentation that looked correct from a distance, which was different from documentation that was correct, a distinction Carmen had learned to navigate without anyone explicitly teaching her.

"He wants the metrics," Tammy said, pushing her cart toward the door. "He doesn't want to know what they cost."

She paused at the hallway junction and glanced back. "Oh — I put in a request for more medium gloves. We're out again. The smalls don't fit anybody and the larges are like putting your hands in grocery bags. So." She shrugged and turned the corner. Her cart's wheels didn't squeak. Tammy's equipment worked because Tammy fixed things herself rather than waiting for maintenance to ignore the request.

At 7:35, as Carmen parked the cart outside Room 114 and lined Harry Zielinski's blister cards against the raised lip of the med-cart top, Tammy called from 110. Mr. Delvecchio had slid crooked against the rail and soaked through the brief and pad. Carmen locked the cart, snapped on fresh gloves, and went in. Between the two of them they rolled him, cleaned him, changed the brief, checked the sacrum again, and hauled the draw sheet flat under his spine. By the time Carmen stripped the gloves, her badge was stuck to her chest, the med pass was forty minutes behind, and Harry's open MAR was still waiting on the cart where she'd left it.

---

By 10:30, the building had settled into its night register. The overhead lights in the hallway were at half-brightness — the institutional dusk that was supposed to promote sleep and mostly promoted a flat, shadowless dimness that made everything look slightly unreal. The sounds of the day shift — therapy sessions, visitors' voices, the clatter of meal trays, call bells answered quickly because there were enough people to answer them — had been replaced by the building's permanent underneath. The ventilation hummed. A wheelchair squeaked on linoleum somewhere on B wing. The TV in Room 104 was still on — Mrs. Cho's Korean dramas, which she watched until she fell asleep with the remote in her hand, and which someone on the overnight shift would turn off around two in the morning. And from Room 110, steady and unchanging, Mr. Delvecchio's moaning, which at this hour carried further because there was less noise to absorb it.

Carmen did her end-of-shift controlled substance count in the medication room. The room was small and smelled like alcohol wipes and the faint chalky dust of pills that had been counted and poured and counted all day. She opened the locked cabinet, pulled each bottle, and counted. Oxycodone five-milligram tablets: she tapped each one onto the counting tray with her fingertip, moving them from one side to the other. Alprazolam, one milligram. Tramadol, one hundred milligrams. She recorded the numbers, compared them to the 3 PM starting count, cross-referenced against the MAR for every dose she'd dispensed. The math was clean. She signed the log and placed it on the counter for the night nurse's countersignature.

The night nurse was a registry temp named Keisha whom Carmen had never seen before. She was already at the station, phone in hand, a travel mug of something next to the keyboard. Carmen gave report: the fall risks, the NPO orders for morning labs, which residents were restless and might ring, which ones hadn't voided and should be checked.

"Got it," Keisha said.

"Mr. Delvecchio in 110 needs to be turned at midnight. The positioning log is on the wall."

"Mm-hm."

Carmen looked at her for a second longer than was professional. There was nothing else she could do about the next eight hours. She washed her hands — twenty seconds, the birthday song in her head, the duration on the hand-hygiene poster so deeply internalized that she did it at home, at Gloria's, at the gas station bathroom — pulled off her badge, clocked out.

The parking lot was dark and held the day's heat in the asphalt. Late August in central New Jersey didn't cool down at night so much as it stopped getting worse — the air was heavy and still and sat on her skin like something with weight. Two of the lot lights still worked. The rest had been out since spring. Carmen's car, a 2019 Nissan Sentra with a dent in the rear bumper from a parking garage incident three months ago that she couldn't afford to repair, was parked under the farther of the two working lights. She sat in it with the engine idling and the AC blowing directly on her face, the sweat cooling in her hairline and in the small of her back where her scrubs had been sticking to her skin since six o'clock. She sat there for a minute, maybe two, and then she put the car in drive.

---

Gloria opened the door at 11:20 in a housecoat, holding a mug of chamomile tea. The house smelled the way it had smelled Carmen's entire life: garlic, Fabuloso, and the votiva candle on the kitchen counter next to the framed photograph of Carmen's father. He'd been dead eight years. The candle burned new every Monday.

"He ate his rice. He didn't eat the beans."

"He never eats the beans, Mami."

"I made them soft, with the ham pieces. The way he likes."

"He's seven. He doesn't like beans."

Gloria's face compressed into an expression that held several firmly maintained opinions about childhood nutrition, the decline of home cooking, and the general state of a generation that would rather eat chicken nuggets than *frijoles negros* made with love and a ham bone. "He's in the back."

Reggie was asleep on the foldout couch in what had been Carmen's bedroom and was now Gloria's sewing room. He was curled on his side with one sock on and one sock off, and a library book about sea turtles lay open on the pillow next to his head. He was small for seven — forty-eight pounds at his last pediatric checkup, which the doctor said was fine, he'd catch up — and when he slept his face still had the soft, unfinished quality of a much younger child, the cheekbones not yet sharpened into the structure that was going to be Carmen's.

She picked him up. He stirred, mumbled something about a whale shark, and dropped back to sleep with the absolute surrender of a child who had never once worried about where he would wake up. She carried him down the hall past the kitchen with its garlic smell and its candle and its counter lined with the same spice jars that had been there when Carmen was in high school.

"Mija, you look tired."

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine. Estás trabajando demasiado."

"I'm picking up a double tomorrow. I'll bring him Friday morning."

"Friday," Gloria said, in the tone of someone deciding whether to argue and then deciding it wasn't worth it. She stood in the doorway and watched Carmen strap Reggie into his booster seat. For a moment her face held something — worry, or the knowledge that worry was useless and she was going to do it anyway — before she raised a hand and went inside.

Carmen drove. Fifteen minutes from Gloria's to the apartment in the kind of light traffic that existed only after eleven on a weeknight in this part of New Jersey — a few trucks on Route 18, the occasional set of headlights coming the other way, the strip malls dark except for the signs. Reggie slept in the back with his head tilted against the booster seat's wing. She didn't turn on the radio. She drove in the specific silence of a person whose entire day had been sound — call bells, moaning, the med cart's clicking wheel, Tammy's flat commentary, Lola's game show, the ventilation hum, Keisha's "got it" — and who needed the quiet the way her lower back needed to not be lifting anyone for the next six hours.

The apartment was on the second floor of a two-story building on a street of two-story buildings, in a neighborhood that had been built for young families in the 1980s and now rented to whoever could make the number work. The first-floor tenant cooked at all hours. The stairwell smelled like cumin and something else — fenugreek, maybe, or cardamom — that Carmen had never identified and didn't dislike but couldn't escape. She carried Reggie up the stairs, his forty-eight pounds heavier than sixty-eight-pound Mr. Delvecchio because Reggie was warm and alive and shifting in her arms, and her shoulders were already stiff from the turns she'd done on Wing A. She got him into his bed, pulled off the remaining sock, pulled up the sheet.

The apartment was two bedrooms, a kitchen she could cross in three steps, a bathroom with a stall shower and no tub. She'd found it by spending three days on her phone between patients, calling every listing under fourteen hundred a month within a twenty-five-minute drive of Lakeview. Twenty-five minutes was the limit — the farthest she could push the commute and still make the pickup from Gloria's and back in time for the next shift. The only place under the line had this second-floor walk-up, a shower stall with cracked caulk, and rent she could cover as long as the Sentra didn't need anything.

She ate standing at the counter: arroz con pollo she'd cooked Sunday and portioned into containers for the week, each one the same size, each one enough. She washed the container, dried it, put it in the cabinet for next Sunday's batch. She set her alarm for 1 PM. The double started at three, straight through the overnight — she needed the hours, and someone would call out, and Lakeview always needed a body. She brushed her teeth. She stripped off her scrubs and dropped them in the hamper. Her other pair, the only other pair, was hanging on the shower rod where she'd washed them by hand two nights ago because the laundromat was $3.50 a load and the math didn't always include it.

She got into bed. The sheets were clean from Sunday. The window AC unit kicked on with a rattle and a hum, and the apartment settled into its own nighttime quiet — the tenant downstairs, the occasional car on the street, the distant steady sound of Route 18 that never fully stopped.

---

The thing that kept her awake was Harry Zielinski.

Room 114A, a single. Eighty-four years old, widower. Dementia, progressive, moderate-stage per his chart and the neurologist's last assessment in April. Every evening he asked about his wife Eleanor — the same questions in the same bewildered order, as though her death were news being delivered to him for the first time. He was angular and thin, with pale eyes that watered under the hallway fluorescents and a faded blue cardigan he wore to every interaction Carmen had ever had with him. He smelled like facility laundry and the close, stale warmth of a room he almost never left.

She'd given him his evening medications at 7:40, with Harry's MAR still open on the cart where she'd abandoned it after Delvecchio's turn. The paper cup sat half-built beside the blister cards. Alprazolam, one milligram. Tramadol, one hundred milligrams, two tablets. Lisinopril, simvastatin. She'd already punched one tramadol through the foil before Tammy called her to 110, snapping it crooked on the score line — one half in the cup, the other left on the tray. Back at the cart, sweating and forty minutes behind, she didn't remember she'd started. She punched out two whole tablets, swept the loose half in with them, and carried the cup into Harry's room. He'd been mumbling about Eleanor when she came in — the looping distress, the plucking at his cardigan, the unfocused eyes.

He took the cup. He looked at the pills.

And his eyes changed.

Not gradually — not a fog thinning, not a slow surfacing. All at once, like a switch had been thrown behind his face. The watery confusion went out of his eyes and something else came in, something precise and impatient and entirely wrong for a man whose chart said he couldn't reliably name the current president. He looked at the pills in the paper cup and then at Carmen, and when he spoke his voice was level and clear and had nothing to do with Eleanor.

"That's two-fifty milligrams. Tramadol's two hundred. You put in an extra half-tab."

Carmen looked at the cup. Two whole tramadol tablets and the extra broken half sat among the rest of his evening pills. A fifty-milligram error. An error she caught at the point of administration, before he swallowed, no harm done.

Except she hadn't caught it. He had.

She took the cup back, removed the extra half-tab, recounted. "You're right, Mr. Zielinski. Thank you. Let me fix that."

And then he was gone. Two seconds, three at most. The clarity left his face the way it had arrived — instantly, completely, like a door closing. His hands went back to the cardigan. His eyes unfocused. He looked past Carmen at something in the hallway or in 1987 or nowhere.

"Eleanor?" he said. "Did you tell her — she doesn't know where I am. She needs to know about the appointment. She always forgets the appointments."

Carmen gave him the corrected dose. He swallowed with the automatic compliance of a man who had stopped questioning what was handed to him. She checked his mouth, documented the administration. She didn't document the extra half-tab — it was caught before it mattered, corrected immediately, no incident report required. She didn't document the moment of clarity because there was nothing in the charting system to describe it. Lucid intervals happened. Dementia was not a smooth downward slope; it was a terrain with ridges and valleys, and sometimes a patient surfaced into a moment of startling coherence before the disease pulled them back under. It was in the textbooks. It was normal.

She'd turned off his overhead light. In the half-dark, he was already talking to Eleanor, the same looping questions in the same gentle distress, and Carmen had stood in the doorway for a moment longer than the schedule allowed before she moved on to the next room, the next cup of pills, the next set of initials on the MAR.

But now, in bed, with Reggie breathing in the next room and the cumin smell coming through the floor and the AC rattling in the window, the moment wouldn't stay filed.

*Two-fifty milligrams. Tramadol's two hundred. You put in an extra half-tab.*

It wasn't an approximation. It wasn't a guess. He'd looked at five tablets in a paper cup, identified the medication by sight, known the prescribed dose, and done the arithmetic in the time it took her to hand him the water. The charge nurse's standing advice about dementia patients was *don't read too much into the good moments, it'll break your heart.* Good was Harry making it through a whole med pass without asking for Eleanor, or lifting his chin for the cup before she told him to. Good wasn't a man catching her dosage error before she did.

And the way he'd looked at her in that half-second before the fog rolled back. He hadn't looked confused or frightened or surprised by his own clarity. His eyes had gone from the cup to her face and stayed there until she took it back. His mouth tightened once before his fingers found the cardigan again and he was asking for Eleanor.

She rolled onto her side. Her pillow smelled like her shampoo and the faint astringent residue of hand sanitizer she could never entirely wash out of her hair. She had a double in fourteen hours and Reggie needed new sneakers and the car payment was due on Friday and the student loan payment was on the fifteenth — six hundred eighty dollars to Navient that left her with exactly enough for groceries and gas and nothing else, no margin, no slack, no room for a mystery she couldn't explain and couldn't act on and didn't have time for.

She closed her eyes. She told herself it was sundowning, or a lucid interval, or one of the ordinary small miracles of a deteriorating brain, and that she'd forget about it by morning.

She didn't believe herself, but she slept anyway.
