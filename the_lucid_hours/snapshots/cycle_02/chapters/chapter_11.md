# Chapter 11

The call bell from Room 116A went off at 3:07 AM. Carmen heard it from the medication room where she was recounting the shift's controlled substance log, trying to make the numbers come out the way Agnes had taught her to make them come out — carefully, with specific purpose, each discrepancy accounted for in the parallel ledger she now kept in her head.

She was in the hallway before the second chime. Lakeview at night was its own creature — half-lit corridors, the ventilation cycling down to save on electricity, every sound traveling farther than it should. Her sneakers on the linoleum. The distant hum of the ice machine. Room 110's soft continuous moan, which had become as ambient to her as highway noise on her drive home.

Room 116A. She pushed the door open.

Earl was standing between the two beds in his undershirt and pajama pants, barefoot on the cold linoleum, and he had one of the facility's thin white towels pressed against the side of Burt's head. His other hand was braced on the nightstand for balance. His breathing was heavy, wet, and the effort of getting out of bed and crossing three feet of floor had cost him something she could hear in the rattle of each inhale.

Burt was on the ground. He'd gone down between the bed and the nightstand — the gap was barely eighteen inches — and he was wedged there on his side, one arm pinned under him, the other reaching toward nothing. His legs were tangled in the sheet that had pulled off the bed with him. The bedside rail was down. It shouldn't have been down. She'd put it up at the start of her shift — she was almost sure she'd put it up — but the rails at Lakeview were old and the latches were loose and Burt moved in his sleep sometimes, reaching for things that weren't there anymore.

The blood was what she saw first and what she'd see when she closed her eyes for the next three nights. Scalp lacerations bled like nothing else, especially skin this thin, this old, the capillaries close to the surface and the tissue paper-fragile. Burt's white hair was dark and matted on the right side. Blood had pooled under his head on the linoleum, a small lake of it catching the light from the hallway, and there were smears on the nightstand's sharp laminate edge where he'd hit. The blood was thin and watery-looking, the way old blood was — less viscous, the body's production line running slower than its losses.

"He fell," Earl said.

Carmen was already on the floor next to Burt. "Mr. Kovacs. Burt. Can you hear me?" She kept her voice level, the nurse voice, the one that cost her effort to hold steady. Her hands did the work — penlight from her scrub pocket, left pupil sluggish, right pupil responsive but slow. She took the towel from Earl's hand and pressed it to the laceration, feeling the split in the skin through the terry cloth, two inches long above his right ear, deep enough that she could feel the hard ledge of skull underneath.

"How long ago?" she asked Earl.

"A few minutes. I heard him get up. Then—" He stopped. The cough came — deep, from his chest, the kind that folded him at the waist. He sat down on his own bed heavily and hacked into the crook of his elbow, and for a moment the only sounds in the room were Earl's coughing and Burt's breathing, which was shallow but regular, which was something.

"It's okay. You did right pressing the bell."

Burt's eyes were open but unfocused. His lips were moving. Carmen leaned closer. Hungarian — he was speaking Hungarian, the language that surfaced when the dementia peeled back the English like old wallpaper. *Erzsébet*, she heard, and then something else she couldn't parse, a long run of syllables that sounded like a question or a prayer. His wife's name, his wife's language, the one she'd used the dictionary to teach him before she died. Before her dictionary became something else entirely.

"Stay still, Mr. Kovacs. Don't try to move." She held pressure on the wound with one hand and pulled her phone from her scrub pocket with the other. 911. She gave the information in the clipped shorthand of someone who'd called ambulances from this building before: 88-year-old male, ground-level fall, scalp laceration approximately two inches right temporal region, altered mental status at baseline with acute change, history of vascular dementia, current medications including fentanyl patch 25 mcg transdermal and oxycodone 5 mg PRN. Possible concussion. She wanted c-spine precaution. She wanted it fast.

She hung up. The room was quiet except for Burt's murmuring and Earl's breathing and the tick of the wall clock that ran two minutes slow because nobody had fixed it in the fourteen months Carmen had worked here. Seven minutes until the ambulance, maybe ten. Central New Jersey at three in the morning, the roads would be empty.

She pulled a second towel from the stack on Earl's shelf and layered it over the first, which was soaked through. The blood was on her hands now, in the creases of her knuckles, under the nail she'd broken last week opening a medication blister pack. The overhead light was off — Earl and Burt slept with it off — and she was working by the light from the hallway and the green glow of the pulse oximeter on Burt's bedside table. She clipped the oximeter to his finger. Ninety-four percent. Low but not dangerous. His hand in hers was cool and slack, and his fingers — those long musician's fingers, the ones that had held a trumpet in a photograph she'd seen a hundred times — didn't grip back.

And then she saw the shelf.

It was behind her, mounted to the wall above the radiator on Burt's side of the room. Three items: a framed photograph of Burt with a woman who must have been Erzsébet — both younger, him holding a trumpet, her laughing at something beyond the camera's edge — a ceramic mug with the Berklee College of Music logo that held two pens and a broken watch, and the dictionary. Hungarian-English, hardcover, faded red cloth binding, spine cracked and reglued with tape that had yellowed to the color of old teeth. The book Erzsébet had given him so he could teach her his language while she taught him to think in hers. The book that, since Agnes had hollowed out the center with a box cutter and pharmaceutical patience, held sixty-three oxycodone tablets and forty alprazolam, wrapped in small ziplock bags that Agnes had labeled in her pharmacist's print.

Carmen looked at the dictionary on the shelf and her hands kept pressing the towel to Burt's head and her mind split into two tracks that ran simultaneously, that she would realize later had always been running simultaneously, that had started the night she walked Harry back to his room and didn't call the police. Track one: patient care, spinal precaution, pressure on the wound, monitor pupils and oxygen, wait for EMS. Track two: when the EMTs came they would assess the room. They might not search the shelf — they were here for the patient, not his belongings. But the hospital would inventory his personal items if they came with him. Or they'd leave the belongings and send a facilities report, and Cliff would dispatch someone to clean the blood off the linoleum, and that person might straighten the room, and if anyone picked up that dictionary and felt how wrong the weight was, how a book that should weigh a pound weighed three — if anyone opened it and saw the hollowed-out cavity packed with labeled bags of Schedule II opioids —

She moved without deciding to move. That was the thing she would think about later, in her car, in her apartment, in the shower with the water too hot — there was no moment of decision, no internal argument, no weighing of consequences. She stood up, keeping one hand on the towel against Burt's scalp, and reached back with the other hand and took the dictionary off the shelf. It was heavier than a book that size should be. She could feel the pills shift inside, the soft rattle of plastic against hollowed-out pages, the weight of a hundred and three tablets that represented roughly eighteen hundred dollars in street value and a federal possession-with-intent charge that would end her career, her freedom, and the life she'd built for Reggie.

"Earl."

He looked at her. He looked at the dictionary in her hand. His face didn't change — not because he was hiding his reaction but because his face had already settled, minutes ago, into the expression of a man who understood what was at stake and had decided to let someone else handle it. He'd been a union organizer. He knew when to act and when to let the person with standing take the floor.

"Keep pressure here," she said. She guided his hand to the towel, repositioning it over the laceration. "Press hard. Don't let go until I come back."

Earl's large hand replaced hers. He pressed. His nails were trimmed short and clean — Tammy did that for him, every Tuesday, with the same clippers she used for three other residents — and there was blood between his fingers now, his roommate's blood, and his hand didn't shake.

Carmen stood with the dictionary against her hip and walked out of Room 116A. Left down the hall, past the darkened rooms where people slept or didn't, past the sub-station where her charting waited half-finished on the desk, past the bathroom where she'd once seen Harry walking at 2:40 AM carrying something in a towel, and into the supply closet at the far end of Wing A.

The closet smelled like industrial bleach and the synthetic lavender of a plug-in air freshener that someone had wedged behind the toilet paper rolls. Mops in a bucket. Cases of latex gloves stacked to her shoulder. The shelf above her head, behind a case of adult incontinence pads — large, overnight, the brand Lakeview bought in bulk because it was cheapest and leaked by 4 AM. Carmen put the dictionary on the shelf, spine facing the wall, and closed the closet door.

She stood in the hallway. Her right hand was tacky with Burt's blood, already cooling and stiffening in the creases of her palm. The fluorescent above her head flickered with its perpetual tick-tick-tick, the dying ballast that maintenance had been promising to replace since August. Down the hall, she could hear Earl coughing.

She walked back to 116A.

The EMTs arrived eight minutes later — two men, young, efficient, moving through the room with the quiet competence of people who did this three times a shift and would do it again before dawn. They stabilized Burt's cervical spine, assessed his Glasgow score at 11, dressed the laceration with hemostatic gauze and a compression bandage, started an IV line in a vein so thin and fragile the younger EMT needed two attempts. Burt kept speaking Hungarian. One of them asked Carmen what he was saying. She said she didn't know. She knew one word — the name he kept repeating, soft and broken, the same syllables over and over — but that wasn't theirs.

They loaded him onto the stretcher and took him out through the side entrance. Carmen followed to the ambulance bay and watched the doors close. The October air was cold and damp, the smell of wet asphalt and dead leaves and the exhaust from the ambulance as it pulled away, lights on, no siren. No siren meant they didn't think he was dying. Just an 88-year-old man with a bleeding head and a disease that was taking him apart one synapse at a time, which didn't qualify as an emergency because it happened every day in every facility like this one, across every state, a slow-motion catastrophe that the system had learned to process as routine.

She went back inside. She washed her hands in the medication room sink — the water running pink, then diluted pink, then clear, the institutional soap leaving her skin tight and dry. She pulled up Burt's chart on the computer and began the incident report. Date: October 1, 2026. Time: 0307. Location: Room 116A, between bed and nightstand. Description of event: *Resident found on floor with scalp laceration, approximately 2-inch, right temporal region. Bedside rail found in down position. Roommate (E. Washington) reports hearing fall, pressed call bell. Nurse responded, applied pressure to wound, assessed LOC and vitals, called 911. EMS arrived 0315, patient transported to Saint Peter's University Hospital for evaluation. Family notification per protocol.*

Her handwriting was steady. She documented everything she was supposed to document.

She didn't mention the dictionary.

---

Burt came back two days later, Saturday afternoon, in a medical transport van, belted into a wheelchair he didn't try to get out of. Carmen was starting her shift when the van pulled up to the side entrance. She signed the transfer paperwork from Saint Peter's — concussion with post-concussive syndrome, six sutures to the scalp laceration, CT scan showing no acute subdural hematoma but noting diffuse cortical atrophy consistent with moderate-to-severe vascular dementia, recommend follow-up with neurology in two weeks — and walked alongside the wheelchair as the transport aide rolled him to 116A.

He took up less room. The hospital discharge summary listed 142 pounds, down from the 151 in his Lakeview chart from three weeks ago, but the number explained only part of it. Something in him had folded inward. The volume of him. The presence. Before the fall, Burt had been half-present — his dementia was real, had always been real, the only one in the ring who wasn't performing — but there had been something still alive in the confusion. He told stories about the Blue Note, about playing trumpet in clubs on Fifty-Second Street in a decade he might or might not have actually worked. The stories looped and stalled and fused with other stories, but they had heat. They came from a place in him that still had opinions about the world. He'd hum — standards, mostly. She'd recognized "Round Midnight" once, and fragments of "In a Sentimental Mood," melodies that surfaced and sank like things seen through water.

He wasn't humming. He sat in the wheelchair with his hands in his lap, those long fingers knotted and liver-spotted, trembling with a fine tremor that was new or that she hadn't noticed before. The sutures on his scalp were covered by a gauze pad taped to his thin white hair. His eyes were open and aimed at the hallway but they weren't tracking anything. They were receiving light the way a window receives light — without processing, without response.

She got him into bed. He didn't resist. Before the fall he had resisted — small, wordless protests, pulling his arm back when she reached for the blood pressure cuff, turning his face from the thermometer, the stubborn physical vocabulary of a person who still had preferences about what was done to his body. Now she lifted his arm and it stayed where she put it. She wrapped the cuff. 128 over 76. Pulse 68. She checked the suture site — dry, no infection, the wound healing the slow grudging way old skin healed, as if the body resented the effort. She changed his fentanyl patch, peeling the old one from his shoulder and pressing the new one to his upper arm, and he didn't flinch, didn't try to bat her hand away the way he used to.

Earl watched from his bed. He had a Sports Illustrated open on his lap — the same issue from three weeks ago, the one that hid the paperback he was actually reading — but he wasn't reading either one. He was watching Carmen check Burt, and his face had the expression she'd seen in photographs of men at union halls: someone counting costs and finding the total higher than the estimate.

"He's settled," she told Earl. "I'll check on him through the shift."

Earl nodded. He looked at Burt, then looked away. Then looked back. It was the looking-back that got her — the way he couldn't leave it alone, the way his eyes kept returning to his roommate the way your tongue keeps finding a broken tooth.

"You moved the book," he said.

"Yeah."

He nodded once. That was all.

Carmen finished the intake assessment. Under cognitive status she wrote: *Decline noted from baseline. Reduced verbal output. Decreased resistance to care activities. Diminished environmental awareness and engagement. Pattern consistent with post-concussive acceleration of existing dementia. Will monitor and update physician at next rounds.*

The words were clinical and precise and they described a man who was disappearing. She could document the disappearance in the language of assessment and monitoring. She could chart it. She could not stop it.

She left the room. The shelf above the radiator on Burt's side was missing one item. The photograph was there — Burt and Erzsébet, the trumpet, the laugh. The Berklee mug was there with its two pens and broken watch. The space where the dictionary had sat was a rectangle of slightly lighter paint where the wall hadn't faded.

---

The ring met at one in the morning the following Thursday, five days after Burt came back. Carmen had started attending meetings after the survey — not every one, but enough that her arrival no longer produced the careful silence it had the first two times. She came because she needed to know what they were planning, and she stayed because this room at this hour was the only place where everything she knew was allowed to be real.

The TV room was the same as always: vinyl chairs in a loose half-circle, the wall-mounted TV dark, the card table with one short leg propped on a folded napkin. Harry preferred the brass reading lamp that Lola had taken from the nurses' break room months ago — it threw warm, limited light that made the room smaller, almost domestic. Like a kitchen. Like somewhere people actually lived. Outside the windows, the parking lot was empty under its orange lights and the October wind pushed leaves across the asphalt in fitful scrapes.

Harry was in his chair, the one nearest the lamp that no one else used. His faded blue cardigan was buttoned one off from the top, and either he hadn't noticed or it didn't matter enough to fix. He looked old tonight in a way that had nothing to do with the performed frailty he assembled for the day staff and the surveyors — the slack posture, the unfocused eyes, the careful theater of diminishment. The skin under his eyes was bruised with sleeplessness, and his hands rested on the chair's armrests without moving, which meant he was working something out that he hadn't solved.

Agnes sat across from him, back straight, wire-rimmed glasses reflecting the lamp. She had nothing in front of her — no notebook, no pillbox organizer. The operation had pulled inward since the fall, contracting the way a hand closes around an injury, and the meetings had shrunk with it. Less inventory to review. Less logistics to manage. More silence to fill.

Lola was by the door, her usual post, though her lookout role had relaxed since Carmen had started sending Devon to take his breaks at the other wing's station. She held a coffee cup in both hands, the tremor in her left making the surface of the coffee shiver against the ceramic. She was watching Harry's face the way she watched everything — through the performance of not watching, her eyes settling elsewhere while her attention stayed fixed.

Earl had come from 116A, where Burt was asleep. He took the chair he always took, the one farthest from the center, closest to the door. He'd brought nothing. He never brought anything to meetings anymore — before, he'd carried the cash envelopes, or the count from his side of the logistics, but that had stopped after the fall. The operation had contracted and Earl had contracted with it, pulling his participation inward the way he pulled everything inward.

"We need to talk about Burt," Harry said.

Nobody answered right away. Lola drank her coffee. Agnes adjusted her glasses with one finger at the bridge. Earl's silence was the same silence he always produced, but Carmen noticed it was aimed differently tonight — toward the empty space to Harry's left where Burt's wheelchair had been parked at every meeting since the ring began, where Earl used to push him in and set the brake and check that the blanket was tucked around his legs.

"He's still prescribed," Harry said. "Oxycodone five, ninety count, refilled last week. Agnes already flagged it for the rotation."

"I flagged it because that is my job," Agnes said. "That does not mean I flagged it for use."

Harry looked at her. "His prescriptions are the highest-value inventory we move. Without the oxy, we're short about eighteen hundred a month."

"He does not know what month it is."

"He didn't know what month it was six months ago either."

"Six months ago he could tell you he wanted to be part of this. He said yes. He understood what yes meant."

Carmen sat in the chair between Lola and Earl, the seat that had become hers by proximity and repetition. She was still in her scrubs from the shift she'd clocked out of two hours ago. Staying late had become its own small deception — the dedicated nurse, the one who cared enough to linger — and she was tired of the irony without being willing to give it up. She listened.

Harry leaned forward. The chair creaked. "We all agreed when we started this. Every one of us. Burt included. He said—"

"He said yes to a man he trusted, in a room he recognized, on a night when he knew his own name." Agnes's voice hadn't risen, but it had compressed — more facts per sentence, less space between them, the pharmacist under pressure accelerating instead of getting louder. Her hands were moving on the armrests of her chair, her fingers sorting nothing, organizing invisible inventory the way they'd organized pills for forty-two years. "He cannot say yes now. He cannot say no. He cannot produce anything that constitutes informed consent, and you know that, Harry."

"He's not being harmed by it," Harry said. "His pain management is the fentanyl patch. The oral oxy sits in his drawer untouched. We take pills he doesn't use and wouldn't know were gone."

"That is not the standard and you know it."

"What standard, Agnes? We're running a pill operation out of a nursing home. There's no ethics board."

"The standard," Agnes said, and her hands stopped moving, "is that you do not distribute a patient's medications without that patient's knowledge or agreement. I do not care that there is no board. I was the board for forty-two years. A patient with diminished capacity does not get spoken for. Not by his family. Not by his doctor. Not by us."

Lola set her coffee on the card table. The ceramic made a small sound against the wood grain. "He's the same Burt," she said. "He's just — he's tired, that's all, mijo. He was always a little tired."

"He is not the same," Agnes said. "You want him to be. He is not."

Lola's hand went to the crucifix at her throat, her thumb finding the small gold cross and rubbing it the way she did when something landed that she couldn't redirect with warmth or a story. She didn't answer. Her left hand trembled around the coffee cup.

Harry turned to Earl. "You live with him. You see him every day. What do you think?"

Earl looked at Harry. The room waited. Earl's silences were never empty — they had the specific weight of a man who'd spent thirty years in negotiation rooms and on picket lines, learning that the moment you spoke mattered as much as what you said. Carmen could hear the clock in the hallway. The lamp buzzing. Her own breathing.

Earl cleared his throat. The wet sound underneath, the rattle she'd been hearing more of and that he kept turning away to hide.

"He fell," Earl said. "He was on the floor bleeding, and he was calling her name, and he didn't know where he was."

"That's not—" Harry started.

"He was calling her name and he didn't know where he was." Earl said it again in the same flat voice. The repetition itself was the emphasis — Earl, who said things once and expected them to land, saying it twice because the room needed to hear it twice.

They sat with that. The reading lamp hummed. Outside, the wind picked up and a branch or a piece of loose gutter scraped across the window glass with a sound like fingernails on a chalkboard.

Carmen looked at her hands. The blood was gone, but she could still feel the drag of it drying in the creases of her knuckles. The dictionary was still in Wing A supply, behind the overnight briefs, with sixty-three oxycodone and forty alprazolam sitting inside it like a second emergency she had left on a shelf.

"He'd want us to keep going."

The word hit after she said it. *Us.* Harry's eyes lifted. Agnes's did too.

"That is not an argument," Agnes said. "That is a guess about what a man who cannot speak for himself would want. I spent three years being spoken for. My daughter decided what I needed. The facility agreed. Nobody asked me. I will not do that to someone else."

"So what do we do?" Carmen said. "His prescriptions. The rotation."

"We pull his inventory from the active list," Agnes said. "His medications are dispensed to him and administered as prescribed. Every tablet. We do not divert them."

Carmen said, "What about the tablets already in the dictionary?"

Harry turned to her. "Earl told me you pulled it before EMS got there. I told Agnes. Lola's hearing it now. Where is it?"

"Wing A supply closet," Carmen said. "Top shelf, behind the overnight briefs."

Lola shut her eyes for a second. "Ay Dios."

Agnes stood. "Good. Then it leaves that closet tonight and those pills leave circulation before dawn."

"That's eighteen hundred dollars," Harry said.

"Then lose eighteen hundred dollars. I will not put loose tablets back in Burt's drawer, and I will not sell them."

She held her hand out to Carmen. "Show me."

Carmen got up. Harry got up more slowly. Earl stayed where he was, looking at the floor a moment longer, then pushed himself to his feet.

"I'm going back to him," he said.

Lola stood too. "I'm coming."

They left first, Earl toward 116A, Lola a step behind him with her coffee cup still in her hand.

The supply closet light buzzed on. Carmen reached behind the case of briefs and brought the dictionary down. Agnes took it from her, opened the cover, and looked at the bagged pills inside without touching them.

"Sixty-three oxycodone," she said. "Forty alprazolam."

Harry stood in the doorway. He did not argue again. He took the folded active list from his cardigan pocket, found Burt's room number, and drew a line through it with the pen he used for everything.

Agnes shut the dictionary and tucked it under her arm. "I will keep this in my room. I will destroy the tablets before breakfast med pass."

Harry folded the list and put it back in his pocket. Agnes walked the dictionary down the hall toward 108A. The line through 116A was still wet.
