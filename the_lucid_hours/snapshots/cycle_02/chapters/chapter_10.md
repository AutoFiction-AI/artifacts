# Chapter 10

Harry had said one o'clock. Carmen had asked for the meeting three days after the survey — three days after she sat in her car unable to drive, feeling the weight of what she'd told the state surveyor settle into a place she couldn't reach to remove it. She'd found Harry alone in the TV room during evening med pass, leaned against the doorframe while Lola pretended to watch a game show behind him, and said, "Show me all of it." Harry had said one o'clock. Nothing else.

So at one o'clock she walked the length of Wing A past Devon sleeping at the nurse's station with his phone face-down on his chest, past Room 110 where Mrs. Rowan was making the low steady sound that Carmen's brain had reclassified as building noise months ago, and pushed open the TV room door.

Harry sat in his wheelchair at the card table — the one the activities aide used for bingo on Tuesdays — wearing the faded blue cardigan he wore to every after-midnight meeting Carmen had attended. Agnes sat across from him. Lola was at her post by the hallway door, one hand resting on the gold crucifix at her throat, her other hand still in the tremor that worsened at night. Earl sat in the vinyl recliner by the far window with his eyes closed, though Carmen could tell from the set of his shoulders that he was listening. Between Harry and Agnes, spread flat under the fluorescent tube that buzzed from a loose ballast, lay Agnes's notebooks.

Four composition books sat under the white buzz, black-and-white marbling gone soft at the corners, Agnes's handwriting packed so tight and even it looked typeset. Agnes didn't look up when Carmen pulled a vinyl chair to the table and sat. She kept sorting pill bottles into short rows, labels turned the same way, caps aligned. Later Carmen would see the logic: buyers grouped by need, the way a pharmacist stages an order.

"Sit," Harry said, which Carmen was already doing.

He started with the money.

"Eighteen months," Harry said. "Since early last year. Gross averages four thousand a month. Forty-eight total, give or take."

Carmen had expected the number to feel larger. Spread across eighteen months and this many hands, it felt almost modest. Modest enough to ruin everyone anyway.

"Marcus gets thirty percent. Twelve hundred. Distribution, gas, exposure."

"Leaves twenty-eight," Carmen said.

Harry turned one of Agnes's notebooks toward her. "Jack. Five hundred. Denise Washington. Four hundred. Lola's church clinic. Three-fifty. Burt. Five hundred. Agnes. Four hundred."

Carmen looked at the page, then at him. "That's twenty-one fifty."

Nobody corrected her. Agnes kept capping bottles. Earl coughed once into his fist. Lola watched the hallway.

"Six-fifty a month stays in reserve," Harry said.

"Reserve for what."

"Money orders. Gas. Replacement stock. A bridge month when somebody's script gets cut off and Marcus has to front it before the next handoff."

"You said distributed."

"It goes out," Harry said. "Same as the rest."

He tapped the first line. Jack Zielinski, his grandson, got five hundred a month toward a fifteen-thousand-dollar hole from a landscaping business in South Brunswick. Jack thought the money came from a certificate of deposit Harry had cashed before Lakeview. Harry's voice stayed level when he said the name, but his finger pressed harder against the paper.

Denise Washington got four hundred a month through a VA supplemental benefit Earl had invented. Coats. School fees. Field trips. Earl never opened his eyes. The cough had settled deeper in him since Carmen first noticed it, wetter and heavier, as if each meeting pulled it up from farther down.

Lola sent three hundred and fifty a month to Iglesia de la Caridad in Little Havana. Money order. No return address. The church ran a free clinic three days a week. When Harry said the church's name, Lola made a quiet sound through her nose and went back to the hallway.

Burt's five hundred covered whatever his body needed that Medicare did not feel like covering — compression stockings that fit, hearing-aid batteries, brand-name carbidopa-levodopa because the generic the facility pharmacy kept substituting made his tremor worse and left him sick to his stomach.

"Agnes gets four hundred," Harry said.

Agnes did not look up. Her share went into a white envelope under her mattress. Helen came every other Sunday with the children and talked to her in the slow, loud voice of someone addressing a diagnosis. Agnes let the money stack.

"And the six-fifty?" Carmen asked.

Harry turned the page. RESERVE / 650 was written at the top in Agnes's narrow block letters. Under it: MONEY ORDERS. GAS. REPLACE EXPIRED STOCK. BUYER BRIDGE. LOSS.

"You run a contingency fund," Carmen said.

"You wanted all of it."

She looked at the ledger again. Two thousand one hundred and fifty to the people involved. Six hundred and fifty to keep the thing from seizing up. It was closer to a business than she'd wanted. More embarrassing than a business, because the books were clean.

"The buyer list," Harry said.

Agnes pushed the newest composition book toward Carmen. Carmen opened it and saw diagnosis headers instead of names. LUPUS-SLE. TYPE 2 DM. CHRONIC PAIN — MUSCULOSKELETAL. HYPERTENSION. HYPERLIPIDEMIA. Under each: dosage, quantity, frequency, margin notes in red.

"No names," Carmen said.

"Need is relevant," Agnes said. "Names are not."

Three entries were marked with red asterisks. Agnes tapped one with the nail of her index finger.

"Read."

Carmen bent over the page. Chronic pain buyer. Oxycodone request. Current meds in the margin: clonazepam, trazodone, nightly alcohol use.

"Respiratory suppression risk," Carmen said. "If Marcus hands her oxy on top of clonazepam and she's drinking, he can put her down."

"Yes." Agnes took the notebook back and tapped the second red mark. "And that is why Marcus does not decide what Marcus sells."

"You trust him with cash and parking lots," Carmen said. "You don't trust him with a med list."

"Correct."

Agnes turned another page. "He gets what buyers tell him. Buyers lie, forget, guess, or repeat whatever a doctor said five years ago. I screen from that. It is an inadequate method."

Harry was watching Carmen over folded hands.

"You see order changes before we do," Agnes said. "Dose reductions. Discontinuations. Pharmacy substitutions. If I have current information, I can keep this cleaner than I am keeping it now."

Carmen looked from Agnes to Harry. "You're asking me to help."

"I'm asking you to read what you already have access to," Harry said.

"I'm not pulling pills off carts."

"Then don't," Harry said.

Agnes nudged the amber bottles until their labels made a straight line. "I need current lists. You see them first. Tell me when they change."

Carmen looked back at the notebook, at the red asterisk Agnes had stabbed with her nail, at the margin note that had turned three scraps of buyer gossip into a clean warning about who might stop breathing.

"I tell you about changes," Carmen said. "That's it."

"That is a start," Agnes said.

She opened the older notebook to a two-column spread. FACILITY MONTHLY MEDICATION COST PER RESIDENT on the left. UNINSURED RETAIL on the right.

"This is what the reserve covers," Agnes said.

The left column was Lakeview's side of the equation: institutional rates high enough to drive one resident's monthly medications past two thousand dollars before anybody blinked. The right column showed the same pills at a retail counter with no coverage and a paper prescription. Hydroxychloroquine, two hundred milligrams, ninety count: three hundred eighty-nine dollars. Rosuvastatin, twenty milligrams, thirty count: two hundred twelve. Metformin, five hundred milligrams, sixty count: eighty-seven. Oxycodone, five milligrams, thirty count: two hundred forty-one.

"Surplus on the left," Agnes said. "Deficit on the right."

Carmen's mind followed the route instead: facility pharmacy, physician orders, med carts, return bins, the way pills piled up inside institutions because asking whether someone still needed them cost more staff time than letting them accumulate. Then the other side of it: people outside these walls paying retail, rationing, going without.

Harry closed the notebook. "I want you to meet someone. A buyer."

"When?"

"Thursday. Your shift starts at three. Be in the lot at two-thirty. Silver Civic, far end."

Carmen handed the composition book back to Agnes. Agnes took it without fumbling, quick and certain.

---

Rosa drove an early 2000s Civic — silver paint going milky at the fenders, a St. Christopher medal on the rearview mirror, a striped beach towel folded over a tear in the passenger seat. She was parked at the far end of Lakeview's lot, away from the entrance and the employee spaces, in the spot where visitors parked when they didn't want to be seen arriving or didn't want anyone to remember them leaving. Carmen knew the spot. Families used it when they came to admit a parent they would not visit again.

Thursday. Two twenty-five. Carmen had arrived early, crossing the lot in her scrubs with her badge clipped and her bag on her shoulder. The afternoon was warm for late September, holding onto summer even with the courtyard trees starting to turn.

Rosa got out of the car when she saw Carmen coming. She was fifty-two and moved like someone whose body charged her for every step. She had dark hair cut close, practical, and she was wearing a fleece vest over a long-sleeved shirt despite the warmth. Lupus made her cold. Carmen knew that before Rosa spoke.

"You're the nurse," Rosa said.

"Yeah."

"The old man — Harry, whatever his name is — he said you wanted to talk."

"I wanted to understand the costs. What you're paying."

Rosa leaned against the Civic and crossed her arms. She studied Carmen with the expression of someone used to explaining herself to people who held some kind of authority over her life and rarely did anything useful with it.

"OK," Rosa said. She turned and opened the passenger door and pulled out a CVS bag. "I went this week. I do that sometimes. Walk in, hand them the prescription, ask what it costs." She pulled a folded receipt from the bag. She had creased it neatly. "Hydroxychloroquine, two hundred milligrams, ninety count. Three eighty-nine at CVS. Three sixty-two at the Walgreens on Route 1. Four twelve at Rite Aid, before that one closed."

"And from Marcus?"

"Forty," Rosa said. "For the same ninety-day fill."

"He puts instructions in the bag too," she said before Carmen could answer. "Spacing, expiration dates, the little warnings the pharmacist used to rattle off when I still had a rheumatologist."

They stood in the parking lot. A medical supply van was backed up to the loading dock with its rear doors open. Two cars passed on Route 27. Wind pushed a paper cup across the asphalt and left it against a curb.

"I was at a dental office in Woodbridge," Rosa said. "Receptionist. Twelve years. The dentist retired, and the new owner brought his own staff." She moved one shoulder. "I looked at COBRA. No COBRA. Small group plan. I looked at the marketplace. Five-forty a month for the premium, three-thousand-dollar deductible before anything helped. I was making sixteen an hour at a temp agency. You can do that math faster than I can."

Carmen knew the corridor Rosa meant. Too much income for Medicaid. Too little for a plan that covered the medication keeping your hands usable. One lost job away from the same kitchen-table arithmetic herself.

"What happens when you don't take it?" Carmen asked.

Rosa looked at her. "You're a nurse. You know."

"I'm asking you."

"Flares." Rosa held up her right hand. The knuckles were swollen, the skin tight and shiny over them, the joints thickened by chronic synovial inflammation. "Mostly joints. My knees, my hands. Last time I went without — this was before Marcus — I ran out for three weeks. Couldn't open a jar. Couldn't button my pants." She looked at her hand. "I called in sick for a week at a job I'd just started. Warehouse data entry. They let me go after day four. Temp agency stopped calling."

She said it flat, like a sequence she'd repeated enough times to stop expecting sympathy for.

"Can I see that one?" Carmen said, nodding at Rosa's left hand.

Rosa extended it. Carmen took it. The metacarpophalangeal joints were warm and boggy with active synovitis. She turned the hand gently and felt the swelling over the ring finger knuckle.

For a moment it was only the hand in hers: heat in the joint, fluid in the tissue, the ring-finger knuckle swollen and tender. She knew what to call that.

"This one's getting worse," she said.

"I know."

"You should get it imaged."

"With what?" Rosa said.

Carmen let go. Rosa put her hand back in her vest pocket.

"I'm not asking you for anything," Rosa said. "I get it. You've got your own situation. Your license, whatever. The old man said you were worried. Fine. Be worried. I'm telling you what I'm doing. I'm buying my medication from the only place I can afford it, and I'm taking it the way my doctor told me to take it back when I could still afford a doctor. That's what's happening."

She got back in the Civic. The engine turned over on the second try — a hesitation, then the catch, the sound of a car that started every morning and might stop one day without warning. Carmen watched her pull out of the lot. Brake lights at the exit, a left signal, and the silver car merged into the afternoon traffic on Route 27 and was gone.

Carmen went in through the front doors with four minutes left before shift. The silk flowers Cliff had put out for the survey were still on the reception desk. Her badge hit her scrub pocket when she clipped it on.

In the medication room, the day nurse was waiting with the controlled-substance log open beside a fresh pharmacy tote. Burt Kovacs's refill sheet was clipped to the top: generic carbidopa-levodopa substituted again. In the return bin, a discontinued trazodone card held twenty-six tablets, sealed, expiration next spring.

Last week those would have been two separate chores. Burt's nausea. A return bin. Something to initial, something to hand off, something to forget.

She counted with the day nurse, wrote the official number where the form required it, left the second count unspoken, and went out to start med pass.
