# Chapter 14

In 110, the resident had gotten stool clear through the pad again, the third time that shift, and Tammy knew before she pulled back the sheet that the woman would be raw. The brief had twisted up in the crease of her thigh and let everything run where it wanted. Tammy rolled her onto her side, got one hand under the hip, and cleaned the mess out of the folds of skin while the woman stared at the wall and made the same low radiator sound she always made when anything hurt.

The woman had a red strip starting over the tailbone, not open yet but close enough that night shift would hand it a wound by morning if they left her flat. Tammy dried her carefully, spread the barrier cream thick with two gloved fingers, worked a dry sheet under her hips, snapped a fresh brief into place, and tucked a pillow at the small of her back so the pressure sat somewhere else for a while. She checked both heels before she pulled the blanket over her. The left one was hot. She floated it off the mattress with a folded towel.

By the time she tied the dirty linen into a bundle, the room smelled like shit, baby powder, and the sour plastic heat the old radiators threw when they first kicked on. Tammy carried the bag into the hall and the smell came with her. It always did. People who talked about this work like it was angels and service and all that never mentioned that part. A smell could stay in your scrubs through a whole ride home and still be there when you peeled them off.

She dumped the hamper in soiled utility, washed up to the elbows, and came back with a clean towel because the woman in 110 liked to rub at her chin after Tammy finished. The towel calmed her down sometimes. It was a small thing and it worked often enough that Tammy remembered it.

When she passed Agnes Chen's room, Carmen was still in there with the door pulled nearly shut. Med pass had ended forty minutes earlier. The cart sat locked at the station. Carmen stood by Agnes's chair talking low, not charting, not checking vitals, just talking in a voice Tammy could hear the shape of without catching words.

Agnes sat by the window in that cardigan she wore like it had grown onto her. Her chin stayed tucked. Her mouth stayed slack in the way that made agency people write words like withdrawn and nonverbal and poor historian. Her eyes did something else. They stayed on Carmen's mouth, then slid to Tammy when she crossed the doorway, then cut back to Carmen so fast it looked practiced.

Tammy kept walking. She had learned a long time ago that if you let people see you noticing them, they either shut down or started performing. Better to finish the job in front of you and file the rest where it belonged.

Mr. Delaney was waiting with the last of his pudding cup on the overbed table and both hands painted vanilla from wrist to thumb. He had Parkinson's and liked to insist he could manage the spoon himself even after the shake got ahead of him. Tammy wiped his fingers one at a time and straightened him more upright because if you left him half-slid in bed he would cough the whole cup back up and then night shift would say he aspirated out of nowhere. "You rushing me again?" he said. "I'm keeping your lungs out of trouble," Tammy said. "That's different." He sniffed, which was his way of conceding the point. When she set the water within reach, he caught the cup clean on the second try. That still counted. People at Cliff's level never understood that half this job was counting what still counted.

At ten-fifty-five Priya came on and Carmen gave count at the station. Tammy was tossing Delaney's wrapper when she heard the numbers. The oxycodone count was ninety. The alprazolam count was sixty. The signatures landed where they always landed. Priya looked tired, Carmen looked neat, and the clipboard looked as innocent as paper always did right before it got somebody fired.

Tammy waited until Priya moved off with the narcotic keys clipped to her waistband. Then she said, "What's going on with Chen?" Carmen didn't look up from the lock. "She's declining fluids again. I was documenting intake." "That took you forty minutes?" "I had charting behind it." Carmen clicked the lock once more after it was already shut, then adjusted her badge against her scrub top. "You know how it gets." "Yeah," Tammy said. "I do." Carmen gave her a quick work smile that had no heat in it. "I've got one note left."

She headed back down Wing A without her coat. Tammy watched the blue of her scrub top turn the corner and disappear around the bend that led toward the TV room and the back cluster of rooms. The move might have meant nothing. People left notes late. People caught up after rough shifts. People also lied in healthcare every day with charting open in front of them and a badge clipped over their heart.

Tammy did one more round before she clocked out because the aides on nights were always walking into somebody else's missed problem. In 110 she checked the pillow under the woman's back and made sure the heel was still clear. In 118A she pulled a wet washcloth out from under Mr. Hennessy's shoulder before it chilled the skin there. In 112A she found Lola Fernandez trying to button the top snap of a cardigan with fingers that would not hold still.

"Here," Tammy said, and took the cardigan gently so Lola would not have to pretend the tremor was just bad light. "I'm making a whole production out of it," Lola said. "You'd think it was a wedding dress." "It's a purple sweater from Kohl's." "Exactly. Too much ceremony." Tammy got the snap through and sat Lola back in the chair by the window. The room smelled like baby lotion and the mint hard candies Lola kept in the top drawer and forgot she had. On the dresser sat a folded reminder card for Roberto's Friday call, his number written in thick marker so Lola could not say she had misplaced it.

"Tomorrow's Friday," Lola said, glancing at the card. "He calls after Wheel. If I'm asleep, don't let me miss him." "I won't," Tammy said. Lola touched the crucifix at her throat as if checking that it had not wandered off. "Good. He gets touchy if I let it ring more than twice. He thinks Chicago people have things to do."

Two rooms later Tammy heard Lola ask Carmen what day it was in that soft drifting voice she used when families visited. Tammy stood in the doorway of 114A with a stack of washcloths in her arms and let the sound travel down the hall to her. The Friday call card was on the dresser. The Thursday amnesia was in Lola's throat. It was one more thing in the pile.

Harry Zielinski was in bed with his blanket folded straight across his lap and the television muttering a game show no one was watching. He looked past Tammy toward the hall.

"Eleanor coming?" he asked.

"Not tonight," Tammy said.

His face did the usual crumple then, the lost one, the old-man confusion that families recognized and staff charted and nobody could bill enough. But last Tuesday she had found him with both feet on the floor and his hand steady on the doorknob, clear as anybody, saying, No, I'm fine. Thank you, Tammy. He had used her name and shut his own door like a man who knew exactly what privacy was for. By breakfast he was back to asking Priya where Eleanor had parked.

Real decline had a shape to it. Tammy knew the shape. Mrs. Okafor had lost words by inches over months, as if somebody were sliding them off a shelf one at a time. Mr. Delaney had good hours and bad hours, but the good ones did not arrive only when certain doors were shut and certain people were nearby. Agnes had come in three years ago mad enough to fill legal pads with complaints. Then one week she went still all at once and the chart called it progression. Tammy had seen progression. Progression did not track conversation with its eyes.

When she clocked out at eleven-oh-six, Carmen's spot was still full by the side entrance.

Home was twelve minutes away if Route 27 cooperated. The studio in Dunellen would still be over Grace and the Christian station she kept on low enough to hear through her floor and high enough to call witnessing. The carpet pad would still smell like the cats of whoever had died or moved or gotten evicted there before her. Friday was technically her day off. None of that made her turn the key.

She backed into the far row facing Wing A, the one she'd used for fifteen years when she wanted ten minutes alone with vending-machine coffee or, back when she still smoked, a cigarette she told herself did not count because she had not bought the pack. From there, if the lights were on, you could see the last bank of windows around the bend, including the TV room. Inside the building the sub-station could not see that room at all. Devon barely saw what was in front of him.

It had stopped feeling random. Tammy was careful with big words because big words turned into incident reports and meetings with Cliff and documentation about team communication. Random was one oxy count off. Random was a travel nurse parking somebody in the TV room because he would not quit ringing. Random was not Carmen slipping into the same set of rooms after count and coming out with that fixed, tidy face.

The state survey had come through in September with clipboards and sensible shoes and fake plants near the lobby desk. Before that week, Carmen had been what she looked like: tired, fast, competent, sometimes prickly, mostly done with everybody by the end of a double. After the survey she started lingering in Harry's room and Agnes's room and Lola's room, with the doors mostly shut and explanations that sounded usable until you laid them side by side.

Tammy sat with the engine running and watched the staff entrance. She remembered Agnes on admission, tiny and furious, correcting the paperwork from the wheelchair because someone had listed the wrong blood-pressure medication. She remembered Agnes refusing applesauce because the thickener ratio was off. She remembered telling a social worker, plain as anything, that her daughter had sold her house out from under her. None of that matched the silent woman by the window now except the eyes.

She remembered Harry last Tuesday. She had been filling water pitchers after dinner because kitchen had shorted them again. Harry's door was open. She knocked with her hip, expecting Eleanor, maybe socks, maybe the bathroom. Instead he looked up level and said, No, I'm fine. Thank you, Tammy. Not slurred. Not circling. Then his hand reached across and pulled the door shut with a clean steady motion that said he had decided something and did not need help deciding it.

She remembered Lola this evening with Roberto's Friday call card on the dresser and the right day in her mouth, then Lola two minutes later asking Carmen what day it was. That one bothered Tammy in a separate way because she liked Lola. Everybody liked Lola if they had blood in them. Tammy had told her things in the shower room she would not have bothered telling people from her own family. The idea of Lola performing anything with Carmen in the room made Tammy feel mean, like she'd missed a joke everybody else had gotten.

None of it proved what it felt like it proved. That was the problem. In fifteen years at Lakeview she had seen a man turn blue on a piece of chicken while she tried the Heimlich on the dining-room floor and somebody from admin yelled for a clipboard. She had seen a night nurse sleeping in the med room with vodka on his breath. She had seen hearing-aid batteries go unordered for a month while Cliff hung fall-festival posters in the lobby and talked about community morale. Strange things happened in that building all the time. Most of them were just neglect wearing a clean shirt.

But Carmen behind 108A, 112A, and 114A after count had a pattern to it, and patterns were what Tammy trusted when feelings got expensive.

Cliff would get nothing from her until she had something he could not wave off as tone. Marcus Webb had gone to the ombudsman two years ago about understaffing and got fired for it. The understaffing stayed. Forty-two residents still got handed to one overnight aide with bad knees and a glowing phone and no prayer of making every round on time. Reporting at Lakeview was how you ended up in the office explaining your attitude while the original problem kept right on happening.

At eleven-twelve Priya crossed the station with a chart pressed to her chest. At eleven-twenty Devon was already folded over the sub-station desk, his shoulders rounded and his phone lighting his jaw blue. At eleven-thirty Carmen's car had not moved. Tammy shut off the engine and let the heater tick down into silence so she would hear if a side door opened.

The cold found the car fast. It came through the glass first, then through the steering wheel, then into her bad knuckles where she'd jammed two of them on a Hoyer lift years ago. She pulled her scrub jacket tighter and kept her eyes on the building. The parking lot lights buzzed. Somewhere down Route 27 a siren rose and flattened out again.

After fifteen years in one place Tammy knew facts that were useless anywhere else and useful as hell there. She knew which hall had the fluorescent tube that stuttered before it died and made Mrs. Moretti think lightning was happening indoors. She knew which rooms smelled like GI bleed before the labs came back. She knew which fire door swelled shut in August and which window on Wing A lined up with the far row if you parked two lanes back. She knew the TV room sat where nobody at the sub-station could watch it without leaving the desk.

She also knew what people did when they thought no one had the patience to notice. They took the long way down empty halls. They lowered their voice only after checking who was nearby. They used real explanations too often, wore them smooth, and called that honesty. Tammy had spent enough years cleaning up after everyone else's little deceptions to recognize the housekeeping around a larger one.

At twelve-oh-two a figure moved past the side-entry glass and vanished before she could place the height. At twelve-ten Priya walked into 116A with a med cup and came back out alone. At twelve-sixteen Devon stood, stretched, scratched his ribs under his scrub top, and disappeared toward the men's room. When he came back he sat down with his phone again. The building settled around its own noises. There was no alarm, no shout, and no crash of a fall.

Her mind did what it always did when nothing else could be done. It inventoried Agnes's eyes, Harry's clear sentence, Lola's Friday card, Carmen's car, Carmen's badge adjustment, Carmen's one note left, and Priya's signature under a count that balanced too politely. Each thing alone would have been the kind of half-story Tammy came home with every week. Together they made her stay.

At twelve-thirty the TV room light came on.

Tammy leaned forward until the seat belt bit her hip. The room was too far for faces, and one light by itself did not mean much. A resident might have wandered. Priya could have parked somebody there with cable and warm milk to keep the yelling off the hall. Staff used empty common rooms for phone calls sometimes, especially the travel nurses who did not want to cry where anybody knew their name.

Still, the TV room was the farthest common room from the station and the care plans said residents were supposed to be in bed by then unless something had gone wrong. The light stayed on. A shape crossed the window, low and wide like a wheelchair. Another shape moved in front of it and back again. Then the first shape shifted a little and held.

Tammy checked the dashboard clock, then the building again. She had no proof that anybody in that room was Carmen or Harry or Lola or Agnes. She had even less proof about why they would be there together after midnight with the blinds half-open and the station blind to it. What she had was the feel she got when she found a dark pressure spot on somebody's heel and knew she was looking at the start of a worse thing if no one changed course fast.

She stayed another ten minutes. No one came out the front entrance. No one used the side door she could see. The TV room light kept burning. When it finally went dark, it did not happen with the quick clean snap of somebody leaving. It clicked off and then on again for a second, as if a hand had missed the switch in the dark, and then it went black for good.

Tammy started the car.

Dunellen after one in the morning looked like somebody had set the town out neatly and forgotten to put people in it. The laundromat sign on North Avenue was dark. A pizza box leaned against the curb outside the closed nail place. Grace's porch light was still on below the apartment because Grace believed darkness invited men and raccoons in equal numbers and both were better discouraged early.

Tammy parked in back beside the leaning fence and sat for a minute with the engine off. The quiet here was not real quiet. The freight line a few blocks over kept up a low iron mutter. A dog barked once and got told to shut up through a window. From below came the thin wash of Christian radio through Grace's ceiling, some woman singing about surrender like it was a thing you chose with clean hands.

The outside stairs held the day's cold in the metal rail. Tammy climbed them with her purse pulling at one shoulder and the smell of the nursing home still warm in her scrubs. By the second landing she could already smell the apartment before she opened it, that old cat smell ground into carpet pad and heated back up every time the radiator pipes clicked alive.

The studio gave her the same first hit it always did: cat piss under lemon cleaner, radiator steam, the stale grease that lived in the little kitchen no matter how often she wiped the vent, and the sweet candle Grace kept burning downstairs that came through the floorboards when the place got warm. The room was one rectangle with a half-wall pretending to separate the bed from the couch and a window unit she had already wrapped in plastic for winter. The plastic snapped softly whenever the draft pushed at it.

She locked the door, set the purse on the counter, and hung her jacket on the brass hook she had unscrewed from the hallway of the old house the last afternoon before Greg changed the garage code. He had installed it himself because he hated clutter on chairs. The screws were stripped now, and the hook leaned a little left, but it held.

The couch sagged in the middle where a spring had gone. The Walmart television reflected her back at herself in a black square: hair coming loose from the knot, teal scrub top dark under the arms, face older at one in the morning than it had any right to be at forty-three. She toed off her shoes and felt grit through one sock because this place could manufacture grit out of nowhere.

She went into the bathroom first because if she sat down before washing up she would stay there. The overhead light made everything look the color of phlegm. She stripped, dropped the scrubs in the hamper, and stood under water that took too long to turn hot. When it did, the heat found every split in her hands. She scrubbed until the smell backed off from her skin, though never all the way. It never all the way left. Care work stayed in the seams.

The towel rack rattled every time the downstairs toilet flushed. Grace flushed twice in quick succession while Tammy was drying off, then the radio got louder for a minute. Tammy pulled on old sweatpants and one of the Lakeview T-shirts they gave staff for survey week, the navy one with the logo cracking across the chest. She had three of them. She wore them because free was free.

The fridge held half a gallon of milk, yellow mustard, eggs, a heel of wheat bread, and the plastic grocery bag with two apples going soft inside it. There was leftover macaroni from Tuesday in a margarine tub and a jar of store-brand salsa she had bought because it was cheaper than decent. On the freezer door she kept a magnet clip with the gas receipt from Tuesday, the car-insurance reminder, and the electric bill she was pretending not to see until after next payday.

Her pay stub from the last check sat under the magnet too, folded twice. She took it down and carried it to the little table by the window that served as desk, kitchen, and landing zone for everything she was too tired to deal with standing up. The chair wobbled unless she kept one foot braced against the table leg. Greg had taken the good kitchen set because technically his mother had bought it for them. Tammy had kept the folding card table from a yard sale and two chairs that never matched.

The stub still said $16.50 an hour in print so crisp it felt insulting. A decent month, meaning one where she picked up enough extra shifts and nobody cut hours because census dipped, left her around twenty-two hundred clear. She turned over a junk-mail envelope and wrote the number down.

Then she started subtracting the pieces she knew by muscle memory. Rent was four hundred seventy-five. The car payment was two-forty. Insurance took one-eighteen. The phone cost eighty-two because every time she tried to lower the plan some man in a headset acted like she was asking to live without electricity. Gas varied, but it was never under ninety and usually worse. Groceries were manageable only if she behaved. Ibuprofen was not optional. Coin laundry kept taking quarters. The copay came due when her left shoulder acted up. The dentist bill still sat there because owing them in daylight felt different from owing them on paper at one in the morning.

She did not write in nice straight columns. She wrote the way she charted when the shift had gone bad, hard enough to dent the sheet under it. By the time she reached the bottom of the envelope, the number left over looked like a joke somebody from administration would call budgeting. It was enough for cereal, cat litter if she had a cat, which she did not, and whatever fresh insult the Honda decided to become next.

She opened the drawer for another pen because the first one had gone dry, and the manila folder with the divorce papers caught on the lip and slid halfway out. It always did that. Everything in a studio ended up jammed against everything else. The Post-it was still clipped to the front because she had never taken it off.

Sorry, T, Greg had written in the round block print he used for grocery lists, oil-change dates, and the note on the old freezer that said eat this first. He had left it on the counter the day he took the rest of his shirts and the drill set and whatever nerve he had needed to decide twelve years of marriage could be boiled down to two words and an initial. Tammy stood there with the dead pen in her hand and looked at it until Grace's radio bled up through the floor with a preacher saying the word grace three times in thirty seconds.

Greg had the house now. Greg had the couch that did not sag, the real mattress, the yellow Fiesta dishes from their wedding, and the blond Verizon woman from Woodbridge Tammy had once looked up on Facebook after half a bottle of Yellow Tail and two nights of bad sleep. The woman had a beach picture with Greg's hand around her waist and a smile that said she had never once had to turn a grown man in bed by herself. Tammy had stared at the photo longer than she meant to, then hated herself for handing them another witness.

She put the folder back too hard and the drawer bounced. Something in the silverware tray rattled like a cheap bell. Her face felt hot, then stupid for feeling hot. Divorce was old news. She knew that. Old news still had a way of waiting under things for the hour when there was no one around to watch you get hit by it again.

On the counter sat the half bottle of Yellow Tail from three weekends ago, shoved behind the toaster. She took it out, looked at the inch left in the bottom, and put it back without pouring. She had work-brain still, and work-brain did not do better after wine. It did arithmetic and replayed rooms and let every small weird thing stand under a bright light until it either made sense or kept refusing to.

The apartment had settled into its night sounds by then. The radiator knocked twice in the wall behind the bed. The plastic over the window gave a dry little pop. Grace coughed downstairs, turned her radio down, then back up one notch because down was never enough. A car went by outside too fast for the street and carried bass with it for three seconds, then the whole place was small and still again.

Tammy looked at the envelope covered in numbers and realized she had been writing about Carmen's shift in the same hard pressure without meaning to, about the count and the car still there and the TV room. If she let the thought sit there unmarked, it would blur by morning into one more thing she was almost sure about and could not prove.

She flipped the envelope to the clean side.

At the top she wrote the date. Under it she printed: At 10:55 p.m., Priya signed the count. On the next line she wrote: At 11:30 p.m., Carmen's car was still there. On the next she wrote: At 12:30 a.m., the TV room light was on and there was movement inside. She added Wing A because details mattered later when people started pretending they had never understood the layout.

Then she stood there with the pen point on the paper and added a few more things she had not meant to write. Carmen was in Agnes's room after med pass. Lola asked for Friday with Roberto's card on the dresser. Harry was clear on Tuesday night. She stared at the last line a second, then left it. If she was wrong, she wanted to be wrong in specifics.

The purse lay open on the couch with a pack of gum, two crumpled receipts, hand lotion, and the little zip pouch where she kept her spare work badge and twenty-dollar emergency bill. She folded the envelope twice and slid it into the pouch behind the bill. It felt stupid and dramatic, like evidence in a show her mother would have watched, but she did it anyway.

Her phone sat plugged in by the bed on a charger that had to be balanced just right or it stopped connecting. She set the alarm for 11:15 p.m. Friday, then another for 11:20 because she did not trust the first one or the charger or herself to sleep through the whole evening the one night she actually could. Friday was supposed to be laundry, a grocery run, maybe vacuuming if she could stand the machine noise. Friday was not supposed to end in the Lakeview parking lot.

She brushed her teeth at the kitchen sink because the bathroom mirror made her feel cornered. The toothpaste tasted medicinal and cheap. When she spit, she saw a thread of pink in the foam where she'd brushed too hard at the gum above the back molar that liked to complain. Another thing not worth a dentist bill until it became impossible to ignore.

In bed, the sheet felt cold on the side that stayed uncovered all day and warm in the middle where the mattress held body heat like a bad memory. She lay on her back and looked at the water stain spreading over the ceiling in the shape of Ohio. Greg used to say it looked more like a mitten. Tammy had spent two winters correcting him and then another year missing the sound of being able to correct somebody in the dark.

Sleep did not come because the chapter of the night had not closed. Her brain kept running the same strip of film: Carmen adjusting the badge, Agnes's eyes cutting over, Lola's Friday card, the TV room window lighting up around the bend. She could still see the shape in the glass, low and wide, maybe a wheelchair, maybe not. It was close enough to matter and not close enough to use.

She turned on her side, then back again. The place smelled cleaner after the shower but the old-cat heat lived under it and kept rising whenever the pipes knocked on. Somewhere below, Grace laughed at something the radio host said and then went quiet. Tammy put one hand over her face and breathed into the heel of it until the itch to get up passed.

If she took it to Cliff tomorrow, he would ask whether she had seen an actual policy violation. He would ask whether fatigue might be coloring her interpretation. He would say Carmen was under strain and Lakeview needed team trust. He would mean protect the license on the narc book until somebody with a credential forced his hand. Tammy had watched that movie enough times to know the ending.

If she was wrong and said nothing, she had wasted a night and maybe some dignity. If she was right and said something too early, whoever was doing whatever they were doing would stop long enough to clean it up. Fifteen years in one building had taught her that timing mattered almost as much as truth. Sometimes it mattered more.

She got up once to check that the alarm had saved and once more to make sure the envelope was really in the purse. On the second trip she stood by the window and looked down at Grace's porch light pooling yellow on the patchy grass. The whole building smelled faintly of old cooking oil and damp carpet and somebody's lavender plug-in fighting a war it could not win.

When she lay back down, she left the bedside lamp off and listened to the radiator tapping in the wall. Tomorrow night she would back into the far row again. She would bring a notebook this time, not a junk-mail envelope. She would watch longer if she had to. She was tired of half-knowing things.

Friday was supposed to be her day off. She closed her eyes and kept the alarm set anyway.
