# Chapter 10

Harry had said one o'clock. Carmen had asked for the meeting three days after the survey — three days after she sat in her car unable to drive, feeling the specific weight of what she'd told the state surveyor settle into a place she couldn't reach to remove it. She'd found Harry alone in the TV room during evening med pass, leaned against the doorframe while Lola pretended to watch a game show behind him, and said, "Show me all of it." Harry had said one o'clock. Nothing else.

So at one o'clock she walked the length of Wing A past Devon sleeping at the nurse's station with his phone face-down on his chest, past Room 110 where Mrs. Rowan was making the low steady sound that Carmen's brain had reclassified as building noise months ago, and pushed open the TV room door.

Harry sat in his wheelchair at the card table — the one the activities aide used for bingo on Tuesdays — wearing the faded blue cardigan he wore to every after-midnight meeting Carmen had attended. Agnes sat across from him. Lola was at her post by the hallway door, one hand resting on the gold crucifix at her throat, her other hand still in the tremor that worsened at night. Earl sat in the vinyl recliner by the far window with his eyes closed, though Carmen could tell from the set of his shoulders that he was listening. Between Harry and Agnes, spread flat under the fluorescent tube that buzzed from a loose ballast, were Agnes's notebooks.

There were four of them. Composition books, the black-and-white marbled kind you could buy at CVS for a dollar nineteen, and Agnes had filled them in handwriting so small and uniform it looked typeset. Agnes didn't look up when Carmen pulled a vinyl chair to the table and sat. She was sorting pill bottles by a system Carmen couldn't immediately read — not alphabetical, not by DEA schedule. Later she'd realize it was by buyer need, grouped into dispensing sets the way a pharmacist would prep an order.

"Sit," Harry said, which Carmen was already doing.

He started with the money.

Eighteen months. That was the first number. The operation had been running since early last year, since before Carmen started at Lakeview, since before Carmen had even applied to Lakeview. Roughly four thousand dollars a month in gross revenue. Forty-eight thousand, approximately, total.

He said the numbers the way he said all numbers — flat, exact, stripped of everything but value. His posture was a man giving a quarterly review: shoulders squared against the wheelchair back, voice level, each figure placed between them like a coin on a table. Carmen had expected the total to shock her. It didn't. Forty-eight thousand dollars over eighteen months sounded like a lot until you divided it by the number of people involved and the number of months they'd been doing this. It was roughly what Carmen made in a year before taxes at a job that was supposed to be legal.

"Marcus takes thirty percent," Harry said. "About twelve hundred a month. His cut for distribution."

"And risk," Carmen said.

Harry looked at her. A beat. "And risk."

Marcus was the one in a car with pills in the center console, the one meeting people he barely knew in a strip mall parking lot, the one who'd go to prison in the unambiguous way — not the way where sympathetic news segments showed elderly faces and the judge considered the defendants' ages and the public defender mentioned diminished capacity. Marcus would get sentenced. He'd do time. Carmen didn't need Harry to explain why thirty percent was a number Marcus might reasonably consider low.

"After his cut," Harry said. "Twenty-eight hundred a month. Distributed."

He turned one of Agnes's notebooks to face Carmen and pointed at a page of handwritten double-entry ledger — old-fashioned, meticulous, the kind of bookkeeping Harry's generation had learned before spreadsheets made it quaint. Revenue by month on the left. Outflows on the right.

Jack Zielinski. Harry's grandson, twenty-six. He'd started a landscaping business in South Brunswick with equipment bought on credit and a fifteen-thousand-dollar hole he couldn't climb out of on what landscaping paid in the off-season. Harry sent five hundred a month to an account Jack believed was funded by a certificate of deposit his grandfather had cashed before the nursing home. The money went through a prepaid debit card Marcus loaded with cash, which showed up in Jack's account as a recurring transfer from a name Jack didn't recognize and hadn't thought to question. Harry's voice didn't change when he talked about Jack. It stayed flat and numerical, the accountant's register, but his hand on the notebook page pressed down a little harder when he said the name, and Carmen noticed.

Denise Washington. Earl's daughter, home health aide in Trenton. Three kids in school who needed coats and fees and field-trip money and the kind of expenses that sounded small when you listed them individually and consumed every dollar Denise didn't have when you added them up. Earl's share — four hundred a month — arrived wrapped in a lie: a VA supplemental benefit program for Korean War veterans that did not exist in any government database. In the vinyl recliner by the window, Earl didn't open his eyes. He coughed once — a deep, wet, rattling sound that came from somewhere lower than his throat — and settled back into his stillness. Nobody commented on the cough. Carmen had noticed it before, during other meetings, and each time it seemed a little heavier.

Lola's share went to Iglesia de la Caridad in Little Havana, a church she'd attended for thirty years before her son moved her north. Three hundred and fifty a month by money order, no return address. The church ran a free clinic three days a week — blood pressure, diabetes screenings, the baseline care that kept people out of emergency rooms and cost almost nothing to provide and was funded by almost nothing because the people who needed it had almost nothing. From the doorway, Lola made a small sound when Harry named the church — not a word, just an exhalation, something between recognition and prayer. Then she went back to watching the hallway.

Burt's share covered the gap between what Medicare said he was entitled to and what his body actually required. A hearing aid — not the four-thousand-dollar kind, not even the decent kind, but something better than the pharmacy-counter amplifier the facility had provided, which turned every sound into the same compressed roar and gave him headaches by noon. Compression stockings that fit his legs instead of the one-size-fits-nothing pairs the supply company shipped in bulk. The brand-name version of his Parkinson's medication, carbidopa-levodopa, because the generic the facility pharmacy had substituted eight months ago made his tremor worse and left him nauseated for two hours after every dose, and nobody had reviewed the substitution because nobody reviewed anything. Five hundred a month, roughly, depending on what needed replacing.

Agnes.

Harry said her name and stopped. He didn't say the amount. Agnes was sitting three feet away, and the amount was hers to claim or not.

Agnes's share sat in a white envelope under her mattress. She didn't explain. Carmen looked at her — at the wire-rimmed glasses, at the precise hands still turning pill bottles label-forward, at the short gray hair and the slight leftward tilt from the stroke that had given her daughter the excuse to put her here. Agnes had no one to send the money to. No grandchildren. No church. No cause she believed in that needed four hundred dollars a month. Her daughter Helen visited every other Sunday with the children and talked to Agnes in the slow, loud voice of someone addressing a condition rather than a person, and Agnes sat through it with the compressed fury of a woman who could conjugate drug interactions in her sleep but who had been classified, filed, and stored. Her money accumulated under the mattress the way her expertise accumulated inside her silence — purposeless, precise, unclaimed.

"That's where the money goes," Harry said.

He said it without editorializing, without framing it as defense or justification. He said it the way he'd said every number: as fact. This is what exists. Carmen could do what she wanted with the information.

She sat with the notebooks for a while. The fluorescent tube buzzed. Outside the window, the parking lot's sodium lights turned the courtyard trees the same flat orange — the leaves were starting to go, it was almost October, and a few had drifted onto the benches nobody sat on at night. The building settled around them with its nocturnal sounds: Mrs. Rowan, the ventilation cycling, the toilet in Room 109 that maintenance had been asked to fix three times and hadn't.

Carmen didn't think about legality. She noticed this the way you notice you've been holding your breath — something that started before she was aware of it and was now simply true. She thought about flow. About the institutional machinery she worked inside: medications prescribed by Dr. Prasad, who saw forty patients a day across three facilities and spent eight minutes per visit and couldn't be expected to hold eighty drug profiles in her head; dispensed by a pharmacy that substituted generics without chart review and delivered in bulk shipments nobody checked against individual care plans; administered by nurses who couldn't flag overprescription without risking their standing with the physician they depended on for orders. The system produced surplus. It overprescribed because underprescribing required conversations, follow-up, clinical judgment — and those things cost staff hours that cost money that Cliff Moorehead's budget didn't have. A resident who was agitated got lorazepam. A resident who couldn't sleep got trazodone. The medications accumulated in bodies and in cabinets and on charts, and nobody asked whether the accumulation served the patient because the question itself was more expensive than the pills.

And on the other side: Rosa with lupus. Derek rationing metformin. Janine cut loose from her pain management because her doctor was nervous about the DEA's opioid-prescribing audits and it was easier to drop patients than fight the board. People whose conditions hadn't changed but whose access had — who needed the same medications they'd always needed and couldn't get them because the system that produced the surplus had no connection to the system that produced the deficit, and the only bridge between them was this room, these people, this card table.

"The buyer list," Harry said.

Agnes pushed a composition book toward Carmen — the most recent one, its spine still stiff. Carmen opened it and understood the organizing principle immediately. The pages were divided not by name but by diagnosis. Agnes's handwriting in ruled columns: LUPUS-SLE. TYPE 2 DM. CHRONIC PAIN — MUSCULOSKELETAL. HYPERTENSION. HYPERLIPIDEMIA. Under each heading: dosages, quantities, frequencies, a coding system Carmen didn't recognize, and in the margins, notes in red ink.

"No names," Carmen said.

"Names are not relevant." Agnes said this the way she said everything — once, without decoration. Her hands kept working the pill bottles, sorting by some internal logic that was as natural to her as a pulse check was to Carmen. "A person with lupus needs hydroxychloroquine. That fact does not change with their name. I organize by need."

Carmen turned the pages. The entries were meticulous. Next to each diagnosis, Agnes had noted contraindications, maximum daily dosages, and potential interactions with medications the buyer might be taking from other sources. Three entries were marked with red asterisks and a notation Carmen could read as clinical shorthand: FLAG — INTERACTION RISK.

"The flagged ones," Carmen said. "You contacted them."

"Marcus contacted them. I provided the guidance." Agnes aligned a row of amber bottles with her fingertips — small adjustments, millimeters. "I do not dispense medications that could harm someone. I have never done that. I will not begin now."

There was no bitterness in her voice. Agnes didn't traffic in bitterness. She trafficked in facts, and this was one: the institutional pharmacy that had substituted Burt's Parkinson's medication without reviewing his chart, that had delayed Agnes's own prescriptions for two weeks during a supplier switch three years ago while she sat in her room in pain that no one checked on because her chart said she was non-verbal — that pharmacy operated under the full authority of the state of New Jersey and the federal regulatory apparatus that licensed it. Agnes's composition notebooks, organized by diagnosis and screened for interactions by a pharmacist with four decades of retail experience, operated from a card table in a room that smelled like vinyl upholstery and the cold coffee Lola had smuggled from the kitchen. Carmen could see which system was more careful. She could see it in the handwriting.

Agnes opened the second notebook — older, its cover softened from use — to a page in the middle. Two columns. The left was headed FACILITY MONTHLY MEDICATION COST PER RESIDENT (INSTITUTIONAL RATE). The right: EQUIVALENT MONTHLY COST (UNINSURED, RETAIL).

The numbers on the left Carmen recognized. A resident at Lakeview might be prescribed eight to twelve medications. The facility's pharmacy billed Medicare at institutional rates, and the per-resident monthly drug cost regularly exceeded two thousand dollars. Some of it necessary. Some of it the institutional reflex to medicate every symptom — agitation, insomnia, appetite loss, anxiety — that might otherwise require staff time the facility didn't have to give.

The numbers on the right were the ones Carmen hadn't seen laid out like this. What it cost to buy the same medications at a retail pharmacy without insurance coverage. Hydroxychloroquine, two hundred milligrams, ninety count: three hundred eighty-nine dollars. Rosuvastatin, twenty milligrams, thirty count: two hundred twelve. Metformin, five hundred milligrams, sixty count: eighty-seven. Oxycodone, five milligrams, thirty count: she didn't need Agnes to tell her what oxycodone cost on the street, but the pharmacy price without insurance was two hundred forty-one dollars, and the markup between what Lakeview paid per unit and what an uninsured person paid at CVS was a factor of six.

"Surplus on the left," Agnes said. She closed the notebook. "Deficit on the right. That is not a moral argument. It is an arithmetic fact."

Harry watched Carmen. He hadn't moved since he finished the distribution breakdown — hands folded on the card table, still, assessing, already calculating what she'd do with what she'd been shown. He had the patience of a man who'd spent a career waiting for ledgers to balance.

"I want you to meet someone," he said. "A buyer. So you see the other side of the numbers."

Carmen looked at the notebooks, at the columns, at the red-ink flags.

"When?"

"Thursday. Your shift starts at three. Be in the lot at two-thirty. Silver Civic, far end."

Carmen closed the composition book and handed it back to Agnes, who took it with the quick, certain grip of a woman who did not drop things.

---

Rosa drove an early 2000s Civic — silver paint going milky at the fenders, a St. Christopher medal on the rearview mirror, a striped beach towel folded over a tear in the passenger seat. She was parked at the far end of Lakeview's lot, away from the entrance and the employee spaces, in the spot where visitors parked when they didn't want to be seen arriving or didn't want anyone to remember them leaving. Carmen knew the spot. Families used it when they came to admit a parent they wouldn't visit again.

Thursday. Two twenty-five. Carmen had arrived early, crossing the lot in her scrubs with her badge clipped and her bag on her shoulder. The afternoon was warm for late September, holding onto a summer that had already ended — the trees in the courtyard were turning but the air hadn't caught up.

Rosa got out of the car when she saw Carmen coming. She was fifty-two and moved like someone whose body charged her for every step — carefully, with a small preliminary adjustment before each motion, the way you navigate a room where the furniture has been rearranged and you haven't learned the new layout yet. She had dark hair cut close, practical, and she was wearing a fleece vest over a long-sleeved shirt despite the warmth. Lupus made her cold. Carmen knew this before Rosa spoke — cold intolerance, Raynaud's, the thermoregulatory disruption that came with autoimmune disease — the same way she'd know a limp meant hip or knee, the automatic clinical recognition that happened before she decided to look.

"You're the nurse," Rosa said.

"Yeah."

"The old man — Harry, whatever his name is — he said you wanted to talk."

"I wanted to understand the costs. What you're paying."

Rosa leaned against the Civic and crossed her arms. Not defensively — the posture of a woman who'd been sitting in a parking lot for twenty minutes and was deciding whether the trip had been worth it. She studied Carmen with the expression of someone accustomed to explaining her situation to people who held some kind of authority over her life and rarely did anything with it.

"OK," Rosa said. She turned and opened the passenger door and pulled out a CVS bag. "I went this week. I do that sometimes — walk in, hand them the prescription, ask what it costs. Just to see." She pulled a folded piece of receipt paper from the bag. She'd creased it neatly, precisely, the fold of someone who kept track of numbers because the numbers kept track of her. "Hydroxychloroquine, two hundred milligrams, ninety count. Three eighty-nine at CVS. Three sixty-two at the Walgreens on Route 1." She put the receipt back in the bag. "Four twelve at the Rite Aid, but the Rite Aid on Cleveland closed, so."

"And from Marcus?"

"Forty dollars."

Carmen didn't say anything.

"A month," Rosa said. "Forty dollars a month for a ninety-day supply. And whoever's doing the packaging — a woman, Marcus won't tell me her name — she checks the expiration dates and puts a slip in the bag. Don't take with antacids. Space four hours from other meds." She paused. "My rheumatologist used to tell me the same thing. Back when I could see my rheumatologist."

They stood in the parking lot. A medical supply van was parked by the loading dock with its rear doors open. Two cars passed on Route 27. The wind moved a paper cup across the asphalt in a slow arc and left it against a curb.

"I was at a dental office in Woodbridge," Rosa said. "Receptionist. Twelve years. The dentist retired, and the new owner brought his own staff." She made a small motion with her shoulders — not quite a shrug, something more resigned and less performative. "I looked at COBRA. The plan was small-group, under twenty employees, so COBRA wasn't an option. I looked at the marketplace. Premiums were five-forty a month, and the deductible was three thousand before anything kicked in. I make — I was making sixteen an hour at a temp agency, and you can do the math on that faster than I can."

Carmen could do the math. Not Rosa's specific math, but the architecture of it — the corridor between making too much for Medicaid and too little for a plan that covered the medications you needed, the gap that swallowed people silently, without the drama of a crisis, just the steady arithmetic of not-enough compounding month after month. Carmen was thirty-one years old and an LPN and she was one lost job from the same corridor. She knew this because she'd sat at her own kitchen table after Reggie was asleep and run the same kind of numbers that all ended at the same wall.

"What happens when you don't take it?" Carmen asked.

Rosa looked at her. "You're a nurse. You know what happens."

"I'm asking you."

"Flares." Rosa held up her right hand. The knuckles were swollen, the skin over them tight and shiny with the specific texture of chronic synovial inflammation — not bruised, not injured, just perpetually wrong, the joints thickened by a disease that treated her own tissue as hostile. "Mostly joints. My knees, my hands. Last time I went without — this was before Marcus — I ran out for three weeks. Couldn't open a jar. Couldn't button my pants." She looked at her hand the way you'd look at a tool you'd been issued that no longer functioned properly. "I called in sick for a week at a job I'd just started. A warehouse, data entry for their inventory system. They let me go after day four. The temp agency stopped calling after that."

She said this without emphasis. Not performing her own suffering, not framing it as injustice, not asking for anything. It was a sequence of events that had happened to her body and then to her employment and then to her ability to pay for the medication that would have prevented the sequence in the first place. She described it the way you'd describe a car accident from a few years ago — something that rearranged things, that you stopped expecting sympathy for because sympathy didn't fill prescriptions.

"Can I see that one?" Carmen said, and nodded at Rosa's left hand, which she'd been holding against her vest pocket.

Rosa extended it. Carmen took it. The metacarpophalangeal joints were warm and boggy — the synovitis she'd studied in her pharmacology textbook and seen in clinical rotations and recognized now by touch the way she recognized a fever or an irregular pulse. She turned Rosa's hand gently, feeling the swelling over the ring finger knuckle, feeling the heat that meant the inflammation was active.

For a moment she was just a nurse holding a patient's hand in a parking lot, checking a joint. It was the most familiar thing she'd done in weeks. This was what she knew. Assess, observe, feel what was wrong and name it. The complications were elsewhere — in the TV room at one in the morning, in Agnes's notebooks, in the numbers she'd signed off on for the state surveyor. The hand was just a hand, and Carmen knew what to do with a hand.

"This one's getting worse," she said, pressing lightly on the ring finger joint.

"I know."

"You should get it imaged."

"With what?" Rosa said. Not angry. A real question with a real answer, and they both knew the answer was the same wall Carmen had seen from her own kitchen table: with what insurance, with what copay, with what deductible, with what money.

Carmen let go. Rosa put her hand back in her vest pocket.

"I'm not asking you for anything," Rosa said. "I get it — you've got your own situation. Your license, or whatever. The old man said you were worried. I understand being worried." She shifted her weight against the Civic. "But I'm telling you what I'm doing. I'm buying my medication from the only place I can afford it, and I'm taking it the way my doctor told me to take it, back when I could afford to see my doctor. That's what's happening. That's the whole thing."

She got back in the Civic. The engine turned over on the second try — a hesitation, then the catch, the sound of a car that started every morning but might not start forever. Carmen watched her pull out of the lot. Brake lights at the exit, a left signal, and the silver car merged into the afternoon traffic on Route 27 and was gone.

Carmen stood in the lot. Her shift started in seven minutes. Through the glass front doors she could see the lobby — the silk flowers on the reception desk that Cliff had put out for the survey and hadn't bothered to remove, the LAKEVIEW GARDENS sign in its welcoming font, the hand sanitizer dispenser mounted by the entrance. Tammy's Corolla was in the employee row. The medical supply van had pulled away from the loading dock, leaving tire marks on the pavement where it had idled too long.

She thought about Agnes's notebooks on the card table. The composition books with their ruled columns: diagnosis, dosage, quantity, interaction flags in red ink. She thought about the two-column comparison — facility cost on the left, uninsured retail cost on the right, the gap between them wide enough to swallow a woman who used to work a reception desk in Woodbridge for twelve years and now couldn't button her pants without a forty-dollar bag of pills from a cookie tin in a nursing home.

Three hundred and eighty-nine dollars versus forty. A ninety-day supply. The medication that let Rosa's hands work — that let her open jars and button clothes and type inventory data and do the ordinary mechanical things a body did when a body wasn't attacking itself. The difference between those numbers was being bridged by four residents and a fired CNA, using composition notebooks and a card table and a prepaid phone, operating between midnight and two in the morning in a room with a broken television and vinyl chairs. And the system that produced the surplus — the one that overprescribed because underprescribing cost staff time, that substituted generics without review, that billed Medicare for all of it and called it care — was the system Carmen worked inside, and was licensed by, and had lied to a state official to keep functioning, and could not, standing in this parking lot with the smell of asphalt and exhaust and the late-September air that couldn't decide if it was still summer, argue against.

She went inside. She clipped her badge to her scrub pocket — the small automatic gesture, badge to pocket, stethoscope check, hair smoothed back. The motions of becoming the nurse. She signed in at the desk, noted the time in the log, and walked toward the medication room to begin her shift.
