# Chapter 17

Carmen was in the medication room doing the evening count when she heard it. Not the words at first — just the cadence. Every nurse learns the difference between the TV room's daytime murmur and the local news anchor's emergency tone, the slight tightening in the broadcaster's delivery that means something happened nearby, something with a suspect, something with a mugshot.

She set down the clipboard and walked to the TV room.

It was mostly empty at 8:40 PM — dinner was over, the last visitors had signed out at eight, and most of the Wing A residents were back in their rooms. Burt was dozing in the recliner nearest the screen, his mouth open, one hand curled around the armrest like he was holding onto something in a dream. The volume was up too loud because nobody had adjusted it after Mrs. Petroski had gone back to her room, and Mrs. Petroski was functionally deaf.

The anchor was describing an arrest in Edison. Prescription drug distribution. A man named DeShaun Briley, thirty-seven, picked up during a traffic stop with approximately four hundred tablets of oxycodone, eighty alprazolam, and an undetermined quantity of hydrocodone in the trunk of his vehicle. Possession with intent to distribute, state and federal charges. Bond set at seventy-five thousand.

Carmen didn't recognize the name. She didn't recognize the face in the mugshot — a heavy young man, scared-looking under the flat police lighting, his left eye slightly swollen like he'd been rubbing it or hadn't slept. But she recognized the volume. Four hundred tablets of oxycodone wasn't a person's stash. It wasn't even a mid-level dealer's weekly turnover. That was the kind of quantity that required a supply chain, and the kind of quantity Marcus's new buyer had been purchasing.

Her hand went to her badge and stayed there. The news anchor moved on to a car accident on Route 18.

She stood in the doorway for another thirty seconds, watching the screen without seeing it, while her mind ran the chain. Briley to Marcus. Marcus to Lakeview. Lakeview to the count discrepancies documented under her name, her license number, her signature on every shift-end log for the past seven months. If Briley had Marcus's phone number in his contacts — if he had a text, a single text, with Marcus's name or a location or a time — investigators would pull Marcus's records. And Marcus had visited this building every week for over a year, signing in at the front desk as Lola's nephew, his name in a visitor log that no one cross-referenced because no one at Lakeview cross-referenced anything.

Carmen turned off the TV room light on her way out. Burt didn't stir.

She went to Harry's room. He was sitting up in bed reading the Trenton Times, his faded blue cardigan buttoned wrong — top button through the second hole — and his reading glasses taped at the left temple where the arm had broken six weeks ago. He looked up when Carmen knocked on the open door, and his face told her he already knew.

"You saw it," she said.

"I saw it." He folded the paper and placed it in his lap. "Edison. That's twenty minutes from here."

"It's the volume buyer. The quantities match."

Harry took off his glasses. Without them, his eyes were sharper — the lenses were for reading, but they also softened his face, made it rounder, made him look more like the befuddled old man he played during daylight hours. "I've been trying Marcus's phone for an hour. His personal one. Goes straight to voicemail."

"Since when do you have Marcus's personal number?"

Harry looked at her the way he looked at her whenever she said something he considered obvious. He didn't answer.

"When's the last time he came in?" Carmen asked.

"Ten days ago. He hasn't been here since the meeting." That was what they called it — the night Marcus had been confronted, had argued back, had been cut off by Earl in a single sentence that ended more than the conversation. "Sit down, Carmen."

She didn't sit down. She adjusted her badge, felt the clip digging into the fabric of her scrub top. "If they trace Briley to Marcus — any connection, a phone record, a text, anything — they follow Marcus here. They pull the visitor log and they see his name every week for fourteen months. They look at the medication records and they see my count discrepancies, documented and signed. Every one of them mine."

"Sit down."

"I can't sit down right now."

Harry put his glasses on the nightstand and lined them up parallel to the edge, the way he aligned everything — pens, papers, envelopes of cash. The accountant's reflex for order, even now. "We clean house," he said.

"Everything?"

"Everything. Pills, records, the phone. Tonight."

Carmen leaned against the doorframe. The hallway behind her smelled the way it always smelled at night — disinfectant and the warm-skin scent of people who didn't go outside, the fading trace of dinner's meatloaf, hand sanitizer from the dispensers every fifteen feet. Familiar smells. She'd worked in this hallway for thirteen months and the smell had become invisible to her, the way her own apartment's smell was invisible, and now it registered again, sharp and specific, the way things register when you know you might be leaving them under circumstances you didn't choose.

"I'll tell Agnes," she said.

"Tell her about the notebooks."

"I know."

She found Agnes at 9:15. Agnes was still awake — she rarely slept before midnight, a habit from four decades of pharmacy shifts that ended at 10 PM. Her roommate, Dolores Gutierrez, was asleep and snoring wetly in the bed nearest the door, the sound of a woman whose body worked hard just to breathe. Agnes was sitting upright in the far bed with a crossword puzzle book open on her lap. She hadn't filled in any squares.

"I heard it," Agnes said before Carmen could speak. "The volume on the TV was up. Mrs. Petroski's hearing."

"Harry wants everything gone tonight."

Agnes closed the crossword book. Her hands were steady — the left-side weakness from her stroke showed in her gait when she walked, a slight drag in the left foot, but her grip had recovered fully within the first year — and she placed the book on the nightstand with the same deliberate precision she placed everything. Pharmacist's hands. Everything in its location. "My notebooks."

"Yeah."

"There are four. Composition books. Black-and-white marbled covers." Agnes's voice was clinical, but the words came faster than usual — closer together, compressed, the way her speech tightened under pressure. "Under my mattress. Under the fitted sheet, between the mattress and the mattress pad. Not under the bed frame."

Carmen looked at her. Agnes's face was what it always was — precise, impatient, stripped clean. But her hands had moved from the crossword book to the mattress edge and were pressing down, her fingertips whitening against the hospital-grade fitted sheet.

"I'll come get them after Devon falls asleep," Carmen said. "Eleven-thirty."

"I'll have them out."

---

Devon was asleep by 11:45. Carmen checked twice — at 11:30, when he was still scrolling his phone with the audio off, and again fifteen minutes later when the phone was face-down on his chest and his breathing had gone slow and even. He'd picked up the overnight because the regular night aide had called out, and Devon worked overnights the way he worked every shift — minimally, resentfully, one earbud in, his attention dimmed to whatever frequency let him avoid being fired for a third time. He was twenty-three. He'd keep sleeping.

Carmen went to Agnes's room first.

Agnes was standing by her bed in a housecoat that had belonged to someone else — the facility distributed donated clothing to residents whose families didn't bring enough, and Agnes's daughter Helen sent the same three outfits in rotation, all a size too large because Helen still pictured the woman she'd been during the stroke recovery, fifteen pounds lighter and unable to say the word "prescription" without it coming out wrong. The housecoat was a faded floral print with a zipper up the front. Agnes held the notebooks out in both hands, the way you hand off something fragile.

Four composition books. Black-and-white marbled covers. The kind sold at every CVS and Walgreens in New Jersey for two dollars and forty-nine cents, the kind Carmen had bought for Reggie's school supplies in September.

She took them. They were heavier than they looked — filled cover to cover, no blank pages. The top one fell open in her arms and she saw Agnes's handwriting: small, square, pharmacist's print that could have been typed. Columns of drug names with dosages. Buyer codes — not names, just two-letter abbreviations. Expiration dates noted in red ink. Margin notes: *Do not combine w/ warfarin. Check renal function. Ro — skip if on new antibiotic, interaction risk. Jn — monitor for signs of tolerance escalation (none observed).* One page had a hand-drawn table of pill counts going back months, each row dated, each column a different controlled substance, every number precise to the individual tablet.

Eighteen months of pharmaceutical expertise applied to an illegal operation with the same rigor Agnes had applied to forty-two years behind a pharmacy counter in Edison. These notebooks were evidence of a federal crime. They were also the most careful, most specific, most conscientious pharmaceutical records in the building — better than what Carmen charted, better than what Dr. Prasad's prescriptions produced, better than anything the state survey had reviewed.

"I'll burn them behind the loading dock," Carmen said.

Agnes looked at the notebooks in Carmen's arms. She was a small woman — five-two, gray hair cut short because she could manage it without help, wire-rimmed glasses reflecting the hallway's fluorescent light. She didn't say anything.

"Agnes."

"Go," Agnes said. Her voice was flat and fast.

Carmen went.

She started with the pills. Harry had the remaining stockpile in two shoeboxes in the back of his closet, behind a row of Reader's Digest condensed books that had come with the room. She retrieved them while Harry sat in his wheelchair by the window, watching the dark parking lot.

"Thirty-six oxycodone," he said without turning around. "Fourteen alprazolam. Twenty-one tramadol. Some non-controlled — the metformin, the lisinopril, a few rosuvastatin. Agnes has the exact count in the second notebook."

"It doesn't matter anymore, Harry."

"No." He turned the wheelchair around. His hands were on the wheels, and the motion was smooth but slow — not the efficient push-and-turn she'd seen in the midnight meetings, when he moved the chair like it was an extension of his body. Tonight the wheelchair looked like what it was supposed to look like: a device for someone who needed it. Carmen watched him and couldn't tell if the slowness was exhaustion or aging or the performance reasserting itself like muscle memory, and for the first time since she'd known the real Harry, she wasn't sure which one she was seeing.

"I suppose it doesn't," he said.

Carmen carried the shoeboxes to the handicapped bathroom at the end of Wing A — single-occupancy, lockable from the inside, a wider toilet with grab bars and a pull cord she'd respond to if someone fell, which no one would tonight because Carmen was the only person in Wing A who was awake and doing what she was supposed to be doing, and she was not doing what she was supposed to be doing. She locked the door.

The first box: oxycodone. She took the ziplock bag — labeled in Agnes's handwriting, *Oxycodone 5mg, 18 ct, exp. 04/27* — and poured six tablets into the toilet. They were small, round, white. They looked like anything. They looked like the mints her mother kept in a dish by the front door.

She flushed. Waited for the tank to refill. The plumbing in Lakeview was from the original 1978 construction — slow, loud, the pipes knocking in the walls like someone rapping their knuckles. She poured six more tablets. Flushed. Waited. The last six. Flushed.

The alprazolam went next. Then the tramadol. Then the non-controlled medications — the metformin and lisinopril and rosuvastatin that Agnes had identified as valuable to uninsured buyers, medications that cost two hundred or three hundred dollars a month at a pharmacy and that the ring had been selling for forty or fifty, medications that Rosa needed for her lupus and Derek needed for his diabetes and that were now swirling through the pipes beneath the building on their way to the New Jersey sewer system.

The process took forty-five minutes. She flushed in groups of five or six, waiting between each flush for the tank to refill, listening for sounds in the hallway — Devon's footsteps, a call bell, any indication that the building had noticed what she was doing. The bathroom was small and poorly ventilated, and the November night was cold outside but the building's heating system ran at a single temperature year-round, which was always too warm, and Carmen was sweating through her scrub top by the time the last tablet disappeared.

When she unlocked the door and stepped into the hallway, Earl was standing outside the medication room.

He was leaning against the wall, breathing through his mouth. His nighttime clothes — sweatpants, a flannel shirt with a fraying collar — hung on a frame that had been losing weight for months, though Carmen only registered it now, standing in the half-lit hallway with an empty shoebox in each hand. His big hands were flat against the wall behind him, the posture of a man who needed the wall more than he wanted to admit.

"Earl, you should be in bed."

"Harry said help."

"I don't need—" She stopped. She looked at him. He was 81 years old and his cough had been getting worse since at least September — she could hear it through the walls during her shifts sometimes, a deep wet rattle that sounded like it was pulling something loose — and he was standing in the hallway at half past midnight offering to carry things. "Okay. Take these back to Harry's room."

She handed him the empty shoeboxes. He took them and turned down the hall, and three steps in the cough hit — a real cough, not for show, the kind that bent him forward at the waist and made his shoulders shake. He stopped and leaned against the wall and coughed into a handkerchief he pulled from his pocket, and the sound of it filled the hallway, wet and thick and rattling. When it passed, he folded the handkerchief and put it back without looking at it. Carmen had been a nurse long enough to know what that meant — the not-looking. You don't check the handkerchief when you already know what you might find and you've decided not to find out.

"Earl."

"I'm fine." Said once. Flat. Finished. He kept walking.

Lola was at the junction of Wing A and the main hallway, sitting in one of the vinyl chairs near the window. She was supposed to be lookout — watching for Devon to stir, for movement from the nurse's station — and she was doing it with the same quiet alertness she'd brought to fourteen months of ring meetings, her eyes tracking the hallway in both directions. But her hands were shaking. She'd taken a coffee mug from the dining room — white ceramic, Lakeview Gardens printed in blue letters that had mostly worn off from industrial washing — and she was holding it with both hands, not drinking from it, just holding it. The coffee inside trembled, small concentric rings moving from the center to the edges and back. Her Parkinson's tremor was always there, but tonight it was worse, and the mug didn't help. The mug was supposed to give her hands something to press against, something to steady, but the tremor came through anyway, and the coffee shook and Lola's jaw was set tight with the effort of pretending she didn't notice.

"Mija," she said when Carmen passed. "How much longer?"

"Almost done, Lola. Stay here."

Lola nodded. She brought the mug closer to her chest, holding it against the front of her nightgown, and her gold crucifix clinked against the ceramic.

Carmen got the notebooks from where she'd left them on the supply closet shelf and went out the back. The loading dock was a concrete platform at the rear of the building, four feet off the ground, with a rusting metal ramp for deliveries and a dumpster that the trash service came for on Tuesdays and Fridays. Not visible from the front entrance or the parking lot. Carmen had checked. She'd also checked the security cameras — Lakeview had three, all pointed at the entrances, one of which had been broken since before she started and had never been repaired because Cliff Moorehead's maintenance budget was a joke that nobody at corporate was laughing at.

The November air hit her when she opened the back door. Forty degrees, maybe less — the kind of cold New Jersey does in early November, not winter yet but the memory of summer completely gone. The loading dock was lit by a single bulb above the door, yellow and weak, throwing a circle of light maybe ten feet across. Beyond that, the back of the property was dark — a strip of dead grass and then a chain-link fence and then the access road that ran behind the strip mall next door. The dumpster smelled like old food and the industrial disinfectant wipes that Lakeview went through in cases of forty-eight. No stars — just the brownish suburban sky, lit from below by the highway.

She found a metal trash can near the ramp — the maintenance staff used it for cigarette disposal and small burns — and dragged it to the center of the dock. She opened the first notebook.

Agnes's handwriting. Page after page. Every drug name spelled out in full, never abbreviated — oxycodone, not oxy; alprazolam, not Xanax; hydroxychloroquine, not HCQ — because Agnes had been a pharmacist for forty-two years and pharmacists didn't abbreviate. Carmen tore out the pages in bunches of ten or twelve, crumpled them, and dropped them into the trash can. She'd brought a box of kitchen matches from the break room — the kind left next to the coffeemaker for birthday candles, a small red box with the striker strip half worn through. She struck a match. The sulfur flared in the cold air and she dropped it onto the paper.

The first notebook burned slowly. Composition-book paper is heavy — that semi-glossy stock that resists flame, curls rather than catches, and needs feeding. Carmen tore more pages and pushed them in. The smoke was white and thin and smelled like a classroom. The ink made the fire flicker blue at the edges.

She was midway through the second notebook when Agnes appeared at the back door.

Carmen hadn't heard it open. Agnes was standing at the edge of the loading dock in her donated housecoat and slippers, her arms at her sides, the cold November air moving through the thin fabric. The firelight caught her glasses and made her eyes invisible behind two small rectangles of reflected orange.

Carmen stopped tearing pages. "You should be inside. It's cold."

Agnes didn't move. She watched the fire. The pages curled, blackened, fell apart — handwriting she'd put down over fourteen months, catalogues of medications and dosage calculations and interaction flags, the whole pharmaceutical architecture of the operation documented with the same rigor she'd applied to every prescription she'd filled for four decades. She watched it the way Carmen had once watched a nurse she'd trained fail a competency — professional grief, specific and unsentimental, the recognition that something well-made was being destroyed because the situation required it.

"I started those notebooks the first week Harry asked me to join," Agnes said. Her voice was faster than usual — the words closer together, compressed, more data per sentence. Her hands were at her sides and they were not sorting, not organizing, not performing the physical categorization that was her version of breathing through it. They were just hanging there. "Fourteen months of inventory. Every tablet accounted for, every interaction flagged, every expiration date tracked to the day. I maintained buyer profiles. Rosa's lupus medication schedule. Derek's insulin timing and when the metformin needed to coordinate. Janine's tolerance levels — I was monitoring for signs of escalating use, which I did not observe." She paused. "Nobody asked me to keep those records. I kept them because that is what a pharmacist does."

Carmen tore another fistful of pages and fed them into the trash can. The fire was hotter now, the draft pulling the flame up, the metal warming under her hand when she steadied the can's rim.

"This is the only professional work I have done in three years," Agnes said.

Carmen looked at her. Agnes was standing very still in the cold, her housecoat moving slightly in the wind off the access road, her slippers on the concrete of the loading dock. Her face was what it always was. She was not going to cry. Agnes Chen did not cry. She stated facts, and the facts were: she had been a pharmacist, and then she had been institutionalized by a daughter who mistook a stroke for senility, and then she had spent three years in a room with a woman who couldn't remember her own name, and the only thing in those three years that had used the expertise she'd spent a career building was an illegal drug operation run out of a nursing home TV room, and now the records of that operation were burning in a trash can on a loading dock in central New Jersey in November.

Carmen went back to burning. She finished all four notebooks by twenty past one. The ash was gray and powdery and collapsed when she stirred it with a stick from near the ramp. Agnes watched the whole time. She stood in the cold for the full forty minutes and did not move and did not speak again. When the last page was ash, she turned and went back inside. She didn't say goodnight.

Earl appeared in the hallway as Carmen came through the back door. He was carrying Lola's cookie tin — the Danish butter cookie tin that had been the handoff container, pills in ziplock bags sealed inside it every week for Marcus to pick up. It was empty now, just a blue tin with a picture of a windmill and some Danish words Carmen had never bothered to read.

"Lola said to get rid of it," Earl said. He was leaning against the wall again, his hand braced on the doorframe of the linen closet. His breathing was wrong — shallow, with a sound underneath it, a low wheeze that Carmen could hear from five feet away. The flannel shirt hung off his shoulders. He'd made two trips tonight, from his room to Harry's and from Lola's room to here, and the two trips had cost him something visible.

"I'll take it." She held out her hand and he gave her the tin. It was light, the cheap kind that dented if you pressed your thumb into it. She carried it out to the loading dock and dropped it in the dumpster, where it clanged against the metal floor and settled next to a garbage bag that had been sitting there long enough to leak something brown.

The prepaid phone was last. She got it from Harry's nightstand — a cheap Samsung flip phone, the kind you could buy at a 7-Eleven for thirty dollars with a prepaid SIM card. Seventeen text messages on it, all from Marcus or to Marcus, the most recent from six weeks ago, the oldest from ten months back. She deleted the messages one at a time, pressing the button with her thumb, watching each one vanish from the screen. Then she turned the phone off and carried it to the loading dock.

She put it on the concrete and stepped on it. The screen cracked but the casing held. She shifted her weight and ground her heel down. The hinge broke with a snap that sounded too loud for the size of the thing. She pried the SIM card out with her thumbnail — it was small and slippery and her fingers were cold — and bent it until it snapped. She dropped the phone casing in one corner of the dumpster and the SIM fragments in another, under the leaking bag.

When she came back inside, her hands smelled like smoke and something chemical — the SIM card's plastic, probably, or the ink from Agnes's notebooks. She washed them at the sink in the medication room with the orange antibacterial soap, the same soap she used twenty times a shift, and washed them a second time, and her hands still smelled like burning.

---

By four in the morning, it was done.

Carmen sat in the TV room. The overhead light was off. The television was off. The room was lit only by the hallway fluorescents coming through the open door, a blue-white half-light that made the vinyl chairs look colorless and the walls look like they were receding. She could hear the building's heating system — a low drone that had been the background noise of her working life for thirteen months — and Burt's snoring carrying down the hall from 116A, and, if she listened, Earl's cough. One cough, then silence, then two more, muffled by the wall and the closed door but audible in the 4 AM stillness of a building where nobody was supposed to be awake.

Harry wheeled himself in at 4:15. He'd changed into his blue cardigan — the good one, the one he wore to every ring meeting, buttoned correctly this time — and Carmen realized only now that it was a superstition, that he'd been wearing this same cardigan to every meeting for fourteen months and she'd noticed without noticing, and that the meetings were over and the cardigan was just a sweater now. He parked his wheelchair by the card table where Agnes used to spread out her notebooks and the ziplock bags and the envelopes of cash. The table was bare. A coffee ring stained the surface — old, from weeks or months ago — and Harry looked at it.

"Done?" he said.

"It's done. Pills, notebooks, phone. Lola's tin."

Harry nodded. His hands were in his lap. Carmen noticed them because Harry's hands were always doing something — calculating, adjusting, squaring the edges of papers, performing the constant small calibrations of a man who ran numbers the way other people breathed. Now they were still. They rested on his thighs, palms down, and they looked like an old man's hands — spotted, thin-skinned, the knuckles swollen with arthritis that he never mentioned because mentioning it would acknowledge a vulnerability that wasn't part of the act or the plan.

"This is over," Carmen said. "No more pills, no more buyers, no more anything."

Harry didn't argue. He'd argued with Marcus — coldly, precisely, with the weight of every number he'd ever counted — but he didn't argue with Carmen. Not tonight.

"It was good while it lasted," he said.

Carmen shook her head. "It wasn't good. It was necessary."

Harry looked at her. The hallway light caught the side of his face — the thin skin over the cheekbone, the pale eyes, the jaw that used to tighten when he was calculating leverage and now just rested in the architecture of his skull. "Is there a difference?"

"Yeah," she said. "There's a difference."

They sat. The building settled around them — the heating system cycling, the squeak of a mattress from someone turning over in a room down the hall, the small mechanical sounds of a facility built in the late seventies and maintained the minimum amount required to pass state inspection. Through the window, the parking lot was dark and empty. Beyond it, the courtyard's three trees stood bare, their branches sharp against the brown suburban sky, all the leaves gone since late October.

"What are you going to do?" Harry asked.

"Go home. Sleep. Come back tomorrow and do my job."

"That's not what I mean."

She knew what he meant. She didn't answer. She pulled her ponytail tighter — it had come loose hours ago, during the loading dock, and strands hung around her face — and adjusted her badge and looked at the empty card table. The coffee ring. The surface where Agnes's notebooks used to open, the drug names in precise columns, the buyer codes in red ink, all of it ash now in a trash can on the loading dock. In a few days the maintenance staff would empty that can and the ash would go to the dumpster and the dumpster would go to a landfill somewhere in Middlesex County, and nobody would ever know that a seventy-six-year-old retired pharmacist had maintained the most careful pharmaceutical records in the state of New Jersey from a shared room in a mid-tier nursing home while pretending she couldn't speak.

"I'm going to check on everyone," she said, and stood up.

"Carmen."

She stopped at the doorway.

Harry was looking at his hands. "Thank you," he said.

She nodded. She left.

Agnes was in bed, awake, staring at the ceiling with her glasses still on. The ceiling was acoustic tile, water-stained in one corner from a leak that maintenance had patched but not painted. Her roommate's snoring was the only sound. Carmen stood in the doorway.

"You okay?"

"Yes."

It was a lie, and Carmen accepted it the way you accept a lie from someone who has earned the right to tell one. She moved on.

Lola's room. Lola was asleep, curled on her right side, the rosary beads wound through her left hand. Her right hand rested on the pillow near her face, and even in sleep the tremor was there — a fine vibration in her fingers that moved the beads against the cotton pillowcase, a faint clicking sound like something small and persistent ticking down.

Earl and Burt in 116A. Burt was asleep, had been asleep through everything — his decline so steady now that the ring ending meant nothing to a man who could no longer remember what the ring was. He was on his back with his mouth open, his wispy white hair pressed flat against the pillow, his long musician's hands slack on the blanket. The Hungarian-English dictionary that used to hold the pill stash was gone — Carmen had moved it to the supply closet months ago, after his fall, and it was still there, hollowed out and empty, a dead artifact of a dead operation. Earl was in the other bed with his eyes closed, but his breathing was wrong — too controlled, too careful, the breathing of a man who was pretending to sleep so nobody would ask him how he was feeling. Carmen stood in the doorway and listened to his breathing and heard, beneath it, the faint wet rattle that had been there for months and was not getting better.

She went back to the nurse's station and did her charting. Overnight rounds: checked on Mr. Kovacs at 11 PM, 1 AM, 3 AM. Checked on Mr. Washington, Mrs. Fernandez, Mrs. Chen. Medication count documented as correct, which it now was, because the medications that would have been short were dissolving in the sewer system beneath the building. She signed the log with the same signature she signed everything — C. Reyes, LPN — and put the clipboard back on its hook.

The front door beeped at 5:48 AM.

Early. The morning shift didn't start until seven, but Tammy always came in early. She said it was because the bus schedule was better before six, and that was partly true, but Carmen had worked here long enough to know the rest of it: Tammy came early because Tammy was the kind of person who showed up, who did the thing, who put on her white sneakers that she replaced every six months when the soles wore through from eight hours a day on her feet at sixteen-fifty an hour and came in before anyone asked her to. She'd been doing it for fifteen years. It was not dedication. It was not passion. It was the specific, unglamorous competence of a woman who did the job right because she didn't know how to do it wrong and nobody was going to pay her enough to make the distinction matter.

Tammy came around the corner in her coat and her scrub pants and stopped when she saw Carmen.

"You're still here."

"Picked up the overnight." Carmen could hear herself, the automatic ease of the excuse. "Devon needed coverage and I was already—"

She stopped. The explanation was too smooth, too practiced, the kind of plausible professional thing she'd been saying for months now, and standing in the nurse's station at ten to six in the morning with her eyes raw and her scrubs smelling like smoke, the smoothness felt like what it was.

"I stayed," she said.

Tammy looked at her. Not casually — the diagnostic look, the scan of a CNA who'd spent fifteen years reading bodies because bodies told you what charts didn't. Carmen knew what Tammy was seeing: the red-rimmed eyes, the loose hair, the exhaustion that went past tired into something structural. The faint smell of smoke on her clothes that the soap in the medication room hadn't fully covered.

Tammy didn't say anything.

She put her bag in her locker. She clipped on her badge and started her rounds, her footsteps going down Wing A — the familiar rubber-soled rhythm, steady and measured, the footsteps of a woman who could change a bed, dress a wound, calm a frightened resident, and chart it all before the next call bell rang, and who did this every day for money that insulted the work.

Carmen gathered her things. She signed out on the overnight log. She put on her coat — it was the same coat she'd worn last November, a black puffy jacket from Old Navy that she'd bought on sale for twenty-nine dollars and that had a broken zipper she kept meaning to fix — and slung her bag over her shoulder and walked toward the exit.

She passed Harry's room.

The door was mostly closed. Lakeview policy required doors to remain openable from the hall, not shut fully, but Harry had pulled it to within an inch. Through the gap, in the strip of visible room, Carmen saw him sitting in his wheelchair by the nightstand. He had something in his hand. A phone — not the prepaid, which was in pieces in the dumpster behind the building, but a different one. A cell phone she had never seen. Small, black, a flip phone like the prepaid but not the prepaid. He was holding it to his ear.

He was talking. His voice was low and Carmen couldn't hear the words from the hallway — just the rhythm, the cadence of short sentences delivered with the controlled precision of his real register. Not the performance. Not the wandering mumble about Eleanor and what day was it and who were these people. The real Harry, at ten minutes to six in the morning, calling someone on a phone that Carmen didn't know existed, saying things she couldn't hear to a person she couldn't identify.

She stood in the hallway for three seconds. She could have pushed the door open. She could have walked in and asked who he was talking to and why there was another phone and why the man who had just sat in the TV room and agreed that it was over was already making calls before the sun came up.

She didn't.

She adjusted her badge. She walked to the front door and pushed through it into the morning. The parking lot was dark and cold and the wind came across it flat, unbroken — the courtyard's bare trees did nothing, and the building didn't block it from this angle. Carmen walked to her car with her coat unzipped because the zipper was broken and her hands in her pockets because they were cold and still smelled like smoke despite the soap, despite the washing, despite the fact that the notebooks and the pills and the phone were all gone now and there was nothing left to smell like except the memory of having done it.

She got in the car and sat with her hands on the steering wheel and the engine off. The dashboard clock said 5:53. In an hour and seven minutes she needed to be at her mother's house to drop off Reggie's lunch, and in two hours and seven minutes Reggie needed to be at school, and in eleven hours she needed to be back here for her regular shift. The math of her life, the same math, the same numbers that didn't change because she'd spent the night flushing pills down a toilet and burning a pharmacist's notebooks on a loading dock. Forty-seven thousand a year. Six-eighty a month in loan payments. Twelve hundred rent. Her license number on every shift log, every count sheet, every controlled substance record at Lakeview Gardens Rehabilitation and Nursing Center.

She started the car. She drove home.
