# Chapter 6

Marcus Webb signed the visitor log at 2:14 PM on a Thursday, printing his name in the same blocky capitals he'd used every week for fourteen months. The girl at the front desk — new, maybe twenty-two, acrylic nails clicking on her phone — didn't look up. He wrote *Lola Fernandez* in the resident column and *nephew* in the relationship column and clipped the visitor badge to his shirt pocket, the laminated edge warm from the previous visitor's body heat.

He knew this building the way a surgeon knows a body he's opened before. The turn past the activities room where three women sat in wheelchairs watching a cooking show at full volume, the host shouting about risotto while one woman slept and another picked at the vinyl armrest with her thumbnail. The smell shifting as he passed the kitchen — today it was some kind of fish, the institutional version where the breading was thicker than the fillet. The hand sanitizer dispensers every fifteen feet, most of them empty or jammed. He'd reported the empty dispensers twice when he worked here. The second time was part of the complaint.

Lola's room was 112A, shared with Betty Ostrowski, who had severe vascular dementia and spent her days facing the window. Betty's daughter had taped photographs to the wall beside her bed — grandchildren, a dog, a house with a flag out front — but Betty looked through them the way she looked through everything, her eyes tracking something the rest of them couldn't see.

"Mijo!" Lola said when he came in, and her face did the thing it always did, this warm crumpling that made her look ten years younger. She was sitting in the vinyl recliner with a magazine in her lap, *People* from three months ago. Her left hand trembled against the armrest. Some days the tremor was barely there. Today it was visible from the doorway.

He bent down and kissed her cheek. She smelled like the coconut lotion Tammy helped her put on in the mornings, and underneath that, the warm close smell of someone who lived in a room that never fully aired out.

"You look skinny," she said. "You eating?"

"I'm eating fine."

"You look skinny. Sit, sit." She waved at Betty's chair, which Betty wasn't using because Betty was in the dining room, or the hallway, or wherever the aides had wheeled her for the afternoon. Marcus sat. The chair's cushion was split along one seam, foam poking through. He'd put in a maintenance request for that chair nine months before he was fired. It was the same chair.

"How's your week been?" he asked, because this was how it always started — fifteen minutes of visit, of being Lola's nephew who came on Thursdays, of the performance that was also not entirely a performance because she was, in the ways that mattered, the closest thing he had.

"Ay, you know," Lola said. "Same. The chicken on Tuesday was okay. Wednesday they gave us something they called a casserole, but I don't know what was in it. I asked Tammy and she said, 'Mrs. Fernandez, I wouldn't eat that if I was on death row.' Tammy's funny. She's — she's having a hard time, mijo. The husband."

"Ex-husband."

"Ex-husband, right. She doesn't say much but I can tell." Lola touched the gold crucifix at her throat, a gesture so automatic Marcus wasn't sure she knew she did it. "Anyway. I saved you some cookies."

She said this loud enough for the hallway. Marcus smiled.

The cookie tin was on her nightstand, between a framed photograph of Tomás and a plastic cup of water with a bendy straw. It was a blue Danish butter cookie tin, the kind you could buy at any dollar store, and Lola kept it stocked with actual cookies on top — pecan sandies from the commissary, three or four, enough to make the tin look right if anyone glanced inside.

Below the cookies, separated by a folded paper towel, were four ziplock bags. Agnes's handwriting on each bag in black Sharpie: the drug name, milligram, count, expiration date. Oxycodone 5mg x 24. Alprazolam 1mg x 30. Tramadol 50mg x 18. Metformin 500mg x 40. The bags were flat, pressed carefully, no air. Agnes packed them like she was sealing evidence, which, Marcus supposed, she was.

He picked up the tin with one hand, popped a pecan sandie in his mouth with the other. "Lola, these are good."

"The ones from the commissary aren't bad. Not like the ones I used to make. Tomás liked the ones with the — what's it called, the almond flour. I can't get almond flour here."

"I'll bring you some."

"You don't have to do that."

"I want to."

She looked at him with that expression she got sometimes, where the warmth turned into something sharper, something that saw him more clearly than he liked. "You're a good boy, Marcus."

He was forty-four years old and she called him a good boy and it still landed somewhere in his chest that he couldn't defend against. He put the tin in the canvas tote bag he'd brought — the bag also contained a paperback western he'd gotten for Earl, a sleeve of Ritz crackers Harry had requested, and a pair of reading glasses from the dollar store that Lola had asked for last week because her prescription pair had a broken arm.

"I gotta get going," he said. "But I'll call you tonight, okay?"

"Okay, mijo. Drive safe. It's hot out there."

It was. Early September and the parking lot asphalt radiated heat through the soles of his sneakers, the air thick and chemical-smelling from the strip of Route 1 traffic that ran past Lakeview's entrance. His car was a 2009 Honda Accord with 187,000 miles and a passenger window that didn't go all the way up, leaving a quarter-inch gap that let in exhaust fumes and highway noise. The AC worked on the first and third settings but not the second. He put it on third and sat for a moment with his hands on the wheel, the tote bag on the passenger seat.

He talked to himself in the car. He'd always done this — his mother used to say he came out of the womb in mid-conversation — but it had gotten worse since she died, or maybe not worse, just more noticeable because there was nobody else to talk to.

"Okay," he said. "Rosa first, then Derek, then Janine. Rosa's off at three, so she'll be there by three-thirty. Derek said he's coming straight from work." He checked the prepaid phone — a Nokia flip phone from 2019 that Harry had insisted on because it couldn't be hacked, which Marcus didn't think was true but didn't argue about because you didn't argue with Harry about operational details. One text from Derek: *running 15 late*. One from Rosa: a thumbs-up emoji, which was the only emoji Rosa used.

The strip mall was twenty minutes south on Route 1, past the used car lots with their flapping pennants and the storage facility where Marcus had kept his mother's furniture for three months before admitting he couldn't afford the unit and donating everything to Goodwill. A half-dead stretch of retail anchored by a Dollar General and a nail salon. The parking lot was big enough for the Kmart that used to be there and now sat empty, its sign removed, leaving a ghost outline on the facade. Marcus parked in the back corner, near the dumpsters, where two other cars were already waiting.

He knew these people. Not the way he'd known his patients at Lakeview — their blood pressure readings, their fall risk scores, their allergies — but in the way you knew anyone you saw twice a month in a parking lot and talked to for ten minutes about their lives while handing them medication the system had decided they couldn't afford.

Rosa Gutierrez got out of her car first. She was fifty-two and she moved like she was seventy, because that's what lupus did when you couldn't afford to manage it properly. She'd been on hydroxychloroquine for eight years — Plaquenil, the brand name, four hundred milligrams daily — and it had kept the joint inflammation and the fatigue and the kidney complications from getting worse. Then she'd lost her job at a dry cleaning plant in Perth Amboy when the plant closed, and with the job went the insurance, and without the insurance the hydroxychloroquine cost her $412 a month at CVS. Four hundred and twelve dollars. She'd done the math once for Marcus: her unemployment was $387 a week. The medication alone would eat more than a quarter of that.

She'd tried the manufacturer's patient assistance program — they rejected her because she had an ex-husband whose income technically disqualified her, even though she hadn't seen him in six years and he paid nothing. She'd tried the generic from a Canadian pharmacy, but the website she found was fraudulent and she'd lost two hundred dollars. Marcus charged her fifteen dollars a bottle. Agnes made sure the dosage and manufacturer were consistent each time.

"Hey, Rosa." He got out with her bag — a single ziplock, metformin and hydroxychloroquine, the hydroxychloroquine added from a separate stash Agnes maintained because it wasn't a controlled substance but it was expensive and Rosa needed it.

"Marcus." She took the bag and put it in her purse without looking inside. She'd checked the first few times. She didn't check anymore. "How's the — how's everyone?"

"Good. Everyone's good." He didn't tell her who supplied the pills or where they came from. She hadn't asked. There was an etiquette to this, a mutual agreement not to examine the thing too closely.

Rosa handed him an envelope. Sixty dollars — two months' worth, because she'd missed last time when her car broke down. She'd written *thank you* on the outside of the envelope in pencil, and Marcus looked at the words and then put the envelope in his back pocket and said, "You good on refills? You got enough for the month?"

"Yeah. The metformin, I can get some of that at Walmart for cheap, but the Plaquenil—" She stopped. She had a way of stopping mid-sentence when she was about to say something that embarrassed her about her own situation. "Yeah, I'm good."

"All right. You need anything else, you text me."

"I will. Thank you, Marcus."

She drove away in a Nissan Sentra with a dent in the rear quarter panel and a child's car seat in the back. Granddaughter, Marcus thought. Or maybe niece. He didn't ask.

Derek pulled in twelve minutes later, still in his work clothes — navy polo with a logo Marcus didn't recognize, khakis, steel-toed boots that didn't match the polo. He was thirty-four and type 1 diabetic and had been since he was eleven. His insulin was covered by the insurance he got through his warehouse job, but the metformin for his secondary glucose management wasn't, and his deductible was three thousand dollars a year that reset in January, which meant that for the first four months of every year he was paying full price for everything that wasn't insulin. He'd been coming to Marcus for five months.

"Yo, what's up." Derek leaned on Marcus's car. He was one of those guys who stood close when he talked, not aggressive, just close, like he'd grown up in a small house with a lot of people and had never recalibrated his sense of distance. "Hey, question. You got any — you know what, forget it."

"What?"

"Nah, it's — my wife's got migraines. Bad ones. Like three, four times a month. Her doctor gave her some shit that doesn't work and she can't go back for three weeks because the office is booked solid. She's been using my mom's leftover Imitrex but my mom's got like four pills left."

Marcus shook his head. "I don't have sumatriptan. Sorry, man. I can ask, but I can't promise anything."

"Yeah, no, I figured. Just thought I'd—" Derek waved it off. "It's all good."

Marcus handed him the ziplock. Metformin, forty pills, which would get him through most of the month. Derek handed him cash — thirty dollars — and they stood there for a minute in the heat, two men in a parking lot, the dumpsters baking in the sun.

"How's the job?" Marcus asked.

"It's a job. They cut our hours last month, so I'm doing some DoorDash on the side. My wife's pissed because I'm never home, but — you know." Derek looked at the ziplock in his hand. "Shit's fucked, man."

"Yeah."

"Like, I'm not even — I'm not trying to game anything. I just need my meds. That's it. That's the whole thing. I need my meds and I can't afford my meds and here we are in a parking lot."

"Here we are."

Derek got back in his car — a Ford F-150 that was too much truck for a guy whose hours had been cut, but Marcus understood that too, the financial decision you made when things were better that became the chain around your ankle when things got worse. Marcus had a CNA certification and a decade of elder care experience and he made twelve hundred a month selling medication to people who needed it out of a parking lot behind a dead Kmart.

Janine was last. She pulled in at 4:10, late, in a station wagon with a handicap placard hanging from the rearview. Janine Coates, sixty, retired from a packaging plant in Edison where she'd worked for twenty-three years. She'd injured her back on the line — L4-L5 disc herniation, confirmed by MRI — and the company's workers' comp had covered her treatment for two years, including the oxycodone prescription that let her sleep and function. Then the claim was settled, the comp ended, and her doctor — Dr. Kaplan, she always said his name like she was reading it from a wanted poster — got nervous about the DEA prescription monitoring program and tapered her off.

She'd tried everything. Physical therapy, which worked but cost her $40 a session with her Medicare supplement, three times a week. Gabapentin, which made her dizzy and didn't touch the pain. Epidural injections, which worked for three weeks and cost her $800 out-of-pocket per shot. Yoga, at the suggestion of a pain management doctor who spent nine minutes with her and billed $350 for the visit.

She was not an addict. Marcus had worked in a nursing home for seven years and he knew what addiction looked like — the urgency, the negotiation, the dose creep, the inability to talk about anything else. Janine wasn't that. Janine was a woman whose back hurt every day and who needed thirty milligrams of oxycodone to make the pain manageable enough to cook dinner and sleep and take her dog for a walk. That was the whole story, and the healthcare system had decided it was too dangerous a story to accommodate.

"Sorry I'm late," she said through her window. "Traffic on Route 1 was a mess."

"No worries." He handed her the ziplock — oxycodone 5mg, twenty-four tablets, which was exactly what Dr. Kaplan used to prescribe before his courage failed. She handed him an envelope. A hundred and twenty dollars. He didn't count it because Janine always had the exact amount and counting it in front of her would have been rude. She also had two other envelopes in the passenger seat, which were bills, which Marcus could see because the envelopes were windowed and one of them was red.

"You doing okay, Janine?"

"My dog needs his teeth cleaned. They want four hundred dollars. For a dog." She shook her head. "I told the vet, I said, I can't afford to get my own teeth cleaned."

"What kind of dog?"

"Beagle mix. He's thirteen. He's a good dog but his breath could knock out a horse." She almost smiled. "I'll see you in two weeks?"

"Two weeks."

She drove away. Marcus sat in his car and counted the money: sixty from Rosa, thirty from Derek, a hundred twenty from Janine. Two hundred and ten dollars. His cut of the larger operation — the portion Harry tracked with his accountant's precision — came to roughly twelve hundred a month, counting all the buyers, not just these three. The twelve hundred covered rent on his basement apartment in North Brunswick, a space he'd found on Craigslist that turned out to be exactly as bad as a nine-hundred-dollar-a-month Craigslist basement apartment in central Jersey would be: low ceilings that he hit with his knuckles when he stretched, a window at ground level that showed him feet and car tires, a bathroom with a shower that drained slow enough that he stood in two inches of water by the time he was done. The remaining three hundred covered gas, food, the phone bill, and whatever was left over, which was nothing.

He drove home. Past the strip mall, past the Wawa where he sometimes bought a hoagie for dinner, past the apartments on Livingston Avenue that were nicer than his. He'd been a CNA at Lakeview for seven years. Not a nurse, but the person who did the work nurses charted about — the lifting, the turning, the cleaning, the feeding, the human contact that kept people from going entirely to seed. He'd been good at it. Not performatively good, not award-ceremony good, but quietly good in the way that mattered: he knew which residents liked the lights on at night and which didn't, who needed their pillow at a certain angle for their GERD, who got agitated if you approached from the left side, who needed to be talked to and who needed to be left alone.

He'd filed the complaint in June 2024 because Wing A was running with one aide for twenty-two residents on the overnight shift, when the state minimum was one for every ten. Devon — the same Devon who now slept at the nurse's station every night — was covering both wings alone, which meant that if two residents needed help at the same time, one of them waited. And in a nursing home, waiting meant lying in your own piss, or lying on the floor after a fall, or lying in bed with your oxygen cannula twisted and your sat dropping. Marcus had found Mrs. Patterson on the floor of her bathroom at 4 AM, her hip already swelling, her call light tangled in the bedsheets where she couldn't reach it. She'd been there for an hour. That was the night he wrote the complaint.

He'd submitted it through the proper channels — Cliff Moorehead first, who'd thanked him for his concern and done nothing, and then to the state ombudsman's office when nothing changed. The state sent an investigator. The investigator found the staffing levels compliant because Lakeview had filed paperwork showing a second aide assigned to each shift, a woman named Marjorie Sims who was on the schedule but did not, as far as Marcus could tell, exist as a physical presence in the building between the hours of midnight and seven. When Marcus pointed this out, Cliff called him into the office and told him his "attitude" was "creating a hostile work environment" and that his "insubordination" — filing a state complaint without going through internal channels first, which Marcus had done, and Cliff knew he had done — was grounds for termination.

He'd been fired on a Tuesday. He'd cleaned out his locker, which contained a spare pair of scrub pants, a jar of Icy Hot, and a photograph of his mother that he'd taped to the inside of the door. He'd walked out past the residents he'd spent seven years caring for, past Harry's room where Harry sat in his wheelchair performing confusion, past Lola's room where Lola watched him go with her hand over her mouth, and he'd driven home to tell his mother he'd lost his job.

His mother was sick by then. Congestive heart failure, diagnosed eight months earlier, managing on a cocktail of medications that Marcus had to help her organize because the dosing schedule was a puzzle only a pharmacist should have to solve — lisinopril in the morning, furosemide twice daily, metoprolol at bedtime, potassium supplements because the furosemide depleted it, warfarin with biweekly blood draws to check the INR. She'd died in her apartment four months later. Not at a hospital, not at a hospice, not surrounded by the well-funded machinery of institutional death. She'd died in her bed, in the apartment above a dry cleaner on Hamilton Street, with Marcus next to her and the television on because she liked the noise and he couldn't stand the silence. He was holding her hand when the breathing changed and he knew what the breathing meant because he'd heard it before, in rooms at Lakeview, that wet irregular slowing that every CNA recognizes. He sat with her for an hour after, because he didn't know what else to do, and then he called 911.

Lola had called the next day. She'd somehow gotten his cell number — Marcus still didn't know how — and she'd said, "Mijo, I heard. I'm sorry." She was the only person from Lakeview who called. Not Cliff, not any of the nurses, not any of the other aides. Lola, from her room at the facility that had fired him, called to say she was sorry about his mother.

The ring had found him three months later. Harry had sent word through Lola: Marcus should visit. Marcus had been doing odd jobs — warehouse temp work, a landscaping crew for two weeks until his knees gave out, a night stocking job at Target that paid eleven-fifty an hour — and he'd gone to Lakeview on a Thursday during visiting hours and signed in as Lola's nephew and sat in Lola's room while Harry, who Marcus had spent years believing was a confused old man who missed his dead wife, laid out a business proposition with the calm, itemized precision of a man who'd spent forty years making numbers do what he wanted.

Marcus's apartment was dark when he got home. The basement meant the light was always wrong — too dim in the daytime, too harsh when he turned on the overheads at night. He had a couch from Goodwill, a television on a plastic crate, a kitchen that was a counter and a hot plate and a mini-fridge. His CNA certification hung on the wall in a frame his mother had bought him. She'd been proud of that certificate. She'd told everyone — her friends at church, the woman at the dry cleaner, the guy who ran the bodega — that her son was in healthcare.

He ate a bowl of cereal standing at the counter because he didn't have a kitchen table and the couch was too low to eat from without spilling. Then he sat on the couch and took out the prepaid phone and called Lola.

She picked up on the second ring. He could hear the television in her room — the news, or a game show, one of those sounds that was just Lakeview's ambient hum.

"Everything went good," he said. "Rosa, Derek, Janine. All three. No problems."

"Good, good." She was quiet for a moment, and he heard the slight rasp of her hand against the phone case. The tremor made it hard for her to hold the phone steady some days. "Agnes wants to know about the metformin count. She said you should have forty left for next time but she wants to be sure."

"Forty went to Rosa and Derek. I'll double-check but I'm pretty sure we're short. I might need fifty next cycle."

"I'll tell her. She'll do the math." Another pause. "Mijo, how's your mom?"

His throat closed. It did this every time, a physical thing, like someone had their thumb on his windpipe. Lola asked about his mother every week. She'd been asking for fourteen months. He'd been lying for fourteen months.

"She's good," he said. "She's got her — her heart stuff, you know, but she's doing okay. She's watching her shows."

"Tell her I said hi. Tell her she raised a good boy."

"I'll tell her."

He didn't know why he couldn't say it. He'd said the words plenty of times to other people — *my mother passed away*, the phrase people used, the clean clinical phrase that kept the conversation moving. But Lola asked with such specific tenderness, such genuine interest in a woman she'd never met, that the lie felt like the only thing Marcus could offer in return. If Lola knew his mother was dead, she'd grieve for him, and he couldn't watch that. He couldn't watch a seventy-nine-year-old woman in a nursing home grieve for his forty-four-year-old loss when she had her own dead husband and her distant children and her trembling hands and the performance she maintained every day just to have a reason to be alert. He lied to protect her from carrying his grief on top of her own, and he lied because saying it would make the Thursday visits something different — would make him the one being taken care of, when the whole point of coming was that he was useful.

"You eat dinner?" Lola asked.

"Yeah. I had — yeah, I ate."

"Cereal isn't dinner, Marcus."

He laughed, a small, involuntary sound that surprised him. "How'd you know?"

"Because I know you. Go make something real. There's rice, you can make rice. Everybody can make rice."

"I'll make rice."

"With something. Rice with something."

"Rice with something. I promise."

He could hear her smile through the phone. "Okay. Goodnight, mijo. Be safe."

"Goodnight, Lola."

He sat on the couch after she hung up. The apartment was quiet except for the boiler in the utility closet on the other side of the wall, which ticked and rumbled at irregular intervals like a mechanical heartbeat with an arrhythmia. Upstairs, his landlord's television murmured. The ceiling was low enough that he could hear the floorboards creak when the landlord's wife walked to the kitchen.

He did not make rice. He sat there with the prepaid phone in his hand and the envelope of cash on the cushion beside him and the CNA certificate on the wall and the low ceiling pressing down and the sound of someone else's life happening above his head, and he thought about Mrs. Patterson on the bathroom floor, and about his mother's hand going slack, and about Janine's red envelope and Rosa's penciled *thank you* and Derek standing in a parking lot saying *shit's fucked, man*, and about Lola's voice asking him to eat something real, and he didn't move for a long time.
