# Chapter 14

The resident in Room 110 had soiled herself again by seven-thirty, which made three times that shift. Tammy rolled her onto her left side, stripped the pad, cleaned her with the efficiency of someone who had done this particular sequence of motions several thousand times in fifteen years, and replaced the linens while the woman stared at the wall and made the low, steady sound she always made — not quite a moan, not quite a hum, somewhere in the register of a radiator that couldn't be fixed. Tammy told her she was almost done. She told her she was doing fine. She balled up the soiled pad and dropped it in the hamper, and the smell — warm, biological, cut with the chemical bite of the barrier cream — followed her into the hallway and sat in her scrubs for the rest of the night.

That was the job. Not the job description, not the intake brochure with the courtyard photo and the woman doing a puzzle in natural light that didn't exist anywhere inside the actual building. The job was bodies. Lifting them, turning them, cleaning them, checking the skin that broke down when nobody turned them fast enough. The job was answering the call bell from Room 104 and knowing before she got there that Mr. Delaney wanted the TV turned up because his hearing aid battery was dead and nobody had ordered replacements since September. The job was Mrs. Okafor's three-times-a-shift incontinence and the fact that there was no CNA coverage on the other wing tonight because Shayla had called out and Cliff hadn't approved the overtime to replace her, which meant the overnight aide was going to inherit forty-two residents with one pair of hands, and he'd handle it the way Devon always handled it, which was to fall asleep at the nurse's station by midnight and let the call bells ring.

Tammy had been at Lakeview Gardens for fifteen years. She'd outlasted four administrators, six Directors of Nursing, and more CNAs than she could count. She'd watched the facility change owners twice — first from the family who built it to a regional chain, then from the chain to a private equity group in Connecticut whose name she'd seen on a memo exactly once. Each time, the carpets got older and the staffing got thinner and the lobby got a new motivational poster, and the work stayed the same because the work was always the same. People got old. Their bodies failed. Someone had to be in the room when it happened.

She clocked out at eleven-oh-three. The night air hit her in the parking lot like a hand on her chest — late October, that particular New Jersey cold that wasn't dramatic enough to feel like weather, just a persistent damp chill that crawled under her jacket and stayed. The trees in the courtyard were nearly bare. She could see the last few leaves on the middle one from where she stood, dark shapes against the parking lot lights. By next week they'd be gone.

Her car started on the second try. She sat in it with the heater on, waiting for the vents to stop blowing cold, and looked at the building. Lakeview at night was a low, flat shape with lit windows scattered randomly — some residents kept their lights on all night, some rooms were empty, the nurse's station glowed steady at the center like a pilot light. She'd been looking at this building almost every night since she was twenty-eight. She could identify which wing had the bad fluorescent tube by the flicker pattern. She could tell from the parking lot whether the overnight nurse was at the central station or the sub-station by which window was lit. These were not useful skills. They were just what happened when you stayed somewhere long enough.

The drive home took twelve minutes. The studio apartment was on the second floor of a converted house in Dunellen, above a woman named Grace who played Christian radio at volumes that suggested either devotion or hearing loss. The apartment was four hundred and seventy-five dollars a month, first and last, no security deposit because the landlord couldn't get one — the previous tenant had kept cats. Not a cat. Cats. Plural. The carpet remembered them. Tammy had tried baking soda, enzyme spray, a rented steam cleaner. The smell lived in the pad underneath, and the landlord wasn't replacing the pad for four seventy-five a month, so the smell stayed, and Tammy had learned to stop noticing it by the third week and then to notice it again every time she came home after being away for eight hours. She noticed it now, stepping inside, the particular ammonia-musk that wasn't exactly cat piss anymore but wasn't exactly not cat piss either.

She dropped her bag by the door. She kicked off her shoes. The apartment was one room plus a bathroom so small the door hit the toilet when she opened it. Her things were here — the couch from the old apartment that Kevin had let her have because it had a stain on the middle cushion, the TV she'd bought at Walmart, the bed she slept in alone. Kevin had the house. Kevin had the house and the good furniture and a woman named Trish or Tricia who worked at the Verizon store in Bridgewater and was thirty-one, which Tammy knew because she'd looked her up once on a night when she'd finished a bottle of Yellow Tail and hated herself the entire time she was doing it but did it anyway. Trish-or-Tricia had blond highlights and ran 5Ks. Her Facebook was public. Tammy had closed the laptop and poured the rest of the wine down the sink, which was the correct thing to do, and then she'd fished the bottle out of the recycling and checked if there was actually any left, which was the honest thing to do.

Kevin had filed. That was the fact of it. Twelve years of marriage, no kids because they'd kept saying next year until next year became a thing neither of them mentioned, and he'd filed on a Tuesday and she'd come home from work — from changing Mrs. Okafor and answering Mr. Delaney's call bell and doing the work that paid sixteen dollars and fifty cents an hour — and the papers were on the kitchen table with a Post-it note that said "Sorry, T." Sorry, T. Twelve years distilled into a Post-it note and an initial, like she was a task he'd finally gotten around to completing.

She'd signed. There wasn't anything to fight about. He made more money, but not enough to owe her anything significant. They'd split the checking account, which had fourteen hundred dollars in it. She'd gotten seven hundred. Seven hundred dollars and a stained couch and a studio apartment that smelled like cats.

Tammy sat on that couch now and ate a bowl of cereal because it was eleven-thirty at night and she wasn't going to cook. The milk was close to turning. She ate it anyway. She watched half an episode of something on her phone — a show about people renovating houses, buying houses, people with budgets of four hundred thousand dollars looking at crown molding and saying things like "it's not really us." She turned it off.

Sixteen fifty an hour. Thirty-three thousand a year before taxes if she worked full-time, which she did, every week. After taxes, insurance that covered almost nothing, and the union dues that Kevin used to call a waste but that Tammy paid anyway because the union was the only reason she'd survived the last two ownership changes without being fired, she cleared about twenty-two hundred a month. Rent took four seventy-five. Car payment, two forty. Insurance. Gas. Phone. Food. She was not one crisis away from not making it. She was already not making it, and had been not making it for so long that not making it had become the baseline, the thing she organized her life around, the way other people organized their lives around mortgages or retirement plans.

She washed the bowl. She brushed her teeth. She got into bed and stared at the ceiling, which had a water stain in the corner shaped like nothing — it wasn't shaped like anything, it was just a stain, and the fact that she noticed it every night and tried to make it into something was the kind of thing that happened when you lived alone in a room and there was nothing else to look at.

Fifteen years at Lakeview. She had started at twenty-eight, fresh off the CNA certification course at the county college, and she'd thought it would be temporary. A year, maybe two, while she figured out what she actually wanted to do. She'd been good at it. She was still good at it. The job rewarded a specific kind of competence — you had to notice things. Skin changes. Breathing changes. The difference between a resident who was quiet because they were sleeping and a resident who was quiet because something was wrong. You had to know who ate and who didn't, who was losing weight, who had a UTI brewing before the lab work came back because you recognized the confusion and the smell. Tammy noticed all of these things. She noticed them the way a mechanic notices engine sounds — automatically, without deciding to, because the alternative was not noticing, and not noticing in this job meant someone got hurt.

She'd seen two residents die on her shifts. One had been expected — a woman on hospice who'd been declining for weeks and went at four in the morning with her daughter holding her hand, which was the version people imagined when they thought about dying in a nursing home. The other had been a man who choked on a piece of chicken at dinner, turned blue, and died on the dining room floor while Tammy did the Heimlich and another aide called 911 and three other residents watched and one of them kept eating. She'd been twenty-nine for that one. She'd gone home and sat in Kevin's car — their car, then — and cried until her nose ran and her eyes swelled shut, and then she'd gone back the next day because she had a shift and somebody had to work it.

She'd seen staff fired for stealing cash from residents' drawers. She'd seen a night nurse passed out drunk in the medication room. She'd seen families visit once after admission, sign the paperwork, and never come back — not once, not for Christmas, not when the facility called to say their mother had fallen or their father had stopped eating. She'd seen the good aides leave because the pay was an insult, and the bad aides stay because nowhere else would have them. She'd seen Cliff Moorehead walk through the building at survey time like he gave a damn, and she'd seen him the rest of the year, which was to say she hadn't, because Cliff spent his time in his office with the door closed, watching census numbers on a spreadsheet.

What fifteen years taught you was that nothing in this building was what it looked like. The clean hallways smelled like disinfectant because underneath the disinfectant was something worse. The cheerful activity calendar on the wall — Chair Yoga, Movie Afternoon, Arts & Crafts — existed for the state surveyors, not the residents. The residents who smiled when families visited sometimes cried when families left, and the residents who seemed angry or vacant sometimes had the clearest sense of what was happening to them.

Tammy knew all of this. She knew it in her hands and her back and her knees, which ached now in a way they hadn't when she was twenty-eight. She knew it and she kept working because the work was real, even when everything around it wasn't.

She lay in bed and thought about Carmen Reyes, who had been acting strange for weeks.

---

It had started — or Tammy had started noticing, which might not be the same thing — around the time of the state survey back in September. Before that, Carmen had been the way she always was: competent, professional, a little tired, good with the residents. She did her job well. Tammy respected that. In a building where most people were either burned out or coasting, Carmen showed up and did the work, and she did it without the martyr energy that some nurses brought, the look-how-much-I-care performance that made Tammy's teeth itch. Carmen just worked. She charted. She did her med passes on time. She called families when she was supposed to call families. She was, in Tammy's fifteen-year assessment, one of the better nurses Lakeview had managed to keep.

But something had shifted. Carmen stayed late now — not double-shift late, which she'd always done for the money, but a different kind of late. She'd finish her charting and then linger, walking the halls, checking on rooms that didn't need checking. She spent time with certain residents. Harry Zielinski in 114A. Agnes Chen in 108A. Lola Fernandez in 112A. Not quick med-pass visits but actual time, with the door closed, which wasn't standard. You closed the door for private care — toileting, bathing, wound changes. You didn't close the door for a chat.

And Carmen had gotten evasive. Not obviously — she was too smart for that. But when Tammy asked her how things were going, Carmen's answers had developed a lag. A half-beat of recalibration before she said "Fine" or "Same old" or "You know how it is." Tammy knew how it was. That was the problem. She knew exactly how it was, and the way Carmen was acting wasn't how it was.

Then there were the medication counts.

The controlled substance count had been off three times since Tammy started paying attention. Three oxy here, five alprazolam there, always documented as charting errors, always explained by the kind of systemic excuse that worked because the system really was that broken — the overnight staff didn't count right, the hybrid system dropped entries, the pharmacy shorted the delivery. These explanations were plausible. Tammy had heard them for fifteen years. They were plausible the way everything at Lakeview was plausible: individually reasonable, collectively suspicious, and nobody with the authority to investigate had the energy to bother.

During the state survey, Tammy had been in the hallway outside the conference room when Carmen was interviewed. She hadn't been trying to listen. The walls were thin and she was restocking the supply cart ten feet away. She'd heard Carmen explain the count discrepancies to the surveyor — calmly, professionally, with the specific competence of a nurse who knew which excuses the state would accept. Overnight staff. Documentation lags. System migration errors. Carmen's voice had been steady and practiced, and the surveyor had moved on.

The explanations were good. They were, in fact, too good. Tammy had given a lot of explanations over the years — to surveyors, to families, to administrators. She knew what a real explanation sounded like. It sounded uncertain. It sounded annoyed. It sounded like someone who didn't know the answer and didn't like not knowing. Carmen's answers had sounded like someone who knew exactly what the answer was and had prepared a different one.

Tammy didn't do anything with this observation. She filed it away the way she filed everything — next to the dying man on the dining room floor and the nurse passed out in the med room and the families who never came back. It was information, and it wasn't her problem. She was a CNA making sixteen fifty an hour with no authority to investigate anything and no reason to believe anyone would listen if she did.

But then she noticed Agnes Chen.

Agnes had come to Lakeview three years ago, after a stroke. Tammy remembered because she'd been on shift the day Agnes arrived, and Agnes had been furious. Not confused, not docile, not any of the things the intake paperwork suggested. Furious. She'd argued with the admissions coordinator. She'd demanded cognitive testing. She'd written letters — Tammy had seen her at the desk in the common room, filling legal pads with small, precise handwriting, addressed to the ombudsman's office, to her daughter, to the state. She'd called her daughter Helen and said things Tammy could hear from the hallway — clear, articulate, blistering things about autonomy and rights and the fact that a temporary speech deficit from a stroke did not constitute cognitive impairment.

Then, about six months after admission, Agnes stopped. She stopped writing. She stopped arguing. She stopped talking, mostly. She sat in her chair and stared at the wall and responded to direct questions with silence or single syllables. The staff noted the change as expected decline. Post-stroke depression, probably. Cognitive deterioration. These things happened. Tammy had noted it too, at the time.

But she'd worked this wing long enough to know what decline looked like, and this wasn't it. Decline was gradual. Decline was Mrs. Okafor, who'd lost language in a slow erosion over eighteen months — first the complex sentences, then the simple ones, then the words, then the sounds that used to be words. Agnes hadn't eroded. Agnes had stopped. Like a switch.

And now, weeks into whatever was bothering Tammy about Carmen's behavior, she'd started watching Agnes more carefully. Not watching — she didn't have time to watch anyone, she had forty-two residents and half the aide coverage she was supposed to have. But noticing. The way she noticed skin changes and breathing patterns. And what she noticed was Agnes's eyes.

Agnes sat in her chair in 108A and stared at the wall. But when people talked near her — when Carmen stopped in the doorway, when Tammy herself came in to check her water pitcher, when the physical therapist documented her "refusal to engage" — Agnes's eyes moved. They tracked. They followed the conversation the way a person followed a conversation they understood. Not the random scanning of someone confused. Not the fixed gaze of someone withdrawn. Agnes was listening. Agnes was comprehending. And she was doing neither of those things when the doctors came through or when Cliff made his rare appearances.

That was the thing Tammy couldn't let go of. Not the medication counts, which might genuinely be charting errors. Not Carmen's evasiveness, which might be personal — the woman had a kid and money problems and a hundred reasons to be distracted. But Agnes Chen's eyes didn't lie. You couldn't fake the direction of attention. You couldn't decide to track a conversation with your pupils while simultaneously being too impaired to understand it. The eyes either followed or they didn't, and Agnes's eyes followed.

And once Tammy let herself see that, she saw Harry.

Harry Zielinski in 114A, diagnosed with moderate-to-severe dementia, wheelchair-bound, asked about his dead wife Eleanor at least four times per shift. He answered Dr. Prasad's orientation questions wrong every two weeks when Prasad came through — wrong day, wrong month, wrong president, the answers wandering through a timeline that didn't exist. He needed help eating. He needed help transferring. He was, according to every assessment in his chart, exactly the kind of resident who required total care and would continue declining until he didn't require anything at all.

But Tammy had worked evenings with Harry for years, and she'd noticed something she hadn't thought much about until now. His confusion wasn't constant. It was variable. He was most confused during Dr. Prasad's visits, during the state survey, during Cliff's quarterly walk-throughs. He was least confused in the evenings, during the quiet hours when the building thinned out and the day staff went home. She'd seen him in the hallway at nine-thirty one night, supposedly on his way to the bathroom, and he'd looked at her — right at her, steady, no confusion in it — and said "Good night, Tammy" in a voice that had nothing wrong with it. Then he'd gone into his room and closed the door, which was a level of motor coordination that didn't match his chart.

She'd let that go too. She let everything go. That was the skill she'd spent years developing — the ability to notice things and then not do anything about them, because doing something meant reporting to Cliff, who would either panic and generate paperwork that went nowhere or ignore her entirely, and either way the thing she'd noticed would continue happening and she'd have wasted her time and whatever small amount of institutional goodwill a CNA accumulated by keeping her mouth shut.

But the observations were adding up. Carmen's changed behavior. The count discrepancies. Agnes's eyes. Harry's variable confusion. Lola Fernandez, who chattered warmly and seemed confused but who Tammy had once seen, during a fire drill six months ago, move with calm efficiency to the exit — not the shuffling uncertainty of someone disoriented but the purposeful movement of someone who knew exactly where she was going and needed to get there. Tammy had been helping another resident at the time and only caught it from the corner of her eye. She'd dismissed it. Adrenaline. Even confused people could surprise you in emergencies.

She didn't dismiss it anymore.

Tammy didn't go to Cliff. She didn't go to anyone. She'd learned a long time ago that the only person in this building who would take her observations seriously was herself. She decided to watch.

Not actively — she didn't have the time or the energy for a stakeout or whatever cops called it. She just kept doing her job and kept her eyes open, which was something she'd always done anyway. The difference was that now she was directing her attention instead of diffusing it. She was looking at specific people. She was noting times. She was paying attention to which rooms Carmen visited and for how long and whether the door was open or closed.

On a Thursday in late October, Tammy finished her shift at eleven. She clocked out, said good night to the overnight nurse — a travel nurse named Priya who was competent but unfamiliar with the residents and would be gone in six weeks — and walked to her car. The parking lot was dark. It had been dark since five. The air was cold enough that her breath showed, and the bare trees in the courtyard looked like the kind of thing someone would photograph in black and white and call art.

She sat in her car. She turned the heater on. She should have driven home. There was nothing keeping her here except the studio apartment that smelled like phantom cats and the silence she'd go back to and the twelve minutes of driving that separated this parking lot from that silence. She sat.

She wasn't doing anything. She was sitting in her car in a parking lot, and that wasn't suspicious or strange; people sat in cars in parking lots all the time. She checked her phone. Nothing. Kevin hadn't texted in three weeks, which was fine because they had nothing to text about, but the absence of his name on her screen was its own kind of presence, the way a missing tooth was a presence. She scrolled through nothing. She looked at the building.

Lakeview at night. She knew every window. The nurse's station glow. Room 110's light, because Mrs. Okafor's overhead stayed on around the clock — nobody had asked Mrs. Okafor if she wanted it off, and Mrs. Okafor couldn't tell them. A few other windows. The building settled into its overnight shape, the shape she'd memorized from all those years of leaving it at this hour — most lights off, the central station holding steady, the hallways dimmed to their half-brightness.

Eleven-fifteen. Eleven-thirty. She should go home. She was tired. Her lower back ached from the lifting, and her knees had that deep, specific soreness that ibuprofen touched but didn't fix. She opened her car door to get out and drive home.

She didn't get out. She pulled the door shut again. She didn't know why. Or she did know why, and she wasn't ready to call it what it was.

Eleven-forty-five. She watched Priya walk past the nurse's station window, chart in hand. Normal. Midnight. The building was still. She could see the faint glow of Devon's phone at the aide's desk near the entrance — he'd be asleep within the hour if he wasn't already.

Twelve-fifteen. Her heater ticked. Her back hurt. She was a forty-three-year-old woman sitting alone in a parking lot in the dark because she didn't want to go back to an apartment that smelled like cats and she couldn't stop thinking about the way Agnes Chen's eyes tracked conversations she wasn't supposed to understand.

Twelve-thirty.

A light came on at the far end of Wing A. The TV room. It was the farthest common area from the nurse's station, around the L-shaped corner, not visible from the aide's desk or the central station. Tammy knew this because she knew the geometry of its blind spots the way she knew the geometry of her own apartment — not because she'd studied it but because she'd lived inside it long enough that the knowledge was physical.

The TV room light was on. At twelve-thirty in the morning. In a wing where every resident was supposedly asleep or supposed to be, according to the care plans and the overnight check schedule that Devon was supposed to follow and didn't.

Tammy watched the window from her car. The light stayed on. She could see movement — not clearly, not faces, just the suggestion of shapes behind the glass, which meant someone was in there. More than one person, maybe, though she couldn't be sure from this distance and angle.

She stayed for another ten minutes. The light stayed on. The shapes moved.

She started her car. She drove home. The twelve minutes passed the way twelve minutes passed when your mind was somewhere else — she registered the turns and the stop signs and the left onto her street without actually seeing any of it. She parked. She went upstairs. The cat smell greeted her like it always did, and she stood inside the door with her jacket still on and her keys in her hand.

She didn't know what she'd seen. She didn't know what it meant. She knew the TV room light was on at twelve-thirty in the morning, and she knew that Agnes Chen's eyes followed conversations, and she knew that Harry Zielinski said good night in a voice that belonged to a man who didn't have dementia, and she knew that Carmen Reyes was staying late and closing doors and explaining things with the smooth readiness of a person who had practiced the explanation before she needed it.

Tammy took off her jacket. She hung it on the hook Kevin had installed four apartments and a marriage ago, the one piece of hardware that had followed her out of that life. She went to bed. She didn't sleep for a long time.

She'd go back. Not tomorrow — she had a day off, and she needed the day off, her body needed it. But after. She'd go back and she'd watch again, and she'd keep watching until the pieces she'd collected added up to something she could name. She wasn't going to tell Cliff. Cliff would do nothing useful with the information, and even if he did, she'd be the one who'd reported it, and fifteen years in this building had taught her what happened to people who reported things: they became the problem. Marcus Webb had reported the understaffing, and Marcus Webb didn't work here anymore, and the understaffing was worse than ever.

She'd watch. She was good at watching. It was maybe the only thing she was still good at, the one skill that fifteen years and a divorce and sixteen fifty an hour hadn't worn down to nothing. She could see what was in front of her, and she could remember what she'd seen, and she could wait.

The water stain on the ceiling looked the same as it always did. Tammy stared at it until her eyes adjusted to the dark, and then she stared at it some more, because the dark didn't change what was there. It just made you work harder to see it.
