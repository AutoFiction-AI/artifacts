# Chapter 5

Carmen waited until Devon left the station.

He did this every night around twelve-thirty — walked to the kitchen, ate whatever the dietary aide had left out, came back in twenty minutes smelling like butterscotch pudding. She'd never had a reason to track his schedule before. Tonight she had timed it on her watch, standing at the sub-station pretending to chart, marking the moment he pulled out his earbuds and shuffled toward the kitchen in his untied Nikes. Twelve thirty-one. She gave him two minutes' head start.

The hallway was dim at this hour, half the fluorescents off, the others buzzing at a frequency she could feel at the base of her skull. Wing A stretched ahead of her — a single corridor of closed doors and the faint ammonia smell that never fully left the linoleum, no matter how many times the overnight aide mopped. The AC had been struggling all week. Late August in central New Jersey and Lakeview Gardens was holding heat like a kiln, the building's cinder-block walls radiating warmth back into the hallways hours after dark. She could feel it through the soles of her sneakers.

She carried her clipboard. She didn't need it — she wasn't charting, wasn't rounding, wasn't doing any of the things a clipboard authorized her to do at twelve-thirty in the morning. But the clipboard was habit, the same way the badge clipped to her scrub top was habit. The tools of a nurse who belonged in this hallway at this hour. Cover.

She passed Room 110 and heard Mrs. Delacroix making her sounds — not words, not cries, just a low continuous murmur that rose and fell like breathing with a voice attached. Carmen had checked on her at eleven. She was fine, or she was the same, which was what fine meant in Room 110.

The TV room was at the far end of the wing, past the supply closet and the handicapped bathroom, the farthest common area from the nurse's station. Not visible from Devon's desk. That was the point — the ring's point, she understood now, the reason they met here and not in someone's room.

The door was open. The television was off. Harry sat in his wheelchair at the card table, his back to the hallway, a newspaper folded in his lap. The only light was the green-white glow of the emergency exit sign above the far door, casting the room in a color that made everything look submerged.

Carmen stepped inside and closed the door behind her. The latch clicked.

"Mr. Zielinski." She stayed standing. "I need to talk to you."

Harry didn't move. His head was tilted forward the way it always was when he was sleeping — chin to chest, mouth slack, the posture of a man who'd lost track of the room and the hour and possibly the year. His breathing was slow, wet, heavy. A thin shine of drool on his chin.

"Harry."

She used his first name. She had never done that before — not in thirteen months of med passes and evening check-ins and gentle redirections when he wandered toward the fire exit asking for Eleanor. He was Mr. Zielinski, the way all her residents were formal names, because the formality was part of the care and Carmen did not cut corners.

He held the performance for two more seconds. She counted — one one-thousand, two one-thousand — watching for the seam, the place where the act would start to peel. She'd seen him break character once, in the hallway, and it had been fast, a light switch. But that had been accidental, a caught moment. This was something else.

Harry lifted his head. His spine straightened — not dramatically, he was eighty-four and his back didn't snap to anything, but the slouch was gone, the way a folded thing unfolds when you pick it up and shake it out. His shoulders squared against the back of the wheelchair. His chin came up. And his eyes, which she'd watched drift and search and lose focus a thousand times during med pass, locked onto her face with a precision that was almost physically uncomfortable. Like a flashlight pointed directly at her when she'd been expecting candlelight.

He wiped the drool from his chin with the back of his hand. One clean motion. Not fumbling, not patting, not the tentative dabbing she'd watched him perform over pill cups and dinner trays. A man cleaning himself up because the costume was off and he didn't need the prop.

"Close the other door," he said.

His voice was different. Not just coherent — she'd heard him coherent, the tramadol correction, the hallway at 2:40 AM — but running on a completely different frequency. The confused Harry spoke in fragments, rising intonations, questions that dissolved before they finished. This Harry's voice was flat and level. Each sentence ended like a period looked: hard, complete, closed.

"It's closed," Carmen said.

"The other one." He nodded toward the far wall, where a second door led to the back hallway. She hadn't noticed it was cracked open — two inches, enough for sound to carry.

She crossed the room and pulled it shut. When she turned back, Harry had folded the newspaper and placed it on the card table. She could see the crossword from here. Completed, in ink, in handwriting that was small and square and precise — nothing like the shaky scrawl in his chart, the trembling Harold Zielinski who couldn't remember how to spell his own last name for the intake assessment.

He waited. He didn't ask why she was there. He sat in his wheelchair with his hands folded on the table, his faded blue cardigan pulled tight across his narrow chest, and he waited the way she imagined he'd waited across a desk from clients for forty years — the patient silence of a man who already knew the number and was letting you arrive at it yourself.

Carmen stayed standing. Her hand went to her badge and she caught it, pulled it back.

"I know what you're doing," she said.

"Okay."

"The medications. I ran a full manual count last night — you're forty-seven pills short across four controlled substances. Oxycodone, alprazolam, tramadol, and hydrocodone." She heard herself falling into med-pass language, the clinical vocabulary that made everything procedural and clean. She stopped. "You're stealing pills. You and the others. Agnes, Lola, Earl — I've seen the meetings, Harry. The TV room, after midnight. I've seen you walk. Not shuffle, not lean on the rails, not — walk. You're not impaired. You're not confused. You're running something."

Harry's expression didn't change. He didn't deny, didn't flinch, didn't offer the scrambling panic she'd half-expected. He looked at her the way she imagined an accountant looked at a balance sheet that was coming up short — with attention, not surprise.

"Sit down," he said.

"I don't want to sit down."

"Then stand." He pulled the vinyl chair nearest to him away from the table, angling it toward her. The metal legs scraped on the linoleum, a sound that in the daytime would have been nothing but at this hour cut through the quiet like something tearing. "But you're going to want to hear this sitting."

Carmen sat. She didn't decide to — her legs made the decision, or the heat made the decision, the TV room's heavy warmth pressing on her like a hand. The chair exhaled under her weight. Old vinyl, cracked along the arms, the cushion held together with facility-issue tape that had gone amber and stiff. She could feel the split upholstery through her scrubs.

"How long," she said.

"Eighteen months. Give or take."

"How many of you."

"Five. Plus distribution."

"Names."

"You know the names."

She did. She'd known since the hallway, since the TV room at 1 AM weeks ago when the animated voices had gone slack at the sight of her, since the count sheets that kept coming up short on her shifts and nobody else's. She knew. She was asking him to say it because saying it made it something she could document, respond to, process through the professional channels that were supposed to exist for moments like this.

"Why are you here, Carmen?"

"Because forty-seven pills are missing and they were dispensed under my license number."

"I know that. That's not what I asked."

He watched her. His pale eyes — she'd always thought of them as watery, unfocused, the eyes of a man looking for someone who wasn't in the room. They weren't. They were sharp and light and specific, the color of old ice.

"Why are you here. In this room. Talking to me."

Carmen pressed her palms flat on her thighs. The fabric of her scrubs was damp — the room's heat, or her own, she couldn't tell.

"Because you need to understand what you've done to me. Not to yourself. Not to whoever's buying. To me." She leaned forward. Her voice was getting quieter, which was what happened when she was truly angry — she didn't get louder, she got smaller, more exact, as if she could control the situation by controlling the volume. "Every pill that went missing came through my med pass. My name on the count sheet, my license number in the dispensing log. If the state board pulls my records — if the DEA looks at Lakeview's controlled substance tracking — I'm the one they see. Not you. You're an eighty-four-year-old man in a wheelchair. They'll put you in front of a judge and the judge will see a confused old man who doesn't know what day it is and they'll give you probation, they'll give you supervised care, they'll write it up as a facility failure. But me?" She stopped. Swallowed. Her hand found the badge again and this time she didn't pull it back — she turned the clip between her fingers, felt the sharp edge of the laminate. "I go to prison. I lose my license. I lose my job. I lose my son."

The room was quiet enough that she could hear the ventilation system laboring two hallways away. The AC compressor kicked on and off in cycles — she'd timed those too, during long overnight shifts with nothing to count but the intervals between mechanical sounds. Right now it was off, and the air in the TV room sat heavy and still.

Harry hadn't moved. He listened the way he waited — with patience that felt less like kindness and more like calculation, a man letting the numbers come in before he responded.

"You done?" he said.

"Don't talk to me like this is a board meeting."

Something shifted in his face. Not softness — Harry's real face didn't seem built for soft. Acknowledgment, maybe. The recognition that a number was real.

"Fair enough," he said. "You're scared. I hear that. But you need to hear me now, because the next thing you do depends on whether you understand what you're actually looking at."

"I'm looking at a federal crime."

"You're looking at five people." He counted them on his fingers, not for drama but the way he counted everything — precisely, by the unit. "Me. Agnes. Lola. Earl. Burt. Burt's eighty-eight. He's got actual dementia — not like me, actual. Agnes is seventy-six with stroke residuals. Lola has Parkinson's. Earl's an eighty-one-year-old veteran." He stopped there, the way he'd stopped before when he'd started to say something about Earl. Whatever it was, he kept it. "We live here. We don't leave."

"That doesn't change what you did."

"It changes what happens if you report it." He pressed his hands flat on the table. She could see his fingers clearly in the exit sign's light — long, thin, steady. She'd watched those same hands tremble over a medication cup, shake while reaching for a glass of water, fumble a spoon in the dining room. They weren't shaking now. "You call the police. The state. The DEA. Pick one. What happens."

"They investigate."

"They investigate. And then they charge. Possession with intent to distribute — Schedule II controlled substances, which means federal mandatory minimums. For five nursing home residents. One of whom can't tell you what year it is." He paused. "You've seen courtroom photos, Carmen. Five elderly people in a courtroom. Agnes weighs a hundred and ten pounds, she can't lift handcuffs. Lola's hands shake. Burt won't understand where he is. The optics are a prosecutor's nightmare and a headline writer's dream. Elderly Drug Ring Busted. Great story. Terrible case."

"So your plan is — what, look pathetic?"

"We don't need a plan for that. To the system, we ARE pathetic. That's the whole architecture." He shifted in the wheelchair. His left knee popped — she'd heard it before, always the left, and now she wondered how long he'd been performing the wince that went with it when actually it was just a knee. "But here's the part that matters to you. The facility gets investigated. Cliff Moorehead's running a nursing home where five residents operated a distribution network for a year and a half under his nose. Lakeview gets cited, potentially shut down. A hundred and twelve other residents — people who had nothing to do with this, people who are actually impaired, actually dependent — get relocated."

He let that settle.

"You know where they go," he said.

"Wherever has beds." Carmen heard herself say it flatly, without inflection, because she did know. She'd worked per diem at three other facilities before Lakeview. She knew what wherever-has-beds looked like — the places that smelled worse, where the call bells rang three times before anyone answered, where the food was Sysco institutional and the medication errors weren't careful accounting like this but genuine neglect.

"Wherever has beds," Harry repeated. "That's on you. If you report."

"That's not fair."

"No."

They sat with that. The institutional clock on the wall — round, white-faced, never accurate — read twelve-fifty-one. Carmen could hear, very faintly, Mrs. Delacroix's murmuring from down the hall. A continuous sound, almost restful if you didn't think about what it meant.

"What do you actually want?" Harry said.

She opened her mouth. She didn't know. That was the problem — she'd come in here with a fact, with forty-seven pills and a count sheet and a license number, and she'd expected the fact to tell her what to do. The fact didn't tell her anything. The fact just sat there, as specific and unhelpful as a number on a ledger.

"I want it to stop," she said.

"All of it. You want me to flush the stockpile, shut down the operation, and then — what. The count stabilizes. You go back to charting normally. Except." He held up a finger. "The discrepancies you've already documented. Those don't go away. Those are on file — your name, your shift, your signature. The forty-seven pills you're short right now? Already in the system. The three oxycodone from — what, six weeks ago? Five alprazolam the week after? You flagged those. You documented them. You moved on."

Carmen's stomach shifted. She'd been so focused forward — what else could go missing, what else could go wrong — that she hadn't looked at the trail behind her. The count sheets she'd already signed. The discrepancies she'd reported to the charge nurse and then accepted the charge nurse's dismissal of. Months of documentation that showed, at minimum, sustained failure to catch what was happening on her shifts under her license.

"Even if we stop tonight," Harry said, "you're exposed. Any audit, any investigation, any routine review of dispensing records — they see the pattern. Chronic shortages on your shifts. Documented and signed. That's not a charting error. That's diversion. And it happened under your name."

"So you're telling me I'm already screwed."

"I'm telling you that calling the police doesn't make you less screwed. It makes you more visible." He watched her. The exit sign's glow made his face look carved from something pale and cold, but his eyes were alive in a way that the face didn't match — working, calculating, reading her the way he'd read a spreadsheet, looking for the place where the numbers moved. "Give me a week."

"No."

"One week. Let me show you the whole picture. The buyers, their situations. Where the money goes. Who depends on it. Then you decide."

"I've already decided."

"No you haven't."

His voice was quiet. Not gentle — quiet the way a number on a final balance is quiet, because it doesn't need to be loud, because it's just the answer and the answer doesn't shout.

"You already decided, or you'd have called the police instead of coming to talk to me."

Carmen's hands stopped moving. She'd been turning the badge clip and now her fingers were still, holding the little rectangle of laminate with her photo and her name and her license number, the thing she'd spent two years and thirty-eight thousand dollars earning the right to clip to her chest.

He was right.

She had come here. Not to Cliff's office, not to the phone in the break room where the state board hotline poster hung, the one that said IF YOU SUSPECT MEDICATION DIVERSION, CALL, in bold red letters she'd read a hundred times without registering. She had come to the TV room. At midnight. To talk to an eighty-four-year-old man who was not what he pretended to be, and she had closed the door behind her, and she had sat down in the vinyl chair when he told her to. None of that was what a nurse who'd already decided to report would do. A nurse who'd already decided would have picked up the phone. Would have documented, called, followed the protocol. Would not be sitting in a warm dark room having a conversation that, she realized now, she could not tell anyone about without incriminating herself further.

"One week," she said.

It came out rough, forced through something in her chest that didn't want to let it pass.

"One week. You show me whatever you think I need to see. Then I decide. And nothing moves in that week — no pills sorted, nothing packaged, nothing goes out. The operation stops while I'm looking at it."

Harry considered this. She could see him running the math — not the moral math, the logistical kind, what a week's pause would do to inventory, to timing, to whoever was on the other end waiting for supply. His face didn't change while he calculated. It was like watching the outside of a machine.

"Okay," he said.

Carmen stood. Her scrubs peeled away from the chair's vinyl with a sound like tape pulling off skin. She picked up her clipboard from the floor where she'd dropped it — she hadn't noticed dropping it, didn't remember the moment, which bothered her because Carmen noticed things, that was the whole problem, she noticed too much.

"We had some trouble with the pharmacy a few years back," Harry said.

He said it the way you'd mention last year's weather — a fact, not a confession. Carmen had her hand on the door.

"The facility switched suppliers. Cost savings. Half our prescriptions got delayed or substituted. Agnes's pain medication got swapped for something that didn't work. I spent three weeks on Tylenol for a hip that needed oxycodone." He folded his hands on the newspaper. "That's not the whole story. But that's where it started."

"I didn't ask how it started."

"I know," Harry said. "But you will."

Carmen pulled the door open. The hallway's half-light poured in, fluorescent and thin, and for a second the TV room's green glow looked like something she was stepping out of — not a room but a condition, a place with different rules. She walked out without looking back.

---

She sat in her car in the parking lot with the engine off and the windows down.

August didn't let go at night. The air was the same weight at one-fifteen in the morning as it had been at noon — heavy, wet, sitting on every surface like an animal that had lain down and wouldn't move. She could smell the dumpster from her parking spot: the sweet-rot undertone that got worse in the heat, layered over the chemical tang of asphalt softening in its third month of summer. Crickets somewhere in the landscaping strip between the lot and the road, loud enough to be irritating, persistent enough to become background.

She replayed the conversation. Not all of it — her mind kept catching on specific moments, circling back to them the way a tongue goes to a chipped tooth. Harry's back straightening. His eyes sharpening. The drool wiped away like greasepaint after a performance. The crossword completed in ink.

The crossword. He sat in the TV room every evening during visiting hours and stared at the newspaper, and she'd thought — everyone thought — that he was looking at the pictures, or that the newsprint was comforting in some vague sensory way, or that he didn't know what a newspaper was anymore. He'd been doing the crossword. In ink. For eighteen months.

She thought about his voice. The real one. She'd spent thirteen months listening to a man ask for Eleanor in a voice that wandered and broke and couldn't hold a full sentence, and under it, the whole time, was this — this flat, precise, completely controlled instrument that counted things and ended conversations and said words like *margin* and *architecture* and *visible*. The voice she'd heard tonight was the voice of a man who had never been confused about anything in his life, or at least not about this.

Carmen turned her badge over in her fingers. The laminate was peeling at one corner — she'd been meaning to request a new one, but new badges cost eight dollars at the HR office and eight dollars was lunch for two days or the difference between hitting the electric bill and carrying it over. The photo was from two years ago. She'd been heavier then. Her hair was longer. She looked, in the photo, like a woman who still believed the badge meant something simple — competence, authority, the right to be in the building and do the work. It didn't mean that anymore. Or it did, but it also meant something else now, something she couldn't clip to her chest and point to.

Her phone buzzed. She'd forgotten she had it — she must have grabbed it from the medication room on the way out. Her mother: *You okay mija? Reggie had bad dream, he's asleep now. Don't worry. Call me tomorrow.*

The text was forty minutes old.

Carmen pressed her forehead against the steering wheel. The vinyl was warm and slightly gritty — the accumulated film of summer, pollen and road dust and whatever came off her hands after twelve-hour shifts. She thought about the forty-seven pills. She thought about her license number on every count sheet, her signature, the careful documentation she'd been trained to maintain because documentation was protection, because if you charted it you could point to it and say *I did my job, I followed the protocol*. Except now the documentation was a trail. The trail led to her. The protection had become the map.

She thought about Reggie having a bad dream and her mother getting up at midnight with her bad hip to sit on the edge of his bed and say *estoy aqui, mijo, estoy aqui* until he stopped crying. Because Carmen wasn't there. Because Carmen was in a parking lot outside a nursing home trying to figure out whether she was still a nurse or had become something else.

She had come to negotiate. The word arrived in her mind fully formed, unambiguous, and she couldn't push it back. She had walked into that room with terms she hadn't articulated even to herself — conditions, limits, a framework for some arrangement she couldn't have described if someone had asked her. She hadn't come to report. She hadn't come to threaten. She'd come to negotiate, and Harry had seen it before she had, because Harry counted things, and what he was counting tonight was her.

*You already decided, or you'd have called the police.*

She could still call. She could call right now. Her phone was in her hand, her mother's text still glowing on the screen. The state board hotline was a 1-800 number printed on a poster she'd walked past twice a shift for thirteen months. She could call and say: *my name is Carmen Reyes, I'm an LPN at Lakeview Gardens, and I need to report a medication diversion involving five residents.* She could say those words. They existed. They were available to her.

She put the phone face-down on the passenger seat.

One week. She'd given Harry one week, and she'd framed it as a concession — a measured period of evaluation, the kind of reasonable professional response a responsible nurse might offer before taking action. But she knew, sitting in the heat with the dumpster smell and the crickets and the grit of the steering wheel against her forehead, that the week wasn't a deadline. It was a door, and she was already through it.

She started the car. The AC coughed to life and pushed air that was warm, then tepid, then something slightly cooler than the world outside. She pulled out of the lot with the windows still down, the moving air not cool but at least moving. In her rearview mirror, Lakeview was a low shape against the sky, a few lit windows — the station where Devon would be settling back in with his earbuds, the night-lights in the corridor, the exit sign's glow in the TV room where she'd left a man who was already planning what to show her.

On the passenger seat, her phone sat face-down. Her mother's text unanswered. The steering wheel hummed under her hands. She drove.
