# Chapter 4

The charting system at Lakeview was a hybrid — half-digital, half paper — that nobody had fully committed to in either direction. Carmen sat at the Wing A sub-station at 2:37 AM, entering vital signs from her 1 AM rounds into the computer while the paper backup waited on a clipboard beside her. The terminal was old enough that it hummed. The screen cast a blue-white wash over her hands and the laminate counter, which was peeling at the corner where someone had picked at it during hundreds of similar overnight hours.

She'd taken the double for the same reason she always did. The car insurance was due in nine days and she was $140 short. The overnight differential was $3.50 more per hour, which meant the sixteen extra hours bought her the gap plus gas money if she didn't eat out. She hadn't eaten since the turkey sandwich from the vending machine at 10 PM, and she could feel it — not hunger exactly, but a flatness in her concentration, a lag between noticing something on the screen and understanding what it said.

The building made its night sounds around her. The HVAC cycled on with a low shudder she felt in the counter surface before she heard it. Room 110 — Mrs. Okafor, ninety-one, end-stage COPD — produced the same thin, rhythmic moan she'd been producing for the seven months Carmen had worked this wing, a sound Carmen had stopped hearing the way you stop hearing a refrigerator. A toilet flushed somewhere in B Wing, probably Devon using the staff bathroom, which meant he was at least awake, which was an improvement over last Tuesday when she'd found him face-down on his folded arms at the nurse's station with a podcast about cryptocurrency playing through one earbud.

Carmen finished the vitals entry for Room 112 — Lola Fernandez, blood pressure 128 over 76, pulse 72, temp 97.8 — and tabbed to the next field. Her eyes were dry. The building's air was always dry, but at night the dryness concentrated, and by 3 AM every shift her contact lenses felt like they'd been sitting on a dashboard in July.

She'd been thinking about the pill count. Not constantly, not the way a person worries at a problem, but the way a splinter works — you forget about it and then you move your hand wrong and there it is. Three oxycodone, five alprazolam. The charge nurse's dismissal. Lola's mouth opening for the pill check with the smoothness of someone who'd practiced the motion. The voices in the TV room — four, five people talking in complete, coherent sentences at one in the morning, and then silence and slack faces the moment she appeared in the doorway.

She couldn't make the pieces add up to anything she was ready to name. The count was wrong, and the patients were wrong, and those two facts pointed in a direction she didn't want to follow because following it meant paperwork and phone calls and an investigation that would land on her shifts, her counts, her license number on the dispensing log.

She clicked save on the vitals screen and stretched her neck. The sub-station sat at the elbow of Wing A's L-shape, which meant she could see down the short leg of the hall — six rooms, all doors closed except 118, whose occupant, Mr. Petrelli, insisted on sleeping with his door open because he'd been a fireman and believed in egress — but the long leg, where the sicker residents and the singles were, angled out of sight past the linen closet.

Movement registered in her peripheral vision before she consciously looked. A shape in the long hallway, just past the angle.

She turned her head.

Harry Zielinski was walking.

Not the shuffle she'd guided through a hundred med passes, the halting, uncertain progress of a man whose legs didn't quite know where to go and whose hands groped for walls and chair rails. Not the arthritic, listing gait she'd documented in his mobility assessment, which she'd done herself three months ago and which rated him as requiring assistance for ambulation.

He was walking. Upright. His spine was straight, or as straight as eighty-four years would allow — a natural curve, not a hunch. His feet moved with the deliberate spacing of a man who trusted his knees, each step placed and lifted, heel to ball. He was carrying something in his right hand, wrapped in a white hand towel from the bathroom, held close to his thigh the way you carry something you don't want noticed.

He was thirty feet away. The hallway was at half-light — every other fluorescent panel was on, the facility's overnight energy-saving measure that turned the corridors into alternating bands of pale light and weak shadow. He moved through them steadily.

Carmen's hands were on the keyboard. She didn't move them. She watched him the way she watched a patient whose vitals were wrong — not with alarm yet, but with the full weight of her attention.

Harry reached the intersection where the long hallway met the short one, where the sub-station was, where she was sitting in the blue-white light of the monitor with her name badge clipped to scrubs she'd been wearing for twelve hours, and he looked up and saw her.

He stopped.

They looked at each other across the linoleum.

It lasted three seconds. In the first second, Harry's face was open — unguarded, the face of a man who'd been alone in a hallway doing what he needed to do, and the expression on it was ordinary irritation at being interrupted, the look you'd give someone who walked in while you were on the phone. In the second second, she saw him recognize what she was seeing and what it meant. His eyes, which she'd spent a year documenting as confused and intermittently oriented, were clear and sharp and reading her the way she'd been reading vitals all night — taking data, making calculations, reaching conclusions. In the third second, his jaw set, and something crossed his face that she would later decide was resignation.

Then he put his left hand on the wall. His weight shifted. His shoulders dropped forward into the familiar hunch, and his knees bent slightly, and his right foot turned inward, and she watched him rebuild the performance from the ground up, piece by piece, like an actor stepping back into costume — the posture first, then the grip loosening on the towel-wrapped object, then the face going slack, the eyes losing focus, the lower lip dropping.

"Eleanor?" he said. His voice was cracked and querulous, the voice she heard every shift. "Is that you? Eleanor, I was looking for — where did you — the bathroom. I was trying to find the bathroom."

Carmen stood up. Her badge swung against her chest and then settled.

"It's Carmen, Mr. Zielinski." Her voice came out the way it always did with residents — steady, clear, warm enough. "Let me help you back to bed."

She crossed the thirty feet between them. The linoleum was cold through her sneakers, the way it always was at night when the HVAC ran low. She could hear his breathing now — not the labored, confused breaths of a disoriented man, but normal breathing, even and controlled, held slightly shallow the way someone breathes when they're trying not to give anything away.

She took his arm. His forearm was thin — she could feel the bones through his skin and the sleeve of his pajama shirt — but underneath the thinness there was tension, muscle engaged and holding. This was not the arm of a man who needed her help standing. This was the arm of a man who was allowing her to hold it because the script required it.

They walked back down the long hallway together. His feet dragged now, scuffing the floor in the shuffle she'd seen a thousand times. He leaned into her, his weight practiced and precise — enough to seem like dependence, not enough to actually require her strength. She could feel the difference the way you could feel a patient faking a range-of-motion test. The muscles were doing the wrong thing. When someone genuinely couldn't support their weight, the lean was dead weight, gravity and collapse. Harry's lean was a performance — she was supporting tension, not mass.

The towel-wrapped object was pressed between his arm and his body. She didn't look at it directly.

"Here we go, Mr. Zielinski. Back to bed."

"Eleanor makes the bed," he said, mumbling. "She always makes the bed. I can't — the corners. She does the corners."

She walked him into Room 114A and guided him to the bed. He sat on the edge, and she lifted his legs — one at a time, the way you do, hand under the knee, supporting the calf — and swung them onto the mattress. His legs were lighter than they should have been for a man who'd just been walking unassisted, and she understood that this, too, was part of it: he was going limp the way a competent person goes limp, deliberately, with control over the limpness itself.

She pulled the blanket to his chest. The room smelled like him — the warm, stale scent of a body that didn't get outside enough, skin that didn't ventilate, clothes that sat in the same dresser for weeks. Under that, the sweet-sour note of medications processed through aging kidneys. Under that, the universal Lakeview baseline: floor cleaner, industrial soap, the faint ammonia ghost that never fully left any room where someone lived in a diaper.

"Good night, Mr. Zielinski."

He'd closed his eyes. His face was slack. The towel-wrapped object was tucked against his side under the blanket, and his hand rested on it, and she could see the knuckles, and the knuckles were white.

She stood in his doorway for a moment. The hallway was empty and half-lit and silent except for Mrs. Okafor's distant moaning and the HVAC cycling down to its lowest register, a sound like the building exhaling.

She looked at the man in the bed, and she thought about the night three shifts ago when she'd heard clear voices in the TV room, and she thought about the pill count, and she thought about the day he'd corrected her on the tramadol dosage — two-fifty, not two hundred, said with the quick certainty of someone who'd been keeping track — and every separate anomaly she'd been collecting clicked into a pattern so obvious she couldn't believe she'd ever considered the alternatives.

She went back to the sub-station and sat down. The vitals screen had gone to sleep. Her reflection stared at her from the dark monitor — tired eyes, hair escaping the ponytail, the particular gray look that twelve hours of fluorescent light put on brown skin.

She sat there for eleven minutes. She knew it was eleven minutes because when she looked at the computer clock it said 2:52, and when she stood up it said 3:03, and in between she didn't think about anything clearly, just sat with the knowledge that was still settling in her the way sediment drops through water — not fast, not clean, just the slow accumulation of something heavy reaching the bottom.

The medication room was off the central nurse's station, between the two wings. It was the size of a large closet — one door, no window, a locked cabinet bolted to the wall, a counter with a sink, and a plastic chair nobody sat in because there was barely room for it. The room smelled like rubbing alcohol and the fine, dry dust that came off pill bottles when you handled hundreds of them.

Carmen locked the door behind her, which was protocol for controlled substance counts. She pulled the keys from her hip — the medication key was the small brass one on the ring with seven others, and she could find it by touch at this point, the way you could find your house key in the dark.

She opened the cabinet. The controlled substances were on the top two shelves, each in its own labeled container, each with a count sheet clipped to the front. The count sheets were simple: date, shift, count, initials. Two signatures per shift — outgoing nurse and incoming nurse, verifying the same number. Carmen's initials were on most of the evening entries. The overnight nurse's initials — Maria or Dolores or whoever they'd pulled from the float pool that week — were on the rest.

She started with the oxycodone. Five milligram tablets, prescribed to four residents on Wing A and two on Wing B. She removed the container, opened it, and counted. She counted slowly, touching each tablet with the tip of a tongue depressor the way she'd been taught in pharmacology, pushing each pill from one side of the tray to the other as she counted it. Eighty-six tablets should have been in this container according to the last verified count plus pharmacy deliveries minus dispensed doses.

She counted sixty-seven. She counted again. Sixty-seven.

Nineteen short.

She wrote the number on the back of a count sheet and moved to the alprazolam. One milligram tablets, prescribed to three residents. Expected count: one hundred forty-four. Actual count: one hundred twenty-eight.

Sixteen short.

The tramadol was next. Fifty milligram capsules, four residents. Expected: eighty-eight. Actual: eighty.

Eight short.

She opened the hydrocodone container. Ten milligram tablets, two residents. Expected: thirty-six. Actual: thirty-two.

Four short.

She stood at the counter with the numbers on the back of the count sheet. Nineteen, sixteen, eight, four. Forty-seven pills.

The ventilation hummed above her. She could hear, faintly through the closed door, the distant rhythm of Mrs. Okafor and the building settling into its own weight the way old buildings did at night, a creak in the ceiling every few minutes that sounded like someone shifting in a chair.

Forty-seven pills across four controlled substances. Not concentrated in one medication, which would suggest a single patient hoarding or a single staff member with a preference. Spread across the formulary in a pattern that looked, to Carmen's eye, like distribution rather than personal use. Someone was taking a variety because someone had a market for a variety.

She checked the count sheets again. Her own initials appeared at the start of each evening shift, and at the end, the numbers matched — she'd counted correctly every time. Which meant the pills were leaving between her departure and the start of the next verified count. Overnight. When Devon slept through his shifts and the hallways belonged to whoever was awake in them.

She thought about Harry's walk. The posture, the stride, the clear eyes. She thought about the rolled towel pressed against his leg. The practiced ease of his return to performance — how quickly the lucid man dissolved into the confused one, how complete the transformation was, as if he'd been doing it long enough that the muscles knew the routine without instruction.

The tramadol correction from her first week surfaced. Two-fifty, not two hundred. He hadn't been confused. He'd been counting. He'd been keeping inventory, and the correction had slipped out the way Carmen herself might automatically flag a blood pressure that was ten points off from where she expected it — a reflex, professional, involuntary. He'd caught himself and gone back to asking about Eleanor, but the number had been exact, and exact numbers didn't come from confused minds.

Forty-seven pills. Dispensed under her license. Missing from her shifts.

She put the containers back in the cabinet. She locked it. She folded the count sheet and put it in her scrub pocket, then took it out, unfolded it, looked at the four numbers she'd written, and put it back.

She unlocked the medication room door and stepped into the nurse's station. Devon's phone was propped against the monitor now, the screen showing a man in a garage talking about engine rebuilds. Devon was tipped back in the office chair with his mouth slightly open, not quite asleep but not registering her either.

Carmen walked past him. She went to the supply closet, got a fresh set of vitals equipment she didn't need, and carried it back to the Wing A sub-station, where she sat down and set it on the counter beside her. She didn't open it.

She knew what forty-seven pills meant. She knew what the Controlled Substances Act said about diversion and what the New Jersey Board of Nursing said about a licensed practical nurse whose medication counts didn't reconcile and what a prosecutor would say about a pattern that extended across multiple substances over multiple shifts. She knew, with the specific clarity of someone who'd sat through the pharmacology module on diversion three times — once in school, once in orientation, once in the annual competency review — that the pills were documented under her name, dispensed under her license number, counted under her initials, and that the gap between what should be in that cabinet and what was in that cabinet was her problem regardless of who had actually moved them.

She sat at the sub-station until 4 AM, when she made her next scheduled rounds. She checked on every resident in Wing A. Most were sleeping. Mrs. Okafor was moaning. Mr. Petrelli was snoring through his open door with one foot hanging off the mattress, and she tucked it back under the sheet without waking him. In Room 112, Lola Fernandez was curled on her side with one hand under her cheek and the other wrapped around her gold crucifix, and she looked like someone's grandmother, which she was.

In Room 114A, Harry Zielinski was on his back with the blanket pulled to his chin and his eyes closed, and he looked like a frail old man dreaming about his dead wife, and Carmen stood in his doorway and watched his chest rise and fall and knew that he was awake and that he knew she was there and that neither of them was going to say a word about it.

She finished her rounds and went back to the sub-station. She had seven hours left on her shift. She charted, answered two call bells, helped Devon turn a patient at 5 AM, and drank the coffee she'd brought in a thermos that was now cold and tasted like the inside of the thermos. The folded count sheet stayed in her pocket. She touched it once, through the fabric, the way you touch a cut to check if it's still bleeding.

The hallway stayed empty. Dawn came slowly through the window at the end of Wing A, first gray, then pink, then the flat white light of a September morning that made the linoleum look like what it was.
