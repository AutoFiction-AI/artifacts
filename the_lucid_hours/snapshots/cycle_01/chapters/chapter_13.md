# Chapter 13

Marcus was sitting in the vinyl chair closest to the door when Carmen came down the hall at quarter past midnight. That was wrong. Marcus didn't sit in meetings — Marcus paced, shifted his weight, leaned against walls and pushed off again like a man who couldn't afford to stop moving. But tonight he was sitting with his hands on his knees, still, contained, and that was how Carmen knew he'd come to ask for something.

The TV room was cold. October had arrived while facilities management debated switching the heating system from its summer settings, and the result was a building that held the day's chill instead of releasing it. The overhead fluorescents were off. They used the television's standby light and a brass lamp Agnes had brought from her room months ago, its shade throwing amber across the card table and leaving the rest of the room in blue-gray shadow. The single-pane windows were black, and when Carmen passed close to one she could feel the cold radiating off it like the opposite of warmth, a presence rather than an absence.

Harry was at the table in his wheelchair, though he'd pushed back from it by a few inches — not close enough to work, not far enough to be out of the circle. A calculation in furniture. Agnes sat beside him with only a coffee cup, no notebooks, no pill organizer. Her hands wrapped around the cup with a grip that suggested she already knew what this meeting was about. Earl was against the far wall, arms crossed, legs extended in his institutional slippers. Next to him, Burt's empty wheelchair was folded and leaned against the baseboard. Nobody mentioned it. Nobody had mentioned it for two weeks, not since Burt had come back from the hospital with his eyes fogged over and his hands forgetting what they were holding. The empty wheelchair was just a shape in the room now, like the TV nobody watched or the fake plant by the window that had been there since before any of them arrived.

Lola was at her position by the hallway door, but she kept turning back toward the group with quick glances that made her tremor visible even in the low light. Her left hand opened and closed around the gold crucifix at her throat.

Marcus had come in during visiting hours — signed the log as Lola's nephew at four-thirty, stayed through dinner, and melted into the building the way he always did. Devon, the overnight aide, had been asleep at the nurses' station since eleven, earbuds in, a true-crime podcast murmuring through tiny speakers. Carmen had heard it when she passed on her med check.

"So," Harry said.

Marcus looked at the floor, then at Carmen, then at Harry. He was checking the room's temperature before he opened his mouth. Carmen had seen him do this with buyers in the parking lot descriptions Harry gave her — read the space, calibrate, then talk. Tonight the calibration took longer.

"I got approached," Marcus said. "By a buyer. Not like our regular people."

Agnes's fingers tightened around her cup.

"A reseller," Marcus said. "He works the club scene, maybe college towns — I didn't go deep on details. He's got his own customers who want oxy. Steady demand. He doesn't care about metformin, doesn't want the blood pressure meds or the generics. Oxycodone, five and ten milligram, and he wants volume."

"How much volume," Harry said. Not a question — a prompt.

"Two hundred tabs a month to start. He'll pay six dollars per milligram."

Agnes set her coffee cup on the card table. The sound it made was small and precise, a single click, and in the quiet room it functioned like a period at the end of a sentence nobody had finished.

"That's—" Carmen started.

"Thirty-six hundred a month just on oxy," Harry said. "On top of existing distribution."

"More like eight grand combined if we keep everything else running." Marcus stood up. There was the pacing — he crossed to the window, turned, crossed back. His sneakers were quiet on the linoleum but Carmen could feel the movement as spatial pressure, the room getting smaller with each pass. "Look, that's double. Harry, you could pay off Jack's debt in what, two, three months? Earl, Denise wouldn't need those weekend shifts anymore. Everybody benefits."

"Not everybody," Agnes said.

"Come on, Agnes—"

"I am not finished." She didn't raise her voice. The room adjusted anyway. "You said he works clubs. College towns. His customers are not people with prescriptions they cannot afford. They are recreational users."

"Some of them might have—"

"They are recreational users buying oxycodone to get high, and some percentage of them will combine it with alcohol or benzodiazepines because they do not know any better, and at six dollars per milligram they will buy enough to kill themselves because they have no concept of opioid tolerance." Agnes spoke faster as she went, facts compressing, the rhythm of a pharmacist who'd spent forty-two years making sure the wrong pill didn't reach the wrong person. "Do you know what the lethal dose of oxycodone is for a naive user? Forty milligrams if they are unlucky. Eighty if they have been drinking. That is eight tablets at ten milligrams. That is one Saturday night."

The room held the number. Carmen felt it settle.

Marcus stopped pacing. "We don't know that's what happens—"

"We know we do not know," Agnes said. "That is the point. When I sort pills for Rosa, I know her condition, her weight, her other medications. When I sort pills for your reseller, I know nothing about the person who swallows them. I have spent my career making sure that does not happen."

"Agnes." Marcus put his hands up, palms out — a peace gesture that didn't land. "I hear you. I get it. But we're already selling controlled substances out of a nursing home. We crossed the line a long time ago."

"We crossed a line," Carmen said. "Not this line."

Marcus turned to her. Something in his expression shifted — not anger yet, but the pre-anger look of someone gearing up. "You sure about that? Because from where I'm standing, the only difference between selling oxy to Janine and selling it to this guy is that Janine makes you feel better about it."

"The difference is Janine has a documented pain condition and a doctor who cut her off because he's scared of the DEA. The difference is she takes it as prescribed."

"She takes it as we give it to her. We're not her doctors."

"We know what it's for."

"So now we're pharmacists?"

"One of us actually is," Carmen said, and heard herself aim the line like a weapon and regretted it immediately — she wasn't trying to score points, she was trying to hold the floor, and the room was moving faster than she could think.

Harry tapped the table twice. Two quiet taps, the kind that came from a man who'd run meetings for fifty years — PTA boards, condo associations, this.

"Two questions," he said. "First, what does this do to our exposure. Second, what does it do to us."

"The exposure's manageable," Marcus said. He sat back down. His leg bounced. "I'd handle the reseller same as everybody else. Separate phone, different location, cash only. He doesn't know about Lakeview, doesn't know where the supply—"

"He knows about you," Agnes said. "That is sufficient."

"He knows a first name and a number."

"He is a drug dealer, Marcus." Agnes said it the way she'd state a dosage — flat, terminal, without decoration. "He has his own legal exposure. His own customers who could be arrested and pressured to cooperate. Every distribution charge creates an incentive for someone in the chain to trade information for a lesser sentence. You add a reseller to our network, you are not adding a customer. You are adding every person he sells to as a potential point of failure."

"Every buyer we have now is a point of—"

"Our buyers are patients purchasing medication for documented conditions. If Rosa is arrested with thirty hydroxychloroquine tablets, the charge is simple possession and a sympathetic judge. If your reseller is caught with two hundred oxycodone, that is trafficking with intent to distribute, and trafficking investigations go upstream." Agnes paused long enough for the word upstream to do its work. "They will find you. And you will lead them here."

Marcus rubbed the back of his neck. His leg was still bouncing. "You're assuming the worst case."

"I am assuming the likely case. Do you know this person's real name?"

"I know what he goes by."

"Do you know whether he has prior convictions? Whether he is currently under investigation? Whether he was sent to you by someone cooperating with law enforcement?"

Marcus didn't answer. The bounce in his leg slowed and stopped.

"A person you have never dealt with before approached you and offered premium prices for Schedule II opioids in quantity," Agnes said. "You did not find him. He found you. That alone should concern you more than it apparently does."

Carmen watched Marcus absorb this — watched the arguments he'd prepared hit the wall of Agnes's specificity and lose shape. Agnes didn't argue the way other people argued. She didn't raise her voice, didn't appeal to emotion, didn't repeat herself. She stated facts in series and let the accumulation do the work. It was like being corrected by a textbook.

But Marcus wasn't done. He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, and when he spoke his voice had changed register — lower, harder, the humor stripped out.

"Let me tell you something. I drive a car with a hundred forty thousand miles on it and a brake light that's been out for two months because I can't afford the bulb and the ticket at the same time. I meet people in strip mall parking lots at nine o'clock at night with Schedule II controlled substances in my glove box. Every time I do that, I'm the one who goes to prison if anything goes wrong. Not Harry. Not Agnes. Not any of you. You'd get the news cameras and the sympathetic interviews — poor confused elderly victims, manipulated by some outside operator. I'd get Rahway."

The room was quiet except for Earl's breathing — the rasp underneath each exhale, the faint wet whistle that Carmen registered every time and told herself she should mention to someone and never did.

"You carry more risk," Harry said. His voice was neutral, the voice he used when he was still calculating. "That's true."

Marcus turned toward him. Something opened in his face — not gratitude exactly, but the relief of being heard after a long stretch of not being heard.

"But that's a separate question from this buyer," Harry said. "The risk isn't just yours anymore. It's the count discrepancy that shows up on Carmen's log. It's the medication room inventory that doesn't balance. Two hundred additional oxycodone a month — that's a shortage we can't attribute to documentation error. That's a pattern that triggers a DEA referral, not just a state survey question."

"So we adjust the—"

"There's no adjustment that covers two hundred pills a month, Marcus. The arithmetic doesn't work. I've been running the numbers on what we can move without triggering automated flags for fourteen months. We're close to the ceiling now."

Marcus looked at Carmen. Under the brass lamp his face was all planes and shadow, and she could see the man he'd been before — the CNA who'd gotten fired for caring too much, the caregiver the system had ground up and spit into a basement apartment. That person was real and present. But so was the person who'd walked into this room with a number in his head that would fix his life, and those two people were pulling in different directions.

"You know what, Carmen?" His voice went tight. "You got here late. Harry set this up, Agnes figured out the supply chain, I built the distribution routes. You showed up and we were already running. So it's real easy for you to stand here and talk about what kind of crime is the right amount of crime."

"That's not—"

"You're pulling thirteen hundred extra a month from this and telling me where the moral line is."

"I don't take money from this." She heard her own voice shift — the contractions thinning, the sentences getting shorter, the formality that rose in her when she was angry enough to stop being casual. It was the voice she used with administrators and surveyors, the voice of someone holding herself together by gripping the professional register like a handrail. "You know that."

"Right. You just take the protection. The part where nobody asks why your controlled substance log doesn't add up. That's free, I guess."

It wasn't free. It was the most expensive thing Carmen had ever accepted, and Marcus knew it, and throwing it at her was his way of saying he'd run out of better arguments. She let the accusation sit. She didn't dignify it.

Lola came away from the doorway. She'd been standing there the whole time, one hand on the frame, the other working the crucifix, and now she moved into the circle with the unsteady gait that Carmen still couldn't parse — Parkinson's or performance or some blend of both that Lola herself probably couldn't untangle anymore.

"I don't like this," Lola said. Her voice was faster than usual, the words running together the way they did when she was scared. "I don't — we had something that worked, we were helping people, and now it's — Dios mio, Marcus, I can't do this. I can't sit here and sort pills that end up God knows where. I'm already—" She stopped. Restarted. "I'm already having trouble, you know? With all of it. With who I am in the daytime and who I am at night and where one stops. I can't add this. I can't carry this."

Her tremor was bad. Her left hand shook against her hip in small rapid oscillations, and she pressed it flat against her thigh to stop it, which didn't work, it never worked, it just moved the shaking somewhere else. Marcus watched her and something in his face softened. Lola was the closest thing he had to family — Carmen knew this, had seen the way he called her Tia when he didn't think anyone was listening, had seen the way Lola touched his face when he visited, the way she asked about his mother who'd been dead for two years and he kept the lie going because it was what she needed. That bond was in the room now, pulling against everything Marcus had just argued.

"Lola," he said. "I'm not trying to—"

"I know, mijo. I know." She wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand. "But that doesn't make it okay."

Harry looked at Earl.

Earl had not spoken. He'd been in the same position against the wall since the meeting started — arms crossed, legs out, his face giving Carmen nothing. His breathing was the loudest thing about him, that persistent rattle underneath each exhale. He'd been coughing more these past weeks, deep and wet, and he waved it off every time Carmen looked at him with her nurse's eye. He was coughing now — or not coughing, holding it in, the effort visible in the way his chest rose and caught before releasing.

"Earl," Harry said.

Earl cleared his throat. The clearing turned into a cough he couldn't suppress — short, hard, rattling, the sound of something loose and heavy moving in his chest. He turned his head away from the group and covered his mouth with his fist. When he was done he wiped his hand on his pajama pants and looked at Marcus.

"We started this because nobody was looking out for us," Earl said. "That's not what this is."

One sentence. The room held it the way a room holds a dropped glass before anyone moves to pick up the pieces. Carmen had been in enough of these meetings to know that Earl's silence was his primary instrument and his speech was something else entirely — rare, weighed, final. When Earl talked, it was because he'd measured the situation and found the single sentence that contained its whole truth.

Marcus stared at him. Not with anger. With something worse — the look of being excluded from a group he thought he belonged to. Marcus had been coming to this building for eighteen months, smuggling pills in cookie tins, meeting buyers who needed medication they couldn't get, sending money to people who'd treated him like family after the institution that employed him threw him out. He'd earned his place in this room. And Earl had just told him the thing he was proposing put him outside of it.

"That's it?" Marcus said. His voice was rough.

Earl didn't answer. He'd said what he had to say.

"You're sitting here telling me what this is about? I'm the one who—" Marcus caught himself, shook his head. He'd already made the risk argument. He could hear it looping. "Fine. Okay."

Harry straightened in his wheelchair. His hands went flat on the table again, the posture of decision.

"Here's what's going to happen," Harry said. "You're going to decline the buyer. You're going to tell him the supply isn't available. You break the contact — no second meeting, no counter-offer, no keeping his number for later."

"Harry—"

"That's the decision."

"It's the wrong one."

"Maybe. We've survived wrong decisions before. We won't survive a trafficking investigation."

Carmen spoke. She'd been running the calculation the whole time they argued — not Marcus's financial calculation but the legal one, her name on every dispensation record, her license number on every controlled substance log, the Board of Nursing file that would end her career and her freedom if any of this surfaced through a reseller's arrest and the chain of testimony that followed.

"Marcus." She waited until he was looking at her. "If you take this deal, I report everything. I go to the Board. I give them the diversion records, the distribution pattern, dates, quantities. All of it."

The room went still. Even Earl's breathing seemed to quiet, though Carmen knew that was her own attention narrowing, the way it did in a crisis when the peripheral sounds dropped out and the thing that mattered filled the whole channel.

"You'd go down too," Marcus said.

"I know."

"You'd lose your license." He paused. "You'd lose Reggie."

Her son's name in this room, in this argument, at one-thirty in the morning in a nursing home where she was supposed to be administering care. She felt the badge under her scrub top pressing against her sternum and she pushed back against the feeling the way she pushed back against everything that threatened to crack her open at work — with procedure, with the next task, with the professional competence that was both her identity and her shield.

"I know what I'd lose," she said.

"And you'd still do it."

"I'm telling you what I'll do."

Harry was watching her. He'd gone completely still — the dangerous still, the Harry who stopped moving when the ledger showed a number he didn't like. Agnes had both hands flat on the table, her coffee forgotten, her posture a mirror of Harry's that she didn't seem conscious of. Lola was crying, silently, one hand on the crucifix and the other pressed against her stomach. Earl was looking at the wall.

Marcus looked around the room. He took his time with it — Harry, immovable behind his calculation; Agnes, who met his gaze without softening or looking away; Earl, who wouldn't look back; Lola, who loved him and was crying because of what he'd proposed; Carmen, who was not going to blink.

"Okay," Marcus said. "Fine. I'll tell him no."

He said it too fast.

Carmen heard it — the speed of the surrender after fifteen minutes of hard argument. He'd fought like a man who believed what he was saying, and he'd given up like a man who didn't need permission. Agnes heard it too; her eyes changed, a micro-adjustment Carmen recognized from watching Agnes check medication labels — the slight narrowing that meant something didn't match. Harry's hands came off the table and settled in his lap, and Carmen couldn't read whether that was satisfaction or a quieter, more worried thing.

"I'll call him tomorrow," Marcus said. He was already standing, already straightening his jacket — gray, clean, pressed. He always kept himself neat. The CNA's institutional habit, the reflex of a person trained to present as competent and trustworthy. "I'll shut it down. It's done."

"Good," Harry said.

"Yeah." Marcus adjusted his collar. He wasn't looking at anyone now. "So. We good?"

Nobody said we good. The question sat there, unanswered, which was its own answer.

"Lola will walk you out," Harry said.

Lola wiped her face with the back of her hand and went to the hallway door. She leaned out, checked both directions — the lookout muscle, automatic even now — then turned back to Marcus. "Vamos, mijo," she said, and the softness of it, the unchanged tenderness, landed on Marcus in a way Carmen could see. His mouth moved. He didn't say anything. He followed her out.

Their footsteps faded down the hall — Lola's shuffle, Marcus's heavier tread — and then there was only the building's night sounds: the ventilation cycling, a call bell pinging in the other wing, the hum of Devon's podcast leaking from the nurses' station.

Agnes picked up the coffee cups and stacked them with the efficiency of someone who needed her hands busy. She carried them to the side table and stood there for a moment with her back to the room.

"He is going to do it regardless," Agnes said.

Harry said nothing. The silence was his agreement — or rather, his acknowledgment that the calculation had already accounted for this outcome and found no variable that changed it.

"He's going to do it," Carmen said. Not a question. Confirmation. She was leaning against the wall and the plaster was cold through her scrub top, the building's October chill pressing through the material like a hand.

"Probably," Harry said. It was the first time Carmen had heard him use the word. Harry dealt in numbers, not probabilities. Probably meant the spreadsheet had a cell he couldn't fill.

Agnes turned from the side table. She looked at Carmen — a direct look, no warmth in it but no hostility either, just the flat professional attention of a woman who'd spent four decades assessing whether the right thing was in the right bottle.

"I will not sort pills for a reseller," Agnes said. "That is my position regardless of what Marcus does or does not do."

"Okay," Carmen said.

"I want that to be clear."

"It's clear, Agnes."

Agnes left. Her footsteps were nearly silent — she moved through the building like someone who'd practiced invisibility and gotten too good at it. Earl followed a moment later, getting up slowly, one hand on the wall, his cough working through him in three hard pulses he turned away from Harry and Carmen to manage. He didn't say goodnight. His slippers made no sound on the linoleum.

Harry and Carmen sat in the TV room. The brass lamp was still on, its amber circle shrunk to the card table where nobody sat. The television's standby light gave the vinyl chairs a faint blue sheen. Outside the windows, the parking lot was dark — it got dark by five now, the days pulling in, and by midnight the lot was a field of black with the orange glow of the two working sodium lights at the far end.

"He was right about one thing," Harry said. "The risk distribution. He carries more than the rest of us."

"I know."

"That doesn't make the deal right. But it makes his argument not nothing."

Carmen looked at the empty room. Burt's folded wheelchair against the wall. The card table where Agnes's notebooks used to be, where pill counts and buyer lists and expiration dates were tracked with a pharmacist's precision. The vinyl chairs holding the impressions of the people who'd just been in them.

"What do we do if he takes it anyway?" she said.

"Count the inventory. See what's missing."

An accountant's answer. Carmen almost smiled but didn't. It wasn't funny. It was the sound of a man whose only tool was a ledger trying to solve a problem that lived in another person's need.

She turned off the lamp. The room went dark except for the hallway light leaking under the door, a thin bright line across the linoleum. She unplugged the television and the standby light died.

"You should get some sleep," she said.

"I don't sleep much." Harry's wheelchair creaked as he shifted. In the dark, his voice sounded different — older, thinner, the voice of a man who was eighty-four regardless of what his mind could still do. "Carmen."

"Yeah."

"If you report, make sure it's because you need to. Not because you're angry."

She didn't know how to answer that. She let the silence stand.

Harry wheeled himself to the door. She watched his silhouette against the hallway light — hunched in the chair, his thin shoulders, the faded blue cardigan. He turned right toward his room and was gone.

Carmen stood in the dark TV room for another minute. She could hear the building breathing — the ventilation, the call bell in the far wing that nobody was answering, a toilet flushing somewhere, a cough from a room she couldn't identify. The building was full of people whose bodies made sounds they couldn't control, whose lungs and bladders and joints announced their decline whether they consented to it or not. She was the youngest person in this wing by forty years, and she'd just threatened to burn it all down because a man she didn't fully trust had agreed with her too quickly.

She went back to the sub-station and charted. She checked on Room 110 — the woman inside breathing with the mechanical rhythm of her oxygen concentrator. She checked on Burt in 116A, asleep with his mouth open, his hands loose on the blanket. Earl's shape was visible in the other bed, the rise and fall of his chest, the rattle audible from the doorway.

She went to the medication room and counted. It was the thing she did when she couldn't do anything else — opened the cabinet, logged the controlled substances, matched the numbers to the sheet. Everything balanced. The oxycodone was where it should be, the alprazolam, the tramadol. The count was clean.

The count was clean and Marcus had agreed too fast and outside the medication room window the October dark sat on the parking lot and she was thirty-one years old and in too deep and the only thing between her and what came next was the hope that a man who'd spent the last hour explaining why he deserved more from this operation had actually agreed to take less.

She put the clipboard back on its hook and locked the cabinet.

She charted the count. She charted it accurately, the way she'd charted everything since the beginning — every number true, every timestamp real, every entry a faithful record of a system that concealed its losses inside the institution's tolerance for error. The charting was honest. Everything around it was not. It was getting harder to tell where one ended and the other began, and Carmen thought of Lola saying the same thing about her performance, about who she was in the daytime and who she was at night, and she closed the chart and put the pen down and sat very still in the medication room's alcohol-and-pill-dust quiet until her shift was over.
