# Chapter 16

Agnes didn't touch people. That was the first thing Carmen had learned about her, back in September when she was still figuring out which residents bit and which ones just looked like they might — Agnes kept a pharmacist's distance from everyone, hands either sorting pill bottles or folded in her lap or gripping the armrests of her chair like she was about to stand but had decided against it. Agnes's hands were instruments, not social tools.

So when Agnes's fingers closed around Carmen's wrist at the sub-station just after eleven, Carmen stopped charting mid-word.

Agnes was in performance mode. Slack face, unfocused eyes, the institutional shuffle of someone who'd had a stroke and never fully recovered. She looked like she was wandering — an 11 PM bathroom trip, maybe, or one of those aimless hall-walks the genuinely confused residents did when their sleep cycles scrambled. But her grip was wrong. Too exact, too located. Two fingers on the inside of Carmen's wrist, right over the pulse point, the way a pharmacist takes hold of a patient who's about to do something stupid with a prescription.

"Mrs. Chen?" Carmen said, loud enough for Devon to hear. Devon was asleep at the main station with his phone propped against the keyboard, a hunting podcast droning through his earbuds. "You need something? Bathroom?"

Agnes's eyes flicked down the hall toward Wing A. Her grip tightened once, then released. She turned and walked toward Harry's room, her slippers scuffing the linoleum in that halting, post-stroke rhythm she'd perfected — a shuffle so convincing that Dr. Prasad had renewed her mobility restrictions twice without questioning them.

Carmen followed.

Room 114A was at the far end of Wing A, single occupancy because Harry had outlasted three roommates and the facility hadn't bothered to fill the second bed. The door was open four inches. Agnes pushed through without knocking. Carmen stepped in behind her and pulled it shut.

Harry was standing between his bed and the window. Not holding anything, not leaning — standing with his weight evenly distributed, his shoulders squared under the faded blue cardigan that he wore to every meeting, buttoned wrong tonight, one button off so the whole thing hung crooked on his thin frame. He was eighty-four years old and he looked it in a way that the performance usually hid — the pouches under his eyes, the scabbed knuckles where he'd knocked them on the bed rail, the white stubble he'd missed shaving because his right hand wasn't as steady as it used to be and that wasn't part of any act.

He was holding the shared prepaid phone.

"Read it," he said. Real voice. Short, flat, final.

Carmen took the phone. The screen was cracked across one corner — it had been cracked since August, since Earl dropped it getting out of the van at the cemetery. A text message, two hours old, from a number she didn't recognize. Not one of the ring's buyer contacts, not any number she'd seen in Agnes's notebooks.

*can do 200 oxy 5s and 100 bars wkly. same price. lmk about thurs*

Carmen read it once. Then again, because the numbers were wrong. They had to be wrong. The ring moved eighty, maybe ninety oxycodone a month. A hundred in a flush month when Dr. Prasad added a new script. Two hundred a week was eight hundred a month. And bars — a hundred Xanax a week, four hundred a month.

"He sent it to our phone," Agnes said. She was sitting on the radiator cover beneath the window, her spine very straight, her hands pressed flat against her thighs. Not folded, not sorting. Pressed, like she was holding them down. "He meant to send it to the buyer. He sent it here instead."

"Two hundred a week," Harry said. He wasn't looking at Carmen. He was looking at the photograph taped above his closet — the landscaping truck with ZIELINSKI & SON painted on the door in green letters. "We have never moved two hundred in a month."

"He is sourcing elsewhere," Agnes said. "Or he is promising volume he cannot deliver. Either scenario is a problem I did not agree to."

The radiator under Agnes clicked and hissed. Late October in central New Jersey, and Lakeview's heating system ran like it did everything else — unevenly, with long gaps between effort. The room smelled like Ben-Gay and the metallic dryness of forced air that wasn't quite warm enough to be comfortable.

"When did this come in?" Carmen asked.

"Nine fourteen," Harry said. "Agnes was checking the phone for a delivery confirmation from last week. She found this instead."

Carmen set the phone on the nightstand, next to Harry's water cup and the crossword puzzle book he never opened. Her fingers were tingling, which was stupid — her body reacting like this was a medical emergency, the adrenaline dump that happened when a fall alarm went off or a resident coded. But nobody was coding. Nobody was hurt. A text message on a cracked prepaid phone, and the numbers on the screen meant the thing she'd spent months trying to hold together had already come apart.

"Call him," Harry said.

Carmen picked the phone back up and dialed Marcus's number. It rang three times.

"Yo." She could hear a car engine, road noise, a bass line from his stereo cut short as he turned it down. "What's up?"

"It's Carmen. Harry needs you to come in tonight."

A pause. The engine noise smoothed out — he was coasting, slowing down. "Tonight? I got — it's almost midnight, I can't just —"

"One o'clock." Harry held out his hand. Carmen gave him the phone. "One o'clock, Marcus. Use the loading dock. Carmen will leave it unlocked."

"Harry, come on, man, what's —"

"One o'clock," Harry said, and ended the call.

He sat on the edge of his bed. The mattress groaned under him and his knees cracked — both of them, a sound like someone stepping on dry sticks. He set the phone down. He looked at Agnes. Agnes looked back at him, and something passed between them that Carmen couldn't read, something built over thirty months of midnight meetings and pill counts and arguments about expiration dates while the building slept around them.

"I will not sort for a reseller," Agnes said. "I told him that in October and I meant it."

"I know," Harry said.

"I will not touch product that ends up at a party in Edison where some twenty-year-old takes three pills because nobody explained opioid naivety to him. That is not what I do."

Harry nodded once. He didn't say anything else. Carmen realized this was his anger — not the shouting kind, not even the cold-lecture kind she'd seen him use on her back in September when she'd first confronted him. This was the accountant's version. A ledger that had been balanced and someone had taken a pen to the numbers without permission. Harry's fury was in the silence between his sentences, in the precise way he set the phone down, in the fact that he was looking at the photograph of his grandson's truck instead of at the two women in his room.

"Get the others," Harry said. "Lola and Earl. Not Burt."

---

Lola arrived in the TV room wearing her housecoat and the gold crucifix she never took off, the one that caught the fluorescent light and threw a small bright point against the wall when she moved. Her tremor was bad tonight — her left hand shaking visibly against her thigh, the Parkinson's worsened by whatever Agnes had told her when she woke her up. She sat in the vinyl chair closest to the door, the lookout's chair, but she wasn't watching the hallway. She was watching Harry.

Earl came in behind her, tall and stooped in his bathrobe, clearing his throat the way he always did before speaking — buying time, settling whatever was in his lungs. His cough had been worse this month. Carmen had charted it twice and he'd waved her off both times. He sat down and folded his hands over his knees and didn't ask why they were here.

Carmen had unlocked the loading dock door at 12:40 and waited in the cold, her breath smoking in the parking lot light. October had turned hard this week — the courtyard's three trees had dropped most of their leaves, and the wind came off the highway with a flat, metallic edge. When Marcus's sedan pulled into the far end of the lot, headlights off, she felt something in her chest that wasn't anger and wasn't fear but sat between them like a coin balanced on its edge.

Marcus came through the loading dock in a hoodie and jeans, moving the way he always moved in the building — quiet, deliberate, the CNA's muscle memory of navigating a sleeping facility. He smelled like the cold outside and something chemical, hand sanitizer or the cleaning spray his car always reeked of. He saw Carmen's face and stopped.

"What?" he said.

"TV room."

She walked him down the hall. He didn't ask questions. When they reached the TV room, Harry was sitting at the card table in his wheelchair — the performance wheelchair, the one he used as a prop, though Carmen noticed he hadn't stood up once since she'd gathered everyone. Maybe his knees were bad tonight. Maybe something else.

Marcus saw the group — Harry at the card table, Agnes standing by the window, Lola in her lookout chair, Earl in the far corner — and his face changed. Not guilt, not yet. Recognition. He knew what this was.

Harry set the prepaid phone on the card table and slid it across to Marcus. The phone scraped on the laminate. Marcus didn't pick it up.

"You sent that to the wrong number," Harry said.

Marcus looked at the phone. Then at Harry. Then at Agnes, who was watching him the way she watched medication interactions — with clinical attention and no sentiment.

"Shit," Marcus said quietly.

"Two hundred oxycodone a week," Harry said. "A hundred alprazolam. Weekly. We have never moved that volume in a month, Marcus. We have never moved half that in a month."

"Harry —"

"You told us you'd walk away from the volume buyer. Three weeks ago, in this room, you sat where you're sitting and you said you'd decline."

Marcus pulled out the chair across from Harry and sat down. He rubbed his face with both hands, the way he did when he was buying time, when the joke he wanted to make wasn't going to land and he knew it. When he dropped his hands, his expression had shifted — not apologetic, not defensive. Something harder.

"I did decline," Marcus said. "For about a week. Then he came back with better numbers and I thought about it and yeah, I took it."

"Without telling us," Harry said.

"Without telling you. Yeah."

The room was quiet except for the radiator in the corner and the distant infomercial still playing in the hallway, a woman's voice describing a vegetable chopper with the intensity of someone narrating a moon landing.

"Where are you getting two hundred a week?" Agnes asked. Her voice was compressed, fast — more facts per sentence, less air between them. Her stress mode. "We do not produce that. We have never produced that. What are you promising that you cannot source from us?"

"I've got other sources," Marcus said. "A guy at a pain clinic in Paterson. Another one through — look, it doesn't matter where. The point is I'm not taking more from you than I was before. Your end doesn't change."

"Our end," Harry repeated.

"Your supply. I'm still moving the same amount from Lakeview. The rest is separate."

"The rest is separate," Agnes said. She said it the way she'd say a medication name — precisely, clinically, with the full weight of someone who understood what the words actually meant. "You are running a distribution operation on top of ours. Using the same phone. The same contacts. The same car you drive here every Tuesday."

"It's a different phone," Marcus said. "Usually. I fucked up. I sent one text to the wrong —"

"One text," Harry said. "One text to the phone that sits in my nightstand. One text that Agnes read. One text that connects your volume operation to our operation to this building to these people." He pointed, a precise gesture, his index finger moving from Agnes to Lola to Earl. "One text."

Marcus leaned back in his chair. The vinyl squeaked. Carmen was standing by the door, her back against the frame, her hand on her badge — she'd been adjusting it without realizing, the nervous fidget she'd never been able to break.

"Okay," Marcus said. "Okay, I hear you. I messed up with the phone. That's on me. But the rest of it — Harry, come on. You knew this was coming."

"I didn't know anything."

"You knew I wasn't gonna just sit there making twelve hundred a month while some dude's offering me five times that. You knew the math didn't work for me the way it works for you."

"The math," Harry said.

"Yeah, the math. Your math. You send money to Jack, a couple hundred a month, and that's — that's fine for you, that's your grandson's truck payment or whatever. Earl sends Denise her four hundred. Agnes has an envelope under her mattress. You're all — you're taken care of, right? Your rooms are paid for, your food shows up, your heat works most of the time." Marcus gestured at the radiator, which chose that moment to click and go silent. "I'm in a basement apartment in Plainfield. I drive a car with a hundred and sixty thousand miles on it. I don't have a pension, I don't have insurance, I don't have a bed I don't pay rent on."

"You agreed to the terms," Harry said.

"I agreed to terms that made sense when there wasn't a better option. There's a better option now."

Agnes turned from the window. "The better option is distributing opioids to a reseller who will sell them at street price to anyone with cash. No condition screening. No dosage information. No interaction checks."

"That's his problem, not mine."

"It is everyone's problem when someone dies." Agnes's voice was sharp — not loud, Agnes was never loud, but the consonants had edges. "When a nineteen-year-old takes six oxycodone because he does not know what he bought and his mother finds him in the morning. That is my problem. That is pharmacology, Marcus. That is what I was trained for forty-two years to prevent."

Marcus looked at the ceiling. "Agnes, I respect you, I do, but you're not my pharmacist. You're — we're not a pharmacy. We're selling stolen pills out of a nursing home. That's what this is."

"They are not stolen," Agnes said. "They are prescribed to us and diverted by our consent."

"Okay, fine, diverted. Whatever word makes you feel better about it."

Lola's crucifix caught the light as she shifted in her chair. She'd been silent the whole time, her hands pressed together in her lap, the tremor visible even from across the room. "Mijo," she said quietly.

Marcus looked at her. The hardness in his face cracked — not all the way, not enough to change what he was going to do, but enough that Carmen saw it. The fissure.

"Lola, I'm sorry I didn't tell you," he said. "I should've. But you would've told Harry and Harry would've said no and we'd be right here anyway."

"You don't know that," Lola said. "You didn't give us the chance."

"Because I already knew the answer."

Harry put both hands flat on the card table. His fingers were spread, the tendons standing out, an old man's hands — knobbed, liver-spotted, steady. "You're out," he said. "Effective now. You don't take product from this building, you don't visit as Lola's nephew, you don't come through that loading dock again."

Marcus stared at him. Then he laughed — short, hard, no humor in it. "And then what? Who's your distribution? Who's gonna carry the pills out the front door, Harry? Agnes? She's gonna shuffle down to the parking lot in her performance slippers and hand a bag of oxy to Rosa? Earl's gonna wheel the van to Paterson and make drops?"

"We'll figure it out."

"No you won't. Because you can't. Because the whole thing depends on somebody who can walk out of here, and that's me. That's been me for eighteen months. I'm the one in the car. I'm the one meeting people in parking lots with Schedule II narcotics in a backpack. If the cops pull me over, I go to prison. You know what happens to you? You get the newspaper story. You get the — " He waved his hand. "The cute story. Elderly residents outsmart the system. Poor old people, they didn't know what they were doing. You'd get sympathy. A human interest segment. I'd get a cell."

"That's not —" Carmen started.

"It's exactly what would happen and you know it." Marcus turned to her. "You know it, Carmen. You've worked this system. You know what a forty-four-year-old Black guy with a prior distribution charge looks like next to five white-haired grandparents in wheelchairs. Tell me I'm wrong."

Carmen didn't say anything. She couldn't, because he wasn't entirely wrong. The racial calculus of the criminal justice system wasn't something she could argue around, and Marcus knew it.

"I got fired from this place," Marcus said, and now his voice had changed — the self-repair had kicked in, the sentences starting and restarting as he tried to find the honest version. "I got fired for — I filed a complaint. About staffing. About the fact that there were twelve residents and one aide on the overnight and somebody was gonna fall and get hurt, and I was right, by the way, because Burt fell and got hurt, which is exactly what I said would happen. And they fired me. They called it insubordination and they fired me. I cared about these people and I got punished for it. So I found another way to — I came back and I found a way to still be useful, to still be part of something, and now you're sitting here telling me I can't look out for myself? That I'm supposed to make twelve hundred a month and be grateful because at least I get to be the one who takes all the risk?"

The room held the silence after that. The vegetable chopper infomercial had ended. The hallway was quiet. Carmen could hear the building's bones settling — the slow click and groan of a structure that had been standing for forty years and showed it in every wall crack and stained ceiling tile.

Lola was holding her crucifix in her right hand, the gold chain tight around her fingers. Earl hadn't moved from his corner. His hands were still folded over his knees, his expression unreadable.

"Marcus," Lola said. "Mijo, please."

"Lola, I love you. I do. But I can't do this for love. Love doesn't pay my rent."

Harry said nothing. He was in ledger mode now — the numbers calculated, the column totaled, the result written in ink. Carmen had seen this before, the night she'd confronted him in September. Harry processed in silence, and when the silence ended, the decision was already made.

"We built this," Harry said. "Agnes and Earl and Lola and me. We built it because nobody else was going to take care of us. It was never about the money."

"It was always about the money," Marcus said. "For me it was. You had the luxury of it being about something else."

Earl coughed. The deep, wet rattle that he covered with his fist, the one that had gotten worse every week — a sound like something was loose in his chest and shifting position. He cleared his throat. The room waited, because when Earl cleared his throat, he was about to say something, and when Earl said something, it landed.

"You're not one of us anymore," Earl said.

Five words. He didn't raise his voice, didn't change his posture, didn't look away from the spot on the far wall he'd been studying since the meeting started. But the words had weight — the specific weight of a man who'd spent thirty years organizing shop floors and knew exactly what it meant to say someone was out. Not a punishment. A fact. A door closing.

Marcus flinched. It was small — a tightening around his eyes, a shift in his shoulders — but Carmen saw it. Earl saw it too, and Earl's expression didn't change, because Earl's expression never changed when he'd said the thing that ended the discussion.

Marcus sat there for a moment. His hands were flat on his thighs, mirroring Harry's hands on the table. He looked at Lola, who was crying silently, the tears running down her face into the creases around her mouth, her tremor so bad now that the crucifix was shaking in her grip. He looked at Agnes, who was watching him the way she watched everything — with assessment, not judgment, though the distinction felt thin tonight. He looked at Harry.

"Yeah," Marcus said. "Okay." He pushed back from the table and stood up. The chair screeched on the linoleum. "I'll stay away from the building. I won't use the phone again. You want me to — I don't know. You want me to return Lola's cookie tin?"

Nobody answered.

Marcus stood there for another few seconds, and Carmen could see it on him — not the anger, not the defiance, but the thing underneath both of those, which was just a man realizing that the people he loved most had told him to leave. His jaw worked. He shoved his hands in the hoodie pocket.

"Tell Burt I said hey," he said. Then he walked out.

Carmen listened to his footsteps down the hall — the CNA's quiet walk, instinctive, even now. The loading dock door opened and closed. A minute later, an engine started in the parking lot, and headlights swept across the window and were gone.

Lola made a small sound, something between a word and a breath. Agnes walked to the TV and turned it off — the screen had been dark anyway, the infomercial block over, but she pressed the power button with the precision of someone who needed to turn off one thing she could control.

Earl stood up, coughed into his fist, and left without speaking. His bathrobe belt trailed behind him on the floor.

Lola wiped her face with the back of her hand. "Ay, Dios mio," she whispered, and followed him out, her slippers catching on the threshold.

---

Harry didn't move from the card table. Carmen sat down in the chair Marcus had left, the vinyl still warm. The room was quiet now — that specific late-night Lakeview quiet where you could hear the ventilation system cycling and the soft tick of the wall clock above the TV and, if you listened for it, the faint moaning from Room 110 that never fully stopped.

The card table still had the phone on it. Carmen picked it up and set it on the windowsill, face down.

Harry was looking at his hands. His fingers were laced together now, resting on the table, and for the first time since she'd known him — the real him, not the performance — he looked his age. Not diminished, not confused. Just old. An eighty-four-year-old man in a cardigan buttoned wrong, sitting in a nursing home TV room at two in the morning because the one thing he'd built that still mattered was falling apart.

"You knew," Carmen said.

"That he'd take the deal? Yeah. When he agreed too fast in October, I knew."

"So why didn't you —"

"Do what? Follow him? We can't leave the building, Carmen. That's the whole problem. That's always been the whole problem."

He unfolded his hands and laid them flat on the table again. His right index finger tapped once — just once, the accountant's tic, like he was entering a figure on an adding machine.

"I started this because they were cutting our medications," he said.

Carmen waited. Harry didn't tell stories. Harry stated facts and let you draw your own conclusions. But something in the room had shifted — Marcus's chair was still pulled out, the space he'd occupied still registering as absence — and Harry was talking.

"Three years ago. Cliff decided to switch pharmacy providers. The new contract saved the facility eleven thousand a year. He put it in his quarterly report — cost savings, operational efficiency. He got a bonus for it." Harry's voice was even, metronomic, the voice he used for numbers. "The new pharmacy was in South Brunswick. They had a centralized fulfillment system — big operation, served thirty-some facilities. They were cheaper because they were bigger, and they were bigger because they cut corners."

He stopped. Looked at the photograph of his grandson's truck, which wasn't here — it was in his room, taped above his closet — but he looked at the spot where it would have been if it were.

"Agnes's hydrocodone got switched to a generic she'd never been prescribed. Different manufacturer, different fillers, different absorption rate. She told the nurse it wasn't right. The nurse told the charge nurse, the charge nurse told Cliff, Cliff told the pharmacy, and the pharmacy said the generic was therapeutically equivalent and that was the end of it." He paused. "Agnes was in pain for two weeks. She's a pharmacist. She knew exactly what was happening to her body and why, and she couldn't get anyone to listen."

"Harry," Carmen said.

"My oxycodone got delayed. Just — delayed. The new pharmacy had fulfillment issues, backlogs, they said it would arrive in forty-eight hours. It took nine days. I was eating Tylenol for a hip that needed oxycodone, and I'm not a complainer, Carmen, but nine days with a bad hip at eighty-one years old is — you don't sleep. You don't move right. You fall." The metronomic voice wavered, just slightly, like a number that didn't round clean. "Earl's blood pressure medication got substituted twice in one month. Lola's Parkinson's prescription arrived three weeks late and when it came it was the wrong dosage. Burt's pain management — Burt was on a stable regimen that had taken two years to calibrate, and they changed his oxycodone manufacturer without telling anyone and he went through a week of breakthrough pain that he couldn't describe because he couldn't always find the words."

Harry's hands were still on the table. Perfectly still. The accountant under maximum pressure, reciting facts without interpretation, which was worse than any emotion he could have shown.

"So we started keeping our pills. Not selling them — keeping them. Agnes set up a system. She inventoried what we had, checked interactions, tracked expiration dates. We were doing what the facility was supposed to do and wasn't. We were managing our own medications because the people paid to manage them had decided it was cheaper not to."

"And the selling?" Carmen asked.

"That came six months later. Marcus was already fired by then. He came to visit Lola, and Lola mentioned that Agnes had noticed they were being over-prescribed — the new pharmacy was auto-filling scripts that should have been reviewed. We had surplus. Marcus knew people who needed it. The math was simple."

He stopped again. The wall clock ticked. The ventilation system cycled off, and the silence got thicker, the kind of silence that pressed against the windows.

Carmen thought about the parking lot outside — dark at five o'clock now, the overhead lights buzzing the sickly orange they always buzzed, the leaves from the courtyard trees scattered across the asphalt in wet brown clumps. She thought about Marcus driving away from Lakeview for the last time, or maybe not the last time, his headlights disappearing down Route 27 toward Plainfield and his basement apartment and whatever deal he'd made with whoever was on the other end of that text.

"He's going to get caught," she said. "Moving that kind of volume."

Harry didn't respond to that. He was looking at his hands again.

"What do we do now?" Carmen asked.

Harry lifted his head. His eyes were clear — not sharp the way they usually were, not calculating. Just clear, the way a window is clear when there's nothing behind it to obstruct the view.

"We wait," he said. "Marcus will either come back or he won't. And if he doesn't, we figure out what we are without him."

"And if he gets caught?"

"Then we've got a different problem."

Carmen sat with that. The vinyl chair was cold through her scrubs — the room's heat had cycled off with the ventilation, and the late October air was seeping through the single-pane window behind her, the kind of cold that was patient, that didn't push but just waited until you stopped being warm.

Harry put his hands on the arms of his wheelchair. For a moment Carmen thought he was going to stand — to push himself up the way he did when the performance was over and the real Harry took his body back. But he didn't. He sat in the chair, and she couldn't tell if it was exhaustion or choice or the kind of decline that didn't announce itself but just arrived, settling into the joints and the muscles and the places where the body had been pretending it was younger than it was.

"Go do your rounds," Harry said. "Devon's gonna wake up eventually."

Carmen stood. She looked at the card table where Marcus had been sitting, the chair still pulled out at an angle. She pushed it back under the table. The scrape of the legs on the linoleum was loud in the empty room.

"Harry."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry."

He looked at her. Then he looked at the dark TV screen, which reflected the room back at them — two people in a nursing home common area at two-thirty in the morning, one in scrubs and one in a wheelchair, the overhead fluorescents washing both of them the same flat institutional white.

"Go do your rounds, Carmen," he said.

She went. The hallway was quiet, the half-light casting long shadows from the handrails. At the nurse's station, Devon was still asleep, the hunting podcast finished, his phone screen dark. Carmen sat down at the charting desk and picked up her pen and looked at the patient log and didn't write anything for a long time.

Outside, the parking lot was empty where Marcus's car had been. The wind moved the last leaves across the asphalt and they made a dry scraping sound that she could hear through the window, or thought she could — the sound of things that had held on as long as they were able, letting go.
