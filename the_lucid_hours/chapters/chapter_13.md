# Chapter 13

Marcus was sitting in the vinyl chair closest to the door when Carmen came down the hall at quarter past midnight. Marcus didn't sit in meetings — Marcus paced, shifted his weight, leaned against walls and pushed off again like a man who couldn't afford to stop moving. Tonight he had both hands on his knees and his feet planted flat.

The TV room was cold. October had arrived while facilities management debated switching the heating system from its summer settings, and the result was a building that held the day's chill instead of releasing it. The overhead fluorescents were off. They used the television's standby light and a brass lamp Agnes had brought from her room months ago, its shade throwing amber across the card table and leaving the rest of the room in blue-gray shadow. The single-pane windows were black, and when Carmen passed close to one she could feel the cold radiating off it like the opposite of warmth, a presence rather than an absence.

Harry was at the table in his wheelchair, though he'd pushed back from it by a few inches — not close enough to work, not far enough to be out of the circle. A calculation in furniture. Agnes sat beside him with only a coffee cup, no notebooks, no pill organizer. Earl was against the far wall, arms crossed, legs extended in his institutional slippers. Next to him, Burt's empty wheelchair was folded and leaned against the baseboard. Nobody mentioned it. Nobody had mentioned it for two weeks, not since Burt had come back from the hospital with his eyes fogged over and his hands forgetting what they were holding. The empty wheelchair was just a shape in the room now, like the TV nobody watched or the fake plant by the window that had been there since before any of them arrived.

Lola was at her position by the hallway door, but she kept turning back toward the group with quick glances that made her tremor visible even in the low light. Her left hand opened and closed around the gold crucifix at her throat.

Marcus had come in during visiting hours — signed the log as Lola's nephew at four-thirty, stayed through dinner, and melted into the building the way he always did. Devon, the overnight aide, had been asleep at the nurses' station since eleven, earbuds in, a true-crime podcast murmuring through tiny speakers. Carmen had heard it when she passed on her med check.

"So," Harry said.

Marcus looked at the floor, then at Carmen, then at Harry. He was checking the room's temperature before he opened his mouth. Carmen had seen him do this with buyers in the parking lot descriptions Harry gave her — read the space, calibrate, then talk. Tonight the calibration took longer.

"I got approached," Marcus said. "By a buyer. Not like our regular people."

Agnes's fingers tightened around her cup.

"A reseller," Marcus said. "Club scene, maybe college towns. I didn't get his whole biography. He's got his own customers who want oxy. Steady demand. He wants tens mostly. Says he'll take fives too if that's what I have, but he priced it simple: eighteen for a ten, nine for a five."

"How much volume," Harry said.

"Two hundred ten-milligram tabs a month to start," Marcus said. "Or the five-milligram equivalent. Same money either way."

Agnes set her coffee cup on the card table. It clicked.

"That's—" Carmen started.

"Thirty-six hundred a month just on oxy," Harry said. "On top of the four grand we're already pulling."

"Seventy-six hundred total if everything else keeps moving." Marcus stood up. There was the pacing — he crossed to the window, turned, crossed back. His sneakers were quiet on the linoleum but Carmen could feel the movement as spatial pressure, the room getting smaller with each pass. "Look, that's double. Harry, you could clear Jack's debt in a few months. Earl, Denise could drop the weekend shifts. Everybody breathes."

"Not everybody," Agnes said.

"Come on, Agnes—"

"I am not finished." She did not raise her voice. The room adjusted anyway. "Clubs. College towns. Those are not patients who lost prescriptions. Those are recreational users."

"Some of them could still have—"

"No." Agnes leaned forward. "Those are people buying oxycodone to get high, then mixing it with alcohol or alprazolam because they think five milligrams sounds small. It is not small. Forty milligrams can kill an opioid-naive person. Eighty if they have been drinking. That is four tens. That is eight fives. That is one Saturday night."

The room held the number. Carmen felt it settle behind her ribs.

Marcus stopped pacing. "We don't know that's what he does. We don't know who he's selling to—"

"We know that you do not know," Agnes said. "That is enough. When I sort pills for Rosa, I know her condition, her weight, her other medications. If I hand this man two hundred tablets and he walks away, I know nothing after that. I have spent my career making sure that does not happen."

"Agnes." Marcus held both hands up. "I hear you. I do. But we're already moving controlled meds out of a nursing home. We crossed a line a long time ago."

"We crossed a line," Carmen said. "Not this one."

Marcus turned to her. His jaw flexed once. "You sure? Because from where I'm standing, the only difference between Janine and this guy is Janine lets you sleep after."

"The difference is Janine has chronic pain and a chart."

"A chart in a building we already steal from."

"We know what it's for."

"So now we're pharmacists?"

Carmen thumbed the edge of her badge through her scrub top. "Agnes is."

Marcus looked at her for a beat too long.

Harry tapped the table twice. The taps were soft and measured. Everybody stopped.

"Two questions," he said. "First, what does this do to our exposure. Second, what does it do to us."

"The exposure's manageable," Marcus said. He sat back down. His leg bounced. "I handle him like anybody else. Separate phone, different location, cash only. He doesn't know about Lakeview, doesn't know where the supply comes from—"

"He knows about you," Agnes said. "That is enough."

Manageable. Carmen had heard administrators use that word about scabies, falls, staffing holes. It always meant the problem was going to land on whoever still had a license.

"He knows a first name and a number."

"He is a drug dealer, Marcus. That matters. He gets arrested, one of his customers gets arrested, somebody wants a deal, names start moving up the chain. You are not adding one customer. You are adding everyone after him."

Carmen pictured a Board packet on cheap white paper with her license number at the top and the controlled-substance log clipped behind it. One arrest. One bargain. One person saying Marcus's name.

"Every buyer we have now could talk."

"Not like this. Rosa gets caught with hydroxychloroquine, that is possession and maybe pity. Your reseller gets caught with two hundred oxycodone, that is trafficking. Trafficking goes upstream. They find you. You bring them here."

Marcus rubbed the back of his neck. His leg kept bouncing. "You're assuming the worst case."

"I am assuming the likely case. Do you know this person's real name?"

"I know what he goes by."

"Do you know whether he has prior convictions? Whether he is currently under investigation? Whether he was sent to you by someone cooperating with law enforcement?"

Marcus did not answer. His leg stopped.

He didn't know. That hit Carmen harder than the money. Up to then she had still been pretending there was some procedure to this, some shape. There was a man in a parking lot and Marcus did not know his real name.

"A man you have never dealt with before approached you and offered premium prices for Schedule II opioids in quantity," Agnes said. "You did not find him. He found you. That should bother you more than it does."

Marcus leaned forward. Both elbows on his knees. When he spoke, the jokes were gone.

"Likely case?" He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Okay. My likely case is I get stopped on Route 1 because the brake light's still out. Two months now. I keep meaning to fix it and then rent's due or the tank's empty or some other shit comes first. I've got Schedule II pills in the glove box. I'm the one standing on asphalt with my hands where a cop can see them. Me. Not Harry. Not Agnes. Not..." He glanced at Carmen. "Not any of you. They put cameras in here, it's poor old residents manipulated by some outside operator. They put cameras on me, I'm the dealer going to Rahway."

The room was quiet except for Earl's breathing, the rasp underneath each exhale, the faint wet whistle Carmen heard every time and kept meaning to say something about.

"You carry more risk," Harry said. His voice stayed neutral. "That's true."

Marcus looked at him, quick, then down at the floor.

"But that's separate from this buyer," Harry said. "The risk is not just yours anymore. It's the count discrepancy on Carmen's log. It's inventory that does not balance. Two hundred extra oxycodone a month is not a documentation error. It is a pattern. Patterns trigger DEA referrals."

"So we adjust the—"

"There is no adjustment that covers two hundred pills a month, Marcus. The arithmetic does not work. I have been running the numbers on what we can move without tripping automated flags for fourteen months. We are close to the ceiling now."

Under the brass lamp Marcus still looked neat — jacket clean, collar straight, nails trimmed. It was a former CNA habit, the kind of neatness that made families trust you with a parent. Carmen believed the part of him that hated carrying the risk. She believed the part that wanted the money too.

"You know what, Carmen?" His voice went tight. "You got here late. Harry had this running, Agnes built the supply side, I built the routes. You walked in after and now you get to decide what kind of crime counts as care."

"That's not—"

"You're pulling thirteen hundred extra a month out of this situation and telling me where the moral line is."

Carmen pinched the badge clipped under her scrub top until the plastic edge bit her thumb. "I do not take money from this. You know that."

"Right. You just take the part where nobody asks why your controlled-substance log doesn't add up. That's free, right?"

It wasn't free. The mark from the badge edge stayed in her thumb even after she let go. She kept her mouth shut.

Lola came away from the doorway. She had been standing there the whole time, one hand on the frame, the other working the crucifix, and now she moved into the circle with the unsteady gait Carmen still could not parse — Parkinson's or performance or some blend of both that Lola herself probably could not untangle anymore.

"I don't like this," Lola said. Her words came quick and crowded. "No, I don't, Marcus, listen. We had our ugly little thing and I could keep it straight. Janine with the bad back. Rosa with the lupus, those poor hands all swelled up. I could put faces on it. Ay, that's what I mean. I could lie to Tammy in the morning and still know who I was lying for. This is some man in a parking lot and then some kid I don't know with his head over a bathroom sink and then God knows who after that. Daytime me is already dropping words on the floor. Nighttime me can't carry strangers too."

Her left hand shook harder. She pressed it against her thigh and the tremor only moved into her shoulder. Marcus watched her trying to hold her own body still with one hand. Agnes's fingers loosened from the coffee cup. Harry kept his eyes on the table.

"Lola," he said. "I'm not trying to make it worse. I'm trying to make—"

"I know. I know, mijo." She wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand. "But that doesn't make it right."

Harry looked at Earl.

Earl had not spoken. He had been in the same position against the wall since the meeting started — arms crossed, legs out, his face giving Carmen nothing. His breathing was the loudest thing about him. When Harry said his name, Earl cleared his throat and the clearing turned into a cough, short and hard and wet. He waited it out, wiped his hand on his pajama pants, and looked at Marcus.

"We kept our own covered when this place wouldn't," Earl said. "I'm not building a stranger's business."

The back of Carmen's neck went cold. Earl almost never spent a full sentence unless he meant it to close a door, and hearing him say our own made something ugly shift under her ribs. She had been telling herself for weeks she was still adjacent to this, not inside it.

Lola's thumb stopped moving on the crucifix. Harry's chin dipped once. Marcus stared at Earl like he had expected backup there and found a wall instead.

"That's it?" Marcus said.

Earl looked away. He did not add anything.

"You're sitting here telling me what this is about? I'm the one who—" Marcus stopped, shook his head, started again. "Fine. Okay."

Harry straightened in his wheelchair. His hands went flat on the table again.

"Here's what's going to happen," Harry said. "You are going to decline the buyer. You are going to tell him the supply is not available. You break the contact. No second meeting. No counteroffer. No keeping his number for later."

"Harry—"

"That is the decision."

"It's the wrong one."

"Maybe. We have survived wrong decisions before. We will not survive a trafficking investigation."

For one second Carmen wanted to let Harry finish it without her. She wanted him to be the one who said no and carried the weight. But her badge was under her scrub top, her name sat on the logs, and silence had started to feel like a signature.

Carmen had been running the legal calculation the whole time: her name on every dispensation record, her license number on every controlled-substance log, the Board file that would start with her if any of this came back through a reseller's arrest.

"Marcus." She waited until he looked at her. "If you take this deal, I report everything. I go to the Board. I hand over the diversion records, the dates, the quantities. All of it."

The room went still. Even Earl's breathing seemed to quiet, though Carmen knew that was her own attention narrowing.

"You'd go down too," Marcus said.

"I know."

"You'd lose your license." He paused. "You'd lose Reggie."

Her son's name in this room, in this argument, at one-thirty in the morning in a nursing home where she was supposed to be administering care. She felt the badge under her scrub top pressing against her sternum.

"I know what I would lose," she said.

"And you'd still do it."

"I am telling you what I will do."

Harry was watching her. Completely still. Agnes had both hands flat on the table, her coffee forgotten. Lola was crying, silently, one hand on the crucifix and the other pressed against her stomach. Earl was looking at the wall.

Marcus looked around the room. Harry sat fixed behind his calculations. Agnes did not blink. Earl had gone back into silence. Lola was crying because of him. Carmen waited.

"Okay," Marcus said. "Fine. I'll tell him no."

He said it too fast.

Carmen heard it. Agnes heard it too; her eyes narrowed a fraction. Harry took his hands off the table and settled them in his lap.

"I'll call him tomorrow," Marcus said. He was already standing, already straightening his jacket. "I'll shut it down. It's done."

"Good," Harry said.

"Yeah." Marcus adjusted his collar without looking at anyone. "So. We good?"

Harry looked at Lola.

"Lola will walk you out," Harry said.

Lola wiped her face with the back of her hand and went to the hallway door. She leaned out, checked both directions — the lookout muscle, automatic even now — then turned back to Marcus. "Vamos, mijo," she said. He followed her out.

Their footsteps faded down the hall — Lola's shuffle, Marcus's heavier tread — and then there was only the building's night sounds: the ventilation cycling, a call bell pinging in the other wing, the hum of Devon's podcast leaking from the nurses' station.

Agnes picked up the coffee cups and stacked them. "He did not agree," she said. "He bought himself until tomorrow."

Harry looked at her. "I know."

"I will not sort oxycodone for that man," Agnes said. "Not one extra tablet. If reseller volume appears, it appears without my hands on it."

Earl pushed himself off the wall with a grunt. "Then watch the count."

He coughed into his fist, hard enough to fold at the waist, and left before anyone answered. His slippers made no sound on the linoleum.

Harry turned to Carmen. "Med room."

Carmen followed him out of the TV room and around the bend to the sub-station. Devon was still asleep with his head tipped back and one earbud hanging loose, the podcast host whispering about a missing woman no one in the building had time to look for. The medication room smelled like alcohol swabs, old plastic, and the chalky dust that lived on blister packs no matter how often she wiped the shelves. She unlocked the cabinet. Harry waited in the doorway with his chair angled sideways so the footrests would not clip the jamb. Agnes stood at the sink, hands flat on the counter, not touching anything.

"Since Burt came off," Agnes said, "there is no cushion. Not for oxycodone. He was our highest-yield patient and I already pulled those cards. If Marcus wants reseller volume, he needs a new source or he starts tearing holes in live orders."

The word patient hit harder than resident would have. Carmen slid out the oxycodone cards and lined them on the stainless counter. Ten milligrams. Five. The labels curled at the edges from months of damp hands and disinfectant.

"Then we make the holes visible," Harry said.

"They're visible to me," Carmen said. "That's the problem."

"More visible," Harry said. "Spot count every time he comes in the building. Not just shift change. If one tablet moves wrong, I want it before breakfast."

Carmen looked over her shoulder. "Tammy already clocks when I'm off pattern."

Harry didn't blink. "Then worry about that after you worry about trafficking."

Agnes pushed her glasses up with one finger. "He was right about one thing. Police see him first. The Board sees you first. The outcome is still ruin."

Carmen peeled back the first blister card. The pills sat under the clear plastic, chalky and intact. She counted aloud because if she did not, the numbers turned slippery in her head. "Eight. Sixteen. Twenty-four."

Harry listened with his hands folded on the blanket over his knees. Agnes watched the labels, not the pills. Carmen moved from card to card, matching sheet to plastic, plastic to drawer, drawer to log. No missing pockets. No punctured foil. No substituted tablets. Everything balanced. That only meant nothing had happened yet.

When she finished, she wrote the totals on a clean scrap of printer paper and then copied them to the controlled-substance sheet. Date. Time. Initials. The official line and the unofficial one sat side by side under her hand.

Harry read the scrap without touching it. "Keep that with you till shift change."

"I'm not starting a second record."

"You already did."

She looked at the paper, at her own handwriting, angry because he was right and more angry because he was making her stay right next to him while it happened.

Agnes took off her glasses and polished them with the hem of her cardigan. "If he comes to me asking for more outside the usual names, I will tell him no once. After that I stop speaking to him about product at all."

Product. Agnes said it the way she'd say stool sample or interaction risk, flat and exact.

Harry nodded. "Fine."

Agnes put her glasses back on. "This is not fine."

She left.

Harry stayed in the doorway while Carmen returned the cards to the cabinet. Metal touched metal. Plastic snapped. She locked the door and tugged it twice.

"He was right about the stop risk," Harry said. "Street exposure is his. Paper exposure is yours."

"I know."

"That doesn't make him right about the deal."

"I know that too."

He watched her for a beat. In the bright medication-room light he looked older than he had in the TV room, the skin under his eyes more purple, the cardigan hanging looser across his shoulders.

"If the count moves," he said, "you wake me first. If I can't stop it, you do what you threatened."

"You mean it?"

"Yes."

"Even if it burns you."

His mouth pulled once, not a smile. "Carmen, we're already in it."

Harry backed his chair out and turned toward his room. The wheel squeaked once before he was around the bend.

At the sub-station, the visitor log was still open where the evening receptionist had left it. Marcus's name sat on the line in neat block letters: WEBB, MARCUS. Relationship: nephew. Resident: Dolores Fernandez, 112A. Time in: 4:32 PM. Time out blank. No supervisor initials beside it. No overnight check mark either. Visitor control at Lakeview was mostly a clipboard and a shrug.

Carmen put her finger over the empty box. One line in blue ink and the lie would move from something everybody cooperated with into something she had certified herself. She could write 1:43 AM. She could write refused to sign out tonight. She could circle the entry and hand the whole page to a state investigator and watch the building start to come apart from there.

Instead she closed the book and slid it back under the clipboard tray. Devon snored once and shifted in the chair, the loose earbud dropping to his shoulder. If state walked in right then, they would get sleeping staff, unsigned visitors, and a locked cabinet that balanced on paper. She hated how ordinary that looked.

Carmen stayed in the medication room. She checked on Room 110 — the woman inside breathing with the mechanical rhythm of her oxygen concentrator. She checked on Burt in 116A, asleep with his mouth open, his hands loose on the blanket. Earl's shape was visible in the other bed, the rise and fall of his chest, the rattle audible from the doorway.

Back in the medication room, she counted again. It was the thing she did when she could not do anything else — opened the cabinet, logged the controlled substances, matched the numbers to the sheet. Everything balanced. The oxycodone was where it should be, the alprazolam, the tramadol. The count was clean.

The count was clean and Marcus had agreed too fast and there was now a second set of numbers in her pocket with no legal standing and too much meaning. Outside the medication room window the October dark sat on the parking lot. She was thirty-one years old and in too deep, and the only thing between her and what came next was the hope that a man who had spent the last hour explaining why he deserved more from this operation had not already decided to find it somewhere else.

She put the clipboard back on its hook and locked the cabinet.

She charted the count. She charted it accurately, the way she'd charted everything since the beginning — every number true, every timestamp real, every entry a faithful record of a system that concealed its losses inside the institution's tolerance for error. The charting was honest. Everything around it was not. Accuracy was turning into one more way to keep it running till morning. She closed the chart, put the pen down, and sat very still in the medication room's alcohol-and-pill-dust quiet, waiting for day shift.
