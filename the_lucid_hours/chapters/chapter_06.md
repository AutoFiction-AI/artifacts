# Chapter 6

The visitor badge was still warm from someone else's body when Marcus clipped it to his shirt pocket. At 2:14 PM on a Thursday, he signed the front-desk log in the same blocky capitals he'd used every week for twenty months while the girl at the desk — new, maybe twenty-two, acrylic nails clicking on her phone — didn't look up. He wrote *Lola Fernandez* in the resident column and *nephew* in the relationship column.

He knew this building the way a surgeon knows a body he's opened before. The turn past the activities room where three women sat in wheelchairs watching a cooking show at full volume, the host shouting about risotto while one woman slept and another picked at the vinyl armrest with her thumbnail. The smell shifting as he passed the kitchen — today it was some kind of fish, the institutional version where the breading was thicker than the fillet. The hand sanitizer dispensers every fifteen feet, most of them empty or jammed. He'd reported the empty dispensers twice when he worked here. The second time was part of the complaint.

Lola's room was 112A, shared with Betty Ostrowski, who had severe vascular dementia and spent her days facing the window. Betty's daughter had taped photographs to the wall beside her bed — grandchildren, a dog, a house with a flag out front — but Betty looked through them the way she looked through everything, her eyes tracking something the rest of them couldn't see.

"Mijo!" Lola said when he came in, and her face did the thing it always did, this warm crumpling that made her look ten years younger. She was sitting in the vinyl recliner with a magazine in her lap, *People* from three months ago. Her left hand trembled against the armrest. Some days the tremor was barely there. Today it was visible from the doorway.

He bent down and kissed her cheek. She smelled like the coconut lotion Tammy helped her put on in the mornings, and underneath that, the warm close smell of someone who lived in a room that never fully aired out.

"You look skinny," she said. "You eating?"

"I'm eating fine."

"You look skinny. Sit, sit." She waved at Betty's chair, which Betty wasn't using because Betty was in the dining room, or the hallway, or wherever the aides had wheeled her for the afternoon. Marcus sat. The chair's cushion was split along one seam, foam poking through. He'd put in a maintenance request for that chair nine months before he was fired. It was the same chair.

"How's your week been?" he asked, because this was how it always started — fifteen minutes of visit, of being Lola's nephew who came on Thursdays, of the performance that was also not entirely a performance because she was, in the ways that mattered, the closest thing he had.

"Ay, you know," Lola said. "Same. The chicken on Tuesday was okay. Wednesday they gave us something they called a casserole, but I don't know what was in it. I asked Tammy and she said, 'Mrs. Fernandez, I wouldn't eat that if I was on death row.' Tammy's funny. She's — she's having a hard time, mijo. The husband."

"Ex-husband."

"Ex-husband, right. She doesn't say much but I can tell." Lola touched the gold crucifix at her throat, a gesture so automatic Marcus wasn't sure she knew she did it. "Anyway. I saved you some cookies."

She said this loud enough for the hallway. Marcus smiled.

The cookie tin was on her nightstand, between a framed photograph of Tomás and a plastic cup of water with a bendy straw. It was a blue Danish butter cookie tin, the kind you could buy at any dollar store, and Lola kept it stocked with actual cookies on top — pecan sandies from the commissary, three or four, enough to make the tin look right if anyone glanced inside.

Below the cookies, separated by a folded paper towel, were four ziplock bags. Agnes's handwriting on each bag in black Sharpie: the drug name, milligram, count, expiration date. Oxycodone 5mg x 24. Alprazolam 1mg x 30. Tramadol 50mg x 18. Metformin 500mg x 40. The bags were flat, pressed carefully, no air. Agnes packed them like she was sealing evidence, which, Marcus supposed, she was.

He picked up the tin with one hand, popped a pecan sandie in his mouth with the other. "Lola, these are good."

"The ones from the commissary aren't bad. Not like the ones I used to make. Tomás liked the ones with the — what's it called, the almond flour. I can't get almond flour here."

"I'll bring you some."

"You don't have to do that."

"I want to."

She looked at him with that expression she got sometimes, where the warmth turned into something sharper, something that saw him more clearly than he liked. "You're a good boy, Marcus."

He was forty-four years old and she called him a good boy and it still landed somewhere in his chest that he couldn't defend against. He put the tin in the canvas tote bag he'd brought — the bag also contained a paperback western he'd gotten for Earl, a sleeve of Ritz crackers Harry had requested, and a pair of reading glasses from the dollar store that Lola had asked for last week because her prescription pair had a broken arm.

"I gotta get going," he said. "But I'll call you tonight, okay?"

"Okay, mijo. Drive safe. It's hot out there."

It was. Early September and the parking lot asphalt radiated heat through the soles of his sneakers. Beyond the low hedge at the edge of Lakeview's property, Route 27 shoved its afternoon line of box trucks, landscaper trailers, and impatient sedans toward New Brunswick. Lakeview sat a little back from the road, as if forty yards of scrub grass could disguise what the building smelled like once you got inside. His car was a 2009 Honda Accord with 187,000 miles and a passenger window that didn't go all the way up, leaving a quarter-inch gap that let in exhaust fumes and highway noise. The AC worked on the first and third settings but not the second. He put it on third and sat for a moment with his hands on the wheel, the tote bag on the passenger seat.

He talked to himself in the car. He'd always done this — his mother used to say he came out of the womb in mid-conversation — but it had gotten worse since she died, or maybe not worse, just more noticeable because there was nobody else to answer back.

"Okay," he said. "Rosa first, then Derek, then Janine. Rosa's off at three, so she'll be there by three-thirty. Derek said he's coming straight from work." He checked the prepaid phone — a Nokia flip phone from 2019 that Harry had insisted on because it couldn't be hacked, which Marcus didn't think was true but didn't argue about because you didn't argue with Harry about operational details. One text from Derek: *running 15 late*. One from Rosa: a thumbs-up emoji, which was the only emoji Rosa used.

The strip mall was twenty minutes south on Route 1. He took Route 27 long enough to catch the turnoff, then merged into the wider, dirtier stream of traffic past used-car lots with flapping pennants and the storage facility where he'd kept his mother's furniture for three months before admitting he couldn't afford the unit and donating everything to Goodwill. The plaza was a half-dead stretch of retail anchored by a Dollar General and a nail salon. The parking lot was big enough for the Kmart that used to be there and now sat empty, its sign removed, leaving a ghost outline on the facade. Marcus parked in the back corner near the dumpsters, where two other cars were already waiting.

He knew these people by refill rhythm and parking habits — who backed in for a fast exit, who kept the engine running, who wanted the bag passed low between the seats like that changed what it was. At Lakeview he'd known blood pressures, fall risks, which shoulder somebody favored when you helped them out of bed. Out here he knew who was late on cash, who checked the count in the lot, who needed to be told every single time not to say the drug name out loud.

Rosa Gutierrez got out first, one hand hooked over the top of the door while she levered herself upright. Lupus had made every movement look negotiated. The first time she'd bought from him she'd apologized before she named the drug, as if need itself was rude. Hydroxychloroquine had kept the swelling out of her knuckles and the kidney numbers from getting ugly until the dry-cleaning plant in Perth Amboy closed and her insurance went with it. CVS wanted $412 a month for the same manufacturer Agnes trusted. Marcus got it to her for fifteen dollars a refill and one standing instruction: keep the old prescription bottle in the car and don't say the name in the lot.

"Hey, Rosa."

"Marcus." She came over with her purse tucked high under one arm. Grocery coupons stuck out of the zipper. "Same as last time?"

"Same manufacturer. Agnes checked."

She nodded and tucked the bag away without opening it. Good. Marcus said, "You still got the bottle with you?"

Rosa patted her purse. "I do."

She handed him an envelope. Sixty dollars — two months' worth, because she'd missed last time when her car broke down. She'd written *thank you* on the outside in pencil. Marcus looked at it, put it in his back pocket with the others, and said, "You good on refills? You got enough for the month?"

"Yeah. The metformin, I can get some of that at Walmart for cheap, but the Plaquenil—" She stopped there, where she always stopped when a sentence threatened to sound too much like begging. "Yeah. I'm good."

"All right. You need anything else, you text me."

"I will. Thank you, Marcus."

She drove away in a Nissan Sentra with a dent in the rear quarter panel and a child's car seat in the back. Granddaughter, Marcus thought. Or niece. He didn't ask. Rosa's part of the day was the clean part, the version of this he could explain to himself without much strain: a woman got the pills that kept her hands from closing up on her and he got sixty dollars and some temporary relief from the idea that every exchange he touched had to be filthy all the way through.

Derek pulled in twelve minutes late and backed his F-150 into the slot beside Marcus a little too fast, one brake squeal and then silence. Music thumped once before he cut the engine. He came over still wearing a navy polo with a logo Marcus didn't recognize, an ID badge clipped to his belt, and steel-toed boots powdered white with warehouse dust. The boots told the truth faster than the shirt did: warehouse first, then whatever else paid after. On his second pickup Derek had told Marcus he'd been type 1 since he was eleven and was sick of people acting like insulin coverage meant he was covered, period. The insulin came through work. The metformin his endocrinologist added for secondary glucose management didn't. Every January the deductible reset like a threat with a date on it.

"Yo, what's up." Derek leaned in close as always, as if the quickest way through shame was speed. Marcus handed him the ziplock low between their bodies and Derek started peeling it open with his thumbnail before Marcus had his hand back.

"Don't do that here."

"I'm just checking."

"Check at home."

A white pickup with *PLAZA MAINTENANCE* stenciled on the door rolled slow past the empty Kmart and toward the dumpsters, the driver doing that bored sweep people did when they wanted to remember license plates later if somebody dumped a mattress or started shit. Marcus took the bag right back out of Derek's hands and dropped it between his seat and the console.

"The fuck, man."

"Exactly," Marcus said. "You want to count, you count at home."

The pickup idled long enough to make both of them look straight ahead. Then it kept moving.

Derek exhaled through his nose. "My bad."

Marcus handed the bag back. Derek shoved it into his pocket this time and pulled out a folded twenty.

Marcus looked at it. "It's thirty."

"I know it's thirty. I got twenty." Derek's jaw worked. "They cut us to thirty-two hours. That's part-time, man. I'm doing DoorDash after the warehouse and it's still not—" He stopped and looked out toward the road. "I'll have the rest next time. Plus the full thirty."

Marcus took the twenty.

"I'm good for it," Derek said.

"I know you are."

Derek stayed where he was, not leaving, which Marcus had already started to worry meant another kind of problem. "Hey, my wife's got migraines. Bad. Laying in the dark for two days at a time. Her doctor gave her some shit that doesn't work and she can't get back in for three weeks. My mom had leftover Imitrex but she's almost out. You got anything like that?"

"I don't have sumatriptan." Marcus kept his voice low. "I can ask, but no promises."

"Yeah." Derek rubbed the back of his neck. "She missed two shifts last week. Her boss said one more and she's done. It's not even dramatic. She just throws up and can't see right." He gave a short laugh with nothing funny in it. "American healthcare, baby."

"Don't text me about it. If I hear something, I'll let you know."

"I wasn't gonna text you."

"I'm saying."

Derek nodded once. "Sorry about the ten."

"Don't worry about it."

But Marcus did. A buyer who opened bags in the lot and came up short was exactly the kind of problem Harry liked solving by subtraction. Derek got back in his truck and pulled out fast enough to throw grit against Marcus's door. Marcus watched him go and already knew he wasn't telling Harry about the shortage. If Harry asked, Marcus would either lie or make the missing ten disappear with his own money. He had not decided which one felt worse.

Janine was last, late enough that the shade had moved and left half the lot bare again. She pulled in at 4:10 in a station wagon with a handicap placard hanging from the rearview. Janine Coates, sixty, got out the way she always did, one hand on the seat, one on the door, jaw tight until she was fully upright. Marcus knew the story because Janine told it in updates, a little more every pickup: packaging plant in Edison, line work, L4-L5 blown out, workers' comp decent until the claim closed. Forty dollars a physical therapy session even with her supplement. Eight hundred for an epidural that bought maybe three decent weeks. Gabapentin made her dizzy. The oxycodone didn't solve anything. It gave her enough room to sleep flat and stand at the stove long enough to make eggs.

"Sorry I'm late," she said. "I stopped at CVS first. Wanted to see if the price had magically become my business."

"How much?"

"One eighty-six for twenty-four." She let out a dry little laugh. "The girl at the counter said it like she was reading me the weather."

Marcus held out the ziplock. Oxycodone 5mg, twenty-four tablets. Janine looked at it and left it in his hand.

"Before I put that in my purse, tell me if you can do next Thursday."

"I can't tell you that because I don't know."

"Then ask tonight." She kept one hand on the roof of the car, saving herself the effort of standing there unsupported. "If it's no, fine. But I need the no while I've still got enough left to plan for it. I can ration on purpose. I can't do surprise pain. Surprise pain means I fall, or I don't sleep, or I sit in that recliner and hate everybody."

"I'll ask."

"Tonight, Marcus."

"Yeah. Tonight."

She took the bag then and handed him a red envelope — a hundred and twenty dollars, exact as always. Two other envelopes sat on the passenger seat beside her, windowed and mean-looking. Electric, water. Maybe the credit card she kept swearing she was gonna kill and somehow never could.

"If it's no," she said, settling herself back against the seat, "then I need to know before I start deciding which days I don't leave the house."

"I said I'll ask."

"I heard you. Pain makes me repetitive." She shut the door, then rolled the window down again. "My dog needs his teeth cleaned too, so apparently everybody in this car is falling apart at once."

"What kind of dog?"

"Beagle mix. Thirteen. Breath like a murder weapon." She almost smiled. "Call me when you know. Not tomorrow if you can help it."

"I will."

She drove away. Marcus sat in his car and counted the money twice: sixty from Rosa, twenty from Derek, a hundred and twenty from Janine. Then he pulled his own wallet from the glove box, peeled out a ten he'd meant to keep for gas, and folded it over Derek's shortage so Harry would see a clean thirty if the envelopes got checked. Two hundred and ten in the stack, only two hundred of it actually from buyers. His cut of the larger operation — the portion Harry tracked with his accountant's precision — came to roughly twelve hundred a month, counting all the buyers, not just these three. The twelve hundred covered rent on his basement apartment in North Brunswick, a space he'd found on Craigslist that turned out to be exactly as bad as a nine-hundred-dollar-a-month Craigslist basement apartment in central Jersey would be: low ceilings that he hit with his knuckles when he stretched, a window at ground level that showed him feet and car tires, a bathroom with a shower that drained slow enough that he stood in two inches of water by the time he was done. The remaining three hundred covered gas, food, the phone bill, and whatever was left over, which was nothing.

He sat there another minute, then went into Dollar General because the apartment had milk that had started to smell wrong and nothing in it that qualified as dinner unless you were being generous. He stood in the checkout line behind a woman buying cat litter and grape soda, doing subtraction in his head before each item hit the belt. He put the ibuprofen back once, then took it again because his knees had started talking on stairs. The cashier asked if he wanted the receipt. Marcus said yes because once money got this tight you kept proof of stupid things like dish soap. Twenty-two dollars and some change. Derek's missing ten had just become cereal and half a tank less than he needed.

He drove home. Past the strip mall, past the Wawa where he sometimes bought a hoagie for dinner when he was feeling irresponsible, past the apartments on Livingston Avenue that were nicer than his. Seeing those buildings always made him think of the job that used to keep him just this side of broke. Seven years at Lakeview, CNA work, not nurse money but steady: lifting, turning, cleaning, feeding, the hands-on part the charts turned into neat little lines. He'd been good at it. Quietly good. He knew which residents wanted the lights on at night, which needed the pillow angled for their GERD, who got agitated if you approached from the left, who needed to be talked through the transfer and who needed you to shut up and just help.

What got him fired was giving a shit out loud. June 2024, Wing A was running one aide for twenty-two residents on overnight when the state minimum was one for every ten. Devon was covering both wings alone. Waiting, in a place like that, meant piss, a fall, an oxygen cannula twisted into trouble. Marcus found Mrs. Patterson on her bathroom floor at four in the morning, one side of her scrub top soaked through, hip swelling under skin thin as paper, call light tangled in the sheets where she couldn't reach it. She'd been there an hour. He wrote the complaint that night.

Cliff thanked him for his concern, did nothing, then acted surprised when Marcus went to the ombudsman's office. Lakeview produced paperwork for a second overnight aide, a woman named Marjorie Sims who existed on the schedule and nowhere else Marcus ever saw, and the investigator called the staffing compliant. The next Tuesday Cliff called Marcus hostile, called him insubordinate, and fired him anyway. By the time Marcus turned onto his landlord's street, his jaw hurt from clenching it.

Marcus's apartment was dark when he got home. The basement meant the light was always wrong — too dim in the daytime, too harsh when he turned on the overheads at night. He had a couch from Goodwill, a television on a plastic crate, a kitchen that was a counter and a hot plate and a mini-fridge. His CNA certification hung on the wall in a frame his mother had bought him. She'd been proud of that certificate. She'd told everyone — her friends at church, the woman at the dry cleaner, the guy who ran the bodega — that her son was in healthcare.

The certificate always dragged him back to the Tuesday he came home with his locker stuff in a grocery bag and told her he'd been fired. By then congestive heart failure had her on a whole pharmacy tray: lisinopril in the morning, furosemide twice a day, metoprolol at bedtime, potassium because the furosemide leached it out, warfarin and biweekly INR draws. Four months later she died in her bed in the apartment above the dry cleaner on Hamilton Street, not at a hospital, not with anybody billing by the quarter hour. The television was on because she liked the noise and he couldn't stand the silence. He knew what the change in her breathing meant before it was over and sat there holding her hand anyway.

Lola had called the next day, somehow with his number. Nobody else from Lakeview did. That January she'd passed along Harry's message: Marcus should visit. By then he'd burned through warehouse temp work, two weeks on a landscaping crew before his knees gave out, and a Target night-stocking job that paid eleven-fifty an hour. He'd gone in expecting maybe condolence money, maybe Harry wanting him to make noise at the ombudsman's office again. Instead Harry, a man Marcus had honestly thought was confused, laid out the business proposition like he was explaining somebody's taxes: supply, margins, buyer need, why Marcus was useful. Marcus should have left. Rent was due in five days. He stayed in the chair.

He ate a bowl of cereal standing at the counter because he didn't have a kitchen table and the couch was too low to eat from without spilling. The milk was still cold from the store. Then he sat on the couch and took out the prepaid phone and called Lola.

She picked up on the second ring. He could hear the television in her room — the news, or a game show, one of those sounds that was just Lakeview's ambient hum.

"Everything went good," he said. "Rosa's set. Derek asked about sumatriptan for his wife. Janine wants next Thursday instead of two weeks. I told her I'd ask."

"Good, good." She was quiet for a moment, and he heard the slight rasp of her hand against the phone case. The tremor made it hard for her to hold the phone steady some days. "Agnes wants the count anyway. She said if Janine is moving up, she wants to know what is actually left, not what you think is left."

"Metformin's fine. Forty went to Rosa and Derek. It's oxy that's the question. I told Janine I'd ask, not promise."

"I'll tell her. She'll do the math." Another pause. "Mijo, how's your mom?"

His throat closed. It did this every time, a physical thing, like someone had their thumb on his windpipe. Lola asked about his mother every week. She'd been asking for twenty months. He'd been lying for twenty months.

"She's good," he said. "She's got her — her heart stuff, you know, but she's doing okay. She's watching her shows."

"Tell her I said hi. Tell her she raised a good boy."

"I'll tell her."

On the cushion beside him sat the folded cash and the grocery receipt. He could hear the softer voice Lola would use if he told her the truth: *Ay, mijo.* Then she'd start asking if he was sleeping, if he was eating, saving him half a dinner roll in a napkin for Thursday. He pressed his thumb into the phone hinge and said nothing.

"You eat dinner?" Lola asked.

"Yeah. I had — yeah, I ate."

"Cereal isn't dinner, Marcus."

He laughed, a small, involuntary sound that surprised him. "How'd you know?"

"Because I know you. Go make something real. There's rice, you can make rice. Everybody can make rice."

"I'll make rice."

"With something. Rice with something."

"Rice with something. I promise."

He could hear her smile through the phone. "Okay. Goodnight, mijo. Be safe."

"Goodnight, Lola."

He sat on the couch after she hung up. Then he got up anyway and went to the kitchenette because standing up felt close enough to obedience to count for something. The cabinet over the sink held a ten-pound bag of rice, one can of black beans, garlic powder, a packet of sazon Lola had bullied him into buying because plain rice, she'd said, was hospital food. He touched the bag and left it there. The sink held one bowl, one fork, one pan with dried egg stuck to the bottom. The apartment was quiet except for the boiler in the utility closet on the other side of the wall, which ticked and rumbled at irregular intervals like a mechanical heartbeat with an arrhythmia. Upstairs, his landlord's television murmured. The ceiling was low enough that he could hear the floorboards creak when the landlord's wife walked to the kitchen.

He did not make rice. He went back to the couch with the prepaid phone in his hand and the CNA certificate on the wall staring back at him. Upstairs, his landlord's wife crossed the kitchen again. Marcus bent forward until his elbows hit his knees and stayed there under the low ceiling while the boiler ticked and Lola's voice kept saying *Rice with something.*
