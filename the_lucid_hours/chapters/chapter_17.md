# Chapter 17

Ten days later, Carmen was in the medication room doing the evening count when she heard it. At first it wasn't the words, just the cadence. Every nurse learned the difference between the TV room's daytime murmur and the local news anchor's emergency tone, the slight tightening in the broadcaster's delivery that meant something had happened nearby, something with a suspect, something with a mugshot.

She set down the clipboard and walked to the TV room.

It was mostly empty at 8:40 PM. Dinner was over, the last visitors had signed out at eight, and most of the Wing A residents were back in their rooms. Burt was dozing in the recliner nearest the screen, his mouth open, one hand curled around the armrest as if he were holding on to something in a dream. The volume was up too loud because nobody had adjusted it after Mrs. Petroski went back to her room, and Mrs. Petroski was functionally deaf.

The anchor described an arrest in Edison for prescription drug distribution. A man named DeShaun Briley, thirty-seven, had been picked up during a traffic stop with approximately four hundred tablets of oxycodone, eighty alprazolam, and an undetermined quantity of hydrocodone in the trunk of his vehicle. He was facing possession with intent to distribute and both state and federal charges. Bond had been set at seventy-five thousand.

Carmen didn't recognize the name. She didn't recognize the face in the mugshot, a heavy young man, scared-looking under the flat police lighting, his left eye slightly swollen as if he'd been rubbing it or hadn't slept. But she knew that number. Marcus's new buyer had been taking orders that size.

Her hand went to her badge and stayed there. The news anchor moved on to a car accident on Route 18.

She stood in the doorway another thirty seconds, watching the screen without seeing it, while her mind ran the chain from Briley to Marcus, from Marcus to Lakeview, from Lakeview to the count discrepancies documented under her name, her license number, her signature on every shift-end log for the past seven months. If Briley had Marcus's phone number in his contacts, if he had a text, a single text, with Marcus's name or a location or a time, investigators would pull Marcus's records. And Marcus had visited this building every week for the last fourteen months, signing in at the front desk as Lola's nephew, his name in a visitor log that no one cross-referenced because no one at Lakeview cross-referenced anything. The ring went back further than Marcus's visits. The paperwork did not.

Carmen turned off the TV room light on her way out. Burt didn't stir.

She went to Harry's room. He was sitting up in bed reading the Trenton Times, his faded blue cardigan buttoned wrong, the top button through the second hole, his reading glasses taped at the left temple where the arm had broken six weeks ago. He looked up when Carmen knocked on the open door, and his face told her he already knew.

"You saw it," she said.

"I saw it." He folded the paper and placed it in his lap. "Edison. That's twenty minutes from here."

"It's the volume buyer. The quantities match."

Harry took off his glasses. Without them, his eyes were sharper. The lenses were for reading, but they also softened his face, made it rounder, made him look more like the befuddled old man he played during daylight hours. "I've been trying Marcus's phone for an hour. His personal one. It goes straight to voicemail."

"Since when do you have Marcus's personal number?"

Harry gave her the flat look he reserved for obvious arithmetic. He didn't answer.

"When's the last time he came in?" Carmen asked.

"Ten days ago. He hasn't been here since the meeting." That was what they called it, the night Marcus had been confronted, argued back, and been cut off by Earl in a single sentence that ended more than the conversation. "Sit down, Carmen."

She didn't sit down. She kept one hand on the doorframe, nails pressing into the paint. "If they trace Briley to Marcus, any connection, a phone record, a text, anything, they follow Marcus here. They pull the visitor log and they see his name every week for fourteen months. Then they start asking why a supposed nephew was here that often. They look at the medication records and they see my count discrepancies, documented and signed. Every one of them is mine."

"Sit down."

"I can't sit down right now."

Harry put his glasses on the nightstand and lined them up parallel to the edge, beside a pen and a folded sheet of paper. When he nudged the half-open drawer shut with two fingers, something small and black clicked once against the wood inside. Carmen's eyes went there and away.

"We clean house," he said.

"Everything?"

"Everything we can touch: pills, Agnes's notebooks, the phone. The visitor log and your old count sheets stay where they are."

Carmen leaned against the doorframe. The hallway behind her smelled the way it always did at night, disinfectant and warm skin, the fading trace of dinner's meatloaf, hand sanitizer from the dispensers every fifteen feet, all of it familiar. She'd worked in this hallway for thirteen months and stopped noticing any of it, the same way she'd stopped noticing her own apartment most days. Now each layer came back sharp and separate because she might be leaving all of it under circumstances she didn't choose.

"I'll tell Agnes," she said.

"Tell her about the notebooks."

"I know."

She found Agnes at 9:15. Agnes was still awake. She rarely slept before midnight, a habit from four decades of pharmacy shifts that ended at ten. Her roommate, Mrs. Delacroix, was asleep and snoring wetly in the bed nearest the door, the sound of a woman whose body worked hard just to breathe. Agnes sat upright in the far bed with a crossword puzzle book open on her lap. She hadn't filled in a single square.

"I heard it," Agnes said before Carmen could speak. "The volume on the TV was up. Mrs. Petroski's hearing."

"Harry wants everything gone tonight."

Agnes closed the crossword book. Her hands were steady. The left-side weakness from her stroke showed in her gait when she walked, a slight drag in the foot, but her grip had come back fully within the first year. She placed the book on the nightstand with the same deliberate precision she placed everything. She had pharmacist's hands; everything had its location. "My notebooks."

"Yeah."

"There are four composition books with black-and-white marbled covers." Agnes's voice was clinical, but the words came faster than usual, closer together, compressed under pressure. "They're under my mattress, under the fitted sheet, between the mattress and the mattress pad. They're not under the bed frame."

Carmen looked at her. Agnes's face was what it always was, precise, impatient, stripped clean. But her hands had moved from the crossword book to the mattress edge and were pressing down, her fingertips whitening against the hospital-grade fitted sheet.

"I'll come get them after Devon falls asleep," Carmen said. "Eleven-thirty."

"I'll have them out."

---

Devon was asleep by 11:45. Carmen checked twice, at 11:30 when he was still scrolling his phone with the audio off, and again fifteen minutes later when the phone was face-down on his chest and his breathing had gone slow and even. He'd picked up the overnight because the regular night aide had called out, and Devon worked overnights the way he worked every shift, minimally, resentfully, one earbud in, his attention dimmed to whatever frequency let him avoid getting fired for a third time. He was twenty-three. She thought he'd keep sleeping.

Carmen went to Agnes's room first.

Agnes was standing by her bed in a housecoat that had belonged to someone else. The facility distributed donated clothing to residents whose families didn't bring enough, and Agnes's daughter Helen sent the same three outfits in rotation, all a size too large because Helen still pictured the woman she'd been during stroke recovery, fifteen pounds lighter and unable to say the word "prescription" without it coming out wrong. The housecoat was a faded floral print with a zipper up the front. Agnes held the notebooks out in both hands, palms flat under them so the spines wouldn't bend.

They were the black-and-white marbled composition books sold at every CVS and Walgreens in New Jersey for two dollars and forty-nine cents, the same kind Carmen had bought for Reggie's school supplies in September.

She took them. They were heavier than they looked, filled cover to cover with no blank pages. The top one fell open in her arms and she saw Agnes's handwriting, small, square, pharmacist's print that could have been typed. Inside were columns of drug names with dosages, buyer codes instead of names, just two-letter abbreviations, and expiration dates noted in red ink. In the margins Agnes had written: *Do not combine w/ warfarin. Check renal function. Ro — skip if on new antibiotic, interaction risk. Jn — monitor for signs of tolerance escalation (none observed).* One page held a hand-drawn table of pill counts going back fourteen months, back to when Marcus had started coming weekly and the business needed a paper brain instead of Harry's head and Agnes's memory, each row dated, each column a different controlled substance, every number precise to the individual tablet.

Eighteen months of pharmaceutical judgment sat inside those fourteen months of notebooks. The ring had started before the recordkeeping did, back when it was just Harry and Agnes making sure people got what Lakeview and the pharmacy switch had failed to provide. These books were the later version, the bigger one, the one that could put all of them in prison. They were also the most careful, most specific, most conscientious pharmaceutical records in the building, better than what Carmen charted, better than what Dr. Prasad's prescriptions produced, better than anything the state survey had reviewed.

"I'll burn them behind the loading dock," Carmen said.

Agnes looked at the notebooks in Carmen's arms. She was a small woman, five-two, gray hair cut short because she could manage it without help, wire-rimmed glasses reflecting the hallway's fluorescent light. She didn't say anything.

"Agnes."

"Go," Agnes said. Her voice was flat and fast.

Carmen went.

She started with the pills. Harry had the remaining stockpile in two shoeboxes in the back of his closet, behind a row of Reader's Digest condensed books that had come with the room. The shoeboxes were the part they could still erase, hidden stock that tied the signed discrepancies in the med binder to a real operation. She retrieved them while Harry sat in his wheelchair by the window, watching the dark parking lot.

"Thirty-six oxycodone," he said without turning around. "Fourteen alprazolam. Twenty-one tramadol. Some non-controlled."

"It doesn't matter anymore, Harry."

"No." He turned the wheelchair around. His hands were on the wheels, and the motion was smooth but slow. Carmen couldn't tell if the slowness was exhaustion or age or the performance settling back into his body. "I suppose it doesn't."

She carried the shoeboxes to the handicapped bathroom at the end of Wing A and locked the door. The first batches went down cleanly. Oxycodone, then alprazolam, then tramadol, then the loose metformin and lisinopril and rosuvastatin in their separate plastic baggies, each flush spaced a little because Harry was right about the pipes and the pipes were older than everybody left on this hall.

The fourth flush didn't take.

The bowl filled instead of emptied. Blue water climbed toward the rim, cloudy with chalk and half-softened tablets that knocked lightly against the porcelain. Carmen stared one half second, then dropped to her knees and reached in with gloved hands because if that water hit the tile the whole wing would smell medicinal by morning. The pipes hammered in the wall hard enough to shake the grab bar. Out in the hall, a chair scraped.

She froze. Devon.

A knuckle tapped twice on the door. Lola's whisper came through the wood, low and quick. "Mija. He's awake."

"Shit."

Carmen scooped what she could into a stainless emesis basin, yanked the remaining baggies back into the shoeboxes, unlocked the door, and stepped into the hall. Lola stood at the junction with her mug braced in both hands, her tremor sending little rings across the coffee. Earl was behind her, one hand on the wall, already breathing through his mouth.

"The toilet backed up," Carmen said. "I can't flush the rest."

Earl looked at the shoeboxes, then at her face. "What do you need?"

"The linen cart. Park it here. If Devon comes down this hall, tell him Burt shit through everything and I need supplies."

Earl nodded once and went, moving faster than Carmen had seen him move in months. The speed cost him immediately. By the time he reached the clean-linen alcove he was coughing, not the little cover cough, the real one, deep and wet and tearing. He bent over the cart handle until it passed, then shoved the cart down the hall anyway.

Lola tightened her grip on the mug. "He asked what the noise was."

"Keep him talking. Five minutes."

"About what?"

"Anything."

Lola gave a small, humorless breath that might once have been a laugh. "That's the part I know how to do."

Carmen abandoned the bathroom. She left Earl's linen cart crosswise in front of the door and shoved the shoeboxes into the soiled utility room, a narrow sour-smelling closet with the hopper sink bolted low into the floor. The hopper was built for bedpans, vomit basins, wash water streaked brown or pink or whatever color a human body turned the world once it stopped obeying. It could take this.

She dumped the tablets into a stainless basin, ran hot water over them, splashed in bleach from the housekeeping shelf, and worked the mess with a tongue depressor until the pills turned to gritty paste. The smell hit fast, pharmacy dust under public-pool chlorine, something chemical and wrong that clawed the back of her throat. She sluiced the sludge into the hopper in three loads, ran more water, and watched the gray-white paste vanish. The blister packs and torn baggies went into a red trash sack under two used incontinent briefs from the morning-care bin because this building generated so much shame and waste every day that one more bag disappearing into the dumpster wouldn't make a dent.

Someone said, from the hall, "Jesus Christ, what died?"

It was Devon.

Carmen stepped out of the utility room with bleach on her gloves and the red sack twisted shut in one hand. Devon was halfway up the corridor in wrinkled scrubs, earbud still in one ear, his hair flattened on one side from the desk. Earl stood between him and the blocked bathroom, one hand on the linen cart, shoulders hitched high from the coughing fit he'd just finished. Lola hovered at the junction with the coffee mug tight to her chest.

"Mr. Kovacs had an accident," Carmen said before Devon could ask again. She didn't rush it. She used her work voice, competent, tired, slightly annoyed. "Bathroom backed up while I was cleaning him. I need another pack of wipes from central and a clean fitted sheet for 116A."

Devon looked past her toward the cart, toward Earl, toward the closed bathroom door. His face pinched the way it always did around bodily labor, disgust first, then laziness, then the fast mental math of whether someone else could be made to do it.

"Burt did that?" he said.

"You want to come smell him and find out?"

He recoiled. "No."

"Then get the wipes."

He swore under his breath and shuffled toward the supply room. Carmen waited until he disappeared around the corner before she let herself breathe all the way out. Earl took one hand off the cart and pressed a folded handkerchief to his mouth. The cough hit him again, heavier this time, and he leaned into it with his whole body. When it passed, he folded the handkerchief back up without looking first. Carmen saw the deliberate not-looking and felt something cold move through her gut.

"Earl."

"I'm fine," he said. He said it once, flat and finished.

"No, you are not."

He put the handkerchief away. "Five minutes, you said."

Carmen took the red sack to the back door, dumped it under kitchen trash and broken-down boxes in the dumpster, and came back inside. Lola was still at the junction, still holding the mug, though the coffee had long since gone cold.

"How much longer?" Lola asked.

"Almost done. Keep him away from the loading dock."

Lola nodded. Her crucifix clicked against the mug. "He thinks Burt shit himself."

"Good."

Carmen retrieved the notebooks from the supply-closet shelf and went out the back. The loading dock sat in the spill of a single yellow bulb, all concrete, rusting ramp, dumpster, chain-link fence, and dead grass, invisible from the front.

She dragged a metal trash can to the center of the dock, tore pages from the first notebook, and lit them with a match from the break room. The smoke went white and thin and smelled like school paper.

She was midway through the second notebook when Agnes appeared at the back door.

Carmen hadn't heard it open. Agnes stood at the edge of the loading dock in her donated housecoat and slippers, her arms at her sides, the cold November air moving through the thin fabric. Firelight caught her glasses and made her eyes disappear behind two small rectangles of reflected orange.

Carmen stopped tearing pages. "You should be inside. It's cold."

Agnes didn't move. She watched the fire. The pages curled, blackened, fell apart, fourteen months of handwriting, and behind that the four unrecorded months before the notebooks existed, when she and Harry had been carrying the whole thing in their heads, catalogues of medications and dosage calculations and interaction flags, the whole pharmaceutical architecture of the operation resisting for a second before it gave.

"I started those notebooks four months after we started," Agnes said. Her voice was faster than usual, the words closer together, more data per sentence. Her hands were at her sides and not sorting, not organizing, not giving themselves a job. "Harry had his arithmetic. I had mine. That was enough until Marcus began coming every week and enough stopped being enough. I put fourteen months of inventory on paper. It has been eighteen months since the first time I corrected a dangerous interaction in this building and understood nobody else was going to keep these people alive." She looked at the burning pages, not at Carmen. "Every tablet was accounted for. Every interaction was flagged. Every expiration date was tracked to the day. I maintained buyer profiles for Rosa's lupus schedule, Derek's insulin timing and when the metformin had to coordinate, and Janine's tolerance levels. I was monitoring for signs of escalating use, which I did not observe."

Carmen fed another fistful of pages into the can. The draft pulled the flame up hard enough to sting her knuckles.

"Nobody asked me to keep those records," Agnes said. "I kept them because that is what a pharmacist does."

The marbled cover caught at one corner, burned slow, then gave way all at once. Carmen could hear the faint crackle of glue in the binding.

"This is the only professional work I have done in three years," Agnes said.

Carmen looked at her. Agnes did not fold her arms against the cold or step back from the smoke. The toes of her slippers darkened against the damp concrete, and she kept watching the pages cave in.

Carmen went back to burning. By twenty past one, all four notebooks were ash. Agnes stayed there until the covers blackened, curled, and dropped into gray powder at the bottom of the can. Then she turned and went back inside without saying goodnight.

Earl appeared in the hallway as Carmen came through the back door, carrying Lola's cookie tin.

"Lola said to get rid of it," he said. He was leaning against the wall again, one hand braced on the linen-closet frame. His breathing was shallow and wheezy.

"I'll take it." She held out her hand and he gave her the tin.

"Devon's back at the desk."

"I know."

She dumped the tin in the dumpster, came back in, and found the prepaid phone in Harry's room where he'd left it under the Reader's Digest books. She deleted the seventeen texts in the medication room instead of outside because Devon was awake now and moving around. Then she carried the cheap flip phone to the loading dock, crushed it under her heel, snapped the SIM card in half, and threw both into the dumpster.

When she came back inside, her hands smelled like smoke and hot plastic and bleach. The orange antibacterial soap only rearranged the smell. She washed twice anyway.

---

By four in the morning, it was done, except for the handicapped bathroom still blocked off by a linen cart and a handwritten OUT OF ORDER note Carmen had peeled off a housekeeping clipboard.

She sat in the TV room with the overhead light off and the television dark. The room was lit only by the hallway fluorescents coming through the open door, a blue-white half-light that made the vinyl chairs look colorless and the walls look like they were receding. She could hear the building's heating system, Burt's snoring carrying down the hall from 116A, and, under everything else, Earl's cough. One cough, then silence, then two more, muffled by the wall and the closed door but audible in the four A.M. stillness of a building where nobody was supposed to be awake.

Harry wheeled himself in at 4:15. He'd changed into his blue cardigan, the good one, the one he wore to every ring meeting. He parked by the card table where Agnes used to spread out her notebooks and the ziplock bags and the envelopes of cash. The table was bare except for an old coffee ring, and Harry looked at it before he looked at Carmen.

"Done?" he said.

"As done as it's getting," she said. "The bathroom backed up. I had to move the rest to the hopper. Devon woke up. Lola stalled him and Earl covered for me."

Harry's face changed very little, but it changed. "Did he see anything?"

"He saw enough to know something smelled bad and I was still cleaning at one in the morning."

Harry nodded once.

"This is over," Carmen said. "No more pills. No more buyers. No more pretending this was under control."

He looked at the empty table. "It was good while it lasted."

"No, it wasn't." The answer came out before she thought about it. "It was necessary. That's different."

Harry rubbed his thumb once over the seam of his wheelchair armrest. "Sometimes it isn't."

She laughed once through her nose, no humor in it. "Ask Agnes if that felt good. Ask Earl."

He didn't answer. Real Harry's silences were choices, not absences.

Carmen leaned forward, elbows on her knees. "Listen to me. In the morning I'm putting Earl in for a chest X-ray. Not next week. Not when he feels like it. Morning. And Agnes is getting a weight check and nutrition note because she's going to stop eating and act like it's not happening. If either one of them gives me trouble, you do not talk them out of it. You do not tell them to wait. You do not run a side operation out of the damage from this one."

Harry looked at her then, directly. "You're setting terms."

"I'm doing my job."

"You were doing your job before."

"No," Carmen said. "I was doing yours too."

The words sat there. She hadn't meant to say them that cleanly, and once they were out she couldn't soften them without lying.

Harry lowered his eyes to his hands. The skin over his knuckles looked almost translucent in the hallway light. "If Tammy asks questions?"

"I tell her the bathroom backed up and Burt shit himself and I was here cleaning it up. That's the lie available. I'm not building you a better one."

He gave the smallest nod. Agreement, or maybe just comprehension.

They sat a minute longer. The building settled around them, heating system cycling, a mattress squeaking somewhere down the hall, the small mechanical sounds of a facility built in the late seventies and maintained only enough to pass inspection. Through the window the parking lot was dark and empty. Beyond it, the courtyard's three trees stood bare against the brown suburban sky.

"What are you going to do?" Harry asked.

"Go home. Sleep a little. Drop off Reggie's lunch. Come back."

"That's not what I mean."

She knew what he meant. Leave, maybe. Apply out. Save herself while there was still a version of that available. She didn't answer. She tightened her ponytail with both hands and looked at the card table. Beside the coffee ring the laminate held a few neat dents where Agnes had pressed hard enough with her pen to mark the surface through the pages. Carmen set her thumb over them, then pulled her hand back.

"I'm going to check on everyone," she said, and stood.

"Carmen."

She stopped at the doorway.

Harry was looking at the bare table again, not at her. "Thank you," he said.

She didn't answer that either. She left.

Agnes was in bed, awake, staring at the ceiling with her glasses still on. The ceiling was acoustic tile, water-stained in one corner from a leak maintenance had patched but not painted. Mrs. Delacroix's snoring was the only sound. Agnes's housecoat hung over the chair by the window, one sleeve dusted with gray ash. Carmen stood in the doorway a second, then moved on.

Lola's room. Lola was asleep, curled on her right side, the rosary wound through her left hand. Her right hand rested on the pillow near her face, and even in sleep the tremor was there, a fine vibration in her fingers that moved the beads against the cotton pillowcase with a faint clicking sound, something small and persistent ticking down.

Earl and Burt in 116A. Burt was asleep, had been asleep through everything, his decline so steady now that the ring ending meant nothing to a man who could no longer remember what the ring was. He lay on his back with his mouth open, his wispy white hair pressed flat against the pillow, his long musician's hands slack on the blanket. Earl was in the other bed with his eyes closed, but his breathing was wrong, too controlled, too careful, the breathing of a man pretending to sleep so nobody would ask how he was feeling. Carmen stood in the doorway and listened to that breathing and heard, beneath it, the faint wet rattle that had been there for months and was not getting better.

She went back to the nurse's station and did her charting. Overnight rounds: checked on Mr. Kovacs at eleven, one, and three. Checked on Mr. Washington, Mrs. Fernandez, Mrs. Chen. Medication count documented as correct for the cart and cabinet at end of shift. There was now also a maintenance slip for the blocked bathroom and a brief nursing note on Mr. Washington's cough that she had not run by Harry and did not intend to. The discrepancy reports from earlier shifts were still clipped in the binder under her name. What was gone was the off-book evidence, the shoeboxes in Harry's closet, Agnes's notebooks, Marcus's texts. She signed the log with the same signature she signed everything, C. Reyes, LPN, and put the clipboard back on its hook.

The front door beeped at 5:48 AM.

Tammy came around the corner in scrub pants and a winter coat, travel mug in one hand. She set the mug on the desk, hung her coat in the locker-room nook, clipped on her badge, and stopped when she saw Carmen.

"You're still here."

"Picked up the overnight." The excuse came out too easily. "Devon needed coverage and I was already—"

She stopped.

"I stayed," she said.

Tammy looked at the loose hair, the red around Carmen's eyes, the bleach stain on one scrub cuff she hadn't noticed, the smoke still caught in her scrubs. Then her gaze shifted to the note on the housekeeping clipboard, to the linen cart still blocking the bathroom down the hall, back to Carmen.

"What happened to Wing A?" Tammy asked.

"Bathroom backed up. Burt had an accident. It turned into a whole thing."

Tammy kept looking at her. Not skeptical exactly. Measuring.

"You smell like a bonfire," Tammy said.

Carmen lifted one shoulder. "Dumpster fire out back. Kitchen trash."

Tammy took that in, or pretended to. Then she picked up her mug again. "Right."

It wasn't agreement. It wasn't forgiveness either. It was the kind of word people used when they had decided not to help you tell the story cleaner than it was.

Tammy put her bag in her locker. She started her rounds.

Carmen gathered her things. She signed out on the overnight log. She put on her coat, the same black puffy Old Navy jacket she'd worn last November, zipper still broken, and slung her bag over her shoulder.

She passed Harry's room.

The door was mostly closed. Lakeview policy required doors to remain openable from the hall, not shut fully, but Harry had pulled it to within an inch. Through the gap, in the strip of visible room, Carmen saw him sitting in his wheelchair by the nightstand. He had something in his hand.

It wasn't a new object after all. It was the same black hinge she'd glimpsed in the nightstand drawer earlier, the thing that had clicked once against the wood when he told her they needed to clean house. It was a phone, not the prepaid, which was in pieces in the dumpster behind the building, but a different one, small and black, a flip phone of the same cheap kind. He was holding it to his ear.

He was talking. His voice was low and Carmen couldn't hear the words from the hallway, just the rhythm, the cadence of short sentences delivered with the controlled precision of his real register. It wasn't the performance, the wandering mumble about Eleanor, what day it was, and who these people were. It was the real Harry, at ten minutes to six in the morning, calling someone on a phone he'd kept out of the cleanup, saying things she couldn't hear to a person she couldn't identify.

She stood in the hallway for three seconds. She could have pushed the door open. She could have walked in and asked who he was talking to and why there was another phone and whether this call was a contingency, a warning, an apology, or the first move in starting the whole thing again.

She didn't.

She kept moving. At the front door she pushed out into the morning. The parking lot was dark and cold and the wind came across it flat and unbroken. The courtyard's bare trees did nothing, and the building didn't block it from this angle. Carmen walked to her car with her coat open because the zipper was broken and her hands in her pockets because they were cold and still smelled like smoke despite the soap, despite the bleach, despite the washing, despite the fact that the notebooks and the pills and the phone were all gone now and there was nothing left to smell like except the memory of having done it.

She got in the car and sat with her hands on the steering wheel and the engine off. The dashboard clock said 5:53. In an hour and seven minutes she needed to be at her mother's house to drop off Reggie's lunch, and before that she needed enough sleep to get through the next set of meds without making a mistake that lived on paper.

She started the car. She drove home.
