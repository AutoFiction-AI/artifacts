# Chapter 15

Earl woke at 5:14. Fifty years on the plant's schedule had fixed that hour in him, and nothing since — not retirement, not Maryann dying, not this place — had reset it. His body was a shift worker's body. It didn't know how to sleep past the whistle.

The room was dark except for the bar of hallway light under the door. Burt breathed in the other bed, a shallow wet sound like a straw pulled through the last of something. Since the fall, Burt slept fourteen, fifteen hours a day. When he was awake he spoke in Hungarian — to his dead wife, to people Earl couldn't see. The doctors said the concussion had accelerated what was already happening. The word they used was "trajectory." Earl understood the word. He'd seen enough trajectories. Steel cooling too fast, a man's back giving out at fifty-two, a local folding after the last round of layoffs. Things headed one direction.

He lay still for ten minutes, listening. The overnight nurse — not Carmen, the one named Janet who walked heavy in her sneakers — passed the door at 5:21. A call bell rang down the hall. The heating system ticked in the walls, radiators warming up for the morning, the building's pipes expanding with a sound like knuckles cracking.

Then the cough came.

It started low, below the ribs, a pressure that built before it broke. Earl sat up and pressed the handkerchief to his mouth and let it work through him. Deep, wet, a sound like something loose and heavy turning over in his chest. His eyes watered. His shoulders seized. He coughed until the muscles between his ribs burned, and then he coughed past that, because the cough didn't care about pain — it had its own schedule, its own clock, and it ran until it was done.

Burt didn't stir. Six months ago, Burt would have said something — "Earl, you all right?" or muttered in English about the noise. Now nothing. The room took the sound in with everything else: the smell of disinfectant and old skin, the hum of Burt's oxygen concentrator, the thinning light of late October coming through a window that faced the parking lot.

Earl got up and went to the bathroom. He closed the door and turned on the light and held the handkerchief under the fluorescent bar.

White. Clean.

He checked the folds with the same care he'd once given a weld seam, looking for the stain that meant the joint was bleeding. Nothing. He folded the handkerchief into quarters and put it in the pocket of the pants he'd laid on the towel bar the night before. Khakis, a flannel shirt, the brown jacket that still fit because he hadn't gained a pound in thirty years — just lost them, slowly. That was how things left you in here.

It was outing day. Last Thursday of the month.

He'd set everything out before lights-out: the clothes, the jacket, and in the jacket's inside pocket, the greeting card. He checked it now, fingers finding the envelope by feel. A generic card — "Thinking of You" — bought from the rack in the lobby near the front desk, where Lakeview sold greeting cards and puzzle books and small stuffed animals for visiting grandchildren. Inside the card, Earl had written "Love, Dad" and nothing else, because Denise wouldn't see it and the person who would didn't need a message. Taped flat against the inside of the card, covered by a second piece of cardstock Earl had cut from a cereal box, were forty-six alprazolam tablets and twenty oxycodone. Each pill fixed to a strip of medical tape, arranged in rows like a sheet of postage stamps. Agnes had counted them out of the bottles she still allowed into the count, most of it Earl's own. Burt's had been out since the fall. Earl had taped them. The work had taken twenty minutes the night before, seated on the edge of his bed while Burt muttered about Budapest.

He dressed. He shaved with the electric razor the facility required — no blades, fall risk, as if he hadn't handled a straight razor for fifty years without cutting himself. The razor buzzed against his jaw and he looked at himself in the mirror: an old man, tall but stooped now, the stoop getting worse each month. His face was thinner than it had been in September. He could see that. The cheekbones more prominent, the skin looser along the jaw. Eighty-one. He'd been bigger once. Two hundred and ten pounds on the shop floor, arms that could hold a beam steady while the crane operator repositioned. Now one-sixty, maybe less. He didn't weigh himself. He didn't want the number.

He brushed his teeth, rinsed, spat. He went back into the room and sat in the chair by the window and waited.

---

The van left at ten. Phil, the maintenance man who doubled as driver, had it running in the circle drive with the heat on. Phil was fifty-eight and had a bad knee from a fall off a scaffold in '09 — not a football story, not a war story, just a man who climbed a scaffold for fifteen dollars an hour and came down wrong. He walked with a careful, testing limp that worsened in the cold, and he kept AM sports talk on the radio at a volume just above silence, a murmur of scores and opinions, low and constant as pipe noise in the halls.

Earl was the only passenger. He climbed into the second row, holding the grab bar with his left hand. Phil waited, watching in the rearview, but didn't offer help. Phil had driven Earl to the cemetery seven times and had learned early that Earl didn't want a hand up.

"Same place?" Phil said.

"Yeah."

They pulled out of the circle drive and onto Route 27. The trees along the road had turned, but the color was already going — the maples brown and bare in patches, the oaks holding yellow leaves that rattled in a wind Earl couldn't feel through the van's windows. The sky was white-gray, the kind of end-of-October sky that sat on central New Jersey like a lid. He watched the strip malls go by. A nail salon, a check-cashing place, a laundromat with a hand-lettered sign in the window: WASH & FOLD $1.25/LB. He'd used a place like that once, after Maryann went to the hospital the last time and he was doing his own laundry for the first time in forty years. He'd stood at the counter with a garbage bag of clothes and the woman had weighed it and told him the price and he'd paid without speaking because the act of paying someone to wash his clothes — an act that meant his wife was dying and his life was changing in ways he couldn't reverse — had closed his throat. Nine dollars and seventy-five cents. He remembered the amount. He remembered everything in amounts.

The pension was $2,340 a month. Medicaid took $2,290 as his patient responsibility — his share of the nursing home bill, computed by a formula he'd read three times and understood perfectly and considered a legalized theft. That left fifty dollars. That was his personal needs allowance. The carnations on his lap had cost $4.49, almost a tenth of what the state said he needed for a month. Fifty dollars bought toothpaste, electric razor heads, stamps, the greeting cards from the lobby rack. It did not buy flowers twice. It did not buy anything for his grandchildren. It did not buy a goddamn thing for Denise.

Denise was forty-four. Home health aide in Trenton, $14.80 an hour, no benefits she could afford to use. Three kids: Keisha, thirteen; Jaylen, ten; Amara, six. Rent was $1,100 for a two-bedroom in a building where the hallway smelled like cooking grease and somebody else's weed. Her Altima had 180,000 miles on it and kept making a belt noise her mechanic said could wait, which meant it couldn't.

The $400 a month Earl sent paid for after-school for Jaylen and Amara. The program didn't bargain. In August Denise had called him from the parking lot, trying to keep her voice level while the aide waited in her coat and the two younger kids sat on the curb with their backpacks between their knees. She was one late payment from losing the slot. Earl had sent the money order the next morning.

He'd told Denise the money was a VA supplemental benefit. Aid and Attendance, he'd called it — a real program, which made the lie better. He'd looked it up at the facility library, on the one computer in the activity room that still connected to the internet, until he could talk about it like a man who'd filed the paperwork himself. She hadn't asked much. Just, "Daddy, are you sure? Four hundred a month?" He'd said, "The government owes me. Korea." It was close enough to an explanation that she let it stand. Four hundred wasn't a fix. It kept the kids out of an empty apartment till Denise got off shift.

Phil turned onto Raritan Road. The cemetery was another fifteen minutes.

"Mind if we stop?" Earl said. He'd said it the same way every month. Phil knew the stop.

"ShopRite?"

"Yeah."

Phil pulled into the lot. Earl got out and went in. The flower bucket was by the entrance, between the shopping carts and a display of pumpkins marked down to $2.99. He picked up a bunch of carnations in cellophane — white and pink, the stems cut short, a sprig of baby's breath that was already browning at the edges. $4.49. He paid with a five at the self-checkout and put the change in his jacket pocket with the fifty-one cents already there. He walked back to the van, holding the flowers low against his hip, and climbed in without help.

Phil pulled out. The sports radio murmured about the Giants' offensive line. Earl held the flowers on his lap and watched the road.

---

The cemetery was off a county road south of New Brunswick, set back from the street behind a stone wall that had been pointed and repointed so many times the mortar was thicker than the stones. The grounds were maintained by a crew that came Tuesdays — Earl knew because Maryann's plot was always trimmer on Wednesdays, the grass clipped close, the edges clean. By Thursday the first day's growth had softened things. He preferred it.

Phil parked the van in the small lot near the maintenance shed. Three other cars — visitors, or maybe the office staff. And Marcus's Corolla, dark blue, parked at the end of the row facing out.

Earl didn't look at it directly. He sat in the second row with the flowers on his lap and waited.

Marcus knew the routine. Within a minute his Corolla pulled forward and stopped alongside the van, driver's window to Earl's window, close enough that the side mirrors nearly touched. Marcus was wearing a gray hoodie, no jacket, which was too little for the weather but Marcus always ran warm.

"Hey," Marcus said through his open window.

Earl cranked the van's window down — an old van, no power anything — and the cold came in, raw, the kind of late-October cold that got to work before Halloween was done. He reached into his jacket pocket and pulled out the greeting card envelope and passed it through. Marcus took it and slid it under his thigh without looking at it. They'd done this seven times. The motions were set.

Marcus reached across to his passenger seat and handed a white envelope through the gap between the vehicles. Business-size, sealed. Four hundred dollars in twenties. Earl put it in his inside jacket pocket, the one the greeting card had been in. The pockets traded contents. An empty one filled, a full one emptied.

"How you doing?" Marcus asked.

"Getting by."

"Keisha still getting those grades?"

Earl looked at him. Marcus knew about the grandkids because Marcus asked about them every month, and every month Earl gave him the same amount of information: enough to answer, not enough to invite more. "She's doing all right."

"Good." Marcus drummed his fingers on the wheel. He had something else to say — Earl could see it in the way he held his jaw, slightly open, the next sentence waiting. But Marcus looked at Earl's face and decided against it. Smart. Marcus was smarter than the others gave him credit for.

"I'll see you," Earl said. He cranked the window back up. Marcus put his window up and pulled out of the lot, and Earl watched the Corolla turn onto the county road and disappear behind the stone wall. Phil glanced in the rearview but said nothing. A greeting card passed between vehicles at a cemetery — it could be anything. Phil had no reason to wonder.

"Take your time," Phil said. He was already settling back with the thermos. He'd wait. He always waited. Earl figured Phil used the cemetery runs as a break — an hour of sitting in a warm van listening to the radio while the building didn't need anything from him. Earl didn't blame him. The building needed too much from everyone.

Earl took the flowers and got out. The cold was worse standing in it. The leaves on the cemetery's oaks had gone amber and were falling in the wind, scraping across the pavement in clusters, collecting along the curbs and against the headstones like small drifts. He walked carefully — the asphalt had cracks and his knees weren't what they were — but his gait was steady. He'd been walking with bad knees since sixty-two, and he knew how to distribute his weight.

The cough came as he crossed the lot — a single bark followed by a tightness that he swallowed down. He cleared his throat and walked into the cemetery.

Maryann was in Section C, fifth row, nine stones in from the path. He knew the count cold: steps from the van to her plot (sixty-four), headstones between the path and hers (eight others, then hers), years since she'd died (four), years they'd been married (forty-three). The headstone was gray granite, modest — he'd picked it himself, the cheapest one that wasn't flimsy, $1,800 installed, paid in three installments that he'd finished six months after the funeral. MARYANN ELOISE WASHINGTON. 1944–2022. BELOVED WIFE AND MOTHER.

He'd wanted to add something else but couldn't think of what. The woman at the monument company had suggested "Forever In Our Hearts" and Earl had looked at her until she stopped talking. Maryann was in the ground, not in his heart. His heart was a muscle that pumped blood. She was here, in this dirt, in this box, and the fact of that — the plain, immovable, inarguable fact of it — was what he came to visit. Not a feeling. A location.

He took the old flowers from the bronze vase mounted on the stone's base — last month's carnations, dried to brown paper, the cellophane faded — and put the new ones in. The vase had an inch of rain-blackened water in it. He didn't change it. The flowers would sit in the old water for a month and nobody would know and it wouldn't matter. He put the dead flowers in the cellophane wrapper from the new ones and set the bundle on the ground to throw away on the way out.

Then he stood there.

"Keisha made honor roll again," he said. His voice was quiet but not a whisper. He talked to Maryann at normal volume. He'd never whispered here. "Second quarter straight. Denise said she wants to do some science program at the community college in the spring, the Saturday one. It costs something. I'll figure it out."

The wind moved the carnations in the vase. A leaf scraped across the base of the headstone and caught in the dead grass at the plot's edge.

"Jaylen got in a fight. Not a bad one — he pushed a kid who said something about his shoes. Denise had to go in and talk to the vice principal. He's not a bad kid. He's ten. You remember what I was like at ten." He paused. Maryann would have said something here — she always had something to say, always had a story or a correction or a piece of context that made the thing he'd said larger than he'd meant it. She'd have said, "Earl, you were worse than Jaylen and you know it," and she'd have been right, and he'd have looked at her and she'd have known he agreed without him saying so.

"Amara drew me a picture. Denise mailed it. It's a fish." He almost smiled. "A purple fish with legs. I don't know what it's supposed to be. She wrote my name on it. She wrote GRANDPA and the R is backward."

He coughed. Not a bad one — just the chest reminding him it was there, the way a foreman tapped you on the shoulder before he said something you didn't want to hear.

"Denise is tired," he said. "She's always tired. She doesn't say it but I can hear it. She sounds like you did, the last year. Not sick-tired. Life-tired." He stopped. He didn't want to say more about that. He didn't want to compare Denise to Maryann's last year, because Maryann's last year had an ending he could see from here — the headstone, the dirt, the dried flowers on the ground.

He stood with his hands at his sides. The cemetery was quiet. A maintenance truck idled somewhere on the far side, near the newer section, and a woman in a red coat was walking between headstones three rows over, but she was far enough away to be a shape, not a person. The oaks filtered the gray light, and the shadows of bare branches moved on the ground like something being drawn and erased, drawn and erased.

"I'm all right," he told her. It was the same answer he'd given Marcus: enough to end a conversation. Maryann would have known it was a lie. She always knew when he was lying, because he lied so rarely that the sound of it was distinctive — like a machine running rough, a noise you noticed because the machine usually ran clean.

He didn't tell her about the ring. He'd never told her about the ring, not in four years of monthly visits, and at first it was because the ring hadn't started yet and then it was because he didn't know how to explain it to a woman who'd believed in rules as firmly as she'd believed in church — not because she was naive but because the rules were supposed to protect people like them, and breaking them was a concession she'd have struggled with. Maybe she'd have understood eventually. Maybe Keisha's science program and Jaylen's after-school and Amara's purple fish would've been enough. But he wasn't sure, and so he didn't say it.

He didn't tell her about the cough.

He picked up the dead flowers, nodded once at the headstone — a small motion, barely visible, hardly more than an acknowledgment that he'd be back next month — and walked back toward the parking lot. Sixty-four steps. The envelope in his pocket. The flowers in the trash can by the maintenance shed. His knees holding.

Phil had the engine running. The radio was on a commercial. Earl climbed in and pulled the door shut and Phil backed out without asking how it went, because Phil had learned that too.

They drove back. Earl watched New Jersey go by through the window and ran through tomorrow's money order — the post office in the lobby had the forms, and the facility's outgoing mail went out Monday mornings. Four hundred dollars. Payable to Denise Washington. In the memo line he'd write "VA A&A Nov," same as every month: an abbreviation of a benefit he'd never applied for, a code that meant his daughter could pay for her children's afternoons.

---

That night, after dinner — pork chop with applesauce, mashed potatoes from a box, green beans that had been green once — Earl lay in bed and listened to Burt breathe.

The room was dim. The hallway light came under the door in its permanent stripe. Burt's oxygen concentrator hummed at a pitch Earl's ears had long ago filed as background. They'd done the same with the plant's compressors forty years earlier. Then he'd retired and found out silence could be louder.

The envelope hung in his jacket on the hook behind the bathroom door. Somewhere in Marcus's car, the greeting card was being inventoried and sorted for people whose insurance had failed them or never existed. Agnes kept the records. Harry kept the system running. Lola watched the door. Burt had been out of the count since the fall; now he was just the man in the other bed, breathing through the concentrator. Carmen had crossed a line to protect them and would cross another eventually — Earl could see it in her face, that old shop-floor look of somebody deciding whether to file a grievance. Some people moved toward the right thing slowly, testing each step. Carmen was testing.

The cough came.

He'd been waiting for it. It always came at night, worse than the morning, as if his chest saved it up during the day and released it in the dark. He rolled onto his side and pressed the handkerchief to his mouth and coughed until his ribs ached and his throat felt scraped raw and his eyes streamed. The sound was bad. Wet. Something deep in the left lung shifted and rattled, a noise he could feel in his back, between the shoulder blades, as if someone were shaking a box of broken parts inside him.

He coughed for two minutes. Maybe three. When it stopped, it didn't stop cleanly — it tapered off, each cough weaker than the last, his body losing the force to produce them. He lay on his side with his mouth open, breathing in small gulps, his hand closed around the handkerchief.

Burt slept through it. Burt slept through everything now.

Earl sat up. He reached for the lamp on the nightstand — a small lamp with a forty-watt bulb that made everything in the room the color of weak tea — and turned it on.

He opened the handkerchief.

White. Still white.

He checked every fold. He held it six inches from his face and examined each surface under the lamp's yellow light. He was looking for pink. For rust. For the stain that would mean the cough had crossed from nuisance to fact, from something he could ignore to something his body wouldn't let him. Hemoptysis — he'd looked up the word on the activity room computer, the same computer where he'd researched the VA benefit. He'd read the causes. He knew what the cough could be. He knew the names for it. He didn't want the name. He wanted the handkerchief to stay white, because white meant he could wait one more day.

If he went to Dr. Prasad and said the word "cough," certain things would happen. There'd be a chest X-ray and blood work, maybe a hospital run if the film showed anything ugly. Lakeview did med reconciliation every time somebody came back from outside care. Orders got compared, bottles got pulled, controlled meds got held till the paperwork matched. He'd watched it after Burt's fall: once the hospital touched Burt's chart, Agnes pulled Burt's prescriptions from the table and they never went back. If a scan found something in Earl's lung, his own oxycodone and alprazolam could disappear into that same process by tomorrow or next week. That part was fact.

What came after was the part he couldn't prove. Maybe Harry and Agnes could cover the loss for a month. Maybe Marcus could move less and Denise would still get her envelope. Maybe the whole thing would wobble and keep standing. Earl didn't know. Fear kept filling in the blanks anyway. In fear's version Denise lost the four hundred, Jaylen lost after-school, and Amara ended up with whatever neighbor Denise could find at six-thirty.

He sat on the edge of the bed with the handkerchief in his lap and his bare feet on the cold linoleum. His ankles were thin. His feet were ashy and the nails were thick and yellow — fungal, probably, the kind of thing that happened when you were old and your blood didn't reach your feet the way it used to. Carmen trimmed them for him every two weeks, kneeling on the floor with clippers, not flinching, not making a face. He'd never asked her to. She just did it.

Telling someone meant running the list in his head, set out like a grievance chain. Carmen would want him to see the doctor. Harry would calculate the operational impact. Agnes would look at him with her pharmacist's eyes and know more from the sound of the cough than the X-ray would show. Denise would drive up from Trenton and sit in the chair by the window and cry, and then what — what would she do with the information? What could she do? She'd worry. She'd add his dying to the list of things she managed on fourteen dollars and eighty cents an hour. He'd give her that weight and take nothing off the pile.

He put the handkerchief back in his pocket. The jacket hung on the hook. The envelope was in the pocket. Tomorrow: the post office form, the money order, the memo line, the outgoing mail. Next month: the same. The month after: the same. As long as the handkerchief stayed white.

He went back instead to one February in the split-level in Piscataway, him at the kitchen table after second shift trying to hide a wad of brown-streaked tissues under the ashtray. Maryann had lifted the ashtray, opened the top tissue, and looked at him until he picked up the phone. She didn't make a speech or start an argument. She just shoved Dr. Klein's number across on the grocery pad and waited. It had turned out to be bronchitis. The antibiotics cost two missed shifts, and Maryann pulled the copay out of the grocery envelope without saying what else that week would go without. If she were here now, the lie would've lasted until breakfast.

Instead he talked to her grave once a month and kept the important parts for himself: no ring, no cough, no fear. He told her about the grandkids and the small facts of their lives, and there was nobody there to push back.

Earl turned off the lamp. He lay back down and pulled the blanket to his chest and closed his eyes. The cough was still there — he could feel it waiting, a heaviness behind his sternum, not ready to surface but not gone either. It would come again. It came every night now. And every morning he would check, and the handkerchief would be white or it wouldn't, and the day he saw color on the cloth would be the day the clock he'd been ignoring started ticking loud enough for everyone to hear.

Not today.

He kept one hand over his sternum and counted off the breaths that would get him to morning.
