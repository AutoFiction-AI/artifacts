# Chapter 7

The children's row sat lower on the hillside than the adult plots, under a scrim of scrub cedar that kept the ground damp long after the sun had burned the upper slope pale. From the upper row it looked like easy work. The stones were smaller. The ground they covered was shorter. Men who had not done it before always glanced down there and thought they might make quick time before dinner.

Della knew better. A child's grave gave you no room to stand right in it and no weight to set your shoulder against. You had to fold yourself almost double, work with the short spade and your hands, and lift by the wrists what the clay had been sucking on for twenty years. The little boxes took water faster than the big ones. Cedar roots crossed them because nobody had dug as deep, and every parent who had ever buried one had wanted the grave tucked close, as if nearness could answer the insult of how little earth the child required. By the time a coffin came loose it had half the hillside stuck to it. Small did not mean light. Small meant awkward and mean.

Silas Wynn stood in the row with his hat brim shadowing the deep grooves beside his nose, coughing into his fist before he set the shovel in. "This part'll take longer than the men up at the office think," he said. "Little boxes'll ruin a back quicker."

Lucille Holt had already marked the section on her clipboard. Her sensible shoes were red to the ankle and her pinned hair had frizzed at the temples in the humidity, though the rest of her still looked as if she meant to keep the day orderly by force of posture. "Then we had best ruin no backs and still get through it," she said. "The weather office in Larkin says storms after three."

A low concussion rolled from the dam works five miles downriver. It arrived through the ground first and the air after, the same way it always did now, a blunt reminder that the men below kept to their schedule whether Old Sion finished its dead or not. Della flexed her left thumb against the handle of the short spade and felt the stiff joint answer with its dull, stubborn ache. She had woken with it already swollen. By noon it would feel twice its size.

Families had come early and gathered at the cedar edge with handkerchiefs, hats, seed-catalog fans, and the patient misery people brought to church when the sermon had gone past comfort and into account-taking. Child graves always drew more watchers than the adult ones. Men who would leave their own father's coffin to strangers would stand all morning for a baby dead three decades because the body had never filled their arms long enough to satisfy them. Della had learned that too.

She saw Willa Vale before Willa saw her. Willa was small and narrow through the shoulders, wearing a black straw hat too heavy for the heat because she only owned two funeral hats and the other one had mouse-chewed lace at the brim. She kept touching the back of a blue enamel bird pinned crooked to the band, checking it was still there. Three paces off stood Clara Rains in a washed-out brown dress with one black cotton glove on her left hand. Clara's bare right hand was broad and freckled; the gloved one had two fingers ruined years ago by lye, and when she got nervous she always worried the glove cuff down and hauled it back in place again, as if she could smooth the hand underneath. The two women had not stood this near one another in fifteen years. The sight of them side by side put a cold bead of understanding down Della's spine before a shovel touched dirt.

Lucille followed her glance. "Is there a problem in that row?"

"There will be if you let folks crowd the men," Della said.

Lucille accepted that answer only in the official sense. She moved the onlookers back with quiet firmness, calling them by surname and not by first name, which helped as much as anything did with valley people. Then the labor began.

The first grave gave them trouble before they even found the boards. The cedar root crossing it was thick as a wrist and tough as cable, and the short spade glanced off stone twice before Silas muttered, knelt, and cut the root with a hand saw from his coat sack. Sweat dropped off his nose into the dirt. By the time the top boards showed through, softened to black bread under the clay, Della's apron front was stuck to her stomach. She slid both hands under one side as the county man on the other end did the same, and the coffin came free in a sucking jerk that nearly sent him backward.

He looked surprised by the pull of it. Della was too busy saving the corner from breaking off to say anything.

They set the little box on the plank table Lucille had requisitioned from the schoolhouse and covered it with clean cloth until the tag was checked. When the move went well, it settled into a rhythm: uncover, free, lift, wrap, mark, load, next. But nothing in the children's row stayed rhythm very long. Somebody always spoke a name from the cedar line and made the air change.

By ten o'clock Della had old pine under her nails and a heat headache starting behind her eyes. She had not wanted June in the child section and had said so at breakfast, but Lucille had asked for another steady hand with the duplicate tags and June wrote a neater print than half the county. Ruthie had settled it by tying a bundle of boiled muslin and saying June would sit at the side table and keep her shoes clear of opened ground. Della had let the matter drop because there were too many other things to hold closed. Now, as the sun climbed and the cedar shade shrank, she kept glancing uphill for them.

The paired graves sat near the far edge of the section where the slope began to dip toward the fever plot. One had a cracked lamb on a limestone chip no bigger than a supper plate. The other had only a fieldstone with faint pencil marks long since gone. Della knew both without looking at Lucille's list. She knew where the twin row started because she had set those stones herself when the men of both houses were too drunk with grief and exhaustion to bother straightness. The summer had been so hot milk turned in the pail before noon. Clara Rains had already had four children, Roy had been laid up with his chest, and Willa Vale had worn her own last stillborn child like a bruise that had spread through the whole house. Della had carried one wet, squalling boy across a yard that smelled of chicken shit, hot dust, and spent blood because there had not been enough of anything in one house and too much emptiness in the other.

She did not look at either woman again. Looking gave the mind ideas it did not need.

"These two first," she said to Silas.

Silas spared her one sidelong look that said he knew exactly which two these were and wished it had happened on a day with fewer eyes. Then he set the shovel in.

The grave with the lamb marker came open narrow and clean. The box had been made from planed pine instead of rough crate wood, a luxury by valley standards, though the metal screws were orange with rust now and the lid bowed in the middle from years of damp. Della brushed the clay from the footboard with her palm. A cheap tin strip still clung there under two nails, the punched letters half-choked with corrosion.

HENRY RAINS.

Clara made a sound from the cedar line, not loud, just air catching in the back of her throat. Willa turned her face away and pressed two fingers to the blue bird on her hatband as if to pin herself in place.

Lucille checked the copied church book. "That accords so far," she said. "Twin sons to Clara and Roy Rains, baptized July second of thirty-nine. One named Henry. The second entry is harder to read."

Della nodded once. "Keep going."

Henry's coffin nearly came apart in their hands. The damp had eaten the sideboards to lace under the cloth lining, and when they lifted, the clay inside shifted with a tiny wet slide, heavier than the box had any right to be. The county man opposite Della breathed out a curse under his breath. She did not scold him for it. She had expected one herself. The lining came up stuck to Henry's back, and under the brown rot of it a row of ribs showed fine as hickory splints. His skull, no bigger than Della's spread palm, tipped toward the broken corner. She slid her hand under it before it could roll free, pinned the loosened cloth over the separate arm bones, and laid him into fresh muslin piece by piece because there was no whole way left to do it.

By the time Henry was tagged, June and Ruthie had come up the path from Cedar Bench. Ruthie carried a pail with a dish towel knotted over the lid and the clean-cloth bundle under her arm. June had the tag book hugged to her ribs and a pencil tucked behind one ear. She was fifteen and long in the limbs now, yet in the children's row she still looked too close in size to the stones. Della felt that as a private insult.

Ruthie took in the crowd first and then the women at its front. "Lord," she said softly, more tired than surprised.

"Set the water by the table," Della said. "Keep June clear."

June had already seen the opened coffin on the plank and the faces beyond it. Nothing much got past her once her attention caught. She said, "Yes'm," and went where she was told, though her eyes stayed on Lucille's church copy.

The second grave fought them harder. The fieldstone had sunk crooked and the top boards lay slanted under the dirt, as if the whole thing had rolled an inch downhill before the clay set. Roots had worked under the left side. Della had to kneel and clear with both hands, scooping out wet red clods that packed under her fingernails and made the old ache in her thumb bloom sharp enough to blur her vision for a moment. The smell that rose when the lid finally showed was not some grand graveyard horror. It was wet wood, metal, cloth rot, and the sour mineral breath of earth that had been closed too long. Still, it laid a memory across her tongue so quick and complete she had to shut her mouth against it: Clara's labor room in July, the sheet dark between Clara's legs, one twin mewling thinly while the other rooted so hard at Della's knuckle he drew blood with bare gums.

Silas pried the coffin free by inches. When the footboard cleared, Della brushed it clean.

The tin plate there read:

JOEL VALE.

Lucille looked up at once. "There is no Joel Vale in the church register for that month."

No one in the row moved after that. Even the cicadas seemed to pull back for a breath.

Lucille ran her finger down the copied lines again, slower this time. "Twin sons to Clara and Roy Rains. Henry buried July tenth. Second twin buried July eighteenth." She looked from the paper to the plate and then to Della. "Mrs. Bledsoe."

Della stayed crouched by the coffin. If she stood too quickly, the ache in her back would show. "You can read."

"I can. I need the sequence."

Pearl Lusk, who had been fanning herself with a seed-corn circular hard enough to bend it, said from the cedar shade, "Looks to me like the sequence is she swapped one."

Pearl's sentence broke the held breath. Murmurs started at once, low and eager. An old woman in a feed-sack hat said, "Folks talked that way then too. They just kept their mouths shut because babies were dying faster than pride could manage." A man farther back answered, "Talking's one thing. Wrong names in the ground are another." Heads tipped together along the cedar line. Seed-catalog fans stopped mid-swing. By the time the accusation crossed back to Della, it came louder and cleaner, as if each mouth had licked it once before passing it on.

Willa stepped forward before Lucille could stop her. Up close the camphor from her clothes chest came off her stronger than sweat. "He died at my place," she said. "That was Joel. I named him after my brother. He slept in my front room and died there."

Clara pulled her glove cuff so hard the seam popped. "He came out of me," she said. Her voice had not changed in all these years; it still had that rough low scrape under it, like a pan dragged over a stove eye. "Both of them did."

Willa's face pinched. "And one of them would have starved in your house."

"One of them starved in yours."

Lucille's composure tightened another notch. "I need only the record relation," she said. "Not a quarrel."

"You won't get one without the other," Pearl said. A fleck of damp snuff had worked loose at the corner of her mouth, and she wiped it away with the back of her wrist. "Whole valley knows Miz Della used to come through a hard birth and leave folks with different names than they started with. I said it then and I'll say it now."

At the side table, June kept drawing her thumb down the edge of the tag book until the paper curled and lay flat again. Della saw her fingers bite deeper into the board cover each time Pearl opened her mouth.

Lucille did not look at June. She kept her attention where procedure allowed it. "Mrs. Bledsoe. Was this child born as one of the Rains twins and buried later under the Vale surname?"

The question was clean in a clerk's mouth. It was not clean anywhere else.

Della got to her feet. The blood rushed from her knees in a dizzy wash. For a second she saw not the hillside but Clara's cabin in thirty-nine: the fly-specked lamp chimney, Roy Rains folded on a chair and coughing red into a rag, Clara on the rope bed with one twin rooting at a breast that gave him almost nothing while the sheet under her hips kept darkening. Willa Vale waited on her back step across the chicken-shit yard with a boiled bottle in one hand and five dollars laid flat on the kitchen table behind her. Della had wrapped Joel in a flour-sack towel and lifted him out of Clara's crook herself while the afterbirth was not even out of her yet. Clara had caught a fistful of Della's skirt and rasped, "Only till I can stand. Only till my milk comes. Don't let her shut the door on me." Della had peeled those fingers loose one by one because Roy could not get up, the powder tin cost money nobody there had, and Willa was already reaching. Then she carried Joel across the yard and set him into Willa's arms.

She said, "Write this down: twin sons born to Clara Rains. Henry buried as Henry Rains. Joel died in the Vale house and was buried as Joel Vale. That's what the wood says and what the church says."

Pearl gave a hard little laugh. "Listen to that. Neat as a hem. Born here, buried there, and the middle gone missing."

The words came out before Della could stop them. "The middle was Roy too sick to stand, Clara bleeding through a sheet, and Willa laying out bottle money on the table."

Silence held for one beat only. Then Pearl leaned into it with relish. "So if a woman is broke and bleeding, you get to choose which house keeps her baby?"

The county man at Della's elbow shifted his weight away from her without meaning to. She felt the turn in that small movement as plainly as if he had spoken: not outrage, not even belief, only a body deciding it ought to stand farther off.

Clara stared at Della with the old wound of the thing laid bare again, the one Della had never been able to stitch closed because there had never been enough thread in the world for that seam. "You told me he'd have a better chance," she said.

Willa answered before Della could. "He did. He had a crib and milk and two people wanting him every minute he breathed."

"He still died."

"So did the one you kept."

The words struck Della like a slap because they were true, and truth said in public almost always sounded crueler than a lie. She watched June hear them. She hated that more than Pearl's snuff-wet mouth, more than the county man's step sideways, more than Lucille's sharpened attention.

Lucille spoke over the murmuring before it could tip fully into chaos. "There is no compensation issue attached to these infant burials," she said. "What I need is an accurate relocation record. Mrs. Bledsoe has supplied one."

"Has she?" Pearl said. "Or has she supplied the version she can stand with now?"

"Pearl," Silas said.

He did not raise his voice. He did not need to. Silas Wynn spent words the way some men spent bullets. The single name came out flat and dry as stone dust, and Pearl looked at him with the quick offended blink of somebody discovering the room had not entirely joined her.

He added, "If you're set on theater, go stage it where folks can sit."

Pearl flushed dark under the powder on her cheeks. "I'm standing where dead children are getting unpacked like store stock, Mr. Wynn. I believe the theater found me."

On another day Pearl's line might have been funny. Here too many people wanted it to be useful.

An older man from the back, voice gone pinched with alarm, said, "If my girl's marker says one thing and the church another, I want it checked now."

A younger voice answered from somewhere Della could not see, mean with the safety of distance. "Bet she's got a whole notebook tucked off somewhere. Bet half this ridge ain't who it's been calling itself."

Lucille turned so fast the papers on her clipboard slapped together. "No one here has seen any such book," she said. "No one will invent evidence because the hillside has turned dramatic."

But the idea was loose now. Hidden books. Secret records. Babies passed across a kitchen before the mother had quit bleeding. A bastard surname rubbed off and another one pinned on clean for Sunday. She had heard every version in one shape or another over forty years, usually mumbled after she left a porch or carried into church by women who would still call for her the next time labor started wrong. Back then those same women had pressed coffee into her hand, counted folded bills into her apron, and pulled her through the door before the neighbors could see. Now Lucille's forms snapped on the clipboard, and every mouth around the row seemed to sharpen when a name could be pinned to a line in ink.

"Enough," she said.

The old public authority was still in her voice. It had not left completely. Heads turned. Even Pearl shut her mouth long enough to hear what Della would spend next.

"You can stand there and make filth out of a week none of you had to solve," Della said. "Or you can let us finish moving these children before the sky opens. I won't do both."

Pearl said, "You already did the filth."

Della looked at her then, full and plain. "If I'd wanted profit, Pearl, I wouldn't have chosen to earn it in blood up to my elbows."

Pearl took that harder than a shout. Her fan stopped moving. A couple of the watchers dropped their eyes, not because they believed Della clean but because the sentence made them picture what she meant. Clara looked sick. Willa looked old enough to split in half down the middle.

Lucille took the opening at once. "Wrap the second child," she said to the men. "Mr. Wynn, if you will. June, record the transfer tag. Mrs. Bledsoe, I need spelling confirmed."

Silas slid both hands under the wrapper. Joel had gone looser in the years than Henry; when the coffin tilted, a small shoulder and a string of ribs sagged away from the body cloth and clicked against the footboard. Della bent at once, cupped the back of the skull through the rot-soft muslin, and folded the loosened bones back in before the county man could snatch at them wrong.

June answered before Della did. "J-o-e-l," she said, voice careful and almost too formal. "Vale. V-a-l-e."

Della turned toward her. June's face had gone pale under the sun and cedar shade, but her hand did not shake over the tag stub. Her steadiness made Della proud and afraid in the same instant. Pride was a luxury. Fear kept the count.

She said, quieter, "That's right."

They worked on because there was nothing else to do. Henry and Joel were wrapped tight in separate transfer boxes, each bundle snugged enough to keep a loose rib or wrist bone from traveling when the wagon hit ruts. Lucille wrote a notation in the margin of her form: `twin sons, birth record under Rains; second burial under Vale by household of death.` The ink beaded on the damp paper for a second before it soaked in. Della could already hear how the line would fare outside the county file: repeated on porches, laughed at in low voices, never enough to close anybody's mouth.

The next two graves opened under a pressure that had changed shape. Before the twin coffins, people had watched as families watch surgery: anxious, reverent, half-hoping the right hands might still produce some miracle from the body. After them, the watchers leaned in with the appetite of jurors. Every board Della touched looked to them like evidence. Every surname Lucille checked against the list made somebody murmur before she finished the line.

Ruthie came alongside the table without being called and began tearing the boiled muslin into the widths Della liked for small remains. She did it with the same economy she used on pie crust and shirt seams, hands fast, mouth set. "You need water," she said.

"In a minute."

"You need it now."

Della drank because Ruthie had long since learned when a sentence in the house was not really a suggestion. The water tasted of tin pail and spring stone. When she lowered it, Ruthie was looking not at her but at the cedar edge, where two women Della knew from laundry pick-up days had turned their heads together and stopped pretending they were not talking.

"They were all smiles Tuesday," Ruthie said under her breath. "Today they'd rather catch fever than hand me a sheet."

There was no accusation in it yet. The lack of it almost made things worse.

The sun dropped lower in the west, thickening the air rather than cooling it. Thunder showed itself only as a bruise beyond the ridge, but the wind had turned. The cedar tops whispered their dry warning. Della pushed the row harder. Her thumb throbbed with every lift. At the sixth grave her forearm cramped so badly she had to straighten and flex her fingers against her apron seam while pretending to inspect Lucille's numbering. At the seventh, one small coffin came up with the underside gone entirely and a scatter of child buttons rolling loose in the mud with a cuff's worth of wrist bones and two heel bones no larger than shelled beans. June made a sound, half breath and half swallowed word. Della crouched, lifted the little bones free of the mud with two fingers, and laid them back into the box before the rain could mash them deeper. Then she put the buttons in June's palm and closed her fingers over them one by one.

"For the box," she said.

June nodded. Her mouth was a straight line. She had started the day as a helper. By midafternoon she looked like somebody learning exactly what kind of town had raised her.

The talk never stopped. It only changed clothes.

A woman with wash-red hands said, "She sat three nights with my sister through a breech birth and took her pay in ham rind. That don't make this clean, but it ain't stealing."

A boy not long out of short pants shot back, "Clean or dirty, if she's kept side pages the county ought to pry them loose."

Then a third voice, older and pleased with itself, said, "County won't stir till a lawful son turns bastard on paper."

Della let the words pass her like gnats because swatting every one only brought blood to the surface. But each time they reached June, or brushed Ruthie, they changed from nuisance to damage. June heard them all. Della could tell by the way her granddaughter had quit asking even the practical questions. Ruthie heard them too and answered by getting quieter, which was how Ruthie prepared herself either to endure a thing or to cut it clean.

When the last grave in the children's row had been wrapped and tagged, the first fat drops of rain were falling one at a time through the cedar. They darkened the plank table and left big cold coins on the transfer boxes. Lucille counted twice before she signed off the section. Her public voice stayed even, but she did not meet Della's eyes until the final number was written. "The row is complete," she said.

Della did not think of the row as cleared, and "complete" was no better if a person meant anything by the word. They had merely emptied it before the rain.

Pearl was first to leave. She tucked the bent seed-corn circular under her arm and moved off down the path fast for a woman who had stood half the day in heat. Della watched her go and knew the story would reach McRae's porch before the rain did.

Clara and Willa lingered opposite one another with the raw unwillingness of people who had once shared a wound in private and now found strangers fingering it. Della might have let them go without speech if Clara had not looked at Joel's transfer box with such naked hate and grief mixed together that the old scene threatened to unroll right there on the hillside.

"Clara," Della said.

Clara looked at her.

"I told you I'd bring him over to nurse if your milk came in."

Clara laughed once through her nose. "You told me that while the sheet was still under me. By the third day I walked that yard with milk through my dress and Willa met me on the step and said he'd just gone down. After that he was Joel Vale to everybody but me."

Della had no point denying any of it. She had watched Clara come across that yard white-faced and leaking milk, and she had let Willa keep the doorway between them.

Willa said, not to Della but to the box, "He did laugh, once. Before he got bad. I know babies don't laugh proper that little, but he did."

It was the only tenderness the day offered, and it came too late to help anybody. Clara's good hand twitched at her skirt, as if she might reach for the box and thought better of it. Then both women went separate ways down the hill without touching.

Ruthie tied the dish towel back over the empty pail. "We need to get home."

June took up the tag book. Her face had settled into a politeness Della did not trust. It was the expression June used on adults she already knew were not telling her everything and intended to see whether they deserved one more chance.

They had barely reached the lane below the cemetery wall when the children came at it sideways, not in a mob but in three valley young ones who had spent the day underfoot, hearing everything adults thought they were not hearing. Lem Harbin led because he always led trouble, a raw-kneed boy in one suspender with a marble bulging his pocket and a fresh scab at his temple. Behind him came Nettie Crowe, missing one front tooth and blowing now and then into a willow whistle she had tucked through her braid, and little Oscar Plitt carrying a dead garter snake on a forked stick like a prize.

Lem planted himself in the lane and grinned at June with the mean bravery children borrowed from home. "Hey, June," he said. "How do you know she didn't trade you too?"

Ruthie stopped so abruptly the water in the pail slapped the lid. "Move."

Lem's grin widened because he had an audience in the form of the other two and because eleven-year-old boys will always test the edge of a woman's shame before they test a man's fist. "I mean it. My mama said Miz Della knows where all the babies come from and where they go after. Maybe June ain't even a Bledsoe. Maybe she's somebody else's throwed-off—"

June cut in before Ruthie could. "My name is June Bledsoe," she said. Her voice had that too-careful shape it took when she was trying not to let anyone call her childish. "You know it because you've been using it since you could talk."

Nettie lowered the whistle from her mouth. "Mama said your granny sells names."

"Your mama buys gossip because it's cheaper than coffee," Ruthie said.

Ruthie's answer would have ended it with most adults. It only excited Lem. He rocked back on his heels and pointed at Della. "Did you take babies, Miz Della?"

Rain ticked in the sycamores over the lane. Della could have lied. She could have barked at him to get home. She could have said what children always hated hearing, that he ought to mind his elders. None of it would have mattered. The question had already done what it came to do.

She said, "Go on, Lem."

Della gave him no anger, and the lack of it disappointed him. He spat in the dirt by Oscar's stick and sauntered off, whistling through his teeth. Nettie followed after one last long look at June's face. Oscar, too young to understand the full pleasure of cruelty, dragged the dead snake behind him and kept glancing back as if hoping somebody would explain.

June had not cried. Della saw the work of it in the way she held the tag book jammed flat against her ribs, the hard edge of it pressed into the thin cotton of her dress.

Ruthie said, "Keep walking."

They climbed the half mile to Cedar Bench in rain that never quite committed. It came as a spit and then a pause, enough to spot the dust and slick the path without cooling it. By the time the cabin came in view, the porch boards were already dark in circles where drops had blown under the roof edge.

Mildred Quillen stood there waiting with a grain sack hugged against her apron. She was sixteen, maybe seventeen, all wrists and shoulders, wearing her brother's castoff saddle shoes unlaced because one foot always swelled by evening. She worried the waxed seam of the sack with her thumbnail until she saw Ruthie. Then she stopped as if caught stealing.

Ruthie did not ask her inside. "What is it, Mildred?"

Mildred looked at Della first, then away. "Mama sent me."

The girl did not have to say more. The sack told it. Ruthie had taken in three shirts and a pair of pillowcases from the Quillen house two nights ago, promising them back Saturday for twelve cents and whatever scraps Mrs. Quillen could spare from the smokehouse drippings. One of the shirts still sat half-mended in Ruthie's basket by the hearth.

"She wants the work back?" Ruthie said.

Mildred nodded, miserable. "She said she'd tend it herself awhile."

Ruthie set the pail down on the porch with a care so exact it was almost violent. "The collar was near done."

"I know."

"And your mama owed for the last wash."

Mildred swallowed. "She said to mark it square when things settle."

When things settle. As if Mrs. Quillen had not already sent the dirt up the ridge, first on her own tongue and now in Mildred's grain sack.

Della watched Ruthie hear the full meaning of it. Not just lost money, though that mattered. Not just insult. Mrs. Quillen had taken a shirt out of Ruthie's basket and twelve cents off the week's table because folks down at the cemetery had gone home wanting something ugly to chew. Ruthie had not moved a baby from one family to another in her life. She had not stood in Clara Rains's room with blood on the floorboards. She had not kept a private ledger or buried Gabriel Wolfe or carried old bargains in her apron pocket. Yet the work was gone from her porch all the same.

Ruthie said, "Wait."

She went inside and came back with the Quillen sack and the half-mended shirt folded on top, the needle still stuck in the cuff where she had left off. She handed it over without apology. Mildred took it with both arms like something heavy and warm.

"Tell your mama," Ruthie said, "that if she wants the twelve cents gone too, she can say that plain next time."

Mildred's eyes filled at once, which made her look younger than she was. "It ain't me," she said.

"I know it ain't."

The girl went down the steps backward once before turning and hurrying off into the spotted rain.

June had stood through the whole exchange with that same careful face. Only when Mildred disappeared down the path did she set the tag book on the porch rail and open her hand. The child buttons from the seventh grave lay in her palm, muddied pearl and tarnished metal, tiny as seeds. She looked at them a long moment and then closed her fingers again.

"Mama," she said to Ruthie, not taking her eyes off her hand, "did they really think that would wash off onto shirts?"

Ruthie let out a breath that might have been a laugh in a less tired world. "No," she said. "They think meanness is cleanliness if enough people do it together."

Then she turned to Della.

Ruthie was not loud under strain. Della had learned that early and had respected it the way one respects a knife that stays sharp without display. The quietness in Ruthie now was stripped to the bone.

"They're docking this house for things you did before I knew it," Ruthie said. "Before June was born. Before Rafe had any say in what this family had to answer for. They're making our supper answer for your old bargains."

Della could have defended herself. She had facts enough. She had dead children and hungry mothers and childless women and the whole long valley history of men who wanted law when it favored them and mercy when it cost them nothing. None of it would have changed the empty space where the Quillen work had been. None of it would have unsaid Lem Harbin in the lane.

So she said the one true thing left in reach. "I know."

Ruthie gave a short nod, as if Della had at least had the decency not to lie to her face. She took up the pail and went inside.

June stayed on the porch. Rain began at last in earnest, soft first, then harder on the roof tin, the sound filling the pauses the way gossip had filled the cemetery. She slid the child buttons into the pocket of her dress and looked out toward the valley, where Old Sion sat hidden now behind the trees and weather, its children's row scraped empty by afternoon labor and left to the storm.

Della stood beside her with mud drying on her skirt and old dirt stiff in the cracks of her hands. Down at the cemetery they had finished the removals before the weather broke. They had come there to do exactly that, and they had done it. By supper the child section would be all tagged boxes under canvas instead of names under cedar. By nightfall Pearl Lusk would have the twin story turned mean enough to travel every porch between Cedar Bench and the river.

June did not cry. She hooked two fingers over the tag book's spine and worried the cloth until it wrinkled under her nail, and that, more than anything, made Della understand how far the stain had already spread. Tears would have kept the thing in the lane with Lem Harbin and Nettie Crowe. Instead June stood there dry-eyed and straight-backed, already braced for schoolyard questions, churchyard stares, and the next porch woman who decided not to bring Ruthie her mending.

Della took the book out of June's hand and went inside with it. She set it beside the lamp on the kitchen table, crossed to the cellar door, and lifted the latch until the swollen wood gave a little under her palm. Ruthie had gone still at the sink behind her.

"After supper," Della said, looking at the door and not at either of them, "I'm bringing the ledger up. Only the grave pages. I'm done letting this valley invent the rest."

The rain thickened. Somewhere downriver the dam sounded again, faint through weather and distance, still keeping time.
