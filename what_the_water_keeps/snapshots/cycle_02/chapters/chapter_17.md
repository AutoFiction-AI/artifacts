# Chapter 17

Limestone Ridge had none of Old Sion's shade or forgiveness. The county had shaved the top of it flat with scrapers and set the graves in straight arithmetic, one white stake to a place, one fresh trench to a name or to the lack of one. The ground was dry enough to ring under a shovel. No cedar roots gripped the slope. No stones leaned shoulderwise into one another as though they had spent fifty years listening to the same weather. The new place sat above the floodline exactly as it was meant to, wind-scoured, orderly, and wrong.

Della stood near the first row with her left thumb braced in her right hand, working feeling back into it. The morning had a cool edge at last, early fall beginning to creep down off the ridge, but the ache in her joints kept the old summer heat trapped deep in her bones. Blank limestone tablets lay on sawhorses in a line, too white, too clean. They still held saw damp in them. When she laid her palm on the nearest one, the cut face felt raw as a plate fresh from grinding. Silas would have hated them. He had once told her a stone ought to spend some time in weather before a man presumed to put grief on it. These had come up from Larkin on a truck bed yesterday afternoon and been leaned out in rank by supper.

One of the tablets had no name chalked across it yet. The sight of that blank one kept finding her eye. Silas had not been dead a week, and the county had already set a blank stone aside for him as if grief could be ordered in the same wagonload as everybody else's. She did not need anybody to tell her whose it was waiting on.

There had been no single prayer to gather the morning. Too many families had arrived carrying their own dead, their own quarrels, their own ideas about what counted as decency. So the ridge broke into knots of people instead. Men in washed hats stood with hands jammed in trouser pockets. Women set down jars of flowers, armloads of cedar clipped from the last trees left above the road, folded pieces of cloth they meant to lay under photographs until the wind made that foolish. Children were told to hush every few minutes and never did. From downriver came the low concussion of the dam works, smaller up here but still steady enough to rattle in the ribs if a person stopped and listened.

Lucille Holt had set a card table near the wagon road and weighted her papers with stones. The wind worried her carbon sheets anyhow. She kept flattening them with the heel of her hand, then reaching under the table to knock gravel from one sensible shoe against the crossbar. Her hair was pinned as carefully as ever, but one strand had worked loose and stuck to the damp skin in front of her ear. She looked at home behind forms and almost comic out here among fresh-dug graves and families who wanted the dead back exactly as they had been, only better recorded.

Under a strip of canvas a few yards off, the county's hired cutter from Larkin worked with mallet and chisel at a trestle bench, a burn scar shining white across two knuckles each time he turned his hand. He hummed through his nose between blows and stopped only when Lucille slid him the next card.

June sat beside her on an upturned crate, copying names in the school hand Della had watched get steadier all summer. She had a pencil nub behind one ear and limestone dust grayed along the side of her hand where she kept wiping sweat and forgetting what she had touched. She did not fidget. That was what made grown people think she was younger than she was. They mistook stillness for softness every time.

Ruthie moved between groups with a lidded jar of coffee in one hand and a cloth sack of biscuits in the other, feeding people because there were not many family fights that could survive a mouthful of hot bread on an empty stomach. She had forgotten to empty her apron pocket after hanging wash at daylight, and two clothespins knocked against her hip each time she crossed the ridge. She had left them there. Della loved her a little for that, for having no time left this far into the story for pretending a woman came to grief tidy.

By nine o'clock Della had already heard three versions of the same widow's surname and one ugly disagreement over whether a stillborn child who had been marked only as BABY GIRL in a church hand ought to stay that way now that county money was paying for stone. Marker language had become the work more than shovels, more than lifting. The dead had been taken from the mud already. It was the names that would not stay put.

Lucille saw Della looking over and lifted one of the order cards. "The county covers two lines and dates where dates can be supported. A third line I can sometimes justify if it is identification and not decoration."

"Decoration," Della said.

Lucille's mouth went thin in the way it did when she was trying not to say what she thought of the county. "That is the word on the form."

Della looked past her to the chalked tablet waiting on the sawhorses at the far end. "And if a family needs the third line because the first two lied for forty years?"

"Then I call it identification and write faster than the office can object." Lucille lowered her voice. "I've settled nearly all of them. Mr. Wolfe's people have not settled his."

Mr. Wolfe's people. There was enough truth and enough lie in that phrase to fill a grave by itself.

Noreen stood by Gabriel's blank tablet with her jacket buttoned wrong and a safety pin closing the gap at her throat where one button had gone missing. She had a little round tobacco tin in her palm and every so often tipped a shard of cinnamon bark from it into her mouth to settle her stomach. She chewed with the same stubborn concentration other people brought to prayer. Vernon had placed himself a little apart from her, broad and well-kept in a black coat too good for ridge dust, folding and unfolding a white handkerchief into sharper squares as if he could press the whole morning back into church order by hand. Rafe stood on the other side of the blank stone in a work shirt washed clean but still carrying dam mud ground white into the seams at the cuffs. He had shaved. It only made the scar at his chin show plainer.

Della crossed to them because Gabriel's blank stone was drawing stray eyes from all over the ridge, and trouble always spread once people had a clean place to stare.

Noreen did not waste greeting. She tapped the blank stone with one knuckle. "This one needs more than a name and a guess."

Vernon said, "The marker needs dignity."

Noreen gave him a look so dry it might have cracked paint. "Your side of the family had forty years to try dignity. You put him in a fever hole and let the ground keep the rest."

"I did not put my brother anywhere," Vernon said. He kept his voice church-level, low and deliberate, like a man who believed control itself was evidence. "And I will not have a gravestone turned into a spite board."

Rafe let out a short laugh with no humor in it. "That rock ain't big enough for all the spite around him."

Della saw his jaw working. He had the look he got when shame came close, as if some part of him believed he could grind it down behind his teeth before anybody heard it.

Noreen ignored Vernon and spoke to Della. "I want it plain. Gabriel Wolfe. Found in the Old Sion fever ground. And I want the rest of it where it can't be denied again."

She meant Rafe. She meant blood. She meant the thing the county could use and the valley could use and Vernon could never again smooth over if it was cut permanent into limestone. She was not wrong to want a witness that could not be stolen. Della knew that. She also knew stone outlived the reasons a person used when ordering it.

Rafe said, "My name doesn't belong on his marker."

Noreen's thin face sharpened further. "Your blood does."

"Blood ain't a marker line."

"It does when people spend a lifetime pretending it wasn't there."

Vernon moved in then, smelling faintly of starch and the hair tonic he used on Sundays. "This is exactly why the stone should hold only his proper name. No inflammatory language. No family accusations."

"You don't get to say proper to me," Noreen said. "Not today."

Lucille came up with her order card in hand and her office voice already on. "What I need is final wording. The stonecutter leaves room for three lines without charging extra, provided the third line pertains to identification."

"Then write it full," Noreen said. "Write that he was Gabriel Wolfe. Write that he was buried in the fever ground under no name. Write that Raphael Bledsoe was his son."

Rafe took one hard step toward the stone. "No."

The word came out flat enough to make June look up from the card table. Ruthie, two graves over, stopped with the biscuit sack still open in her hand.

Noreen faced Rafe clean on. "You'd rather keep wearing the cheaper lie."

"I'd rather not be carved over in front of half the valley."

"This isn't about your pride."

"Hell it ain't."

Vernon made a little sound in his throat, offended to hear the curse near open graves and pleased at the excuse to look offended. Della wanted to slap the satisfaction off his face.

Noreen said, "You think I'm asking for pride? I'm asking for a rock that tells the truth after all of you are gone and I'm gone and he's still under it. Your daddy vanished into that ground because people with better coats than mine decided silence suited them. If his son's named nowhere, that silence is still doing the work."

Rafe's shoulders went hard as hickory. "Don't call me that in front of him."

"Why not? He earned less than the word."

That landed. Della saw it land in the way Rafe's head jerked, in the way he looked suddenly younger than thirty-nine, younger than a husband, younger than a father, just a boy again with a need he could not carry decent in public. She had done that to him. Gabriel had done it first, but she had done her share and more.

Vernon said, "There is no legal reason to connect the boy to that stone."

Rafe swung toward him. "I ain't your boy."

"Then stop behaving like one."

The sentence was polished enough to look harmless until it cut. Della watched Rafe's hands open and close. Old Sion had had crooked rows and roots enough to trip a man before he got into trouble. Limestone Ridge gave anger too much room to run.

"Vernon." Lucille said it sharply, with more steel in her clerk voice than the county deserved from her. "If you are not placing the order, do not complicate it."

"My family is implicated whether I place it or not."

"It was implicated before breakfast," Ruthie said from behind them.

They all turned. She had come up quiet, the coffee jar hanging from two fingers now, the biscuit sack folded shut. There was flour on one cuff where she had wiped her hands without noticing. "You can stand there talking lawful if you want, Mr. Wolfe, but the grave has already been opened, the hearing has already happened, and the whole county has already heard what your family kept. That part is done."

Vernon's nostrils flared. He did not answer her. Men like him never liked being corrected by the woman who washed their shirts in other years.

June had risen from the crate without Della seeing her do it. She came over with Lucille's pencil in one hand and the order card in the other, not bold exactly, not showy, just steady. Her face still had that child's longness in it until she spoke.

"What do you need on the card?" she asked Noreen. "His name, or all the rest folks want to fight over?"

Noreen looked at her as if she had not expected the question from that quarter. "Both, if a stone had any sense."

June nodded once and glanced at the blank lines. "It can't do both. It only keeps what gets cut."

For a second nobody said anything. Della could hear the wind rasping through the bucket of cedar boughs somebody had set down. Down below, a truck gear changed on the road and carried thin through the ridge air.

June looked at the card again, at the three short lines waiting on it. "If you spend them all on the fight, folks'll still pick the piece they like. If you leave off the fever ground, they'll act like he was buried here the first time. What can't be left off?"

It was the hard practical sense of a copyist counting lines and asking what the stone could carry. Della felt something in her chest shift with equal parts pride and fear.

Noreen said, slower now, "His name."

Rafe said, "And where he was found."

Vernon said, "There is no need to mention the fever plot."

Della turned on him. "There is every need."

Her voice came out calmer than she felt, which was when people who knew her best usually had the sense to step back.

"They put him there," she said. "Or I put him there, if you want the burden named exact. Either way, the fever ground's part of the truth of his body. Not because it's shameful. Because it happened. A man doesn't lie in one piece of earth for thirty-nine years and then get carried uphill into clean dirt like the first place never touched him."

Noreen's jaw eased a fraction.

Della went on. "But the stone doesn't have to do every other job at once. It doesn't have to settle inheritance. It doesn't have to sort men into fathers and not fathers for every stranger that comes by. It has to keep this one dead man from being buried wrong again."

Rafe was looking at her now, not the stone. His face had gone closed in the way it did when he was hearing something he did not want and knew was true besides.

June held out the card. "What if it says that?"

Della took the pencil from her and turned the card over to the clean side. Her thumb protested the pressure. She wrote slowly, her letters heavier than June's, less pretty and harder to mistake.

Gabriel Wolfe  
Taken up from Old Sion fever ground  
Reburied on Limestone Ridge, 1958

Lucille read it over Della's hand. "I can classify the second line as identification provenance."

"Say it plain," Ruthie said.

"It will pass."

Noreen read the wording twice. The wind lifted the edge of her jacket and showed the careful let-out seam under one sleeve where she had made room for age with thread that almost matched and did not quite. "It leaves out who lost by it."

"Yes," Della said.

Rafe's mouth tightened, waiting for a fight in that yes.

"Because the stone can't hold that honest," Della said. "It would only hold it hard."

That was the closest she had come in weeks to saying what she meant on the first try.

Noreen's eyes moved to Rafe. "You won't claim him, then."

There it was at last without all the trimming. Not just a name on stone. Not just blood. Money lay under it, land lay under it, the long mean arithmetic of what had been denied and what might still be pried loose if a man were willing to let the county rename him.

Rafe scraped one boot in the gravel. White dust climbed the leather where red clay used to rule. He looked at Vernon, then at the blank field of the stone, then finally at Della. When he spoke, his voice had roughened down past anger into something barer.

"He put blood in me," he said. "That didn't cost him but one night. The rest of it cost her."

The words struck Della low and hard. She could feel the old kitchen under them, the broken chair leg, Gabriel's weight on the floorboards, Eli gone out the door with murder and fear running shoulder to shoulder in him. She could feel, too, every year after, every winter shirt patched, every fever sat through, every dollar stretched, every lie chosen because she had thought a cheaper name might keep a child fed longer than the true one. Rafe had never said any of that to her. She had not expected him to hand it back in front of witnesses.

He kept going, faster now because if he slowed he might stop. "I'm not turning Wolfe for land I haven't seen a penny from and a father that never raised me. Let Noreen have her claim if the county will hear it. Let him be Gabriel Wolfe on the stone. He ought to be. But I'm staying where the work was done."

No one answered at once. Even Vernon had sense enough to keep his mouth shut for a breath.

Then Noreen gave one short nod. She did not smile. She was not made for wasting dignity on false softening. "All right," she said. "But I wanted it asked where everybody could hear."

"You made sure of that," Rafe said.

That would have started another round on a worse morning, but something had broken open and used up the need. Noreen held out her hand for the card. Lucille kept it instead.

"I'll file the order now before anybody reconsiders," she said.

"That's your best skill," Ruthie told her.

Lucille almost smiled. Almost.

When the time came to set Gabriel's stone, Vernon stepped back and adjusted his cuff as if he had some business with the sleeve. Della saw it and put him out of her mind. She moved to take one side of the tablet, but Rafe was already there. He took the heavier end without comment. Noreen took the other side from Della before Della could argue, her hands small and sinewed and stronger than their look. Together they carried the stone over the new-dug strip of earth where Gabriel had been laid again the day before.

The ridge soil was pale under the top crust, shot through with chips of limestone instead of cedar root. It gave back no smell of old graves now, only dry dirt and cut grass and the faint mineral breath of fresh stone. Della missed the dark wet reek of Old Sion all at once so sharply she almost hated herself for it. Wrong ground or right ground, a body ought to answer the hand that lowered it. This place only accepted.

They set the base first. Rafe had to crouch deep to get the angle. His knuckles brushed the dirt. Noreen steadied the tablet while Della packed soil with her hands because she trusted her hands more than any tool for final work. The limestone edge bit her palm. June came up with the cedar sprigs somebody had brought and laid three of them at the foot before the wind could snatch them. Nobody said amen. Nobody seemed to know what kind of prayer belonged to a man first buried under no name and then raised into one after nearly four decades.

At last Noreen touched the fresh-cut letters with the pad of one finger. White dust marked her skin.

"That'll hold," she said.

Rafe straightened, wiped his hands on his trousers, and stepped back. He did not look at Vernon. He did not look at Della either. He looked at the stone as if testing whether it had asked too much of him and decided, for now, it had not.

By noon the ridge had settled into a different sound. Less arguing. More quiet footsteps, more small conversations at graves that no longer had to be conducted over open boxes and rotted boards. The dead had their uphill places. Della moved among them with the marker list in one hand and the ache in her back climbing higher with each hour. Here was Sadie with the two surnames fitted as narrowly as the daughters would allow. Here was a baby no longer reduced to BABY GIRL because June had found the aunt who remembered the given name before it could be lost again. Here was a husband whose lawful wife and field wife had finally let the stone carry only the one thing neither could deny: the years he lay between.

Limestone dust sat in every cuff, and every corrected name still carried the bruise of the fight that had bought it. Even so, the ridge held a truer account than the old lie.

Once, near the wagon, Della passed the still-blank stone set aside for Silas and had to stop. The ridge wind moved across the face of it with a rasping little whisper. He had carved names for other people until quarry dust had settled in the seams of his lungs and turned each breath into work. Now he waited on wording like the rest of them. Della laid her fingers against the unwritten stone and took them away again before anybody could see. There would be time enough for that grief after today's names stopped requiring her.

By the time the last family trailed off down the road and Lucille packed her papers into the county box, the sun had shifted west and given the new cemetery a thinner kind of gold than Old Sion ever took. Limestone Ridge did not soften under evening. It merely went bright and then cold.

Rafe walked home ahead of them with his hat in his hand. Ruthie caught up to him twice and said something low each time that Della could not hear. June carried the copy of the final marker list under one arm as if it were a school composition, though Della knew by the way she kept checking the edge that she understood the paper could matter longer than school ever would.

At the house, Ruthie set beans back to a simmer and fried salt pork with onions because the day had earned grease. She took off her shoes at the door and three pebbles fell out onto the floorboards, one after another, sharp as thrown buckshot. She looked down at them and snorted once through her nose before kicking them aside. Outside, the evening air had turned cool enough that the damp washing on the line would need another hour to dry. Inside, the kitchen gave back the old smells that had held the family together longer than truth had: onion, hot fat, woodsmoke sunk into joists, lye from the washbench, the iron tang from the pump water pail.

June spread the marker list on the table beside the lamp and copied it into a school notebook in a narrower hand than she used for lessons. She made a column for confirmed dates and another for uncertain ones. Where a family had argued and chosen one form, she left a small mark in the margin so a later reader would know there had once been dispute there. She did not ask permission to do that. She simply did it, the way a person does when the work has begun teaching her back.

Della sat across from her with both hands around a cup of reheated coffee. The heat eased the knuckles some. Rafe had gone to the washpan on the porch. She could hear the water slap and the scrape of his palms over his face, over the back of his neck, over the forearms that had carried stone all day.

"You don't need both columns," Della said after a while.

June did not stop writing. "I know."

"Then why make them?"

"Because folks remember guesses as facts if you lay them all in one line."

Della looked at her.

June finally raised her head. "That's what kept happening."

June said it like she was setting a column straight, with plain method and no appetite for drama. Della felt tired enough to be grateful.

"Let me see Gabriel's," Della said.

June turned the notebook. She had copied the inscription exactly as ordered, then left a clean inch of paper below it. The entry sat bare and workmanlike, three exact lines with the paternity trouble left out of the ink, though any fifteen-year-old with a mind for drama could have been tempted.

"Why leave that space?" Della asked.

June shrugged one shoulder. "In case something gets proved later and needs room."

There it was again, that hard little exactness. June handled records the way Della handled forceps: for what they could prove and carry, not for ornament. She had left room for a correction without pretending the old page had known it first.

The porch boards sounded under Rafe's step. He came in toweling his face with the end of a flour sack, his hair still wet at the temples. Without his boots he moved quieter, more like the boy he had been before boots and debt and fatherhood taught him to strike a room first. He smelled of cold pump water and soap not fully rinsed.

Ruthie set his plate down before he asked. "Eat while it's hot."

He sat. For a minute there was only spoon against bowl and June's pencil moving. Della let the quiet hold. Too many things in the house had been forced before they were ready these past months.

Then Rafe said, still looking at the beans, "Lucille asked me once more before she left whether I wanted anything entered with the Wolfe papers."

Ruthie leaned a hip against the stove. June's pencil stopped.

"I told her no," he said. "I told her I wasn't filing under that name and I wasn't coming back next week with some new notion because the orchard got bigger in my head overnight."

Della set her cup down carefully. "All right."

He gave a humorless huff. "That's all you have to say?"

"You have been wanting me not to tell you what to do for years."

"That ain't the same as saying nothing."

She looked at him then. The lamp put hollows under his cheekbones. He was tired past anger, which made him resemble Eli less than he ever had and Gabriel hardly at all. Men were cruelly ordinary when they were worn down. Blood did not change that.

"It won't make your bank note smaller," she said.

"No."

"It won't thaw the Bledsoe claim."

"No."

"It may leave you cussing me come winter."

That got the side of his mouth to move. "I've cussed you through worse than winter."

Ruthie turned back to the stove so they could have the line without her face in it.

Rafe wiped a piece of cornbread through the bean liquor and set it back down without eating. "I meant what I said up there."

Della waited.

"About the cost." He rubbed his thumb against the rough edge of the spoon handle. "Blood is one thing. A man can spend that and never feel it. Raising a child is feed, shoes, quinine, lamp oil, school tablets, all the shit that keeps getting bought and used up. I know what that costs. I know who paid it."

June looked down hard at her notebook, pretending courtesy, hearing every word.

Rafe went on, rougher now because this was not public speaking and he had no wish to sound fine in his own kitchen. "Gabriel can be what he is. I'm not saying different. But I was raised in this house. I worked Bledsoe dirt. I buried Bledsoe folk. If I let the county turn me into a Wolfe now, all they're doing is using his money name to tell me my life before this morning didn't count proper. And I'm not giving them that."

The house stayed quiet after he finished, quiet enough for Della to hear fat spit in the skillet and a truck far down the road shifting gears toward the river. She had thought all summer that truth, once opened, would behave like floodwater and take every bank with it. Maybe some of it did. Maybe some of it only had to be directed into a ditch deep enough to keep it from ruining the wrong field.

"All right," she said again, and this time the words were not empty.

Rafe nodded once as if a contract had been spoken plain.

June lifted the pencil. "Do I write anything in the notebook about that?"

Rafe looked at her, then at the page.

"No," he said. "You write the stone and where he came from. That's enough for paper tonight."

June considered, then nodded and wrote nothing further.

Ruthie brought the skillet to table and sat down at last. One of the clothespins was still in her apron pocket. It clicked against the chair rung when she crossed her leg under. "That's the first sensible thing any of you have said over paper in a month."

Rafe snorted. Della, to her own surprise, almost laughed.

They ate with the stubborn concentration of tired people who still had to rise before light. Rafe kept his eyes on the bowl and went at the beans like a man feeding tomorrow's labor, and the quiet in the room held work, grief, and unfinished trouble. He still had dam work in the morning and debt after that. Noreen still had a claim to push against a county more comfortable with Vernon than with her. The valley was still going under water by the week. Silas Wynn was still dead. Gabriel still had thirty-nine years of dirt no fresh stone could undo.

But the notebook lay open under June's hand, and the line she had copied remained narrow and exact:

Gabriel Wolfe  
Taken up from Old Sion fever ground  
Reburied on Limestone Ridge, 1958

She left the space below it blank. Della watched that blank stay blank while the lamp burned and the house settled around them. Under June's hand it looked like discipline, a margin kept open for whatever the living could prove.
