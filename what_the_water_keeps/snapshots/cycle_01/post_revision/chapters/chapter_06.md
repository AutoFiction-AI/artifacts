# Chapter 6

The heat had got into the hill before noon and stayed there meanly. By the time Della came up the muddy path from Cedar Bench, the red clay had baked a skin over the morning ruts, but underneath it still held yesterday's wet and gave at the edge of her shoes with a soft, sucking pull. The cemetery ridge looked bleached and used up in the white light. Limestone markers threw a glare sharp enough to make her squint. Downriver, too far to see and too near not to feel, the dam works let off one of their low concussions. The sound came through the ground first, a thump under the soles of her feet, then rolled on through the air and died among the cicadas.

She had given the morning over to chores and still not worn the name thin in her head.

The county boys had gone to the schoolhouse office to sign tags and draw their half-day pay. Lucille liked the papers entered before supper if she could get them. That left Old Sion with only the flies, the cedar shade, and Silas Wynn bent over a headstone near the upper row, scraping old lichen from the carved letters with the point of a nail.

He had his hat pushed back and his shirt dark under the arms and down the center of his spine. His shoulders still looked broad from a distance, the old shape of strength not yet willing to admit it had been overruled, but the closer Della came the plainer the failure showed. He had to stop every few strokes and wait with the nail in his fingers while his breath worked itself up from somewhere deep and granular. A cough took him then, not a quick clearing one but a quarry cough that started low and kept hauling until his whole back jerked. He turned his head and spat into the grass. The spit was roped with gray.

When it let him go, he opened a Prince Albert tin and shook out a horehound drop. He sucked it without pleasure, like a man taking medicine he did not believe in but had paid for all the same. Silas had carried those drops as long as Della could remember; he said stone dust left a penny taste on the tongue and horehound was the only thing that could beat it back. The tin lid clicked shut under his thumb.

"You picked the meanest hour to walk up here," he said without looking at her.

"It bought me some privacy."

"Privacy," he said, and made it sound like something rich people believed in.

Della stopped beside the stone. It was one of the old McTeer markers, soft limestone with the lamb worn half blind by weather. Silas had been tracing the child's name back out of the green spread on it. The nail trembled once between his fingers before he set it down.

"I need to ask you something plain," she said.

"Then ask it plain."

She glanced down the slope. The lower ground dropped away into the fever plot, rougher and less kept than the proper rows above. There were no stones there but field rocks and a few rusted tags at the feet of long-sunk graves. The wash that had opened Gabriel's burial was hidden from here by blackberry canes and a tilt of cedar roots. Even so, she could feel where it lay, the way a bad tooth announced itself without being touched.

"Walk with me."

Silas watched her then. His eyes were pale under the shade of his hat, not soft and not especially suspicious either, only tired of being asked for portions of trouble instead of the whole thing.

"If this is about your boy nosing around in the dark, I already heard him."

"It's about the grave."

"Everything's about the grave now."

"Yes."

He took up his tin, put it in his pocket, and pushed himself straight with more care than he would have wanted witnessed. That act alone took enough breath that he had to stand a moment before stepping off. Della turned and went slow on purpose. He was tall enough that people still expected him to cover ground like he used to, but halfway to the lower row he stopped with his fist closed against his ribs and waited through another cough. The sound scraped at the air behind her. She did not pretend not to hear it.

"You ought to sit more often," she said.

"And let them boys set stones like fence posts? I'd die quicker from looking at it."

"You may die quick enough anyway."

"There she is," he said. "The comfort I came for."

But he smiled a little at that, or as close as Silas Wynn came to smiling, and went on.

The fever plot had never taken grass well. Even in high summer it kept a ragged look, thin weeds, raw clay, the ground settling in old blunt ovals where poor families and contagion cases had gone under with less ceremony than the church liked to remember. The washout that had exposed the hidden grave had caved on one side since the last rain. Fresh clay showed there, dark as liver under the crust, and a split length of board jutted through the dirt at an angle. A blue bottle fly rose and settled again.

Della stopped above it. The smell below the ordinary summer smells was the cemetery's own: damp wood waking, old cloth, mineral wet, something stale and enclosed that did not belong to open air. It was not yet the full opened-grave smell. That would come later, with pried boards and light in the box. This was only the first leak.

Silas came up beside her slower than he would once have tolerated. He set his feet apart and worked at breathing through his nose. She could hear the rattle in his chest, a handful of gravel shaken inside a bucket.

"Well," he said. "Look at it. It hasn't gone away."

"That's not what I came to ask."

"I know what you came to ask."

Della kept her eyes on the wash. "Then answer before I have to phrase it."

He rubbed his thumb along the seam of the tobacco tin in his pocket. "If they take it up proper," he said, "yes."

The word landed with no room around it.

She said, "You're too sure."

"No. You're too hungry for maybe."

The cicadas had sawed themselves into one hard sheet of sound. A gust moved up from the river and brought rot with it, algae and hot metal from the disturbed water down below. Della could feel sweat easing under her dress and gathering at the bend of her spine. She wanted him to talk longer before he said anything final. She had wanted that since she left the house. She had come up here like a woman going for bad news from a doctor she knew and mistrusted, hoping he might misread the plate.

"Placement alone won't do it," she said. "Not with Vernon breathing over every shovel. He'll say half the valley once buried men down here who were none of the county's business."

"Let Vernon say it. He likes hearing himself more than being right."

"Location alone isn't proof."

"Good thing I didn't say location alone."

He pointed with his chin toward the break in the ground. "You remember how we laid him."

She remembered too much. The memory did not arrive as narrative; it came as pressure in the hands. The slick drag of wet cloth under Gabriel's shoulders. The dead weight of his hips when the old door they had used for a litter caught on the kitchen threshold. A lantern guttering because her own hands would not stop shaking enough to trim it. Blood going black where it had dried into the grain of the floorboards between the stove and the table. Eli at the back step with his two stiff mill fingers working uselessly at the bib of his overalls, his face gone the color of lard. Not talking. Not saying he meant it. Not saying he had not. Just staring at Gabriel on the door as if another man might still rise out from under him and fix the whole thing.

She said, "I remember."

"Then you remember his left shoulder."

Della looked at Silas.

"You set that collarbone yourself after he came off that chestnut in the Wolfe orchard," he said. "It healed high. Crooked as a knot in green pine. Bones keep a thing like that. Better than people do."

The orchard had been all windfallen apples that week, yellow ones split open in the grass and wasps inside them, Gabriel laughing through the pain because he hated letting anybody see him helpless. She had cut his shirt away with sewing shears and pressed the break back while he bit on a folded dish towel and sweated through the tablecloth. Afterward he had reached for her with the same hand she had just bound against his chest, clumsy with pain and still certain she would come closer if he asked. She had. She remembered the wool horse blanket in the loft rasping the backs of her thighs, the smell of bruised apples and his sweat trapped under the rafters, the way he kept that bad shoulder half-raised even while he pushed into her because she had told him once already she would not set it twice. When she walked home at dusk there was blood from his loosened bandage dried on the inside hem of her shift.

"A crooked collarbone isn't a name," she said.

"No. It's part of one."

He tapped his own upper gum with a dirty fingernail. "So's that tooth he never had. Premolar on the right. He could whistle through that gap after whiskey and thought it charmed women."

Della did not answer. Gabriel had not whistled through it. He had tongued it when he was thinking and smiled a little on one side so the gap showed before the rest of him meant to. Silas knew the coarser version because men held different pieces of one another.

"If the jaw's held together enough, that'll still show," Silas said. "And if it doesn't, the shoulder will. And if somebody wants more than that, I can tell them where his feet were pointing and why. We put him wrong for a fever burial because you didn't have room to dig deeper and I wouldn't roll him into that child row like offal. Anybody who knows this ground will hear it."

Della's mouth tasted of old pennies though she had not taken dust into her lungs the way he had. Below them the slant of the hidden box showed through the wash, only a suggestion of it, no more than a board edge and shadow, but now that he had spoken she could not look at it without seeing the rest. The rough poplar planks Silas had cut from crate wood because there had been no time and no sawmill order, only what he had stacked under his shed for patching henhouse walls. The square nails he drove bent in one place because the hammer kept slipping in his palm. The feed-sack strip Della tied under Gabriel's jaw after his mouth kept falling open, not out of reverence but because she could not bear the dead surprise of it while they worked.

"The boards may be gone," she said.

"The boards may be soft, sure. The man inside them won't have put himself right."

"Forty years changes a body."

"It does." Silas took the horehound tin back out, opened it, thought better of it, and shut it again. "It doesn't make one body into another because it'd be convenient for the living."

She turned on him. "Don't talk to me as if convenience is what made any of this."

He met that squarely. "Then talk to me like I was there."

The words struck harder because he said them quiet. Della looked away first, back to the wash, because anger at him was easier than the rest and would get her nowhere. She had not come for absolution and he had none to give.

"I know you were there," she said.

"No, you know I kept my mouth shut. That isn't the same thing."

He bent suddenly with a cough, one hand on his knee. This one tore through him long enough that Della stepped closer before she could decide whether he would accept the arm. He waved her off while he spat into the clay. There was gray again, and one darker clot in it. He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand and stood slowly, blinking water out of his eyes.

"You need a doctor," she said.

"I need younger lungs and thirty less years of stone dust. A doctor would only charge me for saying so."

"You could stop cutting."

"And eat what? The lichen?"

That was Silas too: no speech about dignity, no self-pity fit for church company, only the mean arithmetic of an old body and no money. The coughing had left a sheen on his face. His shirt clung to the knobs of his collarbones. Della had watched plenty of chests fail in her life, drowned in fever or labor or blood, and his did not scare her by surprise. It scared her by length. A body could spend months making the same argument before it won.

She said, "You could've lied to me."

"I know."

"You have before."

"Only where I thought the lie still had some shelter in it."

The dam thumped again, farther off or else her ears had filled. A crow flapped up from the cedars below and crossed over the fever plot, black against the white afternoon. Della listened to the wingbeats until they were gone. When she spoke again, her voice came out flatter than she intended.

"If they bring him up and it's him, Rafe loses the only clean way through. You know that."

"There wasn't ever a clean way through."

"There was one cheaper than the others."

"For who?"

She opened her mouth and stopped. Silas did not push into the silence. He had the patience of men who worked with stone: let the crack widen by itself. The cicadas kept at it. Somewhere below, water moved hidden under brush and loosened earth.

"For my people," she said at last. "For the ones still eating."

"And Gabriel wasn't one of your people?"

"Don't do that."

"He fathered your son."

She looked at him sharply, though no one else was there to hear it.

"Don't look so astonished," he said. "I might not talk much, Della, but I wasn't born witless. I knew enough that night. Eli knew enough too, which was the whole damned trouble of it."

The memory came harder then because he had named the edge around it. Gabriel alive half an hour longer than he should have been, breath wet in his throat. His hair stuck with blood where the back of his head had met the stove corner. The kitchen smelled of iron and scorched cornmeal and the pear soap Della had used that morning, all of it ruined into one foul sweetness. Eli had stood against the wall with blood on his knuckles and not much on him otherwise, which was how she knew the true blow had been the room itself: the stove corner, the fall, the bad angle that turned a fist-fight into a death. Gabriel's right hand had closed on her apron once, then loosened.

She said, "You don't know everything you think you know."

"I don't need to know how he got under your skirt to know the grave's his."

It was vulgar and exactly calculated not to be more vulgar than necessary. Silas did not waste obscenity. He used it like a pry bar when delicate tools had already failed.

Della folded her arms, less for defense than to hold herself still. "If Eli had stayed, the Wolfes would've finished him."

"I know."

"If Gabriel had been found in my kitchen, same answer."

"I know."

"Rafe would've grown under that name and under Vernon Wolfe's boot besides."

"Maybe."

"Not maybe. Certainly."

"All right. Certainly." Silas's breath whistled once on the word. "What saved the boy then may be skinning him now."

She could not say he was wrong. What scraped was hearing the right measure from him, a man who usually left people alone to furnish their own excuses and live inside them. She had spent thirty-eight years tamping this one down with whatever came to hand: Gabriel was dead. Eli was wild with horror and shame. Vernon was already the sort of man who could make sin into proof when it belonged to poorer people. A child was coming. The valley was small enough to hear anything shouted once. She had counted on the years to pack hard over it. The secret belonged to the dead arrangement of one night and the morning after. She had never named it theft in her own head. She had named it triage.

But Rafe had stood in her kitchen asking for a name as if names were grain in a bin and she was hoarding the last sack. June had heard Gabriel in the dark and carried it away in her thin serious face. Vernon Wolfe breathed easier every year Gabriel stayed absent. And now the county had put shovels into the fever plot and called it administration.

Silas said, "Back then we were hiding blood before sunrise. You had an injured man to send off and a dead one you couldn't leave on your floor. I never argued that. But that night ended. This didn't."

She kept her eyes on the wash because if she looked at him she might have to answer.

"Your boy is trying to buy his way out on another man's bones," Silas said. "The dead man is still down here under a lie. Eli's still being used because he's gone and can't object. You can call that protection if you want. Sounds more like choosing who gets ground up."

"And what would you have me do?" she said. "Walk into the office and tell Lucille Holt the whole valley's built half its households on papers that don't match the sweat that made them, the lye-scoured shifts, the teeth marks buttoned up under good collars? Hand Vernon a better weapon than he's ever had? Tell my son he's not even the right bastard?"

"I didn't say spill every grave and cradle you ever touched."

"No. Only this one."

"This one is enough."

He said it without grandeur. That was what made it unbearable. The words sat between them like a tally marked on a plank: plain, measurable, impossible to haggle down.

Della crouched at the edge of the wash, careful of her skirt, and touched the raw clay with two fingers. It was cool under the crust. Tiny grains stuck in the lines of her skin. Just below the surface the earth had the packed resistance of old digging, the way refilled ground held memory in its layers even after roots worked through it. Her left thumb ached at once from the bend. She pressed harder anyway.

She remembered kneeling here by lantern light with the sweat already gone cold on her body. Silas in shirtsleeves, passing dirt up out of the narrow hole because they had no time to make the grave decent-sized. The lamp set on a stone, flame turned low. Gabriel in the box between them, face bound, his good suit coat folded under his head because Della could not leave it soaked on her kitchen floor and could not bury him outright in it either. Eli standing off once, just once, long enough to look down and then turn away into the dark and vomit among the cedars. By dawn he was gone. She had watched him take the mule path south with a bundle under his arm and his shoulders bent inward, as if he were already making himself smaller for hiding.

She had never told anybody about the coat under Gabriel's head.

"You kept one thing that night that was foolish," Silas said.

She looked up. "What?"

"That coat."

The clay cooled in her palm.

"I knew you'd remember it if I said so," he went on. "You thought cloth might count for kindness. All it counted for was making the box too tight. We had to set his shoulder harder because of it."

Della stared at him.

"That's why I know where the bones will lie," he said. "We forced the left side down. You cussed me for it. I cussed you back. We were both right and both too late."

She could hear it again if she let herself: the dull knock of bone against fresh-cut plank when they settled him, the ugly intimacy of arranging a dead man's limbs in a container built in haste. That sound had lived in her for years without language. Silas had just given it some.

"You remember every rotten detail," she said.

"It's my trade."

"No. Your trade is stone."

"And graves. Don't flatter the first one by forgetting the second."

He eased himself down onto a low field rock because standing had become too expensive. The motion cost him a grimace he did not bother to hide. He sat with his knees wide, forearms on them, taking air in careful portions. Close like this he looked older than sixty-eight. The skin under his eyes had gone almost purple from the coughing strain. One of his bootlaces had been knotted with baling twine where the leather had broken through.

"I didn't come to quarrel with you," Della said.

"Then you came poorly equipped."

She almost laughed, and hated him a little for earning it.

"I came to know if there was still uncertainty left in it," she said. "That's all."

"No, it isn't all. If it was all, you'd have asked from up there and gone home."

He leaned and spat again, this time only clear string and dust. "You came because you wanted somebody besides yourself to carry the final answer. That way when it comes due you can say an old man with bad lungs forced your hand."

"I've never needed a man to force my hand."

"No," he said. "You've done all your worst work freely."

There it was. Dry, mean, accurate. Silas did not speak often enough to dull his edges. Della stood. The blood climbed into her face and then left it colder than before.

"Say that again."

He looked up at her, unfrightened. "I said you've done it freely. You knew the weight of it. That's different."

For a moment neither of them moved. The heat pressed from above and the opened earth breathed from below. A fly worried at the board edge in the wash. Della could have gone then. Pride offered it to her. Walk uphill, leave him with his cough and his righteousness, carry the uncertainty she had come wanting whether he gave it or not. But he had taken uncertainty out of the matter piece by piece, and pride was only another way of delaying when delay had already started to stink.

She said, "If I speak, it doesn't stop with the grave."

"Maybe not."

"Rafe will hear what he can't use."

"He'll hear it anyway, later and worse."

"Vernon will fight."

"Good. Let him get dirty enough for folks to see what color he is underneath."

"You don't have to live in my house after."

"No," Silas said. "I only have to die with my own share of it."

The sentence went between them and held. He did not dress it up. He sat there with dust on his boots, baling twine on one lace, gray on his spit, and spoke of dying the way another man might speak of hauling stone before rain. Della had attended enough bedsides to know when a person had begun measuring time against the body rather than the calendar. Silas was already doing it. Every climb, every cough, every breath bought at a pause had entered his reckoning. That made his patience shorter, not kinder.

"Are you afraid?" she asked before she decided to.

He considered the question without surprise. "Of the coughing? Some. Of the stopping? Less than I'd like."

"That isn't what I meant."

"I know." He wiped his palm on his trouser leg. "I'm afraid of leaving work behind me crooked. Same as always."

That was the most he had ever said to her about himself in one piece. The coughing had pared him down to the few things he could still afford to care about. Della looked at his hands, the knuckles thickened with old breaks, the ingrained white of limestone in the seams. They had helped lower infants no bigger than loaves and men who took four bearers to shift. They had helped her commit the one burial Old Sion was not supposed to contain.

"You think we've left this crooked," she said.

"I think we laid it crooked on purpose and now the ground's pushing back."

The bluntness of it made her almost angry again, but anger had started to feel ornamental. She was too tired for ornament. She wiped her clay-streaked fingers on her handkerchief and looked once more into the wash. The board edge there offered only persistence, the clay still holding the bad slant of the lie. A thing buried badly enough and long enough might still keep the posture in which it had been hidden.

From up on the ridge came a far shout, one of the county boys back from the office, maybe, or some child cut loose after lessons. The sound did not carry words. The cemetery would not stay private much longer.

Della said, "If they ask you in front of people, will you say all this?"

"Yes."

"Even if it starts something you can't finish."

"I won't be the one starting it."

"That is a clerk's answer."

"No," Silas said. "It's a gravedigger's. The starting happened when we shut the box."

She closed her eyes once, only once. When she opened them the heat was no less and the grave no different, but something soft had been scooped out of the center of her thinking. She had been nursing one last comfort: that if she waited, if she kept her tongue and outlasted the county's appetite and Rafe's panic and Vernon's watchfulness, time might do the ugly work for her. It hadn't. It had only preserved the evidence in wood rot and bone tilt and a gap in the teeth.

She had spent years treating silence as a blanket. Silas had just shown her the corners worn through.

"All right," she said.

He nodded, not triumphant. "All right."

She turned uphill. After three steps she stopped and looked back. Silas was still on the field rock, one hand braced on his thigh before he would try standing again. For the first time she saw how narrow his chest had gone above the belt, how each breath lifted almost nothing. He caught her looking and scowled as if meanness itself might hold his ribs together.

"Don't sit here till dark," she said.

"Then don't leave me with all the scenery."

"There's no scenery in this place."

"That's because you've known it too long."

She let that stay. Then she climbed.

By the time she reached the upper row, the voices from the ridge had grown clearer. Men coming back. Paperwork finished. Work waiting. Della paused beside the little McTeer lamb Silas had been cleaning. The child's name showed plainer now through the scraped lichen, not beautifully, only enough. Enough was all the day seemed interested in.

Below her the fever plot lay under heat and insects and the thin black mouths of its shadows. Somewhere in that raw clay an old shoulder still held its wrong shape. Somewhere a gap in the teeth remained a gap. Keeping still would not unknot any of it. Delay had turned into its own appetite, deciding day after day which hunger got fed.

Another dam blast traveled up the valley and entered the stones before it reached the ear. Della put her hand on the worn headstone to steady herself, feeling the stored sun in the limestone, and started toward the voices gathering downslope before she had time to imagine one more excuse.
