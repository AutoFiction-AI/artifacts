# Chapter 9

By daylight Della had the flour bin emptied into a washpan and three jars of last summer's peaches lined up on the cellar step, and the ledger still was not in the house.

She had looked where she had hidden it and where fear said a thief would look next, then in the foolish places a woman only searched when sleep was gone and sense with it: under the ticking of her mattress, behind the stove reservoir, in the coal hod, under the folded Sunday tablecloths that smelled of iron and starch and old smoke. The cellar shelf rasped her stiff thumb raw. Dust from the joists settled into her hair. Somewhere downriver the dam gave one of its low concussions and the peaches trembled in their jars as if even the fruit knew the valley's days were being counted by men who had never eaten from this pantry.

Ruthie stood in the kitchen doorway with her sleeves rolled above her reddened wrists, not stopping Della and not helping either. That was worse. If Ruthie had scolded, Della could have braced against it. If she had joined in, Della could have mistaken that for agreement. Instead she watched with a dish towel pulled through both hands as though she might wring an answer out of the cloth.

"You'll break every jar in here before you bring a book back by force," she said.

"It was here."

"I know where it was." Ruthie glanced toward the open cellar hatch, then back at Della. "I know where it isn't."

Della set another jar on the floorboards harder than she meant to. The ring bit into her palm. Syrup glowed amber behind the glass, thick as old bruise. "Lucille's note said eight."

"It ain't eight yet."

The note lay on the table under the sugar bowl, a half sheet of ruled paper in Lucille Holt's compact hand: Need you in Larkin this morning. County car can take you if ready by eight. Urgent. No explanation, which was explanation enough. Since the ledger had vanished before dawn, every silence around Della had begun to feel shaped.

June came down the ladder from the loft, one stocking on and one hand still pinning up the braid she had slept on. There was blue ink along the side of her middle finger. She looked from the jars on the floor to Della on her knees at the cellar mouth and understood the room before anybody told her.

"You checked the flour?" she asked.

"Yes."

"The medicine shelf?"

"Yes."

June came farther in. "Daddy's tool chest?"

Ruthie said, "Your daddy isn't keeping a lady's notebook under his wrenches, June."

The girl took that in without blinking. She had grown long in the past year and had not yet learned how to carry it. Elbows arrived before the rest of her. When she crouched by the jars her knees made awkward points under her dress, but her face stayed steady, older than fifteen when it turned serious. "Did he know where you kept it?"

Della scraped her fingernail along the underside of the shelf, feeling for the loose board she had once prized up with a butter knife. It gave back bare wood and nothing else. "A body can know too much in a house and still not know the right thing."

That answer was the wrong one and they all knew it. Ruthie let the towel drop against her skirt.

"Then ask it plain," she said to June, though her eyes stayed on Della. "Ask whether he might've taken it."

June asked, because she would. "Might he?"

Della sat back on her heels. The cellar's cool had soaked through her dress at the knees. Above her, the kitchen already held the day's heat, fireless and close. Rafe's cup still stood by the pump basin with a brown half-moon of coffee gone oily at the rim. His work shirt was off its peg. Men left early in July. That proved nothing. Men also reached for whatever might keep the bank man off the porch a month longer and called the reach necessity instead of theft. That proved nothing either.

"I don't know," she said.

June nodded once, as if Della had put a measurement on the table instead of evasion. "If the book's gone, what are you taking to Larkin?"

"Myself."

"Is that enough?"

It had been enough for Sadie Kincaid's daughters. Enough for Lucille, more often than Della liked. Enough for neighbors who would rather ask the midwife than the law how a life had gone. But the answer caught in her now, because a missing book changed the weight of a living witness. Paper sat still. Paper did not age, forget, protect, repent, or love the wrong child too much to tell him the worst thing in time.

"It'll have to do," she said.

Ruthie went to the stove and lifted the lid off the biscuit pan though breakfast had been finished an hour before. The biscuits inside were cold and a shade too brown along the bottoms. She wrapped two in a square of clean flour sack and held them out without softness.

"Eat on the road," she said. "You come back meaner when you don't."

Della took the bundle. Ruthie's fingers were rough with lye and washwater, nails bitten down blunt. The younger woman had mended the same housedress at both hips with thread that did not quite match. Della noticed these things the way she noticed whether a baby cried from hunger or wind, and sometimes the noticing felt like a curse. Everything in the room carried how close they lived to empty: the sugar bowl filled with brown crumbs instead of white, the chair rung wrapped in cord where the wood had split, the window curtain pieced from two flour sacks whose blue lettering showed through if the sun hit right.

"You search while I'm gone," Della said. "Don't leave out the smokehouse shelf."

Ruthie gave her a look flat as a shut door. "I've been searching since before light. You weren't the only one awake."

That shamed Della more than argument would have. She had heard Ruthie moving, had taken the noises for ordinary house sounds, because she was used to imagining her own burden was the largest object in any room.

June stood and wiped her dusty hands on her skirt. "If Lucille knows something, she'll tell you."

"She'll tell me what the county requires her to tell first."

June's mouth pinched at that. She had already learned the difference between an adult answer and a useful one. "Then ask the part after."

Outside, gravel cracked under tires. Ruthie went to the window. "She's here."

Lucille had borrowed the county Ford again, the dull green one with one fender caved in and dry mud pasted up the doors from cemetery roads. She sat straight behind the wheel in a linen dress the color of dishwater and a straw hat pinned close to her carefully fixed hair. Even from the kitchen, Della could see the strain in the set of her shoulders. Lucille usually carried a kind of official neatness around her like a second collar. This morning the collar had slipped. Her sensible shoes were dusty to the ankle, and she was tapping two fingers against the steering wheel as if counting inward.

Della tied on her hat. She had the copied pages in the inner pocket of her apron, folded into oilcloth. Those were not enough and she knew it, but she put her hand there once before she left, the way another woman might touch a pistol.

Ruthie followed her to the porch. The air outside smelled of hot weeds and the faint iron breath that rose from river mud when the mornings turned thick. Cicadas had already started.

"If he filed something," Ruthie said quietly, so June would not hear from the doorway, "don't spend the ride telling yourself he did it out of malice. He did it out of a note at the bank and feed bills in his pocket."

Della fitted the hatpin through the crown with care because care was all she could offer her hands just then. "Those two things lie down together easy enough."

"They do." Ruthie's face did not soften. "That doesn't make them the same."

There was no answer to give that would not start a larger quarrel than the road allowed. Della stepped off the porch. June called after her, "Ask the part after," and then Lucille was opening the passenger door from inside with a jerk that spoke more plainly than greeting.

The Ford smelled of sunstruck vinyl, damp wool, and the courthouse files Lucille carried everywhere, a paper smell already warming into paste. Lucille waited until Della had settled the biscuit bundle in her lap and pulled the door shut before she put the car in gear.

"I hoped you might send word you found it," she said as they rolled down from Cedar Bench.

"I hoped I might too."

Lucille nodded once. She did not ask where Della had hidden the ledger or who might have taken it. That omission felt professional and kind at once, which was about as much kindness as a woman in her office could afford to show.

The road to Larkin ran first between fields already rough with mid-summer neglect, then along the river where the condemned houses sat with windows black and empty, porches gone to weeds, chickens sold or eaten. A mattress leaned against a sycamore outside the Pruitt place, striped ticking gray with dew. Closer to town they met trucks from the dam, broad-nosed and mud-scarred, dragging their own weather behind them. Each passing engine shook the Ford and rattled the loose glass in the window. Lucille drove with both hands fixed at ten and two, jaw set, as if the road itself might pull rank on her if she slackened.

Della ate one biscuit because Ruthie had ordered it and because the hunger under her breastbone had begun to turn mean. The biscuit was dry enough to draw the spit out of her mouth. She had to swallow twice. Lucille did not look over.

"What business is urgent enough to drag me to Larkin without saying why?" Della asked after a mile of the fan belt whining between them.

Lucille kept her eyes on the road. "I thought it better not to put it on paper."

"So it's paper."

"It is." A breath. "I need to tell you in the office where I can show you the filing. If I tell you now, you'll spend the rest of the road asking me questions I can answer better with the record in front of us."

That was Lucille to the bone, even strained: sequence first, feeling after, if feeling must be handled at all. Della turned her head to the window. Dust lifted from the shoulders of the road and laid a fine coat over the blackberry leaves. Somewhere farther off, hidden by the ridges, blasting sounded again, one hard thud followed by a long unsettled shiver of earth. The county, the federal men, the dam, the bank, the church, all of it seemed to live by clocks now. Only the valley people were asked to pretend time remained a thing you could bargain with.

Larkin announced itself by brick fronts and the courthouse cupola rising over the feed store signboards. Saturdays the town smelled of tobacco spit, horse sweat, and market peaches. On a weekday morning it smelled mostly of paper, oil, and men waiting to have their need translated into forms. Lucille pulled up beside the courthouse instead of the usual schoolhouse office annex, which told Della enough to sour her stomach more thoroughly than the biscuit had steadied it.

Inside, the courthouse air was only shade, not cool. Ceiling fans chopped the heat into slow moving slices. People sat on benches under framed notices about probate, tax sale, and road petitions. A farmer in overalls held his hat crushed in both hands. A woman with a baby asleep against her shoulder stared at the floor as if she might find an answer in the worn boards if she fixed her eyes hard enough. Somebody had mopped early; damp pine cleaner floated above the deeper courthouse smells of old varnish, ink, sweat, and the gluey sourness of carbon paper.

Lucille did not pause in the hall. She took Della past the probate room and into the small records office she used when the schoolhouse desks filled up or matters grew too formal for chalkboards and tag strings. The room held two steel cabinets, a tall counter, a typewriter with its cover thrown back, and three stacks of files tied in red tape. On the wall a county calendar showed June though the month had already turned. Someone had not bothered to pull the page.

Lucille shut the door behind them. That sound, light as it was, made the room smaller by half.

"I received a filing yesterday afternoon," she said. "It concerns the fever-plot remains."

Della stood without sitting. "Whose filing?"

Lucille put a folder on the counter and opened it with those precise fingers of hers that never seemed to snag even on cheap paper. "Rafe Bledsoe's."

The name did not strike like a blow. It moved slower than that, the way cold water moved through cloth, taking hold of more of her by degrees. Della stepped closer because there was nothing else to do. Lucille turned the top sheet so it faced her.

The paper had the county heading at the top and a notary seal pressed hard enough to leave a welt in the next page. Beneath the printed lines Rafe's hand sprawled in dark pencil copied over with ink, letters too heavily made, each stroke digging as if he thought force could keep them from being questioned. Raphael Bledsoe. He only wrote the full name when school, church, or money stood over him. The capital R leaned back the same way it had when he was a boy making copy rows by lamplight, angry at the pencil for not obeying. Della saw, against her will, his hand at ten years old with the knuckles still round on it, tongue caught in the corner of his mouth while she held the paper steady so it would not slide. The memory made the affidavit uglier, not softer.

She read enough. That the exposed adult remains in the Old Sion fever plot were, to the best of his knowledge and belief, Eli Bledsoe, white male, approximately sixty-five years of age, missing from Hale County these many years. That the affiant was eldest surviving son and interested party. That county relocation and condemnation records should be amended accordingly. The words were not wholly his. They had Lucille's office in them, or some lawyer's old form, or the courthouse's way of making one man's desperation sound respectable. But the pressure in the signature was his. The hurry was his. The need crouched under every line was his.

Della laid two fingertips on the edge of the page because if she took the whole sheet in her hand she might tear it.

"He swore this yesterday?"

"At four-fifteen." Lucille's voice went clerkly, almost too level. "Before the deputy notary. He had cash for the fee and enough particulars to require filing."

"You filed a lie."

"I received a sworn statement I had no authority to refuse." The words came cleanly, but color had risen above Lucille's collar. "There is a difference."

"There may be in your office."

"There has to be in my office." Lucille's fingers flattened on the counter. "If I start taking only the papers I personally approve, then Vernon Wolfe gets to ask why his get waved through and Noreen's don't. Then the whole thing becomes favor instead of record and we're finished."

Della almost said It already is favor and record together, always has been, but stopped because anger did not make the page vanish. Lucille pulled another sheet from under the affidavit and set it beside the first.

"I sent for you because this part started the same minute he signed."

It was a notice, brief and worse for being brief. Contest period: ten days from filing. Absent sworn objection, identification to stand for relocation purposes and for transmission to condemnation records. Hearing to be set upon timely dispute.

The room gave a little sideways tilt, not enough to make her reach out, only enough that she knew to distrust her legs. She lowered herself into the straight chair by the wall without asking leave. The wood seat had been polished by generations of anxious bodies. It was still damp in one spot from the mop water somebody had slopped under it.

"Read it plain," she said.

Lucille drew a breath, grateful perhaps for something she could explain in order. "Because the grave is unmarked and already opened, the county can't wait indefinitely for every family to settle itself. A claimant can file an affidavit of identification. If no one with material knowledge contests within ten days, I have to record the identification as unopposed for the relocation register. After that it goes forward with the condemnation packet."

"For compensation."

"For compensation, for death proof, for whatever the federal office is willing to treat as settled enough to move money." Lucille's mouth tightened at that. "If somebody contests, we set a hearing before probate and the identification stays provisional until testimony."

Della read the notice again though the words had not changed. Ten days. Not even enough time for a woman to know whether the hens would keep laying through a spell of heat, much less enough to unpick forty years of burial, blood, and harm. Rafe had not only lied. He had lied first, which mattered more than the lie itself once a clerk's stamp came down.

"What counts as material knowledge?" she asked.

"A person who can swear to facts bearing on the body's identity." Lucille did not blink. "That is you."

"Or Silas."

"Possibly, if he will swear and if he can manage the trip."

Della thought of Silas's quarry cough, the way it hollowed him from the inside out. Getting him to Larkin inside ten days would be like hauling a gatepost through mud. Possible, maybe. Not light.

Lucille went on, and the briskness in her tone had a crack in it now, a private impatience with the law she served. "If you say nothing, the county takes your nothing as absence of dispute. Not approval in any moral sense, but approval is not a category the forms ask after."

Della looked from the notice to the affidavit and then away from both. On the windowsill sat a dead fly on its back, legs lifted, a small black thing utterly spent and still somehow indecent in how final it looked. She had laid out bodies with more dignity than this courthouse would grant a name.

"I told him no," she said, though that was not the part Lucille needed. "At the grave I told him no."

"I know you did."

"And now because he wrote it down, it gets a clock."

Lucille's reply was quiet enough that Della had to lean to catch it. "The county loves a quiet signature. It doesn't ask what had to be broken before the hand got steady enough to make it."

That was the first wholly unofficed thing she had said, and it made Della lift her eyes. Lucille looked tired suddenly, younger and older together, like a woman who had spent the week translating grief into headings and was losing patience with grammar.

Della put her hand inside her apron and brought out the oilcloth bundle of copied pages. The cloth was warm from her belly. She set it on her lap but did not unfold it.

"I've got extracts," she said. "Not enough of the old book. Not this."

Lucille looked at the bundle and understood. "You didn't copy the fever-plot entry."

"There wasn't any grave tag to copy against. And I wasn't ready."

"No." Lucille's eyes returned to the affidavit. "He was."

Della let out a breath through her nose. Anger came to her seldom and never fast. It built the way a stove eye heated, dark first, then red at the core where nobody saw until the skillet hissed. She had spent years refusing the county whole truths because whole truths in county hands had a way of coming back as eviction, bastard labels, jail, the preacher at the door, husbands suddenly entitled to children they had never kept warm. She had written babies under safer men, left rings to count that had never touched a courthouse, marked fathers dead or gone when the living version would have ruined the mother and not fed the child. Not for profit. Not to play God, as the gossips said when they wanted the world smaller and cleaner than it was. She had done it because the law came late and hungry and always asked the weakest body in the room to pay in full.

Now her own son had taken a fistful of his need into that same courthouse and used its appetite against her.

She said it before she meant to. "God damn him."

Lucille did not flinch. Perhaps she had expected worse. "He isn't the first poor man to discover paperwork after the wealthy have used it all their lives."

"No. He's just the first one born from my own bad mercy."

The words hung between them, heavier for being only partly true. Lucille was too careful to take the bait in them. She slid the affidavit back into the folder and tapped the notice with one fingernail.

"What matters first is whether you'll contest."

"What does contest require today?"

"A sworn objection on the record that you dispute the identification and have direct knowledge bearing against it. You don't have to name the body this minute if you aren't prepared to do so. But once you contest, the hearing will require more than refusal."

That was fair and also threat. Fairness and threat lived on neighboring shelves in a courthouse.

Della looked at the page until the letters blurred a little from the sweat gathering behind her glasses. Outside the office a typewriter started up with a hard, fast clacking and stopped three lines later. A baby cried once in the hall and was hushed by a mother's palm. The courthouse held everybody's need close to the wall and made it wait its turn.

"If I don't contest," she said, "when do you mark it final?"

"At the end of the tenth day. I date the register and transmit it." Lucille hesitated, then added, "And after that, if you come forward, you're not preventing a wrong. You're trying to unwind one the county has already entered."

That did it. Not the legal distinction itself. The picture of it. Ink drying. A line on a register. Some federal clerk never hearing the name Gabriel Wolfe spoken aloud, only Eli Bledsoe set down on a sheet and moved along because no woman had stood up in time to say no. Della had lived with private sins and public omissions. She knew the weight of a thing left undone until it became structure. A crib not mended became a child on the floor. A bill not opened became a wagon repossessed. A name not contested became the dead man's name forever in the hands of strangers.

She unfolded the oilcloth anyway, though she already knew what was inside. Three copied pages in her own hand, close written and careful, entries tied to graves already opened or hearings already started. Sadie. The Kincaid child. One widow's old surname. None of it helped here. The blank where Gabriel should have been gaped wider than if there had been no pages at all.

Lucille watched her refold the bundle. "If you want time before answering, I can give you until the end of today's office hours. I can't give you more than that without recording that no objection was made when notice was served, and I won't do that to spare your feelings."

That, again, was both harsh and the cleanest mercy available.

Della rose from the chair. Her knees complained with a grit of pain from the cellar crouching and the drive. "Take me where the objection is signed."

Lucille did not let relief show. She only gathered the folder and opened the office door. "It's through here."

The probate room had a higher ceiling and a worse fan. Two windows stood open but admitted mostly street dust and the sound of a truck shifting gears downhill. At the long front table a deputy clerk sat with a stack of forms and a jar of sharpened pencils. She was a fleshy woman in a lavender blouse darkened under the arms, with a hatpin in her lapel shaped like a trout. One shoe had been slipped half off under the table so she could rub the side of her foot against the chair rung where a corn or blister pained her. She looked up without interest sharpened into curiosity, which Della appreciated at once.

"Mrs. Bledsoe," Lucille said, official again, "this is Mrs. Penley. She'll witness the contest filing."

Mrs. Penley pushed the shoe back on with a practiced flex. "Morning." Her voice carried the flat patience of a woman who spent her days listening to people tell the same sorrow in different words. "Miss Holt says you've got grounds to dispute an identification."

"I do."

"Then we'll do it while the judge is still upstairs and the seal's at hand." Mrs. Penley pulled a fresh form from the stack and squared it with the table edge three times before she was satisfied. "Name, residence, relation if any, and the basis of dispute. Put it plain. The law doesn't get wiser when you dress it up."

That made Lucille's mouth twitch the smallest bit. Mrs. Penley dipped her pen, shook off the excess ink with a flick at the blotter, and began.

Della gave her name. Cedar Bench, Brier Valley. Occupation paused on the page until Mrs. Penley prompted, and Della heard herself say, "Midwife. Cemetery relocation worker for the county." The two roles sat queer beside each other, one old and private, one public and temporary.

"And relation to the remains?" Mrs. Penley asked.

"There is no blood relation to the remains as presently identified," Della said.

"That'll do for now." Scratch of nib. "Basis of dispute?"

Della looked at Lucille once. Lucille returned the look but offered no words. This part had to be Della's, because the hearing later would ask whether the objection had begun in her own mouth or somebody else's.

"I have direct knowledge that the remains exposed in the fever plot are not Eli Bledsoe," she said. "I was present in circumstances bearing on the burial of the person in question, and the filed identification is false."

Mrs. Penley wrote without lifting her head. "You willing to swear to that before God and Hale County?"

"Yes."

"Good. Most folks come in wanting county benefits with less stomach than that."

The pen went on. Della watched the words form in another woman's hand and felt the old disgust rising again, not at Mrs. Penley, who was only doing the day's work with her blistered foot and fish pin and calm, but at the whole business of needing the county to believe a thing before a dead man could keep his own name. Gabriel had lain quiet for forty years in red clay and root shadow, and now three women in a hot room were turning his bones into a docket.

Mrs. Penley sanded the page lightly, blew across it, then turned the blotter around. "Read before you sign."

Della read. The language had grown stiffer on the page than it had sounded in the room. That was another courthouse trick: take living speech, strip its grease and weather off, and hand it back bleached. Still, the meaning held. False. Direct knowledge. Contest.

She took the pen. Her left thumb balked around the barrel, swollen from jars and age. Ink spread a little at the first downstroke before she corrected her grip. Della Bledsoe came out smaller than usual but legible. If her signature shook, let it. Truth did not stop being truth because the hand was old.

Mrs. Penley blotted, stamped, pressed the seal. The metal crunch of it on paper sounded to Della like a trap springing. Not on her. On the lie. On the quiet path Rafe had tried to cut through the middle of the county.

"There," Mrs. Penley said. "Hearing notice will issue once Miss Holt sets the slot. Likely next week given the relocation matter. Folks do like to be buried by schedule now." The last line carried just enough bitterness to prove she belonged to the same country as the rest of them.

Lucille gathered the papers before the ink had fully dried. "Thank you."

Mrs. Penley gave the side of her foot one absent rub against the chair rung again. "I thank nobody till after noon. That's when the real fools arrive."

In the hall, Lucille exhaled as though she had been holding her ribs still for the past hour. People were thicker now. A pair of brothers from the north ridge argued in low fierce voices over some deed description. The baby Della had heard earlier was awake and furious, face red as a boiled crawfish while its mother bounced and shushed without effect. The courthouse benches had filled with hats, envelopes, sorrow.

"You've stopped the uncontested filing," Lucille said. "That part's done."

"For today."

"For today is most of how government works."

They moved to a window alcove where a little air slid in past the sash. Below them on the square a truck driver leaned against his cab and ate from wax paper, one boot crossed over the other as if he had no docket with his name on it at all. Della wished briefly for that kind of ignorance. Not innocence. Just not knowing exactly how a form could bite.

Lucille opened the folder once more and tucked Della's contest behind the affidavit. "I need to ask the ugly question."

"Go on."

"When this is called, are you prepared to fight your son publicly?"

The careful word was fight. Not contradict. Not embarrass. Fight. Lucille had finally set procedure down and named the shape under it.

Della put both hands on the windowsill. Dust gritted under her palms. The wood had been painted so many times it felt almost padded, softened not by comfort but by layers of official endurance. On the square somebody laughed. A horse stamped once. The town kept on with its errands while inside the courthouse the dead were being argued into or out of names.

"He started the fight before he knew all of it," Della said.

"That wasn't my question."

"No. I know." She watched a woman hurry across the street with a hatbox under one arm and a child tugging at the other hand. Ordinary life could be almost obscene when the mind was fixed elsewhere. "He's trying to save his house."

Lucille did not answer. Agreement would have been sentimental. Disagreement would have been foolish.

"He thinks if he gets Eli dead on paper the rest comes loose," Della went on. "Condemnation money, maybe the bank eased, maybe a month without somebody asking what he means to do about feed. He isn't wrong about what he needs. He is wrong about the bones."

Lucille's reply came after a moment. "Need makes very competent liars. The county sees them daily."

Della almost smiled at the dryness of that and did not. Her mind had moved past anger and into the colder place where decisions got made. That place often felt like calm from the outside. People called her steady when she was in it. They did not understand steadiness and brutality were kin.

"If I leave that affidavit sitting there," she said, "I'm not standing out of his way. I'm carrying it for him."

Lucille turned her head. "Yes."

"Then silence is lying."

"Yes."

Della looked down at the square until the shapes below settled and the sentence settled with them. There it was. Not a sermon, not absolution, not some late clean principle. Merely the mechanical fact of the thing. In the old years she had told herself a silence could be shelter if she built it around the right woman or child. She had been right often enough to keep believing it. But paperwork changed silence. A void on a porch, in a church book, in a bedroom where nobody asked direct questions, that could still be shelter. A void inside a ten-day contest period was not shelter. It was consent with cleaner handwriting.

Lucille closed the folder. "I'll send the hearing notice to Cedar Bench and to Rafe both. If you find anything before then, written or material, bring it. Your memory counts. It would count more if the county had something it could pretend not to distrust."

That stung because it was true. Della touched the oilcloth bundle through her apron and felt how thin it was.

"I had the book in my house one night too long," she said.

Lucille leaned her shoulder against the wall, exhaustion showing now that the immediate work was done. "You had the book hidden for years because women like you knew exactly what happened when the wrong office got curious. I'm not about to stand here and say the state's hands were clean till your son dirtied them."

No comfort in the words, and none needed. Only accuracy. That, from Lucille, came near tenderness.

Della said, "He is using the county against the very kind of secret the county taught me to keep."

Lucille gave a short, humorless breath that might have been agreement or might have been all the profanity her upbringing allowed in public. "That is as close to the whole book as anybody has said in this building today."

They went down the courthouse steps together. The stone had been worn shallow in the middle by generations of shoes. Della placed her feet carefully. Her knees had stiffened. The sun outside hit like a hot skillet lid lifted too fast. For a second the square wavered white at the edges.

Lucille paused at the car before unlocking it. "Do you want me to take you home direct?"

"No. Stop by the schoolhouse first. I want a copy of what I signed."

Lucille nodded. "You'll have it."

"And a copy of his."

A flicker crossed Lucille's face. "That I can do."

They rode back quieter. The road bucked under the tires where the county had patched spring washouts with fresh gravel. Dust came in through the vent and laid itself on Della's forearms. She did not wipe it off. The contest copy in her lap crackled every time the car hit a rut. She read her own statement three times and each time the same words stood there: not Eli Bledsoe. False. Direct knowledge.

At the edge of the valley, where the road gave a view toward Old Sion's ridge and the river shine below it, Lucille slowed for a herd of cattle being pushed across by a boy with a switch. The cemetery itself was out of sight from this angle, but Della knew where the fever plot lay by muscle more than sight, the lower pocket where the ground held damp longest and cedar roots ran like knuckled fingers through the clay. She pictured the opened edge of the grave, the raw dirt, the waiting coffin wood. Paper in Larkin, clay in Brier Valley. Ten days between them. That was the whole bridge the county had built across the years.

June had told her to ask the part after. The part after was not whether the county would hear her. It would, now that she had signed. The part after was what she would bring when hearing day came besides an old woman's knowledge and an accomplice's bad lungs. The ledger was gone. The copied pages would not save this. Somewhere there had to remain another piece of Gabriel that had not passed through her hands and vanished there.

The thought came and went, not yet a plan, more like the feel of a loose tooth the tongue keeps finding. Lucille said nothing. She knew better than to crowd the first minutes after a filing. A person often needed to sit beside what she had just done and hear how it sounded inside her own bones.

At the schoolhouse office Lucille made the copies while Della waited among tag strings, paste pots, and maps pinned over arithmetic boards. Chalk dust lay in the corners under tables. The coffee on the hot plate had gone to tar. Through the open window came the faint concussion of blasting again, the sound crossing miles of summer air as if the earth itself were swallowing furniture.

Lucille handed over the papers. Rafe's affidavit on top, Della's contest beneath. Two versions of the same grave facing each other through carbon ghosts.

"He signed Raphael," Della said, more to the page than to Lucille.

"He did."

"He hasn't done that since school unless he was asking money from somebody."

Lucille put a clip on another file. "Then he knew what kind of room he was in."

Della folded the copies once, exact and not gentle, and slid them into the oilcloth with the useless extracts. She could not decide which page was harder to bear: the one where her son lied or the one where she finally agreed to speak against him. Both had the county seal. Both would outlast the day.

When Lucille drove her back up Cedar Bench, Ruthie and June were in the yard turning sheets on the line. The laundry flashed white in the heat like surrender flags nobody meant. June saw the papers before she saw Della's face. Ruthie saw the face first.

"What happened?" Ruthie asked from the yard.

Della stood a moment with the door half open, one hand on the Ford's roof. The house behind them held its same patched chairs, same thin sugar, same missing book. Nothing looked changed. That was the trick of paperwork. The world stayed put while the trap was being built.

"He's filed for Eli," she said.

Ruthie's hands stilled on the wet sheet. Water darkened the front of her apron where the cloth touched. June came one step closer, eyes sharpened to points.

"And?" June asked.

Della looked at the papers in her own hand, then at her granddaughter, then at the yard where their laundry and their poverty were hanging out for every road eye to count.

"And I signed against him," she said.

The words landed and held. No crying. No questions yet. Just the weight of what had now been carried out of the house and into the record.

She stepped down from the car. Her legs felt older than they had that morning. But the oldness had direction now. That was something. Not comfort. Not victory. A direction.

"Ask me the rest inside," she said. "This won't stand out in the yard."
