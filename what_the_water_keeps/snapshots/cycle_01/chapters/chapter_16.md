# Chapter 16

By dawn the rain had pared Old Sion down to essentials: mud, cedar root, stone, box wood, men too tired to keep one thing separate from another. The finished rows higher up the hill stood raw and emptied, holes staked for later fill, while the last row on the lower lean still waited under sagging oilcloth and the black drip from the cedars. The river could not yet be seen through the trees, but it made itself known in the low swollen drag of water against water. Every few minutes something down at Marlowe answered with a concussion that came through the ground before it came through the air.

Della came up the half-mile path with her skirt hem already dark to the knee and her left thumb stiff from the cold wet. Yesterday she had sat in the county room and held the line where the law wanted more from her than it had ever earned. Today the state wanted muscle instead of testimony, as if those were cheaper to come by.

Lucille Holt stood beneath a length of tar paper tied between two cedar trunks, trying to keep the tag board dry with one shoulder while she wrote on it. She had wrapped her office shoes in newspaper and left them in her satchel; on her feet were a pair of men's work boots borrowed from somebody bigger, the heels listing out so that she walked as if the mud had already twisted her ankles. Her hair had come loose in one place above the ear, and she kept taking the escaped pin out with her teeth and shoving it back in.

"I called again before light," she said when Della reached her. Her voice kept its office order even with rain tapping the tar paper six inches from her head. "They won't hold the lower gate past six. They won't send another truck after that. The closure order stands."

"For mud."

"For schedule." Lucille made a dry little mark beside the final six numbers on the board. "Schedule does not care what the hill looks like."

It was a foolish thing for anger to fasten on, but Della hated the word order more than she hated gate. Order was what people called a thing once they had made it somebody else's burden.

Below them, beside the stacked stones and the grease-black truck winch, Silas Wynn was uncoiling chain with the patience of a man laying out Sunday clothes. He had a red handkerchief folded into a square in his shirt pocket, so many years washed and ironed that the red had faded to a brick pink, and every so often he took out a peppermint from a Prince Albert tin, bit it in half with his side teeth, and put the rest back as if sugar, like breath, ought not to be wasted. He was coughing before the morning had even opened itself fully. It was not the old clearing cough Della knew from him, quarry dust dragged up and spat aside. This one caught lower and stayed.

"You ought to sit a little before you start," she called.

Silas did not look up from the chain. "And improve what, exactly?"

"Your sense."

He gave the chain one last shake and let it fall in a bright wet loop. "Too late in life for that."

Ruthie came through the gate carrying a lard bucket full of wrapped biscuits, two Mason jars of coffee stoppered with rags, and a bundle of feed-sack strips for tying loose lids. She had yanked a flour-sack apron over her dress and pinned the cuffs of her coat back with clothespins so they would not drag in the muck; one cuff had been mended in blue thread because blue was what she had left, and Della had watched her make such repairs all summer with the same hard economy other women reserved for prayer. Ruthie set everything under the tar paper, scanned the hill once, and started dividing the morning without asking permission from any man there.

"Coffee first if you want it, because after this it'll taste like grave water," she said. "Miss Holt, you've got ink on your cheek. Mr. Wynn, don't you go climbing down till somebody's braced that plank. Mama, your gloves are split at the thumb again."

Della pulled at the torn seam with her teeth and shoved her hand back in. "I'll live."

"You usually do."

There was no sign of Rafe. Della told herself she had not expected one. He had walked out of her kitchen after she put Gabriel's blood in his mouth, and though he had come to the hearing and stood through the county's writing-down of it all, he had not looked at her after. She had watched the side of his face instead: the old scar at the chin gone white, the jaw working as if it meant to grind through something harder than tooth. He had burned the affidavit because there had been nothing else left for him to burn. That did not mean he had forgiven the hand that made it useless.

She was bending to lift the first plank with Ruthie when boots sucked at the mud below and a voice said, "If you put that end down there, the truck's gonna sink to the axle before noon."

Rafe stood just inside the lower gap in the fence, rain dripping from the brim of his hat. He wore his dam slicker over a work shirt gone dark at the shoulders, and the cuffs smelled faintly of river oil and blasting powder even through the wet. Mud had skinned one side of his trousers to the thigh. He looked as if he had come straight from one kind of destruction to help with another.

Ruthie did not turn her head. "Then don't stand there preaching at it. Take the other end."

He took it. Della took the weight with him because there was no decent reason not to, and the shock of the shared pull ran up her arm before she could prepare herself for it. He did not greet her. He did not have to. The fact of his hands on the plank was greeting enough and no kindness at all.

"Left side wants stone under it," he said to the mud, not to her. "You want the winch lined straight or it'll drag the boxes crooked."

"Then line it straight," Della said.

He gave one short nod. That was all.

They worked the first hour with the mean concentration of people who knew time had already cheated them before they laid a hand to anything. The last row held six coffins on the lower pitch of the hill: four adult boxes laid years apart and two child boxes so small they looked insulting to the labor that would still be required of them. Rain had softened the slope until every shovelful tried to slide back where it had come from. The cedar roots held the bank together in one place and tore it open in another. Boards had to be set, reset, braced with stone. Every few minutes the truck engine idled up, coughed, and fell again while Rafe tested the ground.

Della went down first with Silas to open the nearest grave. The clay gave under her boots like wet soap. Old wood had a way of declaring itself before the shovel found it: the smell changed, sweet rot under dirt, damp cloth and iron and that flat old-cold note which was not exactly death and not exactly earth either, but a third thing made of the two. She knelt, felt with her gloved hands, and found the side slat.

"Not the handles," she said when one of the county boys leaned in. "They're gone before your fingers touch them. Side wood. Slow."

The boy, no more than twenty and still vain enough to keep his hair greased under rain, flushed and obeyed. Della did not know his name. He would be the sort to carry this smell into his supper and pretend it had not followed him home.

Silas coughed into his handkerchief, turned his body away to spare the box, and spat red-streaked phlegm into the weeds. When he straightened, his face had gone briefly gray beneath the stone dust. Della saw it and saw him know she had seen it.

"You sit," she said low.

"After this one."

"You said that before we touched it."

He put the handkerchief back in his pocket with deliberate care. "I say a lot of true things more'n once."

The coffin came up mostly whole, which felt like luck until the bottom bowed in the middle and the whole shape sagged against the straps. Della heard Ruthie suck air through her teeth from above.

"Feed-sack cloth," Della said. "Now."

Ruthie was already tearing strips with her hands. She slid down the plank on her heels, apron dark with mud, and passed the cloth under the bowed middle while Della and Silas steadied the sides. Inside, through the split board, Della caught a glimpse of what had once been a man's shirt cuff and the black slither of collapsed coat lining. There was no face. Faces went first or held longest; there was never much law to it.

"Lift on three," Rafe called from the winch. His voice carried over the engine with the sharp edge she remembered from haying weather and debt. "If that bottom gives, it gives in the sling, not on the ground. One. Two. Now."

The box rose. Mud dripped from it in brown ropes. For one bad second the strap slid toward the split, then caught. Della kept one palm on the side until the wood carried beyond her reach and onto the waiting truck bed. She did not let herself watch it settle. There were five more.

By the time the second adult coffin came free, everybody on the hill had lost the illusion that breakfast was still part of the day. Rain soaked through wool and cotton alike. Clay packed itself so thick to boots that each step felt borrowed from the next one. Lucille, whose work was supposed to be tags and lists and signatures later, had mud to the calves and was holding an umbrella over the open registry board with one hand while using the other to tie oilcloth around the ticket strings so the names would not wash off before they reached Limestone Ridge.

"Tag twenty-seven C is Brewer, infant male, with the broken lambstone," she said, more to keep the order of things from fraying than because anyone had asked. "Tag twenty-seven D is Mrs. Penn. Do not switch those just because the boxes are close in size."

Ruthie, crouched beside the child grave with her hair escaping in damp ropes at the neck, said, "If anybody here mistakes a baby box for a grown woman, they need prayer more than paper."

Lucille opened her mouth as if to defend the process, then shut it and gave one brief sound that might have been a laugh if laughter had not been too expensive for the morning. "I only mean the records won't forgive us."

"Records don't forgive anybody," Ruthie said.

Della could feel the hill taking strength out of Silas by the handful. He had gone quieter even than usual, which in him meant danger, and the pauses between his movements had lengthened. He still touched each stone before it was moved, not ceremonially, just with the quick sure hand of a man who had cut too many of them to trust another person to know where the face would flake and where it would hold. When the lambstone over the child grave rocked in the muck, he crouched to pad the carved face with burlap before the boys laid it down.

"Don't scratch the nose off," he said. "That lamb's all foolishness now, but the mama paid for the foolishness same as the rest."

It was the nearest he had come all morning to sentiment, and even that he delivered like an instruction about tools. Della felt the brief pinch of wanting to tell him she had heard it, but there was no use in gratitude while the day was still making demands.

The child box came up light enough to insult the six hands on it. Della hated that part most, the false ease of little dead weight. Men relaxed when they should not, assuming small meant simple. This box had no strength left in the corners at all. The lid came away in Ruthie's hands with a damp little sigh, and inside the white that remained was not bone exactly at first glance but the memory of where bone had laid under cloth gone to mulch.

The county boy with the greased hair made a sound in his throat and stepped back.

"Don't do that," Della snapped. "If you're sick, be sick over there. Don't leave me half a hand."

He swallowed hard and went pale to the lips, but he took the end of the cloth when she thrust it at him. Together she, Ruthie, and the boy lifted what was left into a clean box Lucille had fetched from the truck. Della's fingers passed over something no bigger than a walnut that gave with a soft crack through the rag. A bit of skull. She set it down as carefully as if she were putting a cup back on a shelf.

The rain came harder toward noon, not in sheets but in a steady thickening that blurred the trees down toward the river and turned the truck ruts into trenches with shiny skins on them. Rafe had the first loaded truck backed and ready to go when the right rear wheel sank nearly to the hub in a patch that had looked solid an hour before. The engine climbed and whined. Mud spat up in great clots and laid itself back down.

"Shut it off," Della called. She did not want the wheel chewing them deeper.

Rafe killed the engine, climbed down, and kicked once at the rut as if anger might still be good for something. "We need rock under it. Big enough not to vanish."

"You're standing on a graveyard full of stone," Lucille said, and then seemed irritated with herself for the dryness that slipped out.

"Not carved stone," Della said.

"I know that."

Ruthie handed Rafe a biscuit gone wet around the edges. "Eat that while you're cussing and then go strip the spill pile by the fence. You faint and I will kill you myself."

"Sweet of you."

"You know I mean it."

He took the biscuit. Mud streaked his knuckles. Della saw him hesitate a fraction before he bit into it, as if the simple fact of accepting food from his wife in front of her turned the whole hillside into a room too small for dignity. He chewed anyhow.

Silas had gone to the last adult grave, the one nearest the cedar where the bank dipped worst. By the time Della reached him, he had already found the lid with his shovel and was down on one knee, one hand pressed to his side under the ribs.

"You stop taking starts without me," she said.

"Then walk faster."

"I mean it, Silas."

He looked up. Rain stood in his eyebrows. The cough had roughened the whites of his eyes to pink. "So do I. That gate shuts whether I lay down or not. You want them left open for the fish and the federal men, you go on home. I ain't leaving this row with daylight on it."

There it was, the flat true core of him. It was not tombstone philosophy or bravery. It was just craft and meanness and the old laborer's disgust at half-done work. Della wanted to shake him for how much it sounded like honor when she knew it was mostly stubbornness and habit, but she also knew she would have trusted no other man on the hill with this last coffin.

Rafe came down the plank behind her carrying a pry bar and two fieldstones under one arm. "Truck'll move once I crib the wheel. What's this one caught on?"

Silas tapped the shovel blade against the cedar root. "Root's gone through under the shoulder end."

"Then cut it."

"And jolt the box to kindling."

Rafe squatted in the mud, hat brim dripping off his nose. For a moment he and Silas studied the grave together like men over a bad engine, the dead woman between them merely the fact that made this problem shaped the way it was. Della stood above them with rain running down her collar and thought that blood might lie, names might lie, law might lie, but the angle of a body in labor never did. You learned kinship there against your will.

"We undercut here," Rafe said at last, touching a place with two fingers. "Take the root from beneath and leave the upper bite holding till the strap's got her."

Silas grunted once. "Do it."

They did. Della lay flat in the mud to get her arm under the softened side while Rafe reached with the bar and Silas cut the root in careful little chops that shook his shoulders with each swing. Rain ran into Della's sleeve and pooled in her armpit. Wood fibers gave under her glove. Something with the texture of wet paper pressed against the back of her hand. Above her, Rafe said, "Easy. Easy now," not to her and not to the dead woman, just to the whole hateful arrangement of wood and clay and time.

The root let go. The box lurched half an inch. Silas coughed so hard he had to brace his forehead against the shovel handle.

"Enough," Della said.

He spat red into the grave and wiped his mouth. "You hearing an echo?"

Rafe looked at the blood, then away from it. "Old man."

"Don't you 'old man' me till you've set half the stones I've set."

"At the rate you're going I might get my chance today."

It was not a joke, not really, but Silas showed a corner of his teeth anyway. "Then quit talking and lift."

The truck came free with a sucking groan once Rafe and the boys got rock under the wheel and laid plank ahead of it. The driver, a narrow-shouldered county hand who had been mostly ornamental all morning, took the first load up toward Limestone Ridge with both hands white on the wheel and Lucille beside him holding the tag board across her knees like a hymn book she did not trust the Lord with. She turned once before the truck reached the bend and raised a hand to them, not in triumph. More as if to say she had their names with her and would get them where they were going.

That left the hill suddenly quieter and worse. With the first load gone, there was no longer any useful pretending that plenty of day remained. Noon had already passed into the thick gray afternoon that comes early in storm weather. The men chewed wet biscuits standing up. Ruthie walked from person to person with the coffee jar and did not let anybody take more than a swallow because there were still hours to spend. Della flexed her bad thumb against the heat of the glass when Ruthie pressed it into her hand.

"You've torn the seam clean through," Ruthie said, looking at the glove.

"Then I've."

"I brought the other pair. They were hanging by the stove."

Della looked at her. "You planned for me to be foolish."

Ruthie shrugged one shoulder. "I planned for you to be yourself."

There was affection in it somewhere, but not softness. They were past softness. Della changed gloves under the tar paper while Ruthie used the toe of her boot to shove more stones under the coffee bucket so it would not tip. That, Della thought, was the real shape of family most days. It was not confessions or blood. It was knowing which hand another woman would forget to protect and bringing the second pair before she asked.

The second truck took the two child boxes, two markers, and one adult coffin without incident, if any such hauling could be called that. The trouble waited for the last adult box, the deepest set and the most ruined. When the straps tightened around it, the whole left side gave way and a gush of black water and old cloth poured into the sling. Della smelled hair, sour and long sealed. The county boy swore and nearly lost his footing. Rafe caught the back of his coat and hauled him upright with a jerk that would leave torn seams.

"Stand up if you're gonna help," he said. "Fall somewhere else if you ain't."

Della was already in the grave. There was no room to hesitate. She knelt in the runoff with water soaking through both knees and began gathering what the broken side had surrendered: lengths of rib, a jaw tipped of its teeth years before, the dark braid of a woman's hair still pinned at one end with rusted metal. Hair lasted strange. Buttons lasted. Cheap pine and expensive promises both went to mush. She laid each piece into the clean box Ruthie held open above her. Mud streaked the linen. The rain did not care.

For a moment she remembered, because the braid made her remember, a girl in labor thirty years ago who had refused to take down her hair though the pains were six minutes apart and savage, because her husband liked it braided and she meant to look decent if she died. That girl had not died. Della had forgotten her face and kept the braid. Memory chose its own trash heap.

"Mama." Ruthie's voice came low and sharp. "The shoulder."

Della looked. The upper arm bone had rolled into the runoff channel cut by their own boots and was starting down the slope in the brown water like a stick. Rafe moved faster than thought, dropping flat to snatch it before it slipped under the plank. He came up with mud to the elbow and the bone in his fist. For one breath he just held it there, rain hitting the back of his hand, his face gone blank with something too tired to be shock.

Then he passed it to Della without a word.

She took it. Their fingers touched through mud and rain and bone grit. It was nothing that could be called tenderness. Still it struck her harder than if he had cursed.

By four the hill had narrowed to one task: get the final coffin and the last stack of stones onto the truck and off this slope before the road failed or the gate chained. Everything else dropped away. Lucille came back up on foot from Limestone Ridge, stockings blackened, one cheek scored with a dried line of ink. She had left the driver unloading and walked the three miles back because the truck had to stay for the second load. When Della told her she was a fool, she answered, breathing hard, "Yes, but I can still write straight, and apparently that is a scarce skill today."

Then she rolled her sleeves and took hold of a stone cradle rope.

"Miss Holt," Ruthie said, seeing her.

"Please don't start. The men at the ridge were setting one marker on the wrong base and I could not bear the thought of arriving to discover we'd managed to move the dead only to scramble them ourselves."

"I wasn't going to scold you." Ruthie shifted the rope in her hands. "I was going to tell you your satchel's open."

Lucille glanced down, cursed under her breath in a way so soft it almost sounded theoretical, and snapped the clasp shut. Della would have smiled on another day.

The last stone stack came first, because the stones could not be left scattered if the dark took them. Silas insisted on choosing the order even then, touching faces, feeling fractures, rejecting one arrangement and then another until the weight sat right on the sled. He was sweating under the rain now. That frightened Della more than the blood had. A body that had given up its own weather was a body near done.

"Sit down while they take this load," she said again.

"And have you all crack the Penn stone clean through because nobody watched the vein? No."

"To hell with the Penn stone."

Silas's eyes came to her, mild and stubborn and entirely unsentimental. "Easy enough for the living to say."

She had no answer to that which did not make her sound either foolish or cruel, so she bit it back. The truth was she did not care about the Penn stone. She cared about the chest working under his shirt like a wet bellows worked by a drunk man.

The final coffin had to be raised almost by hand because the truck could not get close without risking the whole bank. Rafe rigged the chain through the cedar fork and took the pull with three others on the far side. Della and Silas steadied the sling. Ruthie knelt at the open clean box with extra cloth ready in case the old wood failed altogether. Lucille stood over all of them reading the tag number out loud every time somebody shifted position, not because they had forgotten it but because a named thing held together better than an unnamed one.

"Thirty-two A," she said. "Mrs. Penn. Thirty-two A."

"We heard you," Rafe said through his teeth.

"Then hear me again."

The box rose two inches and stopped. Cedar root. Rafe leaned harder on the chain. Muscles stood out in his forearms dark with rain. Silas planted both boots and put his shoulder under the sagging end before Della could stop him.

"No," she said.

"Yes."

"You'll tear yourself open."

He gave her a look that would have been almost kind if it had not been so impatient. "Then I will know what did it."

He heaved. The root surrendered with a sound like a wet rag ripped slowly in two. The coffin swung free into the sling. For one long impossible second the whole hill seemed to hold its breath with it: chain taut, men's boots sliding, Ruthie's hands open under nothing yet, Lucille's mouth parted around the next number. Then the weight settled true.

"Go," Rafe shouted.

They went. Up the plank, across the rutted path, onto the truck bed already slick with rain and grave water and rubbed raw with stripped paint. Della kept both hands on the side until there was no more room for her body on the plank. Rafe climbed after the coffin, jammed a stone chock behind it, and threw the chain free. Silas stepped back at last.

He took one breath. Then another that did not seem to reach the bottom of him. Then his knees folded.

He did not fall theatrically. He went down like a man sitting where no chair had been put.

Della was beside him before the others had finished swearing. His pulse jumped wild and thin under her fingers. Blood, bright now, flecked his lower lip and ran into the seam beside his nose. The cough came up from deep enough to sound torn on its way.

"Get the truck out," he rasped before she could speak. "Don't stand over me like mourners. Get it gone."

"Silas."

"Get it gone, Della."

He was right. That made it worse.

Rafe had already swung down from the truck. For one second his face hardened against the choice, and she saw the son in him who would once have taken any wound in a room as an excuse to hate her personally. Then the laborer won. He turned, slapped the truck side twice, and shouted to the driver, "Move. If you bog it now I'll throw you under the wheel myself."

The engine caught. The truck lurched, found the plank, and crawled toward the lower road with the last of Old Sion on its back.

Nobody cheered. There was no one left on the hill simple enough for cheering.

When the truck had gone beyond the gate and only the churned noise of it remained, Della wiped the blood from Silas's mouth with his own handkerchief. The cloth came away redder than it had any right to be. He looked past her shoulder at the empty run of the lower row, the holes standing open and raw where the boxes had been.

"That's all of them?" he asked.

"That's all."

He closed his eyes once, not in peace exactly. More like a man finishing a count.

Ruthie was already stripping the tar paper line from between the cedars. "We're not leaving him here," she said. "Rafe, take the near side. Mama, you get his legs clear. Miss Holt, can you manage the tags and the coffee and your own self all in one trip?"

Lucille swiped rain off her mouth. "I've had to manage worse combinations than that."

"I know you have."

They got Silas into the back of the empty stone sled because there was nothing gentler close to hand. He tried once to object and then spent the breath coughing instead. Della walked beside him on the uphill side all the way to the Bledsoe cabin, one hand on the sled rail so it would not list on the muddy path. Rafe took the shafts with the county boy and pulled. His boots slid in the clay where he had run that same path as a child. Della watched the set of his back and thought that the dead had been moved out of water only to drag the living deeper into it.

At the cabin June had the door open before they reached the yard. She had pulled on Rafe's old gray sweater over her housedress, sleeves rolled past ink-smudged wrists, and she still had a school pencil stuck behind one ear as if exactness might help in any room. Her face changed when she saw the sled, but she did not make a sound. She stepped back, kicked the stool clear of the door with one bare heel, and said, "I've got water on."

"More towels too," Ruthie said. "The old ones. Not the good set."

"I know."

They laid Silas on Della's bed because it was the broadest and nearest the stove. Wet wool and mud and cold iron smell filled the room at once. Rafe stood dripping on the hearth bricks while Della cut Silas's shirt open down the front to spare him the struggle of lifting his arms. Under it his chest looked worse than she had feared: rib shadows sharp, skin stretched shiny over the breastbone, the hollows above the collarbones fluttering with each breath. When he coughed, she heard fluid shift low in the right side and a tearing whistle above it.

This was not only age and quarry dust. Something had given way under the strain. Maybe a vessel had burst, or a place in the lung thin as old paper had torn and was flooding into itself. She had heard that sound before in dying men and once in a woman kicked by a mule. There was no stitch for it.

"Do I go for Dr. Bell?" Rafe asked.

He asked it without looking at her, eyes on the washbasin as if the answer might be floating there. Rain beat the roof. Nine muddy miles to Larkin, and the lower road already near chained. Even if he got the doctor turned around in half an hour, even if the car did not bog, even if God Himself held the bridge up for them, Della knew what would arrive too late.

"You can if you want to watch him come nine miles to hear the same chest I hear," she said.

Rafe flinched as if she had struck him with a board. Ruthie, wringing out a towel by the stove, shot Della a look that said cruel is not always the same thing as useful. Della accepted the look. She did not take the words back.

"Then what?" he said.

"Then we keep him warm and dry and as easy as he can be."

June brought the hot bricks wrapped in toweling and set them near the foot of the bed without being told where. Ruthie had already mixed mustard in a saucer, the sharp smell of it cutting through mud and blood. Together the two women laid the plaster on Silas's chest, covered him in blankets, got him out of his wet trousers, wiped the clay from his shins and feet. Della poured a spoon of whiskey between his lips when the coughing eased enough to let it go down. Most of it did. Enough did.

Lucille appeared at the door once, hatless now, hair damp and half fallen. She held the tag board wrapped in oilcloth and a paper sack against her chest.

"They're all accounted for," she said from the threshold. Her voice had lost the office edges and gone plain with tiredness. "Every last one. I stayed till the markers were stacked under cover."

"Good," Della said.

Lucille looked past her to the bed and to Silas's cut shirt hanging over the chair back. Her face changed by a fraction. "I can send word to the county if needed."

"In the morning."

"All right." She hesitated, then set the paper sack on the table. "Mrs. Penn's granddaughter sent these biscuits up with the second truck. She said nobody leaves a graveyard on a day like this and remembers to eat."

Ruthie gave a tired huff that might have been gratitude. "She's right."

Lucille nodded once and went back into the rain, carrying the tag board with both hands.

Night settled early. It pressed at the windows before supper time, dark and wet and uncurious. The yard ran with water under the sycamore. Somewhere down the slope the chained lower gate clanged once in the wind, a metal note so sharp it sounded almost deliberate. The room kept shrinking around the bed as damp clothes steamed by the stove and silence took up more space than talk.

Silas woke and slept by turns, never fully in either place. Once he pushed at the blanket and muttered for his boots. Once he tried to turn onto the bad side and nearly drowned himself coughing. Della lifted him, felt the raw heat under his skin, the tremor in the back muscles that no blanket would settle. Blood came more often now, not much each time but enough. It streaked the cloth she held to his mouth in thin rust-dark lines. Death did not always arrive with a grand quantity. Sometimes it did its work by teaspoons.

Rafe changed into a dry shirt from the peg by the door and then did not leave the room again. He split kindling under the lean-to in the rain, tracked it in, fed the stove, hauled hot water, emptied the basin, fetched more from the pump. He did each thing as if he had been assigned it by a foreman he meant to resent later. Della understood the method. Labor first, feeling if unavoidable after.

When she sent June to lie down, the girl said, "Wake me if you need more water," and took the pencil from behind her ear only when she reached the door, as if she had just remembered she was no longer at the table copying names.

Ruthie made everybody eat one of the cold biscuits standing up. Even Della. Even Rafe. She herself leaned against the stove with her saucer and chewed without tasting, eyes never straying long from the bed.

"You ought to sit," Della told her.

"I sat during church last Sunday. It ought to hold me awhile."

Silas heard that, or some part of it. His mouth moved. Della bent close.

"What?"

He licked blood from a cracked corner of his lip. His breath smelled faintly of peppermint and old iron. "Under the bench," he whispered. "Blue toolbox. Don't let Ned Pruitt borrow my small chisel. He pries crate lids with it."

It was so exactly himself that Della almost laughed and almost cried, and did neither. "I won't."

"He ruins edges."

"I know."

His eyes found Rafe where he stood by the stove with both hands around the poker. "You hear me, boy?"

Rafe came closer. "I hear you."

"Don't let him."

"I won't."

Silas settled back by a fraction, as if an important legal matter had just been resolved. After a while he said, eyes still shut, "Truck clear the gate?"

"Yes," Della said. "They're all up on the ridge."

He breathed out. The sound caught and rasped but made it out of him. "Good."

That was the only peace he asked for. He wanted no prayer, no absolution, none of the fine words people brought out when a body had quit being useful to them. He only wanted the knowledge that the last row had not been left to drown.

The hours after midnight lost their edges. Rain struck the roof. The stove door opened and shut. The kettle lid clicked as it cooled. Silas's breathing went from rough to rougher. Della sat with her thumb pressed into the pulse at his wrist until her own hand cramped. Every so often memory tried to rise in her, not as story but as touch: Gabriel's coat soaked through on the kitchen floor, Eli's boot print in flour, the hard wet work of making one man's death disappear so another man's life might outrun it. She pushed the past back where it belonged. This room had enough in it already.

Somewhere in the blackest part of the night Rafe said, not looking at her, "He should've quit this morning."

Della kept her fingers where they were. "Yes."

"You should've made him."

"How."

Rafe set the poker down with more care than it deserved. "I don't know."

That was as near to grief as either of them could get without tearing open other things first. Della looked at him then. His hair, still damp from the rain, had curled at the temples the way it had when he was a boy. The chin scar was a pale hook in the stove light. He looked older than thirty-nine and younger than the truth she had given him. A man could be both if life had worked him correctly.

"You came back," she said before she could stop herself.

His face did not soften. "Work was here."

That, too, was true. She took it and did not ask for more.

Near dawn Silas's breath changed. Della felt it before she heard it, a slackening under her fingers, the body losing interest in the argument it had been making all night. The next cough did not rise. It only shuddered in the chest and stayed there. She put one hand behind his shoulder and lifted, but there was nothing left in him willing to clear.

"Silas," she said.

His eyes opened once. They moved to her, then past her to the dim square of window where the first ash-gray light was thinking about morning. He did not look afraid. He looked irritated at the inconvenience.

Then he stopped.

No sermon marked it. No weather broke around it. The room simply lost one of its sounds.

Ruthie covered her mouth with the heel of her hand. June, who had come to the doorway barefoot without anyone hearing her, stood still as a stake. Rafe stared at the bed as if waiting for the chest to reconsider. Della kept her hand on Silas's wrist longer than was needed, because it had been a working hand for so many years and she could not yet tell it there was no more work.

At last she closed his mouth, wiped the last blood from the corner of it, and pulled the sheet to his chest. The rain had thinned outside. Through the window the valley looked washed raw.

Rafe was the first to move. He went to the peg, took his wet coat, and shrugged into it.

"Where are you going?" Ruthie asked.

He paused at the door, one hand on the latch. "There's pine stacked under his shed that'll still be dry in the middle. If we get there before folks start tramping through, we can bring it up."

No one answered him.

He looked once toward the bed, once toward Della, not forgiveness and not accusation either, only the hard plain look of a man stepping back into labor because grief had not yet been given any other shape. Then he opened the door and went out into the first light.
