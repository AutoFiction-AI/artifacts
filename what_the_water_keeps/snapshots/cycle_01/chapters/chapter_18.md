# Chapter 18

By the second week of September the valley had started making water-noises in places that had never belonged to water before. Della heard them before she opened her eyes: a hollow gulp under the lower culvert, the wet slap of small waves against fence posts, something loose and tinny striking, drifting off, striking again. Morning air came cooler now, but it carried a sourer smell than summer had, algae and drowned weeds and the metal tang the river got when men had bullied it out of its bed and forced it to wait behind concrete.

She stepped onto the porch in her stockings and laid one hand on the post until the stiffness left her left thumb enough to let go. The light was thin and ash-colored. Below Cedar Bench, where the Turner pasture used to throw back white fog at dawn, water lay smooth and flat, with only the top wire of the fence showing in one place and the post caps standing up like blackened knuckles. The lane that ran from the church bend to the mill road was no longer a lane all the way through. It went visible, then pale under a wash of water, then gone. A sycamore leaned out of the new shoreline with its roots undercut and shining. Near it a chicken coop door floated faceup and turned slowly as if somebody were looking for the right side to set it on.

Inside, Ruthie had already lit the stove. Grease ticked in the skillet. June turned a page at the table with the careful dry sound she made when she was trying not to wrinkle paper that was not hers. Rafe sat on the chair nearest the door in his work pants and undershirt, boots unlaced, scraping dam clay from the sole with a roofing nail he had straightened and saved for the purpose. He looked older in the early light than he did in full day. The skin under his eyes had gone the color of old dishwater since he took the downstream shifts, and the backs of his hands kept a gray grit in the cracks now no matter how hard Ruthie soaked them.

"It came up another foot in the night," he said without looking at her. His voice had the thick grain of a man short on sleep, but not the old edge that had made every word between them strike. "Lower road's near gone. They had a johnboat tied where Harlan Meeks used to park his truck when the axle heated."

Ruthie slid a plate of fried potatoes at him and set another by Della's place. She had turned the collar on her brown housedress again, inside out this time so the frayed edge hid under the seam, and she still wore a measuring tape around her neck because she had been up late mending two shirts for cash. "Eat while it's hot," she said. "If you're going to stand out there looking at water, do it after you've had something in you."

June glanced up from the school tablet on her lap. Ink had smudged the side of her middle finger blue again, and there was a burr caught in the cuff of her sweater where she had walked through the upper field the day before and forgotten to pick it off. "Can we go down after breakfast?" she asked Della. She did not say to look. She did not need to.

Rafe gave a short sound that was not quite a laugh. "Take a stick if you do. Bank's sloughing on the bend. Looks solid till it don't."

He set his pay envelope by his plate to free both hands for the fork. The envelope was thinner than the week before. Della did not have to touch it to know. Money had a look when there was not enough of it. It made a man eat too fast and keep count while he chewed.

"The bank sent word yesterday," Ruthie said, still facing the stove. "Not another letter. Just that boy from McRae's with his hat in his hands. Same thing in a younger body."

Rafe chewed, swallowed, and reached for the salt. "I know it."

Della sat. The chair scraped the floorboards that would soon belong to nobody. She took one potato, broke it, and watched the steam go. "Word doesn't shrink a note," she said.

"No." He kept his eyes on the plate. "Neither does truth."

The sentence might once have been a start to another day's fight. This time it lay between them plain and flat. He did not ask her to take it back. She did not defend it. Ruthie set down the coffee pot harder than she needed to and poured into the chipped cups.

June said, in the manner she had when she was keeping adults from pretending a thing had not been said, "It changed what went on the stones."

Rafe looked at her then. For a second Della thought he might flare at having his daughter weigh in on a matter that had torn his own name open. Instead he rubbed his thumb along the lip of the cup and said, "It did that."

He drank, set the cup down, and stood. The chair legs tapped once on the boards. "I've got to get down there before full light. Foreman said they're moving men off blasting and onto spillway cleanout. Means muck work and wet boots all day. Better pay, same smell." He bent, tied one boot, then the other. When he straightened he paused by the door, not looking at Della but not refusing her either. "If you go down, don't fool with the low fence. Wire's drifting loose. It'll take your ankle if it catches."

It was not tenderness. It was not forgiveness. It was a man telling his mother where the ground was giving way.

After he had gone and the sound of his truck rattled off the ridge road, Ruthie set more potatoes in the middle of the table and finally sat herself. "If you two are going, go before the wind gets up," she said to Della and June. "And if that road has gone under, don't stand there till the damp gets in your bones like you're waiting for it to explain itself. Water never explains a thing."

June folded her tablet closed. "Can I take the satchel?"

"What for?" Ruthie asked.

June's face stayed steady. "If Gran wants to mark anything."

Della looked at the girl then, really looked. Fifteen was still young enough in some lights to make her mouth look childish, but the look never lasted once she fixed her attention on something. Then the child dropped away and the hard exactness came in. She had Della's broad cheekbones and none of Della's talent for pretending not to notice.

"Take it," Della said.

They went by the upper path first because the lower one was already slick in patches and the red clay grabbed at boots as if it wanted to keep everything the valley had ever held. September had started touching the leaves. Sumac along the fence line had rusted dark at the tips, and the pokeweed berries were black enough to stain through cloth. The air no longer pressed with August's full wet heat, yet the reservoir gave back a different heaviness, a broad cool damp that sat on the skin and worked its way under sleeves.

June carried the satchel across her chest and a cut hickory stick in her hand. Every little while she used the stick not for balance but to test ground before she put her weight on it, patient as an old man. Della liked that and feared it too. The girl was learning a cautiousness she had not yet paid for.

At the first open place on the ridge they stopped. What had been fields below was now an uneven sheet of green-brown water with rows and boundaries half remembered under it. Della could still read the land because she had walked it too many years not to. There was the low place where the Clary mule always bogged in spring, though now only the tops of the reeds showed. There had been the school bus turnaround, though the circle was only a change in color under the surface now, pale clay fading into the deeper green. The Wolfe orchard stood in the distance with water moving between the lower trunks, tree after tree rising out of it up to the knees like men caught and refusing to shout.

June followed her gaze. "Mrs. Acker said they opened her papers again in Larkin," she said. "She said that like it was something to chew slow."

"It's something," Della said.

"It's not money yet."

"No."

June worried at the satchel strap with her thumb. "She brought Mama two jars of peaches yesterday."

Della had seen them on the shelf, both jars cloudier than they should have been because Noreen had been too rushed or too tired when she worked the boiling water. Ruthie had set them up with the others all the same. "People pay how they can."

"Yes, ma'am."

They walked on. Once they passed the stump where men used to sit and spit tobacco on auction days, and Della had the queer sensation of having stepped not into memory but into a room after the furniture had been taken out. Space went wrong when what had organized it disappeared. A valley was not just dirt and trees. It was the turn where a horse shied, the porch where a woman had labored twelve hours and then laughed because the baby came out with both fists tight, the ditch that always took a wheel after hard rain, the place boys met to fish because adults could not see them from the road. Water did not know any of that. It only rose to the line given and then looked for the next one.

By the time they reached the church bend the old road was no longer a road so much as a memory laid under glass. In the shallow part nearest them the wagon ruts still showed through clear water, red clay pale where the current had not muddied it yet. Two yards farther on the ruts softened. Another yard, and there was only color. The culvert below the bend was still taking trapped air from under the wash and letting it out with a hollow glug every few seconds, like a man choking in his sleep.

June stopped beside Della and said very softly, "It goes quicker than I thought."

Della planted the hickory stick and took a step closer to the edge. Her boot sank half an inch. The bank trembled, not enough to fall but enough to answer the weight. A length of fence wire moved just under the surface, slow as a snake in winter. On the far side a mailbox, pulled loose from its post, had drifted into the ditch and sat with its door open, taking on water a little at a time.

"That there was Wallace Kerr's turn-in," Della said, pointing with her chin. "You could smell his sorghum mill before you saw the house. He let the mules stand too long and they all had ring sores on the harness line." She shifted her hand, lower. "And that cut there. We hauled Mrs. Devlin through it on a mattress one wet March because the baby wouldn't wait for daylight. Men cussed every rock between here and Cedar Bench."

June's face stayed on the water. "I don't remember the Kerr place."

"You were too little."

"I remember the Devlin girl with the burn scar. Not the road."

A small wave came in from the wider water below, not the natural push of river current but the slap a wide body makes when it has room to gather itself. It crossed the visible ruts, darkened them, and ran off. For a second the road disappeared altogether. Then the surface settled and the lines came back faint as bruising under skin.

Della felt her left thumb jump once, a dry involuntary pain along the base where too much digging and lifting had made the joint protest every change in weather. She put the hand in her apron pocket and found nothing there but a handkerchief. The copied pages waited at the house under folded linens. Gabriel's page lay separate from them in the drawer by the bed. She had known, ever since chapter and verse were done with in the hearing room and the graves had all been lifted out of Old Sion, that the papers could not stay with her till the end. She had also known that handing them over was not the same as laying down a weight. A thing transferred clean was one thing. A thing that had bitten through blood and shame before it ever reached paper was another.

June said, "Do you think people will lie about it after?"

"About what?"

"About how it was. About who lived where. About whose place was whose. About what happened before the water."

Della looked at the girl. June had not turned her head. She asked her hardest questions that way sometimes, as if giving a person her face would let them duck.

"Some will," Della said. "Some already do."

"Even now that the stones say right?"

"The stones say one kind of right. Paper says another. What folks want to remember is a third thing."

June nodded once. "Then it matters who keeps the rest."

It was as close as the girl had ever come to naming Della's private work without permission. Della did not answer. She watched the water instead.

Wind came up from the open spread below the dam works, cooler than the morning had been and carrying the slap of waves against drowned brush. The surface roughened. A drift of pale foam moved across the roadbed and caught in the fence wire. The clear water near the edge clouded as clay lifted from underneath. Then, in one long minute with nothing ceremonial about it, the road gave way to the water's color. The ruts blurred, disappeared, and did not come back. The last visible hump at Wallace Kerr's turn went from pale red to brown to the same green-black as the rest.

June let out the breath she had been holding. Della had not known the girl was holding it, but then she had not known she was holding hers either until the sound came out of them together.

There was no final cracking sound, no church bell, no sign from God fit for telling later. The old road simply stopped being something a person could use.

"There it is," Della said.

"Yes, ma'am."

They stood until their shoes wicked damp through the leather and the wind started cutting sharper along the wet hem of Della's skirt. On the walk back June did not fill the quiet with talk. She touched one fence post as they passed, feeling the wet grain with her fingertips as if she meant to store that too. At the rise above the pasture Della turned once more and saw only water between the sycamore and the drowned ditch. The road had already begun the quick work of becoming a thing a person had to point at and explain.

Rafe's truck was in the yard when they got back, backed crooked to the shed because he had been in a hurry. He had come home for the noon break and was loading what could still be carried easy: a coil of wire, two feed sacks, the hand corn sheller, a crate of jars padded with newspaper. Ruthie stood in the doorway of the shed with both arms full of quilts and told him where not to drop them. Her hair had come loose from the pins around her temples, and she kept blowing at the strand stuck to her mouth without pausing in what she was doing.

"Well?" she called when she saw them. "Did it swallow it or is it still pretending?"

"It's under," June said.

Rafe slid the corn sheller farther into the truck bed and nodded once, as if that settled a figure he had been carrying in his head. "Figured."

He wiped his hand on his pants and looked at Della. There was dried white cement dust at the edge of his hairline. "Foreman says they'll close the lower spur completely by Wednesday. If there's anything down there you mean to fetch, it needs fetching now."

Della thought of the bedstead Eli had made out of green walnut and then broken one rail on in anger twenty years later. She thought of the cracked blue pitcher by the washstand, the old maternity satchel with its rotten clasp, the cedar box that had held the ledger and then failed to protect it. Objects took on weight again the nearer you came to losing them. Half of that weight was only memory in the shape of wood.

"There's nothing low I need," she said.

Rafe's mouth moved like he might say more. He did not. He lifted the crate of jars instead and settled it between the wheel wells with more care than a man would usually give a crate of cloudy peaches and green beans. When he straightened he saw the damp on June's skirt hem.

"Didn't I tell you not to stand in it?" he asked.

June did not apologize. "I didn't fall in it."

"Not what I asked."

Ruthie cut in before the line could sharpen. "If you're done scolding the only person here who can still see where she's putting her feet, carry that lard can to the truck."

Rafe snorted once through his nose and obeyed. He took the can from her hand. On its lid somebody had written nails in pencil years ago, though it now held buttons, odd screws, and the kind of small metal a poor household kept because one day some bent piece might be the thing that saved a trip to town.

Della watched him lift it. His name had changed in nobody's mouth after all the hearing talk. Men at the dam still called him Bledsoe when they wanted him and something harsher when they didn't. The bank had not forgiven a dollar because Gabriel Wolfe now lay under his own stone. Truth had not put flour in the bin or grease on the axle or shoes on June's feet. But it had taken a certain poison out of the house. Not all of it. Not enough for comfort. Enough that when Rafe warned her about loose wire or asked if there was anything to fetch, the words no longer came shaped like a demand for her to lie.

That evening, after the truck had gone up and back twice with what the family could salvage before damp ruined it, and after Ruthie had fed them all beans, cold cornbread, and the last slice of salt pork cut thin enough to flavor more than feed, Della took the copied pages from the shelf above the bed. She had wrapped them in a piece of flour sack and slid them under the folded Sunday dress because no thief had yet thought to look there for what was not money.

The paper had already begun to curl at the corners despite the dry spell, and her own hand on it looked older than it had when she copied it. Some pages were in pencil, some in ink. A few bore grease spots where she had worked over them at the kitchen table between chores. She had not copied everything from the stolen ledger. She had copied only the pieces that still belonged to the breathing: births the county had never entered right, marriages that existed in kitchens and on porches if not before license clerks, burials misnamed or left blank, notes that could keep one family from being robbed because some man with spectacles preferred a clean line to a true one.

Gabriel's page was not among them. She had taken it out weeks ago and folded it by itself. It carried his name, the date she had fixed as best she could, the fever-plot burial, and under Rafe's birth her own hard spared hand where she had written Eli in the place she had known was not his. That page was not merely record. It was the cut she had made and the stitch she had chosen.

Ruthie came in with the wash basin, set it down, and saw what lay on the quilt. She did not ask permission before sitting on the bed's edge. She was too tired for ceremony. "So tonight's the night," she said.

Della kept her hand on the pages. "Looks so."

Ruthie wrung the rag once over the basin and then laid it across her knee. Her laundress hands were rough and red to the wrist. "If you're giving them to June, don't make a funeral of it."

Della snorted before she meant to. "What else would you call paper about the dead and the near-dead?"

"I'd call it work. Same as any other work. You hand a girl a burden like it's a curse laid from heaven and she'll carry it crooked forever." Ruthie looked at the pages, then at Della. "Give it to her straight. In the light. Let her know what it's for and what it ain't for. And if you mean to keep something back, keep it back on purpose. Not because you lost nerve halfway through."

There were times Ruthie said more in a minute than most people managed with an afternoon and a pie. Della folded the cloth tighter around the stack, then loosened it again. "I know what I'm keeping."

"I figured you did." Ruthie rose, lifted the basin again, and went to the door. "I'll send her in. And I'll stay in the kitchen. Not because it's my business to hear all of it. Because if the girl is to keep this, she doesn't need to learn from the first minute that it has to be done alone."

When June came in she had washed her face and hands for the evening, but ink still stained the webbing between thumb and forefinger. She had changed into the nightgown Ruthie had lengthened at the hem with a strip from an old flour sack, and she held the braid she was halfway through plaiting over one shoulder so it would not drip on the papers from the damp ends.

"You wanted me?" she asked.

Della pointed to the chair by the bed. "Shut the door and sit."

June did. She folded one bare foot under the other leg and set both hands in her lap. Under pressure she always got a little too formal, like she meant to make adults work harder if they wanted to dismiss her. Della respected it because she knew where the habit came from.

She set the cloth-wrapped stack on June's knees. "These are the pages I copied out of the old book before it got took."

June did not touch them at first. "For the cemetery claims?"

"Some. Not all." Della tapped the top sheet. "They're for the living. That's the only reason I copied them. Not so the county can pick clean every poor house in Hale County because it found out my memory's good. Not so a judge can strip a woman of what little shame or shelter she kept because paper would make his shelf neater. For the living. You understand the difference?"

June looked down at the bundle. "I think so."

"Thinking so ain't enough."

June lifted her eyes. "Then tell me plain."

Della sat back. The bed rope creaked under her. "If a man comes asking because he wants more land, and all he's got to lean on is that some dead girl never got her license entered right, you make him do without my paper. If a clerk comes wanting to tidy an old matter that never cost him sleep, you make him do without. If a woman is being turned out because the law says her own life didn't happen the proper way, you look. If a child is being told he belongs nowhere because somebody with a stamp likes a shorter story, you look. If looking will feed a bully more than it'll help the hungry, you keep the cloth tied."

June listened without blinking. "And if I can't tell which it is?"

"Then wait till you can. Or bring it to your mother, because she's got better sense than most judges." Della glanced toward the kitchen. Ruthie made a soft unimpressed sound through the wall, which meant she had heard that and would not object. "And if Miss Holt comes," Della added after a second, "you hear what she wants before you show a thing. She's one of the few who knows record ain't the same as right. But she still works for paper first."

June finally laid her fingers on the bundle. She felt the edges through the cloth, thumb moving over the corners as if measuring thickness. "Will I add to it?"

"If there comes something worth adding and you can swear to it."

"In my own hand?"

"In your own hand and dated, if you know the date. If you don't know, say you don't. A guess dressed up as fact is still a lie."

June nodded. "All right."

Della waited. The room held its breath with her. Through the wall came the small ordinary sounds of Ruthie washing up: tin plate against basin rim, water poured from one cup to another, cupboard door shut with hip and not hand because both hands were busy. Down the ridge road a truck changed gears. Farther off than that, almost part of the dark now, the reservoir slapped at something hollow.

June said, "You're keeping one."

It was not accusation. It was the notice of a girl who missed very little.

"Yes."

"The one about Gabriel."

Della did not ask how she knew. She had not raised fools. "Yes."

June studied her face with that steady, unbeautiful mercy the young sometimes had when they had already seen enough damage to know pity did not mend anything. "Because he was yours before he was the county's?"

Della let out a slow breath through her nose. "Because that page isn't only his. It's mine. It's your daddy's. It's the lie I wrote and the body I hid and the years it sat in my drawer while the wrong name did the work. The others can help the living without dragging all that night behind them. That one can't."

June looked back at the bundle. "If you die with it, it dies too."

"Maybe."

June was quiet a moment. Then: "I wasn't asking for the night."

The sentence struck Della harder than tears would have. She put her hand over the girl's. June's fingers were longer already than Della's had ever been, the knuckles ink-marked, the skin thin and warm. "Good," Della said. "Because I'm not giving you the night. I carried Gabriel up out of that kitchen with blood drying tacky on the sheet and Silas cussing under his breath because dead weight drags different than flour sacks do. I put him in the fever plot by lantern smoke and wrote the wrong father under your daddy's name because I thought a poor child could live under a wrong name easier than he could live under a rich man's notice. That's not paper work. That's what I did with my own hands. I will keep that page while I can still tell what belongs on it."

June did not flinch. Her throat moved once. "Then what you're giving me is the part that can still do some good."

"The part that might," Della said. "Don't make it holier than it is."

A faint smile touched June's mouth and was gone. "I won't."

She untied the cloth carefully and looked at the top page. Della watched her eyes move, left to right, steady and unsentimental. Birth. Witness. Place. Correction. Burial. The girl did not handle it like relic or scandal. She handled it like work.

"This line is missing a month," June said after a moment, tapping one entry with a clean fingernail. "You only wrote spring."

"I only knew spring."

"Then I'll leave spring."

"Leave spring."

June retied the cloth, tighter than before, and set the bundle aside with a small decisiveness that told Della she had already moved it in her mind from gift to responsibility. "Where do I keep it?"

"Not in this room," Della said. "Too many know this room knows things."

Ruthie opened the door with her elbow and came in carrying the lard can Rafe had loaded and then set back down by mistake when he discovered Ruthie meant to keep it. "In this," she said. "It has held worse company and come through. Wrap the pages in another cloth and put a cake tin lid over them inside. Mice can smell paper near as quick as men can."

June took the can. The penciled word nails still showed faint on the lid. "Thank you."

Ruthie leaned against the doorframe, tired enough to do it in plain sight. "Don't thank me like I sewed you a birthday ribbon. Keep it dry, keep it square, and don't let any grandstanding fool make a performance of it."

June nodded once. "I won't."

Ruthie looked at Della. "There. Nobody had to die over it."

Della almost said somebody already had, more than one, but she let it pass. Ruthie had not meant easy. She had meant no theater.

June rose with the bundle and the can in her arms. At the door she stopped. "Gran?"

"What."

"When somebody asks later where the road was, I'm going to tell them right."

Della looked at her until the room blurred a little at the edges from plain tiredness. "You do that."

June went out. Ruthie followed more slowly, pausing only long enough to squeeze Della's shoulder once, hard, practical, no softness in it but no cruelty either. Then Della was alone with the bed, the empty patch on the quilt where the copied pages had lain, and the drawer that still held Gabriel's leaf.

She took it out after a minute. The paper had been folded and unfolded enough that the crease had whitened. Her writing stared up at her in two different selves. One hand had recorded the fact of Gabriel Wolfe's burial in the fever plot. The other, lower down and years earlier, had written Eli where Eli did not belong. She could remember both women's breathing. One had been fast and panicked, the other careful and flat. They were the same woman all the same.

She folded the page small and put it in the pocket of her apron. Then she went outside.

Night had not fully closed yet. The western sky held a dirty yellow where the sun had gone down behind cloud, and the new water below the ridge took that light in broken strips. From the yard she could no longer see the exact place where the road had crossed. Water had widened since morning, or maybe distance and dusk had simply finished the work. The sycamore showed darker now, half its lower branches dragging. Somewhere out across the drowned low ground something wooden knocked twice, paused, and knocked again.

Behind her, through the open window, she could hear June finding room for the can on the shelf by the bed she shared with her mother. Tin scraped wood. Ruthie told her to set a folded sack under it. Rafe came in last, boots heavy on the steps, and the house altered around the weight of him as houses always did around men who had done a day's paid labor in bad weather. He said something low that Della could not catch. June answered. Ruthie laughed once, dry as kindling breaking. Life went on in there with the same poor furniture and the same short money and the same damned need to decide what got kept and what got left for water.

Noreen still did not have her land back. Vernon Wolfe still had breath in his body and a lawyer somewhere in Larkin. The truck note still sat on Rafe's neck. Della's cemetery wage was almost spent. None of that had shifted because bones were named right or a stone had been recut. Truth had not rescued a single acre of Brier Valley from the reservoir. It had not made Gabriel worth less dead or Eli worth more absent. It had not washed Della clean.

What it had done was smaller and harder than rescue. It had put certain names back where they belonged. It had made some lies harder to live in. It had given June, inside that lit room, a bundle of paper that might keep a woman or a child from being rubbed out entirely just because the men with forms were in a hurry. The rest of it, the blood part, the part that had started in a kitchen and ended in a fever plot, stayed hot against Della's ribs where the folded page rested in her pocket.

She stood long enough for the damp to find her knees. Out below, where the old road had lain since before she first bled, the water took one last scrap of paler clay into itself and turned it the same dark color as the rest. Then there was no line at all, only the broad reservoir holding the valley down. Della kept her hand over her pocket and watched until she could not tell road from water anymore. It did not bring the place back. It did not forgive her. It only meant that when the drowned valley was spoken of later, it would not be spoken wrong.
