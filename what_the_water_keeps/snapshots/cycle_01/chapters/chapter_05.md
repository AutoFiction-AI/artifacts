# Chapter 5

By the time Della came up Cedar Bench, the light had thinned to the color of watered honey and the house smelled richer than the pantry had any business promising. Blackberries were bursting in the iron pot on the back of the stove. Ruthie had cut onion into a kettle of beans and new potatoes and let a rind of salt pork turn the broth slick. Cornbread browned in the spider skillet. June stood at the sink with berry scratches crosshatched over her forearms, picking stems from a basin and trying not to watch the road.

Rafe was under the truck with both boots showing. Every so often the heel of one boot thudded against the axle when a wrench slipped. The sound carried across the yard in the same tired rhythm Della had heard all week: metal struck in anger, then not enough changed for the anger to feel useful.

She came in with cemetery dust caked pale at the hem of her dress and the day's four dollars folded small in the pocket of her apron. Lucille had counted it into her hand after the hearing without looking up, clerk-fast, the way a woman passed aspirin in church. Della set the bills on the table beside the salt crock. Money lay thin and accusatory on the oilcloth. Ruthie wiped her hands on her apron, smoothed the singles flat with the side of her wrist, and slid a cracked saucer over them so they would not flutter if the screen door banged.

"Wash first," Ruthie said. "If either of you brings red clay to my clean boards, you can lick it back up."

June's mouth twitched. Della hung her sunbonnet on the peg, pumped water into the basin, and scrubbed at the crescent of dirt set in the stiff groove of her left thumb. The thumb ached where the coffin ropes had burned it earlier. When she flexed it, she thought of the schoolhouse too, of the room gone still while she named which Kincaid daughter had come first and which marriage had held before God even if the county had never bothered to write it neat enough for a judge. She had felt Rafe in the back of that room without turning. A mother knew her own son's anger the way she knew weather in her joints: not by sight first, but by pressure.

Ruthie nodded toward the stairs to the loft. "June, fetch your daddy in before the bread burns."

"I heard that," Rafe called from under the truck.

"Then crawl out and wash," Ruthie said. "I don't want grease on the spoon."

He slid out on his back, hair flattened with sweat, chin scar blackened by the shadow of beard. He had grease to both wrists and a streak across his cheek where he had wiped at his face with the wrong part of his hand. For one second he looked so much like the lanky boy who used to come in from haying and drink straight from the dipper that Della felt her chest go soft with the old reflex of mercy. Then he saw the saucer over her wages and the softness died under his mouth.

"County pay?" he asked.

"Day pay," Ruthie said before Della could. "Same as the others."

He stood there a second longer, water dripping off his fingers into the washpan. "Good to know somebody in this house brought cash home."

"Wash," Ruthie said again. Not sharp. Sharper than if she had raised her voice.

He did. He scrubbed hard, shoulders bunched, as if dirt might come off faster if he hated it enough. June carried the cornbread to table with a folded rag under the skillet handle. Ruthie ladled beans and potatoes into the deep bowl, set out sliced tomatoes shining with salt, and brought the blackberry cobbler last, not because dessert had become part of their manners all of a sudden but because she wanted the table to see it whole before spoons ruined it. The berries had cooked down almost black around the edges. Sugar was expensive enough that the crust looked mean and was not. Ruthie had spent it anyway.

There were nights Della could feel the poverty of the house before she sat down, in the thinness of the stew or the way everybody watched one biscuit dish as if staring might multiply it. This was not one of them. This was a real supper, made by skill rather than ease, and Della's stomach cramped hard at the smell of it because her body had believed all afternoon it would be put off again.

Ruthie waited until they had all taken a first mouthful. That was one of her quiet rules. Nobody started trouble with food still untouched.

Then she said, "June, ask your daddy what made him the enemy of yellowjackets that year he tied the flour sack over his head."

June looked up over her spoon. "Again?"

"Again," Ruthie said. "Miz Della came in too tired to laugh proper, and I worked half this afternoon for them berries. We're getting some use from them."

Rafe had already started to refuse. Della saw it happen in his jaw, the wish to keep hold of his grievance a little longer. But June was grinning now in spite of herself, and Ruthie had set the room to that track on purpose.

"It wasn't yellowjackets," he said. "It was hornets."

"It was yellowjackets," June said. "They come out of the rotten stump by the lower canes."

"You were eight. You don't know what they were."

"I know what your hollering sounded like."

That got him. The corner of his mouth twitched.

Ruthie tipped her chin at him. "Go on."

Rafe tore a piece of cornbread, dragged it through bean broth, and shook his head like a man cornered into telling on himself. "Your gran remembers. We were on the back side of the patch, the hot year before the creek went green, and your mama had it in her head she was putting up six jars before dark. I said I'd go in where the canes were thickest because you couldn't get through there without coming out looking whipped. So I tied that flour sack over my hat and cut eyeholes."

"You cut one eyehole," June said. "The other side was just ripped."

"Worked well enough till I leaned on that stump. Whole damn nest came up mad. They got inside the sack before I got it off."

June gave up and laughed outright. Ruthie did too, quiet and pleased with herself, her reddened hands still around her spoon. Even Della smiled, seeing it plain again: Rafe twenty pounds lighter, bolting through the yard with the flour sack half over one eye, slapping at his own neck while Ruthie stood bent over the porch rail and hollered instructions useless as hymn lyrics. He had jumped straight into the rinse barrel, boots and all, and come up purple with indignation because the berries in the bottom had burst under him.

"You forgot the best part," Ruthie said.

"There wasn't a best part."

"There was. You came out talking brave till your foot hit the barrel hoop and split your pants right up the back."

June put her hand over her mouth. "Mama."

Rafe pointed his spoon at Ruthie. "That was years later."

"It was the same day," Ruthie said. "Because I had to patch both injuries that night."

Della laughed then, not because the story deserved so much but because the room needed it and because for one minute Rafe's face loosened enough to show the boy who had once thought brute determination could outwork insects, weather, and common sense alike. The laugh went through her like warm broth. For a minute the house sounded ordinary: spoon on crockery, June's breathless attempt not to laugh with food in her mouth, Ruthie's dry little snort, Rafe pretending the story bored him while he fed it back better than they did.

Outside the open window the cicadas sawed at the dusk. From far downriver a low concussion rolled up the valley, late blasting or some truck dropping iron. The jelly in the saucered jar on the shelf gave a small shiver.

Nobody spoke through that sound. They listened without meaning to. Every house in Brier Valley had gotten in the habit of listening, same as a body learned to listen for a cough that might turn worse.

Rafe set down his spoon. "How much extra'd they pay for talking?"

Ruthie looked at him once. "Nothing extra."

"Seems they should. You about earned it."

Della kept eating. The beans were soft enough to cut with the side of her spoon. Ruthie had sliced the onions thin so they melted instead of announcing themselves. June was still rosy from laughing and had a blackberry seed caught high in the corner of her lip. Della wanted, for the length of one supper, to keep her hands on what was in front of her instead of what waited below the cemetery cedars.

Ruthie said, "You can let the table set one hour without dragging that schoolhouse into it."

"Can I?" Rafe asked. He was not looking at Ruthie. He was looking at the saucer over the money. "Looked to me like the whole county got an hour of it."

June's spoon slowed.

Della said, "Eat while it's hot."

That was not an answer and all of them knew it. Rafe laughed through his nose.

"I ate standing up at noon," he said. "I can eat cold just as easy." He rubbed his thumb over the seam of the spoon handle, grease still dark in the cracks of his skin. "You stood there this afternoon and fixed Sadie Kincaid's girl better than any lawyer could've. One mouthful from you and a field changed hands."

"It wasn't a field," Della said. "It was standing."

"There you go." He snapped his fingers once, not loud, worse for being quiet. "That's what I mean. You got the right word for everybody else's trouble. You got them stacked neat as jars. When it comes home, you turn stingy."

"Rafe." Ruthie's voice stayed level. "June's right here."

"June's got ears whether I talk or not." He took a breath and changed tracks the way he always did when shame got too near; he widened the road instead of stopping. "I stood in that room like I was twelve years old again. Everybody looking from you to me, waiting to see if maybe your tongue would loosen for your own blood after it had already loosened for the Kincaids."

Della did not tell him she had known he was there from the moment he leaned against the back wall. She had felt the heat of his need between her shoulder blades while Lucille Holt asked questions in clerk's English and Sadie Kincaid's daughters sat with their hands folded like girls at punishment. If she had turned then, if she had let her eyes rest on him even once, the room would have known too much. Or maybe that was what she had told herself.

Ruthie broke her cornbread cleanly in two. "You're not doing this over beans."

He cut a look at her. "I'm doing it over four hundred eighty-six dollars at the bank and seventy-three forty at McRae's and a truck that don't know whether it's alive or prayer-held. I'm doing it over the fact I'm thirty-nine years old and my mother brings home the surest money in this house."

Ruthie said nothing. That was the worst of her silences. They were never blank. They set a fact down between people and let them bruise themselves on it.

Rafe kept going because he could not stand still once he had started. "You think I don't know what folks saw today? They saw you settle somebody else's dead and me standing there with my hat in my hands waiting on you. That's what they saw. A man full-grown, can't clear his own name, can't clear his own land, can't even tell his wife if we're staying in this house through the summer or taking some rented box down by the dam with men pissing off the porch at all hours. You want it plainer than that?"

June had gone still in the way she did when she was listening hardest. Her hand had stopped halfway to the tomato dish. The room seemed to narrow around the table. Della could hear the fat spit once in the stove eye where blackberry juice had bubbled over.

Ruthie said, "June, finish your plate."

June obeyed, but the obedience was watchful. Della could see the question gathering behind her face and hated Rafe for laying it there. Hated herself more because he had only put words to the shape already living in the house.

He turned back to Della. "You can say a man was husband enough when it helps strangers keep a roof. You can swear to births, buryings, whose body lay down beside whose all the way back to mud. But you won't say what lies in that fever plot, and that's the one thing in this valley that might keep us from going under."

Della set down her spoon.

She could have said the line she had used on him before: that she would not swear what she could not prove. It was not a lie. It was also not the whole of it. The whole of it had Gabriel's face in it, and Eli's flat refusal, and a kitchen floor she had washed on her knees while dawn came up thin under the door. The whole of it had Rafe himself in it, sleeping one room over as a child while grown people chose what story would keep him fed and unshot and not yet named bastard by men who smiled in church.

If the bones in the fever plot had belonged to some other household, some other woman's compromise, Della might have found easier courage. That was the filth under all of it, and she knew it.

So she said nothing.

Across from her, June's chair gave one little creak as she shifted. Ruthie looked at Della then, not pleading, not angry either. Only measuring, the way she measured cloth before she cut into it for good.

Rafe waited a beat longer. "Say something."

Della folded her napkin once more though it was already folded. "The beans'll skin over."

He stared. The line that came into his face was not surprise. He had expected refusal. What surprised him, Della saw, was the shape of it. Not a fight. Not even a lie he could get hold of. Just her sitting there with her mouth shut while his shame lay open on the oilcloth between the salt and the cobbler.

"All right," he said. Very soft. "That tells me enough."

"It tells you supper's on the table," Ruthie said.

"No. It tells me she will spend truth where it buys her righteousness and save it where it might cost her something."

Della kept still. Once silence had been the cleanest bandage she owned. Women had sat in her kitchen with torn hems, split lips, seed-warted hands clenched in their laps, and she had known when to speak and when to leave a thing unspoken so they could walk back out into the same world alive. Under Rafe's voice the old skill felt rotten. Like laying a clean towel over spoiled meat.

Ruthie put down her spoon. "Don't you drag righteousness in here like it was invited."

"What would you call it then?"

"I'd call it a bad hour to show your daughter what kind of man you are when you're scared."

That landed. Della saw it land because his face flared and went flat again, all in one motion. Ruthie was the only person in the house who could cut him clean without raising her voice.

He looked at June, then away. "I know exactly what kind of man I am."

"That doesn't mean she needs the lesson tonight," Ruthie said.

June spoke before Della could stop her. "It was money today, wasn't it?"

No one answered.

June set down her fork carefully. "At the hearing. That's why Daddy's mad. Because names are money."

Ruthie said, "Baby, eat."

"I am eating."

"Then do that and not the rest."

The formality in June's voice made Della's neck prickle worse than if the girl had cried. Children cried. Formality meant June had filed the thing away.

Rafe pushed back from the table. The chair legs grated across the floorboards. "Finish without me."

Ruthie said, "Sit back down."

He was already turning toward the door. "I said finish."

The screen slapped behind him hard enough to rattle the saucer over the four dollars. Outside, his boots struck the porch, then the yard. A second later something metal clanged against the truck bed. Not thrown. Set down badly.

No one moved for a moment. The cobbler steamed in the middle of the table with its top still unbroken, an offered sweetness nobody had the stomach to claim.

Then Ruthie rose and began clearing plates with short efficient motions.

"June," she said, "take the berry bowl to the cellar. Upper shelf. Not the deep back. Those jars are already sweating."

June got up at once. Ruthie was using instructions as planks laid over bad ground. Della recognized the habit because she had the same one.

Della said, "Leave the plates. I can do them."

"You worked all day," Ruthie said.

"So did you."

Ruthie lifted two dishes in one hand. "Mine isn't the one men line up to hear."

The sentence had no extra force in it. That was what made it sting. She took the plates to the washpan. June carried the bowl down to the cellar stairs with both hands, long-limbed and solemn, and came back without asking another question.

Della cut the cobbler and served it because not serving it would have turned the whole meal into waste. June ate hers because Ruthie told her to. Ruthie ate standing up at the sink between rinses. Della tasted almost nothing but blackberry skins and the grit of sugar that had not fully melted at one edge where the crust ran thick. Through the open window she could hear Rafe out by the truck, not working now, just moving around it with the blind, object-seeking force of a man who would rather touch metal than sit in his own house.

When the dishes were done June went up to bed with her school tablet tucked under her arm though school was out and had been for days. She carried paper the way some girls carried dolls. Ruthie wiped the table twice after the crumbs were gone.

At the second wiping she said, still looking at the boards, "He ain't wrong to be scared."

Della stood at the stove scraping baked berries loose from the cobbler dish. "I know."

Ruthie wrung the rag and hung it over the basin edge. "That's not the same as saying he's right."

"No."

Another one of those pauses, full and not empty. The lamp flame whispered in the chimney. Outside, a whippoorwill started up from the locust tree below the yard.

Ruthie said, "I don't care what you tell the county. I don't much care what you tell men like Vernon Wolfe. But don't set that boy against his own house if you can help it."

Boy. Ruthie only called Rafe that when she meant his hurt more than his size.

Della set down the spoon. "He set himself."

"Maybe." Ruthie turned then. Her face looked older in lamplight than it had at the stove, not from years but from carefulness. "Maybe you handed him the nails."

Della had no answer worth making. Ruthie blew out the kitchen lamp and left the last coal-red light to the stove mouth. They went to bed in a house still smelling of berries, grease, and the hard sweet fatigue of one decent meal.

Sleep came thin. Della lay on her side listening to the beams tick and June turn once overhead, listening farther out for the soft irregular noises that told her whether Rafe had come in sober, angry, or not at all. Sometime after the moon had shifted enough to put a pale square on the floorboards, the screen door eased open.

Not a gust. A hand.

Della sat up before the latch settled.

She knew the sounds of her house too well to mistake them. Rafe stepped light when he was leaving for work before dawn. He stepped light the same way when he meant nobody to ask where he was going. A second sound followed: the faint scrape of wood against wall. The shovel handle. Then the smaller metal knock of the lantern lifted off its hook.

Della was out of bed before the thought had finished shaping. The floor held the night's cool in it. She pulled her dress over her shift without lacing it proper, shoved her feet into shoes with no stockings, and felt for her own sweater on the peg by touch. The stove had gone black. In the faint window light she saw the kitchen clear enough to know the shovel was gone and the long iron probe Silas sometimes used for soft ground was gone too, the one Rafe had leaned by the back door after borrowing it to set fence posts.

He had said he would get what he needed without her. A foolish man said things at supper all the time. A desperate one put on boots after midnight and walked to the dead.

She lifted the door bar careful not to wake Ruthie. On the porch the air had cooled but not emptied of the day's heat. Damp sat against her face like a rag. The valley below held its own dark shape, the river not visible from the house yet present in the air as metal and algae and the slow rot the dam had stirred up. Cicadas never quit fully now. They only changed shift with the crickets.

Rafe's lantern moved down the path as a low swinging light, already halfway toward Old Sion.

Della started after him and had gone maybe twenty yards before another shape detached itself from the shadow of the porch post. Smaller. Bareheaded. June.

"Go back," Della hissed.

June stopped because the voice was her grandmother's. Stopping was not the same as obeying. In the moonwash Della could see the hem of the girl's nightdress below Ruthie's old cardigan, one sleeve too long and pushed twice up her arm. She had come barefoot and then thought better of it; she held her shoes in one hand by the laces.

"You heard him leave," Della said.

June nodded once.

"Then you heard me tell you go back."

"He took the shovel."

That was answer enough to what kind of child she was becoming. Not rebellious for the pleasure of it. Exact.

Della could have marched her inside by the wrist. She pictured the delay, the minute or two lost in whisper-fighting on the porch while Rafe reached the cemetery alone with iron and anger. She pictured him putting steel into the fever plot because she had chosen the wrong battle.

"Keep behind me," she said. "And if I tell you down, you get down."

June slipped on her shoes without sitting, heel crushed against leather, and fell in behind.

The path from Cedar Bench to Old Sion was easy enough by day if a person watched the roots and knew which stones marked the washouts. At night it tightened. Branch shadows crossed the ground like laid straps. The red clay held yesterday's damp under its dry skin, and twice Della felt it give greasy underfoot where runoff had cut the path. Her left thumb throbbed from the way she gripped her sweater shut at the throat.

Ahead, Rafe's lantern bobbed lower as he started down the last slope. Old Sion lay on its hill shoulder above the river, the named stones first, orderly enough in the moonlight to look harmless, and below them the older scatter where cedars pushed up through family plots and the fever ground slouched downhill as if even the cemetery had never wanted to admit that section belonged to it.

Della had walked this path more times than she could count: for burials, for births in houses beyond it, for her own quiet visits on days she had needed the dead because the living had grown too loud. Tonight the cemetery seemed to meet her halfway. The limestone gave back the moon in flat pale faces. Cedar shadow swallowed the spaces between.

She heard the iron probe before she saw what Rafe was doing. A dull push, then a thin mineral knock. Push, knock. He had hung the lantern from a low branch and was working the probe into the earth with both hands, testing one patch and then another near the lower edge of the fever plot where the ground sloped uneven under weedy grass.

He knew enough to be dangerous. He had grown up under men who found graves by sink and soil give, by the line an old root took around a box it could not breach. He had watched Silas set stones with a string stretched between pegs. He had carried water for Della and held tools and learned what boys learned while nobody thought they were listening.

"Rafe."

He did not start. That meant he had heard them coming and chosen not to stop. He drove the probe again. This time it hit something softer at depth, a little hollowed give that made Della's stomach drop before the metal glanced aside.

"Rafe."

He stood the iron upright and turned. In the lantern light his face looked older and meaner only because fear had sanded all the rest off it. Sweat had dried white at the collar of his shirt. Bits of grass clung to his knees.

"You brought backup?" he asked, seeing June behind her. "That what this is now, a night school?"

June stayed silent. Della could feel her listening.

"I told you go home after supper," Della said.

"And I told you I wasn't waiting."

He bent and took up the shovel. He had already cut two shallow bites into the ground, not enough to expose anything, more insult than excavation, but the fresh clay showed dark and wet. Della smelled the raw, shut-up earth of it at once. Not grave-deep. Deep enough to wake memory.

"You don't know where you're putting that blade," she said.

"That's the point."

The shovel edge went in again with a soft thock. He levered up a slab of rooted grass and flung it aside. The sound was small. In the night it rang.

Della stepped forward. "You'll collapse the lip and make a liar of whatever's left."

He gave her a hard short grin. "There. That's the most you've said all evening."

"Stop."

"You going to tell me where, then?"

"No."

He laughed once. Not amusement. "Course not."

He planted the shovel and leaned both hands on the handle. "I stood there today and listened to you sort out Sadie Kincaid's dead as neat as seed. I listened to you tell Lucille Holt who belonged to what marriage, which girl had claim, which name would hold in court. You didn't need a night to think. You didn't need God to send you a sign. But I ask you one thing that might keep this house standing and you go dumb on me at your own table."

June made the smallest sound then, a shoe scuff in grass. Rafe's head flicked toward it, then back to Della. He did not lower his voice.

"You know what it's like to have folks watch your wife turn collars because you can't buy shirts? You know what it's like to look at your girl's school shoes and calculate how long cardboard'll hold in the soles? I do. You know what it's like to have a banker talk to you like he's giving you extra life every thirty days? I do. I know what it's like to eat your mother's county wages and pretend that don't sit wrong in a man's gut."

Della looked at the shovel. Its handle was one Rafe had smoothed himself years ago with broken glass and linseed. She knew the notch by his right hand. Even here he could not help working an object till it fit him.

"I know what debt is," she said.

He barked a laugh. "Do you. Then here's the part maybe you forgot. Debt don't just live in ledgers. It lives in a man's back. In how his wife looks at him when she thinks he ain't seeing. In whether he can say we're staying or we're packing for some rented room downriver with the rest of the dispossessed cattle. In whether his girl learns her father is a man that keeps things or just watches them go."

The word rented made June's breathing change. Della heard it and hated that too.

"Take her home," Rafe said. "Then come back if you feel like it. Or don't. I'm done asking pretty."

"I am here," June said.

Rafe shut his eyes once, quick. When he opened them the anger had shifted. Not gone. Shamed at being heard by the wrong ears. "That's what I'm saying. You shouldn't be."

Della stepped between him and the cut place in the ground. The disturbed dirt gave a wet sweet rot smell already. "You put that shovel in again and you won't find certainty. You'll find broken wood, runoff, and trouble you don't know how to hold."

"Then teach me."

"No."

It was the first clean hard word she'd given him and it did nothing but sharpen him.

From the dark above them came a cough. Not the little throat-clearing of a man about to speak polite, but the quarry-deep cough that sounded as if stone grit had lined the lungs and meant to stay.

Silas Wynn stepped out from behind the low tool shed by the upper row, hat in one hand and the other hooked under one suspender strap where a length of wire had replaced the missing brass clasp. Moonlight found the chalk in the seams of his fingers. Even at this hour he smelled of limestone dust, old tobacco, and the horehound drops he sucked when the cough ran him ragged.

"I could hear you fools from the gate," he said. "And him with a shovel." He nodded at Rafe. "That made it worth putting on boots."

Rafe turned, not surprised enough. Maybe he had guessed somebody would hear iron on ground. "Good. Then maybe you'll answer where she won't."

Silas looked at the fresh cuts in the turf and his mouth thinned. "Answer what?"

"Whether that body's Eli. Whether it ain't. Whether she's keeping us poor for the sake of a story."

Della said, "Silas."

It came out quieter than she'd intended, and because of that it carried more.

Silas did not look at her. He was looking at Rafe the way old workmen sometimes looked at younger men about to ruin a thing: pity first, then irritation at having to waste pity. "You don't dig blind in fever ground after midnight. That's answer one."

Rafe kicked the loosened sod aside. "I didn't ask answer one."

"Take it anyway."

"Silas."

This time Della put more warning in it. June was still behind her. The night had drawn tight as baling twine. Even the insects seemed to saw softer.

Rafe took a step closer to the old man. "Everybody in this valley knows you been reading stone longer than I've been alive. You know what went where. You know who got a marker and who got a prayer and who got a wagon ride at dusk because decent folks didn't want the grave talked over. So tell me plain. Is she protecting uncertainty or is she protecting somebody?"

Silas rubbed his thumb over the brim of his hat, where sweat had darkened the felt in a long crescent. His cough stayed under his ribs a second time, held by force. "Boy, you keep asking for plain like plain'll make it cheap."

"I'm not a boy."

"No. That's the trouble."

Rafe's laugh was ugly now. "Then treat me like a man and answer."

Della knew before it happened that silence would fail her here too. It had failed at the supper table because Rafe had filled it with his own need. It failed now because Silas had his own tiredness and no reason left to carry hers. The cemetery held old bargains until it didn't. She had always known that. She had only hoped not tonight.

Silas spat into the weeds away from the graves, wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, and finally looked at Della. In that look there was no betrayal, only exhaustion. He had helped her carry one body downhill almost forty years ago. He had kept his jaw shut through marriages, baptisms, crop failures, and all the years Rafe grew from squalling infant to the man with a shovel. He had earned the right to be tired.

"That ain't Eli," he said.

Rafe's whole body went still around the shovel handle. "Then who?"

Della said, "Enough."

Silas's eyes stayed on hers one more beat, giving her the last chance she had not taken at supper. Then he turned back to Rafe.

"Gabriel Wolfe," he said. "Or what the ground's left of him."

The name crossed the cemetery and changed the air. Della felt it physically, as if a door long swelled shut had been kicked open and old weather rushed through. Gabriel. Even after all these years the syllables carried the kitchen's copper stink, the damp wool of his coat, the scrape of table legs on plank floor when Eli hit him and the world went crooked.

Rafe stared first at Silas, then at Della. He did not look shocked the way she might once have imagined. Shock was too clean. What came into his face was a fast harsh rearranging of pieces that had never fit his mother's silence before and now fit badly in a new way.

"Wolfe," he said.

No one answered.

His laugh this time was breath only. "Of course. Of course that's where it runs."

"Rafe," Della said.

He rounded on her. "So that's it? You'll let my kid learn the shape of rent before you'll put a Wolfe in the dirt under his right name?"

"That isn't what this is."

"Then what is it?" He took one step toward her and the shovel tip punched deeper into the ground by accident. He jerked it free and flung it aside, as if even he heard what that sounded like. "You think I don't know what name means? You think I don't know what money sits under a name like Wolfe? Whole damn county does."

June made another small sound then, not fear exactly. Recognition that she had been right to come because here, finally, was the word the adults had been walking around.

Silas said, "Lower your voice."

Rafe ignored him. "You'd tell truth on a Kincaid because it don't come through this house. You'd cross Vernon in daylight because that costs you pride and not flesh. But when it gets down to your own blood, your own table, then suddenly truth turns delicate."

Della could have lied then. Not well. Not usefully. She could have said Silas was guessing, could have said old men heard names in wind, could have grabbed June by the shoulders and hauled her out of there under sheer maternal authority. She did none of it. Gabriel's name had been spoken aloud over the fever plot, and the cemetery itself seemed to listen for what she would do next.

"What I owe the dead isn't the same as what you want from me," she said.

"That's a clerk's answer."

"It's the answer I've got."

Rafe bent, snatched up the lantern, and yanked it off the branch. Light lurched over the stones, over Silas's stooped shoulders, over June's white face in the dark behind Della where he'd finally seen her proper. For the first time that night anger gave way to something more naked.

"You stay out of graveyards after dark," he said to June.

June held his stare longer than a child should have been able to. "Then don't bring a shovel."

That might have been funny in another house. Here it landed like a thrown pebble on tin.

Rafe looked back at Della. "You kept it from me long enough. I'll find the rest elsewhere."

He turned and went up through the stones with the lantern swinging hard, not toward home at first but toward the upper rows where the Wolfe markers lay, tall and pale and better cut than most of the hill deserved. He moved among them as if names might shake loose under his hands if he touched enough granite.

Silas let out the cough he'd been holding. It tore through him, bent him double. He braced one palm on a marker and spat again, angry at his own lungs. Della stepped toward him out of habit and he held up the hat in his hand without straightening, a rough little warning that he needed the cough finished more than he needed tending.

When it eased, he rasped, "I told you."

"I know what you told me."

"You know a sight of things."

"Not enough, apparently."

He straightened slow. The wire on his suspender caught moonlight. "If he keeps after the past, he'll find it with or without your manners on it."

Della thought of Rafe among the Wolfe stones, reading dates by lantern, piecing rumors like fence rails laid crooked. Thought of Vernon hearing his name troubled before the county had forced any opening. Thought of the little cuts silence made when it finally failed and somebody else took over with blunter tools.

Then she heard breathing too near at her side and remembered June.

The girl had come forward two paces without Della seeing. Up close she still smelled faintly of blackberries and sleep. Her braid had half come loose. One cardigan sleeve hung over her knuckles. She was looking downhill where Rafe had gone, but the question in her face was turned to Della.

"Who is Gabriel?" she asked.

Not loud. Not scared. Exact.

Silas looked at Della once more and then toward the gate. "I'm walking home before my lungs stay behind," he said. "Fill those cuts in. Don't leave the ground talking."

He went up the path slowly, coughing into his fist, boots careful between stones. His presence left a shape in the air when it was gone, same as a lantern carried out of a room left the room darker than it had been before the flame arrived.

June waited.

Della bent and set the loosened sod back into the shovel bites Rafe had cut. The earth was cold under the top crust and damp enough to blacken her nails at once. She pressed the grass down with both palms until the slit looked less like a wound and more like bad footing. It was work she could do while thinking.

At last she said, "A dead man."

June did not move. "I know that part."

Della wiped her hands on her skirt and looked at her granddaughter. Moonlight flattened the childness out of June's face and left only the sharpness. Fifteen. Old enough to hear a name change a room. Old enough to know when adults lied by trimming.

"He's a name tied to trouble," Della said. "That's what you need tonight."

"Trouble for who?"

There it was again. The exact next question.

Della looked past her to the lower fever ground, the dark slope where unmarked burials had always taken the valley's unwanted arithmetic: epidemic dead, babies not christened in time, men buried too fast because the cause of death brought shame or fear or simply hurry. She thought of all the ways a name could move money now. Could move blame. Could lift one household and drown another.

"For this family," she said. "For some others too."

June followed her gaze. "Daddy knew it mattered before you said it."

"Yes."

"Did Mama?"

"No."

June took that in. The silence between them now was different from the one at the supper table. Not a refusal exactly. More like a shelf with a jar set high on it, plainly visible, plainly out of reach.

From upslope came the brief flash of Rafe's lantern again between stones, then darkness as he turned it away. He was not coming back yet.

Della said, "We're going home."

June did not argue. On the walk back she kept close, shoes whispering in the dust, one hand holding the cardigan shut at her chest the way Della had done earlier with her sweater. Halfway up the path June said, "When Daddy talked about rent, he meant us leaving."

Della answered after a moment. "He meant fear."

"They sound alike sometimes."

Della had no correction to offer.

The house sat dark except for the dull red eye of one coal that had lived under ash in the stove. Ruthie had not woken or, if she had, had chosen the mercy of not coming out to the porch with questions. Della sent June upstairs with a look. The girl obeyed and paused at the top step only long enough to say, "You heard that name before."

It was not a question. Della looked at the worn edge of the stair tread and said, "Bed."

June went.

Rafe did not return until near dawn. Della heard him on the porch, slower now, boots heavy with more than walking. He washed at the yard pump before coming in, though there was no reason for it unless he meant to leave the night outside his own door. She stayed in the kitchen shadow and let him pass through without a word. The lantern he carried smelled of hot tin and old oil. When he hung it back on the peg his hand shook once, barely enough to stir the light.

He went upstairs without touching the table.

Della sat down in the dark where the four dollars were still under the saucer from supper, unpaid into anything yet, not feed, not note, not mercy. Under her nails the cemetery earth had dried tight. She scraped at it with the tip of her paring knife and watched the black gather on the blade. It looked like very little. Less than a thumbnail's worth. Less than what one name could do.

Above her, June turned once in bed. The rope mattress complained, then settled. A breath later, through the floorboards and almost too soft to count as speech, Della heard the girl try the word under her breath as if testing the weight of it in her own mouth.

"Gabriel."

Della sat with that until the first bird started and the valley began again.
