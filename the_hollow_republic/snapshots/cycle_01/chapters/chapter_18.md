# Chapter 18

The first week of July had gone hot enough to make La Sante smell of limewash and old piss before noon. By the time Elise reached the prison gate, the stone on the outer wall was already sweating under a film of dust, and the line of visitors had closed up so tightly that every basket handle and shawled shoulder kept brushing her sleeves. A woman ahead of her carried two hard eggs in a handkerchief. Another had a loaf under her arm and a bruise yellowing along the edge of her jaw. Nobody spoke above the level required by the walls. The prison took sound and flattened it.

The registry window stood open to a room no larger than a pantry. The clerk behind it had one ear puffed and folded from an old blow, and he kept scratching at it with the feathered end of his pen between names. He took visiting slips, glanced once, stamped them, and pushed them back with the same indifferent wrist. When Elise gave him hers, his eyes dropped to the line, then moved sideways to a ledger thick enough to stop a bullet. He found the page, wetted his thumb, and turned another.

"Delatour," he said. He pronounced it cleanly, as if accuracy cost him nothing. "No visit."

"I was told today."

"You were told badly, then."

He drew a small printed form from beneath the ledger and wrote on it without hurrying. The nib scratched. A fly kept touching the inkstone and lifting away again. At Elise's back, the line compressed by another inch.

"What does that mean?" she asked.

He sanded the line, tipped the excess onto the floor, and slid the form through the gap. "It means the prisoner expired in the infirmary at four-ten this morning. Pulmonary congestion, according to the doctor. Effects withheld pending review. Next."

The word expired sat on the page like a tradesman's bill settled and filed. Elise looked at the hour first. Four-ten. He had still been alive when the bell struck the quarter before dawn somewhere over the city. She had been asleep in her room on rue du Cherche-Midi with the notebook under her pillow and a chair shoved under the latch because there was nobody left she trusted not to come in.

"I want to see him."

The clerk had already reached for the next basket. "That isn't done."

"I saw him two days ago."

"You don't see him now."

"I want the doctor."

"You want a petition." He tapped the printed form with the pen. "If you're family, state so in writing. If you're not, don't waste the paper."

The woman behind Elise sucked air through her teeth. Somebody farther back asked whether the beef in a parcel would spoil before the turn came. The clerk lifted his head a fraction and called, "Next," over Elise's shoulder with the steady patience of a man who believed that every grief came in the same size once it reached his window.

She stepped aside because the line made her step. There was no ceremony to refuse. A big woman with red knuckles planted her basket where Elise's hands had been and began asking after a son in the remand wing. The clerk scratched at his broken ear and opened the ledger again.

Outside the gate, the heat hit with more force than it had in the queue. The street had the white glare of afternoon coming early, and a cart full of cabbages was backing badly around a watering trough while the driver cursed the horse with flat professional hatred. A boy ran past shouting headlines about a ministerial quarrel. Another was selling a racing sheet. Nobody shouted about the man who had coughed blood in a cell until the prison doctor wrote congestion on a line and the clerk passed the day forward.

Elise stood with the paper in her glove until the sweat from her palm softened it. There was no body for her to look at, no last confession forced into a scene fit for papers, no witness except a ledger and a clerk with an old damaged ear. Lucien had not died under flags or speeches. He had died under inventory. She could almost see the room anyway: the narrow iron bed, the basin furred with lime, the blanket that never dried properly once the lungs started giving way. He had spent months teaching children to read on quarry stone, and the Republic had found a way to reduce him to a time of death and a cause written in four quick words.

She folded the paper once and walked because standing still would have made her visible.

Her room was under the roof of a widow who rented by the week and watered the wine she sold downstairs more shamelessly with each rise in temperature. The stairwell smelled of boiled onions and dust from the carpet runner. In the room itself, the air had not moved since dawn. Elise shut the door, drew the bolt, and put the printed death notice beside the washbasin. Then she took Lucien's notebook from under the pillow and the other paper from the hem she had stitched inside her valise weeks ago, the one she had copied in police shorthand after slipping through Vautrin's office in May.

That second paper was thin as onion skin and filled with numbers nobody outside a bureau was supposed to care about. Three cash disbursements to Jules Ferrand. One advance routed through a tobacconist on rue Saint-Jacques. One reimbursement for lodgings. One bonus marked after event, the kind of phrase Vautrin liked because it let men do murder inside grammar. Elise had copied the dates, the sums, and the initials with the old obedient hand of her trade, then hidden the slip and told herself she was waiting for the right use. There would be no righter use now.

She opened Lucien's notebook.

His entries had never tried to sound noble. That was part of what made them unbearable. He corrected official lies the way other men corrected school exercises: patiently, with exact dates and no appetite for thunder. Nadir Belkacem, compositor, arrested on a delivery run, not foreign unknown. Abel Saint-Clair, nineteen, printer's apprentice, not mastermind. Henri Vasseur, cooper's assistant, arrested in error during a street handoff, not courier chief. Names, wages, kin where known, burial where knowable, and, in the margins, the small stubborn facts that kept a body from being filed under a category and forgotten. Nadir made paper animals for Lucie from spoiled sheets. Abel burned two fingertips on hot type and quoted poems to sound older than hunger. Ferrand had dirty money in him from the day he arrived and treated other men's deaths as a route to supper. Lucien's own name appeared only once, in someone else's cramped later addition, and he had crossed out agitator and written schoolmaster first, then organizer second, as if order still mattered.

Elise sat on the bed with the notebook open over her knees until the ticking from the hall clock reached her through the wall. Publishing any of it would do nothing to the dead. Nadir would stay dead. Abel would stay nineteen forever. Lucien would not sit up because some compositor set his name properly in lead and some newsboy bawled it in the street. Worse, if she let the quarry's whole history out in a single generous gush, the police would follow every name that still breathed and finish what the smoke had started underground. Witness was not confession. Witness required selection. It required her to keep harming the living by omission, only for a different reason than before.

She found a carpenter's pencil in the washstand drawer and drew a line through every page that touched a safe address, a surviving child, a woman who had not yet been forced into a file. The graphite blunted fast on Lucien's hard clear script. When she was done, the pages she could use were mostly the dead and the paid.

By the time she left the room again, the prison notice and Ferrand's payment slip were in her pocket, and the notebook sat under her arm wrapped in a dish towel to keep the sweat from soaking through the cover.

The print shop on rue des Cinq-Diamants had no sign larger than its own grime. Elise knew it from an imprint at the foot of a mutual-aid circular Nadir had once corrected in the quarry, a modest line in small type that said Vignot et Fils though there had not been a son at the cases for years. The front room sold envelopes, prayer cards, and cheap school copybooks. The press worked in back, where the air turned metallic from ink and hot lead. A canary in a wire cage hung near the window and kicked seed husks onto a pile of damp proofs.

Madame Vignot looked up from a stone table with a composing stick in her hand. Her left thumb had been flattened broad at the tip by years of locking formes, and she wore a man's wedding ring on a black ribbon under her blouse because, in the heat, her fingers swelled too much to take metal. When she moved, the ring tapped lightly against her breastbone. She took one look at Elise's black dress, the wrapped notebook, and the expression she had not managed to school back into usefulness, and she set the stick down without welcome.

"We're not taking memorial verses," she said. "The church can have those."

"I haven't brought verses."

"That's a mercy. What have you brought?"

Elise put the towel-wrapped notebook on the table and laid the prison notice beside it. "Names. Dates. A police agent the Prefecture paid and then called an anarchist when he had served his purpose."

Madame Vignot did not reach for the papers. "They call half the city anarchist now. The paid part is the line that matters. Sit down before you fall over and stain something."

Elise remained standing. "Belkacem worked here sometimes."

The printer's face altered by a degree so small another woman might have missed it. "When he was short of bread. He set faster than any man I ever had, and he never stopped complaining about my commas. If you're using him to buy tenderness, you'll leave poorer than you arrived."

"I'm using his name because they wrote foreign agitator over him after they killed him."

Madame Vignot wiped her hands on a rag already black at the center and finally took the notebook. She turned pages with the caution of someone who respected paper more than sentiment. The canary shuffled once, clicked its beak, and fell quiet. After the first two entries she lifted the prison notice, read the time of death, then took the thin shorthand slip and held it close to the window.

"This is your hand?" she asked.

"Yes."

"You learned it in an office."

"I learned it where I learned it."

Madame Vignot snorted once. "That's not an answer, but it will do for now." She read the figures again. "If these dates are true, Ferrand was on police money before the tollhouse."

"He was on police money from the start."

"Can you prove the signature?"

"I can tell you which tobacconist's back room they used, which drawer held the cash box, and which inspector signed with initials because he preferred not to leave the whole of himself on a page."

The printer set the slip down carefully. "That's better. Proof is what survives fear. Stories are what fear leaves behind."

Elise heard the dryness in her own voice before she answered. "Then set the proof."

Madame Vignot looked back into the notebook. "How many living names are buried in here?"

"Enough that I've crossed them out."

"Show me."

Elise turned the pages she had marked in pencil. "The dead, Ferrand's payments, Lucien's death in custody, and what the police flattened into labels. Nothing that gets Clemence Rigal or her child found. Nothing that sends men to the laundry yards or the old front rooms."

"So there are still people worth hunting."

"There are children worth not delivering."

Madame Vignot met her eyes then, properly, with no more pretense that this was a simple commercial errand. "If I set this, my shop gets searched."

"If you don't, they remain what the Prefecture called them."

"Do not try to bribe me with decency, madame. Ink has never paid rent."

Elise felt the first crack in her own discipline open. "No," she said. "It has only kept some men from being called dogs after death."

The printer's gaze stayed on her a beat longer. Then she called through the back partition, "Lucette, mind the front and stop feeding the bird sugar." A girl answered indistinctly from beyond the shelves. Madame Vignot tugged the ring on its ribbon once, an absent habit, and pulled a galley tray toward her.

"I can give you a column on the second page of tomorrow's issue and a broadside if the formes aren't seized before dawn," she said. "I won't print anything I can't check against itself. You'll sit there, you'll read proofs, and you'll tell me every place the police lied with their nouns."

Elise pulled up the stool opposite her. "That's why I came."

Madame Vignot handed her a copy pencil and began setting the title aloud as she chose letters. "Dead of the Ferrand Affair," she muttered. "No. Too neat. The dead named after Ferrand again." She spat the word to one side. "Names the Prefecture Used Badly. That will do."

Type clicked against metal. The sounds had a steadier mercy than prayer. Elise read from the notebook while Madame Vignot composed, not every detail, only the ones a printed line could carry without making a legend out of people who had already paid too much for being made into symbols by other hands.

"Nadir Belkacem," Elise said. "Compositor and translator. Twenty-eight. Arrested the third of June during a delivery run. Reported dead in custody after an alleged escape attempt. Body not returned."

Madame Vignot's flattened thumb set each word into place. "Keep going."

"Abel Saint-Clair. Nineteen. Printer's apprentice. Wasn't an organizer. Burned two fingertips on hot type and couldn't stop quoting verse when he was frightened."

The printer's mouth twitched despite itself. "That sounds like half the boys I have trained."

"It sounded like him."

"Then it goes in."

Line by line, the dead came back to the page at the scale they had actually lived: work, age, kin, one habit, one correction. No halos. No absolution. When they reached Ferrand, Elise gave the dates and sums only. Twenty-five francs. Forty. Forty. Bonus after event. Madame Vignot whistled low through her nose.

"That will sting," she said.

"It won't be enough."

"No," the printer replied. "It never is."

She locked the first column, pulled a proof, and shoved it to Elise with fingers still black at the creases. The sheet came out damp and warm. Elise corrected spellings, restored an accent the type had lost, and struck the word fanatic where Madame Vignot had used it ironically in a subheading.

"I won't help them do it from our side," she said.

Madame Vignot glanced at the correction mark and gave a short approving grunt. "Good. We have enough men who turn the poor into theater without charging admission."

When the first page was clean enough to live, the printer lifted the prison notice again. "You should take this to a daily."

"A daily won't print the dead properly."

"A daily doesn't need to. A daily needs to print the payments and the death before hearing. Respectable men enjoy discovering that officials are dirty. They enjoy the dead less. Use their appetite where it exists." She named the paper without looking up. "Le Courrier du Soir. Ask for Saunier. He hates the Prefecture because they feed their scandals elsewhere first."

Elise folded the proofs into the dish towel with the notebook. "Will he print it?"

"He'll trim it, oil it, and make it fit beside horse racing, but he may print enough. That's as much victory as newsprint usually permits." Madame Vignot pulled another sheet from the press and checked the pressure with her thumb. "Go now. If you wait for justice to be ready, the edition closes."

Le Courrier du Soir occupied three noisy floors near the boulevard, and every one of them smelled of dust, wet wool, tobacco, and the electrical panic of men who thought deadlines made them important. A clerk with sleeve garters tried to stop her at the stairs until she set the prison notice in front of him and said the matter concerned Ferrand. Ferrand's name still opened some doors because it had been in every police-fed paper since the tollhouse. The clerk frowned, then sent her up with reluctance and curiosity fighting visibly in his face.

Saunier's office had glass on one wall and no privacy worth naming. He was a narrow man with a pale forelock that refused pomade and a silver fruit knife in his hand. A saucer of soaked raisins sat by his elbow; he ate them one at a time while he read, as if the doctor who had forbidden wine had not yet managed to forbid appetite. He did not offer her a chair until after he had glanced at the death notice and the shorthand slip.

"You are late," he said.

"He died this morning."

"Men usually do choose their moments badly." He pointed with the knife. "Sit."

Elise sat because the room had become small in a way that reminded her of interview cells and office corners, spaces arranged so a person would give more away than she intended. Below the glass partition, copy boys ran with galleys, and someone in the next room was dictating a fire in Batignolles with the delighted speed disasters gave journalists.

Saunier read the payment dates first. "Where did you get this?"

"From the same place Ferrand got his money."

"That answer is cleverer than useful."

"It's the answer you've got."

He looked up at her properly then, taking in the plain black dress, the careful stillness, the hands that had been scrubbed and still carried ink under the nails. "You've got office habits," he said. "Not clerical. Police or law."

Elise said nothing.

"That's close enough to confirmation." He tapped the death notice. "And Delatour. The schoolmaster from Montrouge. They've been preparing him for court all month."

"They prepared him too long."

Saunier grunted. "Perhaps. Or too efficiently. The public likes either version depending on the weather." He opened the notebook to the marked pages. "What is this, exactly?"

"The names and lives of the people they turned into labels to build the case. The ones who are already dead."

He turned a few pages. "Not the survivors."

"No."

"Then you haven't gone fully mad."

"I'm trying not to finish what they started."

Saunier set the notebook down with more care than he had shown the first papers. "If I print every history here, the column becomes an appeal, and appeals are for funerals and opposition weeklies. My readers want the dirt and the mechanism. They want to know whether a police agent was paid to stand near a bomb and whether the accused schoolmaster died before the examining judge could put him in a neat paragraph."

"They can have those things."

"They can have some of them." He selected another raisin, rolled it once between finger and thumb, then ate it. "Don't mistake me. I'm not turning my paper into an anarchist memorial sheet because a woman with a good hand arrives at three in the afternoon with a wound she won't name. I can give you six inches below the fold, perhaps eight if the Batignolles fire stops at one warehouse. I can ask why Ferrand was drawing unofficial money before the outrage and why Delatour was permitted to die without a hearing. I can mention Belkacem by name if the line stays short. I can't convert my subscribers into honest people by edition time."

Elise heard, with unwelcome clarity, how much she had once counted on men like him: cynical enough to use, vain enough to predict. "I didn't come for honest people."

"What did you come for?"

"A stain," she said. "Something that doesn't wash out by tomorrow's police briefing."

Saunier laughed once, not kindly. "Tomorrow's briefing washes out plenty. By next week they will call this an unfortunate irregularity and print a promotion beside it. But a stain is a realistic ambition." He picked up the fruit knife again and used the tip to nudge the prison notice square with the edge of his desk. "Give me the names of the dead you most need unflattened. One line each. Work, age, the lie corrected. Keep the adjectives out of it. Adjectives let officials recover."

Elise opened the notebook to the marked pages. "Belkacem first."

"Because you knew him best?"

"Because they thought nobody would care if they called him foreign and left it there."

Saunier nodded once and pulled a pad toward him. "Good. That has shape."

She dictated. He cut. She put a fact back in when he cut too close to the bone. He let one line survive about Abel's printer's burns because, he said, readers understood burnt fingertips better than revolutionary theories. He would not print every date. He would print Ferrand's payments in a list because lists looked objective even when they accused. He changed paid provocateur to police informant and then, when Elise said that word made Ferrand sound smaller and cleaner than he had been, changed it again to agent under protection.

"Better," she said.

"For whom?"

"For the dead."

Saunier glanced at her over the edge of the pad. "You don't talk like a lover."

"I'm not here as one."

"No. You are here as something worse for business and better for copy."

When he had the column blocked out, he rang for a copy boy and handed the pages off with instructions written in the cramped brutal hand of the overworked. Then he turned back to Elise.

"I don't pay for unsigned allegations carried in by hand," he said.

"I didn't ask you to."

"I know. That's one reason I'm printing them." He stacked the papers into a clean pile. "If the Prefecture sends a denial, I'll print that too. If they sue, I'll ask where Ferrand's money came from under oath and see who becomes uncomfortable. If nothing else, it may improve a dull Thursday."

He meant it as candor, perhaps even as respect. Elise stood with the notebook under her arm again and felt only the precise size of what could not be won. Vautrin would still go into his office tomorrow. The clerk at the prison window would still scratch at his ruined ear and turn pages. But Ferrand's sums would now exist in more than one hand, and Lucien's death would not belong entirely to the prison register.

"Thank you," she said, because measured politeness was easier than anything truer.

Saunier was already reaching for another raisin. "Do not thank newspapers. Just buy them before the police do."

By the time she got back to her room, the light had gone thick and yellow over the roofs. The hall clock downstairs struck six, then seven. Elise lit the lamp despite the heat because there was no good light left and spread the notebook, a blank school copybook, and the proof from Madame Vignot across the bed.

Lucien's hand had been steadier than hers even in prison. She could see where the cough had taken him in two later entries, the letters smaller, the crossings-out harsher, as if the pen had needed more persuasion than usual. He had not written for effect. That had once annoyed her. Men who wanted to change the world usually liked themselves too much in ink. Lucien had liked precision better. He had given her no speech to carry, only names, dates, work, and the refusal to let the dead be filed incorrectly because the state found vagueness convenient.

She began copying.

The nib caught in cheap paper and spat once where the exercise book's fibers rose. Elise blotted it and kept going. She copied only what she could afford to preserve: the dead, Ferrand's sums, the prison date, the small margins in which Lucien had restored a person's proper scale. Her old training came back without being asked. She made columns. She tightened dates. She wrote legibly enough that another stranger, if needed, could use the book after her. The hand that had once produced neat reports for Vautrin now built a ledger against him. It did not redeem the hand. It only changed the direction of its damage.

At Nadir's name she stopped long enough to remember his habit of standing slightly side-on in a doorway, as if he trusted no room enough to offer it his whole chest. At Abel's she remembered the two burned fingertips and the shabby jacket sleeves always falling short of his wrists. She wrote nineteen without adornment. At Lucien's entry she copied schoolmaster, organizer, captured during quarry evacuation, dead in custody before hearing. Her pen hovered after hearing. There was room for more. Not innocence. He had refused that simplification himself. Not martyr either. At last she wrote taught children in the quarry schoolroom and left it there. The line felt small. Small was often the correct size.

The room kept shrinking as the night deepened. Heat stuck her shift to the middle of her back. Ink dried on the side of her hand where it rested against the page. Twice she rose to pour water from the ewer and found it already warm. In the courtyard below, the widow's lodgers argued over cards until somebody lost enough money to overturn a chair. None of it altered the task. By midnight she had half the book. By two she had all the names she meant to keep and a blister starting where the pen pressed against her finger.

Before she slept, or something near sleep, she opened the flyleaf of the copybook. The page waited clean. She set the pen down, picked up the little knife she used for thread, and scraped away a droplet of dried ink near the top margin. The blade roughened the paper. She had not yet written a name there. She found she could not.

At dawn the city felt stunned for almost five minutes. Then carts began again.

Elise bought both papers from different boys on different corners. Madame Vignot's sheet had set the dead on the second page exactly as promised, under the title Names the Prefecture Used Badly. The type was a hair tight in the middle column where the press had bitten too deep, but the facts held. Nadir existed there now as more than a police category. Abel existed as nineteen and apprenticed and burned at the fingers before he was ever blown into legend. Lucien existed as schoolmaster and dead before hearing. Ferrand's payments ran in a list so plain they looked almost modest, which made them filthier.

Le Courrier du Soir had done what Saunier said it would. The column sat below the fold on page three under the headline Questions in the Ferrand Affair. The sums were there. Lucien's death in custody before appearance was there. Belkacem received one line, not enough but more than none. Vautrin's name did not appear. The Prefecture was granted the dignity of abstraction. By noon, Elise suspected, half the edition would be wrapping fruit.

A man in shirtsleeves reading the daily beside a cafe awning barked a laugh through his nose. "Police pay thieves and call it surveillance," he said to no one in particular.

His companion shrugged. "Everyone pays thieves."

Then they turned to horse results.

Elise folded the papers and went east to Charenton.

The road and rail beyond the city shimmered in the heat. At the asylum accounts office, flies worried the corners of the window screens and the clerk kept a peppermint in her cheek that made her words faintly medicinal. Mademoiselle Perrin had a serialized romance tucked face-down beside the ledger, its cover bent soft from rereading, and a tiny burn mark at the cuff where she had once set her sleeve too close to a lamp. She recognized Elise, not warmly, but with the resigned efficiency that long arrears taught.

"You are here about Madame Valette," she said, pulling the ledger closer. "There are still three hundred twelve francs outstanding."

Elise put the money on the desk in counted stacks. Two ten-franc pieces, one five, three ones, then the loose silver to make the rest. "This is for one month."

Mademoiselle Perrin did not touch the coins at once. "Only one."

"It's what I've got for the account."

"It doesn't reduce the arrears."

"I know."

The clerk gathered the money with careful fingers, as if each coin might still argue back. "This carries maintenance through the first week of August. After that, the account reverts to notice. You understand me."

"Yes."

"You've understood me very regularly for a year and a half," Mademoiselle Perrin said, not unkindly. "It hasn't improved the ledger."

She wrote the receipt and blew on the ink. Elise watched the date appear, watched the amount, watched the small official hand convert almost all her remaining money into four more weeks during which her mother would not be put under immediate additional pressure from an office. Four weeks. Less than Ferrand had cost Vautrin some months. Less than a column inch in the wrong paper.

"Will you see her?" the clerk asked.

"Yes."

The women's yard lay behind two doors and a strip of corridor that smelled of cabbage, carbolic, and the damp cloth never quite wrung dry from institutional mopping. The shade plane of the wall had moved enough to gather three patients onto one bench and leave the rest in the glare. Elise found her mother near the far end, sitting with her back straight despite the heat and sorting blue threads into a neat little coil on her lap. She had always saved threads, even when Elise was a girl, winding the good lengths around a card because any color might mend something later. Here the threads came from blanket hems, apron seams, the frayed edge of a ward towel. Her mother smoothed each piece between finger and thumb before adding it to the coil, as if order could still be made by patient salvage.

Her hair had gone whiter in the last months, not gracefully but in uneven chalky streaks that showed where the comb had failed. When Elise sat beside her, she glanced once at the black dress, then at the cuff.

"You still turn hems too narrow," her mother said. "They pull when you wash them."

It was not greeting and not quite recognition either, but it was close enough that Elise felt it under the ribs.

"I haven't washed this one myself."

"You shouldn't pay for bad work, then." Her mother held up one of the blue threads to the light. "This is decent. Most of them are rubbish now. They mix in too much waste."

Elise looked at the coil. "What are you making?"

"A ring for nothing. A cord for nothing. Hands don't sit quietly because doctors prefer it." Her mother wound the thread once around her forefinger, unwound it, and began again. "You're thinner."

"The weather makes everyone look poor."

"No. Poverty does that." Her mother gave the thread another smoothing pass. "Did the landlady raise the soap again?"

Elise almost laughed, not from ease but from the accuracy of the wound. Her mother had always asked after the price before the feeling. "Something always went up," she said.

"It always does." The thread broke at a weak place. Her mother examined the short end with serious disappointment, then tucked both pieces into her apron pocket rather than throw them away. "You should eat more eggs. Your face looks pinched around the mouth."

"I'll see what eggs cost."

"Too much, probably." For a moment her mother's gaze cleared further. She touched Elise's sleeve with two fingers, the old seamstress touch, measuring cloth and daughter together. "Don't let men tread on your good shoes in the train. They never look down."

The sentence hit so precisely that Elise had to stare at the yard gravel for a second before answering. "I'll watch my feet."

"Good." Her mother settled back on the bench and looked toward the wall. "Blue wears better than black, you know. Black shows soap badly."

There was no use saying she had not come to discuss dresses. There was no use saying she had come with one month's safety purchased and no second month anywhere in reach. The ward woman pacing near the gate muttered numbers to herself. A nurse crossed the yard carrying folded sheets. Somewhere inside, another patient had begun to sing the first half of a hymn and forgotten the rest. Elise sat beside her mother until the bench warmed through the fabric of her skirt and the coil of blue thread grew by three careful lengths.

When she rose, her mother did not ask when she would return. She only said, "If you find a proper blue, not this washed-out kind, keep it. There is always a seam later."

Elise bent and kissed her dry temple. The skin smelled of starch, old sweat, and the lavender water the asylum used on visiting days to pretend that the place was less brutal than it was.

"I will," she said, and hated the lie for being necessary.

Back in Paris, the day had thickened into evening haze. The city looked tired rather than changed. Men still leaned over zinc counters. Washerwomen still haggled over starch. A patrol wagon rattled past as if no paper had printed anything inconvenient. On a rubbish heap beside a fruit stall, a torn sheet of Le Courrier du Soir was already darkening under peach juice. Saunier's six inches were doing the work six inches could do, no more. Madame Vignot's weekly might be seized by nightfall. Vautrin would arrive at his office tomorrow to some muttering and a request for explanation, then remain where he was because bureaucracies disliked surrendering their own evidence of appetite.

The copybook felt heavier than its actual paper when Elise carried it up the station steps. She had one small valise, the notebook copied into her own hand, the asylum receipt folded into the inside pocket, and eight francs left after the ticket and a heel of bread. The departure board meant little; any direction away from the city would serve for the first stage. She chose a third-class train with room enough to vanish among commercial travelers, widows, and men looking for day wages elsewhere.

At the ticket window, the clerk barely glanced at her. The transaction required coins, not a soul. That, more than anything, finished the old fantasy that a correct name might save or condemn her in clean proportion. Elise Valette belonged to police files and overdue ledgers. Jeanne Morel belonged to the quarry, to the rooms under stone, to the woman who had arrived in black with a borrowed widowhood and let a courier vanish so she could get in. Neither name was usable whole. One was dangerous, and the other was filthy from service.

She took her seat by the carriage wall, where the wood was warm from the last passenger's back. Across from her, a man slept sitting up with his hat over his eyes. Beside him, a woman unwrapped cold potatoes from newspaper and fed one to a child who kept leaning toward the window for air. The compartment smelled of onions, wool, warm iron, and the stale dust of other departures. No one asked questions. No one cared enough.

Before the train pulled out, Elise opened the copybook on her lap. The flyleaf still waited blank and rough where the knife had worried a speck of dried ink in the night. She took up the pen once, held it there, and then set it back in the spine without writing. The names inside were clear. That had to count for more than the one on the cover.

The station jolted, then slid. Paris began to move away in yards, walls, chimneys, wash lines, and the backs of workshops. Nothing in the city opened for her. Nothing forgave her. She had bought her mother four more weeks, set Ferrand's sums in two kinds of type, and carried the dead into another hand because Lucien could no longer keep them there himself. It was not rescue. It was not innocence. It was the only service left that did not belong to the police.

By the time the train cleared the outer freight sidings, the copybook on her knees still bore no name at all. That blankness was not freedom. It was only the cleanest form exile had found for her.
