# Chapter 5

The paper in Elise's cuff had gone soft from rain and wrist sweat by the time she came back through the wine shop cellar.

She had found it where Vautrin liked to leave such things, folded under a chipped saucer near the privy sink, as if an order could pass for refuse if it lay beside enough dirt. The hand was not his. He never wasted his own. But the style was his down to the bones: NAMES. MEANS. PLACE. BEFORE THURSDAY NIGHT. DO NOT BRING ME PANIC INSTEAD OF FACT.

Aboveground the gutters on rue de la Tombe-Issoire were running brown, and the men pasting notices for the May Day marches had already been cursed twice by the police before noon. Belowground the quarry kept its own weather. Damp climbed the walls. Water clicked somewhere deeper in the chalk. The air smelled of lamp oil, wet wool, old stone, and the cabbage soup Clemence had stretched too far at supper.

Voices were carrying from the schoolroom before Elise had reached the last turn.

Not Lucien's voice. His never carried unless he meant it to. This one came loose and loud and sure of itself, a voice that wanted a wall to hit and a room to take. Ferrand.

Elise went in with the market basket on her arm and saw at once that the room had already been claimed.

Lucien's crate had been dragged near the wall where he taught the children their letters. Ferrand leaned against it as if it belonged to him, dark moustache damp at the edges, cheap cologne failing to smother the stale sweat in his collar. His satchel rested by his boot. The leather looked decent until one noticed the cracked strap and the buckle repaired with string. Abel stood half a pace from him, too intent to hide it, his narrow coat riding above his wrists and the printer's burns on two fingertips bright as little dead moons when he spread his hand to argue. Nadir had not sat down at all. He stayed side-on in the print-room doorway with a composing stick still in his pocket and a paper crane folded from spoiled proof perched on his knuckle like a joke he had not decided to make. Clemence was darning a stocking without looking at it, stabbing the needle through wool hard enough to punish somebody. Lucie had been sent to bed or should have been, but she was on the bench by the wall with one braid half-undone and chalk dust on both knees, copying capital letters with the tip of her finger into a patch of limestone flour.

Lucien looked more tired than the hour deserved. The damp had gone into his cough again. There was chalk on the cuff of his coat and a rent at the elbow he had not had yesterday. He lifted his head when Elise came in, took in the basket, and gave her the briefest nod toward the bench by the lamp.

"Jeanne. You may as well hear the rest of it," he said.

"Then hear it properly," Ferrand said before Elise could answer. "I'm tired of saying the same thing to people who flinch at their own shadow. The city will be thick with workers on Thursday. Thick with police too, yes, but that's the point. Noise, crush, horses, whistles, everybody looking the wrong way. If a thing is going to be done, that's when it's done."

Clemence pulled the stocking tighter over her fist. "If a thing is going to be done, you can name the thing before you start enjoying it."

Ferrand spread his hands. The grin on him said camaraderie. The eyes did not bother with the disguise. "A telegraph junction. A patrol wagon. A review stand if they build one stupid enough. Something that teaches them a march is not a parish outing."

Lucie looked up from the floor. "What's a review stand?"

"A thing that can mind its own business," Clemence said. "Off that bench. Go on."

Lucie did not move. She had her mother's fast face, all alertness and no patience for adult theater. "Will it blow the wall if he brings it here?"

"Nobody is blowing any wall," Lucien said.

Ferrand laughed. "There. That's exactly the trouble. Nobody is blowing any wall, nobody is breaking any gate, nobody is making the bastards feel the week in their bones. We print, we whisper, we pass a hat for rent, and the Prefecture sleeps like a baby."

Nadir flicked the paper crane once so that it turned and settled again. "The Prefecture has cells full of men who did not find it sleepy."

"Cells full of men who got taken one by one," Ferrand shot back. "That's what comes of being worthy. They pick you off tidy as laundry off a line."

Clemence's needle stopped. "If you've got something to say about laundry, you can earn the right with your own shirts first."

Abel smiled at that, quick and unwilling, then checked himself because Ferrand was watching. Elise set down the basket and kept her face as dull as the widow's should have been. The note in her cuff seemed to warm against her skin. Names. Means. Place. Vautrin wanted a usable account before the city filled with bodies and banners. What he wanted from her was shape. Not rumor. Not fear. Something the newspapers could be fed.

Ferrand was trying to give him exactly that.

Or trying to give the room the idea of it.

Elise sat on the bench by Lucie. The child smelled of chalk and onion skin. Someone had mended one of her stockings with thread too dark to match. Elise kept her hands still in her lap and looked at Ferrand as if she were only another frightened listener.

"You say telegraph junction like it's a loaf," Lucien said. "Which junction. With what powder. For what after. Who is fed by it, who is housed by it, who gets out when the district is closed and every rooming house searched. You speak as if consequence were a door that only opens one way."

Ferrand gave him a patient look so false it almost had polish. "Always the schoolmaster's questions. With enough powder, any junction. With enough nerve, any after."

"With enough boys, you mean," Nadir said. "Boys who can be buried cheap."

Abel flushed at once. "I'm not a boy."

"No," Clemence said. "You're a meal and a pair of boots I haven't finished saving from ruin."

Ferrand bent, scooped up his satchel, and dropped it onto the crate with a weight that made Lucie flinch. "Would you all like proof that I'm not talking out of my arse?"

Lucien's voice changed then. It did not rise. It went flatter, which was worse. "No."

Ferrand had already opened the flap.

Inside, under a rag and two folded sheets, lay a coil of green fuse wire, a paper-wrapped cylinder the length of Elise's hand, and a pistol so small it looked mean rather than impressive, a pocket thing for a close hallway and one frightened face. Abel stared at it with the pure hunger of the young, not for blood exactly but for the clean hard glamour of objects that decide a life in a second. Lucie's mouth opened. Clemence was on her feet before the child could speak.

"Are you out of your fucking mind?" she said.

Ferrand held up his free hand. "Easy. It's safe enough sitting still."

"So is a stove until a child falls on it. Shut that bag."

Lucien had not moved from his chair. Only his left hand had gone white on the edge of the table. "You'll take that out of this room."

"This room is why I'm here," Ferrand said. "You think I like carrying merchandise for the pleasure of it? The law is already written. The papers already call every one of us bomb-men. If they're going to make a story from our names, at least let it cost them."

Lucien coughed once, caught it back, and said, "The law is written. That part is true. Which is why I won't help them finish the paragraph."

Ferrand snorted. "By feeding widows? By teaching letters on a chalk wall while they widen the prison yard? Come off it."

"By keeping people alive long enough to outlast men's appetite for panic," Lucien said. "By making sure the women thrown from one room have another room. By printing bail appeals. By teaching a child to read the notice that cheats her mother. You call that small because it does not throw sparks. The state does not call it small. That's why it keeps sending men after us."

Abel had heard the speech before in different pieces. Elise saw the moment it failed him anyway. He shifted his weight, jaw working, eyes on the pistol again. He wanted Lucien to sound older and harder than the room had made him. He wanted Ferrand's cheap promises to be beneath him. But he was nineteen and tired of setting type for appeals nobody answered.

"And if outlasting isn't enough?" Abel said. "If all we do is make ourselves easier to count?"

Lucien turned to him. "Then we still have not improved matters by helping them slaughter a crowd."

"Who said crowd?" Ferrand asked lightly.

Nadir took the answer before anyone else could. "You did, when you said Thursday was the point."

Ferrand flashed his teeth. "No, friend, I said Thursday was cover. There's a difference."

"A difference visible from a hanging rope, perhaps."

Abel rounded on Nadir. "You always do that. You say one cutting thing and stand back as if you've built an argument."

"If I wanted to build an argument, I would ask him what charge he plans, what distance, what route out, and who taught him enough chemistry to keep from blowing his own hand off first."

Ferrand lifted the paper-wrapped cylinder. "You don't need chemistry to frighten a horse."

"No," Clemence said. "You need a street full of workers and one panic and then I'm washing brains out of coats till Pentecost."

Elise felt chalk grit under her palm from the bench. The room had narrowed around the satchel. It was not the bomb itself that did it, not yet. It was the way every eye had found its own use for the thing. Ferrand saw leverage. Abel saw vocation. Clemence saw blood in hems and on cuffs. Lucien saw the state with a gift in its hand. Lucie saw only the size of the blast against the wall where she practiced her letters. Elise saw Vautrin's clean cuffs, the neat office on the Ile de la Cite, the satisfaction with which he would slide an object like that across his desk and call it inevitability.

She made herself speak in the tone Jeanne Morel had built for the room: useful, careful, no edge exposed.

"If there is already talk outside," she said, "could it be police talk first? A rumor they want men to lean toward?"

Ferrand looked at her with quick interest. "You think they need to invent us?"

"I think they like saving themselves trouble."

"There," Lucien said softly. "There is the question."

Ferrand closed the satchel with one slap, but too late. The room had seen enough. "You all think like clerks. Manufactured, not manufactured, rumor, counter-rumor. While you sort your little drawers, the police sort theirs. They don't care whether the first whisper came from them or from us. They care whether, by Friday, the city remembers fear."

"The city remembers hunger more faithfully than fear," Lucien said. "Fear turns into headlines. Hunger sits down at your table."

"Only if you've got a table."

"Then build one."

Ferrand laughed outright. "With what wood?"

"With time," Lucien said. "With labor. With favors owed and repaid. With rooms that stay open. With women who know which landlord will wait three days. With men who can read a warrant. With a printer who works fast and keeps his mouth shut. That's how poor people survive the Republic."

He said it without grandeur. That was what gave it force. There was chalk on his sleeve, a cough he could not hide much longer, and no romance in the shape of him at all. Elise felt the room move a little toward him, not with applause, not with relief, but the way a bench settles back when the heavier body leaves it.

Ferrand saw it too. He moved at once, charm turning oily with urgency.

"Survive?" he said. "That's a priest's word for people too beaten to answer back. What do they remember now, Lucien? Tell the truth. They remember Vaillant because he made their deputies hear the noise in their own bones. They remember Henry because rich men finally understood a cafe could belong to anybody's grief. They don't remember the committees that passed the hat afterward."

Lucie whispered, mostly to herself, "I remember the hat."

Clemence's face changed for one second, as if someone had put a thumb into a bruise. The hat was a real hat, Elise knew, the one that had gone around after Lucie's father died under a cart axle and bought them two months in their room before the landlord started circling again. Ferrand had not known. That was the measure of him.

Lucien knew it too. He glanced at Lucie, then back at Ferrand. "Exactly," he said.

Abel stepped in before the silence could settle against Ferrand. "That's not fair."

Nobody answered. That made him speak faster.

"It's not fair to use her for this. We all know what he means. A hat keeps a room for a month. A pamphlet buys a day if it sells. Then what? Another room lost. Another arrest. Another speech about patience while the men with clubs write the future for us." His hands were shaking a little, whether from anger or the nearness of the satchel Elise could not tell. He hid it by grabbing the back of the crate. "Ferrand's right about one thing. They already call us monsters. They already search on rumor. They already make examples where there is nothing to prove. So what are we preserving? Respectability? It hasn't done much for us."

Lucien let him finish. That was his mistake, or perhaps his only chance. The room leaned toward the younger man now because youth did that to rooms, especially when youth was willing to burn itself where older people had learned the exact price of fire.

"We're preserving each other," Lucien said.

Abel's laugh came out raw. "That's a phrase, not an answer."

"It's the only answer that gets a second day."

"No," Abel said, and now he was looking straight at Ferrand as if the older man's approval had already become a kind of heat. "No. It's what people say after they've forgotten how to strike. You keep asking for names and routes and who gets out after. Fine. Ask me then. If there is a chance to hit them and not just beg to remain in one piece, I'm with him."

The words landed harder than the satchel had.

Clemence set the stocking down. "Abel."

But he was past hearing his own name in her mouth. Shame had made him brighter. Elise had seen boys do it in the cells beneath the Prefecture, talk themselves into courage because the quieter thing would be to admit they were frightened and small.

"I mean it," he said. "If hands are needed, mine are there."

Lucien's face did not break. Only the pause after Abel's last word showed how far it had gone in. He looked not at Ferrand but at the burns on Abel's fingers, at the wrists too thin for the coat, at the boy he had been trying to tutor past hunger and vanity both.

"Then your hands are promised to foolishness," he said. "And I'll say so in front of you, not behind your back."

Ferrand's grin vanished. Contempt showed clean at last. "Always this. If a younger man wants more than old men's caution, he must be vain, must be foolish, must be handled like crockery. Perhaps that's why he's tired of your school."

Nadir pushed off the doorway. The paper crane dropped to the floor between his boots. "Careful," he said. "You're beginning to sound as if you need him stupid."

For one second Elise thought Ferrand might swing at him. The badly healed knuckles on his right hand twitched. Instead he laughed again, shorter this time.

"I need nothing from any man who has not offered it," he said. "But I won't apologize for wanting spine in a room full of menders."

"Menders keep people alive," Clemence said. "And if you ever bring powder in here again, I'll put you out myself, spine or no spine."

Ferrand tipped two fingers to his brow in a mock salute. "Then I stand corrected. The republic will tremble before your wash paddle."

It was Lucien who stood then, finally. Not quickly. He set both palms on the table and rose with the care of a man whose lungs had begun bargaining with him. When he was upright the room altered again. Even Ferrand felt it enough to shut his mouth.

"Listen to me," Lucien said.

He did not raise his voice. He never had to. Carriage thunder passed somewhere overhead, dull through the stone like distant guns under mud.

"The state wants a pageant," he said. "It wants one broken body in the right street so it can call every hungry man a conspirator and every poor woman who lets him through the door an accomplice. It wants children in the papers as proof that corruption runs under the city like sewage. It wants panic because panic is cheap. We're expensive. Bread is expensive. Hiding men is expensive. Teaching a child her letters when half the district says she ought to go blind and obedient into the wash is expensive. That's why I spend myself there."

He put a hand flat on the chalk-marked wall beside Lucie's half-finished letters.

"If any man here wants to die in a blast, I can't stop him once he leaves my sight. But he won't call it strategy in this room. He won't call it care. He won't borrow the hunger of the people who sleep here and spend it on spectacle. Not while I'm standing."

Silence followed, but not agreement. Ferrand's face had gone still in the ugly way of a man who has misjudged how much charm the room would forgive. Abel stared at the wall rather than at Lucien. Nadir bent and picked up the paper crane, smoothed one wing with his thumb, and tucked it into his pocket. Clemence drew Lucie to her by the shoulder, needle still threaded between her teeth.

Elise knew then what she had come to know.

The threat was real in the sense that there was powder in the satchel and a boy willing to carry it. But it was being made in front of her too, coaxed and flattered into shape by Ferrand's appetite for a clean dramatic answer. There had not been a plot waiting in the quarry like a loaded gun. There was a room full of tired people, two children asleep in the next gallery, coal tabs unpaid, a cough that would worsen before summer, and one man trying to turn all of it into a headline with a fuse attached.

Ferrand broke the silence first because men like him always did.

"Fine," he said. "Keep your room pure. I know where to find volunteers who don't need blessing before they act."

Abel looked up at once. Lucien saw it. So did Elise.

"You won't recruit in my refuge," Lucien said.

"Then perhaps your refuge is smaller than you think."

He snatched up the satchel. For a moment Elise thought he would leave. Instead he swung the bag onto his shoulder and smiled directly at Abel, not even pretending subtlety now.

"Come have a cigarette later if you like," he said. "You can tell me whether you want your hands wasted on type for the rest of your life."

Abel did not answer. That was answer enough.

Ferrand ducked out through the narrow passage, carrying his cologne and his bad idea with him. The air in the schoolroom seemed dirtier after he had gone. Lucien coughed at last, a deep tearing thing he had held off too long. Clemence swore under her breath and shoved the mug of cold chicory toward him. Nadir went to the lamp and turned the wick lower as if less light might calm the room.

Lucie, who had hidden her face in Clemence's skirt for exactly three heartbeats and no more, said, "Will Thursday be bad?"

Clemence smoothed the child's braid with the same rough hand she used to knead bread. "Thursday will be Thursday. Hold still."

Lucien drank the chicory, grimaced, and wiped his mouth. "Thursday will be crowded," he said to Lucie. "That's different from bad."

"My shoe leaks in crowds."

That made Nadir laugh once, quietly, because it was the truest thing anyone had said all evening. Even Lucien's mouth moved. Abel did not laugh. He stood in the passage Ferrand had taken, caught between shame and longing so nakedly that Elise had to look away from him to keep from showing she saw.

Clemence handed Lucie the basket of onions. "Take those to the ration room and don't drop them."

Lucie made a face. "They smell."

"That's how you know they're ours."

The child went, carrying the basket against her narrow ribs. Elise rose before she had quite decided to. "I'll help her."

Clemence nodded without interest. She was watching Abel now.

The ration room lay beyond the sleeping gallery in a low chamber where shelves had been hacked straight into the limestone. Bread sat under cloths. Soap bars, wrapped in old newsprint, were stacked beside lamp oil and sacks of flour. The air held a permanent arithmetic: starch, grease, damp burlap, the mouse-sharp smell of stores counted too often because there were never enough of them. Lucie set down the basket and immediately began sorting onions by size as if she had invented the job.

"This one is rotten," she said.

"That one is half rotten," Elise said. "That's a different luxury."

Lucie gave her a suspicious look, then decided the line was worth keeping. "Half rotten," she repeated, pleased. "Like Ferrand's moustache."

Elise almost smiled. "Did you notice that yourself?"

"I notice lots of things. Mama says not to say them all."

"That's wise of her."

Lucie held up an onion the size of her fist. "Will there be marchers with red flowers?"

"Probably."

"Do they get to keep them if the police hit them?"

Elise thought of Vautrin's note, the way he had reduced a city to names and means and place. She thought of the satchel on Ferrand's knee, Abel's face turned toward it as if toward a lit stage.

"Sometimes," she said. "Go wash your hands. You've got onion on them now."

Lucie went with exaggerated martyrdom, muttering to herself about onions and Thursdays. When the chamber was hers alone, Elise reached for the household ledger out of habit more than plan. Clemence kept it on the middle shelf between the lamp oil and the beans, wrapped in a square of apron cloth. The book was there, blunt and swollen, its cover dark from old kitchen steam.

But another book sat behind it, narrower, brown-backed, the sort stationers sold cheap to men who wanted accounts to look respectable.

Elise drew it out.

At once she saw it was not Clemence's. Clemence wrote like she chopped roots: straight down, no ornament, numbers larger than words. This book held several hands. Nadir's neat compositor's script listed paper stock, ink, a hidden bundle of pamphlets above a cooper's shed on rue d'Alesia. Clemence's figures marked soap, beans, bread, lamp oil, one child's winter shawl paid in two parts. Then the later pages changed. The handwriting loosened, swaggering even in ink, letters tilting as if drunk. A different hand had added items the others had never needed to name together. Fuse, two lengths. Caps, six. Pistol, pocket. Powder parcel at Plantes cupboard. Spare room key, cabaret back stair. Thursday penciled twice in the margin and scratched over once, as if secrecy could be improved by indecision.

It was not a bomb plot written cleanly out. It was worse. It was the quarry's ordinary furtive life, the hidden bread and oil and forged papers that kept people housed, cut through now by Ferrand's additions like dirty water entering milk.

Elise turned one more page.

There, in the same loose hand, was a line that made the back of her neck go cold for reasons the quarry itself supplied. Not fear in the abstract. Not some floating pulse. The ledger edge bit her thumb. Damp from the wall found the opening at her collar. Somewhere nearby a drip hit stone with patient, counting regularity.

For review route.

No place named. No target either. Just the phrase, smug in its vagueness, as if any public crush could be enough if it gave the police something to print.

Vautrin could make a case from this book in an hour.

He could raid every cache named in the earlier pages, lift women for storing soap and blankets, take the cooper, the cabaret girl with the back stair key, whatever old printer had agreed to hide paper under his floorboards. He could point to the fuse and the pistol and tell a magistrate the rest was context. He would not be wrong by the standards that paid him.

And Lucien, if shown the book tonight, would see at last how far Ferrand had already put his hands into the quarry's life.

That should have made the decision simple. It did not.

The ledger was proof against the refuge, yes, but it was proof for Lucien too. Proof that Ferrand's talk had not been merely talk. Proof that Abel had been courted with real things. If Elise removed it from the room for Vautrin, she delivered the quarry's throat into the state's hand. If she put it in Lucien's palm now, she started a war inside the refuge before anyone was ready to win it. Abel would lie. Ferrand would vanish. Clemence would count losses while the police closed in. And if the wrong person saw Elise carrying it between chambers, her usefulness down here would shrink to nothing before dawn.

The twenty-eight francs for Charenton came to her with contemptible speed. One book against twenty-eight francs a month. Against her mother's blankets. Against the thin broth the attendants skimmed less when payments arrived on time. Vautrin had never needed to shout. He only had to keep accounts.

Footsteps sounded in the sleeping gallery.

Elise slid the brown ledger under her apron just as Lucie came back in drying her hands on her skirt.

"Mama says where are the small onions," the girl announced.

"In the basket."

"Not those. The smaller small ones."

Elise pointed. Lucie dug through the pile, found three the size of plums, and held them up in triumph. "These are for flavor only."

"That's a sentence Lucien taught you."

"He says flavor matters even when there isn't enough."

The girl's pride in repeating it made something mean and practical in Elise recoil. A child should not have to defend the existence of flavor. Not in a cellar, not to the state, not to a ledger full of hidden bread and bomb caps written in the same ink.

She said, "Take them to your mother."

When Lucie had gone, Elise waited long enough to hear Clemence answer from the schoolroom. Then she wrapped the narrow ledger in the apron cloth from the household book and slipped out the back way, not toward the entrance but deeper, where the quarry narrowed and old work marks survived on the walls like ghosts of arithmetic.

She knew a dry pocket three turns beyond the disused cistern. Lucien had once sent her there for kindling and told her to mind the floor where the stone broke sharp under the dust. It was not on the household routes children used. It was not quite on the map she had memorized either, only on the quarry itself, which was safer.

Her lamp made the chalk flare white and then yellow. Water ticked somewhere behind the wall. At one bend she had to turn sideways to pass, ledger pressed against her ribs, the stone grazing the burn scar inside her wrist through the damp sleeve. The quarry had a way of forcing every choice into the body. There was no clean thinking in it. A person carried what she meant to hide. A person scraped herself doing it.

Voices came from ahead.

Elise put out the lamp with her fingers and flattened herself into the dark recess before she had finished deciding.

Ferrand first. She knew him now by his easy breathing, his confidence in spaces he had not earned. Abel with him, quicker breath, boot sole catching once on loose grit and then pretending it had not.

"I'm not saying tomorrow," Ferrand was murmuring. "I'm saying you don't let an old man make a virtue of delay till the whole city has forgotten your dead."

"He's not old."

"He's old where it counts."

Abel said nothing.

Ferrand went on, voice lower, coaxing. "You saw their faces. Half the room's afraid of a noise before it's even made. Fine. Let them sew and stir soup. But if you want to matter, if you're tired of setting righteous little notices while they drag men off anyway, come have a look at the proper tools. You've got good hands. Quick hands."

"I know my hands."

"Then prove it to yourself."

They passed so near Elise could smell the cheap cologne again, sweet over the rank damp of the passage. She kept still until the sound of them thinned into the quarry's breathing. Only then did she relight the lamp. The match shook once against the box, not from some floating terror but because limestone grit had worked into the sweat on her fingers and made the wood slip.

The niche stood where she remembered, shoulder-high in the wall, a cut made long ago for a work lamp or a saint nobody had bothered to keep holy. A slab of broken limestone leaned against it. Elise set down her lamp, shifted the slab, and felt cold chalk mud under her nails.

She could still turn back.

She could take the ledger to the dead drop before dawn and buy herself another month of usefulness, another month of her mother's bed kept under her. She could bring it to Lucien and watch the room split open before Ferrand had time to bury the evidence. She could even burn it, though that would be the stupidest version of mercy, proof lost to everybody and danger left loose in men's pockets.

Instead she slid the wrapped ledger into the niche until her fingers could barely catch the cloth. She pushed it farther with two knuckles. The stone drank the sound. Then she set the slab back in place and rubbed chalk over the cleaner edge until it looked as old as the wall.

When she straightened, lamp smoke moved across the passage in a thin black seam.

That was the moment it became something more than omission.

Until now she had left things out. She had thinned reports, softened edges, kept Clemence and the child off paper because the alternative had turned her stomach. This was different. Vautrin had asked for fact, and she had put her hand directly over it. Not because the quarry was innocent. It was not. There was powder now, and a pistol, and a boy being led toward both by the need to feel large in history before history crushed him flat. Not because Lucien was right in every particular either. The refuge had its vanity as well as its care. But because the book in that niche contained bread beside fuse, shelter beside theater, and Vautrin would flatten all of it into the same charge without losing a minute's sleep.

Elise picked up the lamp and started back.

At the last turn before the sleeping gallery she could hear Lucien again, quieter now, reading something aloud to Lucie because the child would not sleep after the quarrel. His voice came braided with coughs and patience. Clemence interrupted now and then to correct a hem or a fact. Somewhere beyond them Abel was either with Ferrand or thinking about him. The choice in the wall sat between all those sounds like another hidden mouth.

When Elise reached the schoolroom, nobody looked up at once. That spared her.

She set the lamp down by the onions and said, "The small ones were under the basket."

Clemence grunted.

Lucie held out a scrap of paper on which she had written her own name badly and with full seriousness. Lucien was helping her shape the last letter with one ink-dark finger. Elise looked at the child's bent head, at the chalk wall, at the stocking Clemence had gone back to darning, at the empty patch of floor where Ferrand's satchel had rested.

Vautrin would get no ledger from her in the morning.

He would get something thinner, if he got anything at all.

And if he noticed the difference, if he put his neat hands on the missing weight and followed the absence back to her, then the cost had already begun. The book was in the wall. Abel was in Ferrand's reach. Thursday was coming whether she fed the machine or not.

She sat down beside Lucie and took up the needle Clemence had left on the bench, as if mending a stocking were the natural work of her hands.
