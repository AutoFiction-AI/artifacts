# Chapter 1

Elise had learned to judge an office by what it tried to overpower. This one fought damp with orange peel. A strip of it browned on the little iron stove beside Inspecteur Vautrin's desk, sweetening nothing, only teaching the paper to smell more expensive. Rain ticked against the windows facing the river. His cuffs stayed clean. That was the other insistence of the room.

He had arranged two documents before she came in. The upper sheet was blue, folded twice, and stamped with the Charenton accounts seal. Beneath it lay the thinner gray file that carried her own name in a court clerk's hand, the letters narrow and mean. Vautrin always set his gloves down seam to seam before he spoke, as if leather needed discipline too.

"Sit, Mlle Valette."

She sat. The chair caned her through the wet skirt.

He touched the blue paper with one finger. "This arrived this morning."

"They wrote last week."

"Yes. Accounts offices are patient right up until they are not." He opened the fold and turned it toward her. "Your mother's bed, broth, linen, supervision, medicine when they bother with medicine. Twenty-eight francs due again on Monday. They remain accommodating because I have made them accommodating."

Charenton's name was enough. Elise did not need the rest of the print. She could have supplied it from memory: boiled sheets, old piss caught beneath soap, the iron bedstead that made her mother look already sorted into inventory. On good days her mother called her by her proper name. On bad days she called her sister, who had died at nine. On the worst days she had no use for names at all and clawed the blanket fringe until her nails tore.

Vautrin let her look just long enough for the ward to rise around her. Then he covered the bill with her gray file.

"And this," he said, "remains where it is because I prefer it there."

She did not touch that paper either. He enjoyed leaving the exact charge undescribed, as if vagueness made him generous. There had been theft in it, and forged copying in it, and one magistrate who preferred women useful rather than jailed when useful women were available. The file was not a sentence. It was a hand kept on the back of her neck.

"What do you want?" she asked.

Vautrin leaned back. He was heavy-lidded enough to look bored even when he was taking pleasure. The barbered neatness of him never failed. He kept a city of dirt under his authority and managed not to carry any of it home.

"A refuge under Montrouge," he said. "Old quarry work. We have had fragments for months. Printers. Couriers. A schoolmaster with opinions. Perhaps powder, perhaps only the people who make powder possible. In the present climate, the distinction is administrative, not moral."

Elise heard the word refuge and then the rest of it. "You want entry."

"I want clarity."

"That is not the same thing."

"It often is, if one writes the report properly."

Rain rattled harder against the glass. Somewhere beyond the pane, carriage iron rang over stone. Vautrin took a paper knife in hand and set it down again without using it.

"You will go this afternoon to rue de la Tombe-Issoire," he said. "There is a wine shop with a cellar that reaches where honest cellars do not. A courier named Henri Vasseur will meet you if the street remains quiet. He is expecting Jeanne Morel."

He slid a smaller card across the desk. On it, in his compact hand, were four facts and one lie broad enough to live in: Jeanne Morel, widow, copyist, lately turned out of lodgings near rue du Banquier, in need of work and willing to keep her mouth shut. Enough to stand on. Not enough to rest on.

"Widow?" Elise said.

"You wear black well."

"Copyist is better."

"Naturally. Your own hands improve the fiction."

Ink never wholly left the beds of her nails. She had scrubbed before coming in and carried the stain anyway.

"What does Vasseur know?" she asked.

"Only the alias and that he is to pass you onward if you do not present as an idiot."

"Pass me to whom?"

"A woman called Clemence Rigal, if fortune is kind. Others after that, if you do not waste the opportunity."

The name meant nothing yet. Elise fixed it in place all the same.

"And what do you want brought back first?" she asked. "Names? Layout? Printing capacity?"

"Yes."

She watched him.

He allowed himself the smallest smile. "Names first. Sleeping numbers. Entrances, if you can. The print equipment if there is any worth naming. Confirmation on Delatour if he is present."

"Lucien Delatour is tied to it?"

"There are suggestions. I am tired of suggestions."

She looked at the Charenton seal again. "If there is nothing down there but families and mutual pity?"

"Then you will give me the families and the pity. Shelter is infrastructure. Food is infrastructure. Laundry is infrastructure. A man who prints a tract is less dangerous alone than a woman who can hide him for three months and wash the blood out of his shirt. The laws have improved our ability to understand that."

There it was. Not a bomb crew, then. Not only that. He wanted walls, cots, bread, routes, the names of the people who made a hunted life possible. Something broad enough to drag a district through.

Elise said, "You are building a case before you have the crime."

"Association is a kind of crime now, when the company is poor enough and the papers are good." His tone remained cultivated, nearly patient. "You know this. Do not make me mistake you for sentimental."

Sentimental. He meant expensive.

She thought of Charenton's corridors again. The women with tied sleeves. The orderlies who lifted bodies under the armpits when bodies no longer cooperated. Her mother once had hands fit for lace. Now she scratched at lice, at memory, at whatever thread the ward left within reach. Elise paid to keep her indoors and out of the communal wards where women lay three to a bed when the city remembered its poor too late.

"How long?" she asked.

"The first useful report in three days. Faster if the opportunity is obvious."

"And the dragnet?"

That made him stiller. "What dragnet?"

"You don't send me under a district you already suspect unless other work is moving above it."

"Other work is always moving above everything."

It was not an answer, which was answer enough. He meant to start pulling threads while she was still learning where they ran. If the quarry broke open quickly, so much the better. If it did not, her names would help widen the sweep later. Either way the machine would eat.

Vautrin opened a drawer. From it he took a small round brass marker wrapped in cloth and three narrow slips of paper folded into near nothing.

"Your authorization," he said, indicating the brass. "Show it only if the alternative is prison or a body cart. If you show it casually, I will deny you with a clear conscience."

He touched the slips. "Milk ink. One-use substitutions. The chemistry is ordinary. The handwriting should not be." He pushed them toward her. "Do not keep them all in the same place."

The marker was warmer than the room from having sat in the drawer. One side carried the Surete's stamp, the other only a number. Not a name. Never a name.

"If a uniform recognizes this," she said, "I'm burned."

"If a uniform recognizes this and still obeys the chain of command, you are rescued. The world is full of ifs."

She slipped the brass into her glove and the papers into her reticule. "And if Vasseur is already taken?"

"Then you improvise. That is why I use you."

"Or why you can disclaim me."

"Those are not contradictory statements."

He gathered his gloves and stood. It was dismissal without the courtesy of pretending choice.

"One more thing, Mlle Valette. If you are admitted, do not fall in love with utility. These little nests of idealism grow persuasive when one is fed inside them. Bread, children, injured men speaking of justice. Domesticity has converted brighter people than you."

She almost laughed, which would have pleased him too much. "I know what bread costs."

"Exactly," he said. "That is what makes you suited."

He offered no hand. Neither did she.

In the corridor outside, the prefecture smelled of wet wool, lamp smoke, and the boots of men who did not know their names would remain off the reports. Elise stood beneath a window where the light was bad, opened the hem she had already loosened in her mourning skirt, and stitched the brass marker into it with black thread from her cuff roll. The metal dragged at the cloth once she let it fall. She divided the cipher slips: one into the whalebone channel of her bodice, one into the lining of her glove, one beneath the pasteboard sole of her left boot. By the time she finished, the rain had strengthened again.

On the omnibus south she built Jeanne Morel line by line and sanded her smooth. Widow, yes. Copyist, yes. Lodgings lost because arrears sounded common enough to pass without embroidery. Husband dead of chest trouble because half the city had coughed itself into the grave one winter or another. Not too tragic. Not too memorable. A woman useful with her pen and willing with her back when the pen failed her. The sort of woman nobody remembered until they needed to.

By the time she stepped down near Tombe-Issoire, the rain had thinned to a gray mist that kept every surface damp and undecided. Cart ruts held brown water. The wine shop Vautrin had named advertised ordinary red in wavering letters on the window. Sour wine leaked under the sill and met the street's chalky mud in a smell like rotten fruit ground into plaster.

Elise did not look at the door first. She looked at the men who were pretending not to look at it.

One leaned under a saddler's awning with his hands tucked too correctly in his sleeves. Another appeared occupied by a lame cartwheel across the street. Their coats were civilian enough. Their boots were not. Prefecture cobblers drove heel nails in a square that ordinary tradesmen seldom paid for, and these had the square. The heavier man kept his hat low and watched reflections in the wine-shop glass instead of faces. That was police work. Honest men met eyes or avoided them. Police preferred windows.

The seam in Elise's hem rasped her ankle as she shifted her weight. Vautrin had not denied the dragnet. He had simply declined to schedule it for her.

Henri Vasseur came up from the south carrying a sack on one shoulder and the smell of anise a pace ahead of him. He was thinner than she had expected, with a thickened left ear that looked as if a fight or a factory press had once tried to flatten it. He touched the knot of the sack every few steps to reassure himself the glass inside had not broken. The small private habit did more to convince her of him than any description could have. Men lied with slogans. They rarely lied with nerves.

He spotted her black dress and altered course.

Elise saw the policemen register him at the same moment.

She could still have walked away. If she turned now and took the next side street, Henri might feel the watch in time to drift past and save himself. Clemence Rigal, if she existed, would remain sealed off. Vautrin would still have her mother's bills. He would still have her file. Another route might come. Or not. The gray men under the awning were already starting to move.

Henri lifted two fingers to his cap as if greeting an ordinary acquaintance. "Mme Morel?"

Elise stayed where she was.

"Yes," she said.

His expression loosened. He had good teeth except for one chipped canine that caught when he smiled. "You made better time than I did. Come on, then. Street's filthy and Rigal gets foul when bread's late."

He shifted the sack down to free his hand for the door.

The heavy man crossed the distance in four strides.

"Henri Vasseur."

Henri did not run. That was the first thing that doomed him. The second was the half-turn of his head toward Elise, not with suspicion but with the natural need to place the danger. The policeman took his wrist and bent it high behind him. Glass struck stone inside the sack with a helpless clink. Henri made a shocked sound, small and private, before the pain had time to get its name.

The second officer came in low and hard. His shoulder drove Henri into the wine-shop shutter. Elise heard teeth hit wood. A paper-wrapped bar of soap burst from the split sack and skidded through gutter water. A loaf followed, then a packet of copybooks tied in twine.

"Police," the heavy man said to nobody who needed telling. "Keep your hands where I can see them."

Henri twisted once, not badly, trying more to save the sack than himself. That earned him a blow in the kidneys with the short cane the officer had kept up his sleeve. His knees went out. The man behind him hauled him back up by the collar rather than letting him fall cleanly.

Elise had room for three actions. She could shout. She could run. She could step in and show the brass, burn the alias, and start a life that smelled of prison straw instead of quarry damp. She did none of the three. She bent, quick and plain as any woman saving bread from muck, and caught the copybooks before the gutter took them.

Henri saw her then. Blood had opened his lower lip. Recognition arrived in his face, followed by the faster arithmetic of a courier who knew what a street was for. He swallowed whatever he had meant to say about names.

"Inside," he got out, thickly. "Go on, then."

The officer jerked his arm higher. Something in Henri's shoulder gave with a wet, ugly click.

Elise went.

The wine-shop door opened before she reached it. The woman in the doorway was broad through the shoulders and still wearing her work apron under a man's castoff coat. One boot was laced with blue cord instead of leather, and a child's red mitten hung from her belt by a safety pin, damp and forgotten there. Her hands were raw from lye to the wrist. She took in the street in one sweep: Henri folded against police grip, bread in Elise's arms, the packet of copybooks, the men under the awning who had stopped pretending to be anything else.

"Move," she said. "Unless you want the rest of them."

Elise stepped inside. The woman took the loaf from her, shoved the door with her hip, and stood listening for one beat while the arrest continued outside: Henri cursed once when they searched him, a brief bright curse that cut off when a fist or a palm found his throat.

The room behind the shop was close with sour cask wood, onion skins, and yesterday's mop water. No customers. No proprietor visible. Just a scarred table, two overturned crates, and a trap in the floor partly hidden under sacking.

The woman faced Elise. Up close, her face moved quickly even when the rest of her held still; suspicion crossed it the way sums crossed an abacus. She set the loaf on the table and kept one hand over it, as if bread itself might bolt.

"Who are you?" she asked.

Elise shifted the copybooks into the crook of one arm. "Jeanne Morel."

"That was not the question."

"It's the answer I was sent with."

The woman's mouth flattened. "Sent by who?"

"Henri Vasseur was told to expect me."

"By who?"

Elise let half a breath pass, as if measuring how much ignorance a frightened widow could admit. "A woman on the Bievre. She knew I had lost my room and could write a fair hand. She said there might be work if I kept quiet."

"Name."

"She did not offer one. I did not press."

"Convenient."

Outside, boots scraped. Someone shouted for a wagon. Henri was still close enough to hear if he screamed. He did not scream. Elise wondered if that was courage or the inability to get air back after the hit to the kidneys.

The woman said, "What did he tell you when he saw you?"

"That bread was late and you'd be angry."

One eyebrow moved. Not trust. Calculation.

"Anything else?"

"That the street was filthy." Elise looked at the wet copybooks in her hands. "He was right."

The woman stepped forward and took the packet from her. The twine had cut a red groove into the paper. She touched it, then Elise's sleeve, testing wetness, weight, truth.

"If you're police, you're badly dressed for it," she said.

"If I were police, I'd have left you the bread in the gutter and watched who came for it."

That bought her one second. The woman's eyes sharpened, not softened.

"Maybe," she said. "Show me your writing hand."

Elise did.

The callus along the middle finger was real. So was the faint burn scar inside her left wrist, paler than the rest of her skin. The woman's thumb brushed the side of Elise's forefinger where a pen rested longest. Raw lye hands. Fast-reading face. This had to be Clemence Rigal.

"You do copy work," Clemence said, not granting the whole alias, only the skill.

"Bills. Petitions. Anything people want made neat enough to be obeyed."

"And your husband?"

There it was. Elise kept her voice even. "Dead."

"That part I heard. Of what?"

"His chest went to rags over one winter. He worked in a counting room with a cracked window and called it a draft until he was spitting brown."

Clemence kept hold of her hand one beat longer than needed. "How long ago?"

"Eighteen months."

"No family?"

"Not with room to spare."

"Parish ladies?"

"They wanted gratitude before the bread. I couldn't always pay in the right tone."

That, more than the widowhood, seemed to satisfy something small in Clemence. Not belief. Recognition of a usable complaint.

She let go. "Take off your glove."

Elise did.

Clemence examined the nail beds, the lingering ink, the skin split at one knuckle. "Why come here?"

"Because I need work."

"There's laundress work aboveground. Seamstress work. Factory work if you don't mind losing fingers."

"Factory work minds me. Laundry asks references. Sewing doesn't pay if you're too slow and I'm too slow. Copy work is thin. Rent isn't."

"Where was the room?"

"Near rue du Banquier."

"How much?"

"Nine francs with no heat and the privilege of bedbugs."

"That all?"

"And the landlady's son trying the latch after midnight."

Clemence made a sound that could have been disgust or belief. "Why black if you're that broke?"

Elise looked down at the dress. "Because black dye costs less than a new life."

That got the first brief change in Clemence's face. Not amusement, exactly. A hard little acknowledgment, woman to woman, that money humiliated most cleanly when it had to pass for respectability.

Outside the door, wagon wheels ground close. Someone spat. A man's voice read a charge in the bored cadence of paper becoming flesh.

Clemence said, "Did you know they were on him?"

There was no safety in pretending stupidity. Not after the boots, not after the speed of the arrest. Elise chose the portion of truth that could survive daylight.

"I knew the street was wrong before he reached me."

"And you stayed."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Henri's shoulder had gone under the officer's hand with that wet click. Elise could still hear it. She set her bare fingers on the edge of the table where wine had raised the grain and said, "Because if I walked away, I'd still be hungry and he'd still be taken."

Clemence's gaze did not leave her.

"That's an ugly answer," she said.

"It's the answer I've got."

For the first time, Clemence seemed to make room for the possibility that Jeanne Morel, whoever she was, belonged to the same economy as the rest of them. Not safe. Not good. Merely expensive to discard.

She crossed to the trap in the floor and dragged the sacking clear. Damp air climbed up at once, chalk-cold and carrying old water with it. Somewhere below, a drop struck stone in patient intervals.

"Henri was due to bring this," she said, touching the loaf. "And paper. And lamp oil, though I suppose the bottle's smashed now. He was also due to carry two sacks farther in because the boy who should have done it is down with a cough and I don't have spare backs growing on the wall."

She glanced at Elise. "I also can't let you stand back up on the street now that you've seen this door and his face in the same minute."

"No," Elise said.

"No." Clemence reached for the copybooks, then thought better of giving them to herself and thrust them back at Elise. "Can you carry and keep your mouth shut at the same time?"

"Usually."

"Good. If you're lying, you'll lie lower down where I can watch you do it."

She bent, took the loaf and what remained of the soap, and started down. Elise followed with the copybooks and the sack, heavier now because the broken bottle had soaked everything it had not smashed.

The first steps were wood, steep and mean. The second flight turned to stone. By the third, the walls narrowed enough that Elise had to angle the sack to keep it from scraping. The air changed before the light did. Street damp gave way to chalk damp, colder and more intimate. Wine rot thinned out. Wet limestone, lamp oil, old wool, and breath took its place.

Clemence did not waste the descent.

"Who was the woman on the Bievre?" she asked over her shoulder.

"Dark hair. Forty maybe. Red wrists from lye. She was not in a mood to trade names."

"You are trying to flatter me by accident."

"No. I'm trying to tell you why I listened."

"Mmm."

They turned. The trapdoor's edge of daylight shrank above them.

"How much can you lift?" Clemence asked.

"Enough."

"Enough is a liar's unit."

"A full bucket in each hand if the floor's level. Less if it isn't."

"Can you read numbers upside down?"

"Yes."

"Can you keep a child occupied while her mother works?"

The question surprised her more than it should have. "If the child permits it."

"They never do." Clemence kept moving. "Can you gut a chicken?"

"Badly."

"Can you sew?"

"Buttons and hems."

"Can you hold your tongue when men start talking politics as if politics bakes bread?"

"Better than I can gut a chicken."

That won her a faint snort, the nearest thing to laughter Clemence had offered.

The passage leveled. A lamp had been left in a niche ahead, its flame turned low. White stone caught the light and gave it back grudgingly. Elise saw hand marks on the walls, soot on the ceiling, a groove worn by many shoulders brushing the same turn. The quarry did not open so much as continue, room by hidden room, like a thought the city had buried without killing.

Above them, muffled through all that stone, came the last blurred thud of the wagon moving off.

Henri Vasseur would be in it now with his wrists tied or ironed, his shoulder swelling under the coat, his lip drying black where the blood had started. By nightfall he would be a name on a charge sheet, or not even that if Vautrin decided paper could wait. Elise had watched the policemen take him and used the price of it at once.

Clemence lifted the lamp from the niche and looked back. In the changed light her lye-raw hands seemed almost flayed.

"Listen to me, Jeanne Morel," she said. "You're not admitted. You're useful for the next hour. After that we decide again. If anything in your story shifts when I push it, you'll wish I'd left you to the street. Do you understand?"

Elise adjusted the sack on her shoulder. Broken glass clicked somewhere inside against the neck of the surviving bottle. The brass marker in her hem dragged at her ankle like an extra bone.

"I understand," she said.

"Good. Keep up, then."

Clemence turned and led her farther in.

Elise put her free hand on the wall for the next bend. Chalk dust came away on her palm like flour. Somewhere ahead, beyond the turn she could not yet see, a child coughed, and a man's voice answered too softly to make out the words. She had entry now. She had the refuge Vautrin wanted, or the mouth of it. She had won it by standing still on a wet street while another body paid the cost of motion.

She went down after Clemence into the dark that smelled of lime, soap, and hidden people.
