# Chapter 11

The spoiled proof had lost the same accent mark three times, and Nadir refused to waste decent paper on bad setting. He stood side-on to the packing table, long fingers blackened at the cuticles, turning the ruined sheet into a white bird for Lucie with the quick patience he never spent on adults.

"If she asks for a horse again," he said, folding the neck down with his thumbnail, "I'm leaving her the square and the difficulty. Birds are honest. Horses always look like ministers."

Elise cinched the twine around the parcel of primers and translated notices, keeping her eyes on the knot because the dark beyond the moved press had not yet left her skin. "Tell her it's a goat and she'll forgive you."

"She knows the difference, which is more than I can say for the Republic."

Ferrand laughed from the doorway before the joke had finished landing. He had shaved badly that morning; a little crescent of soap still clung under one ear, fighting with the cheap cologne he poured over himself as if scent could pass for cleanliness. The black satchel rode under his arm, worn smooth at the corners from being handled more than trusted.

"If the schoolroom's done," he said, "some of us have a city to cross."

Lucien looked up from the account sheet spread beside the lamp. Chalk dust had settled in the silver at his temple. He had buttoned his shirt wrong in the hurry of the morning, one hole off at the throat, and Elise wanted with obscene force to reach across the table and set him straight. "You'll cross it no faster for sneering at the hands carrying your paper," he said. "Two stops only. The bookseller first, then the cooper's wife. Back before supper."

His voice stayed ordinary. That was the mercy and the difficulty. A few hours earlier, in the cold dark beyond the moved press, she had had her mouth against the scar under his shoulder and his hand braced at the back of her neck hard enough to leave an ache she could still feel when she swallowed. Now he spoke to her like any other pair of hands in the room. Clemence was at the flour bins. Abel was sorting type in the next chamber. Nobody could be allowed to read her face.

Nadir set the finished bird on top of the bundle for a moment, looking almost pleased with himself. "If she crushes this one too, I'll take it personally."

"She keeps them under her pillow," Lucien said without looking up. "That isn't crushing. That's curation."

Ferrand made a soft, impatient click with his tongue. "Christ, you lot could turn a hanging into a lesson."

Lucien's eyes lifted then, warm no longer. "Then try not to bring one home with you."

Ferrand grinned as if he'd been complimented. He always grinned hardest when the room soured against him. Elise had seen enough men like that in prefecture corridors, half tout and half pet dog, living off the second before someone decided to kick. He shifted the satchel higher under his arm, protective without seeming to notice he'd done it.

They went up by the wine-shop stairs into early June brightness. Warmth lay over the street, but the quarry cold stayed caught in Elise's sleeves and under her breasts. The wine-shop keeper was rinsing sour red from the floorboards above the trap; the smell of yeast and vinegar followed them out. Beyond the door the city had already begun rubbing itself awake in earnest: barrows clattering, a knife-grinder's wheel whining, horse piss drying sharp in the sun, women calling the price of cherries as if summer could be bullied into arriving faster.

The parcel weighed more than it looked. Primers, mutual-aid circulars, translated notices for foundry men who read Arabic better than French, and a wrapped packet of subscription coins sewn into the middle where a quick thief would not find them. Elise carried it against her hip. Nadir carried the second bundle and walked the way he always did aboveground, shoulders angled, making himself narrow in the crowd without ever seeming to yield. Ferrand drifted half a pace behind them, jaunty, idle, his satchel bumping his thigh.

"You don't need to come for both stops," Nadir said without turning. "Unless you've suddenly developed an interest in schoolbooks."

"I've got business near the barrier," Ferrand said. "A man who owes me for fuse wire and gratitude."

"One of those debts is imaginary," Nadir said.

Ferrand laughed again. "You're in a mood."

"I'm in Paris."

They crossed a lane where wet laundry hung from a second-story line and dripped onto the hats below. Elise kept count by habit: three uniforms at the end of the square, too far to matter; a postman; a woman with a basket of eels; two men in dark jackets leaning where men leaned when they wanted not to be noticed. One had his hat low and his boots too good for a carter. The other had the blank face of a clerk waiting to become a hand.

She saw them the moment Nadir saw them. His stride altered by less than an inch. He did not look at her. He said, very mildly, "Don't stop if the woman with the red apron asks for change. She bites."

Then the man with the clerk's face stepped off the curb and said, "Belkacem."

The name struck the air like a slap. Nadir had time to turn his head. No more than that. The second man seized his elbow, not rough at first, almost courteous, and the courtesy was what made it practiced. A uniform came in from the lane mouth. Another from behind the eel woman. The space closed like a purse.

Elise shifted the parcel to free her hand. Ferrand had already stepped back. Not running. Not startled. Simply making room, as if he'd been waiting for furniture to be moved.

"Papers," the clerk-faced man said. "And keep your hands where I can see them."

"You've got my hands," Nadir said.

The answer earned him the blow. Not a fist. A truncheon jabbed low and fast into the back of his knee. He pitched sideways into a vegetable cart, cabbages thudding against wood, his bundle splitting when it hit the wheel. Primers burst into the gutter. A sheet of the Arabic notices sailed once in the air like laundry and came down in horse muck.

The woman at the cart swore and dragged her produce back with both hands. Someone laughed. A boy shouted for his brother to look.

Elise moved before she'd decided to. She put her shoulder into the uniform nearest her and caught only cloth and bone; he turned on her with insulted ease and drove the heel of his hand into her chest. She went back into the cart edge hard enough to bark the breath out of herself. The parcel stayed in her arms because she clutched it on instinct, ridiculous and telling, primers protected while a man hit the stones.

"Leave it," Nadir said. His cheek was against the wheel rim now, one officer folding his arm higher, higher, until Elise could hear the catch in his breath. "Jeanne. Don't."

He used the alias for her and gave nothing else away. Even then.

The clerk-faced man turned Nadir's pockets out with the same expression he might have used counting receipts. A carpenter's pencil. A folded handkerchief. Two sous. The paper bird dropped last, landing in the wet strip beside the curb. The officer stooped, opened it with thick fingers, and smirked.

"Scholar and entertainer," he said. "How useful."

He crushed it back into a white wad and let it fall.

The uniform at Nadir's shoulders yanked him upright only to shove him face-first again against the cart. The humiliation mattered as much as the pain; Elise knew that rhythm too. Make the body clumsy in public, make the crowd complicit, make the arrest itself do part of the work of punishment. One woman looked away with her mouth pinched tight. Another watched openly, choosing her pears while Nadir's arm was twisted until his fingers spread uselessly against the wheel spoke. Ink marked the wood where his hand slid.

"You've mistaken him," Elise said, because she had to say something, because silence from her would have rung louder than failure. "He's carrying school papers."

The clerk-faced man spared her half a glance. "Then the schools are under poor management, madame."

Madame. Not widow, not girl. A little courtesy laid over force like varnish over rot. Elise knew that voice too.

Ferrand reached her then, not to help Nadir but to catch Elise by the sleeve before she could lunge again. His grip was quick and dry. "Enough," he murmured. "You want the whole bloody block taken?"

She jerked free. "Were they waiting?"

"They're police. That's what they do."

He didn't sound winded. He didn't sound surprised. He sounded annoyed at a timetable shift.

The officers moved fast once the crowd had formed. They half dragged, half marched Nadir toward the covered police carriage waiting beyond the lane mouth. One of them kept a hand on the back of his neck as if displaying an unruly schoolboy. His cap had gone; Elise saw the nick in his left cheek dark with sweat and dirt, and saw, more clearly than either, the one place his gaze landed before they pushed his head down into the carriage.

Not on her.

On Ferrand.

It was only a flick of the eyes. It might have meant nothing to anyone who had not spent weeks watching both men dislike each other with increasing exactness. To Elise it landed with the force of a verdict. Nadir had had one instant to spend before the carriage swallowed him, and he had spent it there.

The door slammed. The horse tossed once in the shafts. Then the carriage was away, wheels cutting through the trampled notices and the cabbage leaves and the little crowd already beginning to relax back into trade.

Elise stood in the smell of split vegetables and horse sweat and paper pulp, the shove still hot in her chest. The crushed bird lay near her boot. She bent and picked it up before the gutter took it, smoothing it once with her thumb though it was ruined beyond rescue.

Ferrand stooped for a surviving packet of primers. "No point standing here," he said. "If they've taken him, they've taken him."

She stared at him. His satchel was tucked close under his arm, dry, untouched. He had kept it clear of the whole mess with the care of a man saving his own teeth in a tavern brawl.

"You moved before they touched him," she said.

He gave her an incredulous look, as if she had accused him of breathing. "Of course I moved. I'm not an idiot."

"No," she said. "You're not."

He heard something in her voice then, because the grin went from his face. Only for a second. Then it returned, looser, easier, all appetite and shrug. "Come on, widow. Unless you've got a key to the carriage, all we're doing is furnishing the street with grief."

She wanted to put the parcel down and break his nose with both hands. Instead she gathered the salvageable papers, their edges slimed with gutter water, and walked back toward the wine-shop stairs with Ferrand beside her and Nadir already vanishing into the machine she knew too well: the clerk, the ledger, the waiting room that stank of piss and chloride, the first questions asked before the blood had dried, the specific pleasure some policemen took in crippling printer's hands because print traveled farther than fists.

She kept the crushed bird in her pocket all the way down.

* * *

By the time they got back below, street dirt had dried into a gray tide-mark on Elise's skirt. The quarry air took the heat off her skin at once. Lamp smoke, wet wool, soap, and the mineral chill of limestone closed around her again. At the stair mouth she had one animal hope: that the last half hour might stop there and fail to follow them in.

Clemence was cutting bread in the ration room with the long knife she used for everything from turnips to string. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Red, lye-bitten knuckles flashed as she brought the blade down. Lucien sat at the trestle with the account book open and a stump of pencil tucked behind one ear. Abel was at the far wall, sorting battered sorts back into the case, lips moving over a line of verse under his breath as he worked, not for display but because he liked the feel of language in his mouth when his hands were busy.

Lucie looked up from the floor where she had been drawing letters in chalk dust. One stocking had slumped down into her shoe. "Did Nadir bring the bird?"

Nobody answered fast enough.

Lucien rose first. He read it before Elise spoke; she saw the knowledge enter his face by the way his shoulders set, not by any show. "Where?" he asked.

"Near the market road," Elise said. "Two plainclothes, three uniforms, a carriage waiting."

Ferrand tossed the salvaged primers onto the table. "They had his name before they had his wrists. Clean lift. Fast work."

Clemence's knife stopped on the board. "Was he carrying anything that names us?"

"No," Elise said. "Primers. The foundry notices. Coins sewn in the wrapping."

"How much coin?"

"Not enough to make this the expensive part."

Clemence gave one short nod, the practical arithmetic already moving behind her face. Nadir's wages were gone, his compositing with them, and the Arabic notices would be delayed. One more place in the city's cracked economy would have to pretend the quarry still existed.

Lucien looked from Elise to Ferrand. "Tell it clean."

Elise did. She gave them the lane, the men closing in, the carriage waiting at the mouth, and the papers spinning into the gutter. She did not say that one of the officers had the precise bored tone of the anti-anarchist floor at the prefecture, or that the shove she took had been textbook restraint rather than panic. Those were pieces of knowledge with sharp edges; in a room already full of fear, she could feel them slicing toward her if she handled them wrong.

Ferrand filled the spaces she left with energy instead of fact. "What matters is they took him in daylight because they can. In front of fruit sellers and children. That's the lesson, isn't it? Not the bloody carriage model."

Lucie drew both feet under her and twisted the fallen stocking in her fist. Clemence wiped her hands on her apron and said, "Lucie, schoolroom. Now."

"I want the bird."

"Now."

The girl looked from one adult face to another, saw enough to understand she had been shut out on purpose, and got to her feet with the resentful care of a child trying not to cry. Elise nearly reached into her pocket then. Not yet. The paper inside had become a damp knot from her palm.

When Lucie was gone, Abel said, "Taken where?"

He had set down the composing stick without realizing it; it lay on the floor near his boot. His cuffs were too short again, wrists all bone, one printer-burned fingertip worrying at the seam of his sleeve. He looked very young and was trying not to.

"Local post first, maybe the island after," Lucien said. "If they named him in the street, they'll want the paperwork to follow the theater."

"Theater?" Ferrand barked a laugh. "They had his face in the dirt, Lucien. That's not theater to him."

"No," Lucien said. "It's theater to the people who paid for the carriage."

Ferrand spread his hands. "Fine. Call it what you like. The point stays. We keep printing primers and ration slips and they keep loading us like sacks. How many more times do you want the lesson before you let it penetrate that noble skull?"

Clemence slapped the bread heel onto a plate. "Don't start."

"Why not? Because the child can hear? The child's already hearing boots on stairs, Clemence. We all are."

"Because you've got a mouth full of fuse smoke and nowhere decent to spit it."

That drew a crooked smile from Abel despite himself. Ferrand saw it and fed it at once.

"Look at him," Ferrand said, nodding toward the young man. "At least the boy knows shame when he sees it. Nadir gets hauled off in public and what have we got? Lessons. Tallies. Another careful speech about timing."

Lucien took the pencil from behind his ear and set it down beside the open account book as if he didn't trust himself to keep holding it. "We have an arrest less than an hour old," he said. "We don't yet know where he's booked, who saw, or what else moved with it. Anyone who calls for an explosion before those facts arrives wanting something other than Nadir."

"And anyone who waits for facts while police work a man over wants clean hands more than comrades."

The room seemed to tighten by a foot in every direction. Water dripped somewhere behind the wall. Elise could hear it between the words.

Abel said, too fast, "They'll break his hands."

Nobody answered him at once. Ferrand did not look away. Elise hated him for that accuracy. Printer's hands were always a target. She had once seen a compositor on the prefecture bench after questioning, nail beds black and swelling, fingertips split so he could not feel type for weeks. Ferrand didn't need the memory to know it. He only needed to understand what frightened a room.

"Yes," he said quietly. "If they're serious, they'll start there."

Clemence swore under her breath. Not ornament, only labor and dread. "Enough."

Abel's face went white under the chalk dust. "So what do we do? Set another page? We can print a complaint in three sizes of type if that helps."

Lucien turned to him, held Abel's gaze, and took a breath through his nose as if it hurt. When he answered, the dry turn at the edge of his voice was gone. "We find where he is. We move what can still be moved. We don't hand the police a blast they'll use to bury every name they have not managed to steal yet."

"That's what you always say," Abel shot back. The words came running over each other. "Wait. Count. Endure. As if patience were a machine you can feed bodies into and get justice out the other side. It isn't. It just makes graves neater."

Ferrand's smile widened almost tenderly. Elise could have killed him for the tenderness.

Lucien coughed once into his fist, stopped it, and went on. "Patience isn't holy. It's expensive. That's why I spend it carefully. If we answer this with a crowd and broken glass, all we've done is let them choose the stage and charge us for the tickets."

"Jesus Christ," Ferrand said. "A phrase for the poster. Meanwhile Nadir's getting his fingers opened like oysters."

Elise said, before she could decide how much she meant to reveal, "The carriage was waiting before we turned the lane. That wasn't a random patrol finding a man with bad luck. It was arranged. A public lift. They wanted witnesses."

Ferrand swung toward her. "Exactly."

The one word was triumph, not agreement. She could have bitten through it.

"Exactly means they wanted a story," Lucien said.

"Then give them one worth fearing."

"That's not fear," Lucien said. "That's administration with better copy."

Ferrand snorted. "You've grown fond of copy. Of school slates. Of keeping a tidy little parish under the hill while they come up and down at will. Maybe the old Commune froze whatever nerve was left in you."

Abel flinched as if the insult had hit him, not Lucien. He loved Lucien. Elise had known that long before she knew he also resented him. Both truths sat naked now.

Lucien took a step closer to Ferrand. He was lean enough that anger never made him large, only exact. "Mind yourself."

"Or what? You'll lecture me to death?"

"No. I'll put you outside until you remember this room isn't your stage."

Ferrand lifted his brows, amused, and let the satchel slide off his shoulder onto the bench with a heavy thud. The buckle struck the wood beside the bread knife. "There. The wicked bag's down. You can all stop staring at it and listen. This is what they're counting on, Lucien. That you'll turn every seizure into another discussion about decency. Men like Belkacem disappear because the state knows half of us are too afraid and the other half are too polite."

"I'm not afraid," Abel said.

He said it too quickly, which made it true in the wrong direction. Elise watched shame hit him the instant after the words left his mouth. He straightened anyway, chin up, as if posture could age him.

Lucien looked at him, not unkindly, which was perhaps the worst thing he could have done. "I know you're frightened," he said. "So am I. That's why I won't spend you to soothe it."

Abel went red. Ferrand smelled the blood in the water.

"Spend him?" Ferrand said. "Listen to yourself. The boy isn't flour in Clemence's cupboard. He's a man."

"Then stop shopping for him," Clemence snapped.

Silence held long enough for Ferrand's grin to twitch. He hated direct insult if he couldn't turn it into a joke at once.

Elise saw Abel looking not at Lucien anymore but at the satchel slumped against the bench leg, cracked leather dark with handling. His thumb kept rasping the seam of his cuff until a white thread stood up from the cloth. It was the look of a hungry boy at a bakery window, yes, but hungrier than food. He wanted something the room was not giving him: permission, scale, a shape large enough to hold fear without calling it fear.

She should have said his name then. She should have crossed the room and kicked the bag into Lucien's keeping and told the whole truth about Ferrand in whatever clumsy ruin it made.

Instead she kept calculating what could be proved, what would sound like panic, what would expose the source of her certainty. She had been trained too long to wait for evidence clean enough to survive speech. Nadir was in a carriage because of one kind of waiting. Abel was staring at gelignite because of another.

Lucien drew a breath that hit the cough and failed. He pressed his fist to his mouth, mastered it, and said, "This ends here for now. Clemence, move the children deeper in. Abel, with me after supper. Elise, I want the route written down while it's fresh. Ferrand, keep that bag shut or take it aboveground. I won't have bomb talk in the schoolroom."

Ferrand bowed from the waist, mocking and almost graceful. "As the schoolmaster says."

Clemence jerked her chin toward the bench. "Get that thing away from my breadboard if it's staying."

Ferrand muttered, snagged the satchel by its strap, and dropped it against the leg of the peg rail. He stripped off his coat and flung it over the leather as if cloth could make it respectable. "Abel, hang both of those in the gallery, then. Since we're keeping school."

He went to the basin alcove to wash street dust from his hands, humming under his breath, already trusting the order more than the object deserved. Abel crossed the room at once, gathered coat and satchel together, and carried them toward the sleeping gallery. Elise saw the hard rectangular sag of the satchel under the cloth and told herself Ferrand would keep half an eye on him, that Clemence or Lucien would stop it if it mattered. By the time she understood the purpose in Abel's shoulders, the doorway had already taken him.

* * *

The quarry settled badly that night, not into peace but into an imitation of it. Voices dropped because walls were thin and children listened, not because anyone had calmed. Somewhere in the deeper chamber a baby started, wailed once, then quieted at the breast. The lamps hissed. Limestone gave back the day's heat grudgingly, so that the air felt colder the longer you stood still.

Elise was at the basin in the side alcove, rubbing the brown smear of cart filth from her skirt with soap that smelled faintly of tallow and lye. The stain had set in the weave. So had the officer's handprint in her chest; every breath still found the bruise. She wrung out the cloth and watched gray water stripe the stone.

Lucien came in without warning her. That new knowledge between them made warning impossible in some ways and necessary in others. He stopped at the threshold rather than crowding the alcove. His restraint had always been more dangerous than another man's hunger.

"Did they name him before they touched him?" he asked.

"Yes."

"You said local post first. That sounded less like a guess than I'd like."

She kept scrubbing the same place on the skirt. "A waiting carriage means paperwork already moving. They like the nearest room first. Less chance of losing a body in the street."

"A body."

She shut her eyes for half a second. "That's what the system calls a man until the clerk sits down."

Lucien said nothing. Water dripped from the cloth onto her knuckles. She wished, stupidly, that he would touch her. She was equally glad he did not.

"And Ferrand?" he said.

"He moved before the hands were on Nadir."

"I saw that much in your face when you came in."

"Then maybe see the rest."

The words were sharper than she'd meant. They came out like copy for the ledger, each syllable buttoned and separate. Lucien heard it too. He leaned his shoulder against the stone, not easy, only steady.

"I am seeing it," he said softly, and the full form landed because he wanted it to. "I just need the piece that can be used before I throw him out and lose whatever else he knows."

Used. There it was again, the necessary ugliness she loved him for and hated because she knew its limits. She could have told him then. Ferrand was not merely poison. Ferrand was paid poison. Vautrin's. Mine too, if you follow the line far enough. The confession rose as far as her teeth and stopped there, trapped by the old terror that once said aloud it would become irreversible, no longer something she might yet manage with craft and timing.

Before either of them could speak again, Clemence's voice cut down the passage.

"Lucien."

It was not loud. It did not need to be.

They found the ration room half assembled in panic. Ferrand was on his knees between the bench and the peg rail, dragging coats down with one hand and yanking at blankets with the other as if the satchel might have crawled there out of malice. Clemence stood with both hands braced on the breadboard. Lucie, in her night shift and one sock, had clearly been dragged back out of bed. Chalk dust still marked one knee.

"What's happened?" Lucien said.

Ferrand came up fast, face stripped of charm at last. "The little bastard took it."

Lucien's voice stayed level. "Say what he took."

"My bag."

"Say what was in it."

Ferrand hesitated just long enough to damn himself. "Tools."

Elise said, "Gelignite. And the pistol."

Ferrand wheeled on her. "All right, yes. Enough for a job, not enough for a war. Happy?"

"No," Clemence said. "Who's missing?"

Abel's blanket lay collapsed in the sleeping gallery doorway. His jacket was gone from the peg. So was the better cap he wore when he wanted to look older. On the floor near his mattress sat an opened booklet of Hugo with a torn paper marker halfway through, as if he had meant to come back and finish the stanza after he saved the century.

Lucien crossed to the bunk, touched the cold blanket once, and turned. The cough had left his face. Anger had not. "When did you last see him?"

Ferrand dragged a hand over his mouth. "When I told him to hang the damned thing in the gallery. After that? How the fuck should I know? I'm not his nurse."

"No," Clemence said. "You're the disease."

Lucie spoke into the gap because adults had left it open. "He wrapped the buckle in his scarf."

Every head turned toward her.

Clemence swore. "I told you to stay in bed."

"I was in bed."

"Lucie."

The child lifted her chin. Fear made her more literal, not less. "I was. Then Abel came past. He had Jules's bag and his own scarf around the metal so it wouldn't knock on the wall. I said his laces were loose and he'd fall on the north stairs and he said not to wake you because you'd make a household out of it."

A laugh tried to break out of Clemence and tore itself into something uglier.

Lucien crouched so he was level with the girl. "When?"

"After the shouting. Before the last lamp turn. He smelled like ink and that bad cologne because he'd been near Jules's coat." She frowned, trying to be exact. "He had the pistol in the back of his trousers. I saw the wood."

Ferrand's face changed again, this time into naked fury. "Christ."

Lucie looked from him to Lucien, then to Elise. "Are they breaking Nadir's fingers now?"

The room took the question like a blow to the mouth.

Clemence moved first, catching the girl against her apron with a roughness that was almost tenderness. "Enough. You don't need that in your head."

"It's already in my head."

There was no answer to that worth giving in front of a child. Elise felt the crushed paper bird in her pocket like a second, smaller accusation.

Lucien rose. "North stairs means he wanted the courier exit."

"Or he wanted you to think that," Ferrand snapped. "He has heard enough from me to know three better places than that. Barrier office. Magistrate's door. Half the fucking boulevard if he wants a crowd."

"He heard it from you," Elise said. "That's the point."

"Don't start with me."

"I haven't yet started."

Ferrand took a step toward her. Lucien got between them so quickly the movement looked effortless until the cough caught him at the end of it. He swallowed it down and put one hand flat against Ferrand's chest.

"You stay here," he said.

Ferrand stared. "Are you insane? It's my bag."

"And your mouth helped send him out with it."

"Then let me fix it."

"No."

The word dropped like a stone into water. Simple. Final. Ferrand's nostrils flared. Elise could almost see the calculations moving behind his eyes: whether to strike, whether to laugh, whether the room had turned too far against him for either to work.

"You think he'll listen to you?" he said. "To more patience? He wants a history, Lucien. Not another errand."

"He wants not to feel helpless," Lucien said. "You sold him spectacle as if that were manhood."

Ferrand sneered. "And you sold him waiting as if that were wisdom."

Clemence shifted Lucie behind her. "Argue later. Move now."

No one trusted the same facts. Ferrand thought Abel wanted a crowd big enough to make news. Lucien thought he wanted an act that could be aimed at the men who had taken Nadir. Clemence thought grief itself would choose badly if given shoes and dark. Elise thought Ferrand had been whispering too many possible scripts into the boy's ear for any of them to know which one had stuck. A barracks gate. A magistrate's carriage. A police clerk on a narrow stair. A cafe full of the wrong people. That was the true danger: not a plan, but a young man full of bad theater and enough explosives to make any impulse fatal.

Lucien turned to Elise. "You come with me."

Ferrand barked a laugh. "Her?"

Lucien did not look at him. "She was there. She sees streets cleanly when she's frightened. Better than either of us."

It was praise. It was also a wound. Elise took it anyway. "I'll go."

Clemence said, "If you find him, don't let him start talking in public. Get hold of his hands first."

Only she could have made it domestic and tactical at once. Elise nodded.

Lucie wriggled around her mother's hip. "Give him the bird."

Elise had forgotten the thing for one stunned instant. She took the crushed paper from her pocket. It was soft now, ruined past folding. Lucie looked at it and knew.

"They stepped on it," the child said.

"Yes."

Lucie took it anyway, flattening it on her palm with grave care. "He'll make another."

No one corrected her, not because anyone believed it but because the lie was too small and too merciful to waste breath on.

Lucien had already snatched his coat from the peg. He shoved his arms through the sleeves and winced as the cloth caught at the old scar high in his shoulder. Elise saw it because she had seen the skin itself there the night before; there were costs now even in looking. He checked his pockets with quick schoolmaster precision: coins, notebook stub, no weapon worth naming. He never liked pistols. He liked them less in confined places.

Ferrand moved again toward the stair. Lucien stopped him with the same flat hand, harder this time. "If I find you ahead of me," he said, "I'll assume you aren't trying to save him."

Ferrand's grin came back in pieces. "Then you'd better be quick, old man."

Lucien's hand tightened once in the front of Ferrand's shirt, bunching the cloth at the collarbone. Not enough to make a scene of it. Enough that Ferrand felt the strength still left in him. "Stay under the hill," Lucien said.

Then he let go and turned away before Ferrand could choose a response that would keep his dignity intact.

Elise followed Lucien up the passage at a run. Behind them the ration room was already changing shape around the absence: Clemence dragging Lucie back toward bed, Ferrand swearing low and venomous, the lamp hiss, the chalky scrape of someone's boot on stone. Ahead lay the wine-shop stairs and after them the city, indecently alive in the dark.

When they came out aboveground, night had not made Paris quieter. It had only changed the register. Crockery rang from a cafe. A hand-organ wheezed somewhere near the barrier. Laughter spilled from a door and was cut off when it shut. The air smelled of dust, beer, hot horse, and the last cherries turning sweet in the market baskets. Abel was out in it with gelignite warm against his leg and a pistol in his belt and nineteen years' worth of shame, vanity, hunger, and love all looking for one place to detonate.

Lucien coughed once on the pavement, bent, mastered it, and pointed toward the split of the road without speaking. Elise took the other side by instinct. Neither of them touched the other. They moved fast enough that anyone watching might have thought they were late for something ordinary, and that was the worst part. The city kept its evening, and Elise knew it would have the stalls up by morning, while somewhere inside it a boy with burned fingertips went looking for a blast he could mistake for courage.
