# Chapter 13

The first light Elise caught was not from the broken upper room but from the cellar slit at road level, a blade of yellow moving across the dust whenever someone passed in front of a lamp below. The disused tollhouse near Porte d'Orleans stood just off the press of traffic with one shutter hanging loose and the old octroi plaque gone black with soot, as if the building had already been struck once and left to wait for the second blow. The road was still thick. Carts coming in from the southern villages stood wheel to wheel at the barrier, and people on foot kept sliding through the gaps as if impatience could thin stone or timber. A woman with a rabbit trussed by the hind legs had her basket hooked into the crook of one elbow and a sleeping child on the other hip. Two apprentices in flour-dusted sleeves were sharing a heel of bread. A carter sat on his shaft and scratched the neck of his horse with the tenderness of a priest touching a relic. The last of the day had gone the color of old brass.

Lucien stopped in the lee of a wall to get his breath without announcing that he needed to. Sweat had darkened the collar of his shirt. The newspaper parcel in his coat pocket had bled through; she could smell apricots before she saw the dark stain. He had carried the apricots all day and had still not thrown them away.

"It's him," Elise said.

The tollhouse ought to have been dark. Nobody used it now except boys taking pisses in bad weather and men who wanted a place where no one respectable would follow.

Lucien put one hand on the wall. His cough stayed where it lived these days, just behind the breastbone, and did not come out. "If Ferrand is with him, he won't have come to talk sense."

"He didn't come for sense in the first place."

Another sound reached them through the road noise. Not speech yet. A hollow thud from underfoot. Wood striking stone. Someone moving a crate or a box and trying not to let it sound like work.

Elise looked toward the barrier again. More bodies had filled the approach in the last minute alone. A seamstress with her hat pinned crooked was arguing over a fare with a cabman. A girl of perhaps twelve had a bundle of cress tucked under her arm and kept licking salt from her thumb. A municipal clerk in sleeve garters came out from a wine shop and paused to piss in the weeds behind a post. Ferrand had chosen the place as carefully as any accountant balancing columns. There was enough traffic for dead strangers, enough official stone for symbolism, and enough police within shouting distance to certify the lesson afterward.

A whistle sounded far off to the north. Short. Professional. Not alarm yet. Placement.

Lucien heard it too. His face went bare, stripped of anything a room might once have mistaken for patience. "They're closing."

Elise felt the old arithmetic fall into place with a speed that made her sick. The whistles were too early for chance and too well spaced for some ordinary street quarrel. Ferrand had not brought Abel here merely to let him perform a fit of grief in private. He had chosen the building, chosen the hour, chosen the road, and someone on the police side had chosen how long to wait before tightening the ring. Vautrin was not hurrying to stop a bomb. He was hurrying to inherit one.

"Back door," she said.

They went through weeds and broken crockery behind the tollhouse, keeping low where the wall had collapsed into a nettled ditch. The rear entrance had lost its door years ago. A strip of warped plank leaned across the opening and scraped softly when Lucien eased it aside. Inside, the old office still held the ghost of official life: a nailed counter, a shelf stained by spilled ink, rust marks where a stove had once stood, tax numbers chalked on a wall and half rubbed out by weather and boys' shoulders. Dust lay over everything except the fresh tracks leading inward, boot soles marked in pale crescents on the floorboards.

The cellar stair yawned open at the back.

Elise heard Ferrand before she saw him.

"I'm telling you the timing matters," he said, voice low and coaxing. "If you plant it too soon, you've only frightened a clerk. Too late and the road thins. You want the carts stalled. You want the whole gate bunged up like a bottle."

Abel answered from deeper down. "Stop saying you. I know what I want."

"Do you? Good. Then stop trembling and prove it."

There was a silence thick enough for Elise to hear the road again through the cellar slit: harness chains, a horse blowing through its nose, somebody laughing because they had not yet been taught the shape of the next five minutes.

Lucien stepped forward and she caught his sleeve. His shoulder under her hand was hard with the effort of holding himself still.

"If he sees you first," she whispered, "he'll hear a father before he hears anything else."

"I'm not his father."

"He thinks you're his father when it suits him."

The words stung him. They were meant to. She would not have said them in any other room. He looked at her once in the bad light, understanding both the cruelty and the use of it, then nodded.

They went down together.

The cellar was not much larger than a laundry closet. Barrel racks lined one wall, most of them empty, and the beams were so low Lucien had to bend. The road-level slit let in a ribbon of dust-gray evening and the passing shadows of wheels and skirts. Ferrand had set a lamp on an overturned crate. Beside it lay the satchel Abel had stolen, its mouth open just enough to show waxed paper, fuse cord, and the dull metal glint of the pistol butt. The chemical smell rose out of it, sweet and wrong.

Abel stood by the stair with the pistol already in his hand. He had changed his coat since morning, or perhaps stolen another one in haste. This one was too broad in the shoulder and too long in the sleeve; when he lifted the gun, the cuff slid down over the base of his thumb. His face had narrowed in the last two days. Under the lamp he looked younger than nineteen and more used up than some men of forty.

Ferrand crouched by the crate with a length of fuse between his fingers as if he were cutting sausage. He turned at the sound of their steps and smiled at once, easy as a host whose guests had come sooner than expected.

"So you've come after all," he said. "I was wondering whether the schoolmaster would bring his widow or send her to cry somewhere safer."

Lucien did not look at him. "Abel."

The boy's mouth moved before any sound came out. Elise saw the struggle with hope and pride pass over his face so quickly it was almost indecent to witness. He had wanted Lucien to come. He had also wanted to refuse him. Those wants stood in him side by side like men with knives.

"Don't," Abel said. "Don't talk to me as if this is another lesson."

"Then don't make me watch you die for Ferrand's amusement."

Ferrand gave a short laugh. "You always did know how to start with the grave."

Elise kept her eyes on the satchel. Three steps from Ferrand. Two from Abel. The cellar slit showed a cart wheel crawling past at the height of her shoulder. If the charge went off in the road, that wheel would become spokes in somebody's face.

"He didn't bring it here for you," she said to Abel.

Ferrand clicked his tongue. "No? Who did I bring it for, then?"

"For the papers. For the police report. For every man who wants a dead quarter to hold up in the Chamber."

Abel's eyes jerked to her. "What are you saying?"

She heard Lucien take one careful breath. He knew enough now about the holes in her life to hear danger in the confidence of her voice, but this was not the moment for him to spend that knowledge.

"I'm saying he picked this place because he doesn't care who goes into the account," Elise said. "You, the clerk upstairs, the woman with the rabbit, the child asleep in the cart. It all counts the same once it's printed."

Abel flicked a glance toward the slit window before he could stop himself. Through it the rabbit woman's skirt passed in a blur of dark cloth. A child coughed. The sound was so ordinary it seemed to belong to another world.

Ferrand rose slowly, fuse still in hand. His grin did not leave, but it tightened at the corners. "Listen to her. She sees a crowd and suddenly discovers a conscience."

"I had one before you did."

"Then you've wasted it."

Lucien moved down one more step. "Abel, look at me."

"I'm looking."

"No. You're looking at the part of this you'll remember later, and you've chosen the wrong bit. Look at him." Lucien tipped his chin toward Ferrand. "He was hungry for a bomb before Nadir was in irons."

At Nadir's name the boy's grip on the pistol changed. Not tighter. Worse. The barrel dipped a fraction, then came up again with a tremor running through it.

"Don't say his name as if you've done right by him."

"I didn't say I had."

"You told us to wait."

"I told you not to do their work for them."

Abel's laugh broke in the middle. "Their work? They took him in daylight. They put hands on him in front of half the street and you wanted patience, and bread counted, and a meeting, and one more speech about not giving them what they wanted." He took a step down from the stair, toward Lucien, then checked himself when Ferrand shifted behind him. "Do you know what he said to me that morning? He said the line was crooked on the broadside because I was composing too fast. That was it. That was the last thing. And now he's in some room with them breaking him apart and you still want me to behave."

Lucien's face emptied further. "I want you not to kill a laundress and an apprentice baker for the Prefecture's convenience."

"You don't know who'll die."

"He does," Elise said.

Ferrand stopped smiling.

That, more than anything, frightened her. The charm had not dropped in panic. It had simply become unnecessary. Underneath sat impatience, dull and workmanlike, the contempt of a man forced to wait while other people had feelings in the way of his work.

"Careful," he said. "You'll say more than you mean."

"I've been saying less than I mean for months."

Lucien shot her one sharp look at that, but Abel was faster.

"What does that mean?" he demanded. He had gone pale in a way the lamp made meaner, green at the mouth. "How does she know what he knows?"

Ferrand spread one hand. "Ask her. I'm all curiosity myself."

Elise felt the room narrow another inch. Not from the walls. From time. The whistle sounded again outside, nearer now, answered from somewhere off to the side road. Ferrand heard it and turned his head the smallest amount, like a dog pricking at a familiar call. He knew exactly how much company was on its way.

Abel heard it too. Some part of him understood then, or nearly did. "Who did you tell?"

"No one you need fret over," Ferrand said.

That answer did more than any accusation could have done. Abel stared at him. Hurt crossed his face nakedly, too quick for performance. It was the face of a boy who had given away the last money in his pocket because an older man laughed with him and called him comrade.

Elise stepped into it before Ferrand could plaster over the crack.

"He told the police enough to get them here," she said. "He wanted a road full of witnesses and uniforms close enough to say what happened before anyone else could."

"You lying bitch," Ferrand said, almost mildly.

That word, in his mouth, was less violent than the calm around it.

Abel swung the pistol toward Elise so fast Lucien swore and reached out. The muzzle stopped level with her chest. Up close the gun looked ugly in Abel's narrow hand. His wrist still carried the unfinished look of youth, and the printer's burns on his fingertips showed black against the steel.

"How do you know that?" he said.

She did not move. Behind him Ferrand's right hand had already dropped toward the crate, toward the matches and the cap and the prepared length of fuse.

"Because men like him are always easier to read when bodies are involved," she said. "Because he has never once talked about escape. Because every time he says cause he means audience."

Ferrand gave up the pretense all at once. "Audience is the cause, you little fool. What did you think you were doing here? Writing a sonnet? A bomb in an empty ditch is nothing. A bomb at a gate is a city. That's how you get them to answer."

"By blowing open washerwomen?" Lucien said.

"By blowing open France, if you're lucky."

"That's not France." Elise flicked her eyes toward the slit. "That's supper."

Abel did look then. The cart wheel had moved on. In its place passed the hem of a striped skirt, a pair of muddy clogs, a man's hand dangling a string of onions. Not symbols. Purchases. Hunger. He saw them and his face changed again. Confusion hit him with such force it almost counted as pain.

Ferrand saw it too, and contempt rose cleanly through his voice. "Don't start getting tender now. Tenderness is what they beat into you before they ask for names."

"Shut up," Abel said, still looking toward the road.

"No. You're too near the thing now for one of Lucien's sermons. You light it, you put it under the sill, and you let the quarter hear what was done to your friend. If you die after, so much the better. Boys don't get remembered for hesitating."

There it was. Not martyrdom as belief, but martyrdom as copy. Ferrand wanted noise and a young face to pin beside it. Abel's grief was merely the handier fuel.

Lucien came down the final step. "Abel, put the pistol down."

"Don't come closer."

"I'm close. That's already happened." Lucien's voice had shortened with the rest of him. No schoolroom braid to it now, only stripped wood. "Listen to me. Nadir would have mocked this room before he crossed it. He'd have called the beam rotten and the plan lazy, and he'd have been right. He knew the price of paper. He knew the price of names. He didn't teach you the cases so you could blow a horse to pieces and call it fidelity."

Abel's eyes filled so suddenly Elise thought for one absurd second that he might sob. He did not. He bared his teeth instead.

"You don't know what he'd call anything now."

"No," Lucien said. "That's why you don't get to speak for him with powder."

Ferrand had stopped pretending not to move. His fingers were on the fuse. Elise saw the scratch of the matchbox against the crate as clearly as if he had struck it already.

"Abel," she said.

His gaze snapped back to her, and because he was frightened he came at her with anger.

"What?"

"If he lights it, he'll leave you here with it."

"He won't."

Ferrand's mouth twitched. That tiny thing told her more than any confession.

"He already has," she said.

The match rasped.

Lucien lunged for Ferrand at the same moment Abel jerked the pistol toward Elise's face. Elise drove straight into him, catching his gun wrist with both hands as the shot went off. The report slammed around the cellar like a kicked door. Splinters burst from the beam above her head. Abel cried out in shock more than pain, and the pistol skidded half free in his grip.

Ferrand cursed as Lucien hit him shoulder first, driving him into the crate. The lamp slewed sideways. Flame licked high, then steadied. The satchel tipped and spilled one wrapped stick against Elise's shoe.

Abel was lighter than she had expected and harder to hold. Panic had made him all elbow and knee. He twisted like something caught in a trap and not yet aware it was dying. She smelled sweat, ink, and the cheap wool of the stolen coat. His sleeve slid over her hand and for an instant she had skin, then cloth, then skin again. He tried to wrench his arm free and she drove the heel of her palm into his wrist until the gun fell and rang against stone.

"Let go," he shouted, voice breaking. "Let go of me."

"No."

He struck at her with his free hand. His printer-burned fingers scraped across her cheek and then, in blind desperation, found the inside of her left wrist and bit down through cuff and skin. Pain shot bright and useless through her arm. She slammed her forehead into his mouth. His head hit the wall. Blood started out of his lip at once.

To her left Ferrand had got one arm clear. Lucien was hauling at him, coughing now, one bad breath after another, but Ferrand still had the fuse in his hand and the stump of a lit match between his fingers. He brought the flame down toward the cap with a care that would have suited a clerk totaling figures.

Elise saw the movement and knew all at once that there was no world left in which she could keep everyone alive. The only choice still open was arithmetic.

The satchel lay half under the crate, half free, strap looped toward the stair. If Ferrand got it up into the room above or under the road-side sill, the people outside would take the force at chest height. If it went here, below the floor, the stone might eat enough of it to spare most of them.

She hooked the strap with her foot and kicked.

The satchel slid across the damp floor toward the space beneath the stair, bumping against Abel's shin. He gave a strangled sound and dropped toward it at once, instinct stronger than reason now, whether from zeal or terror she could not tell. She went with him, still locked on his arm. They hit the packed earth under the steps hard enough to jar her teeth. The bag jammed against a post, heavy and soft with packed cylinders.

Ferrand saw where it had gone and lost the last of his composure.

"Move," he snarled at Lucien.

Lucien caught his coat, but Ferrand tore free, leaving a seam in his sleeve. The newspaper parcel tore out of Lucien's pocket. Cherries spilled over the stone, split under Ferrand's boot, and filled the cellar with a sudden sweet smell so wrong in that place it might have been obscenity. He slammed into the stair rail, recovered, and dropped toward the satchel with the smoking fuse clenched between finger and thumb.

"Abel," Lucien shouted.

The boy looked up.

That was the terrible thing. He looked up. For one second the cellar ceased to be a stage in his head and became exactly what it was: rotten beam, split fruit, damp earth, the man he loved bent over and gray with exertion, the man who had flattered him reaching over him with contempt naked on his face, the woman he half wanted to shoot gripping him so hard she shook. He saw it. Elise knew he saw it because shame went through him like a knife.

"I didn't," he said, not finishing whatever sentence had come for him.

Ferrand drove an elbow into Lucien's chest and came on.

Elise threw herself over Abel and the satchel both.

She did not do it out of tenderness. She did it because his body was between the charge and the stair and because there was no time left for gentler arrangements. Abel gave one furious animal cry and tried to buck her off. The bag shifted under her hip. Ferrand's hand closed on the strap. Lucien caught Ferrand around the neck from behind. The fuse spat sparks onto canvas. Somebody's heel hammered against wood. Dust came down from the underside of the steps in little white patters, delicate as flour.

Then the world did not become light. It became pressure.

The blast punched the air out of her chest and drove stone into her mouth. The stair above her cracked like wet timber under an axe. Something hard struck the side of her head. Something hot went through her thigh and stayed there. For an instant she knew nothing except the weight of Abel's ribs under her forearm and the astonishing softness of the satchel giving way beneath them. Then the beam above broke and the cellar filled with chalk, splinters, lamp oil, dirt, and a silence so complete she thought she had gone deaf forever.

She had not. Sound came back as a thin iron whine, and under it a horse screaming outside.

Elise tried to breathe and could not tell whether the obstruction was inside her chest or lying over her. The air was white with dust. It tasted of old lime and burst wood and blood. She coughed mud onto her own hand and saw that the hand was hers only because of the burn scar under the muck.

"Lucien?"

She could not hear her own voice. It came to her as a shape in her skull.

Something moved to her right. A figure dragged itself through the fallen stair, bent nearly double. Lucien. His hair and shoulders were white with dust, making him look suddenly ancient, quarried from the same stone as the room. Blood ran from a cut over one eyebrow, thin as ink. He was coughing with his whole body but still moving.

He dropped to his knees beside her and put both hands under her arms. His mouth formed her name. The first time she did not hear it. The second time the ringing let him through.

"Elise. Elise. Can you move?"

She tried. Pain took hold of her left thigh with a steadiness more frightening than any stab. Not a flare. A lodged fact. She looked down and saw the torn skirt, the dark wetness, the jag of rusted iron or broken hoop sunk through cloth and flesh just above the knee. Blood had already glued chalk dust into a pink paste around it.

Abel was still partly under her.

She rolled enough to see him and wished, uselessly and too late, that she had not.

He had not become anything grand. One side of his head was laid open above the ear where the stair timber had come down. His eyes were open but not seeing. The coat sleeve still covered half his hand. At the cuff, two fingertips showed with the old printer's burns intact, black marks on a hand that had gone the color of wax. His mouth was full of white grit and blood. He might have been a boy dragged out of a chalk pit after trying to hide there. Nothing in him resembled a legend.

Lucien saw where she was looking and closed Abel's eyes with two fingers that left tracks in the dust.

He did it fast, almost angrily, because the police whistles were shrilling now from the road and grief had no room large enough to stand upright in.

Ferrand lay nearer the stair. At first Elise thought the blast had thrown a bundle of rags there. Then the bundle moved. He had lost his moustache to fire on one side and most of his cheek with it. One hand, blackened to the wrist, still clutched the burned nub of fuse as if paperwork might yet be completed. He tried to pull himself forward and got only as far as smearing blood and apricot pulp across the dirt.

Lucien rose to his feet and swayed. Ferrand looked up at him with one working eye. Whatever he meant to say came out wet and shapeless. Lucien did not answer. He kicked the pistol away from reach and bent back to Elise.

From the road came screams, but not the long tearing chorus that would have followed a full blast under the crowd. Individual voices. A horse in blind pain. A woman crying out for her son. Men shouting for water, for room, for a doctor, each in his own throat. The floor had buckled under the slit window and showered the road with timber, glass, and dust. It had not ripped the line of carts open. The stone had taken the worst of it. The tollhouse had kept its dead mostly to itself.

Ferrand made a sound then, a low gargle full of hatred. He was trying to drag his ruined body toward the daylight.

Elise found enough breath to speak. "Don't let him out."

Lucien looked at Ferrand. Then at the way the cellar wall had split, showing the road through a new crack as wide as a hand. Then at the blood on Elise's skirt.

"He isn't going anywhere."

Ferrand's one eye moved between them, still calculating. Even now. Even with his jaw hanging and the skin of his hand gone. Elise understood with cold disgust that if he had possessed one more breath clean enough for words, he would have spent it on the story rather than on pain. He had wanted a useful atrocity. The blast had killed him in the middle of his own accounting.

His burned hand opened. The stub of fuse slipped free. After that he did not move again.

Boots thundered above.

"Here," a man shouted. "Behind. Behind the house."

Another whistle answered almost at once, so close now it seemed to come from inside the broken wall.

Lucien dropped beside her again. "Listen to me."

She turned her head. Dust cracked at the corner of her mouth.

"I need you up."

"Abel?"

The question was stupid and he treated it as if it were not.

"Gone."

She looked past him at the boy's hand where the sleeve had fallen back. At nineteen the bones were still too fine. He had spent half the book trying to widen himself with rhetoric and bad company. Death had stripped him back down to apprenticeship.

"The road?" she asked.

Lucien listened. A wagon wheel groaned outside. Somebody sobbed and kept sobbing. No general panic. No collapsing wall of bodies. Not what Ferrand had wanted.

"Still standing," he said. "Come on."

He snapped the iron out of her thigh before she could brace. The pain was so large it erased the room. When it shrank again she was already biting the heel of her hand to keep from crying out. Lucien tore a strip from the torn inner lining of his coat and rammed it hard against the wound. His fingers shook once and then steadied. The smell of crushed apricots was stronger now, sticky and almost rotten under the chalk.

"You bastard," she said, not sure whether she meant him for the iron or Ferrand for everything.

"Save it," he said, and the dryness of it was so nearly his old voice that she could have wept if there had been any moisture left in her.

He got one arm around her back and hauled her upright. Her bad leg folded at once. He swore then, the word harsh from him because it came so rarely, and turned so that she leaned almost full against him. The bullet scar in his left shoulder must have been screaming. He did not mention it. The broken seam of his coat gaped, and half of the newspaper parcel still clung inside the pocket with crushed apricots mashed into the torn paper.

Above them the rear room filled with running feet.

"Back," Elise said. "Through the ditch."

He nodded and half dragged, half carried her toward the collapsed wall at the rear where the blast had opened a new way out. Something settled in the wreckage behind them. She did not look back again.

The night outside hit her like bad water, warm and full of noise. The back lot was choked with nettles and broken tiles. Lucien got her over the fallen stones one step at a time while the front of the tollhouse erupted in authority. Lantern light swung wild across the road. Men shouted for the area to be cleared, then immediately crowded it themselves. Through the weeds Elise saw the rabbit woman crouched in the gutter with her child under her body and both of them alive. The rabbit had torn loose and was lying in the road with its legs still tied. The carter's horse was down on one knee, flank scored by glass, rolling its eye white. The flour-sleeved apprentices were on their faces beside a wheel, one of them moving, one only stunned. Blood, yes. Cuts. Panic. Not the butcher's table Ferrand had budgeted for.

Lucien got her into the ditch and down among the reeds.

A sergeant's voice carried over the rest, practiced and already arranging the scene. "Bomb attack at the gate. Anarchists. Seal the road. Nobody leaves."

Nobody had yet gone into the cellar to count who was dead.

Elise almost laughed. The sound that came out was blood and spit.

Lucien put one hand over her mouth, not roughly, just enough. His palm smelled of dust, sweat, and fruit.

"Can you be quiet?"

She nodded.

They moved bent double along the ditch behind the wall while the road filled with uniforms. More whistles. More boots. Somebody shouted for a stretcher. Somebody else shouted for names. A witness began speaking too fast and was cut off by another voice, official and smoother, instructing him what he must have seen. Elise knew that tone better than she knew some prayers. It did not gather facts. It allocated them.

At the corner of the lot Lucien had to stop and cough. He turned away from her to do it and blood spotted the weeds. Not much. Enough.

"Lucien."

"Walk."

"You're bleeding."

"So are you."

Even now the answer carried the old schoolmaster's annoyance at wasted observation. It steadied her more than comfort would have done. She put her arm harder around his shoulders and forced the bad leg to take a fraction of her weight. Fire ran up from the wound. The world tilted, corrected, tilted again.

Behind them the tollhouse had started to burn in earnest. The cellar fire found old timber and lamp oil and climbed. Orange light licked up through the broken floor. In that light she could see the road crowd breaking into pieces under police hands, witnesses turned into file material before the smoke had even thinned.

They made the far end of the ditch and the shadow of the wall beyond it. Lucien lowered her there for one breath. Her skirt stuck wetly to her thigh. The hem felt heavier on one side, but pain flattened every smaller sensation into irrelevance.

"Can you manage the lane?" he asked.

"If I can't, you'll drag me."

"That was always the plan."

She looked at him. Chalk had settled in his eyelashes. Blood from his brow cut a clean line down the dust on his face. He was alive. That fact landed in her with a force almost as destabilizing as the blast.

"I should've told you everything," she said.

The words came out before prudence could stop them. Pain had burned through the careful ledgers in which she usually kept herself. For a moment she hated that loss of discipline more than the wound.

Lucien closed his eyes once, briefly, as if taking a measurement.

"Not here," he said.

He got her back up.

They took the lane south, away from the first cordon. Twice they had to flatten themselves against walls while police ran past toward the glow. Once a woman with a torn sleeve and soot on her cheek stared straight at Elise's bloody dress and then, out of some exhausted instinct for class solidarity or simple refusal, looked away. No one wanted more of the night than the night had already handed out.

By the time the whistles thinned behind them, Elise could feel the blood cooling on her leg and turning tacky. Every step pulled at the wound and at something deeper, some binding she had kept tight for months that had now come loose and would not retie to its old shape. Abel dead in the cellar. Ferrand dead with the fuse in his hand. The crowd spared the worst and still marked. The police already calling it what they needed it to be.

From far back at the gate came the first clear word hurled through a speaking trumpet of authority: conspiracy.

No one had looked long enough to know.

She leaned harder into Lucien and kept moving while the road behind them filled with the story Vautrin had wanted all along.
