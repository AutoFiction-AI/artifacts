# Chapter 3

Before anyone called for linen, Elise had already decided which names Vautrin would not get. The quarry had no honest morning, only a thinning of dark and the first sounds of other bodies returning to themselves. Water tapped somewhere beyond the white wall in a patient rhythm that made sleep feel borrowed. Someone turned in blankets two alcoves over. Someone coughed into wool and swallowed the rest. She lay on her side with the pencil stub warm in her palm and built the report sentence by sentence, lining each clause up straight while the filth stayed filth.

Vautrin had not asked for impressions. He had asked for use. She could hear the word in his voice even underground, the flat weight of it, the way he made a human life sound like a tool laid back in a drawer. She had enough now to be useful. She could give him the wine-shop cellar on the rue de la Tombe-Issoire. She could give him Lucien Delatour, schoolmaster, organizer, the man the room bent around without appearing to bow. She could give him the hand press and the sleeping gallery and the ration room with its stacked brown loaves and sour smell of damp flour. She could give him the fact that no bomb materials had yet crossed her hands, only pamphlets, broth, chalk, cough medicine, and work.

What she could not give him cleanly was the shape of the place as it actually lived. A schoolroom meant children. A ration room meant somebody counted. Shirts came up from the gallery rank with sweat and quarry dust and came back folded. Somebody boiled soap, somebody fought the arithmetic of bread, somebody knew which blanket belonged over which narrow shoulders before the night cold settled. If she wrote Clemence Rigal beside the laundry line, Vautrin would not read laundress. He would read leverage. If she wrote Lucie, age ten, he would read door left unbarred.

She waited until the graphite point stopped trembling against the paper. Then she wrote in the smallest serviceable hand she had, the kind clerks skimmed past until it turned up in court. She wrote that Delatour was confirmed in residence and exercised authority over instruction and printed matter. She wrote that the primary entrance remained the wine-shop cellar and that at least one deeper chamber held active press equipment. She wrote that the refuge housed more people than a single conspiratorial crew and likely served as a transit point as well as a workshop. She wrote that no explosives had yet been seen and that time was required to identify the full supply chain.

For the domestic arrangements she wrote one line in the same small hand: Daily maintenance appears collective.

Then she left the next line empty, where Clemence Rigal belonged. Lucie did not appear at all.

She folded the paper into a strip no wider than a matchbox and slid it into the lining of her glove. The glove had split at one fingertip, and the rough edge snagged on the place inside her left wrist where old burn skin stayed shiny and pale. She sucked air between her teeth, more from the memory than the sting. Above her, from the passage mouth, Clemence's steps came down brisk and irritated, heels hard on stone.

"If you're awake, you can carry," Clemence said before she was fully in view.

She came into the gallery buttoning the same dark skirt she wore for work aboveground, a bent hairpin clenched between her teeth while she counted sheets by touch. She did not need much light for linen. Her hands went over the folded pile once, and she separated hotel cotton from private shirts, then shirts from diapers, then the better collars from the men who sweated through them like they were trying to punish the cloth. Soap had taken the top layer off her knuckles years ago. The skin there stayed pink and blunt, filed rough by years of soap.

"I was awake," Elise said.

"I can see that. Get the blue basket. Not that one, the handles are going."

Lucie popped up behind her mother with one braid finished and the other already escaping, a dented tobacco tin tucked under her arm. She kept clothespins in it and sorted them by size, bent ones exiled to one corner of the tin. The bent ones were unlucky. She had explained that to Elise the night before with complete seriousness, then lined the crooked pins along the edge of her blanket so they would not spoil the straight ones.

"I counted thirty-two," Lucie said. "But two of them don't bite anymore."

"Then they're not clothespins, they're firewood." Clemence took the tin, shook it once by her ear, and handed it back. "Stay where I can see you today."

Lucie looked at Elise instead of answering. "Are you coming up too?"

"That's what your mother just told me."

"Good. You walk faster."

Clemence snorted. "That's because she hasn't been bent over washboards half her life."

The trust was still that narrow. She was a second pair of hands, someone who could be sent aboveground if watched and kept below if not. Two days earlier Clemence had weighed every answer Elise gave down to the gram. Now she was trusting her with linen and daylight. Elise had done worse things for less.

They climbed out through the wine-shop cellar while the barrels above them sweated sour red into the boards. Dawn had become a wet gray morning. The street stones held last night's rain in their seams, and the city smelled rinsed but not cleaned. Elise always forgot, in the quarry, how much Paris moved before breakfast. Cart wheels were already scuffing along the ruts. A baker's boy ran past with flour on his sleeve and a mouth full of song. Somewhere farther off a tram bell knocked at the air like a spoon against a pan.

The walk to the laundry yard took them along the Bievre where the river narrowed itself into an ugly working thing, more drain than water in places, cluttered with planks, dye runoff, and the stale breath of wet wool. Clemence worked there irregularly when the quarry's front addresses could spare the risk. She took in whatever came: hotel sheets, aprons from the cheap eating houses, shirts from clerks who pretended not to know where their laundry went as long as it came back white enough to fasten at the throat. Today she had a bundle from a widow who let rooms to medical students and paid late but paid.

Steam was already lifting from the washhouse sheds when they arrived. The yard rang with the slap of cloth against boards, women's voices raised over one another without ever quite becoming talk, and the cough of the river under the pilings. Elise helped unload the basket, set the bundled shirts under an awning, and kept her hand from touching the glove where the report waited. She had used this dead drop twice before on other errands, other names. Vautrin liked places that stank. Men with polished boots did not linger near privies or drainage runs unless they had business too ugly to admit.

Clemence untied the first sheet and frowned at a collar stain that had survived a previous wash. "These people think bluing is magic."

"Maybe it's magic," Lucie said. "It makes yellow lie."

"Only until the next wear."

Elise lifted a soap block from the basket. "Do we need more?"

"If you can get common lye and not be cheated, yes. If not, come back with what you have and I'll do the cheating myself."

It was permission and instruction both. Elise took the coin Clemence flicked at her, tucked the soap under her arm, and crossed the yard without haste. The privies stood against the far wall beside a cracked drainpipe that emptied into the river ditch. The stone there stayed perpetually wet, green in the joints. She stepped inside the nearest stall, bolted the door, and listened.

Outside, the washhouse went on beating and steaming. A child laughed. Somebody cursed a missing apron. No second set of feet paused at the door.

Elise pulled off the glove. The paper strip had softened slightly with the heat of her hand. She slid it into the broken mouth of the drainpipe, pushing until her fingernail could no longer feel it and only the cool slime of the masonry remained. If the pickup man did his work, the note would be gone by noon. If he had been rolled up already, or watched, or bought, the note would sit there like a confession tucked inside rot.

She kept her hand in the crack one second longer than necessary.

The missing lines rang in her head because she knew their proper form: Clemence Rigal, laundress, quartermaster, reads slowly but counts exactly; child present, female, approximately ten; names, ages, uses, points of pressure. She withdrew her hand and wiped the moisture on the underside of her skirt. The cloth came away streaked green.

When she stepped back into the yard the light had strengthened. Steam moved in torn white sheets between the sheds. Lucie was on an upturned crate, pinning a chemise to the line with elaborate care, tongue caught in the corner of her mouth. Clemence stood over a tub with both forearms buried to the elbow, working a shirt collar against the board with a violence that belonged partly to the stain and partly to the life around it.

"He wanted starch but paid for soap," she said when Elise returned. "That's the sort of man he is."

"Did you get it?" Lucie asked.

Elise held up the lye.

"Show me the coin back," Clemence said.

Elise put the remaining copper in her palm. Clemence counted without looking down, simply by weight against wet skin, then grunted approval.

For a quarter hour nothing happened except work. Elise rinsed sheets in brown water that went colder the longer her hands stayed in it. Lucie sorted pins and repeated yesterday's spelling lesson under her breath, not because anybody had asked but because she liked to say the difficult words twice once she owned them. "Republique," she murmured, then frowned. "That q shouldn't get to hide there. It's lazy."

"You'll tell the Academy when they come for inspection," Clemence said.

"I will."

Elise almost smiled. The morning had that treacherous quality some bad days had, the brief appearance of an ordinary shape before the blow arrived. A drover shouted at a mule. Two women argued over whose boiler had been moved. The river stink thickened as the heat rose from the vats. Across the lane a delivery boy flirted with a washerwoman twice his age and got laughed back into adolescence.

Then Elise saw the boots.

There was not one pair but four, then six, placed too evenly. Two uniformed agents came in from the north end of the lane while a closed police wagon rattled into view behind them, its horse blowing hard through the bit. At the south entrance plainclothes men were already taking their positions, hats low, hands free. One of them kept a clerk's stoop until he turned and the cut of his coat showed the room it allowed for a truncheon. No brawl had brought them, and no theft complaint had either. This was a net.

The yard had not understood it yet. That was the advantage a sweep always wanted, the few stupidly full seconds when daily life kept going on its own momentum.

Elise did not think. She put the half-rinsed sheet into Clemence's hands and said, low and quick, "Leave the tubs. Take the child."

Clemence stared at her. "What?"

"Look at the street."

The first shout went up then. It was not panic yet, just authority sharpened for public use.

"Nobody leaves. Papers out. Hands where I can see them."

Another wagon rolled into place beyond the first. One of the plainclothes men pointed toward the wash sheds, and three agents broke that way at once, not running, because men who already owned the ground did not need to hurry.

Clemence's face changed without losing its hardness. Fear made her more exact, not less. Her eyes cut from the north wagon to the south entrance, then to Lucie on the crate. "How many?"

"Enough." Elise took Lucie down by the waist before the girl could ask the wrong question out loud. "Get her shawl. Bring the basket and nothing else."

"Those sheets aren't mine," Clemence said, still looking at the tubs. "If they go missing I pay."

"If you stay for them, you pay in prison."

The sentence landed. Clemence snatched the shawl off the line, wrapped it over Lucie's shoulders, and hooked the basket over her arm. One of the women at the far boiler had started to protest loudly, asking what law had been broken, whose order this was, what right they had to frighten paying customers away. The answer came as a hand on her sleeve and another on the back of her neck. She yelped when her face hit the damp wall.

The whole yard lurched awake at once.

A man bolted from the lane and was clubbed across the mouth before he made three steps. Somebody screamed for her boy. Wet shirts slid off lines into the mud. The horse on the first wagon threw its head, whites showing, while an agent cursed and yanked the reins short. The steam from the sheds trapped the shouts and flung them back. It sounded for one awful moment like the yard itself had started yelling.

Elise saw the pattern because Vautrin had taught her to see it and because once learned it stayed in the bones. They always closed the obvious exits first and checked the river side last. Water penned people. Fences did half the work. The gap would remain by the dye shed until the rear team came through the cooper's court.

"This way," she said.

Clemence caught her arm. "How do you know?"

"Move."

It was too sharp. She heard it herself. So did Clemence. There was no time to soften it.

Elise shoved a dripping sheet into Clemence's free hand. "Over the basket. Bend your head. You're angry at me because I ruined the wash, understand?"

"I don't need acting lessons."

"Good."

She put Lucie between them and drove them toward the dye shed with the purposeful, furious gait of a woman on the edge of a quarrel. The wet sheet hung over Clemence's arm like ordinary labor. Lucie's feet slapped the boards inside shoes already leaking river water. At the mouth of the shed two agents were hauling open a storage chest with so much ceremony it was obvious they expected pamphlets or guns or the one clean proof that would make the whole morning worth the price.

There was a side door beyond them, half blocked by barrels of indigo sludge. Elise had marked it when they came in because she marked all mouths and corners now whether she meant to or not.

"Down," she told Lucie.

The girl ducked instinctively. Elise took the basket from Clemence and banged it hard against a barrel. The lid flew open. Clothespins scattered across the planks and under the men's boots. Lucie gave a small involuntary cry, not for herself but for the pins. One of the agents looked down on reflex, swore, and kicked a straight pin into the sludge.

"Stupid cow," Elise said loudly to Clemence, pitching her voice for the room. "You'd trust a child with anything."

Clemence did not miss the offer. "Then don't drop it next time," she snapped back, full volume, shoving past her with her face set in petty wash-day fury. The move carried all three of them through the side door before the agents had decided whether they mattered.

Outside, the cooper's court stank of wet oak and sour beer. A wagon tongue blocked part of the lane, but the horse had been unhitched and the space under it was open. Beyond that lay a narrow run between fence and wall that led to the tannery gutter and then, if memory held, to the alley behind the chapel storehouse. Elise had used that path once on a different errand. It was not a route a washerwoman would choose unless she had to. Today they had to.

Behind them the yard was breaking into distinct sounds: orders, crying, the smack of bodies against timber, one high furious woman still shouting about her employer's sheets, still naming francs and losses at men who answered only to warrants. It was a better speech than most deputies gave.

Lucie stumbled on the first cobble. Elise caught her by the back of the shawl before she went down in the tannery runoff. The cloth came away slick and cold in Elise's hand.

"Am I meant to run?" Lucie asked, breathless and affronted. "I can't tell if this is running."

"It's moving quickly," Elise said.

"Then yes."

They squeezed between the wagon tongue and the fence. Clemence went first, broad shoulders scraping both sides, the basket jammed against her hip. Elise lifted Lucie under the arms and passed her through, feeling the child's ribs light and birdlike under the wet wool. For one second Lucie clung to her neck without embarrassment, all bone and breath and river chill. Then she was down again, boots skidding.

At the mouth of the next alley a uniform turned the corner.

Elise stopped so hard her soles slid. The agent saw them, hesitated, and looked past them toward the noise from the yard. He had to decide if three wet women counted as quarry or clutter. Elise made the decision for him by hunching over the basket and saying to Clemence, in the flat exhausted voice of labor, "If you've lost the count that's not on me."

Clemence swung round with magnificent hatred. "Of course it's on you. You left the blueing open and then you dropped half my pins. Look at this mud."

She thrust the basket forward. The agent's eyes went to the wet linen, the shawl, Lucie's muddy stockings. His lip curled with the particular contempt respectable men reserved for poor women's work, which was often enough to save them for one more block.

"Clear the alley," he said. "And keep the child quiet."

"Gladly," Clemence said, and brushed past him so close her soaked sleeve left a dark mark on his coat.

They did not run until they had turned twice more and the shouts from the washhouse had thinned into city noise again. Then Clemence caught Lucie up by the wrist and dragged her through a butcher's passage, across a yard where rabbit skins hung like wet gloves, and into the lee of a shuttered coal shed. She let go only when the girl's breathing had turned ragged.

Lucie bent over, hands on her knees. "I lost four pins," she said.

"You can mourn them later," Clemence replied. She was breathing hard herself, but her first act was to look at the basket. "Soap, lye, shawl, two shirts, one sheet. Christ."

The lost laundry cost hit her face more clearly than the fright had, not because she loved linen but because linen turned into rent, into lamp oil, into soup thick enough not to shame a bowl.

Elise leaned against the coal shed and felt the whole route she had just taken throbbing in her hands, in her calves, in the bruise beginning where the basket handle had bitten her palm. She had not realized until then that she was still carrying one clothespin jammed in her fist. It had cut a square mark into the skin.

Clemence saw it. She saw everything when fear had passed and accounts began.

"You moved before they shouted twice," she said.

Elise opened her hand and let the clothespin drop into the basket. "I saw the wagons."

"So did everyone after a second."

"One second matters."

"To some people."

Lucie straightened and wiped her nose on the inside of her shawl. "Were they taking the wash or the people?"

Neither woman answered at once. The silence was long enough that the child looked from one to the other and understood the measure of the lie available to her.

"The people first," Elise said.

Lucie considered that. "Do they wash them in prison?"

"No," Clemence said. "They leave them dirty and call it the law."

She was looking at Elise when she said it. She was not accusing Elise yet, but the line had shifted. The quarry had room for hunger, coughs, politics, even the occasional stolen man. It had very little room for coincidence.

They waited until the lane settled back into ordinary movement. When they finally turned toward the refuge again, Clemence made Lucie walk between them and said almost nothing. At the wine-shop cellar she counted the rescued items aloud under her breath, because counting was the only salvage she had left.

"One sheet. Two shirts. Soap. Lye. Child." She shoved the basket into Elise's hands. "Next time you tell me to leave eight francs behind, you tell me why faster."

"If there's a next time," Elise said.

"There's always a next time for people like us."

Below ground the quarry air took the day's noise out of the ears by degrees. Lucie was sent to the schoolroom to dry her stockings by the stove. Clemence wrung the saved sheet so hard the water ran white with soap into the bucket, then hung it over a rope in the gallery without once looking at Elise. Trust had not broken. It had thinned to transparency.

By late afternoon a chalk mark waited on the underside of the wine-shop stair rail, small enough that anyone else would have mistaken it for scrape or dust. A vertical line crossed once, meaning pickup was complete and Elise was to present herself.

Elise stared at it for a full breath before she climbed on. The message might as well have been Vautrin's hand on the back of her neck.

She told Clemence she was going out for needles and lamp wick. This time Clemence did look at her.

"You've got long errands for a woman with no money," she said.

"Then I'll walk fast."

Clemence wiped both hands on her apron and held the stare a beat too long. "Do that."

The Prefecture offices on the Ile de la Cite always smelled of wet wool, sealing wax, and the human fatigue that paperwork produced when it was backed by cells. Clerks moved bundles of names tied in green ribbon from one room to another with the solemnity of men handling legal truth instead of guesswork sharpened by power. Elise was shown in through a side corridor because Vautrin preferred his deniable instruments to arrive like parcels, not visitors.

He was at his desk with her report laid open beside a tray of other papers. The room had already gone dim enough that the lamp had been lit, and the yellow circle it cast made the polished cuffs of his shirt look theatrical in the sour office light. He had not removed his gloves. He often did not when dealing with informants. Bare hands implied a social category he had no intention of granting.

"Mlle Valette," he said. "Sit down."

When he used her real name he was either pleased or preparing to be disappointed. Today he sounded almost gentle.

Elise sat.

He touched the report with one gloved finger. "This is useful."

There was no comfort in him when he said useful. A dog might have felt praised.

"You have confirmed Delatour, the press, and the cellar entrance. You have even seen fit to note that the refuge serves a larger social function than its operational purpose, at least on present evidence. That is useful. It is also selective." He glanced up. "You write well when you are not sentimental."

"I wasn't aware I'd been sentimental."

"No? Then perhaps merely selective. Do you expect me to believe that a place with bread, lessons, and clean shirts has no one keeping count?"

He slid the page toward her, though not close enough to touch. Her own tight hand looked cleaner on the desk than it had in the privy, more vicious for that reason.

"You give me a schoolroom, instruction, bread, sleeping alcoves, and daily maintenance," Vautrin said, "yet you give me no quartermaster, no laundress, no old men, no children. It is an efficient little miracle you have discovered. Who keeps that place fed, Mlle Valette? Who decides which body gets a blanket and which does not?"

Elise kept her face still. The chair seat was cane and one strand had snapped under the polish, leaving a sharp broken end that pressed through her skirt into the underside of her thigh. She welcomed the pain. It kept the rest of her from showing.

"I've been in place two nights," she said. "I sent what I could stand up in conversation if questioned."

"That is not the same as sending what is there."

"It's the same if you want me to stay there."

Vautrin sat back. He had the heavy-lidded patience of a man who liked making other people spend themselves first. "You are overestimating how delicate your position is and underestimating how tired I am of waiting for the obvious. There is always somebody who boils the water and counts the loaves. There is always somebody whose affection, hunger, or child can be used to loosen a tongue. If you do not identify those persons, I identify the district."

He opened the file beside her report. Inside lay warrants and street lists, each column ruled with such care it looked almost devotional. She saw laundry yards, print addresses, a widow's rooms near Croulebarbe, two wine shops, one rag dealer. Whole scraps of the neighborhood laid out for collection.

"This morning's sweep was not enough?" she asked.

"This morning's sweep was administrative housekeeping." He selected one sheet and signed it without hurry. The nib scratched softly. "What you have given me is structure without hinges. I prefer hinges. They are cheaper. In the absence of those, one takes doors off entire streets until a hand is caught."

He signed a second page.

"You widened it because the report was thin."

"I widened it because I will not permit a refuge of that size to remain illegible." He looked at her again, that cultivated paternal look that made every threat sound like policy. "When you leave me daily maintenance but no laundress, I send men through the laundry yards. When you leave me a schoolroom but no children, I pull lodging books and street lists. When you leave me two wine shops, a rag dealer, and rooms near Croulebarbe, I sign until the blanks acquire surnames."

For a moment the room seemed to tilt toward the desk, toward the signed sheets, toward the green ribbons waiting to bind other names. Elise saw Lucie in the washhouse yard asking if prison washing was different, and the image came not as pity but as a practical picture: the child's wet stockings, the square of mud drying on one knee, the basket lighter by four pins and most of a day's wages. Vautrin would not have bothered with Lucie first if given other leverage. He would have started with the women who worked, the renters, the old men with tabs at the coal merchant. She had known that. She had still hoped to route the damage.

"You wanted proof of a bomb plot," she said.

"I want a case that survives daylight." His voice did not rise. It became more precise instead, each word placed like a stamp on paper. "If Delatour is merely a philanthropic schoolmaster with poor taste in company, tell me that with evidence. If he is housing printers, fugitives, and future bomb men, tell me that with evidence. What I will no longer accept is rooms, bread, and ink without names attached."

He lifted another paper, but this one he did not sign. He let it rest between two fingers. "Charenton has sent a reminder. Twenty-eight francs due this month, and the arrears remain inelegant. I have continued to smooth that difficulty because your work has justified the inconvenience. Do not make me explain to the accounts office that my confidence was misplaced."

There it was: not anger, never the luxury of anger, only accounts.

Elise heard herself answer in the over-clean voice she used when rage needed laundering before speech. "What exactly do you require?"

"I require names, sleeping arrangements, and the domestic authority you have declined to notice. I require any alternate exits and any storage site beyond printed matter. If there are children, you will tell me how many and whose they are. If there are none, you will explain the schoolroom. And if Delatour proves to be no more than a charitable fool, I will make that distinction in the paperwork before he goes to prison. Is that clear?"

"Yes," Elise said.

The phrase was "before he goes to prison." Vautrin had a talent for dropping the essential obscenity into a sentence and letting grammar flatten it.

"You have until the next collection," he said. "Do not send me another elegant half-truth."

He dismissed her with the same hand he had used to sign the warrants.

The corridor outside was crowded now, not with ideologues but with intake. A laundress from some other yard sat on the bench holding her apron bunched in both fists, lips moving as she counted under her breath what must have been missing pieces. A printer's assistant with ink ground into the cuticles stared at the opposite wall while a clerk copied his name wrong twice. A boy no older than the younger printer lads below ground had blood on one cuff where his mouth had split and dried there. The wet wool smell had been joined by soap, fear, cheap tobacco, menstrual iron, horse leather from the wagons outside. The state reduced people to paper only after first taking them through the body.

At the bottom of the steps two agents hauled in another woman still arguing. "Those sheets were on account," she shouted. "If they're spoiled you can pay Madame Sorel yourself, you sons of whores."

One of the agents laughed. The other twisted her arm higher until the curse broke open into a cry.

Elise stopped under the archway because if she kept walking too fast she would look guilty and if she stood too still she would become one more piece of furniture in Vautrin's building. Rain had started again, a thin cold spring rain that darkened the courtyard stones and made the horses stamp. A police wagon waited by the gate with its rear door open. Inside, wet hems and boot soles crowded the floorboards.

By evening the corridor was full of damp aprons, split lips, a printer's assistant with blood dried on his cuff, and women trying to remember what the lost wash would cost.

When she turned back toward Montrouge, the city looked unchanged in the miserable way cities often did. Carts kept moving. Men still bought bread. Bells still struck the hour for whoever believed hours belonged equally to everyone. Under her glove the hand that had pushed the report into the drainpipe still smelled faintly of moss and rot.

By the time she reached the wine-shop cellar, that smell had mixed with soap.
