# Chapter 2

Clemence Rigal brought the morning in one hand and a bucket in the other. When she let the pail strike stone at the mouth of the gallery, the flat iron note ran down the passage and over the sleeping bodies under wool. The quarry never went wholly dark. Somewhere down the passage a lamp kept a low orange bruise on the wall, enough to turn sleeping bodies into mounds of wool and hair silvered by dust. Water dripped in the deeper chambers with patient regularity. Somebody coughed wetly two alcoves away. Far overhead, muffled by yards of stone, carriage wheels passed like distant thunder.

Elise pushed the borrowed blankets off before Clemence had to speak. Limestone grit roughened the corner of her mouth, and the cold had worked halfway through her coat in the night. She counted eight sleepers in the gallery while she folded the blanket Clemence had lent her. There were nine, if the child under the table was not merely a pile of shawls.

Henri Vasseur was almost certainly in a cell by now, if he was still on his feet. Elise let that fact pass through her with the same hard flatness as the cold. Pity did not change bars into doors. It did not cancel what she had done in the street above. She tucked the blanket neatly, because untidiness was the kind of thing people remembered, and rubbed her hands over her sleeves to take the dust off.

"Good," a voice said from the mouth of the gallery. "You're up before I have to drag you."

At the mouth of the gallery, lamp raised, she still looked broad-shouldered enough to block the passage by herself. Her knuckles were roughened white from lye, the skin split in two places and healed badly. She had pinned her hair so tight it seemed to sharpen the planes of her face, but a curl had broken loose and stuck damply to her temple. Elise noticed, absurdly, that Clemence had taken the trouble to tie a faded blue ribbon around the handle of the bucket so Lucie would stop claiming all the pails belonged to the same kingdom.

"Take this to the wash barrel," Clemence said. "Then bring the clean rags from the line. The fever woman's soaked through two dressings in the night, and if you stand there staring much longer you'll owe us rent for the view."

"Where's the barrel?"

"If you can copy a page, you can follow a wall. Left at the saint with no face. Mind your shoulder at the squeeze. And don't put your fingers on anything that looks expensive. We don't own many expensive things, so it isn't hard to learn them all."

She thrust the bucket into Elise's hands and turned away without waiting to see whether Elise obeyed. It was a test, of course. Everything here was a test. The difference was that Clemence's tests smelled of soap, old cabbage, and human exhaustion instead of Vautrin's cologne and office coal.

Elise followed the wall exactly as instructed. The passage narrowed after the first turn until stone brushed both sleeves at once. Moisture pearled on the chalk and soaked through the cuff at her wrist. She kept her breathing even and let her eyes work the space the way she would have worked a stairwell aboveground: lamp niche, blind corner, crack in the floor large enough to catch a heel, side cut on the right carrying colder air. She did not count paces outright. Counting looked like counting. Instead she measured by the time it took the bucket to knock her knee.

The wash barrel stood in a widened chamber beside a rough trestle where shirts and bandages hung in dim rows, their hems stiff with drying lime. Elise filled the bucket from a cistern that tasted of stone even through the smell, then lifted the line's clean rags into the crook of her arm. Behind a blanket strung on wire, someone gave a low animal moan.

"That's not a door," Clemence said behind her. Elise had not heard her come back. "It's a woman with a bad lung and less patience than either of us. Hold the lamp."

Elise did. Clemence ducked through the blanket and Elise followed because refusal would have been stranger than curiosity. The clinic was only an alcove, not large enough for dignity. A woman in a man's undershirt lay on a pallet with her mouth open and her hair plastered to her cheeks. The bandage around her ribs had yellowed through at one side. A basin on the floor held pinkish water and a clot of phlegm dark as rust. Somebody had tried to sweeten the air with vinegar, but the body in the bed was stronger than vinegar.

"She was a seamstress in Batignolles before she was an inconvenience," Clemence said quietly, unwilling to talk over the sleeping woman like laundry. "Now pass me the strips."

Elise handed over the rags. Clemence's fingers, brutal-looking from work, moved with a tenderness that would have embarrassed either of them if named aloud. She loosened the wet bandage, cleaned the seep around the wound, and muttered, "Come on, my girl, don't be dramatic. If you mean to die, at least do it when the soup isn't on."

The woman made a sound that might have been laughter.

Elise had expected a hideout. She had expected a nest of men, perhaps, or a weapons store disguised as a cellar. She found a sickroom where somebody remembered what the patient had done for wages before illness reduced her to a pallet and a basin.

When they stepped back into the passage, a child nearly collided with Elise's hip.

"You took the blue bucket," the girl said accusingly.

She could not have been more than ten. One stocking had slouched around her ankle, and both knees were ghosted white with quarry chalk. Her hair had been braided in haste and was already opening at the nape. She held half a crust in one hand and a length of red thread in the other, caught midway through some private repair. Elise recognized her from the gallery now: not a pile of shawls after all.

"Lucie," Clemence said, "if you've lost another button, you'll sew it on after lesson."

"I didn't lose it. The cloth let go first." Lucie tipped her head to inspect Elise with the frankness only children and police ever used. "Can she write?"

"She says she can."

"Properly? Abel cheats at capitals when he's in a hurry."

"Abel cheats at everything when he's in a hurry," Clemence said. "Go wash your face."

"I did."

"Then wash it where everyone can see."

Lucie sighed with theatrical suffering and went, still clutching the red thread. Elise watched her step around a puddle without looking down. A child who knew the floor that well belonged to this place in the bones. So did the ribbon on the bucket. So did the seamstress behind the blanket. Elise fixed those facts where she fixed the side cut with colder air and the turn by the damaged saint.

Clemence jerked her chin deeper into the quarry. "You've got two good arms. Use them. Take these copybooks to the schoolroom, then tell Nadir the roller cloth isn't his handkerchief. If he ruins another one with ink, he can wipe the press on his own coat."

"And if he says no?"

Clemence's eyes narrowed with a flicker of amusement. "Then you'll know he's feeling well."

The schoolroom lay beyond the sleeping gallery and short of the deeper chambers, a white limestone oval with a ceiling low enough to keep voices humble. The wall at the far side had been rubbed smooth by hands and shoulders until it looked almost deliberate, a chalk-white patch worn into service as slate. Someone had written the morning's arithmetic on it in charcoal.

11 mouths / 3 onions = ?

Below that, in a smaller hand:

je mange  
tu manges  
il mange

Three children sat on crates with copybooks on their knees. Lucie had made it there first despite Clemence's order and was wiping her face with the cleanest corner of her sleeve. Beside her a boy with ears that stuck out like handles was trying to hide a yawn inside his fist. On the other side, a smaller girl traced the edge of a letter with a tongue-tip concentration that made Elise think of lockpicks.

The man at the wall stood with his back half turned, a charcoal nub between long fingers already dusted white. He was lean to the point of thrift, his coat shiny at the elbows, his hair silvering at the temples though his face was younger than the hair suggested. When he shifted to write the denominator more clearly, a cough took him short and hard enough to bend his shoulders for a beat. He waited it out, swallowed, and went on without granting the cough enough importance to interrupt the lesson.

"If eleven mouths share three onions," he said, "what do we call Abel's portion?"

Lucie's hand went up at once. "The part he steals before supper."

The man looked at her over his shoulder and let the joke sit for half a beat. "That's a moral answer, not an arithmetic one."

"It's still true."

"Most true things are no use at all to fractions. Try again."

This was Lucien Delatour. Authority ran toward him from every side of the room without requiring announcement. The children watched him because they wanted the next word. Clemence had sent the copybooks here because of course she had. Even his cough had a place in the daily business, accounted for like bad weather or lamp oil.

Lucie saw Elise first. "The widow's here."

Delatour turned fully then. His face was quarry-pale in a way city men never were unless they spent their days underground or indoors with illness. Fine lines had settled around his eyes. One shoulder rode a little stiff under the coat; old damage had taught it the weather better than the rest of him.

"Then we should behave before she goes back above and reports us for barbarism," he said.

The children grinned. Lucie did not.

"She won't," Lucie said. "She hasn't even seen Abel eat."

Elise held out the copybooks. "Clemence said these were for the lesson."

"Then Clemence outranks all pedagogy." He crossed to take them, and up close she saw chalk in the seams of his knuckles and a patch at one cuff darned with thread too dark to match. "Lucien Delatour."

"Jeanne Morel."

His grip was dry, careful, and brief. No inspector's crushing assertion, no laborer's casual testing squeeze. It was a schoolmaster's handshake, Elise thought unwillingly, and that was the kind of thought she could not have put into a report without hating herself a little.

"Can you add?" Lucie asked.

"Enough to know when I've been cheated."

"Good. Then you can sit by Abel later and slap him if he lies."

From somewhere down the passage a boy's voice answered, "I'm being slandered in rooms I haven't even entered."

Lucien lifted his chin toward the doorway. "That saves time. Come in and defend your honor properly."

Abel Saint-Clair arrived with two composing sticks tucked through his belt like dueling pistols and a smear of ink on one cheekbone. He was narrow, almost unfinished in the limbs, and his jacket sleeves had been let down so often the old stitch lines showed like scars. Two fingertips on his right hand shone pink and hard from old printer's burns. He bowed toward Elise with comic extravagance.

"Madame Widow. If Lucie has promised you violence, I ask only that you aim for the shoulder."

"Set down those sticks before you poke somebody blind," Lucien said.

"If I put them down, Nadir will claim I never worked at all."

"Nadir claims that anyway."

Abel's grin was quick and hungry. It vanished just as quickly when Lucien added, "Sit. You still owe me the plural of manger."

Abel groaned and folded himself onto a crate, knees jutting up. He stole a glance at Elise while Lucien turned back to the wall, the look half flirtation, half appraisal. She knew the type. Young men who wanted to be thought dangerous often used women as mirrors, hoping to catch a harder version of themselves there.

A taller figure filled the doorway more quietly than Abel had. Nadir Belkacem stood slightly side-on even when still, never giving any room all of himself. Ink had darkened the webbing of his hands and settled stubbornly into the lines of his nails. A nick cut pale across one cheek. He held a spoiled proof sheet in his fingers, not reading it now but folding it with patient precision into the body of a paper bird.

"Clemence says the roller cloth isn't your handkerchief," Elise told him.

He looked from the bird to her and then toward the ceiling, inviting the Republic to hear his complaint. "My reputation worsens hourly."

"Because you keep proving it," Clemence called from the passage without appearing.

Nadir lifted the half-folded bird as if offering evidence. "You see. No due process." He finished the fold, pinched the paper beak sharp, and handed the bird to Lucie without breaking the line of his body. "For after verbs. Not before."

Lucie tucked it into the gap at her collar with serious care.

Elise's eyes went past Nadir despite herself. The print chamber opened behind him, deeper than the schoolroom and warmer by a degree from labor and lamp flame. She caught only a slivered view: the copper shoulder of a hand press, dark type cases stacked like drawers in a countinghouse, rags gone black with ink, a drying line of handbills clipped overhead. One sheet held soup addresses in neat columns. Another looked like a wage appeal. A third was no more dangerous than a child's practice page.

Nadir shifted just enough to seal the doorway with his body. "You're taking stock already, Madame Widow?"

Even Abel stopped fidgeting.

Elise let her gaze drop to the paper bird at Lucie's collar and made it her excuse. "I was looking for wages, not trouble," she said. "Henri told me you printed notices. A widow can be forgiven for staring at the thing that might keep her fed."

Abel barked a laugh. "Hear that? She's practical. Clemence will adopt her before she adopts me."

"Don't count on it," Clemence called from the passage.

Nadir watched Elise another second, then gave the doorway back by an inch. "Work comes from there," he said. "The rest gets earned."

The doorway had shown her a working pressroom that day, busy with lists, appeals, and children's copy.

Lucien had begun the lesson again before the room forgot to watch her. "Three onions divided by eleven mouths," he said, tapping the wall. "The answer is ugly, but ugly answers still count."

"That's politics," Abel muttered.

"No," Lucien said. "That's dinner."

The room laughed, even Abel. Elise stood a moment longer than was wise, long enough to see how the white wall took charcoal and gave it back clearer than slate might have done, how Lucie mouthed the endings before writing them, how Delatour moved among the crates with the same attention Clemence had used on the bandage. It was workmanlike gentleness, stripped of theater. There was only the assumption that children under a quarry still needed verbs and fractions.

She made herself turn away.

The passage beyond the schoolroom widened unexpectedly near a timbered post where lamp smoke had painted the ceiling black. Two sleeping alcoves opened off one side, each curtained with blankets. Deeper in, another branch sloped toward the press. Elise took in the split, the draft from somewhere farther below, the sound of water carrying from a second direction. It might have been a second exit, or only a sump. She put a hand to the wall, feigning a need for balance, and marked the seam in the stone with her thumbnail.

By midday the quarry smelled less of night and more of people: soap, ink, damp wool warming under bodies, and onion hitting fat in a pot somewhere ahead. Clemence put Elise to work at the trestle beside the ration room, slicing cabbage so fine her fingers numbed in the cold. The ration room itself was only shelves hacked into rock, but somebody had made it orderly: sacks of lentils tied shut with string, loaves wrapped in cloth, a wedge of cheese sweated dark at the rind, three jars of jam with the labels soaked off and reused. Clemence kept the room the way a banker kept a vault, except that bankers did not have to worry about children stealing sugar or men pretending not to be hungry.

"Not the core," Clemence said as Elise trimmed an onion. "We're poor, not stupid."

"I know how to cut an onion."

"Then cut it like you mean to feed people."

Lucie reappeared with the paper bird in one pocket and charcoal dust on her nose. "Citizen Lucien says I got the fraction right after all."

"Then citizen Lucien can scrape the pot if you're so pleased with him," Clemence said.

"He says three onions for eleven mouths is tyranny."

"He says too many things. Wash your hands."

Lucie leaned close to Elise and whispered, far too loudly, "He says it when he wants us not to notice we're hungry yet."

Clemence snorted. "See? A schoolroom only teaches children to betray you in complete sentences."

The pot thickened with lentils, barley, cabbage, onion, carrot, and the stubborn last goodness of a smoked hock that Clemence had boiled almost to surrender. Elise kept chopping, passing bowls, wiping the board clean with the side of her hand. Labor made time move differently underground. Above, an hour was a clockface. Here it was how long the dough had to rise near a lamp or how soon a fever dressing would soak through again.

When the meal was nearly ready, a man from the deeper gallery came to the trestle carrying a rolled map wrapped in oilcloth. He was older, his beard more salt than pepper, and one boot left a wet print at every step.

"Lucien wants to see whether the west cut's gone soft again," he said to Clemence. "He asked for the map."

"Then take it to him," Clemence said. "My hands are in the pot."

The man set the roll beside Elise for only a moment while he shifted his grip. Oilcloth slipped at one corner. Before Elise could take a second look, he noticed her glance and thumbed the flap back into place. In that brief opening she saw linen backing, yellowed and repaired twice along an edge, with hand-drawn lines crossing in branching pale ink. Elise bent back over the cabbage at once and pushed the scrap bowl nearer Clemence. "Will the leak reach the food?" she asked, all kitchen concern and no curiosity.

"Not if they move the coal and the dry shelf first," Clemence said.

The image stayed. A true map existed, and Delatour kept it close. That knowledge would have to do for now.

She had just passed him the roll when Lucien himself came in from the schoolroom, followed by Abel and Nadir carrying stacked crates for the table. He took the map without flourish and set it on an upturned barrel, weighting one curling corner with the heel of a bread loaf. Abel leaned in at once, impatient as a terrier. Nadir stayed back.

"It isn't a collapse yet," the older man said. "It's a leak and bad stone."

Lucien studied the marked branch, one finger moving along the line with practiced economy. "Then keep the children out of the west cut and move the coal sack tonight. If the wall swells further, we'll lose the dry shelf first."

"And if it doesn't?"

"Then we've moved a coal sack for our health."

Lucien made the matter a coal sack, a dry shelf, and children kept from bad stone. That plainness carried the room. Elise watched the others accept his word because he had answered in the units their lives actually used.

They ate in the sleeping gallery because it was the only chamber broad enough to take everyone without somebody balancing a bowl on their knees. Clemence insisted the sick woman and the man with the swollen ankle be served first. After that came the children, then whoever had been nearest the labor. Ceremony, Elise understood, existed here, but it was built from need rather than rank.

"Sit," Clemence told her, shoving a bowl into her hands. "If you wait for grace, the lentils will get there first."

Elise sat on a crate between Lucie and the wall. The bowl was hot enough to sting through the earthenware. Steam rose with the honest smell of onion and smoked meat. Somebody passed a heel of bread. Somebody else scraped mustard onto a plate with a fingertip because there was too little left to leave any behind. Lucie broke her crust in exact halves before eating either piece.

"You do count," Nadir said across from her, watching the division.

"Only when I need to."

"Everybody needs to," Clemence said. "Some of us simply admit it."

Abel tore bread with his teeth and spoke around it. "If she counts, she's one of us already. Nobody respectable counts lentils."

"Nobody respectable lives in a hole with you," Nadir said.

"That's a personal attack."

"It's geography."

The exchange shifted the room's weight by degrees, just enough. Elise heard who teased whom, who answered Clemence at once and who pretended deafness until the second order. Lucie asked for more broth and received it after the man with the ankle had his share. Nadir wiped a drip of stew from the rim of the sick woman's bowl before passing it behind the clinic blanket. Abel, despite his noise, tore his own crust smaller to leave a piece for the boy with the handle ears. The meal was lean by any ordinary measure. Its generosity lived in the order of serving and the little corrections people kept making for one another. That made it harder to ignore.

It did not mean Nadir had forgotten the schoolroom doorway. Each time Elise's glance wandered toward the deeper passage, she found him already watching her over his bowl, not hostile exactly, but with the dry patience of a man who expected strangers to prove what they were before he handed them metal type and ink.

Lucien ate late, after the map had been rolled again and set out of harm's way. He took the last place free, opposite Elise, and thanked Clemence for the meal because he knew thanks could not be assumed when women were this tired.

"You worked this morning," he said to Elise after a few mouthfuls. It was not quite a question.

"Clemence saw to that."

"She's reliable in these matters."

"So I've noticed."

His eyes rested on her bowl, then on her hands, lingering there with the patience of a man who believed hands told on people. "Henri said you could fair-copy petitions."

The name moved once through the room and settled. Nobody asked where Henri was. Nobody needed the wound opened for the sake of conversation.

"I can copy cleanly," Elise said. "Receipts, letters, notices. Whatever pays."

"Whatever pays" made Nadir glance up again. Clemence did not.

Lucien dipped bread into the broth. "There's always writing. The trick is to find the sort that doesn't hand your landlord a stick to beat you with."

"That rules out half the city."

"On a good day."

Lucie pointed her spoon at Elise. "Can you do capitals better than Abel?"

"A trained hen can do capitals better than Abel," Nadir said.

"You've built a whole doctrine of slander around me," Abel said. "It's impressive, in a small-minded way."

Lucien's eyes creased at the edges. "Finish your supper before you defend your legacy."

Then, because authority in the quarry apparently included mending what it could, he turned back to Elise. "If you're willing, the print room can use a clean hand tomorrow. At the outer table first, where Nadir can keep an eye on you and the cases can stay shut. Nothing dangerous. Subscription lists. A leaflet for a mutual-aid fund. Some school copy."

Nadir tore his crust in half. "If you start taking stock again, Madame Widow, you'll be counting cabbage in the ration room by noon."

There it was: the press, the writing, the deeper access she had come to win. Elise should have felt the old inward click of work proceeding. Instead she felt the bowl hot against her palms and thought of the seamstress behind the blanket, Lucie dividing crusts, the map spread under bread, the equation on the wall turning onions into mathematics because there were not enough onions to leave arithmetic out of it.

"I'm willing," she said.

"Good. We need useful people more than brave ones."

Abel protested at once. "Speak for yourself."

"I generally do."

The room laughed again. Lucien coughed into his fist when the laughter died, a body's interruption, ordinary and unwelcome at once. He took a sip of water, and Elise saw, for one second, how tired he was under the wit and the steady voice. He was a man holding together schoolroom, map, meal, and tunnel leak on a pair of lungs the quarry punished daily.

When the bowls were emptied and wiped with bread down to their last grease, people drifted to their separate duties. The meal had loosened the room's knot and tied it again. Nadir went back to the press with the quiet of somebody who preferred metal type to company but stayed for company anyway. Abel lingered to juggle spoons until Clemence threatened his ears. Lucie tucked the paper bird under her blanket before lesson copy and asked Elise, in a whisper meant to be confidential, whether widows ever stopped being tired. Elise said she did not know. It was the first wholly honest answer she had given underground.

Night came by thickening lamp smoke rather than any visible loss of light. In the alcove Clemence had given her, Elise sat with her back to the wall, a scrap of proof paper on her knee, and set the pencil stub to it in the square obedient hand Vautrin preferred.

Primary entrance confirmed via wine-shop cellar, rue de la Tombe-Issoire.
Resident authority: Lucien Delatour. Hand press active in deeper chamber.
One child, female, approximately ten, present in the schoolroom and sleeping gallery.

The last line sat on the page with the lead still shining. From two alcoves over came Lucie's cough, dry and quick under the blanket, followed by Clemence's sleep-rough murmur telling her to turn on her side if she meant to keep that racket up. Elise put the pencil down. Her fingers had gone white around it anyway.

She read the three lines once, as Vautrin would read them: entrance, leader, proof that families lived below and could be counted, sorted, used. A schoolroom would become indoctrination in a charge sheet. The clinic would become sheltering fugitives. The sleeping alcoves would become a nest. Lucie would become a tally mark no one bothered to name before the boots came through.

Elise pressed the heel of her thumb over "One child, female, approximately ten" until the paper creased and the graphite smeared into a dark bruise. Then she drew a second line through the sentence so hard the tip snapped. The broken grit jumped into her lap. She folded the proof around the two surviving lines, tucked it under her thigh, and sat very still, listening to the quarry breathe around her.

She had come below to map a cell and name its master. By the end of one day she had mapped a household: a laundress who tied ribbon to a bucket so her daughter could keep order in a world built on shortage, a printer who folded birds out of spoiled proofs, a vain young compositor who still broke his bread smaller for a hungry child, a schoolmaster teaching fractions on stone because there was no slate and no sky, and a sick seamstress whose dressings were changed before anyone fed themselves.

Vautrin wanted conspirators. What he meant to crush was a whole social world that had learned to fit itself under rock. Elise kept the folded proof under her thigh and let the first omission stand.
