# Chapter 9

The woman who kept the reserve cache lived over a shop that sold mourning ribbon and paper flowers, the sort that looked respectable by gaslight and shabby by morning. What Clemence had pressed on Elise in the passage before dawn as a damp washing bundle had dwindled to the packet the woman now took with both hands, not because it was heavy but because she handled paper as if it bruised. Broken corset busks rattled in jars along her shelf. She saved every strip of black silk that crossed her counter, saying a widow could always use another piece of darkness.

She untied the string, counted the forged cards with her lips moving, then pressed the wrapped coins flat under her palm.

"Tell Clemence the green box is safer than the stove pipe," she said. "And tell her if the little one needs stockings again, she should stop buying them new. The dead leave plenty."

"I'll tell her."

The woman looked up then, quick and narrow-eyed. "And tell Lucien not to trust any man who arrives washed. Men like that have a room somewhere else to dirty first."

Elise smiled, small and unwilling. "He knows that already."

"Knowing isn't the same as acting on it."

The room smelled of glue, damp card, and the dust that came off cheap velvet when it had been handled too often. Elise had the sense, brief and indecent, of how easy it would be to stay there another minute with the city muffled by cloth and walls and practical gossip. Then she stepped back into the street and pulled her veil down against the light.

She should have turned south at once and gone back toward Montrouge with the errand done. Clemence had not entrusted the money and papers to her so she could wander. The forged cards now sat under someone else's floorboards, the reserve cash under someone else's stove, and that should have been enough risk for one afternoon.

Instead she kept walking north.

The quarry cold stayed in her dress even after the day had warmed. It made the city feel doubly exposed: the horse dung already drying white at the edges, the river smell coming up through the bridges, the bright shop glass that gave back too much of a face if she let herself look. She crossed by habit more than decision, taking streets her body remembered from months of being sent where Vautrin required her. Old routes rose under her feet with insulting ease. Here was the tobacconist who watered his display ferns with last night's coffee. Here the alley where the constables came to piss between raids. Here the shuttered law-copyist's office where she had once spent three days transcribing statements so a magistrate could pretend his certainty had come from careful reading rather than the first rumor handed to him.

By the time the towers and slate roofs of the Ile de la Cite lifted ahead of her, caution had already lost the argument.

The Prefecture stood in its usual posture of stone confidence, as if no one inside had ever sweated through a shirt or lied on paper for money. Petitioners crowded the main steps, hats in hand, each with a different emergency tied in blue ribbon. Elise did not go near them. The side entrance on the quai handled copyists, charwomen, messenger boys, and men too small to deserve the front stair.

The desk sergeant there was trying to stop smoking and had taken to chewing cloves so hard the smell came off him sweet and rotten together. His left cuff had been patched in green thread by a child who had either bad eyes or a sense of joke. He looked at the sheaf of papers under Elise's arm, at the widow's veil, and then past her to a boy carrying an ink crate twice the width of his chest.

Old office work had taught her a set of bodily commandments. She did not hesitate at thresholds. She kept paper in her hands. She never turned at a name unless it belonged to her in that corridor. A woman carrying work could pass through half the state if she did not make anyone feel responsible for seeing her.

She took the nearest tied bundle from the tray by the wall, nodded with the bored haste of a woman already late for someone else's irritation, and went in.

The stone hall breathed out polish, wet wool, sealing wax, and the faint old urine smell every government building eventually admitted no matter how much soap got thrown at it. Boots rang on the tile. A clerk coughed behind a door until the cough turned theatrical and stopped. Somewhere above, a bell gave one sharp impatient note for dictation.

Elise did not head straight for Vautrin's floor. Straight lines drew questions. She climbed one flight, crossed to the copying room, came down another stair, and entered the anti-anarchist corridor from the far end where the light went mean and yellow after midday. She knew the building's useless habits as well as any prayer. Half past one was when one records clerk went out for stew and the other stole ten minutes in the yard to pick his teeth with a matchstick. Half past one was also when Vautrin preferred visitors kept waiting so they would arrive at his door already a little bent.

She had spent too much of her life learning those preferences. They rose in her now with the smoothness of a hand going to a hidden pocket.

The corridor was narrower than she remembered. Or perhaps the quarry had changed her sense of space. Here the walls pressed with varnish and framed directives. Underground, pressure came from rock and the weight of the city above. Here it came from doors shut soft over names.

Vautrin's outer room stood open. His clerk's desk was empty but for a sand-shaker, two stacked dockets, and a heel of bread wrapped in yesterday's racing sheet. The bread had been bitten twice and put down in a hurry. That meant someone nearby.

She would have gone straight to the desk if Vautrin had not spoken.

His voice came through the inner door, measured as ever, each word set down like cutlery arranged before a guest.

"No, monsieur, I am not asking the court to imagine danger. I am asking it to receive what will shortly be recoverable."

A second man answered too low for sense.

Vautrin again: "Pamphlets do not impress anymore. Every student in Paris can be found with pamphlets if one empties enough trunks. Powder is different. Powder can be weighed. Powder can be displayed. It satisfies the eye of the paper and the hand of the magistrate."

Another murmur.

"Then leave the warrant undated," Vautrin said. "Monsieur Ferrand has not yet earned the signature."

Elise had been still before. Now stillness became labor. She stood with the borrowed bundle against her ribs and felt the paper edges bite through her glove.

There was the scrape of a chair inside, then footsteps crossing away from the door. A drawer shut. Water poured into a glass. She had perhaps half a minute, perhaps less. The kind of office time that had shaped her work was always measured in other people's inconveniences.

She set the borrowed bundle on the clerk's chair and moved.

Vautrin kept his outer room with the false disorder of men who wanted subordinates to think genius had no time for neatness. Elise knew better. The active files always lay left of the blotter, the completed ones to the right, the sensitive ones under whatever object would make another hand seem presumptuous. Today the object was a paperweight in the form of a bronze hound, one ear rubbed bright from years of use.

Under the hound sat a thin docket tied with blue ribbon.

She slipped it free and opened it on the clerk's desk, turning the pages with two fingers so the paper would not crackle.

The first sheet was a disbursement register. Special disbursements, Section III. Two entries had been paid already, and a third waited on condition, all in the same dry hand.

`12 April 1895. J. Ferrand. Advance for cultivation expenses: 35 francs. Paid.`

`2 May 1895. J. Ferrand. Supplementary indemnity upon successful introduction to Delatour circle: 40 francs. Paid.`

`14 May 1895. J. Ferrand. Final payment authorized upon delivery of material proof sufficient to support search and detention: 60 francs. Pending.`

Sixty francs for the last step alone.

The number sat on the page with the obscene calm of official ink. Sixty francs was more than two months at Charenton. Sixty francs was enough bread to keep Clemence from watering soup into gray dishwater for weeks. A hundred and thirty-five in total, if Ferrand did as instructed. The state would pay him more to dirty one room properly than it paid Elise to live inside a lie for a month.

An unsigned voucher was pinned behind the last entry, with Ferrand's name entered in a clerk's hard slant and the spaces for signature and violet thumbprint left blank. Memory and contempt made the paper stink of Ferrand's cheap cologne anyway.

The next sheets made the first look honest by comparison.

One was a draft deposition written in legal French so clean it mocked the man meant to speak it. Ferrand was to testify that Lucien Delatour had requested explosive compounds and instructed him to store them near the press room until a suitable public target was chosen. The date line stood blank. So did the exact quantity of gelignite. Ferrand had not yet placed the lie inside a room, but the sentence waiting for him already existed.

Behind it sat the warrant application.

Preventive search and detention under the widened statutes. Subjects: Lucien Delatour, Nadir Belkacem, Abel Saint-Clair, and unnamed associates occupying a concealed site off rue de la Tombe-Issoire. Grounds: clandestine printing apparatus, unlawful association, possession or instruction in the handling of explosive materials to be recovered on site. The list of objects to be seized had already been drawn up in a tidy vertical hand: hand press, type cases, correspondence, school copybooks, address ledgers, chemical materials, detonators.

Lucie's clumsy alphabets could go into a police sack beside powder and come back out for a newspaper illustrator as evidence of breeding.

In the margin, in Vautrin's blue pencil, a note:

`If substances are not yet visible in situ, intermediary to complete arrangement before execution. Delay no longer than one week.`

The phrasing was mild, even courteous, and it landed harder for that reason. In Vautrin's hand, "complete arrangement" turned planting powder into housekeeping: put the thing where it would be seen, leave the room ready for the magistrate's eye.

Footsteps sounded at the corridor end.

Elise pulled the deposition free first, then the ledger sheet beneath it. Thin copies, not originals. Vautrin kept duplicates because he trusted paper more than memory and himself more than everyone else. She folded both once, twice, and slid them inside the cuff of her sleeve. The edge of the warrant snagged her skin and opened a hot bright line across the pad of her thumb.

She had just retied the blue ribbon when the outer clerk came back in with his lunch pail swinging.

He was a narrow man with soup on his waistcoat and a tiny varnished seashell fixed to his watch chain, the sort of thing a child might have pressed into his hand and ordered him to keep forever. He stopped when he saw her at the desk.

"Yes?"

Elise did not turn too fast. Fast looked guilty. Offended looked legitimate.

"This was left in the wrong order," she said, lifting the docket half an inch. "The Ferrand disbursement is sitting on top of the Montrouge seizure requests. If Monsieur Vautrin wants the magistrate papers before four, he'll want them untangled first."

The clerk frowned, more at the possibility of late work than at her. "Who asked you?"

"No one yet. That's how I knew to do it."

He made a face that admitted the logic while resenting it. "Leave it there."

"As you like."

She set the file down and picked up the borrowed bundle from the chair. The clerk had already turned to his desk, prying open the lunch pail. Steam and onion came up between them. He lifted the lid and glanced once at her sleeve, perhaps because of the blood beginning to spot the glove thumb, perhaps because instinct occasionally troubled even small men.

"You're from which office?"

"Copying."

He snorted. "Aren't we all."

Then he sat and began to eat.

Elise walked out without haste. Her back knew exactly how much speed the corridor would forgive.

At the stair landing she heard Vautrin's inner door open. She did not look. Looking was a confession. She kept down the steps with the bundle still under her arm, one hand closed hard over the papers in her sleeve. A draught crossed the stairwell carrying his soap and his clean-cuff smell. It touched her cheek and was gone. Whether he had stepped into the outer room in time to see the hem of her skirt vanish, she could not tell.

The desk sergeant at the side entrance was arguing with the ink boy about a receipt ticket. His cloves and bad temper had both sharpened. Elise let the argument cover her exit and did not breathe properly until the quai wind hit her full in the face.

Only then did she realize how badly her hand hurt.

Blood had dried tacky inside the glove. She took the first side street, found a public lavatory whose door would not quite latch, and stood in the sour dark with one boot braced against the wall while she worked the papers out of her sleeve. They were damp now, not ruined but close enough to make her careful. Her thumb left a red crescent on Ferrand's final payment line.

She used the pin from her collar to lift the stitches at the inside hem of her dress. The Surete marker lay where Vautrin had first told her to keep it, a brass certainty no bigger than two stacked coins, smooth from her own nervous touching over the weeks. She slid the folded copies in beside it and stitched the seam back with fingers that wanted to shake and did not.

The metal and paper rested together against her calf when she stood, approval and evidence stitched into one seam.

In the alley outside, two delivery boys were pissing against the wall and discussing a cyclist who had broken his jaw near the market. A woman farther down shook a rug over a window ledge and sent dust into the light. Paris had room for every appetite it could price. Behind her, inside the Prefecture, Vautrin had left a warrant undated while Ferrand earned the right to fill it.

Elise turned south at last.

The long walk back felt different with the papers hidden on her body. She no longer thought in terms of whether Ferrand was dangerous. That question had gone stale. The question now was whether she meant to let the state build its case in the shape of Lucien's rooms and children's slates and Nadir's hands blackened by honest work. She could still do nothing. Doing nothing was often what service required. She could go below, say the money and papers were safe, keep quiet, and wait for Ferrand to finish proving what Vautrin had already started paying for.

The possibility sickened her less by morality than by texture. She had seen the list. She knew which objects would be taken first, what order boots would use on the stair, how long a hand press took to catalog when a clerk was lazy. Silence was no longer abstract. Silence had itemized itself.

By the time she reached the wine shop on rue de la Tombe-Issoire, evening had flattened the windows to a dirty shine. Wine smell above, chalk damp below. She gave the cellar knock and waited through the scrape of the bolt.

The descent always chilled her more after a day aboveground. The quarry held cold the way offices held secrets: patiently, with no interest in whether human bodies found it convenient.

In the ration room Clemence was kneeling over a crate of turnips with her sleeves rolled and a seam ripped open under one arm where the day had pulled too hard. She was trimming rot from the vegetables with the same blunt knife she used on soap blocks, wasting nothing she could bully back into use.

"The drop?" Clemence asked.

"Done. The green box, not the stove pipe."

"Good." Clemence flicked a thumb-sized black spot from a turnip onto the floor. "You were longer than you should've been."

Elise set the excuse in her mouth and found it too thin even before it spoke. "The streets were thick by the bridge."

Clemence looked at her once, quick and flat. She had the sort of face that read damage faster than comfort. "Lucien's in the schoolroom."

"I need him now."

"Then need him quietly. Lucie's finally asleep."

Elise nodded and went.

The schoolroom smelled of chalk, lamp oil, and the damp mineral breath that came off the walls after a warm day above. Lucien sat at the narrow table with a stack of copy sheets near his elbow and one hand pressed to his mouth while he coughed into the crook of his arm. He had taken off his coat. His shirt sleeves were rolled unevenly, one forearm whiter where the cuff usually kept it from the quarry dust. On the bench behind him Lucie slept curled around a slate, cheek pressed to its hard edge, one scraped knee bare where her stocking had worked down.

Lucien lifted his head at Elise's step. His voice came back low from the cough, plain rather than rough.

"Did the errand hold?"

"It held."

She did not move closer at once. The papers in her hem seemed to have changed the way cloth sat on her body. Lucien watched her the way he listened to children reading aloud, hearing the place where the line would fail before the child knew it.

"There's more," he said.

"Enough that the errand barely matters."

He glanced at Lucie, then rose and crossed to the bench. He eased the slate from the girl's arms, tucked a folded shawl under her head, and brushed chalk dust off her cheek with the back of two fingers. The gesture was practiced enough to belong to him more than politics ever did. When he straightened, his shoulder caught the lamplight and threw the old scar high near the collar into brief relief before the shirt settled back over it.

"Come into the print chamber," he said.

"No. Here. You'll see why."

That made him still.

"Jeanne?"

The gentleness in the name made her hate him for a beat.

Elise stepped to the table, lifted her skirt just enough to reach the inner hem, and worked the folded papers loose. The motion was indecent in a room built for lessons and sleep, which was part of why she did it quickly and without apology. She laid the copies between the copybooks and the lamp.

"Read."

Lucien took the first sheet. His eyes moved once down the page, then back to the top with a harder focus.

"What's this?"

"Ferrand's wages."

He picked up the second page before answering. The silence went long enough for the drip in the far wall to seem to mark it out.

"No," he said finally. "Not wages. A schedule."

"Call it what you like. He's on their books."

He read the draft deposition, then the warrant application. When he reached the margin note in blue pencil, his jaw tightened. He drew one careful breath through his nose and kept reading.

"The blanks are for where and when," he said. "Not whether."

"I know what they're waiting to fill."

He set the papers down with care that was not calm. "Where did you get them?"

"From a drawer the police shouldn't have left to me."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one I've got."

Lucien looked up. Tiredness sharpened him. Under strain his voice lost the patient braid of clauses and went simpler.

"No. You didn't stumble into a loose drawer. You knew which room, which desk, which file would still be warm. You know that corridor too well."

Elise kept her hands flat on the table. "I know offices. Men like Vautrin arrange themselves alike."

"Not alike enough for this." He tapped the margin note with one finger. "Tell me one thing the paper can't."

She heard how near the demand sat to accusation and answered before caution could tidy it. "The desk sergeant at the quai door chews cloves because he's trying to stop smoking. Vautrin's clerk wears a shell on his watch chain and soup on his waistcoat. The live docket was under a bronze hound with one ear rubbed bright. I heard Vautrin tell the man inside to leave the warrant undated until Ferrand earned the signature."

Lucien watched her a moment longer than the answer required. "All right. You were there."

"I was. That's the part that matters."

"Why tell me?"

She had seen Lucie's copybooks listed beside powder. Sixty francs had been put against the act of fouling the press room. If she stayed quiet now, the quarry would become a case file with children in the appendix. Vautrin's blue pencil had made something in her feel skinned.

What she said was, "Because Ferrand isn't trying to watch you. He's furnishing the room for a search. If I keep quiet, Lucie's slate goes into the same police sack as the powder."

Lucien glanced at the sleeping child, then back to Elise. "If I ask what tied you to that building before today, will you lie?"

"If you ask for the whole road, yes."

He nodded once. "Then I'll ask the part that matters now. Is any of this arranged to drive us where somebody else wants us?"

"No." She heard how fast the answer came and made herself slow the rest. "No. If anything, I'm late to a room they've already started furnishing for you."

Lucien studied her face with the same listening look he gave a room when everyone in it was pretending not to be afraid. She let him. A lie could survive scrutiny better than eagerness. At last he asked, "What do you think Ferrand expects next?"

"Access. The print room first, then the stores, anything that lets him point and say conspiracy. If he can't find explosive material there, he'll bring it. If he can't charm Abel into handling it, he'll make the boy feel cowardly for refusing."

"And the warrant?"

"A week if Ferrand manages it, less if he gets impatient. Vautrin wrote that in the margin."

"So he already knows where we're hidden."

"He knows enough for a search if Ferrand gives him something that shines."

Lucien's gaze dropped to the listed objects again: hand press, type cases, correspondence, school copybooks. He touched the last words with his thumb, very lightly, barely enough to keep the page from sliding.

"He wants the children in it," he said.

"He wants whatever makes a clean picture in a magistrate's skull before he writes it down."

"And you came straight back."

"Straight enough. I bled in a public lavatory and stitched the copies into my hem, but I came back."

"Not to save yourself."

That was close enough to the bone that contempt rose in her before shame could. "If I meant to save myself, I'd have kept walking."

The answer struck harder than she intended. Lucien took it without flinching, but the hurt showed in the short pause before he drew breath. He coughed once into the side of his hand, and gathered the papers up.

"These stay with me," he said.

"They were safer where nobody would think to search."

"Maybe." He folded them smaller. "You're still not putting them back in your skirt."

"You think I'll run?"

"I think I don't yet know what you won't do."

The words landed without heat and hit harder for it. He tucked the folded copies inside his shirt, flat against his ribs, then crossed to the passage door and opened it.

"Clemence."

She answered at once. When she came in, she had the knife still in one hand and a strip of turnip skin caught to her wrist. Her eyes went from Lucien to Elise to the empty place on the table where the papers had been.

"What now?"

"Wake Nadir when he comes in," Lucien said. "Find sacks, extra lamp oil, and whatever cloth we've got left for wrapping the type. We move the press before morning."

"On whose bright idea?"

"Mine for the next ten minutes."

Clemence did not believe that for a second. Her face narrowed another notch. "That answer smells thin."

"It'll do for the next ten minutes," Lucien said. "Ferrand's had too much of the print room already."

Her gaze cut to Elise. "And she knows that how?"

"Well enough to make me move iron," he said. "Not well enough for me to sleep easy."

That gave Clemence something worse than suspicion: a sum she could not yet finish. "And her?"

"She works with us," Lucien said. "She doesn't go anywhere alone tonight. Not the print room, not the stores, not the mouth of the passage. If this is a trap, she can sweat under the weight of it with the rest of us."

Clemence shifted the knife to the other hand. "If necessity starts giving orders in a clean skirt, necessity can take the rope end and the screw side."

"I can take the rope end," Elise said.

"And Ferrand?" Clemence asked.

Lucien's mouth thinned. "Ferrand stays out of the deep rooms tonight."

"You're finally listening to the washed bastard?"

"I'm listening to what he means to do."

Clemence gave one curt nod. Action always convinced her faster than explanations did. "Then I'll fetch sacks and wake Nadir. If your necessity slips off alone, I'll know which passage to search first."

She disappeared into the passage already calling for lamp oil and hands.

Lucien listened to her voice recede through the stone bends. When he turned back, his own had gone quieter.

"Tonight," he said. "Before dawn, whether Nadir curses me or not."

Relief loosened one strap around Elise's ribs and left the others in place. "Split the type cases. Don't send them all through one passage. And move the address scraps with the school papers. If they find one without the other, they'll write whatever link they need."

His eyes flicked to her. "You sound very sure of what they'll write."

"I know what they'll write."

"Yes," he said quietly. "That's part of what troubles me."

There it was again, not knowledge but the edge of it.

"Ferrand can't know you know," she said. "If he feels the door closing, he'll rush."

"So will Abel, if he smells a secret."

"Then keep them apart."

Lucien gave her a look of tired amusement. "You're fond of the easier half of other people's tasks."

"Moving iron through those passages isn't the easier half."

"No," he said. "It isn't."

For one moment neither of them moved. Lucie slept on the bench with her mouth slightly open, one hand curled under her chin. Water ticked in the wall. Far off, someone laughed in the sleeping gallery and got hushed. The whole refuge seemed to hang there on the thin fact of not yet being searched.

"This buys us time," Lucien said. "Nothing more."

"I know it only buys time."

"And Jeanne." He stopped there long enough that she felt the missing question gather weight anyway. "When I ask the rest, don't insult me with a small lie."

Elise looked at him, at the chalk dust on his sleeve and the fatigue hollowing the skin under his eyes, at the ribs under which he had hidden the pages and the hand that had just moved a sleeping child before it moved a press. The truth pushed once against the back of her teeth and stayed there.

"Then don't ask tonight," she said.

His expression altered so slightly another person might not have seen it. She did. Hurt again, and a decision not to spend it yet.

"No," he said. "Not tonight."

He crossed to the wall and began clearing the children's slates from the shelf where they would be in the way once the type cases came through. It was practical motion, with no rhetoric in it. His cough caught him halfway through the second armful and bent him double for a moment before he mastered it.

Elise let her skirt fall straight. The brass marker knocked once against her calf when she turned toward the passage.

In the passages outside, the quarry had begun to wake in a new rhythm: Clemence's brisk voice, Nadir being sent for, someone cursing softly over sacks, the first scrape of a crate dragged where it did not usually stand. The state still touched Elise's leg through brass while, all around her, the refuge moved because she had chosen not to keep quiet and because Lucien had refused to let that choice buy her trust.

In the print chamber Clemence thrust a rope at her without looking up and jerked her chin toward the screw. Chalk dust came off the line onto Elise's palms as she wrapped it and leaned back with the others.
