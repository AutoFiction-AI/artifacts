# Chapter 10

By the time they got the screw free, Elise's palms were striped raw where the rope had burned through the chalk dust. In the print chamber, the press had always looked smaller when it stood in its corner with the ink balls hanging beside it and a proof sheet pinned crooked to the wall, only another object the quarry had learned to keep alive. Once Lucien and Nadir had lifted off the frisket and unpinned the tympan, once the chase was set aside and the little drawer of furniture emptied into a flour sack, it turned ugly. The body of it had weight in the wrong places. The copper cheeks were cold with seep and old grease. It did not want to be carried.

Clemence stood over the ledgers on an upturned crate, apron tied tight over her skirt as if this were still laundry and not flight. She wet her thumb before every page turn although the lye had cracked the skin there into white seams. Her fast face moved over names, subscriptions, the book of bread tallies, the wrapper of blank labor cards and copied baptism extracts left in the quarry once the reserve forged papers went aboveground to the mourning-ribbon shop cache, the list of front addresses. She made two piles, what could be replaced and what would hang somebody if the police got their hands on it.

"That one," she said, tapping a narrow account book with a red thread down the spine. "Not because of the figures. Because Berthe writes to us in the margins when she has no other paper."

Nadir took it from her and slid it into an oilcloth bundle. He always stood a little side-on, as if rooms had taught him not to give them his whole chest. Tonight there was a folded scrap of blue handbill tucked into his sleeve, something he had started turning into one of the small paper animals he made for Lucie when the child had to sit quiet. It had gone soft with sweat.

Abel had shed his jacket but not his theater. His shirt clung dark between the shoulder blades, and one suspender had been mended with thread too bright a blue for the rest of him. Printer's burns freckled two fingertips. He kept opening and shutting his hands as though he wanted the night to notice he was suffering.

"Ferrand said rollers," he muttered, looking at the stripped frame. "If we'd got the rollers under it earlier, we wouldn't be standing here all night like draymen."

Nadir did not look up. "Then Ferrand can come back and be a roller."

"He's finding oil."

"He's finding his own skin," Clemence said. "Same as he always does when there's weight."

Lucien coughed into his wrist, waited the cough down, then bent again over the press bed. He had tied a rag around one palm where the metal had pinched him earlier. Damp had darkened the hair at his temples and made the silver there show harder. "Ferrand isn't moving this machine," he said. "We're the ones moving it. Abel, get the second pole under. Nadir, take the records through first with Clemence. Jeanne, stay here a moment."

The false name still made her turn a fraction late when he used it. Elise crossed the room anyway. The floor was slick with old ink and limestone paste. In the lamp's weak circle the press looked less like an instrument than like part of the quarry itself, something dug out of the wall and taught to make words.

Lucien put his shoulder against one cheek of the frame and tested the lift. The effort brought a tightness around his mouth that had nothing to do with temper. "We'll take the body to the survey pocket behind the schoolroom," he said, low enough that the room had to lean toward him. "The type goes after. The records last. We don't use the main turn by the wine stair. If Ferrand has talked to anybody aboveground, that's the first place they'd think to watch."

Abel straightened. "If Ferrand has talked, we're already fucked."

Clemence's head snapped up. "Watch your mouth."

He colored like a child and pushed on because shame always made him louder. "I'm saying what everybody's thinking. He knows where the chamber is. He knows what sits in it. We ought to be asking how Mlle Morel suddenly came back from town full of urgent wisdom about moving presses and hiding names."

The little noises of work dropped out one by one. Clemence's thumb stayed on the same ledger edge, the lamp glass clicked once in her tightened grip, and water beyond the wall counted the space between Abel's breaths. Elise had known this would come. She had heard it in the changed texture of Abel's politeness all evening, in the way Clemence had handed her a lamp without letting their fingers touch.

Lucien did not look at her first. He looked at Abel. "We can ask that question after the machine is out."

"And if the answer matters before then?"

"Then it still matters after, only with the press intact."

Abel gave a short, disbelieving laugh. "That's convenient for her."

Clemence's voice cut in, brisk and exact. "Convenient or not, I don't like guessing in the dark. She carried the money once. That bought her another week. It didn't buy her the keys to every room we have."

Elise could have said that she did not want the keys. She could have said that if she had wanted them, the state would already have them. Both answers were true and useless. She kept her mouth shut because the first rule of surviving a room like this was to make other people spend themselves before you did.

Lucien set both hands flat on the copper cheek. "Jeanne warned us before Ferrand could put his hand on the records. She's here, and he isn't. Tonight that's the arithmetic. She takes the front lift with me."

Abel stared. "You'd put her on the blind side?"

"Yes."

"You don't even know where she got it."

Lucien's eyes came to Elise then and stayed there, steady enough to quiet the room. "No. I don't. I know she came back when walking away would've been easier. I know she's worked every ugly job we've put in front of her. I know the front is the hardest place, which is why she gets it if she wants it."

The choice was public, and because it was public it cost him more than any private kindness could have. Elise felt the cost of it like another weight set into her hands.

"I'll take it," she said.

Clemence blew out a breath through her nose. "Then don't be brave in a foolish way. Be useful."

"She's had practice," Nadir said, and if there was a hook in it, it was a light one. He took the first bundle of records from Clemence and slung it under his arm. "Come on, Abel. Unless you want to stand here and be offended till dawn."

They worked the poles through the frame and wrapped them with sacking where the wood would bite. The first lift nearly failed before it began. The press stuck to the floor with old grime. Lucien counted, they heaved, and the whole body rose no higher than a hand's width before dropping back with a clang that made lamp flames jump.

Clemence swore, not prettily. "Again. Before the whole quarter hears."

The second lift came up true. Copper ground against rope. Elise took the left front, Lucien the right. The blind side meant going backward up the first six cut steps out of the print chamber, heels feeling for stone worn hollow by years of wet boots. Behind them, Nadir and Abel carried the rear poles, taking the heavier share on the slope. The machine moved an inch, then another. Her shoulders screamed at once. Weight ran from the pole through her collarbone into her teeth.

"Left foot," Lucien said, his voice pitched for her and nobody else. "There's a broken edge on the next one."

She found it with her heel, felt the stone crumble, corrected. Chalk grit slid under her sole. The press shifted and all four of them made the same involuntary sound, a short grunt dragged out of the body before pride could stop it.

The passage tightened after the stairs. They could bully weight; the turn was what threatened to kill them. The quarry had not been cut for machines. It had been cut for stone and then for bones and then for people hiding from the law. The ceiling lowered. A buttress of untouched chalk bulged from the wall where the route kinked toward the schoolroom. They had measured it twice with a length of string and still the press looked too broad.

"Tilt on my count," Lucien said.

Abel answered too quickly, already breathless. "I've got it."

Nadir said, "That's not the same as having it."

They tilted. The right cheek cleared. The left caught with a scrape that went through Elise's spine like a knife. Copper shrieked on stone. The frame lurched back toward the rear.

"Hold," Lucien said.

Abel lost his grip for half a second. Half a second was enough. The rear pole dropped against his shoulder, and the weight shot forward into Elise and Lucien. Elise's heel slid. She slammed her free hand against the wall, skinning it open, and felt the whole machine coming down into the narrow place where there was nowhere for a body to get out of its way.

Lucien braced and took it on the bad shoulder with a sound he tried to swallow. Elise saw, in one bright stupid flash, the newspaper paragraph Vautrin wanted: illegal press discovered, radical schoolmaster arrested, documents seized, case complete. Then she stopped seeing paragraphs and looked at stone.

"Higher on the back," she snapped. "Not down. Higher. Nadir, shove his side. Abel, if you drop this again I'll break your fingers myself."

Abel made a hurt, furious noise, but fear took the boast out of him. Nadir set his shoulder under the rear pole and drove upward. Lucien obeyed before pride could argue. The press rose enough for Elise to wrench her body sideways and drag the left cheek past the bulge of chalk. Copper grated, then slid free.

For three breaths nobody moved except to keep the thing from killing them.

Nadir let out air. "Well. She means it."

Abel panted behind them. "I slipped."

"Yes," Clemence said from down the passage with the lamp. "We all saw."

Lucien's face had gone pale under the quarry dust, but his voice stayed even. "We're through the worst turn. Again."

From there the night became one bad angle after another. Nothing finished cleanly. The survey pocket had once held tools or stolen wine or bodies; now it was the last chamber on a mean little branch behind the schoolroom wall, dry enough for paper and low enough that tall men had to stoop. They could not carry the press there whole. They had to set it down twice on the route, each landing a controlled collapse with knees buckling and rope yanking skin away in strips. Elise's arms trembled so hard by the second rest that she had to lean her forehead against the copper and pretend she was only catching breath.

Past the schoolroom, they went by the alcove where the children slept behind hanging blankets. One shifted at the noise. A small face appeared in the gap, Lucie with one braid undone and chalk still ghosting one knee above her stocking.

"Mama?" she whispered. "Are they taking the wall away?"

Clemence did not turn soft. She never did while there was work in front of her. But she crossed the distance in two steps and tucked the blanket higher around the child with those soap-rough hands. "No. Go back under. It's only grown people being idiots with a machine."

Lucie looked past her at Elise and Lucien bent under the load. Fear made her fix on the practical thing, as it always did. "Will there still be lessons in the morning?"

Lucien, carrying the front with the pole jammed across his chest, managed to answer. "If this doesn't kill me, yes."

A breath that might have been a laugh slipped out of her. She disappeared. Clemence stayed one extra second with her hand flat to the blanket, then lifted the lamp again and said, "Move."

They got the body into the survey pocket at last and lowered it onto folded sacks. Elise's knees nearly failed when the weight left her. Her arms kept holding a moment longer, stupid with effort, before they remembered they were empty.

There was no triumph in the room, only more work. Nadir and Clemence started the records across in bundles. Abel went back for the type cases with a look on him halfway between penance and resentment. Lucien rolled his left shoulder once and stopped when pain touched it.

Elise saw the stop. "You should have let me take more of it."

"You were already taking your share."

"That isn't an answer."

He wiped his wrist over his mouth. "No. It isn't."

The type cases were worse in a different way. The press body had been brute weight, honest and hateful. The type demanded finesse when nobody had any left. Each case had to stay level enough that the sorts did not slither out of their compartments and turn an alphabet into scrap. Nadir took the first two with the reverence of a priest carrying somebody else's god. Abel, angry at being watched, tried to prove he could be careful by moving too fast.

Elise saw the slip before he did. The third case struck the door stone, pitched toward his body, and the little lead letters lifted in their trays like grain hit by wind.

She dropped the ledger bundle in her hands and drove her forearm under the falling edge. The case slammed into bone hard enough to light her vision white. She rammed her hip against the wall and held while Nadir caught the other side.

A rain of sorts jumped anyway, peppering the floor. Tiny e's and a's bounced into the chalk dust. One upper-case R spun to a stop by her shoe.

"Slowly," Nadir said, though his own breath had gone sharp. "Slowly, for the love of print."

Abel's mouth opened and shut. "I had it."

Elise looked at the scattered lead, at the compartments still mostly intact, at the blood beginning to run from a split on her forearm into the grime. "No," she said. "You didn't."

It came out flatter than anger, which made it land harder. Shame shut him up at last.

They knelt in the dirt to gather the spilled letters. The work forced them close. Lead chilled her fingertips. Nadir sorted without seeming to look, flicking pieces back to their boxes with infuriating grace.

"Here," he said, dropping a bent comma into her palm. "That one would have wandered all night."

"Thank you."

He glanced at her arm. "You catch type the way other people catch knives."

"Type cuts cleaner."

The corner of his mouth moved. "That's a printer's answer."

"It's a practical one."

"Same thing on a good day."

When they carried the last case in, Abel kept both hands under it and his eyes fixed on the level. Clemence followed with the final bundle of records, including the little red-spined account book and the quarry packet of blank labor cards and copied baptism extracts, rough stock for making new names if the mourning-ribbon cache was ever cut off. Lucien came last with an oilcloth parcel tucked under his arm, smaller than the others and held too close to be communal property. His notebook, Elise thought. Names the police had mislaid on purpose. Names that could still bleed if a clerk wrote them badly enough.

They made the new chamber disappear by degrees. Type cases under a blanket. The press body behind stacked sacks of chalk and two broken surveyor's crates. Records inside a shallow recess curtained with old canvas. Clemence moved through it all with the authority of a woman who knew exactly what rooms did for the people in them. She set the ledgers down, stepped back, changed the angle of a crate by an inch, and the whole arrangement stopped looking deliberate.

Abel hovered, wanting absolution and knowing better than to ask for it. Finally he said, "It's not much of a hiding place."

"No hiding place is much of one once somebody talks," Clemence said.

He flushed again. "That's not what I meant."

"Then mean better."

Lucien should have been tired enough to let the rebuke stand on its own, but he looked at Abel with weary pity instead of impatience. "It buys us time. Time isn't a little thing."

Abel's answer came too fast. "Only if you use it."

There it was again, the young men's quarrel with any plan that did not end in flame. Ferrand's work, crawling under the skin where speeches could not reach. Elise felt it like grit between teeth.

Nadir gathered up the empty sacks. "I'm going to sleep before our little saint discovers another philosophy in the dark."

"I don't need mothering."

"No," Clemence said. "You need feeding and a keeper, but we've had this conversation already."

The room gave up its only honest laugh. Even Abel gave in enough to show his teeth. The laugh helped because it made them briefly human again, not suspects and burdens and hands on a machine.

Clemence picked up the lamp. Her face, in that light, was all work and no softness until she looked at Lucien's shoulder. "You're not carrying another damned thing tonight."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

She turned to Elise then, measuring without liking what she measured. "You're bleeding on my floor."

Elise looked down. The cut on her forearm had made a dark paste with the chalk. "I'll wash it."

"Do that. Then sleep, if you can. We start again in six hours whether the Republic deserves it or not."

One by one they left. Nadir ducked out with his sacks and the ruined blue paper still in his sleeve. Abel followed, shoulders set hard with the effort of swallowing humiliation. Clemence paused at the entrance curtain.

"Lucien."

"Yes?"

"Don't make a chapel of this machine. It's a tool."

He gave her a tired half smile. "That's why we moved it."

"Good. Remember."

Then she was gone too, her steps brisk on the chalk. The light she took with her narrowed the room to the single lamp left on an overturned ink crate. The press, blanketed and hidden, gave off the faint sour smell of metal that had known too many hands.

For a while neither of them spoke. The quarry settled around them. Water dripped. Somewhere in the sleeping gallery a man rolled over and coughed into his blanket. Above all of it, too high and far to matter until it suddenly did, carriage thunder moved over the city streets.

Elise sat on one of the folded sacks because her legs would not hold another minute of standing. The delayed shaking had started in her hands. Now that there was no weight to answer, her body seemed to want to go on lifting anyway.

Lucien noticed without looking directly at it. He set the lamp closer, crouched, and held out a strip of clean rag. "Give me your arm."

"You carried the worst of it."

"I asked for your arm, not a debate."

In any other hour, the schoolmaster in his voice would have needled her. Tonight it eased something. She held her arm out. He poured a little water from the jug over the cut first. The wash stung so hard she hissed. Pink ran down into the dust at their feet.

"There's your practical courage," he said quietly. "Very impressive until it meets cold water."

"I didn't say it was noble."

"No. You rarely do."

His fingers were careful and rough at once. Chalk had settled into the hair on the back of his hand. When he turned her wrist to wrap the rag, his thumb passed over the pale burn scar inside it. He did not ask what had made it. The fact that he might have, and chose not to, disturbed her more than the question would have.

"You should sit down before you fall down," she said.

"I'm sitting down."

He had not noticed he had eased onto one knee to save the shoulder. When he shifted, pain crossed his face so quickly another person might have missed it. Elise did not.

"Show me," she said.

"There's nothing to show."

"Then that makes us both liars."

He looked at her properly then, tired enough that pretense had thinned out of him. "You say that as if it were intimate."

"Tonight it may be."

For a moment she thought he would refuse. Then he sat back against the chalk wall and began unbuttoning his shirt with one hand. His left shoulder moved more stiffly than the right. When the cloth slid down, she saw the older scar high above the shoulder blade, white and puckered where the bullet had gone in, and beneath it the bruise the pole had already started to print into him in darkening bands.

She reached before she had decided to. His skin was cool with drying sweat and powdery with chalk. When her fingers touched the bruise, his breath caught.

"That bad?" she asked.

"Only when a woman with no mercy puts her hand on it."

"Poor man."

"Unspeakably."

The dry wit in it made her want to laugh and do something less harmless than laugh. She fetched the water again, wet a corner of cloth, and wiped the chalk away from the bruise with small strokes. He stayed still for her. The room had narrowed further to lamp hiss, water drip, and the scrape of cloth on skin. There was nothing else.

"You didn't have to defend me in front of them," she said at last.

"I know."

"That sounded expensive."

"Most things worth doing do."

She looked at the bruise instead of his face. "You still don't trust me."

He took his time answering, as he did with everything that mattered. He never made a show of patience; he merely had more of it than other people. "I trust some things about you," he said. "Not all of them. I suspect the missing parts would matter if I knew them. I also think Abel is young enough to mistake suspicion for intelligence, and I won't let Ferrand's filth decide the shape of our nights."

The phrase "our nights" settled low in her body in a way it should not have.

"You make that sound almost domestic," she said.

"God forbid."

"You're tired."

"Very."

"And still pompous."

"A little."

She smiled before she could stop it. He saw the smile and did not answer it with triumph, only with the smallest softening at the corners of his mouth. The softness made him more dangerous than beauty would have.

She finished wiping the bruise and left her hand there. Under the skin she could feel the delayed hammer of his pulse. His shirt had fallen open across his chest. The hollow at the base of his throat was gray with chalk. She knew the smell of printer's ink on him now, the damp wool, the labor-salt. She knew too much and not enough.

"How did you learn to carry like that?" he asked.

The question was gentle, which only sharpened it.

"The same way everybody learns," she said. "By being given heavy things and no choice."

"That's an answer. It's not the whole answer."

"No."

"Will you tell me the whole of anything tonight?"

She could have lied with more care than that. She was too tired for care. "Probably not."

He nodded once, accepting the wound instead of pretending it was not one. "Then I'll settle for what you can manage."

The pity in that would have driven her from the room. The absence of pity kept her there. He was not absolving her. He was making space she had not earned.

Elise set the cloth down. "If this is still comradeship," she said, hearing how private and sharp her own voice had gone, "it's a filthy form of it."

"No," he said. "It isn't that anymore."

He did not move first with his hands. He moved with the look, with the decision not to look away. She crossed the distance because if she let him come to her with that patience she would lose what remained of her nerve. Her mouth hit his harder than she meant it to. His back struck the wall. The breath he let out was almost a laugh and then not a laugh at all because she kissed him again and his good hand came up into her hair.

There was limestone grit between their mouths. She tasted it, and the salt under it, and the fact that he had been carrying half a machine an hour ago. None of it felt separate from the work. His hand slid down the back of her neck and closed there, thumb braced under her ear, holding her to the kiss. When he kissed her jaw she felt the delayed tremor run through herself again, but lower now, lower and meaner.

"Jeanne," he said once, against her cheek, as if asking whether the name would hold.

It did what it always did. It answered for her. She put her hand over his mouth for a second, not to silence him but because the false name hurt more when it was tender, then moved the hand down and began unfastening the rest of his shirt.

He watched her do it. The steadiness of his attention nearly undid her. Men watched for vanity, for permission, for advantage. He watched as if seeing what she chose were its own fact. When the shirt opened she bent and put her mouth on the old bullet scar first. His whole body tightened. It was not recoil. It was memory.

"You don't have to be kind to it," he said, voice low and unsteady.

"I wasn't intending kindness."

An honest sound broke out of him, half breath, half broken laugh. She bit lightly at the skin beside the scar. He took the back of her head in his hand and held her there, palm spread through her hair, because his body had decided to need something.

When his fingers found the buttons at her bodice, they fumbled from fatigue. She pushed his hand away and did them herself, faster, annoyed by the cloth, by the hooks, by the whole expensive architecture of female respectability that still sat between her skin and what she wanted. "That look," she said.

"What look?"

"As if you'd like to blame me for something."

"I'd like," he said, the schoolmaster stripped out of his voice now, "one minute with your mouth on me and no more talk."

The pull of it shot straight through her, sudden enough that she made a sound and hated herself a little for how quickly it came.

"None of what?"

"That look. You're wet in my hand and angry at yourself for it."

"You don't know anything about that."

"Enough."

He hissed when she got her hand inside, then closed his eyes with a discipline that looked almost pious until she moved again and took the piety out of him.

He shrugged out of his coat one-armed and spread it over the folded printer's blankets, swearing softly when the bad shoulder protested. She pushed him back onto it and climbed over his lap before he could waste another word on courtesy.

Under her skirts the night still clung cold to her legs. He got both hands to her thighs and stopped when he felt the tremor there.

"Are you cold?"

"No."

"Afraid?"

She could have lied. "Yes."

He nodded as if fear were no insult. "So am I."

His admission of fear made it possible in a way tenderness would not have. She leaned down and put her forehead to his. "Then don't be careful with me like glass."

"You're not glass."

"No."

"But I'm trying not to hurt you."

"Lucien." She caught his wrist and pulled his hand higher, between the split panels of her drawers. "Then do something useful."

The first touch there nearly folded her in half. She had been tight with strain for hours. Want had been sitting under the strain, mean and bright, waiting for the room to empty. "I came here carrying a press."

"That too."

"Yes," she said, because at this point dishonesty would have been idiotic.

"Good?"

"It means you're still on my lap, and I'm still half afraid you'll bolt."

He looked up at her, breath unsteady. "You sound pleased with command."

"I'm pleased with efficiency."

"Liar."

"Yes."

She rode him harder after that because talking had become impossible. The stone wall answered each movement with a dull scrape against her hand. Lucien's hand, open now instead of gripping, moved once along her spine and stopped at the middle as if even tenderness had to rest.

Then the cough took him for real. It was not the warning cough from before but the deep one from the damp in his lungs. She shifted off him at once and reached for the water jug. By the time she got it into his hand he had mastered it back down, but the effort had left his eyes wet and furious.

"That's indecent timing," she said.

"I've had the same complaint myself."

He drank, handed her the jug, and pulled his shirt together without bothering to button it. She sat beside him on the blankets with her skirts still askew, chemise hanging open, feeling his wetness cooling under the split of her drawers and hating how intimate that felt. "Then I won't ask."

"That's not sensible."

He had given her the cruelest answer available: no absolution, no protection, the choice left where it belonged.

"Clemence loves almost nothing at this hour."
