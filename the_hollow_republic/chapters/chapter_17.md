# Chapter 17

The Charenton papers lay squared to the edge of Vautrin's desk as neatly as cartridges. Blue office stock, red pencil, the asylum seal pressed into two of them so hard the paper had gone white around the stamp. Elise saw the total before she sat down. Three hundred and twelve francs overdue. Twenty-eight due again at the month's turn. Since the cart took Lucien east and the masons set to sealing the quarry mouth, her side pulled when she bent, the half-healed damage from the tollhouse catching under the stays of a dress that still smelled faintly of old smoke no matter how often she beat it out.

Vautrin remained standing for a moment, one hand on the back of his chair, giving the numbers room to begin the work before he spoke. His cuffs were white. The room beyond the glass partition was not. Clerks moved through heat and dust with ink on their thumbs and sweat dark under their arms, but his office held the stale cool of shut shutters and authority. Even the spittoon had been polished.

"Sit down, Mlle Valette," he said. "Standing will not change the sum."

She sat because the pain had already decided for her. "You stopped paying."

"I stopped advancing funds against an operative who had burned state material, vanished from supervision, and declined to deliver the map or the statement subsequently requested of her. The Charenton office was patient for a fortnight. It has now become exact."

He pushed the top sheet toward her with one clean finger. There was another paper beneath it, not a bill but a transfer note. If the arrears were not covered, her mother would be moved out of the paying wing and into the common women's ward. Elise could see the rest without the clerk's hand. She had once followed an attendant through the wrong door while looking for a doctor and had seen the ward by mistake: iron bedsteads close enough for the blankets to touch, chamber pots shoved under frames, a woman with a shaved patch over one ear rubbing her gums on the blanket edge, trying to worry the cloth into food. Her mother had not known her in her small room either, not on that day, but the private room had still left a scrap of dignity to fail inside. The ward took even that.

"What do you want said?" Elise asked.

Vautrin sat at last. He folded his hands over the blotter. "That Lucien Delatour directed the atmosphere that produced the tollhouse outrage. That he knowingly harbored Ferrand, encouraged Saint-Clair's appetite for theater, and used his authority over that refuge to make violence inevitable."

"That is not what happened."

"It is the version a court can use."

She looked at him. "Ferrand worked for you."

"Ferrand received money from several directions, as such men do. He is dead. Delatour is alive, which means he remains available for public meaning."

The phrasing was almost enough to make her laugh. Public meaning. Abel in pieces under brick and chalk, Nadir dead in a room no comrade had seen, the quarry sealed like a grave with people still coughing in her memory, and Vautrin called it meaning. She kept the laugh down because he liked evidence of strain.

"You already have witnesses," she said. "You have police. You have whatever Ferrand told you before he died."

"I have policemen who entered after the fact, a provocateur whose usefulness ended in fragments, and headlines that must be nailed to something sturdier than rumor. You can give me sequence, access, intimacy. You slept in Delatour's rooms. You heard him in private. A woman who has shared a bed is believed in ways a sergent-de-ville is not."

He said it without savoring it. That made it worse. He reduced her body to evidentiary value as neatly as he reduced a dead printer to a category and a boy to an appetite. She thought of the quarry's narrow sleeping gallery, blankets slung from hooks, Lucien waking once with his hand spread stupidly warm across her stomach in the brief idiot peace of sleep, while the world outside kept billing and seizing and classifying. She had betrayed him there before she ever admitted it out loud. Vautrin wanted the rest priced, signed, and shelved.

"And if I do it?" she said.

"Your stipend resumes. The Charenton arrears are settled. The twenty-eight francs go out again at the month's turn. The file records that you overextended yourself in difficult circumstances but returned to usefulness before lasting damage was done."

"My file."

"Among other things."

He let that sit between them. The charges had already assembled: accomplice, destroyed state property, withheld intelligence, concealed known radicals, lies told under instruction, a wanted organizer in her bed, and her return from him with nothing but a scorched dress and a talent for making herself pitiable. The forms existed. He had told her so in other rooms about other people, and she had been cold enough then to admire the mechanism.

She touched the bill with the backs of her fingers. The paper was thick, better quality than anything in the quarry except Lucien's notebook and the map on linen backing. Three hundred and twelve francs. More than three months of her old pay, if Vautrin paid on time. More than a season of Clemence's laundry work when the weather was against her. More money than Elise could raise quickly by honest means, which was to say by none. Her purse held eleven francs and some sous, enough for food if she began counting each loaf, not enough to buy a mother an extra month of calm.

"How long before they move her?" she asked.

"The note says Thursday."

He left the date on the paper. Thursday now meant a bed change, a door opening, an attendant with a docket and a tired face telling her mother to take her things when most of those things were not her own and half of what was hers had already been lost to damp or another woman's hands.

"If I refuse?"

"Then the debt remains what it is, and I am obliged to think more carefully about your own exposure. You understand the difficulty. A confidential operative is a discreet category only while she remains productive."

Elise looked at the transfer note again. The clerk at Charenton had blotted one word too early, so that common had a blue tail beneath it like a stain spreading through linen. She thought of her mother's hair being redone by whichever attendant had the least disgust that day. She thought of the last time she had paid for oranges on the way out because her mother had once liked the smell even after names went loose in her head. She thought of walking into some newspaper office and saying exactly what Vautrin needed in the tone a jury would trust: regretful, factual, intimate. Delatour believed this. Delatour ordered that. Delatour made boys dangerous. It would work because so much of it could be laid over the truth like tracing paper.

Vautrin watched her think with the same administrative patience he brought to a file. He had always understood that the body broke before the creed did, and bills before either.

"No," she said.

His eyelids lowered a fraction. "You should not answer quickly. Quick answers belong to people with savings."

"I said no."

"Because you love him?"

"Because Ferrand has had enough of the page."

That reached him at last. What crossed his face was flatter than rage and meaner for being so. Vautrin leaned back and smoothed an invisible crease from his cuff. "Love is often only vanity with a bruise on it. Still, I am not unreasonable. You may wish to see what you are buying with this refusal."

He opened a drawer and took out a visitor's authorization. La Sante prison, infirmary corridor, same day, signed with the swift pressure of a man used to making doors open for people he would never touch. He laid it over the Charenton bill.

"He asked for you once," Vautrin said. "That was yesterday. I have delayed answering in case you recovered your judgment. You may go now. If you return before six with a statement I can use, the note to Charenton goes out tonight and the twenty-eight francs continue without interruption. If you do not return, I shall assume you prefer principle to comfort. Principle has never yet paid for anyone's keep."

Elise took the prison authorization. She left the bill where it lay because touching both papers at once felt too much like consent.

The corridor outside was all scratch and murmur. A copy clerk with a nicotine-yellow moustache was trimming the edge of a stack of warrants with a paper knife, making the warrants look like devotional cards in his hands; when he looked up at Elise his left eye watered without blinking, and he dabbed it with the same square of linen he used on the blade. He had probably gone home each night to someone who asked whether the city was calmer now. Elise wondered what answer he liked best.

She went down the stairs slowly, one hand on the rail when the pain made the wall seem less dependable than metal. By the time she reached the street, sweat had soaked the waistband of her chemise. Paris smelled of horse heat, drains, and hot bread from somewhere she could not afford to stop. She counted her coins in her pocket while she walked, not because the count might improve but because the fingers wanted work. Eleven francs, four sous, a bent five-centime piece, and the small brass thimble Clemence had once thrown at her during mending because Elise held a needle like a woman born to copying and not to cloth. She had forgotten to give it back. It had ridden out the quarry in her pocket like a petty theft from a life that no longer wanted her.

La Sante rose out of the afternoon in straight lines and official brick, every wall declaring that plumb line and punishment had built this place together. The quarry had closed around bodies because history and theft had eaten it hollow. The prison closed around bodies because the state preferred its holes numbered.

Women waited beneath the outer arch with baskets on their arms and food smells clinging to them: rabbit fat, onion, stale coffee, a packet of cherries going soft in the heat. Elise had brought nothing. The lack shamed her more than the search that followed. A wardress with thyme tucked brown and brittle inside her collar took Elise's permit, squinted at the signature, and said, "Hands out. Pins, paper, coin. If you've brought him a letter or a prayer card, spare us both the lie. The infirmary clerk already let him keep one little memorandum book, and he won't get another scrap from you."

Mme Drouet was stitched in fading thread above the woman's pocket, though the wardress herself was broad as a washtub and old enough to have sons. One boot was laced with blue string instead of leather, and she kept hitching at the hem of her skirt between each instruction, not from modesty but because the seam had come loose and worried her more than the prisoners.

Elise emptied her pocket into the tray. Coins. Handkerchief. The brass thimble. Drouet turned the thimble over in her palm.

"Sensible thing to hold on to," she said, and set it aside with the rest. "You get ten minutes if he doesn't start bleeding again. If he starts bleeding, you step back and let me work."

"Is he in the infirmary?"

"If he were fit for the regular room, you'd be in the regular room."

No cruelty in it. Only practice. Drouet waved her through a second gate.

The prison took her inward by stages: one courtyard, one iron door, a short passage that smelled of limewash and piss, another gate, the echo of keys moving ahead of them before the man carrying them came into view. Elise had learned the quarry by wrong turns, by scraped knuckles and counting breaths between drips. Here the state had already chosen every turn for her. Fourteen paces to the stair. Nine steps up. Corridor to the left. Window high enough to admit light without horizon. Nothing in La Sante asked a body to trust instinct. The place existed to grind instinct down into obedience.

Drouet stopped outside a narrow room where the infirmary overflow had been made into a visiting space by means of a table, two chairs, and the pretense that anything supervised was therefore orderly. "Ten minutes," she repeated. "If you pass him paper, I see it. If he puts that blue memorandum into your hand, I see that too. If you shout, I end it. If he falls, don't catch him unless I tell you."

She did not leave altogether. She settled on a stool by the door, drew a sock from her pocket, and resumed darning the heel with a bone needle, her lips moving as she counted stitches under her breath.

Lucien stood when Elise entered, then stopped halfway upright and put one hand on the table until the coughing passed.

He looked smaller because prison had taken the room around him that usually completed him. In the quarry he had always seemed to belong to whatever wall or bench he leaned against; stone had taken his shape and kept it. Here the chair, table, and iron bed beyond the half curtain all refused him. His coat hung wrong on his shoulders, and a palm-sized blue memorandum book sat in the breast pocket, its cover bruised at the corners and marked with a violet infirmary stamp. There was dried blood in the seam of one nostril and an old yellow bruise under his right eye that had gone the color of weak tea. His beard had come in more silver than she remembered. When he straightened fully, she saw where the wrists had been rubbed raw above the bones, not dramatic, just thorough.

"Did Lucie get clear?" he asked.

He wasted no breath on greeting or accusation; he went straight to the child. His voice had thinned but not lost its shape.

"Yes."

"Clemence?"

"Yes."

He nodded once. "Good. Then we can waste the rest on harder things."

Elise sat because standing made her feel like a witness already. The chair leg was shorter than the others and rocked on the floorboards. "Vautrin gave me the permit."

"I assumed as much."

"He wants testimony."

Lucien smiled with one corner of his mouth, a habit she had first mistaken for ease. "Of course he does."

"He wants me to say you ordered the tollhouse. That Ferrand acted with your knowledge. That Abel followed your instruction. If I do it, Charenton stays paid."

That last part changed his face more than the first. Pity never crossed it; attention did. He lowered himself into the other chair carefully, with the care of a man carrying glass in his chest.

"How much?"

"Three hundred and twelve overdue. Twenty-eight more by the month's turn. Thursday for the transfer."

He looked at the table while he added it in his head. She could see him doing the sum, not because he loved figures but because figures were what poor people got instead of mercy. At length he said, "That's a bargain laid across your mother's bed."

"It would keep her out of the common ward."

"I know what it would keep her out of."

Drouet's needle clicked once against her bone thimble by the door. Outside, someone coughed behind a wall with the loose, wet sound of a man not yet done being beaten.

Elise kept her hands flat in her lap. "If I tell them Ferrand worked for the police, it will sound like a lover trying to save the man she helped ruin."

"Because that is what it will be."

The sentence landed plain, without force raised for effect. He had always sounded most dangerous when he stopped dressing pain in wit.

"I came anyway," she said.

"Yes."

"I don't know why that still surprises me."

"It shouldn't. You've never lacked nerve. Only a place to spend it that didn't rot the hand using it."

He coughed again, turned aside, pressed a cloth to his mouth, folded it before she could see too much, and set it down with his fingers still pinning the cloth tight, trying to press the blood back into obedience. The cloth had been white in some earlier life. Now it carried the rust-dark center of the prison infirmary.

"Listen to me," he said. "If you mean to save me by saying I'm innocent, don't."

She almost answered on reflex and stopped herself because Drouet was in the room and Vautrin might as well have been in the walls. "You're not guilty of that."

"Of the blast? No. Of plenty else, yes. I hid men the Republic wanted. I kept false papers. I printed names they preferred to lose. I let boys talk themselves into being history because I thought time and work would cool them before vanity or grief set fire to them. I was wrong about one of them, and he's dead. Don't drag me into court as some harmless tutor caught among wolves by accident. They won't believe it, and I won't wear it."

"Then what am I supposed to call you?"

"What I am."

"And what is that today?"

For the first time something like tired amusement passed over his face. "A prisoner with a bad cough and a poor opinion of strategy."

She should not have smiled. The smile came anyway and hurt more than the wound under her stays. Drouet glanced up from the sock, saw nothing actionable, and went back to counting stitches.

"He can make her life smaller," Elise said quietly. "My mother's. He has already started. He knew exactly which paper to put on top."

"I know."

"Don't say you know unless you mean Charenton by smell."

That brought his eyes fully to her. She had not raised her voice. Charenton did it for her: chloride, cabbage water, old linen boiled too often, the sweet wrong odor of women who had forgotten when to ask for the pot. Her mother's fingers moved on the blanket, still sorting ribbon lengths from the shop where she had once worked before grief and fever and whatever else had cracked her loose from the world. Elise had learned what a latch and one respectful attendant came to there. Twenty-eight francs a month for a room with a latch and one attendant who still called her mother Madame on good days.

"I mean it," Lucien said. "If you'd sold me for that, I'd have known why. I wouldn't have blessed it. But I'd have known why."

Drouet's needle clicked once. Elise looked at the darkening center of his folded cloth and hated that he had made room for the act without taking any of its filth away.

"That's not what I came for."

"No." He leaned back, shut his eyes for a second, then opened them again. "You came because part of you still wants me to sort the truth so you can live inside the easier half."

She looked at the door, at Drouet's bent head, at the sock growing beneath the wardress's blunt fingers. "Was any of it true?"

Lucien did not pretend not to understand. The quarry had stripped them of that sort of cowardice months ago.

"You know it was," he said. "You also know what else was true. You loved me. You reported on me. You came back after you knew what I was and after you knew what you were. I'm not going to pull those things apart and hand you a version that sits easier in the mouth."

Her throat filled with something mean and hot that was not quite tears and not quite anger. She thought of his mouth on the scar inside her wrist, of the way he had once read proofs aloud while buttoning his shirt because there had been no hour left in the day that belonged only to desire, of Abel dead before nineteen had learned how ordinary his hands were, of Clemence's face when the brass marker dropped from the torn hem. Nothing in it separated. She had known that before she asked. She had asked anyway because wanting the lie did not make her foolishness any less real.

Lucien drew a breath that turned into another shallow cough. When it passed, he glanced toward Drouet. The wardress was knotting thread with her teeth now, giving them the professional privacy of someone who had seen too many people say worse in lower voices.

"There's something else," he said.

He touched the breast pocket first, then drew out the little blue memorandum Drouet had already warned her about. The violet stamp on the cover had smeared into a bruise. Elise knew it at once. She had seen it in the print chamber, in Lucien's coat pocket, under his hand while he copied corrections from prison notices or burial scraps or the names children gave him for adults who had stopped coming home. He set it on the table between them with the care reserved for the last crust in a hungry room.

She stared. "They let you keep it?"

"At intake they opened it, found bread tallies, burial scraps, names nobody in uniform thought worth stealing, and called it an infirmary memorandum. The doctor wanted the hours of the coughing set down. Drouet watched them stamp it and hand it back. After that I wrote smaller."

"What's in it now?"

"Enough."

He pushed the notebook toward her two inches. At the door Drouet lifted her head, took in the movement, and lowered her eyes again to the sock. The bone needle moved in and out of wool.

"Nadir is in there under his own name," Lucien said. "Abel too. Henri Vasseur. Two women from the lodgings on rue des Plantes, called whores in print because the police found men in the room and could not imagine hunger packing bodies any other way. A mason buried under the wrong name because his brother could not pay the clerk to mend the line. Prison dates. Arrest dates. Where they took the bodies after."

Elise put her hand over the notebook but did not take it yet. "Why give it to me?"

"Because you know how their files move. Because you can still read a clerk's page and hear where it lies. Because if I keep it, they will either take it properly next time or let it rot with me, and both are waste."

"I can try to get you out."

His expression changed so little that the refusal seemed to arrive before the words. "No."

"You don't know what I still have."

"You don't have enough."

"I've got old routes. I know people who owe me."

"Then use none of that on a rescue fable." His voice stayed low, but it gathered force by refusing drama. "The quarry is gone. Abel is dead. Nadir is dead. Ferrand has already been turned into whatever the police need a dead agent provocateur to be, which is to say not himself. If they kill me here or try me first and kill me after, I don't need a lawyer's little innocence. I need witness."

The word settled in the room heavier than plea would have.

"With what?" she asked. "A notebook?"

"With the notebook. With what you know about Ferrand's wages, if any of it still sits in your head. With your own hand, if that's all that's left to you. Put the names beside the lies and force them onto the same page. Do that instead of trying to fetch me out through doors that are already shut."

He slid the notebook fully into her reach.

She picked it up. The cover was warm from his body.

"You're asking me to carry this while you stay here," she said.

"No." The dry corner smile again, gone almost before it formed. "I'm asking you to do one thing badly and all the way through. That's harder."

Drouet rose from her stool. "One minute."

Lucien looked at Elise's hands around the notebook. "Hide it."

She slipped it inside her bodice, under the stays, where the pressure of the cover lay hard against the tender line of healing under her ribs. The pain made her suck a breath between her teeth.

"Still bad?" he asked.

"I'm walking."

"That was not an answer before either."

The tenderness in it came too late to be mercy and too early to be farewell. She hated him a little for managing that.

"Lucie asks practical questions at night," she said, because if she did not speak now she would do something idiotic like touch his hand in front of the wardress. "Clemence still counts bread by halves even when there is nobody left to impress. They got through because you stayed."

"Good."

"That's all you have to say?"

He met her eyes. Prison had thinned him, but it had not gentled him into the kind of man she might have deserved more easily. "No. It's only all that fits before she throws you out."

Drouet came to the table, sock in one hand, key ring at her belt knocking lightly against her thigh. Up close Elise saw that the blue string in her boot had been braided from embroidery silk, probably cut out of some child's ruined cuff. Drouet looked from one face to the other, not curious, merely tired.

"Time."

Lucien stood because habit was stronger than pain. He had to put his hand on the chair back again when the cough caught him. Elise did not move to help. Drouet had told her not to, and besides, the room had become too full of rules to risk one more human reflex.

"Elise," Lucien said.

He had not used her true name aloud since the rupture in the quarry.

She stopped in the doorway.

"If they make you choose," he said, "save the book before you save my name."

She nodded because her mouth had gone useless.

Drouet escorted her back through the same limewashed passages. At the search table she handed back the handkerchief, coins, and thimble. Her glance went once to Elise's bodice, where the notebook changed the line under the stays by a finger's width.

"I told you I'd see paper," she said, low enough that the waiting women beyond the rail could not hear.

Elise's hand started toward her ribs and stopped.

"I saw it on the table," Drouet said. "It came in stamped, and I heard enough to know it's a book of dead names. I'm not sending for the matron to paw through your chemise over that. Keep your shoulders square. Don't come in this heat with black wool again. You'll faint and I'll have to carry you."

It was an opinion, not kindness, but it belonged to Drouet and not to the prison, which made it feel larger than it should have.

Outside, the light struck hard off the paving stones. The women with baskets had changed places with another line of women, newer parcels, same faces of waiting worn into different shapes. Elise moved into the narrow strip of shade along the wall and opened the notebook just enough to see the first page.

Lucien's hand was as she remembered it: legible even in haste, each entry given a full line even when the page was cramped.

Nadir Belkacem. 3 June. Arrested on delivery, not in flight. Unarmed. The escape was invented afterward. Body not returned.

Abel Saint-Clair, nineteen. 4 June. Printer. Burn marks on two fingertips. Dead in the tollhouse cellar before the papers made him a doctrine.

Henri Vasseur. 27 March. Taken alive. Sober. Reported missing after, though he had not gone anywhere by choice.

Below the names he had added prison names, corrected spellings, burial grounds, mothers and sisters where he had been able to find them. On one line the printed word inconnu had been scratched out so hard the paper roughened under the nib.

Elise shut the cover and slid it back under her stays. The board edge found the tender seam under her ribs at once. By the time she turned off the prison wall and into the next street, each step had taught her its exact shape.
