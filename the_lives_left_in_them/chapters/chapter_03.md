# Chapter 3

Sloan's number had gotten Mira as far as Blue Hour Laundry. Forty open dryer doors breathed heat into the market dark.

The place sat on the canal market corner under a blue sign that buzzed hard enough to make the window glass tremble. Heat came out every time the front door opened, detergent heat and boiler heat and the sweeter animal note of damp cloth left too long in a basket. Under that there was Cordera itself, salt and fryer grease and tram brake dust. Her work badge stuck damp against her waistband. She stood in it with a milk crate of stolen-looking parts against her hip and watched Jo Villanueva snap a fitted sheet into obedience so sharply the woman folding beside her actually laughed and flinched at the same time.

Jo had a silver braid down her back and the hands of someone who had wrung water out of other people's lives for forty years. The skin over her knuckles looked sanded by soap. Her shoes were the practical black kind that promised no beauty and no mercy. She kept a cigarette tucked above one ear even though the city had not allowed indoor smoking in fifteen years and she had not been caught obeying anything in at least twenty.

"No," she said, before Tavi had even got the second crate through the door. "Absolutely not. You're not making my laundromat smell like cooked skin."

Tavi nudged the door shut with his heel. He had two salvage cases hanging from one hand and wore the same grin he used when the job was difficult enough to interest him and stupid enough to insult him. "Good evening to you too, Jo."

"You can have good evening when I stop hearing from three different women that some claims girl and my upstairs disappointment fixed a hotel cleaner in a room with no license."

Mira shifted the crate before it cut deeper into her forearm. The top layer held tubing, clamps, and a cracked sterile hood whose sterility had become mostly spiritual. "We need a room with running water," she said. "A drain, a locking door, and a circuit that can take a pump."

Jo turned and looked at her properly then. The look wasn't unkind, but kindness had nothing to do with it. Mira knew it from claims, the quick assessment that asked what category of trouble this was and whether it paid. Jo's eyes dropped to the CommonFrame badge Mira had forgotten to take off her waistband. They came back up.

"You need a church," Jo said. "I've got a laundromat."

The front room was full. A man in restaurant whites fed quarters into a machine with one hand while reading game scores off his wristpad with the other. A girl in a green school polo did algebra on the lid of washer nine and licked powdered cheese from her thumb between steps. At the back, an older woman in leopard-print slippers had claimed an entire folding table for hemming a church dress and looked up only to enjoy the argument. Nothing in the room was private. That was exactly why Tavi had asked for it.

People saw things in laundromats and decided, together, what had happened.

Mira had spent the day at CommonFrame writing damage notes gentle enough to survive management review and ugly enough to survive court. She had eaten crackers from a break-room machine for lunch and counted, twice, how far next week's paycheck had to stretch before it reached rent. The milk crate dug into her arm where sweat had already slicked the skin. She felt stupid standing there with illegal equipment and a corporate badge on the same body.

Tavi set his cases down beside a row of humming dryers. "We're not asking for the front room. We need the back. One night to start."

"One night starts all the others." Jo looked at the crate in Mira's arms. "What exactly are you fixing back there?"

"Whatever comes in broken enough to bury somebody," Mira said.

Jo's mouth twitched. "Use a real answer, mija. 'Whatever comes in broken' is how bad churches fill folding chairs."

Mira bit the inside of her cheek. She was tired enough that honesty felt cheaper than salesmanship. "Bargain line repairs. Quiet ones. The kind people can't bring through licensed intake without getting turned into fees."

The woman in leopard slippers gave a low whistle like she'd just seen a decent card on the table.

Jo glanced toward her and raised one finger without looking. The woman went back to her hem.

"I don't want cops," Jo said. "I don't want tissue leaks in my grout. I don't want strangers dying behind my dryers and I don't want some rich idiot wandering in here because he got adventurous in a rental body and thinks the neighborhood owes him discretion."

"Nobody rich is coming to me for discount bench work," Tavi said.

"People with money love bargains almost as much as they love pretending they found them."

That was true enough that even Tavi did not answer right away.

Jo put the folded sheet into a basket, smoothed it once, and came around the table. Up close Mira could smell cloves on her breath and the ghost of smoke in her hair. "What do I get besides criminal exposure and one more thing to mop?"

Tavi opened his mouth. Mira got there first.

"A cut," she said. "Cash, up front, per case."

Jo's eyebrows went up.

Mira kept going because stopping would make it sound negotiable. "We pay for water, electric, and the room. You approve who hears about it. If it draws heat, we shut it down when you say. No arguments."

"How much?"

Mira had done the math on the walk over and hated the math because it assumed clients, parts, and luck. "One in five."

Tavi looked at her, not offended exactly, just surprised. That was fine. Surprise was cheaper than getting thrown out.

Jo sucked her teeth, considering. "One in four if you bleed on my floor."

"One in five and we clean the floor ourselves," Mira said. "Bleach, drain, linens replaced if we ruin them."

Jo let the silence hang. The dryers knocked behind them with the soft blunt rhythm of big bodies turning over in sleep.

"Who else knows?" she asked.

"Right now?" Tavi said. "A hotel cleaner with a repaired shoulder, one courier with loose morals, and whatever saints monitor bad ideas."

"That courier's name is Luz's cousin Berto," said the woman in leopard slippers, without looking up. "He has no morals and two bicycles."

Jo pointed at her. "See? This is what I mean. Nothing stays secret in a room full of socks."

The room laughed, not loudly but enough.

Mira felt herself flush, then got angry at the flush. The whole point was that nothing stayed secret in a room full of socks. Claims had taught her what silence cost when only the company got to keep records. A laundromat ran on another kind of documentation: stains noticed, tears discussed, whose card had bounced, whose kid needed a ride home. Jo was not a neutral hideout. She was a switchboard.

"Then let it spread your way," Mira said. "Call it something members hear about if they need it, not a clinic or surgery."

Jo's eyes narrowed, interested now. "Members."

"Laundry members," Tavi said, catching up. "You already have loyalty cards, don't you?"

"I've got index cards and a pencil."

"Perfect. Old-school criminal infrastructure."

Jo slapped his shoulder as she passed him, not hard but not gentle either. "If anyone asks, you're doing boiler maintenance in the back and special garment restoration for members only. Nobody says clinic. Nobody says repair desk. Nobody says CommonFrame where customers can hear it. If anybody throws up in the folding area, they clean it themselves."

She jerked her chin toward the curtained hallway behind the change machine. "Come see your kingdom."

The back room had once been storage and then, by the look of it, three or four other things after that. Metal shelves held detergent boxes, paper-wrapped lightbulbs, a crate of orphaned socks, and two ledgers thick with Jo's handwriting. A utility sink leaned under a cracked mirror. Heat from the boiler made the small room damp and soft-edged; steam gathered along the ceiling and turned the single bulb into a moon behind gauze. It was the first place Mira had stood all week that felt less like a workplace than a body cavity.

There was enough room for a folding table, a stool, and maybe two people if they liked each other or had no choice.

"This is terrible," Tavi said, with obvious affection.

"It's the room you're getting."

Mira set the crate down and looked at the floor drain, the sink taps, the outlet by the boiler. "The line's grounded?"

Jo snorted. "Mostly."

Tavi leaned to inspect the socket. "Mostly is how great art happens."

"Mostly is how you got that nose flattened," Jo said.

He touched the bridge of it reflexively and grinned. "That was actually an argument about baseball."

"That was actually an unpaid invoice."

Mira found herself smiling before she wanted to. It slipped out because fatigue had taken too much off the top of her self-control. She looked away before either of them noticed.

Jo did notice. Mira could tell by the angle of her head. But Jo only crossed her arms and said, "Here are my rules. You lock the back door. You keep the front room boring. No one under sixteen comes behind that curtain. If the Bureau or city inspections show up, you're boiler maintenance and you look offended that anybody asked. You leave my customers enough machines that they can still wash clothes, because unlike your various crimes, laundry is a basic service."

"Fair," Mira said.

"Also, if you make money in my building, you feed somebody besides yourselves once in a while. I'm not running a starvation startup."

That one landed harder than the rest. Mira thought of the crackers in her bag, of Gabe pretending he had eaten when he had only had coffee. "Fair," she said again, quieter.

Jo nodded once. "Tonight only. If I hate it by dawn, I put the room back to mops."

She started toward the curtain, then turned back. "And take off the badge, mija. You look like you came to deny a claim."

Mira unclipped the CommonFrame badge and put it face down on the shelf beside the orphaned socks.

For the first time since she'd walked in, the room felt possible.

***

The wetbench took shape the way bad neighborhoods rebuilt after storms, from whatever had not yet been hauled away.

Tavi worked fast and talked faster. The main frame had been an old clinic table with one leg replaced by stacked transit guides and a shim cut from a detergent bucket. The pump came from salvage, the filters from a fertility lab that had gone bankrupt, the tissue warmer from somewhere Tavi would not specify because the answer would either be gross or expensive and he was enjoying having one of those left ambiguous. He fitted parts together with the reverence of a mechanic resurrecting a machine no one respectable would admit still mattered.

"Hold that line up," he said. "No, higher. If it sags, it backs up and then everything smells like overcooked bone broth."

"You keep selling the experience," Mira said.

"You'd rather I called it collagen runoff? Because that is the other option."

She held the saline tube while he clamped it into place. Her hands knew bodies from the outside in, bruises graded by color, joint damage logged against liability tables, dried fluids read like weather. Repair work asked for another intimacy. Here the question was not what happened and who owed. It was what still functioned, what could be persuaded, what the body would forgive if given half a chance and cleaner tools than the city thought the poor deserved.

The room got hotter as they worked. Laundry steam seeped under the curtain and mixed with the sour-metal scent of sterilant tabs dissolving in the sink. Tavi had brought six sealant packs, four sterile sleeves, a tray of reusable needles wrapped in paper that had once been white, and half a canister of anesthetic foam whose gauge looked personally offended by how low it was.

Mira set items out in rows because rows calmed her. "This is maybe three serious cases."

"Four if nobody is dramatic."

"Nobody ever says, I can only afford a calm emergency."

"That's because you work in claims and I work in miracles."

"You work in salvage."

"Exactly. Lower overhead."

He snapped on gloves, then peered at the sterilizer setup Jo had let them build out of a stockpot, a rack, and the boiler line. "You know what our real problem is?"

Mira looked at the supplies. "Money."

"Besides money."

"Cover."

"Besides cover."

She glanced at him. "You want me to say everything."

"I do. It makes me feel accompanied."

He tapped the dry-erase board he had scavenged from behind the detergent shelf. The ghost of an old note still showed through: ORDER BLEACH. "We need rules before the first panic walks in here and lies to our faces."

Mira had been thinking the same thing since Jo said yes. Rules were what made chaos billable. They were also what let you sleep without inventing fairness from scratch every twenty minutes. She took the marker from his hand.

At the top of the board she wrote:

BLEEDING / LOAD-BEARING / RETURN-FEE RISK

"That's not a rule," Tavi said. "That's triage in block letters, which, to be fair, I respect."

"It becomes a rule if we obey it."

"Fine. Add one for idiots who wait until something smells sweet."

She wrote:

SWEET ROT JUMPS THE LINE IF FEVER

Tavi nodded, satisfied.

"Price?" she said.

He leaned against the table, thinking. His burn scar showed pale along his forearm where the glove cuff stopped. "Parts cost, plus what we can stand to ask. Cash preferred. Barter only if the barter isn't insulting."

"I can't put 'what we can stand' on the board. Give me numbers."

"Schedules are how you end up sounding like CommonFrame."

He was right and she hated that he was right. She wrote anyway.

PARTS PLUS LABOR. QUOTE BEFORE CUTTING.

"Better," he said. "No tabs unless Jo vouches."

She added it.

"No names on paper," he said.

Mira capped the marker. "Absolutely not."

"You want evidence?"

"I want follow-up."

"I want not going to jail."

She looked at the board, then at the stack of laundry tickets Jo kept by the door. Thin pink slips, numbered, carbon-backed, anonymous unless somebody chose otherwise. They looked like nothing. Which meant they could hold a lot.

"Ticket number only," she said. "Model line if they know it. Symptom, part, and whether the contract is still active."

Tavi groaned theatrically. "You're making forms."

"I'm making memory. Those are different things."

"Barely."

The curtain shoved aside before she could answer. Jo came in carrying two mugs of coffee and the expression of a woman already regretting her own generosity.

"Drink this before you both get clever," she said. "Also, your first customer is asking the front room if the boiler fixes knees."

Tavi looked at Mira over the rim of his mug. "See? Marketing."

Mira set hers down untouched. "We're not even stable yet."

"You're as stable as you can afford," Jo said. "And he's dripping on my tile."

She stepped aside.

The man behind her could not have been more than twenty-eight, but the bargain runner frame he wore had the lean overbuilt look CommonFrame sold to couriers and dock sprinters, calves braided under cheap dermal gloss, shoulders narrow for cutting wind. His left pant leg was slit from knee to cuff, and pink synovial gel had soaked through the fabric and turned the hem dark. He held a bike helmet under one arm. A tiny paper saint was tucked into the helmet foam, and one thumbnail was painted a chipped, hopeful blue.

"Berto Llamas," Jo said. "Luz's cousin. He swears he didn't fall because he was showing off."

"I wasn't showing off," Berto said. He tried to stand on the hurt leg, failed, and laughed through his teeth in a way that was almost a sob. "I was making a left."

"On what, the moon?" Jo asked.

"Subway grate by Ocho. Wheel slipped."

Mira was already looking at the wound. The kneecap housing had split on the lateral edge. Cheap shell, cheap seal, but the real problem was deeper. Every time Berto shifted, the quadricep bundle spasmed ahead of the pain, anticipatory, as if bracing for a second impact.

"Contract still active?" she asked.

"Till nine tomorrow. I've got to return it clean or they hit me with the whole limb."

"How much cash?" Tavi said.

Berto's face did the fast ashamed math poor people did when they had to name a number out loud. "Seventy-six and some transit credit."

Mira looked at Tavi. The parts alone would eat most of that.

Jo reached into her apron, slapped three folded twenties onto the tray beside the wetbench, and said, "You can owe me, not them. And I don't trust him."

"Cruel," Tavi said.

"Accurate."

Berto stared at the money. "Jo, no."

"Pay me back by bringing your aunt's sheets when she stops pretending she can do stairs."

His mouth tightened. That was the first real thing Mira saw on him, deeper than panic or pain. "She told you?"

"She tells everybody the wrong facts and me the useful ones. Sit down."

Berto sat.

The desk began because there was no room left for it not to.

***

By the time Tavi cut Berto's pant leg away, the front room had worked out that something expensive and embarrassing was happening behind the curtain.

Laundry slowed in little pockets. Voices dropped, then rose again with the strained brightness of people performing privacy for each other. Mira could hear Jo out front pushing the room back into its normal shape by force of will.

"Machine six is free if anyone wants to stop eavesdropping and wash those uniforms today," she called.

Tavi laid Berto's leg into the bench cradle and peeled back the cheap dermal seal around the knee. A pocket of fluid burped warm against Mira's glove. Beneath it, the lattice had frayed where the shell's load-bearing mesh should have taken the twist and had not.

"That's a nasty one," Tavi said. "You hit anything after the grate?"

"Just dignity."

"Unrepairable."

Berto barked a laugh, then sucked air because laughing moved the knee.

Mira checked pupil lag, pulse sync, contract port glow at the ankle. Everything was ordinary except the tremor. She rested two fingers against the runner frame's shin and felt the muscle firing before Tavi touched it, overreactive, defensive.

"Berto," she said, "any other symptoms before the fall?"

"Pain?"

"Anything strange."

"Define strange."

Tavi got the irrigation line into place. "In my profession that question has an excellent range."

Berto wet his lips. "Twice this week I got on the platform at Mercado and my whole body locked up. I don't even take trains when I'm working, but suddenly I was sure my foot was stuck between the train and the edge. Like really sure. I could feel the sole peeling off. Smelled hot wire and piss and..." He swallowed. "I thought it was low sugar."

Mira looked up.

"Did it happen after the fall?" she asked.

"Worse after. On the walk here I heard the tram and nearly threw up."

Tavi's eyes flicked to hers for half a second. It was enough. He had heard it too. This wasn't a pain response or ordinary panic. Something had been carried in.

He said, deliberately casual, "Any history of seizure flags on the contract?"

"No. Clean rental." Berto tried to lift his head and look at the wound. Mira put a hand lightly on his shoulder to keep him back. He smelled like chain grease, sweat, and the mint gum he was chewing too fast. "Is it bad bad?"

"It's expensive bad," Mira said. "We're aiming for less expensive."

She heard herself and knew it was a claims sentence, dry enough to travel. Berto almost smiled anyway. Sometimes process language was mercy if it gave pain a number-shaped edge.

Tavi ran the scanner wand over the joint. The cheap display lit the torn mesh in jerking red. Around the tear, faint static fluttered through the superficial signal map. It wasn't supposed to be there. Mira had seen residue artifacts on claims scans, little storms in tissue that should have been fully washed. This one pulsed when the scanner tone rose, as if the body recognized not the machine but the remembered approach of something larger.

Berto heard it too.

His whole leg kicked. "Jesus Christ, platform."

The words came out before he seemed to know he was saying them. He grabbed for the table edge, eyes wide, and Mira felt the panic transfer through the bench frame into her own wrists. For a second the little room narrowed to metal lip, bad angle, the sensation of a shoe sole smearing away under weight that was about to become train weight. It did not belong to her. It did not belong to Berto either. But it touched the room anyway.

Tavi killed the scanner tone.

Silence came down hard. Out front a dryer door slammed. Someone laughed too loudly.

"Okay," Tavi said, gentler now. "Okay. Stay with me. You're here, not there. Knee first, ghosts later."

"I don't have ghosts," Berto said, breathing fast.

"Sure," Tavi said. "Bad wording. Sorry."

Mira reached for the anesthetic foam, checked the gauge, and used less than she wanted. "Listen to me. We can patch the load-bearing damage tonight. The other thing, the platform panic, we can damp some of it if it rides the local lattice. But if this model is carrying overlap, it may flare under stress."

"Overlap," Berto repeated, blank.

"Borrowed residue," she said. "Things not yours."

"Can you take it out?"

The honest answer was not with what they had in a laundry back room on seventy-six dollars and Jo's loan.

"We can get you through return without the knee collapsing," Mira said. "And we can tell you what to watch for next time."

Berto shut his eyes. "Fine. Do it. I need the body till nine."

Tavi worked then with the focus Mira had first noticed in the cramped room where they fixed the hotel cleaner. Outside the joke, outside the performance, he became nearly tender with ruined tissue. He trimmed frayed mesh, laid fresh lattice like careful patchwork, sealed the torn housing in layers so the stress would distribute instead of splitting again. He talked while he worked, not because he needed to hear himself but because the patient did.

"This part's ugly but boring. If I say rude things about CommonFrame engineers, that's for my morale, not because you're dying. Hold still. There you go. That tendon line is cheap as paper, by the way. If you sprint in the rain, they're basically begging to delaminate."

"You say that like I chose the deluxe package," Berto muttered.

"Nobody in this room chose the deluxe package."

Mira irrigated, blotted, checked seal integrity. She could feel the shape of the work settling into her hands, less foreign each minute. Claims had made bodies into documents. Repair made documents into flesh again, or something close enough to keep a person earning.

When she placed her palm above Berto's kneecap to steady the joint for sealant, another jolt ran through the muscle. Weight hit damp concrete. A thigh was already shaking. Somebody behind him shouted not to look down. The borrowed sensation was so quick it barely deserved the name memory. It was still enough to turn the room colder in her mind.

Pattern, she thought.

One impossible model on a Friday shelf she could have lied to herself about. A pattern meant queue.

She wanted that not to be true because it scaled fast. Whatever CommonFrame was doing in the bargain line had already leaked into the city hard enough that couriers were puking at tram bells and calling it low sugar.

Tavi sealed the final layer and sat back. "Try weight."

Berto eased off the table. The repaired knee held. He bent once, twice, face pinched, then nodded. "Shit. Okay. Okay, that's..." He looked from Tavi to Mira and seemed to realize gratitude would cost him more than he had cash for. "That helps."

"Return it slow," Mira said. "Don't sprint on wet metal. If you hear rail noise and your stomach drops, step away from the platform edge before your body decides something for you."

"That sentence is terrible."

"It's also useful."

Berto dug the seventy-six from his pocket in damp folded bills and transit chips. Jo took her sixty back out of the pile without comment and pushed the rest toward Mira. Tavi counted parts on his fingers and made a face like a man swallowing his principles whole.

"Next time," he said, "you come in before the joint opens like a peach."

"Next time I come in for a tune-up, not a harvest."

"That costs extra."

As Berto limped out through the curtain, Jo was already intercepting him.

"You tell anyone useful?" she asked.

"Only people who need it."

"Useful to who? Give me names."

"Luz. My aunt. Maybe Pilar if her hand still does the weird church thing."

Jo closed her eyes briefly, as if consulting a patient god. "Fine. But they come with laundry."

Mira looked at the board, then added another line under the rest.

SAY THE WEIRD PART FIRST

Tavi read it and laughed under his breath. "That might be the whole business plan."

She did not laugh. The scanner's little red flutter was still in her head like afterimage. "How many times have you seen that?"

He wiped his hands on a towel and avoided the question by answering a narrower one. "Enough that I hate bargain-line knees on principle."

"I asked for a count, not a principle."

He met her eyes then. Humor still on the surface, but thinner. "Tonight's not the night for a lecture, Mira. Tonight's the night for keeping the room upright."

That, she thought, was also not an answer.

Before she could push him harder, the curtain moved again.

Jo put her head through. "Congratulations. Pilar brought laundry."

Behind her, Mira saw the first edge of a line.

***

The next six hours made rulemaking feel less like governance than triage with handwriting.

Jo took the front room by the throat and turned it into cover. Every person waiting for the back got a pink laundry ticket, a wire basket, and some version of an instruction. Buy soap. Fold something. Sit near machine four and look bored. If anybody asked, Blue Hour had started a members-only garment restoration service for delicate fabrics and private embarrassments. The story improved each time she told it. By midnight it had acquired a waiting list and a loyalty discount.

People obeyed because Jo sounded like the kind of woman you obeyed if you liked surviving community spaces.

Pilar came first. She was a Dominican hair braider with lemon-yellow glasses and a tiny tattoo of a safety pin behind one ear. She carried a mesh laundry bag full of salon capes and held her left hand close to her chest like a bird she did not trust. The hand itself was uninjured. That was the problem.

"It keeps doing this," she said.

She set a washcloth on the table to demonstrate. Her fingers found the corners, folded, rolled, and twisted the thing into a pressure bandage so fast Mira barely tracked the steps.

"You braid for a living," Mira said.

"I do heads, not trauma."

"Maybe you contain multitudes," Tavi said.

Pilar did not smile. "I was doing a christening set this afternoon and my hand tied off a vessel knot on a baby's dress ribbon. A real one. I know because my brother's a medic and he almost cried laughing. Then at dinner I reached for my glass and my whole arm wanted to brace for impact. I haven't braced for impact in my life, unless you count my first marriage."

That got a short, grateful snort out of Jo, who was leaning in the doorway with her ledger open against her stomach.

Mira checked the port at Pilar's wrist and the superficial motor map. There was no structural damage, just a noisy loop in the borrowed response layer. It was a cheap body with an active contract through Monday morning. The return-fee risk would be high if she reported "uncommanded procedural reflexes" through official channels. CommonFrame would translate that into instability, then debt.

"We can damp the loop," Tavi said. "We can't scrub it clean tonight."

"How much?"

He named a number. Pilar winced, then reached into her bag and added three packets of salon dye beside the cash. "My cousin imports these without taxes. They're worth more than they look."

Tavi examined the packets. "What color?"

"Copper smoke."

"That means nothing."

"It means women pay extra."

"Accepted."

While he worked the dampener patch across her forearm, Pilar told Jo from the table that she had finally left the man who hated her glasses because he said they made her look smarter than him. Jo said any man threatened by corrective lenses deserved his own extinction event. Mira found herself studying Pilar's face while the patch took hold. There was nothing generic about her. She had a tiny notch in one front tooth, probably from childhood, and a habit of tapping her ring finger twice against any hard surface before saying something true.

When the patch settled, the twitch in her hand eased.

"If it starts doing emergency medicine again," Mira said, "step away from whatever sharp thing you're holding."

"You say that like there is a version where I don't hold sharp things."

"Then at least point them away from customers."

Pilar left with her salon capes, two boxes of detergent for appearances, and the expression of somebody not exactly reassured but at least no longer alone with the problem.

After her came a newlywed called Saira Quon in a pearl-pinned scarf and a hotel robe under her street coat because she had dressed in a hurry and had not realized until the laundromat line noticed. She was twenty-three at most, with henna still dark on her hands and the red pressure marks of too-tight ceremonial bangles circling her wrists. Her husband stayed in the front room with a basket of towels on his lap and the fixed, desperate politeness of a man who would rather fight a bus than discuss his honeymoon with strangers.

Saira did not sit until Jo pulled the curtain fully closed.

"I'm not hurt," she said at once. "I mean, not torn. I checked. I just..." She swallowed and made herself continue. "I rented the upgraded body for two nights because my mother said if I was only doing this once I should do it properly, and now every time he touches the back of my neck my stomach drops out and everything below my waist goes rigid like I'm waiting for bad news. It's not his fault."

Mira did not ask whether the mother had helped pay. She did not need to. The robe cuffs were monogrammed with a hotel logo so fancy it practically sneered. The wedding body itself was a bargain-adjacent glamour line, smoother skin, better cheekbone architecture, cheaper internal calibration than the premium brochures promised. Mira had seen the upsell sheets: Romance Package, Confidence Suite, Memory-Muted.

Tavi crouched to scan the lumbar signal. "Could be residue caught in the parasympathetic loops. Could be the body's original stress scripts lighting up under intimacy. Could be your husband is bad with his hands."

Saira stared at him, horrified.

"Ignore him," Mira said.

"I help by widening the sample set."

"No, you don't."

Saira made an unwilling noise that was almost a laugh and then covered her mouth with the heel of one hand. "He's not bad," she said into her palm. "He's careful. That's almost the problem."

Jo, from the doorway: "Men are often careful exactly where they should be useful."

That got the laugh the room had been circling.

Once Saira settled enough to let them work, the problem proved real and ugly in a way Mira recognized. The rear cervical contact points lit with a stress cascade that did not match Saira's breathing or voluntary response. Somebody else's fear was sitting in the body like a hand over the mouth. No consciousness rode in it, nothing that whole. All it held was a preserved flinch with nowhere to go.

Mira hated the specificity of it because specificity made the company harder to excuse.

She held the scanner steady while Tavi laid a soft suppressor strip along the neck and upper spine. "This won't make the body brave," he said. "It'll just stop it from borrowing the wrong caution."

Saira nodded too fast. "That's enough. Brave is my problem."

When the suppressor synced, the body's involuntary lock released in a visible unwinding. Saira sat very still, feeling the difference. Her eyes filled before she looked up, furious at herself for it.

"Don't cry in front of your husband if you want him to keep being normal," Jo advised.

"Jo," Mira said.

"I'm helping. Men panic when women cry in robes."

Saira laughed again, more openly this time. "You sound like my aunt."

"Your aunt sounds competent."

At the door Saira hesitated, then reached into the robe pocket and produced a tiny frosted cake from the hotel minibar, still boxed. "We can't eat this now," she said. "He's too nervous and I'm too embarrassed. Take it."

Jo took it with solemn gratitude. "This is the nicest fee anyone has paid me tonight."

By one in the morning the back room had acquired layers. Used wrappers sat in one bin, clean instruments in another, pink tickets clipped along a string over the sink, marker rules crowding the board. Mira added categories as the cases educated them.

NO PHOTOS

CONTRACT ACTIVE? SAY SO

IF THE BODY HEARS SOMETHING YOU DON'T, TELL US

Tavi added one underneath in bigger letters:

IF YOU WAIT TILL IT ROTS, I WILL MOCK YOU

Jo underlined it twice.

At 1:17 Jo shoved the curtain open with her hip. "I need a yes or a no before she paints my floor."

The woman behind her wore grocery-delivery green gone black under the right ribs. A dish towel was knotted hard around her waist. Each breath pressed pink gel through the cloth in slow glossy beads. She was maybe thirty, with rain-plastered hair and one delivery-app bud still lodged in her ear as if denial needed audio support.

"I can still finish the route," she said.

"That's not what I asked," Mira said.

"Neither did Jo," Tavi said, already moving.

"She's Nessa Aguirre," Jo said. "Third-floor walk-up, case of bottled water, heroic stupidity. She kept telling me it's just leaking a little."

Nessa untied the towel. The bargain stair-flex body had opened along the load seam under the lowest right rib, not a clean cut but a peeled split where cheap shell had sheared off the mesh below. Clear gel and diluted blood glossed the opening. Every inhale widened it another millimeter. The room filled with the sweet-rancid smell of stressed tissue and hot plastic.

"Contract active?" Mira asked.

"Till eight. If I return it like this, they bill me the whole torso."

Mira looked at the tray. Three sleeve wrappers already sagged in the bio bin. One sterile sleeve remained in its paper. The foam gauge had sunk into the red.

"How honest do you want it?" Tavi asked quietly.

"Clinically," she said.

He scrubbed his hands again and flicked the foam canister with a fingernail. "Proper internal weave takes both of us gloved and more anesthetic than that sad little hiss. What we can do is irrigate, lay surface lattice, seal the seam, and bind her hard enough to keep it from opening if she goes straight home."

Nessa's face tightened. "I can't go straight home."

"You can," Mira said. "You can't finish the route. If you lift with that side again, it'll unzip to the mesh and then you'll owe them a torso and a sermon."

"Price?" Jo said.

Tavi named it. Lower than the labor. Higher than mercy.

Nessa flinched, then nodded once. "Do the ugly version."

Mira wanted to tell her there was no version tonight that wasn't ugly. Instead she washed in, set out irrigation, and watched Tavi pull on the last sterile sleeve.

"This is the last clean one," she said.

"I noticed," he said. "Let's act like professionals till we run out of costume."

The foam gave them one pale ribbon across the split and died there. Tavi looked at the dead canister, then at Nessa. "That was all the polite chemistry."

Nessa laughed once, sharp with fear. "Great."

"After this," Mira said, "you get pressure, not comfort. If you need to yell, do it before he anchors the mesh."

"I can hear you both."

"Good," Jo said. "Then we're all aligned."

Tavi had to work one-handed inside the seam, guiding forceps with the sleeved hand while Mira held retractors and gauze at the edge because they could not afford a second person going in dirty. He hated the compromise; she could feel it in the clipped precision of his breathing. Nessa bit down on the rolled washcloth Jo gave her and still made a wet animal sound when he set the first anchor. Her heel slammed the pump stand. The suction line skipped. Mira caught the basin before it tipped, but the motion jerked the curtain half open.

Five faces appeared in the gap. One of them said, "Holy shit," with the full devotional force the phrase deserved.

Jo moved fast enough to blur. She hit the curtain shut with her forearm and turned on the room. "If you aren't the body on this table, you don't touch that fabric. Anybody who peeks loses their place and folds towels till dawn. You hear me?"

Silence answered her, then the timid clink of quarters.

Nessa was crying now without seeming to know it. The tears ran sideways into her hair. "Don't stop," she said around the washcloth. "Please don't stop halfway."

"We're not stopping halfway," Mira said. "We're stopping at what we can close."

That was the truest promise she had in stock. She held to it while Tavi laid external lattice over the split instead of the deeper weave he wanted, sealed the seam in stubborn glossy strips, and cinched a compression binder around Nessa's ribs so tight the woman swore at all three of them and then at whatever god had invented stair deliveries.

When it was done, the tear sat flattened under patchwork and pressure instead of properly repaired. It would hold if Nessa treated her own torso like borrowed glass. It would fail if she forgot.

Tavi peeled off the ruined sleeve and dropped it into the bin. "That's it," he said. "No more open work tonight unless somebody has a miraculous relationship with sterile packaging."

Mira looked at the inside-out sleeve in the waste and felt the night narrow. "Then we stop pretending otherwise."

Nessa swung her legs off the bench slowly, one palm clamped to the binder. "Can I make the return?"

"You can try," Mira said. "You can't run, lift, twist, or be brave for capitalism. If it starts leaking warm, you sit down wherever you are and call somebody who likes you."

"That's a very short list."

Jo held out the pink ticket. "Then don't waste it."

While Nessa counted out damp bills and transit credit with shaking fingers, Jo disappeared into the front room and came back dragging a narrow cashier table from storage. She wedged it outside the curtain, planted the cigar tin on top, stacked pink tickets beside it, and sat down like a woman assuming office.

"New rules," she called. "You tell me if it's open or weird before you get near my curtain. One body past the fabric at a time. If they say morning, you take morning. If you're waiting, you wash something."

Mira went to the board and wrote two more lines in block letters:

ONE BODY PAST THE CURTAIN

NO OPEN WORK AFTER LAST SLEEVE

The new rule got tested within minutes. The teenage busboy with the immaculate side part and the fresh braces stepped up to the table holding his left elbow under a bar towel. He had caught the prep-body on a steam-table hinge and waited because he thought leaking did not count as broken if the joint still bent.

Jo looked at the towel, then at the board. "Morning," she said.

His face went blank with the kind of fear only debt could make that fast. "I start breakfast at six."

Mira checked the elbow at the sink. The seam was small, crescent-shaped, wet at the edges. Fixable in ten clean minutes with supplies they no longer had.

"Listen carefully," she said. "I can wash it, seal the surface, brace it, and keep it from opening wider. I can't repair it properly tonight."

"Will it hold?"

"Till dawn if you're gentle. If you pretend you're fine, maybe not."

"He's a busboy," Jo said. "No one is gentle to busboys."

The kid let out one miserable laugh. "Yeah."

Mira irrigated the elbow, laid a shallow seal over the split, and wrapped it tight enough that he hissed through his new braces. The wrap bought him till dawn and no farther, a delay dressed up with decent bedside manner. She wrote his ticket number at the top of the pink slip and, under it, PRIORITY WHEN WE RESTOCK.

He stared at the words. "You're writing me an appointment in a laundromat."

"I'm writing you priority," Jo said. "Try gratitude."

With the table in place, the rest of the night changed shape. People announced themselves to Jo first, and Jo translated panic into categories harsh enough to be useful. Open or weird. Contract active or done. Cash, barter, or shame. The widower from the fish market became ticket fifteen after Jo wrote SHOULDER PATS ME on the back and shoved him through. The night porter whose calf kept boxing one invisible dance step became ticket sixteen and went in laughing so she would not limp in front of strangers. Nobody else touched the curtain.

A little after two, Mira added another rule:

NO SURPRISE BILLS

If the work was only a patch, they said patch. If the symptoms would come back, they said that too. The lie people could afford least, she realized, was hope sold as completion.

By then the front room no longer sounded like gossip. It sounded like intake. Quarters knocked into metal slots. Jo called numbers. Someone explained model lines near machine four while folding still-warm towels to keep their hands busy. The laundromat had stopped pretending to be cover and become procedure in public clothes.

When the sanitation widower with the clove jar finally sat down and said, "My shoulder keeps trying to comfort me," nobody laughed first. Jo had already taught the room what counted as useful detail. Mira took ticket eighteen from the stack and did not have to explain why the number mattered.

By dawn the laundromat had a desk.

By then metaphor had burned off. The same narrow cashier table Jo had dragged out after Nessa Aguirre's scream pulled half the laundromat to the curtain had stayed put long enough to acquire authority. A cigar tin held cash. A stack of pink tickets leaned against the fish-shaped ceramic dish where hair ties, transit chips, and tips nobody wanted to count in public had begun to collect. Machine nine still sat disassembled in the corner like a martyr to cover stories. On the wall behind the desk, the whiteboard had filled almost to the edges.

DO YOUR LAUNDRY WHILE YOU WAIT

The sky outside had gone from black to the bad gray that made canal water look like dishwater. First tram of the morning rattled somewhere uphill. Mira could hear it before she saw the light smear across the window. Her spine felt packed with sand. She had been on her feet so long the soles of them no longer quite belonged to her.

Tavi sat on the stool with his gloves off and his head tipped back against the boiler, eyes closed. In the weak morning light he looked older than thirty-eight, all the bench years visible at once in the stoop of his shoulders and the pinch between his brows. There was dried sealant on his wrist and frosting on his shirt. One of Jo's orphaned socks had ended up hanging from his back pocket like a defeated flag.

Jo counted cash from the cigar tin in quick clean snaps. "Don't let this inflate your egos. Half of this is because night people are sentimental."

"Night people and your extortionate room fee," Tavi said without opening his eyes.

"Pay it or perform surgery in the canal."

Mira leaned against the sink and did her own count on the margin of an old detergent invoice: cash in, parts out, Jo's fifth. Berto still owed Jo twenty she would never chase like a creditor because she preferred leverage with a human face. Pilar's dye packets sat by the mirror next to Saira's little hotel cake box. The busboy's pink slip, marked PRIORITY WHEN WE RESTOCK, hung from the line over the sink. Nessa's aftercare notes were still trapped in Mira's head because ticket numbers were safer than names. They had made more in one night than Mira had expected and less than they needed. It was enough to buy supplies and enough to continue, but not enough to fix rent, which was worse in some ways.

"We need sterile sleeves, period," she said. "More foam. Better signal pads. And actual records."

Tavi cracked one eye open. "Ticket numbers are actual records."

"Ticket numbers are laundry pretending."

"Exactly. Good records with plausible deniability."

Jo slid a smaller pile across the desk toward Mira. "Your share."

Mira looked at it. The bills were damp at the edges from too many pockets. A ten smelled faintly of fish. One of the twenties had lipstick on the corner. It was cash from the city's private humiliations, and it was cash she needed. It had come because CommonFrame and everyone like it kept turning injury into debt faster than people could heal.

She took it.

She didn't take it with noble reluctance or with shame dramatic enough to purify the act. She took it because Brine Court wanted twenty-four hundred dollars they did not have and Gabe's old frame debt still circled his legal wages like gulls over trash. Relief arrived first, plain and muscular. The moral bookkeeping could wait.

"We're still missing the secrecy part," she said.

Jo laughed so hard her cigarette nearly fell from behind her ear. "Honey, secrecy is over. We have replaced secrecy with manners."

That was true too. By three in the morning the line had stopped pretending not to know what the back room was for. What Jo had built instead was etiquette. Nobody stared when someone came out walking better than they had gone in. Nobody asked which part had seized or torn unless invited. Towels, baskets, and the ordinary chores of the place gave everyone something to do with their eyes.

The older woman in leopard slippers, who had stayed almost the whole night under the pretext of finishing one impossible hem, tapped the glass on the way out and called, "My nephew wants the Tuesday slot if you decide you're respectable."

"We're never being respectable," Jo called back.

"Then put him down for Tuesday."

The bell over the front door jingled again before Mira could answer.

A young father came in carrying a sleeping toddler on one shoulder and a bag of work coveralls in the other hand. He had the pinched expression of somebody who had gone from night shift to parenting without a legal amount of sleep between them. Behind him a woman in grocery-delivery green held her right wrist in her left hand and tried not to look urgent. Neither of them seemed surprised by the desk.

"We closed," Tavi said automatically.

Jo glanced at the two waiting figures, then at the board, then at Mira. "Did we?"

The grocery courier lifted her wrist a little. "Pilar said mornings are for tickets."

The father shifted the toddler, who snored into his neck. "Berto said if I brought wash it counts."

He held up the bag of coveralls like proof of citizenship.

Mira looked at the wetbench, the board, the pink ticket stack, the fish-shaped dish with transit chips glinting in it. She looked at Tavi, who was already too tired to fake annoyance properly. She looked at Jo, who had known this outcome before any of them and was enjoying not saying so.

Preparation had ended somewhere around ticket twelve, though she had not noticed it at the time. By dawn the room had hardened into an address. Favor, one-night crime, revenue stream: those were yesterday's names for it. People had already started navigating toward the new one.

She stepped to the desk and pulled the first pink ticket from the stack.

"Contract active?" she asked the courier.

"Till tonight."

"Say the weird part first."

The woman let out a breath that almost shook. "My wrist keeps trying to sign someone else's name."

Mira wrote the ticket number at the top, then the symptom underneath.

Out front, obediently, somebody picked up a towel.

Jo reached for the front lock and flipped the sign from LAST WASH to MEMBERS.

"Blue Hour restoration desk," she said toward the room, like it had existed long enough to deserve a name. "Morning intake starts now. If you're waiting, fold something."
