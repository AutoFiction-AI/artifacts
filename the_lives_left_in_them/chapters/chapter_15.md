# Chapter 15

Mira had dragged Gabe's boots up from under her chair and into her lap because the triage tech had cut the laces off and tossed them at her like waste. Every time the automatic doors opened, cold salt air came in off the street and lifted the smell the emergency ward still couldn't beat by midnight: wet ash, burned insulation, fryer grease from the all-night stand across the avenue, and under it all the sweet cooked stink of cauterized tissue drifting from somebody else's curtain. The left sole was worn harder at the outer heel, the drag mark he always pretended she imagined.

The baton strike had landed across old damage. The admitting resident called it that, clean enough to chart. Mira had seen Gabe's back when they rolled him to imaging. The bruise was still coming up, a long dark band crossing the pale ladder of old surgery scars, already swelling around the places his body had never repaired right the first time. The hospital gown would not close over the brace line. He kept one hand behind him anyway, trying to hold the thin fabric together out of reflex, modesty surviving things that dignity did not.

Jo stood at the foot of the bed with her key ring wound twice around one detergent-dried hand. Half the tags were blackened. Her practical shoes left damp gray prints on the floor every time she crossed from bed to window and back. She had spent the last hour calling the fire desk, her landlord, two laundry customers whose winter things had still been in the dryers, and somebody's cousin who knew how long municipal seals tended to stay on burned storefronts when poor people owned them.

"If machine eleven smoked Mrs. Mena's church coat," she said to nobody in particular, "she's going to bury me in it. Not even a decent coat. That rabbit-fur trim that sheds all over my clean towels."

It was Jo's version of we are not going to talk about the blood yet.

Gabe had managed to steal three strawberry jelly cups off the dinner tray before the orderly cleared it. They sat on the bedside table beside the paper bracelet with his name on it. He had not eaten the toast. He kept pinching the jelly lids until they clicked, one after another, like he was testing whether his fingers still answered.

"You should sleep," Mira said.

"You should stop saying that to people who can't afford it."

His face had gone gray under the fluorescent light. Pain always sharpened him and erased him at the same time. He looked younger when it was bad, all the swagger boiled off, just her little brother with the mouth he got from their father and the same stubborn forehead pressed damp against a hospital pillow. He had been charming with the intake nurse because charm still bought him faster blankets, then had vomited dryly into a paper basin when they shifted him for scans. Both things were true. That was most of his life.

Jo looked at him. "You want another blanket?"

"I want the one they gave me to stop smelling like old mop."

"That's a no, then."

He smiled without humor. "That's a poor people yes."

The curtain rasped open. Tavi came in with two paper cups of coffee and a square packet of pain patches he had probably argued out of somebody downstairs. His hair smelled like the sink sprayer at Blue Hour after a clogged drain. One eyebrow had a crescent burned thin out of it. Someone had wrapped a bandage around his right hand and badly missed the knuckles, leaving the burn cream exposed in greasy white ridges. He was still wearing yesterday's shirt. It had gone stiff at the cuff where something had dried there, maybe foam suppressant, maybe blood, maybe the gray runoff from the floor where the wetbench died.

"They'll let him keep the stronger patch if he signs the fall-risk waiver," he said. "Coffee's criminal. I got it anyway."

Mira did not take a cup. Jo did. She drank without tasting it, then made a face sharp enough to count as gratitude.

"The fire desk says the front is still standing," she said. "Back room's gone, they think. They'll know more when they stop talking to me like I'm renting from my own shop."

Tavi nodded too fast. He did that when he was trying to look useful instead of guilty. It made the loose tendons in his neck jump. "I can go over when they unseal it. If they unseal it. Maybe some of the hand tools made it. Maybe the floor safe under the parts rack held. Maybe the old sterilizer tank blocked the worst of the heat."

Mira set Gabe's boots on the chair beside her. "Stop."

Tavi looked at her. "I was just saying what might still be there."

"I know what you were doing."

The monitor at Gabe's bedside kept up its soft insect tick. Gabe stopped clicking the jelly lids. Jo lowered the coffee until the paper cup touched the sill with a damp cardboard kiss, and from the next bay over came a wet cough followed by the hiss of a nebulizer mask. Gabe opened his eyes, but not because he had drifted off. He was giving them one last chance to act like adults in front of him.

Mira stood. "Come outside."

Tavi did not argue. The surrender of it scraped worse than a fight would have.

The corridor hummed with old fluorescent ballast and vending machines that sounded like they were chewing nails. Mira walked past the family waiting alcove, past a little shrine somebody had made on the sill with a melted devotional candle and a paper cup of carnations, and stopped beside the ice machine where the floor dipped cold under her shoes. Through the wired-glass window in the stairwell door she could see rain moving sideways in the parking lot light.

Tavi set the untouched coffee on the windowsill. "If this is about the files, I'd do it again."

"This isn't about the files."

"Then what?"

She could hear the old claims voice in her own head, the one that took damage apart into categories because categories were easier to survive than facts. It wanted soft tissue, structural failure, unauthorized modification, liability transfer pending. There was no code for your brother screaming when a baton found the same place a surgeon once had.

"That night in the laundry," she said, "you told me you wrote part of the shortcut."

He scrubbed his mouth with the heel of his good hand. "I said I tuned a failure rate."

"There. That's you doing it again."

His head tipped back against the wall. "Mira, don't do the thing where you make me the whole factory. Sanz swung the baton, not me."

Her voice stayed quiet. "Gabe's in there getting another scan because we were still hiding a person your old bosses printed out of other people's debt. Jo's counting a burned business in coffee cups. You don't get to crawl behind shop talk now. Tell me what you did."

He looked at the stairwell door, at the floor, anywhere but her. When he finally spoke, the first answer came out in the loose technician patter he used when he wanted a room to forget he was scared.

"The bargain line wouldn't hold a clean wash at that price. Strip it too hard, the lattice loses grip, the joints go dumb, and the shells start coming back inside warranty. I found a softer landing."

"That's still bench talk."

His laugh snagged and died. He put both hands on his hips, then hissed because the burn pulled. "Fine. I wrote the tolerance bands. I told them how much residual pattern a bargain body could carry before claims had to call it unstable."

"Residual pattern from who?"

He stared at her. "You know who."

"No. Plain."

The ice machine kicked on with a rattle like loose bone in a tray.

He flinched at the sound. "From people. From whatever they could strip and reuse. Dead people sometimes. Sedated renters. Debtors. Anybody whose contract had enough buried consent to pass legal and not enough money to fight back. We called it stock because that was the word that let us keep eating lunch."

The hallway narrowed around her in the literal sense. The building had been renovated in sections by whoever bid lowest, and the wall by the ice machine bellied out around old plumbing. When she breathed in, the smell of chlorinated mop water slid over the back of her tongue.

"You knew," she said.

"Not all of it at once."

"Did you know enough to quit?"

His jaw worked. "Yeah."

"Did you?"

"No."

"Why not?"

He looked at her then, finally, and there was no performance left in it. "Because the raise hit before my spine did. Because once you tell yourself you're improving a bad system instead of feeding it, you can live inside that lie for a long time. Because rich line got clean wash and bargain line didn't, and I knew what that meant before I had every file in front of me. The fix they wanted from me was never shut it down. It was make the theft land where claims could call it contamination."

She bit the inside of her cheek until copper came up. It was either that or slap him, and she was too tired to do either cleanly.

"You didn't write a shortcut," she said. "You sat there and figured out how much stolen life they could stuff into poor bodies before anybody with a salary had to admit what it was."

"Yeah."

"You helped them decide what poor people had to wear inside their own skin because clean bodies cost more."

"Yeah."

"And then you came into my laundry room acting like you were just the man who hated it."

"I did hate it."

"You profited first."

He nodded once. "Yeah."

She wanted him to defend himself. She wanted something she could cut through. Instead he stayed braced against the wall and let the facts rot between them in the fluorescent hum. The copper taste in her mouth thickened. Without the bench talk, without the clever language, there was only Tavi's burned hand and the work he had named.

"I burned the files because they had names in them," he said after a moment. "Client names, repair notes, addresses, the workarounds that kept people walking. I didn't do that to come out noble. I did it because I knew exactly what a seizure team could do with those records, and because the same hands that helped build the line were the hands on the keyboard. That part is also true."

Mira folded her arms hard enough to feel the ridge of her own ribs under smoke-stiff fabric. "Don't confuse useful with forgiven."

He closed his eyes for a second. "I wasn't asking to be forgiven."

At the end of the hall a child started crying with the raw offended howl of somebody who had not yet learned pain could outlast noise. A cleaner pushed a yellow cart past them, earbuds in, moving around both of them as if they were just another spill to leave for later.

When Mira spoke again, her voice had gone back to claims-room exact. "If you keep standing with us, you say it the way you said it just now. Not aggregate, not residual richness, not optimization. You say people. You say poor. You say you knew."

"I can do that."

"You can do it when it costs."

"Mira."

"No." She looked at the bandage on his hand, the skin puckered bright at the edge of the gauze. "Not to me in a corridor. In a room where they can write it down and try to use it against you. That's the only version I care about."

He inhaled like the air hurt. "All right."

The stairwell door opened behind them. Jo came out backward first, keeping her voice low as she pulled it shut with one hip.

"If either of you are about to make me regret surviving to witness this, save it," she said. Her key ring clicked against the metal push bar. "They're taking Gabe for another scan. He asked for both of you because he enjoys suffering artistically."

Mira moved past her.

Jo caught her sleeve. Detergent and smoke still lived in Jo's skin together, clean labor and ruined labor refusing to separate. "I know what he did," she said quietly, jerking her chin toward Tavi without looking at him. "I also know what he did last night. I don't have room this week for saints. I need hands."

"That makes two of us."

"Good. Then don't waste energy pretending otherwise."

Gabe was back from imaging by the time they returned, flatter with fatigue, his lashes stuck together with sweat. He had opened one jelly cup and eaten it with the edge of a tongue depressor because the spoon they gave him was too flimsy to bother with. The absurdity of it struck Mira so hard she had to look away.

"You two done divorcing in the hallway?" he asked.

"We're not married," Tavi said.

"That answer sounds guilty."

Jo pulled the blanket higher over Gabe's chest with brisk maternal violence. "Your doctor says another twenty-four hours for observation if the numbness stays where it is."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning your left leg is still yours, but everybody wants to keep charging your insurance for the honor of confirming it."

He laughed once, then caught his breath too late and winced so hard the tendons in his neck stood out. Mira sat on the edge of the chair and held the basin before he asked. Nothing came up. His hand found her wrist anyway and stayed there until the spasm passed.

When he could speak again, it came in that unexpectedly clean register he only reached when the joke had burned off.

"If the plan is to hide Lin harder," he said, staring at the ceiling, "then I got hit for evidence, not a person."

No one answered right away.

The monitor kept making its soft indifferent tick. Somewhere two doors down, somebody argued with admissions about a co-pay they did not have. Jo stopped tugging the blanket straight. Tavi looked at Gabe instead of the floor. The sentence settled through the room the way heat leaves bent metal and finds the bend still there.

"There isn't a plan yet," she said.

Gabe turned his head enough to look at her. His pupils were heavy with pain meds but still sharp. "Then don't build the dumb one because you're scared."

Fear had always come to her dressed as care. Keep your head down. Stay off cameras. Use the back stair. Sleep on the floor, not by the window. Wear the hood. Take the long route. Never say your real name where anyone could file it. Mira had called all of that protection because the alternative was unbearable. It had still ended with boots in her lap and her brother under fluorescent light.

She laid her hand over his for a second, careful of the IV tape. "Rest."

"That's not a yes."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

He let her have that much and closed his eyes.

Outside, dawn had not brightened so much as thinned the dark into a colorless wet morning. The city would be awake soon. The raid report would already be writing itself in somebody's system, clean verbs swallowing broken bodies whole.

Mira picked up Gabe's cut bootlaces from the chair and folded them into her pocket like evidence.

* * *

By noon the rain had stopped and the whole block around Blue Hour smelled like wet plaster, burst pipes, and that sugary detergent note that clung after a fire because soap had its own kind of stubbornness. The municipal seal was gone from the front door only because the inspector on scene did not want to stand there while Jo shouted at him in front of neighbors. Yellow tape still hung in the doorway, one strip pasted to the jamb by melted plastic. The front windows had not blown. They had spidered and held, which was somehow uglier. Through the cracks the laundromat looked concussed.

Machine four sat open with a load of shirts turned gray at the collar from soot-water. The folding counter was furred in powder from the ceiling tiles. One whole section of the back wall had gone black where the fire found the old steam chase and climbed. From the threshold Mira could see the shape of the room they had built inside Jo's back room and the shape of its destruction laid over it like a transparency: where the wetbench had stood, where Lin's intake chair had been, where she had once braced a client's split wrist under the warm hum of dryers while Tavi cursed at a clogged suction line, where she and Tavi had fucked against the storage shelves because exhaustion and wanting had turned out to be the same temperature for ten minutes.

Now there was a collapsed metal frame and a fan of char where the parts rack had fallen.

Jo was already inside in rubber gloves and her oldest work apron, the one with a tear near the hem where she kept emergency cash pinned in a folded scrap of plastic. The pocket hung empty. She had a clipboard made from a warped detergent invoice and was writing down every machine number as if the city might pay her for accuracy if she impressed it hard enough.

"Don't step where it shines," she said without looking up. "That's suppressant foam and God knows what else. If either of you slip and split your head, I'm billing the resurrection."

Tavi ducked under the tape behind Mira. He had borrowed a respirator from somewhere. It made his voice flatter, more anonymous, which only sharpened the panic in it. "Back room?"

Jo jerked her pencil. "Look."

He looked.

The rear half of Blue Hour was no longer a room. It was a mouth after an accident, black and wet and full of broken bright things. The dryers along the far wall had burst their glass. One of the carts had melted into a bent aluminum wishbone. Tavi took three steps in and stopped in front of what had been the wetbench. The metal tray had fused to one side, a silver curl embedded in black. He reached toward it before he remembered his hand and swore softly.

Mira did not stop him this time. There was nothing here left to save from heat. Only fragments to account for.

She moved along the folding counter, lifting what could still be lifted. A coffee tin full of unpaired buttons. Two claim tickets glued together by water. A box of nitrile gloves baked into a single blue brick. The cheap desk lamp Lin used for intake, shade blistered but standing. She set that aside. She did not know why. It would never work again. Maybe that was the point.

At the threshold to the back room she found the blue tarp Tavi used to throw over the repair chair whenever a laundry customer wandered too close. Half of it had burned, the edge curled into hard black petals. She saw, with stupid painful clarity, how many hours of their lives that tarp had held in the dark. Bodies under it. Tools under it. Lin under it the first week, not as a person yet in public terms but already somebody they were arranging their days around. Hiding had always required equipment.

Behind her the front bell gave one half-dead jangle.

Mira turned too fast. For one second her body expected boots and warrants.

Lin stood in the doorway with the registry wrapped in clear dry-cleaning plastic and tucked under one arm like something they had carried long enough for it to become a limb. They wore Gabe's old harbor jacket, too broad in the shoulder, the hood down despite the tape and the open street. Their lighter iris caught the window glare and held it. There was soot worked deep into the notch of their left ear. Mira could not tell whether it was from last night or from coming here now against instruction.

"I told you to stay at Brine Court," she said.

"I know."

"Why are you here?"

Lin looked past her into the back room. "Because this is where I lived half my life and none of it counted."

Tavi took off the respirator. "You can't be on the street like this."

"Like what?"

He checked himself too late. "Visible."

Lin absorbed that. They did not look wounded, exactly. They looked like they were filing it in the place where language went to show its bones.

Jo straightened from the counter with a grunt. "I let them in," she said. "If you're going to argue, do it while carrying something useful. Machine nine's coin box is jammed."

Jo was already turning terror into inventory.

Mira crossed to Lin and took the registry from them long enough to set it on the least filthy patch of counter. The cover was still damp from the plastic wrap. She could see where their thumb had rested through the bag, a clean oval in the soot. "You shouldn't have come."

Lin's jaw moved once. "Gabe got hit getting me out."

"That's not your fault."

"It isn't his fault either."

The difference between those two sentences was the whole problem.

Tavi had crouched by the remains of the parts rack. He pulled free a drawer handle, looked at it like it might still become a drawer if he stared hard enough, then let it fall. "I've got a route," he said without turning around. "South yard, freight vans, three swaps before the outer districts. Nobody checks bargain shells hard if the paperwork says maintenance transfer. We could move Lin tonight."

Mira heard how fast her own answer came. "We should."

Her answer came out already shaped into the same bad machinery Gabe had named from a hospital bed: Lin folded into the back of a van under old packing blankets, the registry hidden in a false floor, distance masquerading as safety. By the time the sentence stopped moving, Mira wanted it back.

Tavi stood. "It buys time."

"For what?" Lin asked.

"For us to figure out the next move."

"While I'm where?"

"Somewhere Yaret isn't."

Lin looked at the blue tarp in Mira's hand, then at the steam-blackened hole where the back room had been. When they spoke, their voice was careful in the way it got when the feeling underneath threatened to outrun grammar.

"Every place you've hidden me has been a place somebody else had to keep warm. Mira's apartment. Jo's back room. That little space behind the water heater where the pipes knock all night. Last night Gabe used his body as a door because I was still one more thing to get out before the officers came through. If I leave now, you've still done all of that for something you carry, not someone who can stand up."

Mira felt the rebuke in the objects around them. The tarp. The scorched intake lamp. The folded cots that had once turned this room into shelter after the last train. Protection had been real. So had its shape. Rooms inside rooms. Lives arranged around the problem of concealment.

"Going public could get you seized," she said. "They still have language for property. They have plenty of that."

"They already have it," Lin said. "It's the first language they had for me."

Tavi dragged one hand down his face. Soot streaked the bandage. "Testifying doesn't fix that overnight. It could make it worse before it makes anything better."

"I know."

"Do you?" Mira asked, sharper than she meant to. Then she did mean it. "A public room means your face on record, your serial on record, your voice in transcript. It means CommonFrame counsel calling you contamination while people take notes. It means Yaret gets to look polite at you in a suit instead of breaking your door in. It means there's no going back to Brine Court and pretending the hood is enough."

Lin held still enough that the soot in the notch of their ear and the glare in the lighter iris seemed pinned in place. Mira had seen that stillness before, when too many borrowed memories started knocking at once and Lin had to choose which one got the body.

"I'm scared," they said.

The full form made Mira's head lift. Lin went on before anyone could answer.

"I'm scared enough that my teeth hurt. I keep thinking about the steam in the hallway last night. How it made everything white for one second. How I couldn't see Gabe and could still hear him making that sound when the baton hit. I know what I'm saying yes to. But if I disappear now, then they get to say I was a rumor made by thieves. They get to say Blue Hour burned over bad equipment and grief made the rest of you dramatic. I don't want my life to be the part everyone leaves out because it's inconvenient to print."

Jo set down her pencil. Her eyes had gone glassy with the kind of anger that came after tears and did not need them anymore.

"Well," she said. "That's annoyingly clear."

Lin looked at Mira again, and now the care in their speech changed shape. It got more ordinary, which made it hit harder.

"I know where your kitchen floor sinks by the stove," they said. "I know Gabe swears at weather before he checks the forecast. I know Jo keeps quarters in her apron hem when she doesn't trust the drawer. I know Tavi checks heat with the inside of his wrist because his fingers lie after a long shift. I know the third stair at Brine Court squeals and the fifth one doesn't, and I step over the loud one at night because the woman in 3B yells if she wakes up. Those aren't rumor things. That's a life."

Mira had to look away. The laundromat window threw a net of cracked light over the floor. One strand caught on the serial port at Lin's nape where their borrowed hair had fallen forward and then back again. It would have been so easy to tell them to put the hood up. To make one more small act of concealment and call it love.

Instead she asked, "If we do this, what do you want said first?"

Lin answered without pause. "My name."

Tavi let out a breath that sounded scraped from somewhere low. "All right."

Mira turned to him. "And you."

He met her eyes. Smoke and sleeplessness had hollowed him out. Good, she thought, and hated herself a little for thinking it. "I say what I did," he said. "Plain."

"You say what it cost."

"Yeah."

Jo snapped her gloves off and tossed them onto the counter. "Then stop standing in my wreckage like a family portrait from hell. If you're filing something, file it before Tissue Safety writes the raid as public service and me as an irresponsible small-business owner who stored flammables next to lint."

"What about this?" Tavi asked, looking around.

Jo picked up the clipboard. "This is still mine to mourn. Go make the other mess."

They left by the front because there was no point in sneaking out the back of a burned laundromat under daylight. Mira carried the registry. Lin carried the dead intake lamp. Tavi carried nothing until Jo thrust a scorched metal cash box at his chest and told him to try being useful with a screwdriver later. On the sidewalk the neighborhood had already begun the city's oldest ritual of disaster: looking while pretending not to stare. A delivery cyclist slowed. Two girls coming from the tram stop stopped outright at the sight of the yellow tape. Somebody across the street lifted a phone, thought better of it, lowered it.

Lin did not put the hood up.

The walk to Municipal Arbitration Hall would have taken too long with Mira's legs shaking the way they were, so they caught the tram at the canal market. The car smelled like damp coats and overheated brakes. A man with a lunch pail slept upright with his chin on his chest. Across from them a woman in a floral scrub top kept glancing at the black-smudged plastic around the registry and then away again, city courtesy making room for catastrophe without wanting any details. Every stop felt too public and not public enough.

Mira had spent enough time in Arbitration Hall to know which entrance took witnesses, which one took debtors, which security guard cared more about coffee than contraband, and which forms got disappeared into delay if you used the wrong box. Losing her job had not burned that map out of her. If anything, it sharpened under pressure.

The civic core was all wet stone, wind tunnels, and those new light panels the city kept installing where the old sodium lamps had made everybody look equally sick. The Hall rose out of it in beige civic confidence, too much glass for poor people to feel comfortable entering and too little for anything honest to stay visible once it got inside.

At the security line, the guard barely looked at them. The building ate burned people, tired people, and wet-shoed people by the dozen. The metal detector chirped at the cash box in Tavi's coat pocket, and he surrendered it with a muttered apology. The guard gave him a numbered tray and did not ask why it smelled like smoke.

Upstairs, witness intake was a row of bolted chairs facing three glass windows. The room had that government-building odor of toner, wet wool, and old recirculated heat that somehow made everybody look already defeated. A mother bounced a sleeping child on her shoulder while staring at a screen full of queue numbers. An old man in a transit jacket unfolded and refolded the same citation notice until the paper turned soft.

Mira pulled a ticket. B114.

They waited under a poster about civic transparency printed so cheaply the ink had bled at the corners.

When B114 lit up red, all three of them stood. Tavi stopped half a pace back on instinct, like he still believed the right role for him here was mechanic, not witness. Mira looked over her shoulder until he came level with them.

The clerk behind the glass had pearl nail polish lifting off one thumb and reading glasses hung low on a chain she clearly despised. She did not bother with false warmth. Mira appreciated that.

"Purpose?" the clerk asked.

Mira opened her mouth.

Lin touched her sleeve, light and final. Then they stepped forward until the microphone caught them cleanly.

"I'm here to file witness testimony," they said. Their voice shook once on witness and steadied on testimony. "My name is Lin."

The clerk's fingers paused over the keys. "Surname?"

Keys stopped clacking at the next window. The mother with the sleeping child shifted her weight without taking her eyes off the queue screen. Behind Mira, paper rustled slower. Nobody turned. Nobody had to.

Lin pushed their hair back from the nape with one hand. The serial port sat there in the fluorescent light, ugly and undeniable.

"I don't have one," they said. "You can use the model number if your form needs it."

The clerk looked at the port. Looked at Mira. Looked at Tavi's bandaged hand, at the smoke-black edge of the registry, at the three of them lined up like the start of trouble she was too underpaid to stop. Then she slid a form under the slot.

"Write legibly," she said. "If this turns into a standing fight, they will use bad handwriting against you."

Mira had the paper flat on the counter before the clerk's hand left the slot. Lin reached for the pen before she could offer it.

Their hand trembled only once, right before the first letter. Then they wrote slowly, pressing hard enough to dent the carbon beneath.

Lin.

Lin stood there without a hood, without a back stair, without a tarp, without any careful false name borrowed for a safer afternoon.

Mira laid the registry on the counter beside the form. Tavi set his burned hand flat against the glass like he was swearing into something long before anyone asked him to. Behind them the queue kept shifting, coats dripping onto civic tile, witnesses and debtors and people with parking disputes and custody papers and fines they could not pay, the whole city in line to have itself translated into record.

When the clerk stamped the intake receipt, the sound was small and final. Mira folded the slip once and tucked it into her coat pocket before the ink could smear.
