# Chapter 2

By Tuesday afternoon the appeals room smelled like filtered air losing the fight. Solvent and coolant still came first, the clean corporate lie, but under it the damaged bargain frame on cradle three kept giving off the sweet edge of warmed tissue, sugar left too long on a burner. The H-9 hold light from the weekend kept burning at the back of Mira's head. On Mira's monitor a yellow bar counted down twenty-six minutes until provisional liability posted to the renter's ledger.

Nidia Solis sat across from her in a housekeeping polo the color of weak tea, the hotel name stitched over one breast in gold thread already gone gray with bleach. Her real wrists were strapped in pharmacy braces that had been tightened with safety pins from the tiny sewing kits nicer guests left unopened beside minibars. One of those kits had spilled in her lap while she dug for paperwork. The spill held black thread, white thread, and two pearl buttons the size of aspirin. She had lined them up in a row on Mira's desk without seeming to notice she was doing it.

"I told intake the shoulder was wrong when I picked it up," she said. "It clicked before I got to the tram."

Mira rotated the scan. The bargain body on the cradle was female-coded, medium build, house line musculature, the right arm hanging away from the torso with the sloppy give of a torn support web. Fresh damage glowed angry on the upper layers. Beneath it, buried where a pickup booth camera never would have caught it, an older band of puckered matrix ran around the socket like a healed burn.

If she submitted the scan as it was, the system would do what it always did with undocumented older damage. It would turn the body's history into the renter's fault.

"The pickup attendant note says no visible impairment at release," Mira said. Her public voice came out dry and almost courteous, like she was offering an umbrella instead of the sentence. "Did you sign the acceptance screen?"

Nidia barked one laugh that scraped the room raw. "The one that's already signed when they hand you the stylus? Yeah. I signed that."

On the monitor the pricing ladder waited behind a red triangle. Mira knew the categories by touch now: preexisting substrate irregularity, undisclosed structural anomaly, unauthorized load stress if the system decided the renter had asked too much of leased flesh printed for cheaper work than the work it got used for. Every choice had a dollar amount tucked behind it like a knife folded into a sleeve.

She touched the older scar band on the scan, zoomed in, and let the software estimate age. The answer came back broad enough to protect the company and specific enough to hurt the poor: prior to current rental window, not verifiable to manufacturer release state.

Not verifiable meant billable.

Nidia watched Mira's face the way people watched weather rolling toward laundry strung outside. "Just tell me the number."

Mira clicked through the preliminary fee tree because not clicking through it did not make it less real. The figures populated in stacked white boxes.

Frame retrieval and bench intake: $410.

Joint-lattice reconstruction, estimate only: $1,160.

Tissue contamination scrub: $275.

Manual provenance review, customer-contestable: $340.

Service interruption penalty and temporary account suspension: $1,480.

Security deposit forfeiture pending adjudication: $300.

Provisional total customer liability: $3,965.

The number sat there in clean corporate sans-serif, almost tasteful. Mira made about a quarter of that in a week if payroll had not found a new reason to shave deductions. Brine Court would have loved that number. Brine Court would have sent flowers if she ever came up with that much at once.

Nidia closed her eyes. Her mouth kept moving a second after the rest of her face had gone still, as if she was finishing some private argument. "That's for one shoulder."

"That's before collections fees if it goes to claims court."

"Jesus Christ."

Mira let the silence sit. There was nothing humane to pour into it. Behind the glass wall another adjuster was explaining contamination surcharges to a man in a marina jacket while the man smiled the frightened smile of somebody determined to stay agreeable in front of a machine that could ruin him. The room's white-noise emitters hushed and hissed. From deeper in the annex came the clack of cradle locks and the soft wet slap of a returned body being turned for scan.

Nidia opened her eyes again. They were bloodshot in the corners and bright with the kind of fatigue that had got baked in overnight, not from one bad morning. "If I ask for review, does that stop it?"

It stopped nothing. It froze the debt long enough to wrap it in more language.

Mira kept her hands flat on the desk because if she started rubbing at her cuticles she would look as tired as she felt. "It pauses final posting for up to forty-eight hours. It also creates an adjudication file. There's a fee attached."

"Another fee."

"Yes."

"And then what?"

"Then they look at release records, shift class, strain pattern, maintenance history." Mira heard herself using the house nouns and hated how smooth they sounded. "If they find manufacturer defect, coverage applies. If they don't, the charge stands."

Past Mira's shoulder, the frame on the cradle caught Nidia like a hook. Its face was slack and beautiful in the budget way CommonFrame did beautiful, broad symmetry stamped out to survive strangers. Dried disinfectant had left a milky line along the neck port. "And while they look, I can't book another one."

That was not a question. Mira checked the side panel anyway because ritual mattered in rooms like this. The account flag showed bargain line and labor-class user. She was right.

"Your access would suspend during review."

"So I can't work."

"Not through CommonFrame."

Nidia breathed in through her nose and then stopped because the appeals room had no air worth taking. "I clean rooms at the Saint Meridian. Twelve floors if we're short. Fourteen if the convention people leave early and management gets greedy. I don't mean I supervise the cleaners. I mean I strip sheets and scrub the glass showers where rich assholes shave and leave little gray hairs around the drain like they're doing you a favor. I need the rentals because these" - she lifted both braced wrists a little off her lap - "these won't hold a spray bottle by hour three."

The turn came out plain. It came with no performance and no plea, just the fact laid on the desk between the thread cards and the fee boxes.

Mira had asked hundreds of renters what happened to the frame. Most of them lied from vanity, panic, or habit. Nidia had the scraped-clean air of somebody who'd run out of spare invention somewhere around dawn.

"Show me," Mira said before she could decide whether she should.

Nidia's mouth tightened. Humiliation moved over her face in a shallow wave, quick and visible. Then she put the sewing kit aside and worried at the safety pins one by one with clumsy fingertips. The braces loosened. Her wrists underneath were narrow in the ugly way repeated pain made things narrow. The skin at the base of both thumbs was shiny and overworked. Old steroid injection bruises bloomed green-brown along the inside of one forearm. When she turned her hands over, Mira saw the slight wrong angle in the right wrist where something had set and kept setting itself badly.

"Tendon burn first," Nidia said. "Then the tunnel shit. Then a manager who liked to tell us everybody's body hurts if they want to stay employed. I can still fold napkins. I can still do receipts. I can't wring out a rag and I sure as hell can't lift wet linen all day. So I lease." She nodded toward the rental frame. "That one was my Tuesday-through-Thursday body. Ten-hour housekeeping class. I was on eleven when the arm gave. I had both hands under a king duvet and then I didn't."

The older damage in the scan kept snagging at Mira: chronic strain and scar tissue printed over bad substrate or old injury. The shoulder had been compromised before Nidia ever signed the stylus. CommonFrame had sold her three days of borrowed strength with a break already hidden inside it.

"Did you report the clicking at pickup?"

"I told the kid in the booth the arm sounded cheap."

"Did he log it?"

Nidia gave her a look. "He told me they all sound cheap until they warm up."

Of course he had. A note at release would have made the frame harder to bill later.

The yellow countdown bar slid another notch to the right. Twenty-one minutes.

Mira clicked open the release-booth retention policy anyway, because there were still forms a person had to perform belief in before they stopped believing. Standard pickup footage for bargain labor lines was kept seventy-two hours absent incident escalation. Nidia's rental had released four days ago. Even if it had still existed, review would take longer than a woman with dead wrists and hotel floors to clean could afford.

"If I fight it in court?" Nidia asked.

Mira pictured Municipal Arbitration Hall in the civic core, all polished stone and water stations and seats designed to look generous while they ground a day of wages out of anyone who sat in them. She pictured Nidia there in her tea-colored polo, wrists strapped, taking a number beneath screens that called her liability exposure a neutral matter.

"You'd need the release footage, your shift logs, any complaint made at pickup, maybe a third-party tissue read if you wanted to challenge their age estimate."

"How much is that?"

"More than you want it to be."

Nidia leaned back. The vinyl chair gave a small farting sigh under her. "You people always say it soft."

Mira bit the inside of her cheek. "They train us to say it soft."

Nidia worried the row of tiny thread cards, nudging the black one out of line with a fingernail. "The funny thing is I like the high floors. Everybody thinks I like the tips. It's the windows. You get a slice of the harbor between towers if the weather's clear. Sometimes the ferries look like toys from up there. I rent for the windows as much as the money. My own body's five-two. You spend all day in a service corridor at that height, you start thinking the city forgot to build any sky."

There it was, the detail that had nothing to do with leverage and everything to do with being alive. Mira took it in like something she had no right to keep.

She rechecked the shoulder. The older matrix band had the dense pearled look she had started noticing too often lately, damage or memory or growth trouble nobody wanted to call by a manufacturer name. She thought of the held model in H-9, the undocumented scar under pale ribs, the image copy hidden in her locker. The company line had already cracked in one place. Now here it was again, except this time the crack had a hotel laundry card clipped to it and a woman's wrists tied up in cheap elastic.

"If I don't sign anything," Nidia said, "what happens at twenty minutes?"

"The file moves to provisional capture. Your deposit goes. Your account suspends. Collections mail follows if you don't settle or request review."

"And if I request review, I still lose the account."

"During review."

"Which means I still lose the job."

The yes sat in Mira's throat and spoiled there. Every office version of it turned glossy the second it reached language.

Outside, somebody laughed too loud in the intake queue and got immediately shushed by a receptionist. The sound came through the glass flattened and embarrassed. The appeals room was built to make everyone lower their voice. Pain, debt, and sex residue all had to pass through it at conversational volume.

Nidia pulled the braces back on with slow, ugly effort. Mira watched her fail to close one strap, then watched her use her teeth on it. That decided more than the numbers had.

She clicked away from the fee tree before the system could autosave the preliminary valuation. "Don't sign the liability consent."

Nidia's eyes narrowed. "You saying that because you're kind or because there's some other trap."

"Because once it posts cleanly, I can't slow it without making it worse."

"Slow it how."

Mira opened the manual exception menu. Technical review. Bench re-evaluation. Tissue aging variance. All of them came with audit hooks. She chose the one that bought the most time while sounding the dullest.

`Request: additional substrate aging analysis prior to submission.`

The system asked for justification.

She typed: `Age spread inconsistent with visible tear. Requires local bench comparison before adjudication.`

That was true, which made it easier to lie with.

"This gives me until morning," she said. "Maybe less if somebody upstairs gets ambitious."

"And in the morning?"

Mira clicked confirm and felt the file move from easy corporate certainty into a folder with her name on it. "In the morning I need the shoulder to stop looking like this."

Nidia stared at her. "You got a miracle drawer under there, Ms. Alves?"

One corner of Mira's mouth tried for it and failed. "No."

She closed the scan, printed the tissue layers to old paper because paper still moved through offices quieter than data, and slid the sheets face down into a generic intake folder. Her own reflection flickered in the black of the monitor between windows, dark hair tied back too tight, mole near the temple, mouth already set for whatever bad idea came next.

She had not called Tavi Sloan in three years because a claims adjuster keeping a black-market repairer's number was the kind of stupidity that deserved unemployment. The number still sat in the analog pocket of her wallet, written on the back of a tram receipt by a dock clerk whose illegal knee patch had fooled intake long enough to save him from bankruptcy. Mira had kept it because curiosity and class fear often lived in the same place. You did not throw away exits just because you were still pretending to be indoors.

"Can you get to South Canal after eight?" she asked.

Nidia blinked. "For what?"

"For a second look."

"By who?"

"Don't ask me anything yet that makes me answer out loud."

That got Nidia's attention in a way the fee tree had not. Her face sharpened, pain and suspicion lining up instead of fighting each other. "How much?"

"Bring whatever cash you can stand to lose. Not cards. Cash."

"You're serious."

The damaged shoulder sagged on the cradle. Inside the torn socket, the older scar sat there waiting for the company to call it unverifiable and billable. "More than I should be."

Nidia's throat worked. "If this is some kind of setup-"

"Then you've already got the wrong shoes for it." Mira nodded at the foam slippers peeking from under the chair, patterned with faded cherries. They were clean on top and grimed at the soles, house slippers doing street work because there was no spare pair. "Be at South Canal and Mercer. Blue sign that says laundry. Eight-thirty. If I'm not there by nine, go home and don't answer any collection call until you hear my voice first."

Nidia searched her face like she was checking an unlabeled bottle for poison by smell alone. "Why?"

The honest answer had too many moving parts already. The useful answer was smaller.

"Because that shoulder is older than your contract and they know how to hide that in the wording," Mira said. "And because if I send this up clean, review will turn your wrists into a debt instrument by breakfast."

Nidia let out a breath that shook once at the end. "That's a real sentence."

"I know."

She gathered the spilled sewing-kit pieces with awkward braces and tucked them back into her tote. Before she stood she hesitated, looking again at the rental frame on the cradle.

"If somebody can fix it," she said, "don't make it fancy. I just need it to lift linen. Fancy always breaks faster."

Mira nodded. The line landed harder than anything else had. Around here survival stayed brutally specific: lift the linen, finish the shift, make it to payday.

After Nidia left, the appeals room seemed colder because there was no longer anyone in it trying not to look humiliated. Mira stood with the intake folder under one hand and the timer on her screen running silently toward tomorrow's trouble. Across the annex, cradles moved in steady sequence toward wash, pulp, resale. Claims was not the place where the city's body economy became cruel. Claims was the place where cruelty got grammar.

She slipped the printout into her bag, logged the frame to overnight local review, and spent the rest of her shift hearing the hotel's high floors in the back of her head whenever somebody said premium view surcharge or elevated leisure package or any other phrase that turned somebody else's sky into a line item.

At seven fifty-eight, with the office thinning and the canal outside going the color of old cutlery, she signed the damaged frame out of the bench queue under a waste-transfer code she had no right to use. Sloan's number sat in the analog pocket of her wallet like a hard little splinter.

The body was heavier than its file.
