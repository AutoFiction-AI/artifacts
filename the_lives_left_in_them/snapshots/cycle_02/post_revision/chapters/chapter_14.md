# Chapter 14

The back buzzer had turned into three more clients, a fresh page in the ledger, and Lin licking graphite off their lip again. Mira had barely gotten the first rib scar onto the paper when the front dryers lost their steady coin-fed thump.

The ledger lay open on a flour-sack towel between her and Lin, thick now with handwriting from five different pens, its spine already giving up under the damp. Lin had organized the latest pages by the things digital systems liked to erase first: names no contract had bothered to keep, repeated fractures, serial-port marks, the little bodily facts that made a person harder to pulp into a defect code. Across from Mira, Lin held the book flat with the heel of one hand, their lighter iris flashing pale whenever they looked up into the fluorescent buzz. They had started licking the pencil tip like Jo when the page dragged. Every time they did it they made the same offended face, because graphite tasted like battery dust.

"Read that line again," Mira said.

Lin bent over the previous page. "Left femur spiral set, old, healed wrong. Smelled bleach whenever the memory came up. Said the donor liked sugared fennel after lunch."

"Not the smell. The address."

"Unit eighteen, Marrow Street, over a bakery that ran hot at four in the morning."

Mira wrote it down. The pencil lead snapped. She swore softly and dug the broken point out of the paper with her nail.

The entry belonged to a woman who had spent forty dollars she did not have to find out why her rented hostess body kept waking up with somebody else's limp. Three pages earlier the same crooked femur had turned up in a bargain courier frame. Two pages before that the bakery smell had come through a sex-work lease from the docks. They were not building a theory anymore. They were building a family line for theft.

At the front counter, Jo Villanueva slapped the side of a dying card reader with her detergent-dried palm and called, "Gabe, if this machine asks me to smile for customer satisfaction one more time, I'm putting it through Dryer Six."

Gabe looked up from the cash tin on the folding table by the soap shelves. He had one of Jo's purple markers tucked behind his ear and his lumbar brace showed as a hard rectangle under his faded freight union T-shirt when he leaned. "Do it gentle," he said. "It's elderly."

"You're elderly," Jo said.

"That hurts because it's true."

He said it loud enough for the waiting room to laugh, which was part of why Jo kept him at the front when his back let him. He could take the shame out of a room before people had to show their damage. Today there were eight clients in the laundromat and four more names on the chalkboard list by the change machine. A crossing guard with a reflective sash rolled in her tote sat with her right shoulder actuator clicking every time she lifted her hand. A delivery kid in a rain-dark jacket had one runner calf split open down to the cheap white mesh and was trying not to look at it by doing yesterday's puzzle out of a free paper. Near the spin cycle, an older woman in a salon smock kept smoothing the same patch of vinyl seat while her bargain frame hiccuped somebody else's nursery rhyme under her breath.

Tavi was in the back room behind the hanging sheets, one long-fingered hand already inside the delivery kid's calf while he talked in the loose half-joking patter he used when he wanted fear to stay below screaming level. "You're doing great," he said. "By great I mean badly, but survivably. Hold still another minute or I'll give you the kind of scar that gets noticed at the beach."

"There's no beach in my life," the kid said.

"Then perfect. You're my ideal patient."

The room laughed again. The laugh frayed quick. Everybody in Blue Hour had learned that good humor only covered as much pain as the body could carry.

Mira closed the ledger for a moment and rubbed the heel of her hand over her eyes. They had meant to move the paper records three nights ago, split them between Brine Court and Jo's cousin's nail shop, get the overlap cases and the living-donor chain out of one place before Yaret turned suspicion into a warrant. They had also meant to replace the wetbench coolant pump, pay Jo back for the med-foam order, and catch up two months of rent before Brine Court Housing put a real notice on the door. The rent warning was still folded under the cash tin. The coolant pump still coughed twice before it caught.

Lin watched her. Under stress they sometimes got too still, as if every borrowed life inside them had stopped breathing to hear the same sound. "You're biting your cheek again," they said.

"I know."

"That usually means somebody is lying or somebody is coming."

"Those are not separate possibilities."

Lin almost smiled. Their hand moved to the ledger without thinking, fingers spread over the cover.

The front bell gave a thin jolt. Cold air came in smelling of salt, wet concrete, and official detergent, the expensive kind municipal buildings used so everything could smell clean while people got ruined inside it.

Jo looked up first. The laugh left her face so fast it was like a bulb burning out.

Through the fogged glass Mira saw three dark jackets with Municipal Tissue Safety on the chest, two uniformed city officers behind them, and Yaret Sanz in his neat dark coat carrying a waterproof document sleeve against the rain. He did not hurry. He never hurried. His stillness was half his authority. It made everyone else's motion look guilty.

Jo did not swear. Jo moved.

She slapped both hands on the counter and shouted at a volume Mira had last heard when a dryer belt snapped and nearly took off somebody's thumb. "Gas leak. Everybody up. If you like your eyebrows, move right now."

The whole laundromat jerked toward her.

For half a second even Mira almost believed her. Then she saw Jo's left hand drop below the counter and hit the old boiler bleed she had been threatening to replace since summer. Steam screamed somewhere in the wall. A white plume shoved out through the service vent by the back hall and rolled low across the tile, hot enough to smell the iron in it.

Bodies surged toward the hall in a wet knot of coats and baskets. Chairs scraped. Somebody dropped a mesh laundry bag and oranges rolled under the folding table. The crossing guard clutched her tote and said, "My shoulder."

"Bring your shoulder with you," Jo snapped. "Move."

Yaret opened the door against the bodies trying to get out and raised one hand as if the room had simply become inconvenient. Rain beaded on his close-trimmed hairline. "Owner Villanueva," he said, quiet and precise, "this premises is under seizure authority. No one leaves until we finish documenting the biological materials on site."

"Then document me after I stop everybody from cooking," Jo said. She was already out from behind the counter, shoving people toward the hall with practical shoes squealing on wet tile. "You want a corpse in the lint trap on your report, you make that choice out loud."

One of the younger inspectors hesitated. He could not have been more than twenty-six and his nitrile gloves were already on, but he still looked green around the mouth, the way people did the first time they saw what bargain lines did when a cheap seal went. He had a wedding band threaded on his boot lace, looped twice so it would not fly off. "Sir," he said to Yaret, "if there's a gas issue, procedure says"

"There is not," Yaret said.

Jo barked a laugh right in his face. "Listen to him. My pipes hiss, and suddenly he's God."

Mira was already moving. She snatched the ledger off the table and shoved it at Lin.

"Take this," she said.

Lin caught it against their chest. "Where?"

"Steam chase. Alley. Go to the fish stall shutters and wait if you get clear."

"What about you?"

"Don't do that now."

Tavi's voice ripped through the sheet partition. "Mira. Hands. I need hands or a miracle."

The words hit her in the order they mattered. Yaret was at the front. Clients were in the hall. Tavi had an open procedure underway. Lin held the only paper record that would matter if the room got turned into evidence tags and ash. Mira reached under the ledger table, found the old metal tea tin where she had hidden the anomaly wafer inside tampons and elastic bands, and stuffed the wafer down the front of her bra so fast the cold plastic bit.

An officer shouldered through the door, baton high because people with official sticks always looked happier when the problem in front of them could be rearranged into crowd control. He grabbed the delivery kid by the elbow.

"I'm mid-procedure," the kid said, voice cracking. "My leg is open."

"Then sit down and stop resisting."

"He can't sit," Mira said, and the claims voice came back to her so clean it made her hate herself. It was dry, exact, impossible to argue with if you were still pretending process mattered. "His posterior mesh has separated. If you fold the knee, you will shear the anchor and you will buy him a whole new limb under municipal liability."

The officer looked at the kid's calf, really looked, and loosened his grip.

Yaret's eyes found Mira over the moving bodies. He tipped his head in a courtesy that was worse than open contempt. "Ms. Alves. This is a fortunate opportunity. You can assist us in identifying unlicensed modifications and undocumented stock."

"You hit the wrong side of fortunate."

"You are in no position to define sides."

Steam thickened in the hallway. The crossing guard was crying now, not loudly, just from the indignity of her body making another public demand. Gabe came limping around the folding table with the cash tin under one arm and a stack of clean plastic laundry sleeves in the other.

"Lin," he said, shoving the sleeves at them, "wrap the book. Jo's steam trick is going to turn that paper into porridge."

Lin obeyed. The sleeves crackled around the ledger. Their hands were steady. That scared Mira more than shaking would have.

From the back room Tavi shouted, "Mira, if you don't get in here in the next ten seconds, I'm billing your estate."

She pointed at Gabe. "Take Lin through the chase. Now."

Gabe's mouth tightened under the joke he almost made. That was when she knew he understood how bad it was.

"Yeah," he said. "Yeah, all right."

He took Lin by the elbow and started for the service hall behind the dryer bank, the one Jo used to crawl the hot-water lines when the building decided to leak on her clean sheets. Lin turned once as they went. Mira shook her head hard to tell them to go.

Yaret saw it.

"Officer," he said, still soft. "The back corridor."

Everything narrowed then. The room stopped being a laundromat and became distance, angles, who could get one body through one door before law put a hand on the handle.

Mira ran for the sheets.

Behind them the front room erupted into overlapping orders, customers shouting for bags and medication and shoes, Jo yelling numbers at nobody in particular because numbers made panic sound temporary.

By the time Mira hit the back room, Tavi had the delivery kid half off the wetbench and a foam clamp hissing around the torn calf. His burn-scarred forearm was slick to the elbow. The wetbench monitors still glowed with case files, serial snaps, intake notes, and the half-built map that linked donor traces across three neighborhoods and a dozen bargain shells.

"Tell me Lin's moving," he said.

"Gabe's got them."

"Good. Help me lift."

They hauled the kid upright. He bit into the collar of his jacket and made a sound like somebody sawing wet wood. Mira hooked his arm over her shoulder and dragged him toward the service hall just as the first officer hit the far end of it.

The man came in fast, slipped on condensed steam, recovered, and swung the baton mostly from embarrassment. Gabe had just gotten Lin to the narrow metal door behind the dryers. He turned at the movement, more instinct than strategy, one hand already on the frame to shove Lin through.

The baton landed across the cheap ridge where Gabe's brace ended and his own damaged spine began.

The sound it made was ugly and intimate, plastic cracking into flesh like a laundry pipe bursting inside a wall. Gabe's whole body folded around it. His knees hit tile. His forehead bounced once off the dryer casing. The plastic sleeves around the ledger burst under Lin's grip with a loud wet pop.

Mira felt the impact in her teeth.

"No," she heard herself say, but it came out too low to matter.

Gabe gasped. The air left him all at once and stayed gone long enough to turn everybody toward him. His left leg kicked wrong. Something sharp and ammoniac opened in the steam.

The officer lifted the baton again.

Yaret's voice came from behind him. "Not again. Control hold."

As if that made the first one procedural instead of what it was.

Lin had gone perfectly still. Their face drained so blank it looked manufactured. Steam pearled on their lashes. "It smells like hot pennies," they said, to nobody, to themselves, anchoring.

Gabe looked up through the pain with water running from his eyes and said, every word scraped clean, "If you stop here, he hit me for free."

He shoved the ledger into Lin's arms with both hands even though one of those hands was shaking too hard to close. Then he drove his shoulder into the metal door and half-threw them into the steam chase.

Lin vanished. The ledger went with them.

The officer caught only the back of Lin's borrowed coat. It tore at the seam and came away empty.

Mira lunged for Gabe. The delivery kid nearly went down with her. Tavi swore from somewhere behind, a furious run-on she could not hear whole because blood had started knocking in her ears.

Yaret stepped into the hallway, not hurrying, not helping. His polished shoes took steam and dirty water alike without complaint. "Secure the exits," he said. "The undocumented unit is in flight. Civilian medical support for the injured male. Maintain the site."

"He has a name," Mira said.

Yaret looked at Gabe on the floor, at the cracked brace under the wet T-shirt, at the way Gabe's hands kept trying to push himself up and failing. His face did not change. "Then you should have used it in a lawful setting."

Jo hit the hallway like weather.

She had a wrench in one hand and somebody's inhaler in the other. Her silver braid had come partly loose and detergent foam streaked one cheek. "Out of my way," she said, shoving past an inspector before he understood that being older did not make her slower. She knelt by Gabe, jammed the inhaler at the delivery kid's chest, and put two fingers to Gabe's throat. "You still with me, sweetheart?"

Gabe made a sound that wanted to be a joke and ended up a spit-wet groan instead.

"That's not a yes," Jo said. She looked up at Mira. For one beat the command left her face and the fear under it showed naked. Then it was gone. "Don't move him. Tavi, where are you?"

"Busy deciding whether prison or fire feels more festive," Tavi shouted.

Mira understood before he finished.

She looked past the hanging sheets into the back room. The monitors still glowed. The local backups sat there with intake photos, ports, scars, names, addresses, payment notes, and the humiliating little facts poor clients told you so you could keep them working. Every face that had trusted Blue Hour not to become another arm of the city was in that machine. Every overlap trace that could disappear into seizure and then reappear later as defective property, inadmissible proof, or a door knock at dawn was there too.

Yaret followed her gaze.

"Mr. Sloan," he called, "destruction of evidence will not improve your exposure."

Tavi laughed once, hard and joyless. "Buddy, exposure is the one thing in this room we've got plenty of."

The younger inspector said into his sleeve mic, "EMS requested, possible spinal injury, adult male, conscious, urinary release."

The phrase urinary release did it. To the inspector, Gabe had already become that phrase. He was not somebody who still stole Jo's cigarettes one at a time and claimed gulls were union bosses in bird form. Mira wanted to put her fist through the inspector's teeth for how correct he was.

But EMS meant minutes. Minutes meant choice.

Jo took the officer's baton hand in both of hers and bent one finger back until he cursed and let go. "Go write it down," she said. "That's what you're here for, right?"

The service hall stank of iron steam, piss, detergent, and the burned sugar smell that came whenever cheap nerve mesh got shocked. Somewhere out in the front room a dryer buzzer kept going off because nobody had cleared the load. It sounded cheerful enough to be obscene.

Mira met Tavi's eyes across the hanging sheets.

He did not ask permission.

Neither of them had time for it.

***

The room behind Blue Hour had always been too small for what they asked it to hold.

That was part of why it worked. People told the truth easier when their knees knocked the same cabinet and everybody had to keep making room for the next body. The wetbench took up half the far wall under two bad fluorescent tubes and an exhaust fan that rattled like cutlery in a drawer. Shelves sweated with saline bags, seal foam, barter bottles, labeled jars of screws and couplers and salvaged ports. Half the neighborhood's hidden damage had passed through that room and gone back out stitched, taped, braced, or at least less alone.

Now the room was full of state shoes.

Tavi had slammed the inner latch on the sheet partition and jammed a rolling tray under the knob, which would not stop anyone determined, but it bought the kind of time Blue Hour ran on: ugly seconds pried from other people's certainty. He shoved a compression wrap into Mira's hands.

"Keep pressure on that calf and don't let him look at it," he said, nodding toward the delivery kid, who had slid down the wall and was breathing through his nose in ragged little pumps. "If he vomits, turn him. If he faints, congratulations, problem solved for thirty seconds."

Mira dropped to one knee and took the leg. Warm fluid bled through the foam clamp and into her cuff. The kid's puzzle paper stuck to his palm. He was young enough to still have acne along his jaw and old enough to know exactly what official interest did to a work history. He kept staring at the monitors over her shoulder.

"That's me on there," he said.

"Not for long," Tavi said.

He was already at the bench, fingers flying over the local archive. Under pressure he got more technical, not less, as if the right noun could buy him one square inch of control. Windows opened and vanished, showing serial logs, payment stubs, procedure snapshots, and intake recordings Jo had insisted on making once clients started forgetting what they had told them the first time.

"Bench drive has faces, port scans, lease numbers, tissue lots, notes on who is ducking collectors and who can't let a spouse know they're using a strength frame for warehouse shifts," he said. "It's got Lin's original wake file because I never trusted the cloud and thank Christ for one vice. It's got every overlap episode we thought might matter later. If they walk out with this, they don't need CommonFrame to finish burying people. They can do it off my labeling."

"The registry got out," Mira said.

"Paper got out. Good. Paper can go soft in the rain and still make it to somebody's kitchen table. This drive lets them sort by face before breakfast."

Something heavy hit the door. The rolling tray jumped.

Yaret's voice came through the wood with maddening clarity. "Mr. Sloan. Open the room. There is an injured civilian in need of care and compromised biological material on site. Every additional delay worsens your liability."

Tavi snorted. "Hear that? He's worried about my liability."

He yanked open the lower cabinet and came up with two bottles of sterilant, a tray of blank wafers, and the old cautery wand with the frayed cord. Mira felt the shape of the next minute all at once and hated it.

"Tavi."

He looked at her, actually looked, not at the leg in her hands or the failing latch or the red numbers on the bench. Beneath the speed there was the private thing in him, the reverence that made his guilt worse because it had never been fake.

"They take this room," he said, quieter. "They take everybody whole."

Another hit on the door. Jo's voice from farther off, still moving bodies. "Front is clear except for the lady in the smock, and she's vomiting because men in uniforms give her flashbacks. I'm working on it."

Mira thought of the registry wrapped in plastic under Lin's arm. She thought of the entry she had just written, the bakery address, the living donor with scan-rights hidden inside a dental loan, the thin line between evidence and inventory. She thought of Gabe on the floor outside with his spine ringing like cracked glass. There was no clean thing left to choose.

"Do it," she said.

Tavi nodded once.

The delivery kid heard enough to understand some of it. "My name is Niko," he said suddenly, like he was afraid he would be turned into category before the fire did the rest. "Niko Velez. My mother thinks I'm at a cousin's tonight."

Mira tightened the wrap. "Then you stay Niko Velez."

He laughed once and almost cried.

Tavi flooded the processor bay with sterilant until the sharp alcohol sting overpowered the room. He ripped the storage wafers from their slots and snapped them in half with his thumbs, not because that was sufficient, but because rage needed shape. The fragments went into the metal kidney tray along with intake tags, port images, procedure backups, and a year of ugly practical mercy, all of it fed piece by piece into something that could burn.

The door latch shrieked.

One of the officers had found a crowbar.

Yaret again, stripped now of courtesy and therefore somehow more himself. "Open the room. Final instruction."

Tavi talked faster when the fear got close enough to taste. "Coolant line will pop if the processor gets hot enough. If the coolant line pops, battery arcs. If the battery arcs with lint in that vent, we're all going to have a very educational evening."

"You're saying that like it's a warning."

"It's a eulogy with timing issues."

He jammed the cautery wand into the overloaded strip. Sparks snapped blue at the plug. The frayed cord glowed dull orange near the base.

Mira heard Jo in the doorway then, not coming in all the way because she saw what they were doing and understood at once. Her chest was heaving. She had the salon-smock woman braced against one hip and the laundromat cash tin hooked under her arm by instinct. Even now she had not stopped collecting what could keep people alive until morning.

"EMS is outside," she said. "They want the hall clear."

"Gabe?"

Jo's mouth went flat. "Hurts to breathe, can still curse. We're calling that promising until a doctor tells me not to."

Tavi struck the wand against the tray.

The sterilant lit in a flat blue sheet.

Heat punched the room, mean and close. The hair on Mira's arms crisped before she leaned back. Tavi dumped the burning tray into the processor bay and the flame disappeared inside with a hungry chemical cough.

The monitor flashed white, then black, then white again full of static ghosts.

Outside, somebody shouted.

The crowbar hit the door. The latch tore out of the frame.

The first officer through got a face full of extinguisher foam from Jo. She had grabbed the wall canister without Mira seeing. The man reeled blind and swearing. The salon-smock woman, who had spent the whole evening smoothing one square of vinyl like it might save her, took one look at the chaos and calmly kicked him in the shin hard enough to fold him.

"Move," Jo told her.

"Gladly," the woman said, and spat on his boot on the way out.

Yaret entered behind the foam, ducking the fire arc with insulting composure. The white powder dusted his coat sleeves. He took in the bench, the flame chewing inside the processor, the open cabinet of supplies, Mira kneeling in blood and coolant with a client's leg in her hands.

For the first time that night, something in his face tightened.

"You are destroying material already subject to seizure," he said.

"No," Mira said. "We're choosing who gets to use it."

"Once chain of custody is broken, the baton strike in that hall becomes a compliance action and every incision in this room becomes unlicensed practice on the official record."

Tavi barked out a laugh so raw it almost doubled him. "There it is. Blood with letterhead."

Yaret stepped toward the bench. Tavi stepped into his path with the cautery wand still spitting orange at the tip.

It would have been absurd anywhere else. One tired illegal surgeon in a bloodstained apron facing a Bureau investigator with two officers at his shoulder, armed with a surgical branding pen and bad intentions. But absurdity had never stopped anybody from getting maimed in Cordera.

"Do not," Yaret said.

The words came out low-contraction and clean. He sounded almost ceremonial.

Tavi's own speech frayed down to the shop floor. "Back off. This bench knows too much."

"It knows nothing. Your files are unauthenticated and contaminated by criminal handling."

"Exactly," Tavi said. "You get there eventually."

The coolant line blew.

It went off under the bench with a wet cracking pop and a burst of chemical stink. Greenish coolant sprayed the underside of the cabinet, hit the hot processor core, and flashed into greasy smoke. The battery arc followed a second later, bright enough to punch shadows through Mira's eyelids.

Then the lint in the exhaust vent caught.

That was the real fire: old gray lint caked in the duct above months of steam and fabric dust and everybody's postponed maintenance. Flame ran through it with horrible confidence. One strip of ceiling panel blackened and sagged. Another flared at the seam.

Suddenly the room belonged to oxygen, to the vent and the ceiling seams and whatever caught first.

Yaret swore once, a small furious syllable more shocking than shouting would have been. "Evacuate," he snapped. "Cut mains. Seal the alley."

"Already too late on one of those," Tavi said, and coughed hard when smoke hit.

Mira dragged Niko up by the back of his jacket. His injured leg buckled. Tavi got under his other side. Together they carried more than walked him toward the service hall. Jo was already slapping the emergency power switch by the sink with the heel of her hand. Nothing happened. She hit it again. The dead red button knocked around in its browned plate while smoke kept thickening above the sink.

In the front room people were pushing through the open door into cold rain. The crossing guard had become useful under stress, directing strangers with the same whistle-voice she probably used on traffic. "You, out. You, don't stand there crying on the detergent. You're making it slippery."

Two officers tried to muscle past Mira toward the back again.

"There are tissue stocks," one said, coughing into his sleeve.

"There are people," Mira said, and shoulder-checked him hard enough to put him into a washer.

The machine boomed. Coins spilled from the change tray and skittered under the rows like startled insects.

Jo grabbed the battered metal recipe box where she kept customer tabs, emergency contacts, and three folded twenties she pretended were not there. She looked once at the washer bank, at the front counter, at the rack of pressed shirts waiting for pickup tomorrow that would now come home smelling of smoke if they came home at all.

Then she left them.

That was her choice, Mira realized even in the crush: turning her back on every machine she had nursed through twenty years and taking the box because people would need phone numbers and bus cash before she had room for grief.

In the doorway, Yaret caught Mira's arm.

His grip was precise, more clerk than cop, thumb exactly on the tendon that hurt. "Where is the undocumented unit?"

Smoke had started eating the room behind him. Somebody outside was screaming for water that did not matter anymore.

Mira looked him straight in the face and said, "You had your chance to ask before you sent a baton into my brother's spine."

His hand tightened. "The individual you are concealing is not protected by personhood law."

"Neither are most of my neighbors. You still write them up like they belong to you."

He let go because another ceiling tile dropped between them in a shower of sparks and wet plaster. One of the dryers belched black smoke through the front vent. The salon-smock woman, already halfway down the sidewalk in the rain, yelled back, "My blouse is still in there, and I want that on the record."

It was such a Jo-shaped line that even Jo barked a laugh.

Then the front window cracked from heat and everybody moved faster.

Mira got Niko across the threshold and into the rain. Cold slapped the smoke off her face. Blue Hour's sign buzzed once overhead, blue tube flickering, then went dark. Steam and fire pushed out of the top vent together, making the whole building look like it was trying to keep working while it died.

An ambulance crew shoved through with a stretcher. The younger inspector, face chalked white from extinguisher powder, pointed toward Gabe on the curb under Jo's coat. Gabe was conscious enough to tell the paramedic "If you cut my shirt, my sister isn't tipping you," which made the paramedic snort before he got professional again.

Mira dropped by his side.

Rain had plastered Gabe's hair to his forehead. He was gray around the mouth and trying too hard not to move below the ribs. The crack in his brace had pushed one plastic stay through the fabric and scratched a thin bright line down his side.

"Lin?" she said.

His eyelids fluttered. "Went."

"You saw?"

"I shoved. Hard to miss."

His hand twitched against the wet pavement, searching. Mira took it.

"Don't make that face," he whispered. "I hate that face."

"Then quit earning it."

"Working on it."

The paramedic knelt opposite her. He had a cartoon shrimp pin on his collar and smelled faintly of menthol under the rain. "Ma'am, I need space to assess him."

Mira moved back enough to let the man do his work and no more. The collar went on. The questions came one after another: name, age, where does it hurt, can you feel this. Gabe answered with the exhausted accuracy of someone who knew that being imprecise around institutions cost extra.

Tavi came out of the smoke last, coughing so hard he had to brace a hand on the ambulance fender. His right forearm was reddening fast where the old burn scar had taken new heat. Soot streaked his face into older, harder lines. He looked at Blue Hour once and then away as if the sight had turned intimate.

Jo handed him the recipe box. "Hold that. I need my hands free for murdering the next official who says the word unauthorized."

He took it automatically.

Yaret emerged after the officers, coat wet now, expression back under control except for the powder caked in the seam of one eyebrow. He was speaking into a recorder already. "Unlicensed tissue-repair site compromised by operator destruction of evidence and secondary fire event. Multiple civilians evacuated. One injury requiring transport. Pursuit notice for undocumented unit initiated."

The phrase pursuit notice hit Mira and made the city lengthen around them, every tram stop camera and clinic desk and rental kiosk suddenly another set of teeth.

The stretcher wheels hit a crack in the pavement. Gabe's face went white. He bit down on a curse and lost.

"I'm coming with him," Mira said.

Jo's hand caught her wrist. "No. Listen."

There was still enough command in Jo's voice to cut through sirens.

"You go," Jo said to Tavi. "You know enough medicine to yell if they get lazy. Mira, you come after with me. We've got to hear what is left of the shop from the fire captain and make sure nobody follows that ambulance to Brine Court."

"I'm not leaving him."

"For ten minutes you let him go, because if they tag your building tonight, Lin loses the only place that still knows their shoes. Use your head."

Tavi had already climbed into the ambulance by then because of course he had, recipe box still in one hand, coughing into his shoulder. He leaned back out. "Mira. Go with Jo. I'll call from intake."

"On what?"

He looked at the burning laundromat. "On literally anything not on fire."

The doors shut on Gabe's face before she could answer. The siren did not bother with dignity. It just tore itself open and took him.

Mira stood in the rain and watched the ambulance lights bounce blue-red over the smoke-black windows of Blue Hour.

By the curb, the crossing guard had somehow collected three abandoned tote bags and was asking everybody within range what bus they needed. Niko sat on the back step of the ambulance loading zone with his foam-wrapped calf on a milk crate, refusing transport because transport meant ID and ID meant debt and debt meant not working next week. The salon-smock woman had found one unburned blouse under a bench and held it up like vindication.
Niko kept his wrapped leg planted on the crate as if stubbornness alone could still get him onto tomorrow's shift.

Fire crews took over the block with the brisk boredom of people who saw this kind of ruin every night in richer and poorer forms. Water went in. Smoke came out blacker. One of them asked Jo whether the premises had contained hazardous bioactive media.

Jo lit a cigarette with shaking hands and said, "At this point, honey, the whole city does."

He did not laugh. That was a bad sign.

Yaret crossed back toward them with a damp evidence envelope in hand. Even after all this he still looked pressed. That annoyed Mira more than the baton had, in its own way.

"Ms. Alves," he said, "you should be aware that obstruction, flight assistance, and evidence destruction will all be added to the existing civil posture."

Jo exhaled smoke at his shoes. "You people love posture. Maybe try a spine sometime."

"Owner Villanueva, your business license will be suspended pending review."

"My business is on fire."

"That does not alter the process."

"No," Mira said. "That's the process."

Something in Yaret's expression suggested he had heard versions of that sentence before and had built a life around not letting them in. He held out the envelope.

"Inventory acknowledgment," he said. "You were present."

The paper inside was already beading from the rain. It listed asset lines and approximate values: unlicensed tissue array, one; laundry machines, twelve; pressing station, one; biological stocks, indeterminate. The last line said paper records, none recovered.

For the first time since the door opened, Mira breathed almost all the way in.

Yaret saw her see it. His eyes narrowed, not enough for anyone else to call it a reaction.

"Sign," he said.

She signed because not signing never stopped a bill. The pen tore the wet paper a little where her hand pressed.

When he left, Jo took the envelope and laughed with no humor in it at all. "Look at this. He raids my shop, you two light the wetbench, the fire runs through my lint vents, and they still want a receipt."

Mira did not answer. The order had come out of her own mouth. The vent had taken it from there.

The fire captain finally came over, helmet tucked under his arm, and told Jo what his face had already told them. The back room was gutted. Two dryers had gone. The roof above the service hall had to be opened up for hidden flame. The building inspector would likely red-tag the place until structural review. There would be questions about electrical maintenance, vent cleaning, code compliance, insurance riders, and any undeclared activities on the premises.

With every noun, Jo got stiller.

"Insurance rider lapsed last month," she said, mostly to herself. "I chose the new pump instead. Thought I could catch up after the holidays."

Mira knew that math. Everybody she loved was alive inside some version of it.

Jo crushed the cigarette under her heel. "All right. We go to the hospital. Then we figure out where the hell anybody sleeps."

"Brine Court," Mira said automatically.

Jo looked at her like she was tired enough to be cruel. "Do you want that man at our stoop by dawn?"

Mira looked down the block where Yaret was speaking with an officer under the shelter of a city umbrella. He had already turned Blue Hour into paperwork. Brine Court would take him one decent cross-reference and one bored patrol note: Gabe's mail, Mira's fines, old lease records, Lin's hidden shoes under the radiator.

"No," she said.

"Good. Then stop saying home like the city can't hear you."

They took Jo's old van because it still started if you prayed at it in the right sequence. The cab smelled of cigarettes, bleach, and the fried plantain wrappers Jo swore she had already thrown out. Mira sat with the evidence envelope on her knees and watched smoke smear the rearview glass as they pulled away. Her clothes cooled on her skin one clammy inch at a time. In the glove box Jo found two transit cards, a screwdriver, and forty-eight dollars in mixed notes. That was what had survived the evening in liquid form.

"I've got another two hundred at my sister's," Jo said, driving one-handed and too carefully. "Unless my nephew borrowed it for a crypto miracle again."

"I've got ninety-three," Mira said.

"Eighty-one," Tavi had shouted from the ambulance before the doors closed, patting his pocket like a child announcing lunch money.

Jo made a sound that was almost a laugh. "Terrific. We're a force."

At the hospital intake desk the fluorescent light was a different species from Blue Hour's, cleaner and meaner. It made everybody look already billed. Tavi was waiting by the chairs with soot up his neck and the recipe box at his feet. His right hand was wrapped in gauze from wrist to thumb heel. He stood when he saw them and then sat right back down because the adrenaline had clearly left a hole where his knees used to work.

"He's in imaging," he said. "They're pretending they can move fast if we keep looking scared."

"Can he feel his feet?"

Tavi rubbed his face with the unbandaged hand. "Left mostly. Right comes and goes. He told a nurse her blood draw tray was organized like a war crime, so his personality is intact."

Mira sat because if she did not sit, she was going to drop. The plastic chair stuck to the damp back of her shirt. Around them, hospital night moved on in its usual stripped-down misery: a man sleeping upright with a grocery bag between his shoes, a child in soccer socks coughing into a puffer mask, two women arguing in whispers over whose turn it was to call a mother they both resented. Nobody in the waiting area cared that Blue Hour had just burned. Mira respected them for it.

Jo set the recipe box between her feet and opened it. Inside were customer tabs, phone numbers, three twenties, two tens, eight singles, a rosary somebody had left months ago, and one laundromat key that did not open any door they still had.

"That's all the cash from the desk?" Mira said.

"That's what I could grab without taking a ceiling tile to the skull."

Tavi pushed the hospital clipboard toward Mira with his bandaged hand. "Admissions needs a responsible party."

The form asked for patient guarantor, billing address, employer, secondary insurer, incident location. Under incident location somebody had already written Blue Hour Laundry, then crossed it out and replaced it with Commercial Premises, Unlicensed.

Of course they had.

Estimated charges were not printed yet. There was only a square where they would appear later, after machines and specialists and somebody in finance decided what a damaged spine cost when it belonged to a man who had already run out of officially useful body parts.

Jo looked at the form, then at Mira. "My name is all over the fire report. Yours is all over the Bureau files. Pick the version of screwed that buys him the least attention."

Mira took the pen.

Her fingers smelled like smoke under the hospital sanitizer. When she pressed the page, damp from the rain still hidden in her cuffs, the paper softened.

The fields were name, address, phone.

She wrote her own first. Stopped at the address line.

Brine Court wanted its money on the first of every month whether or not the stairwell lights worked and whether or not city officers ever bothered climbing to see who slept where. Brine Court was the address on her fines, on Gabe's old recovery notices, on every cheap future they had ever tried to stabilize. It was still theirs on paper.

It was also where Lin's spare socks were rolled inside a coffee tin behind the radiator and where a pursuit notice would go looking first.

The clerk behind the glass tapped the counter. "Ma'am?"

Mira crossed out the first line she had written so hard the nib tore through it.

"Temporary housing disrupted by fire," she said, because claims language still came to hand before grief did. "Bill hold requested pending update."

The clerk gave her the flat hospital look that meant no and maybe later. "I still need an address on file."

Jo slid one of the transit cards across the counter and said, "Use mine for now. If collections wants me, they can get in line with every other bastard in Cordera."

Mira wrote Jo's sister's address because it was the only one on offer, though none of them had asked permission yet and she could already hear the future fight in it. The hospital paper drank the ink slowly. Beside her, Tavi leaned back against the vending machine and shut his eyes. Jo kept counting the same bills because they did not become more just by being few.

Somewhere down the corridor Gabe yelled once when they moved him wrong, a short hoarse sound that cut off fast. Mira held the pen hard enough to dent the counter through the form.

By the time she handed the clipboard back, the square for estimated charges was still blank, a white box waiting for numbers. The chart carried Jo's sister's address in blue ink. Yaret had Jo's rain-warped signature in his evidence envelope. Gabe's boots sat under Mira's chair with their laces cut away, tongues wet and slumped out. She hooked them together with one finger and dragged them back against her ankle before anybody could kick them aside. Somewhere out in the city Lin was carrying a wet ledger under a borrowed coat. Mira kept the boots pinned there and stared at the black street grit drying in the eyelets, trying to think of one room left where any of them could arrive before dawn.
