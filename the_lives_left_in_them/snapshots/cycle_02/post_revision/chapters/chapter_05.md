# Chapter 5

On the third-flight landing at Brine Court, Mira paused and looked back at Lin while dawn turned the stairwell windows the color of old dishwater. The building held every smell it always held before the day got hot: salt pushed uphill from the seawall, fryer grease from the breakfast stall on the corner, old mop water, the bleach-bright ghost of detergent drifting over from Blue Hour's vents, and beneath all of it the damp mineral breath of a place that had been patched more often than repaired.

Lin climbed carefully, one hand on the rail, wearing Tavi's coat buttoned wrong over a pair of borrowed scrub pants that had to be rolled twice at the ankle. The coat hid most of the tape at their throat and the sealed port at the nape, but not the newness of every movement. They kept pausing at each landing, listening to the building settle around them. Mira had spent the walk from the laundry telling herself she was doing something temporary and practical, safer than leaving them in the back room and safer than letting Tavi keep a conscious bargain frame next to a wetbench and a stack of stained towels. They were evidence, she had told herself, and this was only hold-shelf logic brought home for one night.

Then Lin stopped on the second landing to let an old woman pass with two grocery sacks biting into her wrists and flattened themself against the peeling wall so politely it hurt to look at.

"Sorry," Lin whispered, though they had taken up almost no space at all.

Nobody who was only evidence apologized to old women in stairwells.

Mira unlocked 3F, shoved the swollen door with her shoulder, and let them in ahead of her. The apartment looked smaller every time she entered it in daylight. It was a galley kitchen the width of a hallway, a front room with a foldout table, a couch that ate springs, and two chairs that disagreed with the floor in different directions, one actual bedroom Gabe used because his back punished couches, and the half-enclosed sleeping nook Mira had claimed behind a curtain clipped to a wire. A radiator under the window knocked even in August, as if rehearsing winter. Rent notices and utility warnings lived on the fridge under a crab magnet with one missing eye.

Gabe's brace was gone from the hook by the door, which meant he had gone out early. His coffee mug sat rinsed and upside down by the sink. He had taken the blue work hoodie too. Mira felt an immediate, ugly wash of relief.

She locked the door, set the chain, and put Tavi's paper bag on the counter. He had packed it like a medic and a worried aunt at the same time: antiseptic strips, a fresh port cap, two nutrient gel tubes, the antibiotic patch he insisted Lin wear another twelve hours. At the bottom sat a stale peach hand pie from the bakery cart that rolled past Blue Hour at five.

Lin had stopped just inside the apartment. Their eyes kept moving, not suspicious, just intent, as if every ordinary object had to be learned from the outside first, from the dish rack to the warped cabinet door under the sink to the wet circle Gabe's mug had left on the table.

"You can sit," Mira said.

Lin touched the back of the green chair, then let their hand fall. "That sounds like a test."

"It isn't. The green one just throws people."

They chose the metal chair and lowered themself with exaggerated care. The chair squealed but held. Mira took off her shoes and checked the time on the cracked wall panel above the sink. She should have been on the tram in twenty minutes. Missing a shift meant money gone, one more attendance mark, one more little official bruise on a job she already needed too much. But she could not bring Lin upstairs half-healed, half-dressed, and carrying someone else's memories in their skin, then leave them alone with a lock they did not know how to use and neighbors who noticed everything.

She picked up the handset by the fridge and called the CommonFrame absence line.

"Food poisoning," she said after the tone, using the calm customer-service voice that made ugly facts sound processable. "Started around four. I can't keep water down. I'll send a clinic note if you need it."

The system chirped once, accepted the lie, and cut her off. She stood with the dead receiver in her hand a second longer than necessary. She was out forty-some dollars after deductions, maybe more if Celia's assistant decided to be exact about coverage. She hung up and opened the fridge.

There were two eggs, yesterday's rice in a plastic tub, half an onion going soft at one edge, a heel of sausage Gabe had been stretching all week, and six hot sauce packets he had skimmed from the dock canteen and stuffed into a mug by the stove. It was enough for breakfast if nobody expected generosity.

"Are you hungry?" she asked.

Lin did not answer immediately. They pressed a hand just below their ribs as if checking with the body itself.

"Yes," they said. "Also frightened. It feels different."

"Good. Means the wiring's still making distinctions."

Lin looked up. "Is that a joke?"

"Not a very good one."

Mira set the pan on the burner. The ignition clicked, caught, and gave her the low blue flame she trusted more than the wall heater. She put in the onion first, then the sausage, sliced thin enough to pretend there was more of it than there was. The smell came up fast, hot and sweet and greasy. Behind her, Lin made a small sound.

Mira turned. "What?"

Lin kept looking not at the pan but somewhere just to the left of it. "She used a plate because the board smelled like bleach," they said. "She kept sliding the onion into the pot with the side of her hand. There was a little burn scar here." They touched the inside of their own wrist. "I don't think it was mine. I mean, not mine exactly."

The onion hissed louder. Mira scraped it before it could catch.

"Do you get that all the time?" she asked, and hated how quickly the question came out in claims voice, not as compassion but as patterning, frequency, trigger, duration.

Lin shrugged one shoulder. "Not all the time. When something fits. Or when it nearly fits. Like a word you can feel in your mouth before you know it."

Mira added the rice. It went cold and white into the hot fat, then loosened under the spoon. She had expected impossible memory, if she let herself expect anything at all, to be rich-people residue: roof decks, hotel sheets, private clubs, stupid expensive leisure with somebody else's blood still in it. The bargain line got abused by the rich, yes, but what Lin kept touching through smell and heat and cheap apartment texture did not feel like borrowed luxury. It felt like the city that stood in line at daybreak with heel pain and lunch in foil.

She cracked the eggs in. The yellow spread, set, broke. The room got warmer by a degree that mattered.

"Can you eat spice?" she asked.

"I don't know."

"Neither does Gabe. He treats that like a political position."

One corner of Lin's mouth twitched. Mira tore open three of the hot sauce packets and worked them through the rice. When she set the plate down, she split the hand pie in half and added one piece to Lin's side without comment. Lin looked at the food with a concentration that made her feel intrusive.

"You don't need permission," she said.

"I know." Lin picked up the fork, frowned at it, and traded it for the spoon with sudden certainty. "She was standing at a sink. The bus came in six minutes. She burned her tongue every morning and kept eating anyway."

"Did she have a name?"

"Not one that stayed."

They took a bite. Their eyes closed, not theatrically, just because the body seemed to need a second to sort out hunger from memory. Mira took her own plate to the sink and leaned her hip against the counter while she ate. She wanted to ask a dozen questions about how many lives were in there, how recent they were, whether these were donor traces, renter traces, or waste left in the lattice from bodies nobody had ever been told were contributing to anything, and what exactly CommonFrame had been printing into its cheap stock when it decided a full wash cost too much.

Instead she said, "After you eat, you should shower. It'll help with the antiseptic stink."

Lin nodded, still focused on the plate. "Do I know how?"

"Probably. But the handle falls off unless you pull toward yourself first. And the hot side lies."

"That sounds more manageable."

Mira washed up while Lin finished. The apartment made its usual morning noises: tram grinding somewhere below the hill, pipes knocking two units over, a radio leaking market prices through the wall, gulls raising hell out at the water. Lin listened to each sound as if choosing which ones belonged in this room and which ones were still threats.

When Mira handed them a towel, they rubbed the fabric between finger and thumb.

"What now?" she asked.

Lin's thumb paused on the towel. "Hear that?" they said quietly. "A stroller wheel kept chirping every few turns. Somebody sang through her teeth so the baby stayed asleep through the radiator banging."

The towel remained only a towel in their hands. That might have been the strangest part. They did not perform the fragment. They just reported it and let it go.

Mira went to the drawer under the sink and found the emergency toothbrush she kept for nights when she was too exhausted to trust herself with the walk from couch to bathroom. It was still wrapped, which was good. She set it on top of the folded towel, then dug through the clean laundry pile for clothes that might pass: one of Gabe's old transport tees, washed thin at the collar, a pair of her charcoal drawstring pants, and two pairs of socks because nobody in the apartment ever found a matched set in time.

She brought the stack to the bathroom door and hesitated before knocking. In claims, naked printed bodies were routine. They came in bruised, split, leaking, carrying other people's perfume and semen and seawater, and the work only happened if she kept her eyes useful instead of embarrassed. In her own apartment, with one extra toothbrush and a towel that still smelled faintly of detergent and her own skin, nakedness felt different. It did not feel clinical or neutral.

"I've got clothes," she said through the door.

"Thank you."

The bathroom latch clicked. Lin opened the door just enough to take the bundle and the toothbrush. Up close Mira could see where Tavi's tape crossed their throat and where the lighter iris caught the weak bathroom light. They looked less uncanny in the apartment than they had on the wetbench. That made them more alarming, not less.

"The body wash is watered down," Mira said. "So if it doesn't foam, that's not you failing."

Lin stared at her for a beat, then laughed once, startled by it.

"All right," they said.

The shower pipes screamed awake. Mira went back to the kitchen and cleaned the pan before the egg welded itself permanently. She moved on instinct, wiping the counter, folding Tavi's paper bag flat, checking the window blind angle so nobody in the opposite building got a good look inside. Her hands wanted lists: medication at noon, port dressing tonight, concealment strategy, what to tell Gabe, what not to tell Gabe, and whether to make Lin stay out of sight if the landlord came for arrears again.

The water ran, then stopped. For a moment there was only the tram and the ticking metal ping of cooling pipe.

"You all right?" Mira called.

"Yes." A pause. "I knew where to put my foot so the tub edge wouldn't cut me."

Mira set the glass down too hard on the counter.

"A lot of people know that," she said, because it was the closest thing to comfort she had.

"Yes," Lin said from behind the door. Water dripped once, twice, into the tub. "That's what frightened me. I just knew where to step, then there was black water after it and a ladder slick with algae. A man kept counting the rungs out loud because if he stopped talking, he'd go under."

When they came out, their hair was damp and curling at the ends, the old transport tee hanging low on one shoulder. Mira's drawstring pants were short on them but workable. The borrowed clothes changed their outline more effectively than any concealment plan had, not because they hid what Lin was, but because they made the wrongness crueler. Someone had always been meant to go on living in a shirt soft from too many wash cycles, someone ordinary enough to pull it on without thinking.

Lin touched the sleeve and went still. "Orange plastic chairs. Wet coats and old fryer oil. Somebody slept sitting up between shifts because going home would've cost the whole rest period. The machine ate half the coins."

Mira wiped her hands on the dish towel. "A bus depot?"

"Maybe."

"That narrows it down to this whole city."

Lin looked down, unsure whether she was mocking them.

"That was a joke," Mira said.

"I know." After another small pause, they added, "It was better than the first one."

Mira snorted before she could stop herself. Lin stood there in Gabe's shirt and her pants and two unmatched socks, still not fully steady in their own balance, and watched her kitchen window brightening over a row of roofs. They had stopped looking like a problem she could file long enough to start looking like somebody who needed a place to put the damp towel when they were done with it.

She pointed to the hook behind the bathroom door. "There. Or Gabe will sit on it and complain like it's a personal betrayal."

Lin nodded solemnly, as if she had given them a rule worth learning.

Mira had just put water on for coffee when the key scraped at the front lock.

***

Gabe came in sideways, shoulder first, paper sack under one arm, brace showing through the back of his T-shirt in two hard lines. He always brought home things from the dock canteen as if he were skimming taxes from a hostile empire: sugar packets, plastic cutlery, the little foil hot sauces he treated as public resources reclaimed for the people. This morning a fistful of them stuck out of his shirt pocket like bright paper feathers.

He kicked the door shut, turned toward the kitchen, and stopped.

"Mira," he said, too calm, which meant three bad theories had already formed. "Please tell me that's a friend and not a debt."

Lin was seated at the table with both hands around the coffee mug Mira had given them to warm. The green chair wobbled under them but held.

"Morning to you too," Mira said.

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

The antibiotic patch wrapper on the counter and Tavi's folded paper bag were enough; Gabe's mouth flattened. He did not straighten all the way. He rarely did first thing after a shift. The left foot dragged just enough on the linoleum to make the fact of it unavoidable if you loved him. If you didn't, he usually talked fast enough to keep your eyes elsewhere.

"Did you bring CommonFrame home?" he asked.

"No."

"That wasn't the question."

Mira opened her mouth and closed it again. Every answer she had came out wrong in advance. Lin read the whole exchange faster than most people read forms and shifted slightly in the chair.

"If it helps," they said, almost shy, "I don't think I'm the most dangerous thing in this room. This chair has been trying to kill me for fifteen minutes."

The chair chose that moment to give a betrayed metallic squeal. Gabe barked out a laugh before he could stop himself. The laugh bent him. He caught the edge of the counter with one hand, rode the pain quietly, and straightened in stages.

"All right," he said. "Fair. The green one has a body count."

He set the paper sack down, peeled the hot sauce packets from his shirt pocket, and dropped them into the mug by the stove. Mira watched the routine settle him. He did it every time he came home, like the apartment needed proof that certain small economies could still be counted on.

"Name?" he asked Lin.

"Lin."

"Gabe." He hooked a thumb at Mira without looking at her. "I came with the apartment."

Lin glanced at the bedroom door, the curtain over Mira's nook, the table with three unmatched mugs on it, the fridge notices, the brace under Gabe's shirt. Their attention made the room feel both shabbier and more honest.

"You want to know if I'm trouble or expense," Lin said.

Mira winced. Gabe's mouth tightened.

"Everybody in here is both," he said. "Question is scale."

Lin's grip loosened on the mug. They nodded once, accepting the terms.

Gabe took the kettle off the burner before it screamed itself dry and poured coffee into his mug. He drank it black and too hot, always had. Then he looked at the extra plate in the sink.

"You called out?"

"Yeah."

"Jesus. You never call out unless there's blood or vomiting."

"There was almost both yesterday."

"Useful answer."

He leaned against the counter and shifted weight off his left leg. The little movements were never little to Mira. She could read a body faster than she could read mood. She could see the strain in the right shoulder from compensating, the careful way he kept his hips square, and the pain patch at the base of his spine that had started to peel at one corner, sweat loosening the adhesive.

Lin watched it too, not rudely. Just accurately.

"Does it hurt all the time," they asked, "or only when you have company?"

Gabe blinked, then laughed again, softer this time. "Mostly when I've got to be charming for free."

"That seems expensive."

"You have no idea."

Mira pulled the paper sack open. Gabe had brought day-old rolls, slightly flattened, and two bruised nectarines wrapped in the market circular. He always picked the fruit no one else wanted, not out of virtue but out of price.

"Eat one before it turns to liquor," he said to Lin, nodding at the fruit.

"Gabe."

"What? If we're pretending none of this is weird, I need structure. Structure is breakfast fruit."

Lin held the nectarine untouched in both hands, waiting for permission. She hated that they still did that.

"Take the nectarine," she said.

They did. Their thumbnail broke the skin. Juice ran over the heel of their hand. They went still a second too long.

"What?" Gabe asked.

Juice ran to Lin's wrist bone. They flinched before the words came. "He swore before the fruit even hit the deck," they said, eyes fixed somewhere past Gabe's shoulder. "Cold enough to make your teeth ache. It had taken two days to go soft enough." They licked the juice from their wrist, embarrassed by the memory and the motion both. "Sorry."

Gabe set his own nectarine back in the sack. "Don't apologize for fruit," he said. "The city charges enough for it already."

He crossed to the table and lowered himself into the safer chair with the deliberate, insulting care of a man half his age and three times as pissed off about it. The effort put a shine across his forehead. He ignored it.

"All right," he said to Mira. "Try again. Who's Lin, why are they in my shirt, and how much of my day is this about to ruin?"

Mira sat opposite him. Lin stayed quiet, mug in both hands.

"They're with me," she said.

"That's not information."

"It's the information that matters first."

Gabe rubbed one palm over his mouth. His eyes cut to Lin, then back to her. He had her same dark hair when he let it grow, though his was cropped close now for convenience and sweat, and the same mole family along the left side if you knew where to look. But his face had always been easier with people than hers, quicker to joke, quicker to deflect, quicker to make strangers think he was less hurt than he was.

"Are we hiding somebody?" he asked.

"Yes."

"From who?"

"Everybody official."

He let out a breath that was almost a whistle. "Great. Love it when the list is concise. Meaning CommonFrame, Tissue Safety, and the landlord if he smells leverage?"

"Yes."

Gabe tapped the table once with two fingers, harder than before. "Then let's use the real version. If one of them clocks Lin in here, Brine Court Housing stops sending pink little threats and finally throws us out. We end up on the curb with twenty-four hundred in arrears and me hauling our life down three flights with a back that already hates brushing my teeth."

Mira held his gaze. "I know."

"Do you? Because you've got your claims voice on. You're saying 'everybody official' like it's a delay fee."

"If Lin goes back, they get wiped or pulped or sent out again under cleaner paperwork. So yes. I know what this can cost us, and I'm still asking you to take it with me."

He looked at her for a long second. "You don't get to spend my spine like petty cash."

Her mouth went dry. "No. That's why I'm saying it plain now. If you tell me to move them, I'll move them before noon. But I'm not going to keep calling them temporary evidence just because that phrase sounds cheaper."

Lin set down the mug. "I can leave if this puts you in danger."

Mira's fingers locked around the mug handle. Gabe's chair legs came down flat on the linoleum.

Gabe looked at them properly for the first time, not just as a problem that had walked into his kitchen wearing his old shirt. He took in the careful posture, the patched throat, the way Lin had chosen the edge of the chair and kept their knees tucked in as if trying not to take up billable square footage.

"Leave and go where?" he asked.

Lin opened their mouth, then closed it.

"Exactly." Gabe rubbed one palm over his mouth. "I needed the price said out loud. Not hidden in Mira-language."

Mira let out a slow breath.

"All right," he said. "Then we keep you. If Brine Court wants one more reason to hate 3F, it can get in line."

Lin almost smiled.

"Does Tavi know?" Gabe asked.

"Yes."

"And?"

"And he sent antibiotics and a hand pie."

"Romantic."

"Don't start."

"I'm injured, not dead."

Lin's eyes flicked between them again, reading the sibling grammar of it faster than Mira liked. Gabe noticed too.

"Ground rules, since apparently we're a boarding house now. The bathroom door sticks. Mrs. Vale on two hears through concrete and believes every problem in the building is caused by unmarried people. If the landlord comes, don't open. If anybody asks, you're Mira's cousin from Bay Four because half the city has a cousin from Bay Four and no one follows up."

"Why Bay Four?" Lin asked.

"Because the landlord hates admitting Bay Four people know where he lives. It short-circuits him."

"That's true," Mira said.

Gabe pointed at the chairs. "Green one throws. Metal one pinches. If you find a third option, tell me. I've been searching for years."

"There's the crate in your room," Mira said.

"That's a stool of principle, not comfort."

Lin looked down at the green chair and said, with more confidence now, "I respect an enemy that announces itself."

Gabe grinned. There it was, the younger-brother swagger he could still pull on like a jacket even when pain had sweated through the collar. "See? They get the place already."

He reached for the nectarine, stopped halfway, and flexed his hand instead. Mira knew the move. It was a pain spike. He would wait it out before anyone could offer help.

Lin said quietly, "You don't have to act more fine because I'm new."

Gabe's gaze snapped to them. For a second Mira thought he might flinch away from the accuracy of it. Instead he huffed through his nose.

"That's an extremely rude thing to say in a stranger's kitchen," he said.

"I'm sorry."

"No, you're not. You're just correct."

Mira stood, crossed behind him, and pressed the edge of the pain patch flat where it was lifting. Gabe let her. That was how bad it was.

"You should lie down for an hour," she said.

"I just got home."

"Exactly."

He tipped his head back to look at her. "This is why people hide things from you."

"And none of you get better at it."

Gabe pushed himself up with a grunt he tried to disguise as annoyance. At the bedroom door he looked back at Lin.

"There's an old blue hoodie on the hook in there if you're cold," he said. "Take it. I hate the cuffs."

"Thank you."

"Also, if Mira gives you one of her serious little practical speeches, nod until she's done. It's easier on the rest of us."

"Go to bed," Mira said.

"See? Like that."

He disappeared into the bedroom and shut the door most of the way, never fully. A few seconds later the bedsprings complained. Then the apartment went quiet except for the tram and the pipes.

Lin looked at the half-closed door. "He was joking before he knew whether I was dangerous."

"That's how he buys time."

"For what?"

Mira gathered the plates because her hands needed occupation. "For the part where he figures out how much something hurts and whether anybody needs to know."

Lin absorbed that. The kettle ticked softly on the stove.

"I think I know people like that," they said.

Mira rinsed the pan. "You probably do."

She meant it one way. Lin heard three.

***

By afternoon the apartment had done what poor rooms did when asked to accept another body. It had shrunk first, then started inventing. Gabe slept behind the bedroom door with the radio low and a heating wrap under his back. Mira had gone out once, fast, while Lin stayed upstairs with strict instructions not to answer the door to anybody who did not say their name first. She had spent nine credits and loose coin at the key kiosk under the tram on a copy of the apartment key, fourteen more at the canal market on a secondhand pillow and a lamp with a shade the color of weak tea, and hated each purchase with the precise irritation of a woman who could convert every object in a bag into rice, rent, or avoided attention.

The money was one cost. The other waited behind the curtain: the only place in the apartment where she could pull fabric shut and go unobserved for six minutes. The copied key in her pocket had receipts for both.

When she came back, Lin was standing in the middle of the front room holding the blue hoodie Gabe had offered but not yet put on, as if they were waiting to be told whether taking it would count as overstepping.

"You can wear it," Mira said, setting the bags down.

"I wasn't sure if 'take it' meant now or in a colder month."

"With Gabe it meant now. He offers things like he's losing an argument."

Lin pulled on the hoodie. The cuffs swallowed their hands. It made them look younger and more tired at the same time. Mira handed over the lamp.

"Help me clear the nook," she said.

Lin turned toward the curtain. "Your bed?"

"It isn't much of a bed."

"It's still yours."

Mira knelt and unhooked the curtain from the wire one clip at a time. The nook behind it was exactly as mean as she had left it that morning: narrow cot, milk crate nightstand, stack of claims manuals under one corner to keep the frame level, one shelf with three shirts, two bras, a tin of coins, and the cheap paperback she had been taking six weeks to finish because she never got far enough through a paragraph before sleep took her. The wall at the back held a watermark shaped like a continent nobody wanted.

"You're taking it," she said. "I'm on the couch. Gabe needs the real mattress more than either of us."

Lin did not move. "That's because of me."

"That's because couches are terrible and I already know exactly how terrible."

She stripped the cot anyway, yanking off the sheet and blanket and tossing aside the flat pillow she had been folding in half for months in favor of the one from the market. Dust came up from the floorboards in a thin bitter smell. Lin knelt beside her and started stacking the crates without waiting for instruction, moving with the strange grace of somebody using half-remembered habits to negotiate new space.

"You don't have to do the heavy part," Mira said.

Lin looked at the crate in their hands. "I think this one mostly has paper in it."

"That isn't the point."

"Then what is?"

Mira sat back on her heels, annoyed before she could explain at whom. "The point is I said leave it," she said. "You're still half patched and I'm trying to get a bed made before Gabe wakes up again."

Lin held her gaze for a moment too long. "I don't know how to let somebody do this."

Neither did she, Mira thought.

They kept working. She moved her clothes to the hall shelf by the bathroom. Lin wiped the nook's single shelf with a rag and came away with gray water. She clipped the curtain back up after shaking it over the tub. The lamp from the market worked if the cord was held a certain way at the base, which felt honest enough for Brine Court. Gabe, half awake at some point, pushed the bedroom door open, squinted at them, and slid an old plastic drawer unit into the hall with his foot without asking what they were doing. Then he closed the door again and left them to it.

When Mira unpacked the pillow, a faint smell of camphor and somebody else's closet came off the fabric. Lin pressed the case flat with both palms.

"What now?" Mira asked.

Lin's fingers stayed on the cloth, moving over the trim as if checking for a satin edge that was not there. "She kept rubbing the satin edge of a blanket till it went gray," they said. "Somebody hummed low so the baby wouldn't hear the pipes knock. There were rust spots on the wall where the radiator spat. She hated that song and asked for it every night."

Mira looked at the cheap floral blanket in her hands. It had no satin edge, only thinning synthetic trim. Lin was not claiming sameness. It was only contact.

"How old was the girl?" she asked.

"Small enough to kick the wall in her sleep." Lin swallowed. "Maybe not small. Maybe I only feel her feet because she was on top of somebody when she slept."

Mira folded the blanket once, then again, suddenly careful with it in a way the blanket did not deserve.

"You don't have to tell me every time," she said.

"Do you want me to stop?"

"No." She set the blanket on the cot. "I just don't want you thinking you owe me a report."

Lin nodded, though they looked as if the difference between telling and reporting still needed translation.

They hung the blue hoodie on the hook at the end of the nook. The one clean shirt Mira had left herself went beside it. The cheap metal bar dipped a little under the extra weight. Something pulled tight under Mira's ribs, ugly and soft at once, and she had to crouch by the floor before Lin could look at her too closely.

She crouched by the floor and shoved the tin of coins farther under the cot with her fingertips. Twenty-three dollars and change was emergency money, not much of an emergency against anything that would actually come through the door. The copied key in her pocket seemed suddenly loud, brass against denim, commitment with teeth.

Lin picked up the little paperback from the shelf. "Do you want this somewhere else?"

"No. Leave it."

They turned it over, reading the back with the care they brought to everything written. Their reading had been functional in the laundry, all labels, monitors, and warnings. Here they lingered. Mira watched their mouth move once over an unfamiliar word, silently testing shape before sound.

"Can I ask something?" Lin said.

"You just did."

Lin looked up, wary again, unsure whether that answer had been permission.

Mira exhaled. "Yes."

Lin held the book against their chest. "What happens to unwanted bodies?"

The radiator knocked once in the wall. Mira shifted and felt the copied key tap her thigh through the denim. From the bedroom came Gabe's muffled cough, then quiet again.

"Why are you asking that now?" Mira said.

Lin glanced around the nook. At the cot, the curtain, the crates that had been moved out to the hall. "Because you've spent money you don't have and given me your bed," they said. "I need the real answer before I put my weight on any of it."

Mira sat down on the bare floorboards because standing had become stupid. The wood pressed lines into the backs of her thighs through her pants.

"Officially?" she said. "Returned bodies get logged, drained, checked for recoverables, then pulped if nobody can lease them again without losing money."

Lin did not move.

"Unofficially," Mira went on, because the tidy lie had already failed between them too many times today, "they get held. Reassigned. Stripped for parts. Sometimes passed around under cleaner paperwork than they deserve. Sometimes nobody even bothers changing the name on the damage history before they send them back out."

Lin's face stayed very still. That was one of their stress tells already, Mira saw. Stillness so complete it turned into its own form of bracing.

"And if a body is..." Lin searched for the word. "Not compliant with that?"

Mira knew too many answers: sedation, wipe, pulp, shelves, the cool filtered smell of CommonFrame disposal, men in aprons hosing pink residue down drains built for scale.

"Then the people who own the paperwork decide what counts as a defect," she said.

"Do you?"

The first lie rose clean and familiar: defect, salvage, inventory. Mira bit it back and felt the cut edge of the copied key press her thigh through the denim.

"No," she said.

Lin waited.

Mira pulled the new key out of her pocket. It was warm from her leg. It was cheap brass, and one fresh cut edge was still slightly sharp. She had put it on a bent ring with a plastic blue tag from one of Gabe's old locker keys because bare keys vanished too easily in this apartment.

"I got this copied," she said.

The blue tag swung once from Mira's hand. Lin went very still, not understanding yet.

"For the front lock," Mira said. "You lift the door a little with your shoulder and turn twice. If the chain's on from the inside, you don't open unless you know the voice. Mrs. Vale will pretend she needs sugar if she thinks there's gossip to be had. Don't give her sugar."

"Mira."

She put the key in Lin's palm and closed their fingers over it. Their skin was warm and dry from handling laundry and curtain clips and the cheap book.

Lin's mouth parted slightly. "Does this mean I live here?"

The room was too narrow for the question. Gabe slept behind one door, her cot waited behind the curtain, and the copied key warmed Lin's palm.

"It means you're not going back," Mira said. "This is your door now. You leave, you come back."

Lin's fingers tightened around the key. When they spoke, their voice had gone very soft.

"No one's ever given me one of these."

Mira almost said, No one ever gave me one either. That was not true. She had had keys before, plenty of them, and none had ever made her chest feel this tight.

"Well," she said, because if she got more solemn than this she might ruin it, "don't lose it. I can't afford another nine credits this week."

Lin laughed through whatever had tightened in their face. It was not much of a laugh. It was enough.

They stood, crossed the room to the front door, and waited with their hand on the lock for instruction. Mira followed. Gabe's blue tag swung lightly from the ring.

"Lift first," she said.

Lin set their shoulder against the swollen wood, raised it the fraction the lock required, and turned the key once, then twice. The bolt drove home with the same ugly scrape it had always made. Behind them, old receipts slumped on the table under a stub pencil, and Gabe's faded green dock-union shirt hung over the radiator to dry, the gull on the pocket cracked nearly through. The apartment was still cramped, still damp, still carrying too much debt and three people's weight badly. But when Lin looked back at her with the key still in the lock, Mira heard the sound for the first time as welcome.
