# Chapter 8

The line bent around the dead change machine, doubled past the detergent wall, and kept growing. By ten-thirty Jo had torn the pink ticket roll off the hook she usually used for broken dryers. Blue Hour had run out of respectable ways to pretend it was anything except what it had become.

Mira stood behind a folding card table with a marker in one hand and a cup of coffee gone gray and bitter in the other and tried to sort urgency from shame before either one turned into a fight. Jo had taped butcher paper to the front of the table and written categories in thick black strokes.

`BLEEDING`

`PANIC`

`MOTION`

`MEMORY`

`I DON'T KNOW`

The last column was already longest.

"If you only came to wash your clothes," Jo said to the room at large, "I need you to decide whether your problem is laundry or God. I can only fix one of those in here."

That got a few thin laughs and one offended sniff from a woman hugging a mesh bag of uniforms to her chest. It also kept the line from folding in on itself, which was what Jo actually cared about. She stood in practical black shoes planted wide on the tile, silver braid down her back, a pencil jammed through it like a skewer, and snapped tickets off the roll with the authority of somebody distributing medicine after a storm.

Mira took ticket twelve from a man who smelled of freezer brine and old aftershave. Noel Lima had a split thumbnail wrapped in athletic tape and a silver tooth that flashed every time the laundromat radio caught an organ chord. He flinched so hard his folding chair scraped backward.

"Not pain?" Mira asked.

"Not exactly." He pressed both fists against his thighs. The borrowed forearms twitched, wanting to rise and guard his face. "Every time I hear church music this body thinks somebody's about to ring a bell and beat the shit out of me. I do fish. I don't even box."

She wrote `PANIC / SOUND TRIGGER` on his ticket. "How long has it been doing that?"

"Since mass. My mother thinks I found religion. I found drywall."

Next was Damaris Vale, who had rented a silver-haired grandmother frame cheap because it was all she could afford for a school-grandparents breakfast. The frame came with soft knuckles and liver spots and a teenager's hands. Those hands kept trying to flick imaginary messages into the air, thumb-fast, restless, slutty with muscle memory. Damaris had stuffed them into oven mitts at some point out of desperation. One mitt was printed with cartoon peppers.

"I smacked my pastor on the ass," she told Mira without preamble. "Not on purpose. These hands got there before I did."

Jo looked up from the ticket roll. "Was he worth smacking?"

"That isn't helping."

"It helped me."

Mira hid a smile and wrote `MOTION / HAND REFLEX LOOP`. Damaris leaned in.

"Can you make them stop doing that little heart shape?" she whispered. "My granddaughter says that's how girls flirt now."

"That wasn't flirting," said a voice from the far end of the table. "That was typing."

Lin had been meant to hand out water and keep the waiting clients from drifting into the back hallway. Somewhere in the last forty minutes they had acquired Jo's spare apron, a legal pad, and half the room's trust. They stood with their hair pinned up under a faded scarf that hid the port at the nape, pencil tucked behind one ear, and listened with the kind of attention that made people hear themselves more clearly. They were not smiling. That was part of why it worked.

"How can you tell?" Damaris asked.

Lin took her hands very gently, oven mitts and all, and watched the thumbs twitch inside the quilted cloth. "Because they're trying to answer someone who isn't here. Flirting usually wants a witness. This is more like reaching for a cup that's not on the table anymore."

Damaris blinked, then barked out a laugh loud enough to startle herself. The restless hands slowed.

Mira had spent the past two weeks trying to keep Lin out of view, out of records, out of the category of notice. Now the room kept turning toward them on instinct. It should have tightened something in her chest. It did, but not enough to make her stop it.

The front windows had fogged from washer heat and bodies. Outside, two more people stood under the awning pretending they were only waiting out a passing mist off the canal. Everybody in the queue had that same look now: chin tucked down, face arranged into casual, eyes cutting sideways to see who else had come to an illegal place to ask what was wrong with the body they could not afford to return officially.

Blue Hour smelled like detergent, salt dragged in on shoes, fryer grease from the market uphill, and the sweeter, uglier edge of tissue solvent seeping under the back-room curtain every time Tavi opened it. The machines hammered and sighed. Somewhere, a toddler complained in steady outraged pulses while his mother fed quarters into a washer and refused to lose her place in line.

Mira turned sideways to let a basket of towels pass and caught sight of Tavi through the curtain gap. He was bent over the wetbench, long fingers inside a client wrist assembly, burn scar bright against his right forearm where he'd pushed his sleeves up. He looked up as if he had felt her looking. Last night came back to her with stupid bodily precision: his mouth under her jaw, the weight of him, the afterward, and then the confession laid over all of it like a second sheet she could not kick free.

He tilted his chin toward the queue. "How bad?"

"Worse than an audit. Better than a fire."

"That's not a category."

"It is today."

His grin showed for a second, loose and tired and almost enough to make him look uncomplicated. Then he saw the room more fully and the grin dropped. "You want first bench on Noel or oven-mitt lady?"

"Take Noel. If he hears another hymn in here and swings on the dryers, Jo will bill us."

"She already does."

He vanished behind the curtain again. Mira hated how easy their work voices still fit. She hated more that the fit helped.

Ticket by ticket the room tried to become a system. A bike courier in a stolen-looking office frame could not stop licking his own molars because the mouth remembered cigarettes he had never smoked. A market porter wearing a glamorous club body three sizes too elegant for his boots kept dropping to one knee every time anybody raised a voice, the frame apparently trained by some old proposal package or apology kink that had leaked through wash. A janitor with bleach-burned cuticles had a rental arm that saluted every time the tram shrieked downhill outside.

Each time the porter went down, he came up grinning too hard and brushing lint off elegant knees that had never been meant for Jo's tile. Across town the same glitch would have happened behind hotel blackout curtains, with a recovery aide pretending not to watch; here Jo nudged him upright with her shoe and told him if he was going to propose in her laundromat he could at least bring flowers.

What Lin did was not softness. Mira saw that almost at once. Lin did not soothe by lying. They sorted. They asked the question beneath the question.

"When your mouth starts tasting smoke," they asked the courier, "does it happen before or after you think about the package?"

"After."

"Then your fear probably grabbed the first door it saw. Sit by the fan. And stop checking your tongue every few seconds. That's like worrying a bad tooth. It tells your body the smoke matters."

The courier obeyed as if Lin had handed down code.

To a woman who had come in shaking because the borrowed legs under her office skirt had begun climbing stairs that were not there, Lin said, "Do the stairs arrive in your knees or in the room first?"

The woman frowned, surprised into honesty. "Knees."

"Good. That's smaller. Sit with your feet flat and let me know if the room lies afterward."

"Good, that's smaller" was exactly the kind of sentence frightened people needed, and Mira stole it immediately because it reduced the fear without insulting anyone.

By noon the butcher paper had new arrows, new notes, and coffee-ring maps of failure all over it. Jo had repurposed two overturned laundry baskets into seating. The woman with the mesh bag of uniforms had come back from the shop uphill with a sack of empanadas and was passing them around the queue without asking names. The actual laundry customers had either fled or surrendered and were now doing wash in companionable disgust beside people discussing alien hand habits and unauthorized grief.

Lin moved from chair to chair, taking symptoms in a neat cramped hand. They never rushed the humiliating part, which meant the humiliating part came out faster. People who had spent three days hiding from official claims clerks or spouses or bosses found themselves telling Lin the truth before they told Mira the billable version.

Mira watched Noel let Lin adjust the angle of his elbows so his borrowed arms would stop climbing into a guard. She watched Damaris hand over the oven mitts without being asked twice. She watched one man in a borrowed funeral suit, huge and sweating and near tears, admit to Lin that the body kept trying to kneel whenever anybody mentioned forgiveness.

Jo came to Mira's shoulder with a stack of torn tickets and said out of the corner of her mouth, "If your tall one leaves the front, they mutiny."

"They're not my tall one."

"Then whose miracle are they? Because I would like to invoice."

Mira looked down the room. Lin was crouched beside a woman old enough to be their mother, explaining something with both hands open as if demonstrating how to carry a bowl without spilling it. The woman's breathing had already slowed to match theirs.

"They're good with frightened people," Mira said.

Jo snorted. "Honey, that's because they're one of the only people in here who doesn't act embarrassed about fear."

The line shortened by three. It lengthened by four. A produce clerk in a green apron knocked on the window and held up a wrist wrapped in a dish towel already leaking through.

Mira took a breath that tasted like hot cotton and canal salt and solvent and knew with perfect financial clarity that Blue Hour had become the sort of place people gave directions to.

***

By two, the pink tickets were up to thirty-one and Jo had written `NO WALK-INS AFTER 4` on the butcher paper even though everybody in the room understood that they would take walk-ins after four if the walk-ins looked desperate enough.

The rain never quite started. It hung over Cordera as a wet thought, close enough to make the air tacky, not close enough to clear the block. Blue Hour's front door kept breathing in more bodies. The bodega owner sent over a plastic crate of bottled water in exchange for getting his sister moved up the list. A salon girl from halfway down the canal came to the window just to ask whether the rumors were true about the place behind the dryers where they fixed carryover cheap.

Mira was writing up aftercare instructions for a tram mechanic whose borrowed shoulder had learned to duck from blows that never came when the door opened and let in a woman with municipal credentials clipped to the breast pocket of a tray-green rain shell.

She had square navy nails chipped at the corners, damp trouser hems, and a stainless lunch thermos under one arm plastered with faded cartoon sardines. The thermos was so battered it had probably outlived marriages. Her badge read `ELSA PAREDES, MUNICIPAL HEALTH AND SANITATION`.

Everybody in line performed normal badly and all at once.

The room did not fall silent. That would have looked deliberate. Instead it grew louder in stupid places. A washer hit its spin cycle with the violence of an alibi. Damaris laughed too hard at something no one had said. One of the salon girls began folding a towel that did not belong to her into an ever tighter rectangle.

Paredes stood just inside the door letting her eyes move over the fogged windows, the extra chairs, the paper cup of tongue depressors Jo had forgotten to hide, the red biohazard sack half tucked under the card table.

"Afternoon," she said. Her voice was tired rather than sharp. "I got a complaint about crowding, bodily waste near public washers, and somebody doing wet work without a partition."

Jo was already moving. "You got a complaint from who, the woman upstairs who thinks steam is a moral problem?"

"Anonymous, which means probably yes."

"Then you know how much I care."

Paredes did not smile. "I still have to look."

Mira felt the inside of her cheek meet her teeth. Lying always made that spot raw by the end of the day.

"We're a laundry," she said, too fast, and corrected. "Obviously. One of the folding tables collapsed and cut my brother's hand. The crowd is because two machines are down and the rain's thinking about happening."

Paredes looked at the butcher paper categories.

"Do your broken machines usually produce a `MEMORY` column?"

Jo leaned over and slapped a new sheet right on top of it. "That's for my church ladies. They do a little grief thing after wash."

"At two in the afternoon on a Tuesday."

"Death doesn't keep office hours."

That, annoyingly, landed. Paredes's face shifted by half a millimeter, enough to show she had either buried somebody recently or knew somebody who had. Then her gaze slid toward the back curtain where solvent smell drifted around the edge in a sweet medicinal ribbon.

"I'd still like to see the rear."

Mira opened her mouth and never got to use whatever lie was next, because at that exact moment the inner dryer on the left clicked done and released a bright two-tone chime into the room. Coconut softener steam rolled out when the door popped.

The woman in the cheap ivory wedding shell made a sound Mira felt in her own teeth.

She had come in ten minutes earlier with a courthouse bouquet of baby's breath crushed in one fist and a veil already sliding off one side of her head. Her name, according to Lin's intake pad, was Paz Renteria, twenty-six, electrician apprentice, and she needed the body for wedding photos and the reception because the base line had a twelve-week wait she could not pay to skip. Her wife, Esme, stood beside her in a suit jacket gone damp at the shoulders, one bobby pin after another tucked into her breast pocket because she kept rescuing them from Paz's hair and did not know what else to do with her hands.

When Paz had sat down she was flushed and embarrassed and trying to joke about the bust seam biting under her arm. Now the softener steam hit her and the joke went clean out of her face.

"No," she whispered to nobody in the room. Then louder: "No, don't start with the balcony."

Her body folded forward so violently the bouquet stems snapped. Baby's breath sprayed over the tile. Esme lunged for her and got an elbow full of satin and borrowed panic.

"Paz, hey. Hey, look at me."

Paz wasn't with her anymore. She was staring into the humid space above the washers as if a hotel room had opened there, something salt-bright and terrible. Mascara dragged down one cheek. The borrowed mouth kept working around sobs that had too much practice in them.

"You said we'd go after breakfast," she said to the empty air. "You said if we walked down to the water first you'd stop being angry. Don't leave with the boat half there. Don't make me hold the stupid flowers by myself."

Every head in the laundromat turned, then tried not to turn, which looked worse.

Baby's breath skittered under the rack of lost socks. Damaris clapped both oven-mitted hands over her mouth hard enough to squeak the quilted peppers, and Noel bent over his knees as if the floor might excuse him from watching a bargain bride beg a ghost not to leave her alone on the water. Jo muttered, "Jesus, honey," and the pity in it came out sounding almost angry.

Paredes took one step toward the scene, officially rather than kindly. "Is she having a neurological event?"

Mira moved before she answered. "She's having a carryover episode."

It was too truthful. She heard that as soon as it left her mouth.

Paredes's eyes sharpened. "A what?"

"A grief thing," Jo said at the exact same time.

Paz tried to stand, snagged the hem of her dress under one heel, and nearly went down. Esme caught her around the ribs and got dragged sideways with her. The room recoiled to make space and found there was no space left to make. The tram mechanic swore. One of the salon girls knocked a laundry basket over with her shin. The toddler from earlier started crying in outraged sympathy.

Paz clutched at Esme's lapels and looked straight through her. "Mateo, please."

Esme's face changed. It wasn't anger. It was worse: the tiny clean cut of being replaced by a ghost in public.

Mira felt the whole mess trying to break open at once. The front room was packed, the back room was illegal, and a municipal official stood at the door while a bride sobbed through somebody else's heartbreak loud enough for the block to hear. Tavi was one curtain away with a client half numb on the bench and no clean way to surface.

She reached for Paz's wrist. The pulse there kicked fast and thin under rental skin. "Paz. Listen to me. You need to sit."

Paz yanked free with surprising strength. "Don't tell me to sit in that chair. He packed while I was in the shower."

Esme closed her eyes for one beat, then opened them and said to Mira in a voice gone flat with fresh married misery, "Can you fix this now? Please tell me you can fix this now."

Paredes took another step. "I need everyone away from her. Ms. Villanueva, if you're conducting unauthorized medical procedures in a public laundry, I'm not going to ignore it because somebody's wearing a veil."

Jo planted herself broadside between the inspector and the curtain. "Then ignore it because it's my damn floor and I said wait."

Everything in Mira wanted to move fast and clean. She wanted to drag Paz back, have Tavi do a dampening pass, shut the room, and undo the evidence. But high pressure in Blue Hour never stayed private. It came with audience built in.

And Lin, from the far side of the table, saw that before Mira did.

They took one chair from the circle, set it in the middle of the room, and spoke in a voice pitched not for intimacy but for reach.

"Esme," they said. "Can she hear her own name right now?"

Esme looked at them, startled. "I don't know."

"Then sit with her anyway."

There was no hesitation in them and no apology either. That steadied the room almost by force.

"Noel," Lin said next, already turning. "Turn off the radio."

Noel obeyed instantly. The organ vanished mid-chord.

"Damaris, can you pick up the flowers?"

"With these whore hands? Absolutely."

That got the wrong laugh out of the room and saved it by a second.

Lin faced Paredes. "She's not contagious. She's embarrassed."

Paredes blinked, perhaps because nobody usually spoke to her that way in a laundromat. "Embarrassment is not a sanitation category."

"No," Lin said. "But grief's common enough I figured the city had paperwork for it somewhere."

Mira did not understand the move until Lin turned back to the room and said, clear as a bell, "All right. If your chair moves, make a circle. If it doesn't, turn where you are. We're doing grief in public for a minute."

For one stunned second nobody moved.

Then Jo, blessedly, understood before everyone else. "You heard them," she barked. "Circle up. If you've ever lost money, dignity, sleep, cartilage, or a man too stupid to stay, congratulations, you're qualified."

Chairs scraped. The line reshaped itself, not neatly, because nothing in Blue Hour ever did. But the room obeyed a new logic because a worse one had just arrived and this was the only available answer.

Paredes stood with her thermos tucked under one arm like a shield, watching poor people reconfigure themselves into something she could not cite without looking like a monster.

Mira caught Jo's eye. Jo jerked her chin toward the back, telling Mira as plainly as words to buy time and move.

Mira got her arm around Paz's waist on one side while Esme took the other. Paz's body fought them half-heartedly, still reaching for a balcony and a boat and a man neither of them knew. Lin stepped closer, close enough for Paz to focus on their face if she could focus on anything.

"Paz," they said. "Is the grief in your chest, or did it get all over the room?"

Paz sucked in a shuddering breath. "Room."

"Good. Then let's move rooms. Sometimes that helps first."

It was the smartest thing anybody had said all day.

They got Paz through the curtain while Jo filled the space behind them with command.

"All right, since we're already here. One thing you lost and one thing you need washed. Keep it short. I'm not your priest."

The first voice was Damaris's, because of course it was.

"I lost the good feeling in my knees around fifty-two, and I need somebody to wash this shame off my hands."

Noel, staring at the silent radio, said, "I lost three teeth and any desire to hear church organs, and I need my work apron cleaned before my mother asks why I'm crying."

The woman with the mesh bag laughed softly into her sleeve. The woman in the office skirt, the one whose knees kept inventing stairs, cried harder. Paredes did not leave. She did not come closer either.

Mira let the sound of the makeshift circle cover the back-room curtain falling shut behind her.

***

The back room held its own weather: warm damp air, hanging sheets to blunt noise, the wetbench lights throwing everything into surgical yellow. Tavi looked up from the client he had just bandaged and took in Paz's dress, Esme's face, and Mira's expression in one quick sweep.

"Jesus," he said. "Front-room bad?"

"Bride bad. Inspector bad. Both at once."

"Of course."

He stripped his gloves off with his teeth and moved to clear space on the side cot. Paz tried to twist away when they lowered her. Satin rasped against vinyl. The smell of coconut softener followed her in like a curse.

"Don't take the room key," she was sobbing. "I paid for the room."

Esme stood at the cot's shoulder with one hand pressed so hard over her own mouth Mira thought she might leave teeth marks in the skin. She still had the courthouse stamp on her wrist, smeared now. She had been married half a day and was already competing with a dead honeymoon.

"Hey," Tavi said to Paz, not unkindly. "Listen to me. I'm going to make the room smaller. That's all. Nobody's stealing anything."

He had that gift in a crisis, the ability to sound like technical instructions were an ordinary kindness. Mira hated him for helping make the systems that broke these people. She also watched his hands and trusted them anyway.

Paz's pupils were blown wide. Her whole body had gone into that ugly overlap rigidity that looked like panic and memory wrestling in the same skin. Mira loosened the beaded neckline enough to get at the pulse points. It was a cheap wedding shell, she thought automatically, with adhesive seams under the lace. It had been a resort package body originally, or some imitation of one. Somebody at CommonFrame had pulped the honeymoon and sold the dress twice.

"Esme," Mira said. "Did the episode start before the dryer opened?"

"No. She was just hot. She kept saying the dress was too tight. Then the smell hit."

"Coconut?"

"I guess. I don't know. Rich-people vacation smell."

"That helps."

Tavi was already drawing a cooling line across Paz's inner wrists with conductive gel. "Probably sensory carryover keyed to hospitality wash. Cheap lace lines get reused from destination packages all the time. They scrub the face, leave the ache."

He said it with enough disgust that Mira almost looked at him, almost let herself. Instead she got a cold pack under Paz's neck.

"Paz," she said. "I'm going to ask you something annoying. Is the water you're seeing daylight or night?"

Paz's breath hitched. "Orange. It was orange."

"Good. Sunset memory, not room memory. Easier to interrupt."

Tavi glanced at her, quick approval passing between them before either could stop it. Work made traitors of clean emotional positions. You could not keep hating somebody in a straight line while their hands helped you.

Outside, muffled through cloth and steam, the grief circle kept going.

"I lost my first apartment to mold and my second to rent," the salon girl from down the canal was saying. "I need this blouse not to smell like fear before my shift."

Jo's voice came after. "That's too long, but accepted."

Even Tavi gave a short unbelieving laugh.

"What's Lin doing?" he asked.

"Saving our asses."

"Good. They're better at front than both of us."

Mira did not argue because she had already learned that. She held Paz's jaw steady while Tavi clipped a small dampening lead behind one ear and watched the woman's face with that ugly precise patience of his.

Esme finally lowered her hand from her mouth. "Is she hearing me at all?"

Mira chose the answer carefully. "Some of her is. Talk to the part that came with you."

Esme stared at Paz for a second like she was deciding whether she could bear that instruction. Then she bent close, took Paz's hand, and said in a voice roughened by humiliation and stubborn love, "Baby, you still owe me half a tres leches cake and one dance where you don't look at a ghost. I'm not letting some dead resort asshole eat my reception."

Tavi barked a laugh before he could help it. Paz made a broken sound that was almost one too. It was good, or good enough. Mira saw the fight inside the borrowed muscles change shape.

"Again," Mira said.

Esme squeezed harder. "You married me in front of Judge Del Toro and your cousin Nia and one florist's bucket we stole from the bodega. You cried before this and those were your tears. Come back for your own tears."

Paz's breath stuttered. Her eyes found Esme's face for a second, lost it, found it again.

The carryover did not vanish. It never did that cleanly. But it loosened enough for her to whisper, hoarse and furious with herself, "I'm so sorry."

"Don't apologize for a body being cheap," Esme said, and that sentence sat in the room like the city itself.

Mira felt it in her spine. Cheap was a design condition, a legal class, and a whole level of acceptable damage.

Through the curtain came Paredes's voice, closer now. "Ms. Villanueva, I heard equipment."

Jo answered immediately. "You heard my old irons. If you want to confiscate those, be my guest, but you'll have to fight every woman on this block for crease rights."

The room outside laughed, not kindly but solidly, with the sound of a neighborhood choosing sides.

Tavi murmured, "She won't leave until she sees enough to call this stupid instead of criminal."

"Can you make it stupid?"

"I can make it less obvious."

He stripped the veil pins from Paz's hair with efficient fingers, handed the wilted veil to Mira, and said to Esme, "Get her out of the dress. Not because I'm charging by the satin, though I should. Because the compression's keeping the loop alive."

Esme stared at him. "In front of everyone?"

"No," Mira said. "Behind the screen."

There was no real privacy in Blue Hour, only kinds of mercy. Mira pulled the hanging cloth panel across and helped Esme work Paz out of the bargain bridal shell inch by inch. The beads scratched Mira's knuckles. Paz cried with quiet mortification now, which was an improvement. Under the dress she wore a plain nude slip, sweat-dark at the spine. Somebody somewhere had marketed the outer layer as romance and the underlayer as practical disposal.

By the time they got Paz into borrowed laundry sweats from Jo's lost-and-found bin, the front-room voices had acquired the rhythm of a real meeting, a bad one improvised out of panic and folding chairs, but real. Mira could pick out Noel's thick waterfront accent, Damaris's scandalized cackle, Jo's corrections, and Lin cutting through with their calm precise prompts whenever anyone spiraled into either too much detail or not enough truth.

"Say whether it's yours or borrowed," Lin was saying. "That matters."

Mira looked at the curtain as if she could see through it.

Tavi followed her gaze. "They're a natural."

"They're visible."

"Yeah." He wiped his hands and met her eyes for the first time since she'd dragged Paz in. "So are we."

There was too much in that sentence: last night, the queue, his confession, the fact that success and failure were now arriving in the same clothes. Mira let none of it open because opening it would take time the room had already spent.

"Help me get her upright," she said.

When they brought Paz back out, wearing gray Blue Hour sweats under her wife's suit jacket and clutching the crushed bouquet stems like evidence, the circle quieted on instinct. The room had changed while she'd been gone. What had started as a cover was now doing actual work. Damaris had taken her oven mitts off and was resting her disobedient hands in plain sight. Noel sat with his forearms loose, no longer braced for bells. Even Paredes, still standing near the door, had set her sardine thermos on top of a washer and stopped looking like she wanted to charge in guns first.

Lin rose from their chair and addressed Paz with the same seriousness they had given everybody else. "Do you want to sit back down or do you want to leave?"

Paz's face went scarlet. "Leave. Forever."

That got a laugh, this one cleaner.

"Reasonable," Lin said. "Can you walk if Esme takes your left side?"

"Probably."

"Good enough."

Paredes watched the two women move toward the door. "She should still see a licensed neurologist."

Mira almost snapped something useless back. Esme beat her to it.

"With what money?" she said, not loudly. "The wedding body was the money."

Paredes's mouth flattened. She looked at the extra chairs, the folded butcher paper, the people still sitting in their accidental circle. Suspicion was still there, and so was recognition, though Mira did not mistake it for approval.

"You need a partition if you're treating bodily events in a public wash space," Paredes said to Jo. "You need separate disposal. You need occupancy posted. And if I come back and see sharps in a rinse sink, I'm not coming back alone."

"Heard," Jo said.

"And whatever this is," Paredes added, with a glance at the circle, "pick one version before the next complaint. Grief group or laundry. The city hates improvisation."

"The city can eat hot lint," Jo said.

Paredes picked up her thermos, looked like she wanted very badly to answer that in kind, and left instead.

The door shut. The room breathed.

No one applauded, thank God. Instead Noel said, "So, uh. Are we still taking numbers or did grief win?"

Jo rubbed both hands over her face. "Sit down, fish boy. Grief is intermission."

And because the city had no mercy and poor people learned to imitate that efficiency when necessary, the room laughed and kept going.

By the time the next three walk-ins appeared at the window asking whether this was the place that handled carryover without paperwork, there was no honest way left to call Blue Hour secret.

***

They locked the front door at nine-thirty and kept talking through the glass for another twenty minutes, pointing people toward tomorrow, next week, whenever they could plausibly promise bodies and labor they did not yet have.

The laundromat after a crush had the look of a field hospital pretending to be domestic: damp chairs, crumpled paper cups, a trail of baby's breath ground into the grout near the dryers, glitter caught in the drain trap, butcher paper folded and unfolded and written on again, and Jo's lost-and-found bin gutted from emergency wardrobe use. The tram mechanic had left behind a Tupperware container of rice and stewed beans in payment. The bride's aunt had paid cash, damp bills rubber-banded with a wedding favor ribbon.

Mira mopped the spot where Paz had dropped half her bouquet and watched the water go cloudy with lint, dust, and one floating pearl that was probably plastic but had looked real enough under crisis light.

My mother thinks I found religion. I found drywall.

The line from Noel came back to her and made her laugh once, short and unwilling. Exhaustion had a low sense of humor. So did Jo.

"If you start losing it on my floor," Jo said from the card table, where she was stacking the day's intake slips, "I'm charging you group rates."

"How much did we make?"

Jo counted again, because counting calmed her. "Cash, eight hundred sixty-two. Plus the beans, plus one bottle of decent tequila from the bride's aunt, plus a promise of rewiring from somebody who may or may not actually know electrical work."

Tavi, cleaning the wetbench with solvent and a tenderness that almost looked obscene, whistled low. "That's not terrible."

"It is if it's evidence," Mira said.

"Everything's evidence if you piss off the right office."

He did not say Yaret's name. He did not need to. It sat behind half their decisions already, a quiet man in a clean collar somewhere down the weeks ahead.

Lin sat at the front table with the legal pad turned sideways and both hands around a mug of reheated tea Jo had forced on them. The scarf had slipped a little at the nape from the heat and haste of the day. They looked pale with fatigue, eyes too bright. Good with frightened people did not mean untouched by them.

"Did I make it worse?" they asked.

Mira stopped mopping. "Which part?"

"The part where everyone saw."

There was no use in pretending not to understand. Lin had done something brilliant and dangerous and could see enough to know both were true.

Jo answered first. "Honey, that room was already seeing. You just made them useful."

Tavi capped a solvent bottle with his wrist. "If we're assigning blame, start with the company that sold a honeymoon breakdown to a woman on her wedding day."

Lin looked at the legal pad. "That isn't what I meant."

Mira set the mop aside and came to the table. Up close she could see the pencil dent along the side of Lin's middle finger, the tiny lavender thread caught on the apron pocket, the fatigue gathering in the careful stillness of their mouth. They had spent hours being the calmest person in a room full of public shame. That cost.

"You made it visible," Mira said. "That is not the same thing as worse."

Lin absorbed that, unsatisfied.

"Visible can still get you raided," they said.

Tavi leaned one hip against the bench. "So can doing thirty-one off-book cases in a week and pretending the block hasn't noticed."

"Thirty-four," Jo said without looking up.

"What?"

"Three after the inspector left. I counted."

That shut the room up for a second.

Mira had known the line was bad. Thirty-four made it arithmetic: thirty-four people in a day, thirty-four bodies or borrowed bodies or frightened renters or something in between, thirty-four reasons Blue Hour no longer fit inside the back room in any meaningful sense.

She looked at the folded tickets, the cash, the wetbench light still warming the curtain from behind, and Lin's notes, neat and unsentimental.

`music trigger before panic`

`knees remember stairs`

`grief in room, not chest`

That last one sat in darker pencil as if Lin had pressed harder writing it.

Jo shoved the money into piles with detergent-dried hands. "You can all stop pretending caution is still on the menu the way you mean it."

Mira looked up. "You want to invite the city in?"

"The city was in here this afternoon, querida. She had sardines on her thermos."

That image stayed with her: Paredes's chipped nails, the battered lunch container, the way she'd heard the room before deciding how hard to push. In Cordera the city arrived as worker and threat and somebody who also had to carry lunch from job to job. It never gave them clean villains when systems could do the work cheaper.

"I want," Jo went on, "for us to stop acting like this is still a side hustle between dryers. We need an actual front. Posted chairs. Posted rules. A better lie or a better truth."

"There isn't a better truth," Mira said.

"Then a better lie."

Tavi dragged a clean cloth over the bench in slow circles. "We also need hours. Real ones. Half the panic today was people not knowing whether they'd be seen before the body got worse. You put fear in a line with no clock and it breeds."

"That's rich coming from you," Mira said before she could stop herself.

He looked at her. There it was again, the thing between them with too many names and not enough time. He could have answered with a joke, an apology, or anger. He chose none.

"Yeah," he said. "It is."

The quiet after that was not comfortable. It was honest enough to work inside.

Jo broke it by sliding the legal pad toward Mira. "Read your tall one's notes."

"They're not my tall one."

"I said what I said."

Mira read. Lin had not just taken symptoms. They had built a triage logic Blue Hour had been fumbling toward for weeks.

`worse with witnesses`

`worse with closed heat`

`needs naming before touch`

`do not sit near radio`

`separate shame from injury`

She felt something ugly and almost hopeful move under her ribs. It wasn't relief; relief was too soft a word for a thing that came with more work attached. It was recognition, infrastructure in pencil.

"You did all this today?" she asked.

Lin nodded once. "People kept answering the same questions in different clothes."

Tavi laughed under his breath. "That might be the whole profession."

Mira turned a page.

At the bottom, after the last numbered ticket, Lin had written one line not about symptoms at all.

`Most clients calm down when somebody tells them the humiliating part is common.`

That sentence was the institution. The wetbench mattered, and the curtain mattered, and Jo's floor mattered, but the sentence was the part people would carry home.

She thought of CommonFrame then, the cold filtered offices, the forms that translated broken flesh into fee schedules, the smug claim that irregularity had to mean misuse because the system could not afford the alternative explanation. People came to Blue Hour because here the humiliating part did not become an invoice first.

And that meant the crowd was not going away.

The front window rattled under a knock.

All four of them looked up.

Outside stood a woman in a canal courier jacket, helmet under one arm, rain finally spotting dark across her shoulders. She had a grocery bag looped over her wrist and held the other hand flat against the glass. Even from inside Mira could see that the fingers were moving a half second ahead of whatever she meant them to do, tapping out some private impatient rhythm.

When Jo cracked the door, the woman did not try to come in. She just asked, "Are the numbers done for tonight?"

Mira could have said yes. She could have told the woman next week. She could have said the risk had climbed too high, that the city's patience had a limit, that Yaret was already sniffing around missing disposal records, and that secrecy mattered more than volume if they wanted to keep Lin alive and the bench intact and Brine Court rent paid for another month.

All of that was true.

The other truth was the one on the legal pad in Lin's hand. It was common enough to need a system, common enough that a municipal inspector could walk into a laundromat full of it and not even look surprised at first, and common enough that people were now knocking after close because rumor had turned Blue Hour into a place on the map.

Mira took the pad, tore off a clean sheet, and started writing in block letters.

`TUES / WED / THURS`

`6 PM TO LATE`

`TAKE A NUMBER`

`NO CLAIMS PAPERWORK`

She looked at Jo. "You still have the extra folding chairs?"

"In the storage cage."

"Bring them out tomorrow."

To Tavi she said, "Can you build a second privacy screen?"

"Out of what?"

"Out of whatever you're always stealing from the world."

That got the ghost of his grin back. "Yeah. I can."

She turned to Lin. "Front intake is yours if you want it. But we change the scarf. Too hot. We'll find something else."

Lin blinked. "You want me at the front?"

"You're already at the front."

The courier woman outside shifted from foot to foot in the rain. Mira walked to the door, handed her the fresh sheet so she could read the hours, and when the woman looked up with the desperate caution of somebody expecting to be turned away, Mira answered before prudence could rehearse its case.

"Tomorrow at six," she said. "Tell the others to come early."
