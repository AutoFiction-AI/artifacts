# Chapter 16

Municipal Arbitration Hall had been built for people who could afford to lose a day.

The intake receipt was still in Mira's coat pocket, its stamped edge gone soft under her thumb.

The steps were shallow and wide and slick with harbor mist, a civic gesture toward dignity that still made everybody climb slowly. Security stanchions snaked across the lobby. Wet coats steamed under the heat vents. A coffee cart by the metal detectors sold burnt coffee for six dollars a cup and stale sesame twists for four, which meant half the room carried hunger around in paper folders with their case numbers clipped to the front.

Mira stood in line with the registry ledger under one arm and felt the building measuring her income before anyone asked her name. The ledger was wrapped in a clear evidence sleeve that made it look like contraband laundry. Inside were gray notebooks, receipts, copied intake pages, band tags, and a strip of medical tape with a serial number written on it in Lin's neat hand. The plastic crackled every time she tightened her grip.

Lin stood beside her in Gabe's dark coat, which fit in the shoulders and drowned the wrists. They had combed their hair flat with too much water, and a stubborn ridge over the left ear had dried up crooked, showing the notch there in a pale clean line. Their face was still in the careful mode they used in public, every expression arriving as if they had checked it against a mirror first. But Mira could see the right thumb rubbing the seam of the coat pocket over and over. Gabe had texted before dawn to say the coat still had a lucky tram token in it from the year he kept missing rent and never once got fined anyway. Lin had found the token and kept it where a person might keep a saint's medal.

Tavi had shaved for the hearing and made himself look more disreputable in the process. The flattened nose and tired eyes came forward when the beard was gone. He wore Jo's nephew's funeral suit jacket over black work pants, and the tie sat wrong because he had tied it too tight, loosened it, then yanked it crooked again. He kept worrying the knot with the hand that had the burn scar on the forearm, a mechanic's version of prayer.

Jo had come in with them as far as the lobby, carrying a canvas grocery tote full of sandwiches wrapped in wax paper and a plastic jar of antacids. Her silver braid was looped through the back of a baseball cap from a defunct karaoke bar, and she had brought three pens, a stapler, and a little packet of instant coffee in case the city tried to charge for hot water too. She planted herself by the wall of case listings and read them aloud for people squinting at the screens.

"If they charge you for sitting down," she told the man behind them, "fall over dramatic. Make it worth the fee."

The man barked one surprised laugh and then winced because his rented chest had an injury brace visible under his sweater. That was the docket line all over. People here ran on fright, paperwork, and jokes used like handrails.

Through the glass doors at the top of the steps, Mira could see the spill of a crowd outside. Clinic regulars pressed against the barricades. A labor group held rain-warped signs about stolen scans. Two local feeds stood ready with shoulder cameras. She spotted at least three CommonFrame employees still in company coats pretending to be part of the general public. Everyone had come to see whether the city would call a living body a clerical problem or something harder to put back in storage.

"Do they always make everybody empty their pockets?" Lin asked quietly.

"Everybody who doesn't come through the side door," Mira said.

Lin nodded at the brushed steel detector arch. "That's good. It would be worse if it were selective."

That was Lin's version of nerves. It was not hand shaking. It was comparative ethics.

Tavi looked toward the hearing room doors and then toward the corridor that led to the staff offices. "If Sanz tries to split them up from us, I want to be the one who starts talking first. I can keep him busy."

"You can keep him irritated," Mira said.

"Same tool. Different angle."

"No," she said. "Not today."

He held up both hands. "I'm not saying I'll set anything on fire."

"You don't have anything left to set on fire."

The line moved. Security trays clapped and slid. Somewhere deeper in the building a microphone shrieked and got cut off fast. Mira felt her stomach turn not from fear exactly but from that old claims-floor reflex that rose whenever a process was about to name a body in the wrong language. She knew the phrases the room on the other side of those doors could use: noncompliant tissue, defect recurrence, improper modification. Once a sentence got entered under the right heading it could become garnish, seizure, or pulp.

Jo touched Mira's elbow. "Eat half now."

"I'm not hungry."

"That was not a survey."

Mira took the sandwich. Fried egg, soft roll, too much pepper, exactly enough salt. Jo never made anything that tasted like a favor. Lin watched her chew and then took their own sandwich from the tote.

"If they ask me what I am," Lin said, not looking up, "I know what I plan to say. I don't know what to do if they ask me what I was."

Mira swallowed. The yolk had gone cold and chalky. "You don't owe them a clean timeline."

"I know. I would still like one."

Before Mira could answer, a low voice behind her said, "Ms. Alves."

Celia Rusk was standing just outside the public line, beyond the brass rope that marked where city staff and lawyers could move faster. She had exchanged her usual immaculate office silk for a dark wool coat cut simple enough to pass for modest until you noticed how well it fit. One of her gloves was on and the other hung from two fingers, because the knuckles on her right hand were swollen and shiny with ointment. She held a flat archive box against her side as if it were nothing more consequential than pastries for a meeting. An expensive black pen was clipped to the coat pocket, and there was a white crescent of toothpaste on the cuff she had apparently missed in the dark.

Mira had imagined Celia entering the hearing in full corporate armor, seated behind counsel, face unreadable. The sight of her here, in the bad public light with her hair not fully tamed and her arthritis visible, did not soften anything. If anything it made the years of choice look more expensive.

"You aren't supposed to be in this line," Mira said.

"I am not," Celia said. Her voice had the clean managerial shape on it, but she was breathing too high in the chest. "Would you step aside for one minute?"

Tavi moved before Mira did, closing the space at Lin's left shoulder. Jo hitched the tote higher on her arm, checked with one quick hand that the stapler was still inside, and planted herself half a step forward.

"If this is another private solution," Jo said, "you picked a poor hallway."

"It's not a solution." Celia glanced at Lin, then back to Mira. "It's documentation, and I don't intend to hand it to security."

That got Mira moving. She told Lin to stay with Tavi and Jo, then followed Celia past a bulletin board of mediation notices into a side corridor that smelled of copier heat and floor wax. The hall was lined with framed photographs of former arbitration commissioners, all of them looking as if they had died annoyed by the public.

Celia set the archive box on a radiator ledge. Up close, the skin under her eyes looked gray. "They locked me out of executive access at six this morning," she said. "That means either someone finally developed a conscience at the top, which I don't believe, or they're preparing to contain liability by trimming the people who know where the records are buried."

"You helped bury them."

"Yes." Celia met that cleanly. "That is one of the reasons I know the depth."

Mira said nothing.

Celia opened the box. Inside were paper printouts, old-style storage wafers in antistatic sleeves, and one slim binder with colored tabs. The first page Mira saw was a grid of model lines and donor-source clusters, each cluster ranked with terms like compliance risk, residual richness, emotional retention, and reclamation cost. In one column, under acquisition profile, were debt categories. Utility arrears. medical default. emergency wage advance. the poor translated into harvesting language.

For a second Mira had the ugly, simple urge to snatch the whole box and run.

"Seed maps," Celia said. "Executive backup set. Donor aggregation and model seeding logic across bargain production for the last four years. Enough to prove intent, if the tribunal wants proof. Enough to hang half the board, if anyone ever decides to mean it."

Mira turned a page. The codes from the anomaly wafer were there, nested inside a much bigger architecture. Reuse corridors. salvage blend tolerances. memory density targets. One flagged note read retain high affective residue for companion inventory trials. She felt her mouth go dry.

"Why now?" she asked. "Because they cut you out first?"

Celia's jaw tightened once. "That is part of it."

"Only part?"

"I grew up in a housing block three streets from the old canal slaughterhouse." Celia rubbed the bare hand with the gloved one, working ointment deeper into the sore joints as if pain were clay she could knead into obedience. "My mother did payroll for men who lost fingers. When the company replaced her with software, she kept balancing checks for neighbors at our kitchen table because poor people still needed somebody who could read the language that took their money. I have spent twenty-five years in rooms where I told myself I was staying long enough to keep the damage measurable. That story was useful to me. It paid for a better apartment than the one we had, and a better doctor than my mother got."

"You had chapter and verse for years," Mira said. "You waited until they were going to eat you too."

"Yes."

The answer was so spare that Mira looked up.

Celia went on before pity could make a mistake. "Don't romanticize this. I'm not here because you converted me. I'm here because the company drafted language yesterday classifying living overlaps as derivative inventory events, which would have made destruction easier if the line had to be cleansed fast. I kept a copy. I could either keep helping them build the wording or I could stop."

"After the raid."

"After the raid," Celia said. "After watching an enforcement action I had spent months pretending would remain proportionate. After realizing they were willing to burn service access for half the city if that was what preservation of asset confidence required. Pick your motive. There are several ugly ones available."

Mira looked back at the binder. Celia's late conscience, late fear, and late self-interest were all real. None of it cleaned the blood off Gabe's brace or the smoke out of Tavi's lungs. But the paper was real too, and right now reality had to be weighed by usefulness.

"Can you authenticate it?" Mira asked.

"Yes, with limits."

"What limits."

"I will testify to provenance, executive circulation, and the meaning of the scoring codes. I will not speculate on board-level oral directives I cannot document. And if you ask me whether I raised concerns internally, I will answer truthfully."

"Which is?"

A little impatience broke through the polish. "That I raised them as retention and litigation concerns, not as moral objections. I doubt that helps my image."

"You still think in image."

"Of course I do," Celia said. "Image is rent paid on time. Image is whose children get braces and whose do not. The fact that it disgusts you does not make it false."

Mira let that sit between them with the radiator hiss.

From the lobby came the murmur of the crowd swelling and then breaking around some new arrival. Mira pictured Lin in Gabe's coat, thumb on the token, waiting to be told whether the city would hear them as a voice or an object. She closed the binder and felt the weight of the box shift into decision.

"If this goes in," she said, "you don't get to stand back and look clean while we bleed for it."

"I have not looked clean all morning."

"That's not the same thing."

Celia's mouth moved as if she might say something sharp, then stopped. What came out instead was flatter, almost old neighborhood. "No. It's not."

They carried the box back together.

Tavi saw the expression on Mira's face and stopped touching his tie. Lin looked at the binder, then at Celia. They did not step back. They just watched her with that unnerving stillness that made most adults feel they were being weighed on a better scale than their own.

"Those are the maps?" Tavi asked.

"They're part of them," Celia said.

Jo took one look at the archive box and blew air through her nose. "Well. If we're going to ruin some people's breakfast, let's do it before lunch."

The guard at security called for the next group.

Mira tucked the ledger under one arm and the seed maps under the other. The plastic of the evidence sleeve stuck against the smooth cardstock binder. Handmade record. Executive record. One survived because poor people had started writing things down when the city would not keep them. The other survived because a woman in better coats had finally decided to keep copies for herself. Together they felt heavier than either should have.

She stepped toward the detector when her number was called.

***

Hearing Room C had a public gallery designed by somebody who believed discomfort improved civic order.

The benches were narrow. The microphones sat just far enough from the chairs to make ordinary posture look guilty. Ceiling vents blew cold air straight down the back of the neck. On the far wall a city seal had peeled at one corner and been pressed flat again, leaving a permanent bubble under the lacquer. Mira knew the type of room. Institutions liked to stage neutrality in spaces that had already decided who belonged there.

The tribunal sat at a raised bench, three people behind slim monitors and sweating paper cups. The chair was a broad-faced woman in a navy jacket with a frayed cuff hidden under a bracelet of cheap green beads; when she turned pages, one thumbnail showed a rough patch of gold polish she had missed removing. The panelist to her right kept cleaning his glasses on the end of his tie. The left-hand one had written the word standing in capital letters on his notepad before anybody spoke.

Yaret Sanz occupied the city's table with two binders, a legal pad, and no visible disorder. His suit was charcoal and exact. The knot of his tie sat perfectly centered. The only untidy thing on him was a crescent of dry skin gone red along one knuckle where he had washed his hands too often. He stood when Mira came in and gave her the same courteous nod he might have offered a woman whose permit application was about to be denied for her own good.

CommonFrame's counsel sat beside an assistant and a stack of tablets. He was a handsome man in late middle age with a fresh nicotine patch just visible at the base of his neck above the collar; every few minutes he scratched carefully around it with the side of one finger, not enough to wrinkle the shirt. His name card read ELIAS VOREN. His cuff links were matte steel, discreetly expensive, meant to imply a person who billed by the quarter hour and considered panic amateur work.

There were more bodies in the gallery than the room officially held. Reporters. neighborhood clients. three bargain-line renters Mira recognized from Blue Hour. A woman from the donor-rights clinic with a tote bag full of notarized affidavits. Two CommonFrame plant workers in disposable boot covers, still in shift pants, smelling faintly of coolant and bleach. Jo in the back row with the sandwiches on her lap like ammunition. Tavi at the end of the front bench, shoulders folded small for once. Lin between him and Mira, coat buttoned, hands flat on their knees.

The bailiff called the matter. The microphones popped alive. Rain hissed at the high windows.

Chairwoman Beal looked over the room. "This is emergency production review in the matter of CommonFrame Biologics bargain-line manufacturing and derivative claims. We will maintain order. We will also remember that a crowded room is not, by itself, disorder." Her gaze lingered a beat on the security officers by the door before she looked down again. "Mr. Sanz."

Yaret rose. "Thank you, Chair. The Bureau seeks immediate preservation authority over relevant production records and biological materials. We also request exclusion of unlawfully altered specimens and any records generated through unlicensed tissue practice. If there are criminal matters downstream, we do not assist them by collapsing evidentiary standards here."

Lawfully altered specimens, Mira thought. She looked at Lin's still hands and had to unclench her own around the ledger sleeve.

Voren stood next, voice smooth enough to sound reasonable from a distance. "CommonFrame supports responsible review. What we do not support is panic, nor the laundering of contaminated black-market material into a public proceeding. The complainants intend to present an illegally modified bargain shell with severe cognitive artifacting as if it were a person competent to establish corporate intent. That is not evidence. It is the visible consequence of unsafe postmarket tampering."

In six polished clauses, Voren had done it: turned Lin back into faulty inventory.

The chair looked to Mira. "Ms. Alves, you are appearing as complainant witness and custodian of the handwritten record?"

"Yes." Mira leaned toward the microphone. Her voice came back at her a fraction too loud. "Former claims adjuster at CommonFrame. Current custodian of the registry ledger and associated intake materials. Also current debtor to the Bureau, if we're listing everybody's interests."

The back benches murmured, and one of the plant workers swore under his breath. Yaret's expression did not change.

The chair's mouth twitched once, not quite a smile. "Noted."

"The Bureau moves to exclude the so-called registry in its entirety," Yaret said. "It is a chainless, unsworn compilation produced in the course of illegal practice, intermingled with hearsay and undocumented biological contact. The same applies with greater urgency to the witness identified as Lin. There is no legal personhood finding. There is no verified chain of identity. There is every indication of contamination."

"Contamination from what?" Mira asked.

"Unlicensed cognitive interference."

"From repair?" she said. "Or from being built that way in the first place?"

"Ms. Alves," the chair said.

Mira stopped. That was the game. Make her look like a woman who could not distinguish feeling from procedure. She could. She had built a salary on the distinction before they threw her out.

Voren rose without waiting to be recognized, then corrected the breach with a courteous incline of the head. "If I may. We have sympathy for injured customers and for workers caught up in rumor. But the law is clear about categories. A composite mnemonic event is not selfhood. Residual affect is not personhood. And an illegally altered shell does not become a legal witness because a roomful of frightened people wishes to make metaphysics out of defect."

Lin did not move. Only the thumb found the tram token in the pocket seam again.

Mira placed the ledger on the table and then set Celia's archive box beside it. The sound of cardboard on laminate was small, but it pulled every eye from the press row to the officers by the door.

"Before you rule on exclusion," she said, "I'd like the tribunal to look at what CommonFrame calls a defect when they think nobody outside the billing chain is reading."

Voren glanced at the box, then at Celia, who had taken a seat one row behind counsel instead of beside him. His hand dropped away from the nicotine patch and stayed there.

Yaret saw the same thing a beat later. If anger passed through him, it did so in a colder language. "What is that?"

"Executive seed maps," Mira said. "Backup archive. Donor aggregation and seeding logic for bargain-line production."

Voren turned fully now. "Chair, we object to any surprise insertion of unverified corporate material, especially material whose acquisition is unknown."

"Acquisition is known," Celia said from the row behind him.

Heads turned all the way from the counsel tables to the back bench.

Celia stood, smoothing the front of her coat with her good hand. "Celia Rusk. Former director of claims review at CommonFrame. I can authenticate the archive."

She was former now, and that landed harder than any slogan could have. Two reporters sat forward at once, and one of the plant workers lifted his head as if he'd misheard.

Voren stared at her as if some internal spreadsheet had just broken a formula. "Ms. Rusk, you were not listed."

"No," Celia said. "That was deliberate."

Chairwoman Beal exchanged a look with the panelist whose glasses kept fogging. "Ms. Rusk, approach. If you are offering authentication, you will be sworn."

Celia went to the witness table with no visible hurry. Up close the limp in her right leg showed on the second step of the dais, not the first. Mira wondered whether pain medication sat in that coat pocket beside the expensive pen. Celia held her raised hand steady by bracing the wrist lightly with the other.

After the oath, the chair gestured. "Explain the box."

Celia placed one palm on the binder. "These are internal executive backup materials from CommonFrame's bargain-line oversight archive. They contain donor-source clustering, mnemonic density scoring, salvage reuse tolerances, and seeding directives for low-cost body production. I retained a copy outside the corporate system."

"Retained for what purpose?" Voren asked sharply.

"Initially?" Celia said. "Risk management."

That earned a brittle laugh from somebody in the gallery.

Yaret stepped in before the reporters and back benches broke into talk. "Are these materials complete?"

"No."

"Then they are curated."

"Everything a company preserves is curated," Celia said. "These are still authentic."

The chair held up a hand. "Enough. Ms. Rusk, do these records indicate whether residual overlap in bargain bodies was accidental?"

Celia looked directly at the chair. For the first time all morning her voice lost the faint velvet pad she used in conference rooms. "No. They indicate it was modeled, priced, and production-managed."

The room made a noise then. It was not shouting. It was worse: the intake of a hundred people reaching the same conclusion at once.

Voren leaned toward his microphone. "Modeled residual retention is not the same thing as consciousness. This is being distorted."

"Of course it isn't the same thing," Mira said. "That's why the maps don't replace the witness."

Chairwoman Beal looked back to Mira. "Continue."

Mira opened the registry ledger to the flagged section Lin had tabbed the night before. The pages smelled faintly of detergent and skin oil under the plastic. "The Bureau wants the registry excluded because it is handwritten. It is handwritten because every digital record we kept either belonged to CommonFrame or got seized in the raid on Blue Hour. The ledger tracks serials, scars, donor traces, residue descriptions, and follow-up contacts across living overlap bodies. By itself, that would be enough to show pattern. With the seed maps, it's enough to show chain."

She slid photocopies to the clerk, who began distributing them down the bench.

"Page three," Mira said. "Model cluster Kestrel-Bargain South. Eight shells linked to debt-scan pool 4C. Registry entries cross-match five of those shells to living or documentable donor histories: a fish-packer's tendon tear on the right ring finger, a C-section scar above the left hip, a nickel allergy response behind the ears, a childhood tibia set crooked after a bus accident, and a recurring phrase in two different overlaps about hiding rent money in a rice tin over the stove. Those matches do not come from repair contamination. They predate repair and repeat across shells CommonFrame seeded from the same donor pool."

The left-hand panelist had stopped writing standing in block letters and started underlining pool 4C instead.

Yaret said, "Anecdotal pattern recognition does not create legal identity."

"No," Mira said. "The company's maps create the sourcing structure. The ledger shows what that structure did in living bodies. And the witness you're trying to exclude is one of those bodies."

Voren's tone sharpened. "Your witness is an illegally altered product. Whatever residual phenomena existed before postmarket interference are now hopelessly tainted."

Tavi made a sound low in his throat. Jo reached forward from the gallery and put two fingers on his sleeve without looking at him. Mira was suddenly so grateful for that tiny motion she almost lost the next sentence.

"If CommonFrame's position is that Lin is merely property," she said, "then they're property carrying stolen donor traces the company priced and managed. If CommonFrame's position is public safety, then this panel needs the witness's condition on the record instead of counsel's adjectives. If the question is consciousness, the maps show design, the registry shows the same outcome repeating, and testimony shows what that outcome looks like in a living body."

The chair tapped her pen once against the desk. "Mr. Sanz."

Yaret stood with his hands lightly resting on the table edge. "The Bureau's concern is not moral rhetoric. It is scope. This tribunal can review manufacturing practice. It cannot declare personhood by spectacle. If you allow a witness with no established legal status to testify as if status were settled, you invite reversible error and contaminate any enforceable order."

He said spectacle the way another man might say sewage backup.

Mira heard herself answer before she had planned the shape of it. "Then don't let the testimony settle personhood," she said. "Use it the way you'd use a disputed intake. Check it against the maps, check it against the ledger, and see whether the label matches the body."

That made the chair look up hard.

"And if the witness speaks nonsense?" Voren said.

"Then you've lost twenty minutes and made a cleaner record," Mira said. "If they don't, and you block the witness anyway, you've left the body at issue out of its own file on purpose."

Silence. Even the vents seemed to pause for that.

Chairwoman Beal conferred in a low huddle with the other two panelists. The clerk carried the seed-map binder up to them. Paper slid. Microphones clicked off and back on.

When the chair straightened again, her green beads knocked softly against the desk. "The tribunal will admit the archive provisionally subject to later challenge on chain and completeness. The handwritten registry will be admitted for limited pattern evidence, not as a sole basis for identity findings. As for the proposed witness Lin" - she glanced down at the name card the clerk had handwritten because there was no official field for it - "this panel is not recognizing personhood today. It is recognizing that a living body whose status is directly implicated by the evidence may provide factual testimony under oath. Limited scope. Mr. Sanz, Mr. Voren, you may object to questions. You may not pretend the witness is not in the room."

The sound that came out of the gallery this time was relief arriving too fast and crashing into anger on the way through.

Lin stood because Mira touched the back of their hand once.

As they moved toward the witness table, the tram token slipped from their pocket and hit the floor with a bright coin sound.

Tavi bent to grab it, but Lin got there first. They picked it up, closed their hand around it, and kept walking.

***

The witness chair was too high for Lin's borrowed shoes to sit flat on the floor.

Mira noticed because she noticed everything stupid and material when panic threatened to become abstraction. The chair edge cut into the backs of Lin's legs. The microphone had to be tilted down twice. The oath card was printed in such tiny type that Lin leaned in close to read it and then looked embarrassed for no reason a person should ever feel embarrassed in front of government furniture.

"State your name for the record," the clerk said.

Lin held the token in one fist under the table. "Lin."

"Full legal name?"

There was a beat in which the reporters and the officers by the door probably expected confusion.

"I don't have one," Lin said. "That is part of why we're here."

The chair gave the clerk a small nod to move on.

Mira kept her hands folded tight enough to hurt. She could smell the hall's cheap wood polish warming under the lights. Rain tapped at the windows. Somewhere in the back row a reporter's stylus kept clicking in a restless rhythm until Jo hissed softly and the clicking stopped.

Mira began gently. She did not have training for direct examination, but she had years of claims interviews with clients too scared to say the costly thing aloud. "How long have you been living as Lin?"

"Since Mira and Tavi did not wipe me." Lin looked at the panel, not at her. "Before that I was under hold as LM-127B in CommonFrame claims."

"Do you understand why you're here today?"

"Yes." A swallow. "To say I am not a defect report. To say the others are not either."

Voren started. "Move to strike the conclusion."

The chair said, "The panel will separate argument from fact. Continue."

Mira nodded. "Can you tell the tribunal what it feels like to be you."

Voren was on his feet at once. "Objection. Philosophical."

Mira turned to him. "Embodied."

Chairwoman Beal considered that, then said, "I will allow a factual answer grounded in lived experience."

Lin inhaled through the nose, slow. Mira knew that tell now. It meant they were ordering the flood.

"It feels crowded," Lin said. "Not like voices. More like when a room has had many dinners in it and the curtains still smell of all of them."

Voren said, "Move to strike the comparison."

"The comparison can stay," the chair said. "Witness, give me the bodily version now."

Lin swallowed. "All right. Bleach can do it. Fryer grease can do it. Once a tram brake screamed on Harbour and I had to sit on the curb because for one second I was in a bus turning over and there was glass under my tongue, except I have never been in a bus turning over in this body." They looked down at their own hands. "My left rib aches when weather comes in. The scar here-" They touched the pale line under the collarbone. "-pulls when I reach too high. I hate canned peaches. I like the sound of laundry carts because it means Jo is about to tell somebody what to do. When I'm frightened I count the clicks in Brine Court's radiator because it knocks seven times before heat moves. Some of that arrived before I woke. Some of it happened after. It all lands in me now."

The reporters forgot to write for a second.

Mira said, "What does the registry do for you."

"It did not make me," Lin said. "It kept every leftover thing from feeling like random damage. It gave names to some pieces. Not all." A tiny twist of the mouth. "More names than CommonFrame gave me."

"Can you give an example."

Lin nodded. "Entry forty-two. Donor trace likely from utility-arrears pool. A woman with a nickel allergy behind both ears and a habit of rolling rent cash into the paper from bouillon cubes because her son never checked the spice shelf. When I first smelled bouillon at Jo's place, I knew my hands wanted to hide money before I knew why. The registry matched that habit to two other overlap entries and then to the seed map cluster. So now I know that memory may have belonged to a woman who still exists in public records even if CommonFrame treated her as usable patterning."

The panelist with the glasses was no longer polishing them. He was staring at Lin like a man whose categories had developed a leak.

Mira asked a few more questions. Lin said they had lived with Mira and Gabe at Brine Court since waking. They said Mira had taught them the registry codes first, then Tavi, then their own stubbornness had finished the job. When Mira asked about the raid, Lin said officers broke the back door, smoke filled the room, Gabe went down on the floor, and the ledger stayed hidden under Lin's coat. As Lin answered, the reporters and the panel stopped hearing concept words and started hearing sequence, object, and body.

Then Voren stood for cross-examination, and the air changed.

He walked to the lectern with papers squared in one hand. Up close he had the kind of clear skin expensive people got from remembering appointments. His nicotine patch had begun to curl at one corner.

"Lin," he said pleasantly, "you understand that human memory is suggestible."

"Yes."

"You also understand that you were subjected to unlicensed repair by a man with no standing credentials and a woman now under civil fine."

Tavi shifted on the bench. Mira did not look at him.

"I understand they kept me alive," Lin said.

"That wasn't my question."

"It's part of the answer."

Voren smiled the smile of a man informing a room he had found the child in the argument. "You claim present consciousness. Yet by your own account your internal experience consists of fragments from multiple unknown sources. How many donors do you believe contributed to what you call yourself?"

Lin looked at him. "I don't know."

"Three? Ten? Fifty?"

"I don't know."

"Then what exactly are you asking this panel to recognize? A collage? A symptom cluster? A contaminated record?"

The word contaminated moved through Mira like a splinter. She thought of Lin in the bath after the raid, soot in the ear notch, water gone gray.

Lin worried the edge of the tram token under the table until the ridged metal flashed white against the knuckle.

"You see the problem," he said softly. "Without origin, you cannot establish a legal self. At best you are describing aggregated recall inside company property."

Lin raised their head. "When you say origin, which one."

Voren blinked once. "Your original human source."

Lin opened their mouth, then stopped.

The chair said, "If you cannot identify one source, witness, say so plainly."

A pulse jumped once in Lin's throat. "I cannot identify one original human source."

Voren spread one hand. "Thank you."

Lin looked down at the token and then back up. "You can leave that line blank on your form," they said. "I'm still sitting here."

Voren's smile thinned. "Move to strike the argument."

"Overruled," the chair said. "Briefly."

"Nothing in me arrived with a label tied to it," Lin said. "It comes by pressure: a smell, a pain, a sentence somebody says at the wrong moment. That's how it comes."

Voren changed angle. "Very well. Let us be precise. Do you have memories that you know did not occur in your current body?"

"Yes."

"Do you have preferences that may derive from those memories."

"Probably."

"Then how can this panel distinguish your supposed selfhood from accumulated residue plus social coaching by interested parties."

Lin glanced toward Mira for the first time, then away again. "By what happened before anybody opened the book."

"Not responsive," Voren said.

"Give the panel the distinction, Lin," Chairwoman Beal said.

Lin took a breath. "Sometimes the registry came first. Usually it didn't. Sometimes Mira read me an entry and it sat there dead. Sometimes I said something first and they found it three pages later. Once I swore a memory was bakery sugar because my mouth went sweet, and Jo circled it and wrote hospital bleach in red because she doesn't trust anybody's first guess, including mine. The red circle is still in the margin."

Voren said, "So you admit suggestion."

"I admit sequence is messy," Lin said. "That's why Jo left the red circle there."

Voren kept his tone mild. "Observation and annotation do not equal legal selfhood."

Mira forced her jaw loose. She had not known until that second how hard she was biting her cheek.

"No," Lin said. "They do show I was there when it happened."

"A camera is there."

That pulled a disgusted breath from the public benches and a muttered curse from the plant workers.

Lin went still enough that Mira's own lungs forgot their job.

"A camera doesn't get embarrassed," Lin said. "A camera doesn't wake from somebody else's nightmare and then feel ashamed because the nightmare wasn't technically earned. A camera doesn't want coffee because the apartment smells like coffee and then discover the first mouthful is awful and still drink it because Jo made it. A camera doesn't hear the word inventory and feel the back of the neck go cold where the port is. A camera doesn't sit in this chair with its feet not even flat and decide not to run."

Voren looked down at his notes, then back up. "Choose not to run from what."

"From this." Lin lifted one hand, palm open to the room. "From being looked at and measured and spoken around. After the raid I could've left. We talked about it. There were places I could've hidden. I'm here because if I stay hidden, your side gets to keep saying the only people in these bodies are absent people or bad repairs. I'm not absent. And I'm not a bad repair."

Yaret rose this time, voice cool. "No one is suggesting absence. The question is jurisdiction."

Lin turned toward him. The movement was small, but the officers by the door, the reporters, even the clerk tracked it like weather. "Inspector Sanz, when your officers came into Blue Hour, one of them stepped on a plastic bin of clamps and swore because he sliced his hand open. I know this because I was under the folding table with the ledger. He bled on the tile and looked shocked by the fact that his own body could still surprise him. Then he kept kicking the table anyway because orders were louder than pain." Lin's eyes stayed on Yaret's face. "Jurisdiction did not feel abstract from under there."

For the first time all morning, Yaret's answer took half a beat too long. "That is not a question before this panel."

"It's for me," Lin said.

The chair said, "Mr. Sanz, you may ask a question or stand down."

He chose the question. "Do you understand that a production freeze could disrupt access to bargain bodies and maintenance services used by thousands of city residents, including workers with no connection to this case."

There was the threat disguised as public responsibility. Mira almost admired the cleanliness of it.

Lin started, "I understand that poor people-"

"Yes or no first," Yaret said.

The chair added, "Answer yes or no first. Then you may explain."

Lin looked at him with sudden sorrow instead of fear. "Yes," they said. The word came out clipped enough to sound bitten. "Because poor people have told me so all month. Because Gabe used them to work when his back wouldn't let him keep his own shift. Because people came into Blue Hour with port rashes, split sleeves, and rent due, and they still needed the body to hold through Friday. I understand that very well." Their mouth tightened. "It's still not a reason to keep printing people full of borrowed hurt and call it access."

Yaret sat.

Mira thought that might be the peak, the moment after which the gallery and the panel had to either feel it or seal over it. But Voren had one more move. He lifted a page from his stack.

"Lin," he said, "is it true that your current body contains a serial port at the nape and a manufacturer identifier under the left scapula."

"Yes."

"And is it true that your tissue composition remains traceable to CommonFrame property stock."

"Traceable, yes."

"Then regardless of your emotional description, the body in question remains manufactured material."

With one steel-tag phrase, Voren had the whole architecture back in place.

Lin's hand went to the back of the neck, not hiding the port so much as feeling it. Mira watched the fingers press there and thought of every time Lin had asked to have the area cleaned because dried saline itched.

"My body is manufactured," Lin said. "So is a knee replacement. So are braces. So are the glucose monitors under children's shirts on the tram. The port still itches if saline dries there. The identifier under my shoulder still catches on a seam. None of that tells you what you're supposed to do when I say no."

Voren spread his hands. "And what question is that."

Lin's gaze moved from him to the panel, then to the gallery, taking in Jo's set jaw, Tavi bent forward as if holding the bench together, the plant workers in the back, the clinic woman with her affidavits, Mira at the table with both hands flat because anything else would shake. When Lin spoke again, the voice had lost some of its care and found more life.

"Whether you still fill out the inventory line once the body in front of you tells you no."

No one spoke.

Rain struck the window harder, blown sideways. Somewhere in the hall a copier started up and ran for several seconds, absurdly ordinary, while the benches, the officers, even Voren's assistant held still around the sentence.

The chair finally said, very quietly, "Do you wish to add anything factual before we conclude testimony."

Lin looked down at the token in their palm, then closed their fist over it. "Yes. When I woke, I thought the worst thing would be pain. It wasn't. The worst thing was hearing people decide what category I fit while I was still on the table. The words changed before the hands did. defect. hold. property. anomaly. witness. They changed me in public before I ever got to say anything myself." They swallowed. "Factually: I'm cold in over-air-conditioned rooms. I prefer food with too much vinegar. I get angry when people stack clean towels on the bed because that means nowhere to sit. I'm frightened of being pulped. I'm embarrassed when I need help reaching legal language. I'm sometimes carrying memories that don't belong to me, and I'm still the one carrying them."

The gold patch on Chairwoman Beal's thumbnail flashed when she reached to mute the microphones for recess.

This time nobody in the gallery murmured. They were too busy being witnesses to something they would not be able to unhappen.

***

The recess lasted forty-three minutes and felt longer because nobody in the corridor trusted their own blood sugar.

People spilled into the hall in clumps, talking too loud or not at all. Reporters hovered and got waved back by the bailiff. Somebody from the donor-rights clinic cried in a bathroom stall with the door unlatched, not out of drama but because she had been standing on her feet since dawn in cheap flats and some truths arrived through the arches first. Jo distributed sandwich halves and antacids with the ruthless efficiency of a field medic.

Lin sat on a radiator bench under a framed poster about mediation services and stared at their own shoes. The tram token lay on the seat beside them, turned over and over by one fingertip. Tavi crouched in front of them, elbows on knees, speaking low. Mira could not hear the words, only the rhythm: joke, correction, technical aside, truth. His natural language.

When she came close, Lin looked up. Their face had gone pale under the hearing-room lights.

"Do I sound rehearsed now," they asked. "I can't tell."

"No," Mira said.

"I did not mean to call his question lazy."

"You meant exactly that."

After a second, Lin smiled. "Yes."

Tavi blew out a breath that might have been a laugh if it were not so tired. "For the record, it landed."

He had taken the tie off and stuffed it into his coat pocket. Without it he looked more like himself and more worn out. The skin under his eyes had gone bruised purple. Mira saw the little tremor in his right hand that showed up when he had not slept enough and had been holding himself together with motion.

"How bad is the production-freeze scenario," she asked him.

He scrubbed a hand over his mouth. "Depends what they freeze. Best case, they stop new bargain shells, preserve data, and keep maintenance lanes open under supervision. Worst case, CommonFrame locks the service portal, claims force-majeure review, and lets every low-income renter in the city twist. Which, you know. Corporate classic."

"Would they do it that fast."

"If their lawyers think access equals admission, yes." He glanced toward the closed hearing-room doors. "And if finance thinks shutdown buys leverage, they'll do it before lunch."

The win already had a hole chewed through the middle of it: stop the factory, strand the people already wearing its bodies.

Celia stood at the far end of the corridor by a dead ficus in a brass planter, phone to her ear, speaking in the clipped under-voice of someone using old contacts before they stopped taking her calls. When she finished, she came over with the face she wore when delivering bad performance metrics.

"They're already discussing covenant shields," she said.

"In English," Jo snapped from two steps away.

"If the order is broad enough, CommonFrame can trigger insolvency protection and use the review as a reason to suspend nonessential maintenance obligations while assets are sorted."

"Nonessential to who," Jo said.

"To the people who call service debtors a market segment."

Mira looked at Celia. "And you know this because."

"Because I sat in those meetings," Celia said. "Because I wrote some of the memo language. Because when a company says stability, it means investor confidence before human continuity."

Nothing in her face asked forgiveness. That, strangely, kept Mira from hating her uselessly.

The hearing-room doors opened. The bailiff called everybody back in.

By the time the tribunal returned to the bench, the gallery had changed shape. Not physically. The reporters were already poised over their pads, the plant workers braced on their knees, the clinic staff leaned forward as if the law might try to whisper and they intended to drag it into full volume.

Chairwoman Beal read from a printed order while the microphones recorded every word.

"This panel finds sufficient evidence of deliberate residual retention, donor aggregation, and materially misleading bargain-line disclosures to warrant emergency intervention. Effective immediately, CommonFrame Biologics is ordered to halt new bargain-line production, salvage recomposition, and destruction of existing overlap-implicated biological stock pending full review. The company will preserve manufacturing records, donor-source maps, maintenance protocols, and all related service data. An independent forensic team will be appointed."

Voren wrote something on a yellow pad without visible reaction. Yaret's face became even more polite, which was how Mira knew he had not gotten the narrower order he wanted.

The chair continued. "The tribunal further finds that living overlap-bearing bodies presented in the record possess direct factual interests that cannot be adequately handled under ordinary defect language. However" - and every public bench tightened on that word - "this panel does not have statutory authority to declare full legal personhood or ownership transfer today. Until a higher determination is made, such individuals will be entered into the civic record as protected disputed claimants. They may not be destroyed, sold, or removed from review jurisdiction without leave of this tribunal."

Protected disputed claimants: that was the phrase. It hit Mira with the force of a door half-opened onto a room still on fire.

Relief went through the public benches first. Then confusion. Then anger as the category finished landing. It meant not pulp and not sale, but it still did not mean person. It meant being held in a better cage.

Lin sat beside her with both hands flat on the bench.

Chairwoman Beal kept reading. "CommonFrame is ordered to produce, within twenty-four hours, a continuity plan for maintenance obligations affecting currently leased and medically dependent bargain-body users. The Bureau will refrain from seizure of the specific witness known as Lin absent further order. Existing criminal and civil questions are reserved."

It was a partial shield, and it came with a crack already running through it.

Voren rose immediately. "CommonFrame objects to the characterization of claimant status and reserves all rights to seek emergency relief."

"Of course you do," Jo muttered.

Yaret stood a beat later. "The Bureau requests clarification that protected disputed status does not impede public-safety supervision."

Chairwoman Beal did not look impressed. "The order is plain. Supervision is not custody. We are done for today."

The gavel tap was light. Administrative power never needed theater if it believed paper would finish the job.

Noise broke loose at once. Reporters surged. The plant workers in the back started talking over each other. One of the clinic women shouted, "Not personhood?" and got shushed by three different people who were already calculating appeals. The officers at the door straightened but did not advance.

Mira turned to Lin. "Are you all right."

Lin's mouth moved, then settled. "I am in the record."

"Yes."

"I am also apparently disputed."

There was no graceful answer to that. Mira put a hand on the back of Lin's coat between the shoulder blades. Under the wool she could feel the living heat of them, and under that the body the city still thought it might sort correctly if given enough forms.

Tavi leaned over from the aisle. "Hey. Protected is not nothing."

"No," Lin said. "It's not."

The disappointment in the sentence was so exact it made Mira look away.

Celia was already collecting her papers when a reporter tried to corner her. She batted the microphone aside with the gloved hand and kept moving. For one second Mira considered letting her go without another word. Then she thought of the service portal, the maintenance maps, the people whose jobs and mobility sat inside bargain shells right this minute.

"Celia."

Celia stopped.

"If they trigger insolvency," Mira said, "what happens first."

"Access locks. Parts holdbacks. Deferred updates. They will call it review stabilization." Celia's voice was quiet enough that only their cluster could hear. "You need the maintenance maps before close of business."

"Do you have them."

"Not here."

"Can you get them."

Celia hesitated. Not from reluctance. From arithmetic. Mira could almost see her counting who still answered her calls, which passwords had been killed, how much of her own remaining shelter she was about to spend.

"Yes," Celia said at last. "If I move now."

Jo looked at her hard. "Then move."

Celia gave a brief nod and left through the side corridor without waiting to be thanked.

That was when Mira's pocket started vibrating.

It was not her phone but Gabe's old backup handset, the one Blue Hour clients still had the number for because nobody trusted central systems anymore. She pulled it out. The screen showed missed call after missed call, then a new voicemail notification and three texts arriving on top of one another.

`Portal says account unavailable. What does freeze mean?`

`My labor frame is due for tendon service tonight. No one answering.`

`They told me my body lease is under review and I can't clock in tomorrow. Please call.`

Tavi saw the screen over her shoulder and said, very softly, "Fuck."

Jo's own phone started going off in her tote. Then the donor-rights clinic woman's. Then one of the plant workers answered his and began swearing with the frightened concentration of a man hearing a machine fail while still standing in front of it.

The room that had come to witness a ruling was turning, in real time, into triage.

Lin looked from the phones to Mira. "This is because of the freeze."

"This is because of the company," Mira said.

"The company is using the freeze."

"Yes."

The distinction mattered. It did not help.

By the time they pushed out through the lobby, the crowd on the steps had doubled. Someone shouted a question about personhood. Someone else shouted about jobs. Rain had thinned to cold mist, turning every coat dark. The camera crews surged toward Lin and then checked themselves at the expression on Mira's face. Over the noise, she could hear the harbor ferries blowing farther off, long flat notes moving through the wet air like warnings nobody had budgeted for.

Yaret came down the side stairs with two officers trailing him, his posture as immaculate as ever. He paused only long enough to say, "Protected disputed status is unstable. Keep your witness available."

Mira looked at him. "They aren't evidence you get to store."

"The order does not say otherwise."

"It says you don't take them."

"Today," Yaret said. "We will see what the next forum says."

He moved on before anger could become argument.

Lin watched him go. "He says forum the way some people say weather."

"Because he thinks weather is inevitable," Mira said.

Jo was already on the phone, voice sharpened into command. "No, listen to me. Don't let them tell you to pulp anything. I don't care what the portal says. Bring the paperwork and bring the body if it can travel. Yes, I know that's not an easy sentence. Move."

Tavi had his own phone out now, scrolling, swearing, dialing. "Service boards are down. Whole bargain portal is stuttering. Mira, if this cascades, people are going to start showing up wherever they think repair still exists."

Blue Hour was gone. The back room had been smashed, the wetbench burned down to salvage metal. There was no wherever left, only whoever.

Mira stood on the arbitration steps with the tribunal order dampening in her hand and understood the shape of the victory. They had forced the city to hear Lin as a living claim on the public record. They had put deliberate creation where CommonFrame wanted defect language. They had stopped the factory for a moment.

And now every cheap body built to keep poor people moving through other people's economies was about to be left in the rain with its maintenance withheld, its legal status contested, and its users told to wait for process.

Lin stepped close enough that their shoulder touched Mira's arm under the coat. "What do we do."

Down in the street, a tram bell clanged and somebody cursed and the cameras kept rolling because civic catastrophe was always good footage if it arrived with enough faces.

Mira looked at the missed calls again, at the order, at the crowd already starting to sort itself into the people asking questions and the people asking where to go. Blue Hour was a burned shell. Jo still had the laundry. For the next few hours, that would have to be enough. Then she folded the damp paper once and put it in the evidence sleeve with the ledger.

"We answer," she said.

It was the only sentence in her mouth that did not feel like a lie.
