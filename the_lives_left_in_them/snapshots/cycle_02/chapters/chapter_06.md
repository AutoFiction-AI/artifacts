# Chapter 6

Lin was awake before the tram started screaming down the hill.

They sat at the table in Gabe's old dock-union shirt, the green one with the cracked white gull on the pocket, sorting used receipts by size. The neat stack on the left was for grocery paper, the soft stack on the right for the glossy warranty slips CommonFrame mailed to renters after every disputed fee. Lin liked the glossy ones because the pen skated. They had written their name three times in block capitals and once in a slanted hand that belonged to somebody older, somebody who had once filled out school forms in a hurry.

"Do offices always smell cold?" they asked without looking up.

Mira had one shoe on and her lunch jammed into the side pocket of her work bag. "This one does."

Lin considered that seriously, then wrote COLD on the back of a tram notice and underlined it twice as if it might help.

Gabe came out of the bedroom dragging his left foot a little, brace still open under his T-shirt, hair pressed flat on one side. He had a spoon in his mouth and a bowl of dry cereal in his hand because milk cost more than cereal did this week. "You going in early or hiding from us?"

"Audit."

"That's worse."

Gabe was right. The audit had started as an ugly rumor on Friday, passed desk to desk with the same flat voice people used for mold, layoffs, and surprise fee reviews. People whispered about missing disposal paperwork, a return packet that did not reconcile, and someone from compliance staying late. Yesterday an all-staff notice had come through with three passive verbs and no names. Records were being reviewed. Access would be standardized. Staff were expected to cooperate fully.

Lin looked up then. Their lighter iris caught the kitchen's weak morning bulb and held it. "Should I not open the door?"

"Don't open it unless Jo knocks first or Gabe says so."

Gabe tipped his bowl toward them. "And if some bastard says he's from maintenance, ask him which apartment flooded last month. If he doesn't know, he can go to hell."

Lin smiled at that, small and real. They had not yet learned how much of neighborhood knowledge was test and how much was affection. Mira put the key in their palm anyway. It was a cheap brass copy from the kiosk by the canal market, still rough at the edge where the machine had worried it. Lin closed both hands around it as if it might leak.

"I'm serious," Mira said.

"I know."

She did not kiss their head or say anything that would make the room softer than it was. Gabe was already calculating how long he could keep the brace off before his back punished him for the vanity. The sink had a plate crusting over in bean grease. Rent still existed. Mira took her bag and left before she could stand there thinking about keys like they meant anything against a warrant.

Outside, Cordera smelled of salt pushed uphill through fryer grease and the canal's machine stink. The annex sat three tram stops down in its usual posture, all white panel and glass trying to look cleaner than the work inside it. On claim-heavy mornings the lobby usually held renters in expensive coats pretending not to look at the bargain-line queue. Today it held employees and security bins.

People filed toward the tables without the usual lobby sarcasm, lunches held out in both hands.

Two guards from building services stood behind folding tables with gray tubs for bags and lunches. The younger one, a woman with a prayer scar on her chin and glitter caught in the seam of one eyebrow, kept apologizing in the exact same sentence to every person she stopped, which made the whole thing feel worse. Mira put her bag down, watched the lid come off her lunch, watched a gloved hand lift the wrapped sandwich Jo had sent with her and squeeze it like contraband could be hidden in bean spread.

"Randomized enhanced exit and entry review," the guard said. She was chewing fennel seeds; Mira could smell them through the filtered lobby air. "Thank you for your patience."

"You keep saying that like anybody has a choice," someone from billing muttered.

The guard's mouth twitched but she did not rise to it. Human resources must have drafted the sentence and told her not to improvise.

Mira got her bag back and badged through. The green light took half a beat longer than usual to answer. It was not enough to mean anything. It was plenty to mean something.

The claims floor had been combed into false neatness. Weekend return bins that normally overflowed with tagged limbs and sealed fluid jars had been lined into straight metal grids. Audit seals sat over cabinet latches in strips of orange that would tear if opened. The smell was the same filtered cold as always until it wasn't, until something sweet and rotten from the intake lab drifted through the vents and reminded everybody what the building really did. Nobody had music on. Nobody had customer scripts open on their side screens. A compliance checklist glowed on every monitor like a threat in bulleted form.

Celia's assistant did not make Mira wait. She rose at once and opened the glass door, which was how Mira knew the morning had already gone bad.

Celia Rusk's office wall was glass on two sides, a kindness only if you liked being visible. She stood when Mira came in, which she almost never did for staff. Her silvering hair was cut blunt at the jaw and sat exactly where she wanted it. She had one hand wrapped around an expensive fountain pen and the other hidden under the opposite sleeve, likely working the sore knuckles she kept busy when she was angry. A tray of printed packets sat on her desk, all tabbed in different colors, the private weather system of a woman who believed paper could still control disaster.

"Sit down," Celia said.

Mira sat.

Celia slid a file across the desk but kept two fingers on it. "Unit LM-127B."

The file itself was thin. The consequences inside it were not.

"You put this unit on administrative hold eleven days ago," Celia said. "The tissue contamination review never closed. The disposal packet was opened, voided, reopened, and then disappeared between intake and archive. I've spent my morning learning about a body I should never have had to know existed."

Mira kept her eyes on the file, not on Celia. "The marrow haze came back inconsistent on the first pass. I held it for a second wash because I thought the renter file was dirty."

"And then?"

"And then the weekend happened."

"It always does."

Celia's tone did not lift. She could make contempt sound like a scheduling note; the insult landed only after the sentence was over. Mira could hear people on the floor through the glass, chairs moving, keyboard taps held too carefully apart. Her own mouth had gone dry enough that she caught herself pressing the inside of her cheek with her molars, the old tell. She stopped.

"There are three other files," Celia said. "They are not identical, but they are adjacent. They are units you touched, with delayed anomaly codes, disposal lag, and one packet with a chain-of-custody gap. That would have been a manageable humiliation on its own. It's now sharing a room with a Tissue Safety inquiry."

Mira looked up.

"About what?"

"If you don't already know, I'm not going to educate you by accident."

Celia answered by narrowing the room: a gate instead of a reply. Mira could push against it and show need, or walk around it and pretend she had none.

"I don't," Mira said.

Celia watched her for a beat. Her thumb worried the cap ring of the pen, tiny metal clicks. "Inspector Yaret Sanz from the bureau will be here at eleven. He's reviewing unauthorized repair indicators in returned bargain stock and missing disposal documentation. Because your queue intersects both issues, you will speak to him."

The room stayed the same temperature. Mira felt colder anyway.

"I've got nothing for Tissue Safety," she said.

"I'm sure you'd prefer that to be true."

Celia released the file. Inside were the usual things made malignant by attention: intake stills, tear maps, residue notes, barcodes, timestamps. On top sat Mira's own hold code in her own exact handwriting from eleven days ago. She had used the most ordinary words possible: Secondary wash pending. It looked innocent enough to charge interest.

"Did compliance ask for me by name?" Mira asked.

"Not yet. They asked for the files. The bureau asked who handled them. That is how names happen."

Mira turned one page. A copy of the disposal requisition had a blank square where final pulp confirmation should have been. No forge, no elegant sabotage, just a hole. The kind of hole that drew more hands.

"I can reconstruct the packet," she said. "Records loses things all the time."

"Records doesn't lose four linked discrepancies and then invite a regulator in for lunch." Celia leaned back. "Do not insult me with a clerical weather report."

There it was, the old worker under the blouse and polish, the woman who had once learned every petty form of theft because being poor inside a system required fluency. It flashed and went smooth again before most people would have noticed.

"Then what do you want from me?" Mira asked.

"Precision." Celia folded her hands. "I want a version of events that is narrow, boring, and supportable. I want to know whether there is a stupid story that can still be cut off before someone from the city turns it into a criminal one. If you made a sentimental call on a damaged unit, tell me now. Sentiment is easier to bury than fraud."

Lin's hands closing around the key came back so fast it felt like a blow. Mira heard the kitchen chair scrape, smelled Gabe's dry cereal. She kept her own hands flat in her lap because if she touched the file she might crease it.

"I held a questionable unit too long because I didn't want to bill the wrong renter on bad data," she said. "That's the story."

"Is it the truth?"

Mira should have said yes quickly. Instead she did what she always did under pressure and reached for procedure like a rail. "There was residue mismatch and undocumented scar tissue. I needed a second wash to avoid an invalid fee assessment."

Celia's eyes narrowed by less than a millimeter. "That was not my question."

"It's my answer."

For a moment Mira thought Celia might smile. She did not. She tapped the file once with the pen and then winced, the smallest tell, as if the movement had sent pain into her knuckles.

"You're not good at lying," Celia said.

"I didn't know that was in the job description."

"Everything is in the job description if it protects the company."

She said company the way priests said God in bad neighborhoods: as both shelter and appetite.

"Here's what will happen," Celia went on. "You will continue your shift. At ten-thirty you will pull an anomaly crosswalk from archives for these four units and any linked disposal tickets. You will bring the summary directly to me. At eleven you will sit with Inspector Sanz. You will answer only what is asked. You will not volunteer. You will not speculate. And if there is anything in your life that has touched unlicensed tissue work, this is the last quiet room in which I can hear it."

Mira kept her face still. Not because she was calm, but because if she let it move, it might move toward home.

"My life is payroll and rent," she said.

"Yes," Celia said softly. "That is true of most crimes worth committing."

When Mira stepped back onto the floor the room seemed louder, though nobody had raised their voice. Chairs rolled. Seal printers spat labels. Somewhere in intake, a returned leisure frame hit a metal tray with the dull lovely weight of fresh meat, and someone swore under their breath because the sound was always worse when it still felt warm.

Mira sat at her station and brought up the day's queue. The audit banner sat across the top of the screen in municipal yellow. ACCESS STANDARDIZATION IN EFFECT. PRESERVE ALL NOTES. DO NOT MODIFY HISTORICAL ENTRIES WITHOUT AUTHORIZATION.

The file on LM-127B was flagged with three separate icons now: internal review, disposal discrepancy, external inquiry support.

Names happened. Celia had been right about that.

***

At ten-twenty-eight, Mira printed the archive request and went downstairs.

The records corridor was colder than the rest of the building because old tissue data still had to pretend it was material. Half the bulbs in the ceiling had a faint blue cast that made everybody look briefly dead. Archive techs moved in fleece vests over office clothes, practical about the cold in a way management always forgot to be. Nasrin Vale was at the service window with a thermos tucked in the crook of one arm and two tiny silver fish sewn crookedly onto the cuff of her cardigan. She sewed one onto every cardigan she owned, one of the processors had once told Mira, because her son believed clothes without fish got lonely in the wash.

"Claims apocalypse?" Nasrin asked, taking the request sheet.

"Apparently."

Nasrin scanned the code and clicked her tongue. "Rusk wants it herself?"

"Direct handoff."

"Then she's afraid." Nasrin lowered her voice and leaned in just enough for Mira to smell clove tea. "When they start asking records for chain-of-custody history before lunch, somebody in a good blouse is afraid."

Mira managed half a smile.

"Bay three," Nasrin said. "You still remember how to mount an archive pull?"

"I'm not that old."

"You're claims old. Different species."

Bay three sat behind a partial screen of frosted plastic. The station was a flat black console, a reader slot, one haptic pad, and a purge light that blinked red when the cold store was spinning up. Mira badged in, keyed Celia's requisition, and waited while the system called old data up out of storage with the soft insect whine of expensive equipment doing something it hated.

On the screen, four serials appeared. LM-127B at the top. Three other units below it that Mira recognized by body damage before memory: a torn quadriceps, a split scalp seal, a throat bruise logged as pressure artifact because the renter had money and the escort agency had more lawyers than shame. All four linked out to disposal tickets with missing terminal verification. All four had anomaly codes entered late or corrected after initial filing.

Celia had asked for a crosswalk. She had asked for it to be boring, narrow, and supportable.

Mira stared at the anomaly fields instead.

The same residue cluster kept surfacing in different combinations: a childhood image, a cross-sex contact pattern, an undocumented scar, drowning panic. She had seen those things one body at a time, ugly and private. In a table they looked like inventory. Her stomach turned not from philosophy but from the very practical obscenity of columns making a market out of people's leaked lives.

There was a filter tab for secondary routing. Claims almost never used it; it belonged to disposal and salvage. Mira clicked it anyway.

The screen asked for elevated authorization. Celia's requisition token was still live because archive pulls stayed open until the requesting supervisor signed for them upstairs. Mira hesitated long enough to know she was doing something stupid and then pressed her thumb to the pad.

Access granted.

The four tickets branched open.

LM-127B: PULP HOLD -> DISPOSAL REVIEW -> ROUTE OVERRIDE W9-SALV/RICH.

The second unit: ROUTE OVERRIDE W9-SALV/MEM.

The third: W9-SALV/RICH.

The fourth: W9-SALV/COMPOSITE.

They were warehouse codes, not public ones. They were internal transfer shorthand, the kind claims was not supposed to see because once people who billed fees started asking where damaged bodies actually went, somebody higher up had to spend the week lying.

Mira felt the room tighten. The feeling was physical, not abstract fear: the cold desk edge against her forearms, the console heat at her knees, the knowledge that Lin had once been a route override with a different name.

She pulled the official summary first because she still understood sequence. The terminal wrote four pages to a clean report file and sent them to print at Nasrin's station. Then a smaller prompt opened at the bottom of the screen:

TEMPORARY ARCHIVE MIRROR LOADED. INSERT SERVICE WAFER FOR LOCAL REVIEW OR PROCEED TO PURGE.

Claims used service wafers when the network stuttered or a supervisor wanted to carry raw data into a room without trusting the building cloud. Thin amber circles in paper sleeves, cheap enough to treat like office junk until one of them held something ruinous. A single supervisor sleeve sat clipped to the side of the console for off-network handoff. Mira stripped it open with her thumbnail. Her fingertips were dry from the cold; she nearly dropped the wafer.

If she wrote the mirror to the wafer and logged it properly, the system would stamp it, number it, and attach it to Celia's requisition. If Celia got it, Legal got it. If Legal got it, it vanished into a folder named privilege and came back six months later as a sentence designed to bill a poor person for breathing wrong.

If she did nothing, she went upstairs with four boring pages and lost the only thing in front of her that looked like a door.

Nasrin laughed somewhere beyond the screen, low and tired. A printer started, stopped, started again.

Mira slid the wafer into the slot.

WRITE MIRROR TO LOCAL MEDIA? the screen asked.

Yes.

Progress climbed in a blue bar. Forty-two percent. Fifty-eight. Eighty-one. At ninety-four the slot clicked and the wafer pushed halfway out. The purge light changed from red to amber and a second line of text appeared.

SCAN MEDIA SERIAL TO COMPLETE LOG.

Of course it did. The mirror wrote first and the chain of custody came after, when somebody married the sleeve barcode to the requisition. Celia's live token had opened exactly that loophole. Her hand was already sweating against the cold plastic edge. She thought of Celia saying You're not good at lying and of Gabe eating cereal dry because milk cost money they did not have.

Mira took the wafer.

The console barked in offense.

MEDIA REMOVED BEFORE LOG.

AUDIT EXCEPTION RECORDED. PURGE CACHED MIRROR?

Nasrin's chair scraped back.

"You jammed bay three?" she called.

Mira dropped to one knee as if her lace had come loose, peeled up the foam insole of her left shoe, and shoved the wafer under it. The edge bit the pad below her toes hard enough to make her suck air through her teeth. The torn sleeve paper sliced her thumb when she crammed it into her pocket. Then she slapped PURGE with the same hand and left a small bloody crescent on the haptic pad.

PURGE CONFIRMED. CACHED MIRROR CLEARED. EXCEPTION FLAGGED FOR REVIEW.

Mira let out air through her nose so slowly it hurt.

Nasrin appeared at the edge of the frosted screen, thermos still in hand. Her eyes went from Mira crouched over her shoe to the console banner.

"Bay kicked the sleeve before I could scan it," Mira said. She got the lace tied on the second try and stood. "I purged the mirror."

Nasrin frowned at the haptic pad. "You bled on my station."

"Paper cut."

"Convenient." Nasrin looked at the gray exception tag still hanging in the corner of the screen. "Records will want that note if compliance comes sniffing."

"Then tell them the bay is old."

"The bay is old," Nasrin said. "You are the one who looks guilty."

Mira took that without answering.

"Good. Your pages are ugly."

At the window Nasrin handed over the printed summary and peered at Mira's face.

"You look freezer-burned," she said.

"Didn't bring a sweater."

"That's because claims people think panic is a coat."

Mira took the pages. "Thanks."

"If this building catches fire, records did not start it," Nasrin said, already reaching for the next request. The line came out worn smooth from use, funny on the surface and rigid underneath.

When Mira turned away from the counter, the wafer bit into her foot through the insole, a steady raw pressure that made every stride feel watched.

Upstairs, conference room two waited with the door open.

Yaret Sanz was already seated at the far end of the table. He had a municipal badge clipped neat to his jacket pocket, close-trimmed hair, and the stillness of a man who believed movement was a confession. A paper cup of annex coffee sat by his hand untouched. Celia stood by the wall screen with her arms folded, one hand massaging the opposite knuckle so discreetly it might have been nothing if Mira had not just spent ten minutes lying to a machine.

"Ms. Alves," Yaret said. He stood because courtesy was one of his tools. "Thank you for coming."

His voice was soft enough that Mira had to lean a little to hear it. Men like him made people move closer before they understood they were being handled.

She put the printed summary on the table in front of Celia and sat. The wafer in her left shoe pressed into her foot like a coin somebody had forgotten inside her.

"This will be brief," Yaret said. "At least at first."

Celia took the pages without looking at them. "My employee has archive work to return to, Inspector."

"Then I will try not to waste her paid time."

There was no edge in the sentence. That made it land harder.

Yaret opened a slim paper folder, not a tablet. Paper again, because everybody got more archaic when they wanted consequence to feel official.

"I am reviewing unauthorized tissue intervention linked to bargain-line returns processed through this annex," he said. "Separately, your employer is reviewing disposal irregularities. Those matters may intersect. I need your account of several files."

"Okay," Mira said.

"You handled claim 77-KP-044, renter Marisol Roldan, six days ago."

Mira knew the file by the shape of the injury before she knew the name. A torn adductor the company had tried to reclassify as misuse because poor people were always accused of using their rented bodies wrong when the factory saved money on tissue.

"Yes."

"Her body was denied extended repair coverage at eleven-forty-three. By nineteen-hundred it had undergone off-license stabilization. Tissue sealant present on return was not CommonFrame issue. Can you explain that?"

"Customers do things after denial."

"I did not ask what customers do in general."

"Then no," Mira said. "I can't explain what happened after she left."

Yaret made a note with a narrow black pen. His handwriting was severe and beautiful. Mira hated him a little for that.

"Did you refer Ms. Roldan to any outside repairer?"

"No."

"Did you suggest that official review could be avoided if she sought unofficial care?"

"No."

That one sat between them longer, not because the word had become less true by repetition but because the true version required too much grammar. She had not referred Marisol by name. She had written down an address and said, If somebody near the canal still knows old vat work, I didn't tell you that.

Yaret turned a page. "Do you know Tavi Sloan?"

Celia's eyes lifted from the summary for the first time.

Mira kept her own on Yaret. "I know the name."

"From where?"

"He used to work vat maintenance."

"That is not what I asked."

"It's where I know it from."

Yaret regarded her with that maddening care, as if he had all day and only one living specimen to study. "Mr. Sloan's name has appeared in a licensing inquiry connected to unauthorized tissue work in the canal market. Your queue intersects cases that later show his repair signature."

Celia said, "Inspector, my staff are not in a position to authenticate black-market stitch work."

"I am aware of their limitations."

The answer sounded deferential until the insult surfaced underneath it. Celia heard it. Her shoulders altered by a degree.

"For the record," she said, "Ms. Alves is a claims adjuster. She assesses damage and fee exposure. She is not a field operator."

"For the record," Yaret said, "field operators do not usually control anomaly coding, hold statuses, and disposal timing."

He did not raise his voice. He did not have to. The words lay down on the table like evidence bags.

Mira felt the wafer through her shoe again and had the absurd thought that if she took the shoe off right now both of them would hear it hit the floor.

"I code what shows up in front of me," she said. "If a file lags, it lags. Half this building still runs on patched readers."

Yaret's attention sharpened, though not visibly to someone who did not already fear him. Mira saw it because she was busy surviving him.

"That is an interesting answer," he said.

"It's a true one."

"Perhaps. What is less clear is why you become more exact when the questions get dangerous."

Mira said nothing.

"Most employees in your position get offended," Yaret went on. "Some of them get frightened. You start itemizing process. You answer implications with workflow. It suggests practice."

Celia said, "It suggests competence."

"Sometimes the two overlap."

He slid one photograph across the table. It was not a body, not directly, but a close tissue still from inside a thigh repair, pale matrix knit shut with a stitch style Mira knew from watching Tavi work shoulder-deep in steam and fluorescent spill. At the lower edge of the image a thread of blue lint was trapped in the sealant.

"This was recovered from a return processed here yesterday," Yaret said. "Commercial laundry fiber embedded in illegal repair resin. It is not proof on its own, but it is enough to make me curious about the canal market, Mr. Sloan, and who is steering rejected renters toward places that can keep them walking long enough to miss a bill hearing."

Mira looked at the lint. Blue Hour used blue press cloth because Jo bought it cheap in wholesale lots. There were probably a hundred laundries in the city that used the same color. There were probably ten.

"Do you know Blue Hour Laundry?" Yaret asked.

"Everybody in this neighborhood knows Blue Hour Laundry."

"Do you know it as a place of business beyond laundry?"

"No."

That one felt worse coming out than the others because it had to pass through Jo's name on the way. Mira kept her hands flat on the table to stop them from going to her face.

Yaret let the silence stand. He was good at that. Better than Celia. Celia filled silence to drive people. Yaret left it empty and waited for people to climb in by themselves.

Finally he said, "Has any unit under your handling been removed from formal disposal without authorization?"

"No."

"Has any conscious or semi-conscious overlap subject been concealed from CommonFrame or the bureau?"

The question was so precise it almost made her flinch. Celia went very still against the wall.

Mira heard the annex around them through the conference glass: a cart wheel ticking once per rotation, a printer beeping for more feed paper, somebody laughing too loudly three rooms away because pressure always made at least one idiot louder. Lin in the apartment, writing COLD on the back of notices. She could not tell if Yaret knew anything or was simply good enough at his job to ask the question that would crack the whole room on probability alone.

"No," she said.

Yaret studied her a moment longer, then closed the folder.

"Very well."

He left her nothing to push against, no clean accusation to deny, only the bureaucratic quiet that meant he would keep coming back.

He took out a second document and slid it across to her. Acknowledgment of inquiry. Preservation order. Her name printed there in bureaucratic black, correct spelling, employee ID beneath it. She was required to preserve messages, notes, physical media, and contact records relevant to the named matters. She was prohibited from destroying, altering, or removing company information without authorization. Failure to comply could lead to civil penalties and referral.

The words named civil penalties. Mira thought of the rent ledger tacked above the sink at home with the amount in red marker. Another fine would not feel like law. It would feel like weather with signatures.

"This does not mean you are being charged," Yaret said. "It means your access and your knowledge are now material to an active inquiry."

"That's comforting," Mira said.

"It is not intended to comfort you."

She signed. The pen scratched louder than it should have.

Celia stepped forward only after the document came back across the table. "I will need a copy for compliance."

"You will receive one," Yaret said.

"Now."

"By end of day."

Their eyes met. There it was again, the difference between them. Celia believed in ownership of mess. Yaret believed in jurisdiction. They were both intelligent enough to understand the other as a rival species.

Mira stood because the room had become too small to sit in. The wafer cut into her foot. She welcomed it.

"Am I done?" she asked.

Yaret looked at her as if the answer might be philosophical. "For this interview."

"Go back to your desk," Celia said before Mira could say anything else. "Do not leave the building without clearing it through me."

That sentence hit harder than the preservation order. It came from management, not law, which made it closer.

Mira took her copy of the notice and went.

***

The rest of the afternoon acquired the ugly slow motion of injury. People on the floor had heard enough to know something had happened and not enough to know what, which was the most efficient way to turn colleagues into furniture. Nobody asked directly. One of the intake women, Inez, pushed a pack of stale crackers onto Mira's desk without comment. Inez always clipped the corners off her ID photos before laminating them because she thought her own face looked kinder in an oval. Mira took one cracker and left the rest where they were.

At three-fifteen her badge stopped opening the lab door.

At three-sixteen a compliance message arrived reclassifying her access as supervised pending audit review.

At three-forty Celia sent a one-line instruction: OFFICE. NOW.

Mira went in carrying nothing.

Celia had removed her jacket. Without it the office looked slightly more honest. The sleeves of her blouse were rolled once, exposing forearms that had long ago belonged to somebody who lifted boxes, not just papers. The official summary Mira had pulled from archives lay on the desk with notes in the margin and one page circled so hard the pen had nearly cut through.

"Sit."

Mira sat.

"Internal compliance has opened an employee review attached to the external inquiry," Celia said. "You're the named staff subject for the disposal chain variance. Congratulations on becoming administratively interesting."

Mira let the word subject settle. There it was, formal and ugly and clean enough to print.

"Are you suspending me?"

"Not today."

"Tomorrow?"

"That depends on what else I learn tonight."

Celia picked up the fountain pen, rotated it once, set it down again. Her arthritic hand had started to swell at the knuckles; the skin looked shiny over the joints. She was angry enough to hurt.

"Your archive summary is too careful," she said. "Records also sent up the bay-three exception you threw under my requisition."

Mira kept her face blank. "You asked for careful."

"I asked for useful. These route overrides shouldn't have been visible on a claims pull, and yet here they are, trimmed into a polite four-page packet with every dangerous implication sanded off. Either you saw more than you gave me or you did not understand what you were looking at. Which embarrasses me less?"

The building hummed around them. Mira could hear a vacuum somewhere in the hall, the night cleaning crew starting early around the conference rooms because compliance hated dust almost as much as it hated witnesses. The wafer had warmed slightly in her shoe from body heat. It felt alive now, which was ridiculous. It was only storage.

"You told me narrow and supportable," Mira said.

"Do not hide inside my instructions."

Celia leaned forward. "Look at me."

Mira did.

"If this is ordinary employee stupidity, I can still route it. If this is outside work with a tissue operator, I need to know before the bureau arrives with seizure forms. If this is something stranger, and I assure you I'm not too old to imagine stranger, then you need to decide very quickly whether you want me learning it from you or from a man who thinks courtesy is a warrant."

For one wild second Mira thought Celia knew about Lin. She did not think Celia had guessed. She thought Celia knew. She felt her own body make the decision it always made under pressure: not confession, not collapse, but detail.

"The route overrides aren't normal," she said. "That's all I know."

"That is not all you know."

"It's all I can prove."

That made Celia stop, not because Mira had become suddenly eloquent but because proof was the dialect Celia trusted even when she hated it.

"Can prove to whom?" Celia asked.

Mira said nothing.

Celia sat back slowly. "You should have come to me earlier."

"Would that have helped?"

"For me? Certainly."

The honesty in it was ugly enough to be useful. Celia did not mean help you. She meant help me. It almost made Mira laugh.

"Go home," Celia said after a long moment.

Mira blinked. "You just told me not to leave the building without clearing it."

"I'm clearing it. Your access is frozen for the evening. You will report directly to my office at eight tomorrow. You will not contact records. You will not contact disposal. You will not contact any external repair personnel if such people happen to exist in your social life. And, Ms. Alves" - Celia's voice lowered, roughened just enough to show the old neighborhood under the polish - "if you've done something that can still be contained, tonight is the hour for understanding that containment is a finite resource."

Mira stood.

"Is that advice?" she asked.

"No. It's class solidarity badly deformed by management."

That almost did make Mira laugh, which would have been a mistake. She nodded once instead and turned for the door.

"One more thing," Celia said.

Mira looked back.

"Security's checking bags, pockets, and badge clips on exit. That's audit protocol. If you were considering any romantic notions about saving yourself with a thumb drive, you are too late."

Mira's left foot went cold inside the shoe. She made herself shrug.

"I don't own anything that romantic."

"No," Celia said. "You don't."

The lobby had changed shifts. The young guard with glitter in her eyebrow was gone. In her place stood an older man with nicotine-stained fingers and a cuff of careful hand-stitched embroidery peeking from under his uniform sleeve, tiny orange flowers on black thread. He inspected bags the way a tired uncle might inspect spoiled food: thoroughly, without appetite. Mira set hers on the table, unclipped her badge when he asked, and turned out her pockets. Sandwich wrapper, work notes, a cracked lip balm, a tram card gone soft at one corner, the inquiry notice folded twice, the badge still warm from her blouse. He turned each thing over and handed it back.

"You can keep the balm," he said. "We're not monsters."

His voice held a rasp from old smoking and maybe choir practice. Mira almost thanked him for the joke. Then Yaret stepped through the side door from the administrative corridor and the lobby shrank around the sight of him.

He was alone now, coat over one arm, expression unchanged from the conference room. He saw Mira at the table, saw the open bag, saw the guard's hands in it. If he found any satisfaction in the scene, he kept it private.

"Ms. Alves," he said. "We may need another conversation tomorrow."

"I'm touched."

"Do not destroy anything tonight."

"You already made me sign the part where I don't."

"Signatures help some people remember."

He said it mildly. He could have been discussing weatherproofing.

The guard zipped Mira's bag and returned it. "All set."

Mira slung it over her shoulder. The wafer bit when she shifted her weight. She did not limp. Years of rent had trained her in more important disguises than that.

Outside, the canal wind came hard around the annex corners and shoved filtered office air out of her lungs. Through the glass lobby wall she could still see Yaret speaking to the guard, Celia crossing the corridor beyond with a file tucked to her side, everyone arranged inside the building's white light like figures in a refrigerated case. Tomorrow at eight she was meant to walk back into the badge and the desk and the questions, with the job continuing long enough to kill whatever it touched.

She started uphill toward Brine Court with the stolen wafer hidden in her shoe and the inquiry notice in her bag, one hard proof and one official threat. Every step reminded her they were both real.

By the time she reached the tram stop she knew something simple and expensive: home was uphill, but Blue Hour was down by the canal, bleach steam caught in the back room by closing time and the kind of help that never called itself help. The wafer bit her foot again. When the next tram lights bent around the track, Mira turned away from the platform. CommonFrame had stopped being a workplace she could use. It was evidence storage with her name already on the shelf.
