# Chapter 2

By Tuesday afternoon the appeals room smelled like filtered air losing the fight. Solvent and coolant still came first, the clean corporate lie, but under it the damaged bargain frame on cradle three kept giving off the sweet edge of warmed tissue, sugar left too long on a burner. On Mira's monitor a yellow bar counted down twenty-six minutes until provisional liability posted to the renter's ledger.

Nidia Solis sat across from her in a housekeeping polo the color of weak tea, the hotel name stitched over one breast in gold thread already gone gray with bleach. Her real wrists were strapped in pharmacy braces that had been tightened with safety pins from the tiny sewing kits nicer guests left unopened beside minibars. One of those kits had spilled in her lap while she dug for paperwork. The spill held black thread, white thread, and two pearl buttons the size of aspirin. She had lined them up in a row on Mira's desk without seeming to notice she was doing it.

"I told intake the shoulder was wrong when I picked it up," she said. "It clicked before I got to the tram."

Mira rotated the scan. The bargain body on the cradle was female-coded, medium build, house line musculature, the right arm hanging away from the torso with the sloppy give of a torn support web. Fresh damage glowed angry on the upper layers. Beneath it, buried where a pickup booth camera never would have caught it, an older band of puckered matrix ran around the socket like a healed burn.

If she submitted the scan as it was, the system would do what it always did with undocumented older damage. It would turn the body's history into the renter's fault.

"The pickup attendant note says no visible impairment at release," Mira said. Her public voice came out dry and almost courteous, like she was offering an umbrella instead of the sentence. "Did you sign the acceptance screen?"

Nidia laughed once. It was not amusement. "The one that's already signed when they hand you the stylus? Yeah. I signed that."

On the monitor the pricing ladder waited behind a red triangle. Mira knew the categories by touch now: preexisting substrate irregularity, undisclosed structural anomaly, unauthorized load stress if the system decided the renter had asked too much of leased flesh printed for cheaper work than the work it got used for. Every choice had a dollar amount tucked behind it like a knife folded into a sleeve.

She touched the older scar band on the scan, zoomed in, and let the software estimate age. The answer came back broad enough to protect the company and specific enough to hurt the poor: prior to current rental window, not verifiable to manufacturer release state.

Not verifiable meant billable.

Nidia watched Mira's face the way people watched weather rolling toward laundry strung outside. "Just tell me the number."

Mira clicked through the preliminary fee tree because not clicking through it did not make it less real. The figures populated in stacked white boxes.

Frame retrieval and bench intake: $410.

Joint-lattice reconstruction, estimate only: $1,160.

Tissue contamination scrub: $275.

Manual provenance review, customer-contestable: $340.

Service interruption penalty and temporary account suspension: $1,480.

Security deposit forfeiture pending adjudication: $300.

Provisional total customer liability: $3,965.

The number sat there in clean corporate sans-serif, almost tasteful. Mira made about a quarter of that in a week if payroll had not found a new reason to shave deductions. Brine Court would have loved that number. Brine Court would have sent flowers if she ever came up with that much at once.

Nidia closed her eyes. Her mouth kept moving a second after the rest of her face had gone still, as if she was finishing some private argument. "That's for one shoulder."

"That's before collections fees if it goes to claims court."

"Jesus Christ."

Mira let the silence sit. There was nothing humane to pour into it. Behind the glass wall another adjuster was explaining contamination surcharges to a man in a marina jacket while the man smiled the frightened smile of somebody determined to stay agreeable in front of a machine that could ruin him. The room's white-noise emitters hushed and hissed. From deeper in the annex came the clack of cradle locks and the soft wet slap of a returned body being turned for scan.

Nidia opened her eyes again. They were bloodshot in the corners and bright with the kind of fatigue that had got baked in overnight, not from one bad morning. "If I ask for review, does that stop it?"

It stopped nothing. It froze the debt long enough to wrap it in more language.

Mira kept her hands flat on the desk because if she started rubbing at her cuticles she would look as tired as she felt. "It pauses final posting for up to forty-eight hours. It also creates an adjudication file. There's a fee attached."

"Another fee."

"Yes."

"And then what?"

"Then they look at release records, shift class, strain pattern, maintenance history." Mira heard herself using the house nouns and hated how smooth they sounded. "If they find manufacturer defect, coverage applies. If they don't, the charge stands."

Nidia looked past Mira at the frame on the cradle. Its face was slack and beautiful in the budget way CommonFrame did beautiful, broad symmetry stamped out to survive strangers. Dried disinfectant had left a milky line along the neck port. "And while they look, I can't book another one."

That was not a question. Mira checked the side panel anyway because ritual mattered in rooms like this. The account flag showed bargain line and labor-class user. She was right.

"Your access would suspend during review."

"So I can't work."

"Not through CommonFrame."

Nidia breathed in through her nose and then stopped because the appeals room had no air worth taking. "I clean rooms at the Saint Meridian. Twelve floors if we're short. Fourteen if the convention people leave early and management gets greedy. I don't mean I supervise the cleaners. I mean I strip sheets and scrub the glass showers where rich assholes shave and leave little gray hairs around the drain like they're doing you a favor. I need the rentals because these" - she lifted both braced wrists a little off her lap - "these won't hold a spray bottle by hour three."

The turn came out plain. It came with no performance and no plea, just the fact laid on the desk between the thread cards and the fee boxes.

Mira had asked hundreds of renters what happened to the frame. Most of them lied from vanity, panic, or habit. Nidia looked like she had run out of spare invention somewhere around dawn.

"Show me," Mira said before she could decide whether she should.

Nidia's mouth tightened. Humiliation moved over her face in a shallow wave, quick and visible. Then she put the sewing kit aside and worried at the safety pins one by one with clumsy fingertips. The braces loosened. Her wrists underneath were narrow in the ugly way repeated pain made things narrow. The skin at the base of both thumbs was shiny and overworked. Old steroid injection bruises bloomed green-brown along the inside of one forearm. When she turned her hands over, Mira saw the slight wrong angle in the right wrist where something had set and kept setting itself badly.

"Tendon burn first," Nidia said. "Then the tunnel shit. Then a manager who liked to tell us everybody's body hurts if they want to stay employed. I can still fold napkins. I can still do receipts. I can't wring out a rag and I sure as hell can't lift wet linen all day. So I lease." She nodded toward the rental frame. "That one was my Tuesday-through-Thursday body. Ten-hour housekeeping class. I was on eleven when the arm gave. I had both hands under a king duvet and then I didn't."

Mira looked back at the older damage buried in the scan. It showed chronic strain and scar tissue that had printed over bad substrate or old injury. The shoulder had been compromised before Nidia ever signed the stylus. CommonFrame had sold her three days of borrowed strength with a break already hidden inside it.

"Did you report the clicking at pickup?"

"I told the kid in the booth the arm sounded cheap."

"Did he log it?"

Nidia gave her a look. "He told me they all sound cheap until they warm up."

Of course he had. A note at release would have made the frame harder to bill later.

The yellow countdown bar slid another notch to the right. Twenty-one minutes.

Mira clicked open the release-booth retention policy anyway, because there were still forms a person had to perform belief in before they stopped believing. Standard pickup footage for bargain labor lines was kept seventy-two hours absent incident escalation. Nidia's rental had released four days ago. Even if it had still existed, review would take longer than a woman with dead wrists and hotel floors to clean could afford.

"If I fight it in court?" Nidia asked.

Mira pictured Municipal Arbitration Hall in the civic core, all polished stone and water stations and seats designed to look generous while they ground a day of wages out of anyone who sat in them. She pictured Nidia there in her tea-colored polo, wrists strapped, taking a number beneath screens that called her liability exposure a neutral matter.

"You'd need the release footage, your shift logs, any complaint made at pickup, maybe a third-party tissue read if you wanted to challenge their age estimate."

"How much is that?"

"More than you want it to be."

Nidia leaned back. The vinyl chair gave a small farting sigh under her. "You people always say it soft."

Mira almost said, We get trained to. What came out was, "I know."

Nidia looked down at the row of tiny thread cards and pushed the black one out of line with a fingernail. "The funny thing is I like the high floors. Everybody thinks I like the tips. It's the windows. You get a slice of the harbor between towers if the weather's clear. Sometimes the ferries look like toys from up there. I rent for the windows as much as the money. My own body's five-two. You spend all day in a service corridor at that height, you start thinking the city forgot to build any sky."

There it was, the detail that had nothing to do with leverage and everything to do with being alive. Mira took it in like something she had no right to keep.

She rechecked the shoulder. The older matrix band had the dense pearled look she had started noticing too often lately, damage or memory or growth trouble nobody wanted to call by a manufacturer name. She thought of the held model on shelf C, the undocumented scar under pale ribs, the image copy hidden in her locker. The company line had already cracked in one place. Now here it was again, except this time the crack had a hotel laundry card clipped to it and a woman's wrists tied up in cheap elastic.

"If I don't sign anything," Nidia said, "what happens at twenty minutes?"

"The file moves to provisional capture. Your deposit goes. Your account suspends. Collections mail follows if you don't settle or request review."

"And if I request review, I still lose the account."

"During review."

"Which means I still lose the job."

Mira did not answer. She did not have an office way to say yes that did not sound like a pamphlet.

Outside, somebody laughed too loud in the intake queue and got immediately shushed by a receptionist. The sound came through the glass flattened and embarrassed. The appeals room was built to make everyone lower their voice. Pain, debt, and sex residue all had to pass through it at conversational volume.

Nidia pulled the braces back on with slow, ugly effort. Mira watched her fail to close one strap, then watched her use her teeth on it. That decided more than the numbers had.

She clicked away from the fee tree before the system could autosave the preliminary valuation. "Don't sign the liability consent."

Nidia's eyes narrowed. "You saying that because you're kind or because there's some other trap."

"Because once it posts cleanly, I can't slow it without making it worse."

"Slow it how."

Mira opened the manual exception menu. Technical review. Bench re-evaluation. Tissue aging variance. All of them came with audit hooks. She chose the one that bought the most time while sounding the dullest.

`Request: additional substrate aging analysis prior to submission.`

The system asked for justification.

She typed: `Age spread inconsistent with visible tear. Requires local bench comparison before adjudication.`

That was true, which made it easier to lie with.

"This gives me until morning," she said. "Maybe less if somebody upstairs gets ambitious."

"And in the morning?"

Mira clicked confirm and felt the file move from easy corporate certainty into a folder with her name on it. "In the morning I need the shoulder to stop looking like this."

Nidia stared at her. "You got a miracle drawer under there, Ms. Alves?"

Mira almost smiled. It felt wrong in the room and stayed mostly private. "No."

She closed the scan, printed the tissue layers to old paper because paper still moved through offices quieter than data, and slid the sheets face down into a generic intake folder. Her own reflection flickered in the black of the monitor between windows, dark hair tied back too tight, mole near the temple, mouth already set for whatever bad idea came next.

She had not called Tavi Sloan in three years because a claims adjuster keeping a black-market repairer's number was the kind of stupidity that deserved unemployment. The number still sat in the analog pocket of her wallet, written on the back of a tram receipt by a dock clerk whose illegal knee patch had fooled intake long enough to save him from bankruptcy. Mira had kept it because curiosity and class fear often lived in the same place. You did not throw away exits just because you were still pretending to be indoors.

"Can you get to South Canal after eight?" she asked.

Nidia blinked. "For what?"

"For a second look."

"By who?"

"Don't ask me anything yet that makes me answer out loud."

That got Nidia's attention in a way the fee tree had not. Her face sharpened, pain and suspicion lining up instead of fighting each other. "How much?"

"Bring whatever cash you can stand to lose. Not cards. Cash."

"You're serious."

Mira looked at the damaged shoulder on the cradle. She looked at the older scar the company would call unverifiable until it had billed a cleaner for its own manufacturing cheat. "More than I should be."

Nidia's throat worked. "If this is some kind of setup-"

"Then you've already got the wrong shoes for it." Mira nodded at the foam slippers peeking from under the chair, patterned with faded cherries. They were clean on top and grimed at the soles, house slippers doing street work because there was no spare pair. "Be at South Canal and Mercer. Blue sign that says laundry. Eight-thirty. If I'm not there by nine, go home and don't answer any collection call until you hear my voice first."

Nidia searched her face like she was checking an unlabeled bottle for poison by smell alone. "Why?"

The honest answer had too many moving parts already. The useful answer was smaller.

"Because that shoulder is older than your contract and they know how to hide that in the wording," Mira said. "And because if I send this up clean, review will turn your wrists into a debt instrument by breakfast."

Nidia let out a breath that shook once at the end. "That's a real sentence."

"I know."

She gathered the spilled sewing-kit pieces with awkward braces and tucked them back into her tote. Before she stood she hesitated, looking again at the rental frame on the cradle.

"If somebody can fix it," she said, "don't make it fancy. I just need it to lift linen. Fancy always breaks faster."

Mira nodded. The line landed harder than anything else had. The poor did not ask for miracles. They asked for the thing to hold through the week.

After Nidia left, the appeals room seemed colder because there was no longer anyone in it trying not to look humiliated. Mira stood with the intake folder under one hand and the timer on her screen running silently toward tomorrow's trouble. Across the annex, cradles moved in steady sequence toward wash, pulp, resale. Claims was not the place where the city's body economy became cruel. Claims was the place where cruelty got grammar.

She slipped the printout into her bag, logged the frame to overnight local review, and spent the rest of her shift hearing the hotel's high floors in the back of her head whenever somebody said premium view surcharge or elevated leisure package or any other phrase that turned somebody else's sky into a line item.

At seven fifty-eight, with the office thinning and the canal outside going the color of old cutlery, she signed the damaged frame out of the bench queue under a waste-transfer code she had no right to use.

The body was heavier than its file.

* * *

South Canal after dark was all wet light and bad optimism. The market stalls had half-shuttered. Fish scales silvered the curb drains. A tram rattled over the viaduct and left the air trembling behind it. Mira pushed the laundry cart one-handed because the other hand needed to look casual near her work badge, as if casual could still be manufactured after you had rolled company property out of a service elevator under a false disposal tag.

The bargain frame lay zipped into a blue transport shroud under a layer of stained towels stolen from the annex rag bins. Warm night air made the plastic sweat. Each time the cart hit a crack, the body shifted with a blunt human weight that no invoice ever fully admitted to. Mira had spent years treating returned frames as units because that was the job. Off company flooring, with street grit jumping under the wheels and fryer grease drifting over from a stall closing down, unit stopped meaning anything useful.

Nidia was already waiting under the blue neon laundry sign, sitting on the concrete planter because her wrists would not let her stand comfortably for long. She had changed out of the housekeeping polo into a thin gray tank and a cardigan with one elbow darned in yellow thread. The cherry slippers were gone, replaced by sneakers whose toes had split wide enough to show sock. She held a paper cup of cafecito in both hands like the heat alone was worth the trip.

"Thought you might not come," she said when Mira reached her.

"I thought about it."

"That's encouraging as hell."

Mira looked up at the sign. `BLUE HOUR LAUNDRY` buzzed unevenly, the `R` half-dead. Steam fogged the lower windows. Behind the glass, rows of dryers turned like slow metal eyes. She had only been in once, years ago, to wash everything she owned after a pipe leak in Brine Court left the apartment smelling like drowned plaster.

"You bring cash?"

Nidia held up a white envelope, embarrassed before Mira even touched it. "Two hundred twelve. I sold the salon card I'd been saving. And my manager still owes me for the extra weekend turn."

It was not enough for a legal shop. It might not be enough for an illegal one with sense.

"Keep it ready," Mira said.

She took the side stair instead of the laundry entrance. The narrow passage smelled of detergent, mildew, and an old frying smell embedded in plaster. Dryers thumped through the wall, making the handrail vibrate under her palm. At the top landing a battery radio muttered harbor weather through static from behind a metal door. Mira knocked once with the side of her fist.

Nobody answered.

She knocked again.

"If that's religion, fuck off," a man's voice called from inside. "If it's rent, I need a minute to respect my own poverty."

Nidia's mouth twitched despite herself. Mira hit the door with the heel of her hand. "Tavi Sloan?"

There was a beat of silence, then the scrape of a lock. The door opened two inches on a chain.

Tavi's flattened nose appeared first, then one dark eye narrowed on Mira, then on the cart behind her, then on the CommonFrame badge clipped at her waist. He had a dish towel over one shoulder and violet antiseptic smudged across the long fingers gripping the door. The room behind him was close and bright, half living space, half bench. A portable wetbench squatted under the window. Ceramic egg cups full of screws and capped syringes lined the sill beside two basil cuttings in cloudy water. The harbor-weather radio sat on a shelf with its volume turned low, still giving tide advisories to nobody who needed them.

"No," he said, and started to shut the door.

Mira caught it with her palm before the chain could bite fully. "I've got a shoulder tear that predates release."

"Then call your supervisor and enjoy your career."

"They'll bill the renter four grand by morning."

"What do you want from me, a parade?"

His eye flicked again to her badge. Suspicion sharpened everything in his face. He was lean the way bench people got lean, bent a little from years spent over surfaces that never rose high enough. A shiny old burn scar rode high on his right forearm. He had not shaved closely, and his gray T-shirt was freckled with bleach. He had the kind of body that looked accidental until it started moving with purpose.

"I know about the triple-anchor elbow on Raul Estevez last month," Mira said. "Claims couldn't clock it, but the stitch tension gave you away."

Tavi stared at her.

Behind Mira, Nidia said, "I'm not with the cops."

"That wasn't my first guess," he said.

"Good."

He looked past Mira properly then, taking in Nidia's braced wrists, the envelope, the cart. His mouth shifted, not softening but recalculating.

"Open the bag," he said.

Mira unzipped the shroud. Warm synthetic skin showed under the towels. The busted shoulder slumped outward, ugly even in laundry-neon blue.

Tavi swore, quiet and technical. He unhooked the chain and pulled the door wide. "Get it inside before somebody with a moral life comes upstairs."

The room smelled of steam, instant coffee, and warmed sterilant. One half held a narrow bed, a hotplate, a stack of chipped bowls, and a shirt drying from the shower rod. The other half belonged to the wetbench: clamps, sealant, a secondhand tissue printer head stripped to its useful parts, lamps on articulated arms, a cooler humming under the table. Dryers below made the floor pulse in a patient rhythm.

Tavi cleared a tray with one sweep of his forearm. "On the bench. Feet first. No, not like it's furniture. The socket's already hanging by prayer and fraud."

He said it to Mira, not unkindly, which irritated her more than if he had snapped. Together they lifted the frame from the cart to the bench. The shoulder shifted under the skin with a nauseating internal slide. Nidia winced even though she was not the one feeling it.

"You see that?" Tavi said, glancing at her. "That face you're making is correct, by the way. Always a good sign when the customer starts from reality."

"I clean hotel bathrooms," Nidia said. "Reality and I are close."

"Beautiful. Stay that way."

He pulled on gloves, then thought better of it and changed to a different pair from a drawer labeled `less contaminated`. Mira watched him work because there was no point pretending she had not come for exactly this competence. He palpated around the shoulder, pressed two fingers into the matrix line, bent close enough to smell the tissue, then snapped for light.

"Hold that lamp," he said.

Mira held it.

He sliced the synthetic skin open along the shoulder seam with a microblade so used it had lost any vanity about being sterile. Under the skin, pale gel filmed the torn support web. Fresh damage had bled clear print medium into the cavity. Beneath that, just where her scan had shown it, an older ring of dense white matrix clung around the socket like candle wax hardened on metal.

"There," Mira said.

"Yeah, no shit, there." Tavi angled the torn fibers apart with long steady fingers. "Fresh rupture on top of old starve. Somebody printed over a bad growth collar and sent it out anyway."

Nidia made a small sound in the back of her throat. "In English."

"In English, they sold you a shoulder that was already half cooked."

Mira looked at him. "You can tell from this."

He gave her a side glance that held amusement and warning together. "I can tell because my eyes work."

He probed deeper, careful now. "See the grain on the matrix? Cheap aggregate. They stretched it too thin at the socket. Then it got loaded overhead for a while before release or in somebody else's shift class, hard to say. Tear was waiting. Your cleaner finished the argument."

Mira heard the phrase before release and stored it. "Can you make it pass a rescan?"

"Pass intake or last a month."

"Intake first."

"Honest answer. Concerning."

He peeled more skin back. The cavity opened like a flower nobody had wanted. Warmed gel and printer sweet drifted into the room, mixing with detergent from below until Mira could not separate cleanliness from rot anymore. She had handled worse at CommonFrame, but there the trays and barcodes made a screen between her and the fact of touch. Here there was only Tavi's bench, Nidia watching with her bad wrists folded to her ribs, and Mira holding light over a corporate body's opened shoulder while harbor weather muttered from the shelf.

"How much?" Nidia asked.

Tavi did not look up. "How much do you have?"

She held out the envelope.

He took it one-handed, thumbed the bills, snorted. "This wouldn't cover the sterilant if I billed like a grown-up."

Nidia's face shut down. "Then say no."

Tavi finally looked at her. Some of the performance went out of him. "If I say no, does that shoulder get younger on the walk home?"

"No."

"Right."

He tossed the envelope onto the tray without counting again. "Sit down before you fall over and make me responsible for two bodies."

Nidia sat on the only chair. The chair complained under her. She tucked her hands between her knees to hide them.

Tavi pointed with his chin at Mira's badge. "You're the bigger problem. How'd a live company adjuster find my door?"

"You weren't hard to find if you knew what repair patterns to look for."

"That's almost flattering." He pressed a clamp into Mira's hand. "Hold this retracted. Not there, unless your plan is to make me dislike you on purpose."

She adjusted. He nodded once.

"I found you because review would bury her," Mira said. "And because the damage is older than the contract."

"That happens."

"Not supposed to."

"Cute." He threaded a filament anchor through the torn support web. "You still saying 'supposed to' like it buys groceries."

Mira felt the old habit rise, the trained office answer that made damage sound processable. She did not give it air. "Can you fix it or not?"

"I'm fixing it."

"Then don't waste time talking."

His mouth twitched. "That's more like it."

The repair got ugly fast. He cut out the worst of the pearled matrix, not all of it because there was no time, only the sections that would keep the joint from seating. The old tissue came away in rubbery white curls. He cursed the whole bargain line, the supplier, the printer head, then himself for still being surprised by any of it. Mira held retractors, suctioned clear gel, passed tools when he named them. Her work at the annex had taught her where structures should lie. Tavi's bench taught her how little the official shop respected that knowledge once it belonged to an unlicensed room and a woman with solvent on her cuffs.

"You've done this on dead runs before," he said without looking at her.

"Not like this."

"No, at work you people get fifteen forms and a trash chute."

"At work we get approved parts."

"Sure. And somehow the shoulders still come out like boiled weeds."

Nidia laughed in spite of herself. It cracked halfway through, but it was a laugh.

Tavi glanced up at her. "Good. Keep doing that. If I have to work in tragic silence, I charge more."

"You already said I couldn't afford you."

"Nobody can afford me. I'm impossible on purpose."

He set a salvage lattice patch into the cleaned gap and warmed it with a narrow-field emitter until the material softened. The smell changed, sweeter and uglier. Mira thought suddenly of carnival almonds burning in a paper cone when she was eight, standing on the seawall with Gabe and their father before all three of them learned what money did to a household. The memory arrived not as sentiment but as insult. Even good smells got stolen by this job eventually.

"There," Tavi said. "Now we persuade it to behave."

He reseated the shoulder with both hands. The socket gave a wet thunk and settled. Mira felt the impact through the retractor handles. Nidia's sneakers squeaked against the floor.

"Did it work?" she asked.

"Depends what standard you're worshipping."

He ran a low pulse through the support web. Muscles in the inert arm shivered. The repaired joint held. He rotated it once, twice, overhead. It wasn't pretty, but it was serviceable.

"For housekeeping?" he said. "It'll do if nobody asks it to become a hero."

Nidia looked at the moving arm the way people in the annex looked at cleared balances. It wasn't relief exactly. It looked more like a temporary pardon.

Mira set the suction aside. "How long?"

"If it goes back into normal pool use, maybe a few rental windows, maybe less. If somebody sends it into load class above what it's printed for, it'll tear again and then they'll blame a renter because that's what they were going to do anyway." He peeled off one glove with his teeth and pointed the bare finger at the open shoulder. "This part here, the old collar, that's factory or pre-release strain. Don't let anybody tell you a cleaner did that in three days."

"They already did."

"Of course they did."

He sealed the skin with quick practiced work, adhesive brush and heat, then buffed over the line until only a faint pink seam remained. Mira had seen licensed technicians take longer to do worse.

"Why are you helping?" she asked before she could stop herself.

He barked a laugh. "Because the money's insulting, the hour's bad, and your badge is giving me hives. Why do you think?"

"I think you hate them."

"That narrows the city to what, half a million people?"

He stripped the bench paper and bundled the old matrix scraps into a waste jar. "Also because she's a cleaner with wrists made of receipt paper and you're standing in my room with a CommonFrame shell under a fake disposal tag. That's not a kindness errand. That's a market signal."

Mira looked at him.

He looked back, finally direct. The humor stayed, but there was counting under it. He was counting risk, supply, leverage, and future need. "You people in claims see the bodies before the debt settles. Before the wording hardens. That's worth more than two hundred and twelve dollars."

Nidia said, "I can get more. Not tonight. Friday maybe."

"Keep Friday. Buy food." He waved that off. "This one is tuition."

Mira did not like the word. "I'm not joining anything."

"Relax. I don't have a club. Clubs get raided. I'm saying if you're going to keep having moral emergencies, have them somewhere useful."

He tossed her a clean cloth. "Wipe the shoulder. No puddles. Intake scanners love puddles. They think shine means honesty."

She wiped. The skin was warm and slightly tacky where the seal had set. Under her cloth the repaired joint felt solid enough to fool a fast morning bench. That fact thrilled and sickened her in equal measure. She was not rescuing the body. She was preparing it to go back into the same system that had printed damage into it and assigned the bill to whoever was poorest nearby. The only life being saved tonight was Nidia's access to labor.

That was not nothing.

It also was not clean.

Tavi saw some part of the thought on her face. "Yeah," he said. "Welcome."

Nidia stood and came closer, slow because she did not trust herself around exposed benches. "Can I use it tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow no," Mira said before Tavi could. "Tomorrow it goes back through claims as serviceable and your account clears. After that, if booking lets you pull the same line again, maybe."

"Maybe."

"Maybe," Tavi echoed. "Don't get romantic about rented meat. It'll break your heart and your budget both."

Nidia looked at him. "You always talk like this."

"Only when I'm trying to charm people."

That got the smallest smile out of her yet. It changed her whole face and then was gone.

Mira checked the time. If she got the frame back into the annex before the first bench cycle, she could rescan it under local review and close the file before anyone asked why a cleaner's shoulder had spent the night aging differently. If she missed that window, the false disposal code became a felony with fluorescent lighting.

"I need to go."

Tavi braced both hands on the wetbench and looked from the repaired frame to Mira to the badge clipped at her waist as if he were choosing which of the three annoyed him most. "Then hear me once so I don't have to repeat myself prettily later."

She waited.

"Cash next time," he said. "Real cash, not apology money. Cases before they hit formal review if you want any room to work. And silence. My name stays out of your office mouth, out of your messages, out of whatever loyal little part of you still thinks procedure can save you. If you bring me a Bureau tail or a company audit, I'll know before you finish climbing the stairs, and then you can take your ethical awakening somewhere else."

There it was, plain enough to build on. It wasn't trust. They were terms.

"That's not a partnership," Mira said.

"No," he said. "It's a warranty."

Nidia, watching both of them, muttered, "Jesus, you two flirt like debt collectors."

Neither of them answered, which made her snort into the back of her hand.

Mira pulled her wallet from her bag, took out the folded notes she had set aside for Brine Court's impossible partial payment, and added them to Nidia's envelope on the tray. Tavi looked down at the new stack, then at her.

"You don't have to do that," he said.

"I know."

"Rent's funnier when it bounces, but not that funny."

"Take it."

He studied her another second, then nodded once and pocketed the envelope without ceremony. "Fine. Now get the shell out before the dryers finish their cycle and Jo comes up to ask why my floor smells like shoulder."

Mira paused with her hands on the cart. "Jo."

"Landlady. Laundry monarch. If she catches you in the stairwell with corporate flesh, lie better than most people do."

Mira noted that.

She and Nidia lowered the repaired frame back into the transport shroud. This time the arm lay close to the torso like it belonged there. Tavi zipped the bag himself, then peeled off his gloves and reached over the radio to tear a strip from a receipt roll. He wrote a number on it.

"Burner changes every week," he said. "Use cash kiosks if you message. If you say anything stupid, I stop answering."

Mira took the paper. The numbers were quick and sharp, a mechanic's handwriting disguised as a doctor's.

"Why give me this now?" she asked.

Tavi turned back to his bench, already wiping it down. "Because you were willing to wheel a tagged company body through canal market for a cleaner you barely know. That's either conscience, rage, or the beginning of market discipline. Any of the three might be useful."

"And if it's stupidity."

"Then you'll fit right in."

By the time they got back to CommonFrame, the eastern sky had started thinking about morning. The annex loading dock was lit in the ugly blue-white spectrum designed to make everyone look already investigated. Mira badged through with the cart, heart beating not in her chest but in the thin plastic edge of Sloan's burner number where it pressed against her palm. Nidia waited out of camera range in the alley, smoking half a cigarette down to the filter because her wrists would not let her waste the second half.

Inside, the night bench was skeleton staffed. A porter waved without really seeing her. Mira rolled the shrouded frame to cradle three, reattached the local review tag, and unzipped the bag with hands steady enough to pass. The repaired shoulder looked dull, almost bored. Under the scanner it held alignment. The old matrix age signature was still there, but now without the spectacular tear to force escalation, it could be classed what it probably had been called in a dozen other files: non-billable substrate variance, internal maintenance.

The annex had a category for every lie it preferred.

Mira selected `serviceable after local bench reset`, then `manufacturer absorb - no renter action established`. The system asked for a note. She gave it one sentence and kept it small.

`Observed age inconsistency did not materially contribute to final service condition after local review.`

It was true enough to get past a sleepy supervisor and false enough to matter.

She hit submit.

For one second nothing happened. The cursor spun. Somewhere in the annex a wash fan kicked on with a long inhaling roar. Mira could hear her own breath in the respirator grille over her mouth, too loud, too human for the room.

Then the file closed clean. Liability field blanked white. Account suspension removed. Deposit release queued. Nidia Solis returned to zero.

Mira stood there with the scanner wand still in her hand and solvent drying silver in the cracks around her nails. The repaired frame moved on toward wash, then reissue, then somebody else's booked need. She had not fixed the company. She had not saved the body. She had pulled one woman out from under the number and sent the damaged product back into circulation wearing better skin.

When she stepped outside to the alley, Nidia was waiting by the dock gate, cigarette gone, eyes raw from no sleep.

"Well?" she said.

Mira held up her screen so she could see the cleared balance. There was no amount and no suspension banner, just the plain closed file.

Nidia stared at it. "That's it."

"That's it for now."

Nidia covered her mouth with both hands, braces and all. The sound that came out was not a sob exactly. It was smaller and stranger than that, like her body had forgotten what to do when something stopped hurting on time.

"I can work tomorrow," she said into her palms.

"Yes."

"Fuck."

Mira almost told her not to thank anybody. Instead she said, "Go home. Sleep if you can. If they call about anything, you tell them the file closed at local review and you ask for the note number. Nothing else."

Nidia nodded hard. Then, because people needed dignity even in alleys, she straightened and said, "The windows on fourteen are better after rain. You can see all the way to the ferry line."

Mira did not ask why she was telling her that now. She knew. It was the only gift Nidia had handy that wasn't cash.

"Good," Mira said. "Keep the sky."

Nidia left toward the tram with her tote bumping her hip and her bad wrists held close. Mira watched until she was gone, then looked down at the scrap of receipt paper in her own hand.

It was Sloan's number.

The terms were cash, cases, silence.

She folded it once and tucked it behind her work badge, where company policy said nothing personal belonged, then went back inside carrying the wetbench smell with her.
