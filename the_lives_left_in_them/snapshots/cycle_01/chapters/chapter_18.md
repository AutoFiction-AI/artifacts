# Chapter 18

By midwinter the old ferry terminal smelled less like abandonment than broth, saline, wet wool, and the hot-metal tang of space heaters working above their pay grade. Salt had eaten lacework into the ticket grilles. The terrazzo floor stayed cold no matter how many rugs Jo scavenged and taped down. But the place no longer sounded empty. It clicked and coughed and argued. Gabe had coaxed one strip of the dead departure board back to life, and every few minutes the plastic letters flipped from INTAKE to SUTURES or BEDS or PORT CLEANING with the same hard clatter that used to send commuters toward the bay. Mira liked the noise more than she trusted herself to admit. It meant people expected the room to go on existing.

She stood behind the old information counter with a clipboard, triage stamps hanging from a shoelace at her wrist, and watched the morning line organize itself into something almost civilized. Wet coats steamed by the doorway. A man in a rented loading frame tried not to limp on a knee that belonged to three different shifts of other people. A student in a bargain nightlife shell kept pressing two fingers to her throat because every time Jo laughed the borrowed larynx answered in a baritone she swore was not hers. Somebody had left a sack of turnips by the fee jar. Somebody else had left twelve dollars, two tram cards, and a zip bag of sterile clamps. The money tin and the produce crate sat side by side now. That was the cooperative in its purest form: enough cash to buy gauze, enough food that nobody fainted in line, never quite enough of either.

Gabe occupied the stool inside the glass dispatch booth like a man who had been born to gossip through cracked municipal equipment. His lumbar brace made a blocky shelf under his sweatshirt, and the orange knit cap one of the night volunteers had made him sat crooked over one ear. He had a harbor-radio earpiece hooked to his collar even though half the terminal could hear him without electronics. A grease pencil rode behind his ear beside a hotel matchbook he kept for luck and never used. When he saw Mira checking the board, he raised his coffee in salute.

"Morning, claims girl. You're behind."

"I'm on time."

"The room isn't. Port leak in coat four. Memory carryover in coat six. One ankle split. One panic attack pretending to be heart trouble. Also a man at the back who says his borrowed jaw won't stop humming church music, and I told him that's triage for God, not us."

Mira glanced past him. "Did you give God a cot number?"

"I gave God blanket C and told him to wait his turn."

Jo ran the soup station out of the shuttered snack bar with the severity of a dock foreman and the timing of a comic who refused stage lighting. Her silver braid was looped up with a clothespin because she had lost the good clip again, and her detergent-dried hands moved between broth tubs, pill organizers, and the donation ledger without ever looking rushed. She wore the same slate shoes built for standing twelve hours at a time and still found energy to insult anyone who shuffled.

"If you're bleeding, say bleeding," she called toward the line. "If you're not bleeding, stop leaning on my table like you're auditioning for widowhood. Mira, your brother gave soup to the man with the active leak again."

"It was one cup," Gabe said.

"It was one cup into my blanket inventory."

That got a laugh out of three people who had come in too tense to look at each other when they hit the door. Jo took the laugh as payment and kept ladling.

At the table nearest the windows, Celia Rusk sat under two blankets and a good wool coat that had once been expensive enough to resent rain. The cuffs were shiny now. She had cut the fingers off a pair of gloves so she could still hold a pen properly, and she was annotating one of the salvaged maintenance maps in blue-black ink with the same fastidious little strokes she used to reserve for executive briefing packets. Her arthritic hands hated the cold. She flexed them against a heat mug between pages, then went back to translating corporate part numbers into things the cooperative could actually find or barter for.

"Rear knee actuator, model B-14," she said without looking up. "If Sloan uses the harbor surplus batch, he needs a two-millimeter shim or the joint will grind itself stupid in a week."

From behind the privacy tarps, Tavi answered, "See, this is why we keep you, Rusk. Not the charm. The other thing."

Celia put a neat line through one number and replaced it with another. "I'm touched by the clarity of your performance metrics."

Jo snorted. "Look at that. She lived with us three months and finally learned how to make a joke."

Tavi pushed through the tarp a moment later with his sleeves rolled and a smear of sealing foam dried white across the heel of one hand. Midwinter had carved the stoop deeper into him, but it had not made him slower. He moved like somebody who had learned to husband every bend and reach after losing the equipment that once did half the work for him. The burn scar high on his right forearm shone pale where the hair never grew back. He had a strip of cartoon-frog tape stuck to his sweater from patching a kid's splint and had not noticed it yet.

"Mira," he said, low enough that only she heard the change in register, "you got another clean basin? This one came in with salt in the port and an opinion about iodine."

She handed him the basin from under the counter. Their fingers bumped. He tipped his chin in thanks, private as a touch.

Then louder, for the room: "If anyone's waiting on lower-back work, eat first. I'm not listening to stomachs complain over a tissue printer again."

"You are absolutely listening," Jo said.

"Fine. I won't enjoy it."

Mira stamped two intake slips, rerouted a case with tendon memory into wash instead of repair, and let the rhythm of the room settle her into the day. Blue Hour had never managed this part. Blue Hour had been steam and secrecy and the animal jump in her chest every time the back door opened. The terminal was colder, louder, less forgiving to bodies, and somehow easier to breathe in. Here the work had spilled out where people could see it. The city still had not decided whether that made them useful or criminal. Men in clean municipal coats still slowed on the curb outside and counted heads. Clients still came in looking first for exits, then for uniforms, then for the nearest lie they could tell about why they were really here. But fear had lost its monopoly on the room. Schedules, broth, clipboards, and Jo's voice had taken back some ground.

The first urgent case was a courier named Lenna whose bargain sprint shell had split along the calf seam after three illegal double shifts. Lenna was maybe nineteen, smelled of fryer oil and rain, and kept apologizing to the body as if it had done her a favor by giving out late instead of on the overpass. Mira cleaned the seam, felt the heat around it, checked for internal contamination, and wrote BED TWO / TAVI / POSSIBLE NERVE CARRY on the card.

"Can you make it stop kicking when I try to sleep?" Lenna asked. "It keeps doing this twitch like it's trying to catch up to somebody."

"Maybe," Mira said. "Can you tell me if it's your somebody or the body's?"

Lenna thought about that longer than most people did. "Mine doesn't run that fast."

That was enough answer for now. Mira circled MEMORY too and sent her through.

The man with the humming jaw turned out to be a bus mechanic in a leased date-night face, embarrassed enough to blush through foundation. Every time he closed his mouth hard, a fragment of hymn rose in his throat, not words, just the tune. He hated hymns. His mother had taken him to church until he was sixteen and dead set against joy. Mira watched the muscles bunch under the borrowed skin and wrote WASH / OBSERVE / NO CHARGE because sometimes the cooperative stayed open by deciding shame was not billable. He looked like he might cry at that and hid it by asking if the soup had celery. Jo shouted from across the room that it had celery, cabbage, and God's indifference.

Near eleven, when the first rush loosened enough for the room to hear itself again, Lin came down from the mezzanine with a canvas satchel banging against their hip and a stack of copied intake forms hugged to their chest. They had grown into the terminal's chaos faster than Mira had. They were not comfortable in it, exactly. Comfortable made Lin sound less alert than they were. But they moved through crowds now with the practiced tilt of someone who knew when to give a frightened stranger space and when to stand close enough that the stranger did not bolt. Today they wore a navy peacoat from the donation rack, too big in the shoulders, and Jo's green scarf wound twice around the throat. In the outer pocket of the satchel, Mira could see the square edges of saved transit stubs lined up under a rubber band. Lin had started keeping them from each trip, date stamps turned outward, like proof that leaving and coming back could both be real.

"Did you eat?" Mira asked.

Lin looked briefly offended. "Jo watched me eat."

"That isn't an answer."

"I had eggs, toast, and a lecture about ferries after dark."

From the snack bar, Jo called, "Then hear it again. If the weather turns, you sleep in the church office and you don't try to become maritime."

Lin set the copied forms on the counter. At the top of each page, in their careful hand, they had added one new line beneath Maker Stamp and Scar Position: Who else knows. Mira had not asked them to. It was better than anything she would have thought to add.

"The South Basin group wants six more of these," Lin said. "And the church nurse says their supply ledger is a joke. She keeps insulin in a biscuit tin with the tea bags."

"That sounds like Jo's influence spreading."

"It sounds like survival," Lin said, then smiled to take the edge off it. "Also she really does use the biscuit tin. I checked."

Mira sorted the pages. Some had notes already clipped to them: a cleaner whose right hand remembered knife work, a warehouse renter who swore his borrowed lungs cried before he did, three unnamed bodies sleeping in the back room of a union office because none of them trusted official intake anymore.

"How many people?" she asked.

"In South Basin? Eight definite. Maybe eleven if the brother who called last night stops pretending he's asking for a friend. Two of them are from the tribunal video. The rest heard it sideways."

"Heard what sideways?"

"That there's a place here where somebody writes it down before they tell you you're defective."

The old protective answer rose in Mira on schedule: it was too soon, too visible, too easy for a city to notice a pattern once poor people started using paper instead of panic. She did not say it. Lin could hear the parts she left out better than most.

"You think I should wait," they said.

"I think I want impossible things. I'd like the law to get better and the weather to warm up and for you to carry three separate knives."

"I've got two."

"That isn't the reassuring number you think it is."

Lin's mouth twitched. Under the fluorescent hum and ferry wind rattling the glass, they looked older to Mira than when they had woken on Tavi's table, and younger too. Less raw. More willing to occupy their own outline. That was not safety. It was harder and better than safety.

"There are more than me," they said, quieter. "If I stay here being the example, they'll keep acting like I'm the only one. I don't want my face doing that work for them."

Mira folded the top page once, hard, to give her hands something to do. "I know."

Lin watched her for another beat, then nodded as if the answer had been adequate, if not pleasant. "Good. Because the south cutter leaves in twenty minutes."

Tavi appeared at Mira's shoulder with a zip pouch of sterile strips and a cheap penlight. "Take these. Don't lend the light to anybody who says they'll give it right back. That's how civilization ends."

Lin took the pouch. "I thought civilization ended in corporate insolvency."

"It was already sick," Tavi said. "I'm talking about my penlight."

He reached past them to tuck the pouch deeper into the satchel, fingers quick and paternal only until he noticed himself doing it. Then he cleared his throat and stepped back.

"If anybody shows you a flank seam with the tissue puckered inward," he said, "don't let them tell you it's a field repair scar. Call us first."

Lin tilted their head. "You're saying that like you don't want me asking why."

Tavi rubbed a thumb over the burn scar on his forearm, an old habit he only seemed to catch after it started. "I'm saying I know what cheap looked like when the other shops got interested. That's all."

Mira heard the rest of the sentence because she always did. That's all and too much. That's all and partly my fault. Lin heard enough of it too. They put the penlight away with careful gentleness.

"I'll call," they said.

Jo packed them a paper bag so overstuffed with food it looked illicit. Gabe wrote three emergency numbers on the inside flap, plus a fourth for a tide mechanic in South Basin who owed him money and hated being reminded. Celia, without ceremony, slid one of the annotated maintenance sheets under the clipped forms.

"If you see maker marks from Pelorus or Eastline," she said, "circle them. They use cleaner resin, but the budget seams delaminate in cold."

Lin blinked. "Thank you."

Celia recapped her pen. "This isn't altruism. I prefer data to rumor."

Jo pointed a soup ladle at her. "Lies. You're getting attached."

"I'm getting accurate," Celia said, and because the room had learned her by now, nobody let her have the last word for free.

Mira walked Lin as far as the south doors, where the wind from the bay came straight through the cracked vestibule and made everybody entering hunch like they had been insulted. Outside, the active commuter slip two piers over still took the noon cutter. Lin's scarf snapped at their shoulder like a flag. The satchel looked too light for what it carried and too heavy for comfort.

"You call when you land," Mira said.

"I know."

"You call if the room feels wrong."

Lin made a face. "All rooms feel wrong at first."

"You know what I mean."

"I do."

For a moment Mira thought Lin might hug her. She had never gotten used to wanting that and not knowing whether asking would make it smaller. Lin spared them both the negotiation. They leaned in, quick and hard, their cheek cold against hers, then stepped back.

"The soup here is better," they said, and the smile that came with it was half mischief, half nerves laid bare. "That's my official comparative report so far."

Then they turned and went down the walk toward the working pier, boots thudding over salt-stiff planks. Mira stood in the vestibule until they disappeared into the line of bodies boarding the cutter, navy coat swallowed by other coats, green scarf the last thing she lost sight of. Behind her the board clacked, resetting from BEDS to CLEAN PORTS. The room had another name in it already.

When she came back from the doors, Tavi was waiting by the supply closet with the expression he wore when he wanted to say something private and did not trust himself to attempt subtlety. He jerked his chin toward the former ticket supervisor's office. Inside, the filing cabinets held antibiotics, thermal socks, condoms, spare batteries, and the little stash of good tea Jo pretended everybody could touch but tracked like contraband. The room smelled of dust, antiseptic, and the cedar block somebody had tucked into the blanket drawer for optimism.

"You look like you're about to wear a trench in the floor," Tavi said once the door shut.

"I was watching Lin leave."

"I gathered that from the haunting widow pose, yeah."

She gave him a look. He lifted both hands in surrender, then used one of them to push a curl back behind her ear with such ordinary tenderness that the insult failed to hold.

"I'm not saying they shouldn't go," he said. "I'm saying if those other rooms start showing the same seam patterns, it's not going to stay small."

"There wasn't any small left once it hit tribunal."

"That's not what I mean."

"I know what you mean."

He exhaled through his nose, frustrated not at her but at the fact that knowledge kept coming shaped like blame. The same hands that now taped splints and cleaned ports had once helped make bargain tissue cheaper, stretch donor residue farther, turn a person into yield. Mira had not forgotten that. Forgetting would have been a lie, and their life together worked better when it did not need those. But she had also watched those hands close the eyes of bodies nobody could save, and steady Lin through nights when memory came in like broken weather, and count out the last of their cash without pretending numbers were mercy. History had not vanished. It had grown joints.

"If you start apologizing in engineering terms," she said, "I'm charging by the minute."

His mouth twitched. "Co-op rate?"

"Extortion."

"Fair."

She put both palms against his sweater and pushed him back into the filing cabinet. The metal thunk made the aspirin bottle on top rattle. He laughed once, surprised, then stopped laughing when she kissed him. His mouth tasted like burnt coffee and clove gum. His hands came to her waist slow at first, asking, then less slow when she answered by biting his lower lip until he swore softly into her mouth. She slid a hand under his thermal shirt and felt the long spared muscle of his side go tight under her palm. Cold air leaked through the window frame. The rest of him was warm.

"This is a terrible room for romance," he said against her throat.

"This isn't romance."

"What is it?"

Mira thumbed the cartoon-frog tape still stuck to his sweater. "Inventory control."

That made him grin, and the grin broke something loose in her chest she had been holding shut since the doors. He kissed her again, one hand at the back of her thigh now, the other braced by her head on the cabinet. There was space for exactly one bad decision in the room and not enough time to enjoy it properly. She liked the honesty of that. She liked that desire could still survive next to blankets, antibiotics, and a ledger full of ruined people who would be back tomorrow. When his palm slipped under her shirt, cold from washing up, she jerked and almost laughed; he used the sound to find her mouth again.

Then Gabe's voice came through the door, muffled but delighted. "If you two are reinventing morale in there, do it quieter. I've got a dock uncle outside crying because his borrowed jaw keeps singing at him."

Tavi let his forehead drop to hers. "Your family has no respect for medicine."

"My family has excellent timing."

"That is the opposite of what just happened."

She kissed the corner of his mouth once more, quick this time, a promise instead of a theft. "Wash up. If the jaw man bites you, I'm not doing your paperwork."

"I'd haunt you terribly."

"You already do."

They stepped back into the terminal separately enough to satisfy nobody. Jo took one look at Tavi's sweater riding half out of his waistband and tossed him a roll of tape like a curse. Celia kept writing, but the corner of her mouth moved. Gabe made a show of not looking at either of them and failed.

The afternoon settled over the terminal in layers: gulls beyond the glass, the flipboard's clack, soup cooling to film at the edges, Tavi cursing at a stripped screw, Jo telling a volunteer not to stack clean blankets beside bleach, Celia discovering an old ferry wiring diagram that could turn the dead upstairs lounge into one more heated ward if they found the right relay. Mira took the next intake slip, then the next. By dusk her wrist ached from stamping and her coat smelled like antiseptic and cabbage. She was tired in the ordinary way, the earned way. When the last daylight went blue over the water, Gabe flipped the board to NIGHT QUEUE and somebody in bed three started laughing at nothing Mira could hear. The sound moved through the room without frightening anyone. That, more than the locks on the doors or the lookout on the curb, was how she knew they had made something sturdier than a hiding place.

Five days later sleet came in sideways off the bay and sanded the terminal windows white by early evening. The heaters lost the argument in the corners. Everybody kept their coats on. Mira was at the intake counter with the cash tin, separating actual money from the day's other currencies: two packets of antibiotic ointment, a spool of copper wire, three transit vouchers, a jar of pickled carrots, and thirty-eight damp dollars folded so many times they had started to feel like cloth. The cooperative had made enough that week to pay for printer resin, lamp oil, and half of Brine Court's newest threat letter. It had not made enough to stop counting anything twice.

On the corkboard above the ledger, Lin's last message was pinned under a tide chart. They had written it on the back of a harbor safety flyer in the careful block hand they used when they wanted nobody to mistake a word: SOUTH BASIN HAS HEAT, BAD TEA, AND FOUR PEOPLE WHO THINK THEY'RE THE ONLY ONES. BACK THURSDAY IF THE BOATS BEHAVE. Under that, in smaller letters: TELL JO THE CHURCH COOK PUT CINNAMON IN THE LENTILS AND IT WAS A CRIME.

Jo had circled CRIME in red pen and added, beneath it, ARREST THEM.

Gabe was off his stool at last, leaning one hip against the dispatch booth and stretching out the foot that dragged when the cold got into it. He had traded the orange cap for a scarf in dockworker stripes and was teaching a volunteer how to reset the board without jamming the letters. Every third instruction turned into gossip. At the repair tables, Tavi was stripping down a rented forearm actuator with a concentration so complete it made him look almost gentle from far away. Up close it never quite did; up close you saw the speed in him, the impatience he kept filing into usefulness. Celia sat near the windows with a space heater at her knees and a ruler laid across one of the copied seed maps, building a cross-index of maker stamps that had started as triage and was turning into a census.

"We're out of the narrow-gauge cannulas again," she said.

Jo, decanting broth into smaller pots for the night line, didn't turn around. "Then write miracle pending on the order sheet and see if God brings any."

"I prefer suppliers who answer the phone."

"Then your standards are too high for this building."

Mira kept one ear on all of them and the rest of herself on the ledger. That was what lead triage had become: not command exactly, because command implied authority the city had never granted them, but stewardship of the mess. She tracked which cots stayed dry, which ports had been flushed, which clients could be trusted with antibiotics and which needed someone to watch them swallow the first dose. She knew whose service fee would clear and whose would not. She knew which names in the registry had started sending other names. The knowledge did not feel like victory. It felt like holding a door against weather with more people leaning on the safe side of it than the frame had been built for.

Tavi came up beside her with his hands still wet and leaned his shoulder into hers for the length of one breath, just enough pressure to register as theirs and not enough to invite commentary from the room.

"You eaten anything solid?" he asked.

"I had soup."

"Soup with Jo supervising doesn't count. She thins it when she gets philosophical."

"That's not how philosophy works."

"It's exactly how philosophy works."

She finally looked at him. There was printer resin under one thumbnail and a new white nick across the knuckle of his left hand. He noticed her noticing and tucked the hand behind the towel like a teenager hiding a cigarette burn.

"Lin says they're back tomorrow," she said.

"If the boats behave."

"The boats never behave."

"Yeah," he said. "They take after the rest of the city."

He did not kiss her. He did not need to. The contact at her shoulder and the ease of the joke sat in her longer than a kiss would have, because there was history under it and no attempt to mop the history up afterward. Then the outer doors banged wide and all of them turned at once.

The woman who came in with the weather looked like she had traveled in pieces and kept losing some on the way. Her raincoat had been shortened by hand with blue thread that did not match anything else she wore. She carried a red lunch tin against her chest hard enough to dent it further, and every few seconds her thumb checked the latch as if the act of arriving might shake it loose. Salt had dried white on her boots. She was maybe forty, maybe younger and just overworked, with one false eyelash hanging on from whatever job had required her to look more rested than she felt. She paused inside the doors not because she was shy but because people who had been chased by official rooms learned to take stock before they sat down in them.

Jo saw her first. Of course Jo saw her first.

"Close that door or pay the heating bill," she said, then took in the coat, the face, the way the woman held the lunch tin like evidence. Her voice changed by half a degree. "Come in, honey. If you're here to faint, do it near the soup."

The woman shut the door behind her. "Lin sent me," she said.

The room did something small and immediate at that. It was not a hush. The cooperative did not hush anymore if it could help it. But attention traveled. Gabe straightened. Tavi dropped the towel. Mira was already moving.

Up close, the woman smelled of bus heat, stale perfume, and the penny-metal tang of old fear. There was a transit band still looped around one wrist, four different punch marks stacked over each other. She set the red tin on the counter carefully, opened it, and took out not lunch but a sheaf of damp papers wrapped in wax paper and a terminal address card Mira recognized at once. They had cut the cards from old ferry schedule stock. On the back, in Lin's hand, was written: SOUTH BASIN, ST. BRIGID BASEMENT, PIA DIZON. Under that: LEFT FLANK SCAR. MAKER STAMP NOT COMMONFRAME.

"I'm Pia," the woman said, touching the note like she needed to make sure her name was still in there. "The one with the light eye said to come before somebody marked this body defective. She said you'd write it down first."

"I'll write it down," Mira said.

Pia let out a breath that seemed to have started three neighborhoods away. "Good. Because I didn't come for charity. I brought records."

She pushed the papers forward. Bus receipts. Rental contracts. A housekeeping schedule from the South Basin conference hotel with somebody else's shifts crossed out and hers added in blue ink. At the bottom was a list of six names and three maker stamps, all written in a hand Mira did not know. Pia had underlined two of them so hard the paper thinned.

That was Lin's work too, Mira saw at once. Lin had not just sent a body here. They had taught other people what counted as evidence.

"Sit," Mira said. "Soup first if you can keep it down. Then I look."

"I can keep it down. I just hate cinnamon now."

Jo, already ladling, looked scandalized. "Then you've had a bad week."

Pia almost smiled at that, surprised into it. It made her look abruptly younger. "Some church woman put it in lentils three nights running."

"Criminal," Jo said, handing over a bowl.

On the corner of Pia's schedule sheet, while somebody else should have been talking through evacuation drill notes, she had doodled the same little fish over and over in blue pen. It was not art. It was just a habit strong enough to survive panic. Mira liked her better for it before she knew anything useful.

Mira took Pia behind the nearest privacy tarp once the first spoonful had steadied her hands. Tavi came in without being asked and pulled the exam lamp lower. Celia followed two beats later with a clipboard and the air of a woman refusing to admit she had hurried.

"You don't need an audience," Pia said, seeing all three of them.

"You're right," Mira said. "You need three different kinds of competence."

That got a thin noise out of Pia that might have been a laugh if her teeth were not chattering.

The borrowed body was a Pelorus budget leisure shell. Mira saw the maker stamp at the nape before she even got the coat off: cleaner lettering than CommonFrame, port collar set deeper, resin grade better by at least one bracket. Somebody with Pelorus money had wanted the budget line to look respectable while it failed. Mira helped Pia peel down the damp thermal shirt beneath the raincoat and found the scar where Lin's note said it would be, tucked along the left flank just under the rib line.

For a second all Mira could think was that the scar was the same lie written in a different hand.

It was pale and old-looking for tissue printed less than a year ago, too neat for field repair, too hidden for any honest service port. The skin around it had the faint inward pucker of something inserted and sealed, not something mended after damage. Mira had touched its twin on Lin often enough to know the chill that came with recognition. It was not identical. Pelorus had cut slightly farther back, maybe to hide under different uniform lines. But the logic was the same.

Tavi bent close, not touching yet. Humor dropped clean out of his face.

"That's not CommonFrame work," he said. "The seam angle's wrong. And the weave..." He put on the loupe and swore softly. "Jesus. They even copied the cheap reinforcement pattern."

Celia held the lamp a fraction nearer. Her pen stopped moving. "Not copied," she said after a moment. "Converged. Or licensed through a shell contractor. Pelorus would never buy directly from CommonFrame if they could avoid the paper trail."

Tavi looked up at her. "You hear yourself?"

"I hear myself exactly." Her voice had gone flat with the kind of anger that preferred spreadsheets to shouting. "If the yield logic moved, it moved because the industry rewarded it. That is how industries behave."

Pia, half out of her shirt and shivering on the stool, looked between them. "I don't care what word you put on it if you can make it stop."

Mira stepped in before theory could keep eating the room. "Tell me what it's doing."

Pia gripped the edge of the stool with both hands. Her nails were clipped brutally short, but one thumbnail still wore a crescent of silver polish she had missed taking off.

"It aches first," she said. "Like somebody left a coin under the skin and it keeps heating up. Then I get..." She frowned, searching without trying to sound clever. "Not pictures, exactly. Uses. That's the closest word. I pick up a room and I know where a crib should go. I smell bleach and I know which one stains school shirts worse. Last night I was changing sheets at the conference hotel and started crying because I thought a boy was late for algebra. I don't have a boy. I don't know any algebra that far inland."

The specificity hit Mira harder than any abstract claim would have. That was how it always happened. Not revelation. Inventory.

"When did it start?"

"Two weeks after the tribunal. Maybe sooner. I thought I was only tired. Then the scar started pulling whenever I bent."

"Any lost time?"

"No."

"Any voices that feel external?"

Pia considered that seriously. "No. It feels more like somebody keeps setting dishes down in my sink."

Lin would have liked that description. Mira wrote it down exactly.

Tavi palpated around the seam, careful and unshowy. Pia flinched only once, when his fingers found the knot of hard tissue under the scar.

"There it is," he muttered. "Same buried access pocket. Different route in."

"You can treat it?" Mira asked.

"I can open and see how much of the interface they left sitting there. Maybe flush some of the inflammatory mess if it hasn't braided too deep. Maybe buy her time."

Maybe was the most honest word in the room. Mira heard Pia hear it too.

"Time for what?" Pia asked.

Celia answered before anyone else could lie kindly. "Time for us to understand what Pelorus thought it was doing."

Jo pushed through the tarp with another blanket over one arm and caught the back end of that sentence.

"No," she said. "Time for her to get warm and not collapse in my building. Science can line up behind supper."

She draped the blanket around Pia's shoulders with a practiced snap and turned to Mira. "Bed four's clear. Gabe can take her papers and copy them after she eats. If there are six more names, they need cards tonight, not tomorrow."

Again, routine took the room back. Nobody here waited for permission to become useful. Gabe was already outside clearing bed four and calling for dry socks. Tavi had washed up again and was laying out instruments on the tray with the brisk profanity he reserved for work that mattered. Celia was making columns on her clipboard: MAKER, SCAR POSITION, LEASE TYPE, REPORTED RESIDUE. Jo was yelling at a volunteer to find the good kettle because the South Basin tea, apparently, had committed fresh crimes against humanity. The room did not freeze around the scale of the problem. It made space.

Mira took the damp papers back out to the counter while Pia changed into a dry shirt behind the tarp. The list of six names had more on it than names. One line read LEFT EAR NOTCH, ASK FOR MARI IF HE LIES. Another had a bus route number and the note WON'T COME IF POLICE COATS IN SIGHT. At the bottom, in Lin's hand again, smaller this time as if added in a hurry, was: THERE ARE TWO IN HARBOR EAST TOO. A UNION WOMAN HAS THEM. CALL AFTER NINE.

The city had started answering itself. It was answering poorly, off-book, with bad pens and stranger's schedules and church basements that tasted like cinnamon. But it was answering. Mira stood with Pia's papers dampening her fingers and felt the old instinct rise again, the one that wanted an endpoint so badly it mistook one good ruling for the shape of justice. She let it pass through her. The terminal around her was too solid for that fantasy now. Too many blankets, too many names, too many people who had already built tomorrow's work into tonight's chores.

When Tavi came up beside her, gloved and ready, he stopped with one hand on the tray.

"If Pelorus has the same access pocket," he said quietly, "this didn't end when CommonFrame went under. It just lost a logo."

Mira folded Pia's papers back into order. "No."

He waited, maybe for anger, maybe for absolution, maybe simply because he had earned neither and still wanted language. The bay rattled sleet against the glass hard enough to sound like handfuls of seed.

"I can do this one," he said at last.

That, at least, was simple. Mira touched the inside of his wrist, just above the glove line where his skin stayed warm even in this building. "I know."

When Tavi opened the seam, the buried interface lay under the tissue like a hard pearl of bad intent. Pia bit down on the folded towel Jo gave her and cursed the church cook, Pelorus, and every man who had ever said the word upgrade like it meant mercy. Mira held the skin apart, suction humming, while Tavi teased free a length of filament so fine it might have been hair if hair came varnished and barcoded. The pocket around it was inflamed but not rotten yet. It bought time, not rescue.

An hour later Pia slept on bed four with the blanket up to her chin, silver thumbnail hooked over the latch of the red tin even in sleep. Gabe had copied the list of names twice, once for the registry and once for the contact board Lin carried between rooms. Jo had bullied two extra boiled potatoes into Pia's bag for when the anesthesia wore off. Celia, after examining the extracted filament under the lamp until her eyes watered, had written PELORUS across the top of a fresh tab in the maker index and underlined it once, hard enough to score the paper.

Mira sat at the counter with the stack of old schedule cards and the cooperative stamp. The stamp had started as Gabe's joke. RETURN TO TERMINAL, it said, in crooked red letters above the address. People took them anyway. They tucked them into wallets and lunch tins and bra cups and shoe linings. Mira stamped three more now while the room settled toward night.

Pia was not fully asleep after all. Anesthetic had made her voice slower, not smaller.

"If the others come," she asked from the cot, "what do I tell them this place is?"

Mira looked up. Past Pia's bed, she could see Jo wiping down the soup station with tired violence, Gabe arguing cheerfully with the board until tomorrow's queue slots lined up, Tavi washing blood from the clamp in water gone pink then clear, Celia holding her aching hands around the heat mug before forcing them back to the pen. Above them, through the high salt-clouded windows, the bay was only weather and dark. Nothing in it said ending.

"Tell them it's Gate Four," Mira said. Then, because that was too much like a code and not enough like care, she wrote the full address on the cards and crossed to the cot. "Tell them somebody will be awake."

She slid the cards into Pia's red tin beside the receipts and the doodled fish and the names already multiplying beyond the edges of one sheet. Then she opened the ledger to a clean page, wrote PELORUS at the top, and beneath it the terminal address, so the next body would have somewhere to go before the city learned the right words for them. The address existed anyway.
