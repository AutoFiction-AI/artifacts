# Chapter 4

By the time Mira rolled LM-127B through the back hall of Blue Hour, the laundry had gone to its night noises. The customer bell was dark. One dryer still turned with a bad bearing whine, then knocked once every revolution like a loose tooth. Steam had settled into the hanging shirts and uniforms so the room felt upholstered in damp cloth. The body rode under a motel sheet on one of Jo's wire carts, ankles tapping the rail each time a wheel found the cracked grout by the sink.

She had signed the transfer under salvage review, which bought her until morning if nobody matched the hold shelf against the internal movement log. Morning would turn clerical lag into a list of things she could not afford: tampering, removal of company stock, falsification of disposal routing. She could have written the categories herself. Claims taught you the difference between a mistake and a billable act. It also taught you how quickly both became the same thing.

Tavi looked up from the wetbench when she came in. He had his tools laid out in weight order on a folded towel printed with lemons, because he treated every borrowed surface like it had to survive him. His long fingers were already gloved, but he still tapped each vial against the burn scar on his forearm before he opened it, a nervous little click-click that he would have denied if anyone named it. Steam had put a shine on the bridge of his flattened nose.

"Tell me again," he said, "why this one gets midnight bench time ahead of the people who are actually paying us."

Mira braked the cart with her knee. "Because if I leave them on hold another day, somebody starts asking why."

"That's a reason you stole a body. It isn't a reason I should touch it."

She pulled the sheet back.

Cheap print skin always looked more expensive asleep. CommonFrame paid for a neutral face and saved money everywhere else. The body was adult, medium height, built to pass through labor shifts, sex shifts, and weekend vanity without offending any target market. Under the back-room bulb the injuries showed what the catalog never did. There was yellowing bruising high on one thigh, fresher mottling at the left shoulder, a pressure mark at the mouth where someone had held it shut too hard or kissed it with their teeth, and the pale rib scar that had brought her here in the first place, a clean old line under the right ribs with no manufacturing note and no repair authorization attached. One ear had a notch in the rim as if somebody had once missed with scissors. At the nape, half-hidden by short damp hair, the serial port sat like an extra idea the body had never agreed to carry.

Tavi's face changed in increments. He was still suspicious. He was also hooked now, and they both knew it.

"That scar is old," he said.

"Older than the renter file."

"The bruising isn't." He leaned in, not touching yet. "Neither is that mouth."

Mira heard herself answer in the dry voice she used at work when people wanted facts without judgment. "The lease declaration listed light leisure, no sex package, no specialty mods, no hazardous use. The residue scan threw a child in water, a hand-counting pattern that didn't match the renter's job history, and a panic spike during pelvic exam. Nothing aligned."

Tavi gave her a look over the cart. "You really know how to sell a nightmare."

"I'm not selling it."

"You dragged it uphill after midnight. That counts."

She ignored that. "Can you wipe the carryover and see what's underneath?"

He stayed bent over the body, eyes moving from scar to shoulder to nape port. "Maybe. Depends what this is. Could be dirty wash and rough use. Could be aggregate bloom. Could be somebody inside CommonFrame getting creative with salvage."

"They're already getting creative."

"Mira."

"I know what I saw."

He straightened. The stoop from bench work was more pronounced when he was tired, and tonight it made him look older than thirty-eight, less roguish than frayed. "I didn't say you didn't. I said maybe don't sound so eager to prove the worst thing first."

That landed because he almost never asked for optimism. Mira folded the sheet down to the body's waist, exposing the scar and the shoulder. The skin was cool from transit, but not storage-cold anymore. She had felt that through the cart handles the whole walk from the tram stop: a returning heat she did not want to interpret.

"If this is an overlap," Tavi said, quieter now, "the wipe might wake it up before it clears it."

She looked at him. "Bodies don't wake up from residue."

He did not answer quickly enough.

The room grew more crowded around the delay. Steam pressed at Mira's back. The dryer with the bad bearing gave another mournful knock. Out front, somewhere past the hanging uniforms, coins ran through a counter tray and stopped. Jo was closing the till. They had maybe an hour before she'd either come back here for the stain solvent or decide she was done lending the room to other people's bad ideas and turn off half the power.

"Tavi."

He reached for the conductive gel. "I said might. Don't make a religion out of it."

That was as close to admission as he was going to give. Mira filed it away because she had spent too long in claims not to notice when people who knew more than the paperwork got careful with verbs.

He worked fast once he started. He clipped two monitor leads beneath the jaw, cleaned the nape port with iodine until the skin around it shone amber, and threaded in a slim diagnostic line while Mira steadied the head. The body gave a reflex swallow at the first touch. Tavi's eyes cut up.

"Residual motor."

"I saw."

"If they buck, hold the left shoulder. That side's already chewed up."

There it was, casual and terrible, the shift in his language before hers. They. Mira did not say anything about it. She slid one hand under the shoulder blade and felt the cheap musculature wake under her palm in shallow, uncertain twitches, as if three different jobs were trying to remember themselves through the same strip of meat.

The wetbench hummed on. A wash map opened over the body in bands of blue-white. Mira did not know the software the way Tavi did, but she knew what bad looked like. Signals bloomed where there should have been one clean lane. A pressure ridge around the rib scar. Dense retention at the throat and pelvis. Cross-fire at the motor strip like a crowd trying to get through a single door.

"Jesus," Tavi muttered. "Look at that."

"What am I looking at?"

"A body that should've been pulped twice."

"Useful."

"I'm serious." He scrubbed a hand over his mouth with the back of his wrist. "There are stacked pathways in here. Not just memory soot. Patterned retention. Somebody didn't wash this line, they composted it."

The word made the room smell worse. Not just detergent and hot cotton now, but the sweetish tissue stink that lived under every repair if you opened enough skin. Mira looked at the pale scar. Somebody had cut in there once, neat as a paid surgeon, and left no note, no fee record, no correction order. Somebody had already treated this body like a place to bury work.

"Start the wipe," she said.

Tavi looked at her a second longer. Then he set the pulse low and put his thumb over the contact pad.

The first current made the body tense from heels to throat. Not a seizure. More like a flinch that had run out of room and used the whole frame. Mira tightened her grip on the shoulder. The right hand opened and closed once, fingers making a hard twisting shape around nothing. Then the wrist jerked inward, knuckles scraping the bench, as if it had become a different hand halfway through the motion.

"You saw that?" she said.

"Yep."

The next pulse sent a ripple across the abdomen. The feet pointed sharply, swimmer-clean, and then the left leg kicked sideways with the blunt force of somebody trying to force open a jammed train door. Tavi killed the current. The monitor did not settle. Lines kept running under the skin map, bright and crossing.

"That isn't postwash noise," Mira said.

"I know it isn't."

The body inhaled.

It was a small sound. Wet, catching, too deliberate to be reflex. Mira froze with her hand still under the shoulder blade. The chest lifted again, deeper this time. The mouth opened around the diagnostic bite block Tavi had set between the molars.

"Take that out," she said.

"If they clamp down on the line-"

"Take it out."

He moved fast. The block came free slick with saliva and a string of pink gel. The body's face tightened as if waking hurt in very specific places. Its eyelids fluttered. Opened. One iris was lighter than the other, gray chipped into brown like old paint showing through.

The body looked at the ceiling first, then at the hanging shirts, then directly at Mira.

"No," it said, hoarse around the gel. "No, don't send me back. Mira, please. Please don't."

The name went through her like the shove of a tram starting up.

Tavi swore and reached for the sedative pen. The body saw that too. Panic hit all at once, not abstractly but in the body itself: the shoulders heaved, the stomach seized, and it rolled half onto its side hard enough to tear the diagnostic line from the nape port. Saline and a thread of blood striped the sheet. Mira caught the torso before it pitched to the floor.

"Easy," she said, hearing how useless it was. "Easy. You're all right."

"No tanks," the body gasped. "No cold room, no, I can work, I can do nights, don't put me-"

The sentence fractured. Its right hand shoved at the bench rail like it was bracing against water. The left hand made a pinching motion in the air, quick and practiced, the sort of hand that could peel shrimp or pick glass out of a drain. Then both hands grabbed Mira's forearm with an animal, desperate strength.

"Hold them," Tavi snapped.

Them. After that the old pronoun would not come clean in Mira's head no matter how hard she tried.

She got her weight over the thrashing body without pinning it flat. The skin was hot now, hotter than it should have been, heat rising through the damp motel sheet and the thin shirt she had worn to work. Under her palm the shoulder kept trying different histories: a swimmer's roll, a laborer's brace, an instinctive curl inward like someone waiting for a blow.

Tavi slapped a coolant patch against the sternum. "Listen to me," he said, too fast, too sharp. "If you tear the line again you're going to cook your own cortex."

The body stared at him in wild incomprehension.

Mira said, "You're in a laundry back room. You hear that dryer? You're here. That's steam. That's soap. Nobody's putting you anywhere right now."

The grip on her forearm did not loosen, but it changed. Less fight. More proof of contact.

"Hot soap," the body repeated. It swallowed and winced. "Coins. Wet cotton."

"Yeah."

"Your hand's rough."

Mira looked down stupidly at where their fingers were wrapped around her wrist, as if they had discovered something private instead of obvious. Calluses. Ink smudge at the base of her thumb from the transfer form. A crescent dent in her skin where their nails had gone in. The body tracked all of it with the horrible focus of somebody whose entire world had narrowed to this room and the woman leaning over them.

Tavi had gone still in a different way. Not calm. Measuring. His humor had burnt off so completely that Mira could see the shape of the technician under it, the man who had spent years with wet tissue and expensive mistakes under his hands.

"Can you follow what I'm saying?" he asked.

The body turned its head toward him. "You're the one with the hot scar."

For one second Tavi looked almost offended on behalf of his own forearm. Then the expression broke under the fact of the answer.

"Okay," he said. "Okay. Good. Stay with me. Do you know where you were before this?"

"No."

"Do you know who you are?"

The body shut its eyes hard. Mira felt the lashes brush the heel of her hand when they opened them again.

"I know stairs," they said. "I know a bus depot bathroom that smelled like bleach and pennies. I know how to hold a tray with six coffees if the lids are bad. I know somebody's little boy in yellow water and I hate him because he's dead and he's not mine to hate. I know this room now. I know you said Mira."

Their breathing hitched on the last word.

"Don't wipe me," they said. "If wipe means less than this, don't."

Mira heard Tavi inhale through his teeth. She knew what he was about to say because she had spent years listening to frightened people wrap themselves in procedure.

We do not know what this is.

It may not be stable.

There are protocols.

He did not get the chance. The body turned their face into the inside of Mira's wrist, as if even the smell of laundry soap and transit dust there might count as shelter, and said in a voice gone raw and small, "Please don't make me go away because it's expensive."

That did it. Not the philosophy of it. Not the impossible speech. The money in the plea. The assumption that annihilation would arrive as a cost-saving measure and everybody in the room would know the terms.

Mira looked up at Tavi.

"Tell me that's reflex," she said.

He met her eyes over the torn line, the lit monitor, the hot damp room that suddenly could not contain what it knew.

"It isn't," he said.

They stopped shaking first.

That was how Mira started measuring the next stretch of time, not by minutes but by what the body no longer did. The kicking stopped. The incoherent hand motions stopped. The breath lengthened enough that Tavi could clean the torn skin around the nape port and seal it with strip mesh instead of holding pressure. Mira got water into them by teaspoon at first, because the first full swallow made them cough until their eyes watered. By the time the dryer out front finally went quiet, they were sitting upright on the bench in one of Tavi's washed-thin T-shirts with the motel sheet wrapped around their legs, both hands around a paper cup of tap water as if the cup might try to leave without permission.

Steam still hung low in the room, but without the emergency in it the heat felt more intimate and worse. Mira had blood drying on the heel of one hand where the line had cut the skin at the nape. Tavi had changed gloves twice and still looked contaminated by the whole thing.

The conscious body on the bench watched them both with exhausting care.

Not blankly. Not like an infant, and not like a machine waiting for prompts. Watched the way people watched when they had learned too early that adults liked to decide your future in front of you if you let them think you were not listening.

Tavi peeled off his gloves. "This can't stay here."

Mira did not answer right away. She was looking at the left ear, the notch in it, the small involuntary movement they made with their thumb along the paper seam of the cup, rubbing it smooth and then rough again. A private habit, maybe new, maybe old. Something to do with having hands and needing them occupied.

"Mira."

"I heard you."

"No, I don't think you did." He pitched his used gloves into the red bin and missed. He did not stoop to fix it. "This isn't a weird repair case. This isn't a client with spillover. This is a talking overlap body sitting in Jo's laundry with my bench log open and your company probably already wondering why one of their units took a field trip."

From the bench, very softly: "Overlap."

Mira turned. "It's the word for more than one memory pattern staying active in one body."

They considered that. "Is that what I am?"

Tavi dragged a stool over with his foot and sat on it backward, forearms across the backrest. That was how he sat when he needed something between himself and what he was saying. "That's what it looks like."

"Looks like," they repeated.

"I don't know enough yet to say more cleanly than that."

Mira did. Or she knew enough for the only part that mattered. "If we report this, CommonFrame takes them."

Tavi looked at her sharply. "Maybe. Or maybe this goes to a lab instead of straight disposal."

"That's not better."

"I didn't say it was."

"Report means seizure, internal review, wash, pulp if unstable, salvage if not. I know the language."

"You know claims language."

"I know what vanishes off my desk."

The body on the bench kept their eyes on Mira. Not because they trusted her yet. Because she was the one using verbs with the fewest curtains around them.

"And wipe?" they asked.

Nobody answered.

They looked from one of them to the other. "When you say wipe, what stays?"

Tavi's mouth tightened. Mira had seen him bluff nurses, clients, inspectors, neighbors, and one very drunk man with a detached thumb. She had never seen him search this hard for a sentence.

"The body would stay," he said at last. "Basic motor, probably. Leaseable function. The present pattern likely wouldn't."

"Pattern," they said.

"I know how that sounds."

"Like cleaner words."

Tavi flinched, very slightly. Mira caught it because she was close enough now to recognize how his face changed when a joke failed to arrive and left him alone with the thing itself.

"Yeah," he said. "Like cleaner words."

The room held there. The dryers were off. Out front a tram bell drifted up from the street, thin through glass and steam. Somewhere under it Jo laughed at something on the radio, one hard nicotine rasp and then silence again. Ordinary city sound. Ordinary late summer heat. Inside the back room, none of it reached far enough.

Mira said, "I'm not sending them back."

Tavi rubbed both hands over his face. "You say that like there's a fourth option where nobody comes looking."

"There is."

"Hide them."

"Yes."

"In what? Under the folding table?" His voice went louder, then rougher, then he reined it in because Jo was twenty feet away and had already been generous enough. "Mira, this isn't just stolen stock. If Tissue Safety gets a conscious body out of an unlicensed room, they don't fine us like idiots running gray-market stitch work. They make an example. They seize everything. They bury Jo. They bury you. They bury me."

"I know."

"Do you?"

She could have answered with statutes. She could have told him the civil fine range for unauthorized tissue handling, the seizure schedule for illicit biological inventory, the way hearing officers said contaminated asset with the same tone some landlords used for mold. Instead she thought of her rent arrears, Gabe's debt notices tucked under the salt shaker because neither of them wanted to see them on the table, the exact shape of Brine Court's stairwell at four in the morning when the hallway light was out again. Hiding a person there was insane. Hiding a person there was also more possible than trusting a corporation that had printed them by the thousand and called it discount inventory.

"I know what the company does to bodies that make administration complicated," she said. "That's enough."

Tavi leaned back from the stool and looked at the bench, not at Mira. When he spoke again the humor had not returned, but something else had: that private register she only saw in him while he was repairing people who could not afford elegance.

"I am not arguing they're property."

The body's eyes snapped to him at the uncontracted form, precise with stress.

Tavi noticed and corrected himself with a grimace. "I'm not arguing that. I'm arguing that I don't know what keeping you alive looks like yet. You're running hot. Your lattice map is filthy. There are four or five retention bands in you at least, maybe more, and if one of them starts eating the rest I can't promise this doesn't turn ugly fast."

They took that in without looking away. "Would reporting me keep me alive?"

"Alive as what?" Tavi asked, and hated himself for how fast it came out.

That was the wrong question. Mira heard it land. So did he.

The body lowered their eyes to the cup. Their thumb went back to the seam. "Alive enough to be useful," they said. "That's usually what people mean."

Mira crossed the room before she had fully decided to. She took the stool from Tavi with one hand and sat in front of the bench so the body did not have to look down at either of them. Up close, the mixed irises were more striking, but what got her was the attention in them. Not innocence. Calculation would have been easier. They were sorting every word for shelter and threat at once.

"Look at me," she said.

They did.

"Reporting you means putting you back inside the system that printed you. I don't know what they'd call it, but I know what they'd do. They'd take you away from the people in this room. They'd decide in private whether you're evidence, salvage, or a defect to clear. You would not get a vote."

"And wiping me?"

Mira made herself answer plainly because anything softer would be a lie. "I think that kills the part of you that's talking to me."

They nodded once. No dramatics. Just the acceptance of a fact properly named.

"Then no," they said.

Tavi let out a breath that almost became a laugh and did not. "You hear how easy you make it sound?"

They turned their head toward him. "It isn't easy. You're both just saying the same thing with different shame."

That shut him up completely.

Mira almost smiled despite herself. Not because it was funny, though it was, a little. Because it was the first sentence in the room that belonged to the person saying it instead of the emergency they had been born into.

"Do you remember anything that belongs to you?" she asked.

They looked down at their hands. "I don't know how to separate that yet."

"Try."

They closed their eyes. For a moment Mira thought she had asked too much. Then they spoke, not in the scattered flood from before but slowly, as if lifting things one at a time from muddy water.

"There's steam on painted walls," they said. "Not here. A stairwell. Green once, then not green because the damp ate it. Somebody downstairs is frying fish or plantains or both. The banister sticks if you drag your hand too slow. Somebody keeps shouting up for..." They stopped and opened their eyes. "Lin."

The name hung there, small and exact.

Mira said nothing.

"Not at me," they said. "Through me maybe. Around me. But it comes clean. Lin, get down here if you're taking the salt with you. Lin, if you let that pot burn I'll skin you. Lin, answer me. I liked how the voice expected an answer."

Tavi's gaze dropped to the floor, then came back. "You want that one?"

They nodded. "If I've got to borrow, I'd rather borrow something somebody called because they wanted me in the room."

Mira felt that under the breastbone, a hard little inward catch that had nothing to do with romance or pity and everything to do with recognition. So many people spent half their lives being summoned only for payment, labor, explanation. To want a name because it carried ordinary impatience from a kitchen or stairwell made more sense to her than any grand speech about identity ever could.

"Lin," she said, testing it.

Lin looked up fast. Not because the name was perfect. Because it had been handed back without argument.

Tavi stood and moved to the monitor. "All right. Lin." He said it like he was fitting a part and checking whether it held. "If we're doing this, we do it with our eyes open."

Mira stayed seated. "Meaning?"

"Meaning this bench log dies. The transfer dies. The serial ping gets muffled if I can manage it without frying the port. Jo does not get surprised by a corporate retrieval team because we got sentimental in her back room. And Lin does not stay here."

Lin said, "I can hear when you're afraid."

"Good," Tavi said. "Use that skill generously."

It was the nearest thing to a joke he had managed since the waking, and even now it carried too much fear to float. Still, Lin's mouth moved at one corner, not quite a smile, more the first test of whether one might be allowed.

Mira stood. "They can come with me."

Tavi looked at her. "Home."

"For tonight."

"You live in a building made of eavesdropping and overdue rent."

"I know."

"Your brother-"

"Will deal with it in the morning."

He stared at her long enough that she understood the objection under the practical one. Not just risk. Commitment. Once she took Lin uphill to Brine Court, this stopped being an emergency in a borrowed room and became part of her life in the way rent, blood, and family were part of it. Tavi saw that before she let herself.

"Mira," he said, quieter now, "if somebody traces this back, you lose more than the job."

She thought of Lin saying expensive. She thought of the shelf at CommonFrame, the file language waiting to flatten them back into anomaly rates and disposition codes. She thought of the way their fingers had gone from combat grip to simple human need around her wrist when she said steam, soap, dryer, here.

"I know," she said again. "I'm still not sending them back."

For a second she thought he would keep fighting. Then he walked to the sink, took a cheap utility knife from the magnet strip, and came back to the bench. He crouched in front of Lin.

"I'm going to cut the wrist tag," he said. "Hold still."

Lin looked to Mira. She nodded. Tavi slid two fingers under the plastiskin band CommonFrame used for internal chain-of-custody and sliced upward. The barcode snapped. A tiny thing. Less sound than the click of his vials against his scar. It still felt like a door giving way.

He handed the severed band to Mira.

"Congratulations," he said to nobody in particular. "We're done pretending this is repair."

He went to work on the monitor after that, hands quick and ugly with fatigue, wiping the active log, forcing a hardware error into the last two minutes of recorded trace, soldering a cheap signal-damp patch onto a length of mesh that he told Lin to keep at the nape under their hair. Mira understood enough to know he was helping in the only language he trusted when things got this bad.

While he worked, she found clothes. A pair of drawstring scrub pants Jo kept for bleach accidents. Tavi's oldest jacket, canvas gone soft at the cuffs. A pair of rubber-soled kitchen shoes from the lost-and-found bin, too big but better than bare feet on the alley grit. Lin stepped into each item with wary concentration, as if clothing itself might contain a test. When the jacket snagged at the shoulder, they muttered, "This sleeve remembers somebody broader than me," and then looked up quickly, embarrassed by their own sentence.

"The jacket remembers beer and solder," Tavi said. "Ignore it."

Lin surprised Mira by huffing a small breath through the nose. Not laughter exactly. An attempt.

When they were dressed, Mira tucked the severed wrist tag into the pocket of her work pants and immediately took it back out. Keeping it felt too much like evidence. Throwing it away felt worse. In the end she dropped it into the sharps tin and listened to it hit metal under gauze, scalpels, and cracked ports. Tiny, final, insufficient.

Out front the lights were half down. Jo had gone home after leaving a note by the register that said LOCK THE GODDAMN BACK and had drawn a smiley face so angry it looked like a wound. Mira switched off the bench lamp. The back room fell into a dim industrial blue from the streetlight in the alley window.

Lin stood very close to the door once she opened it, not crowding her, just staying within the range where a body could follow if the room changed its mind. The night outside smelled of salt, fryer grease, hot brick, and the canal's chemical rot. Somewhere up the hill the tram rattled unseen.

Tavi leaned against the sink, exhausted all the way through now. "Call me if they spike a fever. Or if they start losing time. Or if anybody in your building asks stupid questions."

"That narrows it down."

"Shut up and listen." He pointed at Lin, then at the mesh patch he had fixed over the port. "Keep that dry as you can. If it peels, don't touch the contacts. If you get dizzy, if the room starts doubling, if you smell chlorine where there isn't any, tell her immediately."

Lin nodded with grave attention. "You think I might come apart."

Tavi scrubbed a hand over his mouth. "I think somebody built you out of corners that were never meant to meet. That's not the same thing."

Lin absorbed that. "All right."

Mira had one hand on the door. The alley heat pushed in around her wrist. She looked back at Tavi, at the wrecked sheet, the torn line, the blanked monitor, the room where the whole book of her life had just lost one of its oldest nouns.

"I'll get them out before dawn," she said.

"They're already out," he said.

It was not reassurance. It was worse and truer than that.

Mira put her palm lightly between Lin's shoulder blades and led them into the alley. They moved with caution but not helplessness, one hand holding the oversize jacket closed, the other brushing the brick wall once as if to be sure it was solid. Behind them Tavi locked the service door, one bolt and then the other.

The walk uphill to Brine Court would take twelve minutes if Lin could manage the grade and the shoes did not betray them. Twelve minutes through canal-market runoff, through the smell of fried fish and coolant, through a city that had room for leased bodies, pulped bodies, damaged bodies, debtor bodies, sex bodies, labor bodies, but none at all for the fact of one deciding to stay alive.

Lin kept close enough that their sleeve touched Mira's elbow each time they turned a corner.

By the top of the block she was no longer moving stolen stock through the dark. She was smuggling a person home, and every step after that belonged to a new category of crime.
