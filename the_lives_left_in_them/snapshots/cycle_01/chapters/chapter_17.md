# Chapter 17

By six-thirty the tribunal win had become a recorded message.

Mira heard it from three phones at once: one on Jo's counter, one on speaker in Tavi's hand, one tinny and furious from somewhere in the line already thickening outside the laundry. Thank you for contacting CommonFrame client support. Due to insolvency proceedings and court-ordered asset preservation, all bargain-line service centers were temporarily unavailable. Maintenance obligations were suspended pending administrator review. Please monitor your account portal for updates.

Suspended, unavailable, review: they were the exact kind of clean little lies Mira had once been paid to hear and repeat with a level face. She knew where the bodies went inside those phrases. She knew what got renamed nonessential when a company was trying to make itself look dead in a way the court would find respectable. Flush kits. Skin sealant. Joint sleeves. Wash dampers. Every cheap ugly thing that kept bargain stock from swelling at the ports, shedding collagen at the knees, hearing three lives at once in the middle of a bus transfer. The parts were now assets. The people wearing them were not.

Jo had hauled the front shutter halfway up and jammed it there with a mop handle. Cold canal air dragged detergent smell and old extinguisher powder through the shop. The back room was still a blackened cavity, one wall bubbled from the raid fire, the floor uneven where the wetbench had died. Nobody sane could run repairs in there. Nobody sane would have tried. The line outside kept growing anyway, wrapping past the laundromat windows and into the produce stand next door where someone had started handing out bruised tangerines because waiting people would pass out faster on empty stomachs.

"If your problem is billing, I can't help you," Jo called, standing on an upside-down milk crate in her house cardigan and a church blouse printed with little blue saints she denied owning whenever anyone noticed it. Her silver braid swung against her back when she pointed. "If your leg is leaking, if your memory is cutting in and out, if your arm is stuck around somebody's throat, that is us. Pick one honest emergency and get in the right line."

That moved them better than kindness would have. Mira had a notebook open on the washer lid nearest the door. Her handwriting had gone back to claims-script neatness without asking her permission. Name, serial if known, symptom onset, user contract if any, last maintenance date if they remembered. She had stopped writing warranty status because there was no longer any point in recording a dead god.

The first man in front of her gave his name as Dario Velasquez. He smelled like fish scales and machine oil, and two fingernails on his right hand still carried a coat of gold glitter, chipped but stubborn, from his daughter's birthday the night before.

"Dock frame," he said. "Cheap one. I know. Don't look at me like that."

"I'm not looking at you like anything," Mira said.

"Good, because my wife already did." He swallowed and glanced back at the line as if shame might spread by eye contact. "My elbow locked on shift. Then it unlocked all at once and threw three crates into the water. Supervisor said get it serviced before I come back or don't come back. Portal ate my deposit and now it says account unavailable."

Mira touched the arm where the synthetic biceps narrowed toward the elbow seam. Heat had pooled under the skin. The flex sleeve clicked under her fingers like damp paper being folded wrong.

"How long since it started slipping?"

"Week, maybe. I worked around it."

Of course he had. People always worked around it until the machine made them too expensive to keep pretending. Mira wrote urgent. Tavi leaned over her shoulder, smelling of cold metal and the stale coffee he drank when sleep had lost the argument.

"Batch 8K bargain stevedore," he said. "Sleeve delamination. We can strap it for now, but if the tendon puckers he's dropping the whole forearm."

Dario went gray under the dock tan. "Dropping where."

"Off," Tavi said. "Not right this second if you keep your hands to yourself."

"Tavi."

"What? He asked."

Mira put her hand flat on the notebook to stop herself from tearing the page out. More people pressed at the half-open shutter. A woman with one borrowed knee braced both hands on the windowsill and tried not to scream as the joint buzzed. A teenage courier in a bargain sprint frame kept blinking hard, then turning his head at voices nobody else heard. On the sidewalk, somebody vomited pink wash buffer into the gutter. It ran around a cigarette butt and thinned toward the storm drain like watered paint.

Lin moved through them with a paper cup full of tongue depressors and a box of old transit pencils. They had borrowed Gabe's yellow rain shell, the one with a missing snap at the throat, and the hood kept slipping over one of their mismatched eyes. Every time they pushed it back, they looked a little more annoyed at clothing in general.

"Write your name if your hands work," they told the courier, steady and careful. "If they don't, tell me and I'll write it. Don't make up the date of your last wash. We need the real one."

"I don't know the real one."

"Then say that. It helps."

The kid stared at them, breathing too fast. "Are you a nurse?"

Lin considered the question with irritating seriousness. "No. I'm good at not making people lie when they're scared."

That, apparently, was enough. He handed over his wristband.

Mira's own phone started vibrating across the washer. She looked down and saw a number from the plaintiff coalition, the legal team that had spent the last forty-eight hours explaining to every camera in Cordera why the tribunal mattered and why CommonFrame could not be allowed to bury the ruling in delay. She let it buzz. The city screens over the tram stop across the street were already carrying the bankruptcy headline under footage of the courthouse steps.

COMMONFRAME FILES FOR EMERGENCY INSOLVENCY PROTECTION.

SERVICE OBLIGATIONS UNDER REVIEW.

EXECUTIVES DENY RETALIATORY SHUTDOWN.

The line outside the laundromat read the screens in ugly little waves. Some people cursed. Some looked relieved for half a second because bankruptcy sounded, if you did not know the contracts, like the company might finally be in trouble. Then the relief curdled as the practical meaning worked through the crowd. A bargain body was not a sweater. You could not hang it in the closet until the courts sorted themselves out. People were inside these things now. People were making rent with them, crossing the city in them, fucking in them, lifting with them, hiding in them, surviving in them.

One of Jo's regulars from the corner bodega, Suri Nwoko, limped in on a caregiving frame with her pink salon apron still tied over her coat. She had been arguing about coffee prices with Jo for three years and remained convinced every dark roast in the district was a personal insult.

"Tell me you have seal gel," she said without greeting. "The ankle split on the bus and now my sock is wet in a way that feels expensive."

She hiked the hem. A seam above the borrowed ankle had opened like a bad cut in soft fruit. Clear fluid slicked down into her shoe.

Jo looked at it once. "Sit before I make you sit."

Suri sat. "I hate when you're right."

"That must make your days long."

They got a laugh out of her, which was useful because the laughter kept her from looking too hard at the fluid pooling in the tongue of her sneaker. Mira added her to the list. Tavi was already shaking his head.

"We're out of the cheap gel," he said. "I used the last tube on a shoulder port at dawn."

"We had six tubes yesterday."

"Yesterday the company still wanted these people billable."

Mira looked up at him. "What do you mean, still wanted."

He scrubbed a hand over his mouth, leaving a gray streak of machine dust along his cheek. Two unmatched gloves hung out of his back pocket like dead animals by the wrists. "I mean those parts are behind serialized locks now. Service depots will close first, inventory freeze second. They'll say they're preserving assets for creditors. What they're preserving is resale. Gel, sleeves, dampers, all of it."

"Can they do that with active users still in line?"

Tavi gave her a look that was half pity, half accusation for having once worn a badge. "Mira. You know they can."

She did. She knew the clause numbers too, knew how often insolvency language sat buried under user responsibility, knew the phrases that turned catastrophe into accepted commercial risk. She had spent years telling poorer clients that what had happened to them fell under exclusions. She could feel those old scripts in her mouth now, rancid and ready.

Outside, a man started shouting that the CommonFrame service center on Harrow had chain on the doors and a paper notice behind glass. Somebody else shouted back that the South Quay center had private security and no staff. The shouting spread, then thinned into the rougher noise of panic trying not to use the word panic.

Jo came down from the crate. "Enough." Her nicotine laugh snapped once, not because anything was funny but because her throat did that when she was angry. "We are not fixing a district from the front of my washers."

"We don't have anywhere else," Mira said.

"Wrong. We have somewhere else that's cold and filthy and belongs to a city that already abandoned it, which makes it spiritually perfect."

She was looking past Mira now, out toward the canal road and the slice of gray water beyond the roofs.

Tavi followed the look first. "Jo."

"Don't Jo me. The old ferry terminal has drains, benches, a loading ramp, and enough square footage that people can panic without breathing on my neck."

"It's locked."

"So are rich people's wine cabinets. Keys are one category of obstacle."

Mira felt the answer land before she had the energy to argue with it. The terminal was bigger, colder, closer to open water. Wind came off the harbor there with salt in it and nowhere to hide. It also had old ticket counters, loading bays, public toilets, concrete floors you could bleach, and ceiling hooks left from a time when the city still pretended transit for workers counted as infrastructure. It was twenty minutes on foot from Brine Court, downhill enough for carts, close enough that people knew the route.

Her phone kept vibrating. Legal team again. Then an unknown number. Then Gabe.

She answered him first.

"Tell me you already heard," he said. Pain made his voice brighter than usual, as if it had been polished against a rough edge. "Dock boards are lighting up. People are stuck in lift frames with no support and everyone's acting surprised the company would pull the plug on poor people first."

"We're moving."

"Good. Because there are about forty people on the neighborhood channel asking where to go, and if I answer 'the sidewalk outside Jo's' one more time my thumb is going to detach."

She could picture him in the Brine Court kitchen chair, brace under his sweatshirt, left foot dragging every time he got up, still pretending he was more useful if nobody saw him wince. "Can you get to the terminal?"

"I can get to a chair at the terminal."

"That's enough."

Jo was already barking assignments.

"You," she told Dario, pointing at his good arm. "Carry those folding tables."

"My elbow."

"Use the other one. Congratulations on your surviving elbow."

To Suri: "Take every clean sheet you see and stop choosing by color."

To Lin: "Bring the notebooks, the registry, and the kettle. People tell the truth easier with hot cups in hand."

Lin nodded once and vanished into the hallway.

Mira tore three pages from the notebook, rewrote the urgent list, and shoved the rest under her coat. Victory had lasted one night, maybe less than that. It had lasted long enough for headlines, not long enough for maintenance. She looked at the line, at the wet shoes, the swollen ports, the hands people kept flexing as if obedience could come back by command.

"Everybody who can walk, we're moving to the ferry terminal," she shouted. "If you can't walk, tell us now. If you're carrying a body that won't hold upright, tell us now. We are not leaving anyone here to wait for a company that already left."

The words made the crowd louder, but they also gave it a direction. That was all a city ever was in crisis, Mira thought, a crowd pointed somewhere before it turned on itself.

By the time they started pushing carts downhill, the legal team was on message number four.

Mira did not listen.

***

The old ferry terminal smelled of brine, pigeon shit, rust, and the damp mineral cold of concrete that had not decided whether it belonged to weather or building. Wind came in under the warped doors and traveled the whole length of the waiting hall. It carried gull noise with it and worried the old route maps still bolted above the ticket windows, islands and schedules from a public future that had died so gradually nobody had marked the date.

Jo got them inside with bolt cutters borrowed from the produce vendor's nephew and an expression that suggested municipal property was mostly a rumor rich people told one another. By the time Mira reached the loading hall with the second laundry cart, the chain was in two pieces on the ground and Jo was directing traffic like she had owned the terminal since birth.

"Triage by the ticket windows," she said. "Repairs in the old waiting room. Anyone who faints goes flat against the east wall because that is where the draft is least murderous. If you piss in a corner when there is a functioning toilet fifty feet away, I will remember your face until I die."

People listened. That was half her talent and half the city's hunger for someone who sounded like the sentence had already happened.

They built the place out of whatever the neighborhood could carry fast. Folding tables from the bodega. Plastic bins of gauze and old tubing from three closed nail salons and one veterinary office that asked no questions as long as Jo promised a future favor. Extension cords from a church basement. Two industrial fans, which nobody wanted until Tavi pointed out the reek of open port infections would turn the hall unworkable by afternoon. Someone dragged in a coffee urn the size of a toddler. Someone else brought blankets that smelled of cedar closet and cat.

Mira moved through it with her notebook and the stupid, steady clarity that came when terror had too many tasks attached to it to be called terror. She taped paper signs over the ticket windows. INTAKE. URGENT. PORT CLEANING. MEMORY BREAKS. The last one had been Lin's suggestion, precise and bleak. Not everyone showing up was leaking or splitting. Some were hearing borrowed fathers call them by wrong names in the middle of crossing streets. Some had woken in bargain stock with other people's church songs trapped in their teeth. Some were overlaps who had watched the tribunal and then watched the company abandon them anyway.

Gabe arrived in a cab paid for by somebody he refused to name, swearing at the driver, the curb, and his own left leg with equal sincerity. He lowered himself into a folding chair like a man being folded by someone else, one hand pressed against the lumbar brace under his sweatshirt. He had brought a thermos of burnt coffee, a bundle of charging cables, and a sandwich bag full of hotel pens he had apparently been stealing for years in preparation for exactly this kind of civic emergency.

"Dispatch desk," he said through his teeth. "Don't argue."

Mira opened her mouth.

"Don't. I can sit and yell. It is one of my better skills."

So she put him by the entrance with two notebooks, a power strip, and the neighborhood channel pulled up on his cracked tablet. Within ten minutes he had built a crude routing system for who needed immediate hands-on repair, who could wait for strapping and instructions, who needed bed space, and who had a working vehicle large enough to transport somebody whose bargain frame had gone rigid at the spine. He flirted with old ladies, cursed at dock foremen, and kept slipping one cough drop after another into his mouth because pain made him want sugar and he was trying to save the coffee for later.

Lin took the middle ticket window. They had a stack of index cards, the registry ledger open to one side, and a ceramic sugar bowl from Jo's apartment full of paper clips. Every frightened person who came to the window got the same unnerving attention, as if Lin could not imagine wasting even one of them.

"Tell me what your body did first," they asked.

Lin was not asking what had happened, what contract line had failed, or whether the client had used the frame according to terms. Lin was asking what your body did first. The question pulled people down into sequence and sensation. Mira watched it work again and again. A woman in a labor-assist frame said, "My left hand forgot the broom and kept reaching for a rosary that wasn't there." A man in a courier shell said, "My knees started hearing applause." A sex worker with bitten-off black polish and a wool cap over one ear said, "The port at my neck burns every time somebody calls me honey, and I know how stupid that sounds." Lin wrote all of it. None of them laughed.

Tavi claimed the old waiting room at the back and turned it into a repair bay out of insult, wire, and habit. He swore at the terminal's dead overhead lights until a former ferry electrician named Remy Santiago, who had a little cloud tattooed behind one ear and glitter worked into the cracks of his knuckles, got half the room powered through an exterior maintenance box with a knife and a prayer so profane Mira almost wanted to write it down. Tables lined up under the revived strips of light. Old ferry benches became instrument trays. Someone found a wheeled luggage rack and it became transport for clients who could not bear weight.

By noon the line outside had doubled and the city had started noticing in the only language cities use when poor people solve an official failure in public. Two local feeds sent camera crews. A council aide arrived, looked at the crowd, promised to "escalate resource conversations," and left before Jo could assign him mop duty. Three volunteers from the university clinic came in with masks and the stiff, eager terror of people whose tuition had not prepared them to smell opened port rot. Mira put them on sterilizing tools and saying comforting things with accuracy.

Then Celia Rusk walked in carrying CommonFrame's afterlife in a rolling suitcase with a broken handle.

Mira saw her first as a disturbance in the line, the small involuntary widening people made around expensive wool and managerial posture even after both had plainly had a bad morning. Celia's coat was good cashmere under harbor grit, one cuff dark with sleet where it had dragged. Her silver hair had flattened on one side from sleep or something close to it. In one arthritic hand she carried a leather folio fat with printouts and old-school storage wafers. In the other she gripped the useless half-extended handle of the suitcase like she would rather sprain her wrist than admit it no longer telescoped.

Jo looked up from assigning blankets. "Well. The mausoleum has sent flowers."

Celia took in the room before answering: the intake cards, the blankets, the smell, the way people had already started trusting the ticket windows more than any official desk. Her face changed only at the edges. That was where she had always leaked the truth.

"CommonFrame terminated corporate housing at nine this morning," she said. Moderate voice, complete sentences, as if the terminal were a conference room and she refused to hand panic the chair. "My access survived long enough to pull archived maintenance maps. Not executive systems. The old service schematics. Batch weaknesses, manual override sites, substitutions if proprietary parts are unavailable."

Tavi laughed once, with no humor in it. "How generous of the sinking ship to cough up a diagram."

"I didn't bring it for your gratitude."

"Then why are you here."

The answer came a beat too fast for her usual poise. "Because the company card that paid for my apartment no longer works, because I know what those bodies will do without support, and because you can't improvise your way through a district-scale maintenance collapse on spite alone."

That shut him up for exactly two seconds.

"Watch me."

Mira stepped between them before the room could decide it wanted a fight more than a triage site. Up close Celia smelled faintly of wool, printer toner, and the ginger candies she kept in her coat pocket for a stomach she never publicly admitted went bad under stress. The folio in her hand was damp at the edge where rain had reached it.

"What kind of maps," Mira said.

Celia set the folio on the ticket counter and opened it with hands that stiffened when she spread them. Inside were printed service trees, serial range tables, notes from discontinued maintenance bulletins, hand-marked annotations in dark green ink. Mira recognized one of Celia's expensive pens from the smear on the margin. She had written these herself or corrected somebody else's lies into usefulness.

"Bargain lines only," Celia said. "The premium clients have lawyers and concierge medicine. They will land on their feet or on someone else's. These are the bodies that won't."

Jo snorted. "A revelation."

"No," Celia said, and for a moment the polish cracked enough for old hunger to show through. "An accounting."

A stretcher improvised from a door slab came through the hall before anybody could say more. Suri was on it now, jaw clenched so hard Mira could see the muscles flutter. The split at her ankle had widened. Clear fluid had turned pink at the edges.

"She took three steps and the seal went," Lin called from intake. "She said her calf feels full of bees."

Tavi bent to look. Celia moved to his side without asking permission. He almost told her to fuck off. Mira saw the words gather. Then Celia leaned closer to the ankle seam and said, "Do not flush from the lateral port if this is serial group K-12 through K-19. They reversed the collars after a lawsuit in East Ferry. You will drive gel directly into the sheath and lose the foot."

Tavi froze, then swore anyway because she was right and he knew she was right before he checked. He wiped the seam, found the serial cluster stamped half under the skin fold, and shot Celia a look so hostile it counted as acceptance.

"Fine," he said. "Hold the light."

Celia held the light. Her arthritic fingers trembled around the handle only once. Suri, flat on the door slab, looked up at Mira and said through gritted teeth, "If that woman is who I think she is, and if she saves my ankle, I'm going to be extremely annoyed on principle."

"Stay annoyed," Mira said. "It keeps blood pressure up."

They saved the ankle. Tavi threaded a manual seal around the split while Celia read old service notes without dramatizing the fact that the company had known for years which bargain runs liked to fail where and had gone on selling them anyway. Mira stabilized the calf from below. Pink fluid slicked her gloves. Suri bit a towel and kicked once hard enough to jar the slab. When it was over, she started crying from the release of it and apologized three times in a row because class shame was often louder than pain.

Jo handed her a cup of coffee thick with sugar. "You can cry in my terminal. It is one of the permitted fluids."

That was how Celia entered the room. She was not forgiven and not absolved. She was useful in a way nobody there could afford to refuse.

By late afternoon the terminal had its own ugly order. Urgent repairs in the back. Intake at the windows. Port cleanings near the bathrooms because the drains worked. Gabe at dispatch sending people to borrowed couches, church basements, one union hall whose steward hated CommonFrame more than he feared liability. Remy kept the lights alive with a face of concentrated insult. Lin's pile of intake cards rose higher and higher, clipped into categories nobody in city government had yet imagined would need names.

Outside, the queue bent along the seawall. Inside, the old departure board carried no routes at all, only taped signs and Mira's increasingly blunt handwriting.

NO OFFICIAL MAINTENANCE AVAILABLE HERE EITHER.

YES, WE KNOW THAT IS THE PROBLEM.

If you brought blankets, leave them by the east wall.

If you brought reporters, take them back out.

***

By evening the terminal had turned into the kind of place that made the clean version of events impossible.

The clean version had been simple enough for television. A corrupt biotech company manufactured conscious bargain bodies out of poor people's stolen residue, got caught, lost in public, and now faced justice. Villains, witnesses, verdict, consequence. It fit in a headline. It fit in Lin's testimony, in Mira's firing, in every clip of executives leaving the tribunal with their mouths arranged around the word misunderstanding.

The terminal did not fit. The terminal smelled like bleach poured over old salt and opened skin. It sounded like gulls, coughing, somebody laughing too hard at a stupid joke because the alternative was screaming, metal bowls clanging on concrete, a woman in bay three reciting bus stops that belonged to somebody else's childhood. The terminal contained borrowed limbs, unpaid labor, black-market tubing, municipal neglect, corporate technical manuals smuggled in by a woman who had helped keep the system respectable, and a line of people who did not care whether the narrative stayed clean as long as their frames held through the week.

Mira had not sat down in six hours when the coalition lawyer found her.

Asha Belen wore a white raincoat she never fully buttoned and had a habit of chewing one thumbnail flat while she read. Tonight the coat was speckled with terminal grime and camera dust. She stepped around two sleeping clients on the floor and caught Mira near the old snack kiosk where they were storing sterile wraps.

"I've been calling you all day," she said.

"I noticed."

"There is a bankruptcy hearing at nine tomorrow. We need you, Lin, and at least one of the donor families visible. If the company gets to define this as unfortunate market disruption instead of retaliatory abandonment, the administrator will liquidate parts stock by creditor class and we will spend six months filing motions while people disappear."

Mira kept counting packs as Asha spoke because if she stopped her hands would start listening too hard to the room. "I know."

"Do you? Because the feeds are already shifting. They love the terminal, by the way. They love poor people helping each other in a picturesque ruin. It lets every official in the city act like community resilience is a substitute for duty."

Mira looked at her then. Asha's lipstick had worn off hours ago. There was a coffee stain on her sleeve and one earring missing, which made her look younger and more dangerous than the courthouse version of herself.

"You want me on a dais while this place fills up."

"I want the stock unfrozen and the maintenance depots opened under court supervision."

"You want a story a judge can process."

"Yes," Asha said, too smart to deny it. "Because judges don't rule on smell and suffering. They rule on records."

From the intake window Lin called Mira's name, not loudly, just once. That was enough to put urgency in it. Mira followed the sound through the hall.

A shuttle van had backed up to the loading ramp. The logo on its side was a CommonFrame service mark half-scraped away in a hurry. Two frightened attendants in disposable coats were unloading bodies and trying not to meet anyone's eyes. One of them, a young man with a silver ring through one eyebrow and a cartoon dinosaur pin on his collar, said, "South Quay closure. They told us to transfer unresolved cases to municipal review. Municipal review said there isn't one."

"How many?" Mira asked.

"Nine in the van, maybe twelve more if we go back." He swallowed. "They shut the doors while people were still in chairs."

That was the legal win too. The company had stopped production because the tribunal forced it, yes. Then it had used the stoppage as cover to abandon every body already printed cheap enough to be disposable. Mira could feel the elegance of the public case dying under the loading ramp, one stretcher at a time.

Tavi and Celia were already at the van. Lin stood by the ramp with the registry ledger in both hands. Their face had gone very still.

"This one," Lin said.

On the second stretcher lay an overlap body Mira recognized from the registry sketches, not because she had met him before but because one of the living donor traces had been oddly specific. The left ear had a nick through the upper ridge, healed wrong. The rib scar curved pale under the gown. The registry note from chapter thirteen had read: probable donor chain to Tomas Vale, fishery debt resale, no informed consent, living. The overlap on the stretcher looked about thirty and no age at all. His eyes were open but not tracking. Every few seconds his right hand tapped twice against the rail as if finishing a sentence somewhere else.

"Name?" Mira asked the attendant.

"In system he's listed as salvage cognitive instability, batch LM-304C. No active user. He started seizing when they cut maintenance. We gave him one emergency dampener from the crash cart but that's done."

Tavi had his fingers at the nape port already. "Port's cooking. Lattice temp is up. He needs a deep flush and a manual reset."

Celia's mouth tightened. "If you deep flush on this series, you're going to shear whatever unstable overlap structure is still readable. The maintenance bulletins were explicit. It saves the frame and compromises mnemonic trace."

Asha had followed them to the ramp. Mira heard her inhale before she spoke. "Wait. This is one of the traceable bodies?"

Lin nodded once.

"Then we need documentation before intervention. Photos, serial verification, maybe a court monitor if I can reach one. If we preserve this condition and tie it to the donor file, it strengthens the argument that these are people being abandoned as property."

Tavi looked up from the port. He had reached the point in fatigue where his humor burned off and left only the blunt metal underneath. "If we preserve this condition, he's dead or blank in twenty minutes."

"Not dead," Celia said automatically, then stopped because the distinction sounded monstrous even to her.

Lin looked from Asha to the stretcher. Their fingers tightened on the ledger hard enough to whiten the skin over the knuckles.

"Will he know us after," they asked Celia.

Celia did not perform false comfort. "I don't know."

"Will he know anything if we wait?"

"Probably less."

Asha blew out a breath. "I understand the urgency. I'm not asking you to be cruel for optics. I'm asking you to think one move ahead. If the evidence degrades, the company says contamination. They say rogue repair. They say your terminal is where the facts got ruined."

"They were always going to say that," Tavi said.

"Yes, and I'm trying to stop them from winning with it."

The overlap on the stretcher made a sound then, small and wet and frightened. His eyes focused for half a second on the terminal ceiling.

"Boat's late," he whispered. "Tell my mother the blue bucket's under the sink."

The sentence went through Mira like a hooked wire. It was not because it was poetic. It was because it was so ordinary it hurt. Somebody's mother. Somebody's blue bucket. A domestic instruction that had survived all the grinding that should have erased it. The hand tapped the rail twice again.

Yaret Sanz arrived in the middle of that silence, escorted only by the weather and his own capacity to appear exactly where official trouble thickened. His dark coat was beaded with harbor mist. Damp marked the cuffs of his trousers. He carried a waterproof document sleeve and no visible backup, which was either restraint or theater.

People noticed him fast. The room's noise changed shape around his presence. It was not quieter. It was sharper.

"Inspector," Jo said from three yards away. "If you brought disinfectant, you can stay. If you brought paper, line the bird cages."

Yaret ignored the invitation and the insult with equal care. His gaze settled on the van, the stretchers, Celia, the open folio of maintenance notes on a bench. When he spoke, his voice was soft enough that everyone nearby leaned closer instead of away.

"I am here to inform you that these premises are not licensed for tissue repair," he said. "Any invasive intervention performed here may compromise standing evidence in the bankruptcy and tribunal continuations. Ms. Alves, you understand that."

Mira looked at him over the stretcher. "Do you have a licensed alternative."

"The city is assessing emergency capacity."

"That means no."

"It means the proper channels are being established."

Jo laughed in his face, a bright ugly smoker's bark. "The proper channels are in a van outside drooling on themselves."

Yaret's eyes shifted to the overlap body. "That case in particular should be preserved pending review."

"Preserved how," Mira said. "On ice."

"Stabilized without alteration if possible."

Tavi straightened. "If possible left fifteen minutes ago."

Asha stepped in before the argument could turn into a crowd sport. "Inspector, if the city wants preservation, then the city can place a monitor here now and release emergency stock."

"That authority is not mine."

"Funny how the authority to stop people is always somebody's and the authority to keep them alive belongs to a committee."

Mira barely heard the rest. She was looking at the body on the stretcher, at the skin around the nape port going angry pink, at the little involuntary tapping fingers, at Lin's face. She was looking too at Celia's charts, the notes that would let them use manual overrides and substitute parts, the same knowledge that would dirty every neat courtroom line between official abuse and unofficial rescue. If they used it, the story got harder. CommonFrame would say the network had become an unregulated salvage operation led by disgruntled ex-employees and contaminated evidence. The judge might believe some of it because judges loved a mess only when it stayed on other people.

If they did not use it, then what exactly had they won.

Lin answered before anyone else could.

"If you keep him unreadable on purpose," they said to Asha, to Yaret, to the room, "then you're still using him."

The sentence was quiet. It landed anyway.

Mira felt something settle inside her, not peace, nothing that pretty. It felt more like the click of a latch when a door finally gives up pretending it might swing both ways.

"Tavi," she said. "Do the flush."

Asha shut her eyes for one second. When she opened them she was angry, but not at Mira alone. Maybe Mira was not even most of what she was angry at. "Then I need everything documented as it happens."

"Fine," Mira said. "Document while helping."

She turned to Yaret. "You can witness or leave. If you want standing evidence preserved, start by preserving the person."

For the first time since Mira had known him, something almost like irritation passed visibly over his face. It vanished fast.

"This choice will complicate your case."

"This is my case."

Then there was no time for him at all.

They moved fast because fast was what the body required. Tavi opened the nape port. The smell came first, hot plastic under skin, then the slick shine of overheating gel. Celia read the override sequence from the maintenance chart, every number clean, every instruction stripped of corporate varnish now that it served actual hands. Mira held the shoulders and watched the monitor spike. Lin took the body's hand when the tremor started. Asha, to her credit, started recording serials, times, symptoms, every detail she had just begged them not to alter.

"Flush line," Tavi said.

Mira passed it.

"Not that one," Celia snapped. "Blue collar, not red. Red feeds sheath."

They switched. Gel clouded through the line, first pink, then a grayish milk threaded with black particulate where the cooked residue came loose. The overlap body arched once so hard Mira felt the force of it in her own wrists. His mouth opened on a sound with no room for language in it. Lin did not let go.

"You're at the terminal," they said, bent close. "You're at the water. Stay where the sound is."

Mira had spent years in claims rooms reducing bodies to defect categories and reimbursement brackets. She knew exactly how much easier it was to think clearly when the person in front of you had been turned into inventory first. Here, with salt wind lifting the edges of the drape and a lawyer calling out timestamps and a regulator standing useless five feet away, clarity came from the opposite direction. The person made the record messier. The person made the choice obvious.

"Temp's down," Tavi said.

"Again," Celia said. "Short pulse. Do not full-clear him unless you want a breathing blank."

Mira braced harder. Her gloves were slick. Hot gel slid over her thumb and cooled in the draft. Around them the terminal kept running. Someone laughed by intake. Someone else vomited in a bucket. Gabe was arguing with a driver about wheelchair space. Jo ordered three volunteers to boil more water and stop gawking if they were not going to become useful.

The second pulse took. The body on the stretcher jerked, coughed, and then sucked in a raw, surprised breath that sounded like somebody arriving late to his own life.

His eyes opened. For a second there was nothing organized in them. Then they found Lin's face.

"Blue bucket," he said, weaker now. "Under the sink."

"Okay," Lin said. Their own voice shook once and steadied. "We'll write it down."

Celia exhaled through her nose. Tavi sagged at the shoulders without fully stopping. Asha recorded the recovery time with the flattened stub of her chewed thumbnail pressed against the pen barrel, fury and relief fighting to a draw on her face.

Yaret slid the document sleeve back under his arm. "I will note that you proceeded over objection."

Mira stripped off one glove and reached for the next intake card.

"Note whatever you need," she said. "Then tell your city where to find us."

Outside, reporters were still clustered by the seawall hoping for a sentence that could fit under footage. Mira could hear the faint mechanical chirp when cameras reset focus. She imagined the version of herself Asha wanted on those steps tomorrow: composed, wounded, legible to power. Maybe that version would still be useful. Maybe she would even appear, if there was an hour to spare and someone else to hold pressure on the next opened seam.

But the terminal had made its demand: not purity, not symbolism, but maintenance. It was the ugly, repetitive labor of keeping people from being abandoned by the systems that had already profited from them.

She wrote a new heading across the top of the intake page in letters large enough to be read from three feet away.

CARE FIRST.

Then she called for the next body.
