# Premise Brainstorming

## Brief
Target: Booker. No external creative brief. The premise needed a hard causal engine, room for a large social cast, real moral consequence, and enough strangeness or necessity to avoid polished award-bait drift.

## Candidate Set
- A river-island midwife in Bangladesh is ordered to help officials verify every resident's identity before evacuation payments are issued, but half the island lives inside kinship records she falsified to keep children out of marriage markets and debt. Why it might work: intimate and civic at once, with lies-as-care at the center. Risk: could turn dry if the bureaucratic machinery overwhelms the human web.
- When Manila moves to clear the cemetery where thousands of poor families live among mausoleums, a widow who rents out abandoned graves starts opening coffins to find living relatives before the bulldozers arrive. Why it might work: unforgettable setting, dark humor, strong community engine. Risk: could tip into corpse-mystery sensationalism.
- Facing privatization, the woman who leads a Lagos crew of night-soil collectors blackmails the wealthy households whose septic tanks she empties, until one tank yields the bones of a missing child. Why it might work: fierce class vantage and a naturally filthy, intimate knowledge of the city. Risk: the shock value has to stay subordinate to character and system.
- In 1987 Guyana, a state surveyor returns home to mark forest villages for flooding by a hydroelectric reservoir and finds that the compensation rolls are tied to the lie his family told about his father's death. Why it might work: national project, sibling conflict, landscape under pressure. Risk: can slide toward worthy infrastructure realism if the ensemble is thin.
- After the state seizes a failing pilgrimage town in inland Mexico, the woman who has rented out miracles for thirty years learns the holy spring is being poisoned by the bottling plant her son manages. Why it might work: faith, commerce, maternal betrayal, town-scale stakes. Risk: symbolism could feel heavier than story.
- A deaf butcher's daughter in 1930s Buenos Aires becomes the labor court's indispensable lip-reader just as meatpackers start disappearing union men into the river. Why it might work: vivid institutional role and a sharp way to stage truth, performance, and class violence. Risk: period style could ossify into handsome historical fiction.
- In a near-future English city where tax debt can be paid in certified memories, a care-home cleaner brokers memories on the side to save her brother and is handed a childhood that may be her own. Why it might work: clean speculative pressure tied to class and inheritance. Risk: concept may arrive before flesh.
- During the last year before a Himalayan valley is opened to tourism, a monk ordered to destroy the monastery's ledgers of village grudges starts selling pages to outsiders hunting usable ancestry. Why it might work: archive as tinder, communal history as commodity. Risk: it may run essay-first unless the interpersonal fights are brutal enough.
- In 1970s Naples, the public-housing official who has padded every waiting list learns his daughter has vanished into an occupied tower block days before a televised flat lottery. Why it might work: corruption, family panic, urban ensemble, strong countdown. Risk: the civic-male frame is familiar terrain.
- A Maori woman in Auckland who runs court-mandated family meetings takes cash to keep children out of state care, then a supervised visit ends in a boy's death and every "fix" becomes evidence. Why it might work: moral ambiguity with immediate institutional stakes. Risk: social-work procedure could flatten the novel's heat.
- On a decommissioned Kazakh cosmodrome, the museum cleaner quietly moves squatters through condemned houses so they can qualify for relocation money when demolition begins. Why it might work: singular landscape and a strong link between failed futures and present survival. Risk: atmosphere may outrun plot.
- In a Black Sea sanatorium now housing pensioners and displaced families, the cook runs a swap market in medicines, ration cards, and wedding rings until privatization forces everyone to prove a past they no longer possess. Why it might work: broad ensemble with comic and tragic barter logic. Risk: without a tighter spine it could diffuse.

## Diversity Check
The set was pushed away from one prestige-safe lane on purpose. It ranges from colonial-adjacent and mid-century history to contemporary realism and light speculation; from river islands, cemeteries, forest districts, and pilgrimage towns to courts, housing offices, monasteries, and sanitation routes; from elderly women, working mothers, labor daughters, civic men, monks, and cleaners to both intimate family stakes and town-level upheaval. I also avoided stacking the same engine in different costumes: only one evacuation-through-records novel, one redevelopment-of-the-dead novel, one corruption-countdown housing novel, one concept-led speculative inheritance novel. Candidates that felt too close in underlying structure were either cut or kept out of the shortlist.

## Shortlist
- Bangladesh river-island midwife. Advanced because the premise fuses state paperwork, kinship, gendered survival, and climate displacement into one pressure system. It promises a dense secondary cast and many kinds of consequence without relying on a murder plot.
- Manila cemetery widow. Advanced because the setting is alive, the social scale is rich, and the dead create concrete leverage in every direction. It fell short of first place because the exhumation engine risks pulling the book toward revelation mechanics over human entanglement.
- Lagos night-soil union captain. Advanced because the class vantage is original and the premise has bite, filth, humor, and power. It lost ground because the bones discovery can overconcentrate the novel around a single sensational line of inquiry.
- Guyana dam surveyor. Advanced because it has national scale, sibling conflict, and a built-in countdown. It did not win because it feels one step closer to a recognizable literary-historical mode than the top choice.

Near misses: the Mexico pilgrimage-town novel had strong thematic heft but leaned symbolic; the near-future memory broker felt more designed than necessary; the Buenos Aires labor-court novel had real charge but more risk of turning mannered.

## Selected Premise
On a river island in Bangladesh that the government has finally ordered evacuated, an aging midwife is told to help officials match every resident to a legal name before compensation can be paid. She has spent thirty years inventing fathers, changing birth dates, and rewriting kinship to keep girls out of forced marriages and boys out of bonded labor, and now the adults she saved will lose homes, spouses, inheritances, and one another if her ledger stops being true.

## Selection Rationale
This premise feels most like a necessary novel rather than a decorated setup. Its central lie is not ornamental but structural: every chapter can generate new consequence from the collision between paperwork, love, debt, marriage, property, memory, and survival. It also gives the book Booker-friendly scope without sacrificing intimacy: an older working woman at the center, a whole community pressed through one morally contaminated act of care, and a conflict where truth is not cleansing but destructive.
