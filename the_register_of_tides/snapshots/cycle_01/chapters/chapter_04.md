# Chapter 4

The noon heat sat under Halima's tin roof like another body, large and breathless, turning every paper damp at the edges. She had spread Rahima Bewa's scraps of proof on the woven mat farthest from the cooking place: a widow certificate with the seal half eaten by mildew, a ration card folded so many times the corners had gone white, and a land-tax receipt yellow as old fat. Rahima herself crouched by the doorway with her smoke-dark shawl slipped off one shoulder, keeping a square powdered-milk tin upright between her ankles. She sold puffed rice from that tin at the ghat and polished the lid with ash every Friday, though the metal had long ago given up shining. Today she had brought it half full and laid two guavas beside Halima's knee, both of them worm-marked near the stem. She never came empty-handed, not even when hunger had made the bones in her wrists look separate.

Nura sat at the window with her good pen and a strip of old newspaper under her hand to keep the ink from taking on damp. She was copying names cleanly for whoever came because half the char could not write the same father twice. Outside, somebody was beating a wet lungi against a bamboo post. The river smell pushed in with the light: silt, fish oil, the soft rot of waterlogged jute. Every few minutes the tin roof gave a dry clicking sound as the sun shifted on it.

Halima squinted down at Rahima's receipt through her clouded eye and the good one both. The number was still legible if she tilted the page away from the glare. "This can stand if the rest of your line stands," she said. "Who can speak for the hut besides you?"

Rahima rubbed a cracked heel against her opposite ankle, scraping off a curl of white skin onto the threshold. "Who speaks for widows?" she said. "The goat, maybe. The grocer if he wants his money."

"Don't joke when your paper is this thin."

"I wasn't joking."

Nura did not look up. "Shirin's husband saw her roof go up after the last flood. He can say she's lived there."

Rahima sniffed. "Shirin's husband says whatever will get him a seat near a government man."

"He still has a mouth, and that's what counts this week." Halima flattened the receipt with the heel of her palm. The paper felt warm already, as if it were beginning to cook. "Come tomorrow before school. I'll see whether they let widows through before the men start shouting."

Rahima opened the tin and pushed it toward Nura without ceremony. "Take some before it goes soft."

Nura finally glanced over, and the quick hardness in her face loosened a little. "Keep it. You'll sell it."

"I've already chewed enough river dust today. Eat."

Nura took a pinch and put it in her mouth. At that moment feet slapped up the yard path, faster and heavier than any widow's. Kashem ducked under the low eave without asking and came in trailing the outside with him: fish scales stuck to his calf, wet mud drying pale on the hem of his lungi, sun caught in the chipped edge of one front tooth when he grinned. He always entered as if the room had been keeping his place warm.

"Dai-ma," he said, breath still up from the walk. "You've opened a court here now? Then hear a man's case before all the chickens start claiming they own land."

Nura clicked her tongue and moved the ink bottle farther from his hand before he could knock it over. "If you brought your papers, sit. If you brought your mouth, go cool it in the pond."

"Look at her. Two certificates and one pen, and now she's already the deputy officer." Kashem laughed for Rahima's benefit, because men like laughter when an old widow is looking at them. Then he crouched on his heels across from Halima and took a laminated card from the fold at his waist. He laid it on the mat with more care than he used on anything living. Under it came a folded tax slip and a photocopy so blurred the letters looked chewed. "I'm not here to waste your time. Aminul sir said I should bring whatever links the father's side to the house line. I brought what there is. You tell me what else is needed, and I'll get it."

Halima kept her hand off the card for one breath, then another. It was only plastic and paper, but the old mistake sat under the laminate as hard as bone. At the school two days ago, Aminul had asked the source of the father's name, and she had paused a fraction too long. The whole room had smelled it. Kashem had smelled it most of all.

She drew the card toward her. "I need to see the homestead receipt."

"You can see it after you tell me whether the name on the card is enough."

"Nothing is enough by itself."

"Fine. Is it the main thing, then?"

The chipped tooth was still showing. The grin around it had gone thin.

Halima unfolded the tax slip instead of answering. The ink had spread in one corner where rain had hit it years ago. "Who paid this last?"

Kashem let out air through his nose. "My father's cousin paid it when my father got sick. You know that."

She did know the cousin had paid. She also knew why the line had been made to bend that way, toward a quieter house and away from the other one, the one where a boy's body could be counted against a debt before he had learned to piss standing. She had written a safer name in the ledger on a night when the western bank was sliding in chunks and Rahima's labor blood kept trying to run under the reed mat and into the mud. A dead man without creditors had become father by the pressure of her pen. At dawn the baby had coughed mustard oil and river mucus and lived. That had seemed the larger fact.

Now the living man crouched in front of her, thirty-five and broad through the shoulders, waiting for paper to hold him the way arms never had.

"I know what I know," Halima said. "That doesn't mean the office will accept it because I say so."

"Then say it in the way they'll accept."

"Don't teach me my own work."

"I'm not teaching you. I'm asking you straight." He tapped the card with one finger. "At school you stopped when they said the name. Everybody saw it. If there's some clerical dirt, say it's clerical dirt. If the line is right, say it's right. I'm not a boy standing with an empty stomach asking for a sweet."

Rahima had gone still by the door. Even the tin between her ankles stopped making small metal sounds. Nura's pen hung above the paper without touching it.

Halima slid the card back across the mat. "Lower your voice."

"Why? So the roof doesn't hear?" Kashem laughed again, but there was no room in it. "If the line is clean, why am I the one whispering?"

Outside, a child's running feet slowed near the yard and then moved on. Halima could feel sound leaking through the bamboo slats behind her. On the char a private question only had to survive one wall before it belonged to everybody.

"Bring the cousin tomorrow," she said. "Bring the old receipt bundle from your trunk. If the card and land line won't marry by themselves, I'll see what witness can stitch them."

"Witness to what? That my father was my father?" He leaned closer. Fish and sun and the stale sour of river clothes came off him. "Dai-ma, don't make me beg for my own name in front of widows."

Rahima said, "Widows hear plenty even when nobody speaks to them."

Kashem gave her a quick sideways look, not yet angry, only impatient, the way younger men looked at women whose poverty had become part of the scenery. "This isn't your matter, Buri."

Rahima picked a grain of puffed rice from the lip of her tin and crushed it between finger and thumb. "Bodies make everybody's matter."

Halima did not turn. "Rahima."

Kashem heard the warning in her voice and fixed on it at once. "What body? What matter?"

"The matter of birth," Rahima said. "Since the paper men have started weighing fathers like sacks of grain, maybe birth matters again."

Halima felt the room contract. The roof clicks became distinct. The woven mat under her knees had a broken cane end that kept catching her skin through the cloth of her sari. "You came here about your own receipt," she said to Rahima. "Keep to that."

Rahima looked at her and then at Kashem. Her face never prettied itself for pity. Hunger had carved it too long for that. "He came here for certainty," she said. "You don't make certainty from a plastic card."

Kashem straightened. His performance fell away so quickly it was almost naked. "Then tell me what you make it from."

Halima spoke before Rahima could. "You make it from occupancy, witness, tax proof, and patience. The office wants a chain. That's all."

"That isn't all," Rahima said.

"Enough."

Rahima did not raise her voice. She never needed to. Her words came out as if she were laying tools in a line. "When this one came, the cord was around his neck twice. He slid blue and silent, and Halima had to hook a finger under the loop before he took air. The woman under him bled so hard the mat floated red at one edge. Three days later his navel still wept, and someone had to burn clean cloth and press it there. Someone tied black thread on his left ankle because the right one was swollen from the way he turned inside. You think a card knows that?"

Kashem stared at her.

Nura's chair gave a small scrape on the floor. She had not meant to move. Halima heard it and hated it because now the whole room had acknowledged the shift.

"Rahima." Halima's voice came out low and sharp enough to cut skin. "Stop."

But Rahima had waited too many years to be interrupted cleanly. "The name on that card wasn't there when the child couldn't latch and bit air till his lips cracked. The name on that card didn't sit up while fever turned the first milk sour. Paper fathers arrive dry. Real births don't."

Kashem's hand went to his own left ankle as if the black thread might still be there under the mud and hair. "How would you know that?"

Rahima's fingers closed over the tin lid. The metal clicked once. "I know what I know."

It was Halima's own line, thrown back at her and made filthier by precision.

Kashem looked from Rahima to Halima and back again. The room, which had held him since boyhood as something ordinary and legible, had suddenly grown corners he had never entered. He laughed once, a flat ugly sound. "What is this, then? Some joke old people kept for market days?"

"It isn't a joke," Nura said before Halima could stop her.

Kashem snapped toward her. "Then what is it?"

Nura's chin lifted. That was how tenderness first showed in her, as defiance. "I said it isn't a joke."

Halima put one hand on the mat between them. The woven reeds scratched her palm. "No one is laughing at you."

"That makes it better?" Kashem said. "If nobody's laughing, then what have you all been doing?"

Rahima looked at the floor. "Surviving."

The word lay there too bare. Halima wanted to strike it out of the room. Survival was too close to explanation, and explanation would drag the whole old bank with it: the lender's man waiting outside a laboring hut, Rahima younger and wild-eyed with fear, the dead fisherman whose line could be borrowed because his widow had already remarried away. Those facts had flesh on them still. If Kashem got hold of one, he would tear until he found the others.

She pushed the card toward him again, harder this time. "Take your papers."

He did not touch them. "Tell me who that woman was."

Rahima's face changed by almost nothing. Only the skin beside her mouth tightened. "A woman who bled."

"Everybody bleeds."

"Not for you they didn't."

Halima shot her a look that would once have stopped younger women in the middle of crowning. "You'll shut your mouth now."

Rahima met it. "Will I."

The challenge was tiny. It hit harder than a shout. Halima had pulled babies out of women while husbands wept, while mothers-in-law prayed, while storms took the roof nails one by one. Her voice had held in those rooms. Here it slipped in front of an old widow with cracked heels and a puffed-rice tin. Age had thinned more than muscle.

Kashem snatched up the laminated card and bent it in his grip without quite meaning to. The plastic sprang back. "Was I sold?" he asked.

No one answered quickly enough.

He nodded once, as if the silence itself were a form he had learned how to read. "Good. Fine. So we're at that stage."

"You weren't sold," Halima said.

"Then what?"

"Watch your tongue," she said, because it was the only command that came first.

"No. You watch yours. Don't start ordering me now like I'm ten." He slapped the tax slip against his knee to flatten it, then failed, because the paper had taken its curl years ago and kept it. "At the school I thought maybe there was some mismatch in dates. Some stupid government problem. But you look like that when she talks. She talks like this. So tell me what hole I've been living over."

Halima saw again the storm night in fragments that never aged properly. The hut wall had bowed inward with wet. Rahima had bitten through the end of her sari and spat pink threads into her own hair. The baby had come feet first to the knee, stalled, then twisted, and Halima had pushed two fingers into a place no pain should have had room left in and turned him by force while Rahima made the sound of an animal caught under timber. Later, when the child finally took milk, Rahima had held him with both hands open under him, not cradling, just supporting, as if one wrong pressure might send him back where he had nearly gone. Halima had believed then that a boy saved from one line of men was simply saved.

She should have known better. Saved children grew into men with mouths, claims, and shame.

"There are things," she said carefully, "that were done because the time was bad and the choices were worse."

Kashem barked a laugh. "That isn't an answer. That's a wall."

"It's what you're getting today."

"Today?" He leaned toward her again. "So there is another day. There is another answer."

She did not speak.

Rahima said, "Some truths don't ripen because men demand them."

"And some lies get old enough to stink from one end of the char to the other." Kashem turned on her fully now. The sociable swagger had burned off him. What was left looked younger, meaner, and far less steady. "Why are you talking about my navel and my ankles? Why do you know whether I bit air or milk? Tell me that much."

Rahima's underfed throat moved. She was not a woman who wasted gestures. Even swallowing looked costly on her. "Because some things happen in front of a person and never leave."

"That happened in front of you?"

She said nothing.

Kashem took one step toward her. Nura stood up so fast her stool tipped and hit the wall. Halima planted a hand on his wrist before he got another step.

His skin was hot from walking. The rope-burn ridges across his palm caught at her fingers. For one second the years between them folded the old way: she was the woman who had scrubbed mud off his knees after fever, who had slapped fish bones from his child's throat, who had once smacked him behind the head for throwing stones at a pregnant goat. Then his whole arm went hard.

He shook her off.

Not roughly enough to throw her. Worse than that. He removed her the way a man removed something dirty from his sleeve.

"Don't," he said.

The word landed in the middle of her chest with more force than if he had cursed.

Nura moved around the stool and put herself between Kashem and Rahima without seeming to mean to protect either one. Her voice came fast, already revising itself. "You don't need to stand over her. If you want to shout, shout from there. Or go shout outside and let the whole char earn the story properly."

"You knew?" Kashem asked.

"No."

"You swear that?"

"I'd swear it if I thought you'd hear anything besides yourself right now."

He stared at her, then at Halima. "How many people know?"

Halima answered too quickly. "Not many."

His face changed again. The answer did what silence had not. It made the hidden thing countable.

"Not many," he repeated. "So there is a number. I walked around all these years and there was a number of people carrying my beginning in their mouths."

Outside, the woman beating clothes had stopped. Halima could hear only the soft tin crackle overhead and the goat tied near the back fence pulling at its rope. The whole afternoon seemed to lean in.

"Kashem," she said, keeping her voice level by force. "Listen to me. Your claim isn't dead. Do you understand? Paper trouble is not the same as no claim. Bring the cousin. Bring whoever saw you raised in that house. I can still work on the line."

"The line." He said it as if it were something greasy he had found in his rice. "You want me to walk around gathering witnesses to a line when you won't tell me whether the line is mine?"

"Yours is the life you lived."

"Don't give me old-woman smoke."

It stung because she had nearly given exactly that. She pulled her sari end back over her shoulder, buying a second. "The house you grew up in is still the house you grew up in. The river doesn't change that."

"No. But pens do, maybe."

Rahima said, very quietly, "Pens only do what hands dare."

Halima rounded on her then. "If you have courage today, where was it twenty years ago? Ten? Last year? Don't sit in my doorway and grow a spine out of somebody else's deadline."

The words came out harsher than she intended. Rahima took them without flinching.

"I was where women like me stay," she said. "At the edge. Fed enough to keep moving. Silent enough to be useful."

Kashem let out a breath that sounded scraped raw. "Useful to who?"

Again the room failed him. Again nobody answered the whole way.

He looked down at the papers in his hands as if they might rearrange themselves if he stared hard enough. The photocopy trembled. Halima could not tell whether his hand was shaking or whether the air itself had thickened enough to make everything unsteady.

"I thought," he said, and now the child-plain note had entered his voice, the one he would have died before using in front of men at the tea stall. "I thought I was coming here to ask which document needed a stamp."

No one had anything decent to give that sentence.

He laughed once more, but it broke in the middle. "Look at me. I even washed before coming because I thought I had to sit in front of paper." He scrubbed one palm against his chest and left a dark fish-oil smear on the cloth. "And all this time the paper was sitting in front of me."

Halima heard, under the words, every past kindness shifting shape in him. The extra ladle of rice Rahima had once pushed toward his plate at a funeral. The way she had turned away fast whenever he caught her looking too long. The way Halima herself had scolded him harder than other boys, then slipped him work mending net floats when fishing was bad. Mercy looked different once a grown man decided it had been management.

"Sit down," she said. "You're standing like a fool in a hot room. Sit, and we'll sort what can be sorted."

He looked at the place on his wrist where her hand had been and then at her face. "Was I always work to you?"

The question moved so deep and so fast through her that for a second she could not find its edges. Behind it sat the whole ugly account of her life. Women on mats. Girls hidden in aunt-lines. Birth dates shifted to dodge one danger and land in another. Children becoming evidence. She had called it help because there had been no cleaner word and no cleaner act available.

"No," she said.

"Then what was I?"

She should have lied better. She should have used the brisk public voice that had gotten people through hemorrhage and fever and birth rooms full of bad husbands. Instead her private one rose, the one too full of touch and old weather. "A child I was trying to keep breathing in more than one way."

He flinched as if that were the worst answer yet.

"Fuck," he said softly. Then louder: "Fuck."

Nura shut the window panel with one quick movement. It did nothing for the sound that had already left.

Kashem stuffed the card and slip back into his waist fold badly, corners sticking out. "Don't come to my house with witnesses," he said to Halima. "Don't send anybody. If I need official help, I'll go to the officer. If I need lies, clearly this char is full of them already."

"Kashem."

"No." He stepped backward toward the yard. "And you." He pointed at Rahima without looking at her directly. "If you've got some story about me, keep it unless you're ready to say it with your full mouth. I'm not taking scraps."

Rahima's gaze did not drop. "Then don't take scraps."

That infuriated him more than pleading would have. He pushed out under the eave and nearly collided with the bucket by the door, caught it with his heel, and sent a sheet of water slapping across the yard. Then he was down the path, shoulders tight, walking too fast for the mud and not caring what the mud did to him.

Halima rose after him. Her knees complained, and the mat's pattern had printed itself into her skin. She stepped to the doorway and called his name once.

He did not turn.

She called again, louder. Halfway down the path he stopped. For one impossible second she thought he might come back, that she still held enough of him to bring his body around if not his trust. Instead he stood with one hand at the knot in his waist cloth, making sure the papers were still there, and looked over toward the western edge where the poorer huts sat lower and meaner against the sky.

Rahima's shawl, dark with smoke and age, was visible behind Halima's shoulder from where he stood. It was no more than a patch of cloth in the doorway. Yet Halima could see the thought crossing his back even at that distance: every old look, every shared silence, every piece of food pressed on him by the wrong hand had acquired a second meaning all at once.

Then he spat into the mud and kept walking.

Nura came up behind Halima but did not touch her. "He'll talk," she said. "Maybe not today. But he'll talk."

"Everybody talks."

"I didn't mean gossip."

Halima let the yard settle around her. The spilled bucket water carried a thin stripe of mud toward the ducks. A fly had found the cut side of one guava. From inside came the papery smell of damp documents and puffed rice gone soft in heat. She could feel the chapter of the day closing wrong, the way a wound closed around grit.

When she turned back, Rahima had not moved from the doorway. The old woman's hands were folded over the tin now, not protective, only tired. Up close the skin around her nails was split in half-moons from washing and ash.

"What did you think you were doing?" Halima asked.

Rahima answered without heat. "Opening my mouth before the river takes the bank and leaves me with it still shut."

"You nearly named him."

"Nearly is what you still have."

Nura stooped, righted the fallen stool, and set it by the wall with more care than the act deserved. Her face had gone young in a way anger usually kept hidden. "Is what she said true?" she asked Halima. "Not all of it. The part that matters."

Halima looked at the mat, at the place where Kashem's card had lain and warmed under her hand. The space was empty now except for a smear from his thumb. If she answered Nura directly, one false father would begin speaking to another inside the same room. She did not have strength for both.

"Bring me the dry cloth from the shelf," she said.

Nura's mouth tightened. Stress made her practical before it made her soft. She turned, fetched the cloth, and said nothing more.

Rahima gave one short breath that might have been contempt and might have been pity. "There. That is your answer to everything. A task."

Halima took the cloth from Nura and used it to wipe the damp from Rahima's land receipt though the paper was not wet enough to need it. Her hands wanted work or they would start remembering too cleanly. "My answer kept him alive."

Rahima's voice stayed level. "Alive into what?"

The question scraped. It had scraped before, in other forms, from other mouths. Jamila's perhaps, years later. Nura's soon enough. Halima was tired of being asked for the end of a road she had only ever built one dangerous step at a time.

"Into a chance," she said.

"Chance is not the same as belonging."

"No. It isn't." Halima folded the cloth once, twice, until it was a hard small square in her hand. "And truth isn't the same as mercy either. We knew that when you let me write what I wrote."

Rahima's eyes, dim and stubborn, settled on her. "I let you because I was bleeding and afraid and twenty-three. You kept choosing it after that."

That was fair enough to hurt.

Nura set the ink bottle back upright in the corner where it had been safe before Kashem came. "If he goes straight to Aminul with this mood, it's going to explode at the school."

"I know."

"Then what are you going to do?"

Halima looked out through the doorway to the path Kashem had taken. The mud still held the crisp edges of his heels where he had pushed off in anger. Beyond the middle houses the land sloped away westward, poorer with every step, until it gave itself up to scrub, huts, and the river that took what was least defended first. Somewhere down that line Kashem would be walking and rewriting his own childhood at speed, fitting fresh suspicion over old memory until nothing sat where it had sat an hour ago.

She had once thought the hardest part of cord-cutting was the blade: making one clean motion with a hand that knew it held the difference between attachment and breath. Age had taught her otherwise. The hard part came after, when the child kept living and the cut kept traveling.

"I'm going to school before him tomorrow," she said.

Nura frowned. "To tell Aminul?"

"To see what can still be held shut."

Rahima made a dry sound in her throat. "Nothing stays shut now."

Halima did not answer. She bent over the widow certificate again, though the letters had blurred in her clouded eye. Outside, the roof clicked once more in the heat. From far off came the thin engine whine of a boat turning somewhere in the channel. In the lane a pair of boys shouted, then quieted, then shouted again. Life on the char had not stopped. It had only changed its story about one man.

By evening, Halima knew, Kashem would remember every kindness done to him by the adults in this room and hold each one up to the light like suspicious paper. He would not yet know what the hidden thing was. He would know only that it had shape, that it belonged partly to his body, and that other people had been living around it for years as if it were ordinary. Sometimes that kind of ignorance bit deeper than truth. Truth at least arrived in words. Suspicion had to feed on whatever it found.
