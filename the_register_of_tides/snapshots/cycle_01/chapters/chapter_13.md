# Chapter 13

By the time Halima reached the schoolroom, Aminul had pushed the children's benches into two hard ranks and wiped the blackboard clean except for four chalk headings: NAME, RELATION, YEARS IN HOUSE, WITNESSES. The room smelled of old dust, damp jute, and the sour chalk children sucked when the teacher's back was turned. There had been no rain since yesterday, but the river still made itself known. From the eastern side of the char came, now and then, the low collapsing sound of bank giving way somewhere out of sight, as if a huge wet mat were being dragged over earth.

Nura sat at the teacher's table with ruled paper stacked by her elbow, the small burn mark on her wrist showing each time she dipped her pen. She had tied her hair back too tight. Since the trip to the neighboring char she had moved with a colder economy, as if she had spent a day looking for a father and come back with no use left for softness. She did not look up when Halima came in. She only said, "He's made groups," and slid her chin toward Aminul.

Aminul stood near the board with his folders held a little off his shirtfront to save them from sweat. Even in this heat he had managed a pressed collar. Chalk dust sat on one cuff. "The clerk sent word from the mainland," he said. "The magistrate may come the day after tomorrow. If not then, the next morning. We don't have room for confusion now."

Halima put her palm on the nearest bench. The wood still held the sticky warmth of children. "A house isn't an exam answer," she said.

"No," Aminul said. "But if five people from one yard give me five versions, the affidavit will tear in the first question."

He had arranged the first pile already, with weak papers on top and difficult dependencies underneath, the households most likely to split open when asked to sound official sitting at the very front. He did not say that part aloud, but Halima saw it in the order of the names. He had learned the island well enough to sort danger. That, more than his folders or his cautious beard, made her distrust him.

The first group came in with their hesitation still on them: Rashid with net tar dried black on his cuffs, his wife Parvin carrying their younger boy on one hip, and Khodeja fupu a half step behind them, counting the tablets knotted in the corner of her sari by touch. Khodeja had no business in a schoolroom this early; she usually spent the morning on Rashid's veranda sorting lentils with her bad eyes close to the grain, pausing every few minutes to spit clove into the yard. Today she had oiled the white hair at her temples flat and put on a blouse with one intact hook. She had come as if appearance could keep her from being discussed like a cracked pot.

"Sit," Aminul said, then corrected himself when he saw Khodeja searching for the bench with her feet. "Please. Slowly."

Khodeja lowered herself with both hands and a small animal sound she tried to hide inside her throat. Halima had heard that sound from women laboring, from old men with water in the lungs, from goats dragged by a broken leg. It was the body's unwilling truth. No paper ever held it.

Aminul glanced at the page. "We'll start plain. Give full names, then the relation, then how long you've stayed under one roof. After that I ask the questions the magistrate is likely to ask."

Rashid said his own name first, then Parvin's, then the two boys. When he got to Khodeja he paused just long enough for Halima to feel it in the bench under her hand.

"She's my father's sister," he said.

"And she lives with you?" Aminul asked.

"She stays there," Rashid said.

Parvin shifted the child higher on her hip. "Mostly," she said. "Sometimes she goes west to Khaleka's place."

Khodeja's fingers stopped moving over the medicine knot.

Halima turned her head. "When did she last go west?"

Parvin did not look at her. "I mean, she could. If needed."

Aminul kept his voice level. He was careful like this when he knew a floorboard was rotten. "Listen to me properly. An adult dependent with no clear paper line creates questions. I'm not making a moral point. I'm telling you the risk. If you include her, you need a clean answer for why she's part of the household and who can witness it. If you don't include her, then don't include her anywhere. Don't wobble."

The younger boy had found the chalk tray and was scraping at it with one dirty fingernail. The sound set Halima's teeth on edge.

Rashid said, "The share gets thinner if the household gets bigger. Everyone knows that."

No one answered. The sentence lay in the schoolroom like a bad smell.

Khodeja spoke before Parvin could. Her voice had the rubbed-down grain of an old broom. "Leave me out, then. I can sit with Khaleka till the list goes through. I'm old. I don't need much."

Parvin said too quickly, "No one said that."

"You said enough," Halima said.

Nura's pen stopped. Even Aminul looked up from the page.

Halima stepped closer to Khodeja. She could smell clove, stale cough syrup, and the faint sourness of old cloth dried too slowly. Last winter she had wrapped the old woman's shin after a fall from the veranda step; the skin there had torn like wet paper and healed shiny. Rashid's wife had changed the bandage each morning before cooking. Halima had seen the yellow medicine stains on the family's washing line for a week. Now Parvin stood three feet away pretending this woman floated in and out like weather.

"Where does she sleep?" Halima asked.

Rashid stared at the floor.

"Tell it," Halima said.

"Near the rice drum," he muttered.

"Who gives her tablets?"

Parvin swallowed. "I do."

"Who washed her when she couldn't bend after the fall?"

Parvin's face tightened. "I did."

Halima put her palm flat on the table, not hard, but enough to stop the room from sliding further into cowardice. "Then don't sit here teaching your own mouth to forget her."

Aminul rubbed the chalk dust from his thumb onto his trousers. "And if the magistrate asks why she has no supporting line through husband or sons?"

Halima did not turn toward him. "Then they say the truth. Her husband died. Her son went under in flood season and came back bloated to another char. She has eaten from this house since before that boy there stopped pissing the bed."

The younger boy looked up at the word and grinned. Khodeja did not. Her eyes had gone watery, but she was too proud to let tears fall in front of the others. She only pinched the knot of tablets until the pills made a hard little shape against the cloth.

Aminul said, "Truth isn't the only issue. Sequence is. If one witness says she arrived after the son died and another says before, the clerk will start digging for contradiction. That's what I'm trying to prevent."

"Then prevent contradiction," Halima said. "Don't prevent the woman."

For a moment Aminul looked tired enough to drop the official courtesy from his face. Then he nodded once. "Fine. Again." He lifted his pen. "Rashid. Start from the top. Full names."

This time Rashid said Khodeja's name without swallowing it. The words were not warmer for being repeated, but they stayed in the room. Halima took what she could get.

When the group stood to leave, Khodeja gripped Halima's wrist with fingers dry as fishbone. "Dai-ma," she whispered, keeping her mouth turned away so the others would not hear, "don't waste the government's tin on me."

Halima bent close enough for the old woman to smell the onion she had cut before leaving home. "You hush. You've taken enough less in this life."

Khodeja made a face that might have been agreement or phlegm and shuffled out, one hand still pressed to the medicine knot like she could count herself there if nowhere else.

The next households came faster. Once people understood the rehearsal was real, they did not treat it like advice. They treated it like weather. They braced.

By midmorning the schoolroom had grown hot enough that the chalk lines on the board began to sweat into the paint. Aminul called names, Nura copied statements in a cleaner hand than anyone else on the char could manage, and Halima listened to lives being pressed flatter and flatter so they would slide more easily into official ears.

Bilkis came in with her son Samu on her lap, smelling faintly of stale milk because she had weaned him too quickly in hopes of shrinking herself back down before the hearing. The boy banged a spoon against his own ankle and laughed each time the bone answered. Aminul asked for the marriage date. The room shifted the way rooms always did when sex had to be turned into calendar language in front of men. Bilkis picked at the loose thread on her sleeve.

"He was born early," she said.

From the doorway her mother-in-law said, "Not that early."

Aminul closed his eyes briefly. "Please don't help from the doorway unless asked."

"I'm asked every day by the village," the older woman said, but she shut her mouth after that.

Halima watched Bilkis's face. Shame had put a shine on it that was not sweat. She remembered the birth: the child slippery and furious, the husband not yet fully hers in the eyes of the papers, the mother-in-law counting months more angrily than she had counted prayers. Back then Halima had done what she always did. She had wrapped the baby, cut the cord, and dared the household to love him faster than the arithmetic.

"Say this," Halima told Bilkis, not unkindly. "The marriage paper came late because the imam was marooned by flood. You entered the household before the paper, not after the man. That's common enough that no one can prove it strange."

Aminul's mouth tightened, but he wrote it. He knew as well as she did that there were two kinds of truth on the char: the one bodies remembered and the one offices could survive. Most days the two hated each other.

Jalal's household came just before noon and left a worse taste. Jalal himself wore a lungi with engine grease at the knee and kept cracking his knuckles as if the sound might harden him. His wife Mina had painted her toenails red once for Eid and the color still clung in stubborn islands. With them came Mina's younger sister Munni and Munni's daughter Tuli, who carried a school slate wrapped in a scarf and stared at the room as if she expected to be tested on letters.

Munni had a habit of smoothing the child's hairline with a damp thumb every few breaths, even when it was already flat. The girl tolerated it with the patient contempt of children who know their mothers are frightened.

Aminul asked the questions in order: names, relations, years in the house, witnesses.

Jalal answered for everyone until Aminul stopped him. "Let each person speak for themselves," he said.

Mina said Munni had been with them since her husband disappeared downriver on dredging work. She said it in a thin voice, as if disappearance were a technical term and not the shape of half the men's futures on the chars. When Aminul asked whether Munni and the child were part of the household claim, Mina's mouth pinched.

"She helps with work," Mina said. "She stays because where else will she go? But it's not permanent in that sense."

Munni nodded too fast. "I can stay elsewhere till the hearing if that helps."

Tuli looked up at her mother. "Where?"

No one answered. The schoolroom had gone still except for a fly worrying itself against the window grille.

Aminul said, careful again, "If they're in, they're in. If they're out, they're out. If I present them as temporary labor and a visiting child, you all have to say the same thing without breaking under questions. Understand me."

Nura read back what he had written, her voice flat with disgust she kept tucked inside grammar. "Munni Begum. Temporary resident. Supports herself through domestic help. Child not economically dependent on claimant household."

The sentence made Tuli frown. "I eat there," she said.

Jalal snapped, "Quiet."

But Tuli had already stepped into the crack. She hugged the slate to her chest and looked at Nura instead of the adults. "I sleep by Khala, near the wall where lizards fall from the tin."

Even Aminul's pen paused.

Halima felt the whole room tilt toward the child and away again. That was witness preparation too: teaching a girl exactly when adults preferred her to stop existing.

Munni laughed once, a dry wrong sound. "She's just saying whatever she sees."

"She sees where she sleeps," Halima said.

Mina rounded on her, all the strain of the last weeks showing through the careful wife-face. "And if we list every hungry person who has slept on our floor this year, what then? Will the government give us another room at Nabinpara? Another cooking pot? More rice? Or do we all stand in line for one smaller mercy?"

It was the first honest sentence spoken in the room all day, and because it was honest Halima had no easy answer ready.

Aminul took it as permission to keep going. "This is what I'm saying. The magistrate won't reward confusion. He won't reward generosity either, if it can't be defended. If Munni and the child are permanent dependents, then say that and stand by it. If not, don't improvise tenderness in the hearing and expect the file to carry it."

Munni stared at the board. Nura's copy lay on the table, the words temporary resident still wet enough to shine. Halima remembered Munni three months earlier with blood up to both wrists, helping press Mina's belly after the youngest was born, steady as a trained hand though no one had ever paid her for the steadiness. She remembered Tuli asleep under the same mosquito net as Mina's sons while the rain beat the tin all night. Temporary, Aminul had written. As if storm seasons could be measured cleanly from inside an office.

"Cross that out," Halima said.

No one moved.

"Cross it out," she said again, this time to Nura.

Nura met her eyes for a second, then drew a hard line through temporary resident so deep the nib nearly tore the paper.

Jalal looked from one face to the next and found no rescue. "If we claim them," he said, "and the share breaks smaller, who's feeding everybody after?"

"You already are," Halima said.

"Not under a clerk's eye."

That was the whole thing. Men could live one life in a yard and another in a file for years, but once an office stared at the join between them, the seam began to bleed.

The call to noon prayer drifted thinly from the mosque, and for a few minutes the schoolroom emptied enough for breath. People stood outside in clumps under the hard white light, arguing in low voices they imagined the room could not hear. It heard everything. Children passed at the doorway and peered in at the blackboard as if it were a game. One of them had drawn a boat in the corner of the last page Nura had copied. She blotted it out with the side of her hand before Aminul saw.

When Majed and Jamila came after the break, they brought the opposite problem: too much control. Jamila had dressed for seriousness in a washed sari with a narrow dark border and sat so straight her spine looked nailed together. Majed smelled of engine oil even after scrubbing. He kept running his thumb over the split skin beside his nail as if counting how much work waited at home.

"Please write it exactly as the papers say," Jamila told Aminul. Her voice was calm enough to cut skin. "There won't be any confusion from our side."

Halima heard the cost in that there won't. Jamila had built a whole adult life inside a paper lineage that had once saved her. Now she spoke of it like a wall she meant to keep standing even if someone had to stay outside it. Halima could not blame her. That was the rot underneath every rehearsal. Nobody here was wicked enough for the work being asked of them, but most were frightened enough.

By afternoon Aminul's blackboard had turned into a map of subtraction. It was not whole households gone at once. It was smaller things. A widowed aunt shifted to a cousin's yard for "a few days." A sister renamed hired help. A boy whose mother had remarried told to sleep at the ghat with his fishing uncle till the hearing passed so no one would have to swear he belonged under the first roof. Every adjustment was tiny when spoken aloud. Together they made the char feel meaner than it had in the morning.

Halima had spent thirty years doing the opposite work. A girl abandoned after marriage came back with a swollen belly and no safe father's name for the child, so Halima found one that would hold. A nephew whose brother died on a boat began eating from his uncle's pot, and she taught the household to stop saying guest. A widow moved her mat closer to the kitchen and became, by repetition, part of the family. It had never been clean work. It had been stitched from fear, compromise, pity, and the practical knowledge that hunger did not wait for legal elegance. Still, it had pulled people inward. What Aminul called rehearsal was teaching the island a different hand movement entirely.

When Munni and Tuli came back in near the end of the afternoon to "confirm the revised version," Halima knew the lesson had taken.

Munni did not sit this time. She kept the child's scarf bundle in one hand and spoke with a politeness so flat it made Halima's back ache.

"Tuli will stay with my elder cousin near the tea stalls for a few nights," she said. "I'll help Mina apa in daytime only. That's cleaner."

Tuli had one blue clip left in her hair; the other hung from the scarf by a thread. She did not cry. She only pressed her cheek to Munni's hip and looked at the floor with the exhausted dignity of a much older person.

"Cleaner for who?" Halima asked.

Munni gave the small shrug women learned after too many humiliations. "For the sentence."

No one laughed. Aminul scratched something onto the margin and refused to meet Halima's eyes.

When the room finally thinned, when the last claimant had gone to fetch a witness or hide from one, Halima stepped out to the veranda because the air inside had become unbreathable. The schoolyard was white with late light. Goats nosed at the fence posts. Somewhere beyond the houses a hammer rang on tin, a sharp practical sound from someone already preparing to move.

Aminul followed after a minute, carrying the board duster as though he had forgotten it was in his hand. Up close the care in his appearance had come undone. Sweat had darkened the collar seams. Chalk had settled in the trim of his moustache.

"You think I don't see what this is doing," he said.

Halima leaned one shoulder against the wall. The rough plaster caught at her blouse. "You see it and keep teaching it."

"I'm not teaching cruelty."

"No? Then what did we do all day?"

He set the duster on the windowsill with more force than necessary. Dust rose from it and drifted back into the room. "We prepared people for the kind of questions they're going to face whether you like it or not. If I don't force consistency here, the magistrate will force it there, in public, with no patience and less mercy."

Halima looked past him into the schoolroom. Nura was gathering the copied pages into piles, aligning their corners with sharp taps against the table. Each tap sounded to Halima like a door being shut.

"Mercy," she said. "You call this mercy."

"I call it triage."

The word hit wrong in her ear. It belonged to clinics, to road accidents, to choices made over bleeding people because there were not enough hands. Aminul had meant to sound practical. Instead he sounded like a man who had spent too long among categories and begun trusting them more than flesh.

"These aren't broken limbs on a floor," she said. "They're households."

"And households can still be excluded entirely." His own temper had frayed now; the balance in his sentences was gone. "Tell me I'm wrong. Tell me the magistrate will sit there all day and listen to every cousin, every foster child, every woman who came back from a vanished husband and never left again. He won't. He wants a list he can sign before the launch schedule shifts. I'm trying to get people through the mouth of a bottle."

Halima thought of Khodeja pinching her tablets through the sari knot, of Tuli saying where lizards fell from the tin, of Bilkis having to locate the shame of her son's conception on a calendar while men watched. The schoolroom had stripped ordinary acts of care and made them beg for classification.

"And to make them fit," she said, "you want them to practice leaving each other out before anyone even asks."

"They were doing that already."

"Not like this."

Aminul dragged a hand over his face and came away with chalk on the side of his thumb. "You think your way doesn't cut? It does. Lived complexity, as you call it, still becomes testimony in the end. It still becomes a signature. It still becomes a decision that pays one household and leaves another waiting outside appeals."

She heard the fatigue in him then, the sympathy his job never allowed him to display except as irritation. That was the worst of him and the best: he was not blind. He simply kept going after seeing.

"I know it cuts," she said. Her voice had dropped without her meaning it to. "I've been cutting and stitching this island together since before you learned to shave. But don't stand there and tell me this is truth against lie. It isn't. It's one kind of wound against another. It's imperfect recognition against strategic cruelty. And cruelty is easier to coach."

He did look at her then. Not as an officer. As a man who had perhaps been waiting for someone to say the ugliest thing plainly.

"Strategic cruelty may still get a family housed," he said.

"At whose door does the old woman then die? In whose yard does the child return when the cousin gets tired of feeding her?" Halima pushed herself off the wall. "You can clear a file by moving a mat. You can't clear the hunger that follows it."

For a second she thought he might answer with the hard institutional sentence, the one about limits and regulations and what could or could not be entertained. Instead he looked out toward the western huts where the poorer houses crouched low and separate, as if distance alone might make them invisible to the school wall.

"What would you have me do?" he asked, almost quietly. "Take every contradiction in raw and watch half the claims collapse?"

Halima did not answer at once. The truth was that she had no clean method to offer. Her own life disproved cleanliness. She wanted the magistrate to hear the mess of dependency exactly as it was lived: who carried water, who slept by whose wall, who came back hungry and was not turned away. She also knew what happened when men in offices met mess. They called it fraud and kept their pens dry.

Nura came to the doorway with the copied statements stacked against her chest. "Three households asked for rewrites," she said. Her voice was brisk enough to sound almost detached, but Halima knew the signs of strain in the girl now. She overcontrolled when frightened. "Khodeja fupu's bedding has been sent to Khaleka's cousin's hut on the west side. Munni and Tuli are going to stay near the tea stall. Bashir's stepson has been told to sleep on the boat till after the hearing, because apparently a child on water doesn't count the same."

She handed the papers to Aminul and went back inside before either of them replied.

That was the hearing arriving early, not on a launch or under a magistrate's seal, but in rolled mats, moved plates, and children redirected for a few nights so the sentence would come out cleaner when spoken aloud.

Across the yard, a woman passed carrying an aluminum trunk on her head while an old man walked beside her with a cane and no pace left in him. Near the gate, a boy dragged a bedding roll twice his own width through the dust, leaving a stripe behind him like something erased. The river kept giving its distant soft collapses from the east, patient as paperwork.

Halima stood with chalk grit on her palm and understood at last that the coming hearing would not sort truth from falsehood in any way that mattered to the people living here. It would reward whichever version of care could be made to sound orderly without breaking in the witness box. She had spent half her life pulling extra bodies inside when weather, men, and money tried to shake them loose. Now the schoolroom had begun teaching the char the opposite lesson. Households rolled the mat, shifted the child, said she only came sometimes. By sunset several households had already learned it well enough to act.
