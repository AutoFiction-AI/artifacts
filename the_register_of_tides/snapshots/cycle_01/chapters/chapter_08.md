# Chapter 8

By morning the floor had gone from packed earth to black paste. The men who had searched the house had left trunks open, baskets overturned, and the loft shelf half torn from the wall; the night rain had come through those new gaps as if it had been waiting outside for a way in. Mud sat in the heel dents of strangers. A brass plate leaned against a bed leg, furred with silt. One of Halima's clean birth cloths had slid into the doorway and drunk up the runoff until it looked like something used on a slaughter day. The house smelled of wet tin, mildew, old lamp smoke, and the sour paper smell that rose when ink started giving up.

Nura had already dragged the charpoy into the narrow stripe of gray light and was laying documents across the rope weave one by one. Her split lip had dried into a dark seam at the corner of her mouth. When she bent, Halima could see the small burn mark on her wrist flash pale against the wet brown of her skin, a little moon she had carried since she was thirteen and tried to lift the rice pot barehanded before it was off the stove.

"Not there," Halima said. "If the sun hits too hard, the paper will buckle."

Nura did not look up. "If the sun comes at all, we'll be lucky."

Her voice was flat from bad sleep and from holding pain in the mouth. She moved a marriage paper from the charpoy to an upturned tin trunk, then changed her mind and moved it back. The seal on it had already feathered at the edges. A yellowed land-tax receipt lay beside it, the numbers swelling loose from the page as if they wanted to float off and find a drier house.

Halima crouched slowly. Her back had stiffened in the damp. Every time she lowered herself she felt the old work in it, thirty years of crouching between women's knees, of bracing one shoulder against mud walls while babies came ugly or sideways or too fast. She picked up the tax receipt with two fingers under it, the way she would have lifted a premature infant out of soaked cloth, and slid a dry newspaper scrap beneath. The paper shivered. A corner tore anyway.

"Easy," Nura said.

"I know what's easy."

"Do you?"

The girl kept sorting. That was how the quarrel lived now, not in shouting but in the drag on every useful word. Halima heard the accusation under it: you knew men were coming, you knew rumor was thickening, you still sent me to the doorway with my hands and my mouth and no protection stronger than your name. There was no answer she could give that did not sound either false or small.

Water still dropped through the loosened tin seam over the storage corner. Nura had set a dented aluminum pot there to catch it, and each drop struck with a hard tick that made the room feel tighter. Outside, the char had the washed-out quiet that came after a storm heavy enough to flatten arguments for a few hours. No children yelled. No goat bells. Just a cough from somewhere beyond the lane, the creak of wet bamboo, the river talking low under everything.

On the mat by the wall lay the night's damage in little ruined neighborhoods: Nura's school certificate packet under a brass bowl, three witness statements curled into tubes, two vaccination cards clotted together, a widow's affidavit that had stuck to a piece of old sari cloth and come away with threads embedded in the stamp. Halima recognized the households by folds and smells before she recognized the names. Jamila always wrapped her papers with dried neem leaves against insects. Majed had once brought a receipt with engine grease thumbed into the margin. Rahima's few scraps carried goat smell and the stale ash of her hut. The men who had come searching had not cared which life belonged to which bundle. Once a house was declared dirty, everything inside it became the same heap under a boot.

Nura picked up the certificate packet and glanced at Halima as if daring her to speak first.

"Open it," Halima said.

"I was going to."

"Then do it before the gum sets."

Nura slipped a fingernail under the damp flap. Her careful hands were what always undid Halima. Even in anger the girl handled paper with respect, not the showy respect of officials who held files away from their bodies to protect their shirts, but the practical respect of someone who knew a torn corner could mean a missed exam, a failed application, another year gone. The packet opened with a wet sigh. The top certificate had blotched along the left side, but the name line still held.

"The serial number's there," Nura said. "The seal is ugly but maybe still visible."

"Maybe won't feed a clerk."

"Nothing feeds a clerk except another clerk."

The line would have been funny another week. Halima felt the shape of the laugh and let it die. Nura flattened the paper with the heel of her hand, then hissed when the cut in her lip split wider.

Halima reached automatically. "Let me see."

Nura leaned away so fast the certificate almost slipped from her hand. "It's fine."

"It's cracked open again."

"It's a lip, not a broken mast."

Halima let her hand fall. The skin around the girl's mouth had gone puffy in the night. There was dried blood on the edge of her chin that she had missed washing. Halima had seen worse injuries before first breakfast. She had also seen wounds that mattered because of who delivered them and who watched. This one had been made in her own doorway, by a man young enough to call her auntie if he wanted favor from her, and half the lane had seen it happen. That was what kept working at the cut. Not the force. The publicness.

She turned back to the papers because tasks were still the only shape care could take without begging permission. She spread old lungis near the wall where the air moved best. She pried apart a set of witness forms with the blunt side of a hairpin. She laid Nura's school papers under a cooking pot weighted with stones so they would dry flatter. All morning the house filled with the small sounds of salvage: paper lifted from paper, cloth wrung out, water tipped from bowls, Nura's short breath when a seal smeared, Halima's tongue clicking once in irritation and then stopping because irritation was a luxury for people whose damage was not already done.

By midmorning the light strengthened enough to show what was lost and what was only sick. That was the cruel hour. At dawn everything had looked equally doomed. Under a little light, distinctions began. A land slip could be saved if the ink had stayed inside the fibers. A birth date could survive even when a father's name turned blue and soft. A certificate with one dry corner might still be copied later if they got it to the mainland before mildew took the rest. Hope returned in fragments, and fragments demanded judgment.

Nura held up a folded receipt with two fingertips. "Whose is this?"

Halima squinted. Her left eye clouded first in damp weather. "Turn it."

Nura turned it toward the doorway. "No, the name is gone."

"Let me smell it."

Nura made a face but passed it over. Halima brought the paper close. Fish oil, rust, and the faint medicinal stink of kerosene. "Majed's. He keeps his receipt tin near the engine parts."

"You can smell a mechanic through mud now."

"I can smell carelessness through mud. Write his name on the back while you still know it."

Nura found a pencil stub and did as told. The movement steadied them for a while. Halima named households from scraps. Nura labeled the backs in her neat school hand before the pages dried beyond question. A ration card with the edge chewed by mice. A widow's petition folded into eight exact squares. A copy of a national identity card blurred so badly the face looked drowned. Each item came with a body attached inside Halima's memory: who had sweated over it, who had hidden it in a rice jar, who had begged her to hold it for a few days because their roof leaked worse than hers.

The labor calmed her until she went to the storage corner and saw the fallen basket of birth supplies.

The men had shoved their arms through it looking for hard covers and hidden compartments. Cotton rolls had burst and gone gray with floor water. The glass thermometer case had cracked under somebody's heel. A spool of clean cord had unravelled into mud like pale gut. Halima stood over it a moment too long, seeing not the objects themselves but the impatience that had handled them. Male hands in a birth basket always looked wrong to her, too quick, too sure there was nothing there worth reverence unless it could turn into leverage. She bent and picked up the cord anyway. Mud slid along it. She washed what she could in a bowl and set it aside for household use only. Not for births now. Not after the floor.

Nura was watching from the charpoy. "Leave that. We have enough ruined already."

"Ruined for one thing is not ruined for another."

"That's your answer for everything."

Halima wrung out the cord and hung it on a nail. "Because it's often true."

Nura stared at her a beat longer. Then she said, "They knew where to push."

Halima kept her back turned. "Everybody knows where a house will give if enough men lean."

"I don't mean the wall."

The tick from the rain pot marked the silence. Halima moved past it, went to the loft shelf, and reached behind the tin rice jar where the oilcloth bundle had been wedged after the search. She had hidden it there herself in the last minutes of confusion, when the house was full of strangers and Nura was shouting and some fool boy was laughing because he had found nothing but women's old blouses. The bundle was still there, heavier now from damp. Water had not soaked all the way through, but the cloth felt cold and wrong in her hands.

Nura got up at once. "You're checking it now?"

"Now is when the light is enough."

"Or now is when you finally believe they touched more than the trunks."

Halima set the bundle on the driest corner of the charpoy. The oilcloth had been wrapped and rewrapped over years until it remembered the shape of the notebook inside. She noticed the knot first. Not that it was untied, but that it had been tied back with the short end pulled on top instead of underneath. A small thing. A stupid thing to any eye but hers. She always finished the knot with the long strip tucked flat because loose corners caught dust and made the packet bulkier. Now the corner stood up like a tongue.

Nura saw her pause. "What?"

"Someone opened it."

"You're sure?"

Halima looked at the knot again. "I didn't leave it like this."

Nura's jaw tightened. "One of them."

"Or in the dark after they left. Or when I moved it."

"You don't believe that."

No, she did not. Not because of the knot alone. Because the oilcloth had a new smear along the inside fold, the kind left by a wet thumb pressed in haste. Because the packet felt slightly thinner on one side. Because the search had not been the blind rummaging of hungry boys after gossip, no matter how much shouting and kicking had dressed it that way. It had possessed blind parts, yes. Plenty of boot and spit and male fun. But someone in that room had also been looking with intention.

She untied the cloth.

The ledger lay inside, its black cover bloated at the corners, the stitching along the spine damp but holding. A few loose papers had been tucked around it to disguise the shape years ago, then kept there because on the char even disguises became storage. Nura leaned in over Halima's shoulder. Halima could feel the heat of her, the impatience, the old scent of coconut oil under rainwater.

The first pages of the notebook were safe. Oilcloth and thickness had done their work. Halima turned them with a thumbnail, checking the older entries: births from seasons when the river sat somewhere else, births in houses long gone, births whose fathers were dead, false, absent, or never written. Ink had feathered near the edges, but the dates still held. The names still held. Her own hand stared back at her from younger years, firmer then, less interrupted by pain in the knuckles.

Nura let out a breath Halima had not realized she was holding too. "It's there."

"The book is there."

"That's what I said."

"I heard you."

Halima kept turning. Halfway through the notebook she stopped. Between two thick original pages there should have been three thinner leaves folded twice and tucked deep, newer paper than the rest, copied clean because the old pages they echoed had gone faint with handling. She had not made those copies. Years ago she had found them already there after one of those bad monsoon stretches when too many women, helpers, and borrowed hands had been moving through the house to track every touch. Halima had kept them because they were accurate and because on the char nobody threw away a version of a truth that might one day need proving. The leaves were school-exercise paper, blue ruled, written in a careful hand trying hard to resemble formal script.

Now there were only the pressure marks where they had rested.

She checked again, slower. Turned back ten pages. Forward seven. Lifted the ledger and looked for the leaves stuck to the cover. Nothing. Only the original sheets, damp at the edges and clinging.

"What is it?" Nura said.

Halima did not answer at once. She was seeing the missing leaves in her head, not as paper first but as contents. Three copied extracts. Not random births. Not family stories from the loose, sinking western edge where names changed with every flood. These had all tied to the firmer eastern side, the part of Kadam Char that held a little longer each monsoon and therefore mattered more whenever officials started using the word settlement. One leaf covered the households above the broken tamarind stump, where the soil rose just enough that courtyards stayed dry two days longer than the rest of the island. One covered the strip behind Mofiz's fertilizer shed, where kin and in-laws had thickened beyond what the land could naturally justify. The last held two births and one altered widow-link from the lane near the mosque path, all people Mofiz liked to call his side when visitors came.

"There were pages with it," she said.

"Original pages?"

"No. Copies. Loose."

Nura frowned. "Of what?"

"Selected entries."

"Which ones?"

Halima turned the ledger toward the light even though the missing leaves would not come back by being stared at. "East-side households."

Nura understood fast. That was one of the girl's gifts and one of the things that tired Halima most. Quick minds did not let a person postpone consequence. "His side."

"The higher ground."

"His side," Nura repeated. "Say the name."

Halima shut the notebook. "Names are cheap. Proof is expensive."

Nura laughed once without humor. The split in her lip opened again. She wiped it with the back of her hand and looked at the blood as if it belonged to somebody else's argument. "He spreads a story that you've been selling names. Men come in here and tear up the house. Then the only missing copies are for eastern plots he keeps sniffing around like a dog. How much more expensive do you need it?"

Halima reached for the loose disguise papers tucked around the ledger. They were still there. An old clinic referral. A dead woman's vaccine card. A list of due dates in her own hand. That was what fixed it. If this had been flood work alone, the thin papers would have shifted together, blurred together, glued themselves in a lump. Instead the useless scraps remained and only the extracts were gone.

"Not flood," she said.

"Obviously not flood."

"Not boys grabbing whatever looked hidden either."

"So say it properly."

Halima looked up. Nura's face had sharpened in anger until she resembled Saleha for a beat so cleanly it knocked the air from Halima's chest. Not the whole face. Saleha had been fuller in the cheeks. But the eyes, the refusal to let a lie sit in peace once she had scented it. Halima's fingers tightened on the ledger cover.

"If I say it properly in this lane," she said, "by noon it becomes me accusing a chairman because my own house smells bad."

"Your house smells bad because they put their feet through it."

"And whose word carries further right now, mine or his?"

Nura did not answer because the answer was in the room with them. In the cracked thermometer case. In the wet witness slips that belonged to other households now regretting they had trusted her grandmother. In the cut on her mouth. A house could lose standing faster than it lost furniture. Once people started saying forger, even your own truths arrived needing apology.

Halima opened the ledger again and found the original entries the copied leaves had mirrored. The pages were there, but older, weaker, more difficult to use quickly. One line had bled so badly the father's altered name showed only as a thick black bruise. Another page had taken finger grease for years and gone almost translucent at the fold. The copies had mattered because they turned failing evidence into portable evidence, neat enough to wave in a room before anyone could question the source.

Nura followed her gaze. "Can those still be read?"

"By me."

"That's not the same thing anymore."

No, it was not. That was the whole trouble. Halima's memory had once been enough to place a child, settle a quarrel, keep a girl in school, push a widow into some lawful corner before men could bargain her away. But the island was being translated now. What she could hold in her mouth or in her skin no longer counted unless paper accompanied it, and paper had the loyalty of wet flour.

She traced one of the old entries with her nail, careful not to break the page. She remembered the birth without needing the date. The mother had labored against a bamboo post because the cot rope had snapped. Rain had hammered that night too, though not this hard. The child had come blue and furious. By morning the family was already talking about where on the eastern rise they might shift if the lower bank kept eating itself. The copied leaf had attached that baby, years later, to a household line sturdy enough to claim more than flood relief. Mofiz had stood near that negotiation smiling into his beard, acting as though land arranged itself naturally around the men who could read forms.

"How many?" Nura asked.

"Three leaves."

"Only those."

"Only those."

Nura sat back on her heels. "Then it wasn't the crowd. It was someone instructed."

Halima did not like the relief hidden inside the girl's certainty. Human minds preferred a thief to a disaster because a thief at least proved intention. Rain was harder. Rain destroyed without admitting interest. Yet she felt a version of the same relief herself, lean and bitter. Chaos had shape now. Shape could be watched.

"Who else knows those entries matter?" Nura asked.

Halima thought of Sufia's old comings and goings, her limping stride, the way she used to tuck paan under her tongue before long births and say it helped keep sleep off. She thought of Aminul, too new to know the house. Of Majed, who worried over bolts and engine screws, not genealogy. Of Jamila, who knew enough to fear all paperwork but not which part of the island another man's appetite lay on. She thought finally of Mofiz, slow in public, never hurried except where leverage was about to harden.

"The men who profit from east ground," she said.

"That's still a circle with one body inside it."

"Then keep the body in your own mouth till we can pin the cloth to it."

Nura looked at her as if deciding whether to spit the next sentence or swallow it. "You're always saying wait when waiting costs somebody else."

The words landed clean because they were not only about this morning. Halima felt them pass through older rooms: Saleha's marriage, Nura's papers, Rahima's years of silence, all the places where delay had worn the clothes of prudence while time quietly did a man's work. She might have answered sharply if Nura had been wrong. Because she was not, Halima said nothing.

They worked in the heat that followed. The storm light thinned into white glare. Tin began to throw it back down on them. Nura carried the driest papers outside to the line between the wall posts and clipped them with clothespins borrowed from blouses. From inside, the pages looked like a ragged prayer line: certificates, petitions, a child's immunization card, the blurred copy of an identity card whose owner would not know until later whether his face was still official enough to count. Halima stayed by the ledger and made herself list the missing leaves in order, not by outrage but by content. Household links. Birth dates. Widow transfer. East lane. Fertilizer shed strip. Tamarind rise. If she let anger lead, memory would puff and deform. She needed it flat.

A boy called once from the lane asking whether papers had survived. Nura answered through her teeth that people could collect after asr prayer, not before, and the boy retreated. Halima approved of the answer and disliked the pleasure she took in it. Nura's voice had already learned how to bar a doorway.

Near noon they ate cold rice mashed with salt and green chili because cooking would have meant more smoke in a house already full of wet. Nura stood to eat, leaning against the door frame, one eye on the hanging papers. Halima sat on the charpoy beside the open ledger. The rice tasted of metal from the bowl.

"If you tell Aminul now," Nura said around a mouthful, "he might note it before Mofiz builds another story over it."

"And what do I tell him. That papers I can't show him have been taken from pages I shouldn't even be admitting were here."

"He already knows you have things you have no business having."

"Knowing rumor and receiving accusation are not the same."

"He isn't stupid."

"No. Which is why I won't hand him a half-case that makes me sound panicked."

Nura licked chili salt from her thumb, wincing when it touched the split in her lip. "So we do nothing."

"We sort. We remember. We look at who suddenly grows louder about east-side land."

"That's not nothing."

"Then stop saying nothing."

The girl gave her a hard look and almost smiled despite herself. It vanished quickly, but the air in the room shifted a finger's width. Not repair. Not forgiveness. Only the old working rhythm returning for a moment because the task required two minds and two sets of hands. Halima would take even that.

After they ate, she sent Nura to bring in the sun-struck papers and exchange them for the damp ones. While the girl moved in and out of the doorway, Halima took an old school exercise book from the trunk bottom, tore out the least mold-marked pages, and sharpened the pencil stub with a razor blade. Her fingers protested. She ignored them.

Nura came back holding Majed's receipt by a dry corner. "This one might live."

"Put it under the bowl."

She saw the exercise pages. "What are you doing?"

"What paper failed to do."

Nura set down the receipt slowly. "From memory."

"From memory and from what remains."

"Will that stand anywhere official?"

"No."

"Then why."

Halima touched the ledger. "Because if a thief chose those leaves, I want the choice written before more weather arrives."

That answer satisfied Nura more than the earlier ones had. She came to the charpoy and stood at Halima's shoulder without speaking. Halima began with the simplest line: household name as publicly used, mother as bodily known, father as officially claimed, actual tie where relevant, child birth season, location marker. Not a replacement for the lost copies. A map of the loss itself. The pencil dragged on the damp page. Her hand shook once and steadied. Outside, wind moved through the clipped documents and made them tap one another lightly, like impatient fingernails.

She wrote the eastern households in the order she had first attended them. That was how memory sat in her, not by land parcel and not by office category. A breech birth near the tamarind. The widow who had pretended her sister's boy was her late husband's nephew because otherwise the courtyard would have split. The child with the cowlick everyone said proved a Sheikh man though Halima knew exactly whose body had sweated and cursed through that labor. The more she wrote, the clearer the pattern became. Mofiz had not wanted the whole ledger. The whole ledger was poison. Too much of it could drown any man who tried to swallow it. He wanted only the parts that could be used cleanly, detached from the rot of the rest, made to look like support rather than theft.

Nura read over her shoulder. "He's cutting the island down to the profitable pieces."

"He didn't begin yesterday."

"No. But he came into this house yesterday."

Halima laid the pencil aside. "Listen to me."

Nura folded her arms.

"If I go out now saying Mofiz sent hands for those leaves, he says the storm washed them, or I invented them after, or I'm trying to save face because nothing was found. People will believe whichever version lets them sleep. I won't give him that easy road."

"Then what road."

Halima looked past Nura through the doorway. From here she could not see the eastern rise itself, only the brightness that sat in that direction and the roofs catching more sun than the low west ever did. Even after a storm, the better ground dried first. That was the whole argument of the island condensed into weather.

"The road where he has to show what he already thinks is safe to show," she said. "Men get greedy with papers they believe are theirs. They wave them too early. They build on them. They mention them in front of the wrong ears."

"And until then?"

"Until then we keep count."

Nura pressed her tongue once against the cut in her lip, considering. "You trust greed more than justice."

"I trust greed to arrive on time."

That almost earned another smile. Not quite. But the bitterness in Nura's face rearranged itself into attention, which was better. She took the pencil when Halima held it out and, without being asked, ruled a margin down the side of the page for location notes. Her handwriting was steadier than Halima's. Halima named, Nura marked. Higher east lane. Shed strip. Tamarind rise. Married into Mofiz's side. Widowed under question. Borrowed child absorbed to household. The work grew a shape between them.

By late afternoon the house looked less violated and more stripped after surgery, still ugly, still smelling of wet things, but no longer helplessly open. The trunks had been shut. The driest papers were rewrapped in separate cloths with charcoal packets and neem leaves where possible. The unsalvageable scraps had been stacked in one corner for burning later, though Halima hated to think of fire touching names at all. Nura's school certificate packet sat beneath the brass bowl with a brick on top. Majed's receipt and two witness slips had lived. A widow's affidavit had not. Half a vaccination card had become nothing more than the ghost of a seal.

The ledger went back into oilcloth, this time with the new exercise pages folded beside it. Not proof. Not yet. But not emptiness either.

Nura watched her retie the knot the proper way, flat and tight. "If they come again?"

"They won't come the same way."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer I've got."

Nura touched the side of the bundle with one careful finger, almost reverent, almost angry. "I should've bitten that bastard harder."

Halima looked at the seam in the girl's lip, the dried blood she had finally cleaned off, the steadiness she had rebuilt by force over the day. "Next time," she said, "save your teeth for when it changes something."

Nura snorted. "You say that as if you know."

"I do know."

The girl's eyes met hers then, not soft, not healed, but level. It was the first truly level look between them since the search. Halima accepted it the way she accepted a dry corner on a wet certificate: not enough, but enough to continue.

Evening came early inside the damp house. The tin roof held the day's heat and gave it back slowly. From far off, toward the ghat, a ferry engine coughed and caught. Someone in the lane began reciting prayer in a hoarse voice, not from piety exactly but because routine felt like protection after a night when walls and weather had both gone loose. Halima slid the oilcloth packet behind the rice jar again and sat with the exercise pages in her lap a little longer, reading what she and Nura had salvaged from memory.

The missing leaves no longer felt like the blind aftermath of a storm. They felt pointed. Selected. A hand had entered her house through rumor and male license, then left carrying only the pieces that could fatten a claim on dry ground. That changed the injury. It also changed the work. Rage would only make noise. Proof would need patience, comparison, and a man greedy enough to show the shape of what he had stolen.

She folded the exercise pages small and tucked them under the lamp base where even floodwater would have to rise high to reach. Then she called to Nura to start letting people collect what had survived. There would be questions. There would be faces measuring her while they took back their damp lives. She was not ready for accusation and not interested in defense. Not yet.

When the first knock came on the post, Halima put her palm flat on the charpoy rope and felt how much water still lived inside the house. Then she stood, wiped her hand on her sari, and went to answer it thinking not of outrage now, but of east-side names, of copied leaves, of the order in which a greedy man might try to make them useful.
