# Chapter 10

The lodge room was above a tire shop and beside the bus stand, so the whole building kept shuddering under other people's departures. Each time a coach backed out below, the window glass rattled in its frame and the iron cots gave a small answering tremor, as if the room itself wanted to climb down and leave. The plaster had once been painted a clean blue. Sweat, handprints, and years of damp had turned it the color of old dishwater. A fan with one bent blade churned the hot air without improving it.

Halima set the rolled bedding on the nearer cot and stood a moment with both hands on the bundle, letting her back unhook one knot at a time. The day had been all ferries, office benches, one clerk's face after another, Aminul's careful voice, Nura's silence stretched so tight it had begun to feel like a second strap cutting across the room. She had thought the waiting itself would save her. People on the mainland were always making them wait. A waiting day ate questions. A missed launch, a missing signature, a file sent upstairs and not returned, all of that could carry a person until evening without the person having to say the thing she meant not to say.

Now there was nowhere for the day to go. The office had shut. The last boat back had gone. Aminul had found this room because the cheaper ones downstairs rented by the hour and he would not leave two women in those. That had been plain in the way he did not look at the desk clerk while he counted the notes out. He had only said, with that same office politeness, that he had taken the room across the corridor and would knock if there was word about an early bus.

Nura stayed by the wall under the fan, her school packet clamped to her ribs as if somebody in the corridor might snatch even that. She had taken off her sandals and tucked them neatly beneath the cot. Mud had dried in fine gray flakes around the straps. Her face looked sharpened by the tube light, all edge and intent. The small burn mark on her wrist showed when she shifted the packet from one arm to the other. Halima had seen that wrist wrapped around a bamboo pole during the search, seen the lip split open under a stranger's hand, and even now the girl held herself like someone still braced for another shove.

"Put the papers down," Halima said. "Nothing's going to grow legs in here."

Nura did not move.

Halima took the aluminum lota to the corner basin, found a thin brown thread of water, and washed her face. The basin smelled of rust and harsh soap. Somebody before them had left hair caught around the drain. She wiped her neck with the end of her sari and listened to the bus stand below: conductors barking districts, engines coughing, a radio somewhere trying to sing over diesel. On the char, night sounds spread out. Water, frogs, somebody's pan lid dropped in a yard three houses over. Here every sound hit another wall and came back meaner.

She turned. Nura was still standing there.

"Sit," Halima said. "You've been on your feet since before sunup."

"You too."

"Then let an old woman sit first."

Halima lowered herself to the cot. The springs complained under her. She reached for the food parcel Aminul had left, opened the newspaper wrap, and found two parathas gone leathery, a folded omelet, a wet pinch of salt in paper. Food had weight. Salt, oil, chewing, swallowing. All day she had been waiting for those simple tasks to build a wall. She tore a piece and held it out. Nura took nothing.

"Eat now," Halima said. "You can be angry after."

"I'm already angry."

The room took that sentence and kept it. It seemed to stick to the blue wall with the damp.

Halima put the piece back in her own mouth because there was nowhere else to put it. The paratha tasted of old iron and cold oil. She chewed until it would go down.

"We have to go back tomorrow," she said. "You'll need your head clear."

"My head is clear enough."

"No. Right now it's hot."

"Don't do that."

Halima looked up. "Do what?"

"Talk to me like I'm a pot boiling over. I'm not some woman from the queue you can cool with water and send home."

There it was, not loud, not even rude in the plain market sense, but aimed. Nura came off the wall and sat on the far cot facing her. The packet stayed in her lap. The girl's fingers had found the split in the plastic folder and were rubbing it back and forth.

"At the office," Nura said, "when that clerk asked for the father's NID trail, you looked at the floor. When Aminul said there might be another route, you told me to wait. On the ferry, you told me to wait. Here also wait. For what? Morning? Another office? My hair turning white?"

"Lower your voice."

"Why? The wall will learn I don't know my own father? The wall probably knows more than I do by now."

A bus horn split the air below, long and ugly. Halima waited for it to pass. Nura did not. That was part of youth. Youth thought its own anger could outrun noise.

"Sleep," Halima said. "We will speak in the morning when your mouth isn't running ahead of your ears."

Nura laughed once. There was no humor in it. "No. You don't get to put me to sleep and call it patience. You've done that all my life."

Halima's hand tightened on the food paper until grease soaked into her palm. She wanted to answer with a task, with a correction, with any of the sturdy little fences that had served her for years. Fold the clothes. Check the door bolt. Count the money. None of them would hold. The room was too small. Even the fan seemed to be listening.

"You think I kept this because I enjoyed it?" she said.

"I think you kept it because it suited you."

That landed harder than Nura probably knew. Not because it was fully wrong. Because it had enough truth to hurt.

Halima set the food parcel between them and wiped her greasy hand on the underside of the cot where no one would see. "Your mother's name wasn't spoken in this house for nothing."

"Then speak it now."

The bus stand roared again, and for one wicked second Halima wished the sound would last forever.

It did not.

Nura leaned forward. "Start there. Not with the papers. With my mother."

Halima looked at the girl and saw, not Saleha's face exactly, but Saleha's way of fixing on a thing until the whole room had to admit it existed. Saleha at fifteen with the braid she could never keep tight, red thread between her teeth because she always bit the thread before knotting it. Saleha laughing at a crooked roti and eating the worst-shaped one herself before anyone could tease her. Saleha standing in the doorway during storm season, listening for boats as if the river might call her by name one evening and she would answer.

Halima lowered her eyes to the greasy newspaper on the bed. It was easier to speak to that than to the living face across from her.

"Your mother liked things that didn't belong on the char," she said. "Glass bangles in colors too thin for work. Perfumed hair oil. Sandals with a strap that would have snapped the first week in mud. She was always watching the launch people, the market people, the men who came from outside with shirts still carrying soap smell by afternoon. I used to scold her for staring. She used to say I was jealous because I had married the river and couldn't leave it."

Nura's fingers stopped moving on the packet. She said nothing.

"Monir came with a dredging crew first," Halima said. "Not as a groom, not as anything respectable. Just another man following work. He had one tooth capped in silver and he kept touching it with his tongue when he was pleased with himself. He could lift an engine cover with one hand and make girls stupid with the other. Your mother wasn't the only girl who watched him. She was only the one I had to bury afterward."

Nura flinched as if the bed spring had jabbed her. Halima heard her own sentence and hated its hardness even while knowing softness would have been a lie.

"Did she marry him?" Nura asked.

Halima breathed in the room's damp lime smell, the cheap detergent baked out of old bedding, the corridor smoke seeping under the door. "Yes. No. Not in the clean way a clerk would mean. There was a mullah, two men from his side, one lantern that kept choking in the wind. He said he'd take her after Eid. He took her before. By the time I understood what had already happened between them, she had missed two bleedings and was swearing he would do right."

"And did he?"

Halima let the question sit. Nura's voice had gone smaller, which was worse than shouting.

"He did what men do when the promise gets heavier than the making of it," she said. "He stayed until staying cost him."

Nura looked away at last, not in surrender but so she could think without showing it. The window carried up a gush of frying oil from the stall below. Somewhere in the corridor a child started crying and was hushed by a slap of adult irritation against the wall.

"You let her go with him," Nura said.

"I did."

"Why?"

There was no answer that did not shame somebody dead. Halima chose the one that shamed herself first.

"Because I thought a bad marriage with a named husband would keep her safer than being a girl people could point at. Because gossip fattens faster than fish in a closed pond. Because I had delivered half the island's babies and still couldn't stop my own daughter from believing a man with oil in his hair and lies in his teeth. Because she begged me. Because I was tired. Pick one and then pick all."

Nura gave a short, angry breath. "That's not an answer."

"It's the only one there is."

The fan clacked overhead. Halima's knees were beginning to ache from the angle of the cot, but she did not shift. If she moved now it would look like retreat.

"He took her to the other char for a while," she said. "Then back and forth. Work there, work here, never enough money, always another excuse. When she started swelling with you, he told her not to make her face tragic over a normal thing. He said first babies make women foolish. He said there was time."

The old room in Halima's head opened the way bad rooms always opened: all at once, complete, with smell first. Wet mat. Kerosene. Blood turned sweet at the edge where fever had already touched it. She had spent years keeping that door shut. The bus stand, the lodge, Nura's straight-backed waiting, all of it had shoved it open.

"When the pains began, he wasn't there," Halima said. "He had gone to look for work or drink tea or chase some next day's lie. Saleha was at his sister's place. They sent for me late because the baby was coming wrong and they were ashamed to admit they had waited. I found her on a jute mat soaked through to the floor. She had bitten her own lower lip nearly open. Every time a pain took her she clawed at the bamboo post beside her, and the post was slick where her hand kept slipping."

Nura's hand had crept from the packet to the cot frame. Her fingers were curled around one rusted bar. Halima watched them whiten and went on.

"I turned you inside her with my hand. There was no room, not enough oil, not enough anything. She screamed at me to cut her open. I told her to push and hated myself while I said it. You came blue, then angry. I slapped your feet and you filled that room with your voice. I thought for one foolish breath that maybe the worst had passed."

The corridor child had gone quiet. Even the buses seemed further away. Halima could hear the fan's bad bearing grinding, a small stubborn metal complaint.

"It hadn't passed," she said. "The afterbirth didn't come clean. It came in rotten strips. She kept bleeding. I packed cloth. I pressed. I got Rahima's old iron key hot and laid it against the cord stump because somebody in that room believed heat could frighten death away from a newborn. Your mother looked at me like she was already far and still trying to be polite about it. By dawn the bleeding had slowed and the fever had started. Her breasts had filled with milk by the second night. She was burning and shivering both. You were rooting at her blouse while I was trying to keep her mouth wet enough to swallow."

Nura shut her eyes. Not theatrically. Tightly, like a person bracing under thrown grit.

"Stop if you're only doing this to punish me."

Halima's mouth went dry. "You asked."

"I asked for the truth, not..." Nura groped for the room, for the word, and found instead the plastic packet in her lap, crinkling it until the sound scratched the air. "Not that."

"That is the truth. It has a body."

Nura opened her eyes again, shining with anger more than tears. "And he?"

"Gone."

"Just gone?"

"When I sent a boy for him, nobody could say where he'd gone first. Then everybody could. That is how it works with men like that. Once the woman is dying, the whole bazaar remembers seeing the man's back."

Nura swallowed. "So Monir is real."

"Real enough to leave debts."

"Was he my father?"

There it was. Not the paper question. The body question. The one with no stamp box beside it.

Halima looked at the blue wall because she could not look at Saleha's daughter while answering about Saleha's bed.

"I know he lay with her," she said. "I know she used his name when she still believed a name meant a man would stay. I know nobody else came forward to claim you. If you're asking whether I can swear by Allah and cord blood and my own old hands that no other man touched her in those months, no. I can't give you that kind of clean line. She was married in mouth more than on paper, abandoned in fact before she was buried, and you were left in my arms screaming. That's the straightest version I've got."

Nura stared at her as if the room had shifted under both cots. "So you didn't know. And you wrote him anyway."

"I used his name," Halima said. "The rest I built."

"Built how?"

No point stopping now. She had carried the shame this far. Let it walk on its own feet.

"First for school. Before that, for survival. A girl without a father's line doesn't stay only a child in people's eyes. She becomes a question they think they can handle with their own hands. The schoolmaster asked whose daughter you were before he asked whether you could hold a slate. The mosque women asked whose blood had made you before they asked if you'd fed. Men started saying poor thing in that tone men use when they're measuring future use. I had already buried one girl because a man could come and go and leave only his smell behind. I wasn't going to leave you with a blank space where every bastard in the market could write his own answer."

Nura's face twisted. "So you gave me a lie."

"I gave you doors that opened."

"And now they close."

"Yes."

The word came out before Halima could trim it. It was better that way. Nura deserved at least that much clean edge.

"Do you know what it did to me," Nura said, "sitting there today while they said father as if a person could simply reach behind a cupboard and pull one out? Do you know how many forms I've copied for other people? Father's name, father's NID, father's village. Always a line, always a box. I wrote those boxes with my own hand for women older than you. I wrote mine too. And all this time you were watching me do it."

"I was watching you stay in school."

"You were watching me learn a false thing so well I could teach it."

That sentence went in under Halima's ribs. She had no defense against it except the one she was already tired of carrying.

"Yes," she said. "I was."

Silence settled between them, but it was not empty. It was crowded with Saleha's last room, with school forms, with the cheap lodge bedding, with all the fathers Halima had written into other people's lives and the one she had written into her own house.

At last Nura said, "Did you ever mean to tell me?"

Halima thought of all the later days she had spent like savings: after exams, after monsoon, after the next harvest, after Nura got into nursing training, after there was some version of the world in which truth did not arrive carrying a knife. She heard how cowardly that sounded even inside her own head.

"I meant to tell you after I had secured something first," she said. "A seat in training. A room in the colony. A document nobody could snatch back. I wanted to hand you truth with one other solid thing in the same hand."

"So that I would forgive it?"

"So that it wouldn't only be loss."

Nura laughed again, and this time the sound broke halfway. She put the packet down at last, but only so she could press both hands to her forehead. The cot dipped. The mattress squealed under her weight. She sat that way long enough that Halima almost reached out. She did not. Touch could be greed as easily as comfort.

A knock came at the door, three quick taps, Aminul's knock because even his interruptions were arranged.

Neither of them answered.

He waited, then said through the wood, "It's me. I brought tea."

Nura wiped her face with the heel of her hand and said, "Come."

Aminul opened the door only far enough to edge in sideways. He had changed out of his pressed office shirt into a thin white vest under the same trousers, and without the shirt collar and tucked pen he looked younger and more tired at once. He held two cloudy glasses of tea and a packet of glucose biscuits. The pale band where his watch usually sat was still visible on his wrist; he rubbed it with his thumb as he looked from Halima to Nura and understood enough not to pretend he had walked into an easy room.

"The stall downstairs was closing," he said. "I thought you might want this."

He set the tea on the metal stool between the cots. One glass slopped over and ran a brown thread toward the rim.

"Any news?" Halima asked.

Professional caution came back into his shoulders at once. "The earliest bus is before dawn. If it runs on time, we can reach the ghat and still make the morning launch."

"And the file?" Nura asked.

Aminul glanced at Halima, then back to Nura. "Tomorrow is only return travel. Nothing will move until the magistrate's office sends a date."

"That's not what I asked."

His mouth tightened. Stress made him neater before it made him honest. "Your household matter remains complicated."

Nura gave a low sound of disgust. "Complicated. That's the word you people use when you mean somebody like us has too much life for your boxes."

Aminul took that without offense, or perhaps with the kind of offense a man set aside because he had seen worse and deserved some of it. He sat on the threshold instead of the cot, as if even now he did not want to claim more room than the file allowed him. From the corridor light, Halima could see a biscuit crumb caught in his moustache where he had clearly eaten one on the way up and not noticed.

"All right," he said. "Plainly, then. The state doesn't care about the whole truth. It can't carry the whole truth. It wants one version that stays the same from form to form to witness to list. One coherent story. If a household brings three fathers, two timelines, and a dead woman's silence, the office won't sit there admiring complexity. It'll reject the file or push it so far back that the boats leave first."

The room seemed to shrink another inch. Nura stared at him as if he had merely translated into office Bangla what she had just learned in flesh.

"So what room is left?" Halima asked. "For my house."

Aminul rubbed the watch mark again. When he spoke, the public polish was still there, but the edges had worn thin.

"For many households, the occupancy affidavit may work because the lived arrangement is visible. Husband, wife, children, old mother, one courtyard, neighbors who will swear it. For your house..." He paused, then forced himself through it. "For your house the lived arrangement is visible, yes. But under that, the origin story splits. If we advance the school papers as they stand, the missing trace collapses them. If we correct them toward uncertainty, we expose you to a different kind of challenge. And if we bring in the mother's history, we need a version everyone can repeat without breaking under questions."

"Repeat," Nura said. "Not live. Not know. Repeat."

"Yes," he said.

That yes was not bureaucratic. It was weary enough to count as respect.

He looked at Halima then, not as an officer to a village helper, but as one tired worker to another carrying damage already done. "I've seen families shaved down to fit a register. A stepchild disappears. A second wife becomes a sister. An uncle who paid for the roof stops existing because his name muddies the line. I'm not defending it. I'm telling you the blade we're dealing with."

No one spoke.

Down below, a bus conductor shouted Bogura three times as if repetition could make the district closer.

Aminul pushed the biscuit packet toward Nura. "Eat something," he said. "Anger doesn't travel well on an empty stomach."

It was such an ordinary line, so close to something Halima herself might have said, that Nura almost laughed. Almost. Instead she took the packet, turned it over once, and set it aside unopened.

"If I ask you a question," she said to him, "will you answer as a person or as an office?"

"I'll try as a person first."

"If a name was written to protect someone, does the office care why?"

"No."

"If that name was the only reason she got schooling, does the office care?"

"No."

"If the woman who wrote it was trying to keep the girl from ending up like her mother?"

Aminul looked at Halima. For the first time that day he did not seem to be calculating what he could safely know. He simply looked.

"No," he said again, more quietly. "The office cares whether the story can be carried forward without contradiction in front of a superior. That is all."

Nura nodded as if she had expected nothing else and hated that she had been right. "Then don't tell me to trust the process."

"I wasn't going to."

That, too, was plain enough to land.

He stood, brushing dust from the seat of his trousers. "Try to sleep. We leave before first light."

At the door he stopped and added, not turning, "Whatever version you choose later, choose one the household can bear saying aloud. People break faster than files do."

Then he went out and closed the door softly behind him.

For a while neither of them touched the tea. A skin formed on the top. The fan beat the heat around. Somewhere in the building a man coughed until it sounded like he might turn inside out.

Nura picked up one glass at last and drank it cold.

"One coherent story," she said, staring at the wall. "That's what you gave me."

"I gave you what I thought would hold."

"No. You gave me what would pass."

Halima had no answer ready. That was part of the night's punishment. Nura had finally reached the layer under the kindness.

"Maybe both," Halima said.

Nura set the glass down carefully. "Did my mother know he would leave?"

"Not at first."

"Did you?"

Halima thought of Monir's silver tooth, the way he smiled with only one side of his mouth when he wanted to seem humble. Thought of Saleha smoothing the cheap new sari he had brought, green with a border too bright for her skin, and defending him with the full stubbornness of a girl who thought being chosen and being cherished were the same thing.

"I knew he was light in the hand," she said. "I didn't know how light."

Nura nodded. She was not forgiving. She was sorting.

"Which char did he work from?" she asked.

Halima looked at her sharply.

"Don't start lying again," Nura said. "Not tonight."

Outside, another bus ground its gears and pulled away, the whole lodge giving a long tired shiver after it. Halima told her the name of the neighboring char, the one tied to the dredging men and engine crews, the one she had kept folded away inside her own head because names were bridges and she had not wanted Nura walking over this one.

Nura repeated the name once under her breath until she had it fixed.

Then she lay down on the far cot without undressing, the certificate packet under her cheek like a hard second pillow. She turned her face to the wall. Halima did not ask whether she meant to go looking. She could hear the girl's wakefulness in the way the mattress stayed alert under her, never fully giving itself to sleep.

Halima lay back on her own cot and watched the fan hack at the dark. Saleha had died because a man could leave, because a marriage could be all mouth and no shelter, because truth did not protect a woman once the room closed around her. Nura had grown under a name Halima made with her own hand for the same reason. Between those two facts there had never been innocence, only timing.

Near dawn, when the buses thinned and the first azaan floated thin and metallic through the station loudspeakers, Halima turned her head. Nura was still awake. One hand rested flat over the packet at her cheek, not cradling it now but pinning it there, as if she had learned in a single night that paper was not lineage and still might decide where her body would be allowed to stand.
