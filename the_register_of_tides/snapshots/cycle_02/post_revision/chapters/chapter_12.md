# Chapter 12

Nura left before Halima's first cough broke the dark.

Even in the dark she could still hear yesterday's schoolyard dividing itself: house list on one side, blood list on the other.

The house held that thin hour when even the tin roof seemed to sleep. The lantern had gone out long ago. What stayed was the smell of dried mud from yesterday's sandals, lamp smoke in the cloth hanging by the doorway, and the sour paper smell that never fully left the room now that certificates and copies had been spread, lifted, spread again. Nura stood barefoot by the shelf and packed as if she were going to an exam she had not been told the subject of. She took the folded slip with Monir's name on it, the copy of her secondary-school certificate because she did not trust herself not to need proof of her own existence once she began asking strangers questions, a little money from tutoring tied into the corner of her orna, half a paratha wrapped in yesterday's newspaper, and a pencil. She checked each item twice, then once more, not because the list was long but because fear made her practical before it made her wise.

Halima slept on her side facing the wall, one arm flung out of the sheet, palm open as if she had let go of something in a dream and had not yet realized it was gone. In sleep her face looked older and less defended. The clouded eye sat half-open. Nura had spent enough years beside that face to know that if she waited for permission, she would get either silence or a command to wait until after the magistrate, after the hearing, after this next disaster, after one more thing that needed enduring first. Her whole life had been an after. She took the key from the nail, lifted the door latch slowly so it would not click, and stepped into the gray before dawn without leaving a note.

At the ghat, Shamima was already there, squatting on her heels beside a stack of jute sacks and twisting the loose end of her scarf into a cord around her forefinger. She was Nura's cousin in the elastic char way, older by three or four years, married across one lane and two family arguments, and always in the middle of some repair that was not quite hers. That morning she carried a sewing-machine belt in her cloth bag because her husband's widowed sister on the neighboring char had found a secondhand machine and needed the belt before market day. She had agreed to take Nura because Nura had once filled out a clinic form for her son's chest medicine and because women on chars learned early that one errand was rarely worth a boat fare but two might be.

"If your grandmother asks me, I'm telling her you bullied me," Shamima said when Nura came down the bank.

Her voice was low, but the grin was real. She had one tooth capped in silver that caught what little light there was.

"Tell her you had your own work," Nura said.

"I do have my own work. I don't like mixing it with yours. Yours has fire in it."

The boatman, Mannan, spat into the water and told them to get in or stay home. He was a narrow-hipped man with a knitted skullcap he kept adjusting even when there was no wind, steering with one bare foot while both hands fussed with the engine rope. Nura stepped down carefully. The plank was wet and slimed with old algae. Shamima caught her elbow before she slipped and gave her a look that said she was already regretting this.

The channel between chars was not wide, but it had the authority of something that could still decide to take a person. The engine started on the third pull, coughing diesel over the river's stale-sweet smell. Water hyacinth drifted by in broken mats. Dawn widened, and Kadam Char pulled back into low shapes of tin and bamboo, the school wall a pale smudge, the mosque dome barely catching light. Nura kept her eyes on it until the bend took it away. Only then did she unfold the paper in her hand and look once more at the name she had copied the night before from her birth record. The name on it was Monir, just that, with a father's line that might have been as false as the rest.

Between what Halima had let slip in the bus-stand lodge and what Sufia had muttered later while pouring tea as if she did not care whether Nura heard or not, Nura had gotten three usable things: the name, the neighboring char, and dredger work. That was more than Halima had intended to give. It was enough for a morning's rebellion and maybe enough to kill a fantasy cleanly, which was more mercy than Nura had been offered so far.

Shamima watched the folded slip in Nura's fingers. "You still haven't told me what you'll do if you find him."

"Ask him some questions."

"You're crossing a river for some questions. You could ask questions at home and get shouted at free."

Nura looked at the current where the engine chewed it white. "At home they answer around the thing. I want the thing."

Shamima snorted, then tucked the sewing-machine belt deeper into her bag. "Men aren't the thing. They're the belt that snaps after you've already promised the blouse by market day."

Shamima pressed the newspaper around the belt with two fingers, checking for damp. Nura's mouth twitched at one corner, then flattened again. She rubbed the small burn mark on her wrist and kept her face turned toward the bend ahead.

The neighboring char woke noisier than Kadam Char. By the time their boat nudged into the mud, two boys were dragging a basket of duck feed up the bank, a woman was rinsing blackened tea glasses in a bucket gone gray with old sugar, and someone farther in was beating a rug against a bamboo rail with the rhythm of an argument. The ghat smelled of river slime, green chili frying in old oil, and the ammoniac edge of goat droppings warming under the first sun. A row of tea stalls had been made from tin, tarpaulin, and stubbornness. Men were already standing in lungis with their cups tilted from the saucers, talking about sand rates and fuel as if they had been at it all night.

Shamima jerked her chin toward the second stall. "Ask Momena. She knows who owes what before they know it themselves."

Momena was thick-wristed and quick, maybe forty, maybe older, with a faded orange shawl thrown over one shoulder and the top joint of her left ring finger missing as neatly as if a machine had bitten it off and then lost interest. She kept a carpenter's pencil tucked into the knot of her hair and wrote credit marks on the inside flap of a biscuit carton nailed beside the kettle. When Nura stepped up, Momena did not ask what she wanted. She looked once at Nura's face, once at the careful way she held her folded paper, and pushed a bench with her knee.

"Sit first. If you fall in this heat, I won't carry you."

Nura sat because refusal would have made her look younger.

"I'm looking for someone," she said.

"Everybody is. Tea?"

"No."

"Then you'll need a name that earns the trouble."

Nura unfolded the slip. "Monir. He worked here some years back. With dredger people, I heard."

Momena's hand stopped over the kettle handle. Her gaze moved over Nura with the quick inventory of a woman deciding which Monir, which debt, which ruin.

"You're late for him," she said. "Last story I heard, a propeller chewed him somewhere downriver."

Shamima made a noise through her nose. "And the story before that?"

"Southbound dredger. Before that, fever. Men die three ways before breakfast if they've left debt behind."

The dodge hit Nura harder than it should have. For one stupid second she saw herself going back across the water with nothing but stall talk and diesel on her clothes. Then fear did what it always did to her. It made her orderly. She pulled the certificate copy from her bag, opened it just enough to show the father line, and laid it on the bench between them.

"I'm not here for old tea debt," she said. "His name is in the father box. If there's a paper anywhere with his hand on it, tell me now so I don't waste more boat fare."

Momena's eyes dropped to the page. The carpenter's pencil in her hair tilted as she tipped her head.

"Paper," she said after a moment. "That's different."

She lifted the kettle, poured tea through a cloth strainer into two glasses anyway, and set one in front of Nura. "Drink. Your mouth's already gone dry. If it's that Monir, go behind the dead pond. Third house, blue drum outside. He slept there one flood season and ran his tab right down my carton: thirteen days' tea, four cigarettes he swore he'd pay after wages, one enamel cup. At Babul's pump shed he left a blue bag and kept bragging it had his voter slip in it. Don't start with government. Noorjahan will send you back into the lane. Start with the man."

Shamima took the second glass before Momena changed her mind. "Still writing everybody down?" she asked.

"What else would I do? If I stop marking, every man here starts calling old debt new tea."

She said it while marking something on the biscuit carton with the carpenter's pencil. A little boy came up to the stall carrying a hen upside down. Momena wiped his nose with the end of her shawl without breaking her stare from Nura.

"Go before Noorjahan leaves for the fish yard," she said. "And if she's skinning koi already, don't stand in the doorway talking like a police clerk. Sit and ask clean."

The path to Noorjahan's house cut past a pond that had dried to cracked green slime and mosquito skin, then through a strip of low huts where fish were laid on mats to salt. Dogs slept in the shade under bamboo racks. Women stepped around one another with basins on their hips, speaking in that fast half-shared shorthand Nura always heard among people who had too much to do and no time to turn themselves into speeches. One of them pointed with her chin when Shamima asked for Noorjahan, then called after them, "Tell her I kept the chilies inside. Rain's coming later." Another answered from a doorway that the sky said otherwise. They argued without stopping their hands.

Noorjahan's house had a blue plastic water drum outside and a line of school shirts drying from the eaves, all of them patched at the collar. She was sitting on a low wooden stool in the yard with a basin between her knees, scraping silver skin off tiny fish with the back of a spoon. The spoon must once have belonged to a better set. Its handle had flowers worn down to almost nothing. Noorjahan herself was not old, perhaps not even thirty, but hard use had taken the soft guesses off her face. Her hair was braided and coiled tight. One heel pressed the stool rung; the other foot kept time against the earth as she worked. A little girl beside her was trying to braid red thread into a doll made from rolled cloth and muttering instructions to it under her breath.

When Nura said Monir's name, Noorjahan did not drop the spoon or freeze or perform the shock of memory. She scraped one more fish clean, flicked the scales from her fingers, and only then looked up.

"Which Monir?" she asked.

Shamima, who had clearly decided she would rather finish this once than ferry Nura back half-satisfied, answered before Nura could trip over herself. "The dredger one. Thin. Used to drink tea at Momena's and sleep here sometimes."

"Then you've come to the wrong yard," Noorjahan said.

The little girl looked up. "Ma, is this about the man who stole the mug?"

"Go inside, Juthi."

"I'm braiding."

"Take the doll and braid inside."

Juthi scooped up the limp cloth doll and went, but slowly enough to hear more if she could. Noorjahan wiped her fish-slick hands on the edge of her sari. She still did not tell them to sit.

"I don't keep a clerk's shelf for men who passed through on bad weather," she said. "Momena likes stirring old pots she doesn't have to scrub."

The refusal made something inside Nura tighten. She could hear the scene tipping toward neighborhood quarrel, away from the one fact she had crossed for. She took the certificate copy from her bag again, opened the crease with her thumb, and held it low enough that only the women could see.

"I'm from Kadam Char," she said. "His name is on my birth record. I don't need a love story. I need to know whether he left a paper, a place, or a person who can still say he existed."

That changed Noorjahan's face, not into pity but into recognition. She nodded to the overturned bucket by the wall. "Sit. If this is going to be about papers, I don't want you fainting and making extra work."

Nura sat. The bucket flexed under her weight. Heat from the yard had already climbed into the plastic. She could smell fish blood, dish soap, and the cumin someone nearby had just thrown into hot oil.

"I needed to know if he's alive. If he has any papers. If..." She stopped. The sentence had run out into something she did not want to say in front of a stranger.

Noorjahan cut in before she had to finish it. "If he can stand up and call himself your father for the government. That kind of thing."

Nura hated how quickly her face warmed. "Something like that."

Noorjahan leaned back and let out a short breath through her nose. "Then you wasted your boat fare."

She pinched another fish by the tail and dragged the spoon down its side until scales stuck to her wrist.

"He stayed here one wet season," Noorjahan said. "Slept here when the rain got ugly. Worked near the dredger cut when there was work, disappeared when there wasn't. One day he had kin upriver. Next day he didn't. Every payday he was bringing sugar. You can hear how that ended. Fever took him down and I stood over a pot of coriander water while Momena let the tea run on credit. When the wages stalled he worked the lane house by house. Borrowed from me, from Momena, from Ruksana next door for kerosene, from anybody soft enough to unlatch a door. Last week he was here he made a show of handing Babul a blue bag to keep dry in the pump shed. Said there was a voter slip in it, two crew chits too, and if he lost them again the contractor would skin him. That's all of him I can swear to. The rest was appetite, lies, and one sickbed face."

Ruksana herself appeared in the doorway next door as if summoned by the sound of her name. She was broad across the hips, with a sunflower-printed housecoat under her sari and henna still clinging in orange ghosts to two fingernails. She had a baby balanced on one side, not her own by the look of how carelessly familiar the child's weight sat on her.

"He borrowed kerosene money off me and never brought the bottle back," she said. "Say that right if you're listing him."

Noorjahan clicked her tongue. "See? Women remember the real estate of a man. Bottle, bag, cup, debt."

Ruksana shifted the baby higher and studied Nura. "Who's she to him?"

Nura made herself say it cleanly. "He's on my papers."

Ruksana's mouth tightened with the look of a woman recognizing a misery she had seen too often.

"Come in out of the yard, then," she said. "If you're going to hear ugly things, hear them with water in your hand."

Inside Noorjahan's room it was darker and cooler. The packed floor smelled faintly of damp rice sack. A shelf held a steel plate, two schoolbooks with warped corners, a jar of cumin, and a cracked bottle of cough syrup with no cap. Juthi was on the bed platform, still braiding the doll's red threads. Ruksana set the baby down beside her and poured water from a jug into an aluminum tumbler, then put it into Nura's hand the way women handed one another necessary things.

This time Noorjahan stayed standing by the doorway, one hand on the frame.

"You're not the first girl to come through asking after a man's name," she said. "Just the first one this week."

Shamima barked a laugh before she could stop herself. "Only this week."

"I don't mean him," Noorjahan said. "I mean men. Let one rot in one yard and the stink blows through three."

She nodded toward the house across the lane, where an elderly woman was shaking water from a sari with both arms. "When Monir got fever, that one watched my girl while I went for tablets. When he took money and vanished, Ruksana told Momena before Momena sent her nephew to the dredger boys. When my roof tore in the storm after, Jharna slapped plastic over the rafters till my brother came. They saw the wet bedding, the fish turning in the heat, my girl standing in the yard. Then they put hands to it. That's what matters most days."

Nura drank the water. It was warm and metallic, and the tumbler sweated into her palm. Her thumb slipped on the rim. For a moment she saw Halima under a torn roof instead of behind the shelf of papers. Here survival moved yard to yard in cups of tea, watched children, borrowed kerosene, plastic slapped over a leaking roof.

"Did you ever see the papers in that bag?" Nura asked.

Noorjahan's mouth bent. "Only the plastic wrapper around them. He liked saying he had papers more than keeping them dry. When labor men came counting hands for the dredger contractor, he gave his name fast and his father's name faster. Could've been true. Could've been Tuesday."

Ruksana said, "Ask Babul at the pump yard. If the rats haven't eaten those papers, he'll know. He sees everything and keeps it because nobody else wants it."

Juthi, from the bed platform, said, "He whistles with his nose when he sleeps."

Noorjahan pointed at her daughter without turning. "That won't help her."

"It is if she's going to find him."

"She's not going to find him."

The tumbler clicked once against Nura's teeth before she lowered it. Juthi bent over the doll again, winding the red thread tighter. Ruksana lifted the baby and bounced him once on her hip until he hiccupped quiet. Shamima adjusted her scarf and did not look at Nura.

Nura put the tumbler down carefully. "Did he say where he came from? Anything true?"

Noorjahan scratched at a fish scale stuck to her wrist. "He said once that the river had taken his land. He said once that his brothers had. He said once there was a wife who wouldn't take him back. I never met the land, the brothers, or the wife. What I met was a man who could make a face like a hungry boy and borrow the price of tea with it."

"And you waited for him?" Nura asked before she could stop herself.

It was a rude question. She knew it as soon as it left.

But Noorjahan only shrugged. "For a while. A man says he's coming back with wages after you've already spent your own on his fever tablets, and you wait long enough to see what you bought. In my case it was a thief with a sickbed face. The fish still needed salting before noon."

Ruksana laughed, open and unashamed. Even Noorjahan smiled for half a second.

"Go to Babul," Noorjahan said. "If the bag's still hanging where he put it, that's the last useful thing you're likely to get. Then eat something before you fall over. If you go home empty, at least go home standing."

The pump yard lay near the cut where the channel widened and the bank had been hardened with sandbags that were already splitting at the seams. Two engines sat open under a shade roof, each ribbed with hoses, bolts, and rags black with grease. Men moved around them in the detached hurry of workers on someone else's schedule. The dredger itself was farther out, a rusted hulk moored in brown water, its pipe arcing to the bank like a stiff neck.

Babul was easy to pick out. He was short and square, with one ear folded in on itself from some old infection and a pair of cracked eyeglasses he kept pushing back up with the inside of his wrist because both hands were slick with oil. He listened to Shamima's question while tightening a nut with a wrench so worn it no longer had any paint on it.

"Monir," he said, and spat to the side. "Which Monir?"

Shamima gave up first. "The useless one with the blue bag."

Babul barked out a laugh. A younger worker behind him, all elbows and beard fluff, said, "That narrows nothing."

Nura stepped forward. "Thin. Used to work this cut years ago. Stayed on the char behind the dry pond. Noorjahan said he left papers here."

Babul's laugh faded. He squinted at her over the top of his glasses. "You're kin?"

"He's on my papers," Nura said again, and hated the phrase more each time.

Babul wiped his hands on a rag already too dirty to help. "Ah."

He scrubbed the rag across each palm and kept his eyes on the certificate copy peeking from Nura's bag. "Who told you about the bag?"

"Noorjahan."

"She should've let the mud keep its mouth shut."

He stood a moment longer, as if deciding whether Nura's trouble was worth dirtying his hands twice. Then he crossed to a shelf under the shade roof, shoved aside a coil of hose, and dragged down a small blue canvas bag gone nearly black at the seams with old oil. The zipper had furred red with rust. He set it on an upturned drum and worked the teeth apart with the flat edge of his wrench until the cloth gave with a sound like skin tearing.

Inside were a broken comb, a bidi tin with no lid, and three folded papers welded together in cloudy plastic. Babul slit the plastic with a thumbnail and spread the papers flat with greasy fingers. One was half a voter slip, green-black with mildew. The other two were crew-advance chits. On one, Monir had one father. On the other, he had another. Neither father's name matched the line Nura had copied out the night before. The thumbprint at the bottom of both chits was the same smeared oval, pressed harder on the second as if he had already known by then that paper only counted when somebody else believed it.

The younger worker, whose name turned out to be Selim when somebody shouted for him, leaned against the engine frame. He had rolled his lungi up to one thigh and was using a blade to scrape mud from under his toenails with complete concentration.

"Told you he had one," Selim said.

Babul tapped the papers with a blackened nail. "Same face. Same scar under the chin. Different father whenever a clerk wanted one and there wasn't a beating attached to getting it right. Contractor didn't care. Arms lift sand the same whatever father name a man borrows."

Babul said, "If you need him for some government thing, stop now. Even alive, he'd be no use. Those floating crews change names the way they change landing points. Papers get wet. Cards go missing. Half the fathers in those registers could be one man wearing three stories."

Nura kept looking at the two father names until the letters blurred into the same damp smear. Her jaw had locked so hard the hinge hurt. Even alive, he'd be no use.

She had crossed for a signature, a witness, one fact she could carry home in her bag.

She felt suddenly embarrassed by the careful fold of her certificate copy in her bag, by the neatness of her handwriting around the name Monir, by the private little corner of hope she had protected from Halima's contempt and her own intelligence. She had thought perhaps she could arrive, ask the right questions, and walk back onto Kadam Char with something solid: a workplace, an ID number, a witness, a man gone old enough to be sorry. Instead she had found thirteen days of tea, a kerosene bottle, fever water, and a blue bag full of papers that could not keep one father straight from the next.

Shamima thanked them because Nura had gone quiet. On the way back from the yard she bought two guavas from a boy under a tarp and cut them with her thumbnail, salting the slices from a twist of newspaper she kept in her bag.

"Eat," she said, handing one to Nura.

"I'm not hungry."

"That's not what I asked."

Nura bit into the guava because speaking would have taken more effort. It was hard and almost sour. Salt stung the split place inside her lip where old tenderness still lived from the day men had shoved her in Halima's doorway. The sky had brightened to the bleached white that came before noon heat turned mean. Nearby, Momena's stall clattered with cups. Across the lane, Noorjahan had already gone back to her fish basin, spoon scraping silver skin into a metal bowl while flies worried the rim. Nobody paused for Nura to swallow.

Before they headed to the ghat, Ruksana came hurrying after them with a twist of puffed rice in a page torn from a school copybook. "For the ride," she said, pressing it into Nura's hand. The baby was asleep on her shoulder now, drooling onto the printed sunflowers of her housecoat. "You'll get a headache on an empty stomach. And don't go back asking old people for fairy tales. Ask what got written and who's ready to swear to it."

Nura stared at her.

Ruksana shrugged, suddenly almost shy under the force of her own bluntness. "What are you staring at? You think you're the first girl to learn a man can fill the father box and still not buy you one fist of rice?"

She turned away before Nura could answer, calling to Juthi to come inside before the crows got the fish eyes. Shamima watched her go and said, "Well. That saved us one speech."

The return boat left later than Mannan had promised because Mannan had found an extra passenger with two sacks of duck feed and was not a man who respected anybody's urgency except his own. By the time they pushed off, the sun sat overhead like a hand holding the whole river down. The channel looked flatter now, crueler for being so bright. Diesel fumes thickened in the boat well. One duck-feed sack leaked a sweet dusty smell that made Nura think of school storerooms and damp grain.

Shamima sat with her knees up and the sewing-machine belt looped around her wrist like a black bracelet. She waited until the boat cleared the bank before speaking.

"You got what you came for?"

Nura looked at the water shredding off the bow. "I got enough."

"That's not the same thing."

"No."

Shamima let the answer sit. She knew when not to nag a wound. After a while she said, "You know your grandmother will say she was trying to save you from this."

Nura laughed once, and it came out ugly. "Maybe. She still spent my name before I was old enough to keep it myself."

Nura unfolded the slip with Monir's name and smoothed it on her thigh. Sweat had dampened the edges. The pencil line was still dark. The paper had survived by being dried, recopied, and carried carefully from one danger to the next. Monir's own trail had not survived even him. That was the turn in it. Halima had not only hidden a father from her. She had taken a man who could not keep one father name on two greasy chits and fixed him into Nura's future hard enough that an office might treat him as truth.

The boat slammed down from a chop of current and spray hit her calves. Mannan cursed the engine, then patted the skullcap back into place as if decorum could steady the river. The extra passenger fell asleep sitting up with his cheek against the duck-feed sack. Shamima chewed guava seeds and watched Nura from the corner of her eye.

"So what now?" she asked.

Nura folded the slip once, then again. She did not tear it.

"Now I stop looking for him," she said. "I go home and make her tell me the whole thing. What she wrote. Who heard it. Who'll swear to the house when the magistrate asks."

Shamima tipped her head. "That will go well."

"It doesn't have to go well. It has to work."

The river widened. Kadam Char came back into view in pieces: a run of tin roofs, the school wall with its pasted notices lifting at the corners, the mosque courtyard gone pale in noon glare, children no bigger than fingers on the bank. The sight of it brought no relief. Those roofs held Halima, the shelf of papers, and the questions Nura had stacked in her head like forms waiting for a stamp. Under the readiness sat a harder anger than the one that had sent her out in the dark. Before, she had been angry because Halima had denied her choice. Now she was angry because Halima had made the choice first and then kept the method hidden, as if gratitude should cover the theft.

Mannan nosed the boat into the mud. Shamima stood, slung her bag over one shoulder, and said, "If she starts shouting, come get me after. Not during. I don't fight old women before lunch."

The split in Nura's lip pulled when her mouth finally gave. "Coward."

"Practitioner," Shamima said, stepping off.

Nura followed her into the sucking mud at the bank. The puffed rice crackled in the copybook paper under her arm. By the time she had climbed the path toward the house, she had the questions in order: what name Halima had put into the father line, what relation she meant to defend with it, who could still say how many years the house had held them, and who would stand in a schoolroom and swear to it so Nura's future stayed on its feet when the magistrate asked.
