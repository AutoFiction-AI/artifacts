# Chapter 1

By the time the loudspeaker found the hut, Halima had two fingers inside Rokeya and the baby's head jammed hot and hard against her knuckles.

The voice came thin at first, dragged over water and tin. People on Kadam Char were used to voices arriving warped from the river, traders calling fish rates, the muezzin from a farther bank when the wind tilted right, boys shouting from boats as if the whole char were one yard. This one had the flat-bellied force of office Bangla. It kept repeating a number, a memo, a date. Then it said the name of the island cleanly enough that every person in the hut went still for one wrong beat.

"Don't stop now," Halima snapped.

Rokeya had stopped anyway. She lay half on her side on the jute sack they had spread over the packed floor, one knee sliding open, one heel grinding mud into the weave, her glass bangles shoved high up her left arm because she refused to take them off even in labor. Sweat had pasted her hair to her cheeks. She was nineteen and vain enough to worry, between pains, whether the baby would flatten her nose. Halima liked her for that. A girl who could think about her face with a child halfway out of her had some spite in her yet.

Outside, the mother-in-law started wailing to God before there was anything yet worth wailing over. The old woman had come into the room three times already and had been pushed out three times already. She had a wet sandal tied at the strap with blue thread and a way of pressing both hands to her chest as if every event in the world had been designed to injure her personally.

Rokeya's younger sister Nasima, who still had silver wedding glitter stuck at one temple from a cousin's feast, hissed from the doorway, "Ma, quiet."

The amplified voice rolled past again, nearer now, the district launch circling slow in the channel where the char's edge broke into reeds and fresh-eaten mud. All residents. Immediate assembly. Government order regarding erosion resettlement. Attend at the primary school. Bring existing papers. The words struck the tin roof and came down dented and metallic.

Rokeya made a sound like she was trying to retch and cry at once. "Dai-ma, it's stuck. I told you it's stuck."

"It's not stuck. You're holding it like a miser holds coins." Halima braced her palm low against Rokeya's belly, feeling the baby's shoulder tilted wrong beneath the skin. Heat slicked her forearms. The hut smelled of iron blood, wood smoke, mustard oil, and the old damp that never fully left anything in late monsoon. A fly kept returning to the bowl where Halima had set the boiled razor and black thread. She blew it away without looking.

"Listen to me. When the pain comes, you don't push into your own throat. You push down. Here. Into my hand. Into the floor. If you bite the sound off again I'll slap you."

Rokeya laughed once, a raw little bark, because Halima had slapped women before and everybody knew it. Then the next pain took her. The laugh broke. Her fingers clawed for the rolled quilt behind her back. Halima moved fast, hauling one leg higher, turning the baby's head the width of a thumb, coaxing the shoulder free with the patience she no longer had for most other things in life.

Outside, feet thudded in mud. Men were already running toward the school, carrying nothing, because men always believed the first trip to news should be made with empty hands. A child shouted, "They're saying we'll all be moved." Another child laughed because adults had gone strange.

Halima leaned closer until Rokeya had to see the clouded left eye and the clear right one both. "This child is not waiting for the government. Now."

Rokeya bore down. The tendon in her neck stood out like rope. Something gave. Halima caught the head in both palms, wet black hair slick against her skin, and felt at once the bad danger passing and the next smaller danger arriving. She checked the neck for cord. Clear. Good. One shoulder, then the other, then the whole narrow body slid into her hands with a spill of blood and fluid warm enough to seem almost alive itself.

At that exact moment the loudspeaker said Kadam Char in the tone officials used when they had already put a place into a file.

The baby opened her mouth and nothing came.

Rokeya's eyes went blind with terror. "Why isn't she crying?"

"Because she's busy being born." Halima cleared the mouth with her little finger, rubbed the back hard, tipped the child, rubbed again. There. A cough first, offended and wet. Then the cry, sharp enough to cut through the loudspeaker, through the sandals splashing past outside, through the mother-in-law's fresh praise to God. A girl. Thin, furious, alive.

Rokeya started sobbing at once, from relief more than joy. "Girl?"

"Girl," Halima said.

The mother-in-law made a disappointed sound before she could stop herself. Halima did not bother hiding her glare. The old woman put on grief for the baby's sex only because there was an audience for it. By sunset she would be crooning over the child and swearing she had always preferred granddaughters.

Halima tied the cord with black thread in two quick knots and cut between them. Blood striped her thumb. The placenta had not come yet. She waited, one hand firm on Rokeya's belly, listening with half an ear to the launch outside as if it were an ugly insect that had found a way into the room.

The order repeated. Erosion emergency. Verified households. Compensation disbursement subject to documentary review. All residents to assemble. It had the oily confidence of language composed where the river could not reach.

Rokeya, still crying, said, "Dai-ma, what's verified?"

"Nothing good," Halima said.

The baby rooted blindly against the air. Halima put the child to the mother's breast and pressed again for the afterbirth. It came sluggishly, a dark collapse into the brass bowl, and with it the last of the morning's privacy. Once the placenta was out and she was satisfied nothing more was tearing than ought to tear, she washed her hands in water already pinking in the basin. Her bag lay open by the wall: old cloths, bottle of tincture, string, the tin of balm she had carried long enough for the label to wear away. Ordinary things. Work things. The kind of things a government notice never remembered existed.

When she stepped to the doorway, the char was already leaning toward the school.

People moved through the mud in streams that split and rejoined around puddles, chickens, tethered goats, children too young to understand why being shouted at should make them hurry. The launch sat off the bank with two men and a loudspeaker horn fixed to its roof. Its wake slapped the eaten edge of the char, and a slice of bank gave way with a soft chunking sound, as if the river were biting while the order was being read.

Babul, Rokeya's husband, came stumbling from behind the hut, still with fish scales silvering one forearm because he had been cleaning a net line when the labor turned bad. He wore one toenail black from some old engine accident and always rolled his sleeves twice too neat for a man his age. He peered over Halima's shoulder, afraid to ask first about the baby or his wife in case he chose wrong.

"Both alive," Halima said.

His face emptied and filled again. "Girl or boy?"

"Girl. Go in before your mother teaches the child disappointment."

That got a quick crooked grin out of him. Then he remembered the river, the launch, the school. "They're saying houses with no papers will get nothing."

Halima wiped her hands on her sari and felt the small catch under her ribs that came, lately, when worry and anger arrived together. "They're saying many things. Take me my fee when you have it."

"I've got two kilos of rice now," he said, ashamed before she even answered.

"Then bring two kilos of rice. I'm not marrying into your house."

She left before anyone could turn gratitude into a crowd around her. Her knees complained at the first stretch of open path. Mud sucked at her sandals. The char had that late-monsoon look of temporary survival: tin roofs dull with damp, bamboo fences leaning, courtyards lifted on fresh throwings of silt that would hold until they did not. Everywhere, people were pulling old packets from trunks and shelves because the loudspeaker had said papers, and once paper entered a village sentence it spread faster than fever.

She passed Rahima's western cousin running with a land-tax receipt held over his head under a steel plate. She passed two boys arguing whether voter card and identity card were the same thing. She passed Sufia's tea stall at the ghat already shuttered, though Sufia never shut early unless something worth hearing was happening elsewhere.

By the time Halima reached the schoolyard the notice was up.

It had been pasted crooked to the exterior wall of the primary school, the lower corners already lifting where the plaster held damp. Glue dribbled down like snot. Half the char seemed pressed toward it. Men stood in front because men always stepped between paper and the people whose lives it named, but women were pressing from the sides and behind, necks craned, chins up, children hoisted on hips. Somebody had tracked mud across the low concrete step. Somebody else was crying. A baby, not the one Halima had just delivered, was wailing under the whole human noise like a kettle forgotten on a stove.

Nura stood on the step with one hand braced against the wall to keep from being shoved off. She had tied her scarf back hard from her face and tucked the loose end into her waist so it would not catch. Even in that crush she had found a way to keep the paper flat with her fingertips, smoothing the lower edge as if a notice would behave better if handled properly. The old burn mark showed pale on the inside of her wrist. She was reading aloud in the school Bangla she used for forms and certificates, not because she liked the sound of her own literacy but because the boy who had tried before her had stumbled over every third word and turned the crowd mean.

"Read it properly," a fisherman in a faded football jersey yelled, the red stripes on it nearly gone to pink.

"Then learn to read it yourself," Nura shot back without looking at him.

Halima heard the line about compensation first because the crowd sucked in around it.

"Payment will be made," Nura read, her voice going flatter as the language thickened, "to verified households subject to matching across birth record, marriage record, land occupancy documentation, and national identity information. Mere current residence will not be treated as sufficient claim."

The words hung there, ugly and expensive.

Halima felt the dried blood in her cuticles wake again, as if the sentence had hands and had put them on her. Verified household. She could hear the tears in it before anyone else named them: widows folded into brothers' courtyards without paper, girls shifted between roofs so quietly no clerk had ever seen the move, boys eating in one house and sleeping in another because hunger had its own map. The launch had brought a labor after all. It only wanted names instead of a child.

A widow with turmeric ground into the cracks of her thumbs said, "What does that mean?"

Nura lowered the paper and answered in the char's own rough rhythm. "It means if your house only exists because we all know you live in it, that's not enough for them."

Women at the edges started first, and then the men at the back shouted over them.

"My husband is dead, whose name do they want now?"

Halima knew that voice before she found the face. Jahanara from the western edge, who had coughed through both pregnancies and buried the man who beat mud from his nets with a fever still burning in him. The question struck Halima low and mean. The state would call it inconsistency. On the char it was how people stayed fed after a man was lowered into wet ground.

"My father never had a card."

"The river took the tax slip."

Her palms slicked. She could hear the hidden arithmetic starting already: whose roof had paper, whose roof had witnesses, whose roof had only memory and the willingness of neighbors to swear. Worst of all was that quick turn in the crowd toward any man who might sell certainty. Fear always did that. It made even stupid authority smell like shelter for a minute.

"What about those who built after the last flood?"

"Ask the chairman."

Chairman Mofiz Uddin was already there, of course. He stood a little apart under a black umbrella somebody else was holding for him, though the rain had stopped hours ago. The edges of his beard were dyed too carefully for the char and always bled a rusty tint when sweat reached them. He had on a pressed panjabi the color of old cream and the patient expression of a man preparing to become everyone's bridge to power for a price. When he saw Halima he gave the sort of smile that showed public teeth and private calculation.

"Halima-dai has come," he called, as if announcing reinforcement he himself had arranged. "Make space. Let her hear."

The space they made was not generous. It was the kind made for a person everyone needed at once. Halima felt shoulders, elbows, breath, damp cloth. Her bag thumped against her hip. Blood from the birth had dried in the cuticles of her right hand.

The district men were easy to pick out. They had the mainland habit of carrying paper away from their bodies as if sweat belonged to a lower class of problem. One of them, younger than Halima's youngest dead, held a stack of folders under his arm and kept shifting his grip so the cardboard would not touch his shirt. Trimmed moustache, pressed pale-blue shirt already darkening at the spine, sandals too clean for the schoolyard. He had the weary caution of a man who had learned that one careless sentence could live in a complaint file for years.

He stepped forward when he heard her name. "You are Halima Khatun?"

Nobody on the char used both names unless there was paper involved. Halima looked at him and said, "Who wants to know?"

"Aminul Haque. Upazila records office." He gave a small nod, polite, measured. "We've been told you assist with births here."

"People have been saying that for thirty years. It keeps turning out true."

A few in the crowd laughed because she had given them something harder than panic to hold for a second. Aminul did not laugh, but the corner of his mouth admitted the line had landed.

"Then I need to speak with you." He glanced at the notice, at the crowd, at the chairman, already calculating what could be said in the open without losing control of the day. "Not privately. Here is fine. The initial household lists we received don't reconcile."

"With what?"

"With each other," he said. "Union rolls, voter information, old relief lists, the names attached to homestead plots. One person appears in three houses. Another appears in none. Dead men still anchor active households. Women shift surname spellings from one paper to the next. Children are present without traceable parent links. If we process compensation from this as it stands, half the char will challenge the other half and the district will freeze the file."

He spoke plainly for an official, but the plainness did not make it kinder. Around them, people had gone quiet enough to hear the shape of their own danger.

Halima said, "People move. Land moves. Husbands move. Why are you surprised the paper limps behind?"

"I'm not surprised." His tone stayed even. "But I still need a list I can defend."

That was the first moment she disliked him properly. Not because he was cruel. Men like Mofiz were cruel when it paid. Men like Aminul were worse in a narrower way. They could look at a broken thing with sympathy and still demand it line up in columns before they would touch it.

Mofiz spread one hand toward the crowd. "Our people are simple, Officer-saheb. They do not understand these technicalities. If you explain what documents are required, we will help gather them."

We, Halima thought. He was already stepping onto ground he had not been invited to own.

But Aminul's attention had stayed on her. He had noticed what the others had noticed: that when Nura's voice faltered over a clause she glanced not to Mofiz but to Halima; that women in the back had started pushing forward not to ask the chairman but to catch Halima's eye; that the old schoolmaster, useless on any question after 1989, had just muttered, "Ask Halima-dai, she knows whose child is whose."

The officer shifted the folders under his arm and lowered his voice only slightly. "I will explain the requirement now. Verification sessions begin tomorrow morning in the schoolroom. Each household must establish continuity through whatever records exist. Birth. Marriage. Land occupancy. National identity where applicable."

"Where applicable," Nura repeated from the step, dry enough to make several women snort.

Aminul let that pass. "Where a record trail breaks, I need local attestation from a responsible person who actually knows the household composition over time."

He could have said witness. He could have said elder. He could have said village assistance. Instead he said responsible person and looked directly at Halima.

A shoulder drove into Halima's back, and somebody else's elbow banged the wall hard enough to rattle the notice.

Halima knew, before he made the next sentence, exactly what it would be.

"Chairman sahib tells me you remember most births on the char," Aminul said. "The schoolmaster says you know which women came as brides and which arrived as widows. Others say that when the government nurse did not come, you kept your own dates. If that's even half true, I need you at the table."

There it was.

She could have denied it. She could have laughed and said old people were always credited with memory because it saved everyone else the trouble of keeping any. She could have cursed Mofiz for talking. She did none of those things, because the crowd was full of faces she had seen slick with first blood, first milk, fever, miscarriage, shame. Faces that would be erased cleanly enough if she chose pride now.

The more dangerous truth moved beneath that one. He did not only need her memory. He needed a form of memory that could survive being spoken aloud in front of clerks. He needed fathers repeated that had never lain with the mothers who bore those children. He needed marriage dates nudged forward over already-swollen bellies. He needed dead sisters turned into legal mothers and living grandmothers turned into aunts. He needed the small evasions by which girls had stayed in school, boys had stayed out of bonded labor, widows had stayed attached to a cooking fire instead of dropping off the edge of a household entirely.

He needed the island's private mercy translated into evidence.

Nura had stopped reading. She was watching Halima now with the sharp, fixed stillness that meant her mind was already running ahead and listing consequences. When frightened, the girl became more practical, not less. Halima could see it happening in the set of her mouth.

From the crowd a woman with one side of her scarf dark from nursing cried, "Dai-ma, you'll sit, won't you? They won't leave us to that chairman's mercy."

Another voice: "If she doesn't sit, we're dead."

Mofiz turned his palms outward, all modesty. "No one is speaking of mercy or death. We are only trying to ensure orderly administration."

Halima looked at him. "Then stand aside and let the paper breathe."

That drew a few harsher laughs. Mofiz's smile held, though it tightened at one corner.

Aminul waited. He did not rescue her from the decision by softening it. Halima respected that more than she liked it.

"If I sit," she said, "people will hear things they don't enjoy hearing."

"If you don't sit," Aminul said, "people may lose claims they won't get back."

Simple. Defensive. Ugly because it was true.

Sandals scraped the step. Paper crackled. Bodies leaned in close enough for Halima to smell wet cloth, old fish, betel. The crowd had already named one bad choice duty. The launch passed again on the river, loudspeaker now carrying toward the eastern side. Somewhere beyond the yard a goat screamed because it had tangled itself in rope. Somewhere closer a child asked whether verified meant they were being counted like cattle. Nobody answered him.

Halima wiped her palms again on the end of her sari though they were already dry. "Tomorrow, then."

A rough exhale moved through the crowd. Not relief exactly. More like a hundred people loosening their grip on the same branch at once.

Aminul nodded once. "At eight. Before the room fills."

"It will fill before that."

"Then come before the full room fills."

That, at least, nearly made her smile.

He pulled one form from his folder, glanced at the name on it, then back to her. "One more thing. If you have notebooks, lists, older date books, anything contemporary to the births or marriages in question, bring them."

Nura's head turned toward Halima so fast the movement was almost violent.

Halima kept her face empty. "People on this char barely keep dry rice. You think they keep archives?"

"I'm asking what exists," Aminul said. "Not what should."

It was a better answer than most officials would have found. It still made cold sweat gather under her blouse.

Mofiz clapped his hands for order, already reclaiming the yard. "You've heard the officer. Households will prepare papers. Tomorrow the proper line will be maintained. No pushing. No rumor. We will proceed with discipline."

No rumor, Halima thought, while the man's whole trade was rumor with a receipt attached.

Nura stepped down from the wall and pressed through the crowd to Halima's side. Up close she smelled of sun-warmed paper and the cheap soap she saved for days when she had to read for other people. "You shouldn't have said yes in front of them."

"You think I didn't know that?"

"Then why did you?"

Halima looked past her toward the notice. Verified household. Mere current residence not sufficient. The paste at the bottom had already caught dust. "Because they're not asking whether I like the knife."

Nura said nothing to that. Her gaze had gone back to Aminul, who was fielding questions with careful, maddening restraint, promising process, never outcome. Young, Halima thought. Too young to understand what it meant here when a father was written, when a wife was attached, when a dead man's name stayed alive because it fed the living.

Or perhaps old enough, and only pretending not to know.

The crowd thickened again around the wall. Names were already being shouted, compared, defended. One man insisted his wife had two spellings because the schoolmaster drank. Another demanded to know whether a second marriage counted if the first husband had vanished but not died. Women started leaving faster than men, which meant the real work had begun. They would be digging in trunks by now, opening tins, lifting bedding, calling older sisters, cursing damp.

Halima turned toward home.

Nura walked beside her, faster than Halima wanted and slower than the girl wanted, the compromise of kinship. Mud pulled at the hems of their clothes. Behind them the loudspeaker moved farther off, announcing the same order to the eastern side where the ground held a little higher and men pretended land there would save them from the river's memory.

"Do we have everything?" Nura asked after a while.

"Everything for what?"

"Don't do that."

She did not say granddaughter, did not say guardian, did not say our household. She did not have to. The question had already put the house between them.

By the time they reached Halima's yard the afternoon had turned the tin roof white with heat again. Their house sat low and ordinary near the middle of the char, packed earth swept clean, one bamboo shelf high under the rafters for the things that should stay away from rats and children. Halima had always liked that the house did not advertise itself. Survival here often depended on letting other people think your best possessions were somewhere else.

Nura went straight to the trunk by the bed and began pulling out papers before she had even washed her feet. This was how fear used her: not crying, not praying, but sorting. She stacked things by size, then by purpose, her careful hands flattening each sheet from the middle outward. Old tutoring notebooks. A vaccination card. Saleha's death certificate folded so many times the creases had gone white. Three receipts for tin sheets. One electricity application that had never become electricity.

"Slow down," Halima said.

"Why?"

"Because frantic hands tear paper."

"And slow hands lose money?"

There was the sarcasm first, exactly as Halima had expected. Hurt always reached Nura wearing another face. The girl sat back on her heels and blew hair off her forehead with an impatient puff. "Tell me what they want. Properly."

Halima set down her bag, washed at the pitcher, and watched the water go pink, then clear, then pink again from blood hidden under the nails. She bought herself those few moments before answering.

"They want households they can trace," she said. "Who was born to whom. Who married into which roof. Who holds which strip of land. Whose names match."

"Match where?"

"Everywhere they can make them match."

Nura gave a humorless little laugh. "Then half the people out there are finished."

Halima did not answer, which was answer enough.

Nura held up Saleha's death certificate by one corner. "Will this matter?"

Halima's hand closed so hard on the wet end of her sari that her knuckles hurt. Somebody was pounding rice two yards over, and each strike seemed to land against the sore place at the base of her spine. A child coughed. The hen under the window scratched once, then burst its wings against the bamboo hard enough to shake dust loose. Saleha's name in Nura's hand put the taste of an old sickroom at the back of Halima's throat: fever, metal, stale milk.

"Put that back," Halima said.

"Why? It's a paper."

"It's not the paper for today."

"Everything is the paper for today." Nura laid it down, more carefully than before but not obediently. "They're saying compensation will go by verified household. Whose household are we, then? Yours? My mother's? The dead don't stand in line."

Halima sat on the edge of the bed because the pain had started in her lower back, the one that came when the char wanted too much from her in a single day. "Don't borrow trouble before it reaches the door."

"It came on a launch with a speaker."

That was fair. Halima hated when the girl was fair.

Nura kept sorting, talking as she worked because talk was easier for her with her eyes down. "If they ask for marriage paper, we don't have one for my mother. If they ask for land, we have the tin receipts and that old tax slip in your box, but the name is half gone. If they ask for father's line..."

She stopped there, not because she had forgotten the rest, but because now they had reached the place both of them knew and neither wanted to put a foot on.

Halima said, "Enough."

Nura looked up. Her face was leaner when she was angry, cheekbones sharpening under the skin. "Enough for whom?"

The question sat between them with too many possible answers. Halima did what she did under pressure when she could not yet bear the true line: she moved to task.

"Get the papers off the floor," she said. "The damp comes up faster near evening. And check the trunk lining. There may be an old receipt stuck in the seam."

Nura stared at her for a second longer, seeing exactly what she was doing. Then she clicked her tongue once in disgust and obeyed anyway. She always had practical blood in her, even when she wanted to set the house on fire with questions.

As the light thinned, they worked without much speech. Nura tied the papers into three packets with thread saved from rice sacks. Halima hung the bloodied cloths from the birth over the back fence to rinse in the coming rain. The char's sounds shifted from public alarm to domestic preparation. Lids clanged. Voices called across courtyards, counting documents now the way people counted children before a boat crossing. Somewhere Mofiz's name was spoken too often. Somewhere Aminul's careful voice was already being misquoted into promises he had not made.

After dusk they ate rice with green chili and salt because there had been no time for more. Nura asked nothing while they ate. That worried Halima more than questions would have.

When the lamp was turned low and the char outside settled into the layered noise of insects, distant argument, and river water worrying the bank, Halima waited. Nura lay on her mat with one arm over her eyes. Whether she slept or only wanted Halima to think she slept, Halima could not tell.

The loft shelf ran along the back wall above head height, bamboo slats bowed by years of use. Birth cloths dried there in bundles. Old brass bowls. A broken hurricane lamp. Two sacks of lentils. An enamel basin with a chip sharp enough to cut a finger. Behind those, wrapped inside a piece of faded oilcloth darkened by years of rubbing with mustard oil against damp, was the ledger.

Halima had hidden it badly if the measure was cleverness and well if the measure was habit. No thief ever believed the old midwife's shelf held anything except old midwife's things. The book had survived rats, flood splash, one roof leak, and her own several attempts not to touch it for months at a time.

She stood on the low stool and reached up. Her back pulled. The oilcloth was heavier than its size deserved, as if thirty years of names had weight beyond paper.

From the mat Nura said into the dim room, not moving her arm from her face, "What are you taking down?"

Halima could have lied. She had built whole shelters out of smaller lies. Tonight she did not trust her voice to do it cleanly.

"Something older than your questions," she said.

Nura let her arm fall and looked straight at her. The lamp made her eyes seem darker than they were. "Then it's probably the answer to them."

Halima carried the bundle to the bed and sat. The knots were stiff. She had tied them years ago after the last time a union man came asking for birth dates and left with money from three households tucked into his pocket. She remembered the feel of each knot. She had tied cords on newborn stumps with less care.

The ledger had not begun as a grand thing. At first it had been names in a school exercise book because the government register boat came late and children came when they pleased. Then one father vanished and another man's name had to be written fast before a debt holder claimed the boy. Then a girl's marriage month had to be moved back because her belly had moved faster than the ceremony. Then a widow's sister's child had to become her own on paper because the sister had died and the husband's people would have sold the goats, the cot, and perhaps the child with them. One correction called for another. One mercy made a future need. After that she kept dates, blood lines, whispered truths, the names that could be said in courtyards and the names that could not. Not because she loved power. Because memory rotted if it stayed only in the mouth.

And because sometimes ink could do what men would not.

She untied the first knot.

The room smelled suddenly of mustard oil, mildew, and paper that had lived too long too close to weather. The cover underneath was once red, now the brown-red of dried betel spit. Her own handwriting on the first page had changed over the years from awkwardly careful to brisk enough to be mistaken for certainty. The oldest entries were the worst to look at. Not because they were oldest. Because they still carried the innocence of a woman who had not yet understood that a private correction could harden into a whole public life.

Nura had sat up now, her blanket fallen to her lap. She did not come closer. That restraint, more than any plea, told Halima the night had turned.

"Nani," she said quietly, using the softer name she saved for when anger and fear had to share a mouth, "what is that?"

Halima laid her broad palm over the cover as if it might still be possible to calm the thing by touch.

"Trouble," she said.

By morning it would have to ride at the bottom of her bag, wrapped again and quiet.

Then she loosened the final knot and opened the oilcloth all the way.
