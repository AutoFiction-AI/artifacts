# Chapter 2

The schoolhouse had two rooms, one blackboard, and no corner where a name could be spoken privately. By eight in the morning the concrete floor was already giving back the night's stored heat, and the tin roof had begun its noon work early, ticking and expanding above the crowd before the sun had properly climbed. Aminul Haque had pushed two student desks together for a table. He sat on one side with his folders lifted off the wood by a folded handkerchief so the damp would not crawl into them. Halima sat beside him on a bench made for children, her knees too high, her cloth bag between her ankles. At the bag's bottom, wrapped again and quiet under a towel and a packet of betel leaf, lay the oilcloth bundle she had sworn to herself she would not open unless the day turned vicious.

It turned vicious before the first name was called.

People had packed the doorway and the windows before Aminul could finish arranging his forms. Mud dried in fan-shaped splashes up the wall where sandals had slapped it. A baby wailed somewhere outside. Men leaned on the sill bars and breathed their fish breath and tea breath into the room. Women stood shoulder to shoulder in the yard, sari hems dark with wet earth, clutching tin boxes, plastic file covers, school certificates, yellow tax slips, laminated identity cards with the corners already beginning to peel. Nura kept to the wall with a slate pencil tucked behind one ear and a sheaf of blank sheets in her hand, her burned wrist flashing pale each time she passed a paper to Aminul. She looked less like Halima's ward than like a girl who had arrived early for an exam and discovered the examiners were idiots.

Chairman Mofiz Uddin stationed himself in the doorway and claimed the school by posture alone, more completely than the government or the children who used it ever had. His white panjabi was too well ironed for that room. The dyed edges of his beard had gone the color of rust where the sweat touched them, and he kept pressing two fingers to the clove tucked in one cheek, managing even his own breath. He had brought a red plastic chair for himself and did not sit on it. He stood with one hand on the back and arranged the line.

"Let's keep dignity," he said, smiling at no one and everyone. "Women alone first. Widows. Deserted wives. Let them not stand in the sun all day. Then the older men. Then the rest. Officer-saheb has come from far away. We should show him we know how to do things in order."

Halima turned her good eye toward him. "Order was the line they made outside."

Mofiz spread both hands, all courtesy. "Khalamma, the line outside is one thing. Fairness is another."

It was the kind of sentence he liked: soft at the front, thumb in the throat at the back. Halima heard what sat underneath it. Start with the weak. Start with the ones whose men were dead or gone and whose papers would have holes. Let the room learn early what failure looked like. After that every small contradiction would taste like blood.

Aminul cleared his throat. He was a careful man even in this heat, his moustache trimmed, his shirt still tucked despite the journey from the mainland and the half-charred generator cable lying useless in the corner. He had a charged laptop open, a power bank fat as a brick beside it, and his phone propped in the window where one bar of signal came and went with the breeze. "We will proceed one by one," he said. "Please stay quiet enough that I can hear the claimant."

"Hear and be heard," Mofiz said pleasantly to the yard. "Otherwise people will say later that things were done in secret."

No one moved back. A man at the window hitched both elbows deeper over the sill, and the women in the yard rose on their toes behind him. The schoolroom stiffened into theater.

The first woman he called was Aklima Bewa from the western-edge huts. Halima knew her before the name was halfway out of Mofiz's mouth. Aklima had a left shoulder that always sat higher than the right from carrying water too young, and she stored everything important in a navy school satchel her dead youngest son had once used. Even today, with the whole island pressing behind her, she paused at the table to pull a few dried neem leaves out of the satchel flap. She kept them there against insects. Her hands shook so badly the leaves stuck to her fingers.

"Sit, khala," Aminul said.

"If I sit, I won't get up again," she said, and remained standing.

She laid out her life in damp pieces: her national identity card; her dead husband's card in a plastic sleeve split along one side; a union death certificate with one corner gone soft; a photocopy of a land-tax receipt so pale the ink had faded to a breath-thin ghost on the page. She kept smoothing each sheet flat with the side of her thumb, then looking up to see whether they had become more real.

Aminul began where officials always began, with the document in the best condition. "Your husband's name?"

"Kader Ali."

"Your father?"

"What use is my father now?" she asked.

Mofiz gave a short laugh for the room, turning her answer into something neighborly. Halima could have struck him with the heavy attendance register on the table.

Aminul did not smile. "For the record, khala."

Aklima named her father. Aminul typed. The phone signal caught and dropped. He waited for the screen to load. Outside, someone began shushing a child. Halima looked at the old widow's hands and remembered those hands twenty years earlier slick with mustard oil, bracing against the bamboo post while Halima lifted her second daughter out under a roof that leaked on the bed all night. Kader Ali had held the hurricane lamp then, his own fingers black with jute tar, and barked at everyone until the baby cried. A man who could hold a lamp through twelve hours of labor ought to have left something stronger behind than a half-rotten photocopy.

Aminul checked the death certificate against the identity card. "Marriage registration?"

Aklima blinked at him. "What?"

"Nikah record. Kabinnama. Union copy."

"Kader is dead."

"I understand. But to connect you cleanly to his household, I need the marriage trace."

She looked from Aminul to Halima and back, waiting for one of them to admit the ugly joke. "My clean connection is I went to his house bleeding at thirteen and stayed there thirty-two years. I buried two sons from that yard. Ask any person standing outside."

"Witness statements may help later," Aminul said. He had already reached the point in his voice where sympathy turned technical, where he tucked himself into the grammar of procedure because feeling would only make promises he could not keep. "But on this first pass I need the documentary link."

"There was no kazi here that week," Halima said. "The river had cut the southern bank. The men crossed two months later and did several marriages together."

Mofiz clicked his tongue softly. "If it was done later, perhaps the copy exists somewhere."

"Then go find it yourself," Halima said.

Somewhere near the doorway a cough snagged and turned into a laugh. Mofiz let it pass. He only tilted his head, the picture of injured civility.

Aminul kept his eyes on the papers. "Do you have any registered children whose records carry both your name and Kader Ali's name?"

Aklima produced two birth slips so damp they tried to curl back into themselves. One child had died. The other was married off upriver and not present. One slip had Kader's name. The other, from a different year and different handwriting, named only Aklima and left the father line blank. Halima felt the crowd lean in at the windows, foreheads nearly touching the rusted bars. For a second the blank father line seemed to sit on the desk like an object with edges.

"This one counts," Mofiz murmured, tapping the first slip with a clean nail. "That one causes questions."

"Don't touch my paper," Aklima said, and snatched it back with more speed than Halima would have thought remained in her.

Aminul's face tightened. "Khala, for now I'm marking the household pending due to missing marriage verification and inconsistent child records."

"Pending means what?"

"It means not rejected. Not cleared."

"And that means what?"

He took a breath. "It means you must return with any marriage witness, mosque record, older ration card, or child's full record that links you again to Kader Ali's household."

Aklima stared at the laptop. The insult had a screen now. Her mouth worked once before words came. "My husband had to die clean, then. He died too early. If he'd known he needed to leave more paper, maybe he would have stayed alive for you."

The widow's words dropped over the schoolhouse and put out every cheap laugh. Even Mofiz let them lie. Halima wanted to put a hand on the widow's forearm, to press something living against the administrative blank opening under her feet, but the table had already taught everyone there what touch could not do. Aminul wrote a case number on the top sheet and handed it over. Aklima did not take it at first. When she did, her fingers pinched the paper so hard they shook.

She left without looking at Mofiz. The crowd opened and closed around her. Outside, somebody asked, "What happened?" and somebody else answered too loudly, "No kabinnama."

The morning began not with a rejection, which people might have fought, but with a suspension. A family could dangle by one missing line.

After that the queue changed its own posture. Women opened and shut their document packets again. Men in the yard sent boys running home for trunks that would hold nothing useful. Mofiz kept smiling and kept choosing the order. He did not bring up his fertilizer cousin with the thick file. He did not bring the eastern-side men whose walls were high and whose papers had been dried each year in proper tin boxes. He called Selina, whose husband had left for Bogura three years earlier and continued sending instructions through other people's phones but no traceable address. He called old Nur Islam, who had three tax receipts and no identity card because the photo truck had come on a day he was out netting. He called a girl barely sixteen carrying her mother's marriage papers and her own baby on one hip. Each case taught a new way a life could become unofficial in front of witnesses.

Halima tried to keep people moving by hand where speech would not do it. "Put that under the dry one." "Read the year again." "No, that is your uncle's card, not your husband's." "Stop crowding. If you breathe on the paper any harder it will melt." Publicly she was brisk because softness would have broken the room open. Privately she was furious enough to taste it. She had known most of these people first by the wet weight of them, by which shoulder had bruised in labor, by which husband fainted at blood, by which grandmother slapped a newborn's soles before Halima had to. Now each life had to pass through father fields, husband fields, household head, occupancy trace. Whole years of feeding one another and sleeping wall to wall counted only if someone long ago had carried a stamp across water.

Near noon the heat thickened until it seemed the roof had sagged lower. Sweat slid down Aminul's temple and dropped onto his collar, defeating all his care. Nura brought a steel glass of water and set it beside Halima without being asked. She had stopped writing on her loose sheets. She was listening now, following the logic of the wound. Each time a form stalled she glanced toward Halima, measuring how close the trouble had moved to home.

Mofiz looked out into the yard, squinted in a show of fairness while he worked a knife between ribs, and raised his voice. "Now let us take a straightforward file. Kashem Sheikh. Bring your papers, bhai. Show everyone how a man comes prepared."

Kashem had not been next. Halima knew because she had seen him arrive barely ten minutes earlier, shoulders still glittering with the fine scales that clung after morning fish sorting. He came in grinning anyway, not about to refuse a public summons or let anyone think he feared paper. He was broad through the chest, darkened to teak by river sun, his palms ridged white where rope had burned them over the years. He kept hooking one thumb into his lungi knot and tapping his chipped front tooth with the nail of his other hand, a little tic he had when he needed to look looser than he felt.

"Straightforward costs extra now?" he said, and the room rewarded him with a few grateful laughs.

"Sit and be glorious," Mofiz told him.

"I can be glorious standing."

Kashem dropped a laminated identity card on the table with a flourish, then a folded land slip wrapped in polythene, then a ration booklet swollen with damp. "See? I didn't come naked."

Halima had delivered him before dawn in a hut where the fire would not catch and Rahima kept bleeding through the torn quilts under her hips. He had come feet first, left heel blue from the jam of bone, the cord looped under one armpit so tightly Halima had to hook two fingers under it before his lungs opened and he let out his first furious bawl. Rahima had reached for him with both hands while blood ran down her thigh to the mat. Halima remembered the third night too: Abed Sheikh's widowed sister arriving with a clean gamchha, lifting the boy off Rahima's breast, and making Rahima stay behind the hanging quilt while the child was carried into the front room and given the safer household line before the men tied to her debts could count what work his body might one day owe.

Aminul took the card. "Kashem Sheikh. Father: Abed Sheikh."

"That's what it says when the print is clear too," Kashem said.

Nura suppressed a smile without looking up. Halima almost admired him for not shrinking yet.

Aminul entered the number. The phone signal hung on. He waited, clicked, frowned, clicked again. Then he unfolded the land slip. The paper had once been yellow and was now the color of old teeth. Halima saw the moment the mismatch reached him. His whole face did not change; only the muscles beside his mouth drew in.

"This holding is under late Jalal Mondol's household chain," he said. "How are you claiming it?"

Kashem's grin stayed in place half a second too long. "By living there."

"That's not enough on the first pass. Your card places you under Abed Sheikh. This land slip runs through a different line."

"Abed lived there."

"Not according to this paper."

Kashem leaned across the table until the fish smell on him reached Halima. "Then the paper is stupid."

"Perhaps," Aminul said. "But stupid paper is still what I have to submit."

There was another laugh from the yard, meaner now because it had found a man to bite. Kashem heard it. His shoulders lifted a fraction.

Mofiz stepped in exactly where a snake would have put its head. "No need to excite yourself, bhai. Khalamma will clear it. She knows whose child came from which mat. That's why she's sitting there."

He made it sound like honor. He meant exposure.

Halima said, "If you want this done, let the officer ask and let the man answer."

Kashem slapped his ration booklet open with the flat of his hand. "Ask, then. My father was Abed Sheikh. Dead six years. My hut is mine. My nets are mine. My wife's cooking pot is in that yard. What else?"

Aminul checked the screen again. "The database trail on Abed Sheikh links to a mainland ward code, not this char. There's also no inheritance transfer here from Abed to you. On the paper holding, Jalal Mondol's widow and sons appear. Your name doesn't."

Kashem blinked. The sentence had reached him in a language he should have known and did not. "Mainland what?"

"Ward code."

"I don't live in a code. I live by the canal bend."

Mofiz made a sympathetic noise. Several men in the back echoed it, not because they pitied Kashem but because they enjoyed the shape of his confusion. Halima felt the whole room turning toward her before Aminul did. It was happening already: the state had said no, so the island looked to the woman who had once made yes possible.

Aminul turned then. He kept his voice polite, but the politeness had thinned. "Khalamma, do you know the original household entry here? Was he entered under Abed Sheikh from birth, or by later correction?"

The question was careful enough for an office. In that room it landed like a slap.

For one beat Halima heard nothing except the roof ticking and the ferry horn drifting from the river. Her fingers tightened around the edge of the desk. In her bag the oilcloth bundle seemed to push up of its own will. The names inside had been waiting thirty years for air like this. She saw herself on that third night, copying Abed Sheikh into the father line while Rahima sat behind the hanging quilt with milk soaking the front of her blouse and no standing to speak over the household taking the child. If Halima said any of it now, Kashem would learn in front of the whole yard that the name on his card was borrowed, Rahima would be dragged from the doorway into open claim, and Mofiz would turn the room into a fight over whose compensation share had just grown or vanished.

The pause was not long. It was long enough.

Somebody at the window said, very softly and therefore perfectly audibly, "She knows."

Mofiz's eyebrows lifted with theatrical surprise. "If there's some older register, we should all benefit from its wisdom."

Nura's head came up so fast the pencil fell from behind her ear. Aminul glanced toward the doorway, finally understanding the scale of what he had asked in public, but he did not have the authority to pull the words back.

Kashem looked from Aminul to Halima, and for the first time the swagger drained enough for something younger to show through it. Not suspicion yet. Only the first sting of being made foolish in front of other men. "Khalamma?"

Halima answered the way she answered when a birth room was close to panic: not with the truth people wanted, but with the next task. "The old tax bundle from your house. Bring the earlier receipts. And the ration entry from before the card lamination if you still have it."

"That's all?" Kashem said.

"Bring them."

"I brought what didn't rot."

"Then bring what's left that still has ink."

He laughed once, sharp and joyless. "You want my father's bones too? I'll dig those if it helps."

Two men by the doorway barked laughter and then swallowed it. Mofiz did not laugh this time. He put on concern like a shawl. "No one is insulting you, Kashem bhai. We are only trying to protect legitimate households. If Khalamma recalls something, let her say it clearly. Better one moment's shame than a big mistake later."

The knife showed cleanly now: shame dressed up as civic duty.

Halima turned on him. "You talk like mistakes arrive from the river by boat and not from men's mouths."

Mofiz's smile thinned but did not break. "Then let us trust the mouth that remembers."

Kashem slapped his identity card back into his palm. "Do I stand verified or not?"

Aminul did not hide behind terminology this time. The balance in his sentences had frayed. "Not yet."

The words seemed to hit Kashem physically. His hand closed over the card hard enough to bend the lamination. Behind him, through the bodies at the door, Halima caught one glimpse of Rahima Bewa's smoke-dark shawl and the outline of her small underfed frame. Rahima had stopped outside without entering. She watched from the edge, one hand holding the throat of her cloth bag closed. Then somebody shifted, and she was gone.

"Fine," Kashem said too loudly. "Write pending. Write sky-fallen. Write whatever pretty word you people use when you can't answer a straight question."

He shoved the land slip back into its polythene and stood. The bench scraped. "But next time, don't call me up like some circus goat if all you want is to stare."

He walked out before anyone dismissed him. Outside the yard broke into low speech at once, that urgent thick murmur that meant news was already running ahead of facts. Halima could hear her own name moving through it.

The next claimant came and another after that, but the room had changed species. No one believed in simple checking anymore. Each person who sat at the table sat inside the shadow of Halima's pause. Men looked at her before they answered Aminul. Women tried to read her face the way they once read labor pain. An old mother with a split heel asked, before even putting down her papers, "If your memory says one thing and the card says another, which one wins?" A young husband who smelled of kerosene and pond water pushed his niece behind him and asked whether dependents counted if they had eaten from his pot for seven years but had another father's name somewhere. Aminul kept saying, "One at a time. One matter at a time." The words no longer governed anything.

At a short midday break he rubbed his eyes and spoke to Halima without looking at her. "I should have asked that differently."

"You should have had walls that worked."

"This room is impossible."

"This island is impossible. The room is only honest about it."

Nura handed Halima a piece of gur wrapped in newspaper. Her jaw was set so tight the muscle jumped near her ear. "He's doing it on purpose," she said under her breath, meaning Mofiz.

Halima did not ask who. There was no one else in the room worth that sentence.

Across the doorway Mofiz accepted tea from a boy and thanked him in the voice of a man blessing a donor plaque. Then he said, quite audibly, "We are fortunate. Most places do not have a living archive to help them."

Living archive. Three people near the door repeated it under their breath. By the time it reached the yard it already sounded official.

When work resumed, Halima kept her hands flat on the desk so no one would see her reach toward the bag at her feet. She did not open the oilcloth bundle. She would not give Mofiz that sight. Even so, the next woman in line laid down three papers and kept a fourth folded in her fist. Only when Halima said, "Open your hand," did she slide out a birth slip with the father's line smeared nearly white. Behind her, a man from the eastern high ground bent low enough for Halima to smell cardamom on his breath and murmured, "You'll remember who held your roof tin down last flood, khalamma," in the oily tone people used over small debts. Nura snapped, "Stand back," and three men near the window let out the thin laugh people used when they were already frightened.

By late afternoon Aminul's power bank had dropped to one blinking light. The schoolroom smelled of old sweat, wet paper, and the iron tang that came off damp rusting window bars. Cases were now piling faster than they could be sorted. He marked several pending, two provisionally matched, one disputed by absent heirs. Each new label sounded temporary enough to soothe and final enough to terrify. Halima kept hearing their practical meanings under the language. Come back with more proof. Bring a man who will stand for you. Produce a dead thing that does not exist. If you cannot, prepare to be thinned out.

At last Aminul shut the laptop because the battery would not hold another claimant. Mofiz announced they would resume the next day with the grand generosity of a man granting a festival. People surged toward the desk instead of away from it. In the morning they had wanted the officials. By evening their hands were reaching past Aminul for Halima's sleeve.

Aklima caught her first, gripping her elbow with fingers hard as dried roots. "Khalamma, you tell them I went to Kader's yard a virgin and came out a widow. You tell them that much. One missing kazi can't erase my whole body."

"Bring your married daughter tomorrow," Halima said. "And the neighbor who stood at the cooking fire that week."

"My daughter is two ferries away."

"Then send for her tonight."

Aklima searched Halima's face, trying to tell advice from mercy. Then she nodded once and let go.

Before Halima could stand fully, Majed Ali stepped in from the side, tall and slightly stooped, grease still lodged under his nails from some engine repair he had not finished. He held his cap in both hands instead of wearing it. That was the sort of man he was: mild in public, but his worry always took practical shape. "Khalamma," he said quietly, careful not to be overheard and therefore immediately overheard by everyone nearest him, "when our household comes, speak to what is useful. Not to every old corner of it. Children eat from the front story now."

There was no threat in the words. That made them worse. He was asking her to remember selectively for the sake of a household built on one of her old acts. He would not have named it that way himself. Still, the weight of the request sat exactly there.

Halima looked at the grease half-moons under his nails. "Bring the papers you have."

"I'll bring them."

"And decide before you come what you can survive hearing in public."

His face shifted just once, a tiny flinch, before he put the cap back on and moved away.

Then came a girl Halima barely knew, wanting a younger brother counted though his card named another village. She kept the boy half hidden behind her hip until Aminul asked to see his face. Then an old man asking whether a fostered grandson could remain tied to his cooking fire instead of being thrown back toward drunk uncles; while he spoke he kept one thumb over the corner where the grandson's other surname showed. Then Selina, abandoned by the husband who still occasionally sent voice messages from somewhere inland, saying in a flat hard tone, "If your memory can keep my boy inside my file, don't let men laugh him out of it." As Aminul bent over another form, Selina eased a betel tin toward Halima and clicked it open with her nail. Two folded hundred-taka notes sat under the lime packet. Halima shut the tin with the heel of her hand and shoved it back. Selina's ears went red before she snatched it away.

Kashem waited until almost all of them had gone. He stood by the school wall with his shoulder against a flaking map of Bangladesh painted there years ago for lessons no one remembered. The bravado had returned, but now it had a crack in it. He rolled his bent laminated card between both hands, trying to flatten it by force.

"Tomorrow," he said when Halima came out, "don't stop in the middle like that."

"Then don't come up before your turn when the chairman whistles."

"He called. I came. That's not the point."

"It's one point."

He gave a breath that was almost a laugh. "You know what I mean."

Halima did. Trouble was, she knew too much of what everyone meant now.

Kashem pushed off the wall. Up close she could see the salt dried white in the hair on his forearms. "If the problem is paper, say paper. If it's some old mess, say that too. Don't leave me standing there with all those bastards looking at my face."

The rawness in it showed for one second, then he covered it with a sneer. "A man can take bad news. What he can't take is being handled like a child."

Halima might have lied to soothe him. Once, years earlier, she had been willing to move whole names around to buy children time. Today the room had shown her the price of each such mercy when it matured. She said only, "Bring the older receipts."

"You really do want the bones."

"Bring the paper."

He shook his head, half anger, half bewilderment, and walked off toward the canal path, calling something crude to a friend so that anyone listening would hear only the crude part.

The schoolyard emptied slowly. Mud held prints the way wet paper held ink: briefly, then not at all. Nura came beside Halima with the cloth bag. Her voice was low. "They're saying you have a book that's truer than the government."

"They say many stupid things before evening prayer."

"They're not saying it like a joke."

No. They were not.

From the road toward the ghat drifted the day's final proof. A boy ran past carrying tea glasses in a wire rack and shouted to another, "Forget the laptop. Ask Halima-dai. She's the real office." By the time his sandals slapped out of hearing, two girls at the tube well had already taken up the phrase and were whispering it into their hands.

Halima stood in the school doorway and looked back once at the improvised table. Chalk dust had settled on the wood beside Aminul's neat stacks of forms. On the blackboard someone had written columns in a schoolboy hand: `Name`, `Father/Husband`, `Household`, `Remarks`. Beneath them, in smaller crooked chalk, someone had added: `Ask Halima`. Nura rubbed at it with her palm until the words blurred.

When she finally started home, the bag at her feet had become heavier than it had been in the morning though she had not taken the bundle out. At the first bend a woman from the north lane slipped out from behind a jute screen with a ration booklet tucked under her blouse and asked whether Halima could come after dark, when her brothers were asleep. Halfway down the path a man from Mofiz's tea circle called from his verandah, smiling too much, "Remember carefully, khalamma. Too much memory can drown a house." Near the western lane, Rahima stepped toward her with a folded paper in hand, saw Nura beside her, and tucked the paper back under her smoke-dark shawl before turning away.

By the time Halima reached her own threshold, three children were squatting across from the yard pretending to play knucklebones and watching to see whether her lamp would stay lit after supper. Inside, even with the door barred, the schoolhouse seemed to have followed her home.
