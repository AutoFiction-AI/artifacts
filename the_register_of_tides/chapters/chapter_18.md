# Chapter 18

Outside, the houses on Kadam Char already stood open-backed. Doors leaned in tied bundles against lane walls, bedframes came out through holes where woven partitions had been cut loose, and the air held the hot metal smell of tin sheets recently pried from nails. Halima and Nura came down the lane with the trunk between them, stopping when the handles bit too deep into their palms. Nothing looked abandoned yet. It looked worked on, stripped for parts, a place people were still trying to win from the river one cooking pot and one hinge at a time.

Halima's own house had lost its modesty overnight. Once the bedding came off the walls and the rice bin was emptied and the low shelf cleared of oil bottles, the room showed every stain the years had put there: lamp smoke above the niche, old birth blood scrubbed pale into one corner of the floor, the dark thumb-worn patch beside the doorway where she had steadied herself so often that the mud plaster had turned smooth as fired clay. She stood a moment with her broad palm over that place. The wall still held the night's cool. By noon it would be somebody else's rubble or the river's.

Nura was already at work, fast and mean with it because that was how she handled anything that could still hurt her. She had rolled the mosquito net tight enough to choke it, tied the bedding with sari cloth, wrapped her school certificates and the nursing forms inside two layers of plastic, then pushed the packet high into the tin trunk beneath Halima's old shawls. The small burn mark on her wrist flashed whenever she reached across the room.

"Don't put the scissors at the bottom," she said without looking up. "If they search the bags again, they'll make a drama of it."

Halima dropped the cord-cutting scissors onto a folded blouse anyway. "Let them think an old dai can't travel without steel."

Nura snorted once. It was close enough to a laugh that Halima took it and did not answer. Under the sleeping mat lay the birthing thread, the dented enamel bowl, the little bottle of mustard oil with only a thumb's worth left. She packed them not because Nabinpara would welcome a midwife, but because her hands did not know how to leave such things behind. Bodies would keep opening. Babies would keep arriving indecently on time.

The ledger waited until last on the loft shelf, wrapped in the same oilcloth that had once hidden it from searches, damp, smoke, and women who loved her enough to ask questions she could not bear. Halima took it down and felt the drag of it at once, a stubborn weight. The book knew the shape of her hand by now and would leave it only by force.

She set it on the trunk and went back to packing. Postponement still felt enough like control to fool her hands.

Outside, the lane sounded wrong. Tin usually answered the morning with the small domestic music of lids, bowls, and hand pumps. Today the loudest noises were salvage noises: hammer heads knocking bent nails straight, rope tightening against bamboo, men grunting together under a roof beam, the slap of wet jute sacks against stacked bundles. Somewhere a child was delighted enough by the stripped houses to shout inside an empty room just to hear the echo. Somewhere else a woman cursed because a hen had laid under the bed on the morning she needed it not to.

Jamila came past carrying a jute basket with two hens inside. The birds were bound at the feet and furious about it, their beaks working against the weave. Jamila had tied her sari up high for walking and pinned the loose end with a safety pin at her shoulder so it would not drag in mud. She was dressed too carefully for chaos, which was her way when she felt least protected. Majed followed with a biscuit tin full of engine bolts under one arm and a rolled sleeping mat under the other, grease already pressed black into the edge of his thumb.

"Boat two," Jamila said to nobody in particular, then, because politeness cost less than silence, to Halima: "They stamped us before dawn."

The blue district stamp showed through the plastic sleeve she held under her arm. It was the mark of a verified claim. The word was not printed there, but it might as well have been stitched onto her blouse.

Majed nodded toward Halima's trunk. "If you're late to the ghat, don't come with the big load. Bring the papers first."

"We're not late yet," Nura said.

Majed gave a small shrug. "No one's late until they're late."

Jamila shifted the hen basket higher against her hip. Her mouth looked calm until the last inch of it. "Keep your bedding dry on the boat. The river likes a thing more once you've wrapped it."

Jamila kept her eyes on the hen basket when she said it. Halima heard the old relation under the calm voice, frayed but not dead. She nodded. Jamila moved on. Majed followed, turning once to see whether the tin case under his arm was leaking its bolts.

Sufia limped up next, dragging one end of a tea stall bench while a nephew dragged the other. She had betel stains at the corner of her lips, a clove jar tied into the end of her scarf, and the kind of fury reserved for labor that arrived before tea.

"Inland people drink dead water," she said. "You'll see. Tea never stands up there."

"You haven't reached there yet and you're insulting it already," Nura said.

"That's why I survive," Sufia said, and shifted her grip. "When a place hears me coming, it behaves."

Her nephew laughed and nearly dropped the bench. Sufia smacked the back of his leg with her free hand and kept moving.
Halima watched the scraped bench leg knocking against Sufia's shin and thought how fear made everybody meaner at the mouth. Even a joke came out this morning with its sleeves rolled for work.

By the time they had the bedding roll tied on top of the trunk, the char had taken on the look of a market after rain: crowded, loud, and full of things no one had meant to carry so far. An iron rice measure clanged against a brass plate. A bundle of school slates rubbed chalk dust onto a sack of onions. Somebody had tied three goats together, and each goat believed the other two were responsible for the arrangement.
To Halima the noise had the false busyness of storm prep, all clang and bargaining over objects when everybody knew the real trade was between land and time.

The ghat was worse. It was all bottleneck and temper. Two launches and a cargo boat nosed the bank while men tried to load them in an order that would not capsize anything. Boatmen shouted for heavy trunks first, then changed their minds and shouted for them last. Children were passed hand to hand over bundles. A woman in a red shawl clutched a plastic folder to her chest more carefully than she held the sleeping baby slung across her back. Verified households had their slips wrapped in polythene, folded flat, tucked into blouses, shirt pockets, lungi waistbands. The contested cases stood a little apart, not because anyone told them to, but because a person whose future had been penciled instead of stamped learned quickly how much space the stamped people wanted.
The split looked familiar to Halima. She had seen women in labor rooms stand with that same ugly knowledge on them: the ones who knew the room might save them and the ones who knew it might not.

Aminul moved between the piles in a sweat-stiff shirt that had once been properly pressed. Even now he kept his files tilted away from his body, preserving a clerk's last dignity: paper and sweat should not mix. His trimmed moustache had gone dark with moisture, and the careful balances in his voice had frayed down to usable pieces.
Against her preference, Halima felt a pinch of pity. The job had outgrown his pressed shirts weeks ago, and still he was trying to keep the files dry with his own ribs.

"Launch one, verified households only. Don't argue with me there, argue with the order when the river gives you a desk. Appeal files go on the cargo boat or the second truck after landing. Everyone is moving. Compensation is a different matter."

"Different matter means no money," a man said.

"Different matter means exactly what I said," Aminul replied. "Do you want me to lie to make the morning shorter?"

That shut the man up for one breath and a half.

Kashem stood near the broken mooring post with Rahima's smoke-dark shawl tied around a bundle of cooking things. He had one goat on a rope, rope-burn across his palms, and his appeal packet folded small enough to hide if he needed to, a red hold mark scored across one corner. The swagger he had once worn like an extra shirt had gone. What remained was a hard, ordinary tiredness. He nodded at Halima without warmth and without accusation either, which somehow cut cleaner.

"They've put me with the paperless and the half-dead," he said, looking toward the cargo boat. "At the hearing I was Rahima Bewa household. By dawn I was back on hold because she died before some clerk got her countersignature on the transfer sheet."

Nura said, "You're still standing, so one of those is wrong."

Kashem's mouth twitched, not quite upward. "Give them time."

Near the eastern rise, beyond the worst crowd at the ghat, two brothers were still dismantling a cowshed plank by plank, treating the government order like bad weather that might yet pass. The elder one, Selim, kept stopping to wipe his glasses on the tail of his shirt though the lenses were already clean. His younger brother had wrapped his wife's green sewing machine in a school uniform shirt and was trying to get the crank loose before loading it. Every few minutes someone shouted at them to leave it. Every few minutes they answered by prying at another nail.

Uneven willingness, Halima thought, was another way of saying each household had measured loss differently. The stamped families wanted to get to their numbers before somebody else reached them first. The families with appeals dragged their feet because leaving without proof felt too much like agreeing with the insult. And there were some who had lived long enough beside the river to think delay itself could still bargain with water.

Halima and Nura set their trunk near a bamboo post. Nura looped their bedding rope twice more and checked the plastic around the certificate packet with the fingers she used on exam papers, on forms, on any future that could still be folded square.

"Keep the trunk with us," she said. "If it goes on the cargo boat without us, we'll spend one whole day proving it exists."

"It exists because we touched it," Halima said.

"That's not how offices think."

Halima tugged the rope until the knot bit her palm and said nothing.

The loading went on badly for an hour. Possibly it had been two. Time at the ghat did not move by clocks. It moved by curses, by the depth of the mud around your ankles, by whether a boatman accepted your bundle on the first demand or the fourth. Halima carried the small things because her back had earned the right to refuse the trunk. Still she made three trips with a sack of pots, the birthing tin, and a rolled mat under one arm. The ledger she kept inside the cloth bag hanging from her shoulder, pressed against her side, hot and private as body heat she had no wish to share.

Several times people stopped her for matters that had nothing to do with lists and everything to do with leaving. Sufia asked whether a goat that had eaten soaked rice would shit itself to death on the boat. A child split the skin under his heel on a bent strip of tin, and Halima crouched in the mud to wash grit out with drinking water while his mother hissed that he had chosen the worst possible day to be stupid. Jamila's younger son lost one sandal, then found it floating under someone else's charpoy. Majed kept counting his tools out loud, not because anyone was listening, but because the numbers were a handhold.

People still came to Halima's body before they came to her history. That fact soothed nothing. It only reminded her how many kinds of use a person could survive.

Near noon the river made the sound that always came before bank slippage, a low wet tearing from inside the soil, the sound of ground ripping along its own seam. Halima heard it before most others did. Years of sleeping with ferry engines and bank hunger had tuned that noise into her ribs. She straightened too fast and banged her thigh against the trunk.

"Nura."

Nura looked up at once. "What?"

Another rip came, longer. It did not come from the ghat. It came from the east.

Someone shouted. Then three people shouted the same thing with different words, which was how panic established itself on Kadam Char.

The eastern edge went in sections, not all at once. First a crack opened across the yard beside the half-dismantled cowshed, thin as string and then suddenly wide enough to eat a heel. Selim threw down his hammer and backed away, his clean glasses fogged at last. One corner of the raised kitchen nearest the break tipped, paused in a way that felt obscene, then slid with its clay stove still attached. Bamboo posts snapped dry as kindling. A latrine leaned out, hung there one breath, and went down sideways into brown water. The river took it with a gulping sound and slapped the wall fragments apart.

Nobody stood admiring disaster. That was for people with stronger banks and more spare time. The brothers ran for what they had not yet loaded. Their sister-in-law ran after the green sewing machine and was caught around the waist by two women before she reached the widening edge. One of the boatmen cursed so hard he forgot language and became pure throat. The crowd at the ghat surged backward, then forward, because panic never knew whether it meant run or grab.

A long sheet of tin skidded down the slope toward the crack. Nura lunged and caught the rope tied to it, heels plowing mud. Halima reached for her and got only the back of her blouse for a second before Majed came from the side and hauled the tin up with both hands.

"Leave it if it goes," he barked.

"Easy for you," the younger brother shouted. "Yours is stamped."

Majed let go so fast his palms sprang open. For one ugly half-second Halima thought they would fight there with the river still eating land behind them. Then the ground cracked again, louder, and argument turned back into labor.

The cargo boat took whatever could be thrown. A rolled fence. Two sacks of rice. The green sewing machine without its cover. A bed plank with one leg missing. An old man in a white vest came down the lane carrying a framed calendar page of Mecca and nothing else, the glass already cracked across the Kaaba. He kept saying, to no one who could help him, "The roof I know. The roof I know. The roof I know."

Halima saw the moment leaving stopped being debatable: when even the holdouts started handing up their last ugly, necessary things without asking what waited on the other side.

After that the work became meaner and faster. Bundles were shoved instead of arranged. Chickens went in upside down. Somebody's sack of lentils split and rolled yellow under too many feet to save. Nura climbed into the launch to make space, came back out to drag the trunk toward the plank, then climbed in again with the certificate packet under her blouse. Halima passed up the birthing tin, the mat, the sack of pots. The ledger stayed against her ribs, hot now under cloth and old oil.

Aminul came through the crush with a list in one hand and his voice worn flat. "Final loading. If you're on this side of the bank, you're boarding. If your name is under appeal, that follows you. It doesn't keep you here."

"Easy for paper to say," Kashem muttered.

Aminul heard him. "The death hold travels with you, Kashem. Mainland cross-check, then allotment. Miss this boat and you'll still be pending, only wetter."

Kashem looked past him toward the broken east bank and did not answer.

When Halima finally stepped onto the launch, the plank jumped under her weight because two boys had started across from the other direction carrying a rolled mat longer than either of them. She reached the deck with her knees already shaking from the effort, though not from fear. Fear had been a more private feeling than this. This was fatigue in public, with diesel smoke in it.

The launch pulled away badly, overfull and late. Kadam Char did not offer them a last beautiful view. It offered stacked tin, stripped frames, one last line of women at the water edge beating mud from cooking pots before carrying them inland, and the raw bite in the east where the bank had sheared off and shown its wet brown interior. Halima's house was not visible from here. Or it was, but no longer recognizable as hers.

People on the launch did not speak much at first. Leaving took breath. A child vomited between his mother's feet and she cleaned it with the corner of a shawl without stopping her stare at the receding bank. Jamila sat upright on a coil of rope with one hand under her blouse over the document sleeve, thumb worrying the edge where the stamp sat. Majed crouched beside the engine housing counting under his breath again: spanner, wrench, pliers, small hammer, bolts, wire. Sufia opened her clove jar, chewed one, then closed it angrily when the taste turned to diesel in her mouth.

Kashem stood near the stern with Rahima's shawl around his bundle and the goat tied to the rail. He watched the wake instead of the char. The goat laid back its ears every time spray hit it.

Halima sat on the deck boards with the cloth bag between her feet. Without the ground under her, the ledger seemed heavier. She put both hands on it and felt the corners of the pages through oilcloth, the thickening where damp had once swelled and then dried them, the places where copies had been taken from the whole. Secrecy had never been clean. She knew that now more openly than before. But it had not been simple rot either. She had hidden dates the way women hid cash in hems. She had changed fathers the way people shifted a wall one handspan at a time to keep rain off a child's sleeping place. Men who called such work forgery had never sat in a dark room with a girl too young for marriage bleeding onto a reed mat while her brothers argued about debt outside. Clerks who liked a straight line had never held a newborn and known that one official father meant school and another meant sale.

The book had not made her innocent. It had kept some people alive.

At the mainland landing the launch met trucks, dust, and a new kind of shouting. The river smell broke apart there under diesel, baked earth, and the sour stink of too many bodies climbing down too fast. Officials with chalk moved around the trucks marking loads and calling block assignments for the households already cleared. Verified slips went one way. Appeal bundles went another. The sorting began again before people's legs had steadied on land.

Aminul jumped down first, nearly lost one shoe in the mud, then recovered his balance without comment and started pulling the files into new order. His bag had become an office by force.

"Block C households this truck. Verified widows with attached dependents, over here. Pending appeals wait by the tin shed till I check the transfer list. No one's losing position because of waiting one more hour, so stop telling me the same story twice."

"You'll hear it three times if you listen properly," Sufia told him as she limped past with her kettle.

"I believe that," he said, and for one moment the answer was so nearly human that Halima saw how tired he had become.

Kashem came down carrying Rahima's bundle and nothing of his old swagger at all. He went straight to Aminul, not asking permission first.

"My case," he said. "What happens now?"

Aminul did not reach for comfort. It was one of the things Halima had come to trust in him, against her preference. "The hearing stands. The transfer office marked a death hold because Rahima Bewa died before the relocation countersheet could be signed, and the western-edge occupancy register still has to be matched to her statement. Until that cross-check lands, you travel pending and sleep in transit."

Kashem's jaw worked once. "Don't let them drop her because she's dead."

Aminul looked up. "I'm not planning to."

"Planning isn't the same thing." Kashem shifted the bundle under his arm. The smoke-dark shawl had come loose enough for the rim of an aluminum plate to show through. "She waited thirty-five years to be said once properly. Don't let some bastard in an office fold her up small."

He had stopped trying to sound like a man the yard might respect. The words came out flat and separate, laid down one by one like Rahima's things on a table, each of them daring the office to misplace one more. Halima felt the cloth bag against her ankles like an accusation that had finally learned the right language.

Nura was beside her before Halima knew she had crossed the yard. Dust had settled in the fine hair at her forehead. She had taken off her sandals and tied them to her wrist by their straps because the mud at the landing was eating them.

"You're still carrying it," she said quietly.

Halima did not pretend not to understand. "Yes."

"If you keep it, you'll spend the rest of your life sleeping with one hand on the bag."

"Maybe that was always the life."

"Then keep it for a reason, not because the habit is stronger than you." Nura glanced toward Kashem and Aminul. "If it stays with us, it only stays ours. If it goes with the appeals, at least it can bother the right people."

Nura kept her eyes on Kashem and the file bag. Anything tender came out of her sharpened first.

Halima took the ledger out under the shadow of the truck bed. The oilcloth had picked up boat spray and dust on the same morning, river and road together. She unwrapped only enough to see the page edges. Her own handwriting looked back from the first leaf, names thickened by years, dates corrected in smaller script, one household nudged under another, one father crossed lightly so the line could still be denied if a denial had to save somebody. The book was ugly with use. Good books were usually unused.

Aminul came over because he had seen the oilcloth and knew what it meant. For the first time since he had stepped onto the char weeks ago, he did not speak first. He only held his file bag open.

Halima said, "If I give this, you keep the whole of it. No selected pages. No tearing out what embarrasses a man with a chair."

"Agreed."

"If copies are made, they're made in front of you."

"Yes."

"My household file stays tied to it. Kashem's stays tied to it. The widows whose witnesses died stay tied to it. You don't let them take the easy names and drop the costly ones."

His answer took a second longer, not because he meant no, Halima thought, but because he knew what promises cost in his line of work. "I can attach the disputed files as supporting material. I can certify chain of custody once it reaches the office. I can argue from it. I cannot guarantee a magistrate will love it."

"I'm not asking for love."

Something moved in his face then, not a smile, not comfort. It might have been respect, or resignation to the fact that this old woman had spent the whole month dragging him toward a category of truth his forms had not been built to accept.

"If I take it," he said, "it becomes evidence. That means it stops being yours in the way it has been."

Halima looked past him to the trucks, to Jamila already climbing into one with her stamped sleeve safe against her ribs, to Kashem waiting in the appeal line with Rahima's bundle under the smoke-dark shawl, to Nura standing barefoot in mud, certificates under her arm. The char was behind them, still breaking. The ledger dragged at Halima's hands hard enough to wake the old ache along her shoulder. She knew exactly where it would sit that night if she kept it: under whatever bedding space the disputed were given, close enough for her fingers to find in the dark, useless to anyone not sleeping beside her.

For years she had ridden with that weight on her hip to push a school form past a clerk, to get a girl through a marriage season alive, to give a child a father clean enough for ink. Jamila had her stamp already. Kashem had a red hold mark and a dead mother's shawl. Nura had only papers that could still turn on her. Halima felt the place the book would leave behind along her side and hated it.

She pushed the ledger into Aminul's open bag. He took it with both hands, careful despite the shouting around them. The bag sagged with the weight.

"Write your receipt if you need one," Halima said. "I know what I handed you."

"You should still have a record."

"I am the record."

Nura looked at her then with a quick sideways glance that carried pride, grief, and irritation in one hard line. Halima almost laughed. Instead she held out her hand. Aminul, practical to the bone, put a folded acknowledgment slip into it anyway.

"For the file," he said.

She tucked it into the edge of her blouse without unfolding it.

"One more thing," she said.

"Tell me."

"Don't clean the language. If a child is born before marriage on one page and after it on another, you leave both there. If a woman nursed a boy she could not claim, you leave that too. They can reject a life honestly. They don't get to improve it."

Aminul's tired face sharpened. "I understand."

"No," Halima said. "You don't. But you've listened better than most. Take that instead."

He accepted the correction the way he accepted paperwork: without performance.

When the trucks finally lurched inland, Halima climbed up with only the cloth bag, the birthing tin, and the lighter of the two bedding rolls. The absence of the ledger altered her balance. Twice in the first mile her hand went to the side of the bag and found only folded clothes, thread, a little oil, the receipt slip she had not read. The missing weight was worse than the weight had been. It made a hollow all along her hip.

The road to Nabinpara was dry enough to raise dust and bad enough to punish every spine. People who had spent their lives measuring distance by current and ferry time had to accept jolting land miles instead. The trucks threw them against one another, then apart. A baby cried until it slept from exhaustion. Someone at the back started retching and could not stop. Goats made the whole bed smell rank and hot. By the second hour everybody's face wore the same coat of road on it, verified and disputed alike.

Nura sat with her shoulder against Halima's and read the block marks chalked on the side rails whenever they stopped, trying to get ahead of what waited by naming it first. Once she said, "If the colony has proper light, I can fill forms after dark." Once she said, "Maybe the nursing office will be nearer." She did not say maybe I will still have a future. She had learned better than to ask hope to keep its own accounts.

Halima listened to the land change. River country spoke in slap, engine whine, wet bank crumble, birds lifting from reeds. This inland stretch spoke in wheel-rattle, dry leaves, dogs too tired to bark twice, and the flat ring of hand pumps on hard ground. The silence between villages felt wrong, not peaceful, just empty of water.

They reached Nabinpara in late afternoon under a white sky that seemed to hang lower because nothing on the horizon was moving. The colony had been built in rows, which was the first insult. There were rows of one-room houses on raised plinths, rows of unfinished drains, rows of poles with wires strung but not yet trusted, and rows of numbers painted in black above doorways so fresh that the tar still shone. There were no courtyards shaped by habit, no house fronts turned toward cousins, no shady argument under old trees. The place looked measured with a ruler for people whose lives had never been straight.

At the entrance a clerk stood on a chair and called numbers while another matched slips to keys. The verified households pressed forward with their plastic sleeves open, already measuring doors, walls, roofing, distance to the pump. The appeal cases were sent to a long tin barrack at the edge of the colony where numbered bays had been chalked on partition posts instead of painted above doors.

Jamila got her number before Halima did. It was C-12. The clerk called it twice because Jamila, practical even in shock, had already asked whether poultry were permitted inside the room at night. Majed climbed the plinth first and tested the door latch with repairman's suspicion. Then he ran his eyes along the roof seam and the window frame, calculating in silence which failures would come first. Jamila stood below with the hens and looked up at the number with the hard face she might have given an insult she still had to keep.

When she saw Halima watching, she lifted her chin once. "There's one shelf," she said. "Too high to reach without a stool."

Majed added, "The wall bolts are loose already."

Halima nodded. Ordinary talk, dented but still usable, landed low in her throat, and she took that too.

Sufia ended up two rows over with a kettle in one hand and her clove jar in the other. She put both down on the new threshold, looked around at the dry yard, and said to nobody, "Tea will taste of brick here."

Kashem's turn came later and worse. There was no painted door for him yet. He got a chalked partition in Transit Shed 4, Bay 7. He laughed once through his chipped tooth.

"They've given the goat a better address than me," he said, tying it to a post.

Then, after a beat, softer, more to the smoke-dark shawl folded on his bundle than to any person listening: "You would've hated this, Ma. Not enough mud."

Halima looked away because there were griefs a witness could bruise just by seeing.

Her own allocation came on a yellow transfer sheet. The clerk read it off in full: Pending Appeal, Household Disputed. Temporary Accommodation, Shed 3, Bay 2. He read it without malice. Malice required interest. Nura took the slip, read it twice, and laughed under her breath in the ugly, exhausted way of someone who had expected exactly this and still felt cut by it.

"Bay 2," she said. "At least they believe in counting."

Their bay was a space marked by two bamboo uprights, one rough partition wall, and a concrete floor not yet dirtied enough to look livable. A single hook had been hammered into one post. The roof trapped heat the way all tin trapped heat, but differently here. On the char, heat came damp and alive with river smell. Here it sat dry and metallic, with lime dust under it.

Nura dropped the bedding roll and stood with both hands on her hips, giving the bare floor the hard stare she used on stubborn paperwork, already trying to bully it into becoming home before dark.

"If I sweep first, it will only make more dust," she said.

"Then wait till the dust chooses a corner," Halima replied.

That earned her a real laugh, brief and tired.

She sat at last. The concrete felt alien after a lifetime of packed earth. It was harder, yes, but also less forgiving. Mud held the print of feet for a while. Concrete kept no memory of feet. Through the open doorway she could see the colony lanes filling with the first domestic rearrangements: women spreading wet cloth on new sills, men arguing over where to tie goats, children claiming drains as roads for imaginary cars. Already the place was being bent, a little, by use. Barely, but enough to show habit had started its work.

Jamila appeared in the doorway before dark, the blue-stamped sleeve still tucked under her arm; she did not trust C-12 enough to stop touching proof of it. Majed stood behind her with a coil of rope over one shoulder and one hen complaining softly from a basket at his feet.

"We've got floor space if the shed turns foul tonight," Jamila said. "Not comfort. Just floor. C-12."

Majed nodded toward the tin roof. "If that seam opens in rain, shout."

Jamila's careful tone carried the cost of it: a place offered from inside a verified door.

Before Halima could answer, Nura said, "If the roof starts leaking, we'll come. Till then this is ours."

Jamila looked at her a beat longer than the sentence required. Then she gave one short nod. "All right. Keep your bucket under the left corner if it sweats."

When they had gone, Halima touched the yellow transfer slip folded in Nura's hand and felt how thin a thing could still divide one room from another. Disputed, it said. Temporary. The words ought to have shrunk the bay. Instead Nura's flat refusal had somehow put walls on it.

Outside, voices rose from the lane before the bedding was fully unrolled. A mother-in-law had already started arguing over House 32 and a girl seven months gone. The girl's own mother cursed her for counting before the paint had dried. The battery speaker went on calling numbers over them: C-14, B-9, Transit Shed 2, widow attachment case.

Halima's hand went to the hollow along her hip where the ledger had ridden all morning and found only cloth. She had thought surrendering the book would be the hard part. Then the old pressure arrived before sunset, wearing wedding bangles and someone else's number.

Nura heard the quarrel too. She snapped open the least dusty sheet and spread it on the concrete with a crack of cloth. "If they want an answer, they can bring the girl in and let her speak for herself," she said.

The lane noise did not stop. Neither did the numbers. But a minute later the pregnant girl stood in their doorway with dust on her bangles and a fennel seed caught between her teeth, and the two older women had gone quiet enough to need Halima.

Nura held out the tin mug first. "Water," she said. Then, after the girl drank: "What's your name?"

"Rizia."

Outside, the speaker kept calling numbers, trying to turn them into households. Inside Bay 2, Nura said the girl's name back to her before anybody could count her under another roof, and Halima, empty at the hip, shifted over on the sheet to make room.
