# Chapter 8

Nura had already dragged the charpoy into the narrow stripe of gray light and was laying documents across the rope weave one by one by the time Halima crouched beside her. Overnight the floor had gone from packed earth to black paste. The men who had searched the house had left trunks open, baskets overturned, and the loft shelf half torn from the wall; night rain had used those new gaps before the room had time to cool. Mud sat in the heel dents of strangers. A brass plate leaned against a bed leg, furred with silt. One of Halima's clean birth cloths had slid into the doorway and drunk up the runoff until it looked like something used on a slaughter day. The house smelled of wet tin, mildew, old lamp smoke, and the sour paper smell that rose when ink started giving up.

Her split lip had dried into a dark seam at the corner of her mouth. When she bent, Halima could see the small burn mark on her wrist flash pale against the wet brown of her skin, a little moon she had carried since she was thirteen and tried to lift the rice pot barehanded before it was off the stove.

"Not there," Halima said. "If the sun hits too hard, the paper will buckle."

Nura did not look up. "If the sun comes at all, we'll be lucky."

Her voice was flat from bad sleep and from holding pain in the mouth. She moved a marriage paper from the charpoy to an upturned tin trunk, then changed her mind and moved it back. The seal on it had already feathered at the edges. A yellowed land-tax receipt lay beside it, the numbers swelling loose from the page, ready to drift toward any drier house.

Halima crouched slowly. Her back had stiffened in the damp. Every time she lowered herself she felt the old work in it, thirty years of crouching between women's knees, of bracing one shoulder against mud walls while babies came ugly or sideways or too fast. She picked up the tax receipt with two fingers under it, the way she would have lifted a premature infant out of soaked cloth, and slid a dry newspaper scrap beneath. The paper shivered. A corner tore anyway.

"Easy," Nura said.

"I know what's easy."

"Do you?"

The girl kept sorting. The quarrel had gone quiet and settled into the drag on every useful word. Halima heard the accusation under it: you knew men were coming, you knew rumor was thickening, you still sent me to the doorway with my hands and my mouth and no protection stronger than your name. There was no answer she could give that did not sound either false or small.

Water still dropped through the loosened tin seam over the storage corner. Nura had set a dented aluminum pot there to catch it, and each drop struck with a hard tick that made the room feel tighter. Outside, the char had the washed-out quiet that came after a storm heavy enough to flatten arguments for a few hours. No children yelled and no goat bells cut through it, only a cough from somewhere beyond the lane, the creak of wet bamboo, and the river talking low under everything.

On the mat by the wall lay the night's damage in little ruined neighborhoods: Nura's school certificate packet under a brass bowl, three witness statements curled into tubes, two vaccination cards clotted together, a widow's affidavit that had stuck to a piece of old sari cloth and come away with threads embedded in the stamp. Halima recognized the households by folds and smells before she recognized the names. Jamila always wrapped her papers with dried neem leaves against insects. Majed had once brought a receipt with engine grease thumbed into the margin. Rahima's few scraps carried goat smell and the stale ash of her hut. The men who had come searching had not cared which life belonged to which bundle. Once a house was declared dirty, everything inside it became the same heap under a boot.

Nura picked up the certificate packet and glanced at Halima as if daring her to speak first.

"Open it," Halima said.

"I was going to."

"Then do it before the gum sets."

Nura slipped a fingernail under the damp flap. Her careful hands were what always undid Halima. Even in anger the girl handled paper the way people did when they knew a torn corner could mean a missed exam, a failed application, another year gone. Officials held files away from their bodies to save their shirts. Nura's care came from need. The packet opened with a wet sigh. The top certificate had blotched along the left side, but the name line still held.

"The serial number's there," Nura said. "The seal is ugly but maybe still visible."

"Maybe won't feed a clerk."

"Nothing feeds a clerk except another clerk."

The line would have been funny another week. Halima felt the shape of the laugh and let it die. Nura flattened the paper with the heel of her hand, then hissed when the cut in her lip split wider.

Halima reached automatically. "Let me see."

Nura leaned away so fast the certificate almost slipped from her hand. "It's fine."

"It's cracked open again."

"It's a lip, not a broken mast."

Halima let her hand fall. The skin around the girl's mouth had gone puffy in the night. There was dried blood on the edge of her chin that she had missed washing. Halima had seen worse injuries before first breakfast. She had also seen wounds that mattered because of who delivered them and who watched. This one had been made in her own doorway, by a man young enough to call her auntie if he wanted favor from her, and half the lane had seen it happen. The blow had finished quickly. The publicness kept it open.

She turned back to the papers because tasks were still the only shape care could take without begging permission. She spread old lungis near the wall where the air moved best. She pried apart a set of witness forms with the blunt side of a hairpin. She laid Nura's school papers under a cooking pot weighted with stones so they would dry flatter. All morning the house filled with the small sounds of salvage: paper lifted from paper, cloth wrung out, water tipped from bowls, Nura's short breath when a seal smeared, Halima's tongue clicking once in irritation and then stopping because irritation was a luxury for people whose damage was not already done.

By midmorning the light strengthened enough to show what was lost and what was only sick. Dawn had blurred the whole room into one ruin; this hour divided it. A land slip could be saved if the ink had stayed inside the fibers. A birth date could survive even when a father's name turned blue and soft. A certificate with one dry corner might still be copied later if they got it to the mainland before mildew took the rest. Hope returned in fragments, and fragments demanded judgment.

Nura held up a folded receipt with two fingertips. "Whose is this?"

Halima squinted. Her left eye clouded first in damp weather. "Turn it."

Nura turned it toward the doorway. "No, the name is gone."

"Let me smell it."

Nura made a face but passed it over. Halima brought the paper close. Fish oil, rust, and the faint medicinal stink of kerosene. "Majed's. He keeps his receipt tin near the engine parts."

"You can smell a mechanic through mud now."

"I can smell carelessness through mud. Write his name on the back while you still know it."

Nura found a pencil stub and did as told. The movement steadied them for a while. Halima named households from scraps. Nura labeled the backs in her neat school hand before the pages dried beyond question. A ration card with the edge chewed by mice. A widow's petition folded into eight exact squares. A copy of a national identity card blurred so badly the face looked drowned. Each item came with a body attached inside Halima's memory: who had sweated over it, who had hidden it in a rice jar, who had begged her to hold it for a few days because their roof leaked worse than hers.

The labor calmed her until she went to the storage corner and saw the fallen basket of birth supplies.

The men had shoved their arms through it looking for hard covers and hidden compartments. Cotton rolls had burst and gone gray with floor water. The glass thermometer case had cracked under somebody's heel. A spool of clean cord had unravelled into mud like pale gut. Halima stood over it a moment too long. What rose first was the handling: male impatience, arms shoved deep, the quick certainty that nothing in a birth basket deserved reverence unless it could be turned into leverage. She bent and picked up the cord anyway. Mud slid along it. She washed what she could in a bowl and set it aside for household use only. After a floor like this, she would never bring it near a laboring woman again.

Nura was watching from the charpoy. "Leave that. We have enough ruined already."

"Ruined for one thing is not ruined for another."

"That's your answer for everything."

Halima wrung out the cord and hung it on a nail. "Because it's often true."

Nura stared at her a beat longer. Then she said, "They knew where to push."

Halima kept her back turned. "Everybody knows where a house will give if enough men lean."

"I don't mean the wall."

The tick from the rain pot marked the silence. Halima moved past it, went to the loft shelf, and reached behind the tin rice jar where the oilcloth bundle had been wedged after the search. She had hidden it there herself in the last minutes of confusion, when the house was full of strangers and Nura was shouting and some fool boy was laughing because he had found nothing but women's old blouses. The bundle was still there, heavier now from damp. Water had not soaked all the way through, but the cloth felt cold and wrong in her hands.

Nura got up at once. "You're checking it now?"

"Now is when the light is enough."

"Or now is when you finally believe they touched more than the trunks."

Halima set the bundle on the driest corner of the charpoy. The oilcloth had been wrapped and rewrapped over years until it remembered the shape of the notebook inside. She noticed the knot first. It was still tied, but the short end had been pulled on top instead of underneath. It was a small thing, stupid to any eye but hers. She always finished the knot with the long strip tucked flat because loose corners caught dust and made the packet bulkier. Now the corner stood up like a tongue.

Nura saw her pause. "What?"

"Someone opened it."

"You're sure?"

Halima looked at the knot again. "I didn't leave it like this."

Nura's jaw tightened. "One of them."

"Or in the dark after they left. Or when I moved it."

"You don't believe that."

No, she did not. The knot was only the first warning. The oilcloth had a new smear along the inside fold, the kind left by a wet thumb pressed in haste. The packet felt slightly thinner on one side. And the search had not been blind rummaging by hungry boys after gossip, no matter how much shouting and kicking had dressed it that way. There had been plenty of boot and spit and male fun in it, but someone in that room had also been looking with intention.

She untied the cloth.

The ledger lay inside, its black cover bloated at the corners, the stitching along the spine damp but holding. A few loose papers had been tucked around it to disguise the shape years ago, then kept there because on the char even disguises became storage. Nura leaned in over Halima's shoulder. Halima could feel the heat of her, the impatience, the old scent of coconut oil under rainwater.

The first pages of the notebook were safe. Oilcloth and thickness had done their work. Halima turned them with a thumbnail, checking the older entries: births from seasons when the river sat somewhere else, births in houses long gone, births whose fathers were dead, false, absent, or never written. Ink had feathered near the edges, but the dates still held. The names still held. Her own hand stared back at her from younger years, firmer then, less interrupted by pain in the knuckles.

Nura let out a breath Halima had not realized she was holding too. "The book's still here."

Halima kept turning. "If they've gutted the middle, the cover won't save us."

Halfway through the notebook she stopped. Between two thick original pages there should have been three thinner leaves folded twice and tucked deep, newer paper than the rest, copied clean because the old pages they echoed had gone faint with handling. She had not made those copies. Years ago she had found them already there after one of those bad monsoon stretches when too many women, helpers, and borrowed hands had been moving through the house to track every touch. Halima had kept them because they were accurate and because on the char nobody threw away a version of a truth that might one day need proving. The leaves were school-exercise paper, blue ruled, written in a careful hand trying hard to resemble formal script.

Now there were only the pressure marks where they had rested.

She checked again, slower. Turned back ten pages. Forward seven. Lifted the ledger and looked for the leaves stuck to the cover. Nothing. Only the original sheets, damp at the edges and clinging.

"What is it?" Nura said.

Halima did not answer at once. She set her thumb in one of the pale rectangular hollows and turned back to the original sheets the missing leaves had shadowed. The first still carried her old place marker at the top margin, blunt and brown with age: broken tamarind stump, east rise. Under it Shahnaz's breech-born girl was legible down to the birth month, but the father line had burst into a thumbprint of black where damp and handling had worried it thin. Two pages later another margin word remained clear enough to sting: shed strip. Aklima Bewa's note was split along the fold, late Karim written on one side, Harun, sister's boy, on the other, and the joining phrase between them eaten out so cleanly that only the pressure of vanished letters remained in the paper nap. Farther on, beside mosque-path east, Faruk's birth line still wore Halima's red pencil cross from the year she had to check twice whose courtyard he slept in. The copied leaf that had traveled with that page was gone too.

"There were pages with it," she said.

"Original pages?"

"No. Loose copies."

Nura frowned. "Of what?"

"Selected east-side entries."

Nura understood fast. Quick minds never let consequence wait. "You mean his side on the higher ground."

"Say his name."

Halima shut the notebook. "Names are cheap. Proof is expensive."

She opened the ledger again before Nura could answer. The original entries were still there, but age had thinned them almost to tissue. Shahnaz's father line had become that black bruise. The fold through Aklima Bewa's transfer turned greasy and near-transparent under Halima's nail. Faruk's note clung to the opposite page as if the damp wanted the mosque-path claim shut forever. The copied leaves had mattered because they carried those weak points in clean script: the month, the courtyard marker, the exact kin word that made a boy belong to one yard instead of another.

The disguise papers tucked around the ledger had barely shifted. A clinic referral still lay crooked over the back cover. A dead woman's vaccine card was still there. So was Halima's list of due dates from the last good season. Whoever reached into the bundle had left the useless scraps and taken only the entries that could travel.

Nura wiped fresh blood from her lip with the heel of her hand. "He spreads a story that you've been selling names. Men come in here and tear up the house. Then the only loose copies missing are the ones that make east ground easier to steal. How much more proof do you need before you speak?"

"If I speak in this lane now, it turns into me accusing a chairman because my own house smells bad."

"Your house smells bad because they put their feet through it."

"And whose word carries further today, mine or his?"

Nura's glance moved over the cracked thermometer case, the damp witness slips, and the cut that still kept opening at her mouth. "Then tell Aminul. He can at least put a date on it before Mofiz builds another story over the top."

Halima looked down at the ledger. "Tell him what. That pages I can't show him are missing from a book I shouldn't even admit was here."

"He already knows you've got things you have no business having."

"Knowing rumor isn't the same as receiving a charge."

Nura set the brass bowl down hard enough to make the wet certificates shiver. "That's always your road. Wait till the proof is clean, wait till the room is safer, wait till the man gives himself away. Other people do the paying while you wait."

The words crossed more than the charpoy between them. Saleha's marriage stood up behind them. Rahima's long silence stood up. Nura's own papers stood there too. Halima could have cut back if the girl had been wrong. Because she was not, the answer came slower.

"We don't open the door on only my mouth," Halima said. "But we don't sit here empty either."

Nura did not blink. "Then what do we do?"

"We write exactly what was taken before another hand or another rain gets to it. We keep it separate from the ledger. If Aminul asks, I can put something in front of him besides my temper."

A boy called from the lane, asking whether papers had survived. Nura turned her head and shouted that people could collect after asr prayer, not before. The boy went quiet at once. When she faced Halima again, some of the fury in her had tightened into purpose.

"Good," she said. "Then write."

Halima took an old school exercise book from the trunk bottom, tore out the least mold-marked pages, and sharpened the pencil stub with a razor blade. Her fingers protested. She ignored them. Near noon they ate cold rice mashed with salt and green chili over the charpoy without closing the ledger. The rice tasted of metal from the bowl. Then Halima bent over the page and began with the plainest line she knew: household name as publicly used, mother as bodily known, father as officially claimed, actual tie where relevant, child birth season, location marker.

She worked the eastern households in the order she had first attended them, because memory sat in her by labor and weather, not by plot line or office column. She began with Shahnaz's breech-born girl above the tamarind stump; that one she could rebuild nearly whole because the month, the mother, and the courtyard marker still held even if the father line sat there bruised black. Then she moved to Aklima Bewa behind the fertilizer shed and stopped. When she touched the fold, the page gave a soft tearing sigh. Late Karim on one side. Harun, sister's boy, on the other. Nothing whole between.

"What did the copy call him?" Nura asked.

"That's the part they wanted."

"You remember the house."

"I remember the house," Halima said. "I don't remember whether the clean line called him nephew, dependent, or simply in the courtyard. In a room full of men, those are three different truths."

Nura stared at the broken fold. "Then write the truth you can stand on."

So Halima wrote: Aklima Bewa, shed strip behind fertilizer shed; Harun, sister's boy, sleeping in Karim's yard since cholera year; linking phrase lost from original, copy missing. She hated the note as soon as it landed. It looked weak because it was weak. She ended on Faruk by the mosque path, the cowlick boy Mofiz liked to count as Sheikh blood when visitors came. That page still carried Halima's red cross and the house marker. It did not carry the clean proof that had once made the claim portable. Whenever her hand cramped, Nura took the page and copied the entries into her neat school script, leaving a hard blank in the Aklima line and a margin note: kin word lost. By the time the first sheet filled, they had two versions of the theft and one wound they could not sew closed.

Nura read over her own fresh lines. "He doesn't want the whole ledger. He wants the parts that can stand up without the rot showing."

Halima nodded. "The whole book would drown him. Now the shed-strip line is back to mush and my mouth."

By late afternoon the house had the look of a body stripped after surgery, still ugly, still smelling of wet things, but no longer helplessly open. The trunks had been shut. The driest papers were rewrapped in separate cloths with charcoal packets and neem leaves where possible. The unsalvageable scraps had been stacked in one corner for burning later, though Halima hated to think of fire touching names at all. Nura's school certificate packet sat beneath the brass bowl with a brick on top. Majed's receipt and two witness slips had survived. A widow's affidavit was beyond saving. Half a vaccination card had become nothing more than the ghost of a seal.

The ledger went back into oilcloth, but not with the new pages. Halima retied the knot flat and tight, then slid the rougher reconstruction under the lamp base where even floodwater would have to rise high to reach. Nura folded the cleaner copy inside her school certificate packet between the least damaged papers and set the brass bowl back over it. Another search could still hurt them. Even untouched, the cleaner copy still carried a hole where Aklima Bewa's claim should have joined to late Karim's house.

Nura watched her hands. "If they come again?"

"They'll come for the book."

"And?"

"And they'll leave without the part I can least afford to lose."

Nura touched the side of the packet with one careful finger, almost reverent, almost angry. "I should've bitten that bastard harder."

Halima looked at the seam in the girl's lip, the dried blood she had finally cleaned off, and the steadiness she had rebuilt by force over the day. "Next time," she said, "save your teeth for when it changes something."

Nura snorted. "You saying this changes something?"

"It changes what they can take," Halima said. "Not what the damp already ate."

The girl's eyes met hers then, level at last, neither soft nor healed. It was the first truly level look between them since the search. Halima accepted it the way she accepted a dry corner on a wet certificate: small mercy, still usable.

Evening came early inside the damp house. The tin roof held the day's heat and gave it back slowly. From far off, toward the ghat, a ferry engine coughed and caught. Someone in the lane began reciting prayer in a hoarse voice. Piety had little to do with it. After a night when walls and weather had both gone loose, routine felt like protection. Halima sat for a moment with the ledger hidden again and the new copy hidden elsewhere, listening to the house settle around that division.

The injury had edges now, and one of them was sharp enough to name. If Mofiz reached Aminul first, he could choose the missing kin word on Aklima Bewa's shed-strip line and make the whole claim sound cleaner than it had any right to be. Their new pages could prove the hunt. They could not prove that broken join. That was why the first ferry mattered.

Then she called to Nura to start letting people collect what had survived. There would be questions. There would be faces measuring her while they took back their damp lives. She was not ready for accusation, and she had no interest in defense before she had something firmer to set on the mat.

When the first knock came on the post, Halima put her palm flat on the charpoy rope and felt how much water still lived inside the house. Behind her Nura had already begun repacking the market bag with the papers that had to travel dry. Then Halima stood, wiped her hand on her sari, and went to answer it thinking of east-side names, of the copy under the lamp, of the cleaner version lying inside Nura's school packet where a greedy hand would have to choose differently to reach it, and of the hole still sitting in Aklima Bewa's line like a tooth knocked out, because if Aminul was going to hear this from her he had to hear the gap before Mofiz named it for him.
