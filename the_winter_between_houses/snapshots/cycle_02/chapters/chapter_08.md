# Chapter 8

Smoke sat low over Barrow Lane, and the snow underfoot held every bootprint like a grudge. Noreen carried Elsie's blue-enamel saucepan wrapped in a clean tea towel, tucked under her arm as if enamel could bruise. The pan had only gone next door three nights ago with a mash of carrots and turnip, but she had made herself wait to return it. She told herself she had been busy. She told herself Peter watched the clock every time she crossed the threshold. She told herself there was no reason for two women to stand shoulder to shoulder at a sink when one could hand a thing back and go home.

Somebody on the far corner had burned wet coal again and the smell dragged in the throat. Noreen tucked the towel tighter under her arm and kept one hand against the small, hard pull of her belly. A dense downward weight worked at her while the day pressed from all sides.

She knocked once on Elsie's back door. There was no answer. She knocked again and heard the slow scrape of a chair leg, then Elsie's voice from inside.

"Door's open. Mind the mat, it's soaked through."

Noreen stepped into the narrow scullery and shut the door fast to keep the heat. Heat was generous for what the house held: one stingy strip from the range and the damp breath off washing that would not dry. Elsie stood by the sink in a cardigan with two buttons missing, sleeves rolled over callused forearms, hair pinned up any way it would stay. Her face looked thinner than last month. Her belly did not.

"I brought your pan," Noreen said, lifting it like proof she had come for a single purpose.

"You didn't have to run it back in this weather."

"If I leave your things in my place, I start believing they're mine." Noreen made it sound joking and watched Elsie take the joke and fold it away.

Elsie wiped her hands on a cloth and nodded toward the lower cupboard. "Stick it under there, would you? I can't kneel today without needing a crane to get up again."

"You and me both."

Noreen crouched. The cupboard door under the sink had swollen at the bottom where the boards always drank drips, and the latch caught. She tugged once and nothing moved. She set the saucepan down, braced one palm against the frame, and pulled harder. The door came free with a wet crack and hit her shin. A small tin, two folded tea towels, and a sheaf of papers slid forward and spilled against her skirt.

"Damn thing," Elsie said behind her. "Leave it. I'll sort it later."

"You'll sort it with that back? Don't be daft."

Noreen gathered the papers first because they were already drinking the puddle near the pipe. Her fingers caught blue carbon, cheap cream paper, and one stiff card with a bookmaker's stamp in green ink. She froze long enough for the kettle to tick on the hob and for Elsie to take one step closer.

The top slip read:

HAYES & SON TURF ACCOUNT
T. HARROW
DUE: GBP 9 15s

Below it sat another slip. Then she saw a third with different dates and different stakes, but the same short name in the same blunt hand. A fourth had the corner torn, and at the bottom a pencilled note: settled part, see next week.

Noreen put the slips into a neat pile because her hands needed to do something precise. She kept turning pages. Under the betting slips sat a folded Post Office withdrawal docket with a purple stamp bleeding through from the back. The signature line read Elsie Harrow. The letters leaned right and heavy, the H climbing too tall. Noreen had seen that same upward H on the arrears authorisation in December, when the red-letter envelope had landed at her door by mistake.

She heard herself breathe through her teeth.

"What is it?" Elsie asked. "Has he left rubbish in there again?"

Noreen did not answer at once. She set the saucepan upright, moved the tea towels away from the drip, and spread the slips on the lid as if making room on an altar.

Elsie came down beside her slower than she should have, one hand flat to the cupboard edge, face already wary.

"Tom's betting bits," she said when she saw the first line. "He says they're old."

"Old from last Tuesday?" Noreen tapped a date with her nail. "And Thursday. And yesterday."

Elsie took one slip, frowned, put it back exactly where it had been. "He keeps saying he's squaring it. Men do that talk, don't they. They posture." Her voice had gone airy, the way people spoke to children over cracked glass.

Noreen held up the withdrawal docket. "Did you sign this?"

Elsie squinted. "Looks like my name."

"That wasn't what I asked."

Silence thickened. From next door a cough came through the wall, then a heavy tread. Somebody dragged a coal scuttle across stone. The terrace kept no secrets if the houses decided to listen.

"Let me see." Elsie took the docket and read it with her lips moving. "Thirty-two pounds."

"From your savings."

"He told me he might need ten for the coal man in January. We talked about ten."

"This says thirty-two, and it says drawn in your name on the twenty-first. You were at the clinic on the twenty-first because you came back with the blood test bruise and swore at the stairs all afternoon."

Elsie's mouth opened, shut, opened again. She looked down at her inner elbow as if the old bruise might still be there.

Noreen hated that she remembered the date. Hated more that she remembered because she had wanted excuses to call next door. She kept her voice level.

"There's another one under it."

The second docket was for twelve pounds and change. It came from a different week with the same signature and the same tall H. It carried the same lie of a flourish at the end, like a tail trying to pass for elegance.

Elsie sat back on her heels with a sharp breath and pressed her knuckles to her mouth. "God." She said it flat, without prayer and without shock, as if she needed to place the word somewhere solid.

Noreen stacked the dockets with the betting slips and looked past them into the open cupboard. There were three onions softening in a net bag, half a sack of flour tied with string, two jars of dripping clouded white, and a chipped mug full of odds and ends: a hinge screw, two safety pins, a pawn ticket stub, a small prayer card gone greasy at the corners. They were ordinary things, and they were the things that held a life together. In the middle of them lay paper that could gut a household.

"How much is it in total?" Elsie asked, still staring.

"I haven't added every line yet. It's north of a hundred easy."

"You're sure."

"I'm sure enough to stop pretending." Noreen said it softly because she knew what came after a sentence like that.

Elsie braced one hand on the floorboards and pushed herself upright with a grunt. She moved to the sink, ran cold water into a mug, did not drink it, emptied it, and ran it again. She moved from task to task with no end point. Noreen had seen her do it when panic climbed too high: peel potatoes she did not need, fold cloth already folded, and name every small job until fear had no bare patch to sit on.

"Maybe he used it to clear the pram account," Elsie said. "Maybe there's a reason that's ugly but not ruin."

"Then where's the receipt? Where's one blessed note from him saying what he paid and when?"

"He wouldn't keep it in a wet cupboard."

"No. He'd keep his own name clean and let yours do the dirty." Noreen heard the edge in her voice and forced it back. "Listen to me. I'm not trying to make this worse than it is. I'm trying to stop you being the last one in the room to know what room you're in."

Elsie shut her eyes. Her shoulders dropped, then held there.

"I asked you once already to stay out of my marriage," she said quietly.

"You asked me to keep one letter quiet. I did."

"You looked anyway."

"Your cupboard threw it at my knees."

The line might have been funny in another kitchen. It died in this one.

Noreen rose, feeling the drag in her lower back, and set the whole stack of slips on the dry part of the table. The urge to bolt tightened her calves. She could feel Peter's voice in her head counting the minutes she was not in her own house. She could hear what he would call it. She could hear the correction in his tone, the tidy grammar sharpened like a small knife. She rubbed her thumb over the side seam of her skirt until the stitch bit skin.

"I'll go if you want," she said. "But if I walk out now, you'll have this in your head alone and he'll be through that door before dark with some story about bad luck and mates and one final flutter. You know how he says final like it has a lock on it."

Elsie did not look at her. "Don't quote him to me."

"Then tell me what you need."

A long minute passed. Outside, a cart rattled at the end of the lane and stopped. Someone shouted for salt. The range hissed as dripped water struck iron.

Elsie turned at last, and the politeness was gone from her face, replaced by arithmetic.

"Sit down," she said. "If I stand, I'll keep wiping that sink till we both freeze." She pulled two chairs to the table. "You're going to show me every date, and we're going to do the sums right."

Noreen sat.

She spread the slips by week.

The boundary she had set for herself in the lane melted on the floor with the tracked snow from her boots.

Elsie had always believed that numbers could be tamed if she wrote them in columns. She had not believed in luck; she had believed in lists: coal by the scuttle, bread by the week, and soap to the ounce. She kept money in envelopes with names on them, each folded twice. Even shame could be managed if she named it correctly and put it where no one would step in it.

The papers on her kitchen table would not stay in their column.

She watched Noreen's hand move from slip to slip, forefinger blackened with carbon smudge, and felt the house tilt two degrees to the left. The shift left the cups upright and made every plate seem to lean toward breaking.

"Read this one," Noreen said.

Elsie took the docket and read it again though she had already read it twice. The date matched the week before Christmas. The amount sat in thick numerals. The branch stamp bit through the paper. The signature read Elsie Harrow.

Her own name, written by a man who slept in her bed when he came home warm enough to sleep.

"Could be mine," she said, because defense rose before sense. "I'm tired half the time. Maybe I signed and forgot."

Noreen looked up with a face that held no pity and no impatience, which was somehow worse than either. "Then compare it."

"Compare what?"

"Your actual hand."

Elsie reached to the mantel for the ration book and a pencil stub, then sat again. She wrote Elsie Harrow on the back of a coal chit. Her letters slanted left when she hurried. Her H sat squat, practical, no decoration. Tom's version climbed and looped as if trying to look educated from a distance.

Noreen set the two names side by side.

"He's copied your spelling," she said. "Not your hand."

Anger sparked in Elsie at the calm in Noreen's voice. Elsie pinched one slip between finger and thumb as if she might tear it in half, throw it on the range, and call the whole thing smoke. The motion stalled. She counted instead.

She counted nine pounds fifteen, then seven, then eleven, then fourteen and six. She added three and ten, then twelve and two, and then the lines marked settled part and carried over. By the time she reached the last slip, the total sat at one hundred forty-six pounds and some shillings. Noreen had said north of a hundred. Noreen had spared her the exact weight by less than a pound.

The amount did not land as a number first. It landed as rooms.

It was seven months' rent if the landlord stayed kind.

It was coal to March and then some.

It was baby clothes, blankets, pram instalments, doctor fares, bread, milk, and the cheap oranges Tom only bought when he wanted to be praised for trying.

It was money she had never held at once in her life.

She put the pencil down before the wood snapped in her hand.

"He can't owe that," she said. "No one gives him that much rope."

"Bookies do, till they don't." Noreen touched the edge of one slip where a thumbprint had blurred the ink. "Look here. This note says collect at house if missed again."

Elsie felt a small clean line of cold open between her shoulders. Blizzard fear had shape: road, ice, man in a ditch. This one wore paper: knock-at-the-door fear, signature fear. It was a different weather entirely.

"If they come," she said, "they come to me as well."

"Aye."

"If the Post Office asks, they'll say I signed these."

"Aye."

"And if I say I didn't, I'm calling my husband a forger in public."

Noreen's jaw tightened. "Yes."

The three yeses rang like spoons dropped in a sink.

Elsie stood, then sat again because standing made the child inside her press low and hard. She shifted in the chair, found no comfortable spot, tried anyway.

"I can hear him now," she said. "He'll say it was temporary. He'll say he was covering us till wages settled. He'll say if I make noise we'll both lose everything." Her voice went dry and precise. "He'll say a wife should not humiliate her husband in a winter like this."

"He'll say whatever keeps him one week ahead of the man he owes," Noreen said. "That doesn't make it true."

"True doesn't buy me a thing if I can't pay to prove it."

Noreen almost smiled at that, not from humor but recognition. "Aye. That's the trap."

Elsie pushed the slips into a tighter stack. The table had flour grit under her palms; she ground it back and forth until the skin burned.

"Why did you keep the first letter quiet in December?" she asked.

"Because you asked me. Because you looked like you might split at the seam if one more person knew." Noreen held her gaze. "And because I thought maybe it was one desperate thing. Men do stupid things in December and call them promises. I was wrong."

Elsie nodded once. She remembered that day too clearly now: red stamp on the envelope, Noreen standing on the step with snow in her hair, both of them speaking low so the lane would not hear. She had believed she could contain it then. One lie, one payment, one month and done.

Containment had lasted six weeks.

From next door came the thud of a door, then Arthur's voice through plaster, apologizing to no one in particular as he knocked a pan lid to the floor. Elsie had never seen a grown man so careful with borrowed space. He lined his boots at the mat as if someone were grading him, folded his towel to a square, thanked for tea as if it were a signed favor. She did not dislike him. She disliked that careful men made careless men look like weather you could no longer call accidental.

Noreen caught the sound too and looked toward the wall. "He's in, then."

"Arthur?"

"Aye. Peter won't be far behind, and if he asks where I've been I'll need a short answer."

Elsie heard the warning under the words. She had heard Peter's short answers before and the long corrections that followed.

"Then we do this now." Elsie pulled her housekeeping tin from the shelf above the range. The lid squealed when she opened it. Inside sat coins wrapped in paper, two pound notes folded to soft cloth, and envelopes labeled coal, milk, rent in her compact hand. She counted out loud because the room felt less likely to spin if her mouth kept moving.

"Rent envelope: five pounds, ten. Coal: one pound, four and six. Milk: twelve and three. Emergency: two pounds even." She stopped. "That's what we have in cash unless Tom's wages come Friday, and I don't trust Friday anymore."

Noreen leaned forward, elbows on knees. "You need to keep enough where he expects it. If he sees empty envelopes, he'll start tearing boards up."

"He already checks the cupboards." Elsie gave a humorless breath. "Started last week as if tins have legs."

"Then move only part. Quietly."

Elsie slid one pound note from emergency and folded it into the lining seam of her cardigan pocket. She tucked another beneath the wax paper under the dripping jar. They were old cleaner's habits, to hide things in plain mess.

"I hate doing this in my own kitchen," she said.

"I know."

"No, you don't. Not this exact shape."

Noreen did not flinch. "You're right. Not this exact shape."

That answer, honest and unsoftened, made Elsie trust her more than any comfort would have.

She looked back to the slips. "What do we do with these?"

"If they stay here, he can burn them."

"If they leave here and he notices, he'll know someone's seen."

"He should know someone has."

Elsie gave her a sharp look. "That's brave talk from the house next door. I'm the one in the bed with him."

Noreen's laughter came out thin, a crack more than sound. "Don't I know it."

The room held that line between them, hot and bright, then cooled.

Elsie pulled one betting slip free and turned it over. On the back Tom had written a shopping reminder in pencil: baccy, boot polish, razor blades. She stared at the words until they blurred.

"He wrote this while owing us six months of air," she said.

Noreen swore under her breath, a clean curse laid down like a tool.

Elsie set the slip back in the pile and arranged the stack by date again. If she touched each edge square, she could think.

"I won't confront him with all of it tonight," she said. "Not with no plan and no witness. He can talk circles when he smells panic."

"Agreed."

"Tomorrow I'll go to the Post Office before he wakes, if roads are passable. I'll ask for the account history and tell them no withdrawals without me in person. Can they do that?"

"They can note it if the clerk is decent. If not, ask for the supervisor and keep your voice flat."

"You've done this before?"

"Not with savings. With my mother's rent book when my brother tried to collect and drink it. Different office, same men behind glass."

Elsie nodded. The confession gave her a new detail, a piece of Noreen she had not asked for and now needed.

"I'll take the dockets," Noreen said. "Not the betting slips. If Tom looks, he might think he misplaced one paper. He won't think that about all of them gone."

"Take copies, not originals."

"How?"

"I'll copy by hand now while there's light. Dates, amounts, names. You keep the list at yours. If he burns these, we still have record."

Noreen looked at her with something like respect and something like sorrow. "There she is," she said. "House could come down and you'd still line up the figures straight."

"If I stop doing sums, I'll start screaming." Elsie met her eyes. "And if I start, I don't know when I stop."

Noreen reached across the table and put her hand over Elsie's wrist, just once, firm and warm and gone before it could become anything else.

"Then don't scream yet," she said. "Write."

Elsie fetched the school exercise book she used for shopping tallies and sat back down. Her fingers cramped by the third line, but she kept going.

She wrote each line in order: date, amount, initials, notes.

At the bottom of the page she wrote a second heading: Withdrawals not signed by me.

When she finished, the kitchen had darkened to the blue hour that made all corners look colder than they were. She lit the small lamp and watched the flame find itself.

Noreen read the copied page, folded it twice, and slid it into her brassiere, the only place Peter never searched because he believed decency stopped at cloth.

"I'll keep this dry," she said.

Elsie gathered the originals and returned them to the tin under the sink, exactly where they had been, tea towels over top, door pushed almost closed but not fully latched. She wiped the wet patch on the boards so no mark remained.

"If he asks," she said, "you dropped the pan and swore and left."

"I did swear, to be fair."

"Not loud enough for the lane."

Noreen rose slowly, one hand on the table edge. "Els."

"What."

"This isn't just him breaking your heart. If Hayes sends a collector, he'll shout your name on this step like you're the one dodging payment. If the Post Office checks those dockets, it's your signature they'll call forgery. Don't let him sweet-talk you out of that."

Elsie held the back of her chair until her knuckles blanched. The words "I know" rose first, then "I've always known," and both curdled into lies before she could speak.

"I won't," she said.

Noreen picked up the blue saucepan, now emptied of all purpose except carrying home a story she could not tell aloud, and moved to the door. Before stepping out she turned back.

"If he comes in rough or starts clawing for those papers, don't stand and argue. Knock once on the wall, twice on the pipe, then come through my scullery with your coat. I'll hear."

"Even with Arthur there?"

"Especially with Arthur there. Better one sober witness than none if this turns into police business."

"That sounds like trust."

"It sounds like a man who writes days on his tea bags so nobody wastes one." Noreen's mouth twitched. "I'll trust that kind of daft over a grand speech."

Then she was gone into the lane, boots crunching on old ice.

Elsie stood in the center of her kitchen and listened until the sound vanished.

She looked at the cupboard door, swollen wood, flaking paint, rusted hinge that never sat true. She had cursed that door for two winters and meant to fix it each spring. It had stuck, then given, then emptied its mouth over her knees.

By the time Tom came home, the tea had stewed to bitterness and the lamp glass had filmed with soot. He shouldered the door hard enough to rattle the hook, cap askew, cheeks wind-burned, breath sweet with ale over stale smoke.

"Don't start on me, Els," he said before she spoke. "I need three quid now. Len from Hayes was at the depot. He said if I don't settle tonight, he comes here at first light."

He reached for the housekeeping tin on the shelf. Elsie caught the lid with both hands.

"No. That money buys coal and milk."

"For us, love. I'm doing this for us." He pried her fingers back one by one, not rough enough to bruise and not gentle enough to mistake. Coins clattered in the tin. He took the pound note she had left in emergency and stuffed it into his coat.

Then his eyes dropped to the sink cupboard, to the door sitting wrong in its frame.

"Who was in there?"

"The latch stuck. Noreen helped with the pan."

He crouched, yanked the swollen door, and pulled out the packet of betting slips and dockets from under the tea towels. Elsie stepped forward, hand out.

"Leave those."

Tom tucked the papers inside his shirt. "No one sees these. You hear me? You go to the Post Office or start gossiping over walls, they'll say you signed every line and I'll back them. Keep your mouth shut till I square it."

He was gone before she answered, boots striking the flags, back gate banging once against brick.

Elsie stood with her palm flat on the pipe under the sink, feeling it hum with next door's heat and the code she had not used yet.
