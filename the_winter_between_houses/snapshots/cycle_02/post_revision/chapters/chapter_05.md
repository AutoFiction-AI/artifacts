# Chapter 5

Pain hit before Noreen could rinse yesterday's fat from the roasting tin. It was not the usual pulling ache she had lived with for weeks. This was a hard band, hot and mean, cinching from spine to pubic bone as if somebody had looped wire around her and started turning a handle. By evening, the same storm that had sent runners at dawn was still trying to bury the street lamps alive.

She stayed bent over the sink until it eased. Meltwater from her sleeves dripped onto the flagstones. The window above the tap was crusted white from the inside, and the pane rattled under wind that sounded like sand thrown by the handful.

"Peter," she called.

No answer. He was in the front room with the gas fire turned low to save coins, working through council papers he had brought home because he did not trust the junior clerk with totals. She could picture him without seeing him: wire-rim glasses sliding down his nose, cuffs protected by old shirt garters, neat stacks squared at exact right angles on the table, straight paper enlisted to force straight life.

She rinsed her hands, wiped them on her apron, and took two steps toward the hall before another contraction caught her harder than the first. She grabbed the dresser and waited through it, breath hissing through her teeth. When it passed, warmth spread between her thighs.

For a second she told herself it was urine. Women leaked. Women were not machines. Then she looked down.

Blood darkened the inside seam of her underwear and crawled in a thin line along her left stocking. Not bright, not a clean cut. Brown-red, clotted where it met the wool.

She shouted his name again, louder. "Peter. Now."

He came to the doorway frowning, one finger still hooked inside a ledger page to keep his place. "You do not have to shout through the house."

"I'm bleeding."

He blinked once, more irritated than alarmed. "You have had spotting before. Mrs Keane said strain can cause it. Sit down."

"This isn't spotting." She pressed her palm against her lower belly as another grip of pain rose. "Get Elsie. Get the phone."

"You are upset because of the weather. Everyone is upset because of the weather."

Outside, wind drove snow against the door in dry rattling bursts. The lane had disappeared by afternoon; from the upstairs bedroom she had watched the top of Mrs Bhatti's sign vanish first, then the black iron rail, then half the front gate. The radio had said no buses since noon and no guarantee by morning.

"Peter, for God's sake."

He sighed the way he did when figures failed to balance. "You are not due for weeks. If I wake half the lane and this is nerves, you will have made a spectacle of yourself."

She laughed once, a sharp, ugly bark that hurt her ribs. "I don't care about spectacle. I care about not dropping this baby in the kitchen."

The third contraction bent her. She slid down the dresser to one knee, fingers slipping on the worn varnish. Peter set the ledger on the sideboard with careful hands, still protecting the binding, and crouched beside her without touching.

"Can you stand?"

"Get Elsie."

"I asked whether you can stand."

She grabbed his sleeve and pulled him close enough to smell shaving soap and the cold tobacco he pretended he did not smoke anymore. "If you make me ask again, I will crawl next door and wake her myself."

That, finally, moved him. He straightened, jaw tight. "Stay in here. Do not walk about." He took his coat from the peg, then paused to button every button to the throat before opening the door to the storm.

She listened to him fight the drift on the step, boots scraping, then the short, muffled thud of his fist against the Harrows' wall door.

By the time Elsie came in, Noreen had made it to the kitchen chair and bled through one folded tea towel. She held the towel between her legs and stared at the range, willing the fire not to die.

Elsie arrived half-laced, hair shoved under a wool scarf that had once been navy and now lived somewhere between slate and ash. She had thrown Tom's old depot coat over her nightdress and forgotten to wipe flour from one forearm. A stub of carpenter's pencil rode behind her ear, the one she used for marking ration sums on the inside of cupboard doors where men never looked.

"Show me," Elsie said.

Peter stepped between them. "There is no need for theatrics. She has had a fright."

Elsie did not raise her voice. "Move, Peter."

He held her stare for two beats too long, then shifted a fraction. She passed him anyway, knelt in front of Noreen, and lifted the soaked towel with efficient fingers.

Noreen watched Elsie's face as she checked the blood. Elsie's mouth tightened, then flattened into the polite calm she wore with landlords and matrons.

"When did it start?"

"Fifteen, twenty minutes. Maybe." Noreen swallowed. "Pain's coming in waves."

Elsie took the little clock from her coat pocket, cracked plastic face held together with two strips of plaster, and set it on the table. "We'll count proper. Next one, tell me the second it grabs."

"This is absurd," Peter said. "You are encouraging panic."

"No," Elsie said. "I'm measuring it so we know if she's in labor or not."

"She is not in labor."

"Maybe she isn't. Maybe she is. We don't get to pick by saying it harder."

Noreen felt the next contraction build like a wave under floorboards. "Now," she said.

Elsie clicked the clock. Peter paced two steps toward the hall, then back, then reached for the kettle and set it to boil with abrupt force.

"Fine," he said. "You may time your experiment. Then we can stop this nonsense."

Noreen's pain peaked and broke. She breathed through her mouth and tasted coal smoke. Elsie wrote a number with the pencil stub on the back of an old envelope: `7:14`.

"Seven minutes since the last by your guess," Elsie said. "We'll see the next. Peter, I need clean towels, not the ones by the sink. Top shelf airing cupboard. And bring the paraffin heater through."

"The paraffin is rationed."

"So is blood," Elsie said.

For the first time that night, Peter lost his footing in the argument. The stain kept widening on the chair cushion while he scrubbed his thumb against the ink on his forefinger until it squeaked. Then he went upstairs without answering.

The house settled around them with winter noises: the range ticking, wind under the door, muffled cough from next door through the shared wall. Noreen held Elsie's wrist when the next contraction hit and felt how steady Elsie's pulse stayed.

"I'm sorry," Noreen whispered when it eased. "I dragged you out of bed."

Elsie shook her head. "Don't be thick. You did right."

Noreen tried to joke and could not. "If this baby's daft enough to come now, tell it to wait till after payday."

A shadow of a smile passed over Elsie's face and was gone. "You can negotiate with it yourself. I'm just the clerk."

Peter came back with towels and the heater, set both down too hard, and announced to the room rather than to either woman, "Roads are closed from the roundabout. I saw no lights on the lane except Bhatti's back room."

"Phone the clinic," Elsie said.

"The line has been unreliable all day."

"Try anyway."

He did not move.

Noreen felt another cramp begin, slower this time but deeper, starting in bone. She looked at her husband and saw what had once passed for steadiness: his refusal to waste effort, his certainty that panic killed faster than illness. Tonight it looked like something else. It looked like a man defending his own idea of order while her body leaked through his categories.

"Peter," she said softly, because soft was what reached him when shouting failed. "Ring them."

He went to the hall at last.

Elsie marked `7:23` on the envelope. "Still regular enough to worry." She pressed a folded towel into Noreen's hands. "Squeeze this when it bites. Don't lock your legs."

In the hall, the rotary dial clicked and spun, clicked and spun. Peter's voice came sharp, formal, clipped into the mouthpiece.

"Calderbank exchange? I require maternity... yes, immediately... no, this is not social."

Noreen closed her eyes. Snow hissed against the window. The towel in her hands smelled of sunlight from summer lines long gone.

By the time Peter returned, the line had cut twice and her contractions were five to six minutes apart.

"No answer at clinic," he said. "Exchange says calls are queued because half the district has no power." He aimed the last question at Elsie like a charge sheet. "Are you satisfied?"

Elsie stood. "No. I'm going to ring Keane's number direct."

"You are not turning my hallway into a public office."

Noreen pushed herself upright on shaking legs. "It is not your hallway if I'm the one bleeding in it."

The words surprised all three of them, most of all her. Peter's mouth opened, then closed. Elsie took the chance, crossed to the phone, and began dialing before he could recover.

Outside, snow kept falling, determined to erase every track by morning.

***

The next hour stretched thin as wire.

The first direct call to Keane's flat rang into static. Elsie swore, dialed exchange instead, and came back with clipped instructions from a woman who would not give her name: get Noreen on her left side now, change towels on a clock not a guess, and log every contraction. Elsie moved her to the settee and wedged a folded blanket under her hip before Peter could argue.

The second call reached Keane's downstairs neighbor, breathless and annoyed. Keane had gone to an emergency near the mills and might not get back before midnight. Elsie tore a page from Peter's blotter, wrote Noreen's symptoms and their address in block letters, and shoved it into his hand. "If this towel fills in under half an hour, you walk that to the police box. No debate." Peter started to set the page aside. Elsie caught his wrist, put the pencil in his fingers, and said, "Write the trigger time in your own hand." He stared at her, then wrote `30 minutes` under her line, each letter pressed so hard the nib scored through the paper. After that he pulled on scarf and gloves and used the coal shovel to cut a narrow path from the door to the gate.

The third call connected for less than a minute. Elsie got out, "Dark blood, contractions five to six," before the line screamed and dropped. She set Peter's lamp and greatcoat by the threshold and moved his boots there with them. Peter said, "You are not ordering me into that storm on speculation." Noreen answered through clenched teeth, "If it comes to that, you go. I'm done asking."

Noreen lay on her left side on the settee with a blanket folded under her hips, as instructed by a nurse at the exchange who might not have been a nurse at all. Her knees were drawn up because that hurt least. The paraffin heater breathed hot metal and stale fuel into the room. Each contraction rolled through her in a shape she learned and hated: low warning ache, hard squeeze, knife line down the groin, then shaky relief.

By then the settee cushion had gone damp and cold under Noreen's hip, and each new wave left a finer tremor in her thighs. Elsie still kept the envelope, but she only marked it when the pattern shifted; the rest of her attention stayed on Noreen's breath and the color in her mouth. She had moved her own clock and Noreen's wedding photo to the mantel, clearing space like a woman setting out instruments. Nothing wasted, nothing theatrical, every motion exact.

More frightening than the pain was the way Peter had shifted from husband to clerk. He stood near the doorway with his coat still on, wet shoulders shining in lamplight, polishing his glasses again and again as if clean lenses could turn blood into paperwork. The gesture was almost tender in ordinary hours; now it made Noreen want to throw something heavy.

"You should conserve paraffin," he said after one contraction passed. "We may need it for days."

Elsie kept her pencil moving. "If she goes into full labor this early, fuel won't be your worst problem."

"You are not qualified to make that judgment."

"Neither are you."

Noreen laughed again, thinner this time. "If we die, at least you'll have the argument won."

Peter flinched. He moved to the settee and touched her shoulder at last, fingertips light, uncertain. "You are not dying. Do not say idiotic things."

She almost leaned into his hand from habit. Then another contraction hit and she gripped the blanket so hard her nails tore threads loose.

Elsie counted against the panic rising in the room. "One, two, three, breathe. In through your nose, out through your mouth. Stay with me."

"Don't boss me," Noreen said through her teeth.

"Then boss yourself." Elsie met her eyes. "But breathe."

Noreen breathed.

At half eight, the phone rang.

Peter got there first. "Walsh speaking." His back straightened. "Mrs Keane, yes. She has had bleeding and spasmodic pain for approximately--for about an hour." He cupped the mouthpiece with his palm as if the room were hostile, and delivered the next words in the tone of a man reading a complaint. "I am not saying it is nothing. I am saying two women here are amplifying each other."

Elsie was on him before Noreen could curse. She took the receiver from his hand with a force that made the cord slap the skirting board.

"Mrs Keane? It's Elsie Harrow, from next door. Dark blood, not bright. One cloth soaked through, now it's slower but still coming. Pains were seven apart, now six, one hit at five. Started around seven o'clock." She listened, nodding. "No waters. Skin is warm but not hot. Baby moved after the first rough hour, yes."

Peter reached for the phone. Elsie turned her shoulder and blocked him without ceremony.

Noreen heard only Elsie's side. The missing half scraped at her nerves; each pause opened space for the worst picture her mind could make.

"Yes, she's on her left. Blanket under the hip."

"Kettle's boiled. Towels are clean."

"No, lane is blocked right to the gate. We can't get a motor in."

"Right. I'm writing that now."

She rummaged for a pen, found none, snapped Peter's fountain pen from the mantel with a muttered sorry that was not sorry at all, and wrote on the back of a gas bill.

Noreen kept one hand on her belly and did the sums in her head: one towel soaked, then two, then Peter sent out on foot whether he approved or not.

"Fresh folded towel every half hour," Elsie said into the phone. "If one floods sooner, he goes to the police box on foot."

Peter straightened. "Do not volunteer me like a parcel."

Noreen cut in before Elsie could answer. "Get your boots by the door and shut up."

Elsie listened, nodding once. "Right. If the pains spread out and lose that knife edge, we keep her still and keep watching."

Peter opened his mouth again. Elsie turned away from him and spoke harder into the receiver. "No, we're not putting fingers in. We're not guessing."

"Put that in his ledger," Noreen said, then breathed through another grip of pain.

"Sweet tea in sips, keep her warm but not sweating," Elsie said, writing fast.

She pressed the pen harder as Keane continued. "If blood turns fresh red, or the baby goes quiet, we ring back at once. Yes. I have it."

Elsie handed the receiver to Noreen. "She wants your voice."

Noreen pressed cold Bakelite to her ear. Keane's voice came rough with line noise and exhaustion, brisk enough to cut through fear.

"Nora, listen to me. I can't get a car up your road tonight. Nobody can. That does not mean you are abandoned. It means we manage risk where you are. I need your agreement on three triggers: fresh red blood, no movement, or a cloth soaked in under thirty minutes. If any one of those happens, you send for help on foot at once. Can you do that?"

"I can," Noreen said, and surprised herself by meaning it.

"Good woman. Is the baby moving now?"

"A little flutter, then a kick after the last one."

"That's what I wanted to hear. I'm flagging your name at the top of my call sheet. If lines stay up, I'll ring you in an hour. If lines fail, keep logging and ring exchange every thirty minutes. If you need a runner, put your husband on and keep yourself still. Do I have your yes?"

There was a pause, static hissing. Then, softer: "And Nora, no heroics. Bleeding in pregnancy is never nothing. Anyone tells you different, send them to me."

Noreen answered into the receiver and let the words land on Peter. "Right. Thank you."

When she hung up, the room seemed briefly too quiet, as if all three had been holding breath for permission.

Peter reclaimed his pen from the table and wiped the nib on a handkerchief. "She did not say it is labor."

Elsie folded another towel into a thick pad. "She didn't say it's nothing either."

"Words matter."

"So does blood on your chair."

Noreen wanted to laugh and hit and sleep, all in one motion. She took the new towel, shifted her hips, and bit down on a groan when the next contraction arrived. It had changed: broader, less knife, more pressure. Still frightening. Still real.

Time stopped feeling like numbers and turned into damage control.

She got down two swallows of tea before nausea climbed her throat. She pissed in the chamber pot because standing sent sparks down her thighs.

Each wave stole her voice for a few seconds, so she squeezed Elsie's wrist when it started and tapped twice when it released. Elsie adjusted towels, shifted blankets, and remade the spare bed with old sheets in case they needed more room than the settee.

Peter fed the range and watched the fire as if heat alone could settle the argument. Every clang of the iron door went through Noreen's teeth.

At ten, she passed a clot the size of a plum pit and started crying in soundless bursts she could not stop. Elsie wiped her between contractions with warm water and did not pretend there was dignity in it. Peter stood at the foot of the settee, white-faced, reciting practicalities as if order on paper could stop blood in a body.

The tone hurt worse than the cramp. It told Noreen he was already drafting tomorrow's story, one where this night could be filed as nerves.

"If this stabilizes by midnight, we all need sleep. We can reassess at first light."

Noreen spoke into the blanket. "My body's not one of your tidy plans, Peter."

"I am trying to keep this house in one piece."

"Keep it in one piece somewhere else."

He went to the kitchen and came back with a dry dishcloth bunched in both hands, then seemed to realize what he was doing and dropped it on the side table, ashamed and angry in equal parts.

Around eleven, the contractions began to drift apart. Six minutes became eight, then ten. The pain dulled to a heavy drag across her lower back. Bleeding slowed from soak to smear. She felt three distinct kicks in twenty minutes, each one a blunt thud under skin that made her want to sob with relief.

Elsie did not trust it yet. She kept timing. She kept writing. She changed towels, wrung stained cloths into the bucket, and fed the clock with her eyes like it might betray them if she looked away.

Noreen watched her and thought of last month, of Elsie standing in borrowed slippers at the back door pretending she had come for sugar when she had really come because the silence in her own house was too loud. People said women like Elsie were careful, decent, not made for crisis. They were wrong in the way people were wrong when they mistook quiet for passivity.

Near midnight the phone rang once, then died. No second ring. Dead line.

Peter lifted the receiver, listened to nothing, and replaced it with surgical precision.

"Out," he said.

"Of course it's out," Noreen muttered.

Elsie rose and checked the front latch. "Then we change it. Door on single catch, lamp at the step, note in your coat. If I say run, you run. Nobody waits for a dial tone."

"The bolt stays across at night," Peter said.

Noreen turned her head toward him. "Not tonight. Slide it back."

He held her eyes, then dragged the bolt open with a scrape that sounded like something tearing.

Elsie tucked a hot-water bottle against Noreen's spine and sat on the floor with her back against the settee, knees up, clock in her lap. She looked suddenly older than thirty-one, not in face but in posture: the posture of someone who had learned how much work terror required and had chosen to do it anyway.

"I'm staying till morning," she said.

Peter opened his mouth.

Noreen cut him off. "She's staying."

He held still for a long moment, jaw working as he measured the cost. Then he nodded once. "Very well. But this does not become habit."

Noreen turned her face to the cushion so he would not see the expression she could not smooth in time.

Habit, she thought. As if dependence had not already become habit in this lane, in this winter, in both their marriages.

At twenty past one, a contraction hit that did not crest and ease. It clamped and stayed. Warmth spilled sudden and bright between Noreen's thighs.

Elsie stripped back the blanket, saw the fresh red, and said Peter's name in a voice that made him run.

"The note. Now."

Peter turned toward the window where snow struck sideways. "I cannot leave you two in this condition. Not with her like this."

"You can and you will," Noreen said. "Police box. Bring whoever has boots."

He hesitated one beat too long. Elsie shoved the folded symptom note into his coat and pushed the door open. Cold punched the room. Peter went, lantern swinging.

The house went very quiet after he left, except for Noreen's breathing. Another contraction gathered in her spine before the first fear had drained. It came as a deep pull under her ribs, then dropped hard into her pelvis and locked there. She dug both hands under the settee cushion, knuckles grinding wood, and heard herself making a low animal sound she had never made before.

"Stay with me," Elsie said. "Don't bear down. Breathe to the count."

The pain sharpened until her vision narrowed to the brass tack heads along the settee arm. She tasted pennies. Sweat ran cold from her neck to her shoulder blades. At the top of it she felt pressure so fierce she thought her bones might split, then the grip began to peel away in ragged increments.

When it finally released, she shook so hard her teeth clicked.

Elsie lifted the towel and checked in the lamplight. "Fresh red streak across the middle. Not flooding right now. Darker at the edges. No new clot." She folded a clean towel into place and marked the envelope with a blunt, angry jab of pencil: `1:27 fresh red`.

Noreen lay rigid, waiting for movement. For five minutes the baby stayed still, and by seven her mouth filled with the taste of metal. Elsie kept one palm on her belly and counted aloud anyway, steady as a metronome.

At nine minutes a heel dragged under Noreen's ribs, slow and stubborn. She cried hard enough to choke.

Peter came back thirty minutes later with snow clotted on his eyebrows and two strips of skin peeled raw from his knuckles where the shovel had slipped. A constable at the box had phoned the station: no ambulance could clear Barrow Lane before dawn, but the address was logged for the first team that got free.

Elsie did not let the sentence settle. "We change protocol now. No waiting for calls. Peter, you go again at three with the updated times and bleed note. If one towel floods inside twenty minutes, you don't argue, you run."

"I have already gone once through that lane."

"And you'll go again," Noreen said. "Or you can explain to Keane why you chose your sleep over my blood."

His jaw flexed. "Very well. Three o'clock."

At two ten, another contraction came sharp and short. It started as a dull drag across Noreen's back, tightened to a vise that made her curl around her own belly, then broke with a wet gasp and a tremor in her thighs. Elsie checked again: rust-brown smear over bright threads, less than before, cloth still mostly dry at the corners. She wrote `2:14 less volume` and held the envelope up for Peter to see.

At three, Peter put the coat back on without being asked and went out with the note, lantern, and his wounded knuckles wrapped in dishcloth strips.

He returned before four with no ambulance and no comfort, only word that the station had repeated her address twice and would send whoever got a vehicle free first. Noreen did not sleep after that. She listened to wind hammer the chimney, to Elsie murmuring numbers over each tightening, to Peter at the table under the weak lamp writing a list of who might talk if they had seen him leave at night while her bloody towels soaked in a bucket beside his shoe.

She stopped expecting him to look up.

***

Morning arrived as a diluted grey through frosted glass.

The storm had not broken, but the wind had dropped to a lower, steadier moan. Snow stood shoulder-high against the front wall. The lane was a white trench; any tracks from the night had already blown flat, and only the top half of Mrs Bhatti's sign peered over a drift like a half-sunk boat.

Noreen woke with a dry mouth and a back that felt beaten. For three full minutes, nothing tightened. Then a mild cramp came and went, more warning than attack.

Elsie was already up, rinsing cloths in the scullery sink with water gone pink to rust in the enamel basin. Her sleeves were rolled, exposing the pale grooves where soap had cracked the skin along her wrists. Peter stood at the range buttering bread with careful economy, slicing each piece exactly in two as though symmetry could count for nourishment.

"How far apart now?" Noreen asked.

Elsie checked the envelope. Ink and pencil marks crossed each other in cramped lines. "Last proper one was twenty-two minutes. Before that, eighteen. Bleeding's slowed right down."

"Baby moved?" Peter asked.

She put both hands on her belly and waited. A moment later, a solid heel pressed under her ribs and rolled away. She exhaled through her nose. "Yes."

He nodded like she had passed an exam.

Relief came first and hard enough to make her dizzy. It was not labor. Not yet. She had not lost the baby in the night. The house still stood. She could breathe without counting.

Then anger flooded in behind it, just as strong.

Peter set a plate on her lap. "There, you see. False alarm. Your body responded to stress, and both of you--both of you escalated it."

Elsie wrung out a towel and hung it over the chair back. "Keane called it threatened labor. Not alarm."

"Keane has to assume the worst," Peter said. "It is her profession."

Noreen took one bite of bread and could not swallow it. "Worst case was me bleeding through two towels while you argued about paraffin."

"And yet you are here, stable, exactly as I predicted."

"You did not predict anything. You delayed."

His face sharpened. "I prevented chaos."

"You prevented being embarrassed," she said. "Different thing."

He put down the knife with deliberate care. "I will not be spoken to in this tone in my own house."

Noreen barked a laugh that sent a twinge through her back. "Your own house? I cleaned your own house yesterday on ankles like boiled sausages. I bled in your own chair all night. Spare me ownership for five minutes."

A flush rose under his skin. For a second she thought he might shout. Instead he went colder, grammar stiffening the way it did when he wanted to punish without volume.

"You are exhausted and irrational. I shall overlook this conversation. What I will not--cannot--overlook is Mrs Harrow inserting herself into private family matters and inflaming your nerves."

Elsie stopped wringing the cloth halfway through a twist. Pink water ran off her knuckles into the basin. "If you'd like to call keeping her and the baby alive inflaming, go ahead."

"Do not tell me what kept my wife alive."

"No," Elsie said. "You keep telling us that yourself."

Silence dropped, dense as wet wool.

From the settee, Noreen could hear the wall clock in the front room and, faint beyond it, someone in the Harrow house coughing a smoker's cough that had to be Tom, back at last. The lane would wake soon. Doors would scrape open. News would travel by kettle and coal scuttle whether she approved or not.

Peter straightened his tie, though he was not going anywhere. "This discussion is over. Mrs Harrow, thank you for your assistance. You should return home before rumors begin." He took the front-door key from the hook and slid it into his trouser pocket.

Elsie turned her body toward Noreen and kept her shoulder to him. "You want me to stay till noon?"

Peter answered first. "That is unnecessary."

Noreen held Elsie's gaze. The question was not really about noon. It was about who she called next time, who she trusted to hear pain without calling it hysteria.

"Go get some sleep," Noreen said. "Then come back with your chalk and we'll do sums for food and fuel."

Peter frowned. "Sums can wait."

"No," she said. "They can't."

Elsie gave a short nod. "I'll be back in an hour." She stripped off her apron, pulled Tom's oversized coat tight over her belly, and paused by the door. "Lie on your side. Keep drinking. If bleeding picks up, I don't care what anyone says, we send for Keane again."

"I know," Noreen said.

After Elsie left, cold air and snow crystals still spinning in her wake, the house seemed to shrink around Peter's displeasure.

He gathered the stained towels into a bucket and carried them to the scullery with pinched fingers, handling them the way a clerk handles evidence. "You have made this household look foolish," he said without turning. "Bhatti will have seen lights all night. Half the lane will hear of it by tea."

Noreen watched him from the settee. His shoulders were neat inside his sweater. His hair stayed combed even after no sleep. He was the same man who remembered due dates for every bill, who had once rubbed her swollen feet while reciting a ridiculous poem from school to make her laugh. That man still existed somewhere inside this one. She could see him and no longer rely on him.

"Let them hear," she said.

He turned, startled.

"If gossip keeps me alive," she went on, "I'll buy it a biscuit." She shifted, wincing, and settled her palm over the place where the baby had kicked. "You can be angry about the scene if you want. I'm angry I had to beg you to take me seriously while I bled."

"I took you seriously."

"No. You took your pride seriously."

He opened his mouth, closed it, and turned away to scrub blood from the chair cushion with a ferocity he would later call diligence.

Noreen let him scrub. She let him mutter about soap flakes and ruined upholstery and paraffin usage and the price of replacements, hoping numbers might reassert moral order.

Under all of it, a quieter set of rules began in her own head.

She asked for the gas bill Elsie had written on, flattened it over her knee, and printed in thick pencil:

If pain returned, call Elsie first.

If road cleared, see Keane whether Peter approved or not.

If bleeding brightened, send for help before any argument could start.

She tore the paper in two, tucked one half under the clock on the mantel, and folded the other into her bra.

By noon the contractions had faded to occasional cramps, and the bleeding was only a rust mark on folded cloth. Keane could not get through by phone, but a note came by boy from the exchange saying she would visit as soon as weather allowed and that Noreen was to stay in bed, no lifting, no stairs unless absolutely necessary.

Peter read the note twice, folded it into a rectangle, and tucked it into his pocketbook beside wage slips.

"There," he said. "Official instruction. Rest for a day and this will pass."

Noreen held out her hand. "Give me that."

He stared at her, then gave her the note.

She smoothed it flat and pinned it beside the phone with the bread knife, right over the wallpaper seam where anyone could see it. Under it she pinned her own penciled list.

Elsie came back before dinner with chalk in her coat pocket and a jar of broth wrapped in newspaper. Noreen opened the dresser drawer, took out the spare front-door key, and pressed it into Elsie's palm.

"If this starts again and he says wait, you don't wait," Noreen said. "You come in and ring Keane."

Peter stiffened. "You are not distributing keys from this house."

"I just did," Noreen said.

When Elsie left again, Peter sat at the table with his jaw tight and his hands flat on the wood, not writing anything.

Noreen listened to the quiet and felt no comfort in that either.

The baby shifted once, slow as a fish turning under dark water. Noreen placed her hand there and counted, not contractions this time, but distances: from chair to phone, from phone to door, from door to Elsie's wall, and from that wall to first light, when Elsie would already be at her table doing sums before the lane fully woke.
