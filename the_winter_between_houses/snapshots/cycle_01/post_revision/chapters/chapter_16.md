# Chapter 16

Before Peter started his rounds of the hall, Noreen went for the lower drawer.

The thaw had found Barrow Lane overnight, and the house had started to sweat.

Water ticked off the cracked gutter outside the bedroom and ran down the wall behind the wardrobe in a dark seam, steady as a metronome. Noreen stood with one hand on the wardrobe door and one hand pressed under her breast where milk had leaked through the pad again, waiting for Peter to move from the landing so she could pull the lower drawer clear. Her stitches ached when she bent. Her head felt full of hot cloth.

Peter did not move.

He stood in the passage in his shirt sleeves, his casted wrist held close to his ribs as if the plaster itself had rank and dignity. He had set his ledger and correspondence in a stack on the hall table and aligned every corner before breakfast. Now he watched her with that municipal patience he used in arguments, the one that said time belonged to him and she was borrowing it badly.

"What are you looking for?" he asked.

Noreen kept her eyes on the drawer. "A dry nightdress. The damp's got into everything."

"Ask me and I can hand you one. You are not to rummage through papers while you are still weak."

"I'm not rummaging. I'm trying not to smell like old blood."

He clicked his fountain pen cap open and shut with his good hand, a small sharp sound under the drip outside. He did it when he was holding his temper in a narrow box.

"Mind your language," he said. "This is still my house."

Still. As if he had heard a sentence already and was waiting for the stamp.

Noreen pulled the drawer out anyway. She did it slowly so her belly wall would not seize. Stockings, blouse, two folded towels. Beneath them, wrapped in waxed paper, was what she needed: the clinic card, her ration book, her National Insurance card, the marriage certificate she hated needing, and the cotton tape with the plastic infant transfer bracelet tied through it so she could not lose it in sleep. She slipped the bundle under her cardigan before he could see.

Peter saw.

"Give me that." He held out his good hand. "Now."

"It's my papers."

"Your papers are household papers. You are not an independent filing system."

He stepped in before she could back away and caught the edge of the waxed paper. The cast knocked against her shoulder, a clumsy hard nudge. The shove was deniable in exactly the way he liked: no bruise worth proving, just force in a respectable wrapper. The bundle split. Cards and folded pages slid onto the bedspread and one smaller envelope, old sugar-paper, fell face up with Elsie's handwriting on it.

Nora.

Noreen lunged for it. Peter got there first.

He lifted the envelope between two fingers as if it were damp washing. "From Mrs Harrow," he said. "Private, is it?"

"Give it back."

"No."

He tore the flap with his thumbnail and unfolded the single page. Noreen had forgotten one corner carried a grease thumbprint from Elsie's kitchen and a penciled note at the top that had made her smile when she first read it, before the baby came too early, before blood and gauze and iron taste.

I can still feel your mouth on my wrist where you bit me not to cry out.
You left the mark purple till morning and kissed it before the kettle whistled.
When your hand slid under my shift by the stove, I nearly came standing.
If there were any justice, I'd wake in your bed and call that house mine.

Peter read each line. His face did not change at once. He read to the bottom and read again, lips moving around each word like an accounts clerk verifying totals.

When he looked up his voice was cleaner than soap.

"You have been in her bed and under her shift. In my house." He folded the page with neat edges. "While I worked. While you carried my child."

Noreen's laugh came out thin and wrong. "You were not here half the nights that mattered."

"Do not answer in riddles."

"Don't talk to me like I'm one of your forms."

His mouth twitched, almost a smile, then flattened. "You have given me what I need."

He set the letter on the bed as though marking evidence. "If you attempt to remove that baby without me, I will put this in front of every person who controls nursery access: the bite mark, the hand under her shift, your plan to wake in her house. They will call you indecent and unstable after hemorrhage, and every visit will be watched."

The room tipped. Noreen put a hand against the wardrobe to keep from sitting down on the wet floorboards.

"She's in special care," she said. "You'd do that while she's in special care?"

"Especially then. Institutions prefer order."

"You're threatening your own daughter to frighten me."

"I am protecting my daughter from scandal."

His grammar tightened before every cut. She had seen it for years and still, each time, some part of her expected a husband and got a magistrate instead.

"If this reaches parish ears," he continued, "you will not be viewed as a suitable mother. Do you understand what that phrase means in practice?"

She did. It meant signatures she had never seen and rooms she could not enter. It meant someone deciding who held the pram handle. It meant her milk drying in her bra while another woman fed her child from a measured bottle.

"You won't do that," she said, and heard how little weight it had.

"I will do exactly what keeps the name Walsh from filth." He lifted the letter again. "You are to cease all contact with Mrs Harrow. You are to remain here, recover properly, and present as a wife and mother. In return, I will refrain from immediate action."

"In return." She tasted the words like grit. "You make even mercy sound like rent."

"Everything is rent when someone else is paying."

That line, cold and proud, would have crushed her a month earlier. Today it made something sharp and useful in her chest.

"I bled out on this floor while you were missing," she said. "Keane and Elsie kept me alive. The child is alive because of them. You don't get to write that as if you balanced a column."

"I do get to write it. I write records for a living."

He stepped aside and pointed to the open drawer. "Pack that back exactly as it was. You are not to leave this house without me."

He turned and went down the stairs, each step careful because of the cast, the banister creaking under his shoulder. At the bottom he paused and called up without looking at her.

"I shall return from the office at four. We will draft a statement for Father Mullan and for the unit social office. You will sign it."

The front door shut.

Noreen waited until she heard his shoes on the wet pavement, then moved so fast her vision speckled. She shoved the documents into her cardigan, tucked the transfer bracelet into her brassiere where she could feel its hard loop against her sternum, and crossed to the shared wall. She put her mouth near the wallpaper seam and spoke low.

"Elsie."

Nothing.

She hit the wall twice with the heel of her hand, the old signal for coal in chapter one of this winter, when the worst thing between them had been pride.

A kettle lid clattered next door. Then Elsie's voice, close through plaster.

"I'm here."

"He found the letter."

Silence so complete Noreen could hear the lane water running in the gutter trap.

Then Elsie said, careful and quick, words arranged like crockery so nothing would crack, "Put your papers in your coat lining. He'll search a bag first."
The kettle whistled itself hoarse. Elsie yanked it off the ring and set it down too hard.
"I'm fetching Keane. Wait by the yard gate. Three knocks, then move."

"Elsie-"

"Not now. Three knocks."

Noreen rested her forehead against damp wallpaper. Somewhere below, Peter's clock on the mantel struck ten, each chime thin and legal.

She had six hours before he came back with statements and a pen.


By noon the street smelled of wet coal, thaw mud, and cat piss from the alley where the snow had finally loosened. Noreen sat at Elsie's kitchen table with her coat still on, breath short from crossing two yards and one narrow gate as if she had run miles. Every few minutes milk let down in a sudden sting and dampened the front of her dress. Elsie pretended not to look at the stains and set another clean cloth near her elbow each time without comment.

Elsie had tied her hair up with blue parcel string and forgotten to trim the ends, so they stuck out like two stiff tails over her ear. She did that when she was scared: grabbed whatever was nearest and kept moving. Her fingers were reddened from lye soap, one knuckle split open and mended with sticking plaster. When she spoke, she spoke in instructions, not comfort.

"You've got your card?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Ration book?"

"Yes."

"Infant bracelet?"

Noreen touched her chest. "Here."

Elsie nodded once and looked at the saucepan, counting under her breath as if numbers could hold the room level. "I wrapped bread and dripping for later."
She pressed two safety pins into Noreen's palm, then glanced toward the wall.
"And I stitched twelve shillings into your hem. Not much, but it's there."

"Twelve shillings? Elsie-"

"Don't argue. I sold the good blanket to Mrs Bhatti last week and told you it had moth."

A laugh rose in Noreen and broke into a cough. "You liar."

"Only when useful."

Three hard knocks hit the back door. Keane came in carrying her canvas bag and a damp macintosh folded over one arm, rain beading on the shoulders. She smelled faintly of cloves and antiseptic and had a tea stain on her cuff she never seemed to notice. Her hands were raw at the knuckles, the skin split in white lines from constant washing.

"Right," she said, setting the bag down. "No speeches. Sit still, Noreen. Sleeve up."

She wrapped the cuff around Noreen's arm, stethoscope cold against skin, eyes on the second hand of her watch. The watch had stopped at 3:17 months ago. She used a wind-up kitchen timer clipped to her bag instead and trusted that more than any wristpiece.

"Pressure's high again," Keane said. "Not chapter-twelve high, but high enough with what you've just told me. Bleeding now?"

"Spotting yesterday. Not today."

"Head pain? Light flashes?"

"Sometimes when I stand."

Keane nodded, not surprised, and wrote in block letters on a card. "You can't recover under surveillance and threat. You need supervised lodging now, today, before he files anything that boxes you."

Elsie set mugs down. "Saint Brigid's full. Mrs Denton's niece got turned away Monday."

"I know it's full." Keane took the tea, swallowed once, made a face at the sugar, and swallowed again anyway. "I'm calling Sister Imelda myself."

The back room had no phone. Keane used Elsie's hall where the line worked on clear days and prayed on wet ones. Today it crackled but held. Noreen and Elsie could hear only Keane's side: clipped, polite, then sharpened.

"Yes, Margaret Keane, district midwife, Calderbank patch... No, I know admissions are closed... She is post-hemorrhage, hypertensive, and at active domestic coercion risk... You can call it whatever phrase your board likes; I am telling you she is not safe in that house... Sister, if she seizes there tonight and I have documented refusal of shelter, that is going in my incident report with your name spelled correctly."

A pause. Keane listened, chin tucked, fingers drumming once on the wallpaper, then still.

"Thank you," she said. "One cot, temporary overflow. Curfew eight. No visitors in dormitory. She can manage. She has no infant with her, child is in special care. Yes, I know. Yes. I will sign responsibility at intake."

She hung up and came back to the kitchen.

"You have a place," she said. "Laundry annex cot, not pretty. They want you by half six. Parish van can collect at five-fifteen on return run from the convent linens. Arthur can help load from the lane corner so there is less theatre on your doorstep."

Noreen stared at her hands. The nails were bitten to crescents. Blood from one cuticle had dried black. "How long can I stay?"

"A week confirmed. Maybe two if we can get parish welfare to sign off. After that, they will ask for contribution from maintenance or wages."

"I've no wages."

"Then we file emergency support and maintenance claim. That takes time and he may fight it."

There it was, plain as weather. A bed tonight and nothing promised after.

Noreen felt the room tilt toward absence: no money of her own, no cot for the baby there, no guarantee she could keep visiting special care if bus routes drowned again. Still, there was a door at Saint Brigid she could shut from the inside, and no man with a ledger in the hall.

"I'll go," she said.

Elsie sat down hard opposite her. "You'll go today."

Keane opened her bag and laid one form on the table. "Property list first, so he cannot claim theft. Keep it brief."
Noreen gripped the pencil too hard and the tip snapped. Keane handed her another without comment.
"Next is consent for Saint Brigid transfer," Keane said.
She tapped the lower line, then waited until Noreen looked up.
"After that, one sentence for the unit: maternal access stands unless a court says otherwise. He may bluster. He does not rewrite law by speaking firmly."

"He'll still try," Noreen said.

"Of course he will."

Keane's voice slowed, each step named, as if she were counting contractions. "When he shouts, do not wrestle his sermon. You repeat one line: 'I'm leaving for medical recovery and child welfare.' Do not explain more."
Noreen stared at the tabletop grain. "He'll spit every filthy name he knows."
"Let him," Keane said. "If he blocks the gate, Arthur and I witness it, and I ring police and social office whether they laugh or not."

Elsie looked up. "Police will laugh."

"Perhaps. But two witnesses and a district midwife note make laughter expensive later." Keane met Noreen's eyes. "Do you consent to leave now?"

Noreen nodded.

"Say it." Keane waited.

"I consent to leave today for recovery and my daughter's welfare."

"Good."

Keane pushed the first form across and held out a pencil stub. "Write legibly. Bureaucracy is a weapon, but it cuts both ways when we keep records."

For the next hour they built a departure out of specific nouns: two blouses, one cardigan, nursing pads, ration book, clinic card, a hairbrush with three teeth missing, the infant bracelet, and one photograph of Noreen's mother taken outside a chapel in Galway, rain on the lens like tears she had always denied. Elsie wrapped bread, dripping, and two apples in yesterday's Chronicle. Keane added sterile pads and iron tablets from her bag without writing them down.

When Noreen reached for the drawer where Elsie had once hidden an extra tin of milk, Elsie caught her wrist and shook her head.

"Leave the tin," Elsie said softly. "If he counts, he'll know."

Noreen swallowed. "I hate that you're right."

"You can hate me later."

They checked the clock. Four-ten.

Rain thickened. From the shared wall came the scrape of Peter's chair being set back, then his tread crossing the Walsh hallway. He was early. Her pulse kicked at the stitch line; early meant less time to think and less time to hide.

Keane snapped her bag shut. "Time. Coat on. Case by the back gate."

Elsie lifted the case and swore under her breath at the weight. "What did you put in here, bricks?"

"A life," Noreen said, and almost smiled.

Elsie did not smile back. She looked like she might break the cup in her hand if she set it down too hard.

"Three knocks," she said. "Then you move."


At five-thirteen the parish van turned into Barrow Lane, pale blue under a skin of road filth, wipers squeaking over glass that never fully cleared. On both sides of the street curtains twitched. Thaw had brought people back to windows. Nothing in a terrace stayed private once the weather loosened. Every twitch felt like another witness Peter could lean on later.

Arthur was already in the lane mouth in his board-issued coat, cap pulled low, wet wool dark at the shoulders. He worried the brim between thumb and forefinger while he waited, then dropped his hand the moment he saw Noreen with the case. He never offered opinions first; he offered tasks.

"Mind the third step," he said quietly. "Loose stone by the drain. Give me the handle."

He took the case from her without fuss and tested the weight as if gauging a cable load. "Not too bad. Van's clear at the back."

Keane stood by the open side door of the van with a clipboard under her arm, hair escaping its pins in the damp. "Name and date of birth at intake, then straight to bed rest," she said. "Curfew's strict. Sister Imelda will test you by talking soft and writing hard. Answer cleanly, no extra history."

Noreen nodded and turned toward her own front door one last time because she had to collect the second bag, the one with papers sewn into the lining. Without it, by nightfall she'd be nobody at any desk that mattered.

Peter filled the doorway before she reached it.

He had changed into his good coat and combed his hair flat. In his left hand he held two typed sheets. In his right, the sugar-paper letter from Elsie, now creased along the same fold lines.
He stepped off the threshold and set his casted forearm across the van's side rail, a white bar between Noreen and the open door.

"You are not stepping into that van," he said.

Noreen stopped three feet from him. Rain tapped the enamel number plate above his head.

"Move," she said.

"No. You are in a temporary condition. You are making decisions under influence." He lifted the letter. "Under her influence."

"Peter-"

"Do not Peter me in the street. Listen carefully." His diction sharpened into church-lectern clarity, then slipped. "Step into that van and this leaves the lane tonight. I rang the unit registrar at four-thirty and logged concern that you are being coached to abscond. At nine tomorrow I stand at the magistrates' desk with this letter and my declaration. By noon they can put every ward visit under supervision and require your hostel address on record. Every office will know. Every counter clerk will know. They will know what filth you chose."

From two houses down, a window sash went up a fraction. Somebody coughed and pretended not to listen.

Noreen felt the transfer bracelet press cold against her skin through blouse and coat. Her fear was not an abstraction. It had a plastic edge and her daughter's surname printed in hospital block letters.

"You're threatening me with my baby," she said.

"I am describing consequences." Peter's voice dropped. "Come inside and this can still stay within walls. Go with them and every wall in Calderbank will hear."

He glanced at Arthur, then at Keane. "Mr Hale, this is domestic business. Nurse Keane, you are overreaching your remit."

Arthur shifted the case to his other hand. "Road's pooling again by the viaduct," he said to no one in particular. "Driver needs to go now if he's taking the low route."

Keane stepped closer, boots sinking in thaw mud. "Mr Walsh, your wife has consented to medical recovery lodging. I can refer her directly. If you obstruct movement, I document obstruction and notify police and maternity social office tonight."

Peter gave a brief, unbelieving laugh. "Medical recovery at a hostel? Do not insult language."

"Language isn't the issue," Keane said. "Safety is."

He looked back at Noreen and the veneer cracked for a second: rage at losing sequence.

"You will have no income there," he said. "No wages, no housekeeping, no account access. You cannot buy your own bus fare to the unit. You cannot buy soap. You cannot buy milk cloths. She cannot keep you." His eyes flicked toward Elsie's doorway, where Elsie stood in apron and coat, fists white around the knot of her sleeves. "And she cannot rescue herself."

Elsie stepped down one stair. "I didn't ask-"

Noreen cut across before Peter could turn this into another spectacle. "Stop." Her voice shook once, then steadied. "I know I might have no money. I know I might get turned out in a week. I know what you're going to call me."

Peter lifted his chin. "Then act sensibly."

"No." She took one step forward. "You did not hold me when I was bleeding. You did not sit the nights with the pump while my body thought my baby was here. You did not carry what happened. You carried rules."

His face reddened high in the cheeks. "I carried this family."

"Like debt."

For a moment she thought he might strike her with the cast, clumsy and brutal. Instead he jabbed the typed sheets against her breastbone, hard enough to make her flinch.

"Say you'll come back inside and I'll burn these now," he said.

"I won't bargain."

"Then hear mine." He lowered his voice to a hiss she knew from behind closed doors. "By month-end they will call you unfit, and I will see to it. When your daughter is old enough, she will hear exactly what you are."

Noreen felt something go quiet inside her, a clean line she could stand on. She thought of the dormitory Keane had described, narrow cot, other women breathing in the dark, curfew bell. She thought of waking without waiting for his shoes in the hall. She thought of a week. A week could be used.

"You can try," she said. "But I'm leaving for recovery and my daughter's welfare."

She said it again, as Keane had instructed, steady and plain. "I'm leaving for recovery and my daughter's welfare."

Peter's hand trembled on the sheets. Rain spotted the top one and blurred its first line before he could shield it.

Arthur moved then, not between them in any theatrical way, simply into the practical gap, taking the second bag from inside the door where Noreen had set it earlier.

"All set," he said. "Step's slick. Hold the rail."

Keane touched Noreen's elbow, light and firm. "Van. Now."

Behind them Peter called out, voice suddenly formal enough to be read into minutes.

"By morning Father Mullan and the unit matron will have my account first. You are making a catastrophic mistake."

Noreen did not turn back. She had learned that looking back gave him another surface to write on.

She climbed into the van. The metal floor smelled of damp laundry and diesel. Arthur passed up the cases. Elsie came close enough to press a wrapped parcel into Noreen's lap: bread, apples, a small bar of carbolic soap cut in half to make it look like two. Her eyes were dry and furious.

"Eat before curfew," Elsie said. "And don't let the nuns see you crying if you can help it. They smell weakness like cats smell fish."

Noreen caught her sleeve and squeezed once. "You kept me alive," she whispered.

Elsie looked away toward Peter's doorway. "So did you."

Keane climbed in and banged the side panel. The driver engaged gear. As the van pulled out, Peter stood in his doorway with the letter in one hand and his statements in the other, rain soaking both, still trying to keep the pages flat.

At the lane corner Mrs Bhatti stood under her shop awning with a crate of potatoes, watching openly. Two boys stopped kicking a punctured ball and stared. A woman from number nine crossed herself as if for a funeral.

Public fact, Noreen thought. There it is.

The van hit the pooled water by the viaduct and rocked. Keane steadied the case with her boot and checked Noreen's face.

"Breathe in through your nose," she said. "Slow."

Noreen did. Diesel, damp wool, soap, the sweet rot of thaw mud through the door seam. Her body hurt in specific places: stitch line, breasts, lower back. Each ache was proof she was still in it.

Saint Brigid House rose at dusk behind the old convent wall, brick darkened by rain. Light leaked from basement windows. Inside, the corridor smelled of boiled cabbage and bleach. A bell rope hung by the office door with a sign: CURFEW 20:00 SHARP. NO EXCEPTIONS.

Sister Imelda took Noreen's details without looking up much, spectacles low on her nose, cardigan sleeves rolled to forearms thick from carrying laundry baskets. A silver thimble hung on a chain at her neck, tapping the desk each time she wrote.

"Occupation?" she asked.

Noreen hesitated.

"None at present," Keane said.

"Means of support?"

Noreen swallowed. "Not settled."

Sister Imelda wrote that phrase exactly: NOT SETTLED.

"You understand this is temporary emergency lodging," she said. "One cot, shared washroom, chapel attendance Sundays, chores as assigned when medically fit. Visitors only in front room during set hours. If there is legal dispute, we require paperwork. We are full and have no nursery places."

"I understand," Noreen said.

She signed the intake book. The pen scratched hard through cheap paper, louder than it should have been.

A young woman led her up two flights to the laundry annex dormitory. The woman wore men's socks under her shoes and had stitched her initials into the cuff of her cardigan in red thread. She spoke only once.

"Mind the fourth board. It lifts."

The cot was narrow and iron-framed. A brown wool blanket lay folded at the foot. On the sill, condensation ran in clear lines down to a chipped paint ledge. Somewhere in the house a baby cried, then another answered.

Noreen sat on the cot and took the transfer bracelet from inside her blouse. The plastic had warmed to her skin. She looped it around the bedpost and tied it with the cotton tape, once, twice, firm enough that she would feel it if she reached out in the dark.

No guarantee of income. No guarantee of custody. No guarantee of anything beyond this one bed and the bell that would ring at eight.

Still, the door shut with her on the right side of it.

Back on Barrow Lane, the gutters were already close to their iron lips.

Outside, rain kept falling on Calderbank, washing soot down every brick in town and carrying it all, black and slow, toward the river.
