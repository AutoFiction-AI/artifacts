# Chapter 9

Elsie had her palm on the sink pipe, ready to tap Noreen's code, when the lights went without warning, not a flicker first, not a dimming, just a hard click and then the room lost its edges.

Elsie stood with one hand inside the flour tin and the other on the table, as if the dark might tilt and throw her. The clock on the mantel stopped in the same instant, and for a breath she could hear only her own sleeve brushing her apron. Then the lane answered: a child crying two doors down, somebody swearing in the street, a pan dropped hard enough to ring through brick.

She waited for the hum behind the wall from the Walsh wireless, but that had gone too. She waited for Tom's tread on the back flags, a boot scraping the step, his key missing the lock twice before finding it. Nothing.

The cold moved quickly in a dead house. Without the little electric bar in the front room, without the kettle's thin steam, the damp in the plaster came awake. Elsie found the paraffin lamp by memory, struck two matches before one held, and turned the wick low to spare oil. Its light made a yellow pool that stopped at her elbows.

She crossed to the front window and scraped at the inside frost with her thumbnail. Streetlamps were out all along Barrow Lane. At the corner the sky had a dirty orange glow where the main road still had power, or maybe only firelight from people opening grates and burning whatever they had left. Snowmelt from last week's brief thaw had frozen again into a skin over the cobbles. If Tom was at the pub, he would stay there where there was heat. If Peter was at the council rooms, as Noreen had guessed after supper, he might stay there too.

Through the shared wall she heard a chair dragged, then another. Noreen, still up.

Elsie put on her coat over her house dress and took the scuttle to the coal box in the yard. There were three shovels left. She could call it four if she lied to herself and counted slate dust as fuel. She tipped half into the scuttle and left half where it was. She had done too much arithmetic these last weeks to pretend this night would solve itself.

A knock came from the side door, not loud, just knuckles through wood.

"Elsie?"

Noreen's voice was muffled by scarf and dark.

Elsie opened the door onto a strip of frozen yard. Noreen stood in Peter's old overcoat, unbuttoned across her middle, breath smoking around her face. She held a candle in a saucer and had wax dripped down her thumb to the knuckle.

"You all right?" Noreen asked. "I could hear you moving and then it went quiet."

"I'm upright," Elsie said. "That's about the lot of it."

Noreen scraped cooled wax from her thumb with her nail until the skin went red. "Arthur's out with the board van. Peter's not back. I hate saying this, but if this sits till dawn, two grates are daft. We'll burn through both houses before midnight."

Elsie looked past her into the Walsh scullery, where another candle shook against tile. "Your front grate draws better."

"It does. Come in with what you've got. Bring bedding. We'll keep one fire and keep the pipes from bursting in at least one place."

They traded logistics in clipped turns, each answer landing before either of them had to name what it cost.

Elsie shifted the scuttle higher on her hip. "Give me ten minutes."

"Take five," Noreen said. "My toes are numb already."

Elsie packed in a hard sequence, counting under her breath as she moved. Two blankets, Tom's army-surplus one and the thin flowered one from her mother. A flat pillow. The tin with tea. Half a loaf wrapped in last Sunday's paper. She hesitated over the little packet of sugar, then took it too. On the dresser the photograph from their wedding sat in its cheap frame, Tom's grin broad, her own face careful as if she already knew cameras took more than likeness. She turned the frame down without deciding to.

When she stepped back into the yard with her load, Noreen was waiting by the shared gate with a hot-water bottle tucked under her arm and a rolled mattress dragging behind her.

"Christ," Noreen said softly, seeing the weight Elsie carried. "Here, give us that." She took the tea tin and bread, then the blanket bundle when Elsie shifted awkwardly around her belly. "You don't get points for martyrdom."

"No one has ever accused me of that."

"I've done it, at least twice." Noreen's mouth twitched. "Come on."

They built a camp in the Walsh front room like children in a game, except children did not count coal by handfuls. Noreen knelt by the grate, coaxing flame through cinders with twisted-up account circulars Peter had left on the sideboard. Elsie laid blankets over the settee and floor rug, wedged rolled towels along the bottom of the front door, and pushed a folded sheet against the letter slot to stop the draft. Each task made another corner of fear manageable.

The room took shape in lamp glow: the framed Sacred Heart print above the mantel, Peter's ledgers stacked with string tied precise around them, a cup with three knitting needles and no wool. Noreen's cardigan hung over the chair back, one cuff dark where she'd spilled tea yesterday and not had enough soap to scrub it through. Elsie noticed everything because not looking at Noreen's face was harder than looking.

When the fire found its breath, the coals settled with a sound like dry bones tapping together. Heat rose slowly, mean and precious.

Noreen sat back on her heels and pressed a hand to her lower spine. "There. If it dies now I'll throw Peter's records on top and let him call it civic sabotage."

"I'd pay to see it."

"With what money?"

"Exactly."

The joke landed and did not fix anything, but the air shifted.

Elsie poured water from Noreen's kettle into two chipped mugs and tipped in a pinch of tea leaves so small it looked insulting. They drank anyway. The tannin tasted of pennies and comfort.

Outside, wind shoved at the windows in uneven blows, then dropped. Down the lane somebody sang one line of a hymn and forgot the rest. The houses answered with coughs, a baby waking, the hard scrape of someone feeding a stove. Through it all the wall to her own house stayed silent, and that silence was worse than shouting.

"He'll not be back tonight," Elsie said, before she could stop herself.

Noreen did not ask which he. "No." She stared into the fire. "Peter left after tea with his satchel and his virtue. Said the council had emergency ledgers to balance at the hall and the city would collapse if he did not attend."

"Did you believe him?"

"I believed he wanted a warm room where no one asked him for kindness."

Elsie let that sit. She could hear the small crackle where a seam in the coal opened. "Tom said he was going to look for paraffin."

Noreen snorted. "Aye. In a pint glass."

"Likely."

Noreen leaned her head against the settee, eyes half closed. Candlelight made the hollows under her cheekbones look deeper. "I keep thinking if I can just get through one full night without something breaking, I'd count it as luxury."

Elsie pulled the blanket higher over Noreen's knees. "Then that's the objective, isn't it. Full night. No panic. No broken pipes, no broken bones, no ambulance."

"No husbands," Noreen said.

Elsie met her eyes then and looked away first.

They fed the grate in measured turns. At ten, Elsie went across to her own house for the second lamp and came back fast because the empty rooms had a different cold now, the cold of abandoned places. At eleven, Noreen stood in the doorway of the scullery counting to thirty while Elsie banged the pipe with a spoon handle to keep it from freezing solid. At midnight, they spread the mattress on the floor close enough to the hearth to feel heat on their shins and cool on their shoulders.

Noreen brought down extra blankets from the spare room and a faded eiderdown that smelled faintly of lavender gone stale in the chest. "Arthur won't miss this one," she said. "He says it scratches his chin."

Elsie pictured him once, careful with his boots by the Walsh door, always tying the laces in a square knot as if his fingers had no other shape. "He's particular."

"He folds his socks into pairs like little sandwiches. It alarms me."

The detail should have been funny. It was funny. Still, the room felt too near and too narrow for easy laughter.

Noreen stood with the eiderdown clutched in both hands, suddenly solemn. "Elsie."

"Mm?"

"If your house drops colder than this one before dawn, you stay here. Not in the chair. In the bed. I'm not arguing in this weather."

Elsie opened her mouth to refuse on instinct, and Noreen lifted an eyebrow in that way she had when she knew refusal was pride dressed up as duty.

"In the bed," Noreen repeated. "We're both carrying. I'm not having you wake stiff and scared because you're trying to be proper for furniture."

"It's your house."

"It's a mattress on my floor and my husband is absent in service of municipal paperwork. I'd say propriety has had a bad week."

Elsie's answer came out rougher than she meant. "Don't joke me into it."

"I'm not joking." Noreen's voice dropped. "I'm asking. Stay where it's warm."

A coal popped and sent a fan of sparks against the guard. They both flinched, then both looked at each other and had to smile at the same time.

"All right," Elsie said quietly. "I stay."

Noreen held her gaze, then exhaled through her nose like a bargain struck.

They undressed in increments that pretended to be practical. Shoes first, then wet stockings hung by the grate. Elsie kept her slip and cardigan on, then took the cardigan off when the damp wool chilled her more than bare skin did. Noreen changed out of Peter's coat and into a thin nightdress with blue stitching at the cuffs, the hem mended in two places with darker thread. She rubbed her hands together briskly, blew into her fists, and slid under the blankets with a grunt.

"God save us from heroic women," she muttered. "Come on before I freeze to the rug."

Elsie lowered herself carefully beside her. The mattress dipped and made them roll together a fraction. Their hips touched, separated, touched again when Noreen shifted the hot-water bottle between them.

"Too hot?" Noreen asked.

"No."

"Too close?"

Elsie swallowed. "No."

The fire snapped, settled. The room smelled of coal, paraffin, damp wool, and peppermint soap from the Christmas bar her mother had wrapped in tissue and called a luxury. Wind pressed a low note through the chimney and then released it.

* * *

Noreen kept her breathing slow and even and failed to fool herself. Sleep would not come. She could feel Elsie wakeful beside her by the way the mattress held tension, by the tiny adjustments that never settled into rest.

She had spent years learning how to read silence in a house where words got used as ledgers. This silence was not resentment, not fear exactly. It had heat in it.

Noreen spoke into the dark before she could lose nerve. "You awake?"

"Yes."

"So am I."

The answer sat between them, heavy and alive.

Noreen rolled onto her side to face Elsie. In lamplight Elsie's eyes looked darker than usual, wide and guarded like someone waiting for a verdict. Noreen could not bear the thought of guessing wrong and harming what she wanted most.

"I need to say something before my courage packs up and goes home," she said.

Elsie stayed still, listening.

"If I put my hand on you and you don't want it, you tell me now. Or then. Any point. You won't have to be kind about it."

Fear flickered across Elsie's face, but she did not move away.

"I want you to," Elsie said.

Noreen forced herself not to rush, even as relief hit like drink on an empty stomach. "Because you're cold?"

"I'm cold."

"Because you're lonely?"

Elsie gave a sharp, almost angry little huff. "Yes."

"Because you choose it?"

Elsie held her gaze. "Yes. And because if I don't choose one thing that is mine tonight, I'll go mad."

The words went straight under Noreen's ribs. She pressed her lips together, then said, "All right."

She laid her hand on Elsie's sleeve first, over cloth, and left it there. Elsie closed the distance on her own, pressing her forehead to Noreen's mouth before turning and kissing her properly.

The first kiss was cautious, lips dry and careful. The second came with need. Noreen slid her fingers into the hair at Elsie's nape and felt Elsie shiver.

"Tell me if anything hurts," Noreen whispered.

"You too."

They kissed until the rest of the room thinned out. Noreen felt the seam of Elsie's slip twisted under her palm and smoothed it. She pressed her hand to Elsie's lower back, then slid up under the slip to cup her breast, thumb rough over the nipple until it tightened. When her fingers moved lower, skimming the damp curl between Elsie's thighs, she stopped and waited.

"Yes," Elsie said, already reaching for her.

A breathless laugh broke out of Noreen, half disbelief, half surrender. "Right, then."

Buttons refused fingers gone clumsy with cold and urgency. Elsie fumbled at Noreen's nightdress, and Noreen kissed her wrist and did the last buttons herself. Air hit skin like cold water, then blankets trapped heat back around them.

Noreen touched Elsie with full attention, slow enough to keep asking and be answered without words. Stretch marks, taut skin, the curve and weight of her belly. She pressed her ear against it and grinned when the baby kicked her cheek.

"Bossy already," she murmured.

Elsie laughed, then made a softer sound when Noreen kissed the scar beneath her breast, the one Elsie once called ugly and then pretended she had not said anything at all.

Noreen felt Elsie's hand on her shoulder, then sliding down her thigh under the nightdress. Elsie's fingers parted her, thumb circling her clit before one finger pressed in. She opened for her and heard herself inhale like she had been running.

"Like that," she said, voice gone thin. "Don't stop because you think you should."

Elsie did not stop.

Pleasure rose hard and immediate, sharpened by fear of being overheard through the thin terrace wall. Noreen bit the heel of her own hand when a sound tried to break loose, then reached back one-handed to feed the fire and came straight back to Elsie without losing the rhythm.

They shifted positions twice, first too cramped and then side-on with Elsie's top knee propped on a folded blanket so belly and hips had room. Noreen cupped Elsie's face.

"This all right?"

"Yes. Better."

"Say it if it changes."

"I'll."

Noreen's control broke into prayer and profanity in one breath when Elsie's thumb kept a hard rhythm over her clit and her fingers curled inside. "Sweet Mary, Elsie, right there. Christ."

Elsie kissed her to quiet her, and Noreen bit Elsie's lower lip and did not apologize. Their movements stayed imperfect, cramped by blankets and the little geography of the front room, but every pause was answered and every question got spoken.

"Here?"

"Yes."

"Still?"

"Still."

"More?"

"More."

Then Elsie went rigid with a sudden low cramp. Noreen stopped instantly, hand flat over Elsie's abdomen.

"Pain?"

"Just a pull," Elsie said through her teeth. "Too fast, that's all."

Noreen counted softly under her breath in Irish, thumb drawing circles near Elsie's hip until the muscle released.

"We go slow," she said.

"We're going slow."

"You know what I mean."

Elsie blinked hard and nodded. "I know."

When Elsie came, it took her whole body with it. Her thighs locked around Noreen's wrist and her left hand seized Noreen's shoulder hard enough to sting. She bit the blanket to keep from crying out into the wall. Noreen watched, stunned by her and by the fact of being allowed this close. Her own release followed moments later, sudden enough to steal speech, rocking against Elsie's thigh with her face buried at Elsie's neck while the tremor ran through her.

When Elsie let go, four red crescents had already risen on Noreen's shoulder.

Afterward they lay sweating in a room that still needed coal to stay survivable. Their breathing settled unevenly. Noreen felt both babies moving, protesting the commotion in their own blunt language.

She laughed weakly into the pillow. "If Sister Agnes could see me now she'd set the convent on fire herself."

Elsie kissed the corner of her mouth. "She'd blame me."

"She'd be right in part."

"Only in part."

Noreen laced their fingers. The last of the joke drained out of her. "You still with me?"

"Yes."

"Any regret?"

Elsie stared up at the smoke-marked ceiling for a long beat. "No. Fear, yes. Regret, no."

Noreen shut her eyes, and a tear leaked sideways into her hairline. "Same."

The room had cooled while they were occupied. Duty came back in pieces. Elsie dragged herself up, tucked the blanket around Noreen's shoulders, and fed the grate twice. Noreen reached for the kettle and poured water into the hot-water bottle again, then tucked it at Elsie's feet with a muttered, "Your toes are like stones."

"You've checked that three times."

"I'll check six if it keeps you attached to your legs."

They settled again, this time face to face, knees drawn up, hands under each other's cardigans to share warmth.

Outside, around half two by guess, voices rose at the lane end, men arguing over a stalled lorry. Someone banged metal on metal. Then distance swallowed it and left only wind.

Elsie spoke first, because if she waited until daylight she might start pretending. "This was not an accident."

Noreen stared at her, searching. "No."

"I won't call it one in the morning."

"Nor me."

"I also can't stand in your kitchen at seven and talk about bread as if this didn't happen."

Noreen's mouth twisted. "We still have to talk about bread. But not as if this didn't happen."

Elsie breathed out. "Right."

Noreen shifted onto her back and covered her eyes with her forearm. "Peter will come home with his pen and his face and his questions about coal. He'll count what's gone and decide where to place blame."

"Tom will come in smelling of stale stout and promises."

"Do you want him touched by you tomorrow?"

The question hit like cold water and held there.

"No," Elsie said after a beat. "I don't know what I'll do if he expects it. But no."

Noreen lowered her arm. "Then don't."

"As if it's that simple."

"It's not simple. It's still yours."

Elsie looked at her sharply. Noreen's expression did not soften, but her thumb ghosted across Elsie's wrist, a touch so light it might have been apology.

"And you?" Elsie asked.

"Peter hasn't asked what I want in years," Noreen said. "He asks what is due."

A long silence opened. In it, the lane seemed to breathe as one creature: timber contracting, pipes knocking, old houses settling under frost.

"I keep hearing the wall," Noreen said. "Every sound crossing through. Kettle, cough, bed springs. Half my marriage has happened to an audience of bricks."

Elsie gave a grim smile. "And the other half happened in silence thick enough to drown in."

"Aye."

Noreen reached to the side table, found the stub of pencil Peter used for accounts, and tore a strip from the margin of a circular. She wrote in blocky letters by lamp glow and held it out.

IF POWER STAYS OUT -> BREAKFAST HERE. IF IT RETURNS -> TEA AT YOURS. BOTH BEFORE MEN.

Elsie read it twice and laughed softly despite herself. "You made a military plan."

"I married a clerk. Some infection took."

"This is sensible."

"It's also me saying I'm not vanishing when the lights come back."

Elsie's throat tightened, but not with one of the old stock pains that came and went. This one had shape: the rough paper against her fingertips, the smell of pencil cedar, Noreen's misspelled "returns" corrected with a blunt overstroke.

She folded the note and tucked it into her bra. "Neither am I."

The lamp burned lower. They dozed in short drops, waking whenever the fire needed feeding or a pipe groaned. Elsie dreamed once of walking through her own house and finding every drawer open, every spoon gone, and woke with her hand clenched in Noreen's nightdress.

Near four, Noreen rose to use the chamber pot behind the screen and came back swearing under her breath because her ankles had swollen against the elastic of her stockings. She sat on the mattress and massaged them, jaw set.

"Don't look," she said.

"At what?"

"At me with those nurse eyes."

Elsie moved closer anyway and took one foot into her lap, thumb pressing gently at the indentation above the sock line. "You should rest with them up."

"I should have won the football pools and moved to Brighton."

"That too."

Noreen watched her for a moment, then smiled tiredly. "You're doing it again."

"Doing what?"

Noreen caught Elsie's wrist before she could press another dent into swollen skin. "Turning me into a project."

Elsie let out a breath through her nose and loosened her grip. "Sorry."

"Don't be sorry. Be here."

She pulled Elsie down beside her and kissed her slowly this time, no hunger now, only claim and reassurance.

The dark thinned but did not break. Still no electricity. The fire had eaten most of what coal they had dared give it. Frost traced the inside of the window again where their breath had not reached.

Elsie sat up, yanked her shift straight twice, and began counting aloud what remained. "Half scuttle of coal. Quarter loaf. Tea enough for maybe two mugs. One candle and the lamp at half."

Noreen swung her legs down, tipped the coal scuttle to hear the thin rattle, then checked the lamp reservoir against her thumbnail. "Half's generous."

"Then call it less," Elsie said.

Noreen caught her hand and held it a beat. "Tea first. Then we decide what story leaves this house."

They made tea from yesterday's leaves boiled twice and stood in the kitchen doorway sharing the mug because both had cold hands and one cup held heat better than two thin ones. The yard beyond was a white crust over black ice. No footprints yet from the lane.

"We can't go sloppy about this," Elsie said. "I mean, we can't pretend it was just cold and no sleep."

Noreen leaned her shoulder into the frame. "No pretending. I knew where my hands were."

"If either man asks, he gets pipes and weather and nothing else," Elsie said. "I'm not handing Tom another club, and you don't hand Peter one."

Noreen gave a short laugh with no warmth in it. "Peter would write it in his little ledger and call it proof I'm unfit. Tom would drink it and spit it back at you. So they get frost and plumbing."

Elsie looked down into the tea and saw the wavering reflection of her own face beside Noreen's. "If one of us says stop, we stop. Right then. No debt after. No sulk."

Noreen's face softened. "All right. Stop means stop, and nobody pays for saying it."

Elsie tightened her grip on Noreen's hand. "If your house turns bad, you come through my door. If mine does, I come through yours. Door first."

Noreen set the mug down and took Elsie's hand. "Door first, questions after the kettle's on."

They stood there hand in hand until the cold bit through the floorboards into their feet.

Noreen glanced at the grate, then at the coal scuttle. "If we keep this fed till light, we'll have nothing left for breakfast."

Elsie nodded.

"We bank it now and take the back room. Bed keeps heat better than this floor."

Elsie hesitated just long enough to make the choice again. "All right."

They pinched the lamp wick low, banked the coals to a dull red, and carried blankets upstairs one armful at a time. The back room smelled of old lavender and damp plaster. The iron bedframe complained when they climbed in, but it held warmth better than the mattress by the grate.

Noreen turned onto her side toward the window and caught Elsie's wrist under the blanket. "Before men, remember."

"Before men," Elsie said.

The house settled around them. Somewhere down the landing a floorboard gave one long complaint, and both of them went still until the noise passed.
