# Chapter 6

Elsie woke before the alarm because the cold had teeth again. It bit through the blanket and into the sore points in her hips where she had slept on one side too long. She sat up slowly, one hand under the weight of her belly, and listened to the house before she moved: the soft tick of pipes trying not to freeze, the faint rattle of the sash where the putty had cracked, Tom breathing through his mouth with that smoker's burr in it. A bus went past at the top of the lane, slow and grinding, chains clinking on the rear wheels like loose cutlery.

The inside of the bedroom window had gone white as milk glass. She took her thumb to it and made a coin-sized clear patch. Outside she saw no color at all, only coal smoke held low in the air and the terrace roofs wearing a hard skin of dirty snow. It had not thawed in days. Nothing gave. Nothing drained. The lane had become a narrow trench of boot prints, frozen and refrozen until each step caught the ankle if you took it wrong.

In the kitchen she lit the range with yesterday's newspaper and three careful lumps of coal, not four. She watched how long the kindling took and wrote the time down on the back page of an old school exercise book with Jack and Jill still printed at the top in faded blue. She had started the book in the week before Christmas. She had written meals, weather, what they spent, what they owed Mrs Bhatti, what came into the house that could be counted in ounces or pints. She had stopped writing Tom's promises because promises did not stay still long enough to measure.

She set everything out on the table as if she were laying a body out for identification. She lined up the flour tin, sugar tin, tea caddy, oat jar, and dripping pot. She added two onions soft at the neck, nine potatoes still hard and six that had started to eye and sink, and a heel of bread gone stale enough to knock on wood. She put down one strip of bacon fat wrapped in greaseproof, three eggs, all small, with one cracked shell she would need to use this morning, and four tins: corned beef, marrowfat peas, tomato soup, and condensed milk. She finished with half a packet of barley, a paper twist of pepper, and mustard clinging in the bottom corners of a jar.

She kept going. She opened the scullery door and counted coal sacks by touch because the glass in there had iced over too thick to see cleanly. One full sack was tied at the neck, one half sack slumped and leaked dust, and a bucket of slack sat by the wall. In the cupboard under the sink she found one and a quarter bottles of paraffin, if she counted the heel in the lamp and dared tip it back, soap shaved down to a crescent, and sanitary towels folded from old flour sacks and boiled until the threads looked tired.

When she came back in, Tom had his elbows on the table and her tea caddy open under his nose as if he were inspecting tobacco. He wore his vest and braces and had not shaved. He had one sock on and one heel on the bare stone, rubbing at the cold with his palm.

"What are you doing with all that?" he asked. "Looks like a raid."

"I'm making a two-week plan," Elsie said. "If this weather holds, we can't buy like we used to."

He took a pinch of tea leaves between finger and thumb, rolled them, and dropped them back. "We never bought like rich folk, El. You've always managed."

"That was before the roads kept shutting and before the shop started rationing by queue." She held out her hand for the caddy. "Leave that. I need to know exact amounts."

Tom gave it back with a little smile that was meant to be affectionate and landed as dismissal. "Exact amounts. Listen to you. You sound like old Walsh with his office stamps."

Elsie opened to a fresh page. She wrote at the top: _Fourteen days_. Beneath it she wrote _coal_, _paraffin_, _food_, then drew lines with the edge of a ruler so cracked that it bowed. She did the sums aloud because if she kept them inside her head he would treat them as mood.

"Three lumps to light, three at noon, four to bank," she said. "That's ten a day if we keep one room heated and close off the front. We've maybe one hundred and thirty lumps worth if the half sack is better than it looks. That gives us thirteen days if we don't waste any."

"You count lumps now?"

"Yes."

"Christ." He leaned back, stretched his shoulders, and winced as if the movement had surprised him. "All right then, count lumps. I'll bring more in."

"With what money?"

He did not answer at once. He reached for the enamel mug, drank cold tea, and made a face. "Wages."

"How much this week?"

"You know what it comes to."

"I don't."

He put the mug down too hard. "Don't start in on me before breakfast."

The word sat between them like a nail dropped on tile. Elsie could hear, through the wall she shared with Noreen, the scrape of a chair and then the low drag of someone coughing in a way that was trying not to wake a house. Peter's voice came after it, distinct as if he stood in their own scullery.

"You are to stay in bed," Peter said. "You frightened everybody enough on Saturday."

Noreen answered, her voice thinned but still carrying that quick lift at the end of words that could make a complaint sound like a joke if she wanted it to. "I'm not made of spun sugar, Peter."

Elsie looked at Tom. He gave a small shrug toward the wall as if to say women were all fuss once babies came near. Then he stood, took his second sock from the range rail, and pulled it on.

"I've got an early turn," he said. "And they're cutting hours if men come in late. Keep the fire low till I get back."

"Tom. How much in wages?"

He found his shirt from the peg and shoved his arms through without looking at her. "Enough."

"Enough for what?"

"For this house." He buttoned wrong, cursed, unbuttoned, started again. "For us. Stop pecking at me like a crow."

"I'm carrying this house with figures because the figures are all that hold still," she said.

He bent for his boots. "You're carrying a baby. Leave the numbers to me."

That was the line. Leave the numbers to me. He said it while she was the one scraping paraffin heels from lamp glass and slicing mold off potatoes and deciding whether she could afford to boil nappies or only rinse them. He said it with his hand already out for the last heel of toast.

She let him eat. She let him take the thick end and she did not say anything because saying anything at that minute would have spent fuel she needed later. When he left, he kissed the top of her head like a man exiting a train carriage. The door opened. Cold rolled in. The door shut. His steps crunched down the flagged path.

Elsie counted to fifty. Then she took down the tobacco tin where she kept housekeeping cash. Two shillings and fourpence in coins. A tram token. A bent hairpin. She wrote it all down. She opened Tom's coat pocket where it hung by the door and found nothing but a matchbook from the Railway Arms and a folded betting card for dogs run three weeks ago, circled in blue pencil. The dates had gone soggy from melted snow and dried warped.

By ten the range had settled into a red eye and the room smelled of damp wool and boiled barley water. Elsie put the cracked egg into a pan with chopped onion and let it go soft, then ate standing with her hand braced on the table when the baby pressed down low and held there, blunt and hard. She waited the pressure out. She listened to Noreen's house.

There was no singing that morning. Noreen usually sang under her breath when she cleaned, bits of hymns folded into dance tunes and pop songs from the radio, whatever came first. Elsie heard only short movements: cup to saucer, cupboard door, then quiet. Quiet in that terrace was a warning louder than shouting.

She wrapped a scarf over her hair and carried over the washing basket with two damp aprons laid on top. Under the aprons she put the corned beef tin, the condensed milk, half the barley, and the better of the onions. She added six coal lumps in a flour sack and tied it tight. She did not take paraffin yet because the bottle clinked and smell traveled.

Noreen answered in Peter's old cardigan belted over her nightdress, the hem dark where it had brushed wet stone. She had her hair pinned up with two darning needles crossed like chopsticks, and she held a baby's cardigan sleeve in her hand, mustard wool unraveling from the cuff.

"Don't laugh," Noreen said before Elsie had spoken. She lifted the sleeve. "I've knitted this same inch six bloody times. The wool scratches my fingers and then I imagine it scratching his neck, and out it comes again."

"I didn't laugh," Elsie said. "You're up too long."

"I'm up because if I lie down while he's at work, I hear every drop in the pipes and think one of them is blood." Noreen stepped back to let her in. "Come in before you freeze solid."

The Walsh kitchen felt warmer by maybe two degrees and smelled of cloves and damp plaster. Noreen kept orange peel drying on a saucer behind the range, even now, because she said the house needed reminding that seasons changed. Today the peel had gone hard as shell.

On the table sat a cup of tea gone cold and a saucer full of pills cut in half with a bread knife. Elsie did not recognize the tablets. Noreen saw her looking.

"Aspirin from Mrs Donnelly," she said. "Peter says they're harmless and then tells me not to take anything without asking him. He likes both sides when both sides leave him right."

Elsie set down the basket. "How is the pain?"

Noreen pulled a face and then smoothed it. "Like somebody tied wire around my lower back and forgot to untwist it. Better than Saturday. Worse than I say out loud."

"Any more bleeding?"

"Only spotting."

Elsie nodded. She did not ask if Peter knew. She did not want the answer because she had already decided what she was doing and answers could make a woman hesitate.

She peeled back the aprons. Noreen stared at the tins.

"No," she said immediately. "Absolutely no."

"Yes," Elsie said. "These are staying."

"El, I can't take your food."

"You can and you will."

"Tom will notice."

"Tom notices when it suits him."

Noreen gave her a long look, one eyebrow lifted. "That sounds like a woman who hasn't slept."

"That sounds like arithmetic." Elsie took the tins and moved to the pantry cupboard under the stairs. It smelled of onions and wet cardboard. She put the corned beef behind a sack of flour and set the condensed milk inside an old biscuit tin with cracked family photos on top. She poured barley into a jar marked _Sago_ in blue paint and turned the label inward.

Noreen leaned against the doorframe, rubbing the bridge of her nose with finger and thumb. "If Peter finds it, he'll ask questions."

"Then tell him Mrs Bhatti overcounted and sent you home with charity. He likes the idea of other people making clerical mistakes."

Noreen laughed once, sharp. "That's cruel."

"It's useful."

"Tom will still ask."

Elsie straightened slowly, hand to the small of her back. "Let him. I'm not leaving you to boil wallpaper paste and call it broth because the men need to feel in charge."

Noreen's face softened and then hardened again in the same breath. "I hate this," she said quietly. "I hate needing."

"I know." Elsie reached for her wrist and felt how cold it was. "I hate it too."

They stood like that for a second, not embracing, just palms on wrists like they were checking each other for pulse. Through the front room came the dull click of the Walsh wireless turning on by itself as the power dipped and returned, that old set with the loose knob Peter refused to fix because he called replacement parts indulgence. A dance orchestra swelled, then fell into static.

"Wait," Noreen said. She went to the drawer by the sink, took out a folded paper parcel, and pressed it into Elsie's hand. "Tea. Real tea. Not the sweepings. My aunt sent it at Christmas and Peter thinks I'm saving it for after the baby."

Elsie closed her fingers around it and pushed it back. "Keep it."

"Take half."

"No."

"Take half or I'll put your tins back in your basket and carry them over myself, and then the lane's going to have a show." Noreen's mouth twitched. "Don't test me when I'm in a state and Irish."

Elsie gave in. They split the parcel over the sink with a spoon, each watching the other count. It was absurd and intimate and necessary.

When Elsie left, she carried no visible weight. The basket held only aprons now, and on top Noreen had thrown in the unraveling cardigan sleeve as if she had lent it for repair. On the path between houses, Elsie paused by the coal shed. She looked over the low wall toward the lane. Nobody was there. She lifted six more lumps into her apron, tied the corners, and crossed back to Noreen's scullery door. She did this twice. On the second trip the baby rolled under her ribs and she had to stop, bent over, breath coming thin through her teeth, while coal dust bled black through the apron cloth onto her coat.

By noon she had rewritten the household list three times. She wrote one list as if everyone in the house ate equally. She wrote a second as if Tom took the largest share, because he did. She wrote a third as if she made soup stretch with barley water and gave herself the crusts. The third list was the only one that lasted fourteen days.

She copied that one neatly, then tore it in half and burned the top section with Tom's name against the grate until it curled in on itself and vanished.

She spent the afternoon scrubbing nappies in water that filmed with grease before it reached a boil. She hung them indoors on string by the range so they dripped on her shoes while she peeled potatoes and skimmed mold from jam and patched Tom's shirt cuff with thread darker than the cloth. Every task asked for her hands and gave back a small proof that she had postponed failure by another hour.

At four she put on coat and boots and walked to Mrs Bhatti's shop with the empty paraffin bottle wrapped in a towel. The queue reached the door and bent along the shelves past tins of peaches nobody could afford. Mrs Bhatti stood on a stool by the counter ledger, glasses low on her nose, hair pinned under a scarf printed with tiny red chilies. She was adding up columns with her lips moving and her left foot tapping the rung in time.

"Mrs Harrow," she called without looking up. "You've become very thin in your face. That means your husband is either useless or in love. Which one?"

"The first one today," Elsie said.

Mrs Bhatti barked a laugh, then glanced up and softened. "I can do one pint paraffin, no more. Delivery hasn't come. Coal lorry turned back at the bridge again."

"One pint is fine."

"Your account isn't fine." Mrs Bhatti turned the ledger so Elsie could see the line under her name. "You're one week and three days behind. I'm not pushing you. I'm telling you what ink says."

Elsie slid two shillings across and watched the woman's thumb weigh each coin before she wrote the credit down. Shame was not dramatic. It was this: the scrape of a coin over wood, the pause before somebody chose whether to trust your face.

On the way home, Elsie stopped outside Noreen's back gate and left the paraffin bottle in the milk crate under a tea towel, then knocked once and walked away before the door opened.

By the time dark fell, the house had the close, stale warmth of people trying to make one fire do the work of two.

Tom came in just after seven with snow on his cap brim and ale on his breath. He stamped his boots, swore at the cold, kissed Elsie on the cheek with distracted lips, and held his hands to the range as if he had earned heat by existing. He had brought no coal. He had brought a packet of Woodbines and half a loaf of white bread already cut.

"Good news," he said. "They're talking overtime Saturday if buses keep running."

"Talking is free," Elsie said. She set stew on the table: potato, onion, a little bacon fat melted through, thickened with barley so it looked richer than it was.

"You're in one tonight," he said, trying for humor. "Did somebody steal your halo?"

"Eat before it cools."

He ate fast, spoon scraping enamel in hard circles. Halfway through his second helping he paused and glanced at the pantry. It was a quick movement, but Elsie saw it. After supper he did not go to the chair by the fire. He stayed standing and opened the cupboard above the sink.

"Where did the corned beef go?" he asked.

Elsie kept washing bowls. "It's gone where food goes."

"Don't be smart. We had two tins."

"We had one and a half, if you're counting the one you opened New Year's and left."

"No, we had two full and one soup and one peas." He shut that door and opened the pantry. "And the milk tin was here this morning."

"You're doing an inventory now?"

"Somebody has to."

He crouched and moved things around with an urgency that made the jars knock together. He brought out the flour tin, shook it, listened, set it down. He lifted the tea caddy, frowned. He opened the bread bin and stared as if it might confess.

"How much have you given next door?" he said finally, still crouched.

The bowl in Elsie's hand slipped and clanged in the sink. She put both palms flat on the draining board until the metal cooled under them.

"Mind your mouth," she said.

Tom stood. "That's an answer."

"No, it's a warning."

"I knew it." He laughed once, no humor in it. "I bloody knew it. I come home and I'm the idiot man with empty shelves while Mrs Walsh gets fed on my wages."

"Your wages don't fill these shelves," Elsie said. "My counting does. My queuing does. My hands do."

"Your hands?" He held his own up, palms out, raw where cold had split the skin. "You think I'm out for a stroll all day?"

"I think you don't tell me what money comes in and then you shout when food goes out."

"Because it's theft."

"It's not theft when it's mine to manage."

"Yours?" He took a step toward her. He did not come within striking distance, but he came close enough that she could smell stale beer under the smoke. "Don't start with that modern rubbish. In this house we share."

"Then share the numbers."

He looked away first, toward the dark window where their reflections floated over black glass. From next door came a muffled thud and then Peter's voice, clear through brick and plaster.

"You will not be up those stairs again tonight," Peter said, each word clipped and proper. "Doctor's instruction."

Noreen answered something Elsie could not catch, low and fast.

Tom tipped his head toward the wall. "See? He keeps order. He doesn't have his wife handing out stores behind his back."

"He keeps accounts," Elsie said. "His wife keeps people alive."

Tom's jaw worked. He reached into his coat pocket and pulled out a folded bus ticket book, the kind conductors used, with stubs torn in strips. He flattened it on the table and found a pencil from the mantel.

"Fine," he said. "You want numbers, we'll have numbers."

He wrote the date at the top and began listing: _Tea. Sugar. Flour. Coal. Paraffin. Tins._ He walked to the scullery, came back with the coal shovel, and counted lumps into the scuttle one by one under his breath. He tipped the paraffin lamp, eyed the level, and marked a line with his nail on the glass. He opened the sugar tin and leveled it with a spoon, then drew a pencil mark inside the rim.

Elsie watched in disbelief first, then a kind of exhausted admiration at the pettiness of it. He had found a way to do control that looked like cooperation.

"You're playing shop," she said.

"I'm stopping leaks."

"I'm not a leak."

"Then prove it." He tapped the ticket book. "From tomorrow, every purchase gets written. Every coin accounted. No more mystery parcels, no more favors, no more women trading half the pantry over a fence because they feel sorry for each other."

"You think pity is what this is?"

"I think betrayal is what this is."

He said the last word softer. That made it land harder. It was not rage now. It was injury. He was a man hurt that his authority had not been consulted, as if authority were the same thing as care.

Elsie reached for the wet bowl cloth and wrung it until water ran over her wrists. Her knuckles were red and raw from washing, nails rimmed black from coal dust she had not scrubbed out. She could have lied. She could have said she had sold a tin for paraffin, dropped one in the yard, miscounted. She heard all those easy small lies line up in her head and she let them pass.

"I gave food where it was needed," she said.

Tom's face went blank for one beat, then shuttered. "Right."

"I didn't ask your leave."

"No. You didn't." He folded the ticket book with care that looked almost gentle. "And now I know where I stand."

"You know where your wife stands," she said. "In kitchens. In queues. On icy steps with a paraffin bottle under her coat. That's where your wife stands."

He laughed through his nose. "Don't make speeches."

"I'm not making speeches. I'm tired and I'm cold and I'm done pretending this house runs on your say-so."

The baby shifted hard enough to steal half her breath. She caught the table edge and kept her face still. Tom saw the movement and looked frightened for a flash, then angry at being frightened.

"Sit down," he muttered.

"Don't order me."

"For Christ's sake, El, I'm trying to keep us straight."

"Then start by telling me what's owed."

He rubbed his forehead with heel of hand, smearing coal dust there. "There's bits."

"How many bits?"

"I said there's bits."

"Tom."

"Don't push." His voice rose. From next door the radio clicked off, as if somebody had reached out in the dark and pressed silence over both houses. Tom dropped his voice again. "I can sort it. I just need you to stop making me look like a fool."

There it was, plain at last. He was not asking how to _feed us_, how to _keep warm_, or how to _keep the baby safe_. He was asking her not to make him look like a fool.

Elsie felt something settle in her, not dramatic, only final in a small domestic way. Like setting a jar on a shelf and knowing it belonged there now.

"You're not a fool," she said. "But pride doesn't burn in the grate."

"No," he said. "But pride is what keeps men from being laughed out of their own street."

"Then maybe stop giving the street things to laugh at."

He stared at her. He opened his mouth, shut it, then took the pencil and wrote again in the ticket book, pressing so hard the tip snapped. He swore, sharpened it with his pocketknife, and kept writing. He started a fresh page and drew new columns. He tore a strip from the back and tucked it in his shirt pocket.

"I'll do the shop this week," he said without looking up.

"You can't queue from the depot."

"I'll make time."

"With what money?"

"With mine." He shut the book. "And if anything leaves this house, I hear it first."

He went to the pantry and shifted the jars so their labels faced forward in a neat line. He put the tea caddy in the top cupboard and took the key off the nail by the door, then hesitated. He did not lock it yet. He set the key in his pocket anyway.

Later, in bed, he turned his back to her and lay stiff as green timber. Elsie lay on her side facing the wall she shared with Noreen and listened to the night work itself through brick: kettle singing low, floorboard creak, the cough Peter always tried to swallow. After a while she heard Noreen laugh once, quiet and unbelieving at something, then hush herself.

Elsie slipped her hand under the pillow and touched the corner of the tea parcel Noreen had forced on her, folded in wax paper. She had not opened it. She did not know when she would dare.

In the dark, Tom cleared his throat and said, "We start proper tomorrow."

Elsie did not answer. She watched frost feather the bedroom glass again from the inside and thought of the pantry next door, where two hidden tins sat behind a flour sack like contraband, and of how little it took to redraw a border when everyone was hungry.
