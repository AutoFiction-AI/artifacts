# Chapter 12

Noreen woke before the alarm because her left hand had gone numb under her cheek. The fingers were swollen into thick pale sausages, her wedding ring buried so deep the skin folded over its edge. When she tried to turn onto her back, a weight settled high under her ribs and stayed there, stubborn as a fist.

The house had not warmed in the night. Frost had feathered along the inside of the bedroom window again, and the blanket smelled of damp wool and Peter's hair oil. She sat up slowly and waited for the dizziness to pass. The kettle knocked against the hob downstairs, a dull iron tap each time the flame shook. Arthur was already up in the spare room, moving in his careful linesman way that never quite made a full thud. She could tell him from Peter even through the wall: Arthur placed his boots side by side before he stepped into them, and the soles hit the boards in matched beats.

Her appointment card was tucked into her prayer book on the chair, Thursday 10:40 at Calderbank Maternity Clinic in Keane's block capitals. She had smoothed that card flat three times since Tuesday night, as if the ink might blur and give her one more week. Her ankles had doubled in size anyway. No amount of pretending shifted them.

Peter was at the table when she came down, cast propped on a folded tea towel as if it were something expensive that might scratch. He had shaved and put on his office shirt despite not going in. A man in a sling looked softer, she had once thought. The sling had done nothing to him except make him crueler with his good hand.

He had his little accounts ledger open beside the sugar tin. It had two columns, neat as parish notices, and each figure was pressed deep enough to mark the page below.

"You are late," he said without looking up.

"It isn't eight." She put the kettle straight and reached for the teapot. "I've clinic at ten-forty."

"I know what day it is."

He said it like a correction from a headmaster. The cast turned his right sleeve into a stiff white wing. He moved the ledger with his left hand and drew a line under yesterday's sums.

"Bus fares have increased again. We are not paying for two returns and your tea-shop gossip after."

"It isn't gossip. It's my blood pressure review."

"You were reviewed last month. Then you missed one. Then you claim emergency." He clicked his tongue and made another note. "If you require rest, rest here. It costs nothing."

Noreen set cups out in a row to keep her face level. One for Peter, one for Arthur, one for herself. The fourth cup, blue enamel chipped at the lip, stayed on the shelf where she could see it. She had not put it out since Peter's fall, not with him watching every movement as if kindness itself were contraband.

"Keane said I had to come in when they gave me this slot," she said. "It's not optional."

"Everything is optional if one can count." He pushed the ledger toward her with his good knuckles. "Coal. Bread. Cast plaster fee. Lodger meals. Tell me where the money appears from, and I shall applaud your clinic outing."

Arthur came in from the scullery with his coat over one arm and the smell of outside still clinging to him, wet rope and metal. He set a jar of screws on the dresser before he spoke, automatically sorting them by size while he waited for tea. He did it even half-asleep, thumb and forefinger finding heads and threads as if they were rosary beads.

"Road's slick at the corner," he said to no one in particular. "Bus was ten minutes behind yesterday."

Peter gave him a look that could freeze paraffin. "Thank you, Mr Hale. We can manage our household timetable without commentary."

Arthur did not answer. He took his mug, nodded once to Noreen, and left with the jar in his pocket, screws chiming faintly against one another with each step.

Noreen poured tea and did not drink. Her mouth tasted of pennies and sleep.

"You said you would pay fare when I rebooked," she said.

Peter snapped the ledger shut. "I said we would assess it. We have assessed it."

His voice had that careful municipal tone he used with complaint letters. It was not loud. It was worse than loud, a tidy refusal dressed as reason.

She hated that she knew his patterns well enough to hear the hinge turning. If she asked again, he would move from arithmetic to morality, then from morality to accusation. By noon he would be telling her she had endangered the baby by upsetting herself.

The back door rattled once. Two short knocks, a pause, then one. Elsie's knock, the one they had worked out months ago to spare themselves shouting through walls.

Noreen crossed the kitchen before Peter could stand. Her ankles throbbed inside borrowed boots, each step thick and hot at the joints. Elsie waited under the lintel with a shawl over her coat and her hair pinned anyhow, cheeks red from cold. Snowmelt darkened the hem of her skirt. She held up a tin measure.

"You left this with me," Elsie said, pitching her voice neighbor-bright. "Thought you might need it back."

Peter had followed as far as the doorway to the front room. He did not bother hiding his expression.

"Mrs Harrow," he said. "My wife is not receiving callers this morning. She requires quiet."

Elsie gave him a smile that showed no teeth. "It's one cup measure, Mr Walsh. I won't keep her from what she's got on."

"She has no errands."

"Clinic," Noreen said.

Peter turned his head toward her and did not blink. "No."

He gave her one flat word. He had discovered that plain command carried farther than shouting.

Noreen felt the room tilt for a second, not from fear but from that deep under-rib ache returning with bright edges. She gripped the doorframe and looked past him to the mantel where the clock ticked too fast, always three minutes ahead because Peter liked to arrive first and make people wait.

"I'm going," she said.

"On whose money?" Peter asked. "On whose authority?"

His cast brushed the chair back. Plaster dust came off on the varnish, a white crescent. He did not notice.

Elsie glanced at Noreen once, quick as striking a match. Then she reached into the inside seam of her coat and pulled out a folded bit of wax paper. Coins slid into her palm, silver and copper warmed by her body.

"On mine," Elsie said.

Peter laughed once through his nose. "You can barely keep your own house heated."

"Aye, and still." Elsie counted into Noreen's hand without looking away from him. "Four shillings and six. Return fare and a cup of tea if the clinic runs late."

The coins were heavier than they looked. Noreen felt each edge bite her skin as she closed her fingers.

"I don't need tea," she said under her breath.

"You need not be sentimental, Mrs Harrow," Peter said. "Debt between neighbors becomes gossip by supper."

Elsie shrugged. "Then write it in your ledger if that helps you sleep."

Peter's mouth hardened. For a second Noreen thought he would step forward and block the threshold with his shoulder. Instead he lifted his cast slightly, the smallest reminder of his injury, and spoke with exquisite politeness.

"If anything happens on that road, you will not tell people I approved this outing."

"You don't approve anything unless it has a column and totals," Noreen said, surprising herself.

His face went still. He was most dangerous when still.

"Be back before noon," he said. "Mr Hale requires his dinner, and this house does not run on sentiment either."

Noreen tucked the coins into her glove and stepped outside before he could add one more term.

The air knifed straight through wool. Elsie took her elbow as they crossed the patch of black ice by the drainpipe, not fussing, just steady pressure. They walked in silence past the Bhattis' shuttered shop, past the chapel steps drifted to half-height, past two boys breaking frozen puddles with a broom handle and shouting when the shards jumped. Every few yards Noreen had to stop and shift her boots because her feet no longer bent where feet should bend.

At the bus stop, five people waited under the bent timetable board. A woman with a toddler wrapped in a blanket kept stamping circles into the snow to keep the child warm. A man in a porter cap smoked down to the paper and lit another from the stub.

When the bus finally came, it crawled in second gear and was full to the door. The conductor leaned out, cheeks raw, and shouted that he could take two standing at most. The porter and the blanket woman climbed on. The rest of them watched the rear lights fade into white grit.

Noreen checked the coins again. If they waited for the next, they would miss the appointment and lose the slot. Keane had said plainly on the phone that if Noreen missed again she might not be seen for another week.

Elsie flagged down the first black cab that looked alive enough to move. It skidded half past them before the driver caught, reversed in a spray of slush, and leaned across to crank the passenger door open.

"Clinic?" he said. "Road by the market's blocked. I'll take Chapel Street and you can pray." He had one knitted glove and one bare hand red as meat on the wheel. A canary pin hung from his rear-view mirror, tiny yellow paint chipped off the beak.

Elsie said, "How much?"

"Two and eight if we don't get stuck. Three if we do and I've got to dig us out."

Noreen made a sound she could not hide. Elsie squeezed her wrist.

"Get in," Elsie said.

They rode with their knees pressed together on cracked vinyl, the heater dead, windows filmed white at the corners. The cab smelled of stale tobacco and damp dog. Noreen watched terraced roofs slide by in jerks and thought how easy it was to disappear from one street to the next in weather like this. A missed bus, a delayed call, a woman deciding she did not want to start a fight at breakfast, and by afternoon blood pressure could go from numbers in a notebook to a body on a kitchen floor.

When they reached the clinic, Elsie paid from the same folded packet, adding two pennies tip as if normal generosity could disguise emergency spending. Noreen tried to hand half back.

"No," Elsie said. "You keep your hand shut. We'll sort it later."

"I already owe you for coal and paraffin." Noreen's voice shook from cold and something meaner. "I'm not stacking up like one of his pages."

Elsie looked at her properly then, eyes narrow with worry. "Then don't call it owing. Call it me getting you seen."

Noreen wanted to kiss her, hard, right there under the clinic awning with frozen grit blowing into their collars. Instead she tucked the coins away and followed Elsie through the door.

The waiting room was overheated in bursts, a radiator hissing and stopping like an asthmatic chest. Wet coats steamed on hooks. A pram stood by the wall with no baby in it, just folded nappies and a jar of talc in the tray. Somewhere down the corridor a woman was retching into enamel, the sound thin and furious.

Keane called Noreen in twelve minutes late with her glasses sliding down her nose and a pencil behind her ear. She always smelled faintly of Pears soap and the clove lozenges she sucked to keep from smoking on shift. Today there was blood on one knuckle where the skin had split in the cold, sealed over with clear collodion that caught the strip light.

"Mrs Walsh, coat off, boots off if you can manage," she said. "Mrs Harrow, you stay if Noreen wants you. I'm not spending all morning repeating myself to husbands by telephone."

"Stay," Noreen said before Elsie could pretend she should wait outside.

The exam room was small enough that Elsie's coat brushed the instrument trolley when she moved. Keane wrapped the cuff around Noreen's arm, pumped, listened, frowned, and did it again. The second reading took longer. She licked her pencil tip, wrote two numbers, underlined both.

"Any headache today?"

"Since dawn."

"Where?"

"Behind my eyes. And here." Noreen pressed the heel of her hand under her right ribs.

"Any lights in your vision? Sparkles, black spots, little flies?"

"Last night, when I stood from the chair. I thought it was the fire smoke."

Keane pressed two fingers into Noreen's shin for five seconds and lifted them. The dent stayed as if her leg were dough.

"How long have your boots been tight?"

"A week, maybe two."

"Wedding ring?"

"Stuck."

Keane gave a short grunt that was not judgment, only arithmetic.

"Right. We're not playing at this anymore." She turned the chart toward them so they could see. "Six weeks ago you were one-forty over ninety. Last review, one-fifty-two over ninety-six. Today you're one-sixty-six over one-oh-eight. This is an upward trend, and it's steep." She tapped each line with the pencil in time with the words. "Urine last month had trace protein. Today's sample has more. You have edema to mid-calf and facial puffing. That constellation is preeclampsia. In my training notes they call it toxemia. Call it what you like. It can kill you fast if it tips."

The word kill did not echo dramatically. It sat in the room like another instrument on the tray.

Noreen stared at the chart and could not make the numbers mean body. Numbers were Peter's tricks. Numbers were ledger lines that proved you had failed. She wanted Keane to say pain scale, fever, something she could feel and report.

"I had pressure with my first," she said. "It settled."

"You had swelling," Keane said. "This is different and worse."

She turned to Elsie. "You asked for exact, last time we spoke in the lane. You can both have exact now."

Keane set the pencil down and counted symptoms on her fingers, each one clean and hard.

"If she gets a headache that doesn't ease with rest, tea, or tablets, call me. If she sees flashing lights or loses part of her vision, call me. If pain under the right ribs sharpens, if she starts vomiting, if her face or hands swell suddenly more, if she passes very little urine, if she grows breathless at rest, call me. If she has a fit, protect her head, turn her to the side, and send for ambulance at once. Don't wait for husband permission. Don't wait for weather to improve. Don't wait to see if she settles. Women seize and stroke on this trajectory. Babies detach placentas. I'm saying this in plain language because plain language is what keeps people alive."

Elsie's hands were clasped over her bag so tight the knuckles looked chalked. Noreen could feel the force of her listening like heat.

"What do I do now?" Noreen asked.

"You rest." Keane's voice softened by one degree and no more. "Strictly. Left side when possible. Feet up above hip level. No lifting coal scuttles, no carrying wet laundry, no scrubbing floors, no climbing up and down those stairs for every small thing. Salt moderate. Fluids steady. I want you seen twice weekly at minimum, home visits if roads fail." She glanced at Noreen's hands. "And I need that ring off before your fingers trap blood. Soap and string tonight."

Noreen laughed once, a dry broken sound. "Who is doing my house, then?"

Keane met her eyes. "Whoever prefers you alive."

Noreen looked at Elsie. Elsie looked back, fierce and frightened and already calculating Peter's dinner, Arthur's tray, the coal bucket, the laundry line, and the whole machinery of two households, all the invisible work that kept men warm and convinced they were in charge.

"My husband has a broken wrist," Noreen said finally. "He can't do much."

"He can do whatever men discover they can do when there is no alternative," Keane said. "Can he boil potatoes one-handed? Yes. Can he fetch coal in smaller loads? Yes. Can he stop spending your pulse on pointless arguments? Also yes, though that is advanced training."

Elsie gave a startled snort she tried to hide as a cough.

Keane pulled a fresh form from the stack and began writing in tight block print. She wrote with green ink, always green, because she said black disappeared in bad light and this town lived in bad light half the year. The nib scratched hard enough to hear.

"I'm filing high-risk note," she said. "If transport worsens, I'll push for day-bed observation at Calder Infirmary. Beds are scarce and we both know it. I still put it in writing. Documentation protects you when people say you're overreacting."

Noreen's throat burned. "Will the baby come early?"

"Maybe. Not always. Preeclampsia can force our hand, or labor can come on its own. Our aim is time without pretending we own time." Keane set the form aside and looked at Noreen as if she were a person and not a case number. "How many weeks now?"

"Thirty-one by my dates."

"Then every day matters."

Silence sat for a beat, broken only by the radiator's hiss and stop.

Keane picked up the cuff again and tightened it. "I'm taking another reading before you leave. I want a post-rest number. Lie down on the couch five minutes. Mrs Harrow, roll that blanket and wedge her ankle." She paused, then added, "And both of you listen carefully. Secrecy kills in obstetrics more often than blood does. If there is anyone blocking access, withholding fare, delaying calls, you tell me now or you tell me after in writing. I can't treat what I'm not allowed to see."

Noreen's mouth opened and closed. Peter's name sat there like a fishbone. Beside her, Elsie had gone very still.

"He didn't want me to come," Noreen said. "He said it was unnecessary expense."

Keane did not flinch. "Did he prevent you?"

"He tried."

"How did you get here?"

"Cab," Elsie said. "Paid from housekeeping."

Keane's gaze shifted between them and landed somewhere beyond both, in the space where legal facts and lived facts refused to line up.

"Right," she said. "Then we work with what exists. I will chart transport barrier and husband opposition to attendance. If he objects, he can object to my face."

Noreen let out a breath she had not noticed she was holding. Relief felt shameful, as if she had betrayed somebody by wanting a witness.

Keane finished the second reading, a fraction lower and still ugly. She wrote it down and tore two slips from her pad.

"One for next clinic Monday, one for district check Saturday if roads permit. If roads don't permit, I still come unless I'm physically blocked. Keep a packed bag by the door from tonight: gown, nappies, towel, any papers. And put a list of emergency symptoms on the mantel where everyone can read it. Not hidden in a drawer."

"Everyone?" Noreen asked.

"Everyone who shares your roof." Keane capped her pen. "You are done pretending this is private discomfort. This is public risk."

Outside the room, the corridor had filled with the noon crowd. Women balanced toddlers on hips, husbands shifted from foot to foot near the payphone, a nurse wheeled a tray of sterilized instruments under a sheet. Life continued with terrible normality.

Noreen sat on the bench by the coat rack while Elsie wrestled her boots back over swollen feet. The leather scraped and stuck. Elsie did not rush. She warmed each boot mouth with her hands first, then eased the heel in inch by inch like fitting a child into a tight sleeve.

"Don't," Noreen said quietly. "You'll hurt your back."

"My back is not the point today." Elsie tied the laces and sat back on her heels. "Can you stand?"

Noreen stood. The floor rolled once and steadied.

When they stepped out under the clinic awning, the sky had gone that flat pewter that meant more snow by evening. The road was a churn of gray slush and horse muck. Elsie looked at the symptom slip in her glove as if it were a summons.

The slip listed headache that did not ease, lights in vision, pain under right ribs, vomiting, sudden swelling, little urine, breathless spells at rest, and fit.

The words were on paper, yes, but they were also tasks now, distances to cover, and doors to get through. It was a list of moments where one minute of delay could be the difference between a fright and a death.

Noreen touched Elsie's sleeve. "Say it."

"What?"

"What you're thinking."

Elsie watched a lorry throw brown spray across the curb, then said, very quiet, "I'm thinking we can't keep doing this like a secret errand. Not with that list in your pocket."

Noreen closed her eyes briefly. The wind cut under her scarf and found the damp at the back of her neck.

"If he knows everything, he will lock the doors," she said.

"If he controls every penny and every call, you'll seize in that kitchen while he argues about fare." Elsie's voice stayed level, but it had gone blunt in the way it did when she crossed her own fear. "I won't have it."

They stood close enough that their sleeves touched, two married women on a public pavement making no gesture anyone could convict and all the decisions that would damn them anyway.

"I owe you," Noreen said, because debt language was the only language the day had offered her.

Elsie shook her head. "You owe me nothing. You pay me back when you can if it helps your pride. That is not what this is."

Noreen gave a thin laugh that stung her throat. "Everything in this street is what it's not."

Elsie folded the symptom slip in half, then half again, and tucked it into the front of Noreen's coat where her hand could find it without searching.

"From now on," Elsie said, "if you get one of these signs, you knock my wall and you don't ask permission first. If you can't get to the wall, Arthur hears a kettle whistle better than any man on this street. He'll come. Keane said no waiting, and I heard her."

Noreen looked at her, really looked, at the pale strain around her mouth and the stubborn set of her jaw and the curve of her own late belly beneath the coat. Elsie was carrying life too, and still she was offering minutes and muscle she did not have to spare.

"This could ruin you," Noreen said.

"Tom already ruined enough. Peter can try."

She said it without drama, as if naming weather.

The next bus groaned into view, windows fogged, steps slick. Elsie climbed first, turned, and took Noreen's elbow with both hands. Noreen felt the careful pressure and understood, with a clarity that hurt, that love had crossed into logistics. It had become coins in wax paper, symptom lists on mantelpieces, who held a door, who answered a knock, who spoke first when a man said no.

Inside, the conductor punched their tickets and moved on. Noreen held the rail and breathed through the ache under her ribs. Elsie sat beside her, watching the street blur by, jaw tight, already sorting what could be shifted in both houses before evening. It was not romance now. It was systems.

On Barrow Lane the walls were thin, the money thinner, and death could come quietly between one cup of tea and the next. Elsie knew that in her bones by the time the bus turned the corner. Whatever they had called this before, whatever private name had kept it tender and hidden, it had changed.

From today, every choice they made about each other was also a medical decision.
