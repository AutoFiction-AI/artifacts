# Chapter 15

The ward smelled of boiled sheets, sour milk, and bleach. Noreen woke with her tongue thick as felt and reached for the jug on the locker. Her arm only got halfway there before her stitches pulled and a hot wire of pain ran under her belly. She stopped, breathed through her teeth, and watched the water tremble in the jug from footsteps in the corridor.

Someone was crying two beds down, not loud, just regular. A baby was making a thin lamb sound from behind the nursery doors. Not hers. She knew her own child now by absence, by where the noise did not come from.

The plastic bracelet they had clipped on her at transfer sat against the bone of her wrist: WALSH, FEMALE INFANT, SPECIAL CARE. The letters had rubbed grey where she thumbed them through the night. Her hand still smelled faintly of iodine and old blood. When she lifted the hem of her gown to check the pad, the blood was dark rust now instead of bright, but there was plenty of it. She was still leaking life onto institutional cotton while her daughter lay in a different building under a heat lamp.

A trolley squeaked past the end of the ward. Metal cups. Toast in a covered tin. The woman in the next bed, Mrs Dunn from Sowerby, turned over and pushed her plait off her shoulder with a practiced flick.

"You should ring for tea before they run out," Mrs Dunn said. She had a face full of freckles and a housecoat with tiny blue anchors on it, ridiculous and stubbornly cheerful in March mud weather. "If you wait till matron does rounds you'll get the sad pot."

"I don't want tea," Noreen said.

Mrs Dunn gave her a look over the edge of the blanket, not unkind. "Then ring anyway. Keeps your hand occupied."

Noreen pressed the bell.

Staff Nurse June Firth came in with her cardigan half-buttoned and a men's watch strapped loose over her cuff, the cracked glass mended with clear tape. She always smelled of peppermint because she chewed mints to cover cigarettes between rounds. She took one look at Noreen sitting up and put a palm out as if stopping traffic.

"Easy," she said. "You're not on your feet yet."

"I need to go to special care."

"No."

Noreen swallowed. "You can wheel me."

"No." Firth moved to the end of the bed, checked the chart, and tapped the page with her thumbnail. "You lost a dangerous amount of blood. Your pressure dropped again at four. You fainted trying to stand for the commode. I'm not sending you on two buses and a corridor walk in this state."

"She's there on her own."

"She's not on her own. She's with nurses and a registrar and a machine that's doing exactly what your body can't do this morning."

"She's mine."

Firth softened for half a breath, then hardened again into ward voice. "I know who she's for, Mrs Walsh. I know it every time I write her number. But policy is policy and weather is weather. No maternal transfers until stable mobility, and no exceptions while beds are doubled up. Half this ward's from blocked roads and half special care's from blocked roads. We're all running short."

Noreen looked at the chart without seeing it. "When can I go?"

"If your pulse behaves and you keep food down, maybe forty-eight hours. Maybe longer."

"Maybe," Noreen said, and hated the word.

Firth put two fingers on the bracelet around Noreen's wrist and turned it so the label faced her. "You can send milk on the eleven o'clock run. We can get you started after breakfast. That's not the same thing. I know it's not. It's still something."

She went away before Noreen could answer.

The toast came. Noreen chewed one corner and could not make it pass. Her breasts ached in a heavy, spreading way, skin hot and tight under the gown, as if the body had not received the message that the baby was three miles and three locked doors away. Milk wanted to happen in one room; the child who needed it was happening in another.

By half past nine the ache sharpened. Every small movement dragged. She rang again.

A younger nurse came with a tray: two thick glass bottles, rubber stoppers, labels, and a chipped enamel bowl of hot water. Her name badge said C. MERCER. She had ink on the side of her left hand where she had tested a fountain pen and forgotten to wash it off.

"I'll show you," Mercer said, pulling the curtain. "It's awkward at first. Everybody hates the first go."

"Did she feed at all?"

Mercer shook her head. "Tube feeds. They gave donor for now. Don't look at me like that. Donor keeps tiny ones alive. Your milk will go with the next lot if we can get any."

Noreen sat higher, wincing. Mercer folded a towel, wedged it behind her back, and talked her through it as if teaching someone to mend a torn sleeve: warm cloth first, hand position, press back then forward, do not pinch. The first drops were yellow and sticky and so little they looked like a mistake. Noreen cried anyway, silent, while Mercer pretended not to notice and handed her the bottle.

By the time Keane arrived near eleven, Noreen had filled barely a finger's depth in one bottle and nothing worth capping in the second. Keane shook rain out of her coat onto the lino and set her canvas bag on the chair.

"Do not apologize for quantity," she said before Noreen could speak. "The first day is often this. It comes in proper later."

Keane's chapped hands were pink at the knuckles. Her scarf had come loose and one end was pinned with a brooch shaped like a thistle, dented from years of catches and doors. She looked older than she had in the Walsh kitchen, or maybe hospital lighting scraped everybody down to bone.

"I want to see her," Noreen said.

"I know."

"They keep saying policy like that word should make me grateful."

"It's not for gratitude. It's for queue management and infection control and fear of lawsuits nobody here can afford." Keane pulled the chart closer and read, lips moving. "Your blood pressure's still unstable on standing. Your haemoglobin is very low. If you collapse in a corridor, they lose both of you for an hour while people fetch people. They're trying to avoid that." She looked up. "I'm explaining, not blessing it."

Noreen wiped her face with the heel of her hand. "How is she?"

Keane reached in her bag and took out a folded note, softened with damp from being in her pocket. "Female infant Walsh. Weight four pounds three ounces this morning. Oxygen hood overnight, now in incubator air with saturation acceptable. Mild retractions still. They are watching temperature and sugar."

She delivered it exactly as if reading bus times, each item clipped to fact and then let go. No "she'll be fine." No lies.

"Did she cry?" Noreen asked.

Keane paused. "Yes. Thin, but she did."

That landed harder than any number on the paper.

An orderly came to collect the bottles. Mr Baines, with a railwayman's cap he wore even indoors and fingers permanently brown from carbon paper, held the crate against his hip and counted labels aloud under his breath. He had a stammer on hard consonants, so he tapped the crate with his thumb each time he hit one and waited himself through it.

"W-Walsh," he said, checking the tag twice. "Eleven run. If weather lets, that reaches county by one."

"Can I send a note?" Noreen asked.

Baines looked toward Keane, then back. "One line, love. Not a letter. Sister there says long notes get wet and unread."

Noreen tore the corner off yesterday's menu and wrote with Mercer's pen: Warm her feet if she hates the blanket. She had no idea if newborns hated blankets. She wrote it anyway, because it was a sentence she could place in the same box as the milk.

Baines tucked the scrap under the bottle band and went.

Rain thickened against the windows. Not snow now, but steady brown-water rain that found every crack in old stone and ran down the inside of frames. Thaw weather. The drip from the gutter outside beat time on the sill.

Peter arrived at visiting hour with his hair parted too straight and his right wrist still in plaster, the sling clean as if he had changed it before leaving work. He carried a paper bag of oranges and a file tied with red tape.

"You look better," he said. He stood at the foot of the bed rather than sitting. "Color is returning."

"You can tell from all this flattering light," Noreen said.

He ignored it. "I spoke with admissions and with the consultant's secretary. The present arrangement cannot be altered until you are medically declared mobile. I asked twice. I was very clear."

He laid the file on the blanket, red tape centered like a gift ribbon.

"What is that?"

"Receipts and necessary forms. Taxi to clinic. Additional coal on Tuesday night. Pharmacy charge. Replacement sheets, since ours were ruined. I have put dates against each item so there is no confusion later."

Noreen stared at him. Her body felt hollowed out and packed with sand; she was too tired for fury and too awake for sleep.

"There was blood," she said. "I nearly died."

"Yes." He said it as if confirming an appointment. "And I responded. I am ensuring there is a complete record of what your crisis required from this household."

"From me," she said.

"From us," he said, then corrected himself with careful emphasis. "Primarily from me, because you were incapacitated."

Firth passed the bed at that moment and slowed, reading the room without making a show of it.

Peter lowered his voice but not his diction. "You will need rest and cooperation if you want timely access to the child. The hospital is already wary of disruption. I have represented us as orderly people. Do not undermine that."

The word orderly hung there, scrubbed and heavy. Noreen thought of blood on the Walsh kitchen flags, Keane's hands red to the wrists, Elsie feeding the fire with chair legs when coal ran out. Orderly.

"What does cooperation mean?" she asked.

"It means no scenes. No contradictory instructions to staff. No neighbors intervening in clinical matters."

"You mean Elsie."

"I mean anyone who confuses boundaries." He set the oranges on the locker, one by one. "I am carrying extra burdens and I will expect that to be remembered when decisions are made."

He spoke like a man balancing council accounts after hours, columns neat, all emotion translated into debit and credit so nothing had to be felt by hand.

Noreen looked at his face and, for the first time since she had married him, did not search for the kinder version underneath. She did not think, He is frightened and saying it badly. She thought, He is counting.

Keane stepped back to the bed with deliberate noise from her shoes. "Mrs Walsh needs to express again at one," she said. "Visiting is ten minutes."

Peter gave Keane a tight nod, then returned to Noreen. "I shall come tomorrow with the post and whatever update they provide from special care. Eat your supper. Do not argue with staff."

"Are you going to her?" Noreen asked.

"I went this morning. Incubator row three. She has your mouth."

That was the first useful thing he had said all day. It hit her with such force she had to grip the sheet.

"Did you touch her?"

"Not permitted." He adjusted the sling at his shoulder. "I observed through the hatch."

Observed. Noreen shut her eyes.

When she opened them he had already picked up the red-taped file and retied the knot, as if even in hospital he could not leave a ledger unattended.

After he left, Firth came back with a mug of weak tea and set it near Noreen's hand.

"He is always that tidy?" she asked, not quite casual.

"Only when he wants something."

Firth snorted once. "Then he's like most men with paperwork." She checked Noreen's pulse, fingers warm and brisk. "You're not wrong to hate this, you know. The rules, I mean. People talk like mothers should be grateful for any crumb and never mind the bite marks."

Noreen gave a dry laugh that hurt her stitches. "I don't even get crumbs. I get reports."

"You get reports today," Firth said. "Tomorrow, maybe a wheelchair if your standing pressure behaves. That is not comfort. It is a target."

At one o'clock Mercer returned, and Noreen pressed her thumb into her own sore skin, counted breaths, and filled a little more of the bottle than before. The milk looked thin as rainwater at first, then clouded. A body obeying chemistry when nothing else obeyed.

Outside, somewhere beyond the rain and boiler rattle, a train horn drifted low through the valley. Baines's eleven run would be halfway there by now, her milk sloshing in a crate between other women's names.

She pictured the incubator row from Peter's description and tried to fit a face to the number. Row three. Tiny mouth. Warm her feet.

That evening she took the back page from the hospital menu and folded it into four squares. In the first square she wrote, in pencil stolen from the bedside crossword: What he controls. In the second: What I can carry. In the third: Who will help anyway.

She left the fourth blank.

By the time they discharged her, the lane had turned from packed ice to black mud with a skin of grey water on top. Every cart wheel cut a trench and left it full. The thaw made different noise from snow: drip, slap, gutter-rush, doorframes swelling, boots sucking free of muck. Nothing stayed dry.

Walking from the hired car to the Walsh door took less than a minute and felt like crossing a field after surgery. Noreen held the wall with one hand and her belly with the other while Peter paid the driver in exact coins, counting each piece into the man's palm. Her milk leaked through the pad in her brassiere before she reached the hall.

Inside, the house smelled of damp plaster, boiled cabbage, and coal dust. The scullery bucket under the leak was half full and ringing with drops. Arthur Hale's coat hung behind the kitchen door, sleeves patched at both elbows with darker wool. He was at the table rebuilding a torch from parts spread on newspaper, tongue caught in one cheek while he soldered, patient as a watchmaker.

He stood when she came in and wiped his hands on a rag. "Welcome home, Mrs Walsh. If that bucket overflows before supper, shout. I keep forgetting it when the phone rings."

Arthur's voice had the same reserve as before, but he had put a folded square of old blanket on the chair nearest the fire so she could sit without asking. He pretended that was for anyone, not especially for her.

"Thank you," she said.

Peter set her case down by the stairs. "Arthur has been useful during your absence."

Arthur gave Peter a brief nod and returned to the torch, offering no opinion. The solder hissed. Rain ticked at the back window. In the corner, the tin bath they had used for emergency hot water on labor night was scrubbed clean but still warped at the rim.

Noreen sat and let her bones settle. Home was the same shape and no longer her old place inside it.

By habit she listened for the baby's breath from the Moses basket beside the hearth. Silence answered. The basket was there, lined with the yellow flannel she had hemmed in December, waiting and empty.

Peter brought in tea and, with his good hand, arranged everything within reach as if laying out instruments. Cup. Plate. Bread. A folded sheet of foolscap.

"We need a workable schedule," he said.

"I've just come through the door."

"Exactly why we need order immediately. You must express at six, ten, two, and six again. The ten o'clock bottle can go with me to clinic records when I report before work. The two o'clock can go by Arthur if his shift starts late."

Arthur looked up. "Can do, if needed."

Peter continued. "If weather worsens, we use the eleven train run from county through Baines, as before. I have noted all of this."

He tapped the foolscap. Columns and times. Her body reduced to timetable.

Noreen drank the tea. It tasted of metal from the kettle scale. "And if I want to go myself?"

"When cleared by the district nurse and when the fares can be justified." Peter sat opposite her, straight-backed. "We cannot spend blindly now."

"Blindly." She let the word sit between them.

Peter's eyes went to his notes. "I have advanced three pounds six and four since Tuesday night. Coal, transport, linens, pharmacy, extra rations for Arthur while he covered practical tasks. There will also be clinic fares if this drags."

"If this drags," she repeated, and almost laughed.

"Do not start with tone," he said, still calm. "I am describing facts. You require support. Support has cost. Cost creates obligations."

There it was, clean as chalk on a board.

Noreen pressed her thumb against the edge of the teacup until the heat bit. "To whom?"

"To me," Peter said. "And to the stability of this household, which I maintain."

Arthur busied himself with the torch and did not look up. The radio on the shelf crackled through weather warnings and then dropped to static.

A knock came at the back door before Noreen answered. Arthur rose first, opened it, and Elsie stepped in with rain on her coat shoulders and two string bags cutting red lines into her fingers. She looked pale under her hat, and the curve of her own late pregnancy pulled her weight forward when she stood still.

"I brought things," Elsie said, and shook water from her sleeves onto the mat. "Clean wraps from my line, your blue nightdress mended where it split, and those rubber teats Keane said are less awful than the ward ones."

She set the bags down and, without asking, started unloading on the table in practical order: laundered cloths, wrapped soap, two sterilized bottles in newspaper, a stack of cut labels, safety pins in a match tin so they would not rust.

"Mrs Bhatti said you can add to my book for two weeks," Elsie added. "Milk and bread only. No tins unless you ask her yourself. She was fierce about that bit."

"Thank you," Noreen said.

Peter stayed seated. "We are managing, Mrs Harrow."

Elsie did not meet his tone with heat. She kept her eyes on the labels as she straightened them. "Good. This is me helping manage, then."

"Medical arrangements are already in place."

"Aye, I heard all about arrangements from the whole lane." Elsie lifted the mended nightdress by the shoulders and checked the seam against the light. "This house can keep your arrangements. It still needs dry cloth and clean teats."

Peter's jaw tightened. "There is no need for commentary."

"There's rarely a need, and yet here we are," Elsie said, mild as dishwater.

Noreen watched the exchange and felt something inside her settle, like a chair leg finding level on uneven floorboards.

Arthur cleared his throat. "I'll take the two o'clock bottle, Mr Walsh. Past the substation anyway." He clicked the torch on and off once, as if proving its charge.

Peter stood. "Very well. We will keep this efficient."

When he left for the front room to fetch his coat, Elsie moved closer to Noreen and spoke low, eyes still on the bottles.

"Keane says you can ask for a social worker letter if he starts playing gatekeeper with visits. She'll back it. You don't have to decide today."

"He already made a timetable," Noreen said.

"Course he did." Elsie looked up then, her expression sharp and tired and very steady. "You make your own one on top of his. Quietly."

Peter came back in, and Elsie stepped away at once, handing him the list of sterilizing times as if that was all they had discussed.

"I should go before the lane floods proper," she said. "Nora, I'll come tomorrow with more cloth if you need it."

"Come regardless," Noreen said.

Elsie nodded and left. Arthur followed to hold the door against the swollen frame. Rain blew in and spotted the flags.

After supper Peter brought out the foolscap again, now with a second page clipped behind it.

"Housekeeping," he said. "Because your concentration may be affected by blood loss, I have drafted a temporary arrangement. I will hold the ration book and cash. You can request purchases each morning. I will review before leaving for work."

Noreen looked at him for a long moment. "Like a child with pocket money."

"Like a convalescent who cannot handle strain."

"And when I'm no longer a convalescent?"

"When I judge that circumstances are normal."

He did not hear himself. Or heard and did not care.

Noreen signed nothing. She said she was tired and went upstairs before he could convert refusal into argument.

In the bedroom, the wallpaper seam had peeled another inch from damp. She sat on the bed, peeled the wet breast pad from her skin, and hissed through her teeth at the pull. Milk had dried crusty on the cloth. Blood still marked the inside of her thighs where the pad had shifted on the ride home. Her body was all demand and no authority.

She took the folded hospital menu from under her case lining. The pencil line had smudged but held.

What he controls:

wages

ration book

front and back keys if he wants

my clinic fare unless I hide coins

what story he tells people

special care visiting if he keeps acting as messenger

What I can carry:

one case

documents flat in nightdress hem

infant bracelet

two vests, shawl, nappies enough for one day

my own body, if it stops shaking on stairs

Who will help anyway:

Elsie (cloth, cash, walking)

Keane (letters, pressure chart, referrals)

Arthur (quiet lifts, no questions)

Mrs Bhatti (credit, gossip filter if paid respect)

She stared at the blank fourth square until rain blurred the window and the lane lamp came on yellow. Then she wrote: What he does not get.

my gratitude as payment

the whole truth of what I feel

my daughter as proof I stayed obedient

She did not write Elsie's name there. She did not write love or fear or sin or anything from church language. She wrote practical things, because practical things were harder for men like Peter to argue with.

At ten she expressed milk again, kneeling by the bed because sitting hurt less than standing. The bottle took longer this time. She watched each line rise along the glass and imagined it translated into minutes inside an incubator she had not seen.

Peter knocked once and came in without waiting.

"Have you finished?"

"Nearly."

He stood by the wardrobe, sling hanging empty now that his wrist had improved enough to hold the door. "I will take that bottle in the morning. Do not forget the label."

"I won't."

"And Noreen." He hesitated, choosing words like stamps. "I expect a measure of appreciation for what this has required."

She capped the bottle carefully. "You want me to say thank you for keeping score while I bled."

His face tightened. "I want acknowledgment that households do not run on sentiment."

"No," she said quietly. "They run on someone's unpaid labor and someone else's story about who paid for it."

For a second he looked genuinely startled, as if furniture had spoken.

"You are not well enough for this tone," he said.

"I'm well enough."

"Do not mistake exhaustion for moral clarity."

She met his eyes. "Do not mistake accounting for love."

He left without closing the door fully. The draft from the landing moved the curtain in and out like a breath.

Near midnight Arthur tapped lightly and passed a hot brick wrapped in flannel through the gap.

"For your feet," he said. "The pipes are sulking again."

"Thank you."

He shifted his weight, awkward and kind. "Sister at county rang while you were up. Baby took thirty mil through tube at nine. Kept it down."

Noreen pressed the hot brick to her shins and shut her eyes. Thirty mil. Kept it down. More intimate than any blessing she'd heard all week.

"Did she say anything else?"

"She said her hands are long. Like she'll play piano when she's forty."

Noreen laughed softly, then cried at the same time, both noises small.

Arthur pretended to inspect the banister knob until she had herself again. "I'll be off at six. Leave the bottle by the kettle and I'll carry it to Baines if Mr Walsh is delayed."

"You don't owe me that."

"No," he said. "But it's on my way."

After he went, she slid the bottle into the enamel jug with cool water and tucked the paper list inside her pillowcase. She lay down on the side that hurt less, hand around the transfer bracelet, and listened to thaw water running through the gutter like distant applause for nobody in particular.

Before sleep took her she set tomorrow in order: milk at six, label by quarter past, coins hidden in the hem of the green cardigan, ask Keane about letters, ask Elsie for a spare key ring, say nothing unnecessary to Peter, and keep writing.

The train horn sounded again from the valley, long and low through rain. Somewhere beyond Barrow Lane, bottles with women's names were moving through the dark toward warm machines and tiny mouths. Noreen could not hold her daughter yet.

She could still begin.
