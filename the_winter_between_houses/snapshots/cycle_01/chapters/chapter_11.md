# Chapter 11

By eight in the morning the front step had turned to black glass again.

Noreen saw it through the lace where the frost had not sealed the pane yet: a hard strip of shine from threshold to curb, no grit on it, no ashes, nothing the council promised in handbills and never managed on Barrow Lane unless a man from the council lived on that street. Peter had left heel marks from when he came in at half one, weaving and broad-backed in the hall, one shoe missing and his breath full of stout and peppermint. Those marks had frozen into pale commas in the dark.

She set his mug on the table anyway. Tea gone stewed and bitter because she had brewed it early and forgotten it while she pulled her stockings over swollen ankles that did not look like her ankles anymore. She had her clinic card tucked in the Bible on the shelf, not for piety, for safety. Peter never opened it unless visitors came and he wanted the room to look decent.

The kettle rattled. In the spare room Arthur coughed once, turned a page, and coughed again. He had taken to reading railway timetables in the morning as if trains still obeyed them. Noreen loved him for that useless hope and pitied him for it.

Peter came down with the careful dignity of a man pretending he did not have a hangover. He had shaved a path through one cheek and left white stubble on the other. His glasses sat crooked. He put both palms on the table and looked at the tea as if she had wronged him with it.

"You let that stand," he said.

"I’ll pour fresh. Sit down before you tip over."

"I am not infirm." He drew himself up, then winced at the kitchen light. "Do not begin your fussing."

There it was, the schoolmaster voice, his collar-button voice. If he had gone soft in the mouth with drink he would have been easier to steer. Formal Peter was a harder weather.

"Fine," she said. "No fussing. Drink this rot and enjoy it."

Arthur laughed into his cough in the next room. Peter ignored it with effort. Noreen saw the muscle jump in his jaw.

He drank half the mug to prove a point and grimaced. "I shall fetch the paper."

"You’ll fetch your death on that step. Wait till I throw ashes."

"I am capable of walking ten yards in daylight."

He reached for his overcoat one-handed because he did not want her help and did not trust Arthur to see him ask for it. He got his arm in wrong, cursed under his breath, and straightened the lapels like a clerk presenting himself at a window. Noreen came around with the coal scuttle anyway.

"Peter, just stand there two minutes."

"No."

He opened the door. January light hit the linoleum in a flat blade. She smelled iron air, chimney smoke, the binman’s cart three streets over where no binman should have been because the roads were still half shut.

"Peter."

He gave her that thin smile he wore when he meant to be obeyed. "Do not call after me like a fishwife."

He stepped down.

His foot shot out as if someone had yanked the leg from under him. For a second he hung in a ridiculous half-squat, coat flared, glasses flashing. Then he came down hard on his right side. The crack Noreen heard was sharp as kindling snapping under a boot.

Peter made a sound she had never heard from him, not even drunk: a raw bark that jumped to a high animal cry before he strangled it off. He curled around his wrist in the slush-dirted snow and tried to stand. Failed. Tried again.

Noreen dropped the scuttle. Ash scattered over the frozen step in a useless gray fan. She was beside him before she felt the cold through her slippers.

"Stay down."

"Do not touch me."

"You can order me later." She took his elbow and felt him shudder. "Let me see."

"It is a sprain."

The wrist was already rising, wrong shape, a hump where there should have been a clean line from hand to forearm. She did not need Mrs Keane for that one. Peter saw her face and looked away.

Arthur stood in the doorway in his vest, one boot on, one boot unlaced. Frost had turned his hair pale at the edges. "Need me?"

"Get his scarf and my handbag," Noreen said. "And my clinic card from the Bible. Please."

Peter snapped, "No clinic. I have to report to the office by nine-thirty."

"You’re not holding a pen with that hand for six bloody weeks."

His eyes cut to Arthur at the swearing, then to the neighbor wall where Elsie could hear if she was near the sink. Shame moved through him quicker than pain.

He pushed himself upright with his left hand and nearly blacked out. Noreen caught his coat front and took his weight. She felt how heavy he was when he stopped pretending.

"Inside," she said.

"No hospital."

"You want this set crooked? You want that hand like a hook?"

"Do not dramatize."

She got him over the threshold inch by inch, his boots scraping the sill. Arthur came back with the scarf, bag, and card, and handed her a folded towel too.

"Wrap it," Arthur said softly. "Not tight."

Noreen nodded. Arthur’s own hands shook in the cold. She noticed, absurdly, that he had darned the thumb of his glove with bright orange wool, the wrong color entirely. He had done it neat as surgery.

Peter lowered himself into the kitchen chair and set his wrist on the towel as if it were someone else’s limb attached by mistake. Sweat stood on his top lip.

"Get Dr Melling to come here," he said. "A home call is adequate."

"Home calls are for people with lungs full of water and babies coming out sideways," Noreen said. "You’re going to casualty."

"I am not sitting in that waiting room with drunks and children."

"You are a drunk with a broken wrist."

His face hardened so fast she heard the breath catch in Arthur’s throat. Peter spoke to Arthur, not to her. "Mr Hale, will you explain to my wife that language and hysteria do not improve outcomes?"

Arthur looked at Noreen first. "Outcome’s better if he gets x-rayed today."

Peter stared at him for three full beats, betrayed by grammar and class and pain all at once. Then he bent over his arm and breathed through his teeth.

Noreen moved to the telephone in the hall. The line gave her two dead clicks, then a tone, then nothing. She rattled the hook, dialed again. This time she got exchange and waited through a hiss of weather until a woman’s flat voice answered emergency control.

"Ambulance?"

"For a fractured wrist, no vehicle available," the woman said before Noreen finished. "Road priority is chest pain, collapse, labor, and children under five with breathing difficulty. Can family transport?"

Noreen looked at Peter, at his jaw shaking though he kept his mouth shut. "Yes," she said. "If I can find one."

"Then do that. Keep limb supported. No food in case sedation is required."

No food. Peter had eaten nothing but pride and stale tea. Good.

She rang Bhatti’s shop. No answer. Rang again and got Mrs Bhatti’s son, breathless, shouting over radio music. Yes, their cousin still had the Ford van. No, it might not start. Yes, he would try.

When she came back Peter had buttoned his coat wrong, left over right like a child dressing in the dark.

"Take it off," she said.

"Do not instruct me in dressing."

"Then dress yourself correctly."

He could not. His fingers fumbled at the buttons; he sucked a curse and dropped his left hand to his lap. Noreen rebuttoned him without asking. He sat rigid, as if each touch cost him money.

A knock thudded through the wall first, then at the door. Elsie came in with no coat, just Tom’s old cardigan stretched over her belly and snow in her hair. She held a tin mug with hot water and salt, steam curling into her face.

"Arthur said he’d gone down," she said. Her eyes skimmed Peter, took in the wrist, the sweat, the carefully controlled mouth. "Christ."

"Mind your language," Peter said automatically.

Elsie gave him the mild look she used on men digging holes in the wrong place. "You can dock me a shilling later. Noreen, I’ve got bandage roll and sling pins if casualty’s crowded."

Noreen took the mug. "Thank you. Can you ring clinic for me? Nine-ten slot, Mrs Walsh, antenatal review. Tell them I’m delayed at casualty and I’ll come if we’re out in time."

Elsie glanced at Peter and then back at Noreen. They did not need more words. "I’ll keep trying till someone answers."

Peter said, "My medical business does not concern the neighborhood."

"It concerns your wife," Elsie said. "And she concerns me."

Something hot and dangerous passed across Peter’s face, gone before Arthur reappeared in the doorway with Peter’s hat and Noreen’s boots.

Outside, Bhatti’s cousin got the van started on the third attempt. He was a narrow man with one ear bitten short, maybe by frost years ago, maybe by a machine, and he kept tapping the dashboard twice before each gear as if convincing it to cooperate. He told Noreen his name was Dev and asked no questions once he saw Peter’s arm.

The ride to Calderbank Infirmary took forty minutes for two miles. Dev crawled down untreated stretches while Peter swore at every skid and then apologized to Dev in courtly full sentences when a pothole jolted his wrist. Noreen braced one hand on the seat in front and held Peter’s forearm with the other, feeling each tremor that ran through him. Outside, people walked in the ruts buses had left days earlier, bent into the wind with milk bottles clinking in bags.

At the infirmary doors, Peter straightened and let go of her arm before anyone saw how hard he had leaned.

The casualty clerk had a mustard scarf wrapped twice around her neck and three fountain pens clipped to her cardigan like medals. She took Peter’s details without looking up.

"Name, date of birth, occupation, next of kin."

Peter gave each answer with clipped precision. "Municipal accounts clerk," he added, and the clerk nodded as if that settled the seriousness of his wrist.

"Time of injury?"

"Approximately eight fifteen."

Noreen said, "He slipped on untreated ice outside our house. Landed right-handed."

The clerk finally looked up. "You the wife?"

"Yes."

"Any head knock? Vomiting?"

"No."

"Pregnant, are you?" Her eyes dropped to Noreen’s coat front. "You should sit when you can. We’re short on chairs, mind."

Peter began, "My wife will remain with me," as if preempting a slight.

The clerk pushed a clipboard over. "Sign here, Mr Walsh. Or make a mark if hand won’t do."

He froze. Noreen saw the humiliation land, neat and exact.

"I’ll sign for him," she said.

Peter said, "No." He took the pen in his left hand and wrote with the fury of a man forging his own name. The letters looked like fence posts in thawing mud.

They waited among boys with split chins, an old woman cradling a teapot wrapped in newspaper, and a stevedore with his thumb tied in string. Peter refused to sit until his face went gray, then sat and pretended he had chosen to. Noreen watched the wall clock move toward nine-ten, nine-fifteen, nine-twenty.

At nine-thirty she found the pay phone by the lavatory and fed it her last sixpence. The clinic line rang, rang, rang, then died. She dialed again. A receptionist answered on the fifth try, voice flattened by static.

"Calderbank Maternity, hold please."

Noreen held. Men’s boots squeaked behind her. A child cried as if being peeled.

"Maternity clinic." New voice, brisk. "Mrs Walsh? You were due at nine-ten."

"I know. My husband’s fractured his wrist. We’re at casualty. I’m trying to get across after."

Paper rustled. "You are not seen if you arrive after ten. We have sixteen women booked and two staff absent."

"Please, I only need blood pressure and urine done."

"And edema review, yes." The woman sounded tired, not cruel. "Your notes say swelling increased at last contact. If you develop headache, flashing lights, pain under the ribs, or reduced movement, call immediately. Do not wait."

Noreen pressed her forehead to the cold metal box. "Can you fit me later today?"

"No. Earliest routine slot is Thursday at twelve-forty, if roads hold. I can put a red flag on your file because this is second disruption in three weeks."

Red flag. Peter would hear it as accusation; she heard it as a thin line over a drop.

"Put it on," she said.

"Bring urine sample if possible. Rest your feet elevated in the meantime."

Noreen nearly laughed at that. Rest in whose house.

She hung up and stood still one extra second to keep from crying in a corridor that smelled of carbolic and wet wool.

X-ray showed what she had already known. "Distal radius fracture," said a young doctor with ink on his thumb and a wedding ring too loose for his hand. "Clean break, no displacement after splinting. We’ll plaster from knuckles to elbow. Six weeks, maybe seven. Keep it dry. No lifting."

Peter asked, "Can I return to office duties tomorrow?"

The doctor blinked, then smiled without humor. "Not if you’d like your bones to stay where I’ve put them."

By the time plaster set and discharge papers were signed with Peter’s left-hand fence-post signature again, the clinic had closed and snow had started in fine hard grains.

In the van back home, Peter rested his cast in Noreen’s lap without asking. She kept both palms around it to stop the vibration from the road. Each jolt went through the plaster into her wrists, her shoulders, the small sore place under her breastbone where panic had been living for weeks.

At the corner of Barrow Lane, Elsie stood under a shared umbrella with Mrs Bhatti, waiting. Elsie raised one hand when she saw them. Peter looked away.

Noreen did not.

She held Elsie’s gaze until Dev turned the van and the lane swallowed her from sight.

***

By evening the house had reorganized itself around Peter’s cast as if the plaster carried legal authority.

Noreen set up his chair near the range because cold made him "throb" every five minutes and because he claimed the draught at the table made healing impossible. She moved the mending basket, Arthur’s boots, her own stool. She shifted the medicine bottle to the mantel where Peter could watch it. She lined up spoon, cup, and folded handkerchief on a tray by his left side so he would not ask for each item separately and then blame her for delay.

He asked anyway.

"Water."

"Now tea."

"Not that cup, the chipped one catches at my lip."

"Where is the circular from Accounts? I had it in my briefcase."

"Do not overboil the cabbage."

Arthur came in at six with his hair damp from sleet and a parcel of cod wrapped in yesterday’s news. He set it on the counter and flexed his fingers over the range.

"Queue was out the door," he said. "Woman in front of me paid in coppers she’d warmed in her glove. Thought they’d stick less to her skin."

Noreen smiled despite herself. "Did it work?"

"No. She swore like a docker and gave the fishmonger two poetry lines about his mother."

Peter said without looking up, "Arthur, would you please not encourage profanity at table."

Arthur rubbed water from his eyebrow with his sleeve and gave a tiny bow. "Wouldn’t dream of it."

Noreen fried the cod in the shallow pan with the last spoon of dripping. Peter complained about the smell, then ate all of his portion and half the potatoes she had meant for tomorrow’s breakfast. When he finished he sat back and examined his cast as if it were a document to be audited.

"You will have to write this week’s payment book entries for me," he said.

"I can copy numbers."

"Not copy. Accurately transcribe."

"Same thing if I do it right."

"Language matters, Noreen. Accuracy begins in terms."

He said it calmly, as if he were teaching a child to tie laces. She bit down on the answer that rose up: Accuracy began in not getting drunk enough to crack your own bones. Instead she took the ledger from the sideboard and opened to the right week.

Peter dictated household figures while Arthur washed his plate in the scullery, loud enough that neither of them could pretend not to hear.

"Coal, two sacks on credit, one pound six and four."

"Mm-hm."

"Do not say mm-hm while writing numbers. Repeat them."

She repeated. Her hand cramped halfway through and she flexed it over the paper. The baby rolled low and hard across her pelvis, a movement like a fish turning in a net.

Peter watched her stop. "Do not start that performance. If you needed to sit, you should have paced your effort."

Arthur came back in with a plate towel over his shoulder. "I can take dictation if that helps."

Peter’s smile stayed polite and sharp. "This is domestic accounting, Mr Hale, not a union meeting."

Arthur lifted both hands and turned toward the spare room. As he passed Noreen he set a small folded square of paper by the ledger, hidden under the edge. She waited until Peter asked for his second tablet, then slid it into her apron.

It was from Elsie, three lines in pencil:

`Heard from Bhatti boy: fracture set. I can come by after dark with broth if safe. Tap twice on wall if yes.`

Noreen kept her face blank and fed the note to the range, watching the paper curl black and vanish. Peter noticed the movement.

"What was that?"

"List of washing."

"You burn lists now?"

"Saves clutter."

He held her eyes a second too long. "I have no appetite for neighborhood conferences in my convalescence."

There it was. He had not seen the note, but he smelled movement like a dog scents thunder.

"Elsie asked how you were."

"Mrs Harrow has become unusually invested in this house."

"She heard you hit the step. Everyone heard you hit the step."

Color climbed his neck above the collar. He set his glass down with care that cost him.

"From this evening," he said, "there will be no unsupervised visits between you and Mrs Harrow."

Noreen stared, waiting for the laugh line that did not come.

"You can’t be serious."

"I am entirely serious."

"She is next door."

"Then she is conveniently close for supervised exchanges through proper channels."

"Proper channels." Noreen gave a short, disbelieving laugh. "What are we now, two departments in a ministry?"

"Do not mock me in my own house."

"I live here too."

"You are my wife," he said. "Our household must not become a public corridor. You have shown poor judgment in company lately."

She heard what he did not dare say plain: her laughter with Elsie in the yard, doors closing at odd hours, the way she had started answering him directly after years of smoothing everything over. He did not know the shape of it yet, but he felt the ground move.

Arthur’s door clicked shut down the hall. Not loud, not theatrical, just enough to mark that he had heard and removed himself.

Noreen lowered her voice. "I missed clinic because you broke your wrist. I sat in casualty four hours. I hauled you in and out of that van. I held your cast over every rut in Calderbank and did not drop it once. Do not stand there and tell me who I can speak to."

Peter stood too fast, pain crossing his face before control sealed it.

"I did not ask to fall."

"No, you asked to be treated like a magistrate afterward."

"I asked for order." He breathed once, carefully. "Order is what keeps people alive in this weather. Disorder kills. Improvisation kills. Gossip kills reputations and then livelihoods. I will not have this house run on impulses."

"Your order put me off clinic."

"One appointment is not catastrophe."

"You do not know that."

"I know this: if you wander next door whenever emotion dictates, this house cannot function while I am incapacitated."

Incapacitated. He used the word like a court filing.

She looked at his cast, then at his intact left hand, his intact legs, the mouth still perfectly capable of issuing decrees.

"You’re not incapacitated," she said. "You’re inconvenienced."

His voice dropped to a softness that frightened her more than the shouting would have. "Do not try me tonight."

Silence sat between them, thick as wet wool.

She thought of tapping twice on the wall and hearing Elsie tap back. She thought of not doing it and feeling that omission in her chest all night like swallowed ice.

Peter spoke again, mild now. "Tomorrow you will ring the clinic in my hearing and request another appointment. You will explain the delay respectfully. Then you will remain here unless I accompany you. Is that understood?"

Noreen could have said no. Could have thrown the ledger at him. Could have gone next door in her apron and slippers and dared him to stop her in front of Arthur and the whole lane.

She pictured the lane. Mrs Bhatti’s sharp kind eyes. Mrs Doyle’s front-curtain twitch. Peter, cast white as a flag, telling them all his wife had become unstable in pregnancy. In this year, on this street, men won that story first.

"Understood," she said.

He sat again, relieved enough to show it. "Good. Bring the nighttime tablets."

She brought them. Later she made up Arthur’s camp bed in the spare room because Peter said he might call in the night if his arm cramped and "a witness in residence" was sensible in case she needed help lifting him.

Arthur accepted the extra blanket and looked at her for a long moment. "You all right, Mrs Walsh?"

"No," she said before she could smooth it. Then, because truth felt dangerous in an ordinary kitchen, she added, "I’m tired."

Arthur nodded as if she had handed him a tool he knew how to use. "If you need me, knock twice on the wall. I sleep light."

The same code as Elsie’s note. Noreen almost smiled.

When she climbed into bed Peter was already on his back, cast propped on pillows like an honored guest. He did not touch her. Near midnight he woke her twice for water and once because the sling strap scratched his neck. At two he asked for the chamber pot though he could have managed to the lavatory. At three he slept, finally, mouth open, breath sour with medicine.

Noreen lay awake and listened to the shared wall.

No taps came.

***

In the morning Peter insisted on being shaved before she went out.

"I cannot present myself at the surgery with stubble," he said.

"You’re not presenting anywhere. You’re on a chair by the fire for six weeks."

"Decency is not contingent on mobility."

She warmed water, set a basin, and shaved him with the old safety razor while he lectured her on blade angle. Her own hands were numb from rinsing in cold water. By the time she tied his scarf and settled his sling, the first bus to town had already gone, if it had run at all.

"I will accompany you to the clinic office," he said.

"No you won’t. You can barely get your boot on."

"Then Mr Hale can attend."

Arthur, buttoning his coat in the doorway, said, "I’m on emergency call till dusk. If I miss rotation, half the north feeder stays dark."

Peter gave a thin nod that meant offense logged for later. "Very well. Do not linger, Noreen."

"I won’t."

He held out his left hand, palm up, not to touch her but to receive. "Fare money. I will allocate."

She stared. "Allocate."

"You emptied your purse at casualty. I am managing this week’s cash flow until we understand the wage impact."

She wanted to laugh and spit in equal measure. Instead she watched him count coins one by one from the tobacco tin, each piece clicking table to table: bus there, bus back, two pence margin. No extra for tea, no extra for a call.

"If buses are off?" she asked.

"Then walk directly there and directly back."

"On this ice."

"You are not made of sugar."

Neither was he, and still he had cracked.

She tucked the coins into her glove and left before she said it.

The lane looked beaten, not cleared. Footpaths were rutted with frozen boot prints and cart wheels. At the corner she saw Elsie at her gate shaking a rug, breath smoking in little bursts. Elsie glanced up, saw Noreen, and touched two fingers to her own wrist in a question: him?

Noreen gave a short nod and a tiny twist of her hand to mime plaster. Elsie’s mouth tightened. She took one step forward, then checked herself when she saw Peter’s silhouette at the Walsh window.

Noreen walked on.

Halfway to town she turned back once. Elsie was still at the gate, rug over the rail, watching until Noreen rounded the bakery and disappeared.

The clinic office had moved winter triage to a side entrance because the main steps were iced solid. Women queued in coats over nightdresses, flasks tucked under arms, children clinging to hems. A hand-lettered sign on the door read: `IF YOU HAVE HEADACHE + FLASHING LIGHTS + SWELLING, TELL DESK IMMEDIATELY.`

Noreen stood twenty-three minutes before reaching the hatch. The receptionist today was a young woman with bitten nails painted red and a tiny silver thimble hanging from her necklace. She kept touching it between calls as if for luck.

"Name?"

"Noreen Walsh. Missed yesterday. Casualty with husband."

The woman flipped through cards, found hers, and frowned. "You were due for pressure, urine, and edema review. Mrs Keane marked urgent follow-up after prior bleed episode."

"I know. I need another slot."

"Earliest is Thursday twelve-forty, but that’s contingency clinic. If weather closes roads, it cancels first."

Thursday. Four days if the buses ran, six if they did not. She pictured her feet by Thursday, skin stretched over fluid till her shoes cut lines she could not press out.

"Is there anything sooner?"

"Not booked." The receptionist lowered her voice. "I can put you on the cancellation board. Means if someone doesn’t arrive, you must come within an hour when called."

Noreen almost said yes, then remembered the house telephone that worked when it wanted, Peter by the range answering before she could, Peter deciding which messages were relevant to household order.

"Can you ring Bhatti’s shop if our line fails?"

"We can try one alternate number."

Noreen gave it.

The receptionist wrote in red pencil: `ALT CONTACT: BHATTI STORES`. Then she looked up, expression changing from clerical to human. "Mrs Walsh, I have to read this exactly. If you get bad headache, lights in your vision, pain under right ribs, sudden sickness, or the baby goes quiet, do not wait for Thursday. You come straight to triage or send for ambulance and say antenatal red flag. Say red flag. Understand?"

Noreen nodded.

"Say it back so I know."

"Antenatal red flag. Straight to triage, no waiting."

"Good." The woman slid her a card with the time written in block letters. "Keep your feet up as much as possible."

Noreen tucked the card inside her glove beside Peter’s counted coins.

As she turned from the hatch, she nearly walked into Mrs Keane herself, coming through the side door with snow on her hat brim and that heavy canvas bag dragging her shoulder down. Keane recognized her instantly.

"You missed yesterday."

"Peter broke his wrist."

Keane’s eyes went to Noreen’s face, then to her hands, then down to her boots where edema had made the laces bow outward. "How bad’s the swelling this morning?"

"Worse by night."

"Headaches?"

"A bit."

"How bad a bit?"

"Like someone tapping a nail at the back of my eye. Comes and goes."

Keane swore softly. "That’s not a bit. You needed seeing yesterday."

"I know."

Keane shifted the bag and stepped closer so other women could not hear. "Listen to me. If your husband blocks transport again, you send word through Bhatti or Hale or the police if you have to. I do not care who gets offended. If those symptoms spike, this turns from inconvenience to seizure risk fast. You understand that word? Fast."

Noreen felt heat flush up her neck despite the cold hallway. "He didn’t block. He broke."

Keane held her gaze. "And now he is home, and you are still the one carrying everyone. Do not confuse injury with innocence."

Noreen looked away first. Through the side window she could see two orderlies chipping ice from the ramp with shovels, one singing under his breath in a voice gone hoarse from smoke.

"Thursday," she said. "They gave me Thursday."

"I’ll flag your notes again." Keane touched Noreen’s sleeve once, quick and dry. "If you get those signs, you come in. No permission slips from husbands."

Noreen nodded, because speech had thickened in her throat.

She spent her last two pence on a call from the corridor phone, not to home but to Bhatti’s, asking the boy to pass a message to Elsie: `Thursday 12:40. Red flag. Don’t come over while he’s watching.` The boy repeated it wrong twice, then right.

By the time she reached Barrow Lane her calves burned from walking the final stretch where buses had stopped. Peter was at the table with his cast propped on the account ledger, reading one-handed and underlining with angry strokes from his left hand.

"You took one hour and forty-two minutes," he said without greeting.

"Queue was out the door."

"Appointment?"

She took off her gloves slowly. "Thursday at twelve-forty."

"That is acceptable."

"If weather holds," she said.

"Weather will hold sufficiently."

She almost admired his confidence in the sky taking instruction.

He reached out. "Give me the card."

She hesitated, then gave him the card. He read it, turned it over, and set it under the salt cellar where he put bills due and things he wanted visible.

"You will rest now," he said. "After you prepare lunch."

Noreen stood by the range, one hand on the mantel to steady herself while heat climbed her shins and pain thudded behind her right eye in slow, deliberate taps.

Through the wall came the faint clatter of Elsie’s pans, the rhythm she used when she was worried and trying not to show it. Three fast, one slow, then silence.

Noreen listened to that private code while she peeled potatoes for Peter with fingers gone clumsy from cold and swelling.

Thursday, she told herself.

Four days if roads held.

Longer if they did not.
