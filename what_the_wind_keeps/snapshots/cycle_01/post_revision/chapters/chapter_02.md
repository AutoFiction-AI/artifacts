# Chapter 2

By morning the dust had settled into the house the way a bad remark settled into a church social: everywhere, impossible to answer back, finer than any broom deserved.

Thea woke in her narrow room at the Vale place with grit in the hinge of her jaw and a line of it worked into the seam of the pillowcase. She shook out her stockings over the washstand and watched a gray veil drift down the white enamel basin. Her Sunday dress hung from a nail with a dun cast to it, as if the storm had tried it on in the night. When she put it over her head, the collar rasped against her neck.

In the kitchen Cora already had the stove going. She stood in her work dress with the sleeves rolled once, not twice, which meant she had dressed in the dark and not looked down. The kettle lid jittered. A skillet sat cooling on the back of the range, and the bacon grease in it had turned to a pale, ugly paste under the dust. Cora was scraping the top layer off with the side of a spoon, saving what was underneath.

The front-room window had been wiped in one hard oval, no bigger than a dinner plate. The rest of the glass still wore the storm.

Thea paused with one hand on the chair back. She had not asked, in the night, what Cora had seen there. Cora had come to bed late, or not at all; Thea had heard only floorboards and the pump handle outside and then nothing.

"You don't have to go in if the roof came loose," Cora said. Her voice was flat from tiredness, but not unkind. "No use having the schoolhouse fall on you for form's sake."

"If I don't open it, half the town will decide it's closed forever before noon."

"Half the town has decided worse than that."

Thea might have smiled on another morning. She set her satchel on the table and took the rag Cora had left beside the bread board. The bread itself was wrapped in a flour sack, one heel and three uneven slices from yesterday. Cora looked at the loaf the way she looked at kerosene, needles, lamp wicks, every other thing in the house that could run out.

"Did Dell sleep?" Thea asked.

"Some."

"Did you?"

Cora kept scraping. "I laid down."

That was as much as Thea was going to get. Cora's public voice had come into the kitchen before the light had. Every answer cost exactly the least it could.

Dell came in from the yard with the collar of his shirt up around his mouth and his hair standing every direction at once. He was seventeen and thin enough lately that his wrists looked borrowed from a younger boy. He carried the galvanized bucket they used under the pump, and the water inside had a skin of dust moving on it like drowned pollen.

"Pump tastes like the road," he said, setting it down.

"Strain it again," Cora said.

"That'll only make it taste like cloth."

"Then you'll have had both courses."

He snorted, which was close enough to laughter to count. He glanced at Thea, then at the clean oval on the window, and just as quickly away. Whatever had happened in the front room after he got home from the storm, nobody meant to name it at breakfast.

Cora sliced the bread thinner than hunger liked and fried eggs in the grease she had managed to rescue. The yolks filmed over gray at the edges before they reached the plates. Thea ate because refusing would have been its own kind of rudeness. Dust clicked between her teeth when she chewed. Across from her, Dell salted everything before tasting it, an old habit from years when he still believed more salt could make a poor supper feel deliberate.

"If any of them come in coughing blood, send them home," Cora said.

"I'm not likely to mistake that for mischief."

"You'd be surprised what children will do to get out of sums."

It was the nearest thing to a joke Cora had offered. Thea looked up too quickly. Cora had not smiled; she was using the knife to push crumbs together for the hens. But the line had been meant as company, and because it had been meant that way it went straight through Thea.

She wanted to ask what was wrong beyond the obvious. She wanted to ask whether the shut-down look in Cora's face belonged to fear, grief, or the thing on the window. She wanted to cross the kitchen and touch the raw place at the base of Cora's thumb where the skin always split in spring. Dell sat there with his elbows on the oilcloth, older than some men and younger than he knew. The house was too small for want.

"If I get there and it's a wall of dirt and broken desks," Thea said, reaching for her satchel, "I'll come back by dinner."

"Bring the key back if the lock's torn off," Cora said.

"That what worries you? The key?"

Cora gave her a look then, quick and tired and alive under all the tiredness. "No. But I can do something about a missing key."

Thea took that with her out the door.

The road into town had lost its edges. Fence lines came and vanished in drifts banked ankle-high against the posts. At the Olsen place the front step had disappeared entirely, and the milk can by the gate wore a soft cone of dirt like a winter cap. The wheat west of the road had not been cut, only laid over and powdered until each row looked rubbed out. A dead jackrabbit lay against the culvert with its ears full of silt.

Thea walked with her handkerchief tied over her nose until her own breath dampened it. Even through the cloth the air tasted baked and old, as if the storm had dug up some cellar of the earth and thrown it in people's faces.

Morrow itself was quieter than a Monday had any right to be. Usually by this hour she heard at least a wagon axle screaming for grease, the clang from the blacksmith shed, Mrs. Fenske at the post office window telling somebody to stop leaning on the ledge as if federal property were a porch rail. This morning sound carried in scraps. Hester's bakery door stood open, letting out yeast and scorched coffee. Across the street a man she did not know anymore because dust had made everybody the same color was sweeping the boardwalk only to move dirt from one set of cracks to another. Farmers State Bank had a broom propped outside its door and no customers visible through the glass.

The schoolhouse sat where it always had, plain and square and under-loved. The west side had taken the worst of the storm. Dust had pushed in around the windowsills and under the door, then set up residence. When Thea opened the door, one drift inside had curled itself against the far wall like something sleeping.

She stood a minute on the threshold, not praying, exactly, but postponing the labor.

Then she tied her sleeves back and began.

She propped the door open with a geography primer missing its cover and took the broom from behind the stove. The first push raised a cloud so thick she had to turn her head and cough into her shoulder. The room smelled of chalk, old wool, damp plaster, and the sweet mineral smell that followed every bad dusting of the lungs. The map of the United States above the blackboard had gone the color of weak gravy. A tin cup lay on its side under the front row, half full of dirt. Someone's reader had swollen from the damp in the storm and dried crooked.

More desks were empty than filled before she even took attendance. That was what struck her hardest, more than the mess, more than the broken latch on the north window, more than the dead fly plastered into the inkwell tray. The room had already been going thin before Sunday. Now the storm had shoved its thumb into those absences and widened them for everyone to see.

She set the attendance register on her desk and opened it to the week's page. The names had been written in ink that was beginning to brown with age. Some of the children had sat under her eye for four years. Some had been born into this room before she came, growing through the same arithmetic tables and copybook sentences and the same November colds that made every lesson sound nasal. She had scolded them, admired them, sent notes home about them, and lately watched them vanish.

She made two columns on a scrap of paper: here, and gone.

By the time the first child came in, the second column had grown longer than the first.

Ruthie Havel arrived carrying her little brother's tin lunch pail because, as she once told Thea with complete seriousness, its latch had better manners than hers. She had tied one of her braids with a strip torn from a seed sack printed in blue letters, and there was a yellow butterscotch drop bulging her left cheek.

"Morning, Miss Bell," she said around the candy. "Ma says if the stove's no good I oughtn't stay."

"We'll see if it can be reasoned with."

"It never could before."

Vernon Pike came next, long in the knees, one suspender slipping as usual and a packet of beet seed still in his shirt pocket from whatever job he'd done before breakfast. He smelled faintly of axle grease and slept-in overalls.

"Thought for a minute the schoolhouse blew clear to Kansas," he said.

"Kansas would send it back."

"Not if they looked inside."

That got a brief grin out of Ruthie. More children followed in ones and twos: Lila Kopecek with one man's wool sock rolled below each knee because her mother did not waste cloth on matching pairs, Oran Bellamy buttoned crooked and blinking behind spectacles forever sliding down his nose, and little June Worley, who spoke only after she had rubbed two fingers over the rabbit's foot she kept in her coat pocket for luck. There were seven by the time the morning settled, seven children spread through a room built to hold twice that without crowding.

The desks in between stood open-mouthed. One still held a marble in the corner groove. Another had a pencil stub wrapped in chewing-gum foil. At the back sat the Jensen girls' slate, left clean and ready as if their mother meant to send them in late. The Jensens had gone west on Saturday. Thea knew because Mrs. Jensen had come for transfer papers with her jaw set and a baby's sock pinned to her coat to keep from losing it.

Thea did not call every absent name. She could not bear the echo of her own voice landing in so much vacant wood. She marked the register instead, quiet and exact, while the children beat dust from erasers outside and trailed it back in on their hems.

"We're not pretending this is an ordinary day," she said once they were seated. "If any of you hoped for normal copywork, you have my condolences."

Vernon raised his hand halfway. "Does that mean no sums?"

"No. It means you'll do sums in a dirty room, like pioneers."

"Pioneers didn't have to add fractions," he said.

"That's why so many of them died unhappy."

This time even Oran laughed, though laughing made him cough. Thea let the moment stay. The room needed more than order; it needed a reason to keep being a room where children belonged. She had them wipe their desk tops with damp rags, sort the spoiled primers from the usable ones, and make a list of what the storm had ruined so she could send it to a school board that was itself half packed for departure. Ruthie discovered two beans in the stove scuttle and held them up like treasure. Lila shook dust from the globe and said Nebraska had disappeared anyway, which Vernon informed her was an improvement.

There was more honesty in those small jokes than in most town meetings.

By noon the stove had finally drawn properly and the room had warmed enough to loosen the smell out of wet wool. The children ate at their desks. June Worley picked every raisin out of her bread pudding and lined them on the edge of the inkwell tray before eating them one by one, serious as communion. Vernon traded half an onion sandwich for Ruthie's candy and then declared himself cheated by arithmetic and by women in equal measure, which made Ruthie call him a calf and Lila say that calves had better table manners. Thea sat at her desk with a cup of coffee gone to varnish and watched them be children in spite of the dust. It moved her more than it should have.

During the meal she made the second list: families needing transfer papers, families with mail held at the post office, families simply missing from town since before the storm. She knew too much about people leaving. She knew who had cousins in Greeley, who had an uncle outside Fresno, who had sold harness for cash and who had only said they were going, which was a different thing. The county wanted names and destinations because counties always wanted paper to prove they had noticed suffering in the proper column.

Thea wrote them down because if she did not, no one else would do it cleanly.

When the last copybook had been stacked and the children let out early, they scattered home in a pale wind that lifted the road without becoming weather. Ruthie turned at the gate and called, "If it blows again, I'm sleeping under the desk. Tell Ma."

"I doubt your mother will accept school furniture as kin."

"Then don't tell her till after."

Vernon promised to bring a better stove poker in the morning because the current one "had no morals." After they were gone the room grew larger and sadder at once. Their voices had filled what the missing ones had left. Without them the schoolhouse went back to being an emptied crate.

Thea set the chairs straight and took a damp rag to the inside of the west windows so tomorrow's light might have some chance of entering on purpose. She had nearly finished the first pane when she leaned close to blow grit out of the corner where the putty had cracked.

The glass filmed white beneath her breath.

Then the room on the other side of it was not this afternoon's room.

It was colder. Coats stayed on. The stove in the corner was black and dead. Children's breath moved in pale bursts. The desks were fuller. She knew at once it was January, or late February, one of those thin bright days when the wind took the schoolyard and the children watched it through the windows because there was no sense sending them out.

She heard a voice, small and dutiful, reading from the roster not as attendance but as inventory.

"Soderlund."

Another voice answered, "Gone."

"Barr."

"Gone."

"Meyer."

"Colorado."

Then Ruthie, younger by a season and furious at having to take part in it, said, "Pretty soon you'll have more air than pupils, Miss Bell."

A few children laughed because children laughed when they were frightened and because laughter made a thing feel chosen. In the memory Thea saw her own hand resting flat on the desk, weddingless and chalk-smudged, holding the page steady while the names went on. She remembered the shame of it at once. She had told herself she was teaching them geography, record-keeping, the practical business of the world as it was. In truth she had made them speak their losses aloud so she could keep the room orderly for another hour.

The window cleared.

Thea stepped back hard enough to hit the sill with her hip. The rag dropped into the drift on the floor. For a moment she stood with one hand braced against the frame, hearing the ghost of those voices in the boards. The memory had not accused her exactly. It had done worse. It had returned her own little decency in its full shape and shown the mean edge on it.

Outside, somebody called to a team in the road. The ordinary sound came thin through the glass.

Thea picked up the rag and finished the pane because she did not know what else to do.

At the post office Mrs. Fenske had set the mail in three uneven stacks on the counter and was wearing men's overshoes over house slippers, the rubber buckles flapping every time she turned. She kept a blue pencil tucked over one ear and licked her thumb to separate envelopes in open defiance of the dust.

"You're late for your fortune," she said when Thea came in.

"I wasn't aware I had one."

"Long envelope from Lincoln says otherwise."

Mrs. Fenske handed it across with the faint air of someone giving out bad news and hoping to be contradicted. The paper was cream, better stock than anyone in Morrow used for common correspondence. The return address sat in the upper corner neat and city-sure. Lincoln. Public Library. The words were plain enough, but they tightened something low in Thea's body all the same.

"Came Saturday," Mrs. Fenske said. "Couldn't get the bag through yesterday after noon, for obvious reasons."

"Thank you."

"Well, open it. I've had to look at it all morning."

Mrs. Fenske's curiosity was not malicious. It was simply the town's natural weather. Thea slid a finger under the flap anyway, because refusing would have been louder than reading. Inside was a single typed sheet and a smaller card. She read the first lines standing at the counter while Mrs. Fenske pretended to sort circulars she had already sorted.

The library in Lincoln wished to interview Miss Theodora Bell for an assistant position in circulation and children's work. If she remained interested, could she present herself in the first week of June? References requested. Kindly reply by return post.

The card enclosed named a day and an hour.

For a foolish instant the room tipped around one clean thought: it was real.

The leaving people talked about at kitchen tables had always been smoke-talk, California rising off a mean supper like weather or scripture, some cousin's promise going yellow before it ever turned solid. This was a room in a city, shelves, work indoors, paper that stayed where you set it, and a salary from an institution that expected to exist next month. She had written in February, in a fit of prudence so cold it had embarrassed her even as she sealed the envelope. It had been one application, and that was all. She had told herself it was only common sense to ask. Now common sense had written back.

"Well?" Mrs. Fenske said.

Thea folded the letter once before answering. "An interview."

"For what?"

"Library work."

Mrs. Fenske gave a short impressed click with her tongue. "That sounds cleaner than children."

"Children are cleaner on principle if not in fact."

"You going?"

Thea slid the paper back into its envelope. "I don't know."

That, at least, was true enough to bear saying.

Mrs. Fenske leaned both palms on the counter. "If you ask me, anybody offered a chair in Lincoln ought to take it before the chair learns better."

"Did I ask you?"

"No, and I resent the neglect." The older woman softened. "Write back quick if you're of a mind. Mail's slower every week. Half the roads are more ditch than road now."

Thea nodded and tucked the envelope into her satchel between the attendance register and a stack of arithmetic slates. Even there she could feel its presence like a warm thing.

She did not go straight home. She stood for a minute under the post office awning looking down Main Street, where town life was being performed in gestures too tired for conviction. Hester was out front of the bakery knocking dust from a rug with more determination than strength, the front pane behind her gone milky enough that the cake case showed only as a pale block. A wagon stood outside the hardware store with its bed already loaded: chair frames, two trunks, a birdcage without a bird. On the steps of the bank a man in overalls took off his hat and beat it against his knee until a little weather made itself visible. Across from him the church bulletin board still carried Sunday's text under a film of dirt. The words had gone faint enough that "Remember" was the only one you could read from the street.

Thea thought of the library card in her satchel and felt disloyal all the way through. She did not feel guilty. Guilt was for acts already chosen. This feeling was worse. It was the burden of possibility.

At the Vale place the afternoon had flattened into a colorless gold. Dell was on the back step with a carburetor spread over newspaper, his fingers black to the knuckles and his tongue caught at the corner of his mouth in concentration he would have denied if accused of it. He had one screw lined up by size against the grain of the wood and a fifth missing, which meant he would be savage with the machine by supper.

"Did school survive?" he asked without looking up.

"In a legal sense."

"That's about the best one can say for most things."

Inside, Cora had the sleeves rolled properly now and was standing over the sink with her forearms wet, rinsing dust from potatoes no bigger than plums. A pan of onion had already hit the hot grease, and the smell of it filled the kitchen with something that could almost have passed for prosperity if you did not look too closely.

"How many?" she asked.

"Seven."

Cora nodded once, taking the count as if it were grain. "More than I expected."

"Less than last week."

"Everything's less than last week."

Thea set her satchel on the chair by the door. The envelope inside seemed suddenly loud. Cora went on slicing potatoes into the pan, each piece thin enough to persuade the eye there were plenty.

"Ruthie Havel informed me the stove never could be reasoned with," Thea said.

"She's right."

"Vernon Pike says the poker has no morals."

That pulled the corner of Cora's mouth. "He's inherited his father's gift for blaming tools."

Dell came in then with the missing screw triumphant between finger and thumb. "Found the bastard."

"Mind your mouth," Cora said automatically.

"Sorry."

"You aren't."

"No, ma'am."

Thea laughed before she could stop herself. Dell looked startled into laughing too, which made him instantly younger, almost tender-faced. For a few breaths the room stopped arranging itself around fear. There was only the pan hissing, the wind moving at the corners of the house, and three people glad of a joke because the joke had not asked anything from them.

Supper was fried potatoes with onion, eggs again, and the last of Hester's rye bread cut on the bias to make the slices look generous. Cora had stewed a handful of dried apples until they turned back into something like fruit. Dust had worked its way into the salt cellar and made it clump. Nobody mentioned it. You could either talk about dust all day or live in Nebraska; there was not time for both.

Dell ate standing at the counter for the first few minutes, as if sitting down might let tiredness catch him in the shoulders. He wanted badly not to look like a child who had been frightened yesterday. Thea knew the effort when she saw it. She had been teaching boys long enough to recognize the particular stiffness of male pride before it had earned the word male.

"June Worley still keeps that rabbit's foot in her pocket," Thea said.

"That useless old thing?" Dell dropped into his chair at last. "She had that in fifth reader."

"She says it worked. She's still alive."

"So is the rabbit?"

"Only in theory."

Cora set the apple dish down. "If you're going to torment schoolchildren at table, at least pass the bread first."

Dell obeyed with a grin that did not last but counted while it did. He asked after the roof. Thea told him the west window latch had gone and two primers were ruined beyond redemption. He said he could mend the latch tomorrow if Cora didn't need him in the south field. Cora said the south field could wait until morning. There was no money in broken glass, but there was less in leaving it broken.

That was how care went in the house: sidelong, practical, disguised as scheduling.

Thea nearly brought out the letter when Dell said, "School'll be down to three by planting if folks keep running."

Cora's fork stopped against the plate, not enough to clang but enough for Thea to hear the metal hesitate.

"Eat," Cora said.

"I'm eating."

"Then do more of that and less prophecy."

Dell muttered something into his bread that might have been apology and might have been argument starved before speech. The quiet after it was not hostile. It was only full. Thea felt the envelope in her satchel like a live coal.

If she told them now, Dell would go still and watch Cora. Cora would say the right thing first because she always did when anyone could hear. She would say it was a good opportunity, better work, that Thea ought to go if it was what she wanted. Then, later, perhaps in the dark, the truth would come with less grammar and more damage. Or worse, it would not come. Cora would take the news into herself and hold it there like every other hard thing until her body paid for it.

Thea kept her hand off the satchel. She could not bear either version while Cora was measuring out dried apples with the back of a spoon.

After supper Dell took the carburetor pieces to the shed and Cora heated two kettles of water on the stove. Dust in the hair could saw at the scalp if you let it go overnight. By the time the water was warm, the house had gone dim enough for the lamp on the table to matter.

"Sit," Cora said, setting the washbasin down on the chair by the window.

Thea did. Cora stood behind her with the old horn comb, the good one with only one tooth missing. The first pull through Thea's hair caught hard near the nape.

"Sorry," Cora said.

"It isn't your fault the weather's indecent."

"Plenty of things are the weather's fault."

Thea closed her eyes when the comb went through again. Cora had strong hands, not gentle by nature, but careful when care had somewhere to land. Damp fingers divided strands, found knots, worried them loose. Dust lifted from Thea's scalp in a smell like dry cloth and skin. Now and then Cora wiped the comb on the edge of the basin, leaving gray streaks in the water.

"You should've let me do yours first," Thea said.

"Mine can wait."

"Everything of yours can wait. That's the trouble."

For a moment Cora said nothing. Then the comb paused and moved again. "Don't start mending me at dusk, Thea. I haven't got the strength for it."

There it was, the shut door said plain at last. Refusal dropped between them like a bar laid across wood. Cora could talk about stove latches and school windows and the exact degree of rot in a fence post. She could not, this evening, go near whatever had cracked open in the front room after the storm.

"I wasn't trying to mend you," Thea said quietly.

"No." Cora drew the comb through to the ends. "I know."

The lamp made their shadows long on the wall. Through the open doorway Thea could hear Dell in the shed swearing softly at the machine, then laughing once to himself in disbelief at whatever he had done. A hen shuffled under the porch. The whole farm sounded abraded, tired to the grain.

When Cora finished, Thea touched the cleaner heaviness of her own hair and stood. "Your turn."

"There's no use."

"Sit down."

Cora looked as if she might refuse on principle. Then she did the sensible thing and yielded to exhaustion. She sat on the chair facing the table, her back to Thea, and loosened the blunt pins at her nape. Her hair came down ash-blond and straighter than Thea's, with dust lodged close to the scalp where sweat had held it. Kitchen-scissor ends brushed the collar of her dress. Thea saw the bend in her neck, the redness where the sun took her every year before gloves and hats could keep up, the small white scar under one ear from some farm accident Cora had once mentioned and never explained.

The first touch made Cora breathe in, not theatrically and not like surprise, but with the body's blunt admission that it had noticed.

Thea worked more slowly than Cora had, separating the strands with wet fingers before she used the comb. Dust came away in thin gray crescents. Cora's shoulders had been set hard all evening; under Thea's hands they lowered by degrees. The room smelled of lamp oil, onion still in the pan, and the faint clean soap Cora rationed like medicine.

"Ruthie said she'd sleep under the desk next storm if necessary," Thea murmured.

"Might be safer than here."

"Don't say that."

"Why not? It could be true."

Thea bent closer, partly for the work and partly because Dell's shed noises had gone quiet. "Because you don't get to hand me truth only when it cuts."

Cora's head turned a little, not enough to look back. "All right. The window showed Ezra in August's office with a pen in his hand. He signed."

Thea stopped with the comb halfway through Cora's hair.

Cora went on before pity could get in and spoil the truth. "He bent over the desk like he was sick, but he signed. Then he came home and swore on me and Dell that he hadn't. I watched his hand do it in my own front room this morning. That's what I've got."

"You don't know what August put in front of him."

"No. I don't." Cora's voice roughened. "I only know the lie was his mouth and the writing was his hand, and now both of them are in my house."

Thea set the comb on the table. She laid both hands on Cora's shoulders and felt the hard brace of muscle under the dress. "Cora."

Cora caught her wrist before Thea could bend any lower. "Don't kiss me now. It'll feel like mercy, and I'll hate us both by morning."

Thea went still. Cora's fingers were tight and cold despite the hot room.

"I wasn't offering mercy," Thea said.

"No." Cora let go of her wrist and faced the table again. "That's what scares me."

Thea picked up the comb and finished the braid in silence because there was nothing in her throat that could cross that distance cleanly.

When Dell came in smelling of machine oil and cold, Cora had her hair braided again and Thea was rinsing the comb in the basin. He looked from one to the other and then to the murky water.

"You got half the county out of there," he said.

"Only the half that settled on your mother," Thea said.

"Then there's plenty left for me."

"You can scrub your own head," Cora told him.

"Cruel woman."

"Hungry boy."

He took the heel of bread from the board and went out again before either of them could assign him another task. The back door slapped once, then settled.

Thea carried the basin to the porch to throw out the water. It hit the yard in a dark fan, turning dust briefly to mud before the earth took it. In the west the sky had the exhausted yellow of something bruised. Nothing in it suggested rain.

When she came back in, Cora was at the table with both palms flat beside the lamp, looking not at the flame but past it. The shut-down look had returned, though softer now from weariness and the human relief of being touched. Thea wanted her in all the ways that made life difficult: in bed, yes, but also in daylight, at any table she might ever sit at again, in any room that did not require keeping watch on the windows.

"You ought to sleep," Cora said without looking up.

"So ought you."

"I might, if everybody stops advising me."

Thea moved close enough that the hem of her dress brushed Cora's knee. That alone felt dangerous in a house this small, and because it felt dangerous it also felt true. Cora lifted one hand from the table and caught Thea's wrist for only a second. Her palm was rough, still damp from the steam.

"Thank you," Cora said.

It was for the hair, for supper, for not asking, for being there after yesterday. The phrase had to carry all of it because they did not have the room for particulars.

"Of course," Thea said, and hated herself a little because the words were honest and incomplete.

In her room she lit the smaller lamp and took the envelope out of the satchel. Dust had already found the windowsill again. It would find everything again. She unfolded the letter and read it slower this time, making herself look at the typed date on the card, the hour, the request for references, the certainty of a place that wrote as if walls and budgets and tomorrow were settled facts.

If she answered yes, there would be train times to learn, a dress to mend properly, fare to assemble, and a reason to give that would satisfy the town and not betray the house she slept in. If she answered no, she would do it knowing she had refused a door while it was still open.

Down the hall Dell's bed creaked once. In the kitchen Cora moved about with the last small sounds of the day, banking the stove, setting the bread box straight, making sure morning had at least been invited to arrive.

Thea took out a sheet of school stationery, turned it to the clean side, and dipped her pen. The nib scratched in the small room. Dear Madam. Yes, she would present herself in the first week of June. Yes, she was still interested. By the time she had signed her name, her fingertips were black with ink and shaking hard enough to blur the last stroke. She blotted the page with the corner of an old circular, folded it, and slid it into an envelope with a three-cent stamp she had been saving for county forms.

The reply looked too slight for what it had done.

She carried it back to the kitchen before she could lose her nerve. Cora had finished with the stove and was wiping the table where no crumbs remained.

"I need you to wake me before first light," Thea said. "I've got to go into town before school."

Cora's eyes dropped to the envelope in her hand and came back up. "School business?"

The lie came fast enough to burn. "Partly."

Cora stood with the rag bunched in one fist. Whatever she heard wrong in Thea's voice, she did not press it. "All right. I'll wake you."

That was not trust. It was something meaner to receive and kinder to give: room to keep a thing until morning.

Thea went back down the hall with the envelope in her hand. She set it under the lamp, where she would have to see it at dawn, and undressed in the dark so the paper would stay the brightest thing in the room.

For the first time since she had come to Morrow, the room did not feel borrowed. It felt like a room she had already begun to leave.
