# Chapter 15

By the time August reached Saint's Basin, the moon was still snagged behind a low wool of dust and cloud, and the lantern in his hand had smoked its own glass yellow. He had left the motorcar on the section road because the field track was not a road anymore but two chopped ruts and a soft shoulder where the diggers' wagon had cut in after the last little rain. Each step off the road tugged at his shoes. Mud caught him by the heel and then let go with a wet sucking sound that traveled too far in the open dark.

Knapp's note kept crackling in his coat pocket whenever he bent. Final papers tomorrow. Need full auction schedule, title corrections, deficiency estimates. The words had been typed hard enough to emboss the back of the sheet. August could feel their ridges through the wool as if the man had reached ahead of himself and put fingers into his chest.

The basin lay quartered open where Hester Blum and her little congregation had cut it. Even in the dark he could make out the spoil ridge, the slumped edge where the wall had given way on Dell Vale, the blacker stripe of damp earth showing through powder-gray crust. Someone had left a broken shovel handle propped against a stake. A twist of bandage, dirty now and stiff as paper, had snagged on a clod near the trench lip and fluttered every time the wind found it. That scrap bothered him more than the hole did. A boy's broken arm could be entered in a report as injury at unauthorized excavation. A strip of linen fouled with clay made the thing vulgar.

He set the lantern down and listened.

The fields south of the Vale place whispered the way dry wheat always did at night, a sound like hands worrying cheap cloth. Somewhere farther off a sheet of tin knocked once, then not again. The seep itself made almost no noise. That was what shamed him most. All that argument, all those men, all the papers and years, and the water answered with a patient trickle no louder than somebody wetting a thumb to turn a page.

He had told himself twice on the walk out that he came only to see what sort of nuisance the hole had become. He had told himself the bank held the note, the county watched the site, and a man in his position had a duty to inspect anything likely to become liability. By the time he stood over the black damp, the wording had lost its usefulness. He knew why he was there. He had spent the evening in his rooms above the bank with the lamp turned down and could not keep his thoughts inside their proper channels. Knapp's note had done what Cora's shouting, Hester's coughing accusations, and the whole damned basin business had not done. It had shown him a future in which all his clean files amounted to a better-labeled corpse.

He crouched with more effort than he cared to feel. His knees gave a complaint. Cold went through the cloth at once. The lantern glazed the seep in a narrow oval. Black water, a film of clay, one pale reflection of flame shivering every time he shifted his weight. He remembered this place when it had not been called a place at all, only the wet hollow south of the Vale acreage, good for ducks, bad for wheels, a waste by every useful measure a man of business had been taught to trust. In spring it used to hold sedge and frogs and a rim of greener grass that showed even in July. The memory embarrassed him. It felt sentimental now, and sentiment was a luxury he had spent twenty years training out of himself.

He took off one glove and laid his bare hand against the damp bank. The mud was colder than the air. Grit worked into the lines of his palm. When he lifted his hand, the lantern glass fogged for an instant from the disturbance of heat and breath, and in that yellowed cloud another night rose up under his eyes.

His father stood where the seep lay now, though in the memory there was more water and the stars were clear. He had his hat pushed back and a willow switch in one hand, stripping the bark off it with his thumbnail while Otto Blum spoke. That was his father's way when another man held the floor: not interrupting, not agreeing, merely reducing some living thing to fibers while he waited to overrule it. Otto had his trousers tucked into muddy boots and one suspender hanging loose, as if he'd dressed in a hurry to keep the basin from being murdered before breakfast. He kept scraping the side of one boot against the other, a neat irritated motion, trying to knock off mud that gathered again at once.

"You cut tile through here," Otto said, and his voice came up from the water and the glass both, "you send spring straight out of the land. Not only here. Up there too. The grass goes thin first. Then the top goes."

August's father did not look at Otto while he answered. He bent, snapped the willow switch in two, and tossed the pieces into the dark water. "What goes," he said, "is standing waste. I will not leave taxable ground to cattails because you are sentimental about birds."

There had been three men besides them. August knew that even before he saw himself, younger and narrower, with the survey stakes in his hands and his good Sunday coat buttoned wrong at the middle because he had dressed by lamplight. He was not speaking. He was listening, as he had done all his life. Otto turned to him once anyway, perhaps because there was more use pleading with the son than the father, or perhaps because Otto had never understood how completely a boy could be trained by admiration.

"It is not birds," Otto said. "It is what holds when the wind starts."

His father smiled then. Not broadly. That small bloodless one that meant he had decided a man was inconvenient rather than dangerous. "If the wind starts," he said, "we will meet that weather when God sends it. Meanwhile people need wheat and credit, not a marsh."

The memory ended before anybody moved. It always did that, these visitations: stopped at the exact point where explanation might begin, as if the world were willing to accuse and unwilling to testify. The lantern glass cleared. August saw only his own bent shape and the pinched gold of flame.

He sat back too fast and his heel slid. Mud took him to the ankle. One hand struck the spoil bank, sank wrist-deep, and came up black. He said, very softly and without dignity, "Damn it."

The smell that rose when the mud opened under his weight was thick and old, not the clean smell of fresh rain but something shut away for years and forced up at last: roots, wet clay, a sourness like long-soaked boards. He wiped his hand on his handkerchief and only spread the black farther. The basin did not care how carefully a man dressed.

He ought to have gone back then. Instead he moved the lantern closer and leaned over the water until his own face blurred in it. The flame doubled, stretched, and then the black surface deepened into polished desk wood. Ezra Vale sat on the client chair in August's office, hat in both hands, folding the brim inward and outward until it had a sharp beak in front. He used to do that whenever he lied, or whenever he meant to ask for something he knew he had no standing to demand. August had noticed the habit long before he understood it. Ezra's coat sleeves were dark at the cuffs where he'd washed at the pump and not dried properly. A thread of red feed-sack stitching clung to one cuff. He had probably leaned against Cora's mending basket on the way out. That domestic bit of color made the whole scene worse.

"You said till threshing," Ezra said. In the memory his voice was not angry yet. Only worn smooth from repeating itself. "That was the talk in this room."

August heard his own answer before he saw himself. "The bank discussed a contingent extension under additional security. I said no maturity could stand unless the paper would bear examination."

It was a hateful sentence. Not because it was false. Because it was true in the way a gate can be true when it swings shut.

The memory sharpened. August sat behind the desk, cuffs straight, pen clean, the county examiner's circular open beside the blotter like scripture. He had been thinner then and more certain that thinness proved virtue. Ezra leaned forward with his hat crushed between his palms.

"I gave you the water tract," Ezra said. "You wanted that, you got it. I need the rest of the spring. I can't take this home to her the way it stands."

"You should have taken it home to her before you signed anything."

Ezra flinched as if August had reached across and struck him. August remembered the mean satisfaction of that. He had told himself for years that the satisfaction had been incidental, no more than the heat a man feels from the stove while he performs a necessary task. The basin did not permit that version. The basin returned the heat too clearly.

"Don't preach at me," Ezra said. "If you meant to foreclose, you could've said it plain."

"I mean to keep the bank from carrying a visible indulgence it cannot support."

"That's not plain."

"It is plain enough."

Ezra stood up so suddenly the chair legs barked over the floorboards. Dust on the office window leaped in the vibration. He set both hands on August's desk, leaving crescents of damp where his palms touched the polished wood.

"You've got church money in here," he said. "Widows' money. I know it. I know whose names sit in your drawers. But you've got mine too, and I'm asking you for ninety days that won't cost you a thing if the wheat stands."

"If the wheat stands," August said, and heard himself laying the phrase down like a blade between them, "half the county believes itself rescued. That is not a banking standard."

Ezra's face changed then. Hope went out of it first. Pride stayed another second, maybe two, and then that too. He looked suddenly the age he actually was and not the younger, quicker man people remembered from church suppers and harvest crews. "All right," he said. "Then say it plain."

August had said it. He saw his own mouth move in the black water. "Sign the deed. Pay the interest. We will review again after quarter's close."

No mercy. Only wording.

The surface shivered and the office vanished. For one sick moment he thought the memory was done, but then the lantern flame lengthened and showed him another fragment, later the same night. His office again, empty now except for himself. He stood with the ledger open and the loose-leaf posts unscrewed. His penknife lay beside the inkwell. The original page, the true page with the extension entered under the proper month and his own initials beside it, had already been lifted out and set by his elbow. He remembered thinking not that he was falsifying a record, only that he was reconciling a contradiction between indulgence and survival. His hand moved in the memory with terrible steadiness, sliding the copied page into place, flattening it with the side of his palm, tightening the posts one by one. Not panic. Not haste. Craft.

When the water went black again, August stayed bent over it until his lower back began to tremble. He had not inherited the problem entire. That was the lie he had preferred because it still allowed him to think of himself as a steward standing in a breach made by other men. He had inherited a vocabulary and a habit of arrangement. The choices themselves had been his.

Wind crossed the basin then, small and cold. It touched the wet mud, touched his face, moved on. He thought of Cora in the yard after Ezra's burial, shoulders squared as if posture itself might keep the house from tilting. He thought of Dell, white with pain and fury, letting women carry broth to his bed because the arm left him no choice. He thought, against his will, of Thea Bell's room gone dark at the Vale place and the fact that he had noticed it from the road three nights running. The town was coming apart by inches, private seam after private seam, and he had spent years calling that order.

He stood with effort, nearly left the lantern behind, and turned back toward the road. Mud cooled on his cuff. With each step the ground released him reluctantly, as if the basin had no intention of letting a man off with witness alone.

* * *

He was at the bank before sunup. The stove had gone dead in the night and the office held that special chill of buildings made to look solid while failing at the edges. Dust lay over the counter, the teller cage, the brass rail, even over the green blotter on his desk though he had wiped all of it before locking up. He took the rag from the bottom drawer and wiped again. The motion steadied him until he noticed the dried black crescent on his right cuff and found that no amount of brushing would make it look like anything but mud.

He made coffee on the little plate in back and burned it because he was watching the street instead of the pot. Morrow woke slower now than it used to. No children's feet by the school because there was no school to fill. No line at the bakery. One truck rattled past with its bed half empty of furniture and chicken crates, headed east toward the depot road. August watched it until it cleared the window, then looked away as if he had been caught staring at indecency.

Knapp came just after eight with a leather valise in one hand and his hat in the other. He always brushed the hat before entering any room in Morrow, not with annoyance exactly but with the patient concentration of a man determined not to let western Nebraska into his lining. Today he paused on the threshold to tap the brim against his knee and then took a peppermint from a small round tin before speaking. He let it melt against one cheek while he looked over the office, as if even his breath required mediation in this town.

"Morning," he said. The peppermint made the first word sharper. "You look as if you slept badly."

August did not offer him the easy lie. "I slept little."

Knapp nodded as if that were the expected professional condition of both of them and set the valise on the customer chair instead of the floor. "I hope your stove is better than the hotel stove. They nearly boiled my eggs into bullets."

The detail might have been intended as courtesy. It struck August harder than courtesy should have. Men who meant to ruin a place usually took more trouble to sound formal about it.

"Coffee is ruined too," August said. "If you want some, take it now while it's merely scorched."

"That sounds about right for the morning."

Knapp drank standing up, one hand around the cup, the other still on his hat brim. He had a raw place at the side of his thumb where he kept worrying the felt binding. August had not noticed that before. He had the compactness of a man who rode trains, slept in bad rooms, and called that a career. Not farm labor. Not desk softness either. Something between.

August laid out the auction docket, the corrected title abstract, the note schedule, the tax figures. Paper formed its own kind of barricade between them. He kept aligning the corners without meaning to.

"Here is the Vale file," he said. "The county taxes are rolled in as previously discussed. Sale notice remains set for Saturday. If the weather holds, attendance will be sufficient."

Knapp looked at the stack but did not reach for it. "You still say if the weather holds as if weather were the deciding officer."

"In this county it generally is."

"Not anymore."

The word sat between them. August could hear the front sign creak outside, FARMERS STATE BANK groaning softly on its chains.

"You asked me in July," Knapp said, "whether a clean docket, regularized titles, and a prompt sale season might help Morrow present better at district review. I told you clean files help every place. That was true. What I did not tell you was that your place had already gone past the point where better presentation changed the recommendation."

August folded his hands because they wanted to reach for the papers again. "Recommendation from whom?"

"Lincoln first. Washington after that if they bother looking." Knapp took the peppermint from his mouth and set it on the saucer before continuing. "School's closing. Trade's moving out. The rail spur's under review. Half your acreage west of town is in some stage of delinquency or tax distress. The land-retirement men don't write sermons. They write maps. On their map, Morrow's already mostly a clearance problem."

The office seemed to narrow around August's desk. Not from drama. From fact. The ceiling suddenly felt too low for his body.

"Mostly," he repeated. He heard the pettiness of the word and could not stop himself. "That is a convenient adverb."

Knapp gave him a tired look rather than an offended one. "I use it because there are still people here and I need to speak about them in files built for acreage. I try not to let the file swallow the fact. That's the best I can offer."

"And the bank?"

"The bank may keep its sign awhile. A sign is not a town."

August turned his head toward the window without seeing it. He had been working for months under the miserable consolation that if he kept the ledgers exact enough, if he called notes in the right order, if he gave the county examiner nothing loose to seize, then when the larger men came around Morrow might still look disciplined enough to spare. Not prosperous. Only orderly. An orderly town, he had believed, could argue for continued existence.

Knapp seemed to read some portion of that thought from his face. "I'm not saying paperwork is nothing," he said. "I'm saying you can't ledger your way back into rainfall or people."

"No," August said. "But I can keep this office from appearing criminally managed."

That got Knapp to reach for the file at last. He flipped the top sheet, then the next, his fingertips leaving small clean arcs in the dust. "That is closer to the proper question."

August felt the skin between his shoulder blades cool. "Explain."

Knapp set the pages down carefully. "When a settlement contracts, people at the far end don't remember the weather first. They remember signatures. Whose name accelerated. Whose name certified title. Whose name swore the record was regular. If there is anything in these files that would fail under full review, this is the last reasonable morning to tell me."

The clock on the wall clicked once. Outside, a wagon rolled by so slowly the iron rim seemed to scrape the day open.

"You make that sound charitable," August said.

"It isn't charity." Knapp picked up the peppermint, turned it between finger and thumb, and put it back in his mouth. "It's self-interest of a broader sort. I don't enjoy sending avoidable messes upward. They come back down with more people attached."

August almost laughed. The sound did not quite make it out. "You want tidiness."

"I want legibility."

There it was again: that bureaucratic mildness that infuriated more effectively than cruelty. A cruel man could be hated cleanly. Knapp kept speaking as if he were arranging chairs after a funeral.

"If your question is whether an immaculate set of foreclosure files saves your town," Knapp said, "no. If your question is whether a concealed irregularity ruins the little standing you still have with the district and leaves you personally exposed when somebody starts tracing blame, yes. Those are different questions."

August looked down at his own hands. Mud had dried into the cuticle around one thumbnail. He had scrubbed and missed that.

"Suppose," he said slowly, "a man believed at the time that a particular adjustment preserved the bank from wider panic."

"Then he believed that."

"And suppose he was not entirely wrong."

Knapp's mouth bent, not quite into a smile. "That's the sort of sentence men use when they're about to hand me something I won't like."

August rose before he had decided to. His chair legs grated. "Wait here."

Knapp did not ask where he was going. That restraint made the next minutes worse.

* * *

The vault smelled of iron, paste, and old paper that had absorbed twenty years of sweat from men's cuffs. August shut the door behind himself without latching it. In the dim interior the shelves looked like pews in a chapel built for account books instead of mercy. Cancelled notes. Deeds. Tax receipts. Insurance riders. The family names of Morrow flattened into folders thick with delay.

He went to the plat atlas first, then stopped, because that was where he had moved the page after Cora's first visit to the office about Ezra, not where he had hidden it originally. He had relocated it twice in nine days, each move disguised from himself as prudence. In truth he had been handling his guilt like contraband, never bold enough to burn it, never honest enough to file it.

He found the sheet at last inside the back cover of the atlas between Morrow Township and a list of delinquent parcels clipped from last year's county circular. The paper had taken a shallow bend from the hiding place. Blue-black entries marched across it in his own hand. Note number, borrower, interest received, renewed maturity, collateral reference. Ezra Vale's line sat there with the true month and the additional term plainly entered, a modest mercy no larger on paper than any other line on the page. Small things were how men damned each other. Not with opera. With columns.

He stood with the page in his hand and remembered the sound the loose-leaf posts had made that night: a faint metal squeak, not dramatic enough for sin. He remembered too, because the basin would not let him keep only one side of the matter, that there had been three other delinquent notes in the drawer and two old women with savings in the bank who would have lost bread money if panic broke hard enough. Both things were true. He had protected a failing institution and he had done it by choosing which man could be broken most quietly.

When he came back out, Knapp had opened the Vale file and was making notes in the margin with a pencil sharpened almost indecently fine. He looked up, saw the page in August's hand, and set the pencil down.

August did not sit. "This is the original ledger leaf for the renewal period covering Vale's note."

Knapp held out his hand. August looked at it for a second too long before giving him the sheet.

Paper made a dry whisper going from one man to the other. That was all. No thunder. No gust. No clock stopping. Just the ordinary sound of custody changing.

Knapp read in silence. Once he went back to the top of the page and read the line again. August watched his eyes make the calculation.

"This extends the maturity under the later month," Knapp said.

"Yes."

"And the deed in the file is entered under the earlier month."

"Yes."

Knapp laid the page flat on the blotter and smoothed it with his fingertips, a gesture August recognized with a sick little start as his own. "Did Vale know you reversed the order."

"No."

"Did anyone?"

August could have answered with qualifications. He did not. "No."

Knapp looked up then, full on him for the first time that morning. "Why keep it?"

August had expected anger, perhaps disgust. The plain curiosity was harder to endure. He found that the answer arriving in him was not a respectable one.

"Because destroying a record and displacing it are not the same trespass," he said. "I told myself I had altered sequence for the bank's protection, not abolished fact. Keeping the page allowed me to imagine a line I had not crossed."

Knapp's expression did not change, but something in it settled. "And now."

August looked at the page, not at the other man. "Now there is no use preserving my own distinction if the town is gone anyway."

"That is not confession," Knapp said.

"No." August swallowed coffee bitterness and old mud both. "Confession would include motive in full. I do not have the stomach for that this morning."

He expected Knapp to call him coward. Instead Knapp asked the more damaging question. "Then give me the business version."

August let out a breath. "Ezra Vale signed the deed willingly enough under pressure. The debt was real. The interest delinquency was real. The extension was also real when entered. I later removed its force because the examiner was coming, the bank could not show another soft renewal, and I decided Mrs. Vale's household would bear the cost more quietly than public weakness at the bank."

Knapp held his gaze another beat. "That is at least legible."

The humiliation of gratitude for that word almost turned August's stomach.

"What happens to it now?" he asked.

"It leaves your desk." Knapp folded the page once, carefully, along an existing bend rather than making a new crease. "I attach a memorandum and carry it with the file. District will see it if district asks. The examiner will see it if I judge the title irregularity material to the transfer review."

"You judge."

"I judge first. Others later."

That was mercy of a bureaucratic sort, and August hated himself for recognizing it. He moved back to the desk, pulled a blank form from the drawer, and fed it into the typewriter.

Knapp watched him. "What are you doing?"

"Correcting the part still subject to my hand."

The keys struck hard because his fingers were not steady. Each letter bit through the ribbon with more force than necessary. He rolled the carriage back and began again lower on the page when the first line misaligned. Upon completion of foreclosure sale on the Vale note, Farmers State Bank waives any personal deficiency remaining against Cora Vale, widow, and will pursue no further collection beyond proceeds of sale. The words were ugly. He did not care. They needed only to stand.

As he typed Cora's name, what came to him was not her face in any theatrical sense but her hands at the bank counter two weeks ago, one palm flat on the wood, the other closed around her gloves as if the gloves were the only soft thing left she could afford to crush. He thought of eggs counted for cash, sewing done by lamp, the extra fifty-two dollars a month that had been coming into that house through Thea Bell and was likely gone with the school anyway. A deficiency judgment would not only take the farm. It would reach after the rest of her years with a spoon.

He pulled the sheet free, signed it, sanded the wet ink, and slid it across.

Knapp read faster this time. "Why not postpone the sale instead?"

August had known the question was coming. He had been asking it of himself since the basin. The answer he gave first was the defensible one.

"Because the note remains in default. Because taxes remain due. Because the land will not carry what is owed even if I indulge sentiment for another month."

"That is the bank answer."

August looked past him at the teller cage, at the dust caught in the wire mesh. "The other answer is no prettier."

"Let's hear it."

He was tired enough now that the edges of his diction frayed. "If I stop the sale, I've got to tell the whole town I used one man's desperation to patch a failing office and then rewrote the patch. I've got to put the bank into open disgrace before harvest is even buried. Depositors stampede. Other notes collapse sooner. Maybe the county closes me before winter. Maybe that's justice. It isn't rescue."

Knapp said nothing.

August went on because silence had become less bearable than speech. "The farm is not solvent because I altered a page. It was not solvent before that either. Ezra signed more than one ruin. The basin. The deed. The debt itself. I helped sharpen the blade, yes. I did not forge the whole knife."

Knapp leaned back at last, chair giving a dry complaint. "You know how that sounds."

"Like excuse."

"Like classification," Knapp said. "You still want the damage sorted by proper drawer."

August almost answered no, but that would have been another lie. "Yes."

Knapp put the waiver beside the folded ledger page. "Then this is what you're willing to do: surrender the proof, surrender any claim on her after the sale, and keep the sale."

"Yes."

"You understand she won't thank you."

That, at least, was easy. "She should not."

Knapp studied him with a look August could not read. Not pity. Perhaps only fatigue, one tired official recognizing another species of tiredness. He opened the valise and placed the folded page inside an inner pocket, then laid the signed waiver on top of it. Leather shut over both with a dull snap.

That sound was worse than the handoff had been. The handoff still contained choice. The closing valise did not.

"I'll need a duplicate of this mailed to Mrs. Vale today," Knapp said, tapping the waiver. "No surprises on deficiency after the sale. If you're doing one clean thing, do it cleanly."

"It will be done."

Knapp rose, took up his hat, brushed the brim once more with his thumb, and paused by the chair. "For what it's worth, Mr. Poeschel, most towns die by weather and price together. Men merely help them along and then quarrel over proportions."

August looked at him. "That is meant kindly."

"No," Knapp said. "Only accurately, I hope."

Then he left.

The office settled around the absence almost at once. Wagon noise from outside, the clock, a draft under the back door carrying in the smell of coal smoke from somewhere down the block. August remained standing until his knees reminded him of the basin.

On his desk the Vale file lay open where Knapp had worked through it. The empty space where the ledger page ought to have been was not visible in any literal sense; the page had been hidden elsewhere for days. Still he could feel the vacancy in the room the way a tongue finds a missing tooth.

He made the duplicate notice for Cora. Carbon paper smudged his fingers blue. He addressed the envelope in his best clerk's hand and hated the hand for looking so calm. Then he drew the auction sheet back toward him and checked the date, the hour, the legal description, the improvements to be sold. House. Shed. Chicken run. Equipment worn and partial. Acreage as described. A life translated into lots.

He signed where the bank required his name.

Mud flaked from his cuff and fell onto the margin beside the word SALE. He brushed at it, then stopped. The smear it left looked darker than dust and more honest. Outside, Morrow kept moving through its thin morning with the stubbornness of places that have not yet been informed, in their own hearing, that they are mostly dead already.

August folded the notice, rang for no clerk because there was no clerk to spare, and set it in the out-tray for posting. Then he sat alone at his desk with the open file, the spoiled coffee, and the smell of damp earth drying on his sleeve, and understood that he had not chosen decency. Only a narrower form of ruin.
