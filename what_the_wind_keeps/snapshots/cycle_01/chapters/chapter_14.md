# Chapter 14

By the time Thea came in from town, Cora had boiled the same strip of muslin twice and still could not get the smell of camphor and old sweat out of it. The kettle sat crooked on the stove because the back leg had gone soft in the heat years ago. Every few breaths it gave a little tin complaint. Outside, the evening wind pushed dust along the yard in low, mean skirls that never rose high enough to be called a storm and never stopped long enough to let anybody forget what season it was.

Dell was in the front room on the narrow iron bed they had dragged down from the attic so he would not have to climb stairs with his ribs strapped. He ought to have been sleeping. Instead he lay half turned toward the window, his left arm thick in plaster and sling, his good hand busy with the broken alarm clock he had taken apart three days earlier. He had the little brass works spread on a flour sack beside his hip and was trying, one-handed, to settle a gear back on its post with the point of a nail. He would rather hurt himself than let a machine sit there beaten.

Cora stood in the doorway a moment with the wet muslin hanging from her fingers. His face, in the yellowed light, looked both older and younger than seventeen. The bruise along his ribs had gone from black to a rotten green at the edges. His hair needed cutting. He kept blowing it out of his eyes the way Ezra used to, sharp and impatient, as if hair itself were an insult.

"That thing won't ring if you bully it," she said.

He did not look up. "It won't ring if I leave it broke, either."

"Doctor said rest."

"Doctor says that to folks that can afford it."

He found the gear, missed the post, and swore at it under his breath. Cora crossed in, set the muslin on the chair, and lifted the flour sack by its corners so the screws would not roll into the bedclothes.

"You'll lose pieces."

"I know where the pieces are."

"Do you."

He gave her a quick look then, the look that meant he knew she was fussing because she was frightened and meant to punish her a little for it. Under the meanness there was a plea he would never say plain. He had been asking her, in ten thousand ugly small ways since the cave-in, not to leave him alone with his own helplessness.

The screen door rasped. Thea stepped in with her satchel on one shoulder and the old school geography wrapped in a dish towel against the grit. Dust had worked its way into the hem of her skirt and left a brown tide mark around both ankles. One shoe had rubbed the back of her heel raw enough to show a bright crescent where the stocking had slipped. She smelled of chalk, road dust, and the peppermint lozenges she kept in a tin and rationed as if they were medicine.

"You brought home the whole county?" Cora said.

Thea set the satchel on the table with more care than the books deserved. "Only the bits the board didn't think worth burning."

Dell tipped his head back against the pillow. "They take the globe?"

"Mr. Jurgensen did. Said his youngest wanted to see where California sat."

That got the smallest ghost of a smile out of Dell. "Could've told him. Left."

"West," Thea said, deadpan.

"That too."

She crossed to him, not touching him yet, only looking at the cast with the frank professional concern that always made Dell act as if he minded her more than he did. "How's the arm?"

"Still there."

"And the ribs?"

"Also still there."

"A triumph all around."

He tried not to smile again and failed by a fraction. Cora saw it. So did Thea. It hurt in the stupid way small mercies hurt now, because once they had been common and now they arrived like contraband.

Thea took off her hat. Her hair at the temples had curled damp where the heat had beaten the pins loose. She laid the wrapped geography book apart from the others, near the lamp and away from Dell's reach. The care of that movement struck Cora before the meaning of it did.

"You eat in town?" Cora asked.

"No."

"There's beans left and half a heel of yesterday's loaf."

"That sounds opulent."

Cora turned back to the stove. The kettle had begun its muttering boil. She lifted it off and poured the water into the washbasin where the muslin waited. The camphor smell rose stronger. Dell made a face.

"You could've spared the liniment," he said. "It crawls up my nose all night."

"It crawls into the bruise too. That's the point."

"Smells like Hester's cupboard."

"Hester's cupboard keeps folks alive."

"Keeps them weird," Dell said.

Thea, taking plates down from the shelf, said, "Those are not opposing conditions."

Dell snorted. His nail slipped. A tiny gear bounced once on the iron bedstead and landed in the blanket fold.

"There," Cora said.

"I heard it."

"You're a wonder."

He found it, then winced when the twist of his body pulled at the strapped ribs. He hid the wince badly. Cora looked away so he could keep his pride a second longer. Thea looked away too, though not before Cora caught the tightening around her mouth.

They ate in the front room because Dell did not want carrying back and forth made of him. The beans had skinned over. The bread was dry enough to shave a man's gums. Dell still cleaned his plate and used the crust to chase the last grease around the edge. Thea drank coffee gone thin from being used twice. Cora ate fast, standing. The geography book sat on the table by the lamp all through the meal like another person waiting to speak.

When Dell was done, he wiped his mouth with the back of his hand and nodded at the wrapped book. "What's that one for?"

"Nothing you need tonight," Thea said.

"That means it's either school business or trouble."

"The school is finished," Cora said. "So guess."

He let his head sink back. "Trouble, then."

Thea stood and gathered the plates. "I'll wash."

"Sit," Cora said. "You've been on your feet."

"So have you."

"Sit anyway."

Thea did not sit. She looked at Dell instead. "You taken the evening powders?"

"Took one."

"You were told two."

"Two make me stupid."

"One makes you rude, which is a poorer bargain."

"I'm rude for free."

"Dell." Cora said it flat.

He looked from one woman to the other, hearing something in the room he could not yet name. His gaze snagged on the wrapped book again. Cora saw the thought move across his face: paper, secrecy, adult voices gone too careful. Since the bank he had started listening through walls. Since the trench, through his own pain too.

"I can wash the plate one-handed," he said.

"You can sleep two-handed if you shut your eyes," Cora said.

He muttered, but he let Thea take the dish. When she bent to pick up the flour sack with the clock parts, he caught her sleeve with his good hand.

"Miss Bell."

"What."

"Did they nail the school shut?"

The question came out rougher than he meant it to. His ears had gone pink. Cora felt a small shame at hearing it; he had loved the place more than he wanted anybody to know.

Thea softened in a way she almost never let Dell see. "No. Just emptied it. Mr. Jurgensen took the maps. Mrs. Keeler took the stove lids. The blackboard's still there, though I don't know for what."

"Might leave it," Dell said. "Town could use somewhere to write down who's gone."

He let go of her sleeve and shut his eyes as if he had not said anything worth hearing. Thea stood over him a beat longer, then took the dishes into the kitchen.

Cora came behind with the basin and muslin. Dell had not slept. His lashes lay still, but his right thumb kept moving against the sheet, counting some private measure. Cora knew that motion. Ezra used to do it when seed prices were bad.

In the kitchen, Thea had unwrapped the geography book and set the lamp closer. The old blue cloth cover was cut square through the middle. A packet of folded paper lay in the hollow where pages ought to have been.

Cora put the basin down too hard. Water slapped her apron. "What's this."

"I found it today." Thea's voice had gone formal with strain, each word laid careful as china on a shelf. "Inside the book. Otto's map was in there too."

"And you opened it."

"Yes."

Cora stared at the packet. She knew Ezra's hand before she touched it. Even after all this time she knew the pitch of his capitals, the way his e's pinched narrow when he was ashamed or hurrying. The paper had been folded small enough to hide in a coat pocket. Grease from someone's fingers had gone dark at one corner. Dust sat in the crease like soot in a cut.

"Dell didn't see?" she asked.

"No."

"Good."

Thea drew breath to say something. Cora had already taken the letter.

It had no envelope. Ezra had written Cora's name once at the top and then crossed it out so hard the nib had nearly gone through. Under that, in the cramped slant he used when he was trying to make a page carry more than it ought, he had written:

_If this comes to your hand I did not find the way I kept naming to myself. August said the south flat would hold another wheat year if the basin was cut and the tile run proper. Otto told us plain it was bad country for it and I knew he was right enough to fear him. I said yes anyway._

Cora read the lines twice because the first time they would not hold still.

Thea said quietly, "Read on."

Cora did.

_I thought one more season would put us right with the bank and let Dell stay in school and keep feed through winter. I thought I could bear the wrong if it kept the rest from you. August put papers under my hand and talked extension. Later he changed the dating on the deed and told me if I cried fraud I would lose the place at once and drag all of you under with me._

The room narrowed. Not with dizziness. With accuracy. The dish towel hanging from the oven handle. The chip in the blue bowl. The lamp wick needing trim. The scrape on the table where Dell had whittled a toy truck at nine years old and Ezra had laughed instead of scolding. Every object stood out clean and hard as if the lie had been the only blur on them and somebody had wiped it away.

The last lines on the page were blotted where the pen had stopped and started:

_I told myself I was keeping bread in your mouth. I was keeping shame out of my own. There is a difference I knew too late._

Cora put the paper down. Then she picked it up again as if perhaps her hand had misread it the first time.

"Where's the rest."

"There are two more pages."

"How much did you read."

"All of it."

Cora looked up then. That hurt landed before Ezra's words did: Thea had read her husband's confession alone in the schoolhouse, with no one there to see her face at it, no one there to tell her when to stop. Thea had carried Ezra's last decent thing or last rotten thing, whichever it was, pressed to her ribs all the way home before Cora ever knew it existed.

"You read my husband's hand before I did."

"I know."

"You know."

"I didn't know what it was when I opened it."

"You knew soon enough."

Thea did not answer that. She did not need to. The truth sat between them, plain as the lamp.

Cora folded the pages once, twice, not neatly. "We'll burn it."

Thea's head came up. "No."

"Before Dell wakes proper."

"No."

Cora turned toward the stove. The lid eye nearest the firebox stood orange around the edges. It would take only a second. Paper curled fast. Ink went black, then silver, then gone. Whole lives vanished easier than a rag.

Thea moved faster than Cora had expected after a day in town. She caught Cora's wrist before the letter reached the heat. Her fingers were cold and dust-dry.

"Don't."

Cora jerked back. "Let go."

"Not for that."

"You don't get to tell me what to do with my own dead."

Thea let go then, but only because Cora had said dead, and both of them heard the claim inside it. Cora set the folded pages on the table instead, one inch from the lamp base, where she could keep a hand on them.

"My own dead," Thea said. Her voice was still measured, but the measure had turned cutting. "Is that what he is when the truth is inconvenient."

"He's Dell's father."

"Yes."

"He's the man whose name has kept this place standing after he couldn't."

"Standing." Thea glanced around the kitchen with the smallest bitter laugh. "Cora, the roof lets dust through over the pantry and the bank has your note by the throat."

"And still his name buys things."

Thea stared at her.

Cora heard herself and did not stop. Once she started under pressure, truth came out in the order of use. Feeling had to wait its turn.

"It buys me another sack of mash on credit because the feed man remembers Ezra worked through a winter fever and paid him late instead of never. It buys Dell a father he can still stand to be made from. It buys me one story to hand people when they ask how a woman gets left with debt and a boy and a farm that's half powder. You think a dead man doesn't keep earning in a town like this? He does, if he dies clean enough."

Thea went very still. Cora knew that stillness. It was the one that came over her just before the temper underneath it broke surface.

"There," Thea said. "There it is."

"What."

"You know exactly what this letter costs."

"Yes, I do."

"Then stop pretending I'm asking you to take a philosophical interest in honesty. I'm asking you not to make Dell's life out of a lie because that lie has been useful."

Cora laughed once, sharp and ugly. "Useful. Christ."

"What would you call it."

"Necessary."

From the front room Dell's bed springs gave a long complaint. Both women went silent. Cora listened. No voice followed.

She lowered hers anyway. "He just got out of fever. He can't lace his own shirt with one hand. He wakes up sore enough to curse the boards. And you want me to walk in there with paper saying his father helped cut the basin dry and then got cheated besides. For what. So he can feel better informed while the farm goes to sale."

"So he can know what world he's living in."

"He knows plenty."

"He knows fragments. He knows the bank took too much. He knows the town lies. He does not know that the lie reaches through his own house."

"Not if you hand me that letter."

"No."

Cora put both palms on the table and leaned into them until the wood edge bit through the apron at her waist. The roughness steadied her more than breath would have. She could feel each grain rise against her skin.

"You think this is mercy from you," she said. "Marching in with truth like a banner. It isn't. It's timing. Bad timing. Cruel timing."

"I found it now."

"Then you should've put it back till Dell could bear it."

"And how would I know when that was. After the auction. After we left. After he'd built another six months of himself on the wrong foundation."

"People are built on worse."

"I know that." Thea's voice sharpened. "I'm one of them."

Cora looked up. Thea's hand had gone to the book without seeming to know it, fingers resting on the hacked-out pages as if over a wound. Chalk still dusted the side of her thumb. She had likely spent half the afternoon emptying slates and copybooks, deciding what scrap of a dead school was worth carrying and what the town could let mice take. She had walked home on blistered heels with Ezra's confession tucked under her arm. Cora knew all that and still wanted, with a violence so plain it shocked her, to make her give it up.

"Don't do this to me tonight," Cora said.

"It was already done. He wrote it."

"You know what I mean."

Thea's mouth thinned. "Yes. I do."

Dell's voice came from the doorway behind them. "What letter."

Cora turned so hard her hip hit the table. Dell stood barefoot in the kitchen opening, the sling hanging loose at his side because he had been too impatient to settle it back over his neck. His shirt was unbuttoned wrong, one side higher than the other. He had the lost clock gear pinched between finger and thumb. He must have brought it with him without knowing.

"You shouldn't be up," Cora said.

"I got tired of being discussed horizontal."

"Back to bed."

"What letter."

Thea took one step back from the table. Cora saw Dell's eyes catch that movement. Then saw his gaze drop to the pages in her hand, to the old geography on the table, to the two of them standing too close in a silence already overfull.

"Schoolhouse paper," Cora said.

"That's not how either of you sounds about schoolhouse paper."

"Dell," Thea began.

"No, go on." He lifted the gear a fraction, then let the hand fall when the motion tugged his ribs. "You were just talking plain."

Cora crossed to him and reached for the sling. He flinched away before she could settle it.

"Don't."

"You'll strain the arm."

"Arm's already strained."

He looked at Thea. "Is this about Pa."

Cora said, "Go lie down."

Dell's mouth twisted. "So that's it."

Thea's face changed then, not softer exactly, but less armed. "I found something today," she said. "At the schoolhouse. It concerns your father and August both."

Cora swung toward her. "Thea."

"I'm not telling him now."

"You're telling him enough."

"Enough for him not to think he's crazy hearing us."

Dell leaned his shoulder against the frame because standing hurt and pride hurt worse. "How much did he sign."

Cora stared at him. "You don't know what you're asking."

"I know more'n you think."

"That has never once kept you safe."

"And not knowing has?"

The sentence landed because it was Ezra's cadence under Dell's mouth, Ezra cornered and angry and trying not to sound afraid. Cora hated him for it in that instant, hated the dead man too, hated every inheritance that came without consent.

"Back to bed," she said again.

Dell laughed once without humor. "You both start whispering and then send me to bed. That's your remedy for everything."

"Dell."

His eyes slid to Thea's satchel on the chair, then to the pages in Cora's hand, then, finally, to the place where Cora had seized Thea's wrist hard enough to redden it. The understanding that came over his face was not full. It was worse for being partial. A young man's mind, already sore and suspicious, will make its own bridges faster than any adult wants.

"This ain't only about him," he said.

Cora heard the shape of the cliff and could not get in front of it. "Go lie down."

He held her gaze another moment. Then he looked at Thea instead and asked, with a flatness that took years off his voice, "You leaving."

Thea answered after a beat too long. "I'm sleeping in town tonight."

"At Hester's."

"Yes."

His mouth moved as if on another question. He shut it. He had seen too many people leave Morrow to waste words on the first fact of departure.

"Fine," he said.

Nothing in the word was fine. He turned awkwardly, one arm useless, shoulder blading sharp under the shirt, and went back to the front room with the bed springs squealing at his weight the moment after.

Cora stood in the kitchen listening to the mattress take him. Then came the small hard sound of something metal thrown against the wall. The clock gear, maybe. Or the whole damn clock.

Thea said, "That's exactly why he should hear it from us, not from the crack under a door."

Cora rounded on her. "You think I don't know that. You think I don't know every rotten inch of this. But there isn't a right hour for it and this is the worst one in the world."

"There's only the hour we're in."

"Do not start talking like a circular."

Thea blinked once at the full form, at the coldness Cora only ever used when she wanted to wound carefully. Then she said, much blunter, "No. I won't."

She picked up the letter before Cora could. The folded pages disappeared into the geography book. The geography book disappeared under her arm.

"Put that down."

"No."

"Thea."

"No."

The word was not loud. It did not need to be. It came from a place Cora could not bully.

For a second Cora saw, with humiliating clarity, the exact terms on which she had loved this woman and expected to keep loving her. At night, in the room with the lamp turned down, when Dell slept and the boards had settled and the town lay far enough away to feel beatable, she could touch Thea wherever she pleased. She could ask. She could even beg a little. By daylight, grief came back on like a work dress. Ezra's name, Dell's need, the farm's ruin, the bank note, the town's stare. All of it went on first. Thea had lived in the space left over, and because that space was warm and real and hungry, Cora had called it enough.

"Fine," she said, though nothing about her was fine. "Then take your things if you're so set on town."

Thea looked at her for a long moment. Hurt moved through her face, then hardened. "I will."

She went down the short hall to her room.

Cora followed because she could not stop herself. The room was small and already half stripped by Thea's living in it: one iron bed, one washstand, a nail line where a picture had once hung before the dust ruined it, books stacked on the floor because there had never been a proper shelf. The air held Thea's particular mixture of starch, cold cream, chalk, and the faint medicinal sweetness of the violet water she used so sparingly Cora could count the applications by week. A pair of sensible pumps sat under the bed with the toes neatly aligned. The habit of that pierced Cora almost as badly as the leaving.

Thea pulled the valise from beneath the chair and set it open on the quilt. She moved quickly but not wildly. That made it worse. If she had been weeping or cursing, Cora could have answered it. Order had always been Thea's angriest shape.

"You don't need to make a spectacle," Cora said.

"I'm putting dresses in a bag."

"At sundown. Walking into town from here with a valise."

"The school is closed. I no longer answer to which porch is counting."

"No, only to the rest of your life."

Thea gave a short laugh that was nearer disgust than mirth. "At last we mention it."

She folded a dark dress, then another. She added stockings, underthings, her hairbrush with the missing tooth in the handle, the small blue tin of lozenges, the notebook where she kept children's recitations and grocery figures on alternating pages. Cora watched each object vanish and felt the house altering around the loss before the woman herself had gone through the door.

"You'll come back by morning," Cora said. It sounded like an order stripped of authority.

"No."

"You don't know that."

"I know enough."

"Enough for what."

Thea stopped packing long enough to look at her. "Enough not to sleep in a house where the price of being loved is helping to keep another lie upright."

The sentence struck low and clean. Cora leaned a shoulder against the doorframe because she could not quite trust her legs to hold steady without bracing on something solid.

"Don't say it like that."

"Why not. It is like that."

"It is not."

"Then what is it, Cora."

Cora opened her mouth and closed it. The answers came in a useless crowd. It's a farm going under. It's a boy with one arm for now. It's a dead man who left me debt and pity and a face our son still reaches for when he is half-drugged with pain. It's a town that will crush us both if it gets proof enough. It's the only decent thing I've had in two years and I can't hold it and everything else at once. None of those were the right one alone. Together they sounded like excuse.

"It is not so simple," she said at last.

"I know that too." Thea pushed two books into the valise and had to shift them because the spine caught. "Do not mistake me for someone asking for simplicity. I stayed after Lincoln because simple would've been leaving. I stayed because you asked me to, because I loved you, because Dell needed every pair of hands in that house after the dig, because I couldn't bear one more departure either. I stayed with my whole mind clear about the risks. What I won't do is help you make a saint out of Ezra so the rest of us can go on kneeling in the right arrangement around him."

"Saint." Cora stepped forward. "You think that's what I've done."

"What else should I call it when a man signs off on cutting the basin, hides the paper, lets you think August alone blackened the world, and even now you want his own words fed to the stove before his son can know them."

"He was trapped."

"Yes."

"You say that yes as if it costs you nothing."

"It costs me a great deal." Now the control in Thea's voice slipped. "It costs me the cheap pleasure of despising him neatly. It costs me the comfort of believing the man you married was merely another victim. I know he was trapped. I know men do ugly arithmetic when they think winter is coming and the bank is counting. I know shame can make a person put his whole body behind the wrong door. But his being trapped does not make the lie nourishing."

Cora took another step. "You weren't here for all of it."

"No."

"You saw him in pieces. I saw what it took to keep this place after the crops went bad the first time. I saw him come in with his cuffs white from dry dirt and pretend he had eaten in town so Dell could have the last potatoes. I saw him work with blood in his boot where the leather had split. I saw him stand at the foot of Dell's bed when the croup took hold and swear he would not lose the boy. That man is in this too."

"I know he is."

"Do you. Because the man in your hand is all confession and filth. The man in mine kept this roof over Dell till he couldn't. Those are both true, maybe, but only one of them will the town remember if this gets out before the sale. They'll say he helped do it. They'll say the basin was our own kind's work and August only kept books. They'll spit his name and buy our chairs cheaper for it. They'll make the auction into justice."

Thea's face changed again, grief and anger crossing so quick Cora could not sort them. "And so the answer is to let Dell keep worshipping a ghost that was built to carry groceries."

"Don't you dare talk to me about worship."

"Why not. You ask it of everybody in this house."

"That is a dirty thing to say."

"Is it dirtier than what you asked a moment ago."

Cora reached for the valise and Thea caught it at the same time. The handle twisted. One clasp sprang open. A stocking and the blue tin of lozenges fell to the floor. They both bent. Their heads nearly struck. For one stupid split second Cora had the old instinct, the night instinct, to put a hand on the back of Thea's neck and smooth this thing with touch. She even saw the loose hair at Thea's nape where sweat had dried into a small dark curl.

Thea must have seen the movement coming. She straightened first and stepped back out of reach.

"No," she said.

"I wasn't."

"You were."

"I was trying to help."

"With what. Packing."

"With you."

Thea laughed then, and Cora had never heard a sound less amused. "That's precisely the trouble. You want me where I'm useful. In the kitchen. With Dell's powders. In your bed when the grief gets too loud. But when truth comes in, when daylight asks what I'm to you and what Ezra was to this house, suddenly I'm asked to be practical. Silent. Helpful."

"That's not fair."

"No," Thea said. "Fair was off the table before I ever rented the room."

The words went through Cora like a cold nail. She knew, with the clarity pain sometimes gives, that Thea had been carrying this longer than the letter. The letter had only put a handle on it.

"You think I don't know what I've asked," Cora said.

"I think you know and still ask."

"Because there wasn't another way."

"There's always another way. There's just not always one that leaves the heart looking respectable."

Cora pressed her fingertips to her eyes until grit bit back. "Respectable. Christ. Is that what you think I'm trying to preserve."

"Among other things, yes."

"I'm trying to preserve my son."

"From what. His father's humanity."

"From one more thing breaking at once."

Thea shut the valise harder than necessary. "He already knows enough to hear the crack."

From the front room came a cough Dell was trying not to make sound wet. Cora turned toward it on reflex. Thea watched her do it.

"Exactly," Cora said. "You hear that."

"I do."

"Then stop asking me to take the last beam out over his head."

"It isn't a beam. It's rot. You've just gotten used to the house leaning on it."

Cora stared at her. The sentence was too close to true and too cruel in the same breath. She wanted to slap Thea. She wanted to pull her close enough to stop hearing the words as words and hear only breath. She wanted the afternoon back before the screen door rasped and the geography book touched the table. Wanting made no change in the room at all.

"You stayed," Cora said, because suddenly there was another wound inside the first one and she could not keep from pressing it. "You said you stayed after Lincoln because I asked. You could've said no."

"Yes."

"You could've gone where there are shelves and pay and women who don't ask this of you."

"Yes."

"Then don't stand there like I dragged you by the wrist from the station."

For a beat Thea looked almost stunned. Then she said, very quietly, "That's mean."

Cora hated herself at once and kept going anyway because once cruelty starts earning leverage it becomes hard to lay down.

"It's true."

"Partly." Thea bent, picked up the fallen lozenge tin, and put it in the valise. "I chose you. I chose this place for longer than was wise. I chose your son too, as much as he would let me. I chose dust in my hems and gossip in every store and the pleasure of one closed door at night because you were behind it. What I didn't choose was to become another tool for arranging your dead husband into the shape that hurts you least."

"Hurts me least." Cora almost smiled at the absurdity of it. "Do you think there's been a least."

"No." Thea's own voice dropped now, losing polish. "I think there has been what you can survive. I'm telling you I can't survive my part in it any longer."

That was the true blow. Not the letter. Not even Dell hearing the edges of them. This. Thea drawing a line around her own life and saying here, no farther. Cora had been so deep in her own triage she had half believed endurance was infinite in other people if only the need was grave enough.

She said, after too long, "If you go to Hester's with that bag, Dell will know."

"He already knows something."

"Not enough to keep from guessing wrong."

"Then tell him the right parts."

"And the rest."

Thea met her eyes. "I'm done helping you decide which rest stays buried."

It was not a confession. Not plain. But the room heard it that way. So did Cora's body. Heat rose under her skin not from shame exactly but from exposure imagined so vividly it might as well have been present fact: August's eyes narrowing across his desk, two women named together in the wrong tone at the mercantile, Dell standing in the yard with his face closed up against her. She could not tell whether her fear was for Thea, for Dell, or for the ragged scrap of self still left to her outside the roles she worked in.

"You'd throw all of it open for this piece of paper."

"Do not insult me by making it only the paper."

"Then what."

Thea's throat moved. When she answered, the words came balanced at first and then broke loose in the middle, just as Cora had heard happen only a few times before, always when the truth was already beyond managing.

"It's being hidden until I can't breathe in a room I sleep in. It's hearing you call for me after midnight and then seeing you put me back into daylight as Miss Bell, boarder, helper, whatever harmless thing the house requires. It's loving a woman who asks for my whole body in private and my silence in public. It's being told that this, too, is kindness, because grief came first and debt came first and motherhood came first and the bank came first and the dead came first. Everything comes first. I know the order by heart. I'm saying I can't keep living at the bottom of it."

Cora had no defense ready that did not sound exactly like the indictment.

All she had was anger and need.

"And what would you have me do," she said. "Take Dell by the shoulders and tell him his father sold water for wheat and his schoolteacher's been in my bed. Shall I hang it on the clothesline so August doesn't miss any item."

Thea flinched. Cora knew she had aimed true there and despised herself more for the satisfaction than the cruelty.

"No," Thea said. "I would have you stop making ugliness do the work of honesty. I would have you decide one true thing and stand by it in daylight. Not all of it at once. One thing. But you won't even do that with a letter your husband wrote himself."

"Because he wrote it in shame."

"And you think shame lies."

"It does."

"So does love, when it's cornered badly enough."

The sentence opened something under Cora's ribs. She felt it in the same place grief had lived since Ezra's death, only sharper. Because Thea was not only talking about Ezra anymore. She was talking about Cora. About them.

For a moment neither of them moved. Dust ticked faintly against the window screen. From the front room came the careful turning of Dell on the iron bed, every movement measured against pain. Somewhere outside, a chicken made the low foolish mutter that meant it had settled wrong on the roost and was blaming the dark.

At last Thea fastened the valise. The sprung clasp would not catch. She swore under her breath, a single precise word. Then she cinched a strap around the middle instead.

"Hester said, after the schoolhouse emptied, that if the farm ever got too tight I could have the back room over the bakehouse," she said without looking up. "I laughed at her then."

Cora said nothing.

"She keeps the spare key under the blue crock by the flour bin because she trusts no one to remember where a sensible key ought to be. I will go there."

"You already arranged it."

"No. Hester has simply been alive longer than either of us."

The old woman's dry voice almost seemed present in the room at that. Cora could picture the bakery plain as if she stood in it: the yeast smell, the tins of fennel and horehound, Hester coughing into a square of cloth and then apologizing to nobody. Thea in the back room there. The image felt impossible and immediately real.

Cora moved aside because the valise was coming toward the door and she would not have it knocked from her hands like a comic wife in some traveling show. That pride remained, pointless and fierce.

Thea paused when she reached her. For one instant Cora thought she might touch her after all. Not kiss. Not hold. Just lay a hand on the arm as one human being lays claim to another before a separation. Thea did not.

"I loved him too, in my way," Thea said. "Not like you. Not like Dell. But enough to know he should not be kept clean at the price of all the living."

Cora's answer came out low and rough. "Get out before I say something worse."

Thea's mouth trembled once, not with fear. With the effort of holding back whatever softer thing wanted saying and had no safe place to land.

"That is not difficult tonight," she said.

She went past.

Cora heard the front room fall silent when Dell realized Thea was carrying a bag. The screen door rasped once as she opened it, once as it struck shut behind her. Dell said, "Miss Bell," from the bed, but too late for anything except farewell.

Thea answered from the porch, voice muffled by the screen and evening air. "Mind the sling. It slips when you're angry."

"I know."

"I know you do."

Then her steps crossed the yard, firm despite the blistered heel, the valise bumping once against her skirt every third stride. Cora stood in the hall and listened until the sound of her gave itself over to the wind.

When she turned back, Dell was propped on one elbow in the front room, white around the mouth from the pain of it. The broken alarm clock lay in pieces on the floor. One brass wheel had rolled under the stove-side chair. His good hand gripped the blanket hard enough to wrinkle it into ropes.

"Lie back," Cora said.

"No."

She did not have strength for another order, so she went and picked up the clock parts instead. The little spring had sprung clear. It would take him an hour to set it right, if he bothered.

"Was it bad," he asked.

Cora kept her eyes on the floorboards. "Yes."

"About Pa."

"Yes."

He breathed in shallow through his nose. "And the rest."

Only then did she look at him. He was watching her too directly now for a child and too wounded for a man and exactly seventeen in the miserable middle.

"What rest," she said, because cowardice does not always announce itself grandly. Sometimes it comes in the old tired grammar of delay.

Dell laughed once, then stopped because the laugh hurt. He pressed his good hand against the bandage on his ribs and shut his eyes until the pain passed.

"I heard enough," he said at last. "Enough to know it wasn't only him."

Cora stood there with the broken clock in her palms and no lie ready that could cover all the pieces.

Outside, the road to town held Thea somewhere on it, walking toward yeast and coffee and Hester's back room. Inside, Dell waited with his father's blood in him and questions she had spent two years trying to pace out one careful teaspoon at a time. The kitchen behind her still smelled of beans, camphor, lamp oil, and the paper that had not burned.

There was no sound in the house now except Dell's breathing, the kettle ticking itself cool on the stove, and the wind rubbing dust against the windows as if it meant to come in whether invited or not.
