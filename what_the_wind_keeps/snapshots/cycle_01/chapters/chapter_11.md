# Chapter 11

By daylight Saint's Basin still looked like a lie more than a place.

South of the Vale fields the wheat stubble thinned, the ground sagged, and the color changed from dead gold to a meaner gray-green that could have passed for any bad patch in a county full of them. If Dell had not gone out there with Hester and Otto Blum's map copy and cut down through the powder to black damp weeks ago, he might have walked over it the rest of his life and called it poor ground. This morning there were stakes driven in a rough arc, two shovels already standing in the dirt like grave markers, a pick, a spud bar borrowed from the school furnace shed, and a handful of townspeople trying not to admit they had come to see whether the basin would give up water or just one more public embarrassment.

The heat had started early. By nine the sun had a hard brass look through the dust haze and sweat was already making mud under Dell's hatband. He had his shirt stuck to his spine and the skin across his palms hot and thin from yesterday's handle work before they had even cut the first proper line. Hester stood at the edge of the marked trench with Otto's folding rule in one apron pocket and her cough tied down behind a square of faded muslin at her throat. She had hammered the stakes herself, though Dell had watched her have to stop twice to get her breath. Cora was in men's work gloves too big through the fingers, sleeves rolled high on her forearms. Thea had pinned her skirt up awkwardly for walking in ruts and tied a handkerchief over her dark hair. She looked wrong for a ditch and stubborn enough to stay in it all day.

Nine people, if he counted the boy.

Not all of them were there for the same thing. One woman with a hat strung under her chin by shoe ribbon had brought two empty Mason jars and kept pretending she had only brought them to carry water from the well if anybody needed a drink. A man with his boot toe wired shut had come because any rumor of water reached men like a bell. Another man, all collarbone and tobacco cough, had come because he hated August Poeschel worse than he feared looking foolish. Two others had come with the look people wore when they were too tired to choose between belief and ridicule and had decided to call both of them work. And little Rudy Eckert had come because boys came where tools were. His left suspender was mended with blue yarn instead of elastic, and he kept worrying the gap at the side of his mouth with his tongue where a tooth had come in crooked and stayed that way.

Dell saw the way the adults looked at him before they looked at the line in the ground. Seventeen, almost a man by labor and still a boy whenever anybody wanted him out of danger. He hated the switch in their faces more than he hated the heat. He was the one who had walked the grade. He was the one who had found where the old fill lay lighter over the drainage run. He knew how the slope wanted to carry off anything that turned loose. If the basin was there under all this and if the tile line had really cut it open and bled it dry, he could read the ground well enough to find the wound again. He trusted that more than he trusted memory, and he trusted memory more than he trusted what August said.

"Narrow at the top first," Hester said. Her voice had that dry bakery scrape in it before dawn, only now it was under a summer sky. "Don't give the wall more reason than it already has."

"I know how to cut a trench," Dell said.

Hester looked at him over the muslin. "Then prove it by not talking."

That got a short laugh out of the man with the wired boot toe. Dell took the shovel and went down where Hester had marked the first line. The dirt at the surface was loose enough to fool you, all powder and grit and old roots that gave with a dry hiss. Under that lay a packed layer that took the shovel with a shock up both arms. He liked that part best, if liking had anything to do with it. There was an answer in resistance. The handle told him what the eye could not. A stone knocked hard and clean. Old fill came up crumbly and mixed with trash root. Ground that had once held water and then been cut wrong had a different drag to it, a gummy reluctance that started below the wrists.

Cora came down beside him with the narrower spade. She did not look at him while she worked. She never did when she was afraid for him. She put her attention into the dirt itself, as if the right angle of steel could bully the world into decent behavior.

"Keep your feet under you," she said.

"I've got feet."

"You know what I mean."

He did. He hated that he did. The trench was shallow yet, barely to his shin, but the lip had already started to crumble where the upper dust slipped back into what they cleared. The field south wind came and went in rough breaths, enough to sting the eyes with loose grit and then die long enough to let the heat sit directly on the neck. Thea and the woman with the jars carried away spoil in buckets and on feed sacks. Rudy darted in for broken clods whenever nobody told him not to. Every few minutes Hester had them stop so she could sight down the stakes and adjust by inches.

"Not east," she said once. "It bent south there. Otto marked the bend for a reason."

"I know where the bend is," Dell said, more sharply than he meant.

Hester's eyes held him. "That's your wanting to be right talking. I'm talking about the dirt."

He drove the shovel harder after that, angry because she was right and angrier because she could hear the difference. Wanting to be right had gotten mixed up with wanting something else entirely, and he could not have separated the two if somebody had put a knife to him and told him to name it plain. Water, yes. Proof, yes. Something August could not argue into paper. Something about Ezra that belonged to the ground instead of to whispers in the bank hallway or the sound of his mother not saying his name for a whole evening after she came back from town white with rage. If Ezra had helped ruin this place, Dell wanted the dirt to say it. If he had only begged and been skinned anyway, Dell wanted that too. He was sick of adults standing over him with half a truth in each hand and acting like the split was mercy.

By ten the trench was knee-deep along the marked arc and narrow enough that only one person could work cleanly at a time. The man with the tobacco cough found the first tile by striking the spade edge against it and sending a dull clay note through the ground. Everybody stilled. Dell dropped to one knee and scraped with his fingers until the curve of fired tile showed through, orange-brown under the gray dirt, laid there by somebody who had meant water to go somewhere else.

Hester came down into the cut before Cora could stop her. She squatted slowly, old not from years alone but from labor, and touched the exposed tile as gently as if she were checking a sleeping child's forehead.

"There you are," she said, and for a second there was no irony in her at all.

The woman with the jars crossed herself. The man with the wired boot toe spat to the side and said, "Well, I'll be damned."

"Don't be yet," Hester said. "We've only found what stole it."

They widened along the tile and found where the line ran shallow, then deeper. Dell could feel the grade now. Whoever had laid the drain had been practical and mean in the way practical men often were. Take the low place, give the water an easier path, tell yourself the meadow would turn useful once it held wheat instead of frogs and reeds. He could almost admire the decision if he did not live in what it had made.

He was clearing a choke of roots where the tile joint had shifted when a motor sounded over the field path. Not fast. August never drove fast over rough ground, as if haste itself were vulgar. The dark Ford rolled into view with dust whitening its fenders and stopped crooked near the wagon Hester had brought for tools. August got out in a coat he had no business wearing in that heat, though he had taken the hat off and was rubbing a crease from the brim with his thumb the way he did when he wanted words to line up before he used them.

Dell knew before the man opened his mouth why he had come. Somebody in town had seen the shovels go out before breakfast. Somebody always did.

August took in the trench, the people, the exposed tile. His face changed only a little. It was still enough to satisfy Dell.

"Mrs. Vale," he said. He did not raise his voice. He never did if he could help it. "This needs to stop."

Cora straightened without climbing out of the cut. Dirt streaked one side of her neck where she had wiped sweat with the back of her wrist.

"You can keep walking, then."

"I am not speaking as a passerby. This ground is unstable, the bank has an interest in the property adjoining it, and there are children standing at the edge of a trench."

"Then get the children back from the edge," Hester said.

August looked at her, then at the muslin darkening at her throat where her cough had dampened it through. "Mrs. Blum, I mean this plainly. If this wall gives way, you will have a burial on your hands."

"We have had burials on our hands for years," Hester said. "You have only just taken notice of the dirt."

That landed in the air with the kind of silence that made people decide which side they were already on. The man with the wired boot toe shifted his weight closer to Hester. The woman with the jars moved the jars farther from August with both feet without seeming to do it on purpose. Rudy stared openly at August's car as if the shiny hood might yet prove more interesting than the trench.

August turned his eyes to Dell. Dell hated that most. When adults chose him it was either because they wanted him blamed or because they wanted him managed.

"Del," August said, using the smaller name like a claim. "Get out of there."

"No."

"You are not old enough to understand the liability here."

"I understand dirt fine."

"That is not the same thing."

"It sure is if you're the one in it."

Cora said, "Dell."

He knew what was in her voice. Come out now. Do not make me drag you by the shirt in front of half the town. Do not make me be only your mother when I need to be more than that for another ten minutes. He kept scraping at the tile seam as if he had not heard her.

August stepped closer to the lip. "Your father signed papers he did not understand because he believed labor could outtalk arithmetic. There is no honor in repeating him."

Dell stood so fast he cracked the back of his head on the dirt wall. Pain flashed white. He did not feel it properly.

"Don't you talk about him like you knew a damn thing."

Cora had gone still. Not quiet. Still in the dangerous way, all the movement pulled inward. Thea set down the bucket she was carrying and came closer, her face sharper than Dell had seen it at school or supper either one.

August's jaw shifted once. "I knew enough."

"Then say it straight," Dell said. His tongue stumbled over the start and made him hate himself more. "Say what he signed for. Say what you changed."

The crowd heard that. Dell felt them hear it. Some had likely known pieces already. Some had guessed. Nobody in Morrow ever carried a secret alone for long. But there was a difference between guesswork and a boy saying it into the open where the bank president had to answer or refuse.

August did not answer. That was answer enough. He let his gaze move from Dell to Cora and back again, looking for the place where family shame might do his work for him.

"This excavation ends now," he said. "If it does not, I will have the county look at it under safety authority before dusk."

"Then you'd better look quick," the man with the wired boot toe said. "We're not putting the dirt back for your convenience."

The woman with the jars said, "If there's wet under there, folks have a right to know."

August turned, startled enough by her to show it. She was maybe fifty, with a hat the color of old cream and a red seam ripper tucked through the ribbon because she never kept her hands out of mending long. Dell had seen her twice in Hester's bakery buying yesterday bread. He did not know her name. He liked her immediately for making August have to spend authority on somebody he considered beneath the price of it.

"A right to know what?" August asked.

"What was done," she said. "If you don't care for the wording, fetch another one."

Hester coughed then, a bad one. She bent at the waist and pressed the muslin hard to her mouth until it passed. Nobody moved to help because Hester hated being helped in the middle of work. When she straightened again her voice came out rougher and lower.

"Dell. Keep to the seam. Mrs. Vale, cut the north side back two inches. Miss Bell, get that boy out of the lip if he means to stare himself into the hole."

Thea put a hand lightly between Rudy's shoulder blades and steered him back. "You can carry the bucket and keep your legs," she said.

Rudy, blushing under dust, grabbed the bucket handle as if he had been given command of the whole operation.

August did not leave. He stood with one hand on the car door and watched while Dell went back to work because stopping under that gaze would have felt like laying down flat in front of him. Every shovelful came out heavier now. Damp had started somewhere below the tile, not enough to show yet but enough to change the smell. Not rain. Not the hot-cow stink of a stock pond either. Something greener than the rest of Morrow, buried and sour from being shut up.

Dell heard Cora breathing through her nose the way she did when she was angry enough to cut her own tongue if she opened her mouth too soon. She worked beside him in quick, exact strokes, shaving the north wall back and tossing spoil clear. Thea and the others widened the upper lip. Hester kept sighting the stakes. August stayed where he was, a black note at the edge of everything.

When Dell's shovel bit under the shifted tile and gave in a soft, sudden way, he knew before the dirt changed color.

"Hold," he said.

Nobody listened at first. The rhythm was in them by then. Dig, lift, throw, cough, wipe, dig. He straightened and said it louder.

"Hold up."

They did. Dell crouched, jammed his fingers into the spot under the tile, and pulled up a handful of cold black mud. Water did not run. It gathered. It shone inside the mud like something looking back.

For one absurd second nobody said a word.

Then the whole edge of the trench seemed to breathe in.

Cora came down beside him. Her glove darkened at the fingertips when she touched the mud. "It's wet," she said, and the words were so small they hit harder than shouting.

"Of course it's wet," August said from above, too quickly. "A low place after a season of storms can hold damp below the crust. That proves nothing except poor judgment."

Dell would have answered him, but the mud in Cora's glove had gone brighter than mud had any right to go. Not bright by light. Bright by charge. Wetness slicked the leather and suddenly Dell was not looking at his mother's hand anymore. He was looking at Otto Blum waist-deep in summer grass that reached almost to his elbows when the wind passed through it. Otto was younger than any of them had a right to remember him, hat shoved back, survey stake in one hand and anger in the other. He jabbed the stake down and turned toward men off to the side of the image.

"You cut this basin open," he said, the words wavering in the wet shine, "and the topsoil will go traveling. You won't stop it after."

The picture shook and vanished. Cora jerked her hand back hard enough to spatter Dell's shirt with black dots.

Hester made a sound in her throat that was not surprise. More like recognition arriving late.

"You saw him," she said.

"We all did," said the man with the tobacco cough.

He had. On the steel of his own shovel blade now, where a smear of mud ran thin, there were children on ice. Not one child. Five, maybe six, thick stockings under skirts, caps pulled down, coats flying open because movement itself kept them warm. The basin held winter water in the image, skimmed over silver and hard, and the children were cutting circles across it on skates with their arms out for balance. One girl threw back her head laughing and Dell knew, by the sharp nose and the scarf flung loose behind her, that the woman with the jars had not come for water alone. The woman's mouth opened with no sound in it.

"My sister," she said a moment later. "That was Lucy. Lord."

Rudy forgot his bucket and tried to lean in. Thea caught the back of his shirt before he could slide to his knees at the edge.

"Stay where your feet are," she said, but she was staring too.

The wet seemed to go looking for surfaces. Hands. Shovel backs. The curved lip of the exposed tile. A patch of water shining in the palm print Dell had left on the wall. Memory sprang and vanished too fast to settle, but not too fast to hurt. A boy in overalls driving geese from the reeds. A line of women lifting skirts to wade after a wash tub that had drifted free in spring flood. Otto again, older this time, kneeling to press black soil between finger and thumb while somebody in church clothes stood above him impatient and dry. The basin giving back not one thing but a crowd of things, as if the mud had been holding every witness in suspension waiting for touch.

People forgot caution first and then forgot sides. The woman with the jars was crying without trying not to. The man with the wired boot toe said, "Look there, look there," in a cracked voice he would probably deny later. Even August leaned in despite himself when a glaze of water spread over the tile joint and showed a row of men laying the drain line, sleeves rolled, boots sinking. One of them raised his head and Dell knew the set of the jaw before he let himself know it: Ezra, younger and not yet bowed, passing down tile like labor could pay for whatever bargain had begun outside the frame.

The tobacco-cough man crossed himself and said, "That isn't natural."

"Neither's draining a basin dry and calling it progress," the man with the wired boot toe said.

Dell dropped the shovel.

The memory shifted as if the wet had taken his shock for instruction. The trench wall beside his bare forearm darkened and threw up another image, closer, meaner. Ezra in August's office with his hat in both hands, not the last-year stoop but the beginnings of it, his face thinner than Dell liked to think it had ever been.

"One season," Ezra said. "Give me one honest season and I'll make it hold."

August, younger too, only half in the frame, said something Dell could not catch. The wet shivered. Ezra's mouth pulled tight like a man swallowing whatever was left of pride because swallowing still cost less than losing land in front of his wife. Then the wall was only dirt again.

Dell felt the whole trench tip under him though the ground had not moved.

He had wanted proof. Now proof was on his skin and it was worse than a lie because it showed want plain. Ezra wanting a season. Otto wanting the basin left alone. The town wanting yield out of something that had once only held water and birds and children in winter. Nobody clean in it. Nobody clean and everybody still hurting over what got taken.

August found his voice before anyone else found sense.

"Back from the edge," he said. "All of you. This is exactly the problem. Emotion is not engineering. The wall is sweating. Get back."

He was right about the wall. Dell could see the change now that he had stopped seeing only what he wanted. Damp had begun to stripe the cut, dark veins spreading through the pale face of the dirt. Grains loosened and trickled in quiet little runs. One clod let go from the south lip and dropped by his boot.

Hester saw it too. "Planks," she said. "We should have had more damned planks. Dell, out. Mrs. Vale, get him out while I think."

That should have been the moment. He knew it even then. The moment a sensible man took his proof and climbed clear. But there, under the tile, he could see a packed plug of root and silt where the line had collapsed in on itself. He understood it in an instant the same way he understood a jammed mower blade or a fan belt riding wrong. Break that choke and the seep would show stronger. Water would come plain enough even August could not sermon it away. He could make the ground answer now, not later after the county, not after the bank, not after the story got turned into paperwork again.

"Just one more cut," he said.

"No," Cora said.

"Dell," Thea said from above, and there was more alarm in the shape of his name than he wanted from her or anyone.

August said, "If you stay in there, you are choosing foolishness over witness."

That did it. Dell looked up at him and all he could hear beneath the words was the barn that night months ago, Ezra on the other side of the wall making the kind of sound a person was not supposed to hear from a father, and Dell staying in his bed because morning always came and because he had not wanted to be the child who told on despair. He had listened and done nothing, and in the morning there had been rope and afterward there had been years of adults using hush as if it were kindness.

Not this time.

He snatched up the spud bar, drove the point into the packed choke below the tile, and leaned his weight on it.

Cora cursed. Hester said his name like a warning dropped down a well. Somebody on the edge shouted for him to get out. He heard all of it and put his shoulder harder into the iron anyway. The choke held for one second, two. Then it gave with a wet sucking break.

Cold shot up around the bar. Not a gush. Not miracle. A harder, cleaner seep that pushed black water and mud into the trench bottom and made the whole cut smell suddenly alive, rank with roots and shut-up summer.

The crowd cried out as if one throat had been borrowed for all of them.

Rudy tore free of Thea's hand at exactly the wrong moment.

Maybe he meant only to see better. Maybe he meant to hand Dell the bucket because being useful mattered more to boys than fear did. Dell never knew. He only saw the blue-yarn suspender flash at the lip and then disappear as the boy's foot sheared the softened edge away. Rudy slid in sideways, arms pinwheeling, heels throwing mud.

Dell moved before thought caught up. The body knew some debts too well.

He dropped the bar, lunged through the seep, and caught Rudy under the armpits just as the boy hit the trench bottom. The impact jarred Dell to the teeth. Rudy was all bone and panic and sudden child-stink, not much weight except that fear made him grab. Dirt started ticking down from the south wall in a dry, quick pattern Dell understood with perfect clarity and too late.

"Take him," Dell shouted.

Cora was already flat on her stomach at the lip, one gloved arm reaching. Thea dropped beside her without hesitation, skirt dragging in mud, both women stretched over the edge so far Dell thought the wall would claim them next. Their shoulders slammed together. Neither noticed.

Dell got a hand in the seat of Rudy's overalls and heaved. The boy kicked once against Dell's bad leverage and then Cora had his wrist and Thea had the back of his shirt and they hauled him upward in one jerking motion that showed every person standing there exactly how practiced they were at moving toward the same danger together.

August saw it. Dell saw August see it. There was no time to do anything with that.

The south wall came in.

It did not roar at first. It sighed. A whole slice of wet dirt simply let go and folded toward him, taking the upper lip, a board, and half the space where he had been standing. Dell tried to turn with it, to throw his left arm up over his head the way you did when a cultivator blade kicked or a chain snapped. The collapsing wall hit his shoulder and chest and spun him sideways into the exposed tile. Something in his left forearm knocked once inside his skin, hard and private as a hammer on green wood. Then the dirt put its weight on him and the breath went out.

For a second there was no pain because there was no room for it. Only weight. Wet dirt over his hip, across his ribs, pinning the broken arm under a pressure so exact he could feel each pulse beat around it. His mouth filled with black grit and cold water. He spat and sucked in muddy air that smelled of roots, clay, and somebody's boot sole inches from his face.

Voices came from far off and right near both at once.

"Dell."

"Get back from the lip."

"Jesus Christ."

"Don't pull that side, you'll bring more."

His mother was saying his name. Not shouting. That was worse. He tried to answer and coughed dirt onto his own shirtfront. When he moved the left arm, white tore through him so fast it seemed clean, cleaner than the basin water, cleaner than thought. He made a sound he did not recognize until it was already out in the open for everybody.

"Don't," Hester said, close now, command riding over breathlessness. "Dell, don't fight the wall. We've got you. We've got you if you hold still."

Hold still, as if stillness had ever saved anybody much in this town. But he did. He could not have done otherwise. The weight on his ribs had turned breathing into a mean little trade, each swallow of air won shallow and paid back with pain.

He saw boots above him, edges of skirts, the dark belly of August's coat as the man came nearer despite everything. Hester was on her knees at the cut, muslin gone from her throat now, the cough forgotten. Her hands were already working the soil away from Dell's shoulder with the care of somebody turning loaves from tins. Cora dug barehanded because she had stripped off the gloves and lost them. Her wind-cracked fingers were black to the knuckles. Thea was beside her, not speaking much, only asking once, very calm, "Can you breathe?" as if calm itself might make him able.

He nodded because that was easier than saying mostly. Mud slid into his ear.

August said, "You need the plank under that section before you lift him."

Nobody answered. Then, to Dell's surprise, August crouched and reached for the board that had fallen half into the collapse. The man with the wired boot toe helped him. Between them they braced it against the slumping south face while Hester and Cora cleared Dell's chest enough for a fuller breath. The extra air hurt so much he nearly blacked out from it.

"Arm?" Thea asked.

He looked because he could not stop himself. The left forearm lay at a wrong angle under the muck, not grotesque, worse than that, familiar enough still to be his and wrong enough that the sight climbed into his stomach. Mud was plastered over the skin. The wrist had already begun to swell above his hand. He shut his eyes.

"Don't touch it yet," Hester said.

"I wasn't going to," Cora snapped, and the snap broke on the next word. "Dell, look at me."

He opened his eyes because she had asked in the voice from fever nights and bad weather, the one she used only when command had gone past anger into fear.

Her face above him was streaked with dirt except where sweat had made clean runnels. Thea had one hand flat between Cora's shoulder blades as if holding her in place. It was not a schoolteacher's touch. It was not a boarder's either. It was a touch that knew where Cora would break if she broke. Dell saw August's gaze flick there and away. Even half buried and half sick with pain, he knew that mattered.

"On three," Hester said. "We lift his right side first. Not the arm. August, if you mean to be useful, hold that plank and keep your mouth shut."

The man actually obeyed.

Rudy was crying somewhere behind them. Not loud anymore. Shame had gotten into it. Dell wanted to tell him to shut up, not because the boy deserved it but because listening to a child cry after being the reason the child had something to cry for made the whole thing worse. He could not spare the breath.

"One," Hester said.

Hands dug under Dell's right shoulder, his good arm, his hip.

"Two."

Thea said, very near his ear, "Bite your sleeve if you need to."

"Three."

They rolled him just enough to free the crushed side. Pain came all at once then, complete and indecent. It was in the arm first, a blinding bright line from wrist to elbow, then in the ribs where the wall had pinned him, a deeper spread that made the world narrow to a torn strip. He bit cloth and tasted mud and himself. Somebody said his name again and he thought it was Cora, but it might have been Thea. When they dragged him clear of the trench bottom and onto the stable ground, the sky above looked too large and yellow to belong to the same day.

He lay on a feed sack while Hester knelt by the broken arm and considered it with the terrible respect of a competent person. Thea brought the water bucket. Cora wiped mud from Dell's mouth with the corner of her own sleeve, then seemed to realize what she was doing only after the tenderness was already visible.

"Ribs?" Hester asked.

He tried a bigger breath and saw stars. "Hurts."

"That tells me very little."

"Then it hurts plenty."

The corner of Hester's mouth moved despite everything. "There he is."

She palpated his side with flour-broad fingers, gentle and merciless both. Dell jerked when she found the worst place.

"Bruised," she said. "Maybe cracked, but I don't think broken through. Arm is the ugly part."

"Can you set it?" Cora asked.

"Not here proper. I can make it travel."

Rudy appeared at the edge of Dell's vision, muddy to the thighs, face gone blotchy under dust. The blue yarn suspender had snapped loose completely and hung against his side. He had one of Dell's gloves clutched in both hands.

"I didn't mean-" the boy started.

Dell wanted to be cruel because pain made cruelty easy and because the whole town was looking. Instead he looked at Rudy's shaking chin and thought of a barn wall and the sound of somebody not being saved in time.

"I know," he said.

Rudy swallowed. "You shoved me up."

"Looks like I did."

The boy nodded once, hard, as if the fact needed driving into himself. Then he held out the muddy glove without stepping any closer.

Thea took it from him. "Go sit by the wagon wheel and stay there till your legs remember what they're for."

He obeyed at once. Thea's school voice had never entirely left her, even with mud to her calves.

Hester sent the man with the wired boot toe to the wagon for slats and sent the woman with the jars for binder twine. August said, "He should be moved before the swelling worsens," which was sensible and infuriating in equal measure. Cora did not look at him.

"Dell," she said. "I'm going to hold your elbow. Miss Bell will hold your wrist. Hester's going to straighten it enough for the sling. You hear me?"

He heard her. He wished he had not. He also heard the way she said Miss Bell instead of Thea with half the town standing there, and the way her hand found Thea's forearm before she let go again, a quick press as if the two names were fighting in her mouth and only one could come out safe.

"Do it," he said, because if he waited any longer he might start pleading and never stop.

Hester did not count down. That was kindness. She took the wrist from Thea, found the line the bones ought to have made, and pulled while Cora held Dell steady with both hands and a knee against his hip. The correction itself was brief. The body afterward was not. Pain ripped clear through him, so hard he could not hear the sound he made, only feel it scraping the inside of his throat. Thea's hand came over his mouth without thought, not to silence him exactly but to give him something to press against, and Cora bent so low above him their faces nearly touched.

"That's it," Thea said, too fast, then corrected herself into something steadier. "That's it. It's done."

Cora was crying and furious enough at the fact to make the tears look like another form of labor. Thea wiped one off her cheek with a muddy thumb before either of them remembered who was watching. Cora did not flinch from the touch. That was the part Dell saw August register with that banker stillness of his, the kind that made a man look calm only because he was storing what he meant to spend later.

The splint was ugly and workable: two wagon slats padded with folded feed sack, tied at wrist and below elbow, then hung in a sling ripped from one of Thea's petticoat ties and part of the woman's jar rag. Hester tied the knot herself.

"Don't faint before we get you to the house," she said.

"Wasn't planning on it."

"You don't plan most things that happen to you."

That would have been worth a better answer if Dell had had one. He was shaking now with the aftermath, not cold exactly, though the seep had put cold into his clothes. Every jolt of breath found the bruised ribs and worried them. He could smell the basin on himself, a dark wet smell under sweat and dust and fear. He hated how badly he wanted to look back at the trench.

He managed it anyway when they lifted him.

Saint's Basin had not turned beautiful. The cut was a wound in the earth, sloppy-sided and dangerous, with trampled spoil heaps and tools dropped wherever shock had broken hold of hands. But there, in the bottom where the choke had given, black water still pushed up in a narrow steady ooze and spread through the trench floor until it caught the light in patches. People stood around it differently now. Not like spectators. Not like owners either. Like mourners around a body that had coughed once after being declared dead.

The man with the wired boot toe had taken off his hat. The woman with the jars was crouched near the lip, not trying to fill them now, only watching the seep as if it might show Lucy skating again. Rudy sat by the wagon wheel with both elbows on his knees, staring at the mud on his shins as if he had never worn that much of the earth before. Hester, already swaying a little from the effort she would deny, pointed with two fingers and said, "Nobody goes back in that cut till I say. Shore it from both sides. Cover the lip if you've got boards. It's water, not a blessing."

August brushed mud from his cuff and failed. Dell almost laughed and turned it into a grimace when his ribs objected.

"This site will be closed under safety authority," August said. His voice had recovered its formal shell, but only mostly. There was a crack in it now, anger or fear or both. "I will not have another injury here."

"You'll have trouble stopping every person who saw what they saw," the woman with the jars said. Her shoe-ribbon hat had slipped sideways. She did not fix it.

August ignored her. He looked at Cora instead, then at Thea, then back to the splinted arm in Cora's hold. Dell could not tell whether the man was angrier at the seep, the crowd, or what he had just learned without being told.

"Mrs. Vale," August said, "your son needs a doctor."

"Then move your car out of the wagon track," Cora said.

It was not clever. It was better than clever. August stepped back.

They got Dell into Hester's wagon because it rode softer than the Ford over broken field. Cora climbed up first and settled with her back braced against the sideboard. Thea passed Dell's good arm over Cora's shoulders and then came up after him without asking where she was meant to ride. There was barely room. She made room anyway, one hand under the sling to keep the broken arm from swinging, the other pressed flat over the feed sack at Dell's ribs every time the wagon shifted as if she could hold the pain in place by force.

From that close Dell could smell dust in her hair and starch gone damp in her blouse. Cora's thigh was hard against his. Neither woman seemed aware anymore of how their bodies had arranged themselves around him, only of the fact that they had. On the ground below, August stood with his hat in one hand, looking up at them the way he looked at ledgers when a number had gone missing and he could not yet prove theft. Hester saw him looking. The corner of her mouth sharpened.

"You'll be late to the bank," she said.

August put the hat back on. "This is not finished."

"No," Hester said. "It isn't."

The wagon lurched forward. Pain ran bright from Dell's left arm to the base of his neck and settled behind his eyes. He bit it down and watched the basin slide away in pieces between the jolts: the black seam of seep at the trench bottom, the tools leaned careless in the spoil, the little crowd still held there by water and memory and the knowledge that the ground under all of Morrow might remember more than any of them could afford. He had wanted the place opened. He had wanted it enough to mistake urgency for skill. Saint's Basin had opened all right. It had only taken its payment in bone.
