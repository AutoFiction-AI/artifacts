# Chapter 1

By noon the light had gone the color of old brass. Cora Vale stood on the back step with a bucket of mash knocking her shin and looked west where the sky ought to have been empty. It was not empty. It had weight now, a low-bellied wall rolling in over the wheat stubble, brown at the base and black where it shouldered upward, as if somebody had taken the county to a stove pipe and drawn it through soot.

The warning bell from town started after she had already seen it. The sound came thin on the wind, foolishly late, and got cut to pieces before it reached the yard. She set the bucket down hard enough to slop mash over her shoe and said, to the yard and the hens and God if He was listening, "Damn."

Dell had ridden his bicycle into Morrow after dinner because Ulrich at the garage had said there might be two dollars in a magneto that needed coaxing back to life. Two dollars was lamp oil and coffee and maybe a strip of salt pork if the week turned generous, so she had let him go with a warning to keep one eye on the sky. Thea had gone the other direction on foot, neat as always even on a Sunday, because the north window at the schoolhouse never shut right and she could not stand the thought of readers and maps pasted into mud. She had taken her attendance ledger in a satchel she treated more carefully than most people treated an infant. Cora had watched both of them leave and thought the weather looked mean but not yet murderous. That had been less than an hour ago.

Now murder was coming in plain sight.

She crossed the yard at a run. The hens had already gone stupid with it, running the fence instead of the coop, their wings beating up little gusts of grit around their own feet. Cora flung feed into the dim of the shed to draw them in, then stooped and gathered two by the legs when the rest would not take the hint. Their bodies hit against her skirt, hot and frantic. One pecked her wrist hard enough to raise blood. She shoved them through the coop door, dropped the bar, and turned toward the lean-to where the milk cow had begun to bawl with that raw, offended sound cattle made before bad weather, as if the sky had personally insulted them.

The yard had changed while her back was turned. The west line of the world was gone. Fence posts that had stood black and plain against the fields a minute ago were fading from the bottom up. Dust moved ahead of the storm in low snakes over the ground, hissing through the yard grass, and when she licked her lips she tasted dirt that had not been there a breath before. The clothesline wire gave off a faint high whine. Static lifted the loose ends of her ash-blond hair against her temples.

She got the cow inside, dropped the latch bar, threw a burlap sack over the feed bin, and ran for the house. Every small thing had turned urgent at once: porch chairs, the wash boiler, the pan of peels meant for the chickens, the two front shutters that still hung open because she had wanted light in the parlor while she mended. Since Ezra died there had been nobody to say I'll get that one, and she had learned the ugly arithmetic of storm work alone. One body could not be in three places. One pair of hands never reached the last task in time.

At the kitchen door she snatched the wash boiler up by the handles and shoved it over the threshold with her knee. She hooked the screen, slammed the inner door, and moved room to room latching windows, dropping the wooden bars into place, pulling shades where there were shades to pull. Dust was already threading the seams. In the front room she fastened the west shutters and felt the whole house answer under her palm, a small shudder in the frame as the first hard push of wind struck broadside.

Dell, she thought. Then immediately, angrily, Thea.

She should have sent for them both sooner. She should have kept them close because the day had felt wrong from breakfast on, too warm for April and too still in the mean interval before wind. But that was farm thinking after the fact, the kind that made every mistake look preventable once it had already chosen you.

Cora wrapped a dish towel around her nose and mouth and opened the back door into a world that had gone sepia and loud. Wind took the edge of the door at once and tried to wrench it from her. She leaned her weight into it, got out onto the step, and cupped her hands around her mouth.

"Dell!"

The name blew back in her face. She could not hear herself say it.

She tried again toward the road, squinting through the dirt. Nothing held still long enough to be a person. The yard pump bent and straightened inside the haze like something underwater. A scrap of newspaper spun past so fast she only caught a single printed O before it vanished under the porch. She took two steps off the stoop, then three more, head down, apron snapping around her calves, and that was when she saw a shape at the front gate.

For one clean second relief hit her so hard she nearly laughed. Dell, she thought, and started around the corner of the house.

It was Thea.

She came through the gate sideways, one hand over her eyes, the other knotted in the strap of her satchel. Her hat was gone. Only the hatpin remained, caught crooked in the collar of her Sunday coat like a bit of broken wire. Dust had pasted her dark hair flat on one side of her head and turned the other side wild. Her stockings were laddered at one knee where she must have gone down in the road. Even half blind she still had the satchel cinched to her as if the attendance book mattered to the weather.

"Don't you fall now," Cora shouted, though she could not hear her own voice. She reached Thea just as the younger woman put out a hand and missed the porch post by a foot.

Thea's fingers closed on Cora's forearm with desperate exactness. They were colder than the wind. Dust had packed into the wet corners of her eyes and made gray tracks down her face where tears from the grit had run. Up close she smelled of wool, schoolroom chalk, and the hot mineral stink of storm dust.

"I know," Thea said, or tried to say. The words came raw through the cloth she had tied over her mouth. "You can scold me after."

"Inside."

Cora got an arm around her waist and hauled. The storm caught the front of Thea's coat and bellied it out behind her like a sail. For two stumbling steps it felt absurdly like dancing, the kind done badly at socials by women with no business laughing that hard, and then the porch took their shins and Cora slammed them both through the front door and drove the bolt home with both hands.

The house went dim and howling instead of black and howling. Dust came in under the sill in a fine brown line. Thea bent double coughing, one hand braced on her knee, the satchel still hanging from the other. Cora took it from her and set it on the table before the dust could work deeper into the flap.

"Sit down."

"I don't want to get the chair filthy."

"Everything's filthy."

That, for some reason, made Thea laugh once, sharp and breathless, before she coughed again. Cora took her by the elbow and led her to the kitchen pump. The water in the pail beside it had grit floating on top already. She poured what was cleanest into a basin, found a rag, and tipped Thea's face toward the light.

Her eyelashes were glued with mud. Cora wiped carefully, working from the inner corner out the way Hester had once shown her after Dell got chaff in his eye as a boy. Thea hissed and tried to pull back, then held still by force.

"You went all the way to the schoolhouse in this?" Cora said.

"It wasn't this when I left."

"No, just foolish."

"That too." Thea swallowed, then winced when more dust scratched her throat. "The north window had blown in. I thought I could pin the chart cabinet shut and get back before the worst of it. I was wrong in a very educational fashion."

"Did you see Dell?"

Thea's eyes opened at that. Even reddened and watering they stayed peculiarly exact, as if she could put the world into tidy lines by looking hard enough. "No. I saw a bicycle laid over in the ditch by the church, but I couldn't tell whose."

Cora set the rag down. The basin water had gone cloudy brown.

"It may not be his."

"No," Thea said, hearing the lie in it as clearly as Cora did. "It may not."

For a moment neither of them moved. Wind hammered the west side of the house. Something loose in the yard went skidding along the boards with a long scraping shriek. Thea reached up, perhaps meaning only to steady herself, and caught Cora's wrist. Her thumb landed on the inside of the pulse there, in the clean strip of skin above the flour on Cora's hand. It was not a proper touch. It was not even an accidental one, not with the way Thea's thumb stayed where it was for that one spare beat before she let go.

"Tell me what needs doing," she said.

Cora wanted, absurdly and with full knowledge of how mad it was, to bend and put her mouth against the dust-reddened lid of Thea's eye. Instead she shoved the cleaned rag into Thea's hand.

"Wet every towel you can find and jam them at the sills. Then go upstairs and shut your window proper this time."

"Yes, ma'am."

The faint shape of a smile touched Thea's mouth and was gone again. She moved off at once, already wringing the rag out over the basin. Even under pressure she did not waste motion. She set the satchel higher on the table, out of the line of dust, before she went for the towels.

Cora tied the dish towel harder over her face and went back to the front room. By the time she had knelt to stuff damp cloth into the crack beneath the window, the house was dark enough that she had to light the kerosene lamp on the piano. Noon had gone clear through afternoon and come out the far side into something like eclipse. The lamp flame wavered blue before it steadied. Each time the wind struck, the glass chimney clicked in its metal collar.

Upstairs, Thea's tread crossed the boards, paused, then crossed back fast. A moment later she came down with an armload of blankets.

"The window shut, but the top sash won't sit," she said. "I wedged it with the atlas no child has opened since Christmas."

"Good."

"The parlor north sill is leaking dust like a bad sermon leaks guilt."

"Then give me that blanket."

They worked without speaking for a few minutes, moving from room to room. The house grew smaller with every door they shut. Dust made its own way in regardless, through the keyhole, around the casing, between the floorboards where years of sweeping had not closed anything. It settled on the lamp chimney, on the spoon Cora had left by the stove, on the framed wedding photograph on the shelf where she had long ago stopped looking directly. When the wind rose harder, the hanging lamp in the kitchen swayed just enough to knock against its chain with a small metallic tick that got under her skin worse than the big sounds did.

The animals were no quieter. The cow hit the lean-to wall with her flank once, twice. Chickens battered at the coop boards as if they meant to come through them. Beyond that came the great sustained roar of the storm itself, too broad to call sound exactly. It filled the ears and pressed on the chest. It felt like being buried while still upright.

Cora set her hand on the back latch. "I'm going."

Thea, who had just risen from stuffing a towel under the pantry door, straightened so fast she almost knocked the chair over. "No."

"He's out there."

"And if you open that door now you'll be out there too."

"He's seventeen. He isn't windproof."

"Neither are you."

The answer came too quickly to sound rehearsed. It was not schoolteacher measured now. It was plain and frightened and a little angry. Thea stepped toward her, one hand still wet from the towel, leaving a dark print on her skirt where she had wiped it by habit.

"If you go," Thea said, more quietly, "and he finds his way back, he'll come to a shut house and no lamp in it. He'll think you went after him. Then he'll go back out."

Cora looked at the latch and hated that the sense of it landed. Wind shoved at the door from the other side as if to prove the point. The whole frame rattled in its casing. There was dirt in the corners of Cora's teeth from breathing inside her own kitchen.

"I should've made him stay in town once the sky turned."

"You didn't have the sky in your hand."

"No. Only the boy."

That stopped Thea. The younger woman took half a breath as if to answer, then did not. Her face changed in a small unguarded way that Cora had only seen in the hour just after waking, before Thea remembered to arrange herself for daylight. The lamp showed the dust on her cheekbones and the nick at the corner of her mouth where she worried the skin in her sleep.

Something slammed outside with a violence that made both of them jump. Then came the scream of torn metal.

"The shed roof," Cora said.

She was through the door before Thea could argue. The storm hit like a thrown load of sand. One instant she had a grip on the knob; the next she had both eyes squeezed shut and her head down against grit hard enough to sting through the towel. She got as far as the edge of the porch and saw almost nothing except the shape of the lean-to shuddering under the force of the wind, one strip of tin peeled up and banging itself to death.

Thea was beside her, though Cora had not heard the second door open. She had wrapped a blanket over her head and shoulders like a nun's veil dragged through hell. Together they leaned into the gusts and fought their way to the lean-to. Cora got the dangling strip of tin under one gloved hand and drove it back against the roof beam while Thea, who had no business being any use at such a job and knew it, jammed the pitchfork handle through the loosened bracket to pin it. The blanket whipped free and vanished at once into the storm. Thea swore once, with feeling.

"You do know how to improve a Sunday," Cora shouted.

Thea turned her face enough that Cora could see, even through the dirt, the flash of her grin. "I had other plans."

Then the cow kicked the inside wall so hard the boards boomed, and whatever wildness had broken across Thea's mouth disappeared. They made it back to the porch half blinded, dragging the spare sack of feed Cora had nearly forgotten. When the door shut behind them again, both women stood bent with their hands on their thighs, spitting mud into the sink one after the other like men coming up from a mine.

Dust had found the house in earnest now. The kitchen table held a visible film. So did the stove lid, the breadboard, the blue enamel coffee pot drying upside down by the basin. The air inside had thickened until the lamp light seemed to stop only a few feet from the glass.

Thea stripped off her coat and wrung grit from the hem with both hands. Underneath she wore the pale green dress she saved for Sundays, the one with the tiny self-covered buttons Cora always wanted to undo slowly and never did in daylight. The collar was torn where the hatpin had caught. Her stockings were ripped at the right knee. A bead of blood sat in the scrape and turned brown as Cora watched.

"Sit," Cora said again, this time with less force because what she wanted to do about the torn stocking was not something she could say aloud while the whole town might be blowing away outside.

Thea obeyed. That, too, was part of what made the thing between them feel dangerous. It was never only hunger. It was trust, and trust in Morrow was always the costlier article.

Cora fetched the iodine from the shelf and crouched before her. Thea started to protest, then saw her face and let it go.

"This will sting."

"Everything stings."

"Then you won't notice."

The joke was thin, but Thea gave her a look for the effort. Cora dabbed the scrape clean. Thea's knee was narrow and hard under her hand, the bone close, the skin cold from the storm. She felt the moment the younger woman stopped thinking about the sting and started thinking about the hand touching her. It changed the air between them more surely than the weather had changed the light.

From the road side of the house came a thud. Not the loose slap of debris. Something heavier. Then another, followed by a dragging knock against the front step.

Cora was already on her feet.

"Dell!" she shouted, and the name this time came from somewhere below speech.

She ran through the front room. Dust stirred around her ankles with every stride. Thea was right behind. Cora pulled the bolt, shouldered the door wide enough for a body, and the storm pushed Dell in on top of her.

He hit the floorboards on one knee and both hands, coughing so hard he could not get breath enough to speak. His cap was gone. His dust-light hair stood up in matted points, and his face was stripped raw where he had tied a bandanna too low and the grit had skinned the rest. His left ear bled from the edge like somebody had taken sandpaper to it. He still had his coat over his head, arms tangled in the sleeves, and when Cora hauled it free a spark plug and three cotter pins fell from his pocket and clinked across the floor.

Even now, she thought wildly, even with the whole county trying to scour him off it, he had not dropped machine parts.

Thea slammed the door behind them. Dell got one breath and managed, "Shut the damn draft," before doubling over again.

"It's shut." Cora knelt and got her hands on his shoulders, then his face, then his shoulders again because she could not seem to decide which part of him mattered most. "Where were you? Where in God's name were you?"

"Town." He spat black mud into the crook of his arm. "Where d'you think?"

"Don't get smart with me."

"I'm trying not to choke."

His voice cracked on the last word, and the sound of it cut straight through her temper into the place underneath where fear had been scraping since the bell rang. He was lean and long-limbed and mean-mouthed when embarrassed, all seventeen years of him trying daily to harden into a man, but at that angle on the floor with the grit packed in his lashes he looked about twelve.

Thea brought the basin without being asked. Dell turned his face away from her out of reflexive shame, which made her say, with schoolroom dryness that had been scoured down to its bones, "If you're going to die of pride, do it after you rinse your nose."

"Miss Bell," he managed, appalled.

"Yes, Mr. Vale. Blow."

He did, swearing under his breath at the pain of it, and the rag came away brown-black. Cora kept one hand between his shoulder blades while he coughed. Through his shirt she could feel each breath catch and jerk. He smelled of cold sweat, bicycle grease, and the bitter hot smell that rose from bodies after panic.

"I left the bicycle by the church ditch," he said when he could talk again. "Chain jumped, then the light went wrong. I thought I could walk it. Then I couldn't see the road. I had to crawl the fence line from Weller's place. Lost my cap. Damn near lost a shoe."

"You should've stayed in town."

"At whose house?"

There it was, the real answer, ugly for being practical. Everybody in Morrow had a house too full of its own trouble. If he had chosen wrong, he might have spent the storm in a shed or under a counter or being resented for the bread he ate afterward. Poverty never stayed private in weather like this.

Cora dragged his coat off completely and threw it toward the stove. Dust burst from it in a cloud. "I was about to come out after you."

Dell looked up then. Not defiant. Not even guilty at first. Just startled, as if the idea had not occurred to him that she would step into that blackness for him when she had the house and stock and Thea to hold together.

"You couldn't have found me."

"I would've come anyway."

"You'd have died in the yard."

"Then you'd have had two graves to mind."

The words came out before she could sand them smooth. The room went still around them despite the storm. Even Thea stopped moving.

Dell's face changed. The hard smart look he wore like a new coat slipped, and the boy under it saw her plain. Saw, Cora knew, not just that she had been frightened, but how far the fear had reached. Ezra had died months ago and still the house had not stopped learning the shape of absence. Dell saw then that she had been halfway to imagining the same shape for him.

"Ma," he said, very softly, in the voice he had not used in years unless fever had him or sleep.

She put the back of her wrist against his cheek because her hands were filthy and shaking both. "Get up. Let's get the grit off you before it beds in."

They moved him to the kitchen chair by the stove. Thea heated water. Cora stripped his shirt over his head and found the skin of his shoulders crosshatched red, as if the dust had tried to write on him. His ribs worked sharp under the flesh. There were fresh scrapes on both forearms and a deeper gouge in the heel of his hand where he must have hit gravel going down. When she poured warm water over the cloth and wiped him, the basin turned the color of ditch water in spring.

"Ulrich give you anything for the job?" she asked because the ordinary question felt like a rope she could still throw.

Dell gave a short, disbelieving laugh and coughed on the tail of it. "He gave me the spark plug and said if I got the thing running proper next time he'd see."

"Of course he did."

"It's a good plug."

Thea set a tin cup of water in front of him and, because she could not help herself, turned the fallen spark plug with one finger to inspect it. "He paid you in optimism and scrap metal. This town does have a gift for consistency."

Dell, raw-faced and exhausted, let out one real laugh at that. It hurt him. He put a hand to his ribs and swore.

"Don't encourage him," Cora said.

"I'm encouraging him to survive long enough to resent me tomorrow."

That sounded more like Thea, and because it sounded more like Thea Cora nearly sat down in relief. Instead she made coffee from the grounds she had been saving one extra day and cut the heel off the bread loaf though she had meant to keep it for supper. Dust gritted between her teeth every time she bit down. Dell drank, winced, drank again. Thea perched on the edge of the other chair with her skirt changed out for an old housedress and her torn stocking rolled down while it dried by the stove rail, one knee bare to the lamplight. Cora kept her eyes off it by force.

Outside, the storm held. Darkness at noon had gone on so long it became its own time of day. The lamp burned and burned. Once the sound dropped enough that Cora thought perhaps the worst was past, and then another wave hit, so dense and furious that the front windows went black as cellar walls. Dell flinched at that despite himself. Thea reached without looking and set two fingers on the table near his wrist, not touching, merely offering the fact of another body in the room. He did not move away.

"You should both sleep while you can," Cora said.

"On what breath?" Dell asked.

"On the one you've got."

But nobody slept. Dell dozed and jerked awake coughing. Thea went once to her room and came back with an extra pillow because the kitchen chair was sawing the back of his neck. Cora checked the stove, checked the back latch, checked the lean-to through the slit of the curtain though there was nothing to see but moving brown. She found herself counting objects to hold her head steady: six spoons in the drawer, three cups not chipped, one good lamp chimney, two quilts fit for another winter if the mice did not get them first. It was the kind of counting she had done after Ezra died, when the only way through a day had been to reduce it to what still remained and what absolutely could not be spared.

At some point Dell's head tipped forward onto his chest and stayed there. Thea rose to ease him into the little settle in the front room where he could stretch his legs. She had to wake him enough to move, and he came up mean with exhaustion.

"I'm not a child."

"No," Thea said. "Children complain less elegantly. Up."

He obeyed because she had been his teacher and because he had no strength left to defy anyone. Cora laid one of Ezra's old quilts over him once he was down. The wool still held, in some deep stubborn thread, the ghost of machine oil and the tobacco Ezra had quit twice and never really quit. She tucked the edge under Dell's shoulder and had to look away from the way his face, asleep at last, tipped younger again.

The front room looked as if a sack of flour had burst in it, if flour were bitter and mean. Dust lay on the piano keys, on the arms of the horsehair chairs, on the framed scripture from Ezra's mother, on the glass chimney of the lamp, on the photograph of Dell at nine with a fish too small to keep and a grin too large to hide. It had reached even the center of the table under the crocheted doily where Cora had thought nothing could touch it. There were days by then when grief felt less like sorrow than like housekeeping that never ended. Ezra kept arriving in the form of things that needed wiping down.

Thea stood beside her, close enough that the heat from her body reached through the dust-cooled cloth of Cora's sleeve. "He's all right for now."

"For now."

"You can sit down, Cora."

"After I get this room livable."

Thea made the small sound that meant she knew arguing would only waste breath. "Then at least let me sweep."

"You'll only raise it."

"You always say that as if I learned every useless thing except a broom."

"You did learn Latin."

Thea's mouth twitched. "For three years. I would rather have learned how to keep Nebraska out of a windowsill."

"Nobody's learned that."

They looked at each other then, and what passed between them was not comfort exactly. Comfort was too clean a word. It was hunger, yes, and relief, and the animal knowledge that all afternoon they had been one bad gust away from losing something they had not even been allowed to name aloud. Thea lifted her hand as if to touch Cora's face, then checked it and let it fall to the broom handle instead because Dell lay sleeping ten feet away and the windows, even shuttered, still belonged to the town.

"I'll fetch clean water," Thea said.

Cora nodded. She watched the younger woman go through the kitchen doorway, watched the line of her back in the dim and the careful way she favored the scraped knee without turning it theatrical. Then she took up a damp cloth and went to the front window.

She wanted only to clear a space enough to see whether the porch still stood. The dust on the pane had gone thick as sifted meal. When she laid the cloth to it, the surface dragged under her hand with a faint gritty squeal. She wiped a circle, then leaned close and breathed on the part she had missed.

The glass clouded.

At first she thought she was only looking at her own reflection gone strange in the moisture, her face warped by the wavering lamplight behind her. Then the fog on the pane deepened instead of thinning, and another room came forward in it.

Not this room.

August Poeschel's office rose inside the window as plain as if the bank wall had been built under her hand: the green-shaded lamp on his desk, the ledger stacks with their cloth backs rubbed pale, the narrow calendar tacked crooked beside the safe. August himself stood at the far side of the desk, heavy in the shoulders even with his coat off, his face shaved smooth as Sunday plateware. He had one palm over the upper corner of a document. The gesture was not dramatic. It was almost casual. That was what made it indecent.

Ezra sat bent over the desk in his work shirt with the cuffs frayed white. Not a remembered Ezra from courtship or harvest dances or the first year of Dell's life when he had still laughed with his whole chest. This was Ezra from the end: rangy and prematurely stooped, beard gone blue by supper, the tendon in his neck standing out when he swallowed. His right thumbnail was split to the quick. She had wrapped that thumb in rag three nights before he died after binder twine cut him open.

Cora did not move. She barely breathed.

August said something first, but the wind of the memory shredded the opening words. Then, clear as ink laid fresh, he said, "Here and here, Mr. Vale."

Ezra did not reach for the pen at once. His mouth moved. The pane gave her only part of it.

"You said till harvest."

August's hand smoothed the page once. Twice. "The bank will consider the extension when the papers are regular."

Ezra looked down. Whatever was in his face then was worse than fear and smaller than rage. Shame fit close. It made him look old. He took the pen. For one second Cora saw the exact stubborn set of his fingers, the way he always gripped too hard and pressed the nib as if writing were a thing to be wrestled. Then he bent and signed.

Not a refusal. Not a scene. Not the clean no he had come home with in his mouth that night when she asked whether August had tried to make him put his name to drainage paper.

He signed.

The moisture on the pane thinned at once. August's office broke apart into streaks. Ezra's hand vanished last, the black line of the pen dragging into nothing. Then there was only the Vale front window again, filmed in dust, giving back a blurred piece of her own face.

Behind her, in the kitchen, Thea set the water pail down. The sound was real enough to make Cora flinch.

She put her fingertips to the glass where Ezra's hand had been. The pane was cool. Grit clung to the damp of her breath and stuck to her skin.

He had sworn on her and on Dell that he had not signed. He had stood in this same front room, not six feet from this window, hat in both hands and the shame of August's visit still on him, and said, I didn't give him that. Not that. She had believed him because she loved him and because belief had been cheaper than opening the full account of what was rotting underneath their life.

Now the account had opened anyway.

"Cora?" Thea said from the doorway.

Cora kept looking at the window until the dust blurred flat and ordinary under her hand. When she finally turned, her face felt wrong on her skull, as if the storm had shifted more than weather.

"What's wrong?" Thea asked.

Cora looked past her toward the settle where Dell slept under Ezra's quilt, his mouth open a little for want of good air. She thought of August's hand covering the top of the paper. She thought of Ezra signing anyway. She thought of the lie she had lived inside since the barn, a lie so clean it had almost counted as mercy.

"Nothing," she said, because the word arrived first and because she did not yet know what name the other thing would bear.

But nothing was finished. Nothing was buried. The storm had left dust in the seams of the house and something worse in her hands: proof that Ezra had taken a pen from August Poeschel and put his name where he swore no name had gone. Whatever the weather had not stripped from her that day, this would. She knew that before the last of the wind let go the walls.
