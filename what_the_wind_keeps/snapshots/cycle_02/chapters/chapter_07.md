# Chapter 7

Cora had washed the oilcloth twice and it still held a gray bloom in the cracks, the table keeping faith with the weather instead of with her. She stood in the kitchen with the dishrag twisted tight between both hands and looked at the room August Poeschel had decided a federal man should see: stove blacked as well as cheap polish allowed, flour-sack curtains beaten and rehung, cups turned so the worst chips faced the wall, the enamel basin set mouth-down to hide the rust along the rim. None of it improved the house. It only showed that she had spent the morning trying.

August's note lay under the sugar jar where she had shoved it after breakfast. Mr. Walter Knapp of the resettlement office would call at eleven. Kindly remain on the premises. Kindly had been the word that raised the heat in her, more even than office. There was nowhere else for her to go at eleven on a Wednesday except the farm she was being asked to watch die in good order.

The wind was up enough to rasp the screen door, not enough yet to make a storm of itself. Dust moved low over the yard in nervous little drifts that gathered at the porch steps and broke apart. Out by the pump Dell had the hood off the truck again, though truck was grander than what sat there: a Ford with one fender tied on by wire and a temperament meaner than a kicked mule. He leaned in with a wrench in his fist, shirt stuck dark between his shoulders, wrists all bone and tendon. He had come in late the night before with damp earth on his hands and some tight silence he had not yet spent. When he straightened he looked older from the back than he ever did from the front.

"You want the hood down before he gets here?" he called.

"Leave it."

He wiped his forearm over his face and left a gray streak across his cheek. "He'll look at it and write down we're half junkyard."

"Then let him write true."

Dell made a face at the engine, not at her. "You want me in the house?"

"I want you civil."

"That so."

"Dell."

He set the wrench on the running board harder than he had to. "I heard you."

That was the trouble with him at seventeen. He heard accusation in everything before the words had even settled. Cora went to the door and stood with one hand on the frame. Beyond the truck the wheat fields lay pale and wrong, heads thin, the color not gold yet but a sickened green with gray worked through it, dust inside the stalk instead of merely lying on top. The south strip had blown bare in places. She could see the lighter scars from the porch. Men in offices called that exhaustion. Men who had not buried a husband in the middle of a planting year called it marginal land. She called it June.

By a quarter to eleven she had changed into the blue dress with the least shine at the seams and pinned her hair back harder than usual. The dress smelled faintly of starch and old sweat and the cedar chest. She had mended the left cuff two nights before by lamplight. Now the stitches showed because she knew where they were, and because she knew, she could not stop seeing them.

Dell came up the steps and stood just inside the kitchen, stalled there for a second before he remembered what he meant to fetch. His hair needed cutting. The dust had bleached it nearly white at the top.

"You don't have to pour him coffee," he said.

"I know that."

"I'm saying you don't."

She looked at him then. There was Ezra in the set of the mouth when he was about to make trouble for the sake of not feeling young. There was Ezra nowhere else. "You think I don't know what a man comes for when he rides out with August?"

Dell's eyes slid off hers to the stove. "Maybe he comes to see what a dead place looks like."

"Then he can use his own damn eyes."

Something like satisfaction crossed the boy's face at the curse. He nodded once and went back out, taking the sound of his boots with him. Cora lifted the kettle anyway. There were rules older than debt, older than August, and one of them said you did not let a stranger cross your threshold into heat and grit without offering coffee if you had coffee to spare. Another said a woman alone with a farm note against her name did not get to choose which rules still protected her.

The car announced itself before she saw it. Tires on dry ruts made a flat grinding complaint, then the horn gave one short official bark at the bend in the road where no horn was needed. August always sounded himself where the house could hear. Cora stepped onto the porch and wiped her palms down her skirt.

The bank car came through its own pale cloud and stopped near the pump. August climbed out first, broad in his town coat despite the heat, collar buttoned, hat brim carrying the same dust as everybody else's but somehow not submitting to it. He paused to brush his sleeves with quick exact strokes before turning to the passenger side.

Walter Knapp unfolded himself from the car with the care of a man used to trains and offices more than rutted county roads, but there was nothing soft in the way he landed. He was trim, narrow in the hips, a year or two shy of forty by the look of him, and his hat was a good one that had lost the argument with Nebraska before noon. He took from his pocket a small clothes brush with a cedar handle and whisked the crown and brim as naturally as another man might wipe his mouth. Then he tucked the brush away and gave the house the courtesy of a full look.

"Mrs. Vale," August said. "Thank you for remaining available."

"I was under the impression I lived here."

August's mouth tightened without changing shape. "This is Mr. Walter Knapp, from the federal office."

Knapp took off his hat. His hair underneath was already damp and flattened by sweat. "Mrs. Vale." His voice carried no town ownership in it. Lincoln maybe, or farther east. "I appreciate you seeing me."

She had expected a government smile, something polished and false. What she got was tiredness under discipline, which made her dislike him faster. "You came all the way out. I'd look poor company if I hid in the chicken shed."

That almost got a smile from him, but he thought better of it. His gaze moved to Dell by the truck, to the pump, to the drift packed against the north side of the porch, and back to her. He missed less than was polite.

August said, "If we can begin with the ground, Mr. Knapp."

"Ground's still there," Cora said. "I haven't mislaid it since breakfast."

Dell coughed into his fist, which could have been laughter. August ignored him with the effort of a churchgoing man ignoring a fly.

They started with the yard and the outbuildings. Knapp walked without hurrying and crouched where he wanted a closer look, then brushed his knees afterward with the same little cedar brush, persistent as a man who thought manners might still win an argument with dirt. He asked what had been planted and what had failed, and Cora answered before August could make the farm his story.

"Wheat on the north and west. Kitchen patch mostly gave up after the May blow. Hens still laying enough to keep themselves."

"Any hogs?"

"Not now."

"Milk cow?"

"Sold her in March."

He wrote that down in pencil rather than fountain pen. A notebook bound in black cloth went in and out of his hand with the ease of practice. August carried a folder instead, thick and official, papers squared so hard the edges could have cut.

At the shed Knapp looked over the harness, the hand tools hung on nails, the seed sacks rolled and tied against mice. He did not poke or smirk. That made it worse. He lifted the broken singletree, saw how Dell had wrapped the split with baling wire, and set it back exactly where it had been. In the truck bed the last of the spare parts sat in coffee tins and a wooden peach crate. Dell stepped closer before anybody asked him to, shoulder high and defensive.

"You keep this running?" Knapp said.

"Some days."

"How old are you?"

"Seventeen."

"Still in school?"

"When they can find enough children to call it that."

"Dell," Cora said.

"It's a fair answer," Knapp said, and wrote again.

The boy's face went closed. He hated being made into a line on paper. Cora hated that she understood, and that there was nothing she could do while standing three feet away.

They walked the field edge next, August speaking acreage and arrears whenever there was space for him to fit them in. Cora heard her own life become columns: past-due interest, tax exposure, yield depression. The phrases arrived clean and fat as banker fingers. Knapp stopped at a place where the topsoil had scudded off and the ground showed lighter beneath, hard and veined.

He stooped, scooped a handful, rubbed it between thumb and forefinger, and let it run out. "How deep has the drift been laying along this fence after the last blows?"

"Knee-high some mornings. Bare by supper if the wind changes."

"Any standing water anywhere on the tract after a rain?"

"Not where it does a body any good."

August said, "The well remains serviceable, but the land is no longer behaving as productive acreage ought."

Cora turned on him. "You want it productive after three years of this?"

"I want clarity."

"No. You want obedience."

Knapp straightened and slipped the notebook into his pocket. "Mrs. Vale, I'm not here to quarrel over names for weather."

"That's useful," she said. "Because weather's been here longer."

He took that too without bowing or bristling. Then he looked past her toward the south, where the field rolled away and the air above it danced with heat already though the day was not old. "Some land should not be asked for one more crop just because paper says it still belongs in a ledger."

There it was, neither threat nor contempt, only the calm sentence of a man who had seen enough ruined places to speak of them as categories. Cora felt the back of her neck go hot under her pinned hair. Beside her Dell shifted his weight. August watched nobody; he watched the process.

"It's not the land asking," she said. "It's us."

Knapp's eyes came back to hers. "Yes, ma'am. I know."

He did know, or enough of it to make the knowing intolerable.

Inside the house the air held the cooked-metal smell of the stove though the fire had gone out an hour before. Cora had opened the back window for movement and now wished she had not; dust had gathered on the sill in a new fine line while they were in the yard. Knapp stopped on the threshold, removed his hat again, and gave it another quick brushing before stepping over. The gesture was almost comical. It also told her something plain: the man could not bear grit, and yet his work led him from one grit-choked family to another all summer long. He must have gone to bed every night tasting other people's losses.

"Kitchen first is fine," he said.

"Kitchen's what there is most of."

He looked at the table, the stove, the shelves. Nothing in the room had escaped being handled thin. The blue willow plate over the door had a crack running from rim to river. Two chair seats had been caned with binder twine after the old rush gave out. August remained standing, one hand on the back of the nearest chair, holding to the form that he would not sit before being invited though he clearly meant to sit once the business began.

Knapp asked, "How many in household at present?"

"Myself and my son."

August added, "Miss Bell boards here while school remains open."

Cora could have struck him for saying her name that way, reducing Thea to a line item he had been waiting to produce.

Knapp nodded. "Teacher."

"Yes."

"May I see the occupied rooms?"

For one mean instant she thought of saying no, just to watch what August's face did. Then she thought of what refusal would be written down. She led them through the front room first, where the sofa springs pushed through the upholstery under the antimacassar and Ezra's old chair sat by the cold stove like a witness nobody had dismissed. Dust had worked into the clock glass again. She hated that they would see that before they saw anything else. The house had sweated fear for them.

Her bedroom came next. Knapp kept his eyes workmanlike, taking in the bed, the trunk, the washstand, the dresser drawer that stuck unless lifted as it pulled. He noted the patched mosquito netting and the water stain in the ceiling corner. He kept his face empty and offered no pity. That lack of performance stripped her harder than pity would have.

Then Thea's room. Cora opened the door and stood aside while something private and frightened scraped its nails down her spine. The room was neat in Thea's particular way. Books were stacked square on the crate by the bed, school copybooks tied with string, a blouse with fresh collar stays hanging from the peg, and on the sill sat a little saucer with three hatpins in it and one button waiting to be sewn back on something worth saving.

Knapp took it in with a glance no longer than the others. "Miss Bell pays monthly?"

"She does."

August said, "Her board has been material to the household's cash balance."

Cora did not look at him because if she had, Dell would have seen too much in her face. Thea's room, their room-not-their-room, was reduced to board, money, utility. Yet that was what kept the door respectable. That was how women survived here, by pretending every need arrived with a receipt.

Knapp wrote a line and closed the notebook. "That will do."

The pantry undid her more than the bedrooms, not because there was less in it than there ought to be; she already knew that. It was the decent tone he used when he asked whether she had enough flour for the month, enough jars, enough soap, enough coal oil to last if the road went bad, and she answered him like a woman applying for charity though she had grown much of what had ever entered that room. There were beans in a crock, two sacks of flour with one already low, salt pork almost gone, coffee enough if she mixed chicory in, and soap shaved thin to save it. Every answer felt like laying her underskirts on the line in front of the bank.

At last he said, "Thank you. We can sit down."

August had been waiting for that sentence from the moment he stepped out of the car.

Cora poured coffee into three cups because Dell had gone back outside and because she would not force him into the room for the signing of his own uprooting. Her hand stayed steady until August opened the folder. Then the cups clicked in their saucers. The sound seemed loud enough to bring the chickens up under the window.

The papers were not a single sheet but a clipped stack of forms, typed pages, a county plat, and something with the bank letterhead at top. August set them on the oilcloth where she had scrubbed until her wrists ached that morning. Knapp laid his hat on his knee and folded his hands.

"Mrs. Vale," August said, "Mr. Knapp's office is prepared to consider this tract under the resettlement and land-retirement program, contingent on cooperation."

"Land-retirement," she repeated. "That's a nice clean way to say bury it."

"It is a way to say it without melodrama."

"You'd know about that."

Knapp broke in before August could answer. "Let me say it plain. Some farms out here are too far gone in credit, water, or soil to carry a family without stripping the family first. If a place can be taken out of production before a sale scatters everything you own, there can sometimes be some room left for the living."

"Room where?"

He did not blink. "That depends on openings, kin, county relief arrangements, and whether a receiving place can be found. I can't promise a better farm. I can sometimes prevent a worse end."

There was the mercy of it, small as a hand on a fevered head and just as helpless against the disease.

August tapped the top sheet. "With your signature, the matter can proceed cooperatively. There would be appraisal, inventory, and a voluntary relinquishment of occupancy when the transfer is arranged."

She leaned forward. The paper smelled of dust and ribbon ink and August's office. VOLUNTARY COOPERATION STATEMENT was typed across the top in hard black letters. Lower down: exhausted acreage. submarginal use. occupant consents to vacate upon notice. Her own house, translated into words fit for a shovel.

"Voluntary," she said.

Knapp said, "I know how that reads."

"Do you."

"Yes."

"Because it reads like a man standing in my kitchen with the bank at his elbow and asking whether I'd care to bless my own funeral."

August said, "Nobody is asking you for blessing. We are asking for recognition of fact."

"Fact is my feet are on this floor."

"Fact is the note stands at twelve hundred and eighty dollars plus interest, the taxes are behind, the crop is failing, and the town cannot maintain fictions indefinitely."

She heard Dell outside at the truck, not moving now but listening. The shame of that hit low and dirty. She lowered her voice anyway, which made the room feel even smaller.

"And if I sign, what do I get besides better wording for losing it?"

Knapp answered before August could. "A chance at managed removal instead of a forced sale. Time to sort what can be moved. Possibly transportation help. Possibly placement if something opens. Some priority with relief, depending on county cooperation."

"Possibly," she said.

"I won't lie to you."

"No. You'll tell it neat."

He let that stand. August did not.

"The alternative is not dignity, Mrs. Vale. The alternative is deterioration followed by action under the note."

"You mean auction."

"If necessary."

She looked again at the form. August had placed the pen beside it already uncapped, a little black thing with a gold nib, ready as a tooth. What snagged her was not the threat. August had threatened before. It was the eagerness under the smoothness. He wanted her name on this paper too badly. He wanted it with the hungry patience of a man who needs a door opened from the inside because he does not trust the lock from his side.

"Why now?" she asked.

August's gaze did not shift. "Because conditions are now plain."

"They've been plain all spring."

"Not to the federal office."

"Then why do you need me to say it for them?"

Silence. Knapp's head turned, not much, but enough. Under the cooperation form another sheet had worked loose in the clip, showing a purple county stamp and a date line struck darker than the rest from a second hard hit of the typewriter. August placed two fingers flat over that corner, almost caressing it, and slid the stack square.

"Because that is how orderly transitions are made," he said.

She nearly laughed. The word orderly in this kitchen, with dust in the sugar and a dead man's chair cooling the front room. It would have been funny if it had not been so ugly.

Knapp said, "Mrs. Vale, I've been in places where they waited too long because pride and grief were doing the talking, and then there was no furniture left worth moving and no stock left worth selling and the children went into town in the same clothes they had slept in for a week. I don't think you're a fool. I don't think this is easy. I think this may be the last moment in which somebody can keep a little shape around what is happening to you."

That should have softened her. It should have. Instead it brought a hard bright anger up through her chest because the man was offering shape, treating shape as all a life amounted to once the wheat failed.

"What if I'm not interested in a little shape around it?" she said. "What if I'm interested in not calling my farm dead before you've even dug the hole?"

Knapp rubbed his thumb once over the edge of his hat brim. "Then I would tell you the hole has been under your feet a while."

August seized the moment. "You have delayed enough, Cora. Sign the form."

He used her given name only when he meant to show he believed ownership and intimacy were cousins. She hated that too.

"No."

The word came out flat, with no speech and no higher ground in it, only the fact of her mouth making a wall.

August's expression changed at last, not into anger but into a colder precision. "I advise you to reconsider."

"Do your advising elsewhere."

"Mrs. Vale," Knapp said quietly.

She turned on him. "If you want me off this place, put your names under it. I won't help either of you pretend I chose the spade."

The room held still. Outside, something loose on the truck tapped once against metal in the wind.

August capped the pen with great care. "Very well."

Knapp said, "Mr. Poeschel."

August did not even turn toward him. "In the absence of cooperation, I will move the note forward. You have ten days before I post formal foreclosure notice and begin publication toward sale."

Dell made a sound outside, not a word, just air hitting anger.

Cora stared at August. The ten days did not mean a deputy on the porch by next week. She knew enough to know that. It meant notices, posting, county steps, men coming to price a life by the piece. But ten days meant he had brought the threat sharpened and waiting in the folder before she ever refused. He had come wanting either her surrender or his excuse.

"You had that ready already," she said.

"I had several eventualities ready."

"Christ."

Knapp looked tired suddenly clear through, the heat finally reaching the place in him he kept separate for work. "That's not what I came for."

"But you'll ride away with it," Cora said.

He did not answer. That was answer enough.

August gathered the papers into their stack. The county-stamped sheet vanished again under the plat, leaving her land reduced to lines and fractions, to home place and north strip, with no room anywhere for Ezra's rope in the barn or Dell's bare feet in summer or the place on the porch rail where Thea set her hand when she laughed and forgot to guard herself.

August stood. "I will expect either payment sufficient to cure the arrearage or a change in your disposition before the tenth day."

"You'll expect till hell freezes."

"Hell," August said, settling his hat on his head, "has already shown an unfortunate gift for weather."

That was the nearest thing to profanity or poetry she had ever heard from him. It left a bad taste in the room.

Knapp rose more slowly. He took his hat, then seemed to think better of something he had nearly said. What came instead was, "Mrs. Vale, if you do change your mind, send word through the bank before the notice goes up. A posted paper brings out a certain kind of man."

"I know the kind."

He looked at the oilcloth, at the coffee he had barely touched, at the place on the shelf where the plates did not match. Then he nodded once, a gesture too respectful to be pity and too late to help, and followed August to the porch.

Cora stood where she was until the screen door slapped shut behind them. Only then did she realize her palms were wet. She had pressed them so hard into the edge of the table that the oilcloth's cracked seam had stamped itself in a red line across the heel of each hand.

Dell came into the doorway from outside and stopped there. His face had gone bloodless under the dust. "Ten days."

"To notice."

"Same difference."

"No. It's not."

"It's if you've got no money."

That one landed because it was plain, with no rhetoric in it, only the truth spoken by her son in front of the place where those men had sat.

"Go water the hens," she said.

"I'm not eight."

"Then act older."

He held himself there another second, jaw working, then shoved back through the screen and went around the house hard enough to startle the chickens into noise.

Cora moved to the porch. August and Knapp were by the car, thinking themselves out of earshot because men in town shoes always guessed wrong about country distances. The wind carried words in pieces.

"...cannot run every file by sentiment," August was saying.

Knapp answered lower. "I said nothing about sentiment. I said pressure this blunt may spoil cooperation."

"There is no cooperation to spoil."

"Then what you want is consent after the fact."

August brushed dust from the car door with his handkerchief. "What I want is a clean line through an irregular situation."

Irregular. The word came thin and bright into her like a fishhook.

Knapp said, "Out here there are no clean lines."

"There have to be on paper."

The car doors shut. Gravel ground under the tires. They drove off with August at the wheel and Knapp turned half around in the seat, looking back once at the house with the restless air of a man who had forgotten something he could not name. Then the road bent, and they were gone.

The kitchen after them smelled of coffee and men's wool and the hot metal of August's pen where it had lain a minute on the oilcloth. Cora went inside, lifted the cup Knapp had used, and saw a neat ring of dust settled in the bottom where the coffee had cooled untouched. She threw it into the dishpan hard enough to chip the rim another sliver.

By the time Thea came home the light had thickened toward evening, not storm-dark, just the long yellow flattening that made every object in the house look tired before its time. Cora had scrubbed the table again. August's papers had left no mark she could see, but she kept wiping the same square, trying to work the typed words back up through the cloth by sheer friction.

Thea opened the front door with her satchel against her hip and stopped at the sight of her. School dust lay fine on her dark hair and had dulled the blue of her collar. One glove was tucked into her belt because she had not had patience to button it back on. She looked over Cora's shoulder at the table, the untouched supper things, the cup in the dishpan with the fresh chip in it.

"He came."

"He did."

Thea set the satchel down quietly. "August too."

"Like a preacher with a coffin catalog."

That wrong-time laugh flashed out of Thea before she could stop it, brief and shocked. Then she pressed her lips together. "I'm sorry. I know that's not funny."

"It was a little."

Thea crossed the room. She did not reach for Cora at once. Dell might be at the window, in the yard, on the porch. The house had thin walls and a boy's ears. Instead she took off her hat and set it on the sideboard, buying them both a second. "What did they want exactly?"

Cora pulled August's note from under the sugar jar, then thought better of that and put it back. "A form. Voluntary cooperation, they called it. Voluntary land retirement. Voluntary vacating my own house when told."

Thea's face altered by degrees, not dramatic, just every careful line in it drawing tighter. "And if you signed?"

"I might get to lose everything in a cleaner order."

"That sounds like Knapp."

"He said it plainer than August. Which made him worse."

Thea leaned one hand on the table. Her fingers were ink-smudged at the side from school papers. "Did he offer actual placement or only the promise of being considered for one?"

"Considered. Possibly. If kin could be found, if county relief, if some office somewhere had room on a shelf for me and Dell."

"God."

"That from you?"

"I teach fractions to six children in a room built for twenty-eight. I'm allowed one God when the federal government turns human beings into a filing problem."

The line should have comforted her. Instead Cora felt the humiliation rise again, hotter because Thea could name the machinery of it. "August wanted the signature more than Knapp did."

Thea looked up sharply. "What do you mean?"

Cora gave her the quick ugly pieces only: the walk through the rooms, the pantry counted out loud, August setting down the pen before she had even answered, the second paper under the cooperation form half-hidden in the clip with a purple county stamp on it and the date line mashed darker than the rest. His fingers had gone over that corner the minute she asked why he needed her. Then, out by the car, the word irregular. She kept her voice low and fast, trying to get through the facts before they changed shape on her tongue and ruined whatever use they still had.

Thea listened with the stillness that always made Cora feel both steadied and seen too thoroughly. Twice she started to interrupt and did not. When Cora finished, the only sounds were the screen door ticking in the breeze and Dell somewhere out by the shed knocking metal against metal for no good reason except that he was young and angry.

"He said irregular," Cora said. "Could've meant the farm. Could've meant that paper. I don't know."

Thea's brow furrowed. "A county stamp?"

"Purple. I only saw the corner."

Thea drew in breath through her nose and let it out slowly. Under pressure she always got more precise for a moment; grammar became a handhold. "That might be nothing more than bad office work. It might be a filing copy he didn't want read beside the form. What I know is he didn't want Knapp and you looking at the same page at the same time."

Cora pressed the wet rag flat against the table. Water seeped between her fingers and darkened the oilcloth. "So I'm not crazy."

"I didn't say that either," Thea said, and the edge of a wrong-time smile touched her mouth before it went. "I said suspicion is earned. Certainty isn't. Not yet."

There was the teacher in her and the hungry private woman beneath the teacher both at once, the one who could parse a sentence until it bled and the one who knew exactly where power hid when it dressed itself as manners.

Cora laid the rag aside. Her hands ached from gripping it. "He wanted my name on the top page so nobody had to turn the rest."

"Maybe," Thea said. "Or maybe he only wanted the quickest road open to him. Either way, don't bless it."

For a moment neither moved. Cora could feel the whole day sitting on her skin: Knapp's notebook, August's pen, Dell outside listening to men price the walls. Thea was close enough that Cora could smell chalk and the clean stale scent of schoolhouse dust in her clothes, different from farm dust, finer somehow, with chalk, paper, and old bindings all ground into it. She wanted to put her head against Thea's shoulder like a tired child. She wanted to tear the kitchen apart with her hands. Wanting both at once made her feel mean.

Thea touched the back of the chair between them instead of touching her. "Come upstairs a minute."

"Dell's outside."

"I know."

"He heard enough already."

"Then sit down at least before you fall down and give him another thing to remember."

That was not tender. It was better than tender. Cora sat because her knees had begun to feel hollow without her admitting it. The chair creaked under her. Thea went to the stove, poured what was left of the morning's water into the kettle, and set it on to warm though neither of them wanted more coffee. She needed something for her hands.

"What did Dell say?" she asked.

"Asked if ten days was the same as done."

"And what did you tell him?"

"That it was notice, not done."

"Was that for him or for you?"

Cora gave her a look.

Thea held it. "I'm not trying to be cruel."

"You can stop being careful with me for one hour."

"I do stop," Thea said, and there was the private edge in her now, the one Cora knew from the dark. "You simply prefer me brave and useful over honest and frightened."

Cora turned that over and hated how much of it was true. The kettle ticked as it heated. Outside Dell swore at something mechanical, the profanity thin in the evening air.

"I'm frightened," Cora said at last. Saying it plain cost more than any curse she had spent all day. "Not of leaving. Of letting him write the leaving. Of putting my own hand under his story."

Thea's face changed then. The hard schoolhouse control in it eased and the other self came closer, the one who could look at Cora and make the room alter around her. "I know."

She reached out finally and laid two fingers against Cora's wrist where it rested on the table. Light pressure only. Anyone opening the door would see nothing that required naming. Cora felt the touch all the way up to her shoulder.

"We could still go," Thea said softly. "Before he posts anything. Before the town gets its mouth on it. Lincoln or even North Platte first. We could take the train from the spur and let him sell dust and nails."

There it was: not the interview itself, not the whole life folded inside that letter, but the road out of Morrow set on the table between them like a second form waiting for signature.

Cora closed her hand over Thea's fingers for one heartbeat, maybe two, then let go. "Not yet."

Thea looked down. "Because of Dell."

"Because of August."

"Cora."

"He wants a clean line through something dirty. He can have the dirt first."

Thea laughed once again, without humor this time. "That's the most alarming thing you've said to me all month."

"You knew I was alarming when you moved in."

"I knew you were stubborn. That's not always the same."

The kettle began to murmur. Thea rose to take it off before it boiled. She poured the hot water into a chipped bowl, found the cleanest cloth from the line by the stove, and wrung it out. Then she came behind Cora and laid the heat at the back of her neck where the pins had held her hair too tight all day.

Cora shut her eyes, not from relief exactly but from the shock of being touched where those men had looked but not seen. The cloth smelled of soap and sun and the last dusting of wind from the line.

"He inspected your room," she said.

Thea's hand paused. "Mine."

"Yours. Asked if you paid monthly."

After a second Thea said, "Did you tell him yes?"

"What else."

"Nothing else?"

Cora opened her eyes and turned her head just enough to catch Thea's face in profile. "Nothing else."

Thea nodded. The cloth moved again, slower now. "Then he saw a room. Let him die knowing only that much."

Something in Cora almost broke on that sentence, not because it was grand but because it was small and practical and exactly the size of the mercy available to them.

The screen door banged and Dell came in without knocking, carrying a chicken-water bucket and a mood like weather. He stopped when he saw them, not because anything was visible in the room beyond a schoolteacher standing behind his mother with a cloth in her hand, but because boys his age could smell currents they did not understand and hated not understanding.

"You making supper or holding meeting?" he asked.

Thea moved away first. "Supper, if you're willing to eat what there is."

"I'm willing."

"Then shell peas," Cora said.

"There aren't enough peas to shell."

"Then snap beans and quit talking."

Dell set down the bucket and took the bowl from the shelf with exaggerated martyrdom. Yet the compliance in it eased something by half an inch. Thea went to the pantry for the beans. Cora watched them both, the boy long and angry and still growing out of his father in pieces, the woman narrow-faced and dusty and careful in every public gesture. This was what August's paper had called household occupancy: this, and not the hands, not the labor, not the dead, not the nights.

Dell said, not looking up, "You signing anything?"

"No."

"Good."

The bean snapped in his fingers with a wet crisp sound. "Then what?"

Cora looked at the sugar jar, the note under it, the table scrubbed raw enough for company and still not clean. August's word came back to her, irregular; maybe the land, maybe the paper under his hand. Either way, it had not sounded like weather.

"Then I go see him," she said.

Dell raised his head. "At the bank?"

"Yes."

Thea set the bean bowl down too carefully. "Soon."

"Soon enough."

"Don't go in there only angry."

"I won't."

Thea's eyes narrowed. She knew the distinction she heard in that answer and did not like it. "What then?"

Cora folded the dishrag into a square, once, twice, until it sat in her palm like a thing made for striking. "I'm done letting him talk over paper I haven't seen."

Dell stared from one woman to the other. "Paper what?"

Cora met his eyes. He was not a child, and he was not yet ready either. That was the misery of sons. "Bank paper. Your father's paper. Mine now."

He looked ready to demand more and did not know which question to choose first. That was good. Let him feel confusion for one evening. She had carried enough of it alone.

Thea said, very measured now, "Then don't go in there naming sins. Ask to see every page he laid under that form. Make him spread the stack out in the open."

"He'll know why I'm asking."

"Good. Let him." Thea glanced toward Dell, then back. "Ask for the note. The plat. The county-stamped copy. Ask why he needed your name before Knapp had seen the whole pile."

Cora thought of August's fingers flattening on the form, of the car by the pump, of Walter Knapp, decent enough to be unbearable, telling her there might still be shape left for the living. She thought of the word voluntary and felt her stomach turn.

"And if he won't?"

"Then he can refuse in words," Thea said. "That's worth more than another guess at this table."

"He can lie to my face then," Cora said. "I'll stand there and listen close."

Thea watched her for a long second. Then, to Cora's surprise, the corner of her mouth lifted, not because anything was amusing but because she recognized the choice and could not stop herself from respecting it.

"All right," she said. "Then when you go, go with the exact words. Don't go in there with rage. Rage is what he expects from a debtor woman. Questions are harder on men like August."

"I can ask questions."

"You can ask them like nails."

"That too."

Dell, still baffled, said, "Somebody want to tell me what in hell either of you is talking about?"

Neither answered at once. Beans snapped. The kettle settled. Outside, the light went thinner over the yard and the wheat beyond it looked almost silver with dust.

Cora reached under the sugar jar and took August's note out from beneath it. She folded it once and slid it into the pocket of her apron. The paper warmed fast against her belly. It was barely any warmth, but it was enough.

"Eat first," she said. "Then we'll see what gets told."

Thea's satchel still leaned against the sideboard where she had dropped it, buckled over school papers she had not yet sorted. Cora left it there. One paper at a time was all the evening could stand.
