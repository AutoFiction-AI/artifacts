# Chapter 12

Dell woke her by swallowing the cry instead of letting it out.

Cora had slept crooked in the cane chair with her forearm across the quilt, feeling for him even in sleep the way she once used to feel for a calf in a hard birth, counting on touch more than sight. Before dawn the room was the color of old tin. The lamp had burned low enough to crust the chimney with soot. Dell lay in her bed on top of the sheet because he could not bear weight over his ribs, his left arm splinted between two shaved laths from a peach crate, wrapped in torn pillowcase strips already going gray from sweat and dust. He had managed to turn half an inch onto his right side, and that half inch had turned his face white under the fever.

He kept his teeth shut. That was how she knew it was bad.

She was up before he asked. The enamel basin waited under the chair with a rag folded in the bottom to keep it from clanging. She got it under his chin just as his breath hitched loose. He retched thin yellow bitter into it and shook afterward as if the bed were laid over a thresher.

"Easy," she said. "Get it up."

"I'm not a horse."

"No. Horses are better tempered."

The answer cost him a look. His right eye opened on her, mean and glazed together. Sweat had pasted his dust-light hair to his forehead in little hooks. There was a cut in the scalp above his ear, not deep but ugly with dried blood, and the bruising along his right side had gone from dirt-brown to the color of smashed plums. Hester had bound the arm straight the evening before, jaw set, cough trapped in her chest like she would rather crack herself than let the boys from town see it hurt. Cora had thought the splint looked too rough then. In the night it had become precious, the only thing holding bone from shifting against bone.

Dell spat once more and tried to take the basin with his good hand. His fingers missed the rim.

"Leave it," Cora said.

"I can hold a basin."

"You can't hit one."

He looked away at that, which was how shame came over him now. Not in apology. In refusal. He fixed his eyes on the window, on the flour sack tacked over the lower pane against the light, and let her wipe his mouth like he was eight with summer croup again. The rag came back streaked with bile and the dust they had never fully gotten off him after hauling him in from Saint's Basin. The trench dirt had been black and damp where the seep rose. It still clung in the shell of his ear, under the edge of the bandage, at the roots of his hair. The ground had marked him and followed him home.

Behind the bedroom wall the house was already asking for things. Hens fretted at the run. The pump chain knocked once in the breeze. Somewhere on the shelf in the kitchen a fly worried itself against a jelly glass. The chores had not gone away because her son had come out of the basin half crushed. They had only acquired an audience.

Dell let his head fall back. The movement made him suck air between his teeth. He tried to hide that too.

"How hot?" he muttered.

"Hot enough to be tiresome."

"That isn't a number."

"You think I keep a thermometer tucked in my garter?"

His mouth twitched, almost a smile, and then the pain took it. He closed his eyes. Cora laid the back of her hand along his throat, then his cheek, then under the rough fall of his hair. Fever had a grain to it. Not just heat. A kind of dry, rising insistence, as if the body were trying to fire itself clean from the inside. She had felt it in Ezra once with the winter grippe, and in Dell when he was a small boy with whooping cough, and in calves that lived and calves that did not. Dell's skin had that same kiln heat now, bright and wrong under the sweat.

The door eased open with care. Thea came in carrying the kettle with a dish towel around the handle and a chipped cup balanced in the crook of her fingers. She had not bothered remaking herself after the night. Her dark hair was twisted up anyhow with one proper pin and one straightened paper clip, and the collar of yesterday's blouse had wilted flat from having been slept in. One stocking had been darned at the ankle with blue thread instead of black because blue was what she had. She set the cup on the washstand and did not look at Cora first. She looked at Dell, quick and professional and unwilling to let pity show where he could see it.

"Hester sent willow bark," she said. "And there were two aspirin left in the tin. I crushed one."

"You used both?" Cora asked.

"One. The other is there if he keeps climbing."

Dell opened his eyes again. "Don't feed me bark like a mule."

Thea drew a breath through her nose. "Then don't burn like one."

He would have snapped at that on any other day. This morning he only shut his mouth hard enough to show the tendon in his jaw. Thea held the cup out. Cora slid an arm behind Dell's shoulders and lifted him. The motion jerked an "nnh-God" out of him before he could bite it back, and Cora felt it in her own back as if his body had thrown a hook into hers.

"Drink," she said.

"Smells like fence."

"Drink."

He drank because he had run out of ways not to. A little of it leaked at the corner of his mouth. "God damn it," he said, voice scraped raw. Thea wiped it before he could turn the words into a refusal. He went red at that, a flush visible even through the fever.

"I can do it," he said.

"You would've," Thea said, "if you'd been quicker than the tea."

It was dry enough to pass for schoolroom wit, and that nearly undid Cora. Thea sounded too much like herself. The room was too small for all the parts of life trying to stay separate inside it: Dell in Ezra's old undershirt because his own had been cut off in the yard; Thea at the washstand where she had stood in her slip on gentler nights, pouring water down her own wrists before getting into Cora's bed; the field beyond the curtained glass wearing its failing wheat like a pelt already coming loose. Cora had spent two years keeping those claims from touching one another. Now they knocked together like crockery in a sink.

Thea set the cup down and reached for the edge of the bandage. "I need to look at the hand."

"You saw it yesterday."

"Yesterday you were still deciding whether to faint for effect."

"I didn't faint."

"Dell," Cora said.

He took that tone for what it was. Thea unwrapped the strip nearest the wrist. His fingers were swollen thicker than their shape, the nails lined in black from the basin dirt. The hand had gone shiny with it, puffed and alien, and the sight of it made Cora's stomach cinch harder than the vomit had. Dell saw her seeing.

"Don't start," he said.

"I'm not starting anything."

"Your face is."

Thea's voice stayed level. "Can you feel this?"

She touched each finger in turn. Dell answered without looking, impatient until she brushed the little finger and he hesitated a beat too long.

"That one," he said. "Not right away."

Thea glanced up at Cora, already counting what still worked and what didn't.

"Move them."

He did, slow and ugly, but he did. Thea let out a breath she had been holding and began to bind the wrist again, firmer this time.

"There," she said.

"Feels worse."

"That means it's tighter."

"That wasn't the bargain."

"You don't get bargains this week," Cora said.

His eyes flicked to her and off again. Bargain was a word with too much blood on it in this house. She had not meant to choose it. Or maybe she had, because anger was easier to spend than fear and she was running low on easier things. She softened her voice by hand, not by instinct, and pulled the sheet up over his thighs.

"You need more sleep."

"I need to piss."

The sentence sat there, plain and hateful. Thea looked toward the chamber pot under the washstand. Dell followed the look and went scarlet clear to the tips of his ears.

"Not in here."

"You aren't walking to the privy by yourself," Cora said.

"Then I won't go."

"That isn't a real plan."

He pushed at the mattress with his good hand. The effort twisted his ribs and he went gray again before he got fully upright. Cora caught him under the shoulders. Thea took his right elbow from the other side without asking permission because there was no time left for permission.

"I said no," Dell snapped, not clear whether he meant the chamber pot, Thea's hand, or the fact of being seen weak at all.

"And I said stand up if you can," Cora answered. "Come on."

They got him as far as the porch because that was the compromise he could bear. Morning had come up white and merciless, already hot under the eaves. The yard smelled of chicken droppings, pump iron, and the damp mineral stink the basin mud had left on his clothes in the pile by the steps. Dell leaned one shoulder to the porch post, panting. He had his head down and his hair fell in his eyes, making him look younger for the half second before shame remembered itself and set his mouth hard again.

"Turn around," Cora said.

"I know how."

"Then prove it."

His good hand fumbled at the fly buttons. He could not manage the second one. The left arm hung splinted against his belly, useless as a stick. Thea turned her face toward the yard with a kind of deliberate courtesy that made the moment more naked, not less. Dell got one button free, missed the next, and said, "Come on. Come on, damn you." His fingers slipped again. "Jesus Christ." At last Cora stepped in and worked the cloth open.

He pissed on the toe of his own shoe from shaking. The stream hit the porch dust and made dark stars. His neck went red all the way down under the collarbone.

"Go to hell," he said hoarsely, though there was no one left to address it to except the day.

Cora did not answer. She wiped the leather with the hem of her apron before the urine could turn the dust into caked paste and then hated him seeing that almost as much as he hated needing it. When he sagged, she got her hip under him on instinct, the same way she had done when he was a sleepy child hauled in from the wagon. He was long now, all knees and shoulder blades and the loose weight of a boy nearly grown, but pain returned him to body alone. There was no pride in body alone. There was only what it could and could not hold.

Back in bed he shook with the effort until the mattress rope creaked under him. Thea wet a cloth in the basin and laid it along his throat. Dell flinched and then pressed into it in spite of himself. He watched the ceiling for a while, breathing shallow. At length he said, "Did the water stay?"

Thea looked at Cora. Cora answered because if she did not the silence would tell him too much. "There was still seep when we left."

"Good."

"Good," Cora said, harsher than she meant. "You near took your fool neck off to see it."

He swallowed. His voice went smaller for the first time all morning, which was worse than the meanness had been. "Didn't get my neck."

No, she thought. Only the arm. Only the ribs. Only the fever that had come up in the night and made the bed smell of wet wool and sick iron. Only enough to move the whole weight of the house off its blocks.

He drifted after that, not into clean sleep but into a muttering half-depth where he kept saying things to the air that belonged to the basin. Grade. Hold the wall. Watch the boy. His right hand twitched once as if closing on a shovel handle. Cora sat with the cloth and changed it when it warmed. Thea went out to throw the vomit into the weeds and came back with the hens fed, which ought to have relieved Cora and instead made her feel displaced in her own morning. She took the kettle to the kitchen before that feeling could turn ugly. The stove still wore last night's cinders. Bread ends had gone hard on the board. A fly had gotten into the bacon grease crock. She swatted it away and stood with both hands on the table because stillness was harder work than hauling sacks had ever been.

The south window above the sink looked over the fields. From there she could usually judge the day by wheat and wind alone. Today the view was interrupted by the shape of the basin road in her mind, by the image of men shoveling, boys leaning too near the cut, wet black soil lifting after years of white powder. She had wanted the truth up and breathing. Yesterday it had come up breathing and nearly taken her son down with it.

Thea came in behind her, carrying the aspirin tin and the second cup. "You need something."

"I need ten hands."

"I can be one of them."

Cora took the cup because refusing kindness was a luxury and she had no room for luxuries before noon. The coffee was thin enough to show the bottom of the cup through it, but it was hot. Thea had put in the last spoon of sugar without asking. Cora knew because the sweetness startled her mouth.

"If he's worse by afternoon, he needs a doctor," Thea said.

"From where."

"Wilton. Even if we have to send to the highway and flag somebody."

"And jolt him six miles over ruts with busted ribs."

"I know."

Thea leaned her shoulder to the doorframe, arms folded tight enough to keep from reaching. Public composure sat on her oddly in a kitchen. It belonged on the schoolhouse floorboards, not here with a kettle hissing and Dell half-delirious in the next room. When she grew frightened, she became careful first and blunt after, as though language had to take off its gloves one finger at a time.

"I know," she said again. "I'm only saying stubbornness isn't a treatment."

"And panic isn't either."

"This isn't panic."

"Then what is it."

Thea's mouth thinned. "Arithmetic."

Cora almost laughed at that and could not. Arithmetic had lived too long in the bank and on note paper and in men's mouths. Yet there it was in her own kitchen now, measuring breath against miles, pain against money. She set the cup down harder than she needed to and went back to the bedroom before arithmetic could settle in.

Dell had kicked the sheet off. The cloth on his throat was hot as wash water. She changed it, slid the pillow straighter, watched him fret at sleep with the same furious little movements he used to make when tangles in fishing line beat him. Every few minutes the house asked again for hens, for dishes, for pump water, for the field, for the basin, for the farm itself. Every few minutes Dell dragged a breath and answered for her.

By late morning the light had gone hard enough to show every fly in the room. Cora had managed the chicken mash, drawn two buckets, and stripped the basin-dirt clothes to soak in lye water without once going farther from the house than the pump. She came back from pinning a sheet over the line to the sound of an engine on the road.

The motor died outside her gate with the stuttering cough of August Poeschel's Ford.

* * *

He did not come to the door at once. Cora saw him first through the veil of the hanging sheet: a broad dark shape on the other side of white cloth, standing still as if arranging himself before entry. Then he stepped around it into the yard, hat on, jacket buttoned though the heat already had sweat darkening the band under his arms. Dust had chalked the hems of his trousers. He carried two folded papers and one narrow board under his arm, painted in quick black capitals. He had shaved clean that morning. That was somehow the ugliest part of him.

Thea, at the line with the clothespin basket hooked over her wrist, went still enough to make the pins stop clicking. One of them dropped into the dirt and neither woman stooped for it.

August lifted a hand, not quite greeting. "Mrs. Vale."

Cora stayed on the porch. "You lost your way to town?"

His eyes moved past her shoulder before coming back. That was all. A flick. Yet she felt it land in the open bedroom door where Dell lay in her bed and in the damp sleeve of Thea's blouse rolled to the elbow. August's face did not change. He smoothed one of the folded papers against the board with the flat of his palm, the motion precise enough to count as a tell.

"I have posted the basin closed," he said. "At the road and at the cut itself. No entry until the county can inspect the ground."

Cora looked at the board. *KEEP OFF. CAVE-IN DANGER.* The words were blunt enough for a fence line, but the paper under his hand was bank language, dense and official.

"The county," she said. "Or you."

"The distinction does not alter the risk."

"It alters who gets to call it."

He took that and set it in the ledger place behind his face where he kept things from becoming personal. "A boy was nearly killed yesterday. The walls are unstable. More people will come to look if it is left open. If there is another collapse, the liability will spread farther than excitement seems to understand."

"Liability."

"Yes."

"That what Dell is now."

August's jaw shifted once. "He is a wounded boy. Which is why I am here before rumor outruns fact."

"Fact outran you yesterday."

Thea spoke before either of them could sharpen further. Her voice came measured, schoolhouse-flat, though the basket handle had gone white in her grip. "Did you post guards."

August turned a little toward her. "No. I posted warning and notice. I am not staffing a battlefield, Miss Bell."

"Then the sign is for appearances."

"The sign is because language is what a town still has before it has force."

That almost sounded tired enough to be true. It angered Cora more. He was always worst when he spoke as if the injury had burdened him with philosophy.

"You want it shut till the water sinks back out of sight," she said. "Then you'll say folks imagined the rest."

"I want no more broken bones hauled out of mud."

"You wanted no mud."

"Mrs. Vale." His voice changed only by tightening, not volume. "For once I am speaking only of safety."

The bedroom floorboards creaked inside. "Shit," Dell said into the mattress, and then something thumped the washstand. Cora had been poised to come down the steps. Instead she turned her head toward the room before she could stop herself.

August saw it. Of course he saw it.

"I will leave the notice here," he said. "You can tell Hester Blum if she comes riding at you with righteous purpose. If the boy worsens, send word. That is not charity. I would rather have a live witness than a dead one."

There it was, stripped clean: accountancy spoken plain enough to almost pass for honesty, too barefaced to call itself mercy.

Cora took one step down until she was eye level with him. "Get your paper off my porch."

For the first time something like temper leaked through the banker's careful shave. "You may call it mine if that helps you. The earth did not collapse because I put ink on a page."

"No," Cora said. "Just most of the rest of it."

He looked at her a moment longer than was safe. Then he set the folded notice on the porch rail instead of handing it to her and tucked the board back under his arm. As he turned, his gaze caught once more on Thea by the sheet line, on her rolled sleeves and the clothespins hanging from her fingers like a row of little jaws. He touched the brim of his hat to no one and went back to the car.

The engine took two tries. By the time it rattled away, Dell was calling from the bedroom, voice ragged with pain and temper both. Cora left the paper where August had laid it and went inside.

He had tried to sit and failed halfway. Sweat had darkened the pillow. The washstand basin had tipped, slopping brownish water down the leg to the floor.

"What did he want."

"Nothing worth your breath."

"What did he want."

Cora shoved the basin upright with her foot and got an arm behind him. "He posted the cut. That's all."

"Son of a bitch."

"Save it."

"He can't post water."

"He can post dirt. Lie back."

Dell tried to resist, because resistance was the only property he had full title to anymore, but the effort stripped him fast. He hissed when the mattress took his ribs. His eyes had gone bright and foolish with fever. That frightened Cora more than moaning would have. Dell usually kept his feelings under his tongue like a plug of bad tobacco. Fever loosened them.

"I should've stayed there," he said. "If they'd shored it from the west side—"

"You should've kept your ass out of a collapsing trench."

"Kid would've gone under."

"Then the kid's mother can thank you by letting you keep your arm."

His mouth worked. The word mother moved across his face before he decided against it. "Water was there," he said instead, stubborn as the heat. "You saw it."

Yes. She had seen the black shine gathering in the broken basin floor, the impossible wet under a country that had forgotten wet. She had seen men and boys and women all crowding close to it with the same hungry expression, as if water itself had become testimony. She had seen Dell drop his shovel and lunge because a smaller body slid where the bank gave way. She had seen the trench wall take shape in air before it hit him. There were truths she would not surrender. There were truths she would not pay twice for either.

"I saw it," she said. "Now shut your eyes."

He did not obey, but fever made the refusal weak. He turned his face to the wall and bit down on whatever came next.

Hester arrived an hour later with a coffee can of broth wrapped in a towel and a handkerchief full of bread ends. She came up the steps slower than usual because one heel of her shoe had been re-pegged with a sliver of tin and kept catching in the boards. She swore at the heel under her breath, then at her own breath when it turned into coughing. By the time Thea opened the door, Hester had one hand braced on the jamb and the other pressed to the breastbone under her black dress.

"Don't fuss," she said before either of them could. "I only lent my lungs to a foolish century. They are collecting."

She smelled of yeast, cloves, and field dust. Her broad fingers were flour-caught at the creases as if no amount of washing got a working life fully out. She set the can down on the kitchen table, untied the towel, and peered toward the bedroom with eyes that had gone sharper the more the rest of her wore out.

"How bad."

"Hot," Cora said. "Arm still warm in the fingers. Ribs are making him ugly."

"He was ugly before."

"Thank you kindly."

Hester snorted, then coughed again, smaller this time. "August has hung his little commandments at the basin."

"So he said."

"He had Ezra Ott's boy nail one to the cottonwood by the road. Ezra Ott kept taking his hat off to talk, poor fool, like bad news ought to hear itself respectful. The paper says 'closed pending inspection' and 'public hazard' and some other Sunday-school sins. There is still water in the cut, and already half the men on Main are talking as if stepping past that sign would bring their own notes due by supper."

Thea set out bowls. "Then it'll still be there tomorrow."

Hester turned. "That is a hope, not a fact."

"The wall almost killed Dell."

"And the wall already did what it came to do for August if everybody stays home today and calls it prudence."

Cora took the broth to the stove. Grease had clouded on top in pale eyes. She broke it with the spoon and watched it drift apart. Thea must have brought August's folded notice in off the porch rail; it lay beside the breadboard with the black letters ghosting through the paper. The argument was arriving ahead of itself. She could hear it in the shape of Hester's silence, in the way Thea laid the bread on the board one piece at a time instead of all at once.

"What do you want done," Cora asked.

Hester tapped the folded notice with one floury finger. "I want two men who can tell bad ground from solid and some planks before dark. Shore the west lip where Dell went in. Rope the south side. Put somebody grown at the road so every fool boy in town doesn't go skidding down there to stare at the seep. If August gets to leave one sign and call that safety, he buys the basin back for the price of a board."

Thea's laugh came short and wrong. When she got afraid, sometimes the sound slipped out before she could school it. "Free. Dell's in there burning through the sheet. He pissed on his own shoe because he could barely stand. We've got one aspirin left, no doctor in the yard, and you want more people on that lip."

Hester's gaze went to her, dry as flour. "I want planks under boots instead of boys under dirt."

"And when the planks tip?"

"Then the man on them backs off."

"Men don't back off when half the town is watching. You saw yesterday."

Hester set her knuckles on the table as the cough nudged at her chest. "I saw August ride in after the seep showed and start saying hazard like the mud had learned a new word for him. Leave it till morning and the lip slumps, the water clouds over, and by dinner tomorrow folks will say it was only a wet patch." She coughed once, hard enough to bow her a little. "And Dell's bad judgment."

There it was, plain at last. Thea folded the dish towel once, twice, too carefully. "Dell's bad judgment still broke his arm. If he climbs hotter by sundown, I need a wagon that won't shake him to pieces on the Wilton road. I need cash in hand. I need, no, Cora needs a road out if his breathing goes bad. And if Wilton shrugs, we need rail after that."

Hester's mouth thinned. "You count wages and miles. I'm counting mud. The west side is already undercut."

"Fifty-two dollars when the board bothers to send it," Thea said. "That is what my wages are. That and a room here. It won't buy another boy if the basin takes one. Don't ask me to nod along while more men crawl down there because August nailed a warning to a tree."

Hester grew quieter, not softer. "I'm not asking boys. I'm asking for borrowed planks, a rope, and two men old enough to hear dirt shifting. If it sits, August gets the county behind his paper by morning, and then what Dell got broke for is a board and a rumor."

Thea swung round then, controlled no longer. "And if his fever jumps while you're guarding a rumor? Honest doesn't set a bone. Honest doesn't hire the wagon to Wilton. Honest doesn't keep August from standing on the rail and counting who was in this house when he came."

"For us," Hester said. "For Dell when the heat breaks and he asks what flattened him. For Cora when the auction paper comes. For me because I already buried one child in this dust and I'm done letting weather take the blame for men."

That shut Thea for a beat. Not because Hester had won, only because everybody in town knew which child she meant, and Marta's absence was one of the few names nobody handled lightly. Hester put a hand to the table when the coughing threatened again, rode it down, and went on in a lower voice.

"I know what waiting costs. Don't mistake me."

Thea's answer came softer and somehow harsher for it. "I know what staying costs. That is what I'm saying."

Cora had let them run because each was speaking a truth she needed and could not bear. Now Dell knocked the bed ropes against the wall and said, "Shit. Ma," with the second word dragging thin behind it, and the choice she'd been pretending was not yet on the table put its elbows there.

"Enough," she said.

Neither woman stopped at once. She hit the table with the broth spoon hard enough to ring the bowls.

"Enough."

Silence came then, ragged but real.

Cora looked from one to the other. Hester with her flour-lined hands and the handkerchief dark at the middle where she had coughed into it. Thea with the dish towel twisted so tight in her fingers it might tear, dust on her shoes from a house she kept helping survive even while she argued it should be left. Beyond the bedroom door Dell breathed too fast. Beyond the yard the field stood in the noon glare, thin and stubborn and worth less each week it remained upright.

"That hole got my boy once already," Cora said. "It doesn't get him again today."

Hester's mouth set. "Today becomes tomorrow quick."

"Then let tomorrow come."

"Cora."

"No." Her own voice surprised her by its steadiness. "You can call me a coward tomorrow if you like. You can call me August's accomplice. I don't care. Dell comes first."

Thea closed her eyes at that, not in disagreement. In relief, almost. Which stung.

Hester saw the relief too. Something in her face folded inward, not broken but put away like a letter. "I didn't come to call names," she said. "I came because once folks sit beside a sickbed they start believing the world can wait polite outside the door. It can't. That's all."

She reached for the coffee can, then remembered why she had brought it and left it where it sat. The small mistake hurt more than anger would have. Cora stepped toward her.

"Hester."

The older woman shook her head. "Feed him. Cool him. I know the work." She drew the handkerchief from her sleeve and pressed it to her mouth while the cough took hold in earnest this time, shoulders jerking under the black dress. When she could speak again, her voice had roughened. "I buried Marta because we kept saying tomorrow till there wasn't one. Forgive me if I don't like the word."

Thea's grip loosened on the towel. "Hester, I wasn't—"

"I heard what you were."

It was not cruel. It was worse. Accurate and tired.

Hester picked up the bread ends, reconsidered, and set those back too. "There's a boy down the road with a mouth full of loose talk who owes Dell his life. I can likely borrow his uncle and a pair of planks without either of you. Maybe not. We'll see." She moved toward the door, heel scraping. On the threshold she looked over her shoulder at Cora, not Thea. "If he breaks hotter after sundown, put onion to his feet and vinegar under the arms. And keep him drinking, however cross it makes him."

"I know," Cora said.

"Yes," Hester answered. "You do."

When she had gone, the kitchen felt worse than if there had been shouting. The air had thickened with all the things left unsaid because the said things were already enough to carry.

Thea bent and picked up the dropped clothespin from earlier, though it had not been in the kitchen at all. Only after she straightened did she seem to notice she had come back in empty-handed except for that absurd little peg. She set it on the table beside the bowls and laughed once at herself, sharp and tired.

"That was well managed," she said.

"Don't."

"I wasn't being clever."

"Don't do anything right this minute but breathe."

Thea did, once. Twice. Then she braced both palms on the table and looked at Cora full for the first time since Hester had gone. "I'm trying to tell you the farm won't stop asking. Not because I don't understand Hester. I do. God help me, I do. But August put his paper on your rail and stood there counting who was in this house. He can close the basin with a sign, and in a town this size he can make a look do the rest. Ezra, then you, piece by piece, and now Dell. I don't know what amount would finally satisfy you."

The words should have felt like accusation. They felt like a hand forced against a bruise to see how deep it went.

Cora leaned back against the stove so the hot iron pressed through her dress. "You think I don't know what it's taken."

"I think you know it and keep calling the knowing duty."

"What would you call it."

Thea opened her mouth, revised, started again. "Sometimes I'd call it love. Sometimes habit. Sometimes fear dressed up respectable. I don't know. I only know that if he needs to be gotten out of here, you can't let acreage answer before he does."

Acreage. Not farm, not home, not Ezra's place or Dell's inheritance. Acreage, like Walter Knapp had said it, like August wrote it. Thea knew exactly what she was doing by choosing the cold word. It made Cora hate her for a second and trust her the next.

"You make leaving sound clean," Cora said.

"No." Thea's voice dropped. "I make it sound filthy and possible. Those are different things."

From the bedroom Dell called, slurred with heat, "Ma."

Cora was moving before the second syllable finished. Thea followed but stopped at the door while Cora sat on the edge of the mattress and got the cup to Dell's mouth. He drank three swallows obediently, which frightened her more than refusal would have. A bead of water ran into the hollow above his collarbone. His lashes were wet with sweat, darker than his hair. He blinked at her as if waking from a far bad place.

"Did Hester come."

"She came."

"She mad."

"Everybody's mad. Drink."

He took another swallow. "Should've braced the west side."

"Dell."

"I know."

But he did not know, not yet. He knew the shape of a trench and the speed of a boy slipping in dirt and the sound a body made when the earth struck it. He did not know what it did to a woman to stand between a fever bed and a field and feel both call her by rightful names. He might. He would, if she kept him alive long enough.

When he slept again, it came deeper. The aspirin had found him or exhaustion had. Cora changed the cloth at his throat once more and eased the sheet down to where it would not drag the bruises. His splinted arm lay on the pillow like a thing returned from some other household, not yet claimed by his own flesh. She stared at it until the shape stopped making sense.

Thea remained in the doorway, one hand on the frame. Outside, a little wind got up and set the pinned sheet knocking on the line. The sound was domestic, harmless, almost cheerful. It made Cora want to put her fist through the window.

"If he's worse by dawn," Thea said quietly, "we go. Somewhere with a doctor. Somewhere with less of this in the walls. We can decide the name later."

Cora sat very still. She had spent so long answering danger with staying put that motion itself had begun to feel like faithlessness. When Ezra died, she stayed. When August shortened the note, she stayed. When Knapp measured her house with his eyes and called departure mercy, she stayed harder. Staying had become the grammar of her grief. It was the only tense she trusted.

But Dell lay in the bed she had once defended as the center of everything worth holding, and the bed now felt less like an altar than a raft. The room held his heat, the sour wool smell of fever, the bitter steam of willow bark, the faint clean trace of Thea's soap from where she had washed the vomit basin. The farm beyond the wall did not enter except as glare and demand. For the first time since Ezra's rope had cut the barn beam, Cora could imagine the land continuing without her and feel not betrayal first but relief behind it, small and monstrous and real.

She rose, went to the kitchen, and took down the blue-speckled flour tin from the top shelf. The money inside was the egg money, the sewing money, the thin little stack she had kept partitioned in her mind for feed, for note interest, for a bolt of muslin if things somehow improved enough to deserve muslin. She did not count it for the note. She counted it into her palm and back again for miles, for medicine, for whatever it cost to move a fevered boy where the next wall waiting for him was not one she had spent years patching by hand.

When she set the tin down, Thea was watching from the bedroom door.

"Cora."

The name held caution in it, and hope, and the fear of hoping out loud.

Cora slid the money back into the tin and put the lid on hard. "How long is it by rail to Lincoln?"

Thea did not answer at once. Her face changed with the effort of not reaching for this too fast. "With a change and if freight doesn't foul the schedule," she said, "most of a day. More if we miss the first connection."

Cora nodded once. She wasn't agreeing yet; she was measuring miles against money and Dell's heat.

Then she took the tin back into the bedroom and set it in the drawer beside the bed instead of on the kitchen shelf where money for the farm belonged. She sat with her hand over Dell's hot good hand and listened to him breathe. Outside, the field kept asking. For the first time, she let it ask without answering.
