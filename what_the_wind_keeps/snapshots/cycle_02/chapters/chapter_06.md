# Chapter 6

The half-mended trace lay across Dell's knee while he watched the kitchen door instead of his work. Since breakfast he'd been waiting for Cora to turn her back.

She had set him to mending a harness trace that could have sat another three days, maybe four, and kept finding one more reason to pass through the yard and look at him while he worked. Since the jars at Hester's bakery, she had taken to pinning chores onto him like weights on a fence wire. Check the chicken run. Patch the shed latch. Scrape the grit out of the pump cup. None of it was made-up work. That was what made it harder to fight. Everything on the place needed doing, and Cora knew how to make necessity feel like a hand on the back of his neck.

By noon the kitchen was already hot. Dust had settled in the seams of the tablecloth again, a pale fur in the red checks. Cora stood at the stove with one sleeve rolled past the elbow and a strip of blue sewing thread tied around the crack on her ring finger to keep it from opening any farther. She did not waste words on pain any more than she wasted lard. She only worked around it.

"South fence is dragging," Dell said, making his voice casual and hearing the strain in it anyway. "I ought to look at it before the calves get notions."

Cora did not turn from the skillet. "You ought to finish what's in your hands."

"I will."

"Then say what you mean."

He laid the strap flat on his knee and did not look up. "I mean I can do the fence first and this after."

She set the spoon down hard enough to ring the stove lid. "You going with Hester again?"

Dell hated the quick jump in him when she said it. He hated more that she heard the lie before he spoke it. "I'm going to the south fence."

"That wasn't what I asked."

The room held on the scrape of the spoon against iron. Thea had already gone to the schoolhouse for the day's thin business, and without her there the kitchen seemed built only for work and argument. Dell could smell old coffee, hot grease, and the dust that baked its own smell out of the floorboards by noon. He wished, not for the first time, that Cora would shout when she was angry. A shout could be shoved against. Her quiet kept making him feel small enough to fit in a pocket.

"I said I'm going to the fence."

Now she turned. Her hair, chopped blunt with kitchen scissors weeks before, had started escaping its pins in dry pale strands around her face. "You think I don't know what foolishness looks like when it starts walking out of my yard with a shovel."

"It isn't foolishness."

"It gets to be foolishness if it tangles you in your father's business and leaves you with nothing to show for it."

That made him lift his head. Shame moved in him like something with claws. If he had not heard Ezra crying in the barn that night and lain still under his own quilt anyway, maybe he could have stood there clean and righteous. Instead every step toward the truth felt crooked. He was prying under floorboards after a dead man who had already sunk deep enough to stop answering back.

"Maybe I want something to show for it," he said.

Cora's face tightened, not much, just around the mouth. "Dell."

She said his name when she wanted obedience to remember it was born in love. It almost worked. He looked at the strip of harness leather, at the awl and the spool of waxed thread on the step, at his own hands with the knuckles still dark from axle grease that never washed fully out. If he stayed, he would spend the afternoon stitching a strap and pretending the ground south of the wheat had not started talking.

"I'll be back before supper," he said.

"That isn't permission."

"I know."

He took the shovel from beside the pump house before she could answer. Behind him the screen door rasped once. He thought she might come out and stop him with nothing more dramatic than standing in his way. She did not. That was worse. It left the choice in his hands, where it burned.

The yard gave way to the fields in a few short steps, house shadow to glare. Wheat stood there in mean, uneven strips, ankle-high in some places, gone to bare crust in others, the whole south field looking like a dog's coat after mange. From the porch the ground still passed for flat. Out in it, Dell could see how it had been cut and planed and worried into false straightness over years. Furrows crossed places that wanted to sag. A shallow draw had been shaved almost level with repeated plowing, though the wagon tongue still leaned there if he drove over it too fast. Dust lay drifted against the stubble in lines that showed every petty rise and fall more honestly than the crop did.

Hester waited where the field edge broke at the old plum thicket, one hand on the top wire of the fence. She wore a faded man's straw hat with a dark sweat stain around the crown and had Otto's brass survey pencil tucked through her cardigan buttonhole like a brooch she had no intention of making pretty. When she saw Dell she bit a horehound drop clean through, grimaced at the bitterness, and pocketed the rest.

"You made it out," she said.

"For now."

She studied the house behind him, then the shovel over his shoulder. "Your mother knows."

"She suspects."

"Same thing in a kitchen."

He almost laughed. It came out harsher than that. "You walked all the way from town to tell me I was caught already?"

"I walked all the way from town because I don't trust paper to stay paper in this wind." She tapped the pocket of her apron. "And because you read ground better than I do now. Otto had the knees for it. I had the memory."

That shifted something in him, though not toward softness. Hester was not made for that. But she was not using him as a boy sent to fetch a tool, either. She had brought herself two miles in men's work shoes with her cough sitting under her ribs like a closed fist, and she was giving him the piece of the job she could not do alone.

She unfolded Otto's map out of wax paper. The sheet had been copied in a hand more careful than elegant, the lines darkened twice where a turn mattered. Dell recognized the rough square of the Vale house site, the road west from town, the old section line. South of the fields Otto had drawn a basin shape in shallow curves and, beyond it, a straight mark with little cross hatches and the word tile written so small Dell had to lean close.

The map made it less ghostly and more offensive.

"He was sure?" Dell asked.

Hester looked at the paper instead of at him. "Otto wasn't a man for sure until he'd paced it twice and argued with himself a third time. He knew where the wet held before they taught it not to."

Dell's eyes stayed on the hatch marks. "You said there used to be mint down there."

"Mint and sedge and enough mosquitoes to lift a baby." Her mouth twitched. "Marta came home black to the knees one August from cutting cat-tail switches and I whipped her for ruining the hem. Next day I cried over the same hem while I let it down because she'd grown and I had nothing else to give her. That's the kind of memory land keeps. Not only the grand ones."

It was the first time she had spoken Marta's name to him that season without pausing beforehand. Dell looked away to the field. Wind moved across it in a dull skin, not enough to cool anything. Far off, a meadowlark threw two notes from a post and then shut up as if the day had taken offense.

"Come on," Hester said. "If your mother comes after us, I'd like to have earned it."

They started south with the map between them, Dell carrying the shovel and a shorter spade Hester had brought wrapped in sacking. The land lost its house-made proportions as they walked. Up close the field edge was not a line but a ragged negotiation between what still answered to the plow and what had gone over to weeds and stubborn native grass. Needle-and-thread clung in gray-green tufts where the cultivator had missed. Russian thistle seedlings crowded one drifted ditch in a green so unhealthy it looked almost chemical. In low places a white alkali bloom had skinned the soil like dried spit.

Dell kept finding the marks of bad years layered on one another. Fence posts leaned where the ground had blown from around them. A broken harrow tooth stood half-buried at the edge of the section like a black rib. The road ditch south of the fields had silted nearly full, so a hard rain, if one ever came again, would ride the road itself before it found any place to sink. He had lived on this land seventeen years and knew every gate, rut, and gopher mound close to the house. South of the wheat, where men once said Saint's Basin lay, he realized how much of his knowing had stopped where the useful chores ended.

Hester walked without wasting motion, though every few minutes she pressed a fist to her breastbone until a cough passed. She did not apologize for it. When the wind tried for the map she crouched and pinned the corners with her broad flour-rough hands, muttering at the paper as if it were a stupid child. Dell took a side without being asked.

"The house is there," she said, nodding behind them. "The old cottonwood by the basin edge is gone, but the root hump ought to be something yet. Otto marked the overflow cut on the east side. If the years haven't packed it flat, you'll see the fall."

"Water won't forget down," Dell said.

She glanced at him. "No. Men forget. Water's better at its work."

He kept his eyes ahead. He did not want the approval in her face, even mild as it was. It made him think of Ezra in the machine shed, crouched with a stripped gear in his palm, saying nothing for a long while and then, when Dell handed him the right wrench without being told, only, There. You see it.

The memory struck hard enough that Dell missed a step in a prairie-dog hole and cursed.

Hester waited until he had pulled free. "You all right?"

"I'm fine."

"You don't have to answer everything like it's a dare."

"I'm not."

"You are."

He wanted to snap back at her, but the truth of it came too quick. Since Ezra died he had been speaking as if every sentence had to prove he was older than it felt to be left behind. He shifted the shovel on his shoulder and said, "Then quit saying things that make it easy."

Hester snorted. "God save me from boys who want gentleness while they're busy pretending they don't need any."

They came to the root hump of the old cottonwood, no trunk left, only a weathered swell under the soil and a scatter of punky wood that broke in Dell's fingers like stale bread. South and east of it the land opened out in a broad, hard pale. From the house and road it would have looked like nothing more than thin ground where the wheat had given up. Standing in it, Dell saw differences he had missed before. The field skin changed color by degrees, dun to ash to a darker brown peering through wherever the wind had scoured deepest. The surface had a shallow dish to it, subtle enough a man could walk it without noticing, but impossible once seen to mistake for level. Grass held in a ragged crescent at the west edge, coarser and darker than the rest. There were old reeds here once, Dell thought, though he had never seen them. The shape of them still worked in the soil.

Hester unfolded the map again. "Otto put the west lip here. Tile line crossing out there." She pointed eastward, where the ground looked no different to Dell at first than any other worn quarter-acre of the county. "I always thought he was two paces north in this copy, but his notes were damp that day and the pencil ran."

"Let me see."

She gave him the map without hesitation.

Dell crouched and turned slowly, matching paper to land. The section road was a clean enough line behind them. The house sat west-northwest. The basin curve on the map had been flattened by plowing, but not erased. When he narrowed his eyes he could read the dish by the way the light hit it, a faint sag where dust had collected thinner. Eastward, beyond the shallow center, there was a run of volunteer grass a shade greener than anything around it, almost a seam.

"Not north," he said. "South. Look."

Hester bent, peering under the hat brim. "Where?"

He pointed with the survey pencil. "That line there. Not the grass itself. The sink under it. See how it holds straight while the rest wanders?"

She stayed still a moment, then nodded once. "I see it."

The simple words landed cleaner than praise. Dell handed the map back. Together they walked toward the seam. Up close it was less visible and more convincing. The ground had settled in a narrow shallow trough, no wider than a boot length, running straight enough to shame every natural curve around it. Dell put the heel of his boot in the depression and felt the difference, a minute give under the powder crust where the soil below had once been worked and then abandoned to sink as it liked.

"Start here," he said.

Hester passed him the short spade. "Then start."

The first cuts were miserable. The crust broke like crockery, sharp-edged and dry, and the spade jarred up his arms every time he drove it in. Beneath that was packed clay the color of old dishwater, hard enough to spark a thought of giving up and calling the whole hunt another of Otto's dead certainties. Sweat ran into Dell's eyes. Dust pasted itself to the sweat and made mud of a sort that only insulted the word. Hester knelt beside the hole and scraped loose spoil back with her hands, sorting pebbles from roots with the concentration she might have given pie crust.

After ten minutes they had nothing but a hole to the depth of Dell's hand and a worse temper between them.

"Maybe it's farther east," Hester said.

"Maybe there isn't anything left."

"Maybe you dug crooked."

He looked at her. "I did not."

"Then move six inches and prove me wrong."

That, too, was work instead of comfort. Dell shifted six inches and cut again. He hit the same crust and the same clay, and then the spade struck something with a dull thud, not like stone. He paused.

"What?" Hester asked.

"Nothing yet."

He drove the spade edge in alongside the resistance and pried. A curved shard rolled up out of the dirt, orange-brown under the gray, smooth on the inside and rough on the outside, the broken edge thick as his thumb.

Neither of them spoke for a breath.

Hester took the piece from his hand and rubbed it clean on her apron. "Tile."

Dell's mouth had gone dry despite the heat. "Could be from anywhere."

"From where?" She did not say it cruelly. She only held the fragment up between them. "You think a drainpipe came out here to die by itself?"

He wanted to say yes, to say maybe some fool dumped a broken load thirty years ago, maybe it was part of a well ring, maybe anything. Instead he crouched and widened the hole. The shovel bit, scraped, jarred. Another curved section appeared, not broken this time but still in place, laid end to end with the next in a line so deliberate it made Dell feel briefly stupid for every excuse he had reached toward.

Hester lowered herself to both knees with a grunt. Together they cleared enough earth to expose almost two feet of the run. Fired-clay tile, four-inch, set straight through what had once been the low hold of the basin. The joints were packed with silt. Fine roots had crept into one seam and died there pale. Dell put his fingers to the earth beneath the tile and came back with black mud under his nails.

It was mud.

It was not the false mud of sweat and dust, and it was not the thin smudge at a water trough. This was cold and close and smelled when it opened. The odor came up like a cellar unlatched after winter: root rot, mineral, old weed, something shut away and still alive in the shutness. Dell had not smelled ground like that since before the bad years took the slough by the north pasture down to cracked plates.

Hester closed her eyes once, breathing shallow because a deep breath would have cost her. "There you are," she said, not to Dell, not quite to God either. "There you damned are."

Dell scooped another handful from below the tile and watched water sheen for an instant on the black before the dry air started taking it. His stomach turned over. He had come wanting proof and had not understood that proof might have a smell.

"It didn't just dry up," he said.

"No."

"They cut it."

"Yes."

The word sat between them with the weight of the clay in his hand.

Dell looked out over the shallow dish of ground. Once seen through the tile, the whole place changed. The greener seam eastward was no accident. The slight drop beyond it was not ordinary settling. He could almost read the labor of it backward now: men trenching through wet meadow, laying the line, packing it in, maybe scraping a carry ditch where the outflow ran off toward lower land that had no business taking it.

No ghost had done that. No weather had reached down with a careful hand and set clay pipe in a row.

He stood so fast the blood thumped in his head. "Give me the shovel."

Hester passed it up. "Where?"

"East. If it falls there, it has to go somewhere."

They followed the faint trough another twenty yards. Dell moved quicker now, seeing signs he had walked past before: a small cave-in no wider than a dinner plate, a change in weed growth, the straightness of failure. At the basin's east lip the ground rose a little and then broke into a low bank half melted by years of blowing soil. Beyond it ran a ditch so filled and scabbed over that from the road it would have looked like no more than a wash. Dell jumped down into it. The bottom held cracked powder, tumbleweed roots, and a bent tin can from some later year. The sides had been cut by hand once. Even softened, he could see the tool marks in their shape. They were not rain-rounded. They had been sliced.

Hester stayed at the rim until Dell cleared a patch and held a hand up. Then she sat and slid the rest of the way in, muttering at her left knee.

"That one predicts weather worse than the radio ever did," she said, rubbing it. "Dig there."

He did. Two cuts down the spade hit clay. Three more and the edge shaved against more tile, this line angled through the bank and into the ditch. He widened the hole until the direction showed plain.

Hester gave a dry, ugly little laugh. "They didn't even hide the sense of it. Straight out."

"They drained the middle and gave it a throat."

"That's one way to put it."

He looked at her. "What's your way?"

She wiped her mouth with the back of her wrist. There was dust in the web of wrinkles at the corners of her eyes, flour permanently ground into the skin of her knuckles. "My way is that men who were hungry and scared and greedy in different proportions convinced themselves a meadow was wasted ground, because it stood wet part of the year instead of carrying wheat. They laid tile, they cut the overflow, and they called it improvement. Then they plowed what ought not to have been plowed and blamed the Lord when the topsoil started traveling."

Dell struck the shovel into the ditch wall harder than he meant to. Dirt sloughed down over his boots. "You mean my father."

Hester's eyes sharpened. "I mean more than your father, and I mean less. I don't know what part he put his hands to."

"He farmed it."

"Yes."

"He borrowed against it. Signed things over it." The words were coming too fast now, rough in his throat. "Maybe he knew. Maybe he helped. Maybe everybody but my mother knew and she still gets to pay for it."

Hester did not tell him to lower his voice. There was no one to hear but a lark and the dead ground. "That's closer to the size of it."

He hated her for being willing to say it and was grateful in the same instant. The two feelings met and made something hotter than either. He had spent weeks trying to split the story clean down the middle: August on one side, Ezra on the other, one a thief and the other a man cornered past fairness. The tile in the ditch would not allow that. Whatever Ezra had done or failed to do, he had lived in a world where this kind of taking wore a respectable face. Maybe he had worn it too. Maybe he had hated himself for it later. Maybe hating himself had not repaired a damn thing.

Dell thought of the sobbing in the barn the night before the rope. He had heard it through the boards, a sound like a grown man trying not to be one. He had put his head under the pillow and waited for quiet because he had school in the morning and because fathers were supposed to climb out of their own troubles. He had been punishing himself with that memory ever since, polishing Ezra into something too wounded and noble to have made common ruin with other men.

He drove the shovel down again, not from need now but because his hands required an answer. "I wanted it to be August," he said.

"I know."

"Just him."

"That would've been simpler."

"I don't care about simpler."

Hester let that sit. Wind moved over the lip of the ditch and set a dry whisper going in the tumbleweed stems. Somewhere to the north a truck changed gears on the road to town, the sound carrying thin as wire in the open land.

"That's new," she said at last.

Dell leaned on the shovel handle. "What's new?"

"You not caring about simple."

He almost told her to go to hell. Instead he looked at the black mud drying on his fingers, turning from shine to dull skin in the space of a minute. "I cared," he said. "I think I still do. I just don't trust it."

Hester nodded once, like a woman accepting a price quoted too high but real. Then she pushed herself upright and climbed out of the ditch one slow step at a time. Dell followed, taking the spade and the map and, when she let him, her elbow for the last pull. She did not thank him. He was glad of that too.

They sat on the bank to eat because suddenly Dell was shaking a little and hunger was the easiest lie for it. Hester took a cloth bundle from her satchel: a heel of rye bread, two hard-boiled eggs, and a paper twist of salt damp from the air of her kitchen. She cracked the eggs against the shovel blade and peeled them clean with a speed that spoke of decades. Before handing Dell one she rubbed a bit of shell from the white with her thumb and said, almost conversationally, "I hate eggs cold. Otto used to laugh at me for that. Said I'd gripe on Judgment Day if the angels served supper wrong."

Dell snorted despite himself.

"But I hate wasted food more," she went on. "Eat."

They ate looking over the basin. Dell kept his eyes moving while he chewed: root hump, west lip grass, the straight trough where the tile had settled, the ditch cut east. He counted strides between each mark and set them in his head. If he had to walk back here in the dark or bring Cora to it before wind changed the look of everything again, he wanted the route in his legs: twelve strides from the hump to the trough, seven more to the rise, the ditch just beyond.

Hester brushed salt from her palm. "We're not bringing Cora a story. Not yet."

"No."

"We bring her the place. Later, if we have to, we bring her people enough to look at it and know what they're seeing."

"August'll say it's old work and none of anybody's business."

"August says many things."

"You think folks'll care about a meadow now? With fields failing and banks climbing on their backs?"

Hester looked toward town, though it was hidden by distance and the curve of the land. "Folks'll cross Main to stare at a drunk in the gutter and never once look at the ditch eating their topsoil. Let August stand over black mud in those good shoes of his. He'll sweat like any liar."

Dell rolled the tile fragment in his hand. It had warmed there, though the mud under his nails stayed cool. "You kept telling me this was about the land."

"It is."

"It's about my father too."

"Yes."

He waited for the softening after yes. There was none. That was why he believed her.

Cloud shadow did not pass, because there were hardly clouds any more, but the light altered as afternoon started wearing toward evening. It went flatter and meaner, all the shine burned off it. Dell stood and wiped his hands on his trousers, leaving black streaks that would tell on him back at the house. This time he minded. The stains looked like an answer he did not yet know how to speak.

"We should cover these holes some," he said. "Not all the way. Just enough no fool breaks an ankle."

"That fool might be us next week."

He drove the shovel under one more exposed joint. "We could take a whole length."

Hester was already shaking her head. "And let some bastard say we dug up a drain from God knows where and carried it out for show? No. The proof is the line in the ground. Leave it where it can keep accusing people."

That landed harder than if she had snapped. Dell set the shovel aside. Together they worked the dirt back in with their boots and the flat of the blade, leaving the line readable to anyone who knew to look and invisible to anyone who did not.

By the time they turned back north the heat had gone from brutal to lingering. His shoulders ached. Hester coughed twice into her handkerchief and once stopped long enough to spit and bury the spit with her heel. Dell pretended not to notice the care she took with that small privacy. He was learning there were decent ways to witness a person and indecent ones.

At the field edge she took the broken tile from him and wrapped it in the wax paper with the map.

"I want that," he said.

"You'll get it later."

"Why not now?"

"Because your mother empties your pockets when she washes your trousers, and because a shard in your hand proves less than the ditch itself. I haven't come two miles for her to find this before you've decided whether you're bringing her ground or just pain."

He could not argue. He hated that too.

Hester tucked the packet away. "Come by the bakery tomorrow if you can. If not, the day after. We'll pace it again."

"You think we need to?"

"Otto never trusted a thing he'd only paced once. He'd come home with burrs in his socks, drink his coffee cold, then tramp back out and make sure the ground hadn't fooled him. We'll pace it again."

They stopped where she would angle back toward town and he toward the house. The Vale place sat west in the long light, small and tired and still somehow bossy over the ground around it. Smoke from the cookstove went up straight for ten feet before the wind ruined it. Dell could picture Cora inside, setting beans on the table, counting in her head what was left in the jar, what could be sold, what could be mended, what could be hidden from fear until morning. If he walked in talking about Ezra and theft and tile before she had seen an inch of it, she would hear only a son dragging a dead man through the yard.

"You going to tell her?" Hester asked.

"Not all of it."

"That's not the same as lying."

"Feels close."

"Sure it does. So does lancing a boil. It still has to be done."

He looked south once more. The basin itself was invisible from here, folded back into ordinary failure. That was the part that would make telling dangerous. He could come in hot with half the truth and watch Cora brace against him before he ever got her boots on the right patch of ground. Or he could keep his mouth tighter, bring her out when there was enough light, and make the land do its own speaking.

"I thought if I found something," he said, "I'd know whether to hate him or not."

Hester's face did not soften. She looked at him the way she'd looked at the tile in the ditch: steady, unsparing, unwilling to pretty a thing just because it hurt. "That's wanting the rows straight after a blow. Otto used to come in half-choked with dust, cussing because the field still showed every crooked pass. It didn't make the land forget it."

He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "I know."

"Your father's dead. What he put his foot to isn't. You'd better see the print plain."

Dell looked at the house again, then at the south land beyond it, and felt the shape of his choice settle with the same plainness as the tile line under dirt. He would rather know what ugly thing Ezra had done or helped do or looked away from than spend the rest of his life propping up a saint built out of silence. Saints were useless on a farm anyway. They never lifted, dug, paid, or stayed.

"All right," he said.

Hester touched the brim of her hat, a gesture so slight it might have been to the light instead of to him. Then she turned toward town, broad-backed and stooped and steady, the packet in her apron pocket knocking against her leg with each step. Dell watched until she crossed the road ditch and disappeared in the pale rise of the land.

After that he went home to whatever waited, rehearsing the smaller truth he could stand to say first: the basin was real, Otto had been right, and Cora needed to see it before anybody talked himself into a better lie. He came in after dark with damp earth still on his hands.
