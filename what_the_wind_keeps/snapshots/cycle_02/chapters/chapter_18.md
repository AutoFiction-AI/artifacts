# Chapter 18

The bakery had gone too quiet for a room with dying lungs in it.

Cora woke on the narrow cot behind the counter because the silence had changed shape. The flour sack she had thrown over herself after midnight had slipped to her waist. Her mouth felt lined with grit. Under the worktable sat the valises they had packed after supper, the jar crate wrapped in a quilt, Dell's tool roll, Thea's books tied with clothesline. They had meant to leave after first light. The breadboards were scrubbed. The coffee pot had been filled the night before. Hester had said they would all think straighter after sleep, which was another way of saying nobody in that room had any sense left.

Now the bakery held the cold, dry smell it took on before dawn in late October, yeast gone flat, stove iron giving up what little heat it had kept, herbs in the rafters sharp as medicine. There was no cough from the back room, no muttered complaint, no spoon ringing the side of a cup.

Thea was standing in Hester's doorway in her stockings, one hand braced on the frame. Her hair had come loose down one side and made her face look younger and more frightened at once.

"Cora," she said, not loudly. "It's now."

Cora crossed the floor in two steps and felt every loose board under her bare feet because the room had gone so still. Dell was awake too, sitting up on the pallet he had made by the sacks, blanket around his shoulders, his bad left arm tucked close in the old habit he had never managed to break after the trench collapse. He did not speak. He looked as if speech would crack something and spill it.

Hester lay on top of the blanket instead of under it because she had spent the night too hot to bear weight and too cold to stop shivering. Somebody, probably Thea, had opened the window a finger. The predawn air came in carrying dirt-road cold and the far smell of empty fields. Hester's face had sharpened in the dark hours. Her lips were split in two places. The skin under her eyes had gone the color of dishwater. On the chair beside the bed sat the basin of rag water, a spoon, a bottle of horehound syrup, and the enamel bowl she had been spitting into. What was in the bowl was not fit to look at for long: gray ropey phlegm, threads of blood, the fine black powder of six months' weather worked up from somewhere too deep to clean.

Each breath started low in her chest, stuck, then came loose in a rough climb. Cora had heard calves breathe that way when mud got down where it ought not. She had heard Ezra breathe that way in his last influenza winter before it passed. But this was not a night of waiting to see if a fever broke. This was the season finally collecting.

Hester's eyes moved toward her. They were still themselves, still sharp enough to make a person feel caught in idleness.

"You look hellish," Hester said.

Her voice had not gone soft. It had only gone thin.

"You too," Cora said, and Hester's mouth twitched once at the corner.

Thea lifted the cup. "Try some water."

Hester took a swallow because pride had spent itself before dawn, then coughed it back into the rag tucked under her chin. Her whole body bent with it. The sound seemed impossible to come out of something that slight. Cora got a hand behind her shoulders and felt the shoulder blades hard as kindling through the gown. The cough went on until Hester brought up a clot dark enough to be nearly black. When it was done, she lay back with her eyes shut, using her breath like a woman counting coins she knew would not cover the bill.

"I don't want a preacher," she said after a little while.

Nobody answered at first. The clock by the yeast tins ticked in the next room.

Hester opened her eyes again. "I mean it. I don't want some man that never kneaded a loaf in August telling lies over me."

"He won't," Cora said.

"Put me beside Otto, not by the girl. I've looked at that space long enough."

"All right."

Thea wet the rag and wiped the corners of Hester's mouth. Her hand was steady now because there was work to do. Work steadied her more reliably than comfort ever had. "Do you want me to fetch anybody?"

"No." Hester's answer came at once. "If folks hear the bell after, they'll know. That's enough town for one morning."

Dell had got up without Cora hearing him. He was at the foot of the bed, hair standing every direction, face still puffy with sleep and seventeen-year-old stubbornness. Hester moved her gaze to him.

"You still got the seed?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"Don't call me that now. It makes me sound upholstered." Her breath hitched. She waited it out. "You mind the packet. And mind the blue-lid jar. Don't open it in the bakery. Open it at the basin."

"I know."

"No, you don't. You only know I said it." She looked back to Cora. "He'll leave half this world unopened if nobody nags him."

"I can hear you," Dell said, which would have had more force if his voice had not broken in the middle.

Hester let out something that was almost a laugh and almost another cough. It ended as pain. Her hand fumbled over the blanket until Cora took it. The broad palm that had hauled bread trays and wash tubs and Mason jars all summer felt wrong now, too light and too dry, the bones sitting up in it like stakes under thin dirt.

"You don't need to drag this out for us," Cora said quietly.

Hester turned her head enough to pin her with one tired eye. "I wasn't planning to perform."

Then she did what dying bodies did when the person inside them could no longer keep up the pretense of management. She breathed. She stopped. She tried again. Her throat worked. Thea held the cup near but Hester no longer saw it. Dell had gone white clean through the dust on him. Cora bent close enough to feel the old woman's breath on her cheek, stale syrup and blood and the bakery underneath it, the yeast that had lived in Hester's hands for years. One more breath came, thin as paper tearing. The next did not.

The quiet after it hit the room like a tool set down after ugly work. It had the feel of machinery stopping, of a task completed, of a pump that had finally failed after running too long against grit.

Thea set the cup down without a sound. Dell made a noise once in his nose as if he were about to cough and could not. Cora kept holding Hester's hand because it was warm and because the warmth was leaving quickly enough to make a person stupid with the wish to stop it.

Outside, somewhere east of town, a rooster tried the morning and was answered by none of the others.

Tears had no room yet. There was too much to put in order.

Cora closed Hester's mouth. Thea drew the sheet up and then folded it back again because there was water to heat and a body to wash before the cold made the joints stubborn. Dell went out to the pump in his undershirt with the galvanized bucket and came back with water sloshing over his bare wrist. He set wood in the stove one-handed and took twice the time he would have before his arm was broken. Nobody offered to do it for him. He would have turned mean if they had.

When the water steamed, Cora and Thea carried it in. Thea found the cleanest of Hester's work dresses in the press, the blue one with the cuffs turned so many times the original hem no longer showed. Cora held it up.

"Not the black," she said.

Thea glanced toward the darker dress hanging behind it. "No."

"She hated how it made her look dead already."

"I remember."

They worked without gentleness that would have been a kind of insult. Cora washed the dust and syrup and the final night's sweat from Hester's face and neck, from the hollows under her breasts, from her calves gone stringy with illness. She cleaned the blood from the cracked lips with the edge of the rag. Thea combed out the white hair and braided it once down the back because loose hair had always annoyed Hester when she worked. Between them they got the blue dress on her and buttoned it. The hands would not lie flat over the belly. The fingers kept wanting to curl back into labor. Cora tucked Otto's handkerchief, washed and ironed into a square years ago, under them so they had something to hold.

Dell stayed by the stove until Cora called him in. He came to the doorway and stopped. His eyes went first to the face, then to the dress, then to the braids.

"That right?" Cora asked.

He swallowed. "She'd've cussed the black one."

"I know."

He nodded once, hard, as if nodding were labor too, then went out again. Through the front window Cora watched him cross the street to the livery yard with his hat on wrong and his right shoulder pitched forward against the cold. Ten minutes later he came back with Rudy Menke and the flat wagon.

Rudy had the same one-sided hitch in his walk he had carried since a hay elevator chewed part of his left heel off in 1928. He still wore the damaged boot because he mistrusted new leather and because nobody in Morrow threw out anything that could be laced one more time. The boot made him sound half lame and half impatient on the bakery step.

He took off his hat when he saw Hester laid out. For a second the wind from the open door moved over his flattened hair and Cora saw how much scalp had come up through it the past year.

"Dell said," was all he offered.

"Thank you," Cora said.

He nodded. That was the extent of the ceremony.

There was lumber enough in the bakery shed if you were willing to build a coffin the way poor people built everything: from what existed, with no time for matching boards. Rudy and Dell took down shelf planks and the sound of hammering started up in the yard while Cora locked the front door against customers who would not be coming. Thea tore flour sacks into long strips for padding. Cora found the last coil of rope, two candles, Hester's purse with the key ring and fifty-eight cents in it, and a folded account sheet where Hester had written three names still owing for bread. She put the paper back. The dead did not need debt collected after them.

By the time the sun got high enough to turn the front glass pale yellow, the box was built and set in the bakery with the wagon waiting outside. Dell had driven the nails with his right hand only. The blows had gone slightly crooked at the corners where his arm tired. Cora left them crooked. Straight would have told the wrong story.

They laid Hester in it together. She weighed less than a feed sack half full. That angered Cora more than anything else that morning. A woman who had held a bakery, a marriage, a daughter's grave, a town's memory, and one stubborn boy's questions ought not to go to earth weighing almost nothing.

Mrs. Koenig came just as they were fixing the lid. She had pinned her hat on too fast and the jet bead at the brim hung by a thread, knocking softly against her cheek every time she moved. In one hand she held a bunch of late asters with half the petals browned by dust.

"For the box," she said, and looked past Cora at Hester's face. "She told me last spring my stamp glue was made of horse spit and penny shame. I was still planning to answer her properly."

"Put them in," Cora said.

Mrs. Koenig laid the asters at Hester's feet because there was not much other room. Her mouth worked twice before she got it set again. Then she went back out, probably to tell the town with the precision of a postmistress and the grief of a woman who had run out of recipients.

They took Hester to the cemetery before the day could warm. The wagon wheels made a dry grinding sound on the street. Boards on the storefronts stared back at them. A dog nosed along the gutter, found nothing, and kept going. Cora walked beside the wagon with one hand on the plank because the idea of letting the dead ride unattended through town felt wrong. Thea came on the other side carrying the jar crate wrapped in the quilt. Dell drove Rudy's team, jaw set, hat brim low, already looking too much like a man doing work other people were not ready to see him do.

The cemetery sat north of the church and west of the last line of houses, where the town gave up and the open land took over. Otto's stone leaned a little to the left. The name on it had been cut shallow to save money and the years had worn the letters even shallower, but it still read. Beside it the patch Hester had claimed was mostly buffalo weed and hard dirt, still uncut when they brought her there. Rudy and Dell took the spade to it while Cora and Thea stood with the wagon, trading off once Dell's ribs started to catch. The dirt they brought up was dark for the top foot and then the familiar dead color underneath, powder with pebbles in it.

No preacher came. Cora had meant what she said.

They got the box down with the ropes and the hard part was not the weight. The hard part was the last foot, when the ropes hissed under their palms and the pine dropped that little final distance and made a sound like furniture set down in the wrong room. Dell shut his eyes at it. Thea's mouth pulled tight, then opened again.

Rudy cleared his throat. "She kept my twins fed that winter Mae took sick."

It sounded as if the sentence had surprised him by existing.

"She kept a good many people fed," Thea said.

Mrs. Koenig, who had followed in her own time and now stood with her gloves tucked in her belt, added, "She also knew everybody's sins and never wasted them on entertainment."

That almost got Hester back into the air for a second. Cora could hear the reply she would have made, something dry and exact and a little cruel in the useful direction. The shape of it moved through her and was gone.

Dell looked down into the grave. His voice came out rough from the nose rather than the throat. "She didn't flatter anybody. That was the kind part."

Cora put her hand on the back of his coat between the shoulders because there was nowhere else to put it with the bad arm and the spade and all the hurt pride of him. "No," she said. "It was."

That was enough speaking.

The dirt hit the pine first in clots, then in a steadier fall as the shoveling found a rhythm. Cora did not look away. She watched until the box disappeared, until the mound stood above the ground by half a foot, until the asters were under earth and could no longer do anything. The wind moved across the grass and did not make a sermon out of it. Beyond the cemetery the town sat low and thinned out, grain elevator, church roof, the line of main street, Hester's bakery chimney. It looked less like a place in danger than a place already mostly over.

Rudy stuck the spade in the mound and settled his damaged boot against it. "I'll bring back the wagon after."

"We'll walk," Cora said.

He studied her a moment, then the town, then Dell. "Suit yourself."

After he left, the four of them stood with Otto and Hester together under a sky the color of old tin. Cora pressed her palm against Otto's leaning stone. It felt colder than it ought to have for that hour. Thea set the jar crate down between the graves and loosened the quilt.

"We should go before we start inventing reasons not to," she said.

Her tone was controlled, classroom neat for one beat, which was how Cora knew she was close to breaking. Then Thea looked straight at her and added, more plainly, "If we wait till dark, we won't go."

Cora nodded. "The basin first."

They walked south through town to the road, carrying what could be carried. Dell took the tool roll and one valise. Cora got the heavier case and the bundle of bedding. Thea took the books and the jars because she trusted her hands with glass more than she trusted the road. They left the rest at the bakery: the bread tins, the sacks, the account paper, the worn apron on its nail. Mrs. Koenig would see to the key or wouldn't. That belonged to the dead and to the town.

The Vale place showed itself before they reached it, not because the house was proud but because the fields around it had been flattened so thoroughly there was nothing left to hide behind. The auction man had not moved in yet. The gate hung open. One shutter banged loose on the west side. Somebody had taken the pump handle. The chicken run leaned in on itself as if it had been told the news and agreed.

The road to Saint's Basin cut south before the yard. Cora stopped without meaning to. The house stood there with all the ordinary failure of a house after people had been stripped out of it: no mystery, no music, just window glass gone blind and a line of dust drawn thick on the porch rail where her hand had not wiped it in days.

Thea shifted the crate against her hip. "Do you need a minute?"

Cora kept looking. The kitchen was behind that west wall. Dell's little bedroom was there too, the one where his feet had knocked the rail when he was growing too fast for it, and so was the room where Ezra had pulled off his boots and once, years ago, stood grinning stupid and shy while he shook rainwater out of his hat onto the floor because she had married him despite better information. There was nothing redemptive in the sight of it now. The house had taken what it could and been taken in turn.

"No," she said. "If I start touching things, I'll still be there at supper."

Dell did not look at the house. "There's not much left to touch anyway."

His voice was clipped but not cruel. Cora heard the effort in it. She started walking again.

Saint's Basin held its shape more plainly in autumn than it had in summer. The reopened seam was a dark crescent in the land, the soil there blacker, finer, capable of taking a heel mark instead of only throwing dust. At the bottom, where the dig had bitten and then slumped, a skim of water still gathered. Ice had filmed one edge in lace no bigger than a hand.

The air there smelled of damp root and mineral, with the sweet rot of things that had once grown thick enough to die back into themselves. Cora had smelled it only a few times in her life and each time it had made the rest of the county seem fraudulent.

Dell set down the valise and took Otto's seed packet out of his coat. The paper had softened at the corners from being handled too much. Thea knelt and unwrapped the jar crate on the ground between them. Inside, the jars knocked softly together. The one with the blue lid sat in the middle exactly where Hester had put it.

"She knew we'd open it first if it sat on top," Thea said.

"Mm." Cora crouched and ran her thumb over the scratched enamel of the lid. Hester had marked it with a single nick beside the clasp, not a word, not anything showy. Just enough to tell it from the rest by touch.

Dell looked at the basin instead of the jar. "What if it's bad?"

Thea answered before Cora could. "Then it's bad."

"Helpful."

"I wasn't trying to be pleasant."

That got the edge of a breath out of him, not quite laughter. Cora heard how tired they all were and how near meanness sat to tenderness when people had not slept.

"Open it," she said.

He broke the wax with his thumbnail. The seal gave like old fat. When he twisted the lid free, cold water seemed to breathe out of the jar: goose droppings, dead reeds, iron, the rank sweetness of wet mud turned by boots. It smelled like November, maybe December, a season Cora had almost forgotten could exist in a place like this as anything but exposure.

Dell tipped a little of the dust into his palm. It went dark at once and spread, a stain chasing the lines in his skin. The air over the basin tightened. The skim of present water went dim as a mirror breathed on. Then the whole hollow took it.

Water lifted where there was only memory for it.

The old basin came back in a skin of moving glass wide enough to hold a weather front. It offered no miracle and no salvation, only the loud old abundance of the place. Thin ice jeweled the edges. Brown reeds shouldered up in clumps. A ragged flock of geese dropped from the white sky with their wings braking hard, feet flung down, noise spilling out of them like hinges and brass. Two boys ran the bank hollering and slipped in the mud. A woman in a man's coat laughed so hard she doubled. Dogs tore after one another through the sedge. A wagon stood on the rise with sacks stacked high and a kettle hanging from the side. Somebody had spread blankets on the ground. Somebody else was gutting fish with cold-red hands. The place was busy and crowded, and it smelled of bird shit and smoke and wet wool and soup. It was full of people who had come because the basin held what they needed and because there were enough of them then to make a gathering of almost anything.

Otto stood in the shallows younger and straighter than Cora had ever seen him, trousers rolled, a willow switch in one hand. He was swearing at a boy to stop throwing clods at the geese and smiling while he did it. Hester was there too, broad-backed under a red scarf, carrying a pan from the wagon as if her arms had always belonged to weight. She turned to shout something over her shoulder and Marta, still little enough to be round in the face, came tearing past with goose feathers stuck to one stocking.

Ezra was there too, but he was not the Ezra of the barn rope or the sick last winter or the letter read to a room full of bidders. This one was maybe sixteen, all knees and careless shoulders, dark hair under a cap, holding a pair of skates by the laces. He shoved another boy with his hip, nearly lost his footing, recovered, and laughed at himself with his whole head thrown back. Then he ran down toward the water as if the earth might actually keep him.

Cora felt the laugh before she made it. It rose up out of her in one rough piece because the sight of him almost falling was absurd and because Hester, ten feet off in that lost season, turned and slapped his sleeve as he went by without any more reverence than she had shown him at forty-four. Thea laughed too, one startled syllable. Dell made a sound Cora had not heard from him in months, an unguarded boy's bark of amusement cut short by shock.

The memory held another moment, with more geese settling, a fiddle scraping from somewhere near the wagon, men hauling in a net heavy enough to silver at the edges, women's voices cutting across one another, and children too many to count at once, too many to fit with the town as it stood now.

Then the basin gave the image back to the air. The water skin crumpled. The geese went soundless. Dell's palm was dry again, only dust ground into the lines.

No one moved for a little while.

Thea sat back on her heels. Her eyes were red-rimmed but dry. "I had thought," she said carefully, then stopped and started again. "I had thought the last one might accuse somebody."

"It did worse," Cora said.

Thea looked at her.

"It remembered them alive."

Dell was staring at his own hand. "He looked like an idiot."

"He did," Cora said.

"He looked..." He swallowed and kicked at the damp edge of the basin. "He looked like he belonged somewhere."

Cora could not tell him he had belonged there, not without also telling him that belonging and ruining were often done by the same pair of hands. Nothing she could add felt clean enough to deserve saying. She settled for the truth she had.

"For a while," she said.

He nodded, angry at the nod even while he gave it. That was closer to peace than any speech either of them could have managed.

Thea opened the seed packet. The grains were small and light, not much to show for Otto's long stewardship. She poured some into Cora's palm, some into Dell's, kept some for herself. They scattered it over the dark crescent where the soil still had enough life to take a mark. The seeds looked ridiculous in flight, too little for the acreage of damage. Then they hit the damp ground and vanished into a place built to hide things until their season came.

"It may not take till spring," Thea said.

"If it takes at all," Dell muttered.

Cora rubbed the last few seeds from her fingers. "It isn't a promise. It's only seed."

"That sounds like you trying not to hope," Thea said.

"That sounds like me knowing what fieldwork is."

Thea's mouth shifted, almost smiling. "Fair."

Dell flung the last of his seed harder than he needed to. "If something comes up, it comes up."

There was more in that than grass. Cora let it stand.

She looked once more at the basin, not the remembered one, the real one now: black seam, torn bank, shallow water, the work of human greed and human labor both still visible in it. Hester lay in town with Otto. The place they had named was not healed. It had only been told true.

"All right," she said. "Let's go catch that train."

The depot sat at the east edge of Morrow where the town thinned to track, weed, and cinders. By the time they reached it the sun had gone up to its mean afternoon place, bright enough to show every board split and every pane dirt-filmed. Cora's shoulders burned from the valise. Dell had taken the jar crate from Thea after the basin without asking and now carried it tucked against his ribs, careful despite himself. Thea had put on her hat somewhere along the walk and brushed her skirt with her free hand as if order could be restored by increments.

Lafe Hulett was at the station window with his green eyeshade on, though the office behind him was dim as a cellar. He had a way of worrying the soft bottom of one ear with his thumb whenever he listened for the wire, and he was doing it now while the telegraph clicked and failed to make him any younger. He looked up as they came in and took in the luggage, the quilt around the jars, the cemetery dirt still dried on Dell's cuffs.

"Afternoon mixed is eight minutes behind," he said. Then his eyes shifted to Cora's face and, because he was not stupid, to the black ribbon Thea had tied around one sleeve on the walk over. "Heard about Mrs. Blum."

"This morning," Cora said.

He nodded once. "She used to bring me the burnt rolls. Said I was the only fool in town with no taste worth protecting."

"That sounds like her," Thea said.

He gave the shortest of smiles, printed the tickets, and slid them under the window without another question. That kindness, in Morrow, mostly looked like not making a person narrate herself.

The platform boards were loose and silvered. A freight car sat dead on the spur two tracks over with FARMERS CO-OP half flaked off the side. West of town the elevator stood above everything else the way power kept doing even after it had thinned. The wind was up just enough to move dust in low skirls along the cinders but not enough to turn the day mean.

They set the luggage down in a line because putting things in order delayed departure by a minute and because it was easier to fuss with objects than with the road ahead.

"If the library job's gone, it's gone," Cora said. She was looking at the track, not at Thea. "We'll still need a room somewhere. Dell can find work. I can sew or take in washing."

Thea tucked a loose pin under her hat brim. "Yes."

"I'm not saying that because I think you'll fail."

"I know what you're saying."

Cora turned then, irritated because Thea's voice had gone educated and smooth in that way it did when she was frightened. "Do you?"

Thea met her squarely. "I do. I also know Lincoln may tell me no and I may have to hear it in a building with polished floors and a receptionist who can smell county dust before I open my mouth. I know that part. I'm coming anyway."

There it was, the careful sentence and then the blunt one right after. Cora let out air through her nose.

"All right."

Dell nudged the crate with his boot. "If Lincoln doesn't want us, somebody east will. People have motors everywhere. They break everywhere."

"That's a stirring speech," Thea said.

"I wasn't trying to be stirring."

"No," Cora said. "You were trying to sound older than you are."

He cut her a look. "I'm older than I was in July."

That was true enough to stop her.

The rail gave a far trembling before the whistle reached them. Lafe came out with the flag rolled under his arm. The telegraph office smell followed him, hot dust and oil and old paper. He glanced down the line and then back at them.

"You boarding?" he asked, though the answer was in the luggage.

Cora looked west without meaning to. From here the town was mostly roofs and the elevator and one church point. She could not see the farm. She could see the sky above it, the same sky that had turned black at noon in April and yellow all summer and now sat pale and used-out above October. For one frail second the whole business of leaving felt insane. Hester was under fresh dirt. The bakery key was still warm from her purse in Cora's pocket. The road east might offer work, or insult, or hunger in a different arrangement. The land west of town still knew her boot heel.

Then Dell, reckless in the old way but not stupid anymore, picked up the jar crate and stepped to the edge of the platform before the train had even fully slowed.

"Come on," he said. He was not pleading or commanding. He was simply refusing the stall.

Thea lifted her books and valise. "Cora."

The way she said it landed clean and hard, stripped of romance or sacrifice. It meant that Cora had to choose.

Cora took Hester's purse out of her coat pocket, felt the small key on its ring, and set the purse on the station bench. Lafe saw the gesture and said nothing. She picked up the heavier valise, adjusted the bedding under her arm, and walked toward the opening passenger car as the brakes shrieked and grit blew up around their shoes.

The steps were high. Dell got the crate inside first and turned, catching the valise from her with his right hand, the left still close to his body when he forgot about it. Thea came behind, skirt gathered, face set. Lafe handed up the last bag with one free palm and tipped two fingers at them instead of a hat.

Inside, the car smelled of coal soot, old upholstery, and other people's travel. The seats were worn smooth at the arms. A man two rows up slept with his mouth open over a sample case. A woman in a plum coat held a chicken basket under her feet and kept glancing at it as if she mistrusted silence from any living thing. Cora slid in by the window because if she did not watch the town go she knew she would imagine it instead.

Dell put the jar crate on the seat between his knees. Thea sat opposite with the books in her lap and one glove still unbuttoned.

The train jolted once, twice, then gathered itself and moved.

Morrow went by without ceremony. The depot drifted past. Lafe shrank to a man with a flag. The freight car, the elevator, the church point, the strip of main street, all of it slipped sideways through the dirty glass. For a second Cora thought she could make out the line of the road toward Saint's Basin, the darker nick in the fields where water had once had its own mind. Then the angle changed and the town flattened into its own color, boards and dust and distance made one thing again.

Cora set her hand on the crate. The jars clicked softly inside with the train's sway, a sound like thin glass finding its place against other thin glass and not breaking yet.
