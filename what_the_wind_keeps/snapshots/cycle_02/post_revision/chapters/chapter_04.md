# Chapter 4

Bank air had once steadied him: paper, coin metal, dried ink, sweat long worked into the counter rail. Numbers did not plead. They either added or refused. August let himself into Farmers State Bank before seven. The key dragged in the lock. Dust did that too, found the cut of the wards and sat there like spite. Outside, the light over Main Street was still the color of old muslin, and the dust had not yet decided whether it meant to rise again. He shouldered the door, felt packed grit grind under the threshold, and stood a moment in that cool dim smell.

He crossed to the rear office, set down his hat, and opened the safe. The inner hinges gave their small morning complaint. He brought out the till drawer, the wrapped coins, the note packets banded with paper gone soft at the edges, and laid them in order on the desk under the green-shaded lamp. Dust had crept in everywhere after Black Sunday, not in dramatic drifts now but in the meaner way, inside the seams of the cash drawer, along the teeth of the adding rack, inside the blotter where his wrist rested. For three weeks the work of the bank had doubled. First you did the work itself. Then you did the work of scraping weather out of it.

The front room came into shape as the light improved: teller cage with its brass worn pale by hands, two customer stools rubbed slick at the rung, the water cooler with a chipped blue line round the shoulder, the spittoon beside the stove, the rack of deposit slips that schoolchildren used for scratch paper once they were spoiled by a wrong figure. The bank was not a stage set for anyone's wickedness. It was where Mrs. Gansey brought the money from egg sales rolled in old seed envelopes, where burial drafts were cashed, where the school board's thin account sat beside wheat notes already beginning to smell like lies. If the place failed, the town would not gain virtue. It would lose another door.

He finished his count, entered the opening cash in the daybook, and turned toward the narrow mirror hung beside the hat pegs. He meant only to inspect the line of dust at his collar. Instead he breathed, and the glass went milky under that breath.

The silver darkened. Another room moved inside the room.

His father stood at the loan table with Otto Blum's survey paper spread under one big hand. He was younger than August had allowed him to be in memory, shoulders still broad, mouth still willing to harden into certainty before thought had run its course. Otto had come in from the road with a measuring stake under his arm and mud dried white around the welt of one boot. He never could set a tool down while he argued. The old basin lay on the paper in blue wash, south of the Vale tract, a shallow thumbprint of water and grass.

"That wet holds the spring," Otto said. The memory gave only the one line cleanly, but August heard the rest in the shape of the man's jaw. "You cut tile through there and the ground won't keep for you."

His father flattened the blue wash with two fingers, bent on pressing water out of the county by hand. "We're not keeping a meadow for geese."

Then younger August was there too, not speaking, only standing by the safe with a ledger in both hands, watching while the bargain tipped. The indecency lay with him, not with the father who had been dead nine years and could no longer answer; he had been old enough already to know that a man could be wrong in a room full of paperwork and still have every page come out in his favor.

The mirror cleared at the edges. His own face came back through his father's, heavy now, carefully shaved, furrows at mouth and brow carrying a kind of authority that felt each month more like residue.

He took the ledger ruler from the desk and struck the glass once, hard enough to split it from upper left to lower right. The sound was flat and mean in the empty bank. He struck again. The cheap backing gave way in bright scales. For a second he saw himself repeated in a spray of broken Augusts. Every shard gave the old man inside the frame credit for the damage. Then he wrapped the pieces in yesterday's crop reports, tied the bundle with string, and set it by the stove for disposal.

When Miss Havel came in ten minutes later, she looked at the paper parcel and then at the vacant nail on the wall. She had a habit of touching the split skin beside her right thumbnail whenever she was deciding whether a question was safe. Dust and banknotes opened the crack afresh every day; she kept a blue cold-cream tin in the bottom drawer and fed that thumb like a separate dependent.

"Did something fall?" she asked.

"The mirror gave way."

She glanced once more at the parcel. "Everything is giving way."

"Not everything," he said.

Miss Havel took off her hat, a brown felt one with last year's ribbon turned to hide the fade, and hung it in the back. She moved through the front room setting it to rights with the quick economy of a woman who had been doing two jobs since Mr. Enright went to Colorado and never returned. She opened the cage, shook dust from the rug onto the step, checked the stamp pad, and set the small vase she insisted on keeping by the public pen chain back in the center of the sill. There were no flowers in it, only two stems of fennel gone gray-green from the road edge, but she said the place looked less like a coffin parlor that way.

By eight-thirty the bank had begun its daily performance of normal life. Mrs. Gansey came in with three dollars and change wrapped in wax paper from the chicken yard. She removed her dead husband's spectacles from her purse before she counted the bills, though she had not needed them for months; the gesture was simply part of how she conducted transactions now, and his dead approval still had to be fetched before money crossed a counter. August credited her account, asked after her sister's cough, and did not let his eye travel to the small withdrawal slip already waiting in her passbook for next week.

After her came Rolf Sutter from east of town, hat in both hands, claiming the axle on his binder had set him back worse than expected. Rolf had one ear that reddened whenever he lied. It reddened now. August knew the man had spent part of his last cream check on a train fare to see a girl in McCook and did not begrudge him the impulse. He merely could not finance it.

"Saturday," August said, keeping his voice level enough that Miss Havel at the cage would hear calm and not concession. "I can carry the interest until Saturday, no later."

Rolf's red ear deepened. "I appreciate that."

"Appreciation does not enter the ledger. Saturday does."

Rolf gave a thin smile at that, because people often mistook August's exactness for wit when it was only exactness, signed the renewal slip, and went out into the white street carrying his hat by the brim like something breakable. August watched him through the bank window until he turned the corner. Half the work lay in that too: keeping men from lingering in the doorway with defeated faces, because one defeated face invited a second and a second could turn into a line.

At ten the county examiner arrived from McCook in a dust-whitened Buick. LeRoy Bender came in brushing his sleeves with both hands, though there was no point in it. Dust had breaded him for frying. He was a narrow man with pale lashes and a necktie singed near the knot where cigar ash had eaten it weeks ago. Each time August saw him he was carrying the same round peppermint tin covered in yellow cloth by some careful wife or sister. He set it on the counter before he even removed his gloves.

"Road is filthy," Bender said.

"That is not new."

"No. It's only worsening, which is the distinction that matters."

Miss Havel brought coffee to the back office without being asked. Bender declined sugar, took a peppermint from the tin instead, and let it soften against his cheek while August laid out the current-note register, the delinquent file, and the collateral abstracts. Through the half-open office door the ordinary sounds of the bank went on: cage drawer sliding, pen chain rattling, somebody coughing up dust in the front room and then apologizing for weather, treating it like bad manners.

Bender flipped pages with the backs of his fingers, unwilling to wet them with his tongue in a season when every surface tasted of road. "You have too much paper in extension."

"You would say that in any farm county after a storm like April."

"I said it in Holdrege on Monday and in Indianola on Tuesday. That doesn't make it false here." Bender turned another page, made a small mark in pencil. "State men want to know what is live and what is wishful. By the time your federal land fellow starts sorting farms, anything you've carried out of sentiment will read as concealment."

August sat very still. "Resettlement is not my business."

"No. Paper is. Give me durations and collateral."

August looked at the open register, at figures entered in his own hand across years when weather had seemed severe but still answerable. Drought had first looked like a temporary insult, then like bad luck, then like a test, and only lately had it begun to show itself as a condition capable of outlasting character. He hated the federal language around it because it made collapse sound administrative. Yet he hated more the possibility that some man from outside would come in, read his books, and find not hardship but indulgence.

"These are seasonal accommodations," he said. "Not charity."

Bender sucked the peppermint to one side of his mouth. "Then show me where the season ends."

August did. They moved tract by tract, name by name. Sorensen, carried thirty days. Blakely, doubtful but not yet for sale. Hutter, likely gone west before first payment. The work had the false comfort of dissection. So long as he said the names in order and Bender penciled his marks beside them, the county had edges. It could be classified. It could be borne.

Then Bender tapped the Vale file.

"This one."

August reached for the glass-front cabinet where he kept the active farm notes. Dust filmed the wavering pane. He had meant to have Miss Havel wash it before the examiner came. He had meant many things. He leaned close, turned the key, breathed on the latch without thinking, and the dust woke again.

Ezra Vale stood in the cabinet glass plain enough to have stepped back in from the hall. Sunday coat, elbows gone shiny, hat brim crushed in both hands. He had dirt ground into the seam at one knee and the look so many men had worn in that office in the last years, not yet beaten, merely prepared to bargain away one more necessary thing. Younger August took the deed from him and laid it flat. The memory did not spare him this time. Ezra was gone. The office door was closing. August's own hand, ten years younger and steadier than the one on the cabinet key now, dragged the file back and turned his shoulder toward the hall. His left thumb pinned the page just above Ezra's signature. With the penknife he worried at the month on the date line until the top sheet furred white. Then he set the nib into that little wound and wrote an earlier month over the torn fibers, went back over the last numeral of the day once, twice, until the stroke turned blacker than the rest. He blotted the wet ink with the heel of his hand and kept his palm there a moment, pressing the paper flat as if pressure could make the lie look born in it.

A fine scratching came off the glass, nib or nail or memory refusing to say which, and lodged inside his teeth.

"Poeschel?"

Bender's voice reached him from two rooms away, though the man sat less than six feet behind. August opened the cabinet, took out the file, and closed the pane before the memory could offer him his own justification.

"Here it is."

He laid the file on the desk. Bender thumbed through the renewal notes, the interest entries, the acreage description, the lien language. His brow did not alter, but he grew slower, and slowness from an examiner was worse than anger.

"This has been carried how long past the first default?"

"The husband died."

"The note did not."

August heard in his own answer the beginning of a protest he should not make. "Mrs. Vale has kept interest from going entirely stale. The farm still has standing wheat, poor as it is. There are hens, some sewing income, a boarder with wages."

Bender looked up over the file. "You're offering me household detail now."

"I am offering you facts."

"Then hear mine. I've spent the month opening books just like yours. One file carried past its collateral makes the whole room look soft. One altered date and the state men will sit here till dark turning every page you own."

He said it without relish. That made it land harder. August knew Bender had spent the past month driving from one failing little bank to the next, carrying bad news in figures because figures were the one form of bad news men still pretended to respect. The singed tie, the peppermint, the fine gray dust along the ridges of his ears, none of it belonged to a triumphant man.

"You imagine I enjoy carrying these notes," August said.

"I don't imagine you enjoy it. Nobody does. But if you want these doors to stay open, leave me the hens and the boarder and tell me whether the note is sound."

Miss Havel tapped on the doorframe. "Mr. Knapp is here."

Walter Knapp entered with a hat that had already been brushed three times since he crossed the street and would be brushed three more before he left. He was neat in the way of men whose work required travel but not labor, trim through the middle, collar still unfrayed, shoes expensive enough that Nebraska road dust looked temporary on them. Yet there was a tiredness at the corners of his mouth that kept him from seeming merely ornamental. He apologized to Miss Havel for carrying grit in, then brushed the hat again while saying it.

"Mr. Poeschel. Mr. Bender." He inclined his head toward the file on the desk. "I can take up three tracts this week. Hutter can move at once. Blakely, if you're done nursing him. Vale, if this extension isn't meant to hold. I'd rather not lose another day on any of them."

August did not invite him to sit. "No land improves under federal adjectives."

Knapp accepted that without offense. "No, but the schedule doesn't improve by waiting."

Bender made a small sound that might have been a swallowed laugh or a cough. August left it unanswered.

Knapp held out his hand for the file. August did not immediately yield it. That hesitation was brief, but everybody in the room felt it. Miss Havel, still in the doorway, lowered her eyes to the coffee tray.

"I'm not here to turn anyone out with my own hands," Knapp said, more quietly. "I'm here to sort what Washington will touch first. If you don't tell me which notes can be called cleanly, the weakest paper goes to the top on its own."

August let go of the file.

Knapp scanned the first page, then the second. He did not linger on Ezra's name. He lingered on acreage, security, carrying costs, and the margin notes August himself had made across the winter. When he spoke again his tone remained maddeningly even.

"The Vale place is west of town."

"Yes."

"Dry quarter, household short of cash, title burdened."

"Still occupied."

"Hutter is easier. He's already half gone, and the title won't quarrel. If you want to carry Mrs. Vale to wheat-cutting, give me Hutter and have your defense for Vale by morning. If you don't, put Vale on the list now."

August felt heat move under his collar despite the cool office. "That is a sentence easier said from Lincoln than from a county seat."

"I came from Lincoln this morning, not heaven." Knapp turned the page back. "By ten tomorrow I need your list: which notes can be called cleanly, which will resist. I'm asking for the legal answer, not the moral one. If you don't rank them, I will."

Scrutiny had tipped into triage: a neat deadline dropped onto years of local postponement. He wanted to tell Knapp that a bank president in a dying town did not traffic in hope. He trafficked in delay, which was a rougher and sometimes more useful commodity. But Bender was in the room, and Miss Havel at the door, and the file with its poisoned page lay open between them, and somewhere beyond the front counter Mrs. Dole was asking whether her money order had cleared because her boy in Denver had not written back.

Knapp closed the file and returned it.

"There is usually a connection between the two," August said.

"In my experience there's usually a lag."

When Knapp had gone, brushing his hat yet again as he crossed the front room, August remained standing. Outside the office Mrs. Dole said, too loudly now, "If the line west can mislay a money order, it can mislay other things." Miss Havel answered at once, calm as starch, but the cage drawer slammed harder than usual. Bender put the peppermint tin back into his pocket.

"Reserve figures after dinner," he said.

He gathered his gloves and left August alone with the Vale file on the desk.

The bank did not soften when the room cleared: the dry slam of the cage drawer, somebody clearing his throat, then coughing until the cough took over the whole place and would not be polite. August opened the file again. Ezra's signature sat there with its old strained force, letters pushed too hard at the downstrokes, pressure trying to pass for honesty. Beneath it the date line carried the wound plainly now: one month written over scraped fibers, the last numeral of the day twice as black as its neighbors, the paper puckered where the knife had lifted the first ink.

He rose, took a screwdriver from the supply drawer, went to the cabinet, and loosened the retaining clips that held the wavering glass in its frame. The pane came free warm from the office. For a moment he saw only his own hand through it, large and careful. Then another hand threatened underneath, sleeve cuff and bent head, left thumb pinning Ezra's name while the nib worried that last numeral darker.

He set the pane over the wastebasket and pressed down until it broke. The paperwork smothered the shatter into a soft collapsing music, brittle things giving way under teeth.

Miss Havel looked in. "Do you need me?"

"No."

"Was that the cabinet?"

"The pane was cracked already."

She absorbed the lie with the blankness of long employment. Then, because she was not merely a witness to male ruin, she said, "If Knapp wants something by morning, give him Hutter. Folks already expect that place to go. There's no sense kicking Mrs. Vale before Saturday."

He looked at her properly then. "You have an opinion."

"I've got an upstairs room over my aunt's store and rent due on the twelfth. Opinions come attached."

That shut him up more effectively than reproach would have. She had laid the choice before him plain as a ledger entry. Hutter: clean title, a man already halfway west in rumor if not in fact, a tract Knapp could rank without looking any widow in the face. August opened the lower drawer and laid the Hutter file beside the Vale note. The Hutter papers were thin, almost indecently neat. He could send that quarter to Knapp, let Cora's extension stand to wheat-cutting, and spend the night writing the reserve explanation Bender had not explicitly asked for and might still accept. He stood with one hand on each file until the tendons at his wrist began to ache. Hutter endangered the bank. Vale endangered him. He put Hutter back in the drawer. Then he took out the Vale note, folded a clean sheet into the typewriter, changed his mind, and pulled it free. He did not want Miss Havel retyping the language when the platen misstruck. He wanted the wording in his own hand. He wrote that the present extension would terminate on the first of June, that interest must be brought current by then, that failure would oblige the bank to proceed under lien. He used oblige because required sounded theatrical and force sounded vulgar. He signed it, blotted it, and hated how ordinary the page looked.

At half past four he told Miss Havel he would be out for an hour and took the Ford west toward the Vale place.

Town ended quickly now. One block of feed store and barber pole, another of the bakery and the schoolhouse, then the road loosening into ruts between fields that had once carried promise well enough to invite vanity. The wheat on the Vale quarter stood thin and uneven, a meager green gone gray where the dust had scoured it. Fence posts wore drifts at their windward sides like old men in bad collars. At the farmyard a chicken flapped off the porch rail and landed crooked, one wing missing feathers where some dog or child had once got hold of it and never finished the job.

Cora Vale was at the pump with her sleeves rolled and a dishpan at her feet. She had tied a flour sack over her hair against the grit, but ash-blond strands had worked loose at the temples. Her hands were in the water, red over the knuckles and cracked at the webbing, scrubbing egg baskets with more force than the task required. She looked up when she heard the car, shaded her eyes with a wet wrist, and did not smile.

"Mr. Poeschel."

He stepped out with the folded notice in his pocket and his hat still on. The discourtesy was deliberate; removing it would have felt like soliciting softness. "Mrs. Vale."

She glanced toward the road behind him, checking whether he had brought company. "If this is about the taxes, I told your clerk I'd be in town Tuesday."

"It's about the note."

The pump handle creaked once in the wind and settled. Somewhere behind the house a screen door struck and rebounded. He thought, without looking, that the schoolteacher might be inside. He kept his attention on Cora.

"I've revised the extension."

"Into what."

"The note will stand due on the first of June. Ten days."

She stared at him a second without comprehension, not because the words were difficult but because they were indecently plain. Then she dried one hand on her apron and held it out. He gave her the folded paper. She opened it with care that almost mocked him, eyes moving left to right across the short notice.

"Ten days," she said. Her voice was spare and flat, the kind that made other people fill silence for her. "You told Ezra it'd hold till wheat-cutting. After he died, you told me we'd talk when the stand showed itself."

"Circumstances have altered."

"Whose."

He could have said the county examiner's. He could have said Washington's. He could have said the bank's reserve position, or the way one over-carried farm note invited questions about all the others, or any of the accurate phrases that made men feel respectable while doing ugly work. Instead he heard himself answer with less cover than he preferred.

"Everybody's."

Cora looked past him to the wheat field, then back to the notice. "That field didn't stand any thicker because you drove out in a motorcar and pinned a date on it."

"The bank cannot carry indefinite paper."

"Wasn't indefinite last week."

"Last week no federal review had begun."

"So I catch it because you've got company."

"You are to answer the obligation."

She laughed once, without mirth, and bent to pick up the dishcloth the wind had pushed from the pump curb into the dust. When she straightened, the cloth hung from one hand like something skinned.

"With what money."

He disliked the question because it was the only honest one in the yard. The chicken on the porch rail hopped down and pecked at nothing. From inside the house came the faint clink of crockery, careful, listening crockery. August kept his tone formal because if he let anger into it he would sound personal, and if he sounded personal the whole thing would reveal itself as what it partly was.

"Interest current by June first. Then we may discuss a further arrangement."

"May."

"If there is a basis for it."

"And if there isn't."

"Then the bank proceeds under the lien."

Her face changed very little. People often misread that in Cora Vale. They mistook the economy of her expression for a lack of feeling when in fact it was labor, the same kind she gave to hauling water or mending harness, a practical containment of whatever would otherwise spill and make a greater mess. She folded the notice once, exactly on the crease it had arrived with.

"You should've written that the first time," she said. "Might've spared us the Sunday clothes."

The line struck harder than accusation would have. Ezra's shiny elbows flared in his head, then that maddening scratch at paper after the office door had shut, memory refusing to say whether it accused or merely remembered. August wanted, absurdly, to tell her that he had never meant the thing to stretch this far, that he had tried to hold a structure upright and had spent the years since mistaking the structure for virtue. But explanation would not cleanse anything here. It would only sound like a banker demanding sympathy from a woman at a pump.

"I will expect you Tuesday," he said.

"You'll get what there is."

"That is not always enough."

"No," she said. "I know."

Begging never entered it. He had prepared himself for begging because it could be classified, and for anger because that at least could be answered. This plain acknowledgment, spoken with egg water drying on her wrists and the whole starved farm open behind her, left him nothing procedural to stand on except the paper he had brought.

He touched the brim of his hat then, too late to count as courtesy, and turned back to the car. When he looked once through the windshield before starting the engine, Cora was still by the pump, notice in one hand, dishcloth in the other, upright in the yard, endurance in a flour sack and work shoes, and he had just put a date on that too. He drove toward town with the bank's clean language in his pocket and the dirt road shivering under the tires, and by the time Morrow came back into view he knew two things: the books would read better tomorrow, and the past had not been contained at all.
