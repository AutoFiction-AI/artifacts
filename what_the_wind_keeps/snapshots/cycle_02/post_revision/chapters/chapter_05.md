# Chapter 5

By supper, with August's ten days still rasping in her ear, the dust had gone so fine it no longer drifted in visible threads. It lived in the house. It hid in the fold of the flour sack, under the butter crock lid, in the seam of the table oilcloth, and it rasped under Cora's knife as she pared potatoes at the sink. The window over the pump had the weak yellow look dust gave evening now. Even the light came in dirty.

Dell sat at the table with a dead alarm clock spread in a pie tin. He had lined the gears in a careful half-moon by size and kept touching their teeth with a burnt matchstick, not because that did anything Cora knew of, but because he could not bear a broken thing to lie in one helpless heap. He liked failure better when it came apart into named pieces. The clock case stood open by his elbow, nickel worn to a dull pewter, the little glass face clouded at one corner.

"You going to eat that raw or help me?" Cora asked.

He did not look up at once. "I'm looking for where it binds."

"The potato or the clock."

That got the corner of his mouth, not quite a smile. "Clock."

He was seventeen and all elbows until a machine was put in his hands. Then some steadier thing came over him. He bent close, dust-light hair falling toward the tin, and squinted with one eye shut, listening to a spring that had not made a sound in years. Cora watched him a second longer than she meant to. He had Ezra's long fingers and none of Ezra's ease. Even his patience showed strain; he had to hold it down with his shoulders.

Thea came in from the back step carrying the wash she had snatched from the line before dusk. Her dress had once been navy and now looked permanently weathered, the cuffs let down and rehemmed so neatly the mending showed only when she turned her wrists. There was chalk at the side of one finger and a line of dust where her collar had rubbed her throat. She set the basket on the chair by the stove and pushed loose dark hair back with the heel of her hand. Even tired, she set every object down without a sound. That schoolroom habit stayed on her in public, but there was something hotter under it too, something that broke rank the minute Cora got her alone and that Cora had been refusing all week.

She had been keeping off Thea for four nights. That was the farm phrase for a horse gone touchy, and it was the true one. Since August had sat behind his clean desk and talked about shortened grace in the tone of a man crossing mercy off his books, since the front-room window had thrown Ezra back at her with his head bent over paper he had sworn he never signed, Cora had put work between herself and Thea and tried to make labor do the job of a bolted door. She rose earlier. She stayed out later. She came to bed sore enough to hope sleep might do what will would not. Want only sharpened. It followed Thea's sleeve through a room. It stood behind her when she bent over the stove. It made a fool of Cora's hands whenever a plate passed from one woman to the other.

Thea hung the damp shirts on chair backs and said, "The west room still smells of that vinegar rag. I don't think it ever won't."

"Better vinegar than mouse," Cora said.

"Or schoolhouse paste," Dell put in without lifting his head. "That gets in the back of your nose."

Thea turned to him then, and some of the strain left her face. "You'd know. You used to eat it."

"I was six."

"You were ten and lying about it."

He gave her a look meant to be hard and ruined it by going red at the ears. That, too, he had from no one but himself. Ezra had never blushed. Cora laid the potatoes in the pot and covered them with pump water while Dell nudged a gear with the matchstick so gently the stamped metal seemed too crude a name for it.

They ate with the lamp burning low to save kerosene. The potatoes were mealy and the salt had taken on grit from the air no matter how tight Cora kept the lid. Outside, what was left of the wheat made a dry paper whisper against itself. There was not enough body in it to call it a rustle. There was not enough life in it to sound like crop. Thea pared her bread into little square bites before she ate them. Dell salted everything twice. Cora kept hearing August's voice in the pauses, the way he had made ten days sound generous. She could not look at the front-room window after dark now without thinking of Ezra's hand on the pen, the set of his shoulders, the lie she had been living in because the lie had kept the house upright.

Dell said, "Wind backed south."

Cora lifted her head. "How d'you know?"

"Sound on the shed roof changed." He shrugged at her and then at Thea, embarrassed by his own certainty. "It hits flatter from that side."

Thea said, "Then it'll come through the schoolhouse cracks again. I only just got one row of copybooks fit to touch."

"No children left to dirty them," Dell said.

Thea took that without flinching, which was one of the reasons Cora had gone and kept taking her into bed. Thea did not require prettied-up truth. "There are enough children left for one row," she said.

"For now."

Cora said, "Eat."

He obeyed, though he rolled his eyes at his plate in a way that was pure Dell and almost child enough to hurt her. He finished first, stacked his dishes without being asked, and carried the pie tin into his room flat and steady, careful enough for church silver. One lost screw would have offended him more than hunger. He came back a moment later and stood in the kitchen door with the empty clock case in his hand.

"Ma."

"What."

"You save any of that thin oil from the binder can?"

"Top shelf by the chimney. Rag's wrapped around it."

He nodded, then hesitated. The question that came out was smaller than the pause before it. "If I get it ticking, can I keep it in the shed?"

Cora blinked at him. "What for?"

He turned his face aside, already sorry he'd asked. "I like the sound."

Thea cut Cora a quick look, not pitying either of them, which helped. Cora said, "If you get it ticking, it's yours to put wherever you please."

That made him duck his head in a way she had not seen since before Ezra died. He took the oil and went.

The silence he left was worse than if he had stayed. The house was small enough that nobody ever truly left a room. Dell's movements passed through the walls as clearly as weather. His bed ropes complained once. The tin clicked softly against the floorboards. Then came the scratch of his match against the lamp in there, and after that the patient little silences of him fitting one piece to another.

Thea gathered the plates. "Leave those," Cora said, and reached at the same time.

Their fingers met on a cup slick with dishwater. Thea's hand was cooler than the china. Cora felt the jolt of it all the way up her arm and snatched back too fast. The skin of her palm tingled where Thea had touched her. Thea's mouth set.

"I had another letter today," she said.

Cora kept her eyes on the basin of gray washwater. "From who?"

"Lincoln."

The pump handle slipped in Cora's grip and banged the sink. In the next room Dell stopped moving. For a second the whole house listened.

Cora lowered the handle carefully. "What'd they say?"

"The librarian wrote herself this time. If I can come the first Tuesday in June, the interview is still mine. If I need another day, I ought to wire by Monday."

Cora took a plate and wiped it though it was already clean. "Then you'd better go."

Thea held the dishrag bunched in both hands. "That's all."

"What else is there to say?"

"Anything but 'you'd better go' might do. You nearly took the sink apart."

Cora put the plate down harder than she meant to. "Lincoln's got city water and steady pay. You'd have a room that isn't full of other people's dust and sorrow."

Thea laughed once, too softly for humor, and set the cup in the rack with more care than the cup deserved. "I know what a library is, Cora."

"I'm saying it'd be easier there."

"You're saying it like you've settled the matter of a stove repair."

"I'm saying you ought to take work that won't grind you up."

Thea folded the dishrag in half, then half again, a schoolroom movement of control that only made the anger in it clearer. "And not like the woman you've been walking around all week, dodging me every time my sleeve came near yours."

Cora felt heat come up under the dust on her skin. "Not here."

"No," Thea said, glancing toward Dell's room. "Never here. Never where daylight can see."

The quiet on the other side of the wall deepened. Dell was not close enough to hear every word, but he knew tones. Any boy knew tones in a house. Cora said, lower, "We'll talk later."

Thea turned the folded rag in her hands until her knuckles showed white. "You said that three nights ago."

In his room, Dell cursed softly at the clock in a burst of frustration so naked it almost made Cora grateful. It broke the line of the moment. Thea drew one hard breath through her nose, set the rag flat on the sink edge, and said, "I'll finish these."

"Thea."

"No. Leave it. If I say another word with him awake, it'll be the wrong one."

Her anger went into the work. She washed, dried, stacked, and slid every dish onto its shelf so softly the china scarcely clicked. Cora stood at the table pretending to wipe crumbs while her whole body attended the sound of crockery touching wood, drawer sliding shut, Thea's skirt brushing the leg of the chair. When Thea finally took the lamp and went down the hall, the kitchen looked abruptly meaner, all the patched corners and dust seams laid bare in the poorer light.

Cora went out to the yard on the excuse of the chickens. The air was not cool. Dust had a way of stealing the night's mercy and hanging on to heat after sunset. The henhouse latch held. The birds shifted and muttered in their sleep. Beyond the shed the wheat lay under a moon blurred behind the suspended dirt, each field row rubbed thin, the whole quarter scoured raw, nap gone, threads nearly showing. Harvest, she thought, and nearly laughed. What would there be to harvest if the wind kept at it. There would be a mean little crop, if any. There might be enough to cut for appearances. There might be enough to give a bank man one more figure to put in a ledger before he took the place anyway.

Stay through harvest.

The words came into her whole, unbidden, and sat there heavy as feed in a sack.

She leaned a forearm on the chicken-yard post and closed her eyes. It was the sort of thing a person said when she had no right to say anything larger. Stay through harvest. Stay through the season's end. Stay until the crop declared itself dead or, by some mean miracle, stood. Small words, enormous ask. Farm-sounding enough to pass for ordinary talk if she dressed it right. That made it uglier. It also made it the only honest thing she had.

Thea's wages helped. So did the board money. Coffee, sugar, lamp oil, cloth: those did not pay for themselves. Cora had counted Thea into the household accounts long before she admitted what else she was counting on, because money let a person touch facts without naming what they cost in other ways. But when she imagined Thea gone to Lincoln, the first blow was the cold of that room down the hall. Then came the table with nobody paring bread into little square bites, the kitchen without that voice saying the potatoes needed using up, the bed with no body slipping into hers after the house went quiet enough to risk it.

Under all of that waited the rougher, meaner fear of being left. Ezra had done that one way, the bank another, the weather another. Cora disliked herself for how quickly the mind shoved every loss into one bin if you let it.

She went back in through the kitchen and avoided the front-room window out of habit. She had been doing that too, moving around glass now because any pane might tell on her. The first days after the storm she had breathed on every pane in reach like a madwoman, wanting Ezra again, wanting proof, wanting contradiction. Now she could not bear to see the house offer up another thing she had failed to know. She passed the washstand mirror in the hall with her face turned half aside.

From Dell's room came a faint dry click, then another.

Then, improbably, it began to tick.

It was uneven, tired, but there.

"Ma," he called, not loudly.

She went to the door and leaned against the jamb. His room smelled of dust, boy sweat, oil, and the singed edge of lamp wick. The clock sat on the crate beside his bed with its back still off, ticking with all the determination of a creature too stubborn to know it ought to be dead. Dell sat cross-legged in his undershirt, the pie tin in his lap, grinning at it despite himself.

"Hear it?" he said.

"Hard not to."

"It'll likely stop by morning."

"Then enjoy it tonight."

He nodded. The grin had already faded. He distrusted his own pleasure. "You ought to get some sleep."

She almost asked since when he had become the one saying that. Instead she said, "You too."

He lay back without further argument, one hand tucked behind his head, and gave the little tick all his attention. Not music, exactly. Proof. When Cora pulled the door mostly shut, leaving the crack that kept the room from getting airless, she carried the sound of the clock with her down the hall.

Her bedroom still had too much of Ezra in the furniture and not enough of him anywhere that mattered. His bureau squatted against the far wall with one drawer that never closed true. The iron bed they had shared sat with one side nearly against the wall because there was nowhere else to put it and still let the dresser open. In the washstand bowl the dust had settled gray on white enamel no matter how often she wiped it away. The patchwork quilt at the foot of the bed had once belonged to Ezra's mother, and Cora had spent years disliking it for reasons too petty to confess to anyone, chiefly that the yellow squares were ugly and the seams never lay flat. Now she hated herself for the old pettiness more than she hated the quilt.

She set the lamp lower, unbuttoned her dress, and was pulling the last pins from her hair when Thea said from the doorway, "Did you mean it?"

In the mirror Cora caught Thea in the doorway before she turned. Thea had taken off her shoes. Stocking feet on the boards made her seem both softer and more dangerous. She still held the letter, folded twice, creased thin where her thumb had worried the edge.

"Which part?" Cora said.

"The part where you sent me to Lincoln and called it kindness."

Cora slid the last hairpin onto the washstand. "I wasn't being kind. I was telling it straight."

"No. Straight would've sounded different."

Thea came into the room and shut the door until only the narrowest line of hall dark remained. She did not latch it. She never latched it with Dell in the house. The space between them felt crowded even before she crossed it. Cora could hear the clock through the wall now, a small obstinate tick under the wind's long dry hand on the siding.

"I wanted to tell you sooner," Thea said. "I didn't because there hasn't been one minute in this house this week that wasn't full of paper, dust, or your son. That's part of it. The other part is I wanted to make you say something that wasn't about wages or train fare."

Cora fixed on the letter instead of her face. "How much does it pay?"

Thea shut her eyes briefly. When she opened them, they were bright and hard. "God. There you are again."

"It matters."

"Yes, it matters. Everything here is always counted before it's felt. I know that. Answer me anyway."

Cora stepped closer. Thea did not back up. Dust had settled in the dark curve of her collar seam. Cora wanted to put her mouth there so badly it made her stomach turn. "If it pays enough," she said, "then I don't get to pretend the town might save itself before you need to go."

Thea's face changed, not softened exactly but less armored. "That's closer."

"I'm not good at this part."

"No," Thea said. "You're very good at carrying wood, hauling water, killing chickens, and saying nothing until silence has gone rotten between two people."

That almost made Cora smile. It almost did. "And you're good at saying the thing so close to the bone it leaves no skin on it."

"Somebody ought to."

The tick through the wall kept at them. Cora found herself absurdly grateful for it. It made the room feel less like a held breath. "When would you go?"

"If I write tomorrow, I could catch the train Monday night from Hastings and be there Tuesday."

"That soon."

"Yes."

Cora nodded once. Lincoln was not far on a map and might as well have been another country in cash and trains and what sort of women got to live there by choice. "You want it."

Thea gave a tired, crooked smile with no cheer in it. "Of course I want it. I want shelves that aren't closing. I want windows I don't have to stuff with rags. I want a room where I can breathe without feeling grit move on my teeth. I want wages that are mine and not another fragile local committee's idea of charity. And I want" - she stopped, corrected herself with visible effort - "I want to stop feeling that every decent thing I want has to cancel out every indecent thing."

Cora said, "You aren't indecent."

Thea gave a quick laugh and pressed her mouth flat after it. "Not in your room, no. Outside it I'm one rumor and a board meeting away from being put on the road. You know that."

Cora did know it. Widows got one kind of mercy if they kept their heads down and worked. Teachers got another kind of scrutiny entirely. A widow with a boarder could still be pitied. A schoolteacher with a married - no, widowed - woman in her bed could be made into a lesson. Her attention went to the door, then the wall beyond which Dell's makeshift clock kept time for all of them. "I know."

"Then don't tell me to go and make it sound noble."

"What would you have me do?"

Thea's chin lifted, that classroom motion she had when she refused sloppiness from a child and from herself. "Tell me whether I'm more than where you put your fear when it gets too heavy. Or if I'm only handy when you can't carry it alone."

The words hit hard because they were close enough to something Cora had feared in herself to sting clean through. "No."

"No what?"

"No, that isn't all you are."

"Then what am I?"

Cora could have lied easier than she could answer without dodging. The room was thick with soap, dust, lamp smoke, and the faint sweetness of Thea's skin under all of it. She saw the letter in Thea's hand, the crack in the washstand bowl, the crooked drawer in Ezra's bureau, the poor patched hem of Thea's dress. She started with the nearest piece because it was the only one she could force past her teeth. "You're the only place in this house that doesn't feel claimed by him or the bank."

Thea held silent long enough to make Cora stand in it. When she spoke again, her voice had dropped. "That's not the same as saying you want me."

"I know it isn't."

"Then say the other."

Instead Cora reached for the letter. Thea held it a second longer than needed before letting go. The paper was warm from her hand. Cora set it on the washstand and then, because she had been wanting to touch something living for days and was tired of pretending otherwise, she laid her fingers against the side of Thea's throat where the pulse moved under dust and skin.

Thea shut her eyes, just once. "Don't," she whispered, though she leaned into the touch. "Don't use this to get around the rest."

"I'm not."

"You're exactly capable of that."

"Then stop me if I do."

Cora bent and kissed the chalk-smell off Thea's knuckle first, then the corner of her mouth, then her properly. Thea made a soft sound that was anger breaking and wanting coming through it. For a second she held herself stiff. Then her hand caught in Cora's unpinned hair and pulled once, hard enough to hurt, which was as good as an answer.

They kissed standing between the bed and washstand because the room gave them no graceful way to do anything. Cora had forgotten how quickly Thea lost the schoolmarm composure when she was done holding the line. One hand went to Cora's waist, rough with need, while the other kept fumbling between wanting more and wanting to keep her own dress closed until the answer had been earned.

"When did the letter come?" Cora asked against her mouth.

"Three days ago."

That stung. "Three."

"Yes. While you were busy turning into a useful ghost."

Cora kissed her harder for that and felt Thea's breath catch. "You should've told me."

"You should've touched me before now."

The bluntness in it went straight through Cora's anger and into the softer thing underneath. She got the first buttons of Thea's dress open with clumsy fingers. Her hands were better with wire and harness than shell buttons, but she kept at them. Thea helped at last, impatient, and the dress loosened enough for Cora to put her mouth to the hollow at the base of her throat. There was dust there. There was lye soap under it. There was the salt of the day.

Thea said, very low, "If this is all we do, I'll still go."

Cora lifted her head. "Then tell me what counts."

"Something that survives morning."

The clock ticked. Wind rubbed the siding. Dell coughed once in his sleep or near enough to sleep. Cora turned toward the wall and hated it, hated the whole thin house with its cheap partitions and its ability to make every private thing sound near public. The iron bed sat with one leg almost kissing plaster, the frame old enough to speak if breathed on wrong.

Thea followed her glance and huffed out a breath that might have been the start of a laugh. "Your bed's too near the wall."

"The whole damned house is too near everything."

"That isn't an answer either."

"No." Cora took Thea by the waist and turned her to sit on the bed edge where the frame would not bang so easy if they were careful. "But it's the ground we're standing on."

Thea let herself be sat because she wanted it as badly as Cora did, but her eyes stayed on Cora's face. "You can't work your way out of me."

"Watch me fail, then."

Cora knelt on the braided rug. The boards pressed through it at once. Up close, Thea's stockings were shiny with wear at the ankles and expertly darned at one knee. Cora ran a hand from shoe strap to calf and felt Thea shiver. She unhooked the garter tabs one by one. The tiny snaps sounded loud in the room. Her cracked thumb caught in the stocking lace at the top and Thea sucked air through her teeth.

"Sorry," Cora murmured.

"Do it slower," Thea said, which was not forgiveness but something better.

Cora rolled one stocking down, then the other, and kissed the pale skin banded above where sun and dust ever reached. Thea's knees parted for her with visible effort, the kind that admitted want while pretending to maintain pride. Cora put her forehead against Thea's thigh a moment, not to make a show of reverence but because she had been hungry and there she was, finally allowed to say so with her body if not yet her mouth.

She pushed Thea's dress and slip up together. Thea wore cotton step-ins washed almost colorless, mended too, and Cora had the wild, absurd thought that she loved her for mending underthings no one ought to have seen. Then she put her hand between Thea's legs and felt the heat there, the wet already worked up under anger and waiting, and all thought left her except the coarse gratefulness of being let in.

Thea's hand flew to the washstand to steady herself. "Cora."

"Hush."

"I'm not saying no."

"I know what no sounds like."

She drew the cotton aside and put her mouth on Thea. The first taste of her came mixed with soap and the day's salt and the faint dry grit that had got everywhere, because of course it had. Nothing in this country stayed clean long enough to become symbolic. Thea jerked and then gripped the washstand harder. The basin rattled once. Cora lifted one hand immediately and caught it before it could knock again.

"Easy," Thea whispered, and Cora could not tell whether she meant the furniture or herself.

Cora learned to hold back by force. She went slow because the house had ears, then slower because Thea made a sound low in her throat that seemed to ask for both mercy and its opposite. The rug burned Cora's knees through her shift. The iron bed warmed under her forearm. Thea's thighs tightened around her head, then opened again. When Cora slid two fingers into her, Thea gave a breathless curse that ended in a laugh she bit off with the back of her wrist.

"You laugh at the damnedest moments," Cora muttered against her.

"I know." Thea's voice broke on the second word. "I can't help it when I'm scared."

That nearly undid Cora more than anything else said that night. She curled her fingers and felt Thea go taut. Thea's heel scraped the rug. The bed shifted a dangerous inch and thudded lightly at the wall. Both women froze.

In the next room the clock went on ticking. Dell turned over, cotsprings groaning in one long complaint, then stilled.

Thea bent forward, face buried in her own shoulder, breath sawing. Cora stayed absolutely still until the house settled around them again. Then she reached up, tugged the corner of the sheet free, and pressed it into Thea's hand. Thea understood at once. She bit down on the cloth and nodded.

After that, she came hard and quiet enough to keep the secret, though not so quiet Cora mistook what she had done. Thea's whole body drew up around the force of it. Her fingers slipped on the washstand rim. The basin rocked. Cora caught it with one hand and kept the other between Thea's legs until the shaking ran out of her. When it passed, Thea sat sagged and flushed with the sheet corner still between her teeth, hair half down, eyes bright and furious with pleasure.

"That doesn't survive morning," she said when she could speak.

Cora rose, knees complaining, and kissed the damp at the corner of Thea's mouth. "No."

"Then keep going."

The challenge in it made heat go through Cora all over again. Thea pulled her close, found the buttons of Cora's dress and lost patience with them almost at once. She opened what she could, then dragged the cloth and shift up together with a hunger that felt less polished and more intent. Thea always touched to remember. Her fingertips took inventory. Nothing lazy in it. Nothing merely grateful. Her palm slid over Cora's hip, her thumb along the waistband of her drawers, and Cora had to brace one hand on the wall to keep from knocking the bed herself.

"Now," Thea said, breath against her jaw. "Tell me."

Cora knew exactly what was being asked and hated how naked it made her feel, far more naked than the open dress or the shift bunched at her waist. "Thea."

"No. You don't get to hide inside my body and call it honesty."

Thea's hand went under the cotton and found Cora already slick and aching. Cora bit down on whatever sound tried to come out. The wall was cool against her shoulder blade. The bed rail pressed into the back of her thigh. Thea's fingers, unlike Cora's, were not rough from pitchfork handles and washboards. They knew how to coax rather than seize. That, too, Cora loved her for and resented her for and wanted without reserve.

"Tell me what I'd be staying for," Thea said.

Cora shook her head once.

Thea pushed her up onto the mattress and came with her, skirts bunched at the hips, one stocking still hanging from a calf because they had not had time for order. Her hair had finally fallen. In the lamp's low light it lay across Cora's chest and shoulder like something spilled. She set her mouth to the place below Cora's ear, kissed once, then slid her hand back between Cora's legs and waited there, inexorable.

"Tell me," she said again.

Cora caught the iron rail and held it to keep the bed from tapping the wall. Even so it gave one small complaint, a swallowed noise. She shut her eyes. The truth came out barer than any vow. "Stay through harvest."

Thea's hand stopped.

The clock kept ticking.

"You ask that in June?" Thea whispered.

"Near enough."

"And after."

Cora opened her eyes. The ceiling above them held a brown water stain from two winters back that no scrubbing had ever shifted. She fixed on it because she could not say this looking anywhere softer. "After, I don't know."

Thea stared at her, anger and hurt rising fresh. "That's a cruel little promise."

"It's the biggest one I can stand behind."

"You hear yourself?"

"Yes."

The word came out harsher than she intended. She loosened one hand from the bed rail and touched Thea's wrist where it rested on her. "Listen to me. Stay through harvest if there is one. Stay till the wheat's cut or fails standing. Stay till August makes his next move and Dell does whatever wild thing he's fixing to do. Stay till I know whether I've got a farm or only boards and debt and this room. I can't stand in town and hold your hand. I can't tell you I'll make this place safe because I don't know how and I won't lie to get you to stay. But I want your shoes under this bed. I want your comb on that washstand. I want to hear you in the kitchen at dawn and feel you come through that door when the house is finally dark. That's what I know."

Thea's face changed as she listened. The fight in it settled into something graver. "There. You do it again. Shoes, comb, kitchen dawn. You go hiding in house words the way other people hide in scripture."

"Maybe."

"And if I say yes to that, what am I saying yes to, then? One season in your bed, one season listening for Dell through the wall, and your need every time it outruns your pride."

"Yes."

The bluntness of it landed harder than defense would have. Thea inhaled sharply. Her hand returned to motion, slower now, not merciful but deliberate, and Cora nearly lost her grip on the bed rail at once.

"That's ugly enough to believe," Thea said. "God help me, it is."

"Thea."

"No, let me think." Her voice trembled and steadied. "Do you know what I wanted? I wanted you to say come with me. I wanted you to be foolish and ruin us both in one hard sentence."

"I wanted to."

"But I wouldn't trust you if you did. That's the worst of it."

She bent and kissed Cora hard enough to split the rest of the thought in two. Then Thea moved her hand with less caution and more claim, and Cora's body answered before the rest of her could. She turned her face into the pillow to swallow the noise. The pillow smelled of dust and old sun and the hair oil she used too sparingly. Thea kept at her until the pressure in Cora wound so tight the whole house narrowed to the space between that hand and her own breath. When release came it was not pretty. It took her in a jerk that thudded the bed once against the wall despite everything. She clamped her own wrist between her teeth and tasted the soap not fully rinsed from her skin.

They went still again, listening.

Dell coughed, muttered something in sleep, and the clock went on ticking with infuriating innocence.

Thea dropped her forehead to Cora's shoulder and started to laugh soundlessly, the laugh shaking against Cora's skin until it turned halfway into crying and then into neither. Cora held her because there was nothing wiser to do. Their breaths slowed together by degrees.

After a while Thea rolled onto her back, one arm over her eyes. Cora lay beside her in disordered clothes and felt sweat cooling in every seam the dust had found first. The room smelled of iron bedframe, lamp smoke, sex, and the wet rag from the dishpan still hanging in the kitchen. Outside, the wind had dropped enough that the wheat could be heard again in its papery whisper.

"I didn't tell them yes," Thea said into the crook of her elbow.

"No. You left it hanging there because you wanted me to ask clean."

"I haven't told them no either."

"Then wire for the extra day Monday. Give me till sunup to say the same thing with my boots on. If I go dumb in daylight, you go."

Thea lowered her arm and turned her head. In the softened lamplight she looked younger and more worn at once, the hard classroom planes of her face loosened by what had passed but not erased. "I wanted you to ask for me, not for my wages."

Cora let out a breath. "I did think of the wages."

"Of course you did."

"Then I thought of your room empty. That hit first."

Thea let the answer sit between them. Belief took a second to settle. "That's a different thing."

"Yes."

"Don't make me regret hearing it."

"I won't if I can help it."

"There you go again. You can't promise a thing plain. You have to hitch it to weather."

Cora reached and pushed a lock of hair off Thea's forehead. "You want me to lie prettier?"

"No." Thea caught Cora's hand and kissed the heel of it, the place callused by shovel handles and washboard edges. "I want it the way it sits, only said before I have to drag it out by force."

Cora could not argue. Not honestly. She lay looking at the ceiling stain again, then at the letter on the washstand, small and folded beside the lamp. "Did you want Lincoln bad?"

Thea answered without delay. "Yes."

Cora nodded.

"And I wanted this," Thea went on. "That's the trouble of it. I'd like one life where wanting a bed and wanting decent work don't make me choose which one gets to be respectable."

Cora almost said nobody got that, but the sentence died before it reached her mouth. It would have sounded wise and been useless. Instead she said, "And I'm asking you to risk the cleaner one on me."

Thea sat with that a moment, then pulled her dress the rest of the way off, not with coyness, not performing at all, simply tired of cloth. Her body was narrower than Cora's, pale where the sun did not find her, with a small scar high on one thigh Cora had once asked about and been told came from falling off a gate at thirteen because she had insisted she could climb it in city shoes. Thea had been laughing when she told that story and furious at the remembered embarrassment in equal parts. She had that odd mixture in many things. It was one reason Cora trusted her more than she trusted people who had only one face for themselves.

Cora sat up too and untangled her shift. Her own body felt used in the best and worst ways, heavy with labor even at rest, the skin of her palms rough enough to snag cloth, the pale lines at her shoulders where Ezra's old apron straps had rubbed over the years before she finally cut them shorter. Thea did not flinch from her, which was another of her rare gifts. Admiration was too easy a word for it. What she gave Cora was attention.

"We ought to wash," Thea said, back in her schoolroom voice because if she stayed in tenderness too long it frightened her.

"Mm."

Cora got up and crossed to the washstand. The floorboards were cool under her bare feet. She poured a little water from the pitcher into the enamel basin. It went cloudy at once from the dust the bowl had gathered since evening. She dipped the cloth and wrung it. The smell of wet metal rose sharp and familiar.

She leaned over to rinse it again and breathed across the basin without thinking.

The gray film on the white enamel silvered.

At first she thought it was only water moving. Then the shape came up in it, small and sharp and impossible to mistake: Thea on the bed edge with one stocking hanging from her calf, head thrown back, the sheet twisted in her fist. Cora herself knelt between her knees, one hand braced on the quilt, mouth at Thea's thigh. The image shifted - no, not shifted, gave a second fragment. Cora on her back against the yellow-patched quilt, dress rucked to the waist, Thea bent over her with dark hair falling forward and one hand working under the cotton. The lamp wick burned low in the vision just as it burned low now. This time the basin went straight to the present. It held the thing they had done less than ten minutes before, already keeping its own receipt.

The cloth slipped from Cora's hand into the basin with a wet slap. The stand rang softly.

Behind her Thea said, "What?"

Cora could not answer fast enough. The image held a second more, thinning at the edges like breath on glass. The enamel was slick and cold under her fingers when she caught the rim. Her own bare shoulder showed in the bowl as a ghost around the memory of it.

Thea came up beside her and saw.

For once she had no ready sentence. Her breath touched Cora's upper arm. The room seemed to contract around the basin, the door, the wall behind which Dell's salvaged clock kept taking on the stubborn work of time.

"That was just now," Thea said.

"Yes."

"It can do that."

"Looks like."

The memory in the basin thinned further, the shapes going grainy, then vanished into nothing but gray water and the limp cloth. Cora stared at the blank enamel, wanting to breathe across it again and drag the image back. She did not dare.

Thea put one hand flat on the washstand. "If he'd come in for water."

Cora saw it at once. She saw Dell half-awake, thirsty, pushing the door with his shoulder. She saw morning light on the bowl before she had wiped it. She saw any pair of eyes in any house where dust settled after a storm and nobody could stop the weather from keeping receipts. The thought was not grand. It was domestic, and that made it worse.

"He didn't," Cora said.

"No." Thea swallowed. Her voice came back with effort. "But if I stay, that's part of what staying is."

Cora turned then. Thea stood naked except for the one stocking still around her ankle, hair down, letter on the washstand behind her, the mark of Cora's mouth still visible at her collarbone. There was no decent lie left in the room. "Yes," Cora said.

Thea measured the basin, the wall, the door, the letter, the bed too near the wall and the town beyond it. Then she reached back without looking, took the folded Lincoln letter from beside the lamp, and slid it under Cora's brush instead of into her dress.

"I'll wire them Monday for another day," she said. "I'm not promising you harvest in the dark. But I'm not walking into Lincoln Tuesday pretending this room doesn't change anything. If you still mean it when the sun's up, say it then. Until Lincoln answers, I'm here."

She took the cloth from the gray water, scrubbed the inside of the basin once, hard, and turned it mouth-down on the washstand. A thread of water slid from under the rim and darkened the wood beside the lamp. Only then did she come back to the bed and slide in under the coverlet, one cold foot against Cora's shin, one hand flat on her belly. On the far side of the wall Dell's rescued clock kept its crooked tick. Under the upturned basin, a trapped drop struck wood, then another, then stopped. Cora did not sleep at once. She lay under Thea's hand listening to that crooked tick and thinking of breakfast, of daylight, of having to say the same hard thing with Dell at the table and mean it.
