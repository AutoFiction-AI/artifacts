# Chapter 16

By the time Marisol reached Saint Brigid's parish hall, the cuffs of her jeans were wet with black slush and salt. December had gone from rain to sleet to a hard, mean cold in three days, and everyone tracked it indoors: crushed grit under boots, damp wool steaming over radiators, umbrellas opened like broken birds against the wall. A volunteer in a Knicks beanie stood by the door with a brass thurible hanging from one hand and a roll of blue painter's tape in the other, trying to keep the smoke from setting off the detector. Every time he swung the chain, incense rose in a pale ribbon and got beaten flat by the fluorescent hum.

The first call had hit at dawn, and by evening she had stopped checking names before she silenced the phone.

The hall itself looked like every church basement she'd ever sat in for grief, baptism, and bingo. Folding chairs in narrow rows, some beige, some gray, some the old heavy metal kind with cracked vinyl seats. A table in back held two dented coffee urns, a stack of Styrofoam cups, sugar packets in a glass bowl, and powdered creamer clumped into pebbles from humidity. Someone had brought supermarket sheet cake with white frosting and blue roses that bled dye. The stale coffee smell had settled into the cinder block walls years ago, and incense only sharpened it.

At the front, the casket sat open on a platform draped in burgundy cloth. Salvador lay in the suit Nora had once called "appropriately neutral" in a donor meeting, charcoal wool with a narrow black tie, his tremoring right hand now folded still over his chest, fingers tucked by someone else's careful arrangement. They had powdered the yellow from his skin but not enough. Under the hall lights he looked waxed, the way fruit looked when a grocery sprayed it to hide bruises. On either side of him stood two enlarged photos on foam board: one from the seventies with his hair to his shoulders and paint on his cheek, one recent from the museum brochure where he looked frail and beatific in exactly the ratio marketing liked.

The line for condolences bent around the first row of chairs and then broke into clusters, because no one in that room had one grief. Old neighbors from Flatbush hugged former museum interns. A pair of critics in black turtlenecks stood by the prayer candles whispering into their scarves, both of them holding the same tiny notebook, same black cover, same yellow elastic band. Two women from Gloria's church wore navy coats and practical shoes and kept pressing foil trays of arroz con pollo onto people who said they were not hungry. Hunger did not matter. Showing up did.

Carina moved through it all with a roll of raffle tickets looped around her wrist, tearing stubs for food donations and sliding dollar bills into an old cookie tin with a chipped Santa on the lid. She had a purple folder tucked under her arm with an elementary-school district logo, and every few minutes she pulled out a permission slip, checked a box with a pink pen, and shoved it back without missing a conversation. That was how she handled panic: list, sort, staple, act. Marisol felt a mean little envy at that machinery, the way tasks could keep your hands too busy to shake.

When she spotted Marisol, she pressed two fingers against Marisol's sleeve like a brake and gave her the kind of smile people used when they were trying not to start.

"You're late," Carina said.

"The B train stalled at Atlantic for twenty minutes."

"Everyone's late. He was late to his own wake." Carina glanced toward the casket and then away. "Mami's in the kitchen counting trays. If you're gonna go in there, don't start anything."

"I didn't come to start anything."

Carina's mouth did a fast sideways twitch, not quite a laugh. "You came with that tote bag and your jaw locked like a pit bull. Don't lie to me in church, Mari. It bounces."

Before Marisol could answer, Gloria appeared in the kitchen doorway with a dish towel over her shoulder and hair pinned so tight it made her eyes look sharper than usual. She moved with the careful weight transfer of someone hiding knee pain from an audience. A smear of frosting marked the heel of her hand; she had been cutting cake with a plastic knife that snapped every third slice. Grocery-store vanilla and bleach hit Marisol at once, and her stomach tightened.

"Ven acá," Gloria said softly.

Marisol followed her into the kitchen. Steam fogged the one window over the sink. On the counter sat aluminum pans of pernil, a mountain of paper plates, and three canisters labeled with masking tape: SUGAR, FORKS, MONEY FOR FLOWERS. Gloria opened the SUGAR canister and pulled out a folded envelope thick with condolence cash.

"This is what people trust us with," she said. "You understand? They bring ten dollars, twenty dollars, and they bring stories. Today is not for corrections."

Marisol heard the word trust and felt the old reflex to nod before she understood the price.

"Mami."

"Listen first. You always listen when it's too late." Gloria tapped the envelope against the counter. "Father Efrain will speak. Nora will speak. Carina has a short thing from Leo because he wanted to draw a pigeon and call it Abuelo Bird, and that's what children do at wakes. You're on the program for one poem. The one you sent last night. The soft one."

Marisol felt the folded pages in her tote push against her thigh like heat.

"I sent two drafts," she said.

"You sent one draft and one knife. You won't use the knife in this room." Gloria's voice stayed polite, almost tender, which made the command harder. "I'm asking you as your mother."

"You asked me that before records, before dinner, before everything."

Gloria closed the canister, palm flat on the lid. "And I was right before, even when you hated me for it. We survived because we knew when to shut our mouths."

Marisol held her gaze. "Who survived?"

For a beat Gloria's face opened, then shut. "Not here," she said. "Not in front of these people."

When they came back into the hall, Nora Feldman had arrived in a black coat that still held rain along the shoulders, her tote bag bulging with printed programs and a packet of museum condolence cards on thick cream stock. She had changed from heels into spare matte-black flats, and she kept blinking hard, thumb and forefinger pressing at the bridge of her nose in a rhythm Marisol remembered from suspension week.

Nora touched Marisol's elbow with practiced discretion. "Can we step aside for sixty seconds?"

They moved near the radiator, where the heat knocked damp wool and perfume into a single sweet-metal smell.

"First, thank you for coming," Nora said. "Second, I need to be explicit. Today is high exposure. We have press in the room and at least two livestreams already running. Estate counsel is tracking public statements."

"Is that condolences in museum language?"

Nora did not flinch. "It's risk language because risk is currently what I can control. You're free to read what you choose. I'm urging you not to use unverified allegations or material that implicates private individuals who are not consenting."

"You mean my family."

"I mean people who are alive and vulnerable to harm. Including you." Nora took a breath and shifted register, less polished. "Marisol, there is no legal container left. If this detonates here, it detonates everywhere."

"It already did."

Nora's eyes tracked the casket, the critics, the phone screens, then back to Marisol. "Then don't hand it gasoline."

Father Efrain called for everyone to take seats, and chairs scraped the floor in a ragged chorus. He was short, broad through the middle, with a smoker's rasp and nicotine-yellow fingertips despite his careful nails. A silver inhaler sat half-hidden in his sleeve cuff; every time he raised his hand in blessing, metal flashed. He began the rosary in Spanish and English, voice catching on the first mystery then steadying as the room answered.

Marisol sat in the second row beside Carina, who kept one hand on the purple school folder like it might sprout wings. The incense thickened. Someone's toddler dropped a toy bus and it rattled down the aisle before an aunt snagged it with her foot. Behind them, one of the critics uncapped a pen and whispered, "He never did minor work," in the flat tone of a grade handed down.

Father Efrain's eulogy cast Salvador as penitent craftsman and servant. He spoke about confession in the final month, about a man who had asked for anointing with tears in his eyes, about a soul refined by suffering. "In his final days," he said, "he offered his pain for this city, for young artists, for families in fracture." He held the inhaler in his fist while he said it, thumb rubbing the seam. "He came home to God with humility."

Marisol felt something hard and stupid rise in her throat. Salvador had not come home to anything with humility. He had bargained to the edge. He had dodged, confessed, revised, seduced language until morphine chewed his syntax. He had said I might be your father between jokes about solvent fumes and asked for more ice chips before the nurse returned. He had signed his name as witness on Tomas's death and then spent decades painting men like icons with the rot sanded out.

After Father Efrain, Nora stood at the lectern with her program folded into a narrow strip. She did not read the donor-safe statement Marisol expected. Instead she spoke in that polished museum cadence that could carry three truths and one omission in the same sentence.

"Salvador Rivas altered the visual grammar of figurative painting in this city," she said. "He made flesh look accountable to light. He also insisted, often inconveniently, that institutions include those who were easiest to erase." A pause. "The museum was not always equal to his demands."

That line made a stir. One critic looked up. One board member by the aisle crossed his arms. Nora continued.

"Legacy is never singular. It is made by work, by damage, by repair attempts, by what survives scrutiny. We honor him best by resisting simplification."

She glanced once toward Marisol, quick as a blink, then stepped down.

The third eulogy came from Benny Morales, retired handler from Red Hook, who had once refused to open a crate without coffee and now refused to speak into the microphone because he said microphones lied. He stood in front of the first row with his Mets cap in both hands, nicotine patch lifting from his neck, and told the room how Salvador used to bring empanadas for night shift crews when donors forgot they existed.

"He could be a prick," Benny said, and a ripple of shocked laughter crossed the hall. "He'd scream about humidity like it was murder. But he learned our names. Not all at once. He was vain, not a saint. Still, when my kid got sick, he sold a sketch and left cash in my locker. Never signed it. That's who he was too."

Benny nodded toward the casket, jaw working. "He was a prick and he showed up when it counted, carajo." He sat down before anyone could clap.

Competing ownership unfolded like cards on a table: penitent artist, institutional giant, neighborhood bastard with a useful heart. Each version wanted to close around him and call itself mercy. Each version left out blood.

Carina stood next, holding Leo's drawing up with both hands. The pigeon had a halo and one enormous orange foot. "Leo says Abuelo Bird steals fries but shares with babies," she read, voice shaking once then hardening. "He says art should be in schools that got mold on the ceiling too." That got a soft laugh. Carina smiled at no one in particular and sat quickly, rubbing the chipped edge of her front tooth with her thumb.

The formal part should have ended there. Father Efrain looked relieved, already angling toward prayer over the casket. But Nora touched his sleeve, and he nodded toward the program.

"Marisol Vega," he said. "Poet."

The room tightened in small mechanical ways: chair legs stopped scraping mid-drag, phones lifted to matching angles, and the whispering fell to coat-sleeve rustle and nose-breath. Marisol stood. The tote strap dug into her shoulder.

As she passed the coffee table, Gloria caught her wrist.

"Read the one you sent," Gloria said, not loud but ferocious. "Please. No documents. No accusations. Bury him first."

Marisol met her eyes. "You buried Tomas with a story instead of a body."

Gloria's fingers tightened, then released. "Don't do this to me."

"You already did this to us."

Nora stepped in before either of them could keep going. "Marisol," she said, voice clipped thin now, "if you're going to deviate from submitted text, I need you to understand the exposure is total."

"I understand." Marisol pulled free. "Do you?"

At the lectern, she set down three sheets. The top sheet was the "soft one," a careful elegy with line breaks that behaved. Underneath was the one she had written at three in the morning in the laundromat on Nostrand, while dryers thumped and a woman argued in Creole over a missing sock and a boy slept across two plastic chairs with his math workbook open on his chest. She had written it on legal pad paper with pen pressure so hard it tore through in places.

Her mouth went dry. She took a cup of stale coffee from the side table, swallowed, and tasted pennies.

"I was asked to read a memorial poem," she said into the mic. "I brought one. I also brought the version that keeps me sober. I'm reading that one."

A murmur rolled back through the rows. Father Efrain's hand rose half an inch from his side, then dropped.

She did not look at Gloria when she began.

"At the wake they laid him out
in a suit he hated,
charcoal discipline over a body that used to smell
of linseed, cigarettes, and the mint candies
he stole from nurses like a child.

They crossed his hands
like prayer could fix what those fingers signed.
Witness: Salvador Rivas.
Motel sink. Room 214.
Acute fentanyl and heroin toxicity.
Name of the dead: Tomas Vega,
my father on paper,
my first patron saint in the family church of useful lies.

My mother taught me how to iron cuffs,
how to stretch chicken for three meals,
how to keep rent money in coffee cans marked SUGAR
because men don't rob what they can't imagine.
She also taught me halo work:
how to sand away track marks,
how to call overdose a bullet,
how to forge a martyr out of motel fluorescent light,
how to tell a child your father died for the people
when he died because his blood asked for one more warm flood
and got fentanyl instead.

I'm not clean in this poem.
I bought vodka in a bus shelter in the rain
and drank like I was trying to erase a witness line.
My sponsor says facts before feelings.
Fact: I drank.
Fact: I lied about drinking.
Fact: shame does not resurrect anyone.

He painted saints with dirty feet.
That part was honest.
He painted men as if suffering made them holy.
That part sold.
He painted over T.V. in a triptych,
scraped a face out of gesso,
left the shoulder under gold leaf because maybe guilt has bad aim.

He told me maybe he's my father
with morphine cotton in his mouth,
and maybe wasn't enough for baptism,
wasn't enough for science,
not enough for thirty-four years of blood type math,
but enough to rot every sentence where I named myself.

Still.
Still he brought empanadas to night shift.
Still he called me out when I wrote pretty bullshit.
Still he looked at me once, no theater, no audience,
and said he was tired of making women carry his edits.
He died before signatures could clean this up.
He died leaving his mess on all our doorsteps.

I refuse saint language.
I refuse villain language.
I refuse to let the dead keep hiring us
as publicists for their fear.

Today we have incense, folding chairs, stale coffee,
and a room full of people who loved him from different distances.
If love means anything, it has to survive contradiction.
If grief means anything, it has to tell the truth in full sentences.

So here:
He made work that changed me.
He lied to me.
He witnessed my father's death and stayed silent.
He fed men no one thanked.
He used women for cover.
He taught me to look straight at damage.

I'm done sanding halos.
Bury him with his shadows attached."

The last line hung in the feedback hum. Somewhere near the back, a woman whispered, "Dios mío," not shocked, more tired than anything. A chair leg screamed against linoleum. Then voices rose at once from different parts of the room, too many to sort. Marisol felt her pulse kick in her gums.

"No, mija, not in front of La Virgen and an open casket. We keep vigil first and fight in the parking lot after."

"She cited witness signatures and a tox screen. That's documentary record, not chisme."

"My little cousin's in the hall with frosting in his eyebrows, and y'all turned this into a whole knife-show mess."

"If she's got exhibits, she can file them in daylight with counsel. Right now you are feeding every live account in this room."

Father Efrain stepped to the front with both palms out, trying to catch the room before it broke. "Please," he said. "Please, this is vigil, not trial."

Gloria stood so fast her chair snapped shut behind her. The dish towel slid off her shoulder and dropped at her feet. She crossed the aisle with a limp she usually hid and reached the lectern before Marisol could step back. She grabbed the top page out of Marisol's hand, and the paper edge sliced the pad of her thumb. The sudden blood snapped Marisol from podium trance into daughter panic, hot and immediate.

"You used your brother's death like a blade," Gloria said, blood beading bright against white paper. "At his wake."

Marisol caught the page before it hit the floor. "He was my brother too. You made me kneel to a lie until my knees bled."

Carina shoved between them, one forearm hard across Marisol's ribs, the other hand on Gloria's wrist. "Stop. Both of you. Not over the casket." The shove drove air out of Marisol's chest, and for a second she tasted old vodka panic, metallic and mean.

Gloria yanked her wrist free and pressed her bleeding thumb into the dish towel Carina kicked toward her. "I scrubbed Tomas's vomit out of denim in a laundromat sink at two in the morning. I counted condolence cash because we couldn't afford the burial. I did what kept this family standing."

"Then why'd you make me call him a martyr every year?" Marisol said. "Why'd you put that script in my mouth when I was nine?"

Gloria's jaw jumped. "Because rent didn't care how he died. Immigration didn't care either. Stories kept food in this house."

Carina looked at Marisol, eyes wet and furious. "Leo is in the hallway eating sheet cake. You don't get to blow us open in front of him and call it justice."

"I'm not putting him in a lie the way you put me in one."

She heard her own voice come out harder than she meant and knew there was no way to pull it back into daughter-language now.

Gloria took one breath that sounded like it hurt. "Hear me plain, Mari. Don't come to my kitchen tonight. Don't call me Mami on a microphone and then gut me at the lectern."

She turned and walked down the side aisle with the stiff, deliberate gait of someone keeping one knee from buckling. Carina grabbed her purple folder and coat, then hissed at Marisol, "You want truth? Live with this one. Stay away from Leo till Mami says otherwise." Marisol's knees went weak with the urge to run after them and apologize for everything, even the parts that were true.

"I've already lived with worse," Marisol said, but Carina was gone.

The split became visible in bodies before it became visible online. Gloria's church friends gathered by the exit with tight mouths and purses clutched to their ribs, forming a human wall around her as she put on her coat. Younger artists and museum interns drifted toward Marisol in uncertain semicircles, bodies angled to be seen beside her when the clips went up. Benny stayed seated, staring at his cap. Nora remained near the lectern, not moving, both hands flat against the program stack like she was keeping paper from blowing away in a storm no one else could feel.

A phone camera flashed. Another was already streaming from the side aisle. Marisol saw her own face on one screen, small and grainy, mouth open mid-line. Someone had captioned it LIVE WAKE DRAMA.

Father Efrain started the Our Father in Spanish, voice hoarse with effort, and about half the room joined. The other half kept arguing over him.

"Esto no es content. This is a wake, and some of us are still praying."

"Content? That paperwork is her life with chain-of-custody stamps on it."

"Documents can prove facts, but they cannot tell us what mercy costs."

"Mercy for who, though, the man in the box or the women he left holding the story?"

Marisol stepped down from the lectern and almost tripped on a chair leg. Adrenaline left her knees unreliable. Her palms shook. Not from stage fright. From the old familiar aftershock that used to send her looking for a bottle before the argument in her head could finish its first sentence.

Not today, she told herself. Facts before feelings. She pressed her thumbnail into the edge of her blue day-one chip through the coat pocket seam until pain sharpened her focus.

Near the coffee table, a woman with a red knit hat and cut-off gloves held up a press badge from Brooklyn Arts Wire like a shield. She had a nicotine gum blister pack in one hand and a voice memo app already running.

"Reina Soto," she said. "Can I get a statement?"

"No," Marisol said automatically.

"Off the record context then. Was that poem based on certified records?"

"You're already recording."

Reina lowered the phone by exactly one inch. "You're right. On the record: are you alleging Salvador falsified public narratives around Tomas Vega's death?"

A year ago Marisol would have performed. Tonight she felt too stripped for theater.

"I'm saying my father died of overdose," she said. "I'm saying the martyr story was constructed. I'm saying people used language to survive and to control, sometimes in the same sentence."

"Do you have documents?"

"Yes."

"Will you publish them?"

"Not tonight."

Reina nodded, chewing gum in two short angry bites. "Your mother just exited in tears. Do you regret reading it now?"

Regret came in clean little spikes. Gloria's blood on the paper. Carina's hiss. Leo's pigeon drawing abandoned on a chair. None of it was abstract now.

"I regret making my mother bleed on a page at his wake," Marisol said. "I regret my sister telling me to stay away from Leo. I took that mic anyway, and I won't call a lie a eulogy."

Reina's eyebrows lifted, appreciating the quote and hating herself a little for appreciating it. "Can I attribute that fully?"

"Yes."

Across the room, one of the black-turtleneck critics had cornered Nora near the candle table.

"Chief Curator Feldman, does the museum endorse these allegations?"

Nora's posture went rigidly polite. "The museum does not comment on private family matters during funeral rites."

"But this concerns your retrospective artist and potentially institutional suppression."

"No comment."

"Did you know about overdose records before tonight?"

Nora looked past him, directly at Marisol for a fraction, then back to neutral space over his shoulder. "I said no comment." She turned, collected her tote, and walked out without another word, spare flats slapping softly against the wet tile by the door.

That refusal traveled through the room almost as fast as the poem. People interpreted silence like scripture, according to need.

In the kitchen, someone dropped a tray and chicken grease spread across the floor in a reflective sheet. One of Gloria's church friends muttered, "Jesús," and fetched paper towels. Another woman started wrapping leftover rice in foil with furious efficiency, every fold sharp enough to cut. Benny finally rose, crossed to Marisol, and put his Mets cap back on.

"Kid," he said quietly, "that was ugly and maybe necessary. I hated hearing it, and I'd have hated you more if you'd swallowed it." He squeezed her shoulder once with a hand rough as rope. "Drink water. Not coffee. You're vibrating."

"Thanks, Benny."

"Don't thank me. Go outside before this place eats itself."

Outside, the cold hit like a slap. Breath turned white and vanished. People smoked under the church awning despite the NO SMOKING sign, cupping lighters against wind. Gloria stood at the curb with Carina and two church women, coat buttoned wrong, staring straight ahead at traffic with the blank face she used when she refused to cry in public. Marisol took one step toward them.

"Mami, wait."

Carina moved in front of her. "Don't."

Gloria turned at last. Mascara had bled into the creases under her eyes. "Not Mami tonight," she said. "If you publish before we talk, you do it without me. Forever."

Carina opened the back door of a livery car idling with hazards on. "Text logistics if you want a meeting. No ambush."

Gloria got in. The door shut with a thick rubber thud.

The car pulled away, brake lights washing the slush red. Marisol watched until traffic swallowed it.

She took out her phone, opened the family thread, and typed before she could sand the edges down: `No ambush. Noon tomorrow. Coney boardwalk by the locked lemonade stand. If you don't come, I release the death certificate and conservation packet at 5.` She hit send.

Noon looked practical in writing, like logistics instead of a daughter arranging an ambush after a public funeral fight.

One gray check appeared next to Carina's name. Gloria's line stayed dark.

Her phone started buzzing before she put it back in her pocket. First a text from an unknown number: `saw u at wake. brave.` Then another: `you should be ashamed.` Then a signal alert from Borough Canvas, a local arts site that had been feeding on the museum story since chapter nine: LIVE UPDATE: POET INTERRUPTS RIVAS VIGIL WITH ALLEGATIONS OF FAMILY COVER-UP.

Reina stepped out under the awning, fingers already moving across her screen. She looked up once.

"Piece goes up in fifteen," she said, almost apologetic. "Editor wants top slot before ten."

"Of course."

"If you want to add one line before publish, now's the window."

Marisol thought of every version she could offer to soften impact. She thought of Gloria counting condolence cash into labeled canisters. She thought of Salvador's witness signature in municipal ink, crisp and bureaucratic, no poetry in it at all.

"Add this," she said. "I loved him. I don't trust him. Both are true."

Reina typed it in, read it back for accuracy, and nodded.

When she was gone, Marisol stood alone by the church steps and took inventory the way Luis had taught her. Two feet on concrete. Cold air in, longer air out. Left hand shaking, right hand steadier. No alcohol in her system. No plan to drink. High risk, high noise, no sleep, no family contact tonight. She would need a meeting in the morning, maybe a midnight one if she could get to Sunset Park in time. She would need to charge her phone. She would need to eat something with salt before nausea turned into craving.

Inside, someone started singing "De Colores" off-key, and another voice joined, then another, until the hall sounded like a congregation and a protest at once.

Her phone buzzed again. This time it was a screenshot from a number she recognized as a former workshop student. A headline from Brooklyn Arts Wire had already gone live above a photo of her at the lectern, jaw set, papers in hand.

POET-MOURNER NAMES OVERDOSE, FORGERY CLAIMS AT RIVAS WAKE; FAMILY WALKS OUT.

There was no corridor left between public and private now. No kitchen door to close, no program language to hide inside, no way to put this back into one household and call it contained. Snow started again, fine and dry, collecting on the black sleeve of her coat as she stood on the steps and read the first paragraph twice.

Then she put the phone away, already planning to kill that number and activate a prepaid line before dawn, turned up her collar, and walked toward the train with incense still in her hair and stale coffee on her breath.
