# Chapter 3

By the time Marisol reached Flatbush, the hems of her jeans were dark with rain and bus salt, and the notebook in her tote knocked against her hip with each block. The corner bodega had sold out of marigolds, so she brought two black candles, a loaf of Portuguese bread wrapped in paper gone translucent with butter, and a plastic bag of limes she had not planned to buy. She stood in the hallway outside her mother’s apartment and listened before knocking: the TV low, Leo arguing with somebody in Minecraft, Gloria’s voice from the kitchen saying somebody better not touch that pot with a wet spoon.

When Carina opened the door she had a phone pinned between shoulder and jaw, one sneaker half unlaced, and a stack of school forms under her arm with fluorescent tabs sprouting from the top like feathers. She looked Marisol up and down and stepped aside without hanging up.

“Yeah, Ms. Perez, I hear you. No, I didn’t lose it, they changed the portal again. Hold on, my sister’s here looking like a drenched pigeon.”

Marisol kissed her cheek anyway. “I brought candles.”

“Good, because Mami bought the saint kind and they smell like old man cologne.” Carina pointed the forms at the kitchen. “Put your wet coat on the chair, not the sofa. Leo, move your backpack before your aunt trips and sues us.”

The apartment held the same blended weather it always did in late fall: frying garlic, wet wool, laundry soap, and the honey-wax perfume of prayer candles burning by Tomas’s photo in the corner. Gloria stood at the stove in a faded Mercy Hospital sweatshirt and an apron that said KISS THE COOK in glittering letters worn down to KISS THE C. Her hair was pinned so tight it made her brow look permanently lifted, and every few minutes she nudged her left knee against a cabinet, warning it to behave.

“You’re late,” Gloria said, not looking up from the pot. “Rice is done. Chicken’s almost done. Bread can wait. Bread never saves anybody.”

“It was the B41. And rain.”

“It’s always rain, always train, always excuses.” Gloria turned and kissed her forehead fast, then reached for the candles in Marisol’s hand. “Black? Who died in a nightclub?”

“Bodega was out of white.”

Carina finally ended her call and dropped the school forms on top of the microwave. “Those are dramatic. We’re gonna summon a reggaeton demon with those.”

Leo appeared from the bedroom in socks and a Spider-Man T-shirt too small in the sleeves, carrying a plastic container full of pennies. He had drawn a mustache on his upper lip with marker and forgotten about it. “Tia, look. I counted one hundred and twelve cents. Mami says if I get five dollars, I can buy pigeon feed and become pigeon king.”

“Pigeon king of where?” Marisol asked.

“Church steps. Duh.” He glanced at the black candles and wrinkled his nose. “Those look evil. I like them.”

Carina snapped her fingers at him. “Wash your hands and your face. Your face looks like a failed villain.”

“It’s a mustache.”

“It’s Sharpie.”

He marched toward the bathroom muttering, “Every artist gets hated first.”

Marisol laughed before she could stop herself. The laugh came out rusty; she had spent most of the week in controlled voices, donor-safe voices, museum voices with no elbows. Here, the apartment pressed in from all sides and nobody had room for posture.

She set the bread and limes on the table and moved a pile of unopened mail to make space. There were Con Edison envelopes, a school fundraiser packet, a folded circular from Western Beef, and one letter from Mount Sinai addressed to Gloria with FINAL NOTICE stamped in red. Gloria slid that one under the others without comment.

“Where’s the altar cloth?” Carina asked, already opening drawers.

“In the good pillowcase, top closet shelf,” Gloria said.

“Who put altar stuff in a pillowcase?”

“People with no linen closet.”

The first twenty minutes stayed blessedly useless. Gloria told a story about Ms. Batista downstairs who had started charging the building kids fifty cents to pet her Pomeranian because “he has anxiety and labor has value.” Carina countered with gossip from school about a father who tried to enroll his daughter in second grade using a Dunkin receipt as proof of address. Leo reported that Deacon Ortiz had fallen asleep during youth choir rehearsal and snored through “Pan de Vida” while still holding the tambourine. Gloria laughed hard enough to lean one hand on the counter, and Carina did a dead-on impression of the deacon’s little whistle snore that made Leo choke on his own giggling.

Marisol ate and listened and let the pressure come down in increments. This was what she had promised herself on the bus: no interrogation, no notebook on the table, no prosecutor’s tone disguised as curiosity. A family supper on All Souls could carry memory without turning into court, if she kept her nerve and didn’t lunge. Salvador’s voice still sat in her head from two nights ago, the rough low murmur saying family myth like he was saying mildew, something that fed on dark corners and damp. She was here to test corners, not kick the walls in.

Gloria crossed herself before they sat. They stood around the table while she led the quick version of the prayer for the dead, half Spanish, half English, the one she used when the chicken was getting cold.

“Por las almas de la familia, por Tomas, por abuela Luz, por los que no tienen quien rece por ellos.”

Leo whispered, “And for Mr. Jenkins’s cat, because the cat got hit by a bike.”

“A car,” Carina corrected.

“Bike sounds sadder.”

“Sit,” Gloria said. “God understands cats and your dramatics both.”

They sat. The rice came out fluffy and steaming, studded with peas and little cubes of carrot because Gloria still cooked like vitamins could bargain down everything else. The chicken had crisp skin and a puddle of orange fat collecting at the edge of the platter. Marisol tore bread, squeezed lime over her beans, and watched her mother serve everyone else first, last serving for herself as usual.

She started with weather and work and let the conversation drift where it wanted. Carina complained about a copier that ate report cards. Gloria said her patient in Midwood kept hiding blood pressure pills in a fake fern. Leo asked whether saints had birthdays. Marisol said yes, probably, but the church celebrated death days because Catholics liked homework.

“Catholics like guilt,” Carina said.

“Same thing,” Marisol said.

Gloria gave them both a look. “Catholics like structure. People with structure don’t end up talking to themselves in subway stations.”

“Plenty of structured people talk to themselves,” Carina said. “Have you met any principals?”

The room kept moving. Forks clinked against plates. Rain tapped the fire escape. Somebody upstairs dropped something heavy, then yelled sorry through the floor. Marisol let three beats pass, then set her fork down and asked, “Mami, what year was it when we started doing this dinner at home instead of church basement?”

Gloria did not pause long enough to count as a pause, but Marisol felt it anyway.

“After your grandmother died,” Gloria said. “Basement got too crowded and noisy. It’s cleaner here.”

Carina snorted. “Cleaner? In this apartment? Be serious.”

“Cleaner spiritually.”

“No such thing as spiritually clean with Leo around.”

“Mami,” Marisol said, keeping her tone easy. “I mean like the first year after Papi. Did we go to St. Brigid’s that first All Souls or did we stay here?”

The question came out light, but her pulse kicked once against her throat. Salvador had weaponized dates two nights ago; she needed one date that could survive this table.

Carina answered before Gloria could. “St. Brigid’s. You wore that awful black cardigan with the sequins falling off. You cried through the whole homily.”

Gloria shook her head. “No, that was the year after. First year we stayed here because of your cough, remember? You had that barking cough all week.”

Carina frowned. “That was me with the cough.”

Marisol watched cough turn into science fair and felt the old family vertigo settle in, everyone swearing on a different decade of the same house.

“That was Mari.”

“No, Mami. I missed the science fair because of it.”

“You didn’t miss science fair. You won honorable mention with the volcano.”

“That was sixth grade.”

“Exactly.”

Carina stared. “Mami, what does that even prove?”

Under the table, Marisol pressed her thumbnail into the burn scar on her left wrist until pain shaved the edge off her panic.

Gloria took a sip of water and turned to Marisol. “Why are you asking like this?”

Marisol picked up her fork again and shrugged. “No reason. I’m trying to map the chronology in my head. Salvador said something weird about dates and I wanted to check what I remembered.”

At Salvador’s name, Carina leaned back and gave Marisol the smile she used when a kid tried to hand in copied homework. “Ah. There we go. We’re in museum mode.”

Marisol felt her shoulder blades cinch. Museum mode was exactly what she had promised herself she would not bring to this table, and hearing it named made her want to push just to prove she was not pushing.

“I’m not in museum mode.”

“You’re doing the face.”

“What face?”

“The one where you ask normal questions with your little recorder-brain behind your eyes. Like you’re billing us by the hour.”

Heat climbed Marisol’s neck. Carina was not wrong about the face; she could feel it, that narrow professional calm she wore when she was scared of looking stupid.

Gloria set down her fork a little too hard. “Don’t bring that man to this table.”

“I brought chicken to this table,” Marisol said. “And bread. I asked one question.”

“You asked three questions,” Carina said. “In a row. About a funeral from a hundred years ago.”

“It wasn’t a hundred years ago.”

“It feels like it. I was a kid.”

“So was I.”

Marisol tasted metal at the back of her mouth and kept her shoulders loose, pretending this was still a normal dinner question.

“Exactly,” Carina said. “Kids remember weird pieces. Sequins, coughs, who stole whose socks. Not legal timeline.”

Gloria wiped her fingers on a napkin and looked from one daughter to the other, referee and defendant in the same body. “The dead are not a calendar problem. We cook, we pray, we remember, that’s enough.”

That should have settled it. Instead, Marisol felt the old panic that came when facts went soft in her hands, like paper left in rain.

Marisol kept her voice flat. “It’s not enough if dates don’t match.”

“Match what?” Gloria asked. “A story from a sick man who likes to hurt people with details?”

When Gloria said sick man, the phrase twisted in Marisol’s chest, because it was true and also a dodge, and she hated that both could fit at once.

Marisol felt the old spike of temper and tamped it down with bread, chewing until her jaw hurt. “He said Papi’s march happened after the museum strike, and I thought it was before. I’m asking because maybe I’ve got it wrong.”

Carina gave a short laugh with no humor in it. “Or maybe you’re shopping for cracks.”

“Can you not?”

“Can you?”

Gloria cut in. “Both of you, stop. Carina, pass Leo his water. Mari, keep your voice down. Tomas died, we buried him, and I still took the 5:20 bus to Mercy the next morning because rent was due. That is what happened here.”

“That’s church language—”

“No,” Gloria said. “That is bill language. That is rent language.”

The sentence hit hard enough to quiet everyone for a moment. Even Leo looked up from sculpting a rice mound with his spoon.

Carina recovered first, reaching across Leo to drag his water cup away from the table edge, voice bright and mean in the way sisters perfected by age ten. “You want exhibits? Fine. Leo, cup in the middle—yeah, there. Exhibit A, Mami’s blood pressure cuff. Exhibit B, cold chicken while you grill us again. Exhibit C, me up at 5:45 packing lunch because third grade doesn’t pause for your timeline.”

“I didn’t ask for all that—”

“Then quit poking and eat.”

Marisol put rice in her mouth and tasted almost nothing.

Leo reached for the beans, elbowed his water, and knocked half his plate onto the table. Rice shot across the plastic tablecloth like hail. A fork clattered onto the floor. There was one second of shocked silence, then Carina cursed under her breath, Gloria said “Ay bendito” like she was exhausted by gravity itself, and Leo froze with his eyes already wet.

“It’s okay,” Marisol said, standing.

“It’s not okay, it’s sticky,” Carina said, grabbing paper towels. “Leo, feet up. Don’t grind it in.”

Leo lifted his feet and looked miserable. “I’m sorry. I was trying to make a mountain for the beans to flow.”

“A rice dam project at dinner,” Carina said. “Very engineer. Very doomed.”

Gloria couldn’t help it; she laughed once, sharp and involuntary. “Your father would’ve said same thing. Tomas always made floods with his plate.”

She heard the old pattern return: Tomas as punch line, Tomas as weather pattern, Tomas as the affectionate ghost who never had to stay still long enough for questions.

Marisol crouched to gather grains from the floor and felt her knee pop. At eye level with the chair legs and shoe scuffs she could smell bleach from the morning mop, old grease under the stove, and candle wax softening in the heat. Carina passed her more towels without meeting her eyes.

“Thank you,” Marisol said.

“Don’t thank me, just don’t step on the rice,” Carina muttered.

Marisol stayed crouched, wet paper towels stuck to her fingers. “Then give me one thing that doesn’t move. Did Papi die at home or at a hospital?”

Gloria’s face went flat. “No. I’m not doing dates and places at this table, not with Leo listening.”

Carina straightened with a fistful of towels. “Push that again tonight and you leave. I’m not kidding.”

They reset plates and kept eating. Gloria shifted the conversation to neighborhood survival: who got their package stolen, which landlord finally fixed a boiler, whose son got picked up for tagging the B44 stop with giant silver letters. Marisol contributed where she could, laughed in the right places, and let the current pull away from dates. That was the punishment here, she realized. They did not shout and they did not exile her; they deflected until she either played along or looked cruel for refusing.

After dinner Gloria lit the black candles with obvious skepticism and arranged them anyway in front of Tomas’s framed photo, beside a chipped ceramic Virgen and a supermarket bouquet gone papery at the edges. They said another short prayer. Leo mumbled names of two dead pigeons and one dead YouTuber. Carina squeezed his shoulder, trying not to laugh. Then she sent him to brush his teeth while Gloria packed leftovers into mismatched deli containers.

The apartment moved into cleanup choreography everyone knew by muscle memory. Carina scraped plates and stacked them by size. Gloria wrapped chicken bones for soup stock because no part of anything got wasted in this house, not even cartilage. Marisol rinsed glasses and listened to the faucet’s stutter and the murmur of TV commentators through the wall. She was careful with her breathing. She had not been this physically close to her mother and sister in months without somebody storming out.

“Mari, grab the foil from my room,” Carina said, shoulder-deep in the fridge. “Top dresser, by the fan. If Leo touched it, it might be in his backpack. If it’s not there, check under the bed because this house hates me.”

“Got it.”

As she wiped her hands on a dish towel, a hypothesis clicked into place. Carina hoarded school forms, parish bulletins, and every scrap that proved a date or address. If a memorial card from Tomas’s week still existed, it would be in that bedroom, tucked near the saint cards. One printed date would hold harder than this dinner’s arguments.

The hallway to Carina and Leo’s room was narrow enough that Marisol brushed both walls if she carried anything wider than her own hips. A night-light shaped like a basketball cast weak orange light over the floor. She stepped around a pile of sneakers and a basket of unfolded laundry and entered the bedroom, where a standing fan clicked in tired circles and a poster of planets peeled at one corner.

She paused at the doorway and listened: cabinet doors, Leo whining about toothpaste, Carina swearing at the foil box in advance. Enough cover. She would grab the foil, scan for a dated prayer card, and get out. If Carina caught her rifling around, Saturday dinners were finished.

Foil sat exactly where Carina said, half-used roll on top of a dresser crowded with school IDs, hair ties, hand sanitizer, and a jar of pennies Leo had not finished counting. Marisol touched the box, then shifted the sanitizer and a stack of IDs aside, searching for a prayer card or obituary slip. What surfaced instead, tucked partly behind a devotional card of Saint Michael, was a narrow envelope the color of nicotine. It had no address and no stamp, but faded blue ballpoint on the flap read, B.B. Nov. 89 - G/S. The initials caught in her ribs because Salvador had blown up dinner with dates and Gloria had answered by shutting the door. The flap had been opened and reclosed so many times it held a permanent bow in the paper. She should have turned around right then, and she felt that warning in her throat like a cough. The errand was done, the family was still talking, and nobody had stormed out. She could walk back to the kitchen, hand over the foil, and keep her dignity intact.

Instead, she set the foil down and touched the envelope with two fingers, testing whether it would scald her.

She chose to verify one fact before anybody could fold it away again. She told herself she was only checking whether the label meant anything real, and then she opened it anyway.

Inside were six photographs, all old prints with scalloped white borders gone soft and gray from handling. The top one showed Gloria in front of a brick wall wearing a waitress uniform Marisol had never seen, hair loose to her shoulders, mouth open mid-laugh. The second showed Carina as a toddler in mismatched socks on a tricycle. The third was a group shot at what looked like a church picnic, faces sun-blown and hard to read.

The fourth photograph stopped her cold.

Gloria stood on a boardwalk with the ocean washed out behind her and gulls streaking the sky. She looked younger than Marisol had ever known her, maybe late twenties, maybe thirty, hard to tell with the grain and light. Her arm was around a man in a paint-splattered denim jacket, his head tipped toward hers like they shared one private joke. He had Salvador’s long nose, Salvador’s crooked right index finger, Salvador’s specific amused contempt in the eyes, the look that said he saw through everyone and still wanted their company.

On the back, in blue ink faded to smoke, someone had written: Brighton Beach, Noviembre 1989. After shift. G + S.

Marisol flipped it over, then back again, hoping speed could turn it into somebody else. She did the math automatically, the way she handled subway transfers, relapse days, and invoice due dates. It was pre-1990, years before any version of the family story put Salvador anywhere near her mother’s life. This was not a donor-cocktail sighting or a once-at-a-gallery acquaintance; his arm circled Gloria’s waist, her shoulder pressed into his, and the touch read as practiced. He was embedded in the story, not peripheral to it.

From the kitchen Carina called, “Mari? Did you die in there? I need that foil before this chicken turns into science.”

Marisol slid the photo back into the envelope so quickly she bent one corner. Her hands shook, just enough to make the paper rasp loud in the small room. She forced herself to flatten the corner with her thumbnail, stacked the photos exactly as she had found them, and replaced the envelope behind Saint Michael.

“Coming,” she called.

She stepped into the hallway, then stopped and went back two steps. She told herself she would not steal the photo, because a missing print would be obvious by breakfast and Gloria would burn every other trace. She pulled her phone from her pocket, opened the camera with the sound off, and snapped two shots, front and back, frame tight, timestamp visible. If Carina caught her doing it, she would call it spying and throw her out for good. If Gloria found out, she would lock every drawer and Marisol would never see this envelope again.

Back in the kitchen, Carina held out her hand without looking. “Foil?”

Marisol gave it to her.

Carina tore off a sheet with her teeth because the box edge was dull, then glanced up and squinted. “You okay? You look pale.”

“Hot in here,” Marisol said.

“It’s November. We have one window cracked.”

“Still hot.”

Gloria was portioning rice into containers with priestly attention, pressing each lid down until it clicked. “Take some home. You’re too thin.”

“I’m always too thin to you.”

“Because you skip real meals and eat almonds like a bird.”

Marisol almost laughed, and the almost hurt. She watched Gloria’s hands work: short nails, a knuckle swollen from old cleaning jobs, the vein on the back of her right hand like blue thread. Those hands had scrubbed hospital floors, lifted patients, peeled onions by the fifty-pound bag when she took cafeteria shifts, counted cash at midnight so tuition checks did not bounce. Those hands had also tucked a photograph into an envelope and hidden it behind a saint.

She tried one more time, softer.

“Mami,” she said, “that year Papi died, did he work at the museum right up to the end or had he already stopped?”

Carina made a sound like a brake squeal. “Jesus, Mari.”

Gloria did not look up from the container. “He worked when there was work. Your father was never afraid of work.”

“That’s not what I asked.”

Carina snapped a lid onto the rice. “Not tonight—Leo, rinse and spit, for real. I’m sealing dinner, not running a deposition.”

“He did what he had to do.”

“Mami.”

Now Gloria looked at her, tired and furious and frightened all in one expression. “Tonight we fed the dead. We did what we were supposed to do. Don’t turn this into a police station.”

Marisol held her mother’s gaze. “I’m not trying to arrest anybody.”

“Then stop asking like you already picked a sentence.”

Carina set down the foil and crossed her arms. “You want truth? Here. You come here when you need copy. If it isn’t copy, it’s guilt cleanup. If it isn’t guilt cleanup—”

“That’s not—”

“—it’s you in my kitchen with a tote bag and a question list. I’m wrapping leftovers, Mari. You’re building a case.”

It was a cheap shot and also not cheap at all. Marisol felt it strike the same place shame always found, a fist-sized knot just under her sternum. She could have fought back with chapter and verse: who paid whose MetroCard in which year, who sat through which detox call, who answered whom at three in the morning. She could have listed love as labor and demanded receipts. Instead she said, “That’s fair,” and heard Carina startled into silence.

Nobody spoke for several seconds. The fridge motor kicked on. Rain thickened against the window. Leo shouted from the bathroom, “I can’t find my dinosaur toothbrush,” and the world lurched back to scale.

“In the mug, by the sink,” Carina yelled, still watching Marisol.

Marisol picked up one of the takeaway containers before Gloria could offer again. “I should go. Early start tomorrow.”

“You always leave right when dishes get hard,” Carina said, but the edge had gone out of it.

“I’ll wash two pans fast.”

“Forget it. I’ll do it.”

Gloria wiped her hands on a dish towel and moved to the door with Marisol, limping slightly now that the evening adrenaline had worn off. In the hallway light she looked older than she had at dinner: jaw set, eyes ringed dark, hairpins still locked like armor.

“At least take coffee bread,” Gloria said.

“I already took rice.”

“Take both. Your landlord can’t evict you for pastry.”

The line was almost a joke, almost not. Marisol accepted the wrapped slice and let Gloria tuck it into her tote, mothering by compulsion even while defending a wall.

At the door Gloria touched Marisol’s cheek with two fingertips, brief and practical as checking for fever. “Pray before you sleep,” she said. “When your head gets loud, prayer helps.”

“My head’s fine.”

“Your head is never fine.” Gloria opened the door. “Go before the rain gets stupid.”

In the stairwell Marisol stopped one flight down and leaned against the cool painted cinderblock. She took out her phone and looked at the two photographs she had just taken. Front: Gloria and Salvador on a boardwalk, young enough to still believe the camera was harmless. Back: Noviembre 1989. After shift. G + S.

She zoomed in until the ink broke into pixels. She zoomed out until both of them fit in her palm.

For years she had held Salvador at a usable distance: the famous painter, the manipulator, the dying man with theatrics and morphine and a talent for other people’s weak spots. He had been external pressure, a storm moving in from somewhere else. The photo changed the geometry. He had stood in her mother’s weather before Marisol had language for weather at all.

Upstairs, someone laughed, then coughed, then dragged a chair across tile. Gloria’s apartment door opened and closed. Life resumed its normal rude volume around the secret like water around a submerged stone.

Marisol locked the screen and slid the phone into her inside coat pocket. The images were contraband on a device her family borrowed all the time to check bus routes, school emails, and Leo's game settings. One swipe from Carina would detonate the secret before Marisol had enough proof to survive the blast. Gloria would call her a thief, Carina would cut off Leo, and every old photo in that apartment would disappear by morning.

She descended the rest of the stairs carrying leftover rice, coffee bread, and proof that she had crossed a line she could not uncross. Passive trust was over. At first light she would take the B41 back toward the museum, with evidence and fallout in equal measure.
