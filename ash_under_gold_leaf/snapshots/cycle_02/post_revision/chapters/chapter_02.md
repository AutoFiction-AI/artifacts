# Chapter 2
At five thirty, after Ada's corridor check cleared the hall, the museum gave school groups a bright education room with wipe-clean tables and a sink for paint cups, and the fluorescent lights flattened everybody into the same bad color. Marisol sat alone at the table closest to the door, legal pad squared with the table edge, recorder centered on top of her residency folder like it might behave better under official paper. Facts, not theater, she told herself again. Through the cinderblock wall she heard a cart wheel squeal every eight seconds as a guard pushed stanchions back into storage. The room smelled less like art than cafeteria bleach and the sour sugar of old apple juice.

She had written three lines at the top of the page before he came in.

Objective: test credibility.

No myth language in the questions.

Get one concrete, verifiable claim.

When the orderly opened the door, Salvador did not look at her first. He stared at the sink like he was trying to remember what water was called, then held up his own right hand and flexed each finger, checking it like a tool he no longer trusted. He was taller than any chair let him be, even folded forward in the transport chair, long knees like bent coat hangers under a museum blanket. The skin under his eyes was the yellow of nicotine stains. He carried his pain in stages: first the jaw set, then the breath, and then, if it got bad enough, a tiny dry click in his throat before he swallowed.

"You picked the student room," he said while the orderly parked him. "I like your optimism. Children paint better lies than adults."

"It was available," Marisol said.

"So was heaven."

The orderly gave Marisol a quick apology with his eyebrows, checked the morphine log clipped to the back of the chair, and left. The door shut with a soft seal. Salvador tugged the blanket off his legs and winced when the wool dragged over his shins.

Marisol pressed record. "For the record, today's date is October twenty-ninth, two thousand twenty-six. Interview two, private session, five thirty-seven p.m. Present are Salvador Rivas and Marisol Vega." She looked up. "Are you lucid enough to continue?"

"You people love that word." He leaned back and shut his eyes. "Lucid. As if I'm a window."

"Are you lucid enough to continue?"

He opened one eye, then the other. "Yes. For now. If I start praising governors and dead critics, shut it off."

She let that pass. "Yesterday you said my father didn't die the way my family says he died. I want specifics, not riddles."

He smiled without warmth. "You're thirty-four and still announcing your age with that jaw. Gloria kept the world from chewing you."

"I didn't ask about my mother."

"Everything here is your mother." He tapped the table with one finger, slow, like counting in a song nobody else heard. "And your father. And the lies people tell children because they can't survive their own choices."

Marisol held his gaze. She had trained herself to sit through silence in workshop rooms where the person with power waited for somebody else to volunteer bleeding. This silence had different teeth. Salvador studied her the way a forger studies paper grain, deciding what would hold and what would tear.

"Say it clean," she said.

"Fine. Your family has been painting halos over burns." He said it flat, no theater, and then he looked down at his hand again. "There. That's your sentence for the catalog."

A radiator clanged once in the wall and then went still. Marisol heard her own breath scrape. She wrote the line down exactly, pressing hard enough to score the page below. "What burns?"

"Foil. Lungs. The skin when somebody nods off with hot foil in his hand. Tomas died in an overdose room, not under a protest banner."

"Then give me what I can verify," she said. "Date, address, witness."

"Not tonight."

"Convenient."

He rubbed his thumb against the pad of his index finger, a dry friction like sanding wood. "Necessary."

"Where?"

"Not on a bridge. Not with cameras."

"Where?"

"You don't get to drag a confession out of a body on schedule." He leaned his head back. "I didn't paint on deadline. I'm not dying on deadline either."

Marisol let five seconds pass, then ten, then fifteen. The guard's cart wheel screamed in the hallway and moved farther away. She wrote in the margin: evasive when pinned to location. Then she hated herself for writing it like a field note from a zoo and crossed out zoo before she had even written the word.

"You invited private sessions," she said. "You asked for me. You brought up Gloria's name before I brought up anything. If this is about leverage, say that. If this is about truth, stop performing."

He laughed once and then coughed hard enough to bend forward, forearm across his ribs. The cough sounded wet and expensive; each burst spent something he could not replace. Marisol half stood, then stopped when he raised his palm.

"Don't hover," he said into the cough. "I hate being watched like an appliance about to break."

He took two breaths through his nose and swallowed. "You want one thing you can touch? Tomas had a split callus in the center of his right palm from moving crates before they gave him gloves. He would pick at it when he lied. Always that hand. In meetings. In church. In my studio while he pretended he didn't care about art." He looked at her, and his voice thinned. "He picked at it the last week."

Marisol wrote fast, then forced herself to stop. "You just gave me settings, not proof. Meetings, church, studio. That's atmosphere."

"Good. You're not stupid."

"Then give me evidence," she said. "A day I can put on a request form, a door I can stand in front of, somebody else who was there."

Salvador's head drifted left like a flower stem cut too high. His eyes closed halfway. "Morphine doesn't make me a liar," he said. "It makes it hard to care whether you believe me."

"How much did they give you today?"

"Enough to keep my pancreas from setting itself on fire." He opened his eyes again, irritated now. "Don't turn into a nurse to avoid turning into a daughter."

"I'm not your daughter." The line came out quick and hard. She felt heat climb from her neck into her face. "You're not going to bait me into some fever-dream confession about blood and genius."

He watched that land. He did not smile this time. "Good," he said softly. "Keep that. You will need it."

She shifted tactics. "Tell me what you gain by destabilizing me."

"You think like a contract now. Nora would be proud."

"Answer."

"I gain very little. I'm dying in a borrowed office with bad lights. You think I need your pity or your poems?" He spoke slowly, each phrase dragged through pain but still precise. "I gain the chance to stop one story before I go. The saint story flattened him. It flattened Gloria. It flattened you into a girl praying to a dead man who never existed." He lifted two fingers, shaky but deliberate. "I'm not asking for absolution. I'm saying the frame is wrong."

Marisol could feel the old script in her body anyway, a muscle memory from novenas and photo candles and adults lowering their voices when they said Tomas's name. In family language Tomas was the brave one, the organizer, the man who stood in front when police charged and never came back. She had built half a book around that architecture, fourteen poems and three prose sections, all of it tuned to a man in a rain-soaked jacket raising a hand toward riot shields.

"If the frame is wrong," she said, "you can still be using the crack to crawl out from your own mess."

"Of course I can." He looked almost amused. "Finally, we're speaking as adults."

"So why should I trust anything?"

"You shouldn't trust me." He took another shallow breath, then another. "You should verify me."

She stared. "With what."

"With paper." He blinked slowly. "With logs. With signatures. With the boring machinery that outlives us. I'm not your priest, Marisol. I'm evidence in a failing container."

Her name in his mouth felt like a hand where she did not want one. "Don't say my name like that."

"Like what?"

"Like you earned it."

He made a rough sound that might have been a laugh and might have been pain. "I didn't earn much."

He went quiet long enough that she thought he might be gone for the moment, floating somewhere two inches above the room. She listened to the fluorescent hum, the hiss of the vent, the small involuntary crack of his knuckle when he flexed his right hand. Then he said, without opening his eyes, "Room had green carpet. Burn marks by the radiator from cooked heroin on foil. Tomas stopped breathing before anybody wanted to call it in. You can start there if you need an image to tattoo on your notebook."

Marisol did not move. "Then give me the address and the 911 timestamp."

"You don't get all of it today." He opened his eyes and looked directly at her. "You get enough to stop writing propaganda."

"You think I write propaganda."

"I think you write beautiful certainty." His voice had gone thin, almost gentle, and that made her want to throw something. "Certainty is the favorite paint of cowards."

"That's rich coming from you."

"Yes." He nodded once. "It's."

The nurse who covered his evening meds knocked once and opened the door before either of them answered. Young, Dominican accent, glitter pen clipped to her scrub collar. She apologized for interrupting and checked his wristband while Salvador rolled his eyes and called her General. She ignored him and asked if he had eaten. He told her the museum food tasted like drywall and she told him that was not a food group. When she peeled the foil off the medication cup, her nails flashed bright orange under the fluorescent lights.

Marisol stepped back to give space and watched the room change shape around ordinary care. Salvador took his pills dry and coughed, then accepted water with obvious irritation. The nurse wiped a drip from his chin with a square of gauze and told him he needed to stop trying to impress interviewers with dehydration.

"This one doesn't impress easy," he said, jerking his chin toward Marisol.

"Good," the nurse said. "You don't need more fans."

When she left, the door seal whispered shut again and the air felt meaner for the contrast.

Marisol clicked her pen, once, then again, just to keep her hand from shaking. "One more pass. If I try to verify, where do I start."

"Ask where Tomas worked nights that winter." Salvador's eyes were already drifting. "Ask who signed him in. Ask who signed after."

Night shifts and sign-in sheets were at least a paper trail she could hold, if she could find it.

"At the museum?"

He did not answer.

His throat moved once. She could not tell whether the silence came from pain or strategy, and she hated that he could still make those look the same.

"At the museum?"

He lifted one shoulder. "Maybe don't walk into your mother's kitchen with a tape recorder and righteousness. You're not built for that kind of fire yet."

Heat flashed up her neck. She saw, for a second, Gloria at the stove with a wooden spoon raised like a warning.

"You don't get to tell me what I'm built for."

"Everyone tells you what you're built for." He shut his eyes. "I'm only less polite about it."

The fluorescent buzz stretched thin. For a beat she thought he had slipped back under and left her with half a map.

She waited, and nothing happened.

"Salvador."

Nothing happened again.

"Mr. Rivas, interview is ending at six twenty-three p.m." She reached across the table and turned off the recorder. Even the click sounded loud.

He spoke without opening his eyes. "Bring me something true next time. Not your posture."

The hallway outside felt too wide after the room, polished stone and donor names and a security guard rubbing hand sanitizer until it squeaked. Marisol signed the exit log with a pen chained to the desk and wrote her full name too hard. The guard asked if she needed a car service and she said no before he finished the sentence.

Outside, Eastern Parkway held a damp October dark that turned every headlight into smeared mercury. Bus exhaust folded into the cold. Her phone said 6:41. She had not eaten since noon. The recorder in her tote felt heavier than any object that size had a right to feel.

She started walking east without deciding where she was going.

The first two blocks felt like logistics. She zipped her coat. She pulled the tote strap higher and avoided a puddle with an oil rainbow. She told herself not to step on slick leaves near the curb. By the third block her body had switched tracks. Her mouth went wet. Her jaw clenched and unclenched on its own. A memory arrived whole and unwelcome: the clean plastic crack of a vodka seal giving way. Her shoulders tightened with old debtor panic, the feeling that someone behind her had called her name and she already owed more than she could pay.

She passed a bodega where a man in a Mets cap stacked avocados into a pyramid that would collapse in under an hour. She passed the check-cashing place with the blue sign that buzzed louder than traffic. She passed a daycare with finger-paint turkeys taped to the glass. On the corner of Albany and Lincoln she stopped because across the street a liquor store sign blinked V & L in red neon, half the bulbs dead so it read V _ L, like a missing tooth.

She knew that store too well for someone who had not walked in for thirty months. Cheap quart bottles sat behind plexiglass. The floor near the beer cooler always ran warm. The clerk with the Knicks lanyard never asked for ID after the second week and always slid the bottle over like he was handing over medicine.

Her feet angled toward the crosswalk before her brain filed the motion. She cursed out loud and an older woman under a transparent umbrella glanced at her, then away.

"No," Marisol said, to nobody anyone else could see.

The walk signal changed. People moved around her shoulder first, then hips, city choreography with no sentiment. She could step forward and be at the store in twenty seconds. She pictured it with cruel clarity: palm on the metal pull handle, bell dinging, cold bottle neck in her hand before the cap came off with that bright stupid pop. Her body answered the picture like a dog hearing a can opener.

She pulled her phone out and almost dropped it because her fingers were slick. She opened Luis's contact and stared at his name while the countdown on the crosswalk ticked from eleven to zero.

She did not want a pep talk or a speech. She only wanted accountability before the door.

She hit call.

He picked up on the second ring. The dispatch radio on his end crackled with route numbers, and she heard the small click of the mint tin he kept refilling with fennel seeds on late shifts. "You good?"

"No."

"Where are you?"

"Lincoln and Albany. Across from V and L."

He breathed once through his nose, a sound she had learned meant he was moving into structure. "How close are you to the door?"

"Half a crosswalk."

"Turn your back to it."

She turned. Now she faced a laundromat window full of rotating silver drums, pink socks slapping glass with each spin.

"Done," she said.

"Tell me three things you see."

"Laundromat. Kid licking the window. Guy smoking under a no-smoking sign."

"Good. Three things you hear."

"Bus brakes. Somebody yelling about lotto numbers. My own teeth."

"Where's the nearest meeting right now?"

She knew because she kept the schedule screenshot starred and pinned, because sobriety was routine and ritual and logistics when it worked. "St. Agnes basement, seven o'clock step meeting. Ten minutes if I walk fast."

"Walk there. Stay on with me till you see the church doors."

She started moving north, fast enough that the tote banged her hip. "I talked to him," she said.

"Salvador?"

"Yeah. He said my father wasn't a martyr. He said we painted halos over burns."

Luis was quiet for two steps, then four. "What exactly did he claim and what exactly did he verify?"

"He didn't verify anything. He gave details and pulled back and then gave more." Her breath came short from the pace, not fear yet but heading there. "He said no bridge, no protest. He said there was green carpet and a radiator with burn marks from foil. He kept drifting. He kept baiting."

"Give me anything that survives morning paperwork: a date, an address, a witness who isn't him."

"A month, maybe. No day. No usable address. No witness besides him, and he knew exactly what that does to me."

"Then it's an allegation, not a case."

"It still blew a hole in everything." She took the curb too fast and nearly rolled an ankle. "Jesus, sorry."

"You don't owe me polite language while you're trying not to drink. Keep walking." His voice stayed level, no sympathy sugar. "Listen to me. You're not confronting family off one dying man's theater. You hear me."

"I hear you."

"Say it like you mean it."

She hated that he was right. She hated him for being right in the exact tone that kept her alive. "I won't call anybody until I have documents."

"Again. Like you'll still mean it at midnight."

"I'm not confronting anyone without documents."

"Good. Step one: get through that church door. Step two: sign in and sit where people can see you. Step three: after the meeting, write a clean list with claims, missing data, and what you can verify. Then text it to me before you call anybody in your family."

She almost laughed despite herself. "Only you could make grief sound like dispatch."

"Because dispatch keeps people from dying in dumb ways. What's your next right action in ten minutes?"

"Get to St. Agnes. Sign in. Sit where someone can see me."

"After the meeting?"

"Text you. Eat something with protein. Write down exactly what he said while it's fresh. No interpretation words."

"Good. Add a column for what you can verify." He clicked his tongue once. "And Mari, one more thing."

"What?"

"If this story's real, it will still be real tomorrow morning. Tonight you stay sober and build a record."

She crossed Washington, headlights throwing rain sheen across her shoes. "It doesn't feel like yesterday."

"Let it feel bad. Still no family calls tonight. Notes first, sleep second, confrontation later."

St. Agnes came into view as a block of brown stone and tired stained glass. The church basement entrance sat off to the side under a buzzing floodlight and a handwritten sign taped crooked to the rail: AA DOWNSTAIRS, COFFEE HOT, PLEASE CLOSE DOOR.

"I see the doors," she said.

"Stay on till you're inside."

She took the steps down. Warm air hit her face with burnt coffee and damp wool and old radiator heat. Folding chairs scraped concrete somewhere down the hall. Somebody laughed too loud at something not very funny, the way people laughed before meetings when they were trying to prove they were not one bad hour from disaster.

Luis said, "You got your chip on you?"

"Thirty-month chip."

"Touch it."

She dug in her coat pocket and found the coin by feel, ridged edge and smooth center worn from habit. Her thumb sat on the stamped triangle. "Got it."

"You're choosing this minute. Then the next one." He exhaled. "Go check in. Call me after."

"Wait." She closed her eyes for a second. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Do the work."

The line clicked dead.

In the basement hallway a woman at the folding table looked up from a spiral notebook and smiled like she recognized Marisol even if she did not. She wore a denim jacket patched at one elbow with red thread in a loose star shape and kept tapping a green pen against her ring finger while she spoke.

"First name and last initial," the woman said. "Sign-in's for the room count only."

"Marisol V." She wrote it down and noticed her handwriting had jumped tracks, slanted hard right like it was trying to outrun itself.

"Welcome back," the woman said, and then lowered her voice. "Coffee's bad tonight, but the cookies are weirdly decent."

It was such a specific, unnecessary kindness that Marisol felt tears sting before she could stop them. She nodded and took a paper cup anyway.

The meeting room held thirty metal chairs in a rough oval, one leg on half of them shorter than the others so they rocked if you shifted your weight wrong. Fluorescent lights buzzed above a ceiling stain shaped like Florida. A guy in paint-splattered work pants arranged sugar packets by color on the literature table for no reason except maybe control. Another woman with silver braids peeled an orange in one long ribbon and laid the peel along the edge of her notebook like a border.

Marisol took a chair near the back but not in the corner. Luis had taught her that hiding was just drinking with better posture.

When introductions came around she gave her name and said, "I'm sober today." The words scraped on the way out. It was true and not enough and everything.

People shared in turns. A man talked about losing his MetroCard and bursting into tears on the Q platform because he thought the tears were about the card when they were actually about his brother not answering texts for six months. A home-health aide in pink sneakers said she had screamed at a microwave at four in the morning because oatmeal did not count as dinner and she was tired of pretending she was fine. A young guy with face tattoos said he had walked three blocks behind his old dealer yesterday and did not call out, and he wanted credit for that and also did not trust himself with the credit.

Nobody performed salvation. Nobody asked for proof of worthiness. They reported the weather inside their own bodies and passed the turn.

When it got to Marisol again she kept her cup steady with both hands and said, "I had an interview tonight for work. Somebody said something that cracked a family story I built my life around. I walked toward a liquor store and called my sponsor before I crossed. I'm here because I want to stay where my feet are." The cup trembled hard enough to tap her teeth, but she did not look away. "I also want to set everything on fire, but I can postpone that till I've got facts."

A few people laughed, short and knowing. The woman with silver braids said, "Postponement is an underrated spiritual tool."

After the Serenity Prayer, chairs scraped and coats came on. Marisol stood in line for stale cookies because the sugar steadied her hands faster than pride did. She stepped outside to the basement landing and pulled her phone out.

Before she called Luis she opened the notes app and typed before the details could turn into something prettier. The moment she wrote overdose room, her mouth flooded like she'd bitten a battery. When she typed split callus, she saw Tomas at their old kitchen table worrying that hand open with his thumbnail while he told Gloria he had already paid ConEd. She had used that same hand in poems like a worker's halo.

Interview 2 log, 10/29/26, 5:37-6:23 p.m.:
- Verbatim: "Family has been painting halos over burns."
- Core claim: Tomas died in an overdose room, not at a protest or on a bridge.
- Physical anchors: split callus center of Tomas's right palm; green carpet room; radiator burn marks from cooked heroin on foil.
- Verification lead: identify where Tomas worked nights that winter and pull sign-in/sign-out records.
- Gaps tonight: no usable date stamp, no location I could map, no corroborating witness beyond Salvador, no document cited.

She added one more line and did not let herself edit it: If this is true, I made him cleaner on the page than he got to be alive.

Then she called Luis.

"You stayed," he said by way of hello.

"I stayed."

"Good. Don't read me the whole thing. Give me one line that's verbatim and one lead you can run tomorrow without blowing up your family."

She read him the halos sentence and the note about night-shift sign-ins. He made her repeat both, slower.

"That's your lane," he said. "Lock those two points now. If you wait till morning, grief will edit for style."

"I don't think I can sleep."

"You probably won't sleep much. Still, get home, eat, set two alarms, and lie down sober. If your head won't shut up, count backward in Spanish and English." His voice softened half a notch. "You did the right thing before the first drink. That matters more than your feelings about it."

She leaned against the damp brick of the church wall. Rain had started in a fine mist, enough to bead on her eyelashes. "I'm going to investigate," she said. "Not accuse. Not yet."

A new email banner slid across her screen.

NORA FELDMAN - LEGAL HOLD: Upload all Rivas interview audio to curatorial server by 9:00 a.m. Route all Tomas-related staff questions through my office pending counsel review.

"Hold on," Marisol said. "Nora just put legal hold on this. Nine a.m. upload and no direct staff questions."

Luis gave a low whistle. "There's your new weather."

"If I hand them the only file, they can bury this in process."

"Then don't give them the only file," he said. "Make a mirror now."

"That can get me fired."

"Losing the record can bury your brother twice. Pick the risk you can live with sober."

She crouched under the church awning, unzipped her tote, and pulled out the recorder with the short adapter cable she kept for workshop transfers. Rain dotted the screen while she imported the raw audio file to her phone. At sixty-seven percent she froze, Nora's legal-hold subject line burning across the top of the screen. Then she hit share anyway and sent the file to her private archive address and to Luis with the timestamp in the subject line. Her pulse thudded so hard in her gums she tasted copper.

"Done," she said. "Two mirrors out."

She stared at the wet sidewalk, anger waking where the craving had burned out. "So now I'm racing legal and grief."

"Welcome to your life."

"No family confrontation. Not without records."

"Good. Saturday morning meeting, eight thirty. You coming?" he asked.

"Yeah."

"Bring coffee if you can afford it. If not, bring yourself and an honest mouth."

"I can afford bodega coffee. Not good coffee."

"Nobody in that room trusts good coffee anyway." She heard the smile in his voice. "Go home, Mari. Eat eggs if you've got them."

"I've got two and a bruised tomato."

"That's dinner."

When she hung up, she stayed on the landing another minute, not because she wanted to drink now, but because the urge had left a ghost in her muscles and she did not trust sudden emptiness. Cars hissed by on wet pavement. Somewhere up the block a delivery guy cursed at a locked gate. The red neon from the pharmacy sign across the avenue pulsed on and off over her hands.

She put the thirty-month chip back in her coat pocket and held it there through the fabric, thumb rubbing the edge until the metal warmed.

It was not solved, she thought, and it was not even close. But she was not drunk, and she was not blind.

She stepped into the rain and headed for the Flatbush-bound train, planning one bodega stop for marigolds if they had them, black candles if they did not. Her notebook knocked against her hip with each block as rain and bus salt darkened the hems of her jeans, each step a small, unromantic vote for tomorrow.
