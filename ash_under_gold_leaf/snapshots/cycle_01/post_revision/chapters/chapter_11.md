# Chapter 11

By morning, with the blue day-one chip still in her coat pocket, Ticket B-214 stuck to Marisol's damp thumb while the waiting-room monitor crawled from B-179 to B-180 and buzzed like a dying fly. She had packed her folder like she used to pack workshop handouts: clear sleeve for her birth certificate, copy of her state ID, notarized letter she had paid twelve dollars for at a shipping store that smelled like toner and nail glue, and the yellow legal pad where she had written every date she knew about Tomas in two columns, "family version" and "paper version," because she did not trust memory anymore. She had crossed out the word truth in her notebook and replaced it with verifiable three times. The third crossing had gone through the page.

The municipal records building sat in the rain like a block of wet limestone. People lined up under a shallow awning, shoulders curled against the wind, holding envelopes with bent edges and plastic shopping bags full of old papers. A woman in a red puffer coat had her father's army photo in a freezer bag. A man with two toddlers kept checking the online appointment page on his cracked phone even though the sign on the door said WALK-INS WELCOME, DELAYS LIKELY in block capitals that made likely look like a threat.

Marisol joined the line and counted the money in her coat pocket without taking it out. She had forty-eight dollars in cash. Her debit card had eighty-nine and some change until the copy-shop check cleared on Friday. Certification fees were listed online with the kind of fake clarity that turned into surprise as soon as you reached a window. She had been living inside surprise for two weeks: stipend frozen, badge dead, text from payroll with bullet points about "disbursement review," her landlord's voicemail she kept deleting without listening all the way through.

Security opened the glass doors at eight-thirty and started feeding people in six at a time. The guard at the magnetometer was a broad man with nicotine-yellow fingertips and a tiny radio clipped to his shoulder that played bachata between dispatch calls. His name tag said "R. CEDENO", and he wore a hand-knit scarf in Mets orange and blue that had a loose end he kept tucking back into his collar with patient irritation.

"Folder on the belt, phone in the tray," he said. His voice was rough but not unfriendly. "No coffee past this point. Whoever sneaked coffee in yesterday, you made enemies."

A few people laughed. Nobody admitted it.

Marisol put her bag through the scanner. The tray came out jammed under her tote handle, and Cedeno knocked it loose with the side of his fist, then pointed her toward a kiosk with tickets curling out in pale blue strips. She took number B-214. The monitor above the waiting room showed B-179 in green digits and buzzed every time it advanced, a tired electric fly sound that burrowed under her skull.

The room had plastic chairs bolted in rows, a vending machine with one dead fluorescent tube, and a mural of old New York maps behind scratched plexiglass. The air smelled like wet wool, copier heat, and somebody's menthol cough drops. Marisol sat, opened her folder, checked everything again, and watched people stand and sit and stand and sit while the numbers crawled forward in twos and threes. By nine-fifteen she had rewritten Tomas's date of death four times on a scrap page just to keep her hand moving.

At B-203 she texted Luis: "At records office. Long line. Trying for cert + toxicology abstract."

He replied three minutes later: "Eat something when you can. Facts first. Then breathe."

She had two peanut-butter crackers from the bodega and ate them dry, the salt sticking to the roof of her mouth. She stared at the map mural and thought about martyr paintings, the ones with arrows neat as calligraphy and blood arranged like drapery. Tomas had never gotten that kind of composition. He had gotten a story people could live with.

B-214 flashed at 10:02 and sent her to Window 7.

The woman behind Window 7 had a headset pressed crooked over one ear and a pen tucked into her bun. She did not look up while Marisol stepped to the slot.

"Application," the clerk said.

Marisol slid over the form she had printed and filled at home, plus copies of her ID.

The clerk glanced once, flipped the form back, and tapped the top box with a nail cut short and square. "This is DOH-108. Wrong agency for this office."

"Your website linked that PDF from the death records page."

The clerk shrugged without lifting her eyes. "Website links lots of things. We process MVR-41 for certified legacy record pulls."

"Legacy meaning what exactly?"

"Meaning historical municipal records when you need the full legal certificate, not informational printout. Fill this out." She slid a pink carbon form under the glass with enough force that it buckled. "And you need proof of direct relationship plus notarized kinship affidavit. Originals or certified copies. Next."

"I'm his daughter. I brought my birth certificate and an affidavit."

The clerk had already turned toward the next person. "You can attach those to the right form."

Marisol felt heat rise up her neck. "Can I keep this window? I waited almost two hours."

"Ma'am, if you're still at this window when I call next, security will move you."

She stepped aside with the pink form in her hand and the taste of blood from biting the inside of her cheek. The top of MVR-41 read REQUEST FOR CERTIFIED VITAL RECORD - MUNICIPAL ARCHIVE COPY in dense black type. The boxes were narrow, demanding exact dates and borough codes she did not have. Field 11 asked for "Purpose of Request (check one)." The options were "insurance", "pension", "court order", "genealogical", "other (specify)". The instructions below said "genealogical copies are not certified for legal use".

She chose "other" and wrote: "documentation related to contested public record and family legal interests". She hated the sentence, every careful noun.

Field 14 asked for "Cause of Death Access Requested?" with two checkboxes:
"No (suppress medical details)"
"Yes (if authorized)"
She checked yes so hard the carbon copied through three pages.

Then she hit Field 17: "Known Substance Use at Time of Death (if any)." Not required, the form said, but "may aid retrieval in accidental toxicity cases." There were tiny boxes for "alcohol", "opiates", "stimulants", "unknown", and a line marked "habitual abuse noted in source record: Y/N".

She stared at habitual abuse noted until the words blurred.

At first she thought she had misread. She brought the form close to the fluorescent light and read it again, each letter steady and mean. The phrase habitual abuse sat there like a verdict. It was built to flatten a person into a pattern, to make a whole life fit a checkbox. It made Tomas sound like a stain you circled in red.

Marisol took a breath, stood, and got back in the line for Window 7. There were ten people ahead of her at first, and then there were twelve. The monitor clicked B-221, B-222. When her turn finally came, a different clerk sat there.

Her nameplate read "IMELDA CRUZ". She looked mid-fifties, with dark lipstick gone at the center, silver hoops, and a navy cardigan with one elbow patched in black thread. At the edge of her desk sat a small plastic dinosaur with a chipped tail, neon green. Every time she stamped a page, the dinosaur jumped a millimeter.

"What do you need?" Imelda asked.

Marisol slid the form under the glass. "I need a certified death certificate with cause of death and informant line visible. Tomas Vega. I've got relationship proof. I also need whatever municipal abstract lists witness statements tied to the death event."

Imelda scanned the form, then looked up directly for the first time any clerk had all morning. Her eyes were ringed with blue pencil liner that had smudged at the corners, as if she had rubbed them while reading too many screens.

"You filled most of this right," she said. "Line 6B needs requestor status code, not \"relationship: daughter,\" and you left borough code blank."

"I don't know where he died."

"Then write UNK and give me probable jurisdiction. If you leave it empty, system rejects automatically."

Marisol crossed out daughter in 6B, wrote the status code Imelda recited, then wrote UNK in block letters.

Imelda tapped Field 17 with her pen. "You can leave this blank."

"Why is it on the form?"

Imelda gave a short breath that might have been a laugh if it had less exhaustion in it. "Because this template is older than my son. Because nobody deletes old questions. Because forms survive administrations. Pick any reason and it will be true."

Marisol kept her voice measured. "It says habitual abuse."

"I saw it." Imelda lowered her tone. "You don't have to dignify that line. Leave it empty."

She did, then handed back the packet.

Imelda sorted the pages into two stacks with fast square motions. "Kinship affidavit is notarized by a shipping store notary. That's legal, but this office requires county seal when the request includes medical-detail release."

"What does that mean in practice?"

"It means you need to walk to Room 318, County Clerk satellite desk, get your affidavit authentication stamp. Fifteen-dollar fee. Bring it back before three and I can keep your intake date today. If you miss three, it rolls to tomorrow, and this receipt expires at close. You'd pay the search fee again."

Fifteen plus thirty-eight put her one missed bus fare away from rationing dinner again. She heard rent in every number.

Marisol blinked. "Can I pay all fees now and hold my place?"

Imelda looked past her at the line, then back to her file, then at Marisol's face with the slow assessment of someone deciding whether effort would be wasted. "Cashier is Window 2. Pay search fee now, thirty-eight dollars. Keep receipt. Don't lose it." She stamped the top form with RECEIVED-PENDING in red ink. "Take this stamped sheet upstairs. Show them this and they should move you ahead of walk-ins."

"Should?"

Imelda lifted one shoulder. "I don't control upstairs. I control this stamp."

She slid the packet back. Her ring finger had a deep groove where a wider ring used to sit. The skin there was pale compared to the rest of her hand.

Marisol moved fast. Window 2 ate twelve minutes while the receipt printer stalled and restarted. She paid thirty-eight in cash, watched the clerk count the bills twice, and got a thermal-paper receipt thin as onion skin. She folded it into the clear sleeve with her birth certificate and took the elevator to 3.

She touched the sleeve through her tote twice before the elevator doors opened, checking for the receipt the way she checked for her keys after blackout years.

Room 318 was half office, half hallway overflow. A handwritten sign on the door said "AUTHENTICATIONS - BACK IN 15" even though someone was behind the desk. Marisol waited through an apostille argument before the clerk, a young man with a fresh barber line and a pink Hello Kitty mug, waved her up.

"County seal for municipal records release affidavit," she said, already extending the paper.

He checked the notary block, stamped a blue square seal at the bottom, and slid a payment slip under her hand. "Card or cash."

"Card."

The machine failed twice before accepting. At 1:27 it finally approved, and the release in her chest was so sharp it almost felt like grief.

The elevator down was out of service. She took the stairs, four flights in wet boots, knees burning by the last landing. At Window 7 the line had swollen again. She thought about stepping out for coffee when her hands started to tremble, checked the clock, and stayed put. Missing Imelda's side-wall signal meant losing today's intake and burning another thirty-eight dollars she did not have in cash. Imelda caught her eye from behind another customer's shoulder and lifted two fingers without smiling. Marisol waited by the wall while three numbers passed. At 1:51 Imelda called, "B-214 return."

Marisol stepped up and slid the county-sealed affidavit and receipt under the glass.

Imelda checked each page, then entered data into a terminal that looked old enough to have voted in the nineties. Green text crawled across black. She typed with two fingers, paused, typed again.

"System found three Tomas Vegas," she said. "One Bronx, one Queens, one Brooklyn. Give me date range."

Marisol opened her legal pad. "I inherited two dates. At church we marked June 1992 every year like that was when he died. At home everyone said November 1992. The only hard anchor I've got is a museum handling log that puts him alive in November, so June reads like memorial drift, not death date. I need October through December to catch month swaps and late filing."

"It sounds like family." Imelda clicked a field. "Date range?"

"October first to December thirty-first, 1992."

Imelda entered it. "Mother's name?"

"Gloria Vega. Maiden name Morales."

"Father?"

Marisol held still. "Tomas Vega."

Imelda nodded, eyes on screen. "You want cause-of-death field unsuppressed. That requires Section C release acknowledgment. Sign here, here, and here."

There were three signature lines. Marisol signed all three in the cramped space, ink catching on the paper grain. The second line required initials next to language that read "Requester understands medical terms may be graphic, stigmatizing, or outdated in archival records." She initialed anyway.

Imelda printed a search pull request and fed it to a fax machine with a curled cord. "Archive stack is off-site in Long Island City. Normal pull time is ten business days. Same-day only happens under Administrative Rule AR-17: documented administrative harm, active response window, and a record that's already in the pilot index."

Marisol felt the room tilt a degree. "My residency got suspended. Badge deactivated, stipend frozen, and counsel wants a documentary response this week before review. Does that meet AR-17?"

"If you can document it."

Marisol slid over the suspension notice printout and payroll email she had folded into the back sleeve. Imelda scanned both, lips moving once over a date.

"You said residency. Museum?"

"Brooklyn Museum."

Something crossed Imelda's face, quick as a shadow. "My niece interns there on weekends. She was told not to say your name this week. That's gossip, not criteria."

Marisol's stomach dropped anyway.

Imelda leaned back, stared at the dinosaur, then turned to the terminal. "Criteria are AR-17 plus index availability. It's not guaranteed. It's not a favor. It's a procedural category most clerks avoid because it creates extra audit work."

"What do you need from me?"

"One paragraph, handwritten, describing immediate harm from delay. No poetry. Dates, consequences, plain language."

Marisol almost laughed at the no poetry but swallowed it. "I can do plain language."

Imelda pushed a ruled sheet through the slot. "Print, don't script. Attach to your signed acknowledgment."

Marisol wrote: "On November 29, 2026, my residency stipend and archive badge were suspended by Brooklyn Museum leadership after public dispute related to the circumstances of Tomas Vega's death. I need certified municipal records to verify cause of death and informant identity for pending legal and contractual responses. Delay prevents correction of false public and family record and may cause additional financial harm." She kept adjectives out. She kept rage out. She printed each letter like a first grader.

Imelda read it once, stapled it to the form, and keyed in another code. The printer at her elbow spit out a slip marked "EXPEDITE - ADMIN HARM REVIEW".

"Take this to Records Pull desk, Room 112. Give it to Ms. Chao only. If she's at lunch, wait. Don't hand it to anyone else."

"Why only her?"

"Because she actually reads what she signs." Imelda stamped the slip and pushed it over. "And because I said so."

Room 112 had no windows and two humming scanners the size of washing machines. Boxes of bound ledgers sat on steel carts tagged with barcodes. A woman in a gray turtleneck and fingerless gloves looked up from a stack of index cards.

"Ms. Chao?" Marisol asked.

The woman nodded, took the expedite slip, and read it without expression. Her hair was pinned with two chopsticks, one plain wood and one bright plastic with a little cat face. She circled a code with red pencil and pointed to a bench.

"Sit. We check pilot index first. If no digital mirror, same-day isn't possible."

Marisol sat on the bench and listened to scanner fans and the slap of rubber stamp pads. On the opposite wall a whiteboard listed pull times:
"Standard: 7-10 business days"
"Certified with medical release: 10-15"
"Expedite (approved): same day if indexed"
Someone had added in marker under that: "if the moon cooperates".

At 2:34 Ms. Chao called her name.

"We found one matched certificate. Brooklyn jurisdiction. Date of death November 12, 1992." She did not soften the next sentence. "Cause field is present. Informant field is present. You still want full release?"

"Yes."

Ms. Chao studied her for a beat, then nodded. "Sign final acknowledgment. You'll receive one certified copy and one municipal incident abstract because your request listed witness linkage."

She signed again.

The printing process took almost twenty minutes. First came a plain paper preview that Ms. Chao reviewed with a ruler, line by line, checking that each field aligned with template boxes. Then a heavier cream sheet fed through a different printer and came out with a faint watermark you could only see when tilted. Ms. Chao pressed a raised seal at the lower right corner, then ran a narrow blue ribbon through two punched holes and crimped it with a metal eyelet.

When she slid the document toward Marisol, Marisol did not touch it right away. The paper lay under plexi like an insect pinned for school biology.

"Name of Decedent: VEGA, TOMAS A."
"Date of Death: 11/12/1992"
"Age: 38"
"Manner of Death: ACCIDENT"
"Immediate Cause: ACUTE INTOXICATION - FENTANYL AND HEROIN"
"Contributing Conditions: chronic opioid dependence"

The old term was still there in a notation box on the incident abstract:
"known narcotics abuser per collateral witness"
Typewriter font, all caps where the typebars had hit harder.

Marisol felt the back of her tongue flood with the metallic saliva she got before she cried or threw up. She swallowed and read the lines again, slower, because the first read had been pure impact. The certificate said acute intoxication. It did not say martyrdom. It did not say anything noble. It implied needles and dope and a motel room and a body that stopped because chemistry did not care what story came later.

Ms. Chao tapped the lower half of the incident abstract. "Witness line is here."

Marisol tracked down with her finger.

"Witness/Informant: RIVAS, SALVADOR E."
"Address: 199 Prospect Park West, Brooklyn, NY"
"Relationship to decedent: friend/employer"

The friend/employer slash looked obscene in its neatness.

She had expected a cop, a paramedic, maybe an anonymous desk sergeant. She had not expected Salvador's full name, full address, typed by someone who had never met any of them and still preserved the most intimate betrayal in municipal ink.

Her knees went loose. She sat back on the bench because standing suddenly felt ambitious.

Ms. Chao waited, not unkindly. "Do you need water?"

Marisol shook her head.

"You can request amendment language review if archival terminology is offensive," Ms. Chao said. "It doesn't alter originals. It appends modern terminology notice."

"How long does that take?"

"Usually six to eight weeks."

Marisol gave one dry laugh. "Of course."

Ms. Chao held out a second sheet. "Given the wording note, I'm attaching the current language notice template now. It states that terms reflect period usage and are not endorsed by current department standards. It won't change the document, but it gives context if you're presenting this packet."

"Thank you."

"It's routine." Ms. Chao's mouth tightened at one corner. "It should have been routine before you had to ask."

Marisol took the packet with both hands. The seal was still warm from the press, a faint heat against her fingertips. Paper had weight she could feel in her forearms, absurd and real. She could take this to a lawyer. She could lay it on Gloria's table and point. She could mail a copy to museum counsel and make them answer nouns instead of vibes and donor panic.

None of it made her less alone in that room.

She got back to Window 7 to close intake at 3:18, and the waiting room had thinned into end-of-day silence. Imelda was stacking forms in color-coded bins. She took one look at Marisol's sealed packet and nodded once, like an umpire calling a strike.

"You got it."

"I got it."

Imelda handed over a final receipt with a barcode and clerk code "IC-77". "Keep that with the certificate. If anyone challenges chain of custody, this ties pull, payment, and release."

Marisol tucked it into the sleeve.

Imelda hesitated, then opened a desk drawer and pulled out a tiny tube of hand cream, the cheap unscented kind from drugstores. She rubbed some into the raw skin at the base of her thumbs before speaking again. "My brother died in 2004. Different paperwork, same vocabulary. I filed amendment language notice because my mother couldn't stand seeing "habitual" on anything with his name. It did not fix grief. It fixed one corner of the room."

Marisol met her eyes. "That matters."

"It did to my mother." Imelda capped the tube and slid it away. "Go eat before you read it again."

Marisol almost said she had no appetite, no money for more than a coffee, no idea where she would put all this anger tonight without drinking. Instead she said, "Thank you for the workaround."

Imelda picked up the dinosaur and moved it to the top of the monitor, where it looked like it was climbing. "I gave you a code path you already qualified for. That's not the same thing as mercy."

"Okay."

"Still." Imelda gave the briefest smile. "Use it well."

Outside, the rain had stopped but the sidewalks shone black under a lowering sky. Marisol stood under the awning and checked the seal once, then walked two blocks to a copy shop near Borough Hall. She spent twenty-two dollars on duplicates and fresh clear sleeves, keeping the originals flat in the middle of her tote between notebook and sweater so they would not bend. The city smelled like wet metal and exhaust and roasted nuts from a cart on the corner.

She walked to the station and did not open the packet on the platform even though every cell in her body wanted to. She waited until she had a seat on the train, knees braced against the opposite bench, and then looked again at the witness line until the letters stopped jumping.

"RIVAS, SALVADOR E."

The train doors chimed. A teenager across from her was doing algebra homework on his phone with one earbud in, mouthing numbers to himself. Two construction workers argued softly about whether the Knicks would trade anyone before February. Their voices blurred into rail squeal and station announcements while Marisol held the document flat on her lap like she was handling a thin pane of glass that could cut either way.

She typed a message to Ada:
"I got certified records. It's worse and clearer."
She stared at it, then deleted it before she could decide whether contact counted as help or harm under the museum order.

She called Luis instead. He picked up on the second ring, background noise full of dispatch radios and someone laughing too loudly.

"You good?" he asked.

"I got the certificate and incident abstract."

"You sitting down?"

"On the train."

"Read me the facts. No speech."

She looked at the page and forced her voice steady. "Date of death November twelve, ninety-two. Manner of death: accident. Immediate cause: acute intoxication, fentanyl and heroin. Witness slash informant: Salvador Rivas, full address listed. Relationship marked friend-employer."

Luis let out air through his teeth. "Okay. That's hard fact."

"It feels like a weapon."

"It's a document. You decide how to use it."

She watched her reflection in the dark window between stations, her face doubled over the text. "I thought getting proof would make me less crazy."

"It might make you harder to gaslight. That's not the same as feeling better."

She pressed her thumb against the raised seal through the plastic. "I can go to Gloria tonight."

"You can. Should you?"

She closed her eyes. Her body wanted speed, confrontation, relief, the old burn and then the quiet. Under that she could feel a smaller, steadier thing: the part of her that counted days and knew that urgency was where she broke.

"I don't know."

"Then don't make the biggest call on an empty stomach and a flooded brain. Get off at Atlantic. Eat something hot. Go to seven-thirty meeting in Park Slope. Bring copies tomorrow, not tonight."

She hated how reasonable he sounded. "You always make it sound like transit schedules."

"Because transit schedules keep people alive. And because you listen when I talk like that."

A pause. Dispatch squawked his name in the background.

He lowered his voice. "You did the work today. Don't throw it away by chasing immediate relief."

She swallowed. "I won't."

"You want me to meet you at the meeting?"

"No. I can do it."

"Call after."

The line clicked off.

At Atlantic Avenue she stood on the platform while warm air gusted up from the tunnel. Her hands were shaking now that the objective was complete. Not from craving exactly, though craving sat nearby like a neighbor's dog barking through the wall. From the drop after sustained effort, from the way the nervous system punished you for surviving the task.

She bought a bowl of noodle soup from the Korean place near the station and ate half of it standing at a steel counter because seats were full. The broth tasted mostly of salt and scallion. Her stomach unclenched enough to make room for thought.

When she reached her sublet in Sunset Park, she set the packet on the table and built a second one from the Borough Hall duplicates: copy of certificate, copy of incident abstract, chain-of-custody receipt, and the language-note addendum. She labeled each sleeve in black marker:
"A - Death Cert (Certified)"
"B - Incident Abstract (Witness)"
"C - Retrieval Receipts"
"D - Terminology Notice"

She made a third packet with red tabs for "family," then stopped before filling it. She was not ready to decide which evidence to weaponize and which to carry like confession.

Outside the window, somebody in the next building practiced trumpet scales badly, stopping every few bars to curse and start again. Marisol sat at the table with the sealed envelope and listened to him fail at the same measure until he got it clean. Then he ran it once more, softer.

At seven-ten she put the documents back in her tote, zipped it, and touched the blue day-one chip in the side pocket for no reason except contact. She wanted metal, paper, skin, and facts with edges.

In the church basement meeting room, folding chairs scraped linoleum and burnt coffee steamed in a stained urn. Marisol took a seat near the back and kept her bag looped around her ankle. When it was her turn she did not narrate the whole day, did not turn municipal forms into story. She said, "I spent six hours getting records that proved my family lied about how my father died. I thought I'd feel vindicated. I mostly feel alone and wired. I'm not drinking tonight."

A woman across the circle in a denim jacket nodded once. "Good. Keep coming."

A man near the coffee urn, maybe fifty, Mets cap with a sweat-dark brim, leaned forward. "Keep coming, sure. But don't confuse being right with being safe. I went out over custody paperwork because I needed to win the argument more than I needed to stay sober."

The line hit like a slap. Marisol felt her jaw lock.

The chair looked at her. "What's your plan for the next two hours?"

She almost said she'd be fine. Instead she heard herself answer, "I'm going straight home, no detours. Tea, shower, call my sponsor before bed. And can someone hang on to my bag while we stack chairs? If I keep rereading those pages tonight, I'm going to spin out."

The woman in denim held out her hand. "I got you. You ask me for it when you're ready to leave."

When Marisol let the strap slide off her ankle, the pulse in her throat dropped half a notch. She stayed for cleanup, stacking metal chairs until her hands stopped shaking hard enough to rattle them.

After the meeting, people traded leftovers from a box of supermarket cookies. Marisol stepped into the cold with the packet under her arm and the city opening around her in wet light and bus brakes and sirens two blocks over.

Proof sat in her bag like ballast. It was leverage, finally, something that could not be argued away at a dinner table or in a donor-safe conference room.

It did not call anyone home.
Three days later, she'd carry it to 2B anyway.
