# Chapter 7

Marisol woke before dawn with her jaw clenched hard enough to make her molars ache. Her tongue felt like a strip of carpet left out in winter rain, thick and bitter and impossible to swallow around. The room had gone flat-gray, the kind of light that made every stain in the sublet show itself. A coffee ring marked the floorboard near the mattress. A crescent of dried vomit clung to the inside of the bathroom sink. Her black jeans were twisted around one ankle where she had kicked them off without unbuttoning them.

For ten seconds she did not move because if she did not move she could pretend the pounding in her head came from the upstairs neighbors and not from the bottle she had bought with exact cash at a liquor store on Fourth Avenue. Then her bladder cramped and forced a decision.

She stood, gripped the wall, and waited for the room to stop tipping right. Her throat closed once in a hard dry heave that produced nothing but a rope of sour spit that burned the split in her lip. The tendons under her jaw jumped twice, useless as snapped wires. The mirror above the sink gave her a face she did not trust: eyes puffy and red-rimmed, mascara smeared under one eye in a thumbprint, lower lip split where she had chewed it in her sleep. She ran cold water and drank from her cupped hand until her stomach threatened mutiny, first swallow coming back hot with acid before she forced it down.

Her phone lay under the pillow, hot from charging and heavy with notifications.

On the lamp base, the index card she'd taped up at two a.m. sat crooked, marker bled deep enough to bruise the paper.

Luis (6)

Ada (1)

Unknown Brooklyn number (3)

Copy Shop - Grant (1)

CYP Writing Program (2)

She pressed the side button and let the screen go black again. Her head gave a clean knife-stab behind her left eye. She leaned both palms on the sink and breathed through her nose, counting slow the way she used to count when a craving hit hard in month two and she still believed the right breathing pattern could save a person from herself.

On the windowsill, the legal pad where she drafted lines for the residency essay had two new pages in her handwriting, slanted and violent, written with a pressure that had torn the paper in places.

She read the first sentence and felt her scalp prickle with shame.

I drank because the dead came back with administrative voices.

The next line was worse. It sounded like a grant application written by a corpse.

She had done what she always did when cornered: reached for language that made suffering look expensive. She had tried to turn a Thursday night vodka relapse into something liturgical, something with stained-glass light and blood and history, anything but what it was. It was a drunk woman with a debit card and a lie already queued on her tongue.

She ripped both pages out, balled them, missed the trash can, and did not bother to pick them up.

She unlocked the phone again and opened her banking app before she could talk herself into waiting. Available balance: $112.43. Pending charges: $18.75 liquor store, $2.90 bodega seltzer, $5.50 late-night deli sandwich she did not remember buying. Autopay due in two days on the credit card: minimum $214. Rent arrears: $2,400, deadline December 15, and the landlord had already switched from polite texts to one-line warnings with no punctuation.

Her stomach cramped in a different way then, practical and humiliating. Money had edges. Money did not care why she drank.

The unknown Brooklyn number called again while she stared at the screen. She answered too fast and almost dropped the phone.

"Ms. Vega? It's Celia from Cypress Youth Programs."

Celia had a smoker-rough voice and always wore huge hoops shaped like lightning bolts, gold paint flaking off at the tips. Marisol pictured her in the office on Nostrand, perched on the corner of the desk because she never sat fully in chairs, one sneaker on and one kicked off while she worked through call lists.

"Yeah," Marisol said, and had to clear her throat. "Yeah, I'm here."

"You didn't show yesterday for the paid workshop block. We waited forty minutes. Two kids had already come in from South Shore and one from Brownsville, and then we had to pivot to open study hall. You didn't answer texts."

Marisol closed her eyes. The words workshop block felt like somebody pressing a thumb into a bruise.

"I know," she said. "I know. I messed up."

A paper rustle on Celia's end. "Caroline covered the emergency slot. Program director pulled your Tuesday teen section for this month and reassigned it. That's twelve contact hours."

It was twelve hours. At twenty-five dollars an hour under the grant budget, plus prep stipend, that was just over three hundred dollars gone. She had already spent it in her head on rent and MetroCards and groceries that did not come from the discount shelf.

"Celia, can I call Marcy directly?"

"You can. She's gonna tell you the same thing. This is attendance policy, not personal." A pause, softer. "You wanna know the next intake window, I'll text it."

Marisol pressed her knuckles against her mouth. "Please."

"Okay. Take care of whatever this is. Kids notice when adults vanish." Celia hung up before Marisol could say anything that sounded like self-defense.

She stood there with the phone in her hand until the screen dimmed again. It was twelve hours. One drinking night had burned three hundred dollars down to ash. She could hear Luis in her head without him speaking yet: You can't pay ConEd with metaphors.

The first voicemail from Cypress was timestamped 5:18 p.m. yesterday. The second, 6:02 p.m., had more noise behind Celia's voice, teenagers yelling and a basketball hitting cinderblock. Marisol listened to both all the way through. Then she listened again because repetition felt like penance and because she deserved the exact sound of what she had done.

She made herself open Luis's texts.

`You good?`

`Not hearing from you. Call me.`

`I'm outside St. Luke at 7 if you need meeting.`

`Mari. Pick up.`

`Don't go silent.`

The thread climbed in plain increments: check-in, location, meeting time, then her name alone.

She typed: `I drank yesterday.`

She deleted it.

She typed: `Can we talk this morning?`

She sent that one.

He called in under thirty seconds.

"Where are you?" Luis asked.

No hello, no accusation. His voice carried bus-yard noise in the background, diesel idling and a reverse alarm beeping somewhere far enough away to sound underwater.

"At the sublet," she said. "Sunset Park."

"You alone?"

"Yeah."

"You safe?"

"I'm hungover and broke. So. Relative term."

He exhaled once through his nose, almost a laugh. "Stay put. I'm getting off dispatch at nine-thirty. Drink water. Eat something with salt. I'm coming by."

"Luis, I can come to you."

"No. You're already negotiating. I said stay put."

He hung up.

Marisol stared at the dead line and felt the first hot flare of anger. It was not at him. It was at the way recovery stole her favorite tools. She could not argue tone or interpretation when the facts were this plain. She had drunk. She had disappeared. Children had waited for a teacher who never arrived.

She forced down half a banana gone soft at the edges and two stale saltines from a sleeve in the cupboard. Then she showered, kneeling once when the water turned too hot and nausea rolled over her again. The soap smelled like grapefruit and hospital handwash. She scrubbed until her skin stung. When she stepped out, her left wrist scar looked whiter than usual against the red skin, a pale comma she had stopped noticing years ago.

She dressed in a clean black sweater and thick socks and tied her hair back while it was still wet. She cleaned the sink. She bagged the ripped notebook pages and empty seltzer cans and the bottle with two fingers like it might still bite. Outside, garbage trucks ground along the avenue and the sky shifted from gray to mean silver.

Luis knocked at 9:43, exactly thirteen minutes after his shift ended, carrying two coffees and a bacon-egg-and-cheese wrapped in foil.

He was in three layers like always, transit fleece under an old Yankees jacket under a rain shell that had lost its waterproofing at the shoulders. Heavyset, careful with his left leg when he stepped over the doorframe, gait still protective years after whatever had happened to his knee. His beard had gone more white than black this winter. He held one coffee out without ceremony.

"You look like hell," he said.

"You too."

"That's age. Yours is ethanol."

She let him in.

They sat at her small table with the radiator banging behind them like an impatient landlord. Luis unwrapped the sandwich and slid half toward her.

"Eat," he said.

She did.

He waited until she swallowed twice before he spoke again. "Start at noon yesterday and walk me through. Not feelings first. Timeline first."

There it was, his method. His language was transit language, stop by stop. He trusted sequence more than speeches.

Marisol watched steam leave her coffee in thin ropes. "I left the museum around four-ten. I told Ada I had copy shop inventory at six. That was a lie already, because I knew I wasn't going there. I walked from Eastern Parkway to Franklin, then took the two to Atlantic, then the D to Thirty-Sixth. I bought vodka at six-fifteen. It was cheap stuff in a plastic bottle. I went home. I drank in the kitchen standing up. I missed Cypress at five-thirty and six. I passed out around... I don't know, nine? Ten?"

She stopped because she heard herself beginning to polish it, to shape cadence.

Luis nodded once. "Good. Keep it ugly."

"I woke up at four. Celia called. They pulled my Tuesday section."

"How much money?"

"Around three hundred this month."

"Can you make that up at copy shop?"

"Maybe one extra shift. Maybe not. Grant has finals week coming, less print volume." She rubbed her forehead. "My rent arrears are still twenty-four hundred."

"I know." He tapped the table twice with a thick finger, counting invisible tokens. "So here's the fork. One road is what you do when you're writing: make a myth fast, cast everybody in roles, say relapse means your pain is special. Other road is boring and keeps you alive. Calls, amends, meeting, chip, check-in, sleep, repeat."

Marisol gave a bitter half-smile. "You make it sound like bus maintenance."

"Exactly." He leaned back. "Nobody claps when a bus gets brake pads on schedule. But people don't die when they get done."

She took another bite she did not want.

"I wrote pages last night," she said. "Shit pages. Like I was auditioning for my own tragedy."

"Burn them."

"I did."

"Good. Narrative brilliance is irrelevant without honest amends." He said it flat, not dramatic. "I don't care if you can write this chapter so pretty it makes strangers cry. Did you tell the truth to the people you hit?"

The question landed harder than any insult could have. She looked down at her ink-stained fingers wrapped around the coffee cup.

"Not yet," she said.

"Then that's your job before noon. Celia already heard the first truth. You owe the room the second one."

"You mean tonight."

"No, I mean the ten-thirty at St. Luke. You're coming now."

She laughed once, short and sharp. "I'm still sweating vodka through my pores."

"Great," he said. "Then you'll sound less polished."

She hated him for that sentence and loved him a little for it.

Before they left, he made her call the copy shop and say she could not cover a noon shift because of a personal emergency and she would make up the hours Thursday. Grant, the manager, swore about the timing and then relented after she offered to close. Luis listened without expression, then nodded once when she ended the call.

On the train to Prospect Heights, she stood because sitting made her stomach churn. The car smelled of wet wool, old french fries, and somebody's coconut hair oil. A man in a paint-splattered jacket slept with his head against the pole, mouth open, one boot untied. Two teenagers argued over a cracked phone screen in quick Dominican Spanish. At Atlantic, a woman carrying three reusable grocery bags squeezed in and immediately began humming a hymn under her breath, same four bars looping, steady as a metronome.

Marisol held the overhead rail and let the rhythm of the train knock through her wrist bones. She wanted a drink with an animal clarity that embarrassed her. It was not because she felt sad. It was because she remembered exactly how the first swallow had dissolved the static in her chest, how the heat had spread down her arms and turned every accusation in her head into a low harmless murmur for fifteen minutes.

She thought of the kids at Cypress waiting in plastic chairs with spiral notebooks on their laps, pretending not to care while checking the door. She imagined Celia stalling them with a writing prompt she hated. Write about weather as mood. Write about home as object. Hold still while an adult fails you politely.

At St. Luke's basement, the coffee was already burnt and the folding chairs were in a loose oval that said this was not one of the stricter meetings. A handmade sign on blue poster board read WED MORNING OPEN - NO CROSSTALK - KEEP IT HONEST.

Eileen was at the door with the basket of chips, a tiny woman in a Giants beanie and a denim skirt over thermal leggings, her right thumb wrapped in a cartoon bandage printed with strawberries. She always smelled faintly of Vicks and wintergreen gum. She had a habit of writing names in marker on paper cups so nobody had to ask twice who took what coffee.

"Hey, poet," she said when Marisol stepped in. "You look rough. Sit close to the heat."

Marisol nodded and could not manage words yet.

Luis squeezed her shoulder once and took a chair one seat away, close enough to catch her if she bolted, far enough to let her stand on her own feet.

People came in by twos and threes. A home-health aide in pink scrubs with glitter pens clipped to her badge. A young guy in steel-toe boots who kept rubbing the scar under his chin when anyone looked at him. Mr. Patel from the laundromat near Church Avenue, who never shared but always stacked the chairs when the meeting ended and brought stale butter cookies in a tin shaped like the Taj Mahal.

By ten-thirty-five there were sixteen people in the room. The heater hissed. Somebody coughed deep and wet in the back row. Eileen read the opening in a voice that could have announced bus routes and funerals with equal calm.

"We're here because alcohol beat us and we couldn't beat it alone," she said. "Take what helps, leave what doesn't, and tell the truth in this room. A lie in here is a drink with your name on it."

Marisol felt her pulse in her gums.

Introductions began around the circle.

"I'm Darnell, alcoholic." 

"I'm Priya, alcoholic." 

"I'm Hector, alcoholic." 

Names moved like beads through hands.

When it reached her, she stared at the floor's chipped beige tile and heard herself say, "I'm Marisol, alcoholic." Her voice came out steady enough to pass.

Pass for what, she thought. For alive? For honest?

The topic was accountability after a slip. People spoke in uneven lengths: one man talked ten minutes about his nephew's quince and the open bar; a woman in postal uniform said she had eaten cough syrup by the spoon last week and spent three days pretending she had the flu. Nobody interrupted. Nobody rescued.

Luis spoke when his turn came.

"I'm Luis, alcoholic," he said. "Denial is black ice on Flatbush before dawn. Road looks fine till the bus fishtails. So I run the same checklist: who you called, what money you blew, who got left waiting at the stop. If I start writing voice-over, I'm already in the skid."

He did not look at her once while he spoke. The decency of that made her throat hurt.

By the time the basket reached her row for chips and medallions, she had chewed the inside of her cheek raw. Eileen held up a blue twenty-four-hour chip between finger and thumb.

"Anybody need to restart today?" she asked.

Silence filled the room, thick and electric.

Marisol's left foot began tapping without permission. She put her heel flat and still could not stop the tremor in her calf.

You can wait until tonight, she told herself. You can call Luis after. You can take a private reset. You do not need an audience.

That voice had elegant diction. It used words like audience and private reset and made cowardice sound strategic.

She stood.

The chair legs scraped loud in the room and every head lifted.

"I'm Marisol," she said again, and this time her voice cracked on the second syllable. "I drank yesterday. Vodka. Alone. I missed work and fucked up a youth workshop shift. I'm back on day one."

Chairs creaked and coat sleeves whispered as people shifted. Priya nodded once like she had just heard a weather report she trusted. The young guy in boots stared at his hands. Eileen stepped forward and placed the chip in Marisol's palm.

It was lighter than she remembered and colder, a flat blue coin stamped with ONE DAY AT A TIME around the edge. The metal pressed a clean circle into her skin.

"Welcome back," Eileen said.

Marisol sat down too fast, knees weak. Heat flooded her face, then drained. She wanted to cry and could not. She wanted to run and stayed seated. Her fingers closed around the chip hard enough to leave half-moons from her nails.

For the rest of the shares she kept her eyes on a black gum scar fused to the tile and listened to names of pills, bars, and cousins she had never met. The chip sweated in her fist. When Eileen asked for announcements, a woman in a navy parka by the radiator raised two fingers. "My grandson was at Cypress yesterday," she said, still facing the circle instead of Marisol. "He took two buses from Canarsie. Y'all canceled after he got there." A few heads turned. Somebody's key ring clicked against a chair leg. Marisol's ears burned. "You're right," she said, voice thin. "I left him there with no teacher. I'm sorry." The woman gave one short nod. "Don't give me sorry. Give him Thursday at four-forty-five, if they ever put you back in a room."

After the meeting ended, chairs scraped and winter coats rustled. A few people came over one by one, no speeches.

Priya touched Marisol's elbow and said, "I reset last month after NyQuil and lies. Day thirty-two now. Call if tonight gets loud." She wrote her number on a grocery receipt already covered with pediatric appointment notes for her son and folded it twice before handing it over.

Darnell said, "Good job not waiting till Monday," and went back to helping stack chairs.

Mr. Patel gave her a butter cookie from the tin, serious as communion.

Outside, the cold hit like a slap. Traffic on Atlantic roared past in wet bursts. Luis stood with her under the church awning while people lit cigarettes down the block.

"You did the hard part," he said.

"No," she said. "I did the visible part."

He nodded. "Correct. Hard part starts this afternoon." He lifted two fingers and counted. "One: call Cypress director, not just coordinator, and own it without a performance. Two: daily check-ins with me, nine p.m., no exceptions for 'creative flow' or museum drama. Three: no solo nights this week. Go to your sister's if she'll have you, or my niece's spare couch if she won't. Four: food, water, sleep like it's medicine."

"My sister barely tolerates me when I'm stable."

"Then ask anyway."

"What if Ada asks why I'm weird?"

Luis gave her a look that was mostly pity and mostly not. "You're asking me for permission to keep lying in prettier sentences. Not granted."

She laughed despite herself, then covered her face with her hand. The laugh bent into something close to a sob and then back again.

"I hate this," she said into her palm.

"Of course you do. Shame wants you riding solo. Recovery's route work: same stops, every day."

"Is that in the Big Book?"

"No. That's in the MTA employee handbook." His mouth twitched. Then he turned serious again. "Mari, listen. You didn't lose your life yesterday. You lost your streak and three hundred dollars and some trust. That's real damage. It's also repairable if you stop making art out of hiding."

She let the words settle without arguing. That was new and humiliating and maybe useful.

They walked to the corner bodega because Luis refused to leave her with only coffee in her system. He bought two Gatorades and a container of rice and beans from the steam table that had been sitting there since breakfast. At the register he put a MetroCard refill on the counter and nodded at her.

"Put twenty on it," he said.

"I can't let you keep paying for me."

"This isn't charity. This is logistics. You pay me back next check."

She wanted to refuse on principle, then remembered the balance in her account and shut up.

Back in the cold, she checked the time. 12:14 p.m. She had just enough courage to call Marcy at Cypress before her body talked her out of it.

Marcy answered on the third ring. Her voice was clipped, East New York brisk, with the breathless pace of someone doing payroll while eating lunch over a keyboard.

"Marisol, I was gonna email."

"Please don't do email first," Marisol said. "I need to say this live. I drank yesterday and I no-showed. That's on me. I'm in recovery. I reset this morning. I know I lost the Tuesday section and I understand why. I still want to keep working with the program if you'll let me reapply after policy window."

Silence on the line except distant office chatter and a microwave beeping.

"Thank you for saying it straight," Marcy said finally. "But staffing can't run on maybe. You're off the paid Cypress roster for December."

"I know."

"Hold a second," Marcy said. "Kiara and her aunt are here for drop-in because of yesterday. They asked to talk to you. You're on speaker."

A breath, then a younger voice. "Ms. Vega? It's Kiara from Tuesday."

"Hey, Kiara."

"You said you'd line-edit my college statement before midnight. Deadline's tonight. I turned it in with my comma stuff still wrong."

Marisol pressed her thumb hard into the edge of the MetroCard in her pocket. "You're right. I dropped you."

Another voice came in, older and clipped. "I'm her aunt, Ms. Ortiz. I left my shift early and paid fourteen bucks in train fare so she could make that workshop. We stood in that hallway forty minutes."

Marisol looked at the slush ridges by the curb and kept her voice flat. "You shouldn't have had to do that. I failed both of you."

Marcy came back on. "All right. Kiara, I have your draft. Go sit with Celia."

Paper rustled. A door shut somewhere on Marcy's end.

"Policy is this," Marcy said. "No paid teaching for you this month. If you want January consideration, you do two unpaid observation Saturdays, send me thirty signed meetings in thirty days, and sit for a conduct review on January fifth with me and site ops. Review is not reinstatement."

"I understand."

"Good," Marcy said. "Own it in writing by five p.m. today: what happened, who got hit, and what you do next. If that email reads like performance instead of accountability, we're done."

"It won't," Marisol said.

After she hung up, Marisol stared at the wet sidewalk, gray slush gathered at the curb in ridges like dirty snowdrifts. No replacement shift. No quick money. A month of unpaid proving ground and a review date that could still end in no.

Luis watched her face. "Well?"

"No paid slot," she said. "Maybe a January review if I do unpaid observations and bring thirty meeting slips."

"Then that's your route," he said. "No shortcuts."

He had to head back to the depot by one-thirty. Before he left, he made her put the nine p.m. check-in alarm in her phone while he looked at the screen.

When he finally turned toward the subway, he lifted one hand without looking back. His left leg dragged half a beat on the first step down.

Marisol stood alone under a sky the color of unprimed canvas and opened her fist.

The blue chip sat in her palm, cheap alloy, scratched at one edge, ordinary as a subway token and somehow heavier than her keys. She rubbed it once with her thumb until the stamped letters warmed.

One day at a time did not sound noble. It sounded like rent math and phone calls and saying the same ugly sentence until it stopped tasting like poison.

I drank yesterday.

She said it once more, softly, to the traffic and the church wall and the version of herself that kept trying to turn accountability into performance. Then she put the chip in the small zip pocket of her backpack, next to a pen, a bus transfer, and the folded receipt with Priya's number, and walked toward the train, already drafting the text to Ada because Luis was right about one thing she hated: no solo nights.
