# Chapter 10

By the time Marisol reached the museum, the rain had turned into the thin winter spray that did not look like weather until it soaked the cuffs of your jeans and salted the leather at your toes. She stood under the employee awning with ten other people pressing badges to their coats and blowing into their hands. Her own badge sat in the clear sleeve of her lanyard, black stripe scratched where she had dropped it on the train platform in week two. She rubbed the edge with her thumbnail anyway, as if she could polish luck back onto plastic.

The text summoning her had come at 6:14 a.m.

`Please report to Conference 5B at 8:30 regarding immediate residency compliance review.`

No greeting. No signature line, only the museum domain and a legal cc chain that looked like tiny handcuffs.

She had not slept. She had lain awake in her Sunset Park sublet listening to the upstairs radiator knock like somebody trapped in a pipe, replaying the poem she had posted and then refused to take down. She could still see the comments climbing under it in bright little blocks, half praise, half bloodsport. She had muted the thread and then unmuted it three times before dawn, each time pretending she was checking for factual corrections and not for punishment.

Inside the security vestibule, the guard with the Knicks thermos nodded at her without meeting her eyes. Abdul normally told her who had won last night and complained about his lower back from standing. Today he took her ID, scanned it, and kept it one second too long. His thumb tapped twice on the edge, a habit she had seen when he was deciding whether to ask for a supervisor.

"You can head up," he said finally. "Fifth floor conference corridor."

"You need the badge back?" she asked.

"Keep it for now."

For now. The two words sat on her tongue like aspirin chalk.

The fifth-floor corridor had polished stone that always made shoe steps sound richer than the people making them. Conference 5B was glass on one side and frosted halfway up, enough privacy for expensive decisions and enough exposure to keep everyone polite. Nora was already inside, coat off, reading from a printed packet with yellow tabs. She had switched from heels to her spare flats, black suede with the toes rubbed dull, and her eyes were pink at the inner corners from whatever strain had kept her up later than donor dinner required.

At the far end of the table sat a man Marisol did not know, broad shoulders inside a navy suit that had been altered recently and not well, one sleeve still long enough to crease over his knuckles. He had lined up three sugar packets next to his legal pad by color, white then blue then pink, and had not touched his coffee. Beside him, a silver-haired woman in a plum coat unbuttoned and rebuttoned the cuff around a compression glove on her left hand, checking its fit as if circulation and patience ran on the same clock.

Nora looked up when Marisol entered. Her voice was softer than the room deserved.

"Thank you for coming quickly."

"I live on the subway now," Marisol said, trying for dry, missing by a mile. "You wanted immediate."

No one laughed. Nora gestured to the chair opposite counsel.

"This is Elliot Kline, outside counsel. This is Genevieve Pratt from the Advancement Council."

Genevieve gave one small nod, the kind that acknowledged a person without inviting one.

Marisol sat. The conference chair dipped lower than expected, leaving her slightly below eye line. She hated furniture that did that.

Elliot folded his hands as if about to bless a meal.

"Ms. Vega, this is a formal administrative review under Section Twelve of your residency agreement, reputational harm and contractual compliance."

His voice had the polished plainness of people paid to make damage sound inevitable. He slid a packet toward her. The first page read `NOTICE OF IMMEDIATE SUSPENSION - PENDING INVESTIGATION`. Her own name looked clean and wrong in fourteen-point serif.

She did not touch the packet yet. "Are you suspending me or reviewing me?"

"Both," Elliot said. "Suspension effective immediately. Review as process."

"Over a poem."

Genevieve leaned forward. Up close, Marisol saw dried acrylic blue in the seam of the donor's thumbnail. Not museum blue, brighter, craft-store bright.

"Over public identification of living individuals connected to the exhibition," Genevieve said. "Over direct insinuations of criminal conduct. Over destabilizing partner confidence in the institution's stewardship."

"Partner confidence," Marisol repeated. "You mean donors."

"I mean everyone who keeps the lights on."

Nora spoke before Marisol could answer. "Three committed gifts are currently paused pending response. One lender has requested reassessment of object insurance conditions. Legal exposure is not hypothetical."

Marisol finally picked up the packet. Page two had screenshots of her post, highlighted in yellow like evidence in a cheap trial. She recognized the line breaks, recognized the way she had arranged the accusation to cut on breath. Beneath the images sat contract language she had initialed in intake week while eating a stale vending-machine muffin because she had skipped breakfast to make her first orientation on time.

`Resident shall refrain from public communications that could reasonably be interpreted as defamatory toward museum staff, donors, artists, lenders, or associated families.`

Reasonably. Could. Interpreted. Rubber words. Wide enough to strangle anything.

"I did not name the museum," she said.

"You did not need to," Elliot replied. "Context completed the identification."

"I did not name Salvador either."

"You tagged the roof garden location," Nora said. "And the title of your residency project."

The room went quiet in the way rooms do when they are waiting for shame to finish its first round.

Marisol flipped to the next page. Page three was tables.

`Stipend total authorized: $4,000.`

`Disbursed on intake: $1,000.`

`Undisbursed balance subject to immediate freeze: $3,000.`

`Future disbursement contingent on completion of review and institutional determination.`

`Archive credentials: suspended. Badge access revoked at 10:00 AM today.`

She stared at the numbers until they blurred into the shape of her landlord's voicemail. Two thousand four hundred owed by December fifteenth. Credit card balance six thousand eight hundred and climbing, minimum due like an ankle weight every month. Copy shop shifts that covered groceries if nothing broke. The stipend had been the only money with enough air in it to let her breathe.

"You are freezing all of it," she said.

"The undisbursed balance, yes," Elliot said.

"Because I said what happened."

"Because you published allegations without evidentiary clearance through institutional channels."

"Institutional channels are the problem."

Elliot did not blink. "That statement reinforces our concern."

Marisol turned to Nora. "You asked me for a truth-facing manuscript. Those were your words in the interview room. You said you did not want tribute language."

Nora pressed the bridge of her nose with two fingers, then let her hand fall flat on the table. "I asked for rigor. I did not ask for an uncontrolled public accusation while legal review was active."

"Legal review was slow-walking me until he dies."

"Don't make this about speed," Genevieve said. "This is about trust."

"Trust is what people with money call obedience."

Genevieve's chin lifted, a tiny movement carrying years of gala etiquette. "My son is in residential treatment for fentanyl right now. Don't tell me what I call things because I have money."

The words landed harder than Marisol expected. For a second the room changed shape. Genevieve was not donor plaque metal; she was a woman checking her phone every twenty minutes for updates from a facility upstate that took seven thousand a month and still smelled like bleach and panic. The compression glove suddenly made sense.

Marisol swallowed and felt the old reflex rise, apology as survival, apology as bribe. She did not give it.

"Then you know what a lie costs," she said.

Genevieve's face tightened. "I know what public accusation does before proof."

Elliot flipped to another page. "We are not adjudicating historical truth in this meeting. We are adjudicating contract compliance and risk posture."

"Risk posture," Marisol said. "I'm not a forklift you're parking."

"No," he said. "You are a resident in breach."

Nora's jaw worked once. "Marisol, listen carefully. I fought for this residency model because artists deserve paid access. If this escalates into litigation, that model gets dismantled for everybody after you. I need you to hear the institutional scale."

"I hear it," Marisol said. "I hear that my body is the floor tile you're saving the building from."

She pushed the packet back toward Elliot, then pulled it to her again. The paper was heavier than printer stock, expensive cotton blend, soft like skin and impossible to tear clean.

"What are my options?" she asked. "Not your script. My options."

Elliot glanced at Nora, then answered. "Option one: accept suspension, cease all public commentary connected to the museum and its affiliates, surrender active research materials for legal hold, and await determination. Option two: decline terms and trigger immediate termination for cause."

"There is no option where I keep working?"

"Not while this review is live."

"Supervised access only. Reading room, no reproductions, no public statements. Give me ten days."

"Denied," Elliot said.

"Five days."

"Denied."

"Forty-eight hours."

"Ms. Vega, stop negotiating with numbers," Genevieve said. "The decision is not a timer."

Marisol laughed once, short and ugly. "Everything in this room is a timer. Salvador's pancreas is a timer. Your donor calls are a timer. Rent is a timer. You're pretending this is about decorum so you don't have to say what it's about."

"What is it about?" Nora asked quietly.

Marisol looked at each of them in turn, wanted one face to crack.

"You need the story pretty until the opening."

Nora did not answer at once. Her silence had always been tactical. Today it looked like fatigue.

"I need the institution alive long enough for any story to be told at all," she said.

"Even if it is false."

"Even if it is incomplete."

The distinction was careful and brutal. Incomplete could be sold. False required blame.

Marisol felt heat in her chest, then the cold that followed when anger hit an immovable wall. Her hands were steady on the table. The steadiness scared her more than shaking would have.

"I want counsel of my own present if this continues," she said.

"You are entitled to counsel before a final determination hearing," Elliot said. "Today's action is administrative and immediate."

"Convenient."

"Lawful."

She turned to Nora again, because Nora had once texted her at midnight about a line break and because betrayal from someone who had seen your drafts hurt differently.

"Say one sentence," Marisol said. "One sentence without legal nouns. Do you think I'm lying?"

Nora held her gaze. In that held second, Marisol saw the strain lines deepen around her eyes, saw the woman who kept spare flats in her tote because twelve-hour days turned ankles into liabilities.

"I think you published before you could protect yourself," Nora said.

It was not an answer and it was the most honest thing anyone had said in an hour.

Elliot slid a signature tab across the packet.

"Sign acknowledgment of receipt."

"Is it consent?"

"No. Receipt only."

Marisol read the line three times. `Signature confirms delivery. Signature does not waive rights.` She signed. The pen skipped on the first stroke of her last name and left a snag of ink like a scar.

Elliot gathered his copy, Genevieve's copy, and Nora's copy into separate folders, color coded, decision already archived before her body had fully stood.

"You must surrender your building keys and research-room locker keycard now," he said.

Marisol unclipped the ring from her bag and set it down. The little brass key for locker C-17 had a strip of blue tape around its neck from when Ada organized the drawers by project. Marisol watched Elliot drop it into an envelope labeled `PROPERTY RETURN`, and for one dumb second she wanted to reach across the table and bite his hand.

"Your digital account access will be restricted by noon," Nora said. "You may request personal files through IT."

"You mean poems are now discoverable."

"I mean metadata is now legal terrain."

"Same thing."

Genevieve stood first, flexing the gloved hand against her palm before buttoning her coat. "For what it's worth, Ms. Vega, I did read the piece twice. You're not untalented."

The sentence had the shape of kindness and the weight of a paper cup.

"For what it's worth," Marisol said, "talent doesn't pay Con Ed."

Genevieve's mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "No. It doesn't."

When they filed out, Nora remained one beat longer. She gathered the yellow tabs from the table in a neat stack, then looked up.

"Don't try to borrow someone else's badge," she said. "Security has your photo flagged."

"You think I'm that dumb?"

"I think desperation makes smart people improvisational."

"You're still talking to me like a case note."

"I'm talking to you like someone who doesn't want to watch you get arrested in a lobby."

Nora picked up her tote, the corner where a pair of flats protruded from the zipper, and left.

Marisol sat alone in Conference 5B until the motion lights dimmed and brightened again, deciding whether a room had become empty yet.

At 10:02 she took the elevator down to the conservation level instead of leaving. The hall there always smelled like acetone and wet wool in winter, like everyone had come in from weather carrying small personal storms. She knew Ada's schedule for Tuesdays: solvent testing at nine, photo documentation at ten-thirty, break at eleven if the intern remembered to spell the pigment names right.

She stepped up to the secure door, swiped her badge, and got a red light with a low buzz that sounded too satisfied. She swiped again, and the light stayed red.

She tried not to make a face in front of the camera.

Inside the glass corridor, two interns in nitrile gloves wheeled a crate past, heads bent toward each other, their talk visible and silent. On the far side, Ada emerged from Lab Two in a gray T-shirt under her coat, hair pinned up with the same pencil she used for condition notes. She had a crescent of gesso on her sleeve and her right knuckle scar looked raw in the fluorescent light, as if winter had opened it again.

Marisol lifted her hand, small wave, and Ada saw her. Relief hit Marisol so hard it made her knees loose.

Ada took one step toward the door.

Then Maya DeRosa from HR appeared out of the side office like she had been waiting for exactly that step. Maya wore a green cardigan with a tiny bee pin at the collar, chipped red polish on both thumbs, and the distracted breathing pattern of someone who had left a child with a fever and was pretending not to check her phone.

She touched Ada's elbow, not gentle.

"Dr. Park, one moment."

The badge reader speaker was good enough that Marisol could hear every word through the glass.

"As discussed," Maya said, opening a folder, "you are instructed not to communicate with Ms. Vega outside documented institutional channels regarding restricted materials, residency proceedings, or artist records. Any off-channel contact may be treated as noncompliance. Noncompliance may result in immediate leave pending termination. Do you understand?"

Ada did not look away from Marisol while Maya spoke. She looked like she looked at damaged varnish, still, exact, furious at the chemistry.

"I understand the sentence," Ada said.

"I need verbal confirmation of compliance."

Ada's jaw moved. "I hear you."

"That is not the question."

Ada finally looked at Maya. "Yes. I understand."

Maya checked a box on her form, then offered a pen. Ada signed with the carpenter-pencil she had tucked in her hair, quick diagonal script, and gave the pencil back to herself like a trick.

Marisol stood on the wrong side of glass watching paperwork decide the range of another person's mouth.

Maya turned and saw her, offered the kind of professional smile that had no blood in it.

"Ms. Vega," she called through the door speaker. "Please direct all communication through legal during your suspension period."

Marisol lifted both hands, empty palms. "I came to talk to my girlfriend, not steal a Rembrandt."

Maya did not react. "Please leave this access point."

Ada moved toward the door before Maya could stop her and pressed her hand flat to the glass opposite Marisol's shoulder, not touching, close enough that Marisol could see the solvent cracks around Ada's cuticles.

"Go home," Ada said through the speaker, voice low. "Please."

"I need five minutes."

"You won't get five. Not here."

"Then outside. Noon. Anywhere."

Ada swallowed. "I can't."

Maya stepped back into frame, eyes on Ada now, not Marisol. "Dr. Park."

Ada dropped her hand from the glass. "Go," she said to Marisol, softer. "I'll text."

Marisol wanted to ask if "I'll text" meant tonight, tomorrow, ever. She wanted to say she was not asking for files, she was asking for a person. She wanted to say her body still remembered the shape of Ada's shoulder blade under her palm from four nights ago and that memory did not recognize policy.

Instead she nodded once and walked away before the wanting made her beg.

In the locker room off the loading dock she emptied what was still hers from a cubby with a paper tag now marked `SUSPENDED`. She found two notebooks, a cracked lip balm, a MetroCard with nine dollars left, and the blue day-one chip she kept wrapped in tissue because the edge had started to wear smooth. There was no cassette. The roof-garden tape stayed in her apartment, under clean T-shirts, where no one had jurisdiction yet.

She sat on the metal bench and opened the stipend table again, then took out her phone and opened her banking app beside it, lining up numbers like the meeting had taught her.

Checking: $183.12.

Savings: $0.00.

Credit available: humiliatingly high, functionally gone.

Rent arrears deadline: thirteen days.

The fluorescent light above her flickered with a high insect buzz. She thought about the first drink with a level of detail that felt pornographic: bottle sweating against her palm, the little metallic hiss when the cap broke seal, the fast medicinal fire from tongue to sternum. She could map it minute by minute, body memory more reliable than any witness statement.

She took the blue chip out of tissue and pressed its ridged edge into her thumb until it hurt. One day at a time, stamped in cheap metal, a sentence that had sounded corny the first year and then impossible and then, somehow, useful. Today it sounded like a joke told by someone with direct deposit.

When she left the museum, the rain had thickened into sleet that made the crosswalk paint look greasy. Her phone stayed silent through two bus stops. She climbed onto the B41 anyway, wet cuffs cold against her ankles, and stood because the only open seat had a rip in the vinyl spilling yellow foam like a wound.

At Eastern Parkway, a man in a construction jacket argued with his bank on speakerphone about a pending hold. At Grand Army Plaza, two teenagers compared fake IDs in whispers too loud to be private. At Sterling Street, Marisol's phone buzzed, and Ada's name lit the screen.

The message was eight words.

`Need distance for now. I'm sorry.`

Marisol read it once, then again with the punctuation turned into voice in her head, then once more until the letters stopped being letters and became weather.

She typed `I get it.` Deleted.

She typed `Are you safe?` Deleted.

She typed nothing and locked the phone.

The bus heater coughed dusty heat over everyone's knees. Outside, grocery awnings snapped in the wind and a guy in a halal apron used a broom to shove slush away from his threshold as if he could negotiate with winter by force of repetitive motion. She watched him until the bus pulled off and left him behind, still pushing at water that kept coming.

At her stop in Sunset Park, she bought eggs, a sack of rice, and the cheapest coffee from the bodega where the owner always called her profesora even when she came in looking like she had slept in a stairwell. Total came to eighteen dollars and some change. She paid with debit and felt the machine think before approving.

Up in her sublet, she set the groceries on the counter, took off her wet socks, and spread the suspension packet across the kitchen table under the one good lamp. The paper smelled faintly of toner and the museum's citrus hand soap. She used a red pen to circle every phrase that translated into money, then wrote the actual body-cost beside it.

`Undisbursed balance subject to immediate freeze: $3,000.`  
That amount would have covered three weeks of rent and then some, and now it was gone.

`Archive credentials suspended.`  
She had no lab access, no logs, and no witness chain.

`Public communications embargo until determination.`  
Silence was the condition of maybe.

`Failure to comply may result in termination for cause.`  
That word, cause, functioned as both weapon and stain on every future application.

She read until language flattened into noise, then lay her forehead on the table and listened to the upstairs radiator start hammering again. It sounded like knuckles on a locked door.

At 6:40, Luis called for their scheduled check-in. She let it ring twice before answering.

"Hey," he said. Traffic hissed behind him, dispatcher office noise and somebody cursing about a route delay. "You make it through?"

"Depends what through means."

"Means you didn't drink in the museum bathroom and you're still breathing."

"Then yes."

He was quiet for a second, then: "You ate?"

"Eggs in ten minutes."

"Good. Give me concrete."

Marisol looked at the pages spread on the table like autopsy photos.

"They froze three thousand. Badge dead. Ada got HR'd into a corner. She sent one text."

"What text."

"Need distance for now."

Another pause, shorter.

"That hurts," Luis said.

"Brilliant analysis."

"You called me, so you get analysis."

She rubbed the burn scar on her left wrist with her thumb until the skin warmed. "I didn't call. You called."

"And you picked up. Same difference."

He cleared his throat. "Meeting tonight at seven-thirty. Church basement on Nostrand. You're going."

"I smell like wet dog and lawsuit."

"People have shown up smelling worse."

"I don't want to hear gratitude inventory from men who still have jobs."

"Then don't listen for gratitude. Listen for people not dying."

Her laugh came out cracked. "You can't sponsor me through contract law, Lou."

"Nope. I can sponsor you through Tuesday."

She closed her eyes.

"Okay," she said. "Tuesday."

"Bring the blue chip."

"It is in my hand."

"Good. Eat first. Then shower. Then train."

"You should have been a prison warden."

"Too much paperwork."

He hung up before she could reply. That was his way when he thought more words would turn into excuses.

Marisol stayed at the table another minute, looking at her own annotations, the neat red circles around terms designed to sound neutral while they took away oxygen. She thought about Nora saying I think you published before you could protect yourself. She thought about Ada signing a compliance form with a pencil from her hair. She thought about Genevieve Pratt's gloved hand, rich and shaking, and the line of blue paint under a donor's nail.

Then she stood, cracked two eggs into a pan, and watched the whites seize from clear to opaque. The hiss in the skillet was the closest thing to certainty she had heard all day.
