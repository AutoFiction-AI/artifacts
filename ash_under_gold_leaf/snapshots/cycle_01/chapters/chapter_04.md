# Chapter 4

By the time Marisol got off the B41, the cuffs of her jeans were dark with street water and de-icing salt had ghosted her boots in a powdery crust. The museum steps held yesterday's cold in the stone. She climbed them with one hand inside her coat, fingers on the chipped blue plastic medallion in her pocket that marked thirty months sober, the way somebody touched a saint's medal before crossing traffic. The wind coming off Eastern Parkway smelled like wet metal and diesel. Inside, the lobby heat hit her face too hard, and then security sent her down a side hall where the air went dry and sharp and chemical. Solvent and old canvas dust. The smell always made her think of both repair and damage at the same time.

She signed the conservation log with the short golf pencil chained to the clipboard. Her visitor badge was orange instead of staff blue, printed with her name and a black stripe that meant escort required. The guard at the door did not look up from his monitor when he buzzed her through. He tapped a Dominican bachata rhythm against the desk with a pen cap while a tiny Mets radio clipped to his collar whispered spring training news in static. Marisol caught herself listening for the score and then hated that her mind could jump to baseball when her chest had been tight since dawn.

Ada met her at the end of the corridor beside a rolling cart of boxed nitrile gloves. She had her hair twisted into a low knot with two graphite pencils stuck through it like pins, and she wore the same black fleece she always wore in the lab, cuffs shiny from repeated washing. When she reached for Marisol's badge lanyard to scan it against the interior reader, Marisol saw the thin scar across Ada's right knuckle, white against dry skin that no amount of hand cream ever softened in winter.

"You made good time," Ada said. Her voice stayed low in this part of the building, as if louder vowels could disturb varnish.

"I left before my neighbor started cursing at the boiler. Felt like a miracle."

Ada smiled once, quick. "Don't call miracles in this room. We only believe in process. Come on."

The conservation imaging bay was colder than the hallway and almost perfectly quiet except for the HVAC push above them and a scanner fan that clicked at regular intervals. The triptych sat on an adjustable easel under wheeled lamps, three panels wide, its riverbank scene held in a gray noon that made every figure look one breath from speaking. "Witness at the River" was what the placard said upstairs. Down here it looked less like witness and more like argument. Brushwork thick in the left panel, thinned and glazed through the middle, then suddenly raw at the right where the reeds cut across a body of water so dark it could be paint or oil.

Marisol did not step closer until Ada nodded.

"Ground rules first," Ada said. She pulled a tablet from a drawer and held it between them, screen angled so Marisol could read the bullet points. "Supervised viewing only. No raw file transfer. No photography in the bay. No screen recording. You can take handwritten notes and nothing else leaves this room unless it goes through curatorial approval."

"You said maybe we could export stills."

"I said I would ask." Ada's mouth flattened, not angry, just precise. "Nora said no this morning. She gave us a ninety-minute window and a witness note in the access log. That's already further than policy likes."

Marisol felt heat rise up her neck. She could hear the words she wanted to throw out, all of them reckless, all of them likely to close the door for good. Instead she pressed her thumb into the edge of her notebook hard enough to leave a dent in the cardboard cover.

"Okay," she said. "Show me what we can do."

Ada set the tablet down and looked at her directly. Under the overhead lights her eyes were darker than Marisol remembered, almost flat black.

"Before we start," Ada said, "you need this clear so we don't burn time arguing later. Conservation can prove sequences. We can prove that one layer sits on top of another, that a passage got scraped, that a pigment wasn't available before a certain decade, that varnish was stripped and reapplied. We can prove material events. We can't prove motive. We can't prove intention. We definitely can't prove a family story."

She let that sit, then added, even quieter, "And we can't prove who held the brush unless someone signed under the paint like an idiot."

Marisol gave a short laugh that sounded wrong in the room. "You're saying you'll give me facts and no halo."

"I'm saying I'll give you what the surface tells the truth about. Nothing more, nothing less. Can you work with that?"

It landed harder than an accusation would have. Marisol nodded because the answer had to be yes. If she said no, then she was admitting she came here for confirmation theater, not evidence. She had spent too many years watching people turn dead men into banners to pretend she did not know the difference.

Ada handed her cotton gloves. "Then glove up. Start with raking light. Left panel first."

The lamp rolled across the paint at a low angle, and the river scene shifted into topography. Impasto became ridges and ravines; a shoulder line looked like a broken plaster ledge. Marisol bent at the waist, careful not to breathe directly onto the surface. The oil smelled faintly sweet under the solvent layer, like old linseed and dust warmed by electricity.

"See this here," Ada said, pointing with a capped bamboo skewer. "Edge of the cheek on the kneeling figure. Follow the contour down."

At first Marisol saw only texture, random as cracked mud. Then the light caught a fan of parallel abrasions, each one shallow and deliberate, slanting from temple to jaw. Not accidental wear. Not age. They were too directional, too concentrated. A hand had gone in and taken something away with repetitive pressure.

"Those are scrape marks," Ada said. "Mechanical removal before repaint."

Marisol swallowed. The back of her tongue tasted metallic, as if she had licked a battery. She leaned closer until her breath fogged her mask and she had to step back.

The face had been erased. Not damaged, not lost to humidity, not chipped by transport. Erased. Somebody had worked a tool across that skin until features were gone, then brushed a new passage over the wound.

Her pulse kicked once, hard, and then kept time in her fingertips. She thought of her mother at the kitchen sink after night shifts, scrubbing one dish long after it was already clean, not because grease remained but because motion was how she got through what she refused to name.

"You okay?" Ada asked.

"Yeah." Marisol cleared her throat. "No. I don't know. Keep going."

Ada did not touch her. She only slid the lamp half an inch and kept describing what they were seeing in plain language, every term attached to a visible thing. Abraded ground. Fill material in the grooves. Overpainted flesh tones with a higher zinc white ratio than adjacent original passages. Her words moved like clean tools: no drama, no comfort, just enough force to separate one layer from another without tearing either.

They moved through the left panel and into the middle, where the crowd at the riverbank compressed into dark coats and tilted heads. Ada switched systems, powering on the infrared reflectography rig. The monitor bloomed from black to a ghost image that ignored color and tracked carbon-based underdrawing. Figures rose and thinned and changed posture as the camera advanced across the panel. One painted arm belonged to two different gestures in different eras. A boat mooring post appeared where, in visible light, there was only water.

Marisol stood with her notebook open and forgot to write for six straight minutes.

"Don't trust your first interpretation," Ada said without looking away from the screen. "Underpaintings can be abandoned ideas, not censored truths. Artists revise. Good artists revise more."

"And men with reputations revise what they don't want remembered."

Ada clicked to overlay mode, visible and infrared aligning in translucent layers. "Both things can be true. Keep your hands off conclusions until we have enough points."

The camera crossed into the right third of the central panel. A shoulder emerged that did not exist in the finished version, broad and turned slightly away from the viewer. Then a neck. Then the suggestion of a jawline, half-obscured by later brushwork. Marisol moved before she realized it, stepping closer until her hip touched the cart and rattled a tray of swabs.

"Sorry."

"Stay," Ada said. "Look at the lower edge."

Beneath the overpaint, just above the painted waterline, a blocky mark appeared in charcoal-black, two letters separated by a period. T. V. The strokes were not decorative script. They looked like working notation, quick and functional, the way handlers marked crates in grease pencil.

Marisol felt her stomach drop as if an elevator cable had slipped.

"Can you zoom that?"

Ada already had. Pixel edges broke into soft squares, but the letters held. T.V.

Marisol spoke before she could edit herself. "Tomas Vega."

"Maybe," Ada said. "Maybe initials for something else. Title variation, tonal value, Tuesday vacation, take your pick."

"You don't write Tuesday vacation on a riverbank figure."

"I don't. You don't. I can't testify about what Rivas wrote in a studio forty years ago." Ada's jaw tightened. She kept her tone level. "I can testify that those marks are underneath a later paint campaign and consistent with workshop notation."

Marisol wrote fast, pen biting through two pages. Her handwriting narrowed when she got scared, letters leaning so hard they almost became scratches. T.V. under central-right figure. overpaint layer later. erased left-face abrasions intentional.

She stared back at the screen and saw the hidden man's outline again, then the finished composition above it, saintly and broad and public-ready. The revision was not subtle once you knew where to look. It was surgery done with thick hands.

A soft knock hit the metal door frame. Neither of them answered quickly enough, and the door opened anyway.

Nora Feldman stepped in with a legal pad under one arm and her heels in her hand. She had switched into black foldable flats, the kind she carried in her tote for twelve-hour days, and the skin around her eyes was pink from strain where she had rubbed too hard. Her hair, usually severe, was loosening at one temple.

"I thought I'd find you in here," she said. Her voice carried that polished donor-room cadence that never fully relaxed, even when she was exhausted. She looked from Ada to Marisol to the monitor, and something like dread crossed her face before she smoothed it away.

Marisol straightened. "We found an underpainting with initials."

"I can see that." Nora set the legal pad on a side table without taking off her coat. "And before we sprint off a cliff, I need to state this clearly for both of you. Technical findings are not narrative permission."

The sentence landed in the center of the room like a brass weight.

Nora continued, now in her policy register, each noun clipped clean. "Conservation data supports condition reporting, treatment planning, and scholarly context. It does not authorize public allegations, paternity claims, or accusatory publication under museum imprimatur."

Marisol's shoulders went rigid. "I didn't say I was publishing this tomorrow."

"You posted a poem draft from your notes app three nights ago at one thirty in the morning," Nora said. "It was deleted after fourteen minutes, and six people still screenshotted it. I'm not speaking hypothetically."

Heat flashed through Marisol's chest, part shame, part anger, mostly recognition that Nora had receipts because Nora always had receipts.

"That draft wasn't about this," Marisol said.

"I know what it was about." Nora pinched the bridge of her nose, then dropped her hand. When she spoke again, the legal nouns thinned out and plain speech came through. "Listen to me. I'm not your enemy in this room. But donors, counsel, and a living artist with estate paperwork in motion are a fire stack. If this data gets out as accusation before it's reviewed, Ada loses her job first, you lose your badge second, and the work disappears behind legal hold."

Ada did not look up from the console. "She's right about sequence."

Marisol turned to Ada. "You asked if I could work with facts. I can. But don't ask me to pretend this isn't pointing somewhere."

"I'm not asking you to pretend anything," Ada said. She finally faced her, voice still quiet, but sharper. "I'm asking if you can carry uncertainty without trying to kill it in public."

The question was blunt enough to sting. Marisol heard Luis in it, the way he asked practical questions that sounded simple until she realized they were moral. Can you call before you drink? Can you tell the truth without auditioning for sympathy? Can you wait twenty-four hours?

She let out a breath she had been holding since the letters appeared. "Yes," she said. "I can carry it."

Nora watched her for another second, deciding whether to believe her, then nodded once.

"Good. Then we proceed correctly." She tapped the legal pad. "Ada, you annotate the imaging log and flag for internal review. Marisol, you can remain for supervised note-taking for the rest of your slot. No copies, no screenshots, no mention of initials outside protected discussion until legal signs a route."

"Protected discussion means with who?"

"With me. With Ada when it concerns treatment records. With assigned counsel if we get there." Nora picked up her heels again. "Not with social media. Not with press. Not with family members who may become legal parties."

The last phrase sat between them for a beat too long. Nora knew. Maybe not everything, but enough to aim language like that.

Marisol said nothing. Anything she said right now would come out hot.

Nora's gaze softened by a degree. "I know this is personal," she said. "Personal is still discoverable. Keep your notes clean."

Then she was gone, flats whispering on the epoxy floor, the door easing shut behind her.

The room felt bigger after she left, and colder.

Ada exhaled through her nose. "She did not love finding us ahead of review."

"She talked like I already blew it."

"You almost did, once." Ada gave her a look that was not unkind. "You want me to lie and say institutions forgive improvisation? They don't."

Marisol rubbed her thumb over the burn scar on her left wrist until the skin warmed. Craving did not arrive as thirst right now. It arrived as velocity, as the urge to do something irreversible so she would not have to sit inside uncertainty another minute.

"Okay," she said. "Then let's not improvise. Show me the boring part that gets me to the next document."

Ada's mouth tipped at one corner. "Now you're speaking my dialect."

She minimized the imaging window and opened the collections database, an old interface with gray fields and tiny type that looked like it had survived three software migrations by refusing to die. Rows of accession numbers populated the screen; half the linked records were blank or tagged "legacy import pending." Ada muttered under her breath in Korean, the tone affectionate and profane at once, then switched back to English.

"This is where the world goes to hide," she said. "Condition records, movement tickets, treatment notes, loan paperwork. In theory it's centralized. In practice, some decades are complete and others are a swamp."

"Can we search initials?"

"Sometimes. Depends what field the preparator used and whether anyone standardized it. Most people did not." Ada typed with quick, economical keystrokes. "Let's start with object history and treatment campaigns around the overpaint window."

Dates and staff initials scrolled by. Some entries were crisp and bureaucratic. Others looked like they had been transcribed from coffee-stained cards by interns with no context. One line read simply: surface disturbance addressed prior to travel. Another: figure adjustment per artist request, see attached. The attachment icon was dead.

"Of course," Marisol said.

"Don't insult dead systems," Ada said. "They always get revenge."

She clicked into a movement tab that mapped where the triptych had gone between exhibitions, restorations, and storage. Most routes stayed inside Brooklyn or loaned to institutions Marisol recognized. Then, in the late sequence just before the first major retrospective, a cluster of entries pointed off-site.

Facility code RH-OS Annex B.

Handling manifest group: RH-MAN/TV-113.

Temporary crate reassignment: TV-FIG CORR.

Marisol leaned in so quickly her shoulder brushed Ada's. "There. Scroll back."

Ada did. "You saw it too."

"TV in the manifest code."

"Could be Tomas Vega," Ada said, cautious by habit. "Could also be treatment variant, transfer verification, technician voucher. The system is inconsistent."

"But it's tied to Red Hook off-site."

"Yes." Ada tapped the screen with her pen cap. "And off-site manifests are not in this database. They were kept on paper until recently. Some are scanned, many are not."

Marisol felt the turn click into place, not certainty, not victory, but a directional force she could stand on. Rumor had been family talk, whispered and denied and wrapped in saints' names. This was a code in an institutional field attached to a specific location and a handling record. Something she could request, not merely accuse.

"Can you print this screen?"

Ada gave her a flat look.

"Right. No copies."

"No copies." Ada slid a blank treatment form toward her. "But you can write every character by hand while I watch. Use block letters so you don't misread yourself later."

Marisol wrote carefully, speaking each segment under her breath as she went.

"RH-OS Annex B," she said. "Handling manifest group RH-MAN slash TV-113. Temporary crate reassignment TV-FIG CORR."

"Add the parent accession and imaging log date," Ada said. "If records staff aren't in a generous mood, metadata scaffolding keeps them from pretending they can't find it."

Marisol added the accession number and today's date, then underlined the code once. Her hand trembled on the downstroke and she paused, pressing the pen tip against the paper until the ink made a dark crater.

"You good?" Ada asked.

"Yeah. Just trying not to sprint."

Ada closed the database and returned to the imaging screen. The hidden figure came back, pale and stubborn under the finished paint. For a moment neither of them spoke.

Then Ada said, "You know what scares me most about this part?"

Marisol glanced over. "What?"

"Not that people lie. Everybody lies about legacy." Ada adjusted the focus ring one click, watching the letters sharpen and soften. "It's that material remembers anyway. You can sand a face. You can repaint a shoulder. You can hang the thing in better light and write a cleaner wall text. But under the right wavelength, it keeps a version you did not approve."

Marisol looked at the erased cheek on the left panel, the gouge marks that caught light like healed cuts. "That's not comforting," she said.

"I didn't offer comfort." Ada capped the pen and set it down with deliberate care. "I offered evidence."

A buzzer sounded from the hall: two short pulses, end-of-slot warning for visitors in restricted spaces.

Ada checked the wall clock. "We've got six minutes. Do you need anything else before I walk you out?"

Marisol thought of asking a question she could not unask: Did Salvador ever mention Tomas by name in here? Did he stand exactly where she stood and watch this figure disappear under newer paint? Did Ada suspect any of this before today? She swallowed all of it.

"One thing," she said. "If I request Red Hook manifests through the formal channel, who blocks it first?"

"Depends who's on duty." Ada stripped off one glove finger by finger. "Records desk can stall you on indexing gaps. Registrar can stall you on staffing. Curatorial can stall you on relevance. Legal can stall everything if they smell defamation."

"So all roads lead to no."

"Not always." Ada folded the gloves and dropped them in hazardous waste. "Some roads lead to maybe if you ask in the right order. Start with records request citing conservation-related provenance inquiry. Don't open with family allegations. If they kick it upstairs, then you loop Nora and make her choose in writing."

Marisol nodded, committing sequence to memory like subway transfers.

Ada picked up Marisol's note sheet, scanned it, and handed it back. "Take this. Keep it off your phone until you're out of the building. Also, eat something before you go home. You get mean when your blood sugar crashes."

"That's your professional assessment?"

"That's my exhausted, underpaid, solvent-poisoned assessment," Ada said, and this time the smile stayed long enough to be real.

They shut down the rig together. Marisol helped wheel the lamp back against the wall. The gesture was small, almost ceremonial, like returning tools to a church closet after a funeral meal.

In the hallway, the air felt heavy after the lab dryness. The guard at the checkpoint had switched radio stations and was now humming along to an old salsa track, off-key and happy about it. He glanced at Marisol's badge and held out the bin for surrender without breaking rhythm.

"Long morning?" he asked.

"Yeah," she said. "Useful, though."

He nodded like he understood exactly the ratio between those things. "Best kind then."

Outside, the late afternoon had gone bruise-colored over Eastern Parkway. Buses hissed at the curb; somebody on a bike shouted at a van; a child in a puffer coat dragged a violin case up the museum steps while her father carried a folding music stand and apologized to no one in particular. The ordinary city kept moving, indifferent to revelations, asking for fare and rent and dinner anyway.

Marisol stood at the edge of the plaza and copied the code again onto the back of her metro receipt, in bigger letters this time, no chance of misreading in bad light.

RH-OS Annex B.
RH-MAN/TV-113.
TV-FIG CORR.

She tucked one copy into her wallet behind her ID and one into the notebook. Redundancy was what people did when they had grown up around stories that changed shape depending on who told them.

The cold reached through her coat at the shoulders. She pulled the zipper to her throat and started toward the bus stop, hearing Ada's sentence in her head with each step: I offered evidence.

Not absolution. Not revenge. Not a finished narrative she could carry home and lay on the kitchen table like a verdict.

Evidence.

It was smaller than truth and heavier to carry.
