# Chapter 14

By the time the B44 finally wheezed to the curb on Church Avenue, Marisol had gone from cold to damp and back to cold again. Slush soaked the edge of her jeans where it licked over her boots, and the paper bag with the clementines had started to sag at one corner. She shifted it to her other hand, felt the hard coin shape of the blue chip in her coat pocket, and looked up at Carina's building like she had to relearn where the front door was.

Carina had texted at seven in the morning.

If you're coming, come by ten. Leo has math and catechism worksheet. Don't be late.

There was no emoji and no sister-softener, just logistics, which for Carina counted as mercy.

A man in a Mets cap let her into the vestibule and held the second door with his shoulder while balancing two laundry baskets. The hallway smelled like bleach over old cooking oil, and then, one floor up, like coffee and candle wax and sofrito that had settled deep into plaster over fifteen years of dinners. Marisol paused outside 3B with her fist lifted, not because she forgot how to knock, but because her body remembered chapter twelve in muscle and skin before her head caught up. She remembered the table with documents spread out, Carina's hand on the suitcase handle, and Leo standing in the doorway in his dinosaur pajamas, crying without sound.

She knocked anyway.

Carina opened fast, as if she had been waiting behind the door and did not want to look like she had been waiting. She had her hair in a tight ponytail and a pencil shoved through the elastic, and a stack of school forms hugged to her chest under one arm, already striped in pink highlighter. The chipped edge of her front tooth flashed when she said, "You're two minutes late. I'll count that as weather."

"Bus got trapped behind a delivery truck at Flatbush and Caton." Marisol held up the clementines like evidence. "And I brought fruit as a peace treaty."

"Leo only trusts fruit if it has stickers on it."

"Then we're doomed."

Carina stepped aside. "Take your boots off. He mopped because apparently he's grown now."

The apartment looked both exactly the same and slightly rearranged against her absence. Prayer candles crowded the corner shelf, half of them burned low, glass filmed with soot. A coat rack she did not remember leaned by the door under the weight of two kid-size puffer jackets and one church tote. Leo sat cross-legged on the living room rug with a green composition notebook open in front of him, tongue pushed against one corner of his mouth while he colored a pigeon with a red halo.

He had one sock on and one bare foot tucked under his leg. His sweatshirt said BOROUGH BIRDS in cracked white letters. A baseball card sleeve full of MetroCard swipes and glossy pigeon feathers lay beside him like scientific equipment.

"Tia Mari," he said without looking up, "you know this one by the laundromat that drags his wing? He stole half a churro from me and then pecked Diego's shoelace. He's still a good pigeon, though."

Marisol set the clementines on the table and crouched beside him. "What's his name?"

"Saint Kevin."

She blinked. "That's very specific."

Leo finally looked at her, solemn as a little judge. "Saint Kevin protects blackbirds and weird birds. I looked it up on the church tablet. This one is weird." He tapped the drawing. "Also criminal."

Carina snorted from the kitchen before she could stop herself. "Wash your hands, both of you. Math first, then religion sheet, then we deal with your bird conspiracy files."

Marisol stood and peeled off her coat. "I didn't know saints were handling pigeon crime now."

"Budget cuts," Carina said. "Everybody's cross-trained."

The laugh that escaped Marisol surprised her with its own force, and for one clean second Carina laughed too, loud and unguarded, head tipped back the way she used to at seventeen when somebody told a dirty joke in the church parking lot and Gloria was not there yet. It rang off the cabinets and made Leo grin because he liked noise that meant nobody was about to fight.

Then Carina caught herself, looked down at the sink, and the room reset.

"Table," she said. "We're already behind."

They worked through a packet of long division at the kitchen table while the radiator clicked and hissed like it had opinions. Leo wrote every number with a dramatic pause before committing pencil to paper. He wore a cheap plastic digital watch with the face cracked at one corner, and every three minutes he lifted his wrist and announced the time as if he were dispatching trains.

"Ten twenty-one."

"Ten twenty-four."

"Ten twenty-eight, but maybe it's lying."

"Clocks don't lie," Carina said, sorting a pile of permission slips by grade and homeroom.

Leo drew an X through a wrong answer hard enough to tear paper. "People set them wrong on purpose, though. That's lying by clock."

Marisol took the pencil before he could drill a hole through the worksheet. "Let's just fix number seven. You borrowed from the wrong column."

"I hate borrowing."

"Everybody hates borrowing."

Carina looked over from the sink where she was peeling potatoes into a metal bowl. "Says the woman with twenty-four hundred dollars in rent arrears."

Marisol felt the heat crawl up her throat before the words arrived. "Good morning to you too."

"I'm not insulting you. I'm saying borrowing sucks for everybody."

"You're excellent at tone, you know that?"

"I'm excellent at paying Con Ed before they shut us off." Carina rinsed the knife, wiped her hands on a dish towel, and set a yellow folder in front of Marisol. "Read Leo's writing prompt with him. I've got to answer six parent emails before noon and all of them end with god bless for some reason."

Marisol opened the folder. The prompt said WHAT MAKES A HERO? in thick photocopier ink. Leo had written three lines already in block letters:

A HERO TELLS THE TRUTH.
A HERO SAVES PEOPLE.
A HERO FEEDS BIRDS EVEN IF THEY BITE.

He tapped the page. "Can I put saints too?"

"If your teacher said heroes, write heroes," Carina called without looking up.

Leo frowned at Marisol instead. "But saints are like extra heroes with candles."

"That's one way to define them," Marisol said.

"Okay, then if a saint lies to save somebody, do they lose sainthood?"

The radiator clicked once, loud as a knuckle cracking. In the living room, somebody upstairs dragged furniture and the scrape came through the ceiling in long strokes. Marisol looked at Carina. Carina kept typing on her phone with both thumbs, too fast for accuracy, then erased whatever she wrote and started again.

"Why are you asking?" Carina said.

"Because Father Tomas said saints tell truth no matter what, and then in social studies Ms. Chong said Harriet Tubman used fake names and tricks, and that's lying but good, so who's right?" Leo looked between them. "And if you lie because grown-ups are scared, does God count scared as a reason?"

Carina stacked the forms, squared the edges against the table, and only then answered. "Finish your sentence first. Then theology."

"That's not theology, that's math with people," Leo muttered.

Marisol swallowed, tasted old coffee and toothpaste and shame all at once. "Write that heroes do hard things when the choices are bad," she said. "Write that sometimes telling the whole truth right away can hurt somebody, and sometimes hiding it hurts them later."

Leo's pencil stopped. "That's not an answer."

"It's the only honest one I've got."

He considered that, then wrote slowly, sounding out each word under his breath. Carina did not look at Marisol, but she stopped tapping her phone.

At eleven forty-five, Carina zipped Leo into his coat and announced the mission list in one breath while pulling reusable bags from under the sink.

"Laundromat drop. Dollar Plus for poster board. Pharmacy for Mami's knee refill. Then food, because I'm not cooking a damn thing tonight."

"I can pay for takeout," Marisol said.

"You can pay for your MetroCard refill and keep your lights on. I've got a coupon." Carina handed her one bag. "You can carry this and not wander off into detective mode."

"I wasn't planning to investigate anybody's frozen peas."

"Good."

The bus stop was three blocks away and the sidewalk had the kind of old snow that turned gray at the edges, packed hard where people had walked and polished slick where nobody had salted. Leo hopped over each dark patch as if lava lived under slush. He narrated pigeon sightings in real time.

"Two on the awning, one limping but still bossy, one with oil rainbow neck."

"That's not oil," Carina said. "That's feather color."

"It looks like gas station puddle."

"Fair point," Marisol said.

The bus app said four minutes, then nine, then fifteen, then Delayed with no number. A woman with a stroller cursed quietly into her scarf. Two teenagers bounced a basketball against the bus shelter ad until the glass shuddered. Carina checked the app every thirty seconds and started reorganizing the contents of her bag purely to have something to control.

"We can walk to the laundromat and catch the next one from there," Marisol said.

"With three bags and him? No. He'll run into traffic because he saw a feather."

"I don't run into traffic," Leo said. "I assess."

Marisol barked a laugh she could not hold in. Carina looked at Leo's serious face, then at Marisol, and a second laugh broke out of her, smaller this time but real. She leaned against the shelter post, shaking her head.

"You hear yourself?" Carina said to Leo. "You're eight years old and talking like an insurance adjuster."

"Ms. Chong says vocab gets you scholarships."

"Ms. Chong's right."

The bus finally arrived so full that people at the back door shouted before it stopped. They squeezed in anyway, Leo tucked between them with his backpack on his chest. Every time the driver braked, the whole bus leaned as one body and then snapped back. The heater blew hot plastic air that smelled like wet wool and coins.

At Snyder the bus stalled in place for six full minutes behind a double-parked van with hazard lights blinking like a shrug. Leo fogged a circle in the window with his breath and drew a pigeon beak on it.

"If saints are honest," he said to the glass, "how come churches still have fake flowers?"

Carina closed her eyes for a beat. "We're not doing this on public transit."

"He asked a design question," Marisol said.

"No, he asked a trap. He got that from you."

"He got that from being awake in this family."

Carina's jaw flexed. She did not answer.

They dropped two loads at the laundromat and walked to Dollar Plus for poster board, glue sticks, and the specific black marker Leo claimed was essential to rendering pigeons with moral complexity. Carina kept moving, checking items against a list folded into quarters and soft at the creases from use. Marisol carried bags and read label prices out loud before anything went into the basket, because that was how Carina shopped when money was tight: no surprises at checkout.

At the pharmacy, Carina tapped her card twice before it went through and told the tech, too bright, "The machine hates me personally." Marisol looked away so the embarrassment could stay private. The receipt printed long and mean. Carina folded it once and slid it into her wallet behind Leo's school ID.

By three, all three of them were tired and salt-streaked and mildly hungry in the way that made everyone mean if nobody ate soon. Carina called Golden Dragon from the curb, one hand over her free ear to hear above traffic.

"Two lunch specials, one kids lo mein, extra rice, no shrimp, yes I know it's after lunch, I'm begging, Frankie." She listened, then rolled her eyes. "No, my sister's not dead. She's carrying groceries."

She hung up and gave Marisol the smallest sideways grin. "He asked if I was ordering enough food for a funeral again."

"That's customer service with memory."

"That's Flatbush."

Back at the apartment, Leo dumped poster board and feathers and crayons across the table and declared he was making a map called PIGEONS OF MORAL CHARACTER. Carina put rice on plates, lined up soy sauce packets like ammunition, and handed Marisol a fork without ceremony.

Cheap lo mein steamed in white clamshells, slick with oil and scallion bits and too much sodium, perfect in that specific exhausted way. The sesame chicken was mostly batter and sugar glaze. Nobody pretended otherwise. They ate standing up for the first five minutes because there was not enough elbow room until Leo shifted his project to one side.

"Don't drip on the board," Carina warned.

"Art needs risk," Leo said.

"Art can risk on newspaper, not on school project."

Marisol chewed and watched them argue about glue density like it mattered to federal policy. The ordinary texture of it pressed against her chest so hard she almost mistook it for pain. It was not strategy or testimony or cross-examination. It was just a Saturday where somebody needed tape and somebody needed dinner and somebody needed an adult to sign page three.

Leo slurped a noodle, frowned at his worksheet, and asked the question again with his mouth half full.

"If somebody lies because they're scared and then keeps lying for years, when do they switch from saint to liar?"

"Chew first," Carina said automatically.

He chewed with exaggerated jaw movements, swallowed, waited.

Marisol wiped sauce from her thumb and bought herself three seconds. "Sometimes there isn't a switch. Sometimes both stay true and that's why everybody's mad all the time."

"That's a terrible system," Leo said.

"It is," Carina and Marisol said together.

They looked at each other and almost smiled again, but this one did not make it all the way out.

After dinner, Carina shoved leftovers into containers for Sunday lunch and started a load of darks in the hallway machine. Leo sat with his math corrections and kicked the table leg in a steady rhythm when he got stuck. Marisol crouched beside him and walked him through regrouping twice, then made him explain it back to her to prove he had it.

When he finished, he leaned his head against her shoulder for exactly four seconds, then popped up as if affection were a race he did not want to lose.

"Can we feed Saint Kevin tomorrow?" he asked Carina.

"If it's not raining and if you wear both gloves."

"The blue glove has bad luck."

"The blue glove keeps your fingers attached."

"Can Tia Mari come?"

Carina kept folding laundry, moving from towel to shirt to socks and then stack. "We'll see."

"That's not yes."

"Correct."

Leo made a face at the injustice of adulthood and went to wash his hands for bedtime. The bathroom fan clicked on with its usual rattle. Water banged through old pipes. Carina kept folding for another minute before she spoke.

"He asked for you every night the first week after you left," she said. She did not look up from the laundry. "Then every other night. Then he stopped asking out loud and started asking if birds remember people."

Marisol felt the words land in her stomach like swallowed metal. "I'm sorry."

"I know."

"I shouldn't have made him watch that fight."

"You and me both." Carina snapped a fitted sheet once, hard, and fed the corners together. "Listen. I'm not doing a reunion montage. We had one okay day. That's all this is."

"I get it."

"Do you? Because here's the boundary list and I need you to hear it with your ears, not your poet heart." Carina finally met her eyes. "You can come Saturdays if you text Friday night and confirm morning of. If you're late more than fifteen minutes, don't come because I build my whole day around timing. You don't talk to Leo about Salvador, court papers, overdose details, paternity rumors, none of it. You don't miss and then swoop back with gifts like that fixes anything. If you drink, even once, you tell me before you step in this apartment. If you lie to me, we're done."

Marisol let the list hit without defending herself. The old reflex to argue definitions twitched and died. "Okay."

"Say the whole thing."

"Okay, Saturdays with confirmation. On time. No case talk with Leo. No disappearing-and-gifts routine. If I drink, I tell you first. If I lie, we're done."

Carina nodded once. "Good."

"Can I ask one thing?"

"Depends what thing."

"If I can't make a Saturday because of work, can I do Sunday morning park instead?"

Carina considered. "Maybe. You ask, I decide based on that week's chaos."

"Fair."

"I didn't say fair. I said maybe."

"That's your love language."

"My love language is paid bills and people where they said they'd be."

The bathroom door opened. Leo came out with wet hair combed flat and one pajama sleeve inside out. "Can I have one clementine?"

"After teeth," Carina said.

He groaned and trudged back toward the sink. Marisol started clearing the table to give her hands something to do. She stacked the catechism worksheet under the math packet, capped markers, snapped glue sticks shut, and went looking for tape because Leo had announced from the bathroom that his moral-character map required reinforcement.

"Junk drawer?" she called.

"Top one by the stove," Carina said, wrestling Leo's inside-out sleeve right side out without gentleness.

Marisol opened the drawer and got the usual archaeology: dead batteries, soy sauce packets, keys to locks long gone, a broken rosary bead, coupons expired last spring, a church bulletin from Advent, three takeout menus, and an envelope banded with a brittle rubber band. She moved it aside for tape and saw the pawn slip beneath, yellow paper gone tea-stained at the folds.

She did not mean to read it. She read it before she could not.

PAWNBROKER RECEIPT
BAY RIDGE COLLATERAL
DATE: 03/18/2019
ITEM: WOMEN'S 14K WEDDING BAND, WHITE/YELLOW GOLD, ENGRAVED T.V./G.V.
ADVANCE: $480.00
CUSTOMER: GLORIA VEGA

The room narrowed to the bright rectangle of paper in her hand. The letters T.V./G.V. looked impossible and absolutely familiar, like somebody had carved a grave into a wedding and then pawned it for bus fare.

Carina saw her face and went still. "Put that down," she said, low.

"What is this?"

"What does it look like?"

"It looks like Mami sold her ring."

"Pawned."

"For four hundred eighty dollars?"

Carina took a breath through her nose and glanced toward the bathroom where Leo hummed through brushing. "Close the drawer."

"Cari."

"Close the drawer first."

Marisol slid the drawer shut with the ticket still in her hand. Carina reached, then stopped, then reached again and took the paper carefully by two corners as if it could tear history if she gripped too hard.

"You were on intake list for Harbor House that week," Carina said. "They wanted a deposit before they held your bed. Insurance was fighting about in-network bullshit. You were shaking so hard you couldn't sign your own name. Mami had ninety-three dollars in checking and thirty in cash from Mrs. Ortiz's overnight shift. She took the ring to Bay Ridge the same afternoon."

Marisol stared at her. "Why didn't anybody tell me?"

"Because you were trying not to die." Carina's voice sharpened, then dropped. "And because Mami weaponizes sacrifice like it's a second language. Both are true."

From the bathroom, Leo yelled, "I brushed for a whole song!"

"Good job!" Carina shouted back, eyes still on Marisol. Softer, she said, "She made sure the intake got paid. Then she reminded us for years that she did it, except she always said she sold her chain, not the ring. I found the ticket when I was looking for tax forms last year."

"Did she get it back?"

Carina laughed once, without humor. "With what money?"

Marisol pictured Gloria's hand at the sink, bare where the ring should have been, and realized she had always explained that absence as practical discomfort, soap under metal, swollen knuckles from arthritis. She had not asked. Or she had asked once and accepted the first answer because there were always louder emergencies to tend.

"She lied about what she pawned," Marisol said.

"Yes."

"And she paid for rehab anyway."

"Yes."

"And then she used that debt as a leash."

"Also yes." Carina rubbed her thumb over the fold line in the ticket until the paper bowed. "Welcome to our mother."

Leo ran out in pajamas, mouth smelling like bubblegum toothpaste, and launched himself at Carina's hip. "Can I have two clementines because I did extra brushing?"

Carina folded the ticket in half, then half again, and palmed it before he could see. "One."

"One and a half."

"One."

"Fine."

He took his clementine to the rug and peeled it with intense concentration, building a spiral of rind like he was laying train track. Marisol watched him pull each segment apart and offer one to the pigeon drawing on his poster board before eating it himself.

"Saint Kevin doesn't eat citrus," he told the page. "He's stubborn."

Carina tucked the ticket into the yellow folder with Leo's school forms and closed it. "You're not taking this with you," she said.

"I wasn't asking to." Marisol paused. "Can I take a picture?"

Carina weighed it. "No posting. No poem lines. No coded captions. No using my kid's homework table as your evidence board."

"I'm not doing that."

"Swear."

"I swear."

Carina handed her the ticket for exactly long enough to photograph it. Marisol framed the shot with shaky fingers, made sure the date and amount were clear, then deleted the accidental blurry first image with the edge of her thumb.

"Done."

Carina took it back and slid it deep into the folder, beneath the parent email printouts and Leo's reading log. "You can think whatever you want about Mami after this," she said. "I just needed you to stop talking like she only ever controlled. Control, yes. Also this."

Marisol nodded because words were not stable yet. In her head, Gloria moved through that day in 2019 in brutal sequence: subway to Bay Ridge with her knees throbbing on stairs, waiting under fluorescent lights while a stranger weighed her wedding band, signing a form in careful English because official paper always made her grammar stiff, then getting back on the train to Harbor House with cash in an envelope and not enough money left for dinner. Marisol had been in withdrawal by then, sweating through two shirts and begging for ice chips like prayer.

She had remembered the ice chips. She had not remembered who paid for them.

Leo yawned so wide his whole face changed shape. Carina checked the microwave clock and exhaled. "Bed in ten."

"Can Tia Mari tuck me?"

Carina looked at Marisol, then at the folder, then back. "One story. Short one. No scary saints."

"I know one about a pigeon," Marisol said.

"Of course you do," Carina muttered.

In Leo's room, glow-in-the-dark stars peeled from the ceiling corners and one roller blind rattled whenever wind hit the window. His bed was pushed against a wall painted years ago with clouds that had faded to a thin blue-gray. He shoved three stuffed animals aside to make space for Marisol to sit.

"Story," he ordered.

"Once there was a pigeon who thought he was in charge of traffic lights," she said. "Every morning he sat on the red light and screamed at cars like they worked for him."

"What was his name?"

"Deputy Kevin."

Leo approved this with a solemn nod.

She made the story short like promised. Deputy Kevin learned nothing, stole half a pretzel, and still got fed by the old lady on the corner because love was not a merit system. Leo fell asleep before the ending anyway, fingers curled around the ear of a stuffed shark with one button eye.

When Marisol came back to the kitchen, Carina had packed leftovers into a container and set it by the door with a plastic fork taped to the lid.

"Take it," she said. "You'll get home hungry."

"I said I'd pay for takeout, at least let me--"

"Don't start. Just take the food." Carina glanced toward Leo's room, lowered her voice. "Text me when you get in. If you don't text, I call."

"Okay."

"And Mari?"

"Yeah?"

Carina rested both palms on the table, weight forward, all business again because business was safer than tenderness. "You don't get to use today to prove a thesis. Not about me, not about Mami, not about yourself. We had a day. Keep having days. That's the work."

Marisol put on her coat slowly, like rushing would crack something fragile. "I can do days."

"We'll see," Carina said, but her voice had softened at the edges.

At the bus stop, night wind came off the avenue carrying fryer grease and snowmelt and somebody's cologne too sweet for winter. The delay board glitched between Arrival and No Data. She stood with the leftovers warm against her palm and looked at the photo of the pawn ticket again, zooming in on the engraved initials until the pixels broke.

The engraved initials, T.V./G.V., filled the screen.

It was a wedding band, pawned for her bed in rehab.

It did not erase the lies. It did not absolve the years of staged sainthood, the rehearsed answers, the way Gloria could turn guilt into harness straps and pull. It also sat there, stubborn as fact, a piece of gold converted into four hundred eighty dollars and one locked unit bed where Marisol had shaken and sweated and not died.

When the bus finally came, she took a window seat over the wheel well and let the vibration climb through her knees. Outside, pigeons packed the lip of a closed bodega awning, feathers puffed against cold, still awake under streetlight glare. Leo would have named each one and assigned moral character by posture.

Her phone buzzed with a text from Carina before the bus cleared the next light.

He asked if pigeons dream. I told him ask you next Saturday.

Marisol typed back with cold thumbs.

Tell him yes. They dream about fries and world domination.

Three dots appeared, vanished, appeared again.

10 a.m. Don't be late.

Marisol put the phone in her pocket, pressed her thumb against the edge of the blue chip until the ridges marked her skin, and watched Flatbush slide by in wet neon bands. The city kept moving without verdict or clean absolution, just routes, receipts, and another Saturday she had to make.
