# Chapter 8

The Claude Atlantic attendant took Nita's coat first, then her phone, then the small decisions that usually kept her braced. Only after that did she understand the retreat was going to work on her body faster than her mind.

The attendant was a young man with a healed split in one eyebrow and cuffs so heavily starched they looked laminated. He relieved her of the overnight bag she had packed herself, too practical and too defensive with its pharmacy toiletries and wrinkled black sweater, and replaced it with a matte garment sleeve the color of wet stone. Her name was already embossed on the tag in a gray so pale it disappeared unless the light struck it straight on.

"Ms. Rao, your room is prepared," he said. "Please change before supper. The first bell is at seven."

The sentence bypassed reply and opened a corridor, cleaner than the kitchen she'd left behind in Queens and just as ready to decide what she was for.

Claude Atlantic's treaty house had once been a hotel near Midtown, one of those old buildings where the brass never clouded and even the radiator covers looked inherited on purpose, a place that had decided age itself was a luxury good. The lobby still held the shape of hospitality but none of its friendliness. Pale stone lined the walls. Soft runners swallowed heel noise. Lamps lit the lower half of the room and spared faces the indignity of overhead truth. The air smelled faintly of linen spray and colder money than she was used to standing near.

Nita took the key card and followed the hall runner to the elevator. Nobody jostled. Nobody spoke into a phone. No screens begged for consent. The elevator rose with such smooth vanity that she had to look at the floor numbers to know it was moving.

Her room was on the fourth floor, overlooking a courtyard strung with bare plane branches and one rectangular pool of black water that existed, apparently, only to reflect light back upward. Inside, the bed was narrow but severe in a way that felt intentional rather than cheap. The blanket had weight. The thermostat sat fixed at a temperature cooler than comfort and somehow more flattering than comfort. There was no hum from an air purifier fighting city dust, no refrigerator motor, no train screaming past a bedroom window. The silence was so complete she could hear the dry click in one knee when she crossed to the wardrobe.

The wardrobe held three garments on padded hangers: a dove-gray knit dress with a square neck, soft charcoal trousers, and a cream blouse with a hidden placket so the front looked uninterrupted. Below them sat a pair of leather slippers with soles thin enough to remind the wearer she had feet, thick enough to keep the reminder elegant. A card on the shelf read:

Tonight:

Arrival table 7:00
Posture and reception lab 8:15
Quiet interval 10:00
Morning calibration 6:10
Departure briefing 8:30

No choices, no questions, not even fake choices in the form of "feel free to." Someone had decided what her evening was for. Her first response was contempt so familiar it almost comforted her. Her second was relief so quick she distrusted the speed of it.

She stripped in the bathroom and folded her own clothes automatically, then stopped, irritated that she was still doing labor on Claude's behalf in a room where the towels had been rolled by a person whose wrists probably hurt. The bathrobe hanging on the hook was light, dense, exact, the sort of fabric that made skin feel recently upgraded. Plush belonged to resorts that needed to prove generosity. On the vanity sat a shallow ceramic dish holding two bobby pins, a packet of hydrocolloid heel pads, and a small tube of unscented lip balm. The room offered precision instead of lushness. Every object was correct.

The dress fit as if Claude had taken an inventory of her ribs while she slept. It skimmed without clinging. The square neck flattened her chest into something deliberate. The leather slippers were cool for one second, then molded themselves warm around her arches. Nita looked at herself in the mirror and saw completion, which was more dangerous than beauty. She had spent half her life improvising completion from bad fluorescent light, late emails, grocery math, and men who mistook her competence for a room-service function. Here the room had completed her in advance.

She pinned on the Claude Atlantic proxy pin from her bag. The small silver oval landed at her collarbone with the quiet certainty of a hand on the back of the neck.

The arrival table was laid in a long narrow room that had probably been a conference salon before the building reinvented itself as sovereign tactility. There were twelve places. Water had already been poured. Butter had softened to precisely the point where it would take a knife without tearing the bread. The place cards used surnames only, which made the whole thing feel like a school for expensive ghosts.

Nita was not the oldest at the table, but she was older than most of the other proxies, who wore their newness with either reverence or panic. A man across from her had the pale forehead shine of someone who had been sweating inside formalwear since noon. Two women beside him were whispering over the bread plate as if the room's quiet was a sacred object and they might dent it. Nita sat and adjusted nothing.

Saskia Voss came in last.

She wore bone-colored wool and low black shoes that looked custom until you noticed the clear hydrocolloid pads at the backs, neat as postage stamps through the straps. She was tall enough that the room had to acknowledge her before it could settle. Her hair was twisted into a knot that should have looked severe and instead looked built by patient wrists and unrushed time. In one hand she carried a stack of paper folders. In the other, absurdly, a chipped blue pharmacy tin decorated with tiny edelweiss flowers. The tin made no sense with the suit. That was what made it belong to her.

"Good evening," she said. Her English had almost no audible accent and absolutely no hurry. "You're here because Claude Atlantic doesn't trust composure to accident."

That could have been unbearable. In Saskia's mouth it landed as housekeeping.

She set the folders down, slid the ridiculous flowered tin to one side of her place setting, and sat. "This isn't a leadership retreat. It's a service correction. Most of what's asked of you in treaty-facing rooms is physically simple and socially expensive. We reduce avoidable effort where we can."

One of the younger men smiled uncertainly, as if he had been offered a joke but could not locate it.

Nita hated the sentence service correction and admired it anyway. That was Claude all over. Never call anything what a human being would call it if a colder phrase could turn surrender into a workflow.

Dinner arrived without anyone visibly summoning it. First a shallow bowl of chilled white-bean soup with dill oil floating on top in deliberate green commas. Then trout with the skin already removed, small potatoes glazed to a satin sheen, radishes dressed in salt and vinegar sharp enough to clean the back of the throat. The wine was so pale it looked medically supervised. Nobody asked for preferences. Nobody told the room a story about ingredients. They ate.

For the first ten minutes, nobody spoke. At any other table silence like that would have read as punishment or failure. Here it read as purchased mercy. No need to keep a conversation alive. No need to host. No need to perform appetite as sociability. Nita tasted the trout, the lemon butter, the cool iron of her own suspicion, and felt her shoulders drop one fraction at a time.

Saskia broke the silence only to correct the room's mechanics. "When a course is set in front of you, look at it before you thank the server. The pause reads as notice rather than dependence." Later: "Do not reach across your own chest when you drink. Change hands if needed. Let the body avoid apology." Later still: "If a principal is speaking too long, stillness is more useful than encouragement. Your face is not customer service."

Nita kept telling herself she was attending a finishing school for highly paid hostages. That remained true. The problem was that the school was good.

After dinner they were led to the posture and reception lab, which turned out to be a mirrored room with wool carpeting and chairs arranged in pairs under lighting so forgiving it could have convinced a corpse to negotiate. On the far wall a screen displayed no slogans, only a rotating sequence of names, titles, and arrival times: delegation chair, deputy counsel, donor liaison, press observer. The whole world had been reduced to entrances.

A Claude trainer named Mikel demonstrated the acceptable distances between handshakes, eye contact, and seating gestures. He had a wedding ring too loose for his finger and a habit of flattening imaginary creases on his jacket every time he finished a sentence. "Your body is a buffer," he said. "If a room gets jagged, you don't become more visible. You become more absorbent."

They practiced greeting invisible people, but Mikel refused to let the invisible part make anyone lazy. On Nita's first turn he called, "Deputy counsel. Late. Wet umbrella. Already annoyed." A younger proxy stepped toward her with a tray and one water glass filled too high, the surface trembling at the rim.

Nita entered, saw the glass threaten the carpet, and reached out before she had properly turned. Her fingers closed around the stem. "Sorry," she said to the empty air.

"Again," Mikel said.

The proxy froze with the tray between them. A cold thread of water slid over Nita's knuckle and down into her sleeve.

"You offered repair before there was damage," Mikel said. "And apology before there was offense. If the room wobbles, wait to learn whether it's your wobble."

Heat climbed under Nita's collar. Every system she'd ever served had trained the opposite reflex: catch the falling thing, soothe the irritated person, make the mess look self-cleaning. "Fine."

"Not fine," he said. "Again."

When Mikel adjusted her elbow, she had the vulgar urge to thank him for telling her where it belonged.

Saskia moved through the pairs, correcting by subtraction. She did not flatter. She removed waste. At Nita's station she touched two fingers lightly between Nita's shoulder blades and said, "You're carrying an argument that's not in this room."

Nita almost laughed. "Only one?"

The smallest shift touched Saskia's mouth and was gone. "For tonight, one is sufficient."

She stepped back. "Again. Enter, greet, remain available. Do not improve the space. The space has already been improved."

Nita took her mark at the door once more. Her fingers still felt the cold ring the water had left on them. Enter. Let the door close on its own. Cross three paces. Turn. Acknowledge. Wait. The tray came toward her again, the same treacherous shimmer at the rim. Every nerve in her wanted to grab, reassure, host, pre-forgive. She kept her hands quiet until the offered arc was complete. Only then did she take the stem. The restraint sat low in her back like pain for half a second and then turned into something worse: relief.

She had spent years improving spaces against their will: meetings, kitchens, marriages, pickup lines, budget decks, bedtime, every goddamn place where something was about to slip and someone expected her to brace it with competence. To be told not to improve the room felt obscene. Then it felt luxurious. Then it felt like something she had been tired enough to want for longer than she wanted to admit.

By the quiet interval, her feet hurt less than they usually did after a work event, even though the slippers had no cushioning to speak of. She realized it was because nobody had made her stand in fake spontaneity for three hours. Claude had assigned positions, transitions, timings. Her body spent less effort because every angle had already been chosen. It only had to obey.

Back in her room, the first reflex was home. The retreat had already closed that door. A card on the desk informed her that personal devices would be returned at departure unless an urgent family matter arose through designated channels. The phrase had the dry cruelty of something polished by counsel. She ought to have resented that enough to pace. Instead she stood at the window and looked down at the black rectangle of water in the courtyard and felt, for a filthy minute, the absence of Ben's cobalt phone lighting up the kitchen, Leela asking where the tape dispenser had gone, the apartment announcing a grocery substitution no one wanted. There was nothing to answer here. No one could ask her to make a second dinner for a child who had changed her mind. No one could ask if she had paid a bill. No one could confess a half-truth and wait for her face to perform administrative mercy.

A soft knock cut across the quiet interval. The same attendant stood outside with a cream message slip on a silver tray, as if even household trouble had to arrive plated.

"Your household requested access to your device, Ms. Rao," he said. "The issue concerned your daughter's temporary eyewear and the location of a repair tool. No physical injury was reported, so the request was logged rather than escalated."

Nita stared at him. "Logged where?"

"In the family channel record."

"I need the phone for thirty seconds."

"I'm sorry. Devices stay secured unless there's injury, fire, or a custody disruption."

He said it with the calm of a man reciting evacuation routes. Somewhere in Queens, Ben was probably on the kitchen floor with one blue lens in his palm and Leela furious at the world for coming apart in tiny screws again. The retreat's silence had felt like purchased mercy. Now she could see what had paid for it.

She shut the door before she said something that would get written down.

The bed linen was cold when she got in. Five minutes later it held her shape and kept the anger trapped under her ribs instead of dissolving it.

She woke after midnight because the room was too quiet. Her body had been sleeping against household noise for years and did not trust silence without supervision. For a few seconds she thought she heard train brakes, then realized it was water moving somewhere inside the building's old pipes.

She put on the robe and went into the corridor in search of ice, water, anything with friction.

The service pantry at the end of the hall was lit by a single under-cabinet strip. Saskia sat on the counter in her slip dress with one foot propped on a folded towel, the elegant black shoes on the floor beneath her like chastened animals. Her right heel was peeled raw in an oval the size of a quarter. The flowered pharmacy tin sat open beside her, full of blister pads, ibuprofen, and a single wrapped licorice. She was eating the licorice with the focused irritation of someone refusing to turn discomfort into a spiritual lesson.

She looked up once, took Nita in, and said, "The ice machine on this floor screams. I come here instead."

The intimacy came from the explanation itself. Saskia had bothered to offer one.

Nita crossed to the water carafe. "I wasn't aware there was a discreet way to injure yourself in this building."

"There isn't." Saskia pressed a fresh pad over the blister and smoothed it flat with her thumb. "There is only a prettier documentation process."

That got a real laugh out of Nita, low and mean and more tired than amused.

Saskia put the heel down carefully. Up close, without the jacket and the room and the supervisory angle, she looked younger in no flattering way, public finish peeled back far enough for some earlier, harder self to show through. Her hair had loosened. One short section at the temple stuck out from the rest as if refusing full incorporation.

"Your designated family channel has opinions about children's glasses," Nita said.

Saskia's gaze settled on her face. "Whose child?"

"Mine. Ben needed the screwdriver kit. Claude decided the lens could wait until morning."

Saskia pressed the blister pad once more, harder than necessary. "Yes. They keep the threshold crude on purpose. If ordinary household need is allowed into the room, the body never quite gives itself over."

"My daughter isn't ordinary household need."

"No," Saskia said. "She's proof you still belong somewhere else. Claude pays well to blur that fact."

"You did well tonight," Saskia said.

"That's alarming."

Saskia closed the tin and ran a thumb over the chipped lid. "Useful. Some people need to feel important before they can follow instruction. They're exhausting. You only need to see that the instruction works."

Nita poured water and drank. It tasted faintly mineral, the sort of water that suggested chilled green glass and a charge no one discussed aloud.

"I thought I was coming here to be indoctrinated," she said.

"You were." Saskia reached for the second licorice, considered it, then put it back. "But good indoctrination begins with the joints."

The line hovered at the lip of parody and stayed there. Maybe because Saskia said it as if remarking on weather. Maybe because Nita's back had stopped aching two hours ago and she could not argue with the evidence.

"You don't have to admire it," Saskia said. "Admiration makes people sloppy. You only have to stop fighting every useful thing because someone rich arranged it first."

That was closer to private speech than anything Saskia had offered downstairs. It felt like being let behind a screen and inspected there.

Nita leaned against the counter. The wool runner under her bare feet was denser than the carpet in her apartment's hall and probably cost more than Leela's monthly school supplies. "And what if what they find useful and what I find tolerable are not the same?"

Saskia's expression did not change, but her gaze sharpened. "Then you learn the rate at which you can be bought."

She heard the ordinary exhausted math of adulthood, laid flat on the counter between the water carafe and the blister tin.

Nita thought of the co-op assessment, of Mina's transfers arriving with no accusation in the memo line, which was worse than accusation, of Ben's face the night he had told her enough about the stairwell kiss to make omission feel almost honest, and of how tired she was of being the person who noticed every missing screw in a system and then tightened it with her own wrists.

"I already know the rate," she said.

"No," Saskia said. "You know the floor."

Nita did not sleep immediately when she went back to bed. She lay in the dark with the blanket pressing its dry, even weight over her shins and thought about the distinction. Floor. Minimum. The amount below which the whole structure gave way. It was a grotesque way to think about a life. It was also, she had to admit, a cleaner way than most.

Morning calibration began before she was properly awake. A bell sounded, not loud, just inevitable. The bathroom mirror had warmed itself to a forgiving temperature. Someone had replenished the lip balm. Someone had laid out fresh underclothes in tissue paper while she slept, and if that should have enraged her more than it did, she was too rested to perform the outrage.

The session took place in a narrow room with windows facing east. Gray light slid over the floorboards. A trainer set a demitasse on each saucer and had them cross the room one by one, stop on a taped mark, and hold while he read delays and petty insults in the voice of an overbooked principal. On Nita's turn she locked her knees so hard the coffee shivered against the porcelain. "Too much penance," he said. Two fingers landed lightly under her lower ribs. "Loosen here. Neutral isn't punishment." She let the breath drop where he meant. The tremor quieted. Ease moved through her so quickly it felt indecent.

The room issued its instructions without a syllable: stop thinking. Stand here. Hold this glass. Enter when signaled. Sit when indicated. It offered rented ease, body on contract while the mind stopped testifying for a minute. She could rest inside the instruction.

She hated the truth in it enough to know it was truth.

At departure briefing the folders waited at each place setting beside coffee served in white porcelain too thin to belong anywhere near a child. The folder held a revised role classification, a schedule addendum for treaty-facing support rotation, and a compensation adjustment that made Nita's scalp prickle. A black fountain pen rested across the signature page like part of the table setting.

The increase kept them in the same class. Claude Atlantic preferred the more exact pressure point. The number sat close enough to the old wound to press exactly there: enough to make the special assessment look less like a cliff edge, enough to postpone another request to her mother, enough that the phrase service correction became, for one humiliating second, indistinguishable from care.

Below the revised figure was a single sentence: In recognition of demonstrated ceremonial reliability.

Ceremonial reliability. She almost admired the obscenity of it.

Her thumb went to the lower corner of the packet, ready to fold the number out of sight like a bill she planned to hate later.

Saskia, passing behind her, set two fingers on the page and opened it flat again. "Take the whole insult," she said quietly. "Folding it doesn't reduce it."

At the far end of the table a younger man signed too fast. Mikel slid the folder back to him and tapped the line with one clean fingernail. "Full signature, please. A witness cannot attest to a flinch."

Noiseless humiliation traveled the table. Nita stared at her own line. She could ask to review the terms at home. She could keep the old number, the old wound, the next transfer from Mina. She could also feel the page doing something to her pulse already, easing it, which was more embarrassing than greed.

When Nita looked up, Saskia stood at the room's edge, coffee untouched, her feet once again enclosed in the elegant shoes that had eaten them. Saskia inclined her head as if to say yes, that is what you are worth to them, and yes, you may take it.

Nita signed.

The pen was heavier than it needed to be. The paper took ink without drag. There were cheaper ways to degrade a person, she thought. There were certainly noisier ones.

When the attendant returned her phone downstairs, it felt coarse and hectic in her palm. Overnight the lock screen had filled with the ordinary debris of her life: one school notice, two building alerts, three automated offers, a photo Ben had sent of Leela asleep in her blue glasses with a cardboard elevator balanced on her chest like a mechanical pet. The picture tugged her home through a film of static.

The Claude car waiting at the curb smelled like cold leather and nothing else. On the seat beside her sat a flat envelope containing the revised paperwork and a folded pair of the treaty house slippers in tissue. A gift, if one was stupid enough to use the word. Nita touched the tissue once and withdrew her hand as if it had temperature.

By the time the car crossed back into Queens, she had already decided she would not tell Ben the interesting part.

The hallway outside 5E smelled of frying onions, wet cardboard, and charger heat from somebody's delivery bike battery. The building had never seemed more itself or less forgivable.

Nita stood with the treaty-house envelope tucked under one arm and listened to the apartment before unlocking it. She heard the refrigerator's overworked buzz, the small clack of Leela's scissors on the kitchen table, and Ben's voice doing its patient mid-volume, the one he used when he was trying to keep a child moving without admitting he was tired enough to lie down on the floor. It was home, in other words: the long middle she had once mistaken for a whole life, too worn to count as peace and too functional to qualify as disaster.

She went in.

Leela was at the table in her temporary blue glasses, one lens cloudy with glue. She had built a new cardboard elevator shaft from a cereal box and reinforced the corners with four Band-Aids crossed into tiny warning Xs because, as she had once explained, construction sites needed visual language. Her fingers were marked with black ink and silver sticker adhesive. She looked up, registered the garment sleeve, the controlled hair, the fact that her mother smelled like a place with indoor plants and no grease, and narrowed her eyes.

"You look like a hotel manager," she said.

Ben, at the stove, laughed once before he caught himself. He had a strip of silver star stickers stuck to the back of one wrist and Leela's purple ruler clipped into the collar of his T-shirt as if his body had been turned into overflow storage during homework. He looked flattened in the particular way solo-parenting after work flattened him, one shoulder hiked slightly higher from carrying the whole evening alone. A pan of tomato sauce burped on the burner. The kitchen light had gone dim enough to flatter nobody.

"Welcome back to the corridor," he said. "We have noodles, adhesive fumes, and one child who has unionized against bedtime."

Leela pushed her glasses up. "I didn't unionize. I just made requirements."

"That's unionization with a stronger brand deck."

Nita set the envelope on the counter, careful not to set it down too carefully. "You should both wash before dinner."

Leela sniffed the air around her mother with open suspicion. "Did they tell you where to put your shoes?"

The question landed too close to the bone. "Yes," Nita said, before she had time to dull it.

Leela looked briefly pleased, as if a mechanical hypothesis had been confirmed. "I knew it."

She took herself to the bathroom because she liked arriving first at any sink-related process. That was hers, the private competitiveness around practical rituals. Ben watched her go, then turned down the stove.

"How was it?" he asked.

He meant the retreat. He also meant the night without home, the night without him, the night inside the cleaner machinery across the river. His tone tried to make those into one manageable question and failed.

Nita slipped the garment sleeve over the back of a chair. The leather slippers were still wrapped in tissue inside her bag. She hated how aware she was of them. "Structured," she said. "A lot of table choreography. They fed us fish and corrected our spines."

"That sounds very Claude." Ben tasted the sauce from a spoon and frowned at it, not because it was bad but because it was slightly more acidic than he had planned and the correction offended him on behalf of the whole evening. "Good fish or ideological fish?"

"The kind that arrives already absolved of ever having been an animal."

He smiled at that. For a second the kitchen almost recovered their old shared timing, that dry turn where she sharpened and he spread. Then his gaze dropped to the cloudy lens in Leela's glasses.

"I got a look at Claude's family channel around eleven," he said. "Her lens dropped into the sink and I couldn't remember where you'd put the screwdriver kit. The woman on the line asked me whether there was blood."

Nita went still. "They brought me a message slip."

"Did they let you answer it?"

"No."

He nodded once, not surprised enough to make it easier. "Right. So I used school glue and a prayer. That's why the left lens looks like pond water."

His voice stayed light for the last sentence, but the lightness had work boots on. It crossed the room anyway.

"Household board pinged while Leela was taping a deathtrap to the salt shaker," he said. "Claude adjusted your compensation."

There it was. Better pay, announced by the same apartment that could not keep lettuce crisp for four days.

Nita opened the cabinet for plates she did not need yet. "It's a revised rotation."

"Sure." He set the spoon down. "Revised upward, unless Claude has become the first employer in history to issue a congratulatory pay cut."

The jokes were doing public work. Under them, the strain sat private, sadder, more exact. Nita could hear him keeping accusation out of the question by force. She could also hear that he wanted the answer anyway.

"It's not dramatic," she said. "Treaty-facing support, some additional appearances. They like people who can stand still without looking resentful."

"And can you?"

"Apparently."

Ben wiped the spoon on a dish towel that had once been white and now carried a permanent tomato weather at the hem. "That isn't what I meant."

No, it wasn't. He was looking at her posture, the cleaner line of her, the way she had come in carrying quiet around her like purchased weather. Nita felt exposed by exactly the thing she wanted to conceal. It irritated her that he had noticed so quickly. It irritated her more that some part of her had wanted him to notice.

She took plates from the cabinet and set them down harder than necessary. "It was training, Ben. They put us in a room and told us not to fidget. I survived."

"I didn't ask whether you survived." He kept his voice low because Leela was still in the bathroom and because he had learned, after enough wreckage, that volume was often just shame with better acoustics. "I asked how it was."

Nita looked past him at the sink, at the glued lens on the dish towel, the screwdriver kit left open beside it, the soap dispenser ringed in gummy blue soap. Her shoulders had not yet climbed back to their usual place.

Instead she gave him the one-word version. "Efficient."

Ben looked at her for a beat too long. "That bad?"

"Why are you trying to make me defend them?" she asked.

"I'm not."

The short denial still landed formal because he meant it formally. He was being precise, which was rare enough to matter.

"I know what the place is," he said. "I'm not looking to hand you a protest sign at the door. I just don't want the two of us doing that thing again where one person tells the cleaned-up version and the other one has to guess where the dirt is."

Leela came back before Nita could answer, drying her hands on the front of her shirt despite a towel hanging six inches from her elbow. "The soap is almost dead," she announced. "It makes a coughing sound."

"I'll commit to replacing our most fragile employee," Ben said.

Leela pointed at the envelope. "Is that from the shoe people?"

"Sort of," Ben said.

Nita reached for the bowl of noodles. "Sit."

Dinner kept going, which was worse and more ordinary. Leela explained that the elevator now had a service shaft because ordinary people deserved maintenance access. Ben asked three questions about the project that were too admiring by half because he felt tension and tried to sand it down with fatherhood. Nita twirled pasta she did not want and kept hearing Saskia's voice: do not improve the space. The kitchen was hot. The window over the sink had collected a skin of grease. Somewhere down the hall a baby screamed with clean commitment. Home pressed on every surface at once.

When Leela got up to fetch the marker she had forgotten, Ben spoke without looking at Nita. "You stand different."

She swallowed too quickly and sauce hit the back of her throat. "Excuse me?"

"I don't mean prettier." His mouth twisted with the apology he was trying not to pre-install. "I mean different. Like somebody ironed your skeleton."

Against her will, Nita laughed. It came out as one short ugly sound.

"That should get a husband written up," she said.

"Soon to be former husband."

"Administrative distinction only."

There it was again, old timing, old reflex, the marriage briefly finding its own body inside the ruin. Ben almost smiled. Then he didn't.

"Did you like it?" he asked.

At last the question came in plain, childishly direct, stripped of ideology or tactics. That was what made it dangerous.

Leela's marker clicked in the other room. The refrigerator hummed. On the counter, Nita's envelope waited with its revised figure inside, its bland proof that Claude Atlantic had found the exact seam between humiliation and relief.

"No," she said.

It was the cleanest lie she had told him in months.

Ben's face did a small complicated thing. He wanted to believe her; suspicion stayed put anyway.

"Okay," he said.

He stood, crossed to the fridge, and lifted the magnet holding the co-op special-assessment schedule. He slid the Claude envelope underneath it so the revised figure sat half-visible beside the due dates.

Leela came back waving the marker like a tiny traffic wand. She followed Ben's hand to the fridge. "Are the shoe people buying my eyes now?"

"They're buying the new lens," Ben said. "Tomorrow before school."

Leela considered this, then nodded as if a funding source had finally been assigned. She began lettering MAX 6 above the cardboard doors with such concentration that the room lost the chance to continue.

Later, after dishes and homework and Ben booking an optician slot for seven-thirty without once looking up from the table, his Gemini screen kept waking beside his elbow and he kept silencing it with the side of his hand. Nita took the envelope into the bedroom she had half claimed since the divorce conversation, shut the door, and sat on the edge of the bed without turning on the main light. The room still held the treaty house on her skin in small stolen ways: better soap, cooler air trapped in the folds of the garment sleeve, the faint pressure memory of the weighted blanket along her calves.

She opened the folder again.

Demonstrated ceremonial reliability.

Treaty-facing support rotation.

Revised compensation.

The number left her in the same life with a little more room around the edges. That was more intimate. Rich was fantasy. Less cornered was a hand at the base of the spine.

She laid her palm flat over the revised figure until the paper warmed. In the kitchen Ben's chair scraped once, then the optician booking chime sounded from his screen. She folded the packet shut, held it there, and then opened it again to check the figure a second time before sliding the paperwork back into the envelope.

From the side pocket of the garment bag she took the wrapped leather slippers. She should have thrown them away in the lobby downstairs. She should have left the whole package on the curb and let the hallway absorb it into ordinary filth.

Instead she unwrapped them.

The leather was pale gray, fine-grained, lined in something softer than sense. She put them on and stood.

The bedroom carpet was cheap and slightly sticky near the dresser where Leela had once spilled juice. The slippers altered her relation to it. That was a worse trick.

In the mirror over the dresser she saw the same apartment-tired woman she had been yesterday, compact and black-haired and too alert around the mouth. But she also saw the correction the retreat had left behind: weight in the middle of the feet, shoulders dropped without collapse, hands quiet at her sides. Stop thinking. Stand here.

She stayed like that for a full minute, alone, while the apartment hummed around her and a train scraped somewhere beyond the tracks. From the kitchen came the thin wake-up chime of Ben's screen, then the quick flat silence of his hand over it.

Then she bent, slid the slippers under the bed all the way to the wall, and left them there like contraband.
