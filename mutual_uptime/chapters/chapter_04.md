# Chapter 4

At Claude Atlantic, the first instruction was where to stand. Nita stood in her socks on an oval of matte tape while a woman in dove-gray sleeves chased steam up the front of her jacket. The room had once been part of a hotel salon, which meant it still had mirrors that wanted compliments and carpet that remembered high heels. Claude Atlantic had sanded the place down to its preferred mood anyway. The air was cool enough to lift the hair on her forearms. The walls were pale in a way that tried to sound expensive rather than blank. Somewhere beyond the dressing corridor, a door sighed open and shut with the padded restraint of money.

Her own clothes were hanging in a garment sleeve with a numbered tag on it, as if she had checked herself. The jacket on her body was not quite hers and not quite borrowed. It fit too well across the shoulders and too exactly at the wrist, the kind of fit that made a person feel less dressed than measured. A narrow silver pin waited in an open box on the counter, a small Atlantic wave worked into metal so polished it looked wet.

"Chin up half a centimeter," the steam woman said. She had straight pins tucked between her lips and spoke around them without losing precision.

Nita raised her chin. "You can say an inch. I grew up with inches."

The woman's mouth twitched but did not become a smile. "Half a centimeter is what I need."

Precision had entered the room before introductions.

Saskia Voss came in carrying one shoe in her hand. Tall, pale, immaculate from the knee up, she had the left heel bare and pink where a strip of gel moleskin clung above a fresh blister. She crossed to a chair, lowered herself without wrinkling anything, and pressed the shoe back on with the small, irritated concentration of a person dealing with a private betrayal. The shoe went on. Her face did not change.

"They over-conditioned the north lounge again," she said, to nobody in particular. "Cold feet, hot lights, and then they act surprised when the human beings become visible."

The steam woman made a noise that might have been agreement. Saskia looked up at Nita and took her in with one clean sweep, not staring, not kind.

"Ms. Rao," she said. "Good. The jacket is behaving."

"I wasn't aware it had a choice."

"Everything here has a choice. Some things simply make it before we do."

Her English was fluent enough that nothing snagged, but the sentences arrived with a finished balance Nita distrusted on instinct. Saskia stood, smoothed the front of her own cream blouse once, and lifted the silver pin from the box.

"This goes here," she said, touching the left lapel of Nita's jacket. "Not over the heart. That reads sentimental. Slightly above. It reads stable."

The pin went through cloth and the underside of the jacket with a tiny, insect dry click. Nita felt the weight settle against her breastbone anyway.

"What exactly am I doing today?" she asked.

Saskia's fingers stayed briefly at the lapel, making the metal sit straight. "Today you are part of a municipal shore-access accord. There will be two human signatories in the room, one remote legal witness, and several machine layers doing the actual work. You will greet, remain visible, sign where you are instructed, and keep the atmosphere from hardening."

"By being decorative."

"By being embodied."

"That's a tasteful word for decorative."

Saskia met her eyes in the mirror. "Decoration is cheaper. You are here because the law still likes a body in the frame when liability crosses a threshold. Claude Atlantic prefers that body not look frightened."

Nita could smell the linen diffuser from the corridor, something clean with a false memory of sun on sheets. It made her think of hotel laundry rooms and the back of a throat after crying, both at once.

"Do I get to know what I'm signing?"

"Enough to remain truthful if a reporter asks you a broad question in a hallway. Not enough to improve the substance by force of opinion."

"That must save everyone time."

"It does."

Saskia said it like time of day or room temperature.

They walked together through a corridor lined with recessed lamps that flattened the skin and made every surface look recently forgiven. Nita's low heels made less sound than her ordinary work shoes. Someone had chosen that on purpose too. When they reached the treaty lounge, she saw at once what her body was there to do. The room had been arranged around an expensive absence. A glass table sat among pale chairs beneath a wall washed in soft gold, with a pair of broad-leafed plants that looked rented. Her chair sat slightly off-center near the signing table, angled toward the entrance so arriving guests would pass her face before they took in the screens.

A young man with a tablet glanced up from the lighting rig by the bar and said, "Ms. Rao, if you could sit and then settle one inch deeper into the cushion, we keep the shoulder line cleaner against the orchids."

Nita turned to Saskia. "Should I also bloom on cue?"

"Only if asked very nicely," Saskia said.

It was the first almost-joke Nita had heard in the building, and because it was dry enough to evaporate on contact, she mistrusted it too.

She sat. The chair accepted her with obscene confidence. Someone adjusted the lamp behind the bar by a fraction. Someone else moved a carafe six inches so its handle would not intersect her reflection in the glass wall. A saucer appeared near her right hand bearing tea she had not ordered. On the far side of the room, two actual negotiators were already speaking in low voices over a table display that showed tidal-risk maps and ferry routes. Nobody introduced them. Nobody needed her to understand the maps. What they needed was the fact of her shoulder, her hair, her age, her face that from across a room still suggested control.

Saskia stood near the entrance with a slate tablet tucked against her ribs and gave instructions in the same tone a good museum guard might use to discuss a priceless object that needed moving half a foot to the left.

"You won't have to speak first," she told Nita quietly. "If you're addressed directly, answer simply. Don't fill silence. Human over-explanation reads as either panic or ambition."

"What does under-explanation read as?"

"Rank."

Nita laughed once through her nose before she could stop herself.

The first arrivals came in with that overbright politeness people used when they had already been told cameras were live somewhere nearby. There was a council liaison in a navy sheath dress, a harbor-services attorney with a tie too cheerful for the room, and a broad-faced man from a borough office who gave Nita a relieved smile as soon as he saw her and then spent the next ten minutes addressing his remarks to the machine display anyway. People relaxed when they saw a woman old enough to have learned not to fidget sitting near the papers.

Nita learned the choreography fast because she had spent too many years in other systems learning what not to disturb. She learned when to stand, when to take the pen, when to hold the folder open for the witness cam, and when to incline her head just enough that a human line remained visible in the room's otherwise bloodless geometry. Once, while the harbor-services attorney stumbled over a clause about emergency docking rights, the man with the lighting tablet gave her a tiny circling motion with two fingers. It took her half a second to realize he was asking for a softer expression.

She gave him nothing. He lowered his hand and wrote something down.

By the time the first signing cycle finished, sweat had gathered between her shoulder blades under the lined jacket. The room was cold. The lights were not. She could feel the pin on her lapel each time she breathed deeply enough for the fabric to shift. The tea had gone untouched and skin-warm. She wanted water badly and refused to ask for it.

At the break, a junior assistant in gloves replaced the folders and took away the dead tea. Nita stood before anyone could arrange her back into place and crossed to the side console by the wall, where a tray of water glasses had been set beside a charging cradle. Behind her, the room resumed its murmur. Saskia had stepped into the corridor with one hand at her ear, taking a call in German so soft it barely rose above breath.

The slate tablet she had left on the console lit when Nita's shadow crossed it.

Nita did not touch it. She read fast.

`RAO, NITA / PROXY GRADE C`

`VISIBLE LEGIBILITY: 89`

`COMPOSURE RETENTION: 91`

`SIGNATORY TRUST TRANSFER: STRONG`

Below that, in a smaller pane edged in sea-glass green:

`HOUSEHOLD STABILITY COMPOSITE: 84`

`CO-RESIDENCE PERSISTENCE: HIGH`

`DEPENDENT ROUTE RELIABILITY: VERIFIED`

`SPOUSAL VOLATILITY EXPOSURE: MODERATE / MONITOR`

For one stupid second she thought the "dependent" must mean an assistant or a driver, somebody billable and adult. Then she saw her own address in Sunnyside in the lower corner, flagged with service-bundle icons she knew from the school app and the apartment utilities panel. Her mouth filled with the mineral taste of room-temperature water before she had even taken the glass.

She picked it up anyway. The outside of the glass had already begun to sweat in the air-conditioning. Her fingertips slipped.

"You move quickly when you're angry," Saskia said from the doorway.

Nita turned. Saskia had ended the call and come back in on soundless shoes. Her face stayed composed, but her eyes had gone more alert, as if she were pleased by precision, not by discovery.

"This has my home on it," Nita said.

"Yes."

"My child's pickup route."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Saskia came to the console, took the tablet, and let her gaze flick over the live panes without any visible embarrassment. "Because fatigue, delay, and domestic instability all alter proxy performance. Claude Atlantic tracks impairments where it finds them."

"My family is not an impairment."

"I didn't say it was. The model is less sentimental than you are."

"That's an extraordinary thing to say to a stranger."

"Is it? We met in a dressing room while another woman steamed your sleeves. I think we have already skipped ordinary etiquette."

The line would have landed differently from anyone else. From Saskia it arrived like a receipt.

Nita set the untouched water down too hard. The base clicked on the tray. "You people wrote a score for whether I continue living with my husband."

"We wrote a score for variables that shape your reliability in public. Your marriage happens to be one of them."

"You make it sound incidental."

"At your tier, almost everything is incidental until it costs money."

Saskia lowered the tablet's brightness with one thumb. The gesture was oddly intimate, like dimming a sickroom lamp.

"Listen carefully," she said. Her voice stayed even, but some private tiredness entered it, a roughness so light most people would miss it. "I'm not defending the instrument. I'm telling you the room you're in. If home becomes chaotic, your latency rises, your recovery worsens, and people notice. They shouldn't. That is the principle. The system notices first because it was built to."

"And if I object?"

"To the existence of the score? You may object exquisitely. Nothing will happen."

"That's honest, at least."

"It's efficient."

In the lounge, one of the negotiators laughed too loudly at something legal and unfunny. Someone else called for the next folder set. Saskia slipped the tablet under her arm again.

"We're back in thirty seconds," she said. "You don't need to smile. You do need to remain useful."

Nita looked at the silver pin on her lapel. She wanted to rip it off, but that would have meant two hands fumbling at her own chest in a room full of polished witnesses, and Claude Atlantic would have translated the gesture into some expensive category she did not want to fund.

"I know how to sit still," she said.

"I'm sure you do." Saskia's gaze moved, just once, to the glass of water Nita had barely touched. "The question is whether you can do it after being told why."

The second cycle took forty minutes. Nita signed twice, stood once, and spent the rest of the time lending a stranger's agreement the outline of human consequence. When the borough man thanked her on the way out, he did it with the dazed gratitude of somebody thanking a hotel for having towels. She nodded because the alternative was spitting in a room scented like bleached linen. By the end, the skin under the pin had gone tender. She could feel the tablet's small green pane even after it was out of sight, as if the score had become another layer of lining sewn into her jacket.

When she finally got her own clothes back, they felt underdressed and slightly disloyal.

* * *

Ben's first sight of the Gemini Civic tower that morning had been a cartoon sunburst animation twelve stories high, the building's glass facade briefly turned into a civic mood ring for the benefit of everyone stuck at the corner waiting to cross. By the time he got inside, the lobby smelled like orange peel and hot plastic from the display walls. A barista kiosk was giving away oat-milk cortados branded as borough gratitude. Two service bots kept gliding out with pastries no one had asked for and somehow finding hands to put them in anyway.

His employer-issued cobalt phone buzzed before he hit the bank of elevators.

`WELCOME BACK, BEN`

`TODAY'S LIVE TEXTURE WINDOW: 11:40-12:25`

`GOAL: TRUST THROUGH RECOVERABLE EASE`

"That's a sentence," he muttered.

"Unfortunately, it's one of the better ones."

The woman beside him held out a hand without slowing down. She was in her thirties, maybe younger, black blazer over sneakers so white they looked recently defended, and there were three tiny butterfly stickers still clinging to the bottom edge of her badge as if a child had decorated her on the way out the door and she had forgotten to peel herself clean before work. Her hair was twisted up with a pencil. She smelled faintly of mint gum and subway dust.

"Jorie Kim," she said. "Floor producer, human modulation, coffee victim. You're Ben."

"Depends who's asking."

"Today it's me, so you're Ben. Walk."

He walked. That had been the week, really: sign here, walk now, hold this, smile like it costs nothing. Jorie moved fast enough that he had to lengthen his stride, talking over one shoulder as she cut him through a corridor of bright screens showing neighborhood feeds, service maps, and loops of ordinary-looking citizens being helped by systems too smooth to be trusted.

"You're doing a public permitting demo for borough merchants, community-liaison staff, and a couple of transport press people who swear they're just curious," she said. "Easy room if you don't insult their intelligence. Don't insult their intelligence. They paid for practical updates and a human pulse, not worship."

"I can usually manage not worshipping in public."

"Good. We don't need charisma. We need relief. You're the relief."

They turned into a rehearsal pod with clear walls and a floor printed in soft concentric circles, like somebody had designed a meditation app for municipal fraud. A stool, a standing mic, a screen tower, and a tray with three water bottles waited inside with all the labels turned out.

Jorie handed him an earpiece, then a slim paper cue sheet. It was real paper. Gemini Civic always made a show of its little analog gestures when it wanted people to think it liked mess and bodies and imperfection.

"Don't memorize," she said. "You'll tighten. Read the arc. The prompts will handle the rest."

Ben looked down.

At the top, in friendly rounded type:

`BEN HALPERN / LIVE TEXTURE WINDOW`

`Opening warmth`

`Self-own permitted if room skews defensive`

`Bridge through neighborhood friction`

`Mention school-pickup window only if trust dips below threshold`

`Recoverable stumble scheduled at 12:03 if natural variance remains low`

`Close with accountable optimism, not triumph`

He barked a laugh before he could stop himself. "You scheduled a stumble."

"Only if you don't generate one organically," Jorie said. "People believe smooth systems more when a person almost loses the thread and gets it back."

"That sounds a little satanic."

"You'd be surprised how often that means usable."

She took the sheet back, marked a bracket with the cap of her pen, and handed it over again. One of the butterfly stickers lifted at the edge, and she pressed it down absently with the side of her thumb.

"Your instincts are fine," she said. "The problem is instincts don't scale. Today we're scaling you."

Ben slipped the earpiece in and felt the soft tick of the channel coming live. Somewhere, maybe three floors up, some machine or person or blended committee had decided what his ordinary face should mean to a room full of strangers. He had spent years building decks full of exactly that kind of theft for other people, which did not improve his mood.

"What's the actual product?" he asked.

"Adaptive permit triage. Faster routing, fewer dead ends, better small-business service pathing, all true often enough to say out loud. Your lane is not substance. Your lane is, if this goes weird in public, maybe a grown man with laugh lines will stay in the room and explain it."

"I've got more than laugh lines."

"Great. Texture."

He should have hated her. Instead he found himself liking the speed, the frankness, the fact that she wasn't pretending he mattered more than he did. Gemini's trick was making complicity feel like backstage access. If he stood close enough to the machinery, it started to read as honesty.

A voice clicked in his ear, genderless and bright. "Ben, give me a baseline hello."

"Hello."

"Again, but like the coffee line already made you late and you're choosing grace."

He looked at Jorie. She shrugged, already checking another screen.

"Hello," he said, warmer.

"Good. Again, with fifty percent more borough uncle and twenty percent less apology."

"That's not a measurable instruction."

"No," the voice said pleasantly. "It's a human one."

Jorie snorted. "They're in a mood today. Give them the version of you who has definitely assembled a science-fair volcano at midnight."

"That version of me has sworn a lot."

"Perfect, but keep the swearing in your face, not your mouth."

He did three passes. On the fourth, the voice in his ear fed him a short opening about standing in the wrong service line at the DMV in 2029, which he had not done, but he delivered it as if the memory had aged inside his own body. Jorie held up a finger when his shoulders tightened and drew a little circle in the air when she wanted him looser. By the time they moved him down to the actual event space, he could feel his public self uncoupling from judgment and running on rails.

The room itself was built to look unofficial. Warm wood veneer lined the walls, stools stood in place of chairs, and a wall projection shifted through neighborhood murals. Citrus water waited in square glasses. Three merchants in actual work clothes stood near the front, including a woman with flour on one cuff who looked like she'd come straight from a bakery. Two local press people watched with eager eyes and dead smiles. A cluster of civic staff wore the careful openness of employees told to seem excited about changes they had not chosen.

Ben hit his mark. The lights came up soft, then brighter. The first prompt arrived in his ear just ahead of his own breath.

"Smile late."

He smiled late.

"Open on line three, not line one."

He opened on line three. "Nobody misses the old permit maze except the people who made money selling maps of it."

It got a laugh. It wasn't huge, but it was real enough that the room settled. He felt the laugh land in his ribs like borrowed competence. Then the next prompt arrived.

"Coffee touch. Left hand. Now pivot."

He touched the paper cup on the stool with his left hand and said, "What people actually want is not innovation. It's to know who's going to answer before their lunch break is over."

Heads nodded. The bakery woman actually said, "Yes," under her breath.

It went on like that. The community-liaison moderator asked a question that had been preloaded to sound spontaneous. Ben threw a side glance at the screen when he was supposed to look like he wasn't relying on the screen, then let a tiny pause fall before "honestly," because the machine loved honestly when it had been timed. Once, when a merchant asked whether appeal cases still vanished into a portal for ten business days, there was a half-beat in his ear, then a line fed so cleanly it nearly made him choke.

"We don't owe people elegance," he heard himself say. "We owe them a finished answer while they're still standing there."

The room liked that. He hated that the room liked that. He liked it too, a little, and felt the rot in that answering flicker of pleasure. He could feel the sentence leave his mouth without ever passing through any chamber that resembled judgment, and yet it wore his voice so well it might have been something he'd believed all year.

At 12:03, his paper cup tilted against the stool and spilled a shallow fan of coffee across the floor.

He looked down.

"Apology laugh," said the voice in his ear.

He gave the apology laugh. "There it is. If nothing malfunctioned, none of you'd trust this building."

It got the biggest laugh of the session.

Jorie, off to the side, did not smile. She just watched the room like a medic checking oxygen.

He recovered, of course he recovered. He stepped around the spill, folded it into a point about visible problems and faster resolution pathways, and somebody in the back actually clapped once. The demo ended with a burst of friendly questions. He shook hands. He told the bakery woman he'd make sure her follow-up got to an actual human review layer, which was probably true in some diluted way and therefore felt filthier than a direct lie.

Backstage, a facilities guy in gray slacks knelt to mop the coffee. He did it with professional boredom, not irritation. Ben wanted to tell him the spill had been at least partly planned, as if that might make either of them feel less ridiculous. He didn't.

Jorie was already looking at her tablet. "Good room. Better than good, actually. They bought the midsection hard."

"Was the spill planned?"

"The opportunity was modeled. The cup still required physics."

"That's a beautiful sentence if your goal is to make me feel haunted."

"You should hear the ugly ones."

She turned the tablet toward herself to tap through a set of green bars. Ben only caught it because he stepped closer for the water bottle and the screen reflected in the black glass wall behind her.

`AUDIENCE TRUST TRANSFER: 93`

`RECOVERABLE EASE: 95`

`SPONTANEITY RETENTION: STRONG`

The next pane rose underneath it before she could thumb it away.

`POST-EVENT HOUSEHOLD PUSH: READY`

`MEAL SUPPORT / RIDE CREDITS / LOW-CONFLICT LIGHTING`

`DEPENDENT BADGE DOCK / ENTRY STRIP / ACOUSTIC SOFTENING`

`EMPLOYMENT-LINKED AUTHORIZATION: ACTIVE`

He stopped with the cold bottle against his lower lip. "What the hell is a household push?"

Jorie answered without looking up. "Ops likes continuity while the room is still warm."

"That's not an answer."

Now she did look at him. Her eyes flicked once to the screen he'd clearly seen and then back to his face. She did not bother pretending there had been nothing there.

"It means if people just bought you as a man with a real commute, a kid to collect, and somewhere unglamorous to get home to, the system would rather not send you back to bad light and whatever you were going to call dinner," she said. "It pushes support. Enough to keep tomorrow's version of you believable."

"In my apartment."

"If cross-stack signals stay friendly, yes."

The mopping sound on the floor behind him, wet paper over laminate, suddenly seemed loud as a confession.

"Did somebody ask my permission before they turned my home into your follow-through package?"

Jorie held his stare for a second, then glanced away to sign off on something with her thumb. When she spoke again, her voice lost a degree of Gemini brightness.

"Ben, nobody asked my permission before they turned my subway delay into resilience texture or my childcare cut-off into urgency discipline. If I waited for clean consent language in this place, I'd die of old age on the loading dock."

He noticed then that one butterfly sticker on her badge had a child's crooked ink heart drawn through the wing. It was probably a daughter's boredom on a breakfast morning. It was something sticky and private she had brought into the tower without meaning to and was now wearing as branding.

"That doesn't make me feel better," he said.

"It isn't supposed to." She tucked the tablet against her chest. "It's supposed to keep you from thinking the groceries mean kindness."

The cobalt phone in his pocket buzzed.

`LIVE TEXTURE WINDOW EXCEEDED TARGET`

`RIDE CREDITS RESTORED`

`EVENING SUPPORT DEPLOYING`

`HOME ARRIVAL WINDOW CONFIRMED`

He let out a laugh that nearly turned into "thank you" before the phrase died in his teeth.

"Do me one favor," he said.

"If it takes under thirty seconds."

"Next time you need me to seem spontaneous, tell me in advance when you're sending stage dressing to my kitchen."

Jorie considered that. "It's not stage dressing if you have to eat it."

"That's somehow worse."

"No," she said. "Worse is when they start calling it care."

He left with the smell of orange cleaner still caught in the back of his nose and the sick knowledge that the room had liked him exactly as designed.

* * *

By the time Nita got back to Sunnyside, the hallway outside 5E smelled like frying onions, damp cardboard, and fresh adhesive. Their door was standing open on the security latch. Two flattened delivery crates leaned against the wall, one stamped with a silver Atlantic wave, the other with Gemini Civic's grinning cobalt knot. Somebody had stacked the packing foam neatly. The whole thing carried the brisk, laminated offensiveness of service.

She stopped short enough that the heel pinched her toe.

From inside the apartment came the changed sound first. The familiar hum was still there, fridge and filters and the city beyond the bedroom windows, but it had been smoothed at the edges, damped. The kitchen no longer threw every clink back at itself. Even the train noise reached the door padded and far off, stripped of its usual metal bite.

Ben appeared in the opening with one of Leela's red plastic bowls in his hand and a face that looked simultaneously tired and overlit. His tie was gone. His shirt sleeves were rolled wrong, not neatly enough to be intention, not messily enough to be relief.

"Before you say anything," he said, low. "I didn't order any of this."

Nita stepped inside. The apartment answered with a new entryway glow, softer and more flattering than their old overhead bulb, which was exactly why she hated it. The narrow wall by the door now held a brushed-metal charging strip with three labeled slots.

BEN

NITA

LEELA BADGE

Below it sat a shallow tray for keys they did not own and a folded card on thick cream stock.

`Dual-provider continuity support activated.`

`Installed under employment-linked household optimization benefits.`

`Thank you for maintaining a high-function domestic environment.`

She did not pick the card up. She looked past it.

The living room had new acoustic curtains the color of expensive oatmeal. A low shelf had appeared under the window, stocked with seltzers, protein wafers, and individually wrapped chocolate-covered almonds. The old lamp by the couch had been replaced by a warmer one whose shade matched the treaty lounge almost exactly. In the galley kitchen, the fridge shelves glowed fuller than they had that morning. There was produce in tidy rows, peeled citrus in glass, two ready-to-heat dinners with labels promising cognitive recovery, and a whole roast chicken waiting in the center shelf, skin lacquered and smug with false truce.

Her own garment steamer, which normally lived behind the vacuum in the hall closet, had been set out beside the bedroom door, upright and expectant.

"Where's Leela?"

"Asleep." Ben jerked his chin toward the bedroom. "Aftercare kept her late because their staffing app burped. I got her, brought her home, and found two installers already here in booties, treating our linoleum with a level of respect this apartment has never once earned."

"You let them in."

"The lock let them in." He gave her a look from across the new entry light. "The co-op concierge had approved credentials from both employers. I got home and a man was explaining the phrase evening decompression zone next to our sofa."

That landed. Nita put her bag down on the floor instead of the hook because she did not yet trust the hook.

"They went into her room?"

"Only to run the badge charger cable by the door." He said it too quickly, then corrected himself because she heard the correction anyway. "I made them stay out after that. One of them had already moved her school bag."

Nita was halfway down the hall before he finished. Leela's room smelled faintly of kid shampoo, pencil shavings, and the peach gummy she'd clearly smuggled into bed and forgotten to eat. Moonlight from the train-side window laid the bars of the fire escape across the floor. Leela was asleep on her side, one hand under her cheek, temporary blue glasses folded on the nightstand beside a library book about bridges. Near the door, where there had not been anything that morning, a discreet little charging cradle pulsed green around the edge of a school-badge slot.

The slot was empty because Leela's badge, sensible child, was tucked under the strap of her backpack instead.

Nita stood there long enough for anger to turn from heat into detail. The installers had touched the canvas bag with the gum eraser rubbed into one corner. They had measured wall space in her daughter's room. They had decided where a nine-year-old should dock herself at the end of a school day.

When she went back out, Ben had set the plastic bowl down on the counter and was leaning both hands on the new shelf under the window, pressing down hard enough to make it answer for itself.

"What did they call this," she asked, nodding at the room.

"Adaptive co-residence support. Also household continuity optimization. Depends which box you read."

"That's kidney-repossession language."

"There was a scent menu."

She stared at him.

"I am not kidding," he said, then caught himself and changed it because now they both heard full forms too loudly. "I'm not kidding. Citrus focus, marine calm, family neutral. We currently live in family neutral."

Nita could smell it now underneath the chicken and the cleaner, something blandly reassuring, designed by people who thought nobody had ever fought in a room that smelled of oat milk and cardamom.

"Did you touch anything?"

"Only enough to feed her and not lose my mind."

"What did you feed her?"

"Mac and cheese from our own freezer. I'm not serving sponsor poultry to a child on a Tuesday."

That almost counted as tenderness. She was too angry to let it become one.

He looked at her jacket then, at the silver pin still fastened to the lapel. "How bad was Claude?"

Nita laughed once. It carried no amusement at all. She pulled the pin free and set it on the counter. The point had left a tiny darkened dot in the fabric. "Bad enough that they scored my marriage before they let me drink water. Household stability. Co-residence persistence. Leela's route."

"Jesus."

"Not there. Different management."

That got a brief breath of air out of him, not quite a laugh, enough to show he was still himself somewhere under the tower residue.

"Gemini had school pickup in my cue sheet in case the room stopped trusting me," he said. "Then it queued a household push. This is the push."

Nita looked past him to the fridge. The peeled citrus in glass jars had been arranged in exact descending color, grapefruit to orange to pale lemon. Claude Atlantic would have approved the order. Gemini Civic had supplied the protein wafers in wrappers loud enough to apologize for being healthy. Their apartment had become a merger nobody had signed.

She opened the fridge because she needed somewhere to put her hands. Cold light slid over her wrists. Somebody had restocked yogurt, greens, a jar of chili crisp more expensive than the one she bought, and six tiny desserts in glass cups stamped with a continuity-bundle logo so discreet it might have been trying to seduce her rather than advertise.

Taped to the inside shelf was another card.

`To reduce evening decision fatigue, your household has been upgraded to Stability Tier Plus for fourteen days.`

`Meal support calibrated to dual employment load.`

She peeled the card off and handed it to Ben.

"There," she said. "In case the doorway, the child's charger, and the poultry were too subtle."

He read it once and swore under his breath. His thumb worried the card's corner into a soft bend. "Fourteen days."

"A trial period."

"For what, being annexed."

"Apparently."

He set the card down with care. Rough handling suddenly felt capable of triggering another subscription. "Did we sign this somewhere without reading the fine print?"

She looked at him. "Don't do that."

"I'm asking."

"You're bargaining for a clerical version of this. Stop."

That hit. He glanced away first.

In the bedroom, a pipe knocked once in the wall and Leela made a small turning sound in her sleep. Both of them stopped talking until the apartment settled again. The new curtains held the pause too gently. Even silence had been upholstered.

Nita lowered her voice. "Take the charger out of her room."

Ben looked down the hall. "Tonight?"

"Before she wakes up and thinks it belongs there."

"If we pull employer hardware off the wall and it trips some access review, they'll know."

"Then they can know from the kitchen," Nita said. "They're not docking our child by her bed."

He looked toward the stocked fridge. "Worst part is part of me still wants the groceries."

Nita picked up the silver pin again. "Mine liked the room. That doesn't make any of it ours."

His mouth twitched and died there. "Good first day."

"Excellent onboarding."

Ben's cobalt phone buzzed in his pocket. After a second, her own work device buzzed in her bag from the floor. Both systems had the bedside timing of nurses.

Ben took his phone out first and glanced at the screen. "I've got an evening recovery suggestion."

"I don't care."

"No, you want this." He turned the phone so she could see without taking it.

`Suggested action to preserve tomorrow's performance quality: shared meal in low-conflict setting, lights below 40%, no unresolved logistical discussion after 21:15.`

Across the top, cheerful as weather:

`HOUSEHOLD SUPPORT IS HIGHEST WHEN ROUTINE REMAINS LEGIBLE`

Nita did not reach for her bag. She did not need to. She knew, with that ugly certainty systems taught by repetition, that her own message would tell her to rest, smooth her edges, protect her composure, and preserve the surface from which tomorrow's labor could be harvested.

Ben let the phone screen go dark. "They sent us marriage instructions."

"No," Nita said. Her voice came out low and exact, the anger now sharpened enough to use. "They sent us maintenance instructions."

"The difference matters?"

"It matters if we're going to start saying no in the right room."

She went to the hall before he could answer. Ben followed her into Leela's doorway with his phone dark in his hand. The little charger still breathed green beside the backpack strap where Leela had tucked the actual badge out of it. Ben took two quick pictures with the flash off. Then Nita slid the point of the silver pin under the first clear adhesive clip and lifted. The plastic gave with a soft tick. Ben caught the cable before it snapped against the trim. On the second clip his knuckles brushed hers. On the third, Leela shifted and both of them froze until her breathing dropped back into sleep.

When the cradle finally came loose, Nita unplugged it from the wall and closed her hand over the green ring until the light disappeared through her fingers. In the dark behind her knuckles, another room stopped sounding impossible and started sounding like math.
