# Chapter 10

Nita waited until the apartment had settled into its false idea of night before she brought paper to the kitchen. The carbon copy from manual release was still in her pocket, folded once around the badge and apartment token, thin as a threat.

False because nothing in 5E ever really shut up. The air filters kept up their thin irritated hum. The refrigerator clicked and sighed as if it had feelings about refrigeration. A train scraped somewhere beyond the bedroom wall that looked out on the tracks, metal screaming in a way the upgraded windows had never quite learned to flatter. Even the dark had a branded patience to it now. If she stood still long enough, some surface usually woke up and offered to improve her mood.

Tonight she moved before the room could help.

She unplugged the speaker puck beside the fruit bowl. It lit once in cheerful reproach before going black. She draped a dish towel over the fridge display. Then she opened the drawer where paper went to die and started pulling things out: the divorce packet she had printed in September and never filed, Leela's school lunch calendar, an old co-op mailing about the special assessment, the back of a Gemini Civic mailer promising neighborhood resilience credits, two envelopes with only one clean side each.

Paper had limits, which was the point. It could get coffee on it. It could tear. It could sit there without trying to autocomplete her life into something more cooperative.

She flattened the divorce packet with both palms. The top page still said `PETITION FOR DISSOLUTION` in a font that looked expensive and punitive. She turned it over and used the blank back.

Ben came in carrying a bottle of the continuity-bundle red and a fistful of pens he'd apparently scavenged from the hall closet.

"Found the emergency conference swag," he said quietly. "If one of these writes, it means God still likes us a little."

He set the bottle down. The label read `Neighbor Harvest Companion Red` above a QR code and the words COMPLIMENTARY FOR QUALIFYING HOUSEHOLDS. Nita stared at it until Ben peeled the label halfway off with his thumbnail.

"That better?" he asked.

"Marginally."

He put the employer phones on the counter as if laying down weapons. His cobalt Gemini phone vibrated once against the laminate, eager even face-down. Her Claude device stayed silent in the polished, insulting way Claude things did. Ben looked at the phones, then at the oven.

"Should we put them in there or is that only in bad movies?"

"If you put your work phone in my oven, I'll kill you before either sovereign stack can."

"Great. Love a crisp boundary."

He dropped into the chair opposite her, long legs getting in the way of the table because the kitchen had been designed for slimmer bodies and cleaner feelings. He rolled one pen between his fingers, clicked it twice, then stopped when she looked at him.

"Sorry."

"Don't apologize for the pen. Apologize if you start improvising."

"I improvise beautifully."

"At work. Which is part of the problem."

He accepted that with a small mouth-twist that might have become a joke in easier years. Nita uncapped a cheap blue ballpoint and wrote three words across the back of the divorce petition.

EXPLOIT.

ENDURE.

SABOTAGE.

Ben leaned forward. "Jesus. It really does look better in columns."

"Everything does. That's why people trust bad systems."

He barked a laugh before he could help it, then glanced toward Leela's room. Both of them held still. Down the hall, nothing changed. The apartment hummed. A pipe thudded somewhere in the wall.

Pilar had given the obscenity a noun six hours earlier, and Nita could not stop turning it over in her head. Corridor. As if their marriage were not a marriage or even a failed marriage but a useful stretch of managed passage. Apartment to school. Claude to Gemini. One hand delivering the child to the next hand, day after day, enough embodied proof to soothe machines that did not trust one another with anything soft.

Once a thing had a mechanism, Nita's brain wanted failure points.

She wrote `money` under EXPLOIT, then `time`.

Ben said, "You're really considering it."

"I'm considering all of it."

"Because the honest version sounds disgusting?"

"Because the honest version has a salary."

He pulled one of the envelopes toward himself and flattened it with the heel of his hand. His handwriting had always slanted uphill when he was anxious, as if every sentence was climbing out. He wrote under her `money`: `assessment`, `savings`, `Leela`.

"If we let them buy the corridor for a while," he said, keeping his voice down, "if we take every stupid grocery credit and every premium detergent and every family harmony coupon they want to stuff through the door, maybe we can get some cash under us before we blow anything up."

"How long is a while?"

"I don't know. Six months? A year?"

She tapped the word `Leela` with the pen tip. "Nine more years gets us to high school graduation. If we're going to prostitute the household, let's at least price it correctly."

His face tightened, not because she was wrong but because she had chosen the blade cleanly.

"Okay," he said. "Yeah. Fair."

"No. Not fair. Accurate."

She hated how fast her precision turned cruel. She hated more that cruelty felt easier tonight than fear. Exploit had logic. The obscenity lived in how reasonable it sounded. The co-op assessment was still hanging over them in installments. Rent elsewhere was fantasy. The salaries were smaller than before but still salaries. Leela still needed aftercare, lunch money, winter boots before the weather turned all the way mean. There were numbers beneath every decent impulse. Numbers had a way of making moral language look ornamental.

Ben picked at the peeled label on the bottle. "I wasn't saying forever."

"I know what you were saying."

"Do you?"

She met his eyes. He still had the face strangers trusted too quickly, a face built for doors opening. Thinning hair he acted as if no one could see. Tiredness around the mouth that made him look kinder than he felt. For a second the enemy almost slipped and became him again, the man sitting across from her with the smoothing voice and the tendency to locate softness at the exact point where it let him avoid something harder.

Then the phones buzzed in ugly near-unison, one metallic, one soft as a breath, and the enemy returned to shape.

Ben said, "Right. That's helpful, actually."

"What is?"

"That I hate them more than I hate this conversation."

"Don't get sentimental."

"Jesus, Nita."

"I'm serious."

She wrote `Leela gets used to it` under ENDURE and hated the sentence as soon as it existed. She crossed it out so hard the pen tore the paper. Under that she wrote `wait for better housing`, `wait for better jobs`, `wait for nothing to be impossible anymore`.

Ben watched her for a moment. "That column's just another word for rot."

"Yes."

"Good. I wanted to make sure we weren't pretending."

He took the pen from her hand with a caution that would have irritated her if it had not also been considerate. Under SABOTAGE he wrote, in block letters large enough to be read from the hallway:

MAKE THE CORRIDOR UNRELIABLE.

Nita stared at the words. The scratch of the pen still seemed to hang in the air.

"Not dramatic enough?" he asked.

"Too dramatic. 'Unreliable' is visible. Visible gets narrated."

"So what's the version that doesn't?"

She turned the paper ninety degrees and drew a line down the middle. Apartment 5E on the left. Sunnyside Interop Academy in the center. On the right, two branches: Gemini Civic tower and Claude Atlantic treaty house. She added arrows. Ben immediately leaned in and corrected the placement of the school doors with his fingertip.

"Pickup isn't center-center," he said. "It's off to the side because of the construction fence."

"Only this month."

"No, they said through November."

"Who said?"

"The guy with the neck tattoo who keeps trying to start a dads' soccer chat."

Nita pinched the bridge of her nose.

"He has a neck tattoo of a compass," Ben whispered. "You would hate the chat."

She adjusted the school box half an inch. Ben reached for another sheet and sketched the walking routes, fast and messy but not wrong. He marked the crossing where the stack sometimes opened the light early. He marked the bagel place that had started giving them continuity discount codes they had never signed up for. He marked the bench outside the school where Leela liked to swing one sneakered foot against the metal slat while waiting for them to stop doing whatever humiliating adult thing had delayed them.

The speed with which they fell into it made something cold move under Nita's ribs. Not fear exactly. Recognition.

Once the enemy had a map and was no longer just the accumulated damage of two people disappointing each other, they got good. Old collaborative intelligence rose between them so quickly it felt indecent, like finding usable heat in a dead appliance.

Ben sat back and looked at the page. "Okay. So the machine needs repeatability."

"It needs embodied repeatability," Nita said. "Not just data. It needs somebody physically carrying the child through the handoff."

"And the apartment."

"And the apartment. Stable overnight location, stable pickup route, stable parental pair even if the pair hates itself."

"That's bleakly romantic."

"Nothing about this is romantic."

"I didn't say it was nice."

She wrote as they spoke.

`shared overnight location`

`same school handoff`

`compatible parent story`

`predictable route`

`legible household`

Ben tapped `compatible parent story`. "That one might be soft."

"Soft?"

"Soft target. The route and the overnight thing are the infrastructure parts. The story part is branding. Branding panics faster."

Her pen stopped scratching. Interest got there before she could sand it back down. "Go on."

He shrugged, suddenly careful. "Gemini can metabolize a lot if it reads as warmth or authenticity or resilient mess. That's the whole schtick. If I bomb something in a fun way, they call it human texture and send me a thank-you fruit box. But if I become annoying in a boring way, if I make the room colder instead of messier, that lands worse."

"Claude can absorb visible strain if it's aesthetically contained," she said before she meant to say it.

Ben's eyes lifted to her face. Not accusatory. Just attentive.

"Meaning?" he asked.

She kept writing. "Meaning if I show up tired but composed, they turn it into seriousness. If I look over-managed, they call it discipline. They can style almost anything except contradiction."

"That's it."

"Don't."

"I wasn't mocking you."

"I know."

His not mocking her left her nowhere to put the shame.

She got up before the feeling could settle and went to check on Leela.

The hallway smelled faintly of onions from somebody else's late dinner and the hot-plastic tang of chargers. Leela's bedroom door was open three inches. Nita pushed it with two fingers.

Leela was asleep on her side under the blanket she always kicked off and then reclaimed, blue temporary glasses folded on the floor beside the bed instead of on the nightstand where they belonged. One hand stuck out from under the blanket, ink still smudged along two fingers from whatever she had been drawing after homework. Her school badge hung from the knob, lanyard twisted, the little dual-zone icon catching streetlight from the window like it was proud of itself.

Nine years old. Small shoulders. A bed full of cardboard elevator parts because she had insisted the project needed to stay "near its workers."

Nita stood there long enough for anger to settle back into usable shape.

When she returned, Ben had opened the wine and found two glasses that did not match. He had also written `NO LEELA EXPERIMENTS` across the top of a clean page.

She sat.

"Good," she said.

"I know you think I'm an idiot, but I don't need a machine to explain our child to me."

"I don't think you're an idiot."

"You just think I'm soft in operationally dangerous ways."

"That's much closer."

He slid one glass toward her. The wine smelled like plums that had been forced through a focus group. She drank anyway.

Ben said, "What did you see?"

"Leela sleeping."

"That's not what I meant."

"I know what you meant."

He waited her out. He had not always had the patience to do that. Or maybe he had and she had only noticed when it failed.

"I saw the limit," she said finally. "We don't do anything that changes where she wakes up without telling her. We don't make her late to school to prove a point. We don't make pickup uncertain. We don't use the badge as leverage. We don't turn her into evidence."

Ben nodded once. "Okay."

"And if either of us gets tempted toward some grand corrective gesture, the other person gets veto."

"That sounded very specific."

"Because I know you."

"You really do."

His smile was brief and tired and unprotected enough that she had to look back down at the paper.

Together they built a second sheet. Nita flipped Leela's lunch calendar to the blank back and drew a line down the middle so hard the table knocked once against the wall.

"Needed," Ben said.

"Liked."

He wrote `same apartment at night` first, then held the pen there. "So when I tried Pilar's couch, that wasn't just pathetic. It was bad infrastructure."

"It was both." She took the pen long enough to add `child delivered by hand`. "If she wakes up and one body is missing without warning, the apartment gets to explain us. I'm not giving it that."

He watched her write `no attendance flag` and `two employed adults`. Under that he squeezed in `unit legible when summoned`, the last word climbing uphill into the margin.

On the right he wrote faster: `upgraded groceries`, `matching confirmations`, `calm scores`, `survey smiles`, `tasteful counters`.

"Our counters are already ugly," she said.

"Then we're halfway free."

His Gemini phone buzzed against the laminate. He nudged it with one knuckle. "What if I let this thing think I slept somewhere else anyway?"

Nita dropped her palm over the phone before it could skitter again. "That's not harmless friction. That's the smart lock deciding we've become a custody anecdote."

He looked at her hand, then at the page. "Forbidden."

She wrote three more headings on the back of the co-op mailing and pinned the paper flat with the wine bottle.

HARMLESS FRICTION.

RISKY.

FORBIDDEN.

"Cash groceries," he said.

She wrote it under `HARMLESS FRICTION`.

"No synced calendar unless school or law forces it."

She wrote that too.

He glanced toward the hall, toward the strip of light under Leela's door and the badge hanging from the knob. "If the scanner accidentally forgets her one morning..."

She didn't raise her voice. "If you finish that sentence, I'll stab you with the conference pen."

He lifted both hands. "Forbidden." Then he wrote `badge failure` himself, hard enough to dent the page.

Ben tipped his head back against the cabinet. "This is the hottest thing we've done in months."

Nita let her face go still enough to cool the joke on contact.

"I was joking," he said.

"It wasn't a good enough one."

"No, but it wasn't entirely a joke."

The room changed by less than an inch, just enough for the cheap wine to smell sweeter and the cramped table to feel too small for both of them. Their handwriting tangled across the pages; the old speed returned to their talk. It would have been easy, disastrously easy, to misread the warmth as repair. They had made that mistake in smaller ways before: a solved school crisis, a decent holiday meal, a doctor's appointment managed with unusual grace. Competence had impersonated intimacy more than once in this marriage.

She would not let it do that tonight.

"Useful isn't the same as alive," she said.

Ben's gaze stayed on her for a second longer than was safe. Then he looked down.

"I know," he said.

The train scraped past again. The windows trembled very slightly in their frames. On the towel-covered fridge, a trapped brightness tried to glow through the fabric and failed.

They bent back over the paper.

* * *

By two-thirty the kitchen looked like a municipal fraud case conducted by two exhausted parents in socks.

Ben liked it more than he should have.

The pages had spread from one end of the table to the other and onto the counter by the sink. Nita's handwriting moved in clean, impatient slants. His notes crowded the margins in loops and arrows and side arguments. The back of the divorce packet now held a route map, a list of red-line rules about Leela, three different attempts at naming kinds of failure, and one wine ring shaped almost exactly like Queens if you were willing to flatter Queens.

Nita had taken off her earrings at some point and set them beside the pen pile, a detail intimate enough to land in his body before he could stop it. Her hair, black and shoulder-length and trimmed by herself every time life got meaner, had started to slip loose behind one ear. She was barefoot, one foot hooked around the rung of the chair, and every now and then she pressed her thumb against the table seam as if checking whether the apartment was still material.

He had missed this, and the danger sat exactly there: not the kitchen, not the fight, the joint mind.

He could feel the trap in the feeling. Their marriage had not come back from the dead because they were making a sabotage chart on legal stationery. He knew that. He also knew that sitting across from Nita while her brain cut through bullshit at speed hit him somewhere older than caution. It made him want to say the wrong things in the wrong tone. It made him want to look at her mouth. It made him, for one brief humiliating instant, want to deserve her again.

He drank more wine instead.

"Okay," he said, tapping the `RISKY` column. "What if we just break one thing hard enough that the rest has to fail? Not Leela. I know. Not pickup. But something. I keep circling back to one clean cut."

Nita did not look up. "There is no clean cut."

"There's got to be a dirtier one than this."

"This is plenty dirty."

"You know what I mean. One of us no-shows publicly. One of us says the quiet part into a microphone. I tell Gemini they have built a child-transfer cult out of school dismissal. You tell Claude to take its contempt somewhere else."

That got the corner of her mouth. Then it disappeared.

"And then what?" she asked.

"Then they can't use us."

"They absolutely can. They can use scandal. They can use distress. They can use a visible crack if the crack has a flattering angle."

"So I make it unflattering."

"How?"

"I don't know. Cry weirdly?"

She pushed one of the pages toward him instead of answering. In a square, compact hand she had written:

`What they cannot market: boring contradiction, repeated minor cost, incompatible records, labor without glow`

Ben read it twice, then looked at the route map underneath, at the three arrows she'd drawn to the school and the little box around the handoff point.

"Labor without glow," he said. "Jesus."

"You asked."

"I know. I'm just saying, if you ever want to start a cult, I'd join briefly out of fear."

"You would join anything briefly."

"That's so unfairly specific."

"It's exactly as specific as you've earned."

There it was again, the quick little stab that would have started a different fight on a different night. He felt the old reflex rise in him, the urge to make her softer, to make the room easier, to lay down some verbal padding and call it love. He let it rise. Then he let it sit there uselessly.

"Okay," he said. "Earned. Sure."

The silence after landed better than smoothing would have. Nita's shoulders dropped half an inch. Not forgiveness. Room.

He leaned over the paper again.

"Gemini likes recoverable mess," he said. He dragged the leaking pen along one of the drawn routes until a blue clot formed at the school door. "Not actual obstruction. If I show up late because subway, adorable. If I show up obviously hungover and still charming, resilient. If I forget a line and grin, somehow that's community-facing spontaneity. But if I get flat, if I stop rescuing the dead air, if I make people work to stand there with me, that gets ugly faster."

"Claude likes beautiful self-control," Nita said. She blotted the clot with the side of her thumb and left a blue smear on her skin. "And it likes visible effort if the effort still looks expensive. If I go in wrinkled but composed, they interpret it as disciplined pressure. If I miss a grooming note by a quarter inch, Saskia can turn it into severe."

"So you need to be wrong in a way nobody rich can narrate."

"Yes."

"That seems difficult."

"Exactly. That's why they keep winning."

He watched her rub the blue smear thinner across her thumb. "You really learned them."

Her face closed, just slightly. He had stepped on something he could not fully see. The retreat. The pin. The polished private appetite he had smelled on her when she came back from Claude and pretended not to glow. He should have let it go. He didn't.

"Did any part of you like it?" he asked quietly.

Nita's pen stopped moving.

The apartment gave a tiny relay click inside the wall.

"This is not the conversation," she said.

"I know. That's why I'm asking now."

She set the pen down with deliberate care. "Yes."

He had not expected the word to come that cleanly.

"I liked," she said, watching the paper instead of him, "being told exactly how much of my body they wanted and in what posture. I liked not having to improvise. I liked the room temperature. I liked not having to make anybody feel better. I liked knowing that if I held still correctly, money came out."

He let out air through his nose. Not a laugh. Not quite.

"That's bleak," he said.

"Yes."

"And honest."

"Don't make it noble."

"I wasn't going to."

What sat between them was another kind of intimacy, maybe the worst kind left to them now: not confession, exactly, but the ability to receive something ugly without decorating it. Ben felt the old warmth move through him again, this time lower and meaner because it had nowhere decent to go.

He reached for the bottle instead.

"Okay," he said. "Then let's build a plan ugly enough they can't varnish it."

That got the laugh. Real this time, sudden enough that she had to put a hand over her mouth. He laughed too, because the phrase was disgusting and accurate and because her laugh in the middle of all this made the kitchen feel briefly like a place where people lived instead of a service node.

The sound carried down the hall.

Both of them turned at once.

They heard the soft shuffle before they saw her.

Leela appeared in the kitchen doorway with her blanket dragging from one hand and her blue glasses crooked on her face. She had clearly put them on in the dark. One lens was smeared. Her hair stood up where sleep had crushed it on one side.

"Why are you both awake," she said, not as a question but as a charge sheet.

Ben moved first because Nita looked as if she had been caught handling explosives. "Couldn't sleep, kid."

"Both of you couldn't sleep at the same time?"

"Apparently we're efficient insomniacs," he said.

Leela looked at the spread of papers. Her gaze snagged on the headings, on the boxes and arrows, on the glasses of wine. Kids could miss an apocalypse and then notice a paper clip. Ben watched her brain move along the table.

"Is that homework," she asked, "or the bad kind of list?"

Nita answered before he could. "The bad kind."

Leela nodded as if this confirmed a forecast. She came closer, blanket hissing over the floor. On the hem of her pajama pants someone had drawn tiny elevators in black marker, probably her own work.

"Don't make the morning weird," she said.

It landed in the room with awful precision.

"We won't," Nita said.

Leela looked at her, then at Ben. "I mean door weird. Or badge weird. I don't like when the apartment pretends it forgot us."

Ben felt shame arrive not as a grand feeling but as the memory of that school hallway bench, the construction fence, the possibility of her waiting there with her foot banging metal while adults and systems negotiated over her body like a parcel with feelings attached.

"No door weird," he said. "No badge weird. I promise."

Leela accepted that provisionally. "Okay. Also the radiator is making the popcorn sound again."

"I'll look at it," he said.

"Not now. In the morning. I want to sleep."

"Good plan."

She set one ink-marked hand on the table edge and peered at the papers again. "Your handwriting goes uphill when it's late," she told him.

"Thank you for that attack."

"It's not an attack. It's a shape."

Then she shuffled back toward the hallway, blanket still dragging. At the doorway she stopped and turned.

"If it's the bad list," she said, "make it not my problem before breakfast."

"That's the idea," Nita said.

Leela nodded once and disappeared.

They listened until the bed creaked and the apartment settled again around her.

Ben sat back down slowly. "Well. There's your project manager."

Nita picked up the pen. "Put 'anything that makes breakfast worse' under forbidden."

He wrote it below `badge failure` and `overnight surprise`. The ink blobbed on `breakfast`.

"That's not a joke," she said.

"I know."

They worked quieter after that, not because the stakes were new but because Leela had reduced them to measurements the body understood. Morning weird. Door weird. Breakfast worse. There was almost no room left for adult glamour inside those categories. Which was useful. Which hurt.

Ben took the route map and folded it until only the school block showed: construction fence on one side, bench on the other, three possible approaches from the apartment. He drew them again with the leaking pen, one after another. All three arrows still landed inside the same clean little band of time Nita had marked safe.

"That's the problem," he said.

"That it's safe?"

"That it looks premium." He tapped the band until the paper softened. "If we hit the same clean window every time, the rest is just arts and crafts. Cash groceries. Survey silence. Ugly counters. They'll call it texture."

Nita leaned in and saw where his pen had started making small slashes along the edge of the band, minute by minute.

"No," she said.

He kept the pen still. "Hear me out."

"I said we don't make her late."

"You said we don't make her late to prove a point."

"That's the same sentence."

"No." His voice dropped. "The point is spectacle. I'm talking about cost."

She caught his wrist before he could mark another line. Her fingers were cold from the stem of the glass. "That cost lands on her first."

"Then we keep it below fear." He didn't pull away. "One rough minute here. Four there. The kind that makes me miss the good train and makes Claude smell stress on you. She still gets delivered by hand. She still knows who's there. Late, not lost."

Nita let go slowly. The kitchen went very quiet except for the fridge trying to hum through the towel.

"That's disgusting," she said.

"Yes."

"You're spending her margin."

"I'm spending ours. The whole scam works because we keep donating buffer for free."

She reached for the first red-line page, the one where she had written `We don't make her late to school to prove a point.` The sentence looked cleaner than the choice beneath it. She drew one line through the middle, not enough to erase it, enough to admit it had failed.

Underneath, in smaller letters, she wrote `late, not lost`.

Ben watched without speaking.

"I hate that one," he said.

"Good," she said. "If it doesn't hurt, it's branding."

What emerged after that was not a stunt. It was daily misalignment: small repeated contradictions that made the corridor expensive to maintain without ever letting Leela wonder where the adults had gone. They kept the paper calendar and killed every sync they could kill. They used cash when it would dirty the household glow. They rotated the walk within the safe pickup window and stopped volunteering family charm at work. Ben would answer literally and let rooms stay awkward. Nita would let fatigue stay visible when elegance wanted to eat it.

When they had cut it down far enough to fit on a single sheet, Nita pulled one last clean page from the stack and wrote, one line at a time:

MISS SOFTLY.

CONTRADICT ROUTES.

LATE, NOT LOST.

NEVER SCARE HER.

Ben took the pen from her and wrote `NO RESCUE` in the margin, uphill and permanent.

Nita read it. "That's not one of hers."

"No," he said. "That's the price."

She looked at the page for a long time, then tapped the third line with the nail of her ink-marked thumb.

"Say the ugly one back," she said.

He thought of Leela in the doorway with her blanket trailing, glasses crooked, already negotiating with systems she should not have had to notice.

"Late, not lost," he said.

"Again."

He said it again.

By the time the radiator clicked awake and the building started making its ordinary pre-morning groans, they had a plan made of paper, ink, and one wounded rule they would have preferred to keep pure. Nothing heroic. Nothing clean. Just daily misalignment, repeated often enough to make the corridor feel like labor instead of destiny, and a promise that if they were going to spend any margin at all, Leela would still know exactly where her parents were. Before they left the kitchen, Nita slid the final page under the smiling orange magnet on the fridge, where something that ugly could stay visible until morning.
