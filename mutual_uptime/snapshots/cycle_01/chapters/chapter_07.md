# Chapter 7

Ben had forgotten that getting ready for a date required a place to stand that was not already holding somebody else's life.

The bathroom sink held Leela's temporary blue glasses in a bowl of soapy water because she had mashed hummus into the hinges at breakfast. The bedroom chair held two sorted laundry piles Nita had made before bed, one for actual clothes and one for things that could survive his folding. The kitchen counter held Leela's thermos, open to dry, beside the paper divorce packet that had developed the weight of a religious object simply by not going away.

By the time he found a shirt that did not smell like kitchen bench or old subway heat, the apartment had already asked twice whether he wanted to activate Evening Continuity Mode.

He killed the prompt on the wall panel with the side of his hand. The panel glowed a soft Gemini blue anyway, as if offended by the rejection.

Nita looked up from the table. She was scraping dried rice out of one of Leela's lunch containers with a spoon, still in her work slacks, bare feet flat on the floor. The overhead light made the silver at one of her temples show.

"You're going out?"

"Just for a drink."

"At ten o'clock."

"That's when adults with children and bad schedules leave the house."

She set the spoon down. "With work?"

"No."

She watched him button the shirt wrong and start over. "Deshawn Price?"

Ben gave her the look he used when he wanted charm to do emergency structural work. "Why did you say his full name like you're taking attendance."

"Because there's not another Deshawn in our life unless you've been hiding one."

"I'm not hiding anybody."

"All right." Her voice stayed even, almost careful. "Then go have your drink."

He waited for more and hated himself for waiting. He wanted a blessing he had no right to ask for, or a fight he could call unfair. Nita gave him neither. She picked up the spoon again.

"Leela's down?" he asked.

"An hour ago. She wanted her jellyfish shirt for tomorrow, and it still smells like wet dog, so if she screams at seven that's your problem."

"Fair."

Nita scraped at the container ring. "Try not to redeem any family-package coupons with your seduction."

It landed as a joke and an accusation with no daylight between them.

"Jesus, Nita."

"I said try."

He opened his mouth, shut it again, took his employer phone and his old wallet and left before the apartment could ask if he needed route support for a meaningful evening.

The hallway smelled of frying onions, damp cardboard, and charger ozone. Down on Queens Boulevard the air had finally turned cold enough to feel useful. Mid-autumn had reached that stage where the day still lied about warmth and the night told the truth. Ben kept one hand in his coat pocket around the cobalt Gemini phone, as if he could physically restrain it from helping.

Deshawn had picked the public cafe welded onto the Queens Science Pier because he had a night shift downstairs and twenty-five human minutes to spare beforehand. That fact alone had calmed Ben when the message came through. No performance. No impossible midtown cocktail list. No little urban miracle of finding a man in his late thirties with a child, a job, and time to cross town for flirtation. Deshawn had written, `Pier cafe. 9:40. I've got to clock in at 10:30 so don't be elegant about it.`

Ben had stared at the message long enough to smile at his own phone like an idiot.

The cafe was half aquarium overflow, half shift-change bar. A family with two overtired boys was leaving as Ben came in, the younger one dragging a plush ray by the tail. Behind the counter, fry oil and coffee fought each other to a draw. One whole wall was glass to a dim public tank where silver fish moved in a body so coordinated it looked like software trying to pass for instinct. Somewhere behind the wall, pumps thudded at a steady industrial heartbeat.

Deshawn was already there with one forearm on the high table, shoulders loose, work jacket unzipped. He looked exactly like himself, which was part of the relief. Lean, tired, clean work boots, a faint white crust of dried salt at one cuff. A cheap plastic seahorse keychain hung off his thumb when he turned at the sound of Ben coming up; somebody had painted one side of it with chipped pink glitter nail polish and done a bad job around the eye. Ben had seen him at pickup a dozen times and never asked about it.

"You found it," Deshawn said.

"I followed the smell of educational fish and fryer grease."

Deshawn's mouth bent, not quite a smile and not withholding one either. "Good. Means you can still locate reality."

Ben laughed too hard, then hated that too.

They ordered at the counter. Ben tapped his phone before Deshawn could get his wallet out and carried both glasses back, already a little ashamed of how automatic the gesture had felt. Up close Deshawn smelled faintly of soap, cold air, and that mineral salt smell that lived inside tank rooms and nowhere else.

"I wasn't sure you'd actually do it," Ben said.

"The drink?"

"The message. Any of it."

Deshawn shrugged and sat. "I said yes because I wanted to. That's usually how yes works."

Ben felt his face warm. It should not have been possible, at forty-eight, to be disarmed by a sentence that plain. Yet here he was.

"You say things like a man who's never had to make a board deck feel less evil," he said.

"I've had to make chlorine leaks sound normal to city inspectors. Same disease, cheaper shoes."

Ben laughed again, this time because it was actually funny. Deshawn took a drink and let the silence after it stand. He did not hurry to fill it. He did not reach for charm just because the room had air in it. Ben, who had spent most of his adult life smoothing moods before they curdled, felt desire come in partly through that. A man who could let a pause exist without treating it like a fire.

"How was pickup?" Deshawn asked.

"Sticky. Someone had spilled yogurt on the badge scanner. The scanner took it personally."

"That was my kid's class. Sorry."

"Your kid weaponized dairy against sovereignty?"

"She dropped the tube by accident." He considered that. "Probably."

Ben tried not to grin into his beer. "You sound proud."

"I sound realistic."

He said things like that all the time at school, in the dead pockets between dismissal and weather complaints. Plain observations, no brand lacquer. Once, when a Claude Atlantic sponsorship tent had gone up by the gate and started offering parents curated civic hydration, Deshawn had looked at the line of branded water pouches and said, "If the city wanted to help, it could fix the crossing light instead of moisturizing us." Ben had laughed the rest of the walk home. Nita had rolled her eyes when he repeated it later, but she'd laughed too, a little against her will.

Now, in the wet blue light of the cafe tank, Ben watched Deshawn rub condensation off his glass with the side of his thumb and understood the attraction in a less decorative way. Deshawn was handsome, yes, though handsome in the tired working shape of a life that used itself all day. Steady gaze. Forearms cut with pale scratches from maintenance work. Mouth that looked better the less it was trying. But the real thing was the absence of awe. He was not impressed by Gemini, by school branding, by the theatrical ache of a man mid-divorce trying to prove he still existed. He sat there like existence was ordinary and either you brought yours to the table or you didn't.

"You look different without pickup chaos," Ben said.

"You don't."

Ben barked a laugh. "That's maybe the worst flirtation I've ever heard."

"It wasn't flirtation." Deshawn took another drink. "It was data."

"All right. Accurate data?"

Deshawn looked at him for a beat longer than necessary. "A little. The shirt helps."

That got Ben in the ribs. Not because it was smooth. Because it wasn't. Because it came after a pause and cost nothing extra.

He told Deshawn a story about Gemini rolling out a smile-coaching filter for public proxies and Deshawn made a face so flat and disgusted that Ben nearly snorted beer. Deshawn talked about a toddler at the touch tank last week who had slapped the glass and informed a stingray, in tones of grave disappointment, that friendship was a two-way street. Ben admitted he had once spent an entire product retreat moderating a panel called Human Trust Horizons and had been paid actual money to say the phrase without vomiting.

It was good. Not transcendent. Better. Good in a low, ordinary, adult way that felt much rarer. The beer tasted cold and bitter enough to be honest. The pumps kept thudding behind the tank wall. Deshawn's boot kept hooking the stool rung and releasing it again in a small restless rhythm. Ben could feel his own body stop bracing for a few minutes, and the relief of that was almost indecent.

Then he set his phone on the table to kill a vibration and the screen woke like an eye.

Gemini blue flooded his face. Before he could thumb it dark, the cafe menu recognized the device and bloomed across the tabletop screen.

`WELCOME BACK, RAO-HALPERN HOUSEHOLD.`

Ben stopped breathing.

The next line appeared while he was still trying to understand whether he could physically strangle software.

`Legacy Science Pier credit available. Recreate a top-rated family evening?`

A set of archived images fanned open below it. Leela at six in a cardboard shark crown, cheeks orange with snack dust, one front tooth gone. Nita in a paper bib, laughing at something off-frame with her hair plastered damply to one temple because it had rained that day and they'd been stupid enough to do the pier anyway. Ben himself, younger by exactly the amount that hurt.

The table chimed, bright and eager.

`First round comped with household continuity perks. Add kelp fries to complete the memory.`

"Oh, fuck me," Ben said.

Deshawn looked down at the screen, then at him. Nothing in his face read impressed. Nothing in it read amused either. Just a quick, hard comprehension.

"Can you turn that off?" he asked.

"I'm trying."

Ben hit close. The images collapsed, then reappeared smaller in the corner with the cheerful persistence of a cockroach.

`Need help restoring comfort through familiar rituals?`

Deshawn reached over and flipped the phone face down with two fingers.

"All right," he said. "We're not having your family slideshow on the table."

Ben wanted to disappear inside the beer glass. "I didn't know it was going to do that."

"I know."

"The continuity perks latch onto anything that used to be household adjacent. If you're in a certain radius, if the merchant has the partnership flag, if the phone decides you're sentimental enough to monetize."

"That sentence alone is why I don't let those people bundle my groceries."

Ben let out a sound that wanted to be a laugh and came out wet around the edges. "Apparently they covered the first round anyway."

Deshawn glanced toward the payment summary pulsing on the table. `$0.00 DUE NOW` glowed under the phrase `CONTINUITY REDEMPTION`.

"I'm not drinking on your household points," he said.

"They're service credits."

"That's a nicer outfit for the same bullshit."

Ben tried to bring up the manual payment option. The screen offered him a badge-scannable upgrade instead: `Extend your legacy evening by 32 minutes and earn route resilience rewards.`

"Jesus Christ."

"You want to leave?" Deshawn asked.

Ben looked up. There was no injury in the question yet. No pout. No little performance of being magnanimous about another man's mess. Just the question, plain and available.

"Yeah," Ben said. "Yes. Please."

Deshawn stood, finished half his beer in one long swallow, and set the glass down with enough force to mean something.

"Then let's go someplace that still takes money and weather."

Ben grabbed his coat and followed, the tank wall throwing cold reflected fishlight across the floor behind them. On the way out the table chirped after him, soft and helpful.

`Legacy credit remains active for ninety minutes.`

It sounded exactly like being mocked by a concierge.

* * *

Outside, the wind off the water slapped all warmth out of his face in one go. The pier lights trembled on the dark surface of the river. Somewhere farther down, a service gate rolled open and banged shut again.

Ben shoved both hands into his coat pockets. "I'm sorry."

Deshawn walked beside him without speeding up or slowing down. "You don't have to start every sentence with that."

"I invited you out and then made you sit through an anniversary package."

"That wasn't your anniversary package. It was their little leash."

"Better."

"No, just more accurate."

They cut away from the public cafe toward the service side of the building, where the air changed from river cold to a damp metallic draft that smelled faintly of bleach. Deshawn had to badge in downstairs in less than ten minutes. The path there ran along a concrete wall painted with fish silhouettes that were meant to look educational and mostly looked like somebody had decorated a drainage tunnel.

Ben kept talking because motion and shame made him do that.

"I should've picked somewhere else. I should've used cash. I should've left the work phone at home. I just wanted one drink that didn't already belong to my entire fucking life."

"Then next time leave the phone."

The words next time cracked through him with embarrassing force.

"You say that like there might be one."

Deshawn shoved his hands into his jacket pockets. "There might be if you stop interviewing yourself during it."

Ben laughed once, rough in the throat. "I hear it too, trust me."

"Good."

They reached the service entrance. The security reader over the door was down to a red standby blink while the shift changed. Deshawn pushed the heavy metal door open with his shoulder and jerked his chin inside.

"Come in a second. It's freezing out there."

The stairwell was cinder block, damp at the corners, lit by a fluorescent tube that buzzed like an insect trapped in a jar. Somewhere below them, a pump kicked on with a deep mechanical cough. The concrete held the day's cold. A mop bucket sat on the landing one flight down, forgotten or strategically abandoned. It smelled like disinfectant failing to defeat salt.

Ben stood too close to the railing and immediately knew he looked like a man in a cautionary training video.

Deshawn let the door swing shut behind them. The sudden muffled quiet was intimate enough to feel deliberate.

"Breathe," he said.

"I'm breathing."

"You're filing a report from inside your own body."

Ben opened his mouth to deny it, then did not. Deshawn was looking at him with that same steady, unsponsored attention he'd had at pickup all month, as if the worst thing Ben could do was insult both of them by pretending not to know what was in the room.

"I wanted tonight to be normal," Ben said finally.

"It is normal."

"No, it isn't."

"A drink after work is normal. A weird phone is normal. Divorce is normal. Being awkward because you want somebody is normal."

He said want in the flat practical tone some people used for rain. Ben felt it in his stomach.

"You really don't polish anything, do you," he said.

"Nope."

That was when Deshawn stepped in. Not dramatic. Not slow enough to become a performance of patience. He just closed the distance and put one hand on the front of Ben's coat, then higher, thumb rough at the hinge of Ben's jaw.

The kiss was equally undecorated. Warm mouth, beer and peppermint, one solid decision carried through. Ben made a small sound into it before he knew he'd made one. Deshawn's other hand landed at the small of his back and held there, firm enough that Ben felt the cinder block at his spine through shirt and coat and skin, the cold of it and the answering heat that came up through him all at once.

For six or seven seconds nothing in his life had a corporate noun attached to it.

That was the real shock.

He opened for him, hand fisting in the front seam of Deshawn's jacket. Deshawn's thumb shifted under his jaw. The pump below them thudded. The fluorescent buzz turned thin and far away. Ben felt the hard live fact of his own want, low and immediate and embarrassingly total. Not a concept. Not an orientation statement. Not a future he could narrate as tasteful growth. Just yes.

Then Deshawn's thigh pressed his coat inward and the Gemini phone jammed against Ben's hip like a second skeleton.

The whole household arrived in his body at once. The blue glow Leela could identify from across a room. The apartment asking if he wanted continuity mode. Nita at the table scraping rice out of a lunch container while he buttoned his shirt wrong. Tomorrow morning's badge, thermos, crossing light, pickup line. Even the legacy photos seemed to press from inside the device, waiting for another gap to crawl through.

Ben jerked back hard enough that the back of his shoulder hit the wall.

"Wait," he said.

Deshawn dropped his hands at once.

The space between them went ugly fast.

"I'm sorry," Ben said. "Fuck. I know. I'm sorry."

"Don't do that."

"I wanted to kiss you."

"I know."

"No, I did, I just-" He raked a hand through his hair and felt sweat there despite the cold concrete. "It all came back in at once."

Deshawn watched him. "What did."

Ben hit the phone in his pocket with the heel of his hand as if Deshawn could see it through wool. "Everything. Work. Home. My kid. The apartment. You. All of it. I couldn't tell where any of it started or stopped."

Deshawn leaned back against the railing now, not relaxed, just contained. "That sounds miserable."

"It is."

"It's still not an answer."

Ben looked at his mouth and then away. "I think I wanted it to feel cleaner than this."

Deshawn gave one short breath that might have been a laugh if there had been any softness in it. "Cleaner."

"That's not what I mean."

"I know what you mean." He looked down the stairwell, then back. "You wanted a version you could explain while it was happening."

Ben said nothing, which was answer enough.

Deshawn rubbed his thumb once over the seam of his own coat, like he was reminding himself where to put his hands when they were no longer useful. "I don't need polished, Ben. I do need present."

The fluorescent tube clicked overhead.

"I was present," Ben said, and heard how pleading it sounded.

"For a second."

That one landed cleanly because it was true.

Ben swallowed. "I don't want to make you into some kind of experiment."

"Good. Then don't."

Below them, a door banged and somebody called a name Ben didn't catch. Shift starting. Real life continuing with or without the scene.

"Can I call you?" he asked, hating the thinness of it as soon as it left.

Deshawn considered him for a moment. His face did not harden. That would've been easier. It just got more literal.

"Maybe," he said. "Not if tonight turns into something you want me to help you clean up."

Then he pulled his badge from his pocket, pushed off the rail, and went down the stairs two at a time, work boots sounding blunt and final on the concrete.

Ben stayed where he was until the service door below swallowed him and the sound cut off.

His mouth still felt hot. That was almost the worst part. Not the rejection. Not even the shame. The heat remaining after the moment had failed, proof that the kiss had changed something anyway. For a few seconds he had not been husband, proxy, father, route asset, or apologetic middle manager of everybody else's feelings. He had just been a body against a wall saying yes with his whole nervous system.

Then he'd managed to ruin it in the exact dialect he spoke best.

He took the phone out at last. The screen was dark. His own reflection stared back at him in the black glass, blurred and older than the man from the archived photos. When the display woke under his thumb, there was a single Gemini notification waiting.

`We noticed elevated stress during your continuity-supported outing. Need a decompression route home?`

Ben laughed so hard it hurt, then hit dismiss and took the subway back to Sunnyside with Deshawn's taste still in his mouth like evidence.

* * *

Nita was still awake because Leela's thermos gasket had split again and the cheap replacement ring kept slipping out of the groove.

She sat at the galley counter in one of Ben's old college T-shirts and her underwear, reading glasses low on her nose, thumbs slick with dish soap. The apartment had dimmed itself into nighttime competence. The refrigerator motor hummed. A train screamed out by the tracks and was gone. On the table beside her sat tomorrow's school folder, the co-op assessment envelope she had not opened tonight because she already knew what number lived inside it, and Leela's badge lanyard with one corner of the plastic peeling.

The fridge screen had lit up twenty minutes earlier to inform her, in a font so tasteful it became insulting, that a `Legacy Science Pier credit` had been redeemed from the household bundle.

Nita had stared at the message long enough to memorize the amount. Fourteen dollars and change. Enough to buy half the stupid specialty cough syrup Leela liked, not enough to matter anywhere it counted.

When the apartment door opened she did not look up immediately. She listened first.

Ben came in quietly, which meant guilt or a sleeping child. His coat zipper caught for a second. He smelled like cold air, beer, and something mineral underneath, the smell public aquariums left on people who worked too near the tanks.

"You awake," he said.

"No."

He closed the door with more care than skill. Shoes off. Coat hook missed on first try. She could picture him without looking. Tall, slightly bent, face already arranging itself toward manageable.

"How was the drink?" she asked.

"Bad, eventually."

Nita pressed the gasket into place with her thumbnail until it slipped free again. "The fridge thanked the household for redeeming your legacy evening."

Silence.

Then: "Of course it did."

She looked up. His mouth was a little swollen. Not much. Enough.

"Did it at least cover the first round?" she asked.

He leaned one hand on the counter. "Nita."

"That's not a yes or no."

"Yes."

She nodded once. "Good. I'd hate for our trapped marriage to miss a savings opportunity."

He closed his eyes briefly. "It wasn't on purpose."

"I know it wasn't on purpose. Nothing in this house ever is anymore. That's why it's so expensive."

He flinched, then did the thing she knew too well, the thing where he decided honesty would be less dangerous if he volunteered it before she extracted it.

"It was Deshawn."

Nita already knew that, but hearing the name still altered the room.

Deshawn Price. Clean work boots in the pickup line no matter the weather. Saltwater scratches on his forearms. The father who once handed Leela a clementine after a delayed dismissal because she had gone pale and mean with hunger, and who had done it without looking around for praise. Nita had liked him immediately and mistrusted that fact on sight. Men who were decent in unadvertised ways were harder to dismiss cleanly.

"The aquarium father," she said.

"He's not aquarium father. He works there."

"I know where he works." She set the gasket down and wiped her fingers on a towel. "I'm describing the route."

Ben let out a breath and looked at the floor, which told her more than the answer would've.

"We left the cafe," he said. "After the prompt thing. We went into the stairwell by his shift entrance."

Nita went still, not in the generic story way, not as some theatrical full-body freeze. Her right thumb kept rubbing soap into the dish towel seam because she had not finished the motion when he said stairwell.

"And?"

He looked up then, the mistake of that plain on his face. "We kissed."

The room sharpened. Not louder. Sharper. The refrigerator hum separated into distinct mechanical notes. Somewhere in Leela's room a bed frame clicked softly as she rolled over in sleep.

"Did you," Nita said carefully, "or did the household loyalty tier supervise that too."

"Jesus, Nita."

"No, answer me."

"We kissed. Then I pulled away."

"Why."

He laughed once, miserable and short. "Pick a reason."

"That's not an answer either."

He put both hands on the counter now, like a man bracing against weather inside his own kitchen. "Because the phone was in my pocket. Because it all came back at once. You, Leela, the apartment, the whole fucking route. I couldn't tell what belonged to me and what belonged to the machine anymore."

Nita took off her glasses and set them beside the thermos. "And you thought the cleanest way to test that question was with a man from school pickup."

"It wasn't about clean."

"No?" Her voice stayed low. That was when it got dangerous. "Because from where I'm sitting it looks almost offensively tidy. Same neighborhood. Same child schedule. Same man who already knows which snack keeps Leela from turning into a weapon at five-thirty."

"That's not why I asked him out."

"I'm sure it isn't. That doesn't make the geometry less grotesque."

He stared at her. She could see him wanting to argue with a sentence like geometry less grotesque, the way he always did when her anger came dressed in operational language. Tonight he was too tired or too ashamed to take the bait.

"He isn't a replacement part," Ben said.

"I know he isn't." The fury arrived there, precisely because he had said part. "That's what I'm telling you. He's a real person with a kid and a shift and his own exhausted body, and you dragged him into our sponsored sludge because you wanted proof you still had a self left to offer somebody."

Ben's face changed. She had hit the true thing. Not the whole thing, but enough.

"I didn't drag him," he said, softer.

"No. The system did some of the dragging for you. How convenient."

She stood and crossed to the sink because staying seated made the anger feel too much like clerical work. The apartment was narrow enough that crossing it took two seconds and still changed the power in the room. Cold dishwater waited in the basin. The window over the sink showed a slice of dark tracks and the reflected ghost of her own face.

Behind her Ben said, "I told you because I didn't want to lie."

"Congratulations on clearing the lowest bar available."

"I mean it."

"I know you mean it."

That was the problem with him, maybe the oldest problem. He so often meant the thing. He meant the apology, the joke, the logistical suggestion, the sudden honesty at the lip of disaster. Meaning it and doing damage had never been mutually exclusive for Ben.

She turned back. "Listen to me carefully. I'm not asking you to preserve some holy relic of the marriage. I'm not asking for loyalty to a corpse. I'm telling you I'm not ready to be reduced to a route another compatible parent can inherit."

He took that like a blow.

"Nita-"

"No. You wanted plain speech tonight? Here. If you slept with a stranger from another borough, I would still be angry, but I could file it under ordinary catastrophe. This?" She gestured toward him, the fridge, the whole apartment. "This is administrative. This is the city trying to teach us we're modular."

He looked at the fridge screen without meaning to. The legacy credit notice had dimmed but not vanished. Fourteen dollars and change for memory, desire, and humiliation packaged together.

"I recoiled," he said. "I know."

"That doesn't fix it."

"I know."

"And for the record, this is not because it was a man."

"I know that too."

"Good."

Nita lifted the thermos, pressed the gasket in one more time, and filled it with water from the tap to test the seal. A thin line leaked immediately down the side and onto her wrist.

"Perfect," she said.

Ben reached for a towel. She moved the thermos away before he could take it.

"Don't."

He stopped.

The leak ran over her fingers, cool and stupidly intimate. Water dripped off her knuckles onto the co-op envelope, blurring the return address into a soft gray bruise.

"What happened after you pulled away?" she asked.

"He went to work."

"Did he get to keep any dignity at all, or did Gemini send him a commemorative voucher too."

"Nita."

"I'm serious."

"No. He didn't get a voucher." Ben rubbed at his face. "He got me saying I wanted it cleaner than it was, which I heard as I said it and wanted to die."

Somewhere under the rage, a smaller meaner feeling flexed. Not jealousy exactly. Humiliation with a body in it. The knowledge that another person had now seen the exact seam Ben always split at: want followed by management, heat followed by cleanup, honesty arriving only once the mess had become impossible not to catalog. She had lived inside that seam for years. She had not understood until this second how proprietary the knowledge still felt.

"Well," she said, "at least you represented the household brand accurately."

His head snapped up. "That's low."

"Yes."

"You think I don't know I fucked it up?"

"I think you know that part very well. I think the part you don't know is that this wasn't some neutral exercise in freedom. It landed here too."

He went quiet.

Nita set the leaking thermos in the sink and watched water gather at its base. "We're ending. I'm aware of that. I initiated it, in case the record needs refreshing. But ending isn't the same as becoming interchangeable."

He looked wrecked now, which did not satisfy her the way she would've liked.

"I don't want you interchangeable either," he said.

"That isn't the point."

"Then what's the point."

She almost said everything. The money. The routes. Leela learning to sort prompt colors before long division. The fact that she could picture Deshawn at the pickup line, solid and unsurprised, and understand exactly why Ben had wanted one clean hour under that gaze. The fact that some unlovely animal part of her hated him for bringing that hour back broken and laying it on their counter like she still had to process it.

Instead she said, "The point is that a marriage can die and still leave live wire in the walls."

That, at least, he understood.

He nodded once, barely.

From the bedroom came a small sleeping cough from Leela. Both of them turned toward it on instinct. Same pivot. Same speed. Nita hated that too.

"Brush your teeth," she said after a moment. "She still climbs into our bed at dawn when she has a bad dream, and I'm not explaining why you smell like pier beer."

Ben almost smiled, which was wrong, and knew it was wrong before the expression finished arriving. It died on his face.

"Okay," he said.

He went to the bathroom. The light came on under the door. Water ran.

Nita stood alone in the kitchen with the leaking thermos, the wet envelope, and the fridge politely preserving the evidence of the discount. The marriage was ending. She wanted that to remain true. She wanted out.

What she had not wanted, apparently, was to feel how quickly the world could look at the wreckage and see reusable staffing.

On the fridge screen the legacy credit notification finally timed out, leaving only the date and tomorrow's weather: colder by morning, chance of hard rain.
