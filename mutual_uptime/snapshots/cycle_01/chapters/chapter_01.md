# Chapter 1

On the Tuesday Nita decided to stop managing the end of her marriage, the kitchen was sweating. The September damp had gotten into the cabinet doors and the paper grocery bags and the soft skin of the peaches Ben bought because Leela only liked them for two days out of every six. Apartment 5E always hummed, but tonight the refrigerator motor sounded smug. Leela sat at the narrow end of the table in her temporary blue glasses, building an elevator out of a cereal box and two chopsticks, her fingers gray with pencil and school glue. Ben was unloading groceries and talking too much about the rumor that everybody with a title above coordinator was about to be turned into "retained interface personnel," which was the kind of phrase that arrived in an email before a salary lost a third of itself.

Nita had gotten her own version at 4:17 p.m., subject line in that falsely calm slate blue her company used when it wanted to sound responsible about harm. Mandatory in-person continuity review. Bring government ID. Do not bring a device assistant into the meeting room. She had read it standing in the loading bay at work with a pallet jack whining behind her and had felt, not surprise exactly, but the last useless stitch giving way. On the 7 train home she had decided that if she waited until after tomorrow, she would wait until after the co-op assessment notice, after parent night, after the next salary adjustment, after Leela's fall cold, after one more tolerable evening. She knew her own disease by then. She could turn anything into an argument for later if later came wrapped in enough chores.

Leela held up the cereal-box shell. "Does the door look more like a real door if it's silver or if it's dirty?"

Ben leaned over her shoulder with a peach in one hand and a bunch of cilantro in the other. He had not taken his tie off yet. He rarely did that until the house gave him permission. "Dirty silver," he said. "Real things are always kind of disappointing up close."

Leela considered that with the seriousness of a grant reviewer. "That's useful."

She had trimmed her own bangs in August with the paper scissors from the school supply drawer and had left them uneven on purpose after the first bad cut, insisting symmetry looked fake. Nita respected that. She respected very little at the moment, but she respected a child who could ruin her own hair and then commit to the result.

The fridge lit up with a pale green card.

FAMILY SAVER WINDOW: 11 MINUTES LEFT  
Replenish yogurt tubes, cumin, detergent sheets, sandwich fruit, cumin, dishwasher tabs.

The duplicate cumin sat there like a typing mistake nobody had cared enough to fix.

Ben glanced up and laughed through his nose. "See? Even the fridge thinks we need structure."

Nita was standing at the sink with one hand in a bowl of cloudy rice water. "Can you put the fish on the bottom shelf this time?"

"I always put the fish on the bottom shelf."

"Last week you put it above the berries and the package leaked."

"That was one time."

"That was Saturday."

He put the fish on the bottom shelf without replying. That was one of his gifts, the speed with which he could make a room look as if nothing had happened in it. He did not exactly apologize and he did not exactly deny, and if you were tired enough you sometimes mistook that for peace.

Leela pushed her glasses up with her gluey wrist. "Can the elevator skip five?"

Nita turned. "Why?"

"Because five is where it gets stuck in our building."

Ben smiled. "That's slander."

"It's a machine observation."

Nita dried her hands. "Brush teeth. We're done for tonight."

Leela looked at the cardboard car, then at the chopsticks, then at the clock on the oven. She was nine and had already learned the domestic value of stalling by caring visibly about craft. "I need one minute for the pulley."

"You needed one minute eight minutes ago," Nita said.

Ben set the cilantro down and reached for the spool of thread Leela had stolen from the junk drawer. "I can tie the pulley if that helps."

Nita felt the decision harden again. This was how it always happened. It did not happen through betrayal dramatic enough to point at cleanly or through one unforgivable act, but through Ben's endless little talents for smoothing a moment just enough that it passed without changing shape. He could add three soft minutes to a night and call it kindness. He could turn postponement into atmosphere.

"No," she said. "She can finish it tomorrow. Leela. Teeth."

Leela took off the glasses and polished them on the hem of her T-shirt, an old school shirt that said FUTURE CITIZENS INVENT HERE in peeling vinyl. "Tomorrow it won't dry the same."

"It's cardboard," Nita said. "It'll still be cardboard."

Leela slid off the chair. "That isn't how glue works."

She took the elevator shell with her down the narrow hall like a wounded pet. Ben watched her go, then looked back at Nita with that particular expression that was not yet opposition, only disappointment on behalf of the world's nicer possible version.

"You didn't have to hit it that hard," he said quietly.

"I told her twice."

"She was making a pulley."

"She's going to fourth grade, not bridge construction."

He flinched, not because the line was cruel, although it was, but because it was loud enough to carry. The apartment was small in the way that turned every private sentence into a building amenity. You could hear onions frying on six, somebody's twins crying on three, the delivery bikes coughing awake in the hall. From the bedrooms came the soft crash of a drawer and then the faucet running.

Ben lowered his voice the way he did with clients and frightened dogs. "What happened at work?"

The question irritated her on contact. Not because it was irrelevant, but because it was the first move in his favorite game: locate the event that had produced the feeling, solve around the event, leave the structure intact. If a forklift hit you, Ben wanted the name of the forklift. If the floor gave way, he wanted to know whether anyone had filed a ticket.

"Nothing happened at work," she said. "Work is happening at work. That's separate."

"It doesn't sound separate."

"It isn't. That's the point."

He waited. She could feel him waiting, making room around her sentence for the nicer next sentence she was apparently supposed to supply. The fridge brightened again.

FAMILY BUNDLE BONUS  
Add taco-night proteins now. Tuesday eligibility expires at 8:30 p.m.

The screen filled with glossy chicken thighs, a brick of grated cheese, tortillas in a smiling stack. There was a cartoon pepper wearing sunglasses in the corner. Nita looked at it and had the clean, stupid thought that if she did not speak before the pepper disappeared she deserved her own life.

"I want a divorce," she said.

The kitchen kept humming.

Ben still had one hand on the refrigerator door. He did not move it. Behind him the screen continued to offer her fourteen percent savings on a family quantity of food she no longer wanted to buy under that name.

"Okay," he said after a second, too fast. "Okay. You don't mean tonight, though."

Nita laughed once. It came out ugly and short. "What does that sentence even mean?"

"I mean you mean it generally. I get that you're serious. I'm not doing the thing where I say you're tired and don't know what you're saying. I'm just saying maybe tonight isn't the night we define it."

"If not tonight, when?"

"Tomorrow is impossible, for one thing. We both have whatever those meetings are. Leela has school. We're already running hot. We can talk this through when we're not standing in front of a cheese advertisement."

"You think the problem is the cheese advertisement."

"I think the problem is that everything is bad and maybe we don't do this at full volume with our daughter awake."

"I waited until she was in bed."

"She's not asleep."

"Then maybe don't keep extending the evening every time I try to end a task."

He shut the refrigerator and leaned against it, crossing his arms because he never knew what to do with his hands when charm failed him. He was tall, slightly stooped, tie still on, with the kind of face strangers trusted faster than they should. Nita used to watch women relax around him at school events and feel grateful. Lately it only made her tired.

"What happened?" he asked again, softer now. "Because if this is about the email, or the calls from your mother, or the co-op board suddenly deciding concrete is made of gold, then that's one conversation. If there's somebody else, that's another one. If you're saying the thing we're both too scared to say about the way it's been for a while, then that's a third one. I'm not trying to dodge it. I'm trying to know which fucking cliff we're standing on."

"There isn't somebody else."

"Okay."

"That would at least be interesting."

He looked at the floor. "That's not fair."

"No," she said. "Fair would have been saying this six months ago. Fair would have been not letting you keep acting like if you were helpful enough in the kitchen the marriage would stop rotting."

He opened his mouth, but she kept going because if he got hold of the rhythm he would turn it into a calmer meeting.

"I can't keep doing this version of us where every real thing gets delayed because the timing isn't ideal. You always need the room cooler first. The kid asleep first. The bill paid first. The quarter closed. The right week. The right month. You can turn an entire life into a soft launch."

"That's not what I've been doing."

"It's exactly what you've been doing."

"I've been trying to keep us from blowing holes in the walls every night."

"No. You've been trying to keep the evening functional. Those are different things."

The faucet in the bathroom shut off. Small feet moved once in the hall. Ben heard it too. His eyes cut toward the doorway, then back to Nita. His voice dropped another notch.

"Can you not do this like this?"

She dropped her own voice to match his and made it worse. "Like what. In complete sentences."

"Like you're cross-examining me."

"If you answer one question, I'll stop."

"Ask one question."

"When were you going to notice this was over if I didn't say it in words simple enough for the refrigerator to follow?"

His face changed then, not into anger yet but into the hurt that came when he could not flirt or soothe or organize his way out of the frame she had put around him. "That isn't simple," he said. "That's theater."

"You want theater? Fine. You made me into the person who notices all the leaks and dead batteries and dentist forms and school emails and your father's birthday and the fact that there are three avocados turning black in this kitchen because you bought them to feel like a man with a household plan. And I made you into a man I could not trust with a calendar without checking behind him. There. Does that sound less theatrical?"

"Jesus, Nita."

"There's not one part of our life that doesn't feel managed to death."

"By both of us."

"Not equally."

"No," he said, and some iron finally entered his voice. "Not equally. You don't get to say you were the only one carrying this thing because I was there too. I did pickups. I did baths. I stayed when it got ugly. I kept this place livable."

"You kept it pleasant in bursts."

"That counts."

"It counts less than you think."

The kitchen panel chimed.

WIND-DOWN REMINDER  
Household quiet hours begin in 9 minutes. Recommended lighting shift available.

The bulbs over the sink dimmed automatically by half and warmed toward amber. Ben stared at them. For one absurd second Nita thought he was going to cry over a light setting.

From the hall came the smallest scrape. A foot against paint. Nita turned and saw, just for a second before it vanished, the edge of Leela's blue glasses catching refrigerator light. She had not seen the whole child, only proof of witness.

Ben pushed himself off the fridge. "Great," he said. "Great. She heard that."

"She heard a word."

"A word is enough."

"Then maybe that matters to you now because I used the wrong channel."

"Don't do that."

"Don't tell me how to say it."

He rubbed both hands over his face, tie askew now, voice fraying into the fast, rolling clauses he reached for when he wanted a better version of reality to volunteer itself. "I'm not telling you not to say it. I'm asking whether we can maybe, for five minutes, remember she's nine and the walls are paper and whatever happens next is not just about us detonating in the kitchen because the fridge wants taco meat."

Nita looked at the cartoon pepper still smirking from the darkened panel and almost admired the timing.

"What happens next," she said, "is that I'm done being married to you."

He shook his head, not in disagreement, but in that helpless little recoil people did when handed paperwork they recognized before they had read it. "That sounds so clean when you say it."

"It isn't clean."

"No, it isn't."

Silence settled badly. It was the kind that still contained standing water and exposed wiring.

In Leela's room, a bedframe creaked once. Then there was nothing.

Nita picked up the peaches and put them in the fruit bowl one by one because if she did not put her hands somewhere she was going to start throwing the wrong things. Ben did not help. He stood in the middle of the kitchen with his shoulders tipped forward, as if apology were a physical climate he had to lean into.

"I'm putting her down," he said finally.

"No."

"She heard me too."

"This isn't a shared crisis-management task."

"Everything in this house is a shared crisis-management task. That's part of the problem, remember?"

The line was good enough that she almost smiled, which made her angrier. Ben saw it and did not smile back. He went down the hall without waiting for permission. Nita heard his low voice through the wall, gentler than he had been with her in months, talking about pajamas and the missing left sock and how the elevator could have a proper pulley tomorrow. She hated the tenderness because it was real. She hated it more because it was late.

She stood in the kitchen while the smart lights completed their wind-down and the room turned flattering and false. On the fridge the taco-night window expired. The pepper vanished. The screen replaced it with a calmer suggestion.

PLAN TOMORROW'S FAMILY BREAKFAST?  
High-protein options matched to household route timing.

Nita touched the glass until the panel went black.

By the time Ben came back, Leela's door was shut and the apartment had entered its night voice: the air filter's tired rush, the rails screaming faintly two blocks over, somebody above them dragging a chair across tile as if punishment were a hobby. Nita had not moved far. She was sitting at the table now with the damp grocery receipt flattened under her palm. She had circled nothing on it, but he knew the posture. It was the posture she took when an expense had offended her enough to become evidence.

He wanted, idiotically, some version of the evening that still contained steps. He did not want optimism. He was not stupid enough for optimism. He wanted steps because he could live inside them for years.

"She's in bed," he said.

Nita nodded.

"I told her we had loud grown-up voices."

"And?"

"And she said, 'I know.'" He pulled out the chair across from her and sat. "So that's where we are."

Nita stared at the receipt. "Yes."

The problem with hearing your marriage was over was that after the first hit, your mind immediately started doing facilities work. Where would the body go? Where would the medication go? Which parent would tell school? Who could afford another place in this zip code? Ben hated that his first instincts were logistical because Nita was right: he could use logistics the way other people used prayer. The couch existed. The rent existed. Morning existed whether or not his feelings achieved the right dignity.

"Okay," he said, and heard himself trying to make the word less doomed than it was. "Immediate question. Do you want me out of the bedroom tonight?"

"I don't want a husband asking that like he's offering to switch seats."

"I'm asking where I'm sleeping."

"So am I."

He blew air out through his nose. "All right. Fair."

The fridge brightened behind him again, lighting his shoulder blades green.

LATE ADD-ON OFFER  
Double breakfast fruit and school-safe snack cakes. Confirm by 10:00 p.m.

He did not turn around. "If I rip that thing off the wall, do we owe the co-op?"

"Probably."

"Great."

He stood, hit dismiss without looking, and sat back down. The plastic chair cushion gave its usual little fart of trapped air. In another mood one of them would have laughed. Nita only watched him.

"I can take the couch," he said.

"Then Leela wakes up and sees you there."

"She's already not under the impression that this is going beautifully."

"That isn't an argument."

"No, it's a fact."

"You love facts when they help you avoid choosing."

He felt the sentence go in. "That's not fair either."

"I know."

It would have been easier if she sounded triumphant. She sounded exhausted enough to pass out upright. Her hair, which she had hacked at herself again two weeks ago over the bathroom sink, kept catching on the collar of her blouse where the cut had gone a little blunt. He knew that because he still noticed small physical things about her before he could stop himself. That knowledge had nowhere useful to go now.

"Do you want to tell her tomorrow?" he asked.

"Before school? No."

"After?"

"I don't know."

"We should know."

"You think I don't know that?"

He lifted both hands. "I'm not saying it like that."

"You always say it like that."

"How, exactly?"

"Like the problem would respect us if we convened properly."

He almost said that problems often did respect proper convening. He almost said this was why people had meetings and calendars and custody agreements and fucking checklists. He did not say it because even he could hear how much it would prove her case.

Instead he asked, "When did you decide?"

She looked up then, directly at him, which was worse than the receipt. "On the train."

"Tonight."

"Yes."

"So this wasn't something you were sitting on for months."

"I've been sitting in it for months. I decided tonight."

"What changed on the train?"

"Nothing changed. I ran out of postponement."

He leaned back and looked at the ceiling. There was a water stain over the light fixture that the co-op kept promising to assess in a future maintenance cycle. He had once made a joke that it looked like New Jersey. The joke had been good enough to survive two dinner parties. Now the stain only looked like damage with a slow imagination.

"I wish you had said something before the point where you're done," he said.

"I did."

"Not this."

"No. Not this exact noun. I said I was lonely in this house. I said I felt like the person doing continuity for a corporation nobody admitted existed. I said every time something got hard you turned into customer service with stubble."

"That's incredibly insulting."

"Yes."

"You've really been practicing."

"No," she said. "I've been not saying it. That's different."

He wanted to defend himself and found, to his surprise, that the available material felt cheap in his mouth. He did do pickups. He did know the school login, the exact drawer where Leela kept Band-Aids, the exact way Nita liked the bathroom fan turned on during showers so the mirror would not drown in fog. He also knew how often he had used a joke or a snack run or a gentle hand on Nita's back to get them through one more ugly patch without admitting the patch was the terrain. Both things were true. One of them was just less flattering.

"I was trying to keep the place habitable," he said at last.

"For whom?"

"For all of us."

"No. For the version of us that meant you never had to watch anything actually end."

He said nothing.

She pushed the receipt away. "Look at us. We don't even know where you're sleeping."

"Fine," he snapped, more sharply than he intended. "Then tell me. Bedroom, couch, floor, bench, hallway, fire escape, polite grave. Give me the location and I'll put a pillow on it."

Her mouth twitched despite herself. "There it is."

"What."

"You only get honest when cornered hard enough."

"I'm cornered hard enough."

"Good."

He stood and paced the length of the galley because sitting there felt too much like waiting for performance notes. The floor stuck slightly under his socks where somebody had spilled juice at breakfast and wiped it badly. At the counter he picked up his phone and looked at the time, then at the overnight rates within five miles, because some part of him still believed data might produce dignity.

The cheapest room near LaGuardia cost more than he had in checking if he wanted groceries before Friday. A pod hotel in Midtown offered a "co-parent transition package" with discounted breakfast and two toothbrushes in separate colors. One family suite in Long Island City had a sponsored field-trip tie-in. The app knew enough about him to offend him efficiently.

"Well?" Nita said.

He turned the phone around for one second and let her see the rates.

She looked, not long, then away, with no pity in it, only arithmetic.

"Right," she said.

"Right."

Money entered the room and the room got smaller. It always did. It brought with it the co-op assessment they were still pretending not to be angry about, the school fees that came disguised as voluntary contributions, the grocery total that climbed while the bags got lighter, and tomorrow's meeting at work with whatever antiseptic ruin waited inside it. Ben felt for one dizzy second that they had chosen the exact worst week to become truthful, which was so obviously true it circled back toward stupidity. Every week had been the worst week for something.

"So you stay," Nita said. "Tonight."

"Apparently."

"That isn't a concession. It's math."

"I know what math is."

"Do you?"

He looked at her. "There it is again."

"What."

"The way you get crueler when you're scared."

She went very still, which on Nita was not passivity but compression. "And you get nicer when you're scared," she said. "Which is worse."

He laughed then, because she had landed it and because the laugh came out of someplace close enough to despair that resisting it would have looked theatrical. "Jesus Christ."

"Don't make me the only one saying true things tonight."

He sat down again. "Fine. True thing. I don't know how to do this without trying to make it feel less bad every ten minutes."

That changed something, not enough to help, but enough to shift the air. Nita looked at him the way she sometimes did when Leela finally admitted to breaking something expensive: angry first, then briefly sorry the truth had needed so much force to appear.

"I know," she said.

He had not expected the mercy of that. It made him furious for a second.

"Then why say it like I'm running a scam?"

"Because sometimes you are," she said. "Not a deliberate one. But yes. You take the ugliest part of a thing and make it livable enough that nobody has to decide whether to leave. That's still a scam if it lasts long enough."

He thought of the last year in shards: laughing through dentist bills, ordering better takeout after a fight as if sesame noodles counted as repair, having sex once in late spring because Leela slept at a friend's and the apartment happened to be quiet, then behaving the next day as if tenderness had renewed itself from that one administrative anomaly. He could not even argue with her cleanly because the evidence was in his own habits.

"So what," he said. "Lawyer tomorrow?"

"I don't know."

"Mediator."

"Maybe."

"You must have looked something up."

"Not because I was excited."

"That wasn't what I meant."

"I know."

She got up before he could say anything else and crossed to the little printer on the shelf by the pantry, the one they mostly used for school forms because both of them distrusted signing anything important on glass. Ben watched her wake the machine. The old rollers complained. The blue light blinked. Then the printer began spitting pages with that cheap office smell of hot dust and warmed plastic.

"You already had it ready?" he said.

"I had a tab open."

"Nita."

"What. You want me to be less serious?"

He listened to the pages come out: petition, instructions, fee schedule, child-related filing requirements. The bureaucracy of ending a marriage was obscene in a different key from the marriage itself, all boxes and calm. When she lifted the stack, the top page caught on the tray and tore a little at the corner. She smoothed it flat with her palm.

"Paper makes it harder for the house to pretend not to see it," she said.

"That's one of the bleakest things you've ever said."

"It's also true."

She brought the packet back to the table and set it between them. Ben looked down at the black type and the blank lines waiting for names, dates, addresses, child custody arrangements they had not discussed because they had barely managed to discuss a couch.

"I don't have somewhere to go," he said.

This time the admission came out plain, with no cushion around it.

Nita rubbed the bridge of her nose. "Neither do I."

"I know."

"No. I mean I don't have some secret clean exit. There isn't an apartment hidden in my bra. There isn't a dignified second life waiting under the sink. There's this place and whatever tomorrow does to our jobs and the fact that if one more app offers me a discount for buying in family quantities I am going to put a plate through the wall."

He glanced automatically at the plates.

"See?" she said.

"That was reflex."

"Exactly."

The clock on the microwave turned over to 12:00, not because it was midnight but because the house had nudged into low-power sync again and the appliance had taken that as an excuse to forget time. Ben stared at the blinking zeros. One more machine asking him to set it. One more small order waiting for human fingers.

He got up and pulled a blanket from the hall closet. Nita watched but did not comment. He laid it on the couch and sat on the edge of the cushion. The springs pressed back through the fabric. If he slept there, his feet would hang off the armrest. If he took the kitchen bench, his back would seize by dawn. If he went into the bedroom as usual, the room would smell like surrender.

"We'll tell her after school," he said. "Not before. I know you don't know, but that's my vote."

Nita looked at the divorce packet, then at the dark hall. "Maybe."

"Maybe isn't a plan."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

He wanted to keep pushing until they had something firmer than maybe. That was the old reflex. He could feel it pawing at the air between them. But the pages were warm on the table. The light over the sink had gone from amber to almost intimate. Somewhere beyond the bedroom wall, Leela turned over and the mattress gave a small careful groan, the sound of a child trying not to make the grown-ups notice she was still awake.

They stayed where they were. Nita at the table with one hand on the packet. Ben on the couch without taking off his shoes. Nobody left. Nobody forgave anyone. The apartment hummed around them like a machine pleased to have preserved the unit for one more night.

At 6:18, when the windows were only beginning to gray and neither of them had slept, Leela came down the hall dressed for school in yesterday's argument, which was to say she was quiet, precise, and fully operational. Her blue glasses were back on. She held the elevator project against her chest with both hands. Sometime after bed she had added a second cereal-box shell, not attached yet, just held beside the first with painter's tape ready.

She looked at the blanket on the couch, the papers on the table, and then at her project.

"Should I make two buildings now," she asked, "or are you still figuring out the doors?"
