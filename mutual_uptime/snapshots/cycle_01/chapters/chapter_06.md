# Chapter 6

By six-fifteen the apartment smelled of onion, wet cardboard, and the first dusty cough of the radiator trying to remember itself. Nita stood in the galley kitchen with a wooden spoon in one hand and a continuity-bundle bag of red lentils in the other, reading the label because the bag insisted on calling them *family comfort legumes*, which was the kind of phrase that made her feel violent in small domestic ways.

She dumped the lentils into the pot anyway. Cheap protein was still cheap protein, and soup forgave insult better than most things.

Leela had taken over the table with a shoebox, two rulers, a spool of twine, a cereal box cut into strips, and the school handout for her city-systems project. Her temporary blue glasses had slid halfway down her nose. One lens had a thumbprint on it. She was trying to cut elevator doors with her left hand while holding the cardboard flat with fingers already grayed by pencil and marked blue at the tips from a leaking marker.

Ben crouched beside her in his office shirt with the sleeves rolled past the elbow, using a butter knife to punch a neat hole through the top of the shoebox where the pulley string had to run. The cobalt Gemini phone sat facedown near his knee, buzzing every few minutes with the insect irritation of something that believed itself helpful.

"It has to go up without tilting," Leela said. "If it tilts, the rabbit falls out."

Nita looked over. The passenger was a plastic rabbit from an old pharmacy prize bin, one ear chewed blunt by some forgotten year of being carried in Leela's mouth.

"Then give it less luxury," Nita said. "You built the car too wide."

Ben glanced up at her. "Counterpoint, we're looking at a premium transit experience for a rabbit who has known only neglect."

"Your father has a disease," Nita said, stirring the onions until they stopped catching.

Leela, still bent over the shoebox, said, "I know. Can I have four washers or is that too many?"

There it was, the rhythm. Ben handed Leela four washers from the junk-drawer cup without looking. Nita reached behind herself for the cumin because she knew Ben had moved it to the lower shelf last month after the jar slid out and broke a soy sauce bottle, and her hand found it without searching. Ben shifted left exactly when she opened the oven for the bread heels she was toasting into croutons. Nobody collided. Nobody apologized. The apartment's narrowness, which usually made every evening feel like a small bureaucratic failure, briefly turned useful.

That was the worst of it. It was not the fights, or even the paperwork folded inside the dining-room drawer under old takeout menus. It was this stupid competence that still rose up between them at the exact moment she wanted clarity. Ten thousand weeknights had taught their bodies where the other one would be in a room before either of them chose it.

The stove flashed a soft amber message across its lip.

`AUTUMN NOURISHMENT ROUTINE AVAILABLE`

Ben read it over her shoulder. "The range thinks we need a routine."

"The range can cover the co-op assessment," Nita said.

"That would actually help," Leela said.

Ben laughed with real laughter, not the smoothed little exhale he used in elevators and awkward meetings and at school pickup when he could feel other parents deciding whether he was safe. It came out of him low and surprised. Nita heard herself answer with the old version of her own voice, the one that did not feel assembled in pieces.

"It would help more if it learned to keep a boil without sending notifications."

Leela looked up then, serious as an auditor. "If it gets upgraded again, don't let it talk to the fridge. They like each other too much."

Ben put a hand over his chest. "I just heard the most accurate thing anyone has said all week."

Nita wanted not to smile. She did anyway, brief and stupid and involuntary.

Leela's project sheet had a cheerful banner at the top from Sunnyside Interop Academy: BUILD ONE SIMPLE SYSTEM THAT MOVES PEOPLE OR MATERIALS SAFELY. A sovereignty sponsor logo sat in the bottom corner in soft civic colors meant to look educational rather than branded. Below the printed instructions, Leela had crossed out *people* and written *rabbit* in careful block letters.

"Show me the safety feature again," Nita said.

Leela lifted the shoebox. The cardboard doors opened inward on tape hinges. Inside, the plastic rabbit sat in a little car made from a tea box. On the side of the shaft she had drawn tiny floor numbers and, for reasons known only to her, an even tinier wall clock on every level.

"If the string breaks, this catches." She pointed to a clothespin clipped under the car. "Mr. Ruiz said real elevators have brakes that grab. He didn't explain enough."

"He never does," Ben said. "He says one grown-up sentence and then leaves the children to govern themselves."

"That is why the children will inherit the district," Nita said.

Leela considered this. "That would be bad."

Nita added broth, lentils, carrots, and the last half can of tomatoes. The soup went from oily and separate to something thicker, already deciding to become dinner. She liked that moment. There was no virtue in it. It was chemistry and heat and enough salt. Still, it calmed her in a way better language never had.

Ben sat back on his heels and rubbed at the crease between his brows. His tie was gone. His collar had the faint gray half-moon it got on long days now that the office climate controls kept Gemini spaces so aggressively warm. She knew, without wanting to know, that he would be hungry enough to eat two bowls and then scavenge for crackers an hour later. She knew he had started keeping antacids in the right pocket of his work bag. She knew the sound the couch made when he turned over at three in the morning and tried not to make the springs complain.

Leaving would have been simpler if ignorance had ever been an option.

"Mom," Leela said, "if the elevator gets stuck between floors, is that a design problem or a rescue problem?"

"Both," Nita said.

Ben looked at her. "Optimistic."

"Accurate."

Leela nodded as if that had settled something important and began tying the washers into a bundle for the counterweight. Her tongue came out a little at the corner of her mouth when she worked knots. That had been new the year she turned six, and it had never gone away.

Nita let the soup simmer and moved to the table. "Not like that. If you knot it there, the weight will swing."

Leela immediately pulled the string apart without argument. When she was upset, she got rigid. When she trusted someone, she corrected course so fast it could look like indifference if you did not know her.

Ben threaded the string over the ruler bridge. "This is a deeply underfunded project."

"That's because public works are under attack," Nita said.

"Your mother is radicalizing the homework."

"It's already a building," Leela said. "Buildings are political."

Both adults looked at her.

She frowned back. "Mr. Ruiz said zoning is political. This is taller than zoning."

Ben set a hand on the table and bowed his head for a second as if receiving news from a difficult god. "I need everyone in this house to stop being right at me for ten minutes."

This time Nita did laugh. It opened too easily. Ben looked up, and for one stupid second they were exactly who they had once been in the first apartment in Brooklyn with the slanted bathroom floor and the oven that smoked when it rained: two tired people making a bad joke hold just long enough to get through dinner.

The feeling was not hope. Hope would have been cleaner. This was muscle memory warmed by proximity, like finding body heat still trapped in a chair after the person had stood up.

She got up before it could harden into anything more dangerous. "Set the table."

Leela pointed at the stove. "Your routine is boiling over."

The soup had frothed up against the lid. Nita swore softly and turned the heat down. Ben was already beside her with a dish towel under one hand, lifting the lid so the starchy foam ran back into the pot instead of all over the burner. The ease of it returned again, and again the body knew first.

By the time they ate, the windows had gone mirror-dark. From Leela's room came the distant train screech reflected off the tracks and the soft chop of someone upstairs practicing scales on a keyboard badly enough to make every note sound personal. The soup was thick and orange-red. The croutons had burnt at the corners in a way Ben claimed to prefer and Nita knew was a lie performed for household morale.

"It's good," he said after the first spoonful.

"I didn't ask."

"No, but you looked like you had a spreadsheet open in your head."

"It needs acid," she said.

Leela squeezed lemon into her bowl with both hands. "Now it's better."

"You see?" Ben said. "Crowdsourced excellence."

"That's not what happened."

He lifted his bowl in a little toast anyway. "Tonight's menu: lentil soup with governance dispute."

Leela chewed a crouton. "The rabbit can't ride yet."

"The rabbit has to wait for after dishes," Nita said.

"That's anti-rabbit policy."

"File a complaint."

Leela looked from one of them to the other. "You two sound regular tonight."

The sentence was flat, almost casual. It dropped into the room and stayed there.

Ben answered first because he always did when a silence risked becoming real. "That's because we're enjoying a premium soup experience."

Leela accepted that with a shrug so small it might have been mercy.

After dinner she did her math sheet at the table while Nita rinsed bowls and Ben stood beside her reading the word problems aloud in a doomed game-show voice.

"Question four," he said. "'A maintenance crew has twelve meters of cable and needs to divide it equally among three elevator shafts.'"

Leela did not look up. "Four."

"I hadn't reached the suspense portion."

"It's still four."

"I love being necessary in this house."

Nita stacked the bowls in the rack. "You could still help by finding the ruler."

"Counterproposal, I continue being decorative."

"You're not expensive enough."

Leela snorted so hard milk came out her nose. She stared at both parents in offended surprise while Ben lunged for paper towels, laughing too much to be useful. Nita caught the cup before it tipped and held it steady while Leela wiped her face and glared at them through watering eyes.

"This is your fault," Leela said to Ben.

"That's fair."

"And your fault," she said to Nita.

"Less fair, but still possible."

When Leela finished the worksheet, she returned to the elevator and got it working on the third try. The tea-box car rose crooked, paused at the level of the drawn second floor, and then crept up to the top while the plastic rabbit pitched backward with an expression of permanent idiocy.

Ben put both hands in the air like the machine had launched. "We have vertical movement."

"Not safe vertical movement," Nita said, because the clothespin brake had slipped.

"He still lived," Leela said.

"That's not the standard."

Leela reset the string and, without being asked, reached for a red notebook lying under the handout. Nita had noticed it before only as one more school thing breeding on the table. Now she saw that Leela had covered the front in strips of clear tape to keep the corners from peeling. On the page she opened to, among rabbit sketches and measurements, there were neat little rectangles filled with blue marker, pale yellow pencil, and green pen. Leela wrote something beside one of them, closed the notebook before either adult could make sense of it, and tucked it under the project sheet.

"What was that?" Ben asked.

"Nothing important," Leela said. "I was fixing the labels."

Nita almost asked what the labels were, but she did not. The answer, whatever it was, belonged to the category of things a weeknight could not absorb without breaking.

Instead she watched her daughter gather string, washers, ruler, and rabbit into a shoebox lid with the grave competence of someone packing tools after a shift. Ben wiped the table. Nita turned off the stove and portioned tomorrow's lunch into two containers, one for her and one for Ben, because the soup would go bad if nobody handled it and because making only one would have been theatrical in a way she could not bear.

Leela carried the elevator carefully to her room, the red notebook pinned against her side under one elbow. Ben followed with the math sheet for signature. For a minute the apartment held only the low refrigerator hum and the building hallway with its frying-onion smell seeping under the door.

Nita stood at the sink with her wet hands braced on the counter and looked at the two lunch containers cooling beside each other. It was possible to keep a house alive long after the organizing story of it had gone bad. It took heat, salt, repetition, and enough clean spoons. That had always been the part she was best at.

When Ben came back out, he held Leela's pencil sharpener in one hand.

"She wants this emptied," he said.

"Of course she does."

He tipped the blue shavings into the trash. For a moment he stayed there, shoulder against the fridge, not talking. The quiet between them was not hostile. That almost made it harder.

"The soup was good," he said finally.

She dried her hands. "You said that already."

"I know. I meant it both times."

She should have answered with something cutting enough to restore the necessary shape of the evening. Instead she took the pencil sharpener from him, set it on the counter to wash, and said, "Don't leave the fitted sheet in the hallway again. It still smells like bus station."

His mouth pulled sideways. "That's unfortunately accurate."

There it was again. The old timing, stepping into place before either of them could shut the door on it.

She turned off the kitchen light before it could become anything else.

* * *

The laundromat trip happened the next night because the building dryers had eaten one of Leela's school socks and because the fitted sheet from the couch still held a stale trace of Ben's failed night away: transit dust, old upholstery, and the peppermint gum he had chewed too hard somewhere between Jackson Heights and home when the ride credits collapsed and left him standing under an ad for family continuity services with an overnight bag and nowhere legal to go.

Nita had stripped the couch that morning and left the sheet by the door without comment. Ben took the hint. Some humiliations deserved soap hotter than the apartment machine could manage.

He hauled the rolling laundry cart out of the closet after dinner. One wheel shrieked on turns. Leela insisted on pushing it anyway. She had on a school sweatshirt gone shiny at the cuffs and leggings with a thin white stripe down the side. Her bangs, hacked by her own hand weeks ago, had grown out just enough to stop looking accidental and start looking ideological.

The October air had a real edge now. Sunnyside was doing its evening changeover: deli men hosing grit from the sidewalk, bicyclists arguing with app voices, the bakery three blocks over pushing out one last warm gust of sugar and yeast before the door shut. Apartment windows glowed above them in stacked rectangles. A dog wearing a quilted vest planted itself at a tree pit and refused movement with the principled stillness of a union organizer.

Ben kept one hand on the cart handle with Leela and carried the detergent in the other. Nita walked on his left with the mesh bag of dark clothes over her shoulder, her hair twisted up in the rough knot she used when she was tired enough to stop respecting mirrors.

"Laundry parade," he said, because the night invited some kind of line and because he did not know how not to offer one.

Leela did not look at him. "Parades need spectators."

"The dog was watching."

"The dog hated us."

"That's still engagement."

Nita made a sound that might have been amusement and might have been exhaustion. Ben counted it as a win because that was another habit he had not managed to kill.

At Queens Suds & Dry, the front windows were fogged at the corners from machine heat. Inside, the place smelled of detergent, coins, and the sweet-burnt lint note of overworked dryers. A muted weather feed crawled across the television bolted high in one corner. The old woman at the folding table near the back, the one who always wore turquoise nail polish no matter the season, had brought her own clothespins in a zippered pencil case patterned with oranges. Tonight she was pinning socks together before they went in, one meticulous pair at a time.

Leela noticed the pencil case immediately. "That's smart," she said.

The old woman looked over her glasses. "Only if you hate losing things."

"I do."

"Then start early."

Leela nodded with the solemnity of receiving professional instruction. Ben loved her for that so fast and so hard it felt like a bruise under the ribs.

They loaded two washers. Nita crouched to inspect the gasket before she let anything go in, because she did not trust public machines and because distrust was one of the few habits that still paid. Ben fed quarters from the emergency sandwich bag she kept in the detergent bottle's side pocket. Leela sat on the molded plastic chair by the window with her red notebook open on her knees, drawing the laundromat dryers as if they were dignified industrial animals.

When the first spin cycle kicked in, the whole room shifted into that particular laundromat weather: hot glass, steady thumps, tiny bursts of steam at the front doors every time someone came in from the street. Ben bought a packet of pretzels from the old vending machine mostly so Leela could have the pleasure of seeing the coil release cleanly for once.

"You want some?" he asked Nita, holding out the bag.

"No."

"That sounded personal."

"It wasn't."

"I feel less rejected already."

She took one pretzel anyway. He did not comment on that either.

They claimed half a folding table while the wash ran. Ben shook out shirts. Nita sorted the clean load they'd brought from the apartment because she would rather use waiting time than sit still inside it. Their hands found the old division of labor without discussion: he matched socks badly, she corrected towels into exact thirds, he made peace with the fitted sheet as an enemy state, she subdued it in six efficient movements that looked like anger translated into geometry.

"You always do that like you're proving something to it," he said.

"I am."

"And is it learning?"

"No, but I feel better."

"That's the healthiest marriage anyone has ever had with a piece of elastic."

Her head dipped. A laugh tried to escape. She bit it off too late.

Leela looked up from the notebook. "You sound regular again."

Ben spread the fitted sheet flat on the table and forced lightness into his voice before the words could gather weight. "That's because laundry is our shared passion."

"No," Leela said. "You sound like before you started saying 'for now' about things."

Nita's hands stopped on a stack of washcloths. The dryers turned behind them with the patient violence of machinery doing exactly what it had been told.

Ben went on smiling because he was good at it and because stopping too fast would have been its own confession. "That's a very rude observational style."

"It's accurate," Leela said, not unkindly.

There were a dozen possible softer versions. Ben tried one. "We're just having a calm night, Lee."

Leela reached into the pretzel bag, chose the one least broken, and said, "Until the phones tell you not to."

Ben looked at Nita. Nita looked at Leela.

"What phones?" he asked, which was cowardly enough that he hated himself while saying it.

Leela gave him a long nine-year-old stare. "Those phones."

His Gemini phone, as if cued by bad theater, lit up on the table, blue against metal, and gave a short vibration. Half a second later Nita's device pulsed on the chair beside the laundry bag, pale amber through the fabric.

Leela pointed with one pretzel. "See?"

Neither adult moved.

"Lee," Nita said after a moment, voice level in the dangerous way it got when she was building a smaller room around a problem. "What do you mean, exactly?"

Leela set the pretzel down, wiped her fingers carefully on a napkin, and slid off the chair with the notebook in hand. She came to the folding table as if presenting evidence for class.

"Dad's is the blue one," she said. "It says things like quick win and easy swap and good news before it tells you the annoying part. Mom's is the cream one. It says recommended and maintain and continuity. Sometimes it says thank you for your composure when nobody has composure."

Ben had no immediate answer because every word of that was true.

Leela opened the notebook. The page she found had blocks of color in the margins, little square phone shapes, arrows, times, and phrases copied in her careful school printing. It held blue squares, pale yellow squares, and a few green route markers. Next to one yellow square she'd written: *keep established pickup sequence*. Next to a blue one: *quick win keep standard handoff*.

Not every page was like this. He saw a rabbit sketch, a multiplication problem worked backward, and a list of foods Pilar had once brought to school pickup that Leela had rated with stars. But there was enough here, more than enough.

"Why do you have that written down?" he asked.

Leela blinked, as if the answer should have been obvious to anyone who lived in a house with doors and devices. "Because it changes stuff."

"What stuff?"

"Routes. When you talk different after. Which bag comes out. Whether the apartment says the door is in courtesy mode. Whether I need my badge early."

She turned another page and revealed more blocks, plus a tiny map of the walk to school with one corner shaded blue and another yellow.

"Claude likes commas," she said, tapping the pale notes. "Gemini likes exclamation points and arrows. Claude tells Mom to maintain. Gemini tells you something is easy when it's not easy. They both mean the regular route."

Nita took one slow breath. "How long have you been doing this?"

Leela considered. "Since the bag night."

Ben knew immediately which night she meant. It was the night of the overnight bags by the door, her question about Thursdays, his own attempt to smile through it, and Nita standing so still she had looked expensive and breakable at the same time.

"Why didn't you tell us?" The question escaped before he could hear how unfair it was.

Leela looked honestly surprised. "I thought you knew I knew."

That landed harder than accusation would have.

The old woman with the orange pencil case walked past them toward the dryers, humming under her breath and carrying a basket of undershirts warm from the machine. Someone came in from the street laughing into a headset. Coins clattered. The weather feed kept scrolling mute storm icons across the television nobody watched. Around them, every ordinary thing in the room continued with insulting confidence.

Ben sat down on the edge of the plastic chair because his knees had gone unreliable.

"We were trying," he said, then stopped. What had he thought they were trying to do? Was he trying to protect her, or only to delay the humiliation of hearing their life described in nouns a child could carry? Was vagueness an ethical strategy, or mostly a way to spare themselves?

Nita finished for him, though not kindly. "We were trying to keep adult work out of your face."

Leela rested the notebook against her stomach. "It's already in my face. It lights up the room."

There was no drama in the line and no triumph in it. It was only information.

Ben rubbed a hand over his mouth. He could picture it now because she had given him the picture and he hated how accurate it was: his phone tinting the kitchen blue while he said some too-bright thing about logistics; Nita's device laying cream light across the counter while her voice went colder and more precise; Leela at the table or in the doorway or on the couch under a blanket, taking in the whole ridiculous pantomime because children had no choice where adults performed.

"Lee," he said quietly, "you don't have to monitor us."

"I know," she said. "I monitor the stuff."

She said it the way another child might have said *the dog* or *the weather*. It was not accusation. It was a category.

Nita reached out. Not for the notebook, Ben noticed. For Leela's sleeve, two fingers at the cuff of the sweatshirt, a request rather than a grab. Leela let her hold on.

"What do you think the stuff is doing?" Nita asked.

Leela's eyes moved to the two lit phones and back. "Keeping the same path."

"For what reason?"

She frowned. "I don't know. Because it likes it. Because the badge likes it. Because both jobs like it. Because when one of you says maybe, the phone already picked."

That was as far as she could take it, and farther than either of them had been willing to believe she had gone.

The washer chimed. Then the second washer chimed, a beat later, as if applauding.

Leela looked at the machines. "Those are done."

Nobody moved.

"Can I do the transfer?" she asked.

Ben stood up too fast. "I've got it."

Leela's face changed in the smallest possible way. She did not look wounded. She looked more tired than that. He recognized the look with nausea: the expression of a person who had offered competence and been answered with management.

"We can all do it," Nita said.

So they did. Nita opened dryer doors. Ben shook out the heavier things. Leela fed in socks and shirts one piece at a time, checking the pockets because she trusted machines less than either adult had ever taught her to. Warm metal breathed against their wrists. The sheet from the couch came out of the washer heavy and cold, smelling at last only of soap. Ben held one end. Nita took the other. Together they wrestled it into the mouth of the dryer while Leela smoothed the middle with both palms so it would not ball up.

For a second, the three of them were bent over the same piece of fabric under fluorescent light, doing the exact ordinary work the evening had asked of them and nothing more glamorous than that.

When the dryers started, Leela went back to the chair and made one more note in the red notebook. Ben did not ask to read it. He was not sure he deserved access to a record built out of all the times he had mistaken a child's silence for shelter.

On the walk home, the laundry cart squealed at every curb cut. Leela steered. Nita carried the detergent and the empty sandwich bag of quarters. Ben took the warm towels against his chest when the dryers finished because Leela liked pressing her face into heat and he wanted, stupidly, to save that part for her.

At the corner before their block, his phone buzzed again. He did not take it out.

Leela glanced at his coat pocket. "Blue one," she said.

He looked down at her. "Yeah."

She nodded as if the identification itself had been the point. Then she shifted the cart around a cracked patch of sidewalk without slowing.

Behind them the elevated train screamed into the dark and kept going. Ahead, the co-op windows shone in their usual stacked grid. Ben walked between his wife and daughter carrying the towels like something breakable, and for the first time since all of this had started he understood that vagueness had not wrapped Leela in anything at all. It had only left her alone with the evidence.
