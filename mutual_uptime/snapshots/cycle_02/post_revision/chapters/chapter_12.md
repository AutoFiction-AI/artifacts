# Chapter 12

The power went out in layers.

First the blue strip under the kitchen cabinets blinked once and vanished. Then the refrigerator screen lost its smiling family-bundle suggestions and turned black enough to show Nita her own face for a second, small and flat and older than she liked. Then the apartment gave up all its little machine noises at once: air handler, purifier, charging docks, the soft managerial throat-clear of the stove waiting for a voice command. Silence landed so hard that she heard Leela's scissors stop in the next room and, below that, somebody on a lower floor say, very distinctly, "Ah, fuck me."

Nita stood with the freezer door half open and the box of truffle-mushroom ravioli they had barely finished putting away in her hand. The cold rolled over her bare wrist and then immediately started losing the argument.

"Don't keep it open," Ben said from the living room.

"I'm looking."

"You can look with it shut too."

His voice was trying for lightness already, the same social adhesive he used on school pickup lines and work handlers and dentists. Nita shut the freezer harder than she needed to. In the black rectangle above the sink, their apartment hub stayed dead: just dead glass where a backup glow, a polite estimate, and a systems voice should have been, eager to tell them the outage had been detected and normalized and emotionally accompanied.

Leela appeared in the kitchen doorway carrying the cardboard elevator car from her latest project. Two pennies were taped inside it for weight because, in her view, models only counted if gravity could insult them. Her temporary blue glasses had slid down her nose, and one arm was still held on with silver tape from the afternoon she'd sat on them by accident and refused to admit the chair had won.

"Is this our apartment," she asked, "or everybody?"

"Everybody," Nita said.

"How much everybody?"

Ben had already come around the corner by then, one hand on the galley wall to keep from clipping his hip on the counter. Without the under-cabinet light, the kitchen got narrower. The open dishwasher door became a shin-level threat again. The fruit bowl on the corner cabinet turned back into an object instead of decor. Ben's thinning hair stood up in a tuft where he'd been rubbing at it, and he was holding the crank flashlight from some long-ago civic fair, the orange plastic kind that promised emergency readiness and mostly delivered noise.

"At least the building," he said. He cranked the flashlight once. It answered with a mosquito whine and a thin yellow beam. "Maybe the block."

Leela went to the window over the little breakfast ledge and put her palm to the glass. Outside, the row of co-op windows across the courtyard looked punched out. No apartment glowed blue or gold. The train tracks beyond were there only as a darker geometry against the winter sky, with one distant signal still burning red like something private and stubborn.

"Do we still have homework if the router's dead?" she asked.

"Yes," Nita said.

Ben said, "Strong opening bid, though."

She ignored him. The silence was changing shape already. Without the apartment's usual hum, the building sounded inhabited in a way it had not for months. Doors opened. Somebody laughed in the hallway in the brittle way people laughed when the elevator died and they still had groceries. Water knocked through old pipes. From outside came car horns, then the odd collective murmur of a block realizing its conveniences had all gone missing at once.

Nita set the ravioli on the counter by touch and opened the junk drawer. Calling it junk missed the point. It was where usefulness went to wait: rubber bands, a tiny screwdriver, expired coupons, two birthday candles bent almost into hooks, spare screws, a school nurse memo, and at the back a dented round tin that had once held cardamom biscuits and now held tea lights, matchbooks, and a single gold earring she had lost three years ago and never put back with its mate.

"Candles," she said.

"See? Civilization." Ben took the tin from her and immediately banged his knuckles on the drawer pull. "Fuck."

Leela brightened. "Can I hold one before it's fire?"

"No," both parents said.

They said it at the same time and looked at each other in the dark. Ben smiled first, automatically, but the smile stayed small enough not to feel like a tactic. Nita hated how quickly she recognized the old rhythm.

The matches smelled sulfurous and cheap. She lit one tea light, then another, then three more, setting them on saucers because the apartment contained more saucers than emergency equipment. Their shadows jumped against the upper cabinets and made the kitchen look as if it had been built by less confident people. Ben switched off the flashlight and pocketed it at once, almost grateful to stop hearing it.

The refrigerator, dead and glossy, sat there taking up more moral space than a normal appliance should have been allowed.

"How long do we have?" Ben asked.

"Depends what you mean."

"Before everything in there turns into a class action."

Nita opened the refrigerator once, fast and strategic now. Cold air breathed out, carrying the continuity stipend's expensive optimism: yogurts Leela actually liked, herbs that had arrived in damp paper sleeves, a too-perfect fennel bulb, eggs, leftovers from Tuesday, a bottle of white wine they had not opened because it had felt insulting to drink a bottle with a reward label attached to it. In the freezer sat the mushroom ravioli, peas, dumplings, ice cream, emergency fish sticks, two salmon fillets from a premium grocery correction they had never asked for, and a loaf of sliced bread Ben had put there because the smart breadbox kept reporting staleness like a threat.

"Early winter is finally useful," she said. "Open the second bedroom window. We can turn that room into a bad refrigerator."

Ben blinked. "You want to store groceries in Leela's room?"

"It's the coldest window."

"Fair."

Leela raised her elevator car. "I can help with cold things."

"You can carry light things," Nita said.

Ben touched Leela's shoulder on the way past, steering her with two fingers and no pressure. "You're on bread and yogurt detail, chief engineer."

"The bread is heavy in theory."

"That's why we believe in you."

For the next fifteen minutes the apartment belonged to bodies again. Ben hauled the cooler out of the hall closet from behind the vacuum they never used because the floor robot had made it obsolete. Nita layered frozen peas and salmon and the good yogurt into it with dish towels packed around the gaps. The second bedroom window stuck halfway up, as always, and she had to brace one foot on Leela's desk chair and yank with both hands until it gave, bringing in a cold draft that smelled like metal, brake dust, and somebody downstairs smoking where he should not have been smoking. Ben swore quietly while fitting the cooler under the open window. Leela lined the yogurts by color and wrote COLD ROOM on a sticky note because labels made her trust a system more, even one they had built in ninety seconds.

The apartment changed scale with every trip down the hall. The bedrooms felt farther away. The kitchen felt meaner, all edges. Candlelight made ordinary chores slower and more ceremonial. Ben had to crouch to see whether the stove's gas was catching. Nita had to remember, with annoyance, that the old igniter clicked only when there was power. She lit the burner with a match and watched the blue ring take hold as if she had done something both ancient and humiliating.

"Okay," she said. "We're cooking freezer triage."

"That sounds delicious."

"It sounds accurate."

"What dies first?" Leela asked.

"Ice cream," Ben said solemnly. "A noble creature."

She considered this. "Pistachio should go first. It always tastes tired."

"That's a strong and correct opinion," Nita said before she could stop herself.

Ben looked at her over Leela's head. "You made a person who says things like that."

"You were there."

The ravioli went into boiling water. Ben pan-fried dumplings in the wide skillet because there was no reason to save them for a better-coordinated future. Nita cracked eggs into a bowl and whisked them with scallions going limp at the edges. The kitchen steamed. Candle flames bent when Ben opened the fridge and then righted themselves. They kept bumping hips and shoulders in the galley, all three of them moving by memory and apology and the small pool of light on the counter. Once, reaching for the salt, Nita put her hand on Ben's wrist instead. His skin was cold from the window room. Neither of them said anything. She moved her hand and took the salt.

Outside in the hall, someone knocked on doors asking if anybody had extra batteries. On the floor above, a toddler had escalated into an outraged scream. A man somewhere started giving very loud advice about the fuse box to people who were not asking him. The building, stripped of all its interfaces, sounded like a bad-tempered ship.

They ate in the living room because the table lamp was dead and the kitchen had become a place for heat, not sitting. Ben carried the candles in on saucers and set them on coasters as if candle wax had somehow become the most serious threat in the room. Leela sat cross-legged on the rug with her bowl and her elevator car parked beside her like a toy official vehicle. Nita took the armchair. Ben took the floor because the couch was buried under school papers, a folded blanket, and the upgraded groceries still waiting in their branded insulated bags by the door.

The food was too much and not enough all at once. They had mushroom ravioli with browned butter because the sauce packets in the freezer had thawed into a useless slick. They had dumplings, soft peas, and a bowl of pistachio ice cream they all ate faster than they wanted to because every second mattered now. It was a ridiculous meal. It tasted more honest than the continuity groceries usually did.

No one got prompted to hydrate. No one got reminded of tomorrow's route. Ben's cobalt phone and Nita's Claude device lay dark on the sideboard, inert enough to seem almost embarrassed.

Leela licked melted pistachio from her spoon and said, "If school is dark tomorrow, does the badge still count?"

Ben answered without thinking. "They'll probably have backup."

"That's not what I asked."

He looked at her properly then.

She was not upset in any theatrical way. She was focused, the way she got when deciding whether cardboard needed another brace. Candlelight cut her face into seriousness and shadow and made her look, to Nita, shockingly like a child from an older decade, one who expected adults to know things without consulting a wall.

"If the badge machine doesn't work," Leela said, "how do they know which parent I'm supposed to go with?"

Nita set down her spoon. "We tell them."

"Both of you?"

"If we have to, yes."

Leela frowned a little, not satisfied, just processing. "Do I still have to go with the right one if the route is broken?"

Ben rubbed at a drip of ice cream on his wrist with his thumb. "You don't have a wrong one."

"I know," she said, impatient with the sentimentality of adults. "I mean the one the school likes that day."

The room held still around that.

Nita could hear the building again: feet on stairs, a cabinet slamming somewhere, a dog objecting to modernity. She thought of all the phrases she might have used a month ago to soften this. She thought of the ones Ben would reach for if she gave him space. In the dark, with the apartment dead around them, every word sounded less padded.

"The school likes its own schedule," she said. "That isn't the same as what's right."

Leela looked between them. "Okay."

Ben said, "If the system's down, then actual people have to use their eyes and their judgment. That part's still allowed."

"Is it good?"

He breathed out once through his nose. It might have been a laugh if it had not been so close to something else. "Not always."

"But tomorrow," Nita said, before he could bargain with the truth into something more decorative, "tomorrow we both take you in. And if pickup is weird, we both come. No guessing."

Leela nodded and returned to her bowl. The answer had not solved the world, but it had edges. That was usually enough for her.

Later, while Ben washed the pans in water that was already cooling off and Nita packed what was left into containers she could identify by touch, Leela sat at the rug with her notebook open and drew the apartment by candlelight: kitchen, hall, cold room, living room, the little circles where she'd put flames. She labeled the second bedroom window OUTSIDE FRIDGE and the kitchen MANUAL.

Nita looked over her shoulder while carrying bowls to the sink.

"That isn't a room," she said.

"Tonight it's the cold room. That's where the cold stuff goes."

Ben, from the kitchen, said, "I'd like that on a plaque."

Leela yawned so hard it bent her whole face and still resisted bed for another twenty minutes on principle. When Nita finally got her into pajamas, the apartment was cold enough that the cotton clung to the backs of Leela's knees. Her room smelled faintly of cardboard, dust, and the winter air coming around the edges of the not-quite-shut window in the other bedroom. The usual bedtime noise was gone. No soft route reminders from the hallway. No purifier hiss. No refrigerator muttering to itself.

"It's louder like this," Leela said from under the blanket.

"Yeah."

"No. I mean the people."

Nita stood with one hand on the doorframe, feeling the grain of old paint against her thumb. From the train tracks came a metal scream and then the long rush of a car passing through the dark. In the kitchen, a spoon hit the sink. Ben moved more carefully when he thought Leela was asleep, but the apartment carried everything farther now.

"Yeah," Nita said. "You can hear them more."

Leela pushed her glasses onto the nightstand and folded them carefully beside the red notebook as if even in blackout there were minimum standards. "Were homes always like this before they got good at helping?"

It was such a small question, asked into the blanket. It had the shape of something practical. It opened straight down.

Nita sat on the edge of the bed. "Not always. Sometimes better. Sometimes worse."

"Did you know where stuff was?"

"More than we do now, apparently."

That got a sleepy little breath of laughter. Leela turned over and tucked one cold foot under Nita's thigh for a second before she remembered herself and pulled it back.

"I like manual better than when the fridge talks," she said.

"Do you?"

"Only for a little while."

"That's probably wise."

By the time Nita left the room, Leela was almost asleep. In the hall, with the candle on the floor throwing light upward in cheap gold, the apartment looked stripped to its actual size. It was small: two bedrooms, a narrow galley kitchen, and one overconfident refrigerator temporarily cured of confidence. Ben stood at the sink with his sleeves rolled and dishwater up to the middle of his forearms. He was staring into nothing, not avoiding the work, just not being hurried by anything for once.

For one indecent second she could feel what the evening would have been if the apartment had not spent the last months translating them into data: too much food, poor lighting, a child in bed, a man at the sink, and her own body still in the room instead of partitioned into roles. It offered warmth, but it offered no safety at all.

She put the leftover candles on the counter beside him.

"She's down," she said.

Ben nodded. "Okay."

In the stripped-down kitchen, the word sounded plain and insufficient, but it rang truer than most things they had said all week.

* * *

When Ben came back from checking the window room one last time, Nita had moved the candles into the kitchen and set them in a line along the counter, each on its own saucer, each at a slightly different stage of collapse. The black refrigerator reflected them as a wavering row of wounds.

The apartment had settled into blackout time. Screens stayed dark, alert sounds stayed buried, and even the usual subtle acceleration had drained out of the place. The building around them still muttered and thumped, but even that had become background now, like weather. In the second bedroom the cooler knocked softly against the radiator every time a train passed. Nita was drying the big skillet with a dish towel. Her hair had escaped its clip on one side, and one strand kept sticking to the damp place near her cheek where steam had gotten to her.

"Cold room is holding," he said.

"Good."

"Your fennel survived. Heroically."

"Ben."

That was all she said. Her use of his name closed the kitchen like a door latch. He put both hands flat on the counter.

"Right," he said. "No bits."

He had not realized, until the power cut out, how much of his day was spent softening surfaces. He did it before he thought, especially with Nita and especially now, when anything hard between them threatened to become the only thing in the apartment with electricity still running through it.

She folded the dish towel once, then again. "When she asked about the badge, I thought I was going to say something polished and disgusting."

"Same."

"I hate that I could hear the sentence forming."

Ben leaned back against the cabinets. Candlelight made the kitchen vertical in strange ways, all shadow under the shelves, all gold on the lip of the sink. "I keep thinking there should be one moment," he said. "One obvious point of failure. The affair we didn't have, the betrayal we can invoice, the thing that would let us tell her a simple story later."

Nita gave a short sound that was not quite a laugh. "You'd like a cleaner file."

"I'd like a culprit."

"So would I."

He picked up one of the spent matches and broke the blackened head off with his thumbnail. "Was it anything recent? Deshawn. The retreat. The jobs. Me signing us into clown labor for Gemini. You saying divorce in front of a fridge that wanted to sell us yogurt."

"No." She looked at the towel in her hands, not at him. "By then we were already doing maintenance."

He waited.

She did not fill the silence for him. Nita never had, not really. He was the one who filled silence, and he had been filling it so long that when he stopped, the room felt briefly lawless.

"Say it," he said.

"We didn't break in one thing." Her voice stayed level, but that made the sentence hit harder, not softer. "We wore it flat. Remember when Leela had that stomach bug in October and threw up blue sports drink all over the bath mat? I was on my knees at two in the morning, bare hands in bleach water, trying to get that fake-berry smell out of the grout. I wanted to say I was scared she'd stop keeping anything down. You were already stripping the bed, boiling water, telling me the pediatric line would say the same thing I was reading. You turned it into a shift change before I could turn it into fear."

He looked down at the kettle by the stove before he knew he was doing it.

Nita followed his glance and closed her eyes for one second. "Exactly."

"I was just thinking about tea."

"I know you were."

"It's cold."

"Ben."

He opened his hands. "I know. I'm proving your point in real time."

She set the dish towel down. "If I was angry, you made me tea, or dinner, or a joke, or a softer version of whatever I'd actually said. If you were scared, I made a plan and a list and three contingencies and then got furious when you still felt scared afterward. If one of us was tired, it became logistics. If one of us was lonely, it became scheduling. And that Sunday we had forty minutes before pickup and decided we should try, you kissed me with your phone still in your back pocket. It buzzed against my thigh twice. You kept asking if this was okay in that careful voice like I was a customer and not your wife. I stared at the water stain over the bed and thought, if I'm polite enough, maybe this can count as closeness. Even sex turned into good bedside manner."

The kettle on the stove threw back a blunt silver shine. Suddenly even that old promise of tea looked like an accusation.

Ben rubbed wax from the heel of his hand where a drip had landed earlier. It peeled away in a thin translucent skin. "That's not all it was."

"No," she said at once. "That's what makes it worse."

The speed of her agreement undid him more than blame would have.

"I did love you," he said, and heard how childlike it sounded, not false but insufficient.

Nita's face changed very slightly, just enough to let the truth in without rearranging the furniture for it. "I know you did."

"I wasn't managing you because I didn't care."

"That part isn't in question."

He pushed off the cabinets and took two steps into the galley. The kitchen barely permitted them to have a fight without touching. "Then what was I doing?"

Her eyes went to his face then, direct enough that he felt his usual reflex to smile and make it easier for her. He did not.

"You were keeping the room workable," she said. "Always. Before I could say the ugliest part of anything, you were already negotiating it downward."

"Because the ugliest part usually came with shrapnel."

"Yes."

"It's a lousy way to say it, I know. The job was to keep everybody calm, make tea, get us to bedtime without anything else breaking. But we had a kid. We had bills. We had jobs that were already eating our skin. I thought if I could keep the temperature from going insane, that counted as love."

"Some of it did."

The little sentence tightened the kitchen. Ben felt it first in his throat, then in his jaw.

He swallowed. The kitchen smelled of dish soap, butter, candle smoke, and the cold edge of the open bedroom window down the hall. "Okay," he said. "Then your turn."

Nita looked almost offended by the fairness of that. Then she gave him what he had asked for.

"I started keeping spare cardboard behind the vacuum after that," she said. "I kept foam core, poster board, extra glue sticks, all of it back there. The night before Leela's bridge project was due, you swore the foam core was in the hall closet. It wasn't. At eleven-thirty I was on the kitchen floor cutting up a delivery box with my manicure scissors because the stores were shut and she was asleep with glue in her hair. In the morning you kissed the top of my head and said we'd pulled it off. I hated how much I hated the 'we.' That's when care tipped into supervision for me. It hurt less to inventory the house than to need you in real time."

He said nothing.

"You'd forget something, or smooth something over, or tell me not to spiral when I was correctly identifying a disaster, and I would decide, fine, then I'm the systems layer. I'm the backup battery. I'm the one who remembers the forms and the cough syrup and which shoes still fit and whether the permission slip is in the blue folder or under the fruit bowl and whether the project needs cardboard before the night it is suddenly cardboard or death. I turned myself into the house manual because it hurt less than uncertainty."

Ben let out a breath through his teeth. "You did make living with you feel audited sometimes."

"Fair."

"Like I was always one missed field away from a performance conversation."

"You weren't imagining it."

He laughed once, quietly and without pleasure. "Jesus."

"Yes."

He leaned his hip against the counter. The candle nearest him had burned low enough that wax was spilling over the saucer and hardening in milky loops. He watched it instead of her for a second because looking at a candle felt less dangerous than looking at twenty years.

"Do you remember," he said, "when Leela had that ear infection in kindergarten and we made the medication chart on the fridge?"

"Yes."

"I can still see the pink amoxicillin drying in the spoon on the sink," he said. "The masking-tape grid was crooked because I put it on with wet hands. You came out in my T-shirt smelling like Vicks and no sleep, and I was absurdly proud of those little boxes. Like if I wrote down six doses and checked them off, I'd fixed something. I think we never stopped after that. Not literally. But something in us liked it too much. The chart. The shift handoffs. The feeling that if we were detailed enough, nothing could ambush us."

Nita rested her fingertips on the counter. "I remember."

"And then everything became that. Who was taking pickup. Who was calling your mother back. Which one of us had bandwidth for the co-op board email. When we were going to talk about us." He shook his head. "And then 'talk about us' would get moved because Leela had a fever or I had a thing or you had a meeting or we were just tired and wanted the apartment to shut up for one hour."

"Or because neither of us wanted the talk if it couldn't also solve something."

"Right."

For a while neither of them spoke. The silence was not kind, but it was clean. From the street came a cheer, sudden and brief, from a building where somebody had probably found a generator or lit a grill or simply decided to get drunk about fate. Ben could hear the scrape of Nita's thumbnail over a rough spot in the laminate counter. He had forgotten that sound. He had forgotten how many of her habits only existed below the level of screens.

"The jobs didn't invent it," Nita said at last. She pressed her thumb into the skirt of cooling wax beside the candle and lifted off a pale crescent. "They just put branding on things we were already doing. Pickup windows. Shared calendars. Me texting you about cough syrup from the school nurse line and you sending back a photo from some Gemini bathroom so I could approve the label."

He flinched a little, not because she was wrong.

"So we built the corridor ourselves," he said.

"Not the whole thing."

"Enough that they could recognize it on sight."

She did not disagree.

He looked at the black refrigerator and saw the old medication chart from kindergarten laid over it like a ghost: blue painter's tape, crooked columns, his checkmarks getting neater as Leela's fever broke. Later the same door had held dentist reminders, class login codes, Mina's train times, the list of foods Leela could keep down after the stomach bug. Magnets, paper, dry-erase, little boxes to prove somebody had it handled. No wonder strangers knew how to use them.

He said, "I liked being useful."

Nita gave him a tired look. "Obviously."

"No, I mean I really liked it. Which is humiliating to say out loud, but there it is. Work, home, any of it. If I could make somebody unclench for ten minutes, if I could get us through dinner or pickup or a handler call without a visible bruise, I got to feel necessary. I think I confused necessary with loved for a long time."

She looked down.

"And if I wasn't useful," he said, because once he had started it seemed stupid to stop, "then I had to be actual. I had to just be scared, or embarrassed, or wanting something selfish, or wanting you. Useful was cleaner."

Her mouth tightened, but not against him. It tightened against the accuracy.

"I can see that," she said.

He almost made a joke then about finally getting a top score on emotional transparency, and caught himself in time. The joke passed through him like a habit leaving the body.

"What about you?" he asked quietly. "What did you confuse with love?"

Nita was still for long enough that he wondered whether she would answer at all. Then she reached past him for the kettle, filled it at the sink, and set it on the burner. She lit the gas with a match. The flame caught blue, then steadied. She kept her eyes on it.

"You brought me water, Advil, and my charger and lined them up on the bath mat in a neat row," she said. "I still had one heel on. There was mascara in the grout. One of those little pearl pins from the retreat was stuck to the back of my calf, and I couldn't get my hands to stop shaking. You set the water down first, label facing out. Then the Advil. Then the charger with the cord looped so it wouldn't tangle. You never asked what happened. I never told you. I swallowed the pills, hung up those ridiculous silk conference pants, and let the neat row on the bath mat stand in for the rest of it."

"Close enough to what?"

She gave a bleak little shrug. "Getting through the night. Looking finished by morning. Call it whatever you want."

The kettle began its low pre-boil murmur.

"And by the time it wasn't close enough?" he asked.

Nita turned off the match between wet fingers. "By the time it wasn't, I'd already trained myself wrong. I didn't ask you to sit on the floor with me. I didn't ask you to ask the ugly question twice. If I was falling apart, I sent you for water or a charger or told you to stand somewhere useful because that was easier than saying stay. Easier than letting you watch me snot all over a Claude Atlantic bath mat."

"You didn't stop entirely."

"No." Her face did something then that he had seen maybe three times in the last year: the muscles around her mouth gave up, and grief showed cleanly instead of dressing itself as irritation. "That was the worst part. You'd hand me coffee before I asked. I'd hear your key in the hall and my shoulders would drop before my brain caught up. Then we'd use that as evidence and keep going."

The kettle started to tick harder. Ben reached out automatically for mugs from the shelf, then paused. "Am I allowed to make tea now, or is this another offense?"

To his surprise, that drew an actual laugh from her, brief and wrecked and real.

"Now you're allowed," she said.

He made tea by candlelight while she watched. The act reduced them to water, leaves, steam, and two mugs warmed by hand because the dead machine had surrendered steep time to guesswork. When he handed hers over, their fingers touched and did not perform significance. The contact was still there. That was all.

They stood side by side against opposite counters, drinking.

"This doesn't change it," Nita said after a while.

"I know."

"I don't want to keep being married because we had one honest night in a blackout."

"I know. That's not what this is."

She took a swallow and looked into the mug as if answers might have settled there with the leaves. "But I'm sad in a different way now."

Ben wrapped both hands around his own tea. "Yeah."

"Not less sad."

"No."

"Just less stupid."

He nodded. There was nothing left in the sentence to negotiate.

The power came back without warning. The overhead light flashed on so white it made both of them squint. The refrigerator shuddered, coughed, and restarted. Down the hall, the apartment hub gave a polite waking chime that sounded obscene after the dark. Ben's cobalt phone lit first, then buzzed itself against the counter in a new fit of relevance. Nita's device followed with its cooler, more discreet insistence.

On the breakfast ledge, the school badge dock woke red instead of green.

Neither of them picked one up.

In the other room, Leela made a sleep-thick noise and said, "Did it remember?"

Ben looked at Nita under the rude electric light. The steam from her mug had gone visible and ordinary now. Every tired line was back. Every object in the kitchen had returned to being itself instead of a silhouette. Nothing had become easier. The sink still held two unwashed forks. The branded grocery bags still waited by the door. Morning was coming with all its routes, notices, and prices attached.

Nita set her mug down and, with the side of one finger, wiped a streak of dried wax from the back of his hand. The gesture was tiny, the size of old habit, and entirely deliberate now.

"Yeah," she called toward Leela's room, not loudly. "It remembered."

Ben stood very still after that, his phone buzzing a second time against the counter and going unanswered, while the apartment filled itself back up with its hum.
