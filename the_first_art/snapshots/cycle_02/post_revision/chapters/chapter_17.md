# Chapter 17

At the stone table in the innermost room, farther from the blocked outer corridor, the coupling pattern was almost visible. Gavin had been staring at the same three lines of notation for the better part of an hour, the manuscript's representational shifts moving in slow cycles that his translation framework could only approximate, and he thought he was beginning to see it — not understand it, not yet, but see it. The ward-maintenance cycle and the substrate's ground state shared something. A resonance. A harmonic. The Builders' notation for this was dense and layered, showing relationships that collapsed when he tried to render them in the runic shorthand Babbling had given him, but the shape of the thing was there. The wards reached into the substrate's base grammar and synchronized with it, and when that synchronization held, the wards regenerated. Or reinforced. Or something the Builders had a glyph for that mapped onto no English verb he knew.

He was reaching for his quill to make a note when the substrate screamed.

Not screamed — there was no sound. But the perceptual channel the First Art had opened in his cognition, the one that made the substrate feel as immediate as heat on skin, convulsed with input. Hundreds of spells, all at once. Combat magic. The specific churn of offensive and defensive castings meeting, rebounding, shattering, recasting. Trying to read through it was like holding a book up to a lamp at point-blank range — not painful, exactly, but obliterating.

He gripped the edge of the stone table. The stone was cold under his fingers — it was always cold down here, the sub-collection's deep limestone chill that never warmed regardless of the season — and he held on to the solidity of it while his substrate perception convulsed. The delicate coupling pattern he'd been studying drowned in the noise. He couldn't distinguish the ward-maintenance notation from the cacophony of war magic flooding the layer beneath everything. It was like trying to hear a single voice in a stadium. The voice was still there — the coupling was real, the resonance was real — but it was buried under a wall of signal he couldn't filter.

The battle. The one everyone had been expecting. Harry Potter and whatever the rest of it was — the Order, the DA, the resistance movement Miren had been feeding supplies into for months. He'd felt the preliminary disturbances earlier in the evening: the castle's defensive enchantments engaging, the massive protections someone — McGonagall, probably — had raised around the school grounds. Those had registered as a distant hum in the substrate, tolerable, the kind of ambient interference he'd learned to work through. This was different. This was hundreds of people trying to kill each other simultaneously, and every spell was a substrate event, and the aggregate was a white-out in the perceptual channel he couldn't close.

He waited. Maybe the fighting would settle into a pattern, something rhythmic enough to read past, like the ticking of a clock after an hour in the same room. It didn't. Combat magic was chaotic by nature — no two spells at the same interval, no regularity to the destruction, just raw concurrent noise.

A minute passed. Two. The noise thickened. Someone in the castle above him was casting Fiendfyre — he could feel it as a specific kind of substrate agony, the cursed fire's magical feedback loop kicking itself larger and larger, a note that swelled until everything around it went thin. He flinched. The coupling pattern was gone. The fragile connection he'd been tracing between the ward-maintenance cycle and the substrate's ground state had dissolved like ink in moving water, and he'd need to start over, and he couldn't start over while the substrate was screaming.

"Shit," he said, to the manuscript, to the luminous walls, to the cold mineral air of the sub-collection's innermost room.

He sat for another five minutes, jaw tight, the tremor in his left hand doing its familiar flutter against the table edge. The Fiendfyre subsided — someone had put it out or it had consumed what it was fed — but the general combat noise only worsened. More spells, more casters, more disruption. The pattern he'd been studying was irretrievable. He'd been weeks getting close to it — weeks of twelve-hour nights in this room, translating the same dense passages, trying to hold the shape of the coupling mechanism in his mind long enough to see its structure. And now the thread was gone, scattered by the noise of a war he wasn't in, and the irritation he felt was so sharp it tasted like metal in the back of his throat. He knew, in the distant way you know things about yourself that you can't do anything about, that the irritation was the wrong reaction. That the right reaction would be fear, or urgency, or the basic survival instinct that sent those three third-years running for the Room of Requirement. But the wards were failing and the coupling mechanism was the key and the war had just cost him weeks of work, and what he felt was angry.

He packed the manuscript pages into his bag one by one, careful with them, as he always was. The bag was a school satchel with an ink stain on the front flap from third year. He slung it over his shoulder, pushed his chair back from the stone table, and walked through the three outer rooms of the sub-collection — the shelves carved into ancient stone, the self-lit walls, the dry cold that smelled like limestone — and, where the blockage in the outer corridor had shifted enough to leave a narrow way through, stepped into the seventh-floor corridor.

***

Sound arrived first. The real kind, the kind that worked on eardrums. Explosions from somewhere below — deep concussive impacts that he felt through the floor. A structural groaning from the castle walls, stone stressed past its tolerance. Voices: screaming, shouting spells, a high frantic wailing that could have been a student or the castle's own wards protesting the damage being done to them.

The corridor floor was covered in grit and stone dust. A section of ceiling had come down thirty feet from where he stood, rubble piled waist-high and a jagged hole punched through to the floor above. Through the hole he could see flashes of red and green light. Spellfire.

He stepped over the rubble. A suit of armor had toppled against the wall, its halberd lying across the corridor like a barricade. He edged around it. The suit's visor had been blown open, and the interior was scorched.

He needed to go down. The fighting was concentrated on the upper floors and the grounds, judging by the substrate distortion — the signal was strongest above him and to the north, where the main entrance opened onto the grounds and the lakeshore. If he went down and south, toward the third or second floor, the classrooms on the quieter side of the castle, he might find enough distance from the combat to work. The substrate noise wouldn't vanish, but it might thin to something he could think through.

He took the staircase at the end of the corridor. One entire landing was missing — blasted away, the stone steps dangling over open air, a fifteen-foot drop to the floor below where he could see more rubble and what looked like a fallen torch bracket still smoldering. He gripped the iron railing and climbed around the gap where the landing had been, his bag swinging, his wet shoes slipping on broken stone, his fingers white on the rail. He kept going down.

On the sixth floor, three students ran past him. Two girls and a boy, younger than him — third-years, maybe — heading for the staircase that led up to the Room of Requirement. One of the girls was carrying a tabby cat pressed against her chest with both arms, the cat's mouth open and panting, its claws sunk into her robes. She had tear-tracks cutting through the dust on her face but she wasn't crying now. She was just running. None of them looked at Gavin. He stepped to the side to let them pass and continued down.

On the fifth-floor landing, a window had blown in. Night air came through, cool and smelling of smoke and wet earth and the green scent of May. Past the broken glass he could see the grounds — not the gentle darkness of an ordinary night but the violent flickering of combat. The green-white flash of Killing Curses in the distance. The orange glow of something burning near the Quidditch pitch. Figures moving on the lawn, small at this height, casting and falling and casting again. The sound was closer here: the percussive snap of dueling magic, someone yelling a name he didn't catch.

He turned the corner into the fifth-floor corridor and stopped. Two people were fighting ahead of him. A student — an older boy Gavin didn't know, Ravenclaw by his tie, backed against a stone column — was casting Shield Charms that kept splintering on impact. The Death Eater facing him was a broad-shouldered woman with her hair tied up in a tight knot. She had a wedding ring on her wand hand, and her robes had been altered — hemmed shorter at the ankle, practically, as if she'd expected to be on her feet tonight and had prepared for it. She cast with the unhurried regularity of someone hammering a nail, each spell hitting the boy's shield and breaking a little more of it.

The duel blocked the direct route. There was a side passage twenty feet back that would take him around to the next staircase.

Gavin turned and took the side passage. Behind him, the Ravenclaw's shield broke — he heard it go, the specific brittle sound of a Protego failing under sustained force. Gavin glanced back because the sound made him look. The next spell hit the boy high in the shoulder and spun him into the column hard enough that the back of his head cracked against stone. He slid down, leaving a dark wet smear on the pale rock, and ended twisted on the floor with one leg folded under him and his wand two feet away. The Death Eater stepped forward. Gavin kept walking. The side passage was narrow and cold and empty, and he followed it to the fourth-floor staircase.

On the fourth floor, the fighting was sparser. A portrait had been burned through, the canvas curled and blackened, the frame still on the wall, the subject gone. The neighboring portraits were empty too — fled to frames in safer parts of the castle, or just fled. Water was running from somewhere — a burst pipe, maybe, or a spell that had hit plumbing — pooling along the flagstones in a thin sheet that reflected the torchlight in wavering orange. He stepped through it. His shoes soaked immediately, the cold spreading through the leather. There was glass on the floor too, from a shattered window or a broken display case, and he heard it crunch under his steps and adjusted his path without thinking about it — automatic as stepping around a puddle on pavement.

He was crossing the fourth-floor landing, heading for the stairs down, when the curse came.

A corridor opened to his right. He'd been passing it without looking — his attention was mostly on the substrate distortion and whether the signal was weakening as he descended (it was, slightly, which supported the idea that the castle's enchantment architecture acted as a propagation medium with distance-dependent attenuation). A man came out of the corridor at a run, a thin man in Death Eater robes that were too large for him, the sleeves hanging past his knuckles so that only his fingers and his wand were visible. He had blood on his face — his own or someone else's, there was no way to tell — and he was casting at everything in front of him, panicked, not aiming. His wand moved in sloppy arcs and spells flew from it in a scatter.

One of the spells came at Gavin. A Stunner, probably, or possibly something worse — the wand movement was imprecise enough that the caster himself might not have known what he was throwing.

Gavin was thinking about the castle's enchantment architecture as a substrate waveguide. If the castle's own permanent enchantments created a structured medium for substrate propagation, then the distortion from combat magic wouldn't follow a simple falloff with distance — it would follow the castle's magical geometry, channeling along corridors where the enchantments were densest, pooling in junctions, and possibly creating dead zones where the architecture cancelled the noise out. The thought was half-formed, more geometry than language. The curse came at him from the right, a red-white streak in his peripheral vision.

The reflex was faster than thought. He didn't decide to do anything. The substrate around the incoming spell — the specific grammatical structure that allowed magical force to propagate along that trajectory with that intent — became less valid. It didn't meet a shield or a counterspell. The agreement that made the curse a curse relaxed, loosening like a knot in warm water. Between one foot of air and the next, the spell lost coherence. It wasn't deflected or absorbed. It simply stopped being a spell. The light went out of it and there was nothing there.

Gavin turned the corner and continued toward the staircase, still working through the waveguide idea. If the castle created dead zones, and if he could sense where they were, he might find a workspace with genuine substrate quiet even during a battle. The key would be finding a room at the intersection of —

Behind him, in the corridor he'd just left, the Death Eater was standing still. Gavin was already gone, already thinking about something else, and the man's confusion — the staring at his own wand, the checking whether the wand still worked, the baffled look at the empty space where his spell had been heading — belonged to a story Gavin was not in.

He found the classroom on the third floor by trying doors. The first two were locked. The third opened onto a room that had been used for Arithmancy — desks shoved against the walls, a blackboard still covered in chalk equations in a small precise hand, dust on every surface. The windows faced south, away from the heaviest fighting. Through the glass, the grounds were dark and quiet on this side of the castle. Inside, the substrate noise was still there — a constant grind at the edge of perception, like the hum of electrical equipment in a room you've been in too long — but it was manageable. He could think through it.

He pulled a chair to the center of the room, set his bag on the nearest desk, took out the manuscript pages, and arranged them in the order he'd been working from. The desk was wooden and scratched and had someone's initials carved into the corner — R.H., in careful letters, the work of a student who'd been bored in Arithmancy. He sat down. The chair creaked under him. The manuscript pages lay under his hands, their not-quite-parchment surface faintly warm as always, as though the material retained heat from a source that had nothing to do with the room's temperature.

He found the section on the ward-maintenance coupling. He picked up his quill. Outside, a long way off, something exploded and the windows rattled in their frames.

He worked.

***

He didn't know how long it had been when the door opened.

The substrate noise had shifted several times — peaks and troughs as the fighting concentrated and dispersed across the castle and grounds, weather systems of violence that he tracked with the peripheral attention he'd learned to keep on the substrate while the rest of his mind worked. The coupling pattern was coming back. With sustained concentration and the relative quiet of this room, he'd managed to relocate the section of notation that described the relationship between the ward-maintenance cycle and what he'd been thinking of as the substrate's idle state — the base condition of the magical grammar when no active spells were distorting it. The coupling was there. He could see the shape of the self-repair mechanism, could almost read the Builders' notation for how the wards and the substrate harmonized, and if he could hold the thread for another —

The door opened.

Miren stood in the doorframe.

She looked like the battle. Stone dust in her hair, her plait half undone, loose strands stuck to the sweat on her neck. A cut above her left eyebrow, deeper than a graze, still bleeding sluggishly — a line of blood had run into her eye and she'd wiped it away, smearing red across her temple. Her robes were torn at the right shoulder, showing the shirt underneath. A burn on her left forearm, the skin red and blistering in a stripe that ran from wrist to elbow, the kind of wound that came from a near-miss with something incendiary. She was gripping her wand in her right hand, her knuckles bloodless around it. She was breathing through her mouth — fast, shallow, the breathing of someone who had been running and fighting and couldn't stop either one.

She looked at Gavin. Sitting at a desk. The manuscript open. His quill in his hand. His bag on the floor.

Neither of them spoke for a moment. The muffled sounds of the battle came through the walls and floor, distant, like noise from another building.

"People are dying," Miren said.

"I know."

"Gavin." She took a breath. "People I — kids from our year. Ernie Macmillan's group got pinned down near the greenhouses. There's a kid — Colin something, fourth year, Gryffindor — he's been running messages between the barricades and he's tiny, he's going to get himself killed. There are Death Eaters inside the castle. In the corridors."

"I know."

"Then what the fuck are you doing."

It wasn't really a question. Her voice came out clipped and raw, worn thin by however many hours she'd spent out there. She wasn't trying to sound outraged. She looked too tired for that. She looked like she'd reached the point where this was simply what came out when she found her best friend sitting at a desk during a war.

"I'm working on the wards," he said. "The coupling between—"

"The wards." She said it flat. Not mocking. Flat was worse than mocking. "Right."

"Miren—"

"No. Don't." She wiped her face with the back of her wand hand, smearing blood from the cut across her cheekbone. She didn't seem to register doing it. "I thought you might be hurt. When I couldn't find you in the Great Hall, or at the barricades, or anywhere people were actually—" She gestured with her wand. At the walls. At the battle beyond them. "I went looking. Because I was worried."

He didn't answer. His left hand was trembling on the desk — the old Cruciatus tremor, which surfaced when his body was under stress even when his mind wasn't.

"And you're here." She looked at the manuscript spread across the desk. At his quill. At the notes he'd been making on a scrap of parchment in his ugly hybrid notation. "Doing this."

"It's not—"

"I know it's not homework, Gavin, I'm not—" She stopped. Pressed her lips together hard enough that they whitened. When she spoke again her voice was quieter. "Can you help?"

"With the wards, I think the coupling mechanism—"

"Not with the wards. Right now. The battle. Can you help with the battle?"

He looked at her. She had a smudge of ash on the side of her neck and that flat brightness in her eyes he'd learned to read as fear she was holding down by force. He'd known her since they were eleven. Usually he could tell what that look meant. This time he only knew she wasn't leaving without an answer.

"Yeah," he said. "I could do something."

Something shifted in her expression. "What do you mean, something?"

He put the quill down. His left hand was still trembling and he moved it to his lap.

"The combat magic. Not all of it from here. The battle's too loud for that. But the Killing Curse and the Cruciatus and a lot of the rest share the same bit of grammar underneath. In quiet I think I could cut that out everywhere. Right now I could do it locally — a corridor, a room, maybe a barricade if I was standing in it. Not block spells. Make that patch of air stop agreeing with them."

She stared at him. The cut over her eyebrow dripped. A drop of blood fell past her eyelid and she blinked it away.

"How long would that take?" she said.

"To set one up? A minute, maybe two, if I could hear it cleanly. It wouldn't hold. I'd have to keep doing it wherever the fighting moved." He stopped. Her face told him the technical specifics were not what she wanted. "Yeah. I could help."

"Then do it."

He'd known she would say it. He'd known since she walked through the door — maybe since the night months ago when he'd sat in the sub-collection and understood both what the First Art could do to the war and what the ward failure would do to everything, and understood that the two things could not both be his problem.

"I can't," he said. And then, because that was wrong and Miren could always tell when he was being imprecise: "I won't."

"You won't."

"If the wards fail, Miren — I've told you this. If the wards fail, everyone dies. Not the people in this castle. Everyone."

"Everyone's dying now."

"No. Some people are dying now." He rubbed his face. His fingers smelled like the manuscript — that faint mineral warmth that never entirely washed off. "And I could go make one corridor safer for a few minutes, or get one group through, and then another. But if I stop working on the wards for that, the wards keep failing. And the wards..."

He looked for a word that wouldn't sound monstrous and couldn't find one.

"The wards what?" she said.

"The battle is small," he said. "Compared to the other thing. That's — sorry, that's a terrible way to put it. But the battle is small."

Miren didn't respond. She stood in the doorway with blood drying at her temple and looked at him.

"People I know are dying," she said. "People you know."

"I know."

"Duncan's out there."

The name landed. Not in the substrate — in the old human place where names still had weight. Duncan. Thin, careful Duncan who ate slowly and laughed at bad puns and who had been systematically brutalized for ten months because of who his parents were.

"I know," Gavin said.

"And you won't help."

"If the wards collapse, Duncan dies anyway. Everyone does. And I'm the only person who's working on it. There isn't anyone else who can even see the wards. Nobody else knows they exist. If I stop—" He was aware his voice had gone flat under pressure, and he couldn't do anything about it. "There isn't anyone else."

"There might not be anyone left by morning."

"There won't be anyone left at all if the wards fail."

He heard himself. He knew how it sounded. He knew because he'd heard it before, inside his own head, across months of nights in the sub-collection while the school collapsed around him. The argument was rational and it sounded insane and both of those things were true.

The battle sounds filtered through the walls. Somewhere above them, something collapsed — a floor or a ceiling giving way, the deep groan of old stone breaking, the crash and rattle of debris settling.

Miren was quiet for a long time. When she spoke, her voice was different.

"You really believe this," she said. Not a question.

"Yeah."

"That the whole world's going to end."

"Not end. The wards fail, and what's outside them... it doesn't attack. It's just there. And if the wards aren't there, it and us are in the same space. And that's—" He'd tried to explain this before and it had never worked and it wasn't going to work now. "It'd be like trying to live underwater. Not because the water's trying to kill you. Just because you can't breathe it."

Miren looked at him. He looked back. She was still holding her wand in her white-knuckled grip. The burn on her forearm was blistering in the classroom's dusty light. She needed Dittany. She needed whoever was doing healing work down in the Great Hall to clean that cut and dress the burn and she'd been up here, looking for him, instead.

Her mouth went still. She looked from his face to the manuscript and back again, once, as if checking whether she'd heard him right. The cut over her eyebrow had started bleeding again. Blood slid down beside her nose and hung on her upper lip.

"Gavin," she said. Just his name.

"Yeah."

She didn't say anything else. She turned and walked out and pulled the door shut behind her. The latch clicked. Her footsteps went down the corridor — fast, because she was going back to the battle, because that was what Miren did with terrible things. She walked into them.

Gavin sat in the quiet classroom. The sounds of the distant battle came through the walls in broken pieces — a muffled boom, a run of spellfire, someone shouting from too far away to make out the words. Then feet hammered past his door. Something heavy hit the stone hard enough to jar the latch. The door jumped open an inch. Two students were dragging a boy in torn Gryffindor robes by the wrists, his heels knocking the flagstones. A red-haired girl ran beside him with both hands clamped over the right side of his stomach. Blood kept forcing itself between her fingers and painting the floor in short dark streaks. "Keep pressure on it," she said. "Don't you let go."

His left hand was trembling against his thigh. He lifted it onto the desk and pressed it flat against the wood, against the initials someone had carved there during Arithmancy, and held it until the tremor stilled.

The room smelled like chalk dust and old wood and, faintly, smoke. Through the south-facing windows, the grounds were dark. Miren was out there with her burn and her cut and her wand. Duncan was out there somewhere. The boy in the corridor was alive now, maybe, and bleeding into other people's hands. The whole war was out there, finishing or not finishing, and he was in here.

The manuscript page was in front of him. The coupling pattern. The ward-maintenance cycle. The thing he'd been close to before she walked in.

He picked up the quill. He worked.

***

The substrate noise shifted and changed as the hours passed. He tracked it with the peripheral awareness he'd developed over months of practice — the part of his attention that kept half an eye on the substrate's state at all times without pulling focus from the work at hand. Peaks of combat rolled through the castle in waves. A surge of dark magic on the ground floor, concentrated and vicious. A lull. Then fighting in the corridors again, spellfire crackling through the castle's magical architecture like static through wiring.

He kept trying to relocate the coupling pattern and kept losing it. Every time he thought he had the relation between the ward-maintenance cycle and the substrate's ground state, another burst of combat flexed the whole layer sideways and the notation flattened back into motion. He filled half a page with false starts in his ugly hybrid notation. None of them held.

Then the substrate shuddered.

He looked up from the manuscript. The sound from the battle hadn't changed — the same muffled booms and crashes, the same distant voices. But the substrate had convulsed with a single massive event. Two forces colliding. The distortion was enormous, pulling the substrate into a deep sag around the impact, a warping so large it made every other spell-event of the night seem like background static. Something was happening in the heart of the castle — the Great Hall, maybe, or somewhere close to it. Two concentrations of magical force, one pressed against the other, the substrate groaning under the weight of both.

He sat very still. The distortion peaked, held, and then a structure broke.

It was not a wall or a spell. It was a structure in the substrate itself. Something Gavin had perceived as a constant — a background feature of the magical world, a binding that connected dozens of individual signatures to a central node, a web of agreements that had been load-bearing for the whole architecture of dark magic that had defined wizarding Britain for the past year. It snapped. Instantly. Cleanly.

The substrate rippled outward from the break. It passed through the classroom, through him, through the castle walls. He felt it wash over the grounds outside. Dark Marks — he could perceive them as specific substrate agreements, bindings that tied individual casters to the central node — unraveled at the same time. Dozens of them. The agreements that sustained them stopped being valid and dissolved back into the substrate's ground state like salt into water.

The combat noise changed. The organized structure of the fighting — Death Eaters working in coordination, their magic synchronized through the shared binding — collapsed into confusion. The rhythm broke. Spells stuttered. A final surge of combat flared as the remaining fighters processed what they were feeling, and then the fighting started to die.

Potter. It had to have been Potter, though Gavin didn't know the details and didn't need them. From somewhere below the south windows came a shout that was half cheer, half sob. He glanced up in time to see a man in torn black robes reel sideways on the lawn, drop his wand, and clamp both hands over his left forearm like it had turned white-hot. The student facing him, in scorched school robes, hit him with a Stunner while he was still staring at his arm. He went down in the churned mud and didn't get up.

That would be it, then. The central node of the dark magical framework had gone, and every dependent structure was coming apart with it.

Good. The noise might thin enough now that he could work.

The classroom had done what he needed while the battle was at its loudest. The real work was still down in the limestone rooms, with the manuscript spread properly and enough stone around him to cut the worst of the interference. He stacked the pages, slid them into his satchel, and left.

The castle sounded wrong in a new way on the walk back. The steady battering of battle had broken into scattered bursts — one shout, two spells close together, someone crying behind a shut door, then nothing for ten seconds. On the fifth-floor landing a student sat against the wall with both hands locked around another girl's calf while blood soaked the torn sock and dripped between their fingers onto the stair. Gavin stepped around them when the kneeling girl snapped, "Mind the blood," without looking up.

By the time he reached the sub-collection, organized combat had thinned to scattered impacts that rolled through the stone every minute or two. He sat on the limestone floor with the manuscript open on his knees and tried to pull the coupling pattern back into focus before it flattened again. Another volley somewhere above dragged the substrate sideways. The pattern dissolved.

He turned the page and kept reading through the thinning noise.
