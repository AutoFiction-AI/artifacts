# Chapter 8

When he went back the following night, the notation in this section was denser than anything he'd encountered before. The patterns interlocked at scales his translational framework could barely hold — each decoded element revealing relationships to a dozen others, each of those connecting to a dozen more, until the structure he was building in his mind felt like trying to hold the shape of the entire castle at once when all you could manage was a single corridor.

Three hours passed. Or four. Time moved differently in the sub-collection — no windows, no change in the luminescent light from the walls, no sounds from the castle above. The stone rooms existed outside the rhythms of school life, and the longer he worked in them, the more that separation felt like a feature, not an accident. The sub-collection had not been designed for human scholars sitting on the floor with their legs going numb. If it had been designed at all, it had been designed for something that didn't need chairs.

The manuscript lay across his knees, its not-parchment surface faintly warm against his robes. Gavin shifted his weight and felt the cold of the flagstones through the thin fabric, sharp enough to remind him he had a body that was not especially comfortable. He'd brought a quill and scratch parchment for notes, but he hadn't written anything in over an hour. The notes he'd been taking were useless — approximations of approximations, English words circling concepts they couldn't hold.

The ward structure was the first thing the translation gave him. He'd known, from the section on the Builders, that wards existed — planetary-scale agreements set before the departure. But the earlier section had described them in passing, the way a description of a house might mention the foundation. This section was the foundation itself.

The wards were agreements, like everything in the First Art, but agreements at a scale that made the substrate beneath a Levitation Charm look like a scribble in the margin of a vast book. They interlocked across the planet's surface in patterns his mind could only hold in fragments — he could manage a local section the way you could manage a single floor of the castle, but the whole structure slipped away every time he tried to see it at once. The Builders had not constructed the wards the way wizards constructed spells. They had negotiated them into existence, convinced the substrate itself to register Earth in a particular way — or rather, to stop registering it. To agree that the planet was not present in a category of perception that the notation could represent but that Gavin's language could not name.

Not hidden. Not shielded. Unregistered. The distinction mattered in ways he could feel pressing at the edge of his understanding but could not quite articulate. A shield implied an attack it defended against. Registration implied something that perceived. The wards did not defend against perception — they edited the conditions under which perception of Earth was possible, so that nothing outside them could perceive the planet the way nothing perceives something that has never existed. The planet was still here. The wards made its being-here a local fact rather than a universal one.

He sat with that for a while. The stone was cold under his legs. His left hand was trembling — it did that now, the Cruciatus aftereffect that came and went, worse at night, worse when he was tired. He pressed it flat against the floor until the cold numbed the shaking, and continued.

The notation's representational movement changed.

The interlocking patterns that described the ward structure were still present, but they had thinned. Not uniformly — in specific areas, the relationships between agreements had weakened, the way fibres in a rope thin before they snap. The thinning was not recent. Gavin's translational framework could not map the notation's time-representation cleanly to years, but the scale was centuries. Many centuries. The wards had been designed by beings who maintained them as part of their existence, the way humans maintained breathing, and the beings had left, and nobody had breathed for the wards since.

He worked through the degradation section slowly. Each decoded passage made the picture worse. The thinning was not linear — it compounded. Areas where the wards had degraded became less capable of supporting adjacent areas, which degraded faster, which weakened the sections next to them. A cascade failure in slow motion. Not imminent by human time — still years or decades away from critical thinning — but already past the point where the original structure could recover on its own. The wards needed maintenance the way a cathedral needed maintenance, and there were no builders and no tools and no one who knew the building was there.

Except him.

He kept translating. The next section was where the notation broke down.

The Builders' notation system could hold substrate relationships with brutal precision — agreements, dependencies, the rules under spells. It could map any magical concept Gavin had seen. At the boundary pages it hit something it could not render. When Gavin tried to follow the boundary patterns into the still center of the page, his eyes lost focus so violently they watered. The faint sense of substrate he'd learned to feel under every magical thing simply stopped there, cut off cleanly enough to make his stomach drop.

The notation represented this by failing where it touched it.

Gavin had turned four pages of the manuscript before he understood what he was looking at. The notation on these pages was sparse — a few patterns at the margins that described the ward boundary, the innermost edge where the wards met what was beyond them. The centre of each page was not blank. The material was the same slightly warm, not-parchment surface throughout. But the notation had stopped moving. The representational shifts that characterised every other section of the manuscript — the constant, living rearrangement of patterns showing relationships between ideas — were absent here. Worse: when he let his gaze rest there for more than a second, the warm material under his thumb went cold and the room tipped half an inch to the left. He had to look away and swallow before it settled.

And at the edges of that stillness, Gavin could read what the Builders had managed to encode. It was not intent or hunger or anything that could notice Earth the way a mind noticed. It was simply the state of things where substrate had never taken hold. The wards were the coastline. Beyond them was sea.

At the weak points it bled through. The nearest patterns lost precision first. Clean relationships went cloudy, then smeared broad enough to make his molars ache when he tried to track them. Another of those brief, nauseating sways ran through the room. His quill slid off his knee and clicked against the stone.

The Earth-side notation failed in order. A line that had meant persistence went soft. The branch connected to it lost its anchors. A cluster of symbols he had already translated twice stopped resolving into meaning at all. He knew what he was looking at because the manuscript kept showing it to him: substrate rules becoming approximate, then unstable, then absent.

He tried once to keep looking. Pain drove straight in behind his eyes. For one second the room went dead: no wall-light, no old stone enchantments, no tiny dormant charge in his own wand. It all slammed back only when he jerked his head away.

Full failure was contact. Let the wards go and the substrate came apart on touch: exact, then ragged, then gone. The thought left a copper taste at the back of his tongue.

All of magical life — every spell, every enchantment, every magical creature, every ward and charm and curse that had ever been cast or ever would be — depended on the substrate. The substrate depended on the wards. The wards depended on maintenance by beings who had left and were not coming back.

Gavin put the manuscript pages down.

The room was very quiet. The luminescent walls gave off their steady, sourceless light. The air was dry and cold and tasted of limestone. Somewhere above him, a thousand students and a hundred staff members were sleeping or patrolling or plotting resistance against a dark wizard they believed was the worst thing in the world, and none of them knew that the world itself was a thin place held together by agreements that were coming apart.

He tried to take a breath and the breath did not work. It went in but it went in wrong, too fast, too shallow, and the next one was worse. His hands were on the stone floor and they were shaking — both of them now, not just the left — and the luminescent walls blurred at the edges of his vision, and he thought very clearly: I'm hyperventilating. And then he stopped thinking clearly and just breathed wrong for a while.

The stone floor was cold against his palms. Then against his knees, because at some point he'd tipped forward. The manuscript pages had slid off his lap and lay on the floor beside him, their surfaces still faintly warm, still shifting at the edges with representational motion that continued whether anyone was reading or not. When he blinked, the still center of the boundary page stamped itself on the backs of his eyes as a flat grey coin, and every time it appeared his chest clenched again.

He stayed on the floor until his breathing dragged itself back into rhythm. He had not calmed down. His body had simply refused to keep misfiring forever. The shaking eased from violent to a steady tremor that lived in his hands and in the muscles of his thighs. His face was wet. He had not known he was crying.

He pressed his cheek against the stone and felt the cold of it and stayed there for a while longer. The room was the same. The walls glowed. The air was dry. Nothing had changed except that he now knew what was going to happen, on a timescale too long for urgency and too short for comfort, and he was the only person who knew it, and he was fifteen and his best subject was Herbology and he got Acceptables in Ancient Runes.

After a while he sat up. He wiped his face on the sleeve of his robe. He gathered the manuscript pages carefully, because his hands were still shaking and the pages were the only copy of the only description of the only problem that mattered, and if he damaged them there was no one to consult and no replacement and no second chance.

He put them in the leather folder he used to carry them through the castle. He stood up. His knees ached from the stone. His left hand was trembling badly enough that he had to press it against his side to hold the folder steady.

He walked out of the sub-collection and through the shifting seventh-floor corridors and down six flights of stairs and through the barrel-entrance to the Hufflepuff common room and past the embers of the common room fire and into his dormitory and put the folder in his trunk and got into bed without undressing and did not sleep.

---

The next day he went to classes because not going to classes attracted attention, and attention under the Carrow regime meant questions, and questions meant answers he did not have. He sat through double Charms and Herbology and then Ancient Runes. He took notes in none of them. In Herbology he repotted a Flutterby bush and got soil under his fingernails and thought about the wards. In the corridors between classes, students moved around him the way water moves around a stone in a stream, and he let them.

Professor Babbling's classroom was on the third floor, small and lined with reference texts that predated the school's current crisis by centuries. The class that day had been comparative Futhark systems, and Babbling had taught it the way she taught everything this year — carefully, within the syllabus, without tangents, because tangents could lead to questions and questions could lead to the Carrows taking an interest in what was being taught in Ancient Runes.

The other students filed out after class. Gavin stayed in his seat.

Babbling was at her desk, sorting student work into two piles. She had a system — green-ink comments for content, red for notation errors — and her handwriting was small and precise even on the worst papers. She'd taken off her reading glasses and they hung on their chain against her cardigan, which was the same dark grey wool she seemed to wear every day, its cuffs ink-stained past rescue. There was a half-eaten ginger biscuit on a saucer by her elbow that she appeared to have forgotten about.

She looked up when she realised he hadn't left.

"Mr. Tench." She set down her quill. "Is there something I can help you with?"

He'd meant to start with the translational framework — to anchor the conversation in something she'd already helped him with, to build from the known to the unknown. Instead he said, "The wards round Earth are coming apart."

Babbling looked at him for a long moment. Then she said, "Close the door, please."

He closed the door. When he turned back, she had moved the student papers to one side and was watching him with an expression he couldn't quite read — academic interest and concern and something else, something more cautious.

"Sit down," she said. "And start from the beginning. Not from the conclusion."

So he did.

"The section after the Builders leaving," he said. "It isn't just history. It's the ward structure. Or a record of it. I can't tell which because the notation doesn't split instruction from description the way we do."

Babbling folded her hands. "What wards, precisely?"

"Round Earth. Not just here." He heard how that sounded and pushed on before he could stop. "The whole planet. They're what make Earth fail to count. Unregistered is the closest word I've got. The manuscript's term is more like making the planet miss a kind of perception altogether."

Babbling's brows moved a fraction. "Fail to count."

"Yeah. To anything outside them." He rubbed at his mouth with the back of his hand. "The Builders set that up before they left. It's still there, but it's thinning. Not all over at once. In patches. The links between agreements go stringy, then the bits next to them start going as well. It's a cascade."

"And the evidence for that cascade is in the notation itself?"

"Yes. I mean, that's where I saw it first." He shook his head. "Not saw. That's not quite right either. The manuscript resolves it that way. The relationships lose precision. They go from exact to approximate. And at the boundary pages it gets worse."

He stopped. His mouth had gone dry.

Babbling waited.

"I keep reaching for the wrong nouns," he said. "Outside the wards there's a state where magic doesn't take. The notation goes dead when it touches it. The room sort of lurched when I looked too long. Half an inch sideways. My wand went dead for a second. The wall-light did. The agreements nearest the boundary start smearing out."

"The room went sideways," Babbling said.

"I know." Heat rose in his face. "I know that's not evidence. I'm trying to tell you what it was like because I don't have better words. But if the wards fail all the way, it's not a breach like a wall coming down. It's contact. The substrate stops holding. Spells don't break. The thing that lets spells exist stops applying."

He heard himself revising almost every sentence. Babbling let him do it, only stepping in to pin down a term or make him say whether a word belonged to the manuscript or to him. By the time he reached the end, her expression had changed from surprise to something more careful and more closed.

When he finished, Babbling was quiet for a while. She picked up the ginger biscuit, looked at it, put it back down on the saucer.

"Mr. Tench," she said. "I would like to ask you several questions, and I would like you to answer them honestly."

"Yeah. Of course."

"How many hours per week are you spending on this manuscript?"

He hesitated. "A lot."

"How many."

"Maybe twenty. Thirty, some weeks."

She nodded, as if the number confirmed something. "And your other coursework? Your OWL preparation?"

"It's — I know it's slipping. But this is more —"

"I am not asking as a rebuke. I am asking because the information matters." She paused. "Are you sleeping?"

"Some."

"That is not an answer."

"Four hours. Sometimes five."

Babbling's hands were folded on the desk. The ink stains on her fingers were dark in the classroom's lamplight. "Mr. Tench. When a reader tells me that the planet has been made to 'fail to count,' and that a room went 'sideways' while he was reading, I hear an account of experience. A vivid one." She stopped. "That is not yet the same thing as a fact about the external world. You understand the distinction?"

He nodded.

Her fingers went to the chain of her reading glasses. "What you have described is a cosmological model. A fascinating one — the ward-structure concept is unlike anything in the established runic literature, and under different circumstances I would be very interested to examine the relevant notation." She hesitated, and Gavin watched her revise something internally, pulling back from whatever she'd been about to say. "However. The interpretation you are placing on this model — that the wards are currently degrading, that a condition beyond them poses an existential threat — that is an extraordinary claim."

"I saw what it does —"

"Let me finish." Her voice was kind but had the edge of precision that did not allow interruption. "Ancient manuscripts frequently contain cosmological speculation. The Sumerian cuneiform records describe catastrophes that never occurred. The Ogham prophecy tablets — which I spent seven years studying — predict the end of magical civilisation approximately once per century. Speculative eschatology is one of the most common genres in pre-medieval magical writing. It is entirely possible that the manuscript you are translating contains a real description of an ancient ward system and a speculative, theoretical model of its potential failure. These are not mutually exclusive."

"It's not speculative. The notation shows —"

"The notation shows what your translation renders." She said it gently. "I gave you a framework for approaching the cipher, and it is a good framework — I believe that — but every translational framework introduces interpretive latitude." She paused. "I am not questioning your diligence. I want that to be clear. I am questioning whether a single student, working alone with an imperfect translation tool, can draw the conclusion you are drawing from notation that — by your own description — becomes representationally ambiguous precisely at the point where the most dramatic claims are made."

Gavin opened his mouth and closed it.

"I think," Babbling said, more softly, "that you have been working very hard, under conditions that would be stressful for anyone, and that the manuscript has become a place where some of that stress has collected." She reached for the teapot on the shelf behind her desk — it was always there, always warm, charmed to stay that way — and poured a cup without asking whether he wanted one. She slid it across the desk to him. "This has been a terrible year. The things that are happening in this school — the detentions, the atmosphere, the uncertainty — these produce anxiety that has to go somewhere. And sometimes it goes into the work that occupies us most deeply, and the work begins to feel more urgent than it is, because it is carrying weight that belongs elsewhere."

The tea was milky and too sweet. He drank it because she'd given it to him and because the warmth of the cup was the first pleasant thing he'd felt since the sub-collection floor.

"Could you look at the section yourself?" he asked. "The ward section. If I brought you the pages —"

"No." The word was quiet and final. "I cannot involve myself further with research that falls outside the approved syllabus. Not this year." She looked at him, and what was in her face was not unkindness but the particular sadness of a person choosing self-preservation over curiosity. "Do not bring those pages to me, Mr. Tench. If the Carrows see you carrying unauthorized material into my classroom, it will not be treated as scholarly enthusiasm. It would put both of us at risk, and I cannot pretend otherwise."

"Yeah," he said. "I understand."

"I would recommend sleep, Mr. Tench. And some attention to your other subjects. Your OWL marks will matter, whatever the manuscript says about the rest." She picked up the ginger biscuit and ate it, finally, as if the conversation had used up whatever restraint had been keeping her from it. "Close the door behind you when you leave."

He closed the door behind him. The third-floor corridor was empty, afternoon light slanting through the tall windows, dust floating in it. He stood there for a moment with sugar stuck to his molars, his eyes aching from the shift out of Babbling's lamplit room, and the understanding that the most qualified person he could reach thought he was projecting.

---

He found Miren in the common room after dinner. She was on the floor by the fire with a Muggle Studies textbook open in her lap — Alecto Carrow's version, the one that described Muggles as subhuman — annotating it in the margins with corrections. She'd been doing this since September, filling the margins with the actual facts she knew from her mother, as if quiet factual resistance in the margins of a propaganda textbook could change something. Gavin did not know if it could. But Miren did it anyway, every week, in small precise handwriting that looked nothing like her usual scrawl.

She had mud on her shoes from somewhere — the greenhouses, or the grounds. Her hair was in its usual plait, though strands had come loose around her face, and she was biting the end of her quill in a way that suggested the annotations were costing her something she would not admit to.

"Hey," she said when she saw him. Her voice was careful — the tone she'd used with him since the argument in November, warm but keeping a millimetre of distance. "You look awful."

"Can I talk to you about something?"

"You're already talking to me."

"I mean — something specific. Something I need you to hear."

She looked at him for a moment, reading something in his face. Then she closed the propaganda textbook and set it aside. She turned toward him, and he saw the shift — the way she went from casually present to fully focused, her whole body going still in the way that was more intense than most people's engagement. When Miren decided to listen, she listened with everything.

"OK," she said. "Talk."

He sat down on the floor next to her, cross-legged, close enough to the fire that he could feel it through his robes. The common room was mostly empty — a few first-years playing Gobstones in the corner, a seventh-year asleep on the sofa with a textbook over her face, one shoe off and one shoe on. Private enough.

He said, "I found the next section."

Miren nodded once for him to keep going.

"The bit after the Builders leaving. It says the wards round Earth aren't just there. They're failing."

Her face did not change much. "The wards that make Earth hidden."

"Hidden makes it sound too simple." He scrubbed a hand over his hair. "More like the planet drops out of the count. The nearest word I've got is unregistered."

"Unregistered to what?"

"To anything outside them. Or outside the category they're built for." He winced. "I know that's not normal speech. The Builders put agreements round the whole planet so magic here only has to be true locally. That's not right either, but it's closer. And the agreements are wearing thin."

"How can you tell?"

"The links between parts go soft." He held his hands apart over the rug as if the shapes might sit between them if he gave them enough room. "One weak patch makes the next patch carry more. Then that bit starts going as well. Like mortar washing out of a wall. The whole thing doesn't drop at once, but it has started."

Miren tucked one leg under herself. "And outside them?"

He looked at the fire because he could not look at her and say it straight. "That's the worst bit. It's the far side where every agreement starts slipping on contact. The boundary pages go dead when they touch it. The room lurched when I looked too long. My wand went dead for a second."

"You keep calling it a condition and then talking about sides and edges."

"I know." He let out a breath that almost turned into a laugh and didn't. "I don't have a better word for where the wards stop. It's a condition. An outside. More like the bad step on a stair, the one that holds till weight hits it and then gives all at once. The place where magic won't stay true."

He heard how mad that sounded and kept going anyway. He told her about the centuries of degradation without maintenance, about the Builders not dying but leaving, about the way the notation stopped moving at the center of the boundary pages. He kept backing up and starting again. Miren did not interrupt except when a word needed nailing down. She listened the way she always listened when something mattered, with her whole face and shoulders and stillness.

When he finished, the fire had burned lower. The first-years had gone to bed. The seventh-year was still asleep on the sofa, her shoe still half off.

Miren was quiet for a while. She pulled a loose strand of hair behind her ear. Then she said, "So. The world's ending."

"Not — not ending. The wards are. If they fail completely, then... yeah. Magic stops working. Everything that depends on magic stops existing. But it's not —" He caught himself. "It's not soon. Decades, maybe longer. But it's happening, and nobody knows except —"

"Except you."

"Yeah."

The fire cast moving shadows across her face. The common room smelled of earth and warm wood and the faint undercurrent of whatever the kitchens had been cooking — boiled potatoes, something with cabbage. The heat from the hearth made the skin under his collar damp while his hands stayed cold.

"OK," she said. "Can I ask you something?"

"Yeah."

"What'd you get on the last Runes assessment?"

He stared at her. "What?"

"Your Ancient Runes mark. End-of-term assessment. What was it?"

"I — Acceptable. Why does —"

"And the one before that?"

"Acceptable. Maybe a Poor on the practical. Miren, what does that have to do with —"

"Just — hang on." She held up a hand, not to silence him but to hold the space for what she was putting together. "The manuscript. The notation. You said even Babbling couldn't read it without giving you a framework first. She said it was pre-Futhark, pre-everything."

"Yeah."

"And you've been translating it. You. On your own. With a framework she gave you for a completely different notation system."

"It's not — the framework was a starting point. I've built on it. The notation teaches you how to read it, that's part of how —"

"Gav." She said his name the way she said it when she wanted him to hear what she was actually saying, not what he was hoping she was saying. "You're middle of the class in Runes. That's just true."

"I know that, but —"

"No, wait." She pulled her other leg under herself. "You're asking me to believe you've cracked something no one's cracked, with a method that wasn't built for it. And the proof — the only proof — is that months alone with this book have made you see exactly what the book says is there."

"That's not —"

"I can't test it, Gav. Babbling can't test it. Nobody can unless they let the same thing happen to them. So what am I supposed to stand on here? Except you?"

She was not angry. That was the worst part. She was not dismissing him out of frustration or spite. She was looking at him with the careful, painful attention of someone trying to find a place to put her weight and finding none.

"I know it sounds mad," he said. "I do. But I'm not asking you to mark my translation. I just need someone else to know it isn't..." He looked down at his own hands. "It isn't me making up a disaster because the year's shit."

"And then what?" she asked.

He blinked. "What?"

"Say I say yes. Fine. Real wards, real threat, real outside." Her voice stayed low, but the pressure in it changed. "Then what are you asking me for? To come down there with you? To cover for you when you skip dinner and classes? To help you carry pages about the end of magic while there are actual people in this school who need things done tonight?"

He opened his mouth.

Miren went on. "I have Duncan trying not to vanish in plain sight. I have people asking me to move things and pass things and keep track of who can safely go where. I have this." She tapped the propaganda textbook on the rug between them. "I can't take the little room I've got and spend it helping you disappear further into that place."

He opened his mouth and found nothing in it. From anywhere outside his own head, the manuscript was a closed loop: the change it caused was the only proof that the change was real.

"I can see the substrate," he said at last. "Underneath spells. Under the lights in here. It's there."

Miren's expression tightened, not with contempt but with fear. "I believe you're seeing something. I'm not taking the piss. But I don't know if it's the world or what that book has done to you, and I can't make choices for other people on maybe."

That hurt more than if she'd laughed. A log shifted and sent a spray of sparks across the hearthstones where they glowed for a moment and went out. Gavin flinched at the bright snap of them. The room had gone hot enough to prickle under his collar, but his hands stayed cold, the tremor ticking in his left one against his knee.

"I don't know what to do," he said.

Miren's face changed. The careful analytical expression shifted into something softer, more worried, closer to the way she'd looked at him before November. She reached over and took his hand — the left one, the one that was trembling — and held it without saying anything.

"I think you need to stop," she said quietly. "Not forever. Just for a while. Sleep. Do some homework. Come to dinner. Be a person for a bit."

"I can't stop."

"You can. You're choosing not to."

"If what I'm reading is right —"

"Then making yourself ill tonight doesn't help," she said. She squeezed his hand and let go. "It just means the only person who thinks he can do anything about it is too wrecked to think straight. If you want help, I'll help with food and sleep and keeping you from falling over. I won't help you go further in."

He wanted to argue. Instead he looked at the dying fire, at the seventh-year asleep with her shoe half off, at Miren's textbook waiting where she'd set it down. Her corrections filled the margins in tight rows. Looking at them made the back of his neck go hot.

"OK," he said.

"OK you'll stop?"

"OK I hear you."

She looked at him for a long time. The fire was dying, reduced to coals that pulsed with heat. The common room had gone dim.

"I'm worried about you," she said. "That's all. I'm not — I just want you to be all right."

"I know."

"Are you going to bed?"

"In a bit."

She stood up. She picked up the propaganda textbook and tucked it under her arm. At the foot of the stairs to the girls' dormitory, she turned back.

"If it's real," she said. "If the wards and the — all of it. If it's actually real. Then someone needs to fix them. And it can't just be you, Gav. You know that, right? You can't carry something like that alone."

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

She went upstairs. He sat by the dying fire for a long time. The coals ticked as they cooled. The common room settled into its nighttime quiet — the creak of old wood, the distant sound of the kitchens cleaning up, the low hum of the earth around the walls that he'd never noticed before this year.

Both of them had been kind. That made the loneliness worse. If they had laughed, or told him to stop being stupid, he could have gone upstairs angry. Instead he'd been handed tea and worry and a grip on his shaking hand, and none of it changed the fact that he was alone with it.

They were both wrong, too. He knew because the boundary pages had left a mark on him: when he let the memory of them back in, part of the common-room dark went flat and depthless, and the small everyday hum of magic in the room vanished there. His stomach dropped every time it happened. The outside did not think about him. It did not think at all. It was simply there.

He went to bed. In the dark he listened to his dormitory mates breathing in their sleep and found himself counting the separate rhythms, trying to tuck his own breath between them. They had enough to be frightened of already without this on top of it. Each time he shut his eyes, the dead center of the page came back behind them. Not as an image. As a place his thoughts skidded off and could not cross. The skin along his forearms tightened with cold, though the dormitory was stuffy with sleeping bodies. Somewhere above and beyond, the wards still held. Thinner than yesterday. Thinner than the day before that. Nobody else in the room knew it.

Gavin lay on his back and stared at the underside of his bed canopy and went over it again because there was nothing else to do. Nobody was going to help him. He was not good enough to do it alone. It was still going to be him, because there was nobody else and the manuscript was the only thing he'd found that even hinted at what to do. By morning he knew he would go back to the sub-collection, because deciding otherwise changed nothing. After a while he closed his eyes. He did not sleep. Much later, without noticing when it happened, he did.
