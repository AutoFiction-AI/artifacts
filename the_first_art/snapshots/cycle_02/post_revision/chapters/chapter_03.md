# Chapter 3

The first flight on the route he'd mouthed to himself in the dark left his knees throbbing. Gavin stopped on the landing, hand tight on the banister, and waited for the ache to ebb before he made himself keep climbing toward the seventh floor.

This was not strategy or discipline. He'd waited three days because the bruises from Carrow's detention still ached through his kneecaps whenever he climbed stairs, and the seventh floor was a long way up from the Hufflepuff basement. His left side was tender where a stinging hex had caught him across the ribs — whether Carrow had aimed there or was just a bad shot, Gavin hadn't decided. Either way, stairs were a problem.

He was going tonight because the ache had settled from sharp to dull and because he had not been able to stop thinking about the catalogue entry. _Sub-Collection C. Pre-Foundation Acquisitions._ It listed a shelf number in handwriting so old the ink had gone the color of dust and a title in a language he didn't recognize. The ink, at least, had stayed still.

He left after dinner. Miren was helping a second-year with a Switching Spell at one of the common room tables, her patience visibly thinning as the boy turned a teacup into a slightly different teacup for the fourth time running. Duncan had gone upstairs early, which he did most nights now — not because he was tired, Gavin thought, but because the common room required a version of himself that was getting harder to maintain. Nobody asked where Gavin was going. Nobody was watching him. He was the kind of person people's eyes moved past without stopping, and that had never bothered him, and tonight it was useful.

The seventh floor was quiet. It was an hour before curfew, but students avoided the upper corridors after dark this year — too many empty classrooms where Filch could find you, too close to the route the Carrows took between their quarters and Snape's office. Gavin kept his wand out and walked quickly, counting turns off the route he'd memorized in the catalogue room three nights ago. He turned left at the tapestry of the dancing trolls. He went straight past the suit of armor with the dented visor and one gauntlet missing. He turned right at the landing where a tall window looked out onto the grounds. The lake was a flat dark shape, and beyond it the Forbidden Forest was a darker one, and the sky above both was clear and cold and full of more stars than he'd ever seen from London.

The corridor he needed should have been the next left turn.

It wasn't. He stood at a junction he was certain had not existed on Sunday night, when he'd stumbled through these corridors with his knees bleeding through his trousers. The left branch went down — he could see the top of a spiral staircase. The right branch ended at a wall twenty feet away, lit by a single torch that popped and hissed in its bracket. Neither was right.

Gavin turned around and went back to the suit of armor. He counted again. He turned left at the tapestry, went straight past the armor, and turned right at the landing with the window. The junction was gone. In its place the corridor continued straight ahead in a shallow curve, and its far wall wore a pale wash of light.

There was light, though: a thin, pearl-grey sheen over the stone. It seeped from every surface at once, floor to ceiling, and held steady on every edge. The corridor looked scrubbed clean by it. The glow had the settled presence of something older than torches.

He stopped walking. The stone underfoot had changed. His body caught it first, the same quick wrongness as stepping onto a stair a fraction higher than the rest. The blocks were larger here, paler, smoother. The grey had a chalk-and-ash cast the rest of the castle's stone never carried, as if it had been quarried from some deeper, cleaner layer of earth. A pale seam ran between the blocks, rough as dried paste from a few feet away and glittering like packed salt when he bent closer.

The air was different. It was colder by several degrees, and it was cave-dry, the kind of dry that came from stone holding the same air for centuries. It smelled of minerals, clean and cold, of limestone and still water and nothing alive.

Gavin walked in.

The corridor opened into a room. It was small — he could have crossed it in four long strides. The ceiling was low, maybe seven feet, and the sourceless light was a little brighter here, enough to see clearly by. Shelves were cut directly into the walls, and the stone of the shelves and the stone of the wall were the same continuous piece. Each shelf rose out of the wall with the smooth finish of river-cut rock. The shelves looked formed rather than carved, like a riverbed throwing up ledges where water had worried through different layers of stone.

A few objects sat on the lower shelves. There was a roughly round stone whose curves looked too even to be accidental, as if water or hands or time had kept correcting it. Beside it sat a piece of something that might have been metal — solid, smooth, and colored like tarnished straw seen through a skin of oil, yellow with a green-blue slip at the edges. Looking at it produced the same feeling as hearing a chord with one note slightly off — you knew something was wrong but you could not say which part. He did not touch either of them.

Through a doorway in the back wall he found a second room, smaller than the first, with six shelves cut into the stone on each side. A few held objects — another piece of the wrong-colored not-metal, a flat disc of something translucent, what appeared to be a very small cup made of the same pale stone as the walls. The light was the same sourceless glow. The air was the same dry mineral cold.

The silence was specific. He noticed it here, standing between the two doorways, his breathing the only sound. It had weight to it, as if the room had been holding its breath for centuries. The still air seemed layered, settled in place like sediment in water. His own breathing seemed too loud, too wet, too alive for the space.

Through another doorway he found a third room. It was the smallest of the three. Three shelves were cut into each wall. Most of them were empty.

The manuscript was on the middle shelf of the far wall, and he knew it was the manuscript because there was nothing else it could be. The catalogue had said _sheet collection, unbound, material unclassified_, and that description was accurate in the most useless possible sense — true and completely worthless as preparation.

The sheets were stacked loosely, not bound. He reached for the top one and his fingers met a skin-thin sheet with a faint waxy drag, softer at the edges than parchment and more supple than paper. It was thin and flexible and faintly warm. The warmth sat in it like heat held in paving stone after a long August day, except that it was October and this room was cold enough that he could feel the chill in his teeth.

The sheet did not feel old. That was the wrongest thing about it. He was standing in a room that smelled like it had been sealed since before the castle was a hill, holding something that the catalogue listed under _Pre-Foundation Acquisitions_, and it felt as if it had been made this morning. It felt unworn, untouched by the slow roughening that took the shine off everything else. The process of getting old was something that happened to other materials, and this one had not agreed to it.

He held the sheet up to the sourceless light.

There were marks on its surface. That much was clear. They lay dark against the warm-toned material in lines and clusters with the deliberate order of writing. Some stood alone like posts. Others hooked into one another or folded inward, building compact shapes inside larger ones. They shared the hard purposeful feel of the Sardinian temple marks and Balkan fragments in his Ancient Runes textbook, but they did not share their human logic.

These marks belonged to no category he knew.

Some of them bore a passing resemblance to Elder Futhark symbols, no more trustworthy than a face in a cloud. You could see it if you wanted to, but the resemblance was a product of your own pattern-matching, not anything the marks had intended. Other marks were shapes he had no category for: compound curves that did not resolve into anything he could name, angles that seemed to shift depending on where he focused, elements that might have been individual symbols or might have been parts of larger structures or might have been something outside the distinction between individual and composite entirely.

And they moved.

They did not move like wizarding photographs. Gavin had been in the magical world long enough to know how that worked. Portraits moved because they were depictions of people, and the depicted people moved as people moved. Photographs captured moments and replayed them. Even the writing on the Marauder's Map — everyone in school had heard the stories — tracked physical positions through physical space. All of it was mimetic, a picture of a thing doing what the thing did.

The marks on the sheet shifted with the slow purpose of pieces being set out for use. Two marks that had been adjacent separated. A third, from the far side of the sheet, drifted closer. A new configuration settled into place. The pattern had direction. It behaved like an explanation happening in front of him, only in a grammar he could not hear. It suggested a relationship between the marks, or between whatever concepts the marks represented, or between something Gavin did not have a word for because the word did not exist in any language he spoke.

He put the first sheet back and picked up another. It was made of the same material. It held the same warmth. It carried the same impossible resistance to age. The marks were different — different shapes, different arrangements — but the quality of their movement was the same. The relational patterns changed, but the feeling did not: he was watching something organized and meaningful and completely, absolutely beyond him.

He went through six sheets. Then ten. Then all fifty-seven. His fingers were tingling from contact with the warm material in the cold room, or from something else he couldn't identify, and his wand-light was starting to feel intrusive against the clean sourceless glow of the walls.

He counted the stack three times and got fifty-seven each time.

He looked around the small stone room with its seamless shelves and its ageless light and its mineral silence, and he thought about what he was going to do. Taking the manuscript felt like theft, even without a sign or a lock or a checkout card or Pince to forbid him. But the sub-collection was not the library. It was not part of the castle in the same ordinary sense as the classrooms and corridors and dormitories. It was older and it was other, and the things on these shelves had been placed here by someone or something that had assumed they would stay here, and that assumption had held for longer than the rest of Hogwarts had existed.

Gavin gathered the fifty-seven sheets into a stack. They were lighter than they should have been — lighter than the same volume of parchment, lighter than seemed right for how solid they felt. He slid them into his school bag between his Charms textbook and a roll of Potions notes.

The walk back through the three rooms felt shorter than the walk in. The corridor's pale stone gave way to the castle's regular grey, and a torch appeared on the wall, and then another, and the quality of light changed from sourceless and steady to the familiar flicker and shadow of a normal Hogwarts hallway. The air warmed. He could smell stone dust and old wood and the faint overlay of cooking from the kitchens somewhere below. He was standing on the seventh-floor landing by the window, and the grounds outside were dark, and the lake reflected a thin moon.

He went back to Hufflepuff. The barrel entrance thumped its rhythm and rolled open. The common room was mostly empty — three sixth-years near the fire, one of them with her legs draped across the other two's laps while all three read from the same textbook, arguing in murmurs about the properties of Bubotuber pus. Gavin crossed to the dormitory stairs and went up and put the manuscript in his trunk, underneath his spare robes and the Hufflepuff scarf he never wore because he didn't care about Quidditch, and he closed the lid and got into bed.

The sheets were down there in the dark, under the wool and cotton of things that aged normally. He could still feel the warmth of them on his fingertips, or thought he could. He lay on his back and stared at the canopy of his four-poster and tried to think about something else.

He was still trying when Owen Kirby came in an hour later, brushed his teeth at the bathroom sink with the noisy thoroughness he brought to everything, and fell asleep within minutes. Owen's breathing had the wheezing inhale-and-whistle that Gavin had been hearing since first year — Owen's lungs had never properly agreed with the castle's damp — and tonight the sound was almost comforting in its familiarity. Owen had left an unfinished letter to his mother on his nightstand, the quill laid across it at an angle, careful sentences visible even in the dim light. He wrote to her every week. He never said why.

Gavin listened to the whistle-and-pause. The manuscript was under his robes under his scarf in the trunk at the foot of his bed, and the marks on the fifty-seven sheets were moving in the dark, rearranging themselves into configurations that meant something to someone and nothing to him.

It took two weeks for the excitement to curdle into something worse.

The first three nights, he worked with the careful optimism of someone who believed the problem had a solution and the solution was effort. After the dormitory went quiet — Owen's whistle, Ben Alderton muttering about Quidditch formations in his sleep, the scratch and shuffle of someone's Pygmy Puff in its cage — Gavin pulled the sheets from his trunk, spread them across the mattress inside the closed curtains of his four-poster, lit his wand, and got to work.

He started with substitution analysis. If the notation was a cipher — each mark standing for a letter or a rune — then what he needed was the frequency distribution. He assigned numbers to the marks. Mark 1, angular, appeared twelve times across the first three sheets. Mark 2, a compound curve, appeared nine times. By the end of the first night he had forty-seven provisional marks and two pages of counts.

By the middle of the second night he was crossing numbers out. Shape was not enough. A mark that looked identical on one page sat beside different neighbors on the next and behaved like a different thing. Orientation changed it. Distance changed it. Whether a curve opened into a cluster or away from it changed it. He wrote _unit may be relation, not symbol_ in the margin of his notebook and stared at the sentence until it stopped feeling like language and started feeling like the problem itself.

He spent the next several days in the library pulling everything he could find on non-standard notation systems: goblin metalwork engravings, the Mermish syllabary, pre-Roman runework from the supplementary reading list Babbling had posted at the start of term. He found _Comparative Symbolic Systems of the Mediterranean Basin_ on a high shelf near the Restricted Section and spent an evening with it, cross-referencing illustrations of Sardinian temple inscriptions and Basque funerary notation against what he remembered from the manuscript. A few shapes triggered a flicker of recognition — a curve here, an angle there — but the useful part of the exercise was the part where each resemblance died. Every real writing system on the page assumed that marks stayed themselves long enough to be read. His did not.

He was careful to do this research openly, as if it were coursework. Anyone watching — and he was aware, now, that anyone could be watching, that this year the watchers included people with the authority to hurt you — would have seen a fifth-year doing extra reading for Ancient Runes, which was both boring and plausible and therefore invisible.

On the eighth night, lying on his back in the dark at two in the morning while Owen breathed and Ben muttered and the castle made its slow settling noises around him, Gavin thought: the marks were not writing.

He did not mean that in the sense of some other form of record — not pictures, not diagrams, not music notation. Writing was marks standing for other things. Letters stood for sounds. Runes stood for concepts or sounds or both. The manuscript did not feel like that. It felt closer to the thing itself, whatever the thing was. It was not a description of movement but movement arranged on a flat surface where a description ought to have been.

He did not know what that meant. But it was new, and because it was new he held onto it as if it were progress.

Miren noticed. She always noticed things.

It was a Wednesday of the second week, and they were at lunch, and Gavin sat at the Hufflepuff table shoving potatoes from one side of his plate to the other while thinking about whether the relational movement of the marks might encode temporal information, whether the sequence of configurations mattered, whether what he needed was a timeline instead of a key.

"You're not eating."

He looked up. Miren was across the table with her hair in its usual plait and her robe sleeves pushed up past her elbows as she always wore them, and she was looking at him with an expression he recognized as the thing that came before concern — the attentive noticing that, given enough evidence, would harden into worry.

"I'm eating," he said. He ate a potato to prove it.

"You've been weird lately."

"I haven't been —" He stopped. "Weird how?"

"Like that." She pointed her fork at him. "I keep having to drag you back into the room. You've been doing it for over a week."

This was accurate enough that it was hard to argue with. "I'm just tired. Haven't been sleeping great."

"Since the detention?"

"Yeah," he said, and it was easy to let her think it was the detention, which had been bad enough to explain some sleeplessness. His knees still ached in the mornings. The stinging-hex bruise on his ribs had turned yellow-green and was taking its time about fading.

"I heard Harper got it worse," Miren said, and the conversation shifted to the familiar territory of who had been punished for what and how badly, which was the background noise of social life at Hogwarts this year — the constant low-grade accounting of damage. Gavin ate his lunch and contributed small agreeing sounds at the right moments and thought about temporal encoding and whether he'd been looking at the sheets wrong this whole time, reading them as static documents when they might be something closer to — what? A process? A demonstration?

Duncan was sitting on Miren's other side, eating with the focused attention he brought to meals this term. He had lost weight since September — not dramatically, but his collarbones showed through his neckline in a way they hadn't before, and his school robes had a careful mend over the right shoulder from something he'd never explained. He ate everything on his plate and two pieces of bread from the basket and did not volunteer anything to the conversation.

"There's pudding," Miren told him. "Treacle tart."

"I'm all right."

"Take some anyway. You like treacle tart."

Duncan took some. He ate it with the slow thoroughness of someone who'd learned to take food seriously when it was available. Miren watched him eat and did not say whatever she was thinking, and Gavin registered the exchange with the same passive attention he gave the weather through the enchanted ceiling — something happening in the world around him that he took in without deciding to do anything about it.

That evening, back in his curtained bed with the sheets spread across his knees, Gavin tried the temporal approach. He selected Sheet 3 — the one where two marks in the upper left had maintained a consistent proximity even while everything else shifted — and set his watch on the mattress beside him. He mapped the full configuration at the start, then again at ten-minute intervals.

After an hour he had six maps. After two hours he had twelve. Laid in sequence, the page looked less like writing than a set of instructions acted out too slowly to follow. The two marks in the upper left held together through every change. Everything else bent around them.

That was enough to feel like a lead. He went back through the rest of the manuscript and found three more locked pairs — on Sheets 9, 14, and 16. For nearly an hour he believed they were fixed points, the equivalent of margins or coordinates, something you could sort the other sheets around. He spread page after page across the bed, grouping them by where the paired marks sat and how the surrounding clusters leaned toward them. The whole mess began to look almost organized.

Then one of the pairs on Sheet 14 opened just wide enough for a third mark to slide between them and stay there. The grouping collapsed at once. He stared at the page until his eyes watered and wrote down the new arrangement anyway, because having been wrong did not make the clue feel less real. It made it worse.

By the third week, he was angry.

The anger was specific and self-directed. The manuscript existed. The marks meant something. The relational movement was organized. All of this was obviously, frustratingly true, and the only obstacle between the truth and understanding was that Gavin Tench — fifteen years old, low Acceptable in Ancient Runes, middling marks in everything, exceptional at nothing — was not smart enough to bridge the gap. The problem in front of him had been set by someone or something that operated at a level of complexity he could not reach. He didn't know who or what had made the manuscript, but it had not been made for him.

He kept trying anyway, because the alternative was stopping.

He fell asleep in History of Magic on a Wednesday. Binns did not notice — Binns noticed nothing, had possibly not noticed that he himself was dead — but the girl beside him, a Ravenclaw with four different-colored quills arranged in a neat row and notes so dense and organized they looked like a printed textbook, nudged his elbow when his head slid off his hand and cracked against the desk hard enough to make the front row turn around.

"Sorry," Gavin said, straightening up. His neck ached. His eyes felt as if they'd been rolled in grit.

"You were drooling," the Ravenclaw said. She had a smudge of green ink on her left index finger and the weary patience of someone who had sat next to worse. "Binns didn't notice, but everyone else did."

"Sorry. Thanks."

She turned back to her color-coded notes without comment. Gavin opened his textbook to a page about the Goblin Rebellions of 1612 and stared at it. The ink on the page did not move. He thought about the paired marks he'd started calling stable and whether their consistent proximity implied a semantic connection or whether he was seeing structure in noise because he needed there to be structure in order to justify what he was doing with his nights.

He should stop. He knew this. He could put the sheets back in his trunk and close the lid and catch up on three weeks of neglected homework and get some sleep for the first time since the sub-collection. He could come back to the manuscript later — next year, maybe, or after OWLs, or when he'd learned more in Ancient Runes and had better tools and was older and could think more clearly. That was the sensible thing. Miren would have told him it was the sensible thing, if he'd told Miren, which he hadn't, because there was nothing he could tell her that would make sense. _I found some sheets in a hidden room and they've got marks on them that move and I can't read them and I've been trying every night for three weeks._ Even in his own head it sounded thin. It sounded like something a person made up to justify not sleeping.

He put the sheets away that night. He lay in the dark and listened to Owen's breathing and the Pygmy Puff and Ben Alderton saying something about a Wronski Feint in his sleep, the words slurred and earnest, and the castle making its settling noises all around them — the tiny groans of stone adjusting to stone, the sound of a building that was a thousand years old and still working out its relationship with gravity.

He got the sheets out again twenty minutes later.

He did not have a new approach. He had one thought he could not leave alone: if the paired marks were not fixed points, they might be directions. They might not be the answer itself. They might just tell him where on the page to look next. It was a thin distinction, almost stupid, but it was enough. He spread Sheet 14 and Sheet 5 across his knees, set his notebook open beside them, and waited for the next change.

Ten minutes. Twenty. The paired marks on Sheet 14 loosened, drew tight again, and held. On Sheet 5 a cluster near the center leaned, not toward the margin like he'd assumed, but inward, toward a stray mark he'd already crossed out in his notes. He mapped the new arrangement, then the next one. Each time he told himself he'd stop after one more shift. The castle pipes knocked in the wall. Owen rolled over and whistled in his sleep. Gavin kept watching.

At half six Owen's alarm clock went off with its usual tinny rattle and Gavin flinched so hard he nearly dropped the top sheet. He shoved the pages into a stack too quickly, caught one under his thumb, and felt a stupid pulse of panic at the thought of tearing it. He flattened it, slid the whole stack into his trunk under his robes, and stood up so fast the room tipped sideways. His mouth tasted stale and metallic. The inside of his eyes felt sanded.

He splashed water on his face in the bathroom and looked up at the mirror. He looked tired in a specific and unflattering way — not dramatic, not interestingly haggard, just a kid who wasn't sleeping enough, with bruised half-moons under his eyes and a ridge on his cheek from the edge of a page.

By the time he reached breakfast the porridge had skinned over. Miren and Duncan were already halfway through. The place beside Miren had been taken by a second-year with a bandaged thumb and an armful of books, so Gavin tore two pieces of toast from the rack at the end of the table and stayed standing.

Miren looked up. "Did you just wake up?"

"Not exactly."

Her eyes narrowed. "Have you slept at all?"

"A bit."

"No, you haven't."

Duncan glanced at him, then back down at his plate. "You've got ink on your face," he said.

Gavin scrubbed at his cheek with his sleeve and found nothing because the mark was on the other side.

Miren pushed the marmalade toward him with two fingers. "Eat something before Potions. And try not to fall into the cauldron."

"I'm fine."

She gave him a flat look that said she didn't believe that for a second. The bell rang. Gavin swallowed his toast too fast, burned his tongue on tea, and left with the taste of tannin and orange rind sitting sour at the back of his mouth.

Potions first period was Strengthening Solution, which he had brewed before and should have been able to make half awake. The classroom smelled of damp stone, bruised leaves, and the sharp medicinal tang from twenty open ingredient jars. Slughorn had the instructions on the board. Miren was one table over, Duncan beyond her, and Gavin set to work with the hard careful focus of someone trying to fake being functional.

He measured. He chopped. He stirred. The liquid in his cauldron thickened from pale green to a deeper color that ought to have meant he was on track. Then three darker eddies gathered near the rim in a shape so close to the triangular cluster from Sheet 5 that his mind snagged on it at once. For one stupid second he wasn't looking at the potion anymore.

"Gavin."

Miren's voice cut across the room.

He looked down. He'd tipped in the next ingredient without waiting for the simmer to settle. The potion gave a thick wet cough and surged up in a ropey grey froth that slopped over the lip of the cauldron. Miren caught his sleeve and jerked it back before it dragged through the spill. The stuff hit the bench and began eating at the varnish with a sour, vinegar-sharp stink.

Several heads turned. Slughorn was on him a second later, wand out.

"Back, back," he said, siphoning the mess off the table in one swift motion. He peered into Gavin's cauldron, sighed, and looked up over his glasses. "Tench, that's hopeless. Tip it out and start again. And if you're ill, tell me before you brew something that takes the finish off my desks."

Heat climbed up Gavin's neck. "Sorry."

"Yes, well. Less sorry, more awake."

A few people laughed. Not much. Enough.

Miren didn't say anything until the end of class. Gavin had managed a second batch barely fit to bottle, and Slughorn let it pass only because there were twelve cauldrons to inspect and he was already behind. When the bell rang and benches scraped back, Miren waited by the door with her satchel hanging off one shoulder.

"You nearly set yourself on fire."

"I know."

"No, I don't think you do." She kept her voice low, but there was nothing soft in it. "You're not just tired, Gav. You keep missing what's right in front of you, and now Slughorn's noticed."

Gavin shifted his grip on his bag. "I've just been working late."

"On what?"

He opened his mouth. Nothing came out that could survive being said aloud.

Miren watched him fail. "Right," she said after a second. "Fine. But whatever it is, it's not only yours anymore if you're melting the table in Potions."

She went ahead into the corridor before he could answer. Duncan, coming behind her with his books clutched tight to his chest, gave Gavin a brief look Gavin couldn't make anything of and followed.

That evening Gavin sat in the common room with his Charms essay open to a blank page and lasted eleven minutes before he went upstairs. Once the curtains were shut around his bed, the pull was immediate. Sheet 5 lay where he'd left it in the stack. He set his watch beside it and waited.

At 10:40 the three-mark cluster narrowed again. This time he saw what he'd missed that morning: the point did not aim at the isolated mark itself but at the empty space just beside it, as if the absence mattered as much as the mark. His tiredness dropped away so fast it almost felt like relief. He pulled every page that carried the isolated mark, then every page with a narrowing three-mark cluster, then his notebook. By the time the arrangement loosened he had half the manuscript spread around him again and no more idea what he was looking at than when he'd started. He wrote down the page numbers anyway, turned to the next sheet, and kept going.

He woke to Owen's alarm at half six with his wand still lit against the bed curtains and Sheet 11 pressed into his cheek hard enough to leave a crease. By dinner he'd been late to Herbology, given Flitwick the wrong wand movement when called on, and forgotten his Potions essay altogether. When he came into the Great Hall, Miren and Duncan were already eating. There was space farther down the bench. The place beside Miren was taken up by Duncan's bag and a stack of parchment.

Miren looked up once as he set his plate down at the end of the table. Her gaze went to his face, lingered on the bruised dark under his eyes, and moved away.

"Pass the bread," she said to Duncan.

He did. They went on talking in low voices about a first-year who'd cursed his own quill to bite him. Gavin tore his roll apart and listened without joining in. For the past two weeks Miren had been asking if he'd slept, if he'd eaten, if he was even in the room with them. Tonight she didn't ask anything. She had started adjusting around him instead.

By then Gavin knew one thing, even if he knew nothing else: he wasn't going to get anywhere alone, and if he wanted help he'd have to risk letting someone else see one page. The only question was which one.
