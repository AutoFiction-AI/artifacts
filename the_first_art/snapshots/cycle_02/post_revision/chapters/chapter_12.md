# Chapter 12

The next afternoon, Carrow was peeling an orange when they came in. He stood behind his desk with his sleeves pushed up past the forearms, digging his thumbnails into the rind and dropping strips of peel onto the wood in damp curls. The classroom smelled of citrus and, underneath it, the flat mineral odour that never left the stones anymore — scorched air, old copper, the residue of whatever had happened in here during the last lesson. The fifth-years filed in and sat along the walls.

The desks had been shoved against the stone since November, clearing the center of the room into an open floor that Gavin had stopped thinking of as a teaching space and started thinking of as a place where things happened to people. Students sat on the flagstones with their backs to the wall, schoolbags between their knees, wands out because you needed your wand out for Dark Arts or Carrow noticed. Gavin took his usual spot near the back left corner, where a torch bracket cast enough shadow that Carrow's gaze tended to skip past him. It wasn't reliable. Nothing was reliable. But the shadow felt like something, and this year Gavin took what he could get.

Duncan was already seated three places to his left. He'd been reading something when Gavin came in — not a textbook, something smaller, a folded piece of paper he'd been holding close to his face in the way of someone with a private thing in a public place. He put it away when the room started filling, tucked it into his trouser pocket with a quick, practised motion. It was a letter from home, probably. Duncan's parents wrote through the Muggle post, which got screened at the gates, and the letters that made it through were rare enough that Duncan carried them in his pocket for days. Gavin had seen him refolding one once, creasing the paper along the same lines with his thumb until the fold was soft as cloth.

Duncan set his wand across his knees and sat very still. He was thinner than he'd been at Christmas, and Christmas had been thin. His school robes sat on his frame the way a sheet sits on a chair — you could see the shape underneath but the fabric didn't touch where it was supposed to. His fair hair needed cutting and he hadn't cut it, because cutting it meant going to the fourth floor, where the barber portrait worked, and the fourth floor was near Alecto Carrow's office. Duncan had learned the building's geography as a system of risks, and his shaggy hair was a map of routes he'd stopped taking.

A girl from Ravenclaw — Lisa Turpin, who always sat near the door and kept her bag half-packed as if she might need to leave in a hurry — was the last one in. She pulled the door shut behind her, and the sound of it closing was the sound of something sealing.

Carrow finished his orange. He ate it in sections, pushing each one whole into his mouth and chewing with his lips open, the wet sounds audible in the quiet room. When he was done he wiped his hands on his robes, leaving damp smears on the black fabric, and looked up at the class the way you look at a shelf of supplies to decide which one you need.

"Right," he said. "Last week we did control. How your intent shapes the curse. Who remembers?"

Nobody answered. This was normal. Answering in Carrow's class meant being noticed, and being noticed meant being used.

"Nobody. Brilliant." He picked at something between his teeth with a thumbnail. "Means I've got to show you again, don't I? Can't have you lot going soft. The Dark Lord's got standards, even if you haven't."

He came around the desk. His gait was heavy, slightly bowlegged, the walk of a man who took up space because taking up space was the thing he was best at. He scanned the room. Gavin watched his eyes move from face to face with the casual assessment of someone sorting objects by usefulness.

"Flett."

Duncan's hands went still in his lap. That was the only visible change — his fingers, which had been lightly pleating the fabric of his trousers, stopped moving and pressed flat against his knees.

"Up. Come here."

Duncan stood. He picked up his wand and walked to the center of the room, and Gavin watched the walk — each step measured, each foot placed carefully, nothing hurried, nothing reluctant, nothing that could be read as defiance or fear. Duncan kept his weight even and his body quiet and watched the floor in front of him. He stopped in the center, turned to face Carrow, and waited with his wand at his side.

Carrow looked at him. There was a moment — brief, almost invisible — where something in Carrow's face shifted, and Gavin thought it might be uncertainty — not compassion, not hesitation, but the quick, involuntary calculation of a man who knew he was performing for an audience he couldn't see — Snape, the Dark Lord, whoever Carrow imagined was judging whether he was hard enough for the job he'd been given. Then it was gone. Carrow bit a piece of skin off his thumb, a habit Gavin had seen before, and raised his wand.

"The thing about the Cruciatus," he said, addressing the room, "is that most of you don't mean it. You wave and you say the word and nothing happens because you haven't got the bottle. That's what intent is. You've got to want it. You've got to find the part of them that you reckon deserves it — the bit that's wrong, the bit that shouldn't be here — and you push the curse at *that*." He looked at Duncan. "Some of them make it easy."

He flicked his wand.

Gavin saw it at two levels.

At the level everyone in the room could see, red light hit Duncan in the chest and Duncan's body stopped belonging to him. His legs folded and his back arched and he hit the floor with the boneless, graceless impact of something dropped from a height. His wand clattered away across the stone.

At the substrate level — the level Gavin had been learning to perceive since the manuscript had restructured his cognition, the level under spells and inside them — something else happened. The rules governing Duncan's nervous system were there, running quietly, the ordinary agreements that said nerve signals corresponded to physical states, that pain mapped to damage, that the body's alarm system was tethered to reality. The Cruciatus folded those rules. Smoothly. The ordinary agreements gave way to something simpler: maximum signal everywhere, no referent, pain with no source and no location and no purpose except its own continuation. Gavin could see the substitution directly now, as plainly as he'd seen the visibility rules around the quill. It was a single edit. One agreement suspended, another imposed in its place.

Duncan screamed.

The sound filled the room from wall to wall. Gavin felt it in his ribs. It started in Duncan's chest and came out through his throat as raw noise, shapeless, nothing Duncan was choosing to make. His fingers scraped the flagstones. His heels kicked against the floor with a rhythmic, involuntary motion, the muscles in his legs firing and firing in response to signals that had no origin in anything that was actually happening to his body. His back arched again, lifting his shoulders and hips off the stone while his head stayed down, the tendons in his neck standing out like cords, and his mouth was open wider than Gavin had thought a mouth could open — the jaw at a wrong angle, the lips pulled back, the teeth visible.

Gavin sat against the wall with his bag between his knees. His left hand was shaking — the intermittent tremor from his own Cruciatus, months ago, which came back at unpredictable moments and was there now, his fingers tapping against the leather of his bag in a rhythm he wasn't choosing. He watched Duncan's face and he watched the substrate beneath the curse and they existed simultaneously in his perception, the human reality and the mechanical reality, and neither cancelled the other out.

The sound Duncan was making changed. It broke into shorter bursts as his diaphragm spasmed, and in the gaps between bursts Gavin could hear the smaller sounds the scream had been covering — Duncan's nails on the stone, the wet click of his jaw clenching and releasing, the scuff of his shoes. Someone in the room was crying. Gavin didn't look to see who. Lisa Turpin had her face turned away and her hands were pressed flat over her ears and her bag was still half-open at her feet.

The curse held — five seconds, then eight. Carrow stood over Duncan with his wand pointed down and his face had settled into the expression of a man doing something that required effort but not doubt. The satisfaction was not theatrical. It was the plain, workmanlike satisfaction of a person who has been told he is good at this specific thing and has chosen to believe it.

Gavin looked at the substrate again.

The edit was clean. Simple. One rule suspended, one substituted. Structurally, it was the same kind of operation he had performed on the quill's visibility — looser even, cruder, a blunt overwrite rather than the careful loosening he'd attempted in his single successful practice. If he reached into the substrate beneath the curse and restored the original agreement — put the page back, cancelled the substitution — the pain would stop.

Could he do it?

He didn't know. His one successful edit had been on a stationary object with no competing intent, and it had cost him a nosebleed and an hour of blurred vision. This was a live curse held in place by Carrow's will, and Carrow was three metres away with his wand out. The gap between making a quill slightly less visible and disrupting an active Cruciatus Curse in a room full of witnesses was real. It might be unbridgeable.

He didn't try.

He let the doubt sit there because it meant he did not have to move. His hands stayed on his bag. His wand stayed across his knees. Duncan's heels hammered the flagstones.

Carrow lifted the curse. Duncan stopped making noise. His body kept moving for several seconds — involuntary shaking in his legs, his fingers opening and closing on the stone, the aftermath of a nervous system that couldn't believe the signals had stopped. He lay on the floor with his cheek pressed to the flagstone. His breathing was fast and shallow and audible across the room. His eyes were open and they were not looking at anything.

"See?" Carrow said. He sounded the way he'd sounded after the orange — casual, finished, moving on. He wiped his wand hand on his robes. "That's what intent looks like. Now. Pair up."

The room shifted. Students stood, shuffled toward each other, began the routine that had become familiar enough to be merely awful. You found someone you could trust to say the word and withhold the intent, someone who would aim the wand and produce the minimum — a sting, a twitch, nothing like what Carrow had just demonstrated. The pairing happened quickly and without discussion, partnerships established weeks ago through the grim arithmetic of who would hurt you least.

Gavin stood. His legs worked fine. Everything worked fine. He paired with Michael Corner, a Ravenclaw who had bitten the inside of his cheek raw over the past months and who always tasted blood during these exercises. They took turns. Gavin's Cruciatus produced a faint shudder in Michael's arm, nothing more, because Gavin could not make himself mean it and the spell knew. Michael's produced a cramp in Gavin's shoulder that faded in seconds. They repeated it twice because Carrow was watching, and both times the result was the same — an echo of a curse, an intention refused.

On the floor between the paired students, Duncan had not been told to get up. He lay where he'd fallen, his breathing slower now but still audible, the wet sound of someone whose mouth was full of something he hadn't swallowed. His lip was bleeding. He'd bitten through it during the curse, or his face had hit the stone when he fell — Gavin couldn't tell which, but the blood was there, running down Duncan's chin in a dark line, collecting in the hollow of his throat, dripping onto the flagstones in a small pool that was almost black against the grey stone in the torchlight. Students stepped around him as they paired up. They stepped carefully. Nobody touched him.

"Get up, Flett," Carrow said eventually, not looking at Duncan, sorting through papers on his desk. "You're in the way."

Duncan got up. The movement took a long time. He got his arms under him, locked his elbows, pushed himself up to his hands and knees. His arms were shaking with a fine, persistent tremor. He got one foot under him, then the other, and stood, and swayed, and steadied himself. He walked to the wall with the same careful, measured steps he'd used to walk to the center of the room, except now his body was unreliable and the careful steps were also effortful, each one a negotiation between his intention and his legs' willingness to participate. He sat down against the wall and picked up his bag and held it in his lap and did not look at anyone.

His lip was already swelling. The blood had crusted on one side and was still wet on the other, and the swelling pulled his mouth into a lopsided shape that made him look both younger and older than fifteen — younger because of the vulnerability, older because of the damage.

The bell rang. Gavin packed his bag. He walked out of the classroom behind three other students and did not look at Duncan and did not look at the blood on the floor and his hands were steady except for the left one, which shook with its own old damage, separate from everything that had just happened.

***

He went to the sub-collection instead of the common room.

He did not want to be in the common room, where Miren would ask what had happened in Dark Arts and where Duncan would arrive eventually and sit in his armchair and say he was fine. He did not want to be around anyone. He took the seventh-floor corridors that rearranged themselves — today the route was short, three turns and the staircase that wasn't always present and the door that looked like a wall to anyone who hadn't walked this way before — and then he was in the small stone room with its cold air and its mineral smell and its light that came from the walls themselves, older than the castle's torch system, older than anything in the building except possibly the manuscript itself.

He sat at the stone shelf he used as a desk. The two quills from last night were still in his bag, ready for the comparison he'd meant to make. He took the manuscript from beside them and opened it to the page he'd been working on and looked at the notation and could not make himself read it.

He was running through the practical question step by step, using the same method Babbling had taught him for translation problems.

The Cruciatus operated through a substrate rule substitution. One agreement suspended — nerve signals correspond to physical states — and another imposed — maximum signal everywhere, no referent. It was a crude edit, a blunt instrument at the substrate level. Structurally simpler than the visibility adjustment he'd made to the quill. The edit itself was not the problem.

The problem was the live cast. Carrow's will held the substitution in place. Disrupting it would mean reaching into the substrate beneath an active curse, maintained by a caster with a wand, and restoring the original agreement while the caster was actively sustaining the replacement. He had done one substrate edit, on a quill, in an empty room. The distance between that and this was the distance between lifting a stone and holding back a river.

He probably couldn't have done it.

But he hadn't tried. He hadn't reached for the substrate. He hadn't tested whether Carrow's intent would resist. He'd let the question sit there until Carrow lifted the curse and the question became irrelevant. In the sub-collection he looked at his hands on the stone shelf and could still see them on the bag in his lap, staying where he'd put them.

He became aware of the cosmic rationale beginning to assemble itself at the edge of his thoughts. The wards. The thing beyond them. The scale of what was actually at stake, the framework that made Duncan's suffering — real, specific, happening to a person Gavin knew — into a smaller thing inside a larger thing, a local cruelty inside a planetary emergency. The framework where spending cognitive resources on disrupting one curse in one classroom was a misallocation when the substrate itself was degrading at a scope that made human cruelty look like weather in a single room. The framework was there, available, and it might even have been true.

He shut it down. The argument kept trying to finish itself anyway — wards, scale, triage — and he shut the manuscript with both hands, hard enough that the sound cracked in the little room.

He pulled one of his working sheets across the stone. If he was going to keep replaying the class, he could at least fix the structure while it was still clear.

He wrote the ordinary agreement first. Then the overwrite. The symbols came faster than the coupling equations ever had. His hand did not hesitate once.

When he tried to mark the counter-edit — the place where correspondence between signal and state would have to be restored — the symbols would not stay put. He drew the relation once, then again. Each time the line broke under his attention and slid back into marks he could recognize as failure without knowing how to correct them. The place where he would have had to act in class stayed blank in the middle of the page.

His nose started bleeding.

It came without warning, without him laying a hand on the substrate. The first drop landed in the blank space and spread through the ink. The second hit the stone beside the manuscript. He tilted his head forward and let it drip and did not wipe it because his handkerchief was in his bag and his bag was on the floor and reaching for it felt like an effort his body was not prepared to make.

When the bleeding eased, he folded the working sheet in half and tucked it under the manuscript before he could decide not to.

He thought about the letter Duncan had been reading before class. It was something from home — the Muggle post, screened at the gates, most of it confiscated. The letters that got through were handled with the careful attention of a person holding something that might be the last of its kind. There was an entire life folded into that letter — a kitchen in Sheffield, parents who wrote on regular paper with regular pens, a world where Duncan was not a Muggle-born in an occupied school but someone's son who'd gone away for the year and would come back. Gavin had watched the person who carried that letter convulse on a stone floor, and his dominant thought — not the only thought but the one with the most weight, the one that had occupied the most space in his skull — had been whether the substrate edit was technically feasible.

The nosebleed slowed. He wiped his face on his sleeve and looked at the manuscript page. The notation moved in its slow, representational way, configurations shifting to show relationships between concepts he was beginning to read, and he could not engage with it. Every time his attention settled on the patterns, the pattern from the classroom surfaced instead — the clean substitution that had turned Duncan's nervous system into an instrument of its own torture.

He put his forehead down on the stone shelf. The surface was cold. The air smelled of limestone and old dust. The light from the walls was steady and unconcerned. He sat like that for a long time with his forehead on the cold stone and the blood drying on his lip and did not do anything that could be called thinking, because the thoughts that were available to him — the practical question and the moral question and the question of what kind of person sits and watches — did not lead anywhere he could go.

After a while he picked his head up and wiped his face again and opened the manuscript and made himself read. The notation resolved slowly, requiring the sustained attention that crowded out other thought, and the crowding-out was the point. He read. The translation problem absorbed him. The image of Duncan on the floor did not disappear, but it shifted to the edge of his attention. He kept running into it anyway. He worked on the ward-substrate coupling problem until the light from the walls dimmed — it did that sometimes, following a cycle that had nothing to do with the castle's rhythms — and then he packed the manuscript and went back down through the rearranging corridors, which took longer this time, twenty minutes instead of five, the castle not cooperating.

***

Miren was in the common room when he came through the barrel entrance. She was standing near the notice board, talking to Ernie Macmillan, who was holding a rolled-up piece of parchment and nodding at something she'd said. She saw Gavin and broke off from Ernie and came over.

She had a bruise on her jaw. It was fresh — purplish and dark, the size of a thumb, on the left side below her ear. It hadn't been there at breakfast. She didn't mention it. Ernie had glanced at it while they were talking and hadn't mentioned it either, which meant it was DA-related and therefore not discussed in the open common room.

"I need you to do something," she said. She sat down across from him at the study table where he'd dropped his bag. "Lookout. Tonight. An hour, probably less."

"What's it for?"

"Supply run. Healing paste, dittany, some bandages. Neville's lot have a delivery coming through the passage near the greenhouses and someone's got to watch the east corridor while they bring it in."

"Can't someone else do it?"

"Everyone else is already doing something." She said it flatly, without emphasis. It was a statement of logistics, not a plea. "It's an hour, Gav. Stand at the corner by the Charms classroom and if you see anyone coming, send red sparks. That's it."

He wanted to say no. The task itself was easy. That wasn't the problem. He knew what would happen. He would stand at the corner and his attention would slide away from the corridor and toward the coupling problem he'd been working on, and he would miss something or react late and it would be worse than if no one had been watching at all. He knew this about himself. He knew it with the specific certainty of a person who had been watching his own attention wander for months and could no longer pretend it was accidental.

"Yeah," he said. "All right."

Miren looked at him for a moment. He didn't know what she saw — the dried blood in his nostrils, probably, and the ink on his jaw where he'd been resting his chin on his hand in the sub-collection, and whatever his face was doing. She didn't ask about any of it.

"Ten o'clock. The junction by Charms. I'll come get you."

She went back to Ernie. They bent over the piece of parchment together. Gavin watched them for a moment and then stopped watching because the parchment was DA business and he had not earned the right to know what was on it.

Duncan was in the armchair by the fire. He was sitting as he sat now — pulled in, knees drawn up, his body occupying as little space as possible. His lip was swollen and dark on one side, the cut splitting the skin in a line that would scar if it wasn't treated, and it probably wouldn't be treated because the healing paste was being smuggled and hadn't arrived yet. He had a book open on his knees but he wasn't reading it. One hand was tucked into his robe pocket, thumb moving lightly against whatever paper was folded there, the same small back-and-forth motion over and over as if he was checking it hadn't gone anywhere. He was looking at the fire with the fixed, inward gaze of someone whose attention was not in the room.

Gavin got halfway out of his chair. Duncan lifted a hand to his mouth, touched the swelling there, and winced. The sound from the classroom came back whole. Gavin stopped hard enough that the heel of his shoe scraped the floor. He stood there with one hand on the strap of his bag, not saying anything, until the moment had gone too long to be casual. Then he sat down again.

He opened his bag and took out the Charms essay he'd been writing and turned it over to the blank back and started sketching notation. The coupling problem was there, where he'd left it — the ward-maintenance principles assumed a uniform substrate density across the planetary surface, but the local density around Hogwarts was anomalously high, thickened by centuries of magical activity, and the coupling equations collapsed unless he accounted for the differential. He'd been trying to model it all week. The notation kept resolving into configurations he couldn't interpret, which might mean the differential was too complex for his current notation skills or might mean his initial assumptions were wrong. He worked on it until ten o'clock, filling the back of the parchment with symbols that Babbling would have recognized as notation and nobody else in the room would have recognized as anything.

Miren came back. "Ready?"

They left through the barrel entrance and went up the stairs in near-darkness. The castle was quiet — the Carrow curfew meant most students were sealed in their common rooms by nine, and the corridors had the hollow, over-large feeling of a building that should have been full of people and wasn't. Their footsteps were loud on the stone. Water was running somewhere inside the walls — the thaw, snowmelt seeping through the outer stonework — and the sound made the castle feel less like a school and more like a thing that was slowly, imperceptibly eroding.

Miren walked ahead, moving with the quick, quiet confidence of someone who had done this before, who knew which corridors had monitoring enchantments and which flagstones squeaked and where Filch ran his patrols on Friday nights. She'd been doing this work for weeks, probably months — the supply runs, the lookout shifts, the small logistical acts of resistance that didn't make anyone a hero but kept people fed and healed and hidden. The bruise on her jaw was part of it, the cost of a specific incident she would not discuss.

They reached the junction. Miren pointed. "There. You can see down both directions. Anyone comes — Carrow, Filch, Snape, anyone — send sparks. Don't wait to identify them."

"Got it."

"I'll be at the greenhouse passage. Twenty minutes, maybe less."

She turned and walked away down the corridor. Gavin watched her go — the practical plait, the quick stride — and then she was around the corner and he was alone at the junction with his wand in his right hand and the east corridor stretching in two directions, lit by torches at wide intervals that left dark gaps between them.

He watched. He scanned the corridor in both directions, listened for footsteps, kept his wand ready. The stone walls were damp — he could feel the cold moisture in the air, could see the faint sheen of water on the nearest wall where it caught the torchlight. The sound of dripping was louder here, closer to the castle's outer shell.

Three minutes passed. He watched the corridor and it was empty and the silence pressed on his ears and the coupling problem was right there, sitting at the front of his mind with the patient insistence of something that would not be ignored.

He held it off for another minute. He counted flagstones. He tracked the torch flames. He tried to anchor his attention to the physical fact of the corridor — the cold, the damp, the dark at either end — and for sixty seconds it worked.

Then the coupling problem expanded. If the density differential was logarithmic rather than linear, the notation's collapse into unreadable configurations might not be a failure — it might be a description of a fundamentally different kind of relationship, one that his linear assumptions couldn't accommodate. The ward-maintenance principles were designed for a civilization that lived in the substrate as naturally as humans lived in air, and their mathematics assumed a perceptual range that Gavin didn't have, and the notation might be collapsing because it was trying to show him something his mind wasn't shaped to receive yet. If he approached the differential as a perceptual limit rather than a mathematical error—

He heard something and his body jerked and his hand tightened on his wand. The corridor was empty — just a torch guttering, shadows rearranging themselves. His heart was beating hard in his ears.

He went back to watching. He held the coupling problem at bay for another thirty seconds before his mind slid back to the differential. If he reframed the logarithmic—

Then he heard footsteps. Real ones, not a torch or a shadow or the water in the walls. Someone walking down the east corridor from the direction of the Astronomy Tower, an unhurried pace, steady and confident.

He raised his wand. His left hand was shaking — the tremor, which had been quiet all evening, was back — and he transferred the wand to his right hand, which was steady, and aimed at the ceiling and sent up red sparks.

He was late. The sparks burst over a seventh-year with a DA coin on the front of his jumper, dark hair, and a fresh scratch across one cheek. It was one of Neville's people. From the greenhouse end, out of Gavin's sight, came a muffled thud and then the brittle crash of glass on stone. Someone swore under their breath and cut it off.

The runner stopped, pressing himself flat against the wall until the sparks died. Then he looked at Gavin.

"You the lookout?"

"Yeah. Sorry. I didn't — sorry, I thought you were—"

"I was meant to have ten seconds." The runner kept his voice low. Then he moved on down the corridor, faster now, and Gavin was alone again.

He kept watching. This time he held his attention on the corridor by force, keeping his eyes fixed on the dark at both ends, and the effort was physical — a pressure behind his eyes, a strain in his forehead, the specific sensation of holding a door shut against something pushing from the other side. The coupling problem pressed and pressed and he kept the door shut and the twenty minutes passed and nobody else came.

Miren came back from the greenhouse end. She had a bag over her shoulder that hadn't been there before, heavy enough to pull the strap tight against her jumper.

"Any trouble?" she asked.

"I set sparks for Tom."

She looked at him. "Late."

He didn't say anything.

She shifted the bag higher on her shoulder. The canvas was wet along one side and smelled sharply of dittany. "We got the paste and the bandages. The dittany smashed in the drain."

"Sorry."

"If you can't do twenty minutes, say so next time."

"Come on, then."

They walked back to the common room in silence. It wasn't easy silence. Miren kept half a pace ahead of him and never looked round. She'd asked him for twenty minutes of attention. He'd lost the corridor to a notation problem after three. The bag over her shoulder was lighter than it should have been and smelled sharply of the dittany they'd lost in the drain. He couldn't tell whether she was angry, exhausted, or simply done talking about it, and he did not ask.

The common room was nearly empty. Duncan was still in the armchair by the fire. His book had fallen shut on his knees and his hands were loose at his sides. His eyes were closed now, and Gavin couldn't tell if he was sleeping or just holding still because holding still was easier than anything else.

Miren dropped the supply bag by the notice board for Ernie to deal with and went over to Duncan. She crouched beside his chair and took a small tin from the bag. Duncan opened his eyes when she touched his shoulder.

"Other side," he said. "Letter's in this pocket."

She shifted without comment and dabbed paste at the split in his lip. Duncan sat very still through it, one hand still covering the pocket, and when she was done he said, "Thanks," as if the word ought to settle the whole exchange. Miren rested her hand on his shoulder and left it there. The thumb inside Duncan's pocket stopped moving over the folded letter.

Gavin went upstairs. He sat on his bed and opened the manuscript to the coupling problem and worked on it. After ten minutes his nose started bleeding again, a slow seep that he caught with his handkerchief, tilting his head forward so the blood fell on the cloth and not on the pages. He kept working. The notation shifted under his attention, configurations resolving and collapsing, the logarithmic differential still resisting his attempts to frame it in terms his mind could process.

In the common room below, one floor down and a spiral staircase away, Miren sat with Duncan by the fire. Gavin sat on the edge of his bed with the manuscript open and the handkerchief balled red in his fist.

He worked until his vision blurred and the notation dissolved into shapes he could not read. Then he shut the manuscript and sat for a long time with the bloody handkerchief in his fist, listening to water move in the walls. Sleep didn't come.
