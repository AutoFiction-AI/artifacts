# Chapter 16

On the fifth floor, broken glass still covered the corridor outside what used to be the Muggle Studies classroom. Someone had taken out the display case holding a trophy from the 1987 Inter-House Herbology Competition — Gavin couldn't tell whether that had been defiance or accident — and a scorch mark shaped like a handprint blackened the left wall while the ceiling tiles above the doorway hung blasted loose, exposing old iron brackets and something that looked like copper pipe except Hogwarts didn't have pipes. He stepped over the glass and kept walking.

The substrate around the scorch mark was still unsettled. He felt it before he saw it: the agreements governing the wall's surface were slightly loose where the spell had landed, the stone's certainty about its own composition frayed at the edges. Two days ago the mark would have drawn him into a closer look, the residual substrate distortion interesting in a clinical way. Now he just noted it and moved on. The castle was full of small wounds like this. Every corridor had them.

Somewhere above him, something heavy fell and something lighter shattered. The sounds reached him through layers of stone — a whump, then a bright spray of breaking glass, then voices he couldn't distinguish. He paused and listened. No screaming. He kept going.

The school had been coming apart for the past week in increments. Each day's damage looked manageable until you noticed students moving through the corridors like they answered to somebody other than the school.

Gavin turned the corner onto the seventh floor and nearly walked into a group of four students heading the other direction. They were carrying things: a canvas bag that clinked with potion bottles, a folded blanket, what looked like a first-aid kit with Madam Pomfrey's seal on the clasp. The girl in front was a sixth-year Hufflepuff named Elara Stelwick, and she had a burn scar on the back of her left hand, shiny and oval, from November when Carrow had made them practise fire-conjuring on each other. She carried the canvas bag with that hand as if she'd stopped noticing the scar months ago. The boy behind her — tall, thick-set, wearing trainers under his robes instead of proper shoes — was looking past Gavin with the fixed stare of somebody waiting for a shout.

The shout came.

"You lot. Stop."

Amycus Carrow was striding up the corridor with his wand out, sleeves shoved up, sweat darkening the collar of his robes. He still had a few strides to go, but the old rules of the school said the shout should have been enough. Elara slowed. Nobody else did.

"Hospital wing," she said, before he could speak again.

"Don't bullshit me. Give it here."

He caught the bag strap in one fist and yanked. Bottles knocked together hard enough to crack. Elara braced and held on. The tall boy behind her shifted his weight like he was about to put a shoulder into Carrow and was still deciding. Carrow's wand came up.

Somebody farther down the corridor shouted, "Move, Elara."

A Shield Charm hit Carrow from the side hard enough to twist him half around. His shoulder slammed the wall. The curse he fired went into the loose ceiling above the old Muggle Studies door and blew plaster dust and one sharp tile shard across the passage. Something wet burst inside the bag at the same time, and the smell of dittany came up green and raw over the old iron smell in the grout. Elara tore the strap free. A strip of broken glass opened her knuckles and blood ran bright over the burn scar.

They all moved at once. Gavin stepped sideways because standing in the middle of other people's decisions was how you got hit. The four students shoved past him toward the stretch of corridor that led, if the Room wanted it, to the Room of Requirement. Amycus straightened with white dust on his sleeve and his jaw working. He half-raised his wand again, saw two more students at the far end with theirs already out, and didn't cast. "Get back here," he shouted. Nobody did.

That was what had changed. Two months ago he'd have had someone screaming on the floor by now. Now the Room of Requirement was swallowing students faster than the Carrows could drag them back out, and the students still left in the corridors were carrying blankets, dittany, and stolen food in the open. Neville Longbottom had gone in first, three or four days ago, and after that there'd been a steady trickle of Gryffindors, Hufflepuffs, and Ravenclaws choosing the Room over class. Their friends covered for them with excuses so transparent they were basically insults. Gone to the hospital wing. Visiting family. She was ill. Increasingly, the Carrows let the lies stand.

At the substrate level it was worse. The school's magical structure — the layered agreements that held the castle together, the wards within wards that governed everything from the shifting staircases to the enchanted ceiling — was under stress. It vibrated slightly, like a window in a strong wind. Every hexed door and blasted corridor and practice duel left a small residue in the substrate, and the residues were accumulating. Gavin felt the buildup behind his eyes before it tipped over into pain.

The rumour had reached even him. Harry Potter had done something. He'd been seen, or he was coming, or he'd already come. The details shifted by the hour — broke into Gringotts, escaped on a dragon, spotted in Hogsmeade — but the specifics didn't matter as much as the fact that the student body had started believing them. Something was moving. The people who'd been enduring the regime with their heads down were lifting them.

The corridor sequence to the sub-collection was unchanged. The seventh-floor passage shifted and reconnected itself in its usual not-quite-predictable pattern, and the temperature dropped as he moved deeper into the older stonework. The torches ended. The walls took on their own faint luminescence, mineral-cold and steady. The air went dry and smelled of limestone and nothing else.

The sub-collection was as he'd left it that morning. The manuscript lay open on the stone shelf he'd been using as a desk, held flat by two pieces of quartz he'd taken from the Hufflepuff common room's decorative rock collection. The notation on the exposed pages moved in its slow, representational way, re-presenting the same relationships from different angles the way rotating an object showed different faces. He sat on the floor, pulled his legs under him, and read.

The ward-maintenance section was the worst part of the manuscript. The entity section was more disturbing, the passages describing what existed beyond the wards rendered in notation that taxed his perception until his nose bled and the edges of his vision went grey. The maintenance section was worse in a different way: it was practical, it was necessary, and it was impossible.

The operations it described were not hard in the way a difficult spell was hard, where more power or more precision or more practice could close the gap. They were hard the way arithmetic was hard for a dog. They assumed a cognitive architecture that Gavin did not have. The Builders who'd created the wards had maintained them through acts of unified perception — holding simultaneous awareness of all the variables in a local ward structure and adjusting them together as if closing a hand. All the fingers moved at once because they were part of the same motion.

The specific operation he'd been working on for three days involved a thin place in the ward structure he'd mapped from the Astronomy Tower in March. The repair required simultaneous adjustment of what the notation represented as seventeen interlocking parameters — the permeability coefficients, the coherence thresholds, the phase relationships between adjacent segments. Each parameter depended on the current state of all the others. You couldn't adjust one and then adjust the next because adjusting one changed the context for the next.

He could hold six of the parameters in his awareness at once. On a good day, with rest and quiet and no substrate noise from the school above him, he could sometimes manage seven. The procedure needed seventeen.

It was like being given a recipe that called for an oven he didn't own and couldn't build. The recipe wasn't wrong. He was just the wrong species of cook.

He'd spent three days looking for a workaround and the manuscript offered none, because the manuscript had been written for readers who could do what it described. He was the wrong reader. The wards continued to fail. Nobody else was reading.

He read. The notation shifted under his attention, showing him relationships he could almost follow, connections that resolved when he held his focus at exactly the right angle and dissolved into noise when his concentration slipped. The spring warmth of the upper castle did not reach the sub-collection. The stone under him was the same temperature it had been in January. He sat on the cold floor and failed to understand and read again.

---

He left the sub-collection two hours later because he needed to eat and because the afterimages had started. The notation's representational movement was lingering in his peripheral vision even when he looked away from the manuscript — shapes that shifted and re-presented at the edges of what he could see, as if the notation had imprinted itself on his visual processing and wouldn't let go. He'd learned in January that this meant he'd pushed too far and needed to let his eyes settle. That lesson had cost him a bruised shoulder and a cracked pair of glasses when he'd walked into a door frame because the edges of solid objects had gone slightly uncertain.

The seventh floor was empty. The corridors had the held-breath quality of a building that knew something was coming — not the ordinary between-classes quiet of students moving purposefully, but the taut silence of a space that had been cleared. Outside the windows, the grounds were absurd with spring. The lawns had turned the bright, almost edible green of late April, and there were flowers in the courtyard below — daffodils, mostly, and something purple — and the late-afternoon light came in at the angle that made the Scottish Highlands look painted. A warm end-of-April day. Birdsong from the forest edge. Completely wrong, given everything.

He took the back staircase down toward the kitchens. Fourth floor, third floor, the connecting passage behind the tapestry of Barnabas the Barmy — the shortest route if you wanted to avoid the main corridors and the remaining Carrow patrols. He was thinking about the maintenance problem. Specifically, he was wondering whether the seventeen-parameter operation could be decomposed into a sequence of smaller operations, each involving six or fewer parameters, performed in order rather than simultaneously. It would be like doing long division instead of seeing the answer whole — slower, uglier, but possible for a mind that couldn't hold the full picture. The question was whether the sequential approach would preserve the interdependencies or destroy them. He thought it might destroy them. He was thinking about this when he heard the kid.

A small sound. The caught, pressed-down sound of someone trying to be quiet because being loud was dangerous this year.

He stopped. The passage behind the tapestry was narrow and lit only by the glow that seeped through the tapestry's weave. It took him a moment to find the source: a boy sitting on the floor against the left wall, knees drawn up, cradling his right arm against his chest. A first-year. Gavin could tell by the size of him — small, sharp-faced, not yet used to his own proportions. Ravenclaw tie, the blue barely visible in the dim passage. His robes were too big for him, bought with room to grow or handed down from someone larger, and his shoes were scuffed pale at the toes in the way that meant he kicked things when he walked, a habit he probably wasn't aware of. His right wrist was bent at an angle that wrists did not go.

Gavin crouched. "Hey. Can I see?"

The boy looked up. His face was white and his eyes were wet but he wasn't crying, which was either courage or shock. In his left hand — the good hand — he was holding a folded piece of parchment, tight enough to crease it.

"I fell," the boy said. His voice was thin and careful. "The stairs moved. I wasn't — I was going to send a letter and the stairs just—"

"Yeah. They do that. Let me see your wrist."

The boy held it out. The break was clean — Gavin could feel it before he touched the skin, the way he felt everything now, the double image of the physical and the structural. The radius was fractured at a specific point. At the substrate level, the bone's agreements about its own integrity had been interrupted: the rules that said this bone is whole, continuous, load-bearing had a local exception at the point of impact, and that exception was generating pain signals that the boy's nervous system was translating into the white face and the wet eyes and the bitten lip.

Gavin reached for the break.

What happened next took less than a second.

His mind went to the substrate. It skipped past the Episkey he'd used before on scraped knees and small cuts, past the spell and past his wand, to the agreement that defined the bone's structural state. The fracture was an exception to the bone's default rules. Exceptions could be revoked. His attention closed around the point of the break like a hand closing around a stone and he began to edit, making the fracture less structurally valid, reducing the exception's claim on reality, so that the bone's default state — whole, continuous, bearing weight — simply reasserted itself the way water filled a hole.

He did it before thought caught up. His hand never went to the wand. The intention never formed in words. His perception had seen the break and his mind had gone to the substrate the way another person's hand might have gone to their wand — automatically, below the level of decision, with the same blind speed as catching a falling glass.

The boy gasped.

Gavin stopped.

He'd been doing it for perhaps half a second. The bone was already partly corrected. The process was correction at the level of structure, and the distinction mattered enormously even though it was invisible to anyone who couldn't perceive the substrate. Magical healing knit tissue: it told the body's existing processes to work faster, reconnecting what had been separated, building a callus, leaving a remodelling scar that Madam Pomfrey could read like a signature. What Gavin had been doing was different. He'd been removing the fracture's validity — making the break less real, so the bone's wholeness simply reasserted. No knitting. No callus. No scar. The bone wouldn't have been healed; it would have been a bone that had never been broken, in a body that had broken it three minutes ago.

Madam Pomfrey would notice. If she examined the wrist, she would notice. A bone with no history of fracture in a child who had just fractured it — that gap would be visible to any competent healer.

He pulled back from the substrate and drew his wand.

"Episkey."

The spell was rough. His wand work had been deteriorating all year and the Episkey landed with too much force — the boy flinched, and the bone knit with an audible crack that made them both wince. But it knit. Magically. Tissue reconnecting, callus forming, the swelling beginning to subside. The mundane, explainable kind of healing that left the right residue.

The boy's face went from white to a raw, blotchy pink as the pain dropped.

"Better?" Gavin said.

The boy flexed his fingers carefully. "Yeah. Yeah, it's — ow. But yeah." He looked at Gavin's wand, then at Gavin, with the expression of someone trying to decide whether to trust what had just happened. "Thanks."

"Don't put too much weight on it tonight. If it still hurts tomorrow, go see Pomfrey."

"She's been — they say she's really busy. With the Room people and everything." The boy tucked his mended wrist against his chest and got up, still clutching the folded parchment in his good hand. His too-big robes dragged on the stone floor. "Thanks," he said again, and went, his scuffed shoes receding down the passage until the sound faded and the held-breath quiet of the castle settled back in.

Gavin stayed where he was. Crouching in the narrow passage behind the tapestry, one knee on the cold floor, his wand still out.

The decision had happened below thought. That was the thing. His conscious practice of the First Art — the deliberate, effortful sessions in the sub-collection where he tried to hold six variables and failed at the seventh — was clumsy, approximate, exhausting. The gap between intention and execution was wide enough to make him feel stupid on a daily basis. But the reflexive edit he'd just done on the boy's wrist had been precise. Clean. Faster and more elegant than anything he'd managed in deliberate practice. His unconscious mind was better at this than his conscious mind, which meant the First Art had settled into whatever layer of cognition handled the things you did without deciding to do them — catching, flinching, breathing, the operations that ran beneath the surface of thought.

He thought about what would have happened if he hadn't caught it. The boy would have walked away with a perfect wrist. A wrist that had been corrected at the level of reality rather than healed at the level of magic. Nobody would have known, probably. Nobody would have looked closely at a first-year's mended wrist. But probably was the problem. If someone had looked — if Pomfrey had examined it, if anyone with training had checked — the absence of healing would have been conspicuous. A bone with no history of injury. An impossible clean gap where the evidence of fracture should have been.

And the next question, the one they wouldn't know to ask but that Gavin couldn't stop asking himself: what did it mean for a living body to be edited at the substrate level? Healing worked with the body's own processes. Substrate correction worked around them. The bone's biology hadn't healed; the bone's rules had been rewritten. Was there a cost to that? A side effect? The manuscript didn't say, because the manuscript didn't contemplate its techniques being used on beings whose bodies relied on fixed biological rules. The Builders hadn't had bodies in the sense that mattered. They wouldn't have needed to ask.

He stood up. His left hand was doing the intermittent tremor — the Cruciatus remnant from November that came and went, came and went, tied to stress or fatigue or both. He pressed it against his thigh until it settled. Then he put his wand in his pocket and went to the kitchens.

The house-elves gave him bread and cold chicken and a mug of tea that was too hot to drink. He sat at the long table in the kitchen's warmth and ate without tasting. The kitchen smelled of roasting meat and baking bread and something sweet — a pudding being assembled for a dinner that fewer students attended each day. Two house-elves were arguing near the ovens about the quantity of potatoes, a dispute conducted in high, rapid voices and resolved by a third elf who intervened with a serving spoon and a pointed look. Gavin ate his bread and drank his tea when it had cooled enough and thought about the substrate edit and the boy's wrist and the fact that his reflexes had outstripped his conscious ability, and what that meant for the work, and whether it was a sign of progress or something else.

---

He went back to the sub-collection after dark.

The route was harder at night because the Carrows still patrolled, and their patrols had become erratic in the past few days. Even Filch seemed to have lost his stomach for catching students when the students who got caught ended up in Carrow's detentions, and the detentions had become the kind of thing that Filch, for all his petty cruelty, had not signed on for. Amycus had been spotted on the first floor at midnight and the seventh floor twenty minutes later, which either meant the castle was moving him or he was moving himself in the panicked, purposeless way of a man who'd been told something was coming and couldn't decide what to do about it. Gavin thought it was the latter. The substrate around Carrow had a specific quality these days — a tight, contracted feel, like the magic in his vicinity was pulling inward, bracing.

He reached the sub-collection without meeting anyone. The entrance — not a door, just a place where the corridor ended and older stone began — was as he'd left it. The air was dry and cool and smelled of mineral dust. The faint luminescence of the walls was steady, unchanged by anything that happened in the castle above. The manuscript lay open on its stone shelf, the quartz weights holding the pages, the notation moving in its slow patient loop.

He sat. He tried the sequential-decomposition approach he'd been considering on the way to the kitchens: take the full operation and break it into sub-operations small enough for a human mind to do in series. For the first two, it almost looked workable. The notation accepted the adjustments. The local structure held. On the third, the earlier changes started drifting under his hands. By the time he pulled back, the thin place he'd been trying to repair was thinner by a margin he could perceive.

He sat back and breathed through the sick drop of having made it worse. The cold floor had settled into his lower back and ankles where his robes didn't cover. He pulled his robe tighter and made himself look at the page instead of away from it.

The notation's point was plain enough now. The operation had a single geometry. If he chopped it into pieces, the geometry vanished. He found a passage he'd translated weeks ago and re-read it. The manuscript's nearest phrase for the maintenance act was, in Gavin's rough version, a single shape made of attention.

That was new, or new enough to matter. Shape. Effort and time had been the wrong variables. Shape was the thing. He turned the word over while the cold of the stone seeped through his robes.

Then something changed.

It was subtle at first. A faint shift in the substrate set his teeth on edge. The substrate flexed well beyond the sub-collection's small stone rooms, a large-scale perturbation pressing against the edges of his perception.

He set the maintenance problem aside and attended.

People. A lot of people moving into place. A gathering. The substrate registered the convergence as pressure and vibration at once, the preparatory hum of many wands and many intentions lined up in roughly the same direction. He couldn't tell who they were or what they wanted, not from substrate perception alone. But the scale was large. Dozens at least, maybe more spreading out beyond what he could map cleanly.

He waited. The perturbation grew.

Over the next hour it intensified from a background pressure to a steady, unmistakable distortion. More people were arriving or taking positions somewhere beyond the castle's outer edges. The ward structures overhead — the vast interlocking patterns that he perceived as something between architecture and music, beautiful and degrading at their margins — began to resonate with the buildup below them. Dust sifted from the ceiling joints and settled across the manuscript in a pale grit. The quartz weights clicked against the stone shelf. Somewhere in the outer room an old seam gave a sharp crack.

Then feet hammered past the entrance.

Gavin got up and stepped to the threshold. Three students came through the old corridor at a half-run, lit by the wall-glow in brief jerks. The first was a seventh-year Ravenclaw Gavin knew by sight, wand out, soot black on one cheek. Behind him two girls were carrying a younger boy between them. The boy's left sleeve had burned away from wrist to elbow. The skin underneath was wet and pink and starting to blister, and every time his heel struck stone he made a short animal noise through his teeth. One girl's hands were wet to the wrists; when she hit the wall rounding the corner she left a red print there.

"The back stair's gone," the Ravenclaw said, not really to Gavin, more to the corridor itself. "Other way. Keep moving."

They were already past him. Burnt wool and dittany and blood hung in the air after them.

Gavin went to the turn where his route back normally opened. The passage had shifted while he'd been reading. Where the narrow descending run should have been there was seamless limestone and a spill of fresh mortar powder across the floor. The way he'd used all year to get back to the kitchens and Hufflepuff was gone.

He stood with dust on his hands and felt the school above him filling. His problem lay in what the conflict would do to the substrate. He had made his assessment of the war months ago and nothing in the intervening weeks had changed it. The Voldemort war was a human-scale event in a human-scale conflict, and the worst outcome — the regime permanent, the persecution institutional, the Ministry a puppet of a man who called himself Lord and believed blood determined worth — was terrible at the scale of human suffering and irrelevant at the scale of what Gavin was working on. The wards did not care who sat in the Minister's chair. The thing beyond the wards, patient and incompatible and as indifferent to wizarding politics as weather was to the arguments of ants, would continue to press against the thinning barriers regardless of which faction won the argument above.

But if the gathering outside turned into a major magical battle — hundreds of people casting simultaneously, agreements invoked and violated and broken faster than he could separate them — the ward-maintenance work would become impossible. The fine-grained perceptual tracking he needed required a quiet substrate. A surgeon needed a still table. What was coming would be an earthquake if it broke open here. And now the castle had eaten his way out on top of it.

He would lose hours at best, maybe days, while the corridors reconfigured or the fighting cleared. The wards did not have days. The thin places he'd mapped in March were thinner now. The degradation was constant and patient. Each day that passed without maintenance was a day the margins eroded a little further, and the margins were where the wards would eventually fail, and the failure would be quiet and total and would not announce itself to anyone except the boy standing in an old corridor with mortar dust on his hands.

He went back to the manuscript. The pages were dusted white. He brushed grit off the lower half of the page and tried to settle into the notation again, but every fresh vibration pulled the work apart before it could take shape. The red handprint in the outer corridor kept snagging at the edge of his attention.

More feet passed at irregular intervals. Once he heard someone say, "Hold that there," followed by the wet cough of somebody trying not to choke. Once the old stone overhead rang hard enough to sting his teeth. It was still the last night of April, the castle filling and bracing before the battle, but even that much pressure was enough to rough up the substrate until the building hummed with it.

He took the manuscript, the quartz weights, and his notes into the innermost room and set them on the stone table there. It put more wall between him and the blocked corridor, though not enough to shut the noise out. The floor still answered distant impacts. Dust still shook loose from the ceiling joints in fine pale threads.

He sat in the dry limestone cold of the innermost room, the manuscript open before him on the stone table, the quartz weights ticking whenever the floor answered the noise above. The wards overhead were thin and thinning. The thing beyond them continued in its way, mindless and incompatible with everything that existed below the wards in the way too much load was incompatible with a beam already splitting along the grain: through the dull fact of pressure. His usual route out was gone. Smoke and blood smell had reached even here. The notation jittered on the page.

Gavin Tench, fifteen years old, mediocre at everything except the one thing that mattered and not good enough at that, sat at the stone table with the blocked corridor somewhere beyond two turns of old limestone and tried to keep the Builders' notation steady in his head while the world above him braced for a fight that had not started yet, over a problem he could solve in seconds and wouldn't, because the wards were failing, and the wards were all that mattered, and the wards did not care about the war.

He brushed dust from the margin and went back to the same section that had started to snag at him. The notation moved through another slow cycle. He read.
