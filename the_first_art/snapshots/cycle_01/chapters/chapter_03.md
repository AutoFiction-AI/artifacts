# Chapter 3

He waited three days before going back.

This was not strategy or discipline. The bruises from Carrow's detention still ached through his kneecaps whenever he climbed stairs, and the seventh floor was a long way up from the Hufflepuff basement. His left side was tender where a stinging hex had caught him across the ribs — whether Carrow had aimed there or was just a bad shot, Gavin hadn't decided. Either way, stairs were a problem.

On the third evening he went, because the ache had settled from sharp to dull and because he had not been able to stop thinking about the catalogue entry. _Sub-Collection C. Pre-Foundation Acquisitions._ A shelf number in handwriting so old the ink had gone the color of dust, and a title in a language he didn't recognize. The ink, at least, had stayed still.

He left after dinner. Miren was helping a second-year with a Switching Spell at one of the common room tables, her patience visibly thinning as the boy turned a teacup into a slightly different teacup for the fourth time running. Duncan had gone upstairs early, which he did most nights now — not because he was tired, Gavin thought, but because the common room required a version of himself that was getting harder to maintain. Nobody asked where Gavin was going. Nobody was watching him. He was the kind of person people's eyes moved past without stopping, and that had never bothered him, and tonight it was useful.

The seventh floor was quiet. An hour before curfew, but students avoided the upper corridors after dark this year — too many empty classrooms where Filch could find you, too close to the route the Carrows took between their quarters and Snape's office. Gavin kept his wand out and walked quickly, counting turns the way he'd memorized them from the catalogue room three nights ago. Left at the tapestry of the dancing trolls. Straight past the suit of armor with the dented visor and one gauntlet missing. Right at the landing where a tall window looked out onto the grounds. The lake was a flat dark shape, and beyond it the Forbidden Forest was a darker one, and the sky above both was clear and cold and full of more stars than he'd ever seen from London.

The corridor he needed should have been the next left turn.

It wasn't. He stood at a junction he was certain had not existed on Sunday night, when he'd stumbled through these corridors with his knees bleeding through his trousers. The left branch went down — he could see the top of a spiral staircase. The right branch ended at a wall twenty feet away, lit by a single torch that popped and hissed in its bracket. Neither was right.

Gavin turned around and went back to the suit of armor. Counted again. Left at the tapestry, straight past the armor, right at the landing with the window. The junction was gone. In its place the corridor continued straight ahead, curving gently to the left, and at its far end, where a torch should have been, there was no torch.

There was light, though. It was faint and steady and it came from the walls or the ceiling or the stone itself — he could not tell. It was simply there, the way air was there, without a source he could point to. It was not warm and not cold, not firelight or sunlight or wandlight. It was light that had always been there and did not need to explain itself.

He stopped walking. The stone underfoot had changed. He noticed it the way you notice a step that's slightly higher than the others — his body registered the difference before his brain caught up. The blocks were larger here, paler, smoother. The grey was different from the rest of the castle's grey. Not darker, not lighter. Just a different grey, from a different geology, cut by different hands or by no hands at all. The joints between the blocks held something that looked like mortar from a distance, and like nothing he could name from close up.

The air was different. It was colder by several degrees, and it was dry the way a cave is dry — not the absence of dampness but the presence of stone that had existed at this temperature and this moisture content for so long that the idea of damp belonged to a different place entirely. It smelled of minerals. Clean and cold. The smell of limestone and still water and nothing alive.

Gavin walked in.

The corridor opened into a room. It was small — he could have crossed it in four long strides. The ceiling was low, maybe seven feet, and the sourceless light was a little brighter here, enough to see clearly by. Shelves were cut directly into the walls, and the stone of the shelves and the stone of the wall were the same continuous piece. There were no joints, no seams, no chisel marks, no evidence of any tool. The shelves looked like they had formed that way, the way a riverbed forms ledges where the water runs over different kinds of rock.

A few objects sat on the lower shelves. There was a stone, roughly round, slightly too regular to be a river stone and not regular enough to have been deliberately shaped. Beside it sat a piece of something that might have been metal — it was solid and smooth and the color of it was wrong. Not silver, not copper, not iron, not bronze. It was a color he had not seen before, and looking at it produced the same feeling as hearing a chord with one note slightly off — you knew something was wrong but you could not say which part. He did not touch either of them.

Through a doorway in the back wall he found a second room, smaller than the first, with six shelves cut into the stone on each side. A few held objects — another piece of the wrong-colored not-metal, a flat disc of something translucent, what appeared to be a very small cup made of the same pale stone as the walls. The light was the same sourceless glow. The air was the same dry mineral cold.

The silence was specific. He noticed it here, standing between the two doorways, his breathing the only sound. It was not the silence of an empty room where sounds happened to be absent. It was the silence of a room that had not had sound in it for a very long time — so long that the air had settled into the stillness the way sediment settles in water. His own breathing seemed too loud, too wet, too alive for the space.

Through another doorway, a third room. The smallest of the three. Three shelves on each wall. Most of them empty.

The manuscript was on the middle shelf of the far wall, and he knew it was the manuscript because there was nothing else it could be. The catalogue had said _sheet collection, unbound, material unclassified_, and that description was accurate the way saying the ocean was wet was accurate — true and completely useless as preparation.

The sheets were stacked loosely, not bound. He reached for the top one and his fingers met something that was not parchment, not vellum, not paper, not any material he'd handled in five years of schoolwork and library books and Ancient Runes transcription exercises. It was thin and flexible and faintly warm. Not body-warm — not like something a person had been holding. It was warm the way stone was sometimes warm in August, holding the day's heat after sunset, except that it was October and this room was cold enough that he could feel the chill in his teeth.

The sheet did not feel old. That was the wrongest thing about it. He was standing in a room that smelled like it had been sealed since before the castle was a hill, holding something that the catalogue listed under _Pre-Foundation Acquisitions_, and it felt as if it had been made this morning. Not fresh, not new. It had simply never been subject to aging. The process of getting old was something that happened to other materials, and this one had not agreed to it.

He held the sheet up to the sourceless light.

There were marks on its surface. That much was clear. They were dark against the warm-toned material, and they were arranged in patterns that implied organization, that implied meaning, that implied someone had put them there for a reason. But they were not letters. They were not runes. They were not the pictographic notations he'd seen reproduced in his Ancient Runes textbook — the Sardinian temple marks, the Balkan fragments — things that were archaic and difficult but recognizably human in their impulse to record.

These marks were not recognizably anything.

Some of them bore a passing resemblance to Elder Futhark symbols, the way a cloud sometimes resembles a face. You could see it if you wanted to, but the resemblance was a product of your own pattern-matching, not anything the marks had intended. Other marks were shapes he had no category for: compound curves that did not resolve into anything he could name, angles that seemed to shift depending on where he focused, elements that might have been individual symbols or might have been parts of larger structures or might have been something outside the distinction between individual and composite entirely.

And they moved.

They did not move the way wizarding photographs moved. Gavin had been in the magical world long enough to know how that worked. Portraits moved because they were depictions of people, and the depicted people moved the way people moved. Photographs captured moments and replayed them. Even the writing on the Marauder's Map — everyone in school had heard the stories — tracked physical positions through physical space. All of it was mimetic. A picture of a thing, doing what the thing did.

The marks on the sheet were not mimicking anything. They shifted in place — slowly, with a deliberation that was somehow harder to watch than speed would have been. Two marks that had been adjacent separated. A third, from the far side of the sheet, drifted closer. A new configuration settled into place, and the arrangement was not random and was not mechanical and was not repetitive. It was showing something. A relationship between the marks, or between whatever concepts the marks represented, or between something Gavin did not have a word for because the word did not exist in any language he spoke.

He put the first sheet back and picked up another. The same material. The same warmth. The same impossible not-aging. The marks were different — different shapes, different arrangements — but the quality of their movement was the same. Different relational patterns, but the same sense of watching something organized and meaningful and completely, absolutely beyond him.

He went through six sheets. Then ten. Then all seventeen. His fingers were tingling from contact with the warm material in the cold room, or from something else he couldn't identify, and his wand-light was starting to feel intrusive against the clean sourceless glow of the walls.

He counted the stack three times and got seventeen each time.

He looked around the small stone room with its seamless shelves and its ageless light and its mineral silence, and he thought about what he was going to do. Taking the manuscript was theft. Not exactly — there was no sign, no lock, no checkout card, no Pince to forbid him. But the sub-collection was not the library. It was not part of the castle the way the classrooms and corridors and dormitories were part of the castle. It was older and it was other, and the things on these shelves had been placed here by someone or something that had assumed they would stay here, and that assumption had held for longer than the rest of Hogwarts had existed.

Gavin gathered the seventeen sheets into a stack. They were lighter than they should have been — lighter than the same volume of parchment, lighter than seemed right for how solid they felt. He slid them into his school bag between his Charms textbook and a roll of Potions notes.

The walk back through the three rooms felt shorter than the walk in. The corridor's pale stone gave way to the castle's regular grey, and a torch appeared on the wall, and then another, and the quality of light changed from sourceless and steady to the familiar flicker and shadow of a normal Hogwarts hallway. The air warmed. He could smell stone dust and old wood and the faint overlay of cooking from the kitchens somewhere below. He was standing on the seventh-floor landing by the window, and the grounds outside were dark, and the lake reflected a thin moon.

He went back to Hufflepuff. The barrel entrance thumped its rhythm and rolled open. The common room was mostly empty — three sixth-years near the fire, one of them with her legs draped across the other two's laps while all three read from the same textbook, arguing in murmurs about the properties of Bubotuber pus. Gavin crossed to the dormitory stairs and went up and put the manuscript in his trunk, underneath his spare robes and the Hufflepuff scarf he never wore because he didn't care about Quidditch, and he closed the lid and got into bed.

The sheets were down there in the dark, under the wool and cotton of things that aged normally. He could still feel the warmth of them on his fingertips, or thought he could. He lay on his back and stared at the canopy of his four-poster and tried to think about something else.

He was still trying when Owen Kirby came in an hour later, brushed his teeth at the bathroom sink with the noisy thoroughness he brought to everything, and fell asleep within minutes. Owen's breathing had the wheezing inhale-and-whistle that Gavin had been hearing since first year — Owen's lungs had never properly agreed with the castle's damp — and tonight the sound was almost comforting in its familiarity. Owen had left an unfinished letter to his mother on his nightstand, the quill laid across it at an angle, careful sentences visible even in the dim light. He wrote to her every week. He never said why.

Gavin listened to the whistle-and-pause. The manuscript was under his robes under his scarf in the trunk at the foot of his bed, and the marks on the seventeen sheets were moving in the dark, rearranging themselves into configurations that meant something to someone and nothing to him.

It took two weeks for the excitement to curdle into something worse.

The first three nights, he worked with the careful optimism of someone who believed the problem had a solution and the solution was effort. After the dormitory went quiet — Owen's whistle, Ben Alderton muttering about Quidditch formations in his sleep, the scratch and shuffle of someone's Pygmy Puff in its cage — Gavin pulled the sheets from his trunk, spread them across the mattress inside the closed curtains of his four-poster, lit his wand, and got to work.

He started with substitution analysis. If the notation was a cipher — each mark standing for a letter or a rune — then what he needed was the frequency distribution. He assigned numbers to the marks. Mark 1, angular, appeared twelve times across the first three sheets. Mark 2, a compound curve, appeared nine times. He catalogued forty-seven distinct marks before the system broke down, because Mark 23 and Mark 31 might have been the same mark in different orientations, or they might have been genuinely different marks that resembled each other, and the distinction mattered for frequency analysis and he could not determine which was the case.

And the marks kept moving.

That was the fundamental problem with every approach he tried. A symbol he'd catalogued as Mark 7 would shift its position relative to Mark 12, and the shift would change the apparent relationship between them, and relationships between marks seemed to carry as much meaning as the marks themselves. Frequency analysis assumed that symbols held still. These did not.

He tried treating the movement as data. He mapped the positions of twenty marks on Sheet 1 at the start of an evening, then checked again an hour later, then an hour after that. The positions had changed, but not in ways that repeated or cycled. The movement was — he kept reaching for this word and it kept being insufficient — relational. The marks moved in response to each other, adjusting distances and angles the way a mobile shifts when you touch one hanging piece and the rest swing to rebalance. Except a mobile settled. The marks did not settle. They found a new arrangement and held it for a while and then found another.

He tried ignoring the individual marks entirely and looking at the arrangements as wholes. He spent two nights on this, mapping the overall shape of each sheet's configuration at intervals and trying to identify the pattern at the level of the whole page rather than the individual symbol. The patterns were complex and organized in a way that made him feel thick, the way sitting through an upper-year Arithmancy lecture made you feel thick — you knew structure was there, you could almost see the shape of it, but you couldn't grab hold of any single piece firmly enough to pull yourself in.

He tried the library. In his free periods he pulled everything he could find on non-standard notation systems: goblin metalwork engravings, the Mermish syllabary, pre-Roman runework from the supplementary reading list Babbling had posted at the start of term. He found _Comparative Symbolic Systems of the Mediterranean Basin_ on a high shelf near the Restricted Section and spent an evening with it, cross-referencing illustrations of Sardinian temple inscriptions and Basque funerary notation against what he remembered from the manuscript. A few shapes triggered a flicker of recognition — a curve here, an angle there — but every resemblance evaporated when he went back to the actual sheets. The library's marks were writing. His marks were something that used the same surface-level format as writing without being writing in any way that mattered.

He was careful to do this research openly, as if it were coursework. Anyone watching — and he was aware, now, that anyone could be watching, that this year the watchers included people with the authority to hurt you — would have seen a fifth-year doing extra reading for Ancient Runes, which was both boring and plausible and therefore invisible.

On the eighth night, lying on his back in the dark at two in the morning while Owen breathed and Ben muttered and the castle made its slow settling noises around him, Gavin thought: the marks were not writing.

Not in the sense of being some other form of record — not pictures, not diagrams, not music notation. The whole category of written communication might be wrong. Writing was marks standing for other things. Letters stood for sounds. Runes stood for concepts or sounds or both. Pictographs stood for objects or actions. The marks on the manuscript were not standing for anything. They were doing something that the human concept of representation did not include.

He did not know what that meant. He turned the thought over in his mind like a stone in his pocket, and it sat there being smooth and opaque and useless.

Miren noticed. She always noticed things.

It was a Wednesday of the second week, and they were at lunch, and Gavin was not at lunch — he was sitting in front of a plate of food at the Hufflepuff table pushing potatoes from one side to the other while thinking about whether the relational movement of the marks might encode temporal information, whether the sequence of configurations mattered, whether the thing he needed was not a key but a timeline.

"You're not eating."

He looked up. Miren was across the table with her hair in its usual plait and her robe sleeves pushed up past her elbows the way she always wore them, and she was looking at him with an expression he recognized as the thing that came before concern — the attentive noticing that, given enough evidence, would harden into worry.

"I'm eating," he said. He ate a potato to prove it.

"You've been weird lately."

"I haven't been —" He stopped. "Weird how?"

"Like that." She pointed her fork at him. "You're sitting here but you're not here. You've been doing it for over a week. You're always somewhere else."

This was accurate enough that it was hard to argue with. "I'm just tired. Haven't been sleeping great."

"Since the detention?"

"Yeah," he said, and it was easy to let her think it was the detention, which had been bad enough to explain some sleeplessness. His knees still ached in the mornings. The stinging-hex bruise on his ribs had turned yellow-green and was taking its time about fading.

"I heard Harper got it worse," Miren said, and the conversation shifted to the familiar territory of who had been punished for what and how badly, which was the background noise of social life at Hogwarts this year — the constant low-grade accounting of damage. Gavin ate his lunch and contributed small agreeing sounds at the right moments and thought about temporal encoding and whether he'd been looking at the sheets wrong this whole time, reading them as static documents when they might be something closer to — what? A process? A demonstration?

Duncan was sitting on Miren's other side, eating with the focused attention he brought to meals this term. He had lost weight since September — not dramatically, but his collarbones showed through his neckline in a way they hadn't before, and his school robes had a careful mend over the right shoulder from something he'd never explained. He ate everything on his plate and two pieces of bread from the basket and did not volunteer anything to the conversation.

"There's pudding," Miren told him. "Treacle tart."

"I'm all right."

"Take some anyway. You like treacle tart."

Duncan took some. He ate it with the slow thoroughness of someone who'd learned to take food seriously when it was available. Miren watched him eat and did not say whatever she was thinking, and Gavin noticed the exchange the way he noticed the weather through the enchanted ceiling — something that was happening in the world around him that he registered without deciding to do anything about it.

That evening, back in his curtained bed with the sheets spread across his knees, Gavin tried the temporal approach. He selected Sheet 3 — the one where two marks in the upper left had maintained a consistent proximity even while everything else shifted — and set his watch on the mattress beside him. He mapped the full configuration at the start, then again at ten-minute intervals.

After an hour he had six maps. After two hours he had twelve. He laid them out in sequence and looked for the pattern.

The configurations changed, but not in a way that cycled or repeated. Each new arrangement was unique. The two stable marks in the upper left held their relationship to each other, but their position relative to the rest of the sheet drifted slowly, and the rest of the sheet rearranged itself around them in ways that were clearly organized but completely opaque.

He looked at the twelve maps for a long time. The Pygmy Puff scratched in its cage. Owen's watch ticked on the nightstand, a faint, steady sound beneath the wheeze of his breathing. The candle in Gavin's wand-light flickered and the shadows of the four-poster curtains swayed.

He could see that the marks were doing something. He could see that the something was structured and purposeful and consistent. He just could not read it. The gap between seeing that a pattern existed and understanding what the pattern meant was exactly the width of his own limitations, and no amount of effort was going to close it without a tool he did not have.

He found three more pairs of marks with consistent proximity — on Sheets 9, 14, and 16. He noted their positions and the other marks they drifted near. He felt a surge of focus when the third pair emerged, the concentration sharpening the way it did when you spotted a pattern in a Runes exercise and thought you were about to crack it — but the feeling dissolved almost immediately, because the four stable pairs had no obvious relationship to each other, no shared feature he could extract, no key that unlocked anything further. They were stable in a shifting field, and that was all he could say about them. It was not enough.

By the third week, he was angry.

The anger was specific and self-directed. The manuscript existed. The marks meant something. The relational movement was organized. All of this was obviously, frustratingly true, and the only obstacle between the truth and understanding was that Gavin Tench — fifteen years old, low Acceptable in Ancient Runes, middling marks in everything, exceptional at nothing — was not smart enough to bridge the gap. The problem in front of him had been set by someone or something that operated at a level of complexity he could not reach. He didn't know who or what had made the manuscript, but it had not been made for him.

He kept trying anyway, because the alternative was stopping.

He fell asleep in History of Magic on a Wednesday. Binns did not notice — Binns noticed nothing, had possibly not noticed that he himself was dead — but the girl beside him, a Ravenclaw with four different-colored quills arranged in a neat row and notes so dense and organized they looked like a printed textbook, nudged his elbow when his head slid off his hand and cracked against the desk hard enough to make the front row turn around.

"Sorry," Gavin said, straightening up. His neck ached. His eyes felt as if they'd been rolled in grit.

"You were drooling," the Ravenclaw said. She had a smudge of green ink on her left index finger and the weary patience of someone who had sat next to worse. "Binns didn't notice, but everyone else did."

"Sorry. Thanks."

She turned back to her color-coded notes without comment. Gavin opened his textbook to a page about the Goblin Rebellions of 1612 and stared at it. The ink on the page did not move. He thought about the stable pairs and whether their consistent proximity implied a semantic connection or whether he was seeing structure in noise because he needed there to be structure in order to justify what he was doing with his nights.

He should stop. He knew this. He could put the sheets back in his trunk and close the lid and catch up on three weeks of neglected homework and get some sleep for the first time since the sub-collection. He could come back to the manuscript later — next year, maybe, or after OWLs, or when he'd learned more in Ancient Runes and had better tools and was older and could think more clearly. That was the sensible thing. Miren would have told him it was the sensible thing, if he'd told Miren, which he hadn't, because there was nothing he could tell her that would make sense. _I found some sheets in a hidden room and they've got marks on them that move and I can't read them and I've been trying every night for three weeks._ Even in his own head it sounded thin. It sounded like something a person made up to justify not sleeping.

He put the sheets away that night. He lay in the dark and listened to Owen's breathing and the Pygmy Puff and Ben Alderton saying something about a Wronski Feint in his sleep, the words slurred and earnest, and the castle making its settling noises all around them — the tiny groans of stone adjusting to stone, the sound of a building that was a thousand years old and still working out its relationship with gravity.

He got the sheets out again twenty minutes later.

He did not have a new approach. He had the same failed methods and the same gap between what the marks were doing and what he could understand. But he spread the sheets across his knees and lit his wand and looked at them, and the marks moved in their slow, deliberate patterns, and he watched them the way you watched a fire — not because you expected to learn anything from the flames, but because looking away had become harder than looking.

In the morning his eyes were dry and his Charms homework was four days overdue and he'd missed the first twenty minutes of breakfast. The seat next to Miren was taken by the time he reached the Great Hall. He ate toast standing at the end of the Hufflepuff table and went to Potions and produced such a dismal Strengthening Solution that Slughorn peered into his cauldron, said "Good heavens," and moved on without comment.

That night he spread the sheets out again. The marks shifted into a configuration he had not seen before — a cluster on Sheet 5 rearranged itself so that three marks formed a triangular shape that seemed to point toward a fourth mark he'd been ignoring, one he'd classified as isolated because it never seemed to participate in the clusters. He stared at it. The triangular arrangement held for about ten minutes and then dissolved into something else, and the fourth mark drifted away, and the moment was gone.

He wrote it down in his notebook. He did not know what it meant. He did not know if it meant anything. He turned to another sheet and watched the marks for another hour and fell asleep with his wand still lit and the sheets still spread across his knees, and woke to Owen's alarm clock at half six with a crick in his neck and a crease from Sheet 11 pressed into his cheek.

He folded the sheets into his trunk and went to wash his face. In the bathroom mirror he looked tired in a specific and unflattering way — not dramatic, not interestingly haggard, just a kid who wasn't sleeping enough, with circles under his eyes and a dent in his cheek from falling asleep on something he shouldn't have been holding.

He went to class. He sat through Transfiguration and Charms and tried to concentrate and mostly didn't. He ate dinner and sat in the common room and did not do his homework and went to bed and waited for the dormitory to go quiet and got the sheets out again.

He did not think of this as a decision. It was just what his evenings were now — the thing he did instead of homework and sleep and sitting in the common room with people who would have talked to him if he'd been present enough to talk to. If anyone had asked him why, he would not have been able to give an answer that made sense, because the only honest answer was that the marks were there and they meant something and he was the only person looking at them, and putting them away felt like leaving a sentence half-read. Not because he understood the sentence. He didn't understand a word of it. But the sentence was there, and it was addressed to someone, and nobody else was trying, and so he kept sitting in his curtained bed with his wand-light guttering and his homework undone and the sheets spread warm across his knees, turning from one to the next to the next while the marks moved and moved and meant whatever they meant without him.
