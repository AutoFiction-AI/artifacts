# Chapter 10

Gavin woke to the sound of pipes gurgling in the walls. The kitchens beneath the Hufflepuff dormitories ran early on Christmas morning, and the warm-water pipes that cut through the stone behind his headboard ticked and knocked as they heated. It was the only sound. The other four beds in the room were empty except for Malcolm Preece's, and Malcolm was still asleep, his blankets pulled up to his eyebrows with one foot sticking out at the bottom, bare and pale.

There was snow against the window. Not the charmed interior snow that sometimes drifted through the common room, but real snow piled against the ground-level glass, dimming the light to a blue-grey wash. The fire had gone out overnight. Gavin's breath showed when he sat up.

At the foot of his bed, three things. His mother's parcel, wrapped in brown paper and tied with the same string she used to bundle newspapers for recycling, her handwriting neat and small on the tag. A package from Miren, soft, wrapped in pages torn from the Daily Prophet — she'd used the employment section, which was the only part that didn't carry war news these days. And a card from his aunt in the cream-coloured envelope she always used.

He opened his mother's parcel in bed, sitting cross-legged with the blankets over his shoulders. A jumper — hand-knitted, dark blue, too large. She always knitted them too large because she was convinced he was growing faster than he was. A tin of fudge with a note that said *Merry Christmas love Mum and Dad, the cat knocked over the tree again, Dad fixed it with a Sticking Charm so now we can't move it until June, we love you.* The fudge smelled like toffee and butter and the kitchen at home, and he ate a piece before he was fully awake. It was perfect. His mother's fudge was the only thing in his life that was consistently, reliably perfect.

He put on the jumper. It hung past his wrists, and the neck was wide enough that it kept slipping off one shoulder, but it was warm and it smelled like the wool basket in the sitting room and he was not going to take it off today.

The common room, when he came down, was almost cosy. Someone — Sprout probably, or one of the house-elves who had always liked the Hufflepuffs best — had strung fairy lights around the barrel entrance and along the mantelpiece. They blinked in a pattern that was almost regular but not quite, one strand amber and the other white, and a few of them had given up entirely and just glowed a steady dim orange. A garland of holly ran along the window ledges, slightly lopsided, pinned with more enthusiasm than precision. The effort was visible, and it made the room better — not in spite of the imperfection but because of it. Someone had cared enough to put this together in a year when caring about decorations could look foolish.

The fire was built up high, logs as thick as Gavin's arm, and the heat of it reached the armchairs by the round windows. A small tree stood in the corner, its branches heavy with conjured ornaments — some of them moving, little gold birds that flapped and resettled and flapped again, one of them missing a wing — and beneath it a handful of parcels that hadn't been collected yet.

Miren was already there, cross-legged on the rug in front of the fire, still in her pyjamas with a jumper pulled over. She had a mug of tea resting on her knee and was reading a letter that Gavin knew from the handwriting was from her mother — the same round, careful script he'd seen on the packages Miren's mum sent every month, always wrapped in brown paper and smelling faintly of shea butter. Miren's plait was coming undone on one side, loose strands curling near her ear. She looked up when he came down the stairs.

"Happy Christmas," she said.

"Happy Christmas."

"Your mum do the jumper?"

He looked down at himself. "Yeah."

"It's enormous."

"She thinks I'm growing."

"Are you?"

"Not really."

Miren set the letter aside and reached behind her for a package. "Here. From the box my mum sent."

The package was light. He sat down on the rug across from her and unwrapped it — more Daily Prophet, the Quidditch scores this time. Inside was a paperback, well-thumbed, the cover creased and cracked at the spine. *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* by Douglas Adams. The pages were yellowed and soft at the corners, and someone — Miren's mother, probably — had dog-eared several of them and underlined a passage near the back in pencil.

"It's Muggle," Miren said, watching his face. "My mum's favourite. She's read it about a hundred times. She said if you're going to be weird and antisocial you might as well be weird and antisocial with a good book."

"Your mum said that?"

"More or less." She took a sip of her tea. "It's about a bloke whose planet gets demolished to build a bypass."

"A what?"

"A bypass. Like a road. A Muggle road. Just read it."

He turned it over in his hands. The back cover had a coffee ring on it and a price sticker from a shop called Blackwell's, half peeled away. He ran his thumb along the worn spine and the book fell open to one of the dog-eared pages. "Tell your mum thanks."

"Tell her yourself, I'll give you her address." Miren pulled her knees up. "She worries about you. She's never met you and she worries about you. I don't know how she does it."

"I've got yours," he said. "Hang on."

He went back upstairs and came down with his gift for her, wrapped in parchment because he'd forgotten to buy wrapping paper and then the Hogsmeade weekends had been cancelled and he'd never found another source. The shape was obvious — a lumpy rectangle — and when she unwrapped it, she held up a hand warmer made from two pieces of felt sewn together with large, uneven stitches, filled with something that shifted inside when she tilted it.

"It heats up," he said. "You tap it with your wand."

She tapped it. Nothing happened for about four seconds, and then the left side went suddenly, aggressively hot — hot enough that she shifted it to her other hand with a sharp intake of breath — while the right side stayed cool.

"It does that," he said. "The charm's a bit — I couldn't get it to distribute evenly. It's meant to warm up all at once but the felt's thicker on one side and the heating charm sort of pools there."

"It's very warm on this side."

"Yeah. That side works."

She turned it over and pressed it between both palms. The heat spread slowly, not quite reaching the edges but radiating through the centre in uneven waves. A faint smell of singed wool rose from it and then faded. "I like it," she said, and she sounded like she meant it, which was worse than if she hadn't, because it meant she could see exactly how many hours he'd spent on it and how comprehensively the charm had failed.

"I'll fix it," he said. "After Christmas. The felt needs to be the same thickness all the way through, I think that's the problem."

"Don't. It's got character." She tucked it into the pocket of her pyjama bottoms and left her hand there, wrapped around it.

Duncan came down fifteen minutes later, moving with the careful deliberation he'd had since October, one hand on the banister, each step considered. He was wearing a jumper that didn't fit him anymore — too loose across the shoulders where he'd lost weight, the cuffs hanging past his knuckles — and he had the particular expression Gavin had come to recognise: a kind of watchful stillness, as though he'd forgotten it was a day when nothing bad was scheduled to happen and his body hadn't caught up yet. He carried a small cloth bag and his wand was in his other hand, though he put it in his pocket when he saw them.

"Happy Christmas," Miren said.

"Yeah. Happy Christmas." He sat down on the sofa nearest the fire and pulled his feet up underneath him. His socks were mismatched — one grey, one dark green — and he tucked his hands into his sleeves in the way he'd started doing when he didn't want people to see them shake.

They exchanged gifts. Duncan gave Miren a set of Gobstones he'd found in the bottom of a common room drawer and cleaned up — he'd polished them until they caught the firelight, amber and green, and they were better than anything available in the castle this year. He gave Gavin a bar of Honeydukes chocolate that he said he'd been saving since September, which might have been true. The wrapper was faded and the chocolate had bloomed slightly, a pale film across the surface, but it was Honeydukes and it was real.

"Thanks, Dunc."

Duncan nodded. He was looking at the fire.

Miren gave Duncan the scarf she'd been knitting for weeks — Gavin had watched it grow in the common room, Miren's needles clicking while she argued about DA logistics or complained about Alecto Carrow's propaganda assignments. It was purple and noticeably wider at one end than the other.

"It's lovely," Duncan said, wrapping it around his neck. He touched the wider end with his fingers, assessing.

"It's wider at that end."

"It's meant to be wider at that end."

"No it isn't."

"It is now." He adjusted it so the wider section covered his chin. His hands, Gavin noticed, were steadier today. Or maybe it was just that the scarf gave them something to hold.

They sat for a while. The fire popped and settled. The fairy lights blinked their not-quite pattern. The sounds from the kitchen below shifted from preparation to production — a low clatter of pots, the rush of steam through the pipes, the faint warm smell of roasting meat rising through the stones. Gavin opened his aunt's card, which contained a long account of her garden and a wish for a happy term, and he set it on the arm of the sofa and did not think about how distant her world sounded from his.

---

The Great Hall at midday was a strange sight. The four house tables stretched the length of the room as they always did, built to seat a few hundred students each, but the people present would have fit comfortably around two. Thirty, maybe, scattered across the benches in no particular house order because there was no point keeping it. A Gryffindor fourth-year sat between two Hufflepuff girls. A cluster of Slytherins had colonised one end of the Ravenclaw table because it was closest to the fire. A pair of first-years from Gavin's own house sat at the Gryffindor table and nobody said anything about it.

The enchanted ceiling showed white sky and snow falling in thick, slow flakes. The snow met the actual warmth of the hall and dissolved into nothing about six feet above their heads, so there was a visible line where winter stopped and the room began.

Sprout was there, and Flitwick, and Slughorn, who had brought his own wine and was already pink-cheeked by the time the food appeared. McGonagall sat with her back very straight, wearing a tartan scarf over her robes, and she had placed a cracker at every seat, which must have taken her half the morning given how far apart the students were sitting. Snape was not present. The Carrows were not present. Their absence was the day's first and best gift.

The food appeared the way it always did — platters of turkey and roast potatoes and sprouts and bread sauce, tureens of gravy, bowls of cranberry jelly that wobbled when you reached past them — and for a few minutes the only sounds were serving spoons and the particular industrious quiet of people filling their plates. The food was good. The food was always good at Hogwarts, even this year, even under occupation, because the house-elves cooked the way they'd always cooked and nobody had thought to interfere with them.

Duncan took seconds of the potatoes before he'd finished his firsts, spooning them onto the edge of his plate with his head slightly down, not looking to see if anyone noticed. Gavin noticed. Miren noticed. Neither of them said anything. There was something in the way Duncan ate — deliberately, with concentration, taking more because there was more and nobody was going to stop him — that was not about hunger. It was about the fact that this was a table where he was allowed to eat as much as he wanted without calculating what it would cost him.

Crackers. Gavin pulled one with Miren and lost — the snap sent a burst of purple smoke across the table and left him holding the short end while Miren got a paper crown and a small hand mirror that shouted compliments when you looked into it. "Lovely eyebrows!" it shrieked, and she turned it facedown on the table.

"Yours are nice too," Gavin said.

"Shut up."

Duncan's cracker produced a joke that he read aloud in a flat voice: "Why did the wizard bring a ladder to the pub? Because he heard the drinks were on the house."

"That's the worst one I've ever heard," Miren said.

"Last year's were worse," said the sixth-year across from them — Keir Dunbar, who Gavin knew mostly from Quidditch tryouts that neither of them had made and from the common room, where Keir could usually be found playing chess against himself and cracking his knuckles one at a time, methodically, left thumb to right pinkie. He was doing it now, working through his left hand between bites of turkey. He had a red paper crown on and it sat crooked over his dark hair. "Last year I got one that said 'What do you call a sleeping Kneazle? A catnap.' And the prize was a thimble."

"A thimble?"

"A thimble. I've still got it. Use it for nothing." He reached for the gravy. "At least nobody's here to ruin it this year."

"Keir," Miren said, quietly.

"I'm just saying." He poured gravy over his turkey. "It's nice, isn't it? Just eating. Without — you know."

Nobody answered that directly. Flitwick, at the staff table, was trying to charm a cracker into singing a carol and not quite managing it — the cracker produced a series of strangled squeaks and then went silent. The Gryffindor fourth-year laughed at something one of the Hufflepuff girls said. At the far end of the hall, Slughorn poured himself another glass and said something to McGonagall that made her almost smile.

Then Keir leaned forward and put on a face. Gavin recognised it before the first word came out — the jaw pushed forward, the eyes narrowed to mean little slits, the shoulders hunched up as if cruelty were a physical posture you climbed into.

"Right," Keir said, in a voice that was Amycus Carrow's voice — the thick nasal rasp of it, the vowels flattened and shoved through his teeth. "Which one of you lot wants to show me what you learned? No volunteers? That's all right." He jabbed a finger across the table. "You. Come up here."

He was pointing at Duncan.

Duncan's fork stopped. Just for a fraction of a second — his shoulders drew in and his face became the blank, careful surface he wore in the corridors now, the one that meant he had gone somewhere behind his own eyes to wait — and then it passed and he was smiling, the measured smile he used for everything, and Keir saw it and the impression died.

The silence at the table was not the silence of awkwardness. It was the silence of people who had been reminded of something they were trying, for one day, to set down.

"Sorry," Keir said. His voice was his own again. He was looking at his plate and his paper crown had slipped down over one ear. "That was — sorry. I didn't mean to —"

"It's fine," Duncan said. "It was a good impression."

"It wasn't, though."

"No," Duncan said. "It was."

The table was quiet for a long moment. Miren put her hand on Duncan's shoulder — briefly, lightly, the way you'd check whether a surface was hot. Duncan didn't move. The cracker joke lay on the table between his plate and Gavin's, and nobody picked it up.

Then Flitwick's cracker finally sang. The sound was thin and wobbly, a tuneless rendition of "God Rest Ye Merry Hippogriffs" that came out of the cracker in a reedy falsetto and died halfway through the second verse, and one of the first-years at the Gryffindor table clapped, and the moment passed. Not gone — moments like that didn't go — but it moved sideways into something they could sit with. Gavin ate his sprouts. They were overcooked and tasted like every Christmas he'd ever had here, and they were perfect.

---

After the meal they went back to the common room and played Exploding Snap until the cards singed Miren's eyebrows and she declared the deck faulty. Duncan said it wasn't the deck, it was her technique, and Miren said her technique was fine and the deck was possessed, and they argued about it with the low-stakes pleasure of people who did not care about the answer. Gavin's left hand trembled when he held the cards — the fine, intermittent tremor that had lived there since November, coming and going without pattern — and he steadied it against his knee when it was his turn and didn't think about why.

They played Gobstones with Duncan's cleaned-up set until the stones squirted something that smelled powerfully of boiled cabbage, and then they stopped playing Gobstones. They tried the hand warmer again and it worked better the second time — still uneven, still too hot on the left side, but Miren said it was improving with age, like cheese, and Duncan said that was the worst comparison he'd ever heard.

Miren read bits of the Hitchhiker's Guide aloud to prove that Muggle books were funnier than wizard ones. She might have been right. There was a section about a whale that Gavin found funny in a way that sat uneasy in his chest — something about the scale of it, the smallness of a creature against a vastness that didn't know or care — but he laughed anyway, because Miren was doing voices for the characters and Duncan was eating a piece of Gavin's mother's fudge without asking, which was, in its way, the best thing that happened all day. The automatic easiness of someone reaching for your food because he had forgotten, for a few hours, to be careful about what he touched and took and occupied.

The light through the snow-packed windows shifted from blue to grey to the amber of a winter evening lit mostly by fire. Keir Dunbar was in the corner with his chess set, playing both sides and cracking his knuckles between moves, the paper crown still on his head. Two first-years had fallen asleep on the sofa nearest the fire, one of them still wearing her cracker crown, which had slipped down over her nose.

Gavin sat in the armchair by the window and read. He got to page forty of the Muggle novel and the words started sliding past his eyes without catching, and he let them. It didn't matter. The fire was warm and the room smelled like the kitchens below — roasting things, bread, something sweet that might have been the treacle tart the elves made on holidays — and Miren was explaining to Duncan why Exploding Snap was a game of pure luck, and Duncan was holding his ground for once, his voice slightly louder than it had been in weeks, and the fairy lights blinked on and off in their imperfect pattern.

He did not go upstairs to the trunk where the manuscript waited. He did not think about the notation that had been rearranging itself in the margins of his sleep for weeks, the shifting structures that pressed at the edges of his attention even when he wasn't reading. He did not think about the agreements beneath spells or the civilisation that had left or the wards that separated everything from what pressed on the other side. He thought about the chocolate Duncan had given him and the fudge his mother had made and the jumper that was too large and how Duncan had eaten fudge without asking and how good that was. He thought about what a bypass was and why you'd demolish a planet to build one. He thought about the whale.

He was fifteen and sitting with his friends on Christmas Day and the war was somewhere else and the other thing, the larger thing, was also somewhere else, and for one day both of those distances held.

---

He woke in the dark.

The dormitory was cold again — the fire had burned down to ash and the pipes had stopped their ticking. Malcolm Preece was breathing steadily in the bed across the room, the slow rhythm of someone deeply, unconcernedly asleep.

Gavin lay still for a moment, trying to locate what had woken him. No sound from the corridor. No footsteps, no voices, no distant disturbance. The castle was as quiet as it ever got at this hour — the deep-night quiet where the stones themselves seemed to settle into their own weight, and the only sounds were structural. Wind against the window. The packed snow cracking faintly against the glass as the temperature dropped.

He sat up and looked at the window. Nothing. The ground-level view showed the banked snow against the glass, backlit by the faint luminescence the castle's exterior stones gave off at night. Through the frost he could see the suggestion of the grounds — the lawn, the dark weight of the forest's edge, the white blankness beyond.

He got out of bed. He didn't decide to. His body was already moving, feet on the cold stone, reaching for the jumper his mother had sent. He pulled it on over his pyjamas, found his shoes by the bed, and padded out through the dormitory and the common room — empty now, the fire banked to red coals, the fairy lights still blinking at nobody — and up the spiral stairs to the corridor above.

The barrel entrance let him out near the kitchens. The corridor was dark and he walked by wand-light, his footsteps louder than they should have been on the flagstones. He wasn't going anywhere in particular. He was following something that was not a sound and not a sensation in any category he could name but something between — a directional pull that had been in his body since the manuscript had begun changing how he processed things. It had been there for weeks, faint, like a draught from an invisible gap in a wall. Tonight it was less faint.

He found a window on the first-floor corridor, not far from the passage that led to the greenhouses. Tall, narrow, arched, the glass old enough to have ripples in it. It looked out over the grounds toward the lake. The snow had stopped falling. The sky had cleared.

He pressed his hand flat against the glass. The cold went through his palm and into the bones of his wrist and he held it there.

The sky was the same sky he had looked at for five years. The same stars, the same winter constellations in the same positions. Orion was up, tilted at the angle he always assumed by late December, the three belt stars sharp and clean. Sirius burned low near the trees. The grounds below were white and blue and silver and motionless, the snow unbroken except where someone had walked to the greenhouses earlier in the day and the footprints had already half-filled.

And there was something else.

It was not in the sky. It was not behind the sky or above it or beyond it in any direction that his body knew how to process. The word that came was *against*. Something pressing against something. The way you might lean your weight against a window and the glass would hold — would not bend or crack — but the pressure would be there, real and structural, transmitted through the pane into the frame, into the wall, into the stone.

The glass held. The pressure was there.

He had read about the wards in the manuscript. He had translated the section — slowly, badly, losing half the nuance to his own inadequacy — about the structures the Builders had put in place before they left. Vast interlocking agreements that made Earth undetectable. That filtered the planet out of a category of attention the way a one-way mirror removes a room from view. He had understood this the way you understand a fact in a textbook: as information. True, but somewhere else. A thing that was happening at a remove from his own body.

This was not at a remove. This was in his hand against the glass. The cold of the window was one thing and the pressure behind the cold was another thing, and his body knew the difference even if his language didn't. The wards were there — not visible, not audible, not occupying any sense he had been born with, but present the way the floor is present beneath your feet when you stop thinking about it. Structural. Load-bearing. Holding.

And against them, on the other side, something leaned.

Not a creature. Not a mind. Not anything that wanted or planned or targeted. Just a condition — present and vast and utterly indifferent to the barrier between them. The way a river presses against a bank without knowing the bank is there. Not hostile. Not aware. Simply occupying space, the way weather occupies sky, and incompatible with everything on this side. Incompatible the way deep water is incompatible with breathing. Not because of malice. Because of what it was.

His hand was numb. He pulled it from the glass and the red marks of the cold on his palm took several seconds to fade. He pressed his fingers together and they were stiff and white.

The stars were the same. Orion was the same. The grounds below were white and still. Somewhere in the castle behind him his friends were sleeping — Miren with the hand warmer on her bedside table, probably cool by now because the charm had a half-life of about two hours, and Duncan in the purple scarf because the dormitory was cold and the scarf was warm, and Malcolm Preece with his blankets pulled to his eyebrows and his one foot sticking out. The fudge tin was on Gavin's trunk with three pieces gone. The book about the demolished planet was face-down on the arm of the chair where he'd left it.

The wards were thinner than they had been.

He didn't know how he knew. The manuscript had described degradation in notation he could barely follow — terms that had no English equivalents, structural relationships he had to hold in his head like shapes in four dimensions that kept collapsing to three. But standing here with his hand still marked by the cold, he did not need the notation. He could feel it the way you feel ice thinning on a lake — not broken, not failing in any way you could point to, but less than it was. The margin between what held and what pressed had narrowed. Not by much. Not enough to see or measure or prove to anyone. But the day he had just lived — the fudge and the crackers and the sprouts and the fairy lights and the jumper that was too big and the book and the game and the scarf and the whale — all of it sat on one side of a barrier that was thinner tonight than it had been last month, and would be thinner next month than it was tonight, and nobody in the warm sleeping castle knew.

He stood at the window for a long time. The sky did not change. The pressure did not change. The stars were beautiful and precise and ancient and they did not know either.

He pulled the curtain across the window and went back to bed.
