# Chapter 8

The notation in this section was denser than anything he'd encountered before. The patterns interlocked at scales his translational framework could barely hold — each decoded element revealing relationships to a dozen others, each of those connecting to a dozen more, until the structure he was building in his mind felt like trying to hold the shape of the entire castle at once when all you could manage was a single corridor.

He'd been at it for three hours. Or four. Time moved differently in the sub-collection — no windows, no change in the luminescent light from the walls, no sounds from the castle above. The stone rooms existed outside the rhythms of school life, and the longer he worked in them, the more that separation felt like a feature, not an accident. The sub-collection had not been designed for human scholars sitting on the floor with their legs going numb. If it had been designed at all, it had been designed for something that didn't need chairs.

The manuscript lay across his knees, its not-parchment surface faintly warm against his robes. Gavin shifted his weight and felt the cold of the flagstones through the thin fabric, sharp enough to remind him he had a body that was not especially comfortable. He'd brought a quill and scratch parchment for notes, but he hadn't written anything in over an hour. The notes he'd been taking were useless — approximations of approximations, English words circling concepts they couldn't hold.

The ward structure was the first thing the translation gave him. He'd known, from the section on the Builders, that wards existed — planetary-scale agreements set before the departure. But the earlier section had described them in passing, the way a description of a house might mention the foundation. This section was the foundation itself.

The wards were agreements, like everything in the First Art, but agreements at a scale that made the substrate beneath a Levitation Charm look like a scribble in the margin of a vast book. They interlocked across the planet's surface in patterns his mind could only hold in fragments — he could manage a local section the way you could manage a single floor of the castle, but the whole structure slipped away every time he tried to see it at once. The Builders had not constructed the wards the way wizards constructed spells. They had negotiated them into existence, convinced the substrate itself to register Earth in a particular way — or rather, to stop registering it. To agree that the planet was not present in a category of perception that the notation could represent but that Gavin's language could not name.

Not hidden. Not shielded. Unregistered. The distinction mattered in ways he could feel pressing at the edge of his understanding but could not quite articulate. A shield implied an attack it defended against. Registration implied something that perceived. The wards did not defend against perception — they edited the conditions under which perception of Earth was possible, so that nothing outside them could perceive the planet the way nothing perceives something that has never existed. The planet was still here. The wards made its being-here a local fact rather than a universal one.

He sat with that for a while. The stone was cold under his legs. His left hand was trembling — it did that now, the Cruciatus aftereffect that came and went, worse at night, worse when he was tired. He pressed it flat against the floor until the cold numbed the shaking, and continued.

The notation's representational movement changed.

The interlocking patterns that described the ward structure were still present, but they had thinned. Not uniformly — in specific areas, the relationships between agreements had weakened, the way fibres in a rope thin before they snap. The thinning was not recent. Gavin's translational framework could not map the notation's time-representation cleanly to years, but the scale was centuries. Many centuries. The wards had been designed by beings who maintained them as part of their existence, the way humans maintained breathing, and the beings had left, and nobody had breathed for the wards since.

He worked through the degradation section slowly. Each decoded passage made the picture worse. The thinning was not linear — it compounded. Areas where the wards had degraded became less capable of supporting adjacent areas, which degraded faster, which weakened the sections next to them. A cascade failure in slow motion. Not imminent by human time — still years or decades away from critical thinning — but already past the point where the original structure could recover on its own. The wards needed maintenance the way a cathedral needed maintenance, and there were no builders and no tools and no one who knew the building was there.

Except him.

He kept translating. The next section was where the notation broke down.

The Builders' notation system was designed to represent substrate relationships — the agreements that made magic work, the rules beneath the rules. It could represent any magical concept with a precision that made wizarding language look like finger-painting. But the thing beyond the wards was not a magical concept. It was not a substrate state. It was the absence of substrate, or — not even that. An absence implied a space where something could be. What was beyond the wards was a condition in which the substrate had never existed and could not exist, the way fire cannot exist underwater. Not because something prevents it. Because the conditions are wrong.

The notation represented this by not representing it.

Gavin had turned four pages of the manuscript before he understood what he was looking at. The notation on these pages was sparse — a few patterns at the margins that described the ward boundary, the innermost edge where the wards met what was beyond them. The centre of each page was not blank. The material was the same slightly warm, not-parchment surface throughout. But the notation had stopped moving. The representational shifts that characterised every other section of the manuscript — the constant, living rearrangement of patterns showing relationships between ideas — were absent here. The notation had reached a boundary beyond which it had nothing to show.

And at the edges of that stillness, Gavin could read what the Builders had managed to encode. A description by negation. By what the outside was not.

Not a creature. Not an intelligence. Not a force. Not hostile, not indifferent, not anything that implied a stance toward the wards or what they protected. The notation described the outside the way a geographic survey might describe the ocean — as an environment, a condition, a state of things that simply obtained over a very large area. The wards were the coastline. What was beyond them was the sea. And the sea did not care about the coastline because caring was a thing that happened on land, in the local conditions that the coastline demarcated, and the sea was not land.

But the sea eroded coastlines. Not through will. Through presence.

Where the wards thinned, the outside condition pressed closer. Not pushed — pressed, the way water presses against a dam without intending anything. The weight of what is on the other side. And where that weight pressed against thinned wards, the substrate agreements on the Earth side of the boundary became less stable. The notation showed this as a kind of blurring — the precise relationships between substrate rules becoming approximate, uncertain, the way a clear image becomes unclear when the glass between you and the image develops cracks.

If the wards failed entirely — if the thinning reached its critical point and the cascade failure completed — the substrate would be exposed to the outside condition directly. And the substrate would dissolve. Not because the outside was corrosive. Because the substrate was a local anomaly, an arrangement of rules that could only hold in the specific conditions the wards maintained, and without the wards, those conditions would cease to exist. The way ice dissolved in warm water. The ice was not attacked. The ice had only ever been possible because of the temperature, and when the temperature changed, the ice stopped being ice.

All of magical life — every spell, every enchantment, every magical creature, every ward and charm and curse that had ever been cast or ever would be — depended on the substrate. The substrate depended on the wards. The wards depended on maintenance by beings who had left and were not coming back.

Gavin put the manuscript pages down.

The room was very quiet. The luminescent walls gave off their steady, sourceless light. The air was dry and cold and tasted of limestone. Somewhere above him, a thousand students and a hundred staff members were sleeping or patrolling or plotting resistance against a dark wizard they believed was the worst thing in the world, and none of them knew that the world itself was a thin place held together by agreements that were coming apart.

He tried to take a breath and the breath did not work. It went in but it went in wrong, too fast, too shallow, and the next one was worse. His hands were on the stone floor and they were shaking — both of them now, not just the left — and the luminescent walls blurred at the edges of his vision, and he thought very clearly: I'm hyperventilating. And then he stopped thinking clearly and just breathed wrong for a while.

The stone floor was cold against his palms. Then against his knees, because at some point he'd tipped forward. The manuscript pages had slid off his lap and lay on the floor beside him, their surfaces still faintly warm, still shifting at the edges with representational motion that continued whether anyone was reading or not. The notation did not care that he'd stopped. The wards did not care that he'd understood. The outside did not care about anything, because caring was not a thing it did.

He stayed on the floor. His breathing slowly regularised — not because he calmed down but because bodies have limits on how wrong they will let breathing go before they take over and do it properly. The shaking eased from violent to a steady tremor that lived in his hands and in the muscles of his thighs. His face was wet. He had not known he was crying.

He pressed his cheek against the stone and felt the cold of it and stayed there for a while longer. The room was the same. The walls glowed. The air was dry. Nothing had changed except that he now knew what was going to happen, on a timescale too long for urgency and too short for comfort, and he was the only person who knew it, and he was fifteen and his best subject was Herbology and he got Acceptables in Ancient Runes.

After a while he sat up. He wiped his face on the sleeve of his robe. He gathered the manuscript pages carefully, because his hands were still shaking and the pages were the only copy of the only description of the only problem that mattered, and if he damaged them there was no one to consult and no replacement and no second chance.

He put them in the leather folder he used to carry them through the castle. He stood up. His knees ached from the stone. His left hand was trembling badly enough that he had to press it against his side to hold the folder steady.

He walked out of the sub-collection and through the shifting seventh-floor corridors and down six flights of stairs and through the barrel-entrance to the Hufflepuff common room and past the embers of the common room fire and into his dormitory and put the folder in his trunk and got into bed without undressing and did not sleep.

---

The next day he went to classes because not going to classes attracted attention, and attention under the Carrow regime meant questions, and questions meant answers he did not have. He sat through double Charms and Herbology and then Ancient Runes. He took notes in none of them. In Herbology he repotted a Flutterby bush and got soil under his fingernails and thought about the wards. In the corridors between classes, students moved around him the way water moves around a stone in a stream, and he let them.

Professor Babbling's classroom was on the third floor, small and lined with reference texts that predated the school's current crisis by centuries. The class that day had been comparative Futhark systems, and Babbling had taught it the way she taught everything this year — carefully, within the syllabus, without tangents, because tangents could lead to questions and questions could lead to the Carrows taking an interest in what was being taught in Ancient Runes.

The other students filed out after class. Gavin stayed in his seat.

Babbling was at her desk, sorting student work into two piles. She had a system — green-ink comments for content, red for notation errors — and her handwriting was small and precise even on the worst papers. She'd taken off her reading glasses and they hung on their chain against her cardigan, which was the same dark grey wool she seemed to wear every day, its cuffs ink-stained past rescue. There was a half-eaten ginger biscuit on a saucer by her elbow that she appeared to have forgotten about.

She looked up when she realised he hadn't left.

"Mr. Tench." She set down her quill. "Is there something I can help you with?"

He'd meant to start with the translational framework — to anchor the conversation in something she'd already helped him with, to build from the known to the unknown. Instead he said, "The wards are failing."

Babbling looked at him for a long moment. Then she said, "Close the door, please."

He closed the door. When he turned back, she had moved the student papers to one side and was watching him with an expression he couldn't quite read — academic interest and concern and something else, something more cautious.

"Sit down," she said. "And start from the beginning. Not from the conclusion."

So he did. He sat in the chair closest to her desk and told her about the section of the manuscript that described the ward structure — the planetary-scale agreements that made Earth unregistered, the interlocking patterns, the system the Builders had left in place when they departed. He told her about the degradation — the centuries-long thinning, the cascade failure, the absence of maintenance. He told her about the thing beyond the wards — the condition, not a creature, not hostile, just there, pressing against the boundary the way water presses against anything.

He self-corrected twice. Three times. He kept reaching for the right words and finding that they did not exist in English or in any runic vocabulary she'd taught him. He said "it's like" and then revised. He said "not exactly, more like" and then revised again. He watched her face for signs that the meaning was landing, and what he saw instead was the gradual settling of a professional judgment.

When he finished, Babbling was quiet for a while. She picked up the ginger biscuit, looked at it, put it back down on the saucer.

"Mr. Tench," she said. "I would like to ask you several questions, and I would like you to answer them honestly."

"Yeah. Of course."

"How many hours per week are you spending on this manuscript?"

He hesitated. "A lot."

"How many."

"Maybe... twenty. Thirty, some weeks."

She nodded, as if the number confirmed something. "And your other coursework? Your OWL preparation?"

"It's — I know it's slipping. But this is more —"

"I am not asking as a rebuke. I am asking because the information matters." She paused. "Are you sleeping?"

"Some."

"That is not an answer."

"Four hours. Sometimes five."

Babbling's hands were folded on the desk. The ink stains on her fingers were dark in the classroom's lamplight. "Mr. Tench. What you have described to me is a cosmological model. A fascinating one. The ward-structure concept, in particular, is unlike anything in the established runic literature, and I would be very interested to examine the relevant notation under different circumstances." She hesitated, and Gavin watched her revise something internally, pulling back from whatever she'd been about to say. "However. The interpretation you are placing on this model — that the wards are real, that they are currently degrading, that a condition beyond them poses an existential threat to magical life — that is an extraordinary claim."

"I know how it —"

"Let me finish." Her voice was kind but had the edge of precision that did not allow interruption. "Ancient manuscripts frequently contain cosmological speculation. The Sumerian cuneiform records describe catastrophes that never occurred. The Ogham prophecy tablets — which I spent seven years studying — predict the end of magical civilisation approximately once per century. Speculative eschatology is one of the most common genres in pre-medieval magical writing. It is entirely possible that the manuscript you are translating contains a real description of an ancient ward system and a speculative, theoretical model of its potential failure. These are not mutually exclusive."

"It's not speculative. The notation shows —"

"The notation shows what your translation renders." She said it gently. "I gave you a framework for approaching the cipher, and it is a good framework — I believe that — but every translational framework introduces interpretive latitude. I am not questioning your diligence. I am questioning whether a single student working alone with an imperfect translation tool can draw the conclusion you are drawing from notation that, by your own description, becomes representationally ambiguous precisely at the point where the most dramatic claims are made."

Gavin opened his mouth and closed it.

"I think," Babbling said, more softly, "that you have been working very hard, under conditions that would be stressful for anyone, and that the manuscript has become a place where some of that stress has collected." She reached for the teapot on the shelf behind her desk — it was always there, always warm, charmed to stay that way — and poured a cup without asking whether he wanted one. She slid it across the desk to him. "This has been a terrible year. The things that are happening in this school — the detentions, the atmosphere, the uncertainty — these produce anxiety that has to go somewhere. And sometimes it goes into the work that occupies us most deeply, and the work begins to feel more urgent than it is, because it is carrying weight that belongs elsewhere."

The tea was milky and too sweet. He drank it because she'd given it to him and because the warmth of the cup was the first pleasant thing he'd felt since the sub-collection floor.

"Could you look at the section yourself?" he asked. "The ward section. If I brought you the pages —"

"No." The word was quiet and final. "I cannot continue to assist with research that is not part of the approved syllabus. Not this year." She looked at him, and what was in her face was not unkindness but the particular sadness of a person choosing self-preservation over curiosity. "I hope you understand why."

"Yeah," he said. "I understand."

"I would recommend sleep, Mr. Tench. And some attention to your other subjects. Your OWL marks will matter, whatever the manuscript says about the rest." She picked up the ginger biscuit and ate it, finally, as if the conversation had used up whatever restraint had been keeping her from it. "Close the door behind you when you leave."

He closed the door behind him. The third-floor corridor was empty, afternoon light slanting through the tall windows, dust floating in it. He stood there for a moment with the taste of someone else's tea in his mouth and the understanding that the most qualified person he could reach thought he was projecting.

---

He found Miren in the common room after dinner. She was on the floor by the fire with a Muggle Studies textbook open in her lap — Alecto Carrow's version, the one that described Muggles as subhuman — annotating it in the margins with corrections. She'd been doing this since September, filling the margins with the actual facts she knew from her mother, as if quiet factual resistance in the margins of a propaganda textbook could change something. Gavin did not know if it could. But Miren did it anyway, every week, in small precise handwriting that looked nothing like her usual scrawl.

She had mud on her shoes from somewhere — the greenhouses, or the grounds. Her hair was in its usual plait, though strands had come loose around her face, and she was biting the end of her quill in a way that suggested the annotations were costing her something she would not admit to.

"Hey," she said when she saw him. Her voice was careful — the tone she'd used with him since the argument in November, warm but keeping a millimetre of distance. "You look awful."

"Can I talk to you about something?"

"You're already talking to me."

"I mean — something specific. Something I need you to hear."

She looked at him for a moment, reading something in his face. Then she closed the propaganda textbook and set it aside. She turned toward him, and he saw the shift — the way she went from casually present to fully focused, her whole body going still in the way that was more intense than most people's engagement. When Miren decided to listen, she listened with everything.

"OK," she said. "Talk."

He sat down on the floor next to her, cross-legged, close enough to the fire that he could feel it through his robes. The common room was mostly empty — a few first-years playing Gobstones in the corner, a seventh-year asleep on the sofa with a textbook over her face, one shoe off and one shoe on. Private enough.

He told her about the Builders first. The civilisation that had used magic as a medium of existence, not a tool. She'd heard fragments of this before, from the argument in November, but he hadn't been coherent then and she hadn't been willing to hear it the same way. This time he went slowly. He told her about the departure — not death, relocation, to somewhere the manuscript's notation could represent but that his translation could only approximate. He told her about the wards — the planetary-scale agreements that made Earth unregistered, invisible to something outside them that the Builders had understood and protected against.

Then he told her about the wards failing. The centuries of degradation without maintenance. The cascade failure in slow motion.

Then he told her about the thing outside.

He struggled here, the way he'd struggled in Babbling's office. The same words that had failed him there failed him again — not a creature, not a force, a condition, an environment — and he watched Miren's face for the moment when the scale of it landed. What he saw instead was attention, steady and fierce, tracking everything he said without giving him anything back.

When he finished, the fire had burned lower. The first-years had gone to bed. The seventh-year was still asleep on the sofa, her shoe still half off.

Miren was quiet for a while. She pulled a loose strand of hair behind her ear. Then she said, "So. The world's ending."

"Not — not ending. The wards are. If they fail completely, then... yeah. Magic stops working. Everything that depends on magic stops existing. But it's not —" He caught himself. "It's not soon. Decades, maybe longer. But it's happening, and nobody knows except —"

"Except you."

"Yeah."

The fire cast moving shadows across her face. The common room smelled of earth and warm wood and the faint undercurrent of whatever the kitchens had been cooking — boiled potatoes, something with cabbage. Everything around them was warm and specific and small.

"OK," she said. "Can I ask you something?"

"Yeah."

"What'd you get on the last Runes assessment?"

He stared at her. "What?"

"Your Ancient Runes mark. End-of-term assessment. What was it?"

"I — Acceptable. Why does —"

"And the one before that?"

"Acceptable. Maybe a Poor on the practical. Miren, what does that have to do with —"

"Just — hang on." She held up a hand, not to silence him but to hold the space for what she was putting together. "The manuscript. The notation. You said even Babbling couldn't read it without giving you a framework first. She said it was pre-Futhark, pre-everything."

"Yeah."

"And you've been translating it. You. On your own. With a framework she gave you for a completely different notation system."

"It's not — the framework was a starting point. I've built on it. The notation teaches you how to read it, that's part of how —"

"Gav." She said his name the way she said it when she wanted him to hear what she was actually saying, not what he was hoping she was saying. "You get Acceptables in Ancient Runes. You're not — I'm not being mean. You know you're not top of the class. You're not bottom either, but you're in the middle. And you're telling me you've decoded something that nobody in the entire history of runic scholarship has ever decoded. Something that rewrites everything anyone knows about magic. Something that Babbling — who's been doing this for thirty years — looked at and called speculative."

"She didn't have the full context. She only saw a few pages, months ago, and she didn't —"

"I know. I know. But listen to what you're saying. You're a fifteen-year-old who gets average marks in the subject, and you're telling me the whole world's going to end and only you can see it."

She was not angry. That was the worst part. She was not dismissing him out of frustration or spite. She was looking at him with the careful, painful attention of someone weighing an extraordinary claim against the available evidence and finding the evidence thin.

"I know how it sounds," he said.

"Do you? Because it sounds —" She stopped, then finished the thought anyway. "It sounds like you've been in that room too much. Alone. Not sleeping. Working on something nobody else can read or verify or even look at without — what, reading it for months until their brain changes? That's your evidence? Something that only works if you've already done the thing you're trying to prove is real?"

He didn't have an answer. She was describing the problem precisely — the trap that made the First Art unfalsifiable from the outside. The manuscript changed how you thought, and the changed thinking was the evidence, and if your thinking hadn't been changed you couldn't evaluate the evidence. A perfect closed loop, and from inside it the wards were real and failing and from outside it he was a tired teenager with an old book.

"I can see the substrate," he said. "Underneath spells. I told you about this — at breakfast, the Levitation Charm —"

"You told me you could see something nobody else could see. That's —" She was choosing her words. "Gav, I believe you think you're seeing something. I do. But I can't — what am I supposed to do with that? You're telling me to trust that you can perceive something invisible, based on your word, and your word is backed up by a translation that nobody can check, done by a student who's not exactly brilliant at the subject."

The word landed harder than she'd meant it to. He could see it in her face — a flinch, the awareness that she'd been more direct than she'd intended. But she didn't take it back, because she didn't take things back.

"I'm not brilliant," he agreed. "I know that."

"So how do you know the translation's right?"

"Because the things it describes — the substrate, the agreements — I can see them. They're real. I wouldn't be able to see them if the translation was wrong, because reading the manuscript is what —"

"That's circular. You can see it because you read it, and you know you read it right because you can see it."

She was not being cruel. She was being clear, which was worse.

He looked at the fire. A log shifted and sent a spray of sparks across the hearthstones where they glowed for a moment and went out. The common room was so ordinary. The round walls, the plants in the windows catching only indirect light, the sleeping seventh-year, the Gobstones abandoned in the corner by the first-years. Everything in it was warm and human and small, and beyond the castle walls and beyond the sky and beyond the wards the outside condition pressed with the patience of something that had no patience because patience was a property of minds and it did not have a mind.

"I don't know what to do," he said.

Miren's face changed. The careful analytical expression shifted into something softer, more worried, closer to the way she'd looked at him before November. She reached over and took his hand — the left one, the one that was trembling — and held it without saying anything.

"I think you need to stop," she said quietly. "Not forever. Just for a while. Sleep. Do some homework. Come to dinner. Be a person for a bit."

"I can't stop."

"You can. You're choosing not to."

"If what I'm reading is right —"

"And if it's not, you've spent months in that room instead of sleeping, and your marks are shot, and you look like you haven't eaten properly since October." She squeezed his hand and let go. "I'm not saying you're wrong. I'm saying I can't tell if you're right, and neither can you, not really, and in the meantime you're making yourself ill over it."

He wanted to argue. The arguments were there — the substrate perception was real, the wards were real, the notation was too complex and too internally consistent to be speculative fiction from an ancient civilisation. But the arguments depended on evidence that only he could access, and from where Miren was sitting, they were the claims of a tired boy who'd found an old book and lost himself in it.

"OK," he said.

"OK you'll stop?"

"OK I hear you."

She looked at him for a long time. The fire was dying, reduced to coals that pulsed with heat. The common room had gone dim.

"I'm worried about you," she said. "That's all. I'm not — I just want you to be all right."

"I know."

"Are you going to bed?"

"In a bit."

She stood up. She picked up the propaganda textbook and tucked it under her arm. At the foot of the stairs to the girls' dormitory, she turned back.

"If it's real," she said. "If the wards and the — all of it. If it's actually real. Then someone needs to fix them. And it can't just be you, Gav. You know that, right? You can't carry something like that alone."

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

She went upstairs. He sat by the dying fire for a long time. The coals ticked as they cooled. The common room settled into its nighttime quiet — the creak of old wood, the distant sound of the kitchens cleaning up, the low hum of the earth around the walls that he'd never noticed before this year.

Both of them had been kind. Babbling with her tea and her careful, academic alternative. Miren with her hand on his and her perfectly reasonable question about his grades. Neither of them was wrong. He was mediocre. His translation was unverifiable. His evidence was circular. A reasonable person, given the same information, would conclude exactly what they had concluded — that a stressed boy under an authoritarian regime had found a fixation that was metabolising his anxiety into grand cosmic narratives.

They were both wrong, too. The wards were real. The degradation was real. The outside was real and patient and vast and it did not care about the wards or about him or about anything, because caring was not a thing it did, the way seeing was not a thing a stone did. It simply occupied its space the way it always had and always would, without effort, without awareness, without anything at all.

He went to bed. He lay in the dark and listened to his dormitory mates breathing in their sleep — the small, ordinary sounds of fifteen-year-olds who had enough to be frightened of already without knowing the rest. Somewhere above and beyond and outside, the wards held. Thinner than yesterday. Thinner than the day before that. And the thing beyond them — the condition, the environment, the default state of a reality in which magic had never been more than a local exception — was there the way it had always been there. Not waiting, because waiting was a thing that minds did. Just there. The way the ocean is there whether the seawall knows it or not.

Gavin lay on his back and stared at the underside of his bed canopy and knew that nobody was going to help him, and that he was not good enough to do it alone, and that it was going to have to be him anyway because there was nobody else, and after a while he closed his eyes and did not sleep and did not sleep and eventually slept.
