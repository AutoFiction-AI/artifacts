# Chapter 13

The Astronomy Tower was the coldest place in the castle and Gavin hadn't brought a cloak.

He sat on the stone floor with his back against the parapet, his school robes pulled tight around his chest, and felt the wind come through the gaps between the crenellations in bursts that flattened his hair and then died and came again. He'd been up here for a while. An hour, maybe. Long enough that his hands had stopped just being cold and had moved into the numb stage where the fingers didn't bend right when he tried to make a fist.

March in Scotland. The thaw had started but hadn't committed — patches of snow still on the mountains, the grounds mostly mud with green pushing through in places, the lake half-frozen and half-not in a way that looked like a decision the water couldn't make. From up here the Forbidden Forest was a dark shape pressed against the hills, and below it the lawns sloped down to Hagrid's cabin, which was dark. Hagrid might be in the forest tonight. Gavin didn't know. He hadn't paid attention to Hagrid's schedule in months. He hadn't paid attention to most things in months.

He'd come up because the sub-collection was too small. The walls of those cut-stone rooms had started to press. Fourteen weeks of working there — the limestone smell in his clothes, the faint glow of stones that predated everything else in the castle — and the air had begun to feel used. After dinner he'd meant to go back down to the barrel entrance and the common room but he'd kept walking. Up the stairs past the library landing. Up more stairs. Past the Dark Arts classroom, dark now, the door closed. Up again. The Astronomy Tower because it was the highest place he could reach, because there was sky here, because the walls opened.

A telescope mount jutted from the parapet near his left shoulder. Cold iron, bolted into stone. He touched it without thinking and then left his hand there because the metal was cold enough to feel sharp and the sharpness was something real and immediate and unrelated to anything he'd been translating. The manuscript was in his trunk, four hundred vertical feet below him, and he could feel the distance like a taut thread in his awareness. That was new — the sense of where it was when he wasn't with it. Not a pull, exactly. More like being aware of a sound source even when you weren't listening to it.

The sky was clear. No moon worth noticing — a thin paring low over the western mountains, already going down. The stars were the hard, specific kind you got when there was nothing between you and them and the nearest city light was forty miles south. The constellation Gavin always looked for — Orion, the only one he actually knew — was high and tilted over the forest, three belt-stars in a line.

He looked up.

And the wards were there.

Not suddenly. Not with a flash or a sound or anything dramatic. They were there the way the stars were there — present all along, perceivable now because something in him had adjusted, except it wasn't his eyes that had adjusted and it wasn't darkness they'd adjusted to. His substrate perception had been deepening since January, since the quill, since the nosebleeds started. At Christmas he'd felt the wards as pressure — something leaning on glass — and in the sub-collection he'd caught edges of them, fragments, like hearing a few notes of something you couldn't quite place through a wall. But up here, on the tower, with the sky open above him and no stone ceiling or castle architecture between his perception and what was overhead, the wards came clear.

They were enormous.

That was the first thing. They extended farther than the sky — not across it but through it, occupying a layer of reality that the sky didn't know about. His brain did its best: it rendered what his restructured cognition was perceiving as something between structure and weather, something that had spatial qualities because spatial was how his mind handled large-scale pattern. The wards arced overhead in interlocking curves that went past the horizon in every direction. They weren't lines, exactly, but if he'd had to draw them on a piece of parchment they'd have been lines — sweeping, intersecting arcs, each one an agreement about what Earth was and what could notice it.

He sat with his back against the parapet and his mouth slightly open and looked at something no one else in the school could see.

The wards were beautiful. He hadn't expected that. The manuscript had described them as structures, functional, engineered, and he'd pictured something utilitarian — a fence, a wall, a shield. They were nothing like that. They were patterned in ways that reminded him of the interference shapes you got when you dropped two stones in still water and watched the ripples cross, except each ripple was itself a structure of extraordinary intricacy and the patterns where they crossed were more intricate still. They nested. A large curve contained within it smaller curves that mirrored the large one's shape at a different scale, and those contained smaller ones, and at whatever resolution his perception could manage there were more — more detail, more structure, more. It was like looking at a snowflake and realizing the snowflake was the size of a continent.

He didn't have words for what they were made of. They weren't made of anything in the way a wall was made of stone. They were agreements — the manuscript's term, the best his translation could manage — rendered in the substrate itself. Each arc was a statement about visibility and attention and category, woven into the grammar beneath magic with a precision that made wand work look like a child scratching letters in dirt. The Builders had done this. Beings for whom magic was air had sat down — or whatever they did instead of sitting — and written, into the deepest level of how reality organized itself, a set of interlocking agreements that said: this planet is not here. Do not notice it. There is nothing to notice.

And it had worked. For however many thousand years, it had worked.

Gavin pulled his knees up to his chest and wrapped his arms around them. The wind pushed at him and he barely registered it. His hands were numb. He was cold in the specific, bone-deep way you got cold when you'd been sitting on stone in March without a cloak for too long, and the cold wasn't important because the wards were beautiful and they were failing and he could see where.

The thin places. He'd known about them from the manuscript — the sections he'd translated in December, the passages about degradation and maintenance protocols designed for a civilization that had left. But knowing was words on a page. Knowing was concepts he'd assembled from imperfect translation and then panicked about on the sub-collection floor. Seeing was different. Seeing was worse.

The thin places weren't holes. They were areas where the interlocking curves had weakened — where the nested agreements had become less forceful, less definite, the way a path worn through grass wasn't a gap in the grass but a place where the grass had thinned. The ward structures in these areas still existed but with diminished conviction. The patterns were still there. They just weren't saying what they said as firmly.

He could see three from the tower. Two were large — spanning what felt like enormous tracts of the ward surface, though his sense of scale was unreliable because the wards weren't operating at a scale where human distance measurements meant much. One was above the Forbidden Forest and slightly east, an area where the intersecting arcs had separated from each other enough that you could perceive what was between them. The other was further north, barely at the edge of what he could resolve, a wide zone where the nested structures had degraded unevenly — some scales still strong, others thinned to almost nothing, like a rope with half its strands frayed through.

The third was small. It was almost directly overhead.

He looked at it. It was a place where the ward patterns had worn smooth, like the letters on an old gravestone that the rain and the wind had been at for centuries. The agreements were still legible. Still present. But they'd lost their precision. The grammar that said *this planet is not here* was, in this small patch, saying something more like *this planet is probably not here*. The filter was thinner. Not gone. Thin.

And through the thin places, he could sense what was beyond them.

He'd expected — if expected was the right word for something he'd been dreading for months — that perceiving it would be different from reading about it. He was right. It was different. But not in the direction he'd feared. He'd been afraid of a presence. Something that would notice him noticing it, something looking back, something with intention. A monster, even though the manuscript said it wasn't one. You could be told something wasn't a monster and still picture a monster because that was what human minds did with threat — they gave it a face and a direction.

It didn't have a face. It didn't have a direction.

What was beyond the wards wasn't a thing. It was a condition.

His mind kept reaching for analogies and they kept breaking. It wasn't darkness, because darkness was just the absence of light and this wasn't an absence of anything — it was full. It wasn't pressure, because pressure implied a force pushing somewhere. It wasn't cold, because cold was a thing his body understood and his body had no equipment for this. The closest he could get — and it was wrong, he could feel the wrongness of the approximation even as he formed it, the way you could feel a translation going sideways — the closest he could get was this: on this side of the wards, the substrate had rules. Magic operated according to a grammar. Agreements were possible. Things could be what they were because there were structures that made being-what-you-were a condition that held.

Through the thin place overhead, what he sensed was a place where those structures thinned and thinned and then stopped. It was like lowering your hand into lake water in November — the warmth of air giving way gradually to cold, and then the cold deepening until your fingers didn't feel like fingers anymore and the signals your nerves were sending stopped making sense. Except it wasn't temperature. It was the substrate itself becoming less applicable, less valid, the rules that governed how magic worked attenuating the way sound attenuated as you approached vacuum. On this side, rules. On the other side, something that didn't have rules in any sense that applied to Earth or magic or life.

The manuscript had used two analogies that Gavin kept coming back to. The first was the relationship between wizards and flobberworms — not predator and prey but a gulf of scale so great that the smaller thing was beneath the concept of notice. The second was a water table rising. Not pressing through the wards. Not trying to break them. Just the level of something climbing, slowly, the way groundwater climbed, and the things above the water table — the roots, the foundations, the soil — becoming less and less viable as the water reached them. The water didn't want to drown the roots. The water didn't want anything. The water was just rising.

Sitting on the tower with his knees against his chest and his numb hands clutching his own elbows, Gavin felt the truth of those analogies shift from words he'd translated to something he could sense in his changed perception. It was worse than he'd imagined. It was worse because the condition beyond the wards was indifferent. If it had wanted to destroy Earth, that would have been terrible but comprehensible — there would have been a relationship, however predatory, and relationships implied shared categories. Something that hunted you at least knew you existed. But the condition didn't want anything. It wasn't the kind of thing that could want. It occupied — not space, something — and in the whatever-it-was that it occupied, the notion of Earth was not a possible thought. Not because Earth was too small to notice, though it was. Because noticing required categories and the categories that included Earth were not categories that obtained out there.

The wards were the only reason the question didn't arise. The wards said *nothing here* and the condition — because it didn't care enough to look closely, because looking closely wasn't a thing it did — accepted the statement. But the wards were old and no one had maintained them and the statements were getting quieter.

He sat on the stone floor of the Astronomy Tower and the wind pushed at him and his nose was running from the cold and his left hand had the tremor, the one that came and went since the Cruciatus, and he thought: the adults can't help with this.

Not because they were unwilling. Because they couldn't perceive it. You couldn't fix what you couldn't see, and you couldn't see this without the First Art, and the First Art required months of cognitive restructuring through a manuscript that existed in a single copy in a sub-collection that didn't appear on any map. The adults were busy with a war. Dumbledore was dead. Snape was whatever Snape was. The Ministry had fallen. The Order of the Phoenix was scattered and hiding. Every competent witch and wizard in Britain was occupied with a war about blood purity and political power and none of them — not one — knew that the war was a family argument in a house that was on fire.

Gavin was not smart enough.

That was the thing that settled in his stomach like swallowed ice. He'd known it before — Miren had pointed it out, gently, and she'd been right. His Ancient Runes marks were mediocre. His Transfiguration was worse. He'd failed his mock OWL. The manuscript had been designed for minds categorically different from his, and even with the cognitive restructuring he was still Gavin Tench, average at everything, exceptional at nothing, a kid whose best Switching Spell turned teacups into lumps of clay. He'd managed one successful First Art edit — a quill that was slightly less visible — and it had given him a nosebleed that lasted twenty minutes and blurred his vision for an hour. The wards were a planetary-scale structure built by beings who used magic the way humans used oxygen, and the maintenance protocols in the manuscript called for interventions of a sophistication so far beyond what he'd achieved that the gap between his ability and what was required was not meaningfully different from infinity.

He was fifteen. He didn't know enough. He couldn't do enough. The problem was too large and he was too small and the people who might have helped were killing each other over something that would be irrelevant if the wards failed.

He sat on the tower and didn't move.

Time passed. He wasn't tracking it. The stars moved in the slow way stars moved — Orion's belt shifting westward, the paring of moon gone now behind the mountains, the sky rotating around its pivot point. The wind died and came back and died again. His body was cold in a way that had gone past discomfort into a kind of background fact. The stone under him was stone. The iron telescope mount was iron. His robes were damp from the wind and the beginning of frost that was forming on every cold surface.

The wards hung overhead, beautiful and degrading. The three thin places. The condition beyond, patient and indifferent, rising like water.

He could have gone inside. He didn't.

---

The sky began to change.

It happened the way dawn always did — slowly at first, the deep black overhead lightening to a dark blue that was almost black but not quite, the stars fading from the east as the light came up behind the mountains. The ridgeline caught the first change: a thin line of grey along the peaks, then something warmer creeping down the western faces as the sun found the mountains from below the horizon.

The wards were still there. They hadn't disappeared — they weren't optical, had nothing to do with visible light — but Gavin's attention loosened. The part of his perception that rendered the ward structures grew tired the way any sense grew tired when you held it at maximum effort for too long. The curves receded. They were still present if he reached for them, a faint architecture of agreements arcing overhead, but they stopped dominating his awareness and the morning came in to fill the space they left.

The morning.

March dawn in the Highlands. The sky went from blue-black to blue-grey to a pale, clean blue that promised nothing warmer than what was already here. Frost had settled on the parapet during the night — Gavin noticed it now, thin and white along the edges of the crenellations, on the iron of the telescope mounts, on the stone floor around him where his own body heat hadn't reached. His robes were stiff with it. His trousers were damp and cold against his legs. He was very cold and he noticed that now, finally, the way you noticed cold when the thing that had been overriding it went quiet.

Below him, the grounds resolved in the growing light.

The lawns were patchy — dead brown and muddy where the last snow had melted, green in strips where the grass was coming back, churned up near the Quidditch pitch where somebody had been walking back and forth for no reason anyone could see. The lake was a flat grey mirror, still partly frozen at its northern edge where the sun didn't reach until afternoon. The Whomping Willow was a bare skeleton across the water, leafless, its branches motionless in the still air. The greenhouses caught the early light and threw it back in irregular flashes, their glass dirty from a winter's worth of weather.

Hagrid's cabin. Smoke was coming from the chimney now — thin, grey, the kind you got from a fire that had burned low overnight and was being coaxed back to life. The pumpkin patch beside it was bare dirt, nothing growing yet, but the soil had the dark, wet look of ground that was thawing and would be ready for planting soon. A light came on behind the curtains. Fang barked once — a deep, muffled sound that carried up through the morning air.

The Forbidden Forest was waking. The trees at its edge were still bare but there was a different quality to them in the March light — the branches not quite empty, buds present though not open, a thickening at every tip that would be leaves in a month. Birds were starting up. Not many. A few early songs from the forest edge, thin and territorial, staking out the morning the way birds did when spring was still a question rather than an answer.

Gavin watched a crow land on the Quidditch pitch and walk across the mud with the deliberate, important stride of a bird that had business. It stopped, tilted its head, jabbed at something in the ground, came up with nothing, and walked on.

Below the tower, in the courtyard, the first students appeared.

Two Ravenclaws — blue-trimmed scarves, walking close together, heading toward the Great Hall. One said something and the other laughed, the sound carrying up in the way sound carried on cold mornings, thin and clean. A first-year came out of the entrance hall and crossed the courtyard at a trot, small inside a coat he was wearing over his robes, late for something or cold or both. A group of older students followed, talking, their voices a murmur from three hundred feet up.

Gavin watched them.

They didn't know. None of them knew. They were worried about the Carrows and the war and whether Harry Potter was alive and whether the DA's last message drop had been discovered and who would be called up in Dark Arts today and what was for breakfast. Those things were real. The Carrows were real. The war was real. Duncan's bruises were real. The Cruciatus was real. Every one of those fears was specific and justified and the right thing to be afraid of and none of it would matter if the wards failed, because if the wards failed there would be nothing left for any of it to happen to.

He thought about the Hufflepuff common room. The barrel entrance, the round earth-coloured walls, the plants in the windows that got only indirect light and grew anyway. The way it smelled of whatever the kitchens were making because the kitchens were right there and the scent seeped through the walls — bread in the morning, roast in the evening, that particular warm starch smell during exams when the house-elves put out biscuits at midnight. He thought about sitting by the fire doing homework badly. He thought about Miren arguing with a sixth-year about Quidditch standings that didn't matter because there was no Quidditch this year, arguing anyway because arguing was what Miren did when she was scared. He thought about Duncan before — before the Carrows, before the careful blankness, before he'd learned to monitor every word. Duncan who had opinions about Sheffield Wednesday and would defend them. Duncan who'd mentioned his mum's cooking that one night in the common room, the thing with tinned peaches, and for a moment the person he'd been had surfaced through the person he'd had to become.

He thought about the first-year he'd helped with the Levitation Charm back in December, sitting in the common room for forty minutes explaining a wrist movement he himself couldn't do properly. A small, scared kid. The kid had gotten the spell. He'd smiled.

The first-year in the courtyard below — the one who'd come out at a trot — had stopped to retie his shoe. He knelt on the cold flagstones, fingers working at the lace, and Gavin watched from the top of the tower.

He loved this world. This stupid, mundane, small world. The courtyard with its uneven flagstones and the weeds growing between them that nobody cleared because who cared about weeds when there were Carrows. The castle with its shifting corridors and its enchanted ceiling and its ghosts who complained about the temperature. The common room that was too warm and too crowded and smelled like bread. The lake. The forest. The Quidditch pitch that nobody was using this year and the grass going to mud. The greenhouses with their dirty glass. Fang barking once in the morning. The crow on the pitch with its important walk and its unsuccessful stab at the ground. All of it. Every specific, irreplaceable, ordinary piece of a world that was fighting a war while something that didn't know the word *war* or the word *world* or the word *fighting* leaned against the glass.

The boy in the courtyard got his shoe tied and went inside for breakfast.

Gavin's grief was not for himself. He understood that clearly, sitting on the tower with frost on his robes and his nose running and his left hand trembling against his knee. He'd be dead too if the wards failed — him and his manuscript and his mediocre understanding and his nosebleeds and his one blurry quill. He was part of what would be lost. But the grief wasn't about him. It was for the boy who'd just tied his shoe. For the Ravenclaws who'd laughed. For the first-year he'd helped with Charms. For Miren and her arguments. For Duncan, who'd already been hurt enough this year and whose worst problem should have been the Carrows because the Carrows were the kind of problem that ended — wars ended, regimes fell — and what was leaning against the wards wasn't the kind of problem that ended. For his parents, who didn't know where he was or what he was doing and wrote him letters about the garden and the cat. For Hagrid, stoking his fire. For every person in the Great Hall eating breakfast and worrying about the things people worried about and having no idea that all of it — every OWL exam and every Quidditch match and every war and every act of cruelty and every act of kindness — existed inside a structure that was wearing thin.

The grief was for the scale of the not-knowing. A whole world, not knowing. Going about the business of being a world — fighting, eating, studying, hurting each other, helping each other — inside a bubble of conditions that were maintained by something they couldn't perceive, protected from something they couldn't imagine, and the bubble was thinning and the only person who could see it was a fifteen-year-old Hufflepuff sitting on a tower in his school robes, freezing, who wasn't smart enough.

More students crossed the courtyard. The sun cleared the mountain ridge and the grounds went from grey to the specific gold-brown of early Scottish spring — mud turning amber, the green patches of grass brightening, the lake catching light and throwing it in a line toward the forest. A door opened somewhere below and the breakfast sounds drifted up — faint, muffled, the clink of tableware and the low communal murmur of people eating together.

Gavin wiped his nose on his sleeve. His fingers left a damp streak on the wool. He looked up one more time. The wards were there, faint now in the morning light but perceivable if he reached — the arcs and intersections, the nested agreements, the three thin places with their worn-smooth grammar. The condition beyond, patient as geology. The small patch almost directly overhead where *this planet is not here* had become something less certain.

He stood up. His knees cracked. His lower back ached in the specific way stone made you ache when you'd sat on it for most of a night, a deep bruised feeling that went into the hip joints. His feet had gone past numb into the territory where he wasn't sure they'd hold him and he stood slowly, one hand on the parapet, waiting until the pins-and-needles started and he could feel the stone under his soles. The telescope mount was warmer now under his other hand — sun-warmed on its eastern face, the iron going from cold to cool.

He went down the stairs. The spiral staircase was dim after the open tower and he went slowly, one hand on the wall, feeling the stone under his fingers change from the wind-smoothed outer blocks to the rougher interior masonry. The castle was waking around him. Footsteps in a corridor below. A door. The particular morning sounds of a school that was occupied and frightened and still, despite everything, functioning as a school.

He reached the bottom of the stairs and stepped into a corridor that smelled like breakfast. Eggs. Toast. The warm-bread smell that meant the kitchens were going and the house-elves were doing what they did and the day was starting the way days started.

A second-year he didn't know passed him, walking fast, tie loose and shirt untucked, a piece of toast in one hand. The boy glanced at Gavin — his rumpled robes, his frost-damp hair, whatever his face looked like after a night on a cold tower — and kept going. The boy had a smudge of ink on his cheek. His socks didn't match. He was eating his toast in the efficient, distracted way of someone who had somewhere to be and hadn't given a single thought that morning to what was overhead.

Gavin watched him disappear around the corner and then walked toward the Hufflepuff common room. He'd need to change into dry robes before class. He had Charms first period and a Potions essay due tomorrow that he hadn't started and would fail because he didn't have time for Potions essays anymore, because what he had time for was a manuscript designed for a dead civilization that contained maintenance instructions he couldn't follow for a structure he wasn't capable of repairing.

He went down the stairs toward the barrel entrance. The corridor near the kitchens smelled like bread, warm and specific and alive. Through a doorway he caught a glimpse of a house-elf carrying a platter of sausages, small and serious-faced, absorbed in the work of feeding people.

The barrel entrance was ahead. He could hear voices inside — Hufflepuffs getting ready for the day, the sounds of a common room doing what common rooms did in the morning.

He tapped the barrel in the right rhythm and went in. The warmth came first — the fire up, the room heated, the contrast with the tower so sharp that his face prickled and his hands began to ache as the blood came back. Students at tables, eating, talking. A sixth-year was grumbling about a Herbology assignment. Someone laughed at something someone else had said. A copy of the *Daily Prophet* lay on a side table, its headline about the war — always about the war.

He went to his dormitory and changed into dry robes. His fingers were clumsy with the buttons. His left hand shook. He dressed and picked up his Charms textbook and his wand — his wand, which he kept forgetting he had — and went to class.

The day began. The wards arced overhead, beautiful and failing, holding and thinning. The world went on beneath them, small and loud and precious, not knowing.
