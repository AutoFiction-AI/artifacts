# Chapter 9

The exercise was simple. The manuscript described it in three lines of notation that Gavin had decoded weeks ago: perceive the agreement governing the candle's flame, hold the perception steady, and adjust. *Adjust* was the word he used because the manuscript's actual term sat somewhere between *renegotiate* and *edit* and *speak differently to* — three English words where the original used one symbol that contained all of them and none of them exactly. He had been trying the exercise every night for the past two weeks. The candle burned the way candles burned.

He sat on the stone floor of the sub-collection with his back against the wall, the manuscript open across his lap. The room was cold — December cold, the deep mineral kind that came up through the stone itself and settled into his legs and the base of his spine. The sub-collection was always colder than the rest of the castle, and this deep into winter the chill had a permanence to it, as if the limestone had made a final decision about temperature and would not be moved. The room's own light — faint, steady, a luminescence from the walls that had nothing to do with torches or spells — was enough to read by. But the exercise needed a target. Something small whose rules he could see.

He could see them. That was the part that made him want to put his head through the wall. The flame's flicker was built on agreements — heat and air and fuel and the specific relationship between combustion and the magical substrate that made fire in a magical building behave the way wizards expected fire to behave. He could see the edges of those agreements now, dim and structural, layered beneath the visible flame like a second architecture. When he let his focus settle past the light, the substrate showed itself: not as colour or shape but as a kind of pressure, a sense of rules being applied, the way you could feel the weight of deep water against your ears before you saw anything in the dark.

He could not touch any of it.

He tried again. He narrowed his focus on the flame, let his perception drop below the heat and light into the structure underneath, and reached — not with his wand, not with a word, but with something the manuscript called *intention made structural*. He had no better understanding of the phrase than the first time he'd read it. He reached for the agreement the way you reached for a word you knew but couldn't find, the one sitting just behind your tongue, almost there, almost —

The candle flame burned. The sub-collection walls glowed their ancient glow. The stone was cold against his back and cold under his legs.

Nothing happened.

He sat back and pressed the heels of his hands against his closed eyes until the pressure built colours. His left hand trembled — the Cruciatus tremor from November, the nerve damage that surfaced in the cold or when he was tired or when he tried the exercises. He pressed his palm flat against the stone floor until the trembling settled to something he could ignore.

The manuscript had been designed for someone else. Gavin understood this the way he understood his shoe size or his Charms grade: as a fixed fact about himself that was not going to change because he wanted it to. The exercises assumed a mind that was already partway to understanding the substrate, that needed only structure and practice to complete a transition it was nearly ready for. Gavin's mind was not nearly ready. Gavin's mind was adequate at History of Magic and passable at Ancient Runes and mediocre at the rest. The manuscript was trying to teach something that required a capacity he did not have, and his response to this was to keep trying because the alternative was to stop, and if he stopped then nobody was doing this at all and the wards would go on thinning and nobody would notice until noticing was the same as dying.

After an hour his nose bled. A thin line from his left nostril that he caught with his cuff before it reached the manuscript's pages. The nosebleeds had started the previous week, the first time he'd held the substrate perception long enough for something behind his eyes to protest. The headaches were worse — a pressure at the front of his skull that felt less like pain and more like the sensation of something being fitted too tightly, as if the inside of his head had been measured for a thing that was slightly too large for it.

He cleaned his face with his wand — *Tergeo*, the cleaning charm he could still cast — and packed the manuscript into his bag. Half past eleven. He walked back to Hufflepuff through corridors that were empty and dark and cold. His footsteps echoed in a way that made the castle feel twice its size. He passed the third-floor stretch where someone had scratched DUMBLEDORE'S ARMY into the stone and someone else — a Carrow, probably, or a student who had decided cooperation was safer than solidarity — had gouged through the letters with something heavy. Both sets of marks were still visible, the words and the violence done to them, and nobody had repaired the wall.

The barrel entrance to Hufflepuff was warm against his knuckles when he tapped the rhythm. Inside, the common room smelled of earth and warm wood and the faint yeast-and-honey scent of whatever the kitchen elves were baking below. The last coals of the fire gave the round room an orange glow that turned the copper pots on the shelf into small, warm suns.

He climbed the dormitory stairs and sat on his bed with his notebook and translated one more passage by wandlight — ward geometry, the relationship between the wards' physical structure and their substrate resonance — before his eyes gave out. He understood perhaps a third of it. He wrote guesses in the margins, crossed out two, wrote new guesses over the old ones, and fell asleep with his quill still on the pillow.

The days had a shape he could have drawn with his eyes closed. He woke at six in a dark dormitory, dressed in the cold — his tie knotted badly, his robes never quite straightened — and went to breakfast in a Great Hall that was emptier each week. Students had been leaving all term. Families pulling children out. Muggle-borns disappearing into the registration apparatus and sometimes not coming back. Nobody counted the empty seats aloud. Counting meant knowing how many were gone, and knowing meant sitting with the number, and nobody wanted to sit with the number.

He ate toast. He drank pumpkin juice. Miren sat next to him and Duncan sat across, and the three of them occupied their section of the Hufflepuff table the way people occupied a waiting room — close by default, not by attention.

"Did you finish the Herbology essay?" Miren said one morning. She was eating porridge and tearing bread into small, precise pieces, her hair in the tight plait she wore on days she had things to do after class. She had been running DA logistics — Gavin didn't know the details and she didn't offer them — and on those mornings the plait was a signal, a small, practical flag.

"No," Gavin said.

"It's due today."

"I know."

"You haven't opened the Herbology book once this week. I've been sitting next to you."

"I'll do it at lunch."

"No, you won't."

She was right. He wouldn't. He would spend lunch in the sub-collection trying to decode a passage about maintenance protocols that he'd been fighting for three days. The Herbology essay was about the medicinal properties of Fanged Geraniums and the Fanged Geraniums were not going to matter if the wards failed and the Herbology essay was not going to affect whether the wards failed. He couldn't say this. He had tried saying this kind of thing in December, to Babbling and then to Miren, and it had produced tea and sympathy and a fair point about his Ancient Runes marks and he had not tried again.

He ate his toast. Miren ate her porridge. Duncan tore his bread into smaller pieces and ate half of them and lined the rest along the edge of his plate. He did this most mornings — took a portion, divided it, ate the smaller division. The weight he had lost since September showed in the way his robes hung on his shoulders, in the sharpness of his wrists where the bones were visible through the skin. He wore his collar buttoned to the top, always, even in the warm common room, as if loosening it would expose something underneath that he was keeping covered.

He went to classes. He sat in Charms and copied notes that turned, three lines in, into manuscript notation — his handwriting shifting from the round letters of English into the angular marks of the First Art's cipher as his attention slid below the surface of Flitwick's lecture. Flitwick was teaching nonverbal casting, a fifth-year OWL requirement, and Gavin couldn't get his Silencing Charm to work without the incantation. He had managed it last month. Now the spell kept sliding off the edge of his intention before it reached his wand, as if his mind had found a new track and the old one was closing behind it. His wand work was deteriorating — not in sudden collapses but steadily, like a slope, the coordination between intention and spell and wand movement losing its precision because his intention was learning to operate at a level where wands were irrelevant, and the transition was making him worse at the thing he'd been merely mediocre at before.

History of Magic was the easiest class to disappear in. Binns droned and the room dissolved into grey inattention and Gavin translated undisturbed for forty-five minutes, his notebook filling with notation and guesswork. The boy beside him — a Hufflepuff called Patterson, who spent every Binns lecture arranging his Chocolate Frog cards in rows on his desk, sorted by some system involving colour-coding and tiny pencil marks on the backs — glanced at Gavin's page once, saw the unfamiliar symbols, and went back to his cards without comment. Patterson had freckles and bitten nails and a way of mouthing the names on the cards as he sorted them, his lips forming a silent catalogue. The habit was no stranger than Gavin's own work, and neither of them remarked on what the other was doing.

Ancient Runes was the only class he still attended with both eyes. Not because Babbling's lectures connected to the manuscript — they were working through standard Futhark, exercises he could have done in half the time — but because the framework she had given him months ago was still producing results, and occasionally a concept from her lecture would land against a problem he'd been carrying and something would shift. The cipher's structural invariants, the patterns she had taught him to look for in unknown notation — these were the tools that let him grind forward a sentence at a time.

Babbling did not look at him differently this term. She did not mention the manuscript. She called on him in class, marked his essays with her usual terse comments, and treated him as a student who was adequate at Runes and nothing more. If she remembered the notation he had shown her in October — the notation she had recognised as unprecedented, pre-Futhark, possibly pre-anything — she did not let the memory show on her face or in her voice. Gavin respected this. He also felt, faintly, the specific loneliness of being forgotten by the one person who had seen what he had found and chosen to look away.

The Transfiguration mock OWL was on a Thursday, the last week before the Christmas break. McGonagall had announced it with the clipped efficiency of someone who disapproved of the timing but lacked the authority to change it. The mock exams had been compressed into the final two weeks of term — pushed forward, rushed, because nobody was certain, this year, that the OWL examiners would come at all in June.

Gavin revised the night before. He set the manuscript aside for two hours and practised Switching Spells at his desk — teacup to rat, cushion to quail, the standard exercises. The teacup went passably. The cushion was poor. He forgot to attempt the pincushion because he'd drifted into a passage about ward-substrate coupling and lost the time.

The examination room was the Transfiguration classroom with the desks pushed to the walls, students standing at individual stations with their targets lined up: a teacup, a cushion, a wooden box. McGonagall stood behind her desk with her marking parchment and her quill and the dark-green robes she wore every teaching day. The left cuff of her sleeve was frayed — a single thread hanging loose from the hem, visible when she moved her hand to write. It was the kind of small disrepair that McGonagall would not have tolerated in September, and its persistence into December said something about the year that a speech could not have said.

The Switching Spell was the second task. Gavin raised his wand, visualised the transformation — teacup to rat, standard OWL exercise, he had done this a dozen times in practice — spoke the incantation, and watched the teacup collapse into a lump of grey-brown clay.

It was not a rat. It was not a malformed rat or a rat with the wrong ears or a rat that had retained some ceramic quality. It was a lump of wet clay the size of his fist that sat on the desk and did nothing because it had never been alive and never would be. It was formless and slightly damp and it looked like something left behind by a process that had given up halfway through.

The Ravenclaw at the next station — a girl with ink-dark fingers and a quill tucked behind her ear that she seemed to have forgotten was there — glanced at his desk, saw the clay, and turned back to her own work with the careful speed of someone who did not want to be caught looking.

Gavin stared at the clay. He knew what had gone wrong. He had reached for the Switching Spell and his mind had gone to the substrate first — to the layer of agreements beneath transfiguration, the grammar that made the spell possible — and the two intentions had collided and produced neither. The clay was what happened when you tried to speak two languages at the same time and got nothing comprehensible in either. A collapsed meaning. A thing that was not a teacup and not a rat and not anything.

His wand felt wrong in his hand. Not physically wrong — same length, same weight, same wood — but the relationship between his intention and the wand's response had shifted, subtly, the way a familiar door handle felt different when you'd been turning it with the other hand. The wand worked by channelling intention through a specific grammar. Gavin's intention was learning a different grammar. The two were beginning to interfere with each other, and the interference produced clay.

He moved on to the third task — a Vanishing Spell on the wooden box — and managed to make it transparent rather than invisible. Half marks at best. He was fairly sure he didn't care.

After class, McGonagall asked him to stay. She looked at him over her spectacles — a look that in September had been her standard assessment of insufficient effort but that had softened, over the term, into something closer to concern. The particular concern of a teacher who had watched too many students come apart this year and had attributed each case to the obvious cause.

"Your practical work has deteriorated significantly since September," she said. McGonagall spoke in clean, measured bricks of language, each word placed precisely. She did not soften her sentences and she did not rush them. "I am aware that this year has placed extraordinary pressures on all of our students."

"Yes, Professor."

"If you would find it helpful to speak with Madam Pomfrey, or to arrange additional revision sessions —"

"I'm fine." He'd said it too fast. "Thank you. I'll — I'll revise more."

She looked at him for a moment. The frayed thread on her cuff trembled in the draught from the window. Then she nodded and turned back to her marking, and Gavin left.

The corridor outside was cold. Somewhere ahead of him he could hear Gryffindors talking about Christmas plans, their voices carrying the specific forward-leaning energy of people counting days until something good happened. Gavin walked toward Hufflepuff and the sound faded behind him.

He did not feel ashamed of the clay. That was the thing he noticed as he descended the stairs — the absence of shame where shame should have been. A year ago, failing a mock exam badly enough to be spoken to afterward would have sat in his stomach for days. Not because he cared deeply about Transfiguration but because he had always cared about adequacy, about sitting in the middle of every distribution, exceptional at nothing but terrible at nothing either. The clay should have bothered him. It didn't. It sat in his memory like a fact about the weather — real, registered, not actionable. His problems were elsewhere and larger and also not going well, and the total was that he was failing at everything: bad at the magic other people did, bad at the magic only he could see, bad at being present for the people who needed him present. He was spread across two kinds of failure and competent at neither.

He went to the sub-collection and tried the candle exercise again and produced nothing and bled from his nose onto his sleeve and went to bed.

The Hufflepuff common room emptied early that December. Students went to bed when they could because sleep was the one place the Carrows couldn't reach, and by half past ten most nights the round room held nothing but the fire and the potted plants and the quiet clatter of the kitchens through the south wall.

Gavin stayed up. He worked at the round table near the hearth, the manuscript pages spread beside his notebook, and the common room's warmth was better for translation than the sub-collection's stone cold. He could sit here with his candle and the fire's last coals and work until one or two in the morning and the only cost was tiredness, and tiredness was a thing he could carry.

Duncan stayed up too. Not every night, but often enough that Gavin had stopped tracking when it started. Duncan would appear in the armchair by the hearth after the others had gone, a textbook on his knee, and sit there. The textbook was always open. Duncan's hands were always still on the pages. The pages did not turn.

Tonight was one of those nights. Past midnight, the fire burned down to embers that ticked as they cooled. The room was lit by Gavin's candle and the last orange glow from the hearth, and the shadows in the corners were deep and close. Through the south wall, the kitchen sounds continued — a house-elf's footsteps, the faint clink of something being stacked, the rhythms of work that went on regardless of the hour.

Duncan was wearing the green jumper. It was a Muggle thing, knitted, the colour of moss on a north-facing wall. The left elbow had a patch in a slightly different green where it had worn through and been mended, the stitches visible if you looked, uneven in a way that said *hand-sewn by someone who cared more about warmth than neatness.* He wore it every evening when his robes came off. It was too big for him now. The sleeves covered his hands entirely, the cuffs bunched around his fingers, and the body of it hung loosely on his frame in a way that remembered a shape Duncan no longer filled.

Gavin was working through a passage about substrate maintenance — the same passage he had been fighting for three nights. The notation was recursive, referring to itself, folding inward like a sentence that contained its own grammar. He was writing his fourth attempt at the same clause when Duncan spoke.

"Do you think about food much?"

Gavin looked up. Duncan was watching the embers, or the space just above them, the thin column of heat rising from the coals.

"What?"

"The food here. It's fine, it's not bad. It just always tastes like —" He moved his hand, a small searching gesture. "Like someone described food to them and they made a really accurate version of the description. But it's always the description. You know?"

Gavin put his quill down. "I've never really thought about it."

"No, it's — forget it. Stupid thing to say."

"It's not stupid."

Duncan looked at him briefly, then back at the fire. "My mum does this chicken thing. She puts it in a big tin with lemon and potatoes and slides it in the oven and the fat runs down off the chicken onto the potatoes and the bottoms go crispy and the tops go soft from the juice. She doesn't weigh anything. She doesn't time it. She just puts it in and checks it when she feels like checking it." He was pulling at the cuff of his jumper, rolling the fabric between his thumb and forefinger, a small repeated motion. "My dad sets the table wrong every Sunday. Every single time. Forks on the wrong side, spoons where the knives go. He thinks it's hilarious. Mum shouts at him. He does it again the next week."

A coal fell through the grate. The soft sound of it — a whisper of ash, a small settling — was the loudest thing in the room.

"Then she puts the tin right in the middle of the table and everyone just eats out of it because no one can be bothered doing extra washing up."

Gavin sat with this. The kitchen was in the room now — the table with the wrong knives, the tin, the steam, the shouting that wasn't real shouting but the kind families did because it was theirs. A Sunday in a house where magic was something that happened at school and chicken was something that happened at home and the distance between the two had once been a simple train journey.

"That sounds good," Gavin said. It came out flat and obvious, the kind of thing you said when someone had offered you something real and you were holding it with the wrong hands.

"Yeah." Duncan closed his textbook. He did not mark the page. He had not been reading it. "I was going to go home for Christmas. The form came back approved — all stamped, everything official." He said *official* the way you said a word whose meaning had changed while you weren't looking. "But I've been thinking about whether —"

He stopped. Not a trailing off but a stop. The sentence continued somewhere inside him and he chose not to let it out.

"Whether what?"

Duncan looked at him. His face had thinned since September in specific, countable ways — the jaw sharper, the skin beneath his eyes darker, the softness gone from his cheeks. He looked the way people looked in photographs from hard years. Not ill. Not starving. Just pared down, the excess taken, the face that remained belonging to someone who had been fifteen for a very long time.

"Whether it's safe. For them. Whether going home means someone starts watching the house. Whether they're already watching and me going back makes it worse." His hands were in the jumper's pockets, hidden. "Whether I'd come back. If I went."

"You should talk to Miren," Gavin said. "She keeps track of this stuff — who's gone home, who's come back. She'd know if —"

"I know. I know she would." Duncan's voice was steady. Steadiness was the thing he had chosen when all the other options had closed. "I'm not asking you to fix it. I'm just." He looked at the fire. The embers were dimming, the orange fading toward grey. "I miss the chicken."

A sound came out of him — not a laugh. Something shaped like a laugh but hollow at the centre, a reflex that his body made because what it was replacing was worse.

Gavin looked at Duncan's hands, buried in the green wool. He knew about the shaking. He had seen it in November, after a Muggle Studies lesson that Duncan had come back from walking stiffly, his face the particular blank of someone who was holding everything behind his expression. Gavin had seen the tremor in Duncan's fingers when he'd reached for a glass at dinner. Duncan had seen Gavin see it. Neither of them had said anything. They were both carrying the same year in different parts of their bodies — Gavin's left hand, Duncan's hidden hands — and the damage was different in origin and in weight but identical in what it meant: that this year was leaving marks that would not come off when the year ended.

He should say something more. He knew this. He knew it with the simple, useless clarity of a person who could see the right action and was not going to take it. He should ask about Muggle Studies. He should ask whether Carrow had done something specific, something recent, something that explained the weight loss and the unturned pages and the hands that hid in pockets. He should offer to go to McGonagall or Sprout or anyone — any adult with the standing to intervene — or he should offer the smaller, simpler thing: to walk with Duncan to his classes, to sit next to him at meals in a way that meant more than sharing a table, to turn his attention fully toward this person and hold it there instead of letting it slide, always, back toward the manuscript.

He did none of these things. Not because he had weighed Duncan's need against the wards and chosen the wards — that decision, when it came, would be conscious and ugly and he would make it with his eyes open. This was not that. This was simpler and worse: he did not know how to begin. The distance between what Duncan needed — protection, solidarity, someone who would orient their whole attention toward him and say *I see what is happening to you and it is not acceptable* — and what Gavin could offer was not a distance he had chosen. He had drifted into it, the way you drifted past a turning on a road, gradually, without a single moment you could point to, until the turning was behind you and you were somewhere else and going back felt like a thing that a different version of you would have to do.

"Duncan," he said.

Duncan waited.

"The chicken thing. With the potatoes — the crispy on the bottom. That really does sound good."

Duncan's mouth moved. Not a smile. Something in the territory of a smile — a small, worn shape that acknowledged the attempt without pretending it had landed. A shape that said: I know what you're doing, and I know it's not enough, and I'm going to let it be what it is because we are in the same room at midnight and this is what there is.

"Yeah," Duncan said. "It is."

He stood. He tucked the Potions textbook under his arm — the textbook he hadn't read, whose pages hadn't turned, which he carried the way you carried something that had once meant you were a student. His socks were visible below the hem of his robes as he climbed the stairs — Muggle socks, thin stripes of red and blue, the kind that came in plastic packets from shops that sold everything for a pound. The green jumper hung loosely on his frame. It remembered the body that had filled it in September.

Gavin was alone in the common room.

The fire had died to ash. Through the wall, very faintly, the kitchen sounds went on — crockery, footsteps, the distant steady rhythms of preparation for a morning still hours away. The potted plants on the windowsill were dark shapes in the candlelight, their leaves curled toward the indirect light they wouldn't get until dawn.

He looked at his notebook. The passage he had been translating was still there in his cramped handwriting, four attempts at the same clause stacked on top of each other:

*The structure requires maintenance at intervals incompatible with the lifespans of its current inheritors.*

He had written it differently each time and each time it had meant the same thing. The wards were designed for beings who lived longer than humans. The maintenance cycle assumed a continuity of attention that did not exist on Earth. The gap between what was needed and what was available — in lifespan, in capacity, in the sheer cognitive architecture required to do the work — was the same gap Gavin sat inside every night when he tried the exercise and produced nothing.

He picked up his quill. He dipped it. He read the next line of notation and began the slow, wrong, necessary work of turning it into something his mind could hold.

Outside, snow fell on the Hogwarts grounds, and the castle was quiet, and the wards — invisible, immense, older than the school and thinner than they had been yesterday and thinner than they would be tomorrow — held.
