# Chapter 16

The fifth-floor corridor had been safe two days ago. Now there was a scorch mark shaped like a handprint on the left wall and the ceiling tiles had been blasted loose above the entrance to what used to be the Muggle Studies classroom, exposing old iron brackets and something that looked like copper pipe except Hogwarts didn't have pipes. Gavin stepped over a pile of broken glass — someone had taken out the display case holding a trophy for the 1987 Inter-House Herbology Competition, though he couldn't tell whether that had been defiance or accident — and kept walking.

The substrate around the scorch mark was still unsettled. He could feel it before he saw it, the way you could feel a bruise through a sleeve: the agreements governing the wall's surface slightly loosened where the spell had landed, the stone's certainty about its own composition frayed at the edges. Two days ago the mark would have drawn him into a closer look, the residual substrate distortion interesting in a clinical way. Now he just noted it and moved on. The castle was full of small wounds like this. Every corridor had them.

Somewhere above him, something heavy fell and something lighter shattered. The sounds reached him through layers of stone — a whump, then a bright spray of breaking glass, then voices he couldn't distinguish. He paused and listened. No screaming. He kept going.

The school had been coming apart for the past week. Not the sudden, dramatic kind of collapse — not a revolution, not a battle — but the slower kind, the kind where each day's damage seemed manageable and then you looked up and realized the structure had shifted. Dumbledore's Army had moved from graffiti and coded resistance to something that was nearly open warfare. He'd passed two seventh-years yesterday casting Shield Charms at each other in the Charms corridor, drilling, and neither had looked at him or tried to hide what they were doing. A third-year Ravenclaw had hexed Alecto Carrow's classroom door with a Colloportus so layered that she'd had to cancel her afternoon session and wait for Flitwick to undo it. Flitwick had taken two hours. Gavin suspected he could have done it in twenty minutes.

And the Room of Requirement had started swallowing students. That was the word his mind offered: swallowing. They went in and didn't come back. Neville Longbottom had gone first, three or four days ago, and since then a steady trickle had followed him — mostly Gryffindors, some Hufflepuffs, a few Ravenclaws who had calculated that the risk of hiding was now lower than the risk of attending class. They left their beds unmade and their trunks half-packed and their friends covered for them at mealtimes with excuses so transparent they were clearly deliberate, not real cover stories but small acts of defiance in themselves. Gone to the hospital wing. Visiting family. She was feeling ill. The excuses dared the Carrows to call them liars. Increasingly, the Carrows didn't.

That was the part that registered even in the back of Gavin's mind, where the ward-maintenance problem ran like a programme he couldn't quite close. Amycus Carrow had gone quiet. He still patrolled the corridors, still assigned detentions, still stood at the front of the Dark Arts classroom with his sleeves pushed up and sweat darkening the collar of his robes. But the volume had dropped. The commands that used to fill a corridor now came out clipped and uncertain, and the students who heard them no longer flinched the way they had in October or November or February. The fear was still there — Carrow was still dangerous, still armed, still backed by a regime that used the Cruciatus the way other schools used Saturday detention — but it had thinned into something more like wariness. Students tracked him the way you tracked a dog that might bite. They didn't drop their eyes anymore.

Twice in the past two days Gavin had seen Carrow standing alone at the end of a corridor, not patrolling, not going anywhere. Just standing. His jaw worked and his hands opened and closed at his sides. He looked like a foreman on a site where the workers had stopped listening, calculating whether to shout louder or walk away, and aware that neither option would help. Gavin had seen his sister Alecto at breakfast this morning, eating quickly with her head down, not scanning the tables the way she usually did. They were both nervous and the nervousness was legible even without substrate perception.

At the substrate level it was something else. The school's magical structure — the layered agreements that held the castle together, the wards within wards that governed everything from the shifting staircases to the enchanted ceiling — was under stress. Not breaking, not failing. Just vibrating slightly, like a window in a strong wind. The amount of hostile magic being cast inside the school had increased, and the agreements that maintained order were absorbing it. Every hexed door and blasted corridor and practice duel left a small residue in the substrate, and the residues were accumulating. Gavin could feel them the way you could feel a headache building behind your eyes before the pain started.

The rumour had reached even him. Harry Potter had done something. He'd been seen, or he was coming, or he'd already come. The details shifted by the hour — broke into Gringotts, escaped on a dragon, spotted in Hogsmeade, coming to Hogwarts — but the specifics didn't matter as much as the fact that the student body had started believing them. Something was moving. The long stalled weight of the war had shifted, and the people who had been enduring the regime with their heads down were lifting them.

Gavin turned the corner onto the seventh floor and nearly walked into a group of four students heading the other direction. They were carrying things: a canvas bag that clinked with potion bottles, a folded blanket, what looked like a first-aid kit with Madam Pomfrey's seal on the clasp. The girl in front was a sixth-year Hufflepuff named Elara Stelwick, and she had a burn scar on the back of her left hand, shiny and oval, from November when Carrow had made them practise fire-conjuring on each other. She carried the canvas bag with that hand as if she'd stopped noticing the scar months ago. The boy behind her — tall, thick-set, wearing trainers under his robes instead of proper shoes — was chewing on the string of his hood and staring straight ahead.

Elara glanced at Gavin. He glanced at the bag.

"You didn't see us," she said.

"Yeah, all right."

They passed. He felt the substrate shift as they moved toward the Room of Requirement — the Room's architecture flexing to receive them, its agreement with the castle expanding in a way that operated more like a negotiation than a spell, closer to what the Builders had done than to any wand magic. He'd noticed that weeks ago. It didn't help with the maintenance problem. He let it go.

The corridor sequence to the sub-collection was unchanged. The seventh-floor passage shifted and reconnected itself in its usual not-quite-predictable pattern, and the temperature dropped as he moved deeper into the older stonework. The torches ended. The walls took on their own faint luminescence, mineral-cold and steady. The air went dry and smelled of limestone and nothing else.

The sub-collection was as he'd left it that morning. The manuscript lay open on the stone shelf he'd been using as a desk, held flat by two pieces of quartz he'd taken from the Hufflepuff common room's decorative rock collection. The notation on the exposed pages moved in its slow, representational way — not animated, not alive, but re-presenting, the same relationships showing themselves from different angles the way rotating an object showed different faces. He sat on the floor, pulled his legs under him, and read.

The ward-maintenance section was the worst part of the manuscript. Not the most disturbing — that was the entity section, the passages describing what existed beyond the wards, rendered in notation that taxed his perception until his nose bled and the edges of his vision went grey. The maintenance section was worse in a different way: it was practical, it was necessary, and it was impossible.

The operations it described were not hard the way a difficult spell was hard, where more power or more precision or more practice could close the gap. They were hard the way arithmetic was hard for a dog. They assumed a cognitive architecture that Gavin did not have. The Builders who'd created the wards had maintained them through acts of unified perception — holding simultaneous awareness of all the variables in a local ward structure and adjusting them together, the way you might close your hand. All the fingers moved at once because they were part of the same motion.

The specific operation he'd been working on for three days involved a thin place in the ward structure he'd mapped from the Astronomy Tower in March. The repair required simultaneous adjustment of what the notation represented as seventeen interlocking parameters — the permeability coefficients, the coherence thresholds, the phase relationships between adjacent segments. Each parameter depended on the current state of all the others. You couldn't adjust one and then adjust the next because adjusting one changed the context for the next.

He could hold six of the parameters in his awareness at once. On a good day, with rest and quiet and no substrate noise from the school above him, he could sometimes manage seven. The procedure needed seventeen.

It was like being given a recipe that called for an oven he didn't own and couldn't build. The recipe wasn't wrong. He was just the wrong species of cook.

He'd spent three days looking for a workaround and the manuscript offered none, because the manuscript had been written for readers who could do what it described. He was the wrong reader. The wards continued to fail. Nobody else was reading.

He read. The notation shifted under his attention, showing him relationships he could almost follow, connections that resolved when he held his focus at exactly the right angle and dissolved into noise when his concentration slipped. The spring warmth of the upper castle did not reach the sub-collection. The stone under him was the same temperature it had been in January. He sat on the cold floor and failed to understand and read again.

---

He left the sub-collection two hours later because he needed to eat and because the afterimages had started. The notation's representational movement was lingering in his peripheral vision even when he looked away from the manuscript — shapes that shifted and re-presented at the edges of what he could see, as if the notation had imprinted itself on his visual processing and wouldn't let go. He'd learned in January that this meant he'd pushed too far and needed to let his eyes settle. That lesson had cost him a bruised shoulder and a cracked pair of glasses when he'd walked into a door frame because the edges of solid objects had gone slightly uncertain.

The seventh floor was empty. The corridors had the held-breath quality of a building that knew something was coming — not the ordinary between-classes quiet of students moving purposefully, but the taut silence of a space that had been cleared. Outside the windows, the grounds were absurd with spring. The lawns had turned the bright, almost edible green of early May, and there were flowers in the courtyard below — daffodils, mostly, and something purple — and the late-afternoon light came in at the angle that made the Scottish Highlands look painted. A warm day. Birdsong from the forest edge. Completely wrong, given everything.

He took the back staircase down toward the kitchens. Fourth floor, third floor, the connecting passage behind the tapestry of Barnabas the Barmy — the shortest route if you wanted to avoid the main corridors and the remaining Carrow patrols. He was thinking about the maintenance problem. Specifically, he was wondering whether the seventeen-parameter operation could be decomposed into a sequence of smaller operations, each involving six or fewer parameters, performed in order rather than simultaneously. It would be like doing long division instead of seeing the answer whole — slower, uglier, but possible for a mind that couldn't hold the full picture. The question was whether the sequential approach would preserve the interdependencies or destroy them. He thought it might destroy them. He was thinking about this when he heard the kid.

A small sound. Not crying — the caught, pressed-down sound of someone trying to be quiet because being loud was dangerous this year.

He stopped. The passage behind the tapestry was narrow and lit only by the glow that seeped through the tapestry's weave. It took him a moment to find the source: a boy sitting on the floor against the left wall, knees drawn up, cradling his right arm against his chest. A first-year. Gavin could tell by the size of him — small, sharp-faced, not yet used to his own proportions. Ravenclaw tie, the blue barely visible in the dim passage. His robes were too big for him, bought with room to grow or handed down from someone larger, and his shoes were scuffed pale at the toes in the way that meant he kicked things when he walked, a habit he probably wasn't aware of. His right wrist was bent at an angle that wrists did not go.

Gavin crouched. "Hey. Can I see?"

The boy looked up. His face was white and his eyes were wet but he wasn't crying, which was either courage or shock. In his left hand — the good hand — he was holding a folded piece of parchment, and he was holding it tightly, the way you held something when everything else had come loose.

"I fell," the boy said. His voice was thin and careful. "The stairs moved. I wasn't — I was going to send a letter and the stairs just—"

"Yeah. They do that. Let me see your wrist."

The boy held it out. The break was clean — Gavin could feel it before he touched the skin, the way he felt everything now, the double image of the physical and the structural. The radius was fractured at a specific point. At the substrate level, the bone's agreements about its own integrity had been interrupted: the rules that said this bone is whole, continuous, load-bearing had a local exception at the point of impact, and that exception was generating pain signals that the boy's nervous system was translating into the white face and the wet eyes and the bitten lip.

Gavin reached for the break.

What happened next took less than a second.

His mind went to the substrate. Not to the Episkey he'd used before on scraped knees and small cuts — not to the spell, not to his wand — but to the agreement that defined the bone's structural state. The fracture was an exception to the bone's default rules. Exceptions could be revoked. His attention closed around the point of the break like a hand closing around a stone and he began to edit: not healing the bone but making the fracture less structurally valid, reducing the exception's claim on reality, so that the bone's default state — whole, continuous, bearing weight — simply reasserted itself the way water filled a hole.

He hadn't decided to do this. He hadn't reached for his wand and then changed his mind. He hadn't even formed the intention to use the First Art. His perception had seen the break and his mind had gone to the substrate the way another person's hand might have gone to their wand — automatically, below the level of decision, the way you caught a glass before you decided to catch it.

The boy gasped.

Gavin stopped.

He'd been doing it for perhaps half a second. The bone was already partly corrected. Not healed — corrected. The distinction mattered enormously and was invisible to anyone who couldn't perceive the substrate. Magical healing knit tissue: it told the body's existing processes to work faster, reconnecting what had been separated, building a callus, leaving a remodelling scar that Madam Pomfrey could read like a signature. What Gavin had been doing was different. He'd been removing the fracture's validity — making the break less real, so the bone's wholeness simply reasserted. No knitting. No callus. No scar. The bone wouldn't have been healed; it would have been a bone that had never been broken, in a body that had broken it three minutes ago.

Madam Pomfrey would notice. If she examined the wrist, she would notice. A bone with no history of fracture in a child who had just fractured it — that gap would be visible to any competent healer.

He pulled back from the substrate and drew his wand.

"Episkey."

The spell was rough. His wand work had been deteriorating all year and the Episkey landed with too much force — the boy flinched, and the bone knit with an audible crack that made them both wince. But it knit. Magically. Tissue reconnecting, callus forming, the swelling beginning to subside. The mundane, explainable kind of healing that left the right residue.

The boy's face went from white to a raw, blotchy pink as the pain dropped.

"Better?" Gavin said.

The boy flexed his fingers carefully. "Yeah. Yeah, it's — ow. But yeah." He looked at Gavin's wand, then at Gavin, with the expression of someone trying to decide whether to trust what had just happened. "Thanks."

"Don't put too much weight on it tonight. If it still hurts tomorrow, go see Pomfrey."

"She's been — they say she's really busy. With the Room people and everything." The boy tucked his mended wrist against his chest and got up, still clutching the folded parchment in his good hand. His too-big robes dragged on the stone floor. "Thanks," he said again, and went, his scuffed shoes receding down the passage until the sound faded and the held-breath quiet of the castle settled back in.

Gavin stayed where he was. Crouching in the narrow passage behind the tapestry, one knee on the cold floor, his wand still out.

He hadn't decided. That was the thing. His conscious practice of the First Art — the deliberate, effortful sessions in the sub-collection where he tried to hold six variables and failed at the seventh — was clumsy, approximate, exhausting. The gap between intention and execution was wide enough to make him feel stupid on a daily basis. But the reflexive edit he'd just done on the boy's wrist had been precise. Clean. Faster and more elegant than anything he'd managed in deliberate practice. His unconscious mind was better at this than his conscious mind, which meant the First Art had settled into whatever layer of cognition handled the things you did without deciding to do them — catching, flinching, breathing, the operations that ran beneath the surface of thought.

He thought about what would have happened if he hadn't caught it. The boy would have walked away with a perfect wrist. A wrist that had been corrected at the level of reality rather than healed at the level of magic. Nobody would have known, probably. Nobody would have looked closely at a first-year's mended wrist. But probably was the problem. If someone had looked — if Pomfrey had examined it, if anyone with training had checked — the absence of healing would have been conspicuous. A bone with no history of injury. An impossible clean gap where the evidence of fracture should have been.

And the next question, the one they wouldn't know to ask but that Gavin couldn't stop asking himself: what did it mean for a living body to be edited at the substrate level? Healing worked with the body's own processes. Substrate correction worked around them. The bone's biology hadn't healed; the bone's rules had been rewritten. Was there a cost to that? A side effect? The manuscript didn't say, because the manuscript didn't contemplate its techniques being used on beings whose bodies relied on fixed biological rules. The Builders hadn't had bodies in the sense that mattered. They wouldn't have needed to ask.

He stood up. His left hand was doing the intermittent tremor — the Cruciatus remnant from November that came and went, came and went, tied to stress or fatigue or both. He pressed it against his thigh until it settled. Then he put his wand in his pocket and went to the kitchens.

The house-elves gave him bread and cold chicken and a mug of tea that was too hot to drink. He sat at the long table in the kitchen's warmth and ate without tasting. The kitchen smelled of roasting meat and baking bread and something sweet — a pudding being assembled for a dinner that fewer students attended each day. Two house-elves were arguing near the ovens about the quantity of potatoes, a dispute conducted in high, rapid voices and resolved by a third elf who intervened with a serving spoon and a pointed look. Gavin ate his bread and drank his tea when it had cooled enough and thought about the substrate edit and the boy's wrist and the fact that his reflexes had outstripped his conscious ability, and what that meant for the work, and whether it was a sign of progress or something else.

---

He went back to the sub-collection after dark.

The route was harder at night. Not because of Filch — even Filch seemed to have lost his stomach for catching students when the students who got caught ended up in Carrow's detentions, and the detentions had become the kind of thing that Filch, for all his petty cruelty, had not signed on for. But the Carrows still patrolled, and their patrols had become erratic in the past few days. Amycus had been spotted on the first floor at midnight and the seventh floor twenty minutes later, which either meant the castle was moving him or he was moving himself in the panicked, purposeless way of a man who'd been told something was coming and couldn't decide what to do about it. Gavin thought it was the latter. The substrate around Carrow had a specific quality these days — a tight, contracted feel, like the magic in his vicinity was pulling inward, bracing.

He reached the sub-collection without meeting anyone. The entrance — not a door, just a place where the corridor ended and older stone began — was as he'd left it. The air was dry and cool and smelled of mineral dust. The faint luminescence of the walls was steady, unchanged by anything that happened in the castle above. The manuscript lay open on its stone shelf, the quartz weights holding the pages, the notation moving in its slow patient loop.

He sat. He read.

The seventeen-parameter problem. He tried the sequential-decomposition approach he'd been considering on the way to the kitchens: take the full operation and break it into sub-operations of six or fewer parameters each, performed in sequence rather than simultaneously. It was a trade — elegance for accessibility, parallel processing for serial. Like looking at a painting through a keyhole, one section at a time, and hoping the sections would add up to the whole.

He got through the first two sub-operations. The notation cooperated, or seemed to — the adjustments to the first six parameters took hold, and when he moved to the second set, those adjustments seemed to hold as well. For a brief, reckless moment he thought it might work.

The third sub-operation showed him why it wouldn't. The parameters in the third set depended on the simultaneous state of the parameters in the first and second sets, and by performing them sequentially he'd disrupted those states — the first set had drifted while he worked on the second, and the second was already shifting in response to the drift in the first, and the whole structure was now less stable than before he'd started. He'd made it worse. Not much worse. A fraction. But the thin place was thinner by a margin he could perceive, and the margin was his fault.

He sat back. Breathed. The cold of the sub-collection floor had settled into his lower back and ankles where his robes didn't cover. He pulled his robe tighter and looked at the notation and understood, again, the shape of the problem.

The Builders had maintained these wards as a single continuous act of perception and adjustment. The operation was singular. The seventeen parameters were not seventeen separate things being tracked but seventeen aspects of one thing, the way the fingers of a closing hand were not five separate motions but one motion distributed across five digits. To decompose the operation into pieces was to miss the point in the most fundamental way possible — like trying to understand a chord by playing the notes one at a time. Each note was correct. The chord was gone.

He needed to hold seventeen variables at once. His mind held six. The gap was not something practice could close. It was architectural — a limitation of human cognitive hardware, the same way a human eye couldn't see infrared. Not a failure of effort but a fact about what he was.

He went back to the notation. There had to be another way. The Builders had left the manuscript as a teaching tool; they'd anticipated that someone would need to learn this. Maybe not a human someone — maybe they'd anticipated another Builder, or a being with the right cognitive architecture, returning to perform the maintenance. But the manuscript was addressed to a reader, and Gavin was the only reader it had, and the wards were failing at a rate that was constant and patient and would not wait for a better student.

He found a passage he'd translated weeks ago and re-read it. The notation described the maintenance operations as something the manuscript called — in Gavin's rough, imperfect translation — a single shape made of attention. Not a sequence. Not a checklist. A shape. The way a sphere was not a stack of circles but a single geometry.

He didn't know how to make a shape out of attention. But the phrase nagged at him. Shape. Not effort, not force, not knowledge. Shape. He turned the concept over while the cold of the stone seeped up through his robes.

Then something changed.

It was subtle at first. A faint wrongness in the substrate — not wrongness, exactly, but a shift, the way you felt a weather change in your teeth before the sky showed it. The substrate flexed. Not here, not in the sub-collection's small stone rooms, but broadly: a large-scale perturbation pressing against the edges of his perception.

He set the maintenance problem aside and attended.

People. A lot of people. Moving toward Hogwarts with intention — not the ambient, everyday intention of students going about their routines, but concentrated, directed magical will, the kind that came from people gathering for a specific purpose. The substrate registered the convergence the way air registered sound: a single person casting a spell was a tap. What Gavin was feeling was the buzz of a crowd before anyone spoke, the vibration of many instruments being tuned at once. He couldn't tell who they were or what they wanted, not from substrate perception alone. But the scale was large. Dozens of people. More. Moving with purpose.

He waited. The perturbation grew.

Over the next hour it intensified from a background pressure to a steady, unmistakable distortion. More people were arriving. More magical will was concentrating. The ward structures overhead — the vast interlocking patterns that he perceived as something between architecture and music, beautiful and degrading at their margins — had begun to resonate with the buildup below them, the way a glass hummed when you ran a wet finger around its rim. The resonance didn't damage the wards or strengthen them. It just made them audible — made them ring — so that the cosmic structures he'd been studying were now overlaid with the vibration of human conflict gathering below.

The notation on the manuscript pages trembled. Not physically — the pages were still, the quartz weights held them flat — but in his perception. The representational movement that was normally smooth and trackable was now jittering slightly, the way fine print jittered on a surface that was vibrating. The substrate noise from the gathering was interfering with the delicate perceptual work the ward-maintenance required. His ability to hold even six parameters was dropping. The operational equivalent of trying to thread a needle while someone shook the table.

He thought: this is going to be very bad for the work.

Not the conflict itself. He had made his assessment of the war months ago and nothing in the intervening weeks had changed it. The Voldemort war was a human-scale event in a human-scale conflict, and the worst outcome — the regime permanent, the persecution institutional, the Ministry a puppet of a man who called himself Lord and believed blood determined worth — was terrible at the scale of human suffering and irrelevant at the scale of what Gavin was working on. The wards did not care who sat in the Minister's chair. The thing beyond the wards, patient and incompatible and as indifferent to wizarding politics as weather was indifferent to the arguments of ants, would continue to press against the thinning barriers regardless of which faction won the argument below.

But the substrate noise of a major magical battle — the distortion caused by hundreds of people casting simultaneously, the sheer volume of agreements being invoked and violated and broken — would make the ward-maintenance work impossible. The delicate perceptual tracking he needed, the ability to hold his six parameters in simultaneous awareness and search for the shape that might let him hold more, required a quiet substrate. A surgeon needed a still table. What was coming would be an earthquake.

He would lose days. The wards did not have days. The thin places he'd mapped in March were thinner now. The degradation was constant and patient. Each day that passed without maintenance was a day the margins eroded a little further, and the margins were where the wards would eventually fail, and the failure would be quiet and total and would not announce itself to anyone except the boy sitting on a cold stone floor in a part of the castle that existed before the castle did.

He turned back to the manuscript. The notation shimmered with the substrate disturbance, the representational movement overlaid with the tremor of gathering war. He narrowed his focus. Tried to read through the noise. The parameters slid in and out of his perception — four, five, a brief flash of six before the substrate vibrated and he lost the fifth. He tried again. Four. Five.

Above him, in the castle that was technically the same building but felt like a country he'd emigrated from, people were choosing. Students were picking sides with the finality of people who understood that this choice wouldn't be reversible. Adults were arriving through passages Gavin had never used — the Hog's Head tunnel, whatever routes the Order had, the channels that the DA had built over a year of careful, dangerous work. Wands were being checked. Loyalty was crystallising into the two simple categories that war demanded. The weight of the wizarding world's unresolved conflict was concentrating on this castle, tonight, and the building hummed with it.

The notation trembled. He lost his fifth parameter again.

He sat in the dry limestone cold of the sub-collection, the manuscript open before him, the quartz weights catching the faint wall-light, and he worked. The wards overhead were thin and thinning. The thing beyond them continued in its way, which was not malicious, which was not intentional, which was simply incompatible with everything that existed below the wards in the way that a rising sea was incompatible with a drawing in the sand — not through will but through presence, through the patient, geological fact of being there. The substrate flexed and hummed with the approaching battle. The notation jittered on the page.

Gavin Tench, fifteen years old, mediocre at everything except the one thing that mattered and not good enough at that, sat on the stone floor and tried to hold six variables in his mind while the world above him prepared to tear itself apart over a problem he could solve in seconds and wouldn't, because the wards were failing, and the wards were all that mattered, and the wards did not care about the war.

He turned a page. The notation moved. He read.
