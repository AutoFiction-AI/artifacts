# Chapter 15

The shift happened on a Tuesday morning in late April, though Gavin only worked out that it was Tuesday afterward, checking the class schedule he'd stopped following. He was walking to breakfast through the third-floor corridor when he noticed that the Permanent Sticking Charm on the portrait of Barnabas the Barmy was failing along its lower-left edge.

Not the portrait itself, not the frame. The agreement beneath the charm — the substrate commitment someone had made centuries ago, probably a long-dead Hogwarts custodian who'd wanted the painting to stay put — was thinning where it anchored to the stone. Old mortar crumbling between bricks, the structure sound but the joints going. A decade more and Barnabas would start to swing.

He kept walking. He was three steps past the portrait before he understood what had just happened.

He hadn't translated. He hadn't seen the portrait and then reached back through the notation he'd memorized, matching the visible effect to a substrate grammar, working out what was there the way he'd been doing for months — slow, effortful, one concept at a time, like reading a foreign language with a dictionary open beside the text. He'd just seen it. The agreement, the thinning, the timescale. All of it at once, the way you saw a word in English and knew what it meant without sounding out the letters.

He stopped in the corridor. A seventh-year Ravenclaw carrying an armful of textbooks so tall she had to tilt her chin to see over them edged past with a muttered "excuse me," and Gavin stepped aside without looking at her. His hands were shaking — the left one worse than the right, as it always was since the Cruciatus — but the shaking wasn't from fear. Something in his head had settled into place the way a dislocated joint settles back, and the relief was physical and strange and slightly sickening.

He went to breakfast.

The Great Hall was three-quarters empty, which had been normal since October. Students clustered at their house tables in tight groups, leaving wide stretches of bare wood between them. The enchanted ceiling showed a spring sky — high cirrus, pale blue, the kind of morning that made the grounds look like they belonged to a different school than the one Gavin was attending. He sat at the Hufflepuff table and looked up at the ceiling and saw it for what it was.

Seven agreements, interlocking. The display agreement, which mirrored the actual sky through a perceptual filter. The containment agreement, which kept weather on the correct side. The light-sourcing agreement, which permitted the mirrored sky to illuminate the hall as if the ceiling were absent. Four structural agreements that maintained the first three and ensured they persisted across centuries without maintenance. He could see where each agreement committed into the substrate — slight distortions, compression points, like nail-marks in painted wood. Places where the substrate had been shaped by ancient intent and held that shape.

It was neat work. Economical. And utterly boring at the grammar level. Like discovering that a cathedral had been built using six types of brick.

He served himself porridge. Someone had put a jar of golden syrup at the Hufflepuff end of the table, which was unusual — syrup was normally over at Ravenclaw — and Gavin poured it on his porridge without thinking about who had moved the jar or why. The syrup was too sweet, the expensive kind that meant it was probably a gift from someone's family. He ate it anyway.

The seat to his left, where Miren had sat every morning for five years, was empty. He noticed this the way he'd noticed the failing charm on Barnabas: as a fact about the arrangement of things, present and requiring no response. She was sitting further down the table with two sixth-year girls who were involved in the DA supply network. He could see the back of her plait, the straight set of her shoulders. She didn't look over.

Across the hall, a first-year was showing a friend a Colour-Change Charm on a napkin. The napkin went from white to a patchy, uneven green, and the friend laughed, and the first-year tried again and the green improved. Gavin watched the substrate flex with each cast. Two sentences in the same grammar, the second more cleanly parsed than the first. The effort and the improvement were visible at both levels simultaneously: the napkin getting greener, and the agreement underneath becoming crisper. He could see where the first-year's intent was imprecise — the colour target was vaguely *green* rather than a specific green — and where the second attempt narrowed the specification. The substrate didn't care about the imprecision. It accepted both sentences. It accepted everything.

He finished his porridge, wiped the bowl with a piece of bread, and left the Great Hall.

Walking through the entrance hall, he passed the hourglasses that tracked house points. The rubies, sapphires, emeralds, and yellow diamonds were low in every column — the Carrows deducted points for sport and nobody had the energy to earn them back — but Gavin barely glanced at the point totals. What he saw were the enchantments: four identical substrate agreements, each one a counting mechanism linked to a conditional display. Cleanly constructed, probably Rowena Ravenclaw's work, with the specific elegance of someone who thought enchantments should be mathematically tidy. He could see the original designer's habits in the way the agreements were anchored — a preference for symmetrical commitment points, like someone who hung pictures at perfectly even intervals.

He was noticing everything. That was the strange part. Not that he had to work to see the substrate — he'd been doing that for months, with increasing facility — but that he couldn't stop seeing it. Every enchantment, every charm, every spelled object in the castle was now legible at two levels simultaneously: the surface, where a door locked and a candle floated and a staircase moved, and the grammar, where agreements sat in the substrate like syntax underlying a spoken language. It was like literacy — once you could read, you couldn't walk through a city without reading the signs. The shapes were always also words.

The corridors were busy. Students moved between classes in the tight, purposeful way that had replaced the old between-class chaos — nobody dawdled, nobody stopped to chat in the open. The Carrows had made corridors dangerous. You could be grabbed for anything, assigned a punishment for loitering, for looking wrong, for being in a group of more than three. Students moved like people in a downpour, heads down, pace brisk, and Gavin moved with them, his bag over one shoulder, his left hand in his pocket where the tremor was less visible.

Outside the Charms classroom, two fifth-years from Gryffindor were practicing Stunning Spells on each other. This was Carrow's homework — practice offensive hexes, demonstrate proficiency in class, and do not come unprepared — and the Gryffindors had the grim, dutiful look of students who understood that showing up without competence meant a different kind of lesson. The taller one cast first. Red light, clean trajectory, decent form. The shorter one went down, hit the wall, slid to sitting.

Gavin leaned against the opposite wall and watched.

At the spell level, a Stunning Spell was straightforward: directed magical energy organized to temporarily disrupt the target's conscious function. First-year understanding. But at the substrate level, what Gavin saw was different in kind, though not in complexity. The spell was a sentence. The grammar that made the sentence valid was an agreement: it is possible for directed magical intent to disrupt consciousness at a distance. This agreement was part of a broader class — the entire family of offensive magic shared a root agreement about the permissibility of one magical actor affecting another's physical or cognitive state without consent.

The shorter Gryffindor got up, shook his head, and cast back. His Stunner was shakier — the substrate sentence had a grammatical wobble in it, like a clause with a misplaced modifier — and the taller one dodged rather than blocking, which was probably smart.

Gavin could see how to fix the shorter student's form. Not at the wand level — he'd be useless at coaching Stunning Spells, his own were mediocre on a good day — but at the substrate level. The grammatical wobble was in the intent specification: the caster was targeting the other student's general area rather than committing to a specific disruption mode. If Gavin could have said *tighten the subordinate clause that specifies where in the target's cognition the disruption applies*, the spell would have landed cleanly. But there was no way to say that in any language the student spoke, and the advice would have sounded like nonsense.

He pushed off the wall and walked on.

He was halfway to Ancient Runes when he heard it.

The sound came from around the corner of the fourth-floor corridor: Amycus Carrow's voice, thick and carrying, saying something Gavin couldn't make out. Then a scream.

Gavin stopped. Other students stopped too — you learned to stop when you heard Carrow, learned to press yourself against the wall and become small, because movement attracted attention and attention attracted punishment. Three Ravenclaw girls ahead of him went still against the stone. A heavy-set Hufflepuff sixth-year named Marcus Belby — not the famous one, a different Belby, a quiet kid who always wore a second scarf even indoors and smelled faintly of the greenhouses where he spent his free periods — put one hand flat against the wall as though bracing for something.

Gavin looked around the corner.

Carrow had a student against the wall. A boy, small — third-year, maybe — with ink-stained fingers and a school tie knotted too short so the fat end barely reached his sternum. Gavin had seen him in the library before. He always sat at the same table near the Restricted Section rope, always had the same book open — Wilbert Slinkhard's *Defensive Magical Theory*, which was ironic or defiant or both, because the Carrows had pulled it from the syllabus in September. The book was on the floor now, spine cracked, pages bent where it had landed.

The boy screamed again.

At the wand level, Gavin saw what every student in the corridor saw: the Cruciatus Curse, red-orange light from Carrow's wand held steady, a child in agony. The boy's back arched and his heels drummed against the stone floor and his hands clawed at nothing and the sound he made was not really a scream but something past a scream — a high, raw exhalation that went on too long, that kept going, the sound of a body producing noise because noise was all it could do.

At the substrate level, Gavin saw something else entirely.

The Cruciatus was a sentence. A single grammatical construction, built on a specific agreement in the substrate: it is valid for directed magical intent to activate a target's pain signalling without physical cause. That was the whole mechanism. Not a complex grammar — simpler, in fact, than the Stunning Spell the Gryffindors had been practicing. The substrate agreement that made the Cruciatus possible was six words long, if you translated it into the closest English approximation. Six words. And the agreement was not enforced by anything special. It was not deeper, not more protected, not anchored by a stronger commitment than the agreement that governed Levitation Charms or Colour-Change Charms or the Permanent Sticking Charm on Barnabas the Barmy's portrait.

It was just a rule. Sitting in the substrate alongside every other rule. A rule that said: this kind of sentence is valid. This kind of effect is permitted. This kind of pain is grammatically possible.

Gavin looked at it and saw where he would edit.

The knowledge arrived without anything that felt like a revelation. He had edited substrate agreements before — the quill, the visibility rules, other small experiments that had cost him nosebleeds and headaches and hours of blurred vision. The Cruciatus agreement was different in scale, yes. But the mechanism was the same. He could see the structure. He could see the anchoring points, the way the agreement committed into the substrate. He could see, with the same automatic clarity with which he'd seen the thinning mortar on Barnabas's portrait, exactly where to make the adjustment that would render the Cruciatus grammatically invalid.

Not block it. Not counter it. Not shield the boy from its effects. Just edit the rule. Make the sentence unsayable. The way you could remove a verb from a language and every sentence that depended on that verb would become nonsense — not wrong, not forbidden, just empty. The words would come out and mean nothing. Carrow's wand would move and the intent would form and the grammar would not support it and the curse would simply not happen. A sentence with no verb. An operation that referred to nothing.

Carrow lifted his wand. The boy crumpled. He lay on the stone floor breathing in fast, shallow gulps, his ink-stained fingers twitching against the flagstones. His book lay beside him, pages fanned open to a chapter on Shield Charms.

"Get up," Carrow said. He was sweating. He always sweated when he used the Unforgivables — Gavin had noticed this before, months ago, the way everyone noticed things about Carrow while trying not to be seen noticing. But now, with the substrate visible, Gavin understood the sweating differently: the physical cost of the Cruciatus was not borne by the caster's magical reserves but by the caster's neural engagement with the substrate agreement. Carrow's body was responding to the act of participating in a pain-grammar, even from the casting side. The sweat had nothing to do with exertion. It was his nervous system registering what it was doing.

Carrow wiped his forehead with his sleeve. He had pushed the sleeves of his robes up, the way he always did, as if punishment were manual labour.

The boy got up. Slowly. One hand on the wall. He picked up his book and held it against his chest with both arms and walked away with the careful steps of someone whose legs had not yet agreed to hold him. Ink on his fingers. Tie too short. A third-year who liked reading about Shield Charms in a school where shields weren't enough.

Gavin watched him go.

The Ravenclaw girls peeled themselves off the wall and hurried away. Marcus Belby exhaled and adjusted his scarf and went wherever he was going. The corridor emptied in seconds — quickly, quietly, everyone pretending they hadn't seen, because seeing meant remembering and remembering meant being a witness and being a witness was dangerous.

Gavin did not move. He was looking at the place in the substrate where the Cruciatus agreement sat, and he was following the grammar outward, and the structure that opened up was like a door into a room he hadn't known was adjacent.

The Cruciatus and the Killing Curse shared a root.

They were different sentences — different effects, different intents, different mechanisms at the wand level. But they were both built on the same foundational agreement: it is valid for directed magical intent to override a target's bodily sovereignty. The Cruciatus overrode it to produce pain. The Imperius overrode it to produce control. The Killing Curse overrode it to terminate life.

One root agreement. Three branches. And the root was no deeper in the substrate than any other root.

The Killing Curse — Avada Kedavra, the spell that had shaped Voldemort's war, that had killed Harry Potter's parents and Cedric Diggory and hundreds of others, that was the reason every witch and wizard in Britain went to sleep frightened — was a grammatical construction resting on a substrate agreement about as complex and about as fundamental as the one that governed Summoning Charms.

It was a rule. Rules could be rewritten. He had rewritten rules before. Smaller ones, simpler ones, but the process was the same. The substrate didn't care about the moral weight of the agreements it hosted. It didn't distinguish between the grammar of pain and the grammar of levitation. It held all rules with the same impersonal fidelity. A shelf that didn't know or care whether it carried children's stories or weapons manuals.

Gavin could see how to make the Killing Curse impossible. Genuinely impossible — like dividing by zero, like conjugating a verb that no longer existed in the language. He could edit the root agreement that all three Unforgivables depended on, and every Killing Curse in the world would fail mid-cast, because the universe would no longer contain the grammar that made it a valid sentence.

The corridor was empty now. He checked his watch. He was fourteen minutes late for Ancient Runes. There was dried blood under his left nostril from a nosebleed he hadn't noticed starting, and his left hand was trembling against his thigh.

He wiped his nose on his sleeve, shifted his bag to his other shoulder, and walked to class.

He was thinking about Voldemort's war.

---

Gavin skipped dinner. He'd been skipping dinner more often than not since the break with Miren — not because of the break itself, not exactly, but because eating in the Great Hall meant sitting among people he used to talk to and now didn't, and the energy required for that performance was energy he'd rather spend on the manuscript. He took a bread roll from the kitchens — the Hufflepuff common room was adjacent, the house-elves knew him by now, they didn't ask questions — and ate it dry on the walk to the seventh floor, dropping crumbs on the stairs. The bread was good. He didn't notice.

The sub-collection was cold, as it always was — the dry, mineral cold of deep stone, older than the castle's heating charms, older than the castle itself. The light from the walls was faint and steady, the luminescence that had been there before anyone thought to install torches. The corridor sequence that led here had taken twelve minutes tonight. Sometimes the castle's shifting architecture made it faster, sometimes slower, and Gavin had stopped trying to predict which. He sat on the floor with his back against the shelf that had once held the manuscript and spread the pages across his knees. The stone was cold through his trousers. He'd stopped bringing a cushion weeks ago because it was one more thing to carry and the cold had become part of the routine, like the nosebleeds, like the headaches, like the tremor in his left hand that flared when he was tired.

The notation moved. It always moved — not in the looping, repetitive manner of wizard photographs, but representationally, the symbols shifting to show relationships from different angles, rotating perspectives the way you might turn a glass in your hand to catch new light. Gavin watched the notation and understood it and the understanding was not translation. He was reading the way you read in your own language: the meaning arrived without a processing step, the symbols and the concepts they carried collapsed into a single act of comprehension.

He was working on ward-substrate coupling. The section of the manuscript that described how the planetary wards drew on the local magical substrate to sustain themselves. It was the hardest thing he'd tried to understand — designed not for a fifteen-year-old human brain but for the Builders, for minds that had operated magic as a medium of existence — as fundamental and unconscious as breathing. The specific problem he'd been staring at for three days was a coupling description that his cognition kept sliding off of, like trying to hold the shape of a four-dimensional object in a mind built for three dimensions. He could get two of the variables and the third would dissolve. He could hold the third and lose the relationship between the first two.

But tonight his mind kept returning to the corridor.

He could end the war.

The thought had the quality of standing in a kitchen and realizing you could burn down the house — the capability just there, mundane, available, and the gap between having it and using it a gap of decision, not of power.

He could make lethal magic impossible.

He sat with this. The notation on the manuscript page shifted in front of him, and he let it shift without tracking it, because the ward-coupling problem could wait for the time it took to think this through properly.

The mechanics were not particularly complex. The root agreement that enabled the Unforgivable Curses was a substrate structure he could now perceive clearly, the way he perceived the degrading mortar on Barnabas's portrait or the grammatical wobble in a fifth-year's Stunning Spell. He would need to identify the full extent of the agreement — all its anchoring points, all the downstream grammars that depended on it — and then edit it. Not remove it entirely, because that would be crude and might destabilize dependent systems he couldn't predict. But he could narrow its scope. Adjust its terms. The agreement that allowed directed magic to affect another person's body was necessary — you needed it for healing charms, for Stunning Spells in a duel, for a dozen legitimate interactions. But the specific sub-grammar that enabled lethal and torturous override could be made invalid without touching the rest.

It would take time. He estimated — and the estimate was rough, because he'd never attempted anything at this scale — perhaps three weeks of focused substrate work. Maybe four. The nosebleeds would be bad. The headaches would be worse. But the work was within his capability. He could see the architecture. He could see the path.

Three weeks, and every Killing Curse in the world would fail. Every Cruciatus would produce nothing. Every Imperius would slide off. Voldemort's entire war machine would lose its most terrifying weapons overnight, and neither Voldemort nor any of his Death Eaters would understand why, because the change would be invisible to anyone who couldn't perceive the substrate. They would cast their curses and nothing would happen, and they would try again and nothing would happen, and the confusion would give the other side — the Order, the DA, the Ministry holdouts, whoever was left — time to organize and fight on terms that didn't include the threat of instant death.

He could save hundreds of lives. Probably more.

Gavin wiped blood from his nostril — another nosebleed, the small, slow kind that came from sustained substrate perception even without active editing — and looked at the manuscript pages spread across his knees.

The ward-coupling problem looked back at him.

Three variables, interlocking. The planetary wards drawing on the local magical substrate to sustain themselves, and the draw thinning because the coupling mechanism was degrading, and the degradation accelerating because the wards had been designed for an Earth whose magical substrate was denser than the current one. The Builders had set the wards before they left. In the millennia since, the substrate had thinned — whether from natural decay or from the cumulative demands of generations of wizarding magic or from some process the manuscript described in notation Gavin couldn't yet parse — and the wards were starving. They still held, but the way a campfire holds in light rain: less each hour, the heat dropping, the flames lower, the wood hissing, the core shrinking.

He'd felt the wards from the Astronomy Tower in March. The spatial pressure of their thinning — not visible, but sensible, like a ceiling settling, all creak and give overhead. They were thinner now than they had been then. They would be thinner in May. How much time he had before they reached a critical threshold, he didn't know — the manuscript's notation on failure modes was in a section he hadn't fully decoded yet.

He might have years. He might have months. He might have decades. He didn't know, and the not-knowing was its own pressure, constant and low and impossible to set down.

What he did know was this: if the wards failed, the thing beyond them would not attack. It would not invade. It would not arrive in any sense that implied intention or direction. It would simply be there — a rising water table, indifferent and total — and its presence would be incompatible with magical life. Not through hostility. Through a condition that did not support what was currently living here, the same way a waterlogged field didn't support a dry basement.

Everyone would die. Not just wizards. Everything magical — every charm, every enchantment, every ward, every magical creature, every magical plant, the entire substrate infrastructure that the wizarding world didn't know it existed within and depended upon. The Muggles would probably survive — the condition beyond the wards was incompatible with magical life specifically, not with life broadly — but everything that made the wizarding world possible would cease to function, and the cascading failures would be catastrophic in ways Gavin could model roughly but not predict.

He sat on the cold stone floor of the sub-collection and held these two problems in his mind at the same time.

Three weeks to end the war. Three weeks of ward study lost.

The wards were failing on a timescale he could perceive but couldn't measure precisely. What he could feel — every time he let his perception open to that scale — was that the margin was not generous. The thinning was not leisurely. He didn't have the luxury of solving both problems sequentially, first the war and then the wards, because the wards might not wait.

And there was the other thing: the war was ending. He could feel that too, though not through the substrate — through the castle's ordinary social atmosphere, the rumours that filtered down even to a boy who had stopped participating in the school's communal life. Harry Potter. Something was building. The DA was more active than it had been all year. The Carrows were losing control, reacting with escalating cruelty because escalation was the only tool they had. The regime was cracking.

If the war would end on its own — and it might, it might not, but it was moving toward something — then spending three weeks rewriting the grammar of lethal magic would be spending three weeks on a problem that was already being addressed. Not by him. By other people, braver people, people who had chosen the fight he hadn't chosen.

But the wards were not being addressed by anyone. No one else could perceive them. No one else knew they existed. If Gavin didn't work on them, no one did, and the thinning continued, and the campfire in the rain burned lower, and eventually the fire went out and the cold came in.

The mathematics was simple, and the simplicity was the worst part of it.

The war killed people. Hundreds, maybe. The ward failure would end everything magical on Earth. The scale difference was not a matter of degree — it was a difference in category, the distance between a house fire and the sun going out. Against the ward failure, Voldemort's war was a rounding error. A local disturbance inside a system-level collapse. An argument about paint colours while the foundation cracked.

Gavin sat with this and waited for the decision to feel like a decision.

It didn't.

It felt like arithmetic. Like being asked whether to repair a leaking pipe or a cracking dam and understanding that the question answered itself. The pipe was leaking and people were getting wet and that was real and bad. But the dam held back an ocean, and if it cracked further, the pipe would be irrelevant because there would be no building for the pipe to leak in.

He thought about the boy in the corridor. The ink-stained fingers twitching against the flagstones. The sound he'd made that wasn't a scream. The book pressed to his chest afterward, the one about Shield Charms, and the way he'd walked away on legs that didn't trust themselves yet. That boy would be Cruciated again. Tomorrow, next week, whenever Carrow felt like it. Other students would be Cruciated. Some would be killed, in the battle that was building — Gavin could feel the approaching conflict as a perturbation in the substrate, a pressure-change like weather building, the distortion of large-scale magical forces gathering toward a single point. People he'd sat next to in the Great Hall would die. People who'd passed him in corridors for five years without either of them learning the other's name.

He was going to let it happen, and it wasn't because he didn't care. He sat with the boy's face and the sound the boy had made and the ink on the boy's fingers, and he cared about it, and the caring didn't change the arithmetic.

He picked up the manuscript page he'd been working on. The ward-coupling problem. Three variables, interlocking. He'd been losing the third when he held the first two, for three days now, and the frustration of it had become its own weather, a constant overcast in his working hours. He looked at the notation and let it shift and this time — because his mind was running differently tonight, because the corridor had clarified something about what he could and could not afford to spend his time on — he felt the third variable hold.

Not clearly. Not fully. But he could feel its shape the way you could feel the shape of a word you'd forgotten — it was there, just past where he could close his hand, and if he kept working he might reach it.

He worked.

The sub-collection was silent except for the faint mineral settling of old stone, the sound of a building so deep in the castle that the ordinary noises of school life — footsteps, laughter, doors — didn't reach it. His nose bled intermittently. He wiped it on his sleeve, which was already stained brown in overlapping rings from earlier nosebleeds, a history of evenings spent doing exactly this: sitting on cold stone, reading notation that moved, trying to hold more than his mind was built to hold. He kept reading. The coupling mechanism. The local draw. The wards overhead, invisible to anyone without substrate perception, thinning — slowly, structurally, with the kind of patience that made it easy to pretend it wasn't happening.

Somewhere in the castle above him — in a corridor or a common room or the Great Hall — someone cast a spell. He felt it as a flutter in the substrate, a tiny sentence spoken in the grammar he could now read without effort. A homework charm, probably. Or a hex thrown in anger. Or a curse thrown in cruelty. He couldn't tell from the substrate alone, because the substrate didn't distinguish. It held all sentences with the same impersonal fidelity, the valid and the vicious, the Levitation Charm and the Killing Curse.

He could change that. He was choosing not to.

Outside the castle, past the corridors and the grounds and the spring warmth he couldn't feel from down here, the world was green and growing. The Forbidden Forest stirred with things waking after winter. Flowers pushed through cracks between the courtyard flagstones, ordinary and persistent. In the greenhouses, the plants that Professor Sprout tended were doing what plants did in April. In the lake, the giant squid was doing whatever the giant squid did. The world was performing its small, seasonal renewal, as it did every year, oblivious to everything except the sun and the soil and the rain.

And above all of it, visible only to Gavin if he opened his perception to that scale, the wards stretched in vast interlocking patterns across the sky — beautiful, ancient, failing. Thinner than they had been last month. The thing beyond them leaned against the thinning barrier with the patience of geology, the patience of weather, the patience of a condition that didn't know it was waiting because it didn't know anything at all.

Gavin turned a page. The notation shifted, showing the coupling mechanism from a new angle, and something in the relationship between the first two variables caught at the edge of the third, and he leaned forward, his nose bleeding onto the stone floor, and tried to understand how a long-departed civilization had solved a problem he was only beginning to know how to ask.

Students slept in dormitories behind stone walls, inside a castle inside a country inside a world inside a thinning shell of ancient agreements, and none of them knew how thin the shell had become, or that the only person working on it was a fifteen-year-old Hufflepuff with a nosebleed and bad handwriting, sitting on a cold floor, choosing the world over the war.
