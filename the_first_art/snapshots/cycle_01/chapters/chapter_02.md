# Chapter 2

The catalogue didn't have it.

Gavin had been through every drawer that made sense and a few that didn't. The library's filing system went by topic rather than author, which meant he had to guess what heading someone, decades or centuries ago, had decided fit a manuscript about pre-wand magical substrates. He tried "Substrate." He tried "Pre-Wand." He tried "Magical Theory (Historical)" and "Foundational Studies" and "Notation Systems" and, feeling slightly desperate, "Old." Nothing. The manuscript that Bagshot cited in a footnote on page 742 of her *History* was not listed. There was no misfile, no cross-reference. It simply wasn't in the system.

He leaned against the cabinet and looked at the footnote again. It was still there, small and parenthetical: a reference to a manuscript in the Hogwarts collection concerning what Bagshot called, with apparent distaste, a "speculative account of a pre-wand magical substrate." She'd dismissed it in the same breath she cited it. The kind of footnote you could read fifty times and never notice if you weren't in the specific frame of mind that made it snag.

The library was quiet. It was always quiet this year, but the quality had changed — less the comfortable hush of students working and more the silence of people who were trying not to be noticed. Two fourth-year Ravenclaws sat at a corner table, their heads close together over a single textbook, and a sixth-year Gavin didn't recognize was copying something from a reference volume near the window. The window showed darkening grounds. October had settled in and the afternoons were shortening fast, the sky going grey and staying there.

Madam Pince was at her desk.

She looked worse than Gavin remembered from last year. Thinner in the face, and she'd developed a habit of arranging the quills on her desk by length — lining them up, adjusting one, lining them up again — that had the rhythm of something she didn't know she was doing. A pair of dragonhide reading gloves sat on the desk beside her, the fingertips worn smooth from decades of handling old parchment. She'd been wearing them earlier; now they sat neatly aligned with the quills, another object she was keeping in order.

He walked over. She looked up before he'd gotten within ten feet, which was normal for Pince. What wasn't normal was the way her eyes went immediately to his hands, checking what he was carrying.

"Mr. Tench."

"Hi. Um, Madam Pince. I'm looking for a manuscript that's supposed to be in the collection, but I can't find it in the catalogue."

She didn't respond immediately. She picked up one of the quills and put it back down in exactly the same position. "The catalogue is comprehensive."

"Yeah, I know, but this one — it's cited in *A History of Magic*. Bagshot references it. A manuscript about pre-wand magical substrates. She says it's in the Hogwarts collection."

Something happened in Pince's face. A slight tightening around her mouth, as if she'd bitten down on something she didn't want. She reached for the reading gloves and began folding them, precisely, along creases that were already well established.

"Bagshot's footnotes aren't always reliable," she said.

"Right, but she specifically says it's here. 'In the Hogwarts collection.' So either she's wrong about it being here, or —"

"She is wrong." Pince folded the gloves a second time, which wasn't necessary, and set them down. "I have managed this collection for twenty-seven years, Mr. Tench. If a manuscript existed under any heading related to pre-wand magical substrates, I would be aware of it. Bagshot cited a great many sources she never verified personally. That footnote is an error."

She said it the way you'd say something you'd decided was true before the conversation started.

"OK," Gavin said. "What about the Restricted Section? Could it be —"

"The Restricted Section is restricted. You'd need a signed permission form from a member of staff, which I strongly doubt anyone will provide this term." Her voice dropped half a register, and she glanced toward the library entrance and then back. "In any event, I've reviewed every item in that section within the past three years and there's nothing matching your description."

There it was. The speed of the answer. She'd gone from "it doesn't exist" to "you can't access it" so fast that the two statements overlapped, and neither one matched the look on her face, which was the look of someone who wanted him to leave.

Gavin stood there for a moment. He had the textbook under his arm and his bag was heavy on his shoulder and the fourth-years were glancing over and Pince was staring at him with the particular intensity of a woman who had built her life around the orderly management of books and was now watching everything she managed come under the authority of people who would burn them.

"OK," he said. "Thanks."

He walked back to the table where he'd left his things. He packed his bag slowly. He thought about what had just happened.

If Bagshot were simply wrong — if the manuscript didn't exist — Pince would have said so with the satisfied certainty of a cataloguer correcting a sloppy author. She wouldn't have raced to the Restricted Section. She wouldn't have preemptively blocked access. She wouldn't have looked at the door.

The manuscript was real. It was somewhere. And Pince either didn't know where it was, which seemed unlikely for a woman who'd managed the collection for twenty-seven years, or she did know and wasn't saying.

He walked out of the library and took the stairs down toward the common room, thinking about the Restricted Section.

***

He went at half past midnight.

The three days between the conversation with Pince and the night he actually tried it were spent considering the risk, which was considerable. Sneaking into the Restricted Section had always been a punishable offense, but "punishable" had a different meaning this year. Last year it would have meant lost house points and a detention scrubbing trophies. This year it meant the Carrows.

He thought about it anyway. The footnote kept pulling at him. He'd catch himself in Potions or Charms thinking about what "pre-wand magical substrate" might mean, and the thought had a particular quality — not urgent, not obsessive, just there, the way a loose tooth was there. You couldn't stop checking it.

On Thursday night he lay in his dormitory listening to his roommates breathe and decided to go. The decision didn't feel brave. It felt like the next thing.

The corridors were dark. Gavin didn't risk a Lumos — too visible from too far away. He navigated by touch and memory, one hand trailing the wall, counting the turns. The castle at night had its own sounds: the settle of old stone, the drip of water somewhere below, the occasional groan of a staircase repositioning itself floors above him. His shoes were too loud on the flagstones. He took them off and carried them and his socks were immediately cold and wet from the stone.

The library door was locked. He tried Alohomora, which worked, which surprised him — he'd expected something more serious. The door swung open into the dark and the smell of the library hit him, the particular combination of old paper and binding glue and dust that had been settling in the same place for centuries.

The Restricted Section was at the back, behind a rope. In daylight the rope looked like a boundary. At night, in the dark, it was just a rope. He stepped over it.

His wand-hand wanted to light up. He let it, barely — the faintest Lumos he could manage, just enough to read the spines. The books in the Restricted Section didn't like being looked at. Some of them whispered. One, bound in what looked like actual skin, vibrated faintly against its shelf as he passed. He ignored them and scanned the titles, looking for anything about substrates, pre-wand magic, anything that matched Bagshot's description.

He was in the third row, crouching to read the lower shelves, when he heard the shuffle.

It wasn't footsteps. It was a shuffle — the particular scraping gait of someone who walked with one foot dragging, accompanied by a thin, reedy sound that might have been breathing or might have been a cat.

Filch.

Gavin's stomach dropped. He killed the Lumos and stood still. The dark closed in and the whispering books seemed louder. He heard the lantern before he saw it — the creak of the handle, the faint hiss of the flame. Then the light came around the end of the row, uneven and jumping, thrown through a cracked glass that cut the beam into crooked segments.

Argus Filch stepped into the Restricted Section holding his lantern high, and Mrs. Norris wound between his ankles, and he was smiling. It was the smile of a man having a good night. Under the old regime, catching a student out of bed had been the highlight of Filch's week. Under this one, it was something better, because now there were consequences he didn't have to administer himself.

"Well, well," Filch said. His voice had a wheeze behind it. His cardigan was buttoned wrong — one button off from the bottom, so the whole thing sat skewed across his chest — and a cold sore sat at the corner of his mouth, raw and uncovered. He didn't seem to know it was there, or he'd stopped caring. "Out of bed, in the Restricted Section, no permission slip. That's a nice evening you're having."

"I was just —"

"Save it. You're not the first and you won't be the last. On your feet. Shoes on." He noticed the shoes in Gavin's hand and his smile widened. "Trying to be quiet about it. That's an extra helping, that is."

Gavin put his shoes on. His hands were shaking and he fumbled with the laces and Filch watched him fumble with the patience of a man who didn't mind waiting.

Mrs. Norris sat on the floor and cleaned her paw. She was old, Gavin noticed. Her eyes were the same lamp-yellow they'd always been, but the fur around her muzzle had gone white and there was a faint rattle in her breathing.

Filch marched him out of the library and down two corridors and up a flight of stairs and through a door Gavin had never been through before, into a room that had been a storage closet and was now bare. The floor was stone, the walls were stone, and the only furniture was a desk and a chair, both shoved against the far wall. The room was cold — the kind of cold that gets into your knees the moment they touch the ground.

Amycus Carrow was sitting on the desk.

He wasn't doing anything. He was just sitting there, legs dangling, as if he'd been waiting, and maybe he had. The Carrows rotated detention shifts, and Filch had known where to bring him, which meant this was organized. Carrow wore his robes with the sleeves pushed up to the elbows, his forearms thick and pale and hairy, and he was chewing something — gum or a toothpick or the inside of his cheek — with a slow, grinding motion that made his jaw look even more lopsided than it was.

"Restricted Section," Filch said, with the pleased air of a man making a delivery.

"Right." Carrow looked at Gavin. He didn't look angry. He looked the way you'd look at a task you didn't mind doing. "What's your name?"

"Tench. Gavin Tench."

"House?"

"Hufflepuff."

"Year?"

"Fifth."

Carrow nodded slowly, still chewing. "Hufflepuff. Fifth year. In the Restricted Section at —" He looked at Filch.

"Quarter to one," Filch said.

"Quarter to one in the morning without a permission slip." Carrow slid off the desk. He was shorter than Gavin expected, shorter standing than he'd looked sitting, squat and physically graceless in a way that made the authority he carried seem borrowed. His face had a pushed-in quality, like a thumb had pressed between his eyebrows when the clay was still wet. "What were you looking for, Tench?"

"A book. For a — for homework."

"Homework." Carrow said the word like he was tasting it. "At quarter to one. In the Restricted Section. What class?"

"Ancient Runes."

"Babbling's class?" Something crossed Carrow's face — not recognition exactly, but the flicker of a man sorting through what he knew about the staff and what he might do with it. The flicker passed. "Kneel."

Gavin didn't understand at first. He stood there.

"On the floor, Tench. Kneel down."

He knelt. The stone hit his knees and the cold went straight through his trousers and into the bone, and the cold was the first thing, the thing he would remember later when the rest got blurred. The stone was uneven. His right knee had landed on a ridge in the flagstone and it dug in like a blunt edge.

"Arms up. Over your head."

He raised his arms.

"You'll hold them there," Carrow said. He walked around behind Gavin. His shoes were heavy on the stone, unhurried. Filch was still in the doorway. Mrs. Norris had gone. "The thing about detentions is that they're supposed to teach you something. That's the whole point. So what are we teaching tonight? We're teaching Tench that rules exist. That the Restricted Section is restricted for a reason. And that when you break a rule, there's a cost." He came back around and sat on the desk again. Settled in. "Arms up, Tench. I can see them dropping."

They weren't dropping. Not yet. His arms were straight above his head and the position was uncomfortable but holdable. For now.

After three minutes, his shoulders began to burn. After five, the burn spread into his upper arms and across his back, and his hands started to tingle. The cold of the stone floor had moved up from his knees into his thighs, a dull, deep ache that was getting worse in a way that had no ceiling to it.

After ten minutes, his arms began to shake.

"Dropping," Carrow said.

"I'm not —"

The stinging hex caught him across the shoulders.

It wasn't like pain from impact — there was no weight behind it, no sense of being struck by something physical. It was more like someone had dragged a wire across his skin and then set the wire on fire. The line of it ran from his left shoulder blade to his right, and his body tried to arch away from it, but there was nothing to arch away from because the hex was already done. It just burned where it had been.

Gavin gasped. The sound came out smaller than he expected. His arms had dropped to his sides, both of them, and his hands were clenched into fists.

"Arms up."

He raised them. The hex-burn across his shoulders screamed at the movement. He held the position. His arms shook.

Carrow sat on the desk and watched. He chewed. He hummed, toneless and low, the kind of sound you'd make while waiting for a kettle to boil.

Gavin's arms dropped at sixteen minutes. He didn't decide to let them drop. His muscles gave out — the deltoids first, then the whole structure, and his arms came down like they'd been cut loose. The stinging hex hit his upper arms this time, both of them, a crossing pattern, and the pain layered on what was already there and his vision went white at the edges for a second and then came back. He heard himself make a sound.

"Arms up, Tench."

He raised them. They went up slower this time and stopped lower, his elbows bending where they hadn't bent before. The ridge in the flagstone was still digging into his right knee and he couldn't shift off it without Carrow seeing.

The next drop came at twenty-two minutes. Then nineteen. Then fourteen. Then ten. Each time, the hex. Each time, the order to raise them again. Carrow never raised his voice. He never showed frustration or impatience. He sat on the desk and delivered the hexes with the mechanical regularity of a man performing a task that required no emotional investment, and between the hexes he talked — about the importance of rules, about what happened to students who thought they were special, about blood purity and the natural order — the phrases sounding memorized and slightly wrong in his mouth, like lyrics he'd learned phonetically. He didn't care about the content. He was filling time.

After the first hour, the intervals between drops shortened to five or six minutes. Gavin's arms would go up and begin shaking immediately and the shaking would build until his muscles failed and the arms came down and the hex came and the arms went up again. The cycle was designed to be unwinnable. That was the point. The task existed to create failure, and the failure existed to justify punishment, and the punishment existed because Carrow had the authority to make it exist and the specific, small-souled pleasure of exercising that authority on a fifteen-year-old who'd been looking for a book.

The hex-burns had stopped being individual events. They overlapped now, layered across Gavin's shoulders and arms and upper back in a lattice of hot lines that his shirt pressed against. The pain had a particular quality: it kept burning at the same intensity instead of fading the way a cut or a bruise would. His knees were worse. They'd gone from cold to numb to a deep, grinding ache, and the right one was definitely bleeding where the ridge in the stone had worked through the fabric of his trousers.

At some point Filch left. Gavin heard the shuffle of his shoes in the corridor and then it was just him and Carrow, and Carrow's humming was the only sound in the room besides Gavin's breathing, which had gone ragged.

"How long?" Filch had asked, earlier, from the doorway. The question was practical, not sympathetic. He wanted to know if he could leave.

"Hour should do it," Carrow had said. "Maybe two. We'll see how he does."

It was closer to three.

"You can go," Carrow said eventually.

Gavin tried to lower his arms and couldn't. They were locked above his head in a cramp that felt like the muscles had solidified. He brought them down in stages, his shoulders making sounds he'd never heard from his own body, and then he tried to stand and couldn't do that either. His knees wouldn't unbend. He had to lean to one side and use the wall and pull himself up while his legs did something that wasn't quite supporting him, and Carrow watched it all without helping or commenting, still chewing, still sitting on the desk with his legs dangling, and the watching was part of it — the lesson extended to include the indignity of a boy unable to stand up on his own.

He stood. His legs felt like they belonged to someone larger and less coordinated. The burns across his back and shoulders and arms throbbed in unison, a single pulsing heat.

"Off you go, Tench. Stay out of the Restricted Section."

Gavin left. He didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that wouldn't be another opportunity for Carrow to teach him something.

***

The corridors were empty and cold and Gavin walked through them badly.

His knees had been locked in position for three hours and each step sent a spike up through his thighs. The hex-burns were settling into something more like sunburn — a constant, radiating heat across his back and arms that the cold air of the corridor sharpened rather than soothed. His palms had been clenched for so long that the fingernail marks were bleeding, half-moons pressed into the meat of each hand. His robes had a wet patch on the right knee where the stone had worked through.

He needed to get back to the Hufflepuff common room. He was on the third floor. The route was familiar — down two flights to the ground floor, left past the Great Hall, down the last stairs to the kitchen corridor, and the barrel entrance at the end.

He went down the first flight without problems. The second flight moved while he was on it. The staircases at Hogwarts shifted constantly, and normally you just waited for them to reconnect or took a different route, but Gavin's legs were unreliable and the shift caught him off balance and he grabbed the railing and held on and the staircase deposited him on a landing he didn't recognize.

He should have gone back up and waited for the staircase to cycle around. He didn't. He was tired and his knees hurt and he wanted to be in bed, so he went forward, through a door that was standing open, into a corridor that was narrower than it should have been at this level of the castle. The stone was different here. Darker. Rougher-cut. The torches that normally lined the Hogwarts corridors were gone, and the light came from somewhere he couldn't identify — a faint luminescence from the walls themselves, not warm like firelight but cold, the color of old ice.

He stopped.

The air was different. Drier, cooler, with a mineral quality that reminded him of the time his parents had taken him to a cave system in Devon when he was eight. Limestone and stillness and the particular pressure of being underground even when you weren't underground.

He was on the seventh floor. He was certain of it because the staircase had gone up instead of down and the landing had been marked with a VII carved into the archway, barely visible under centuries of soot. The seventh floor was where the Room of Requirement was, though this was nowhere near the tapestry of Barnabas the Barmy. This was somewhere else entirely, a part of the castle he hadn't been in before and hadn't known about.

The corridor turned, and then turned again, and opened into a small room.

It was barely a room — more of an alcove that someone had given a ceiling. The walls were the same dark, rough stone, and the sourceless light was the same, and there were shelves cut directly into the rock. Not attached, not built — carved out, as though the room had been made around them. Most of the shelves were empty. A few held objects Gavin couldn't make sense of in the dim light, things that were the wrong size or shape for books.

Against the far wall was a cabinet. Small, wooden, very old. The wood was almost black with age and the brass fittings had gone green. It had three drawers, narrower and deeper than the ones in the library catalogue, and the labels on the drawers were in a hand Gavin didn't recognize — written in ink that had faded to a brown so pale it was barely legible.

He opened the first drawer.

The cards inside were not paper. They were something thinner, smoother, slightly stiff — the material of very old documents, though he couldn't name it. Each card was smaller than the library's catalogue cards, about the size of a playing card, and the writing was in the same faded brown ink.

He could read it, though. It was English — old, but English. Each card listed a title, a description, and a location expressed in terms that meant nothing to him: directions that referenced rooms by qualities rather than numbers. *Third left past the cold room. Below the room where the air hums. Past the narrow place, up, and right at the smell of water.*

He went through the cards one by one. His hands left faint red smears on them because his palms were still bleeding where the nails had dug in, and on the fourteenth card he found it.

The title was not in English. It was in a notation he didn't recognize — a set of marks that didn't map to any alphabet he knew. But below the title, in the same faded brown handwriting, someone had written a description: *A teaching instrument concerning the substrate upon which all secondary magical structures depend. Not to be removed.*

Gavin read it twice. His knees throbbed. His back burned. The light from the walls was too dim and he had to hold the card close to his face to make out the location written below the description: *Past the narrow place, up, through the room of empty shelves, left where the stone changes color.*

He put the card back in the drawer and closed it.

He stood there in the small room with its carved shelves and its ancient cabinet and its cold, dry air, and the pain in his knees and back felt very far away — not because it had lessened but because something else was closer. He'd been looking for this in the wrong place. The library catalogue didn't have the manuscript because the manuscript wasn't in the library. It was here, or somewhere connected to here — in a part of the castle that operated by different rules, in rooms that didn't appear on any map he'd seen, catalogued in a system that predated the Hogwarts library by what might have been centuries.

He looked at the shelves again. The objects on them were still indistinct — dark shapes he couldn't resolve. He didn't touch them. The card had said *Not to be removed*, and the instruction was not addressed to him specifically, but it had the quality of a rule that was still in force.

He needed to leave. His knees were bad and his back was bad and it was sometime between three and four in the morning and he had Potions at nine. He left the room and went back down the corridor, and the corridor was cooperative this time — it led to a staircase that went down, and the staircase connected where he needed it to, and within twenty minutes he was at the barrel entrance to the Hufflepuff common room, tapping the rhythm, and the barrel opened, and the common room was dark and warm and smelled of earth and whatever the kitchens were baking for morning, and he went to his dormitory and got into bed without undressing and lay on his stomach because his back wouldn't tolerate the mattress any other way.

His shoulders and arms pulsed. His knees felt like they'd been worked over with hammers. His palms stuck to the sheet where the half-moon cuts had dried tacky.

He thought about the card. The notation he couldn't read. The description in old brown ink: *a teaching instrument concerning the substrate upon which all secondary magical structures depend.* The location directions that assumed you could identify a room by the temperature of its air or the sound a wall made.

He had found it. Not the manuscript itself, but where it was. And he'd found it not because he was clever or skilled or brave — he'd found it because Carrow had beaten him for three hours and the staircase had moved and he'd been too tired and hurt to go back the right way.

His right knee was definitely bleeding. He could feel it, a warm stickiness inside his trouser leg that was different from the rest of the pain, more ordinary, more familiar. Like a skinned knee from falling off a bicycle, except he hadn't fallen off anything. He'd been kneeling.

He closed his eyes. The room was dark and his roommates were breathing and tomorrow he had Potions and his knees were ruined and his back was a map of hex-burns and somewhere in this castle, in rooms that ran by rules older than the library, there was a manuscript that Bathilda Bagshot had dismissed in a footnote and Madam Pince had lied about and nobody was looking for except him.

He fell asleep with his shoes on.
