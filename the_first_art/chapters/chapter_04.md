# Chapter 4

Once he'd decided to risk showing someone one page, he spent three days choosing which page to bring.

The manuscript had fifty-seven sheets — he had counted them twice, turning each one by the edges with his thumbs clear of the notation because even now he still half expected the material to react if he handled it like an ordinary page. Most of the sheets were dense with notation that moved when he looked at it, relationships between symbols shifting like tidal patterns he could not predict. But a few pages were sparser. One near the middle had what looked like paired entries: a symbol cluster on the left, a simpler arrangement on the right. If any page was a key, this was it.

He folded it carefully — the material was not paper and did not crease easily, taking the fold with a faint resistance, like bending something that was used to being flat — and slid it into his Ancient Runes textbook between the chapter on Elder Futhark variations and the one he had not read yet.

If he was going to risk it with anyone, it had to be Babbling.

Ancient Runes was the last class on Wednesdays. The classroom was on the third floor, a small room that still smelled like a school — ink and old binding glue and the specific warmth of reference books that had been absorbing body heat from generations of students leaning over them. Professor Babbling had not changed the room this year. The shelves were still lined with comparative notation charts and stone fragment replicas and the framed rubbing of the Rök runestone that Babbling touched sometimes when she passed it, an absent, habitual gesture, like checking a doorknob.

The class was smaller this year. Fourteen students instead of twenty-two. Three had not returned to Hogwarts. Five had dropped the elective because OWL preparation was difficult to prioritize when the school was what it was. Gavin had almost dropped it himself, but Ancient Runes was one of the subjects where his marks were merely poor rather than embarrassing, and he liked the work. Babbling was a good teacher. She treated her students as if they were capable of adult attention, which was either flattering or exhausting depending on the week.

Today she was working through consonantal mutation patterns in late Anglo-Saxon runic inscriptions. Gavin took notes. The notation on the folded page in his textbook lay against the chapter heading like a foreign body the book was trying to reject.

He waited until the classroom emptied. This required less strategy than he had expected — the sixth-years left in a group, still arguing about a translation exercise, and the two other fifth-years were out the door before Babbling had finished writing the homework on the board. Gavin packed his bag slowly.

"Mr. Tench." Babbling was at her desk, sorting through the quiz papers from the previous week. Her reading glasses were on their chain, resting against her sternum. "Is there a question about the assignment?"

"No, I — it's not about that." He was already regretting this. She would think he was wasting her time. She would think it was a prank. She would look at the page and identify some known system that Gavin should have recognized from the Year Four curriculum, and then he would have to stand there being embarrassed while she explained it.

He pulled the textbook from his bag and removed the folded page.

"I found something," he said. "Some notation I can't identify. I was wondering if you could — I don't know. If it's anything."

He unfolded the page and set it on the edge of her desk, careful not to put it on top of her papers. The notation shifted as he smoothed it flat, the left-column symbols rearranging their internal relationships in a slow drift that made the page look almost alive.

Babbling looked at it.

Then she stopped looking at the quiz papers, and the pen in her hand went still, and she picked up the page by one corner and brought it closer.

She put on her reading glasses.

For perhaps ten seconds she said nothing. Gavin watched her eyes move across the notation, tracking the symbol pairs, going back to the top, starting again. Her lips parted slightly and then closed. She set the page down on a clear space on the desk and leaned back in her chair. She took one slow, visible breath before she spoke, and when the question came it was almost too even.

"Where did you find this?"

"In the library. Sort of. In an old —" He stopped. The sub-collection was not something he wanted to explain. "An old reference collection. It was uncatalogued."

"I see." Babbling looked at the page again. She touched the edge of it with one ink-stained finger, testing the material. "This is not parchment."

"No."

"And this notation — it is moving. Not in the photographic sense. The relationships are active."

"Yeah. I've been watching it for — for a while. It shifts, but it's not random. There are patterns."

Babbling took off her reading glasses and cleaned them, which Gavin recognized as the thing she did when she needed a moment. She threaded them back on and looked at the page a third time.

"If I had to place it anywhere," she said, "I would put it before the families we teach as distinct had separated into recognizable forms. A few of the joins remind me, very faintly, of early western Mediterranean stones, but the resemblance stops at shape. The organizing rule is doing something else entirely, and I do not know any attested system that behaves like this." She stopped with her mouth still slightly open, then pressed two fingertips to the desk. "Those paired entries look instructional to me. One form is elaborated, the other reduced. That is how primers behave."

"A teaching text," Gavin said.

"Possibly. I would want to examine more of it before drawing conclusions. Considerably more." She paused. Her fingers were very still on the desk. "How much more is there?"

"Fifty-six other pages."

Something tightened in her face — not a whole expression, just a brief hard line at the corners of her mouth — and then it was gone. She nodded once, briskly.

"I am not going to ask you where the full manuscript is stored, Mr. Tench. And I am not going to ask what you intend to do with it. I think you understand why."

He did. The Carrows. Unauthorized research. A professor showing interest in an uncatalogued manuscript that dealt with unknown magic was not something that could be explained away this year.

"But I can give you tools." She stood, crossed to the shelf behind her desk, and ran her finger along the spines until she found what she was looking for: a thin book, green cover, badly foxed. She pulled it out and held it for a moment before offering it. "Wexworth's comparative notation study. It is not widely read — Wexworth's conclusions were unfashionable in his time and the field has not revisited them. But his method for approaching unknown systems through structural invariants is, I believe, sound."

"Structural invariants?"

"Elements that remain constant regardless of content. In any notation system designed to encode meaning — as opposed to decorative or purely symbolic use — there are structural features that persist across different messages. Positional relationships. Frequency distributions. Boundary markers between conceptual units." She was speaking faster now, the academic in her overriding the caution. "You will not be able to read your manuscript by matching its symbols to known runes — I can tell you that now. Whatever this system is, it does not derive from any tradition I have studied, and I have studied all of them."

Gavin listened and held the information carefully, aware that she was giving him something she would not give again.

"But Wexworth's method does not require derivation. It identifies the architecture of an unknown system — the grammar, if you like, before the vocabulary. You begin with what repeats, what occupies fixed positions, what varies. You establish the structural skeleton and then fill in meaning by isolating units that behave like definitions, like your paired entries." She gestured at the page on her desk. "It is slow work. It is tedious. If there are fifty-seven pages, you are looking at —" She stopped herself again, and the caution came back into her face like a curtain drawn. "You are looking at a significant amount of time."

"I'm not in a hurry," Gavin said, which was not true, but he did not have a way to explain the urgency he felt about a manuscript he could not read.

Babbling held out the Wexworth. "When you have finished with it, put it back on my desk yourself. Not in the return pile. The return pile passes through too many hands."

He took it. "Thank you, Professor."

She nodded. Then she began straightening the quiz papers again, which was a dismissal. But as Gavin reached the door, she said: "Mr. Tench."

He turned.

"The notation on that page is genuinely unprecedented. In twenty-three years of teaching Ancient Runes, I have not seen anything like it." She looked at him over her reading glasses. "I hope you are being careful."

He was not being careful. He was being fifteen and obsessive, which was as far from careful as he knew how to get. But he said, "I am," and she accepted it because she needed to, and he left with the folded page in one pocket and the Wexworth in the other, and the door closed behind him on the last expert who might have helped him, standing at her desk with quiz papers she was no longer reading.

The Wexworth was ninety-two pages of dense, dry scholarship that assumed its reader had a working knowledge of at least three runic systems and a tolerance for long footnotes. Gavin had a shaky grasp of Elder Futhark and no tolerance for footnotes. He read it anyway, twice, in the common room over three nights while the fire burned low and the kitchen smells shifted from dinner to bread dough to the cold-grease emptiness of the very late hours.

The method was ugly. Wexworth called it "a set of procedures for establishing minimum structural correspondence between an unknown notation system and the concept of notation itself." In plain language: before you tried to read an unknown script, you watched what the marks did on the surface. You watched which shapes kept turning up in the same slots, which cuts kept opening between units, and which clusters held together even while everything around them drifted. Wexworth's procedures for testing those habits were laborious and mechanical and, Gavin suspected, exactly what he needed.

He started with the page he had shown Babbling — the paired entries, the possible key. He catalogued every distinct symbol. Forty-three, which was within the range of a syllabary or an expanded alphabet but too many for a simple alphabetic system and too few for a logographic one. He tracked their positions. He counted frequencies. He noted which symbols appeared in fixed positions and which moved freely.

It was brutal, meticulous work. He did it in the margins of his Potions notes, on scraps of parchment, on the back of a Transfiguration exercise he had already failed. He worked in the common room mostly, because the common room was warm and the sub-collection was not, and because sitting in a room with other people — even if he was not talking to them — kept him tethered to something. He sat in the same armchair every night, the one nearest the fire with the cushion that listed left, and the manuscript lay open on his lap hidden inside his History of Magic textbook.

His Potions essay came back with a D. His Charms practical went badly enough that Professor Flitwick asked him to stay after class, looked at him for a long moment, and said he knew this was a difficult year for everyone. Gavin said thank you and left and then forgot, halfway through dinner, that he still had not started the Herbology reading due the next morning.

Most nights produced nothing. He would sit with the Wexworth open and the manuscript on his lap and the two systems would refuse to talk to each other, and he would stare at the same cluster for an hour and go to bed at two in the morning with a headache and ink on his fingers and no progress.

One night he kept coming back to a repeating three-symbol knot that appeared on thirty-one of the fifty-seven pages. By frequency it ought to have been something grammatical: a conjunction, a boundary marker, a bit of connective structure the notation used everywhere because sentences needed glue. He had written *marker?* beside it on a sheet of Potions parchment and underlined it twice.

The barrel lid thudded open behind him.

Duncan came in from Muggle Studies with his sleeves dragged down to his wrists and a roll of parchment crushed in one hand. The title line showed where his grip had buckled it: *The Natural Superiority of Magical Blood.* Miren was right behind him, carrying his book bag because he was not using his left arm properly.

"Can you get some water?" she said to Gavin.

"Yeah. In a second."

She looked at him for a beat, then went herself.

When she handed Duncan the glass, she said, very low, "You can throw that away."

"She'll ask for it," Duncan said.

He was speaking flat, not upset, which was worse. Gavin watched Miren take the essay from him anyway and smooth it once along the table edge. Then he looked back down because the knot on the page had shifted while he was not looking and he was afraid he would lose the relation again.

An hour later the common room had thinned to six people and the fire had burned down enough that the orange light was all at floor level. Somewhere upstairs a bed knocked once against the wall. After that came the sound of someone being sick and trying not to make noise about it. Gavin half rose from the chair.

The cluster on the page tightened by keeping the same pressure between its parts while everything around it drifted. The outer two marks leaned toward the middle one and stayed leaning. The whole three-mark shape held its own pressure. Gavin sat back down hard enough to jolt the armchair and pulled the paired page out from under the Wexworth.

The knot had fooled him. The true boundary marker was elsewhere: the small curved mark that kept surfacing between clusters no matter how the notation drifted. That one held its place like a nail in timber. This three-symbol form arrived whole and self-contained. On the paired page the left-column entry narrowed, on the right, into that same compact knot with the side-hooks pared away.

He went back through his notes, checked every place the knot appeared, and felt the use of it gather around the marks themselves. Whenever a line set intention facing material, the knot settled between them and held the spacing steady. It turned up where the surrounding symbols stopped sliding and stayed caught. He wrote *agreement* on the edge of the Potions parchment and stared at it until the word stopped looking like English.

The word sat on the page like a clerk's stamp. It felt too legal, too human, too much like signing something. But it was the closest thing he had: two things pulled into the same terms and held there so hard that his own jaw had gone tight before he noticed.

Upstairs, the pipes knocked. Someone crossed the dormitory floor above him in quick steps. Gavin did not get up.

After that the work became possible, though still slow enough to make his eyes ache. The next foothold came from the same root with a hooked mark riding its edge, still there after the notation drifted, changing the relation rather than replacing it. He tentatively read it as "renegotiation" — the old knot with one side pulled open. Another cluster kept laying a flat bar across the join like a latch dropped into place; wherever it appeared, the surrounding symbols stopped flexing. He read that as "permission," the mark that let the join close instead of slipping. A fourth word kept dropping one band of marks behind another as though the sheet had depth and he was looking through it. He could only render that one as "layer" or "depth" or, when he was tired, "the thing under the thing."

That was the word that finally cracked the rest of the manuscript open. He looked back through the clusters he had marked and saw the same structure everywhere: intention set against material, then the joining knot, then the marks that tightened or loosened the hold. The page kept talking about the catch between them, the way a want found the world and stayed fast. Under every spell he had ever seen, there seemed to be a deeper layer of terms deciding what could catch and what would slip. Wandwork moved inside that layer. If the layer could be altered, the spell riding on it could be altered too.

He sat with this for a long time one night, the common room empty, the fire down to embers, the Wexworth open on his knee and the manuscript showing him relationships he could almost but not quite follow. Outside the round windows, rain ran down the glass in thin late-October tracks, and the grounds were dark, and the cold from the stone floor crept up through the soles of his shoes.

Magic, apparently, was a set of agreements that could be renegotiated.

The meaning outran him. He understood the words he had decoded, and the words pointed at something enormous, and the shape of it was just beyond the edge of what he could hold in his head. It was like trying to see the building from inside the wall — knowing the wall was part of something, feeling the weight of everything it was holding up, but not being able to step back far enough to see the structure.

He closed the manuscript. His fingers were stiff from the cold. He flexed his hands and put the Wexworth and the manuscript back in his bag and sat for another minute, staring at the round window and the dark beyond it.

He went to bed. The decoded fragments sat behind his eyes, strange and partial and stubbornly suggestive, and he lay there for a long time not sleeping while the rain hit the windows and the common room settled into its late-night sounds — the fire clicking as it cooled, the pipes in the walls, someone's owl making a low sound from the dormitory above.

---

Duncan came back from Muggle Studies on a Thursday in late October with bruises on both forearms.

He was wearing his robes with the sleeves down, which was not how he usually wore them — Duncan ran warm, always had, and rolled his sleeves to the elbow as automatically as blinking. But the left sleeve had ridden up as he set his bag down at the dinner table, and Gavin saw the marks before Duncan pulled the fabric back into place. Purple and yellow, the colours of a bruise that was at least a day old, distributed in a pattern that was not from falling or bumping into something. Someone had gripped him there. Someone with fingers.

Duncan sat down. He pulled a plate toward him and put food on it in neat, measured amounts: potatoes first, then carrots, then a spoonful of stew. He chewed without looking at either of them. His face had gone blank in the way Gavin had first noticed on the train, careful enough that even his eyes seemed to stop short of meeting anyone else's.

Miren was across the table. She had seen too. Her eyes tracked the line where Duncan's sleeve met his wrist, and then she reached across the table and moved the bread basket six inches closer to him without saying anything.

"Thanks," Duncan said.

"There's rolls left. The ones with the seeds."

"Yeah."

He took a roll. He ate it. They sat there. The three of them at the Hufflepuff table, in the same seats they had occupied since first year — Miren across from the boys, Duncan on the inside, Gavin on the aisle — and the silence between them was not hostile but it was not comfortable either. It was the silence of people who knew something was happening and had agreed, without discussing it, to leave it in the silence.

The Great Hall was the same as it was every evening — the enchanted ceiling showing an overcast autumn sky, the low hum of conversation from the other tables, the clink of forks on plates. At the staff table, Alecto Carrow was eating something and not looking at the students, which was worse than when she did look, because it meant she had finished whatever she wanted to do today and was satisfied with it.

Gavin ate his dinner. He was thinking about positional classifiers — specifically about a recurring three-part structure in the manuscript's longer clusters that might be a verb form, a way of indicating action or change within the agreement framework. The modifier that suggested renegotiation appeared to be part of this verb structure, and if he could isolate the other components —

"How was your day?" Miren asked Duncan. Her voice was ordinary. The question was ordinary. Everything about the way she asked it was ordinary except the way her hands were folded on the table, very still.

"Fine," Duncan said. "Long."

"What did Carrow cover?"

"The usual. Why wizards are better." He said it flat, not bitter. The flatness was its own kind of statement. "She had me read mine out loud. We had to write essays about the natural superiority of magical blood. I wrote a good one. She gave me an E."

Miren's jaw tightened. Then she said, "You want pudding? I think it's treacle tart."

"Maybe in a bit."

They were managing something between them — Gavin could see the shape of it, the way Miren offered practical things instead of the emotional ones she wanted to offer, the way Duncan accepted the practical things and let the emotional ones stay unspoken. They had a rhythm for this that was new and practiced, and it had developed without Gavin while he kept telling himself he would get up in a second.

A first-year farther down the table knocked over a goblet and Miren flicked her wand to catch the pumpkin juice before it hit someone's homework. She was good at that — the quick, practical spell, the instinct to help before thinking about it. Gavin would have noticed the spill and reached for his wand and by then someone would have been mopping up with their napkin.

Duncan ate his roll. He did not take another one. His left hand stayed on the table with the fingers loosely curled, and when he reached for his goblet with his right hand, the motion was stiff in a way that suggested the bruises extended past his forearms.

Gavin thought about saying something. He thought about what he would say — *are you all right, what happened, can I help* — and each of these felt like a door he should have tried earlier, when the ask had only been a glass of water and a walk upstairs and the damage had not yet settled into Duncan's face this thoroughly. Duncan did not want to be asked now. That was obvious from the sleeves pulled down and the careful face and the way he had said "fine" and meant *do not pursue this.* Then Gavin's mind went back, automatically, to the verb structures and the renegotiation modifier and the shape the decoded fragments were making. He swallowed against the taste of gravy and could not get a word out.

"I'm going to the library," he said eventually. "After dinner."

"Okay," Miren said.

"You can come, if you want."

"I've got a thing." She did not specify what thing. She had been not-specifying things lately — meetings, errands, a network of activity that Gavin understood was related to the DA without ever being told directly. He did not ask. She did not offer.

Duncan said nothing. He kept breaking his roll into pieces on his plate, thumb pushing through the crust, forefinger and middle finger pinching each piece smaller before he let it drop. He had neat, square-tipped fingers. The nails were bitten short, which was new — they had not been bitten at the start of term. Before this year, Duncan's hands had been steady enough to build model ships in the summers, something he had mentioned once in third year with the offhand embarrassment of a boy who suspected his hobby was childish. Gavin had not thought about it since. He thought about it now, looking at the bitten nails and the bruised forearms and the careful, methodical destruction of a bread roll.

Gavin left the table and went to the library. He worked on the manuscript until Madam Pince chased him out at closing, then went back to the common room and worked until the fire died and the cold crept up from the stone floor and the last of the kitchen warmth bled out of the walls. He decoded another cluster that night: a broad-bottomed form that kept turning up under the joining knot, taking the weight of the rest of the entry. *Foundation,* maybe. *Ground.* *Substrate* if he let himself use the academic word. Whatever the English was, the agreements stood on something. The manuscript was trying to teach him to see the load-bearing layer itself.

He closed the Wexworth and the manuscript and sat in the empty common room for a while. Through the round windows the grounds were invisible in the dark, but he could hear the rain, and somewhere past the rain the edge of the Forbidden Forest was a sound more felt than heard — the deep vegetable presence of old trees in weather.

Duncan's bruises were still behind his eyes: the purple and yellow on the pale skin of his forearms, finger-shaped; the careful way he had moved his right arm reaching for the goblet; the roll broken into eight careful pieces on his plate, then ten, then fragments; the quick glance Miren had given Gavin in the common room days earlier before she went for the water herself.

Gavin did not go check on him. He did not find Miren to talk about it. He sat for a while longer and then went to bed, sliding the manuscript into his trunk the way he always did now — facedown, under the spare robes, where nobody would look and where the faint warmth of the material was absorbed by the fabric and became indistinguishable from the warmth of ordinary things. The decoded words stayed with him in the dark: *agreements, renegotiation, substrate.* They were starting to cohere into something — a shape, a system, a way of understanding magic that had nothing to do with wands or incantations or anything his professors could teach him. The paired entries still gave him somewhere to start. The later pages were getting denser, stranger in a way he could feel before he could name it.

Duncan's bruises kept breaking into it: the finger marks on the pale skin of his forearms, the stiff reach for the goblet, the roll reduced to crumbs. Gavin lay on his side and started again from the paired entries every time they came back.
Behind that, the later pages kept pressing at him: denser, changed somehow, the sort of thing the common room wasn't going to be enough for much longer.
