# Chapter 15

He was three steps past Barnabas the Barmy before he understood what had just happened.

On the way to breakfast, he'd noticed that the Permanent Sticking Charm on the portrait of Barnabas the Barmy was failing along its lower-left edge.

Not the portrait itself, not the frame. The agreement beneath the charm — the substrate commitment someone had made centuries ago, probably a long-dead Hogwarts custodian who'd wanted the painting to stay put — was thinning where it anchored to the stone. Old mortar crumbling between bricks, the structure sound but the joints going. A decade more and Barnabas would start to swing.

He kept walking.

He hadn't translated. He hadn't seen the portrait and then reached back through the notation he'd memorized, matching the visible effect to a substrate grammar, working out what was there the way he'd been doing for months — slow, effortful, one concept at a time, like reading a foreign language with a dictionary open beside the text. He'd just seen it. The agreement, the thinning, the timescale. All of it at once, the way you saw a word in English and knew what it meant without sounding out the letters.

He stopped in the corridor. A seventh-year Ravenclaw carrying an armful of textbooks so tall she had to tilt her chin to see over them edged past with a muttered "excuse me," and Gavin stepped aside without looking at her. His hands were shaking — the left one worse than the right, as it always was since the Cruciatus — but the shaking wasn't from fear. Something in his head had settled into place the way a dislocated joint settles back, and the relief was physical and strange and slightly sickening.

By the time he reached the Great Hall, he suspected something in his head had shifted. The enchanted ceiling made him sure. It was Tuesday in late April, though he only worked that out afterward, checking the class schedule he'd stopped following.

The Great Hall was three-quarters empty, which had been normal since October. Students clustered at their house tables in tight groups, leaving wide stretches of bare wood between them. The enchanted ceiling showed a spring sky — high cirrus, pale blue, the kind of morning that made the grounds look like they belonged to a different school than the one Gavin was attending. He sat at the Hufflepuff table and looked up at the ceiling and saw it for what it was.

Seven agreements, interlocking: a display agreement mirroring the actual sky through a perceptual filter, a containment agreement keeping weather on the correct side, a light-sourcing agreement permitting the mirrored sky to illuminate the hall as if the ceiling were absent, and four structural agreements that kept the first three going across centuries without maintenance. Each one committed into the substrate at a visible scar — slight distortions, compression points, like nail-marks in painted wood. Places where ancient intent had shaped the substrate and held it there.

It was neat work. Economical. And utterly boring at the grammar level. Like discovering that a cathedral had been built using six types of brick.

He served himself porridge. The seat to his left, where Miren had sat every morning for five years, was empty. He noticed this the way he'd noticed the failing charm on Barnabas: as a fact about the arrangement of things, present and requiring no response. She was sitting further down the table with two sixth-year girls who were involved in the DA supply network. From where he sat there was only the back of her plait, the straight set of her shoulders. She didn't look over.

He finished his porridge, wiped the bowl with a piece of bread, and left the Great Hall. He was noticing everything. That was the strange part. Not that he had to work to see the substrate — he'd been doing that for months, with increasing facility — but that he couldn't stop seeing it. Every enchantment, every charm, every spelled object in the castle was now legible at two levels simultaneously: the surface, where a door locked and a candle floated and a staircase moved, and the grammar, where agreements sat in the substrate like syntax underlying a spoken language. It was like literacy — once you could read, you couldn't walk through a city without reading the signs. The shapes were always also words.

The corridors were busy. Students moved between classes in the tight, purposeful way that had replaced the old between-class chaos — nobody dawdled, nobody stopped to chat in the open. The Carrows had made corridors dangerous. You could be grabbed for anything, assigned a punishment for loitering, for looking wrong, for being in a group of more than three. Students moved like people in a downpour, heads down, pace brisk, and Gavin moved with them, his bag over one shoulder, his left hand in his pocket where the tremor was less visible.

Outside the Charms classroom, two fifth-years from Gryffindor were practicing Stunning Spells on each other. This was Carrow's homework — practice offensive hexes, demonstrate proficiency in class, and do not come unprepared — and the Gryffindors had the grim, dutiful look of students who understood that showing up without competence meant a different kind of lesson. The taller one cast first. Red light, clean trajectory, decent form. The shorter one went down, hit the wall, slid to sitting.

Gavin leaned against the opposite wall and watched.

At the spell level, a Stunning Spell was straightforward: directed magical energy organized to temporarily disrupt the target's conscious function. First-year understanding. But at the substrate level, what Gavin saw was different in kind, though not in complexity. The spell was a sentence. The grammar that made the sentence valid was an agreement: it is possible for directed magical intent to disrupt consciousness at a distance. This agreement was part of a broader class — the entire family of offensive magic shared a root agreement about the permissibility of one magical actor affecting another's physical or cognitive state without consent.

The shorter Gryffindor got up, shook his head, and cast back. His Stunner was shakier — the substrate sentence had a grammatical wobble in it, like a clause with a misplaced modifier — and the taller one dodged rather than blocking, which was probably smart.

Gavin could see how to fix the shorter student's form. Not at the wand level — he'd be useless at coaching Stunning Spells, his own were mediocre on a good day — but at the substrate level. The grammatical wobble was in the intent specification: the caster was targeting the other student's general area rather than committing to a specific disruption mode. If Gavin could have said *tighten the subordinate clause that specifies where in the target's cognition the disruption applies*, the spell would have landed cleanly. But there was no way to say that in any language the student spoke, and the advice would have sounded like nonsense.

He pushed off the wall and walked on.

He was halfway to Ancient Runes when he heard it.

The sound came from around the corner of the fourth-floor corridor: Amycus Carrow's voice, thick and carrying, saying something Gavin couldn't make out. Then a scream.

Gavin stopped. Other students stopped too — you learned to stop when you heard Carrow, learned to press yourself against the wall and become small, because movement attracted attention and attention attracted punishment. Three Ravenclaw girls ahead of him went still against the stone. A heavy-set Hufflepuff sixth-year named Marcus Belby — not the famous one, a different Belby, a quiet kid who always wore a second scarf even indoors and smelled faintly of the greenhouses where he spent his free periods — put one hand flat against the wall as though bracing for something.

Gavin looked around the corner.

Carrow had a student against the wall. A boy, small — third-year, maybe — with ink-stained fingers and a school tie knotted too short so the fat end barely reached his sternum. Gavin had seen him in the library before. He always sat at the same table near the Restricted Section rope, always had the same book open — Wilbert Slinkhard's *Defensive Magical Theory*, which was ironic or defiant or both, because the Carrows had pulled it from the syllabus in September. The book was on the floor now, spine cracked, pages bent where it had landed.

Carrow had his wand out, pointed loosely at the boy's chest. "Told you about that, didn't I. Told you last week." He glanced at the book on the floor. "You're carrying that round my corridor like I won't notice. You think I'm thick?"

The boy said something Gavin couldn't hear.

Carrow raised his wand.

The boy screamed again.

At the wand level, Gavin saw what every student in the corridor saw: the Cruciatus Curse, red-orange light from Carrow's wand held steady, a child in agony. The boy's back arched and his heels drummed against the stone floor and his hands clawed at nothing and the sound he made was not really a scream but something past a scream — a high, raw exhalation that went on too long, that kept going, the sound of a body producing noise because noise was all it could do.

At the substrate level, Gavin saw something else entirely.

The Cruciatus was a sentence. One tight construction, built on a substrate permission that let directed magical intent light up a target's pain signals without laying a hand on them. That was all it was. Simpler, in fact, than the Stunning Spell the Gryffindors had been practicing. Translated into the closest English Gavin could manage, the permission was six words long. Six. And nothing about it looked specially guarded. No deeper anchoring. No harder lock. It sat in the same kind of join as Levitation Charms, Colour-Change Charms, the spell holding Barnabas to the wall.

Gavin looked at it and found the loose join.

The knowledge arrived without anything that felt like a revelation. He had edited substrate agreements before — the quill, the visibility rules, other small experiments that had cost him nosebleeds and headaches and hours of blurred vision. This was larger, yes, but not stranger. The anchoring points stood out at once. One of them would take the change.

He did not have to block it or shield the boy. He only had to spoil the fit between intent and effect. Carrow could slash his wand through the air and mean it all he liked. Nothing would take.

Carrow lifted his wand. The boy crumpled. He lay on the stone floor breathing in fast, shallow gulps, his ink-stained fingers twitching against the flagstones. His book lay beside him, pages fanned open to a chapter on Shield Charms.

"Get up," Carrow said. He was sweating. He always sweated when he used the Unforgivables — Gavin had noticed this before, months ago, the way everyone noticed things about Carrow while trying not to be seen noticing. But now, with the substrate visible, Gavin understood the sweating differently: the physical cost of the Cruciatus was not borne by the caster's magical reserves but by the caster's neural engagement with the substrate agreement. Carrow's body was responding to the act of participating in a pain-grammar, even from the casting side. The sweat had nothing to do with exertion. It was his nervous system registering what it was doing.

Carrow wiped his forehead with his sleeve. He had pushed the sleeves of his robes up, the way he always did, as if punishment were manual labour.

"Pick it up and go," he said. He was already looking past the boy, down the corridor.

The boy got up. Slowly. One hand on the wall. He picked up his book and held it against his chest with both arms and walked away with the careful steps of someone whose legs had not yet agreed to hold him. Ink on his fingers. Tie too short. A third-year who liked reading about Shield Charms in a school where shields weren't enough.

Gavin watched him go.

The Ravenclaw girls peeled themselves off the wall and hurried away. Marcus Belby exhaled and adjusted his scarf and went wherever he was going. The corridor emptied in seconds — quickly, quietly, everyone pretending they hadn't seen, because seeing meant remembering and remembering meant being a witness and being a witness was dangerous.

Gavin did not move. He kept looking at the place in the substrate where the Cruciatus sat and tracked its anchoring outward. The pattern opened fast.

The Cruciatus and the Killing Curse shared a root.

Different surface effects. Same buried permission: directed magic could reach into another person's body and overrule it. Pain. Control. Death. Three branches off the same join.

And that join was ordinary. Not sealed off. Not hidden behind anything grander than age and habit. It sat in the substrate beside hundreds of other load-bearing pieces the castle and the world relied on.

Avada Kedavra — the spell that had shaped Voldemort's war, that had killed Harry Potter's parents and Cedric Diggory and hundreds of others — rode that same buried piece. So did the Imperius. Gavin could see the cut now. Not a shield, not a counter-curse, not anything as visible as a duel. A change at the root, and every killing curse in the world would hit air.

The corridor was empty now. He checked his watch. He was fourteen minutes late for Ancient Runes. There was dried blood under his left nostril from a nosebleed he hadn't noticed starting, and his left hand was trembling against his thigh.

He wiped his nose on his sleeve, shifted his bag to his other shoulder, and walked to class.

He was thinking about Voldemort's war.

---

Gavin skipped dinner. He'd been skipping dinner more often than not since the break with Miren — not because of the break itself, not exactly, but because eating in the Great Hall meant sitting among people he used to talk to and now didn't, and the energy required for that performance was energy he'd rather spend on the manuscript. He took a bread roll from the kitchens — the Hufflepuff common room was adjacent, the house-elves knew him by now, they didn't ask questions — and ate it dry on the walk to the seventh floor, dropping crumbs on the stairs. The bread was good. He didn't notice.

The sub-collection was cold, as it always was — the dry, mineral cold of deep stone, older than the castle's heating charms, older than the castle itself. The light from the walls was faint and steady, the luminescence that had been there before anyone thought to install torches. The corridor sequence that led here had taken twelve minutes tonight. Sometimes the castle's shifting architecture made it faster, sometimes slower, and Gavin had stopped trying to predict which. He sat on the floor with his back against the shelf that had once held the manuscript and spread the pages across his knees. The stone was cold through his trousers. He'd stopped bringing a cushion weeks ago because it was one more thing to carry and the cold had become part of the routine, like the nosebleeds, like the headaches, like the tremor in his left hand that flared when he was tired.

The notation moved. It always moved — not in the looping, repetitive manner of wizard photographs, but representationally, the symbols shifting to show relationships from different angles, rotating perspectives the way you might turn a glass in your hand to catch new light. Gavin watched the notation and understood it and the understanding was not translation. He was reading the way you read in your own language: the meaning arrived without a processing step, the symbols and the concepts they carried collapsed into a single act of comprehension.

He was working on ward-substrate coupling. The section of the manuscript that described how the planetary wards drew on the local magical substrate to sustain themselves. It was the hardest thing he'd tried to understand — designed not for a fifteen-year-old human brain but for the Builders, for minds that had operated magic as a medium of existence — as fundamental and unconscious as breathing. The specific problem he'd been staring at for three days was a coupling description that his cognition kept sliding off of, like trying to hold the shape of a four-dimensional object in a mind built for three dimensions. He could get two of the variables and the third would dissolve. He could hold the third and lose the relationship between the first two.

But tonight his mind kept returning to the corridor.

The war had become, absurdly, something he might be able to end.

The thought had the quality of standing in a kitchen and realizing you could burn down the house — capability just there, mundane, available, the gap between having it and using it a matter of decision rather than power. Not by killing Voldemort. Not by helping in any way the people around him would have recognized as help. At this level, the war's worst machinery reduced to a grammar problem. The buried permission that let magic reach into a body and overrule it sat there waiting to be spoiled. There were two scales to it. The fast one was local: a corridor, a room, perhaps even Hogwarts itself if he were inside the pressure and willing to keep correcting for drift. Minutes, maybe, if he didn't care about anything beyond the boundary. In a fight, he could probably make Unforgivables stop taking inside a bounded patch of world. The permanent one was bigger and filthier. To cut the root cleanly everywhere, without mangling healing charms or ordinary school hexes, he'd have to trace the full spread of the grammar through the substrate, follow every dependency, and alter it at depth. That was weeks of work. The sort that ended with blood drying on his mouth and the wall-light doubled in his vision. But if he did it right, every Killing Curse in the world would hit air. Every Cruciatus would die in the caster's wand. Every Imperius would slide off like spit on glass. Voldemort's side would lose its cleanest tools overnight, and neither Voldemort nor any of his Death Eaters would know why. They would cast and nothing would happen. They would cast again and still nothing would happen. The confusion alone would buy the other side room to breathe. That would save hundreds of lives. Probably more.

He wiped blood from his nostril and looked down at the ward-coupling problem on his knees. When he let his perception open to it, the room changed around him. Pressure settled through the crown of his head and into the roots of his teeth. His ears clicked as if he were going down too fast, though he was sitting still on cold stone. The wall-light beside him went thin in his vision — not dimmer, but strained, its agreement stretched tight as wire. The wards were drawing harder and getting less. Their coupling to the local substrate was fraying, catching, fraying again. The Builders had set them for an Earth whose magical ground had been denser than this one. In the millennia since, the substrate had thinned — from age or use or some process the manuscript described in notation Gavin still couldn't hold for long — and the wards were starving. If they failed, the thing beyond them would not attack or invade or arrive with any meaning a person could answer. It would simply be there, total and indifferent, and magical life would stop working in its presence. Not just wizards. Every charm holding a roof up or a secret shut. Every ward. Every enchanted object. Every magical creature. Every magical plant. The whole hidden structure the wizarding world lived inside without knowing it existed. Muggles would probably survive the first fact of it, because the condition outside was incompatible with magic rather than life in general, but the wizarding world itself would come apart all the same. He didn't know whether that threshold was years away or months or some uglier span in between. What he could feel, every time that pressure settled on his skull and teeth, was that the margin wasn't generous.

The war, meanwhile, wasn't waiting politely while he decided. Even from the edges of school life he could feel it bunching toward an end. Miren had been sitting with DA girls at breakfast instead of him. Gryffindors were drilling Stunning Spells in corridors before class because nobody trusted practice time to come later. Students carried bandages and contraband food under their robes, moving with the sharp, purposeful pace people used when they expected to be stopped. Potter's name moved through the castle in fragments — not openly, not safely, but too fast and too often to be only idle rumor. The Carrows were still dangerous, but there was a frantic quality to them now, the ugly extra force of people who could tell the floor had started shifting under their authority. The regime was cracking. That didn't mean the war would end well. It meant it was already moving without him, with or without anything he chose to do in this room.

That made the choice short and filthy. The boy in the corridor was still in him: heels hammering stone, fingers jumping after the curse stopped, the sound torn out of him that had kept going after a throat should have given out. More of that would happen tomorrow. More next week. More during whatever battle was building toward Potter. If Gavin turned aside now, he could spend weeks taking apart the war's root grammar everywhere, or later minutes carving out some temporary patch where the worst curses failed and then failed to keep failing unless he stayed on it. If he stayed here, he might keep the wards from slipping one stage nearer collapse. Comparisons stopped helping after a point. He was sitting on a cold floor deciding that children being tortured at school was the smaller emergency. The fact stayed ugly. It didn't stop being true. He thought of the boy's book pressed against his chest, *Defensive Magical Theory*, pages bent where it had hit the ground. He thought of Duncan in class months ago, split lip and silence. He thought of people he knew only by table position in the Great Hall and the shape of their backs in corridors. He was going to let the war keep happening. Not because there was a better way to say it. Not because saying "world" first changed what the choice was. Simply because the wards were the bigger failure and no one else was working on them. He set the page square on his knees and went back to the coupling.

He picked up the manuscript page he'd been working on. The ward-coupling problem. Three variables, interlocking. For three days he'd been losing the third when he held the first two, and the frustration of it had become its own weather, a constant overcast in his working hours. He looked at the notation and let it shift and, because his mind was running differently tonight, because the corridor had forced the scale of the problem into a shape even a fifteen-year-old could name, he felt the third variable hold for a little longer than it had before.

Not clearly. Not fully. But long enough to hurt.

He worked.

The first hour went badly. The notation would hold still for one relation and slip on the next, as though his mind could pay for only one piece of the problem at a time. When he widened his perception far enough to feel the full load of the ward-coupling, the room seemed to lower by fractions. His shoulders kept tensing against pressure that wasn't really weight. Twice his ears clicked hard enough to hurt. Once the pain ran down into his jaw and his back teeth rang with it, a thin metallic ache that made him stop and press his tongue to them one by one. Blood slid warm over his upper lip and onto his chin. He wiped it with his sleeve, smearing old brown into fresh red. Then the third variable stayed in place for three full breaths. Then for six. Not understanding. Not even close. But contact. The coupling wasn't static. It flexed. It compensated. When the local substrate thinned, the wards pulled harder. When they pulled harder, something in the local grammar roughened under the strain. He couldn't hold the whole relation long enough to be sure he'd seen it right, but the possibility that the failure could speed up was enough to make his stomach drop.

The sub-collection was silent except for the faint mineral settling of old stone, the sound of a building so deep in the castle that the ordinary noises of school life — footsteps, laughter, doors — didn't reach it cleanly. His nose bled intermittently. He wiped it on his sleeve, which was already stained brown in overlapping rings from earlier nosebleeds, a history of evenings spent doing exactly this: sitting on cold stone, reading notation that moved, trying to hold more than his mind was built to hold. He kept reading. The coupling mechanism. The local draw. The wards overhead, invisible to anyone without substrate perception, thinning — slowly, structurally, with the kind of patience that made it easy to pretend it wasn't happening until the pressure bit down behind his eyes again and reminded him.

By Wednesday morning the castle had started carrying visible damage from one day to the next. A smashed wall sconce on the second floor was still on the ground at lunch. Broken panes in a stairwell had been stuffed with old towels instead of repaired. Someone had scrubbed blood from a stretch of fourth-floor wall and not done it well enough; the stone kept the darker colour in its pits. A sixth-year Ravenclaw with singed sleeves was marched to the hospital wing by two girls who looked furious rather than frightened. In the Great Hall, students kept sitting in the wrong places so they could talk low over supplies and scraps of news. Every time one of the big doors opened too hard, half the room looked up. Gavin noticed the damage and the substrate beneath it at the same time now: cracked plaster and the loosened agreements that held it in shape, broken glass and the hairline strain around the charms that had failed to keep it whole. The school was getting ready for something nobody could stop by pretending it was still April and nearly exam season.

He still went to class, mostly because not going drew attention and attention wasted time. In Ancient Runes, Babbling spent twenty minutes on memorial stones from Northumbria while every third head in the room turned toward the corridor at sounds that would have been ordinary in any other year: running feet, a slammed door, a shout cut short. In Potions, three cauldrons were left uncleaned because the students who should have cleaned them had been called away for questioning and nobody wanted to ask by whom. At breakfast the next morning, Marcus Belby had a bruise blooming yellow under one eye and an extra scarf looped around his wrist like a bandage he hadn't bothered to hide. A younger Hufflepuff passed along a packet of dittany wrapped in homework parchment with the same ordinary motion someone would have used for jam. The work of keeping people going had moved into the open without becoming safe.

He passed Miren twice in those days. Once outside Herbology, arms full of jars and folded strips of linen, jaw set, not surprised to see him and not stopping. Once in the Great Hall, bent over a scrap of parchment with two older girls and Duncan on her other side, their heads close while her finger tapped out some route or schedule Gavin couldn't hear. Duncan looked thinner than he had even a week ago. His hands were inside his sleeves. No one at the table looked up. Gavin took toast from the far end, left before tea, and spent the walk upstairs trying not to think about the fact that he recognized half the people in the school now by injuries.

At night the route to the sub-collection kept changing in ways that would have felt whimsical in any previous year. Now it felt evasive. The turn by the tapestry of trolls opened on Thursday and refused him on Friday. A stair that normally rose toward the library dumped him on the sixth floor beside a window blacked out with blankets. Once he passed two second-years dragging a crate of bottled pumpkin juice toward the Room of Requirement and both boys froze until they recognized him, then relaxed only enough to keep moving. Hogwarts was helping somebody, or at least not stopping them. Gavin noticed that too, and kept walking because any conversation that began with *Can you carry this?* would end somewhere he wasn't prepared to go.

Somewhere in the castle above him — in a corridor or a classroom or the Room of Requirement, if the rumors were right — someone cast a spell. He felt it as a flutter in the substrate, a tiny turn of intent he could now read without effort. A homework charm, probably. Or a hex thrown in anger. Or a curse thrown in cruelty. The substrate registered only that something had taken hold, not whether it belonged in a classroom or a torture chamber. He could change that. He was choosing not to.

On the second night he forced himself to sketch the war problem properly before touching the manuscript, because leaving it half-thought had started tugging at him whenever a curse cracked through the castle above. He drew two columns on the back of a Transfiguration handout. Local invalidation: fast, bounded, unstable, dependent on his presence or on anchors he didn't yet know how to set without drift. Global invalidation: slow, deep, permanent if done cleanly, catastrophic if done badly. He stared at the words until the ink blurred. The outline didn't change anything. It only made the shape of what he was refusing easier to name.

Sleep became another interruption. He would lie in the Hufflepuff dormitory with the curtains half-drawn and feel, under the ordinary creaks of pipes and beds, the far larger strain overhead. Not sound exactly. Not touch exactly. More like knowing a beam was carrying too much weight before it bowed. Once he woke with his jaw clenched so hard his molars hurt and had to sit on the edge of the bed until the feeling passed. Around him other boys breathed, snored, turned over. Someone muttered Potter's name in his sleep. At dawn Gavin would get up, wash dried blood from under his nose, and go to breakfast if he thought the calories were worth the time.

Outside the castle, past the corridors and the grounds and the spring warmth he couldn't feel from down here, the world was green and growing. The Forbidden Forest stirred with things waking after winter. Flowers pushed through cracks between the courtyard flagstones, ordinary and persistent. In the greenhouses, the plants that Professor Sprout tended were doing what plants did in April. In the lake, the giant squid was doing whatever the giant squid did. The world was performing its small, seasonal renewal, as it did every year, oblivious to everything except the sun and the soil and the rain.

And above all of it, visible only to Gavin if he opened his perception to that scale, the wards stretched in vast interlocking patterns across the sky — beautiful, ancient, failing. When he looked at them too directly, the pressure came back at once: skin tightening over his scalp, the bridge of his nose aching, the sense of something heavy leaning on a door somewhere above the weather. Thinner than they had been last month. The thing beyond them leaned against the thinning barrier with the patience of geology, the patience of weather, the patience of a condition that didn't know it was waiting because it didn't know anything at all.

Sometimes, when he came up for food or sleep, the castle felt too bright in the wrong places. Torches hissed with overfed flame. Classroom shields showed little whitish stress-lines in his perception where they'd been hit and patched and hit again. The Hufflepuff common room smelled of bread, damp wool, and burn ointment. Younger students had stopped taking off their shoes before curling up in armchairs. People were storing water bottles under tables. On Thursday evening he came through the barrel door and found three first-years asleep fully dressed with their robes as blankets, as if they'd meant only to sit down for a minute between alarms. No one asked him where he'd been. No one asked him to stay. He took another roll from the platter by the fire and went back upstairs before anyone could decide to notice him.

By Friday he could hold the third variable long enough to know why it kept escaping him. The notation kept asking for relationships all at once, and every time he shifted attention from one to the next the first one started to blur. He still couldn't tell whether the gap was in him or in the translation layer his mind was using to fake understanding. He only knew the manuscript had been written for a reader to whom this problem would have felt natural, and nothing about Gavin Tench had ever felt natural in that direction. He sat with the page until the back of his neck cramped and his nose dripped onto the stone between his shoes. Then he kept going anyway, because discouragement was only useful if there was another person to hand the work off to, and there wasn't.

Gavin turned a page. The notation shifted, showing the coupling mechanism from a new angle, and something in the relationship between the first two variables caught at the edge of the third, and he leaned forward, his nose bleeding onto the stone floor, and tried to understand how a long-departed civilization had solved a problem he was only beginning to know how to ask.

Students slept in dormitories behind stone walls, inside a castle inside a country inside a world inside a thinning shell of ancient agreements, and none of them knew how thin the shell had become, or that the only person working on it was a fifteen-year-old Hufflepuff with a nosebleed and bad handwriting, sitting on a cold floor, choosing the world over the war.

Over the next few days, the damage in the castle stopped looking temporary. A trophy case shattered and stayed shattered. Windows cracked and stayed cracked. Corridors that had looked ordinary that morning showed broken glass and scorch marks by supper. Gavin noticed that too, with the ordinary part of him and the part that now read the substrate underneath everything. He kept working, bad handwriting on borrowed parchment, blood drying on his sleeve, trying to hold three interlocking variables in one human mind while the war above him gathered itself for the last push.
