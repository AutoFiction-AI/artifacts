# Chapter 18

The bookstore was on Seventh Avenue, half a block from the tea shop where she used to meet Simone on Saturday mornings. Nora hadn't been to this part of Park Slope since April. She noticed the avoidance as she walked past the tea shop's window — its chalkboard menu, the same barista with the septum ring — the way you notice you've been taking a different route to work and can't quite remember deciding to.

July in Brooklyn was its own animal. The heat sat on the sidewalk like something with weight, and the hydrangeas in the brownstone gardens had gone huge and almost indecent, the blooms heavy enough to bow the stems toward the iron fences. Nora walked with her bag against her hip and her hair up because she couldn't stand it on her neck in this weather, and she thought about how she used to text photos of the hydrangeas to Caleb because he was a landscape architect who would appreciate them, and how Simone had probably Googled "Portland hydrangea varieties" to write his response.

She didn't think about it the way she used to — the hot, corrosive way that had kept her up until three in the morning in April and most of May. The thoughts came now like a station she drove past on the highway: there it was, and then it was behind her, and the road kept going.

The bookstore was called Understory. It was narrow and deep, the kind of place where the shelves were too close together and you had to turn sideways past the poetry section, and it smelled like old paper and the bergamot candle they burned at the register. Nora had come for a reading by Antonia Ferris, who'd written a novel about a woman who forges her dead mother's paintings and sells them to collectors who think they're buying undiscovered work. Nora hadn't edited the book — Antonia was at a different house — but she'd blurbed it, and Antonia had sent a nice note, and it was a Tuesday night in July and the alternative was her apartment and her own company, which was fine, which was actually fine most of the time now, but sometimes fine needed an evening off from being fine.

She found a folding chair near the back. The store had set up thirty chairs and maybe twenty were filled — a good turnout for a Tuesday in summer, when half of literary Brooklyn was upstate or on the Cape. She pulled a manuscript out of her bag — one she was editing, printed and bound with a rubber band because she liked reading on paper when she was doing structural work — and read a few pages while the store's owner fussed with the microphone stand.

She'd told Margo in April, three days after the confrontation. They'd been in the office kitchen, both holding mugs of the bad coffee from the machine that no one had the authority or the will to replace, and Nora had said it all at once, without preamble, the way you pull a splinter — Caleb was an AI, Simone built him, the money went to Simone, all of it, I found out, I confronted her, it's over. Margo's humor had dropped away. Her jaw set. She went very still — the quiet she reserved for things that actually mattered, and said, "How much?" And Nora said, "Twelve thousand nine hundred dollars." And Margo said, "Shit, Nora. Are you going to the police?" And Nora said, "No." And Margo looked at her for a long moment and didn't argue, and that restraint — the effort of it visible in the way she pressed her lips together and put her mug down carefully, as though the mug were the thing that might break — was the kindest thing anyone had done for Nora in months.

She'd told Diane on a Sunday in late April. She'd taken the LIRR out to Long Island and sat at the kitchen table while her mother sliced onions for a chicken nobody needed, and she'd said, "Something happened with Simone." Her mother stopped cutting. The knife held motionless above the board, one hand still resting on the onion. She listened without interrupting, without gasping, without saying she'd known something was wrong, although Nora suspected she had — suspected it the way she suspected her mother had known things about her whole life that Nora believed she'd successfully hidden. When Nora finished, Diane set down the knife and held Nora's face in both hands — her palms damp from the onion, the sting almost welcome — and said, "That girl." Not Simone. *That girl.* The way you'd say it about someone who had once been close enough to be family and was now too far away to be named. Then Diane went back to cooking, and the kitchen filled with more food than two people could eat in three days — the chicken, a casserole, a green salad, rolls from the bakery on Main Street — and Nora understood that the quantity of food was the only language her mother had for how frightened she was.

She'd told no one else. When people asked about Simone — and people had stopped asking — she said they'd drifted apart.

Nora hadn't gone to the police. She had deleted Hinge and WhatsApp and the number that had never been connected to a phone, and she hadn't replaced these deletions with anything — no new apps, no new habits, just the small, clean absence of spaces on her home screen where the icons used to be. She didn't know how to explain the not-reporting to herself, so she'd stopped trying. It lived in her like an editorial instinct — she knew the decision was right before she could articulate the rule it violated. Something about the word *victim* and how it would fit in her mouth. Something about the story she'd have to tell a detective, the specific humiliation of narrating her own credulity to a stranger with a desk and a form. And then Simone herself — a piece of it she could not yet name and did not plan to pursue. The $12,900 was gone. She could afford its loss. She couldn't afford what pursuing it would require her to become.

The store owner tapped the microphone and introduced Antonia Ferris, and Nora slid the manuscript back into her bag and settled in.

The man who sat down next to her during the introduction had a coffee from somewhere else — a paper cup with a lid, condensation beading down the side. He was maybe late thirties, tall enough that the folding chair didn't quite work for him, knees angled out and halfway into her space. He was wearing a short-sleeved button-down with a small stain near the second button that he either hadn't noticed or didn't care about. He had a pen in his hand — not a phone, a pen, an actual ballpoint — and he was clicking it against his knee in a rhythm that suggested he didn't know he was doing it.

"Sorry," he said, noticing her glance at the coffee. "I know. I'm that guy. But the iced coffee here is bad and I'm not going to lie about it."

"Their iced coffee is terrible," Nora said. "It tastes like they brew it hot and pour it over ice and call it a process."

"That's literally what they do. I watched them do it."

She almost smiled. He went back to his pen-clicking and she went back to listening, and Antonia Ferris was good — nervous and funny and specific in the way that meant the book had cost her something to write — and Nora listened and thought about the forgery plot and the particular sadness of selling your dead mother's style because you couldn't sell your own.

During the Q&A, the man leaned over and said, low enough that it didn't interrupt, "She's better than the book."

Nora looked at him. "You've read it?"

"I teach it. Summer syllabus. Contemporary fiction. My students think the ending's too ambiguous, which means it's the right ending."

"Where do you teach?"

"Brooklyn College." He clicked his pen twice. "What were you reading earlier? You had a manuscript."

"I'm editing it. I'm an editor."

"What's it about?"

"A woman who moves to a small town in Maine and discovers the previous owner of her house left journals hidden in the walls." She paused. "It's better than it sounds. The journals are doing something structurally that I don't think the author fully realizes yet, which is the fun part of my job."

He nodded and didn't ask a follow-up question about the journals, which was either incuriosity or the rare social intelligence of knowing when someone had said exactly as much as they wanted to about a thing. The Q&A ended. People stood and milled. The man stayed in his seat for a moment, clicking his pen, and then said, "I'm Adam."

"Nora."

"Do you come to these a lot?"

"I used to. I'm starting again."

She didn't explain what she meant, and he didn't ask. They talked for a few more minutes — about Antonia's book, about the particular absurdity of teaching contemporary fiction to twenty-year-olds who believed anything published before they were born was historical — and when he asked for her number she gave it to him.

She gave it to him without the negotiation she'd expected. No risk assessment, no cataloging of red flags, no running the interaction through the filter she'd built in the months since. She gave him her number because he'd asked, and because he had a stain on his shirt and a pen he couldn't stop clicking and knees halfway into her space, and because the alternative was to carry her caution like a manuscript she'd never stop revising, adding conditions and qualifications until the original sentence was buried.

She walked home through the long July light, down streets that were still warm at eight-thirty, past people eating dinner on stoops and a man hosing down the sidewalk in front of his bodega and two teenagers sharing earbuds on a bench, both of them nodding to whatever was playing. She felt nothing. Then, somewhere around Fourth Avenue, she felt something — small, uncertain, barely a thing at all. Not hope exactly. Not readiness. Something closer to the moment before you open a door when you can feel the air from the other side pressing through the gap at the bottom. She didn't push toward it and she didn't push it away. She let it be what it was and she walked home.

---

Simone's apartment in Philadelphia was on the second floor of a rowhouse in Fishtown, and it smelled like the previous tenant's cooking — something fried, something that had soaked into the walls and intended to stay. She'd been there since May. She'd found the sublet through a Facebook group, signed the lease over email, and driven down from New York with everything she owned packed into a rented Hyundai and four trips up the narrow stairs.

The apartment was smaller than her Williamsburg studio, which she would not have thought possible. One room, a bathroom, a kitchen that was really a wall with a stove and twelve inches of counter. She'd hung nothing on the walls. The candle she used to burn — the floral one, too sweet, the one Nora always wrinkled her nose at and Simone kept buying anyway — she'd left behind in a box of things she donated to the Housing Works on Bedford Avenue. She'd donated a lot. She'd kept what fit in the car.

She had a new job. UX at a health-tech company in Center City — smaller than the fintech startup, less money, fewer people who cared whether the interface had any elegance or just functioned. Her manager was a man named Trevor who wore the same gray quarter-zip to every meeting and said "let's circle back" without irony and "synergy" without shame. Trevor had a framed photo of his golden retriever on his desk and he brought in cookies his wife baked on Fridays, and he was kind in the mild, untested way of people who had never had to choose between being kind and surviving. Simone did her work. She did it competently. She left at five-thirty because there was nothing to stay for and no one who noticed when she left.

She'd deleted the OpenClaw account the night after the confrontation, sitting on the floor of the Williamsburg apartment she'd already decided to leave. The deletion process had taken four clicks — Account Settings, Delete Account, Confirm, and a final *Are you sure?* that felt like the platform's single moment of moral inquiry. She'd been sure. The dashboard disappeared. The conversation logs, the sentiment analysis scores, the prompt engineering notes she'd written and revised and tested with the care of someone building a product she believed in. The financial sidebar with its neat column of amounts. Four clicks and Caleb Mora ceased to exist for the second time, the first being when he'd never existed at all.

She hadn't tried to contact Nora. She hadn't written an email or a text or a letter. She'd thought about it — the thinking was involuntary, reflexive, the same neural pathway she'd used for seventeen years still firing on its old schedule. She'd think: *I should tell Nora about this.* The *this* could be anything — a restaurant with a punning name that Nora would groan at, an article about publishing layoffs, the specific quality of evening light on the Delaware River that would make Nora say something about Turner or Constable, some painter Simone had learned about only because Nora talked about painters with the careless fluency other people had for weather. Then the thought would complete its circuit and she'd understand, again, that there was no Nora to tell. Not because Nora was gone. Nora was in Brooklyn, alive, working, walking through a July that Simone could picture down to the hydrangeas on the brownstone blocks. But the Simone who'd had the right to call Nora and say *look at this light* was the Simone who hadn't done what Simone had done. And that Simone was the fiction.

Her debt was still over thirty thousand dollars. The $12,900 from Nora — she could recite the number without effort, as automatically as her own Social Security number — had gone to the back rent that stopped the eviction and to credit card minimums she'd barely been meeting. It hadn't touched the principal underneath. Since the move she'd paid five thousand from her savings and her tax refund. She calculated these numbers some nights, not because they changed but because the calculating was the only penance that felt proportionate. What she owed and what she'd stolen occupied the same column of her life, and if there was a structural irony in that, she no longer found it interesting.

She sat in her kitchen at night because she didn't have a living room and her bedroom was too small for anything but sleep. She sat at the table she'd bought from a woman on Craigslist for forty dollars — it wobbled; she'd shimmed one leg with a folded takeout menu from a Thai place she hadn't tried yet — and she drank tea because she'd stopped drinking wine in May. Not because she'd had a problem. Because wine was what she and Nora drank together, and the taste of it carried a freight of specific evenings — Nora's couch, Nora's laugh, the way Nora held her glass with both hands when she was telling a story she was excited about — and Simone couldn't afford the memories that arrived through her mouth at nine in the evening when her defenses were already down.

She thought about the friendship the way she imagined exiles thought about the country they'd left. Not with nostalgia — she'd forfeited nostalgia along with the right to say *I miss you* or *remember when*. Nostalgia required a clean conscience, or at least the kind of selective memory that let you hold the good without feeling the bad underneath it. Simone couldn't separate them. Every memory of Nora laughing had a shadow version: the Simone who was already watching, already cataloging, already thinking about what she could use. She didn't know which came first — the friend or the architect of the fraud. She suspected, on her worst nights, that there had been no first. That she'd been both from the beginning, and the OpenClaw project hadn't created anything new in her but had simply given the second self a tool and a dashboard and a daily schedule.

What she thought about most often was Nora's face in Simone's old apartment that last night. Not the anger — the anger had been extraordinary, almost surgical in its discipline, Nora in her most editorial mode, every sentence placed where it would do the most structural damage. What Simone thought about was the moment before. Nora had said *Tell me about OpenClaw*, and there had been a fraction of a second when Simone's body understood before her mind did — the blood leaving her hands, the fork she'd been holding suddenly weighing more than a fork should weigh — and then her mind caught up. And in the space between those two arrivals of understanding, every justification she'd ever built — the experiment, the curiosity, the unfairness, the debt, the story she'd told herself about a thing that got out of hand rather than a thing she'd walked into on purpose, every morning, for months — just wasn't there anymore. The way a wall isn't there after a demolition. She was standing in the open space where her reasons used to be, and Nora was looking at her, and Nora's face held something worse than fury. It held clarity. The particular expression of someone who had finally understood something they'd been looking at for a very long time.

She rinsed her mug. She went to bed. The Philadelphia night was quieter than New York, which she still hadn't adjusted to — the absence of sirens, of bass from passing cars, of the building's pipe complaints that had been a kind of company. She fell asleep listening to a neighbor's television through the wall, the sound too muffled to make out, just the murmur of someone else's drama and the occasional rise of a laugh track. She did not dream about Nora, which was worse than dreaming about her, because it meant the loss was settling in, becoming ordinary, becoming the shape of her life rather than an interruption of it.

The manuscript on Nora's desk was four hundred and twelve pages, double-spaced, and the author had a habit of ending chapters with one-sentence paragraphs that were supposed to land like revelations but mostly landed like someone dropping a plate. Nora had flagged eleven of them. She would keep two. The rest needed to earn their isolation or rejoin the paragraph above.

It was a Thursday afternoon in late July, and the office was half-empty — people with beach houses had left by noon, and people without them had discovered urgent reasons to work from home. Margo had gone at three, citing a dentist appointment that Nora was fairly sure was fictional. The air conditioning hummed its familiar note, a half-step flat of correct, a pitch so specific to this building that Nora heard it in her sleep sometimes, like the ocean after a long day at the beach.

She was on page 287. The narrator was describing a morning after a difficult conversation — a woman standing in her kitchen, realizing that the argument from the night before had changed something she couldn't change back — and the sentence read: *She stood at the counter and felt the whole morning open up around her, wide and terrifying and new.*

Nora read it twice. *Wide and terrifying and new.* The rhythm was right — three beats, escalating — but *terrifying* was doing too much work, telling the reader what to feel rather than trusting the moment to generate the feeling on its own. And *new* was too clean, too resolved. A morning after a bad fight didn't feel new. It felt like the old morning with a piece missing.

She deleted *terrifying and new* and sat with the gap. She typed *and strange and not yet hers.* Read it back. *Not yet hers* — that was closer. The woman in the kitchen hadn't arrived at her new understanding yet. She was standing at the edge of it, and the edge was where the sentence needed to live.

She changed *wide* to *unfamiliar*, because the morning wasn't actually wide — it was the same size as every other morning, it just didn't have the shape it used to have, and *unfamiliar* captured that without reaching for grandeur.

*She stood at the counter and felt the whole morning open up around her, unfamiliar and not yet hers.*

Nora read it one more time. She saved the file. The satisfaction was small — a sentence slightly improved, a manuscript fractionally closer to the book it was trying to become. No one would notice the change except the author, who might read the revision and feel only that it was better without being able to say why.

She picked up her pen and moved to page 288.
