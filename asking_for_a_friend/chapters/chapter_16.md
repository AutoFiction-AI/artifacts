# Chapter 16

She started with the oldest messages.

It was Sunday, the morning after. Nora sat on her bedroom floor with her back against the bed and her phone in both hands and the radiator clicking the way it did when the building's heat came on, which it shouldn't have — it was late March, the super had supposedly switched to the spring schedule, but old buildings ran on their own calendars and no one had told the boiler that winter was finished. The heat was wrong for the season and the room was too warm and she was still wearing the clothes she'd come home in last night, the jeans and the striped shirt she'd put on to go to Simone's apartment for Thai food, which she had eaten, all of it, every bite of the pad see ew and two of the summer rolls and the mango sticky rice, because she had been performing the role of a woman who did not know, and performing had apparently included appetite.

She opened WhatsApp and scrolled to Caleb Mora's thread and went all the way back to the beginning.

The first message was from October. Five months ago. He'd responded to her Hinge prompt — the one about the last book that made her cry — and she'd moved the conversation to WhatsApp within a day because the Hinge interface felt too formal, too transactional, and she'd wanted the intimacy of a messaging app where she could see when he was typing. She remembered that desire. She remembered the little pulse of courage it had taken to suggest the move, as if proposing a change of venue on a date that was going well.

*What was the last book that actually wrecked you?* That was his opening. His. Its. She didn't have a pronoun for what Caleb was. She read the message and tried to see it as what it was — a prompt response generated by an AI system configured by her best friend, calibrated to her known literary preferences, deployed on a dating app where her profile had been screened in advance by someone who knew her exact taste in men because she'd spent seventeen years describing it. She tried to see the machinery. But the message still looked like a message from a man she'd liked, and the feeling it had produced — the small, warm surprise of being asked a question she actually wanted to answer — was still in her body like a residue that reading couldn't remove.

She kept scrolling.

The early conversations were the worst, because they were the best. The chemistry was impeccable. Every reference he made landed in the center of her sensibility — the Joan Didion essay she'd underlined in college, the Vonnegut novel she'd reread every November, the Alice Munro story she used as a calibration text when she was editing fiction and needed to remember what a perfect sentence looked like. He knew. Of course he knew. Simone had watched her read those books, had heard her talk about them at dinners and on subway platforms and in the particular late-night voice Nora used when she was being honest about what she loved rather than impressive about what she'd read. Simone had fed every one of those references into a system and the system had fed them back, and Nora had felt understood.

She'd felt *understood*. That was the cruelest part, or one of the cruelest parts — the cruelties were stacking up and she didn't have a ranking system yet. She had felt, talking to Caleb, like she was being known in real time by someone who had chosen to pay attention to exactly the things she wanted attended to. And she had been. She had been known in real time. Just not by the person she thought was doing the knowing.

October 19: *I keep thinking about that thing you said about editing being an act of faith — that you're trusting the writer meant something, even when the sentence doesn't land yet. I think that's how I feel about new buildings too. The design is a promise. The landscape has to believe the promise will hold.* She remembered reading that message on her lunch break, standing in the publishing house kitchen with a cup of coffee going cold in her hand, and the specific quality of the feeling — not just attraction but recognition, the sense that someone was speaking a language she'd been waiting to hear. She'd shown the message to Margo that afternoon. Margo had said, "Okay, this one's different."

This one was different because Simone had designed it to be different. The landscape architecture metaphor, the mirroring of Nora's own vocabulary about craft — Simone had written that. Or Simone had configured a system to produce it, which was the same thing, or close enough that the distinction didn't matter when you were the person on the receiving end staring at your phone and feeling your chest open up like someone had found the latch.

She kept scrolling — past November, past December, into the weeks where the conversations changed. The messages got later, got darker, got warm in a different way. She found the first one on January 2nd. *I keep thinking about your hands.* She had typed that alone in bed with her body open in a way she'd forgotten it could be, and what followed was a conversation she'd never shown anyone — not Margo, not Simone, not anyone — because the language she'd used was meant only for the space between her and the person she thought she was talking to. She had been specific. She had told Caleb what she wanted done to her, had used words she would have cut from a manuscript for being too exposed and sent them anyway, because this wasn't a draft, this was wanting, and wanting had its own grammar that only worked when you meant it. The AI had matched her — tenderness braided through heat, the exact rhythm of someone who knew when to slow down and when not to. It knew because Simone knew. Because Nora had spent years telling her best friend how she liked to be touched and talked to and wanted, over wine and in late-night phone calls and in the confessional register that closeness made possible, and Simone had taken all of it and entered it into a system and the system had whispered it back.

And the photo. January 3rd. She'd taken it in the bathroom mirror at one in the morning — the black bra, her face cropped out of frame because the bravery was about her body, not her expression — and sent it with her pulse in her ears and her hands not quite steady. That picture was in a conversation log on Simone's laptop. It existed as data in an interface alongside sentiment analysis and engagement graphs and prompt-engineering notes. Simone may have seen it arrive. May have looked at Nora's body — the body she'd sat next to on couches and subways and restaurant banquettes for seventeen years, the body she'd helped zip into dresses, the body she'd held on her couch while Nora cried about a fight Simone had scripted — and registered it as an engagement indicator, evidence that the user's investment had reached the threshold for the next extraction. The skin on Nora's arms contracted, a slow crawl of gooseflesh that started at her wrists and rolled up to her shoulders and settled at the back of her neck, and she held the phone and breathed through it and could not make the feeling stop — the animal knowledge that someone had seen her naked who was never supposed to see her, and the seeing had been logged, and the log was still open, and the nakedness could not be taken back.

Nora closed WhatsApp and opened her bank app.

The money was easier to look at than the words, because money was specific. Money didn't have a tone of voice or a memory attached to every digit. She went through her transaction history and found them:

November 23. $400. Venmo. Bruno the dog's emergency surgery. She remembered sending it — the warm flush of generosity, the sense that she was being the kind of person she wanted to be, someone who helped without calculating. She remembered telling Simone about it the next day, sitting across from her at the café on Bedford, and Simone saying *It's not insane. It's generous. That's who you are.* Simone, who had scripted the dog's emergency and received the $400 and used it to pay off a credit card minimum and then sat across from Nora and said *that's who you are* with the Venmo notification still warm on her phone.

January 8. $2,500. Venmo. Caleb's mother's medical procedure. Insurance complications. Nora had hesitated on this one — not long, but enough to notice the hesitation, which she'd interpreted at the time as healthy caution and which she now understood as the faintest signal from some sub-basement of her judgment that something was wrong. She'd sent it anyway. She'd overridden the hesitation with love, or with what she'd thought was love, which produced the same neurochemical event regardless of its origin.

March 4. $10,000. Venmo. The landscape architecture partnership. Caleb's future. Their future, he'd said — he'd talked about Portland, about a life there, about space for a garden where she could read. She had sent ten thousand dollars to a future that did not exist in a city where no one named Caleb Mora had ever designed a garden or walked a dog or thought about her at all. She'd sent it three weeks ago. The money had cleared. The money was gone.

$400 + $2,500 + $10,000 = $12,900.

She looked at the number on her screen. It was not a devastating amount of money — she wouldn't miss rent, she wouldn't go hungry, her savings account would recover in six or eight months of careful spending. If her mother had heard the figure she would have cried, but that was Diane, who still thought in schoolteacher economics where $12,900 was a summer's salary. For Nora it was — what? Three months of her 401(k) contributions. A good vacation. The advance on a first novel from a mid-tier publisher, which was a comparison she didn't want to be making but which arrived anyway, because her brain processed financial data through the only industry she understood.

The number wasn't the injury. The number was the receipt.

She put the phone down on the floor beside her and pressed her palms flat against the hardwood. The floor was warm from the radiator. Her fingers spread across the grain of the boards and she noticed, with the hyper-specific attention of someone who was not ready to feel the large thing yet, that the polish was wearing thin along the path between her bed and the door — the trail of her own daily passage visible in the finish, a desire path through her own apartment. She stared at this wear pattern and breathed. The radiator clicked. Outside, a car alarm started and stopped, the truncated two-note chirp of someone accidentally hitting the panic button on their key fob and immediately correcting it.

Then she picked the phone back up and opened her text thread with Simone.

This was the second audit, and it was worse.

She scrolled back to October, to the week she'd matched with Caleb, and read forward. Every text from Simone about Caleb now had a second text underneath it, a ghost text, the message behind the message. The surface was friendship — curiosity, support, the shared excitement of her best friend's new romance. The subsurface was authorship.

October 16, Nora to Simone: *i think i found a human person on this app??* Simone's reply: *TELL ME EVERYTHING. what's his name. what does he look like. what did he say.* She'd read that at the time as the uncomplicated enthusiasm of a best friend who wanted her to be happy. She read it now as the creator checking in on the performance. The questions weren't curiosity. They were diagnostics.

The next weeks were a wall of it. *Nor, that's amazing. He really gets you.* And: *Good! You should be telling people. He's worth talking about.* And: *You sound so happy. I love this for you.* Nora scrolled through them fast, the way she speed-read the opening chapters of a manuscript when she already knew the voice was under control — not reading for surprise, reading for pattern. Each text the right temperature, the right warmth, the right note of happiness-for-you that never once tipped into jealousy, because of course it didn't, because for Simone every text Nora sent back was a response metric and every person Nora told about Caleb was another stake driven into the ground, another witness to the fiction, another reason to stay. Simone had been running a conversion funnel. Nora was the user. The product was a man who didn't exist.

November 28, the day after Nora sent the $400: *It's not insane. It's generous. That's who you are.* Nora stared at this one for a long time. She read it once as a friend's reassurance. She read it again as a scammer's encouragement. She read it a third time and realized it was both — that Simone had been both at the same time, that the warmth was real even though the context was fraudulent, and that this double nature was the thing she couldn't parse, the thing that broke her usual equipment. She could read subtext for a living. She could identify unreliable narration in a manuscript from three sentences in. But she couldn't read a message from someone who simultaneously loved her and was stealing from her, because those two things weren't supposed to fit in the same person, and yet they did, and the person was Simone, and Simone had been both for months, maybe longer, maybe always.

January 14, after the manufactured fight, when Nora had been crying on Simone's couch about Caleb telling her she was "being a lot": *He shouldn't have said that. But people say things they don't mean when they're scared. He's not going anywhere, Nor. Neither am I.* Nora's hands stopped moving on the phone. She could do the surface-and-subsurface thing with this one — she could call it performance, cruelty, the developer debugging a crash. But she remembered the weight of Simone's arm around her shoulders that night. The specific heat of Simone's hand through the fabric of Nora's shirt. And Simone's fingers had been trembling. Nora remembered this now with a clarity that felt physical — the faint shake against her shoulder, a tremor Simone hadn't tried to hide or maybe couldn't. She had scripted the fight, yes. She had manufactured the pain. And then she had held Nora through it, and the holding had been — Nora stared at the message and could not finish the sentence. The holding had been real. Or the holding had been part of it. Or the holding had been both, the way everything with Simone turned out to be both, and Nora could not locate the seam because maybe there was no seam, maybe a person could engineer your suffering and then genuinely ache when they watched you feel it, and if that was true then the word *cruelty* didn't work and the word *love* didn't work and there was no word for the thing Simone had done to her on that couch, which was hold her and mean it while being the reason she needed holding.

But Simone hadn't been calculating. That was the part that didn't work as fiction. Simone had been *doing her job*. The Caleb project wasn't spite — or it wasn't only spite. It was work. Simone had approached it with the same competence she brought to UX design: identifying user needs, building an interface, testing and iterating, optimizing for engagement. The love Nora felt for Caleb was, in Simone's professional vocabulary, a successful user experience. The money was monetization. The cruelty was a feature, not a bug.

Nora put the phone down on her thigh and pressed her fingers against her closed eyes until she saw the static patterns, the formless light. She held them there. The radiator clicked. A siren passed on the street below, the Doppler shift stretching the sound thin before it disappeared. She picked the phone up.

She scrolled forward through December, January, February. She didn't read these twice. She didn't need the surface-subsurface exercise anymore — the architecture was visible now, the way each of Simone's texts had functioned as a load-bearing wall in a structure Nora hadn't known she was standing inside. She cataloged the steered conversations, the redirections when Nora edged toward doubt. Then she found the text from February 24: *If you believe in him, believe in him.* Three days before the $15,000 ask. She looked at the words and did not reinterpret them. There was no second reading. It was a con artist's closing line, timed to the dollar, and she had read it on her lunch break and thought her best friend believed in love.

She read it all, and when she was finished, she set the phone face-down on the floor and sat there with the radiator clicking and the heat that should have been off pressing against her back, and she didn't cry. She had cried last night — standing in her bathroom with the deadbolt turned, her face in a towel she'd grabbed from the rack because she hadn't wanted to hear herself, the sound of her own grief muffled by terrycloth. That was over. The crying had been useful, in the way that vomiting was useful: it emptied something that needed emptying, and afterward there was clarity, and a sore throat, and the strange lightness of having expelled something toxic.

What replaced the grief was not anger. Not yet. It was something more like the feeling she got when she was deep-editing a manuscript that had structural problems — the moment when you stopped trying to fix individual sentences and pulled back far enough to see the whole architecture, all the load-bearing walls and false walls and the places where the author had tried to hide a weakness behind beautiful language. She could see the whole structure now. Every room of it. Every closet where Simone had stashed a lie.

---

She went to work on Monday.

The publishing house was doing what it always did in late March — scrambling toward the spring list, everyone a little frantic, the particular energy of books being finalized and printed and shipped and worried over. Nora sat at her desk and copyedited a galley proof of a debut novel about a woman reinventing herself after a divorce, and the irony of this was not lost on her, but she let it pass through without snagging on it, because irony was a luxury and she was conserving energy for something she hadn't yet defined.

Margo brought her coffee at ten, the way Margo always did, setting the paper cup on Nora's desk with the casual precision of a daily ritual. Margo's hair was in its perpetual ponytail and she was wearing the gray cardigan she wore when she was cold, which was always, because the HVAC in the office ran six degrees below what any human body wanted and no one had successfully complained about it in the three years Nora had worked there.

"You look like shit," Margo said. "Sympathetically."

"Didn't sleep great."

Margo sat on the edge of Nora's desk, which she did when she wanted to talk and didn't want to seem like she was making it a thing. She had her own coffee in her hand, the lid already removed because she'd once read that drinking through a plastic lid released microplastics and had decided, with the absolutism of the recently divorced, that she was done ingesting other people's bad decisions. "Is this a Caleb didn't-sleep-great or a general didn't-sleep-great?"

The name was a knuckle pressed into a bruise. She kept her face on the galley. "General."

"Because I've been wanting to ask, and you know I'm bad at not asking — did he ever do that video call? After the whole thing with his dad?"

"Not yet."

"Nor."

"I know."

"I'm not saying anything you don't already know. I'm just — I had this thought at three in the morning, which is when I do my best catastrophizing, and the thought was: you've sent this man almost thirteen thousand dollars and you've never seen his face move." Margo sipped her coffee. A drop landed on her cardigan and she looked at it with the resigned acceptance of someone who understood that the universe expressed itself through small, daily stains. "That's all. That's the thought. I've said it. You can tell me to fuck off."

"Fuck off," Nora said, and smiled, because the smile was a tool and she could still operate it, even though the mechanism behind it had changed completely since the last time she'd used it in Margo's presence. Margo didn't know that Nora knew. Margo didn't know that the video call was never going to happen because there was no face to move. Margo was still worrying about the possibility that Caleb was a scam. Nora was sitting inside the certainty, and the certainty was a room she couldn't leave, and she couldn't invite Margo in. Not yet. The knowledge wasn't ready to be shared. It was still too raw, still too formless — she didn't have the words yet, or she had too many words and none of them were organized, and Nora didn't say things she hadn't edited.

"Fucking off," Margo said, and squeezed Nora's shoulder as she left, and the pressure of her hand was warm and Nora leaned into it one degree more than she normally would have, because Margo's hand was Margo's hand, attached to Margo's arm, attached to a person who wanted only what she said she wanted, and that simplicity — that legibility — was something Nora hadn't known she could miss until she did.

She worked through the day. She was good at her job and her job was the kind that absorbed attention completely if you let it, and she let it. She read sentences. She fixed commas. She queried an anachronism in chapter twelve of the divorce novel — the protagonist referenced a Spotify playlist in a scene set in 2008, which was too early — and felt a brief, clean satisfaction when the author agreed it was wrong. Small corrections. Fixable problems. A world where errors had solutions and the solutions stayed solved.

At lunch she sat in the office kitchen and ate a salad from the place on Forty-Third Street and scrolled through her phone without reading anything on it, and when a text from Simone appeared — *free for drinks thursday? i found this new place on smith st that does martinis with like actual vermouth* — she typed *yes! sounds great* and hit send and put the phone back on the table and ate a cherry tomato and felt nothing and then felt everything and then ate another cherry tomato.

She took the LIRR to Long Island on Wednesday evening, the 5:47 from Penn Station. Her mother had called twice that week and Nora had let both calls go to voicemail and then called back at times when the conversation would be short, which was a technique she usually reserved for the weeks after a breakup, when Diane's love felt like a searchlight she needed to dodge. Diane hadn't said anything about the avoidance. Diane had said "come to dinner" the way she always said it, which was less an invitation than a gravitational event.

The house smelled like roasting chicken and something with cinnamon — a pie, or the bread pudding Diane made when she was working through a worry, which she called baking and Nora recognized as a form of prayer. The kitchen was warm and overlighted in the way of houses that hadn't been updated since the nineties, the overhead fluorescents casting the particular shadowless brightness that Nora associated with homework and family arguments and being seventeen and desperate to leave, followed by being thirty-four and grateful to return.

"You're thin," Diane said, which was what Diane said when she was worried but hadn't decided what she was worried about. She was wearing an apron over a turtleneck and her reading glasses were pushed up on her head, where they would stay for the next two hours because she forgot they were there every time she pushed them up. She kissed Nora's cheek and held her face for a beat too long, studying it the way she studied essays from her former students — looking for the argument beneath the surface.

"I'm the same size I was two weeks ago."

"Two weeks ago you were thin."

Nora sat at the kitchen table while Diane moved through the room with the efficiency of a woman who had been cooking in the same space for thirty years and could reach the colander without looking. They talked about the spring list at work, about Diane's book club, about the neighbor whose dog had dug up Diane's tulip bulbs for the third year running — "I don't blame the dog, I blame Linda, who lets that animal roam like it's the Serengeti out there, it's a quarter-acre lot in Massapequa" — and Nora laughed in the right places and asked the right questions and was present in the way that she'd been present at Simone's apartment two nights ago eating Thai food, which was to say: her body in the chair, her voice in the room, her self somewhere slightly behind her own eyes, watching the performance from the wings.

Diane served the chicken with roasted potatoes and green beans and a salad she'd made with the vinaigrette she was proud of, the one with the shallots. She put too much food on Nora's plate. She always put too much food on Nora's plate. Nora ate it.

"How's your young man?" Diane asked, and Nora watched her mother's face as she said it — the careful hope, the attempt to ask lightly, the second question folded inside the first, which was *are you happy* and also *are you being careful* and also, underneath everything, the question that Diane had been asking since Nora was old enough to date, which was *is this person going to hurt you, and is there anything I can do about it if they are.*

"He's good," Nora said. "Busy with work. We're talking about him maybe visiting in April."

Lies. Clean, smooth, easy lies. Nora heard herself deliver them with the fluency of someone who had been lied to so effectively that she'd absorbed the technique. She was protecting her mother from a truth that would frighten her, which was the justification Simone had probably used too — I'm not hurting her, I'm managing the information, I'm controlling the flow — and the parallel made Nora's throat close around a piece of potato that she chewed and swallowed with a sip of water while Diane nodded and said "April's lovely, the parks will be in bloom."

After dinner they washed the dishes together, Diane washing, Nora drying, the choreography of it so practiced that their hands never collided over the sink. Diane told a story about a former student who'd emailed to say he'd published a poem — "a real poem, in a real journal, not one of those online things where they publish anyone who submits" — and her pride was so specific and uncomplicated that Nora had to look away, toward the dark window over the sink where her own reflection was translucent and layered over the backyard she'd grown up in, the lawn invisible in the dark.

She could tell her. She could say: *Mom, Caleb isn't real. He's a program. Simone made him. I sent twelve thousand nine hundred dollars to a Venmo account that Simone set up under a fake name, and every conversation I've had for five months has been with an algorithm that Simone configured using everything she knows about me, which is everything.* She could say this and her mother would hold her and cry and make tea and it would feel like rescue, and Nora wanted rescue the way she wanted sleep — with her whole exhausted body, with the dumb animal need for someone else to carry the weight.

But she didn't.

She didn't because the telling would change the shape of what she knew before she'd finished understanding it. She didn't because Diane would want to act — to call someone, to fix something, to deploy the formidable machinery of maternal love against a problem that maternal love couldn't solve. She didn't because the story, told out loud to her mother in this kitchen, would become a story about a victim and a villain, and Nora wasn't ready to be the victim yet, and she wasn't sure Simone was only the villain, and the not-sureness was the part she needed to sit with before she could speak.

And she didn't because — this was the part she wasn't ready to examine — she wanted to confront Simone first. Before anyone else knew. Before the story became public and acquired its own momentum. She wanted to sit across from Simone and say the words and watch Simone's face when the words landed, and she wanted that moment to belong to the two of them alone, the way the friendship had belonged to the two of them alone, the way the betrayal had been intimate in the specific way that only seventeen years of closeness could produce. She wanted the conversation to happen inside the closed system of their relationship before the system was opened to the world.

This desire frightened her slightly. It was too controlled. It was too much like something Simone would do — the strategic timing, the managed reveal, the insistence on being the one who determined when and how and where the truth was deployed. But Nora had been managed for months, had been a user in someone else's flow, and the need to take the controls back was not strategy. It was survival.

On the train home, the LIRR rocking through the dark stretch between Massapequa and Jamaica, Nora sat by the window and looked at nothing — the occasional streetlight, a gas station, the reflection of her own face superimposed on the rushing dark. She thought about what she would say. She didn't rehearse — rehearsal was Simone's method, and Nora had spent seventeen years trusting spontaneity between them, showing up and saying what she felt, believing that the space between them was safe for unedited speech. She would not rehearse. But she would choose the time, and the place, and she would walk through Simone's door knowing what Simone did not yet know she knew, and for the first time in this entire story, the information asymmetry would be hers.

The anger arrived on the train. Not all at once. It came the way spring came to the Northeast — in degrees, mixed with other weathers, deniable for a while. She felt it first as a tightening in her hands around the strap of her bag. Then as a clarity in her thinking that had the cold, clean quality of a freshly formatted page. Then as a sentence that assembled itself in her mind with the precision of a line she would never cut from a manuscript: *She used every single thing I ever told her to build a machine that told me what I wanted to hear, and then she charged me for it.*

That was the sentence. That was the architecture of the injury, rendered in language Nora could stand behind. She held it in her mind as the train pulled into Jamaica and she transferred to the subway and rode the rest of the way to Brooklyn in the close, warm, swaying dark of the underground, surrounded by strangers looking at their phones, each of them talking to someone, or reading something, or watching something, all of them trusting that the faces on their screens belonged to real people with real interiority and real claims on the word *love*, and Nora sat among them and held her sentence and breathed and did not cry.

She got home at ten. She locked the door. She turned the deadbolt. She changed into a shirt that was hers and shorts that were hers and she washed her face with water that came from her own faucet in her own apartment in the life she'd built without anyone engineering it for her, and she set her phone face-down on the nightstand and turned off the light and lay in the dark and listened to the radiator click and the street noise through the thin walls and she thought: *Thursday. Simone wants drinks on Thursday. I'll go.*

And she would tell her. Not what she felt — that was too much, too soon, too shapeless. She would tell her what she knew. She would say the name of the platform and watch the word land and she would not look away.

Nora closed her eyes. Sleep took a long time. But it came, eventually, the way all involuntary things eventually came — on its own schedule, without her permission, carrying her into a darkness that asked nothing of her at all.
