# Asking for a Friend — Novel Outline

## Narrative Engine

**Primary mode**: Dark comedy / tragicomedy with character study
**Secondary engines**: Domestic thriller (financial manipulation arc), speculative fiction (AI agent technology)

The comedy arises from dramatic irony, situational absurdity, and the gap between what Nora believes and what the reader knows. The tragedy arises from genuine emotional and financial harm inflicted by the person who knows the protagonist best. The speculative element — an AI agent sophisticated enough to sustain a romantic relationship — creates pressures specific to this premise: the uncanny valley of digital intimacy, the ethics of manufactured emotion, the question of whether feelings produced by deception are still real feelings.

This is not a thriller. The tension is relational and psychological. The "villain" is not a stranger but the person who holds the protagonist's hand while the protagonist cries about the man the villain invented.

## Point of View

Close third person, past tense, alternating between **Nora Kaplan** (the woman being deceived) and **Simone Orsi** (the best friend running the deception). The first two chapters are Nora's POV; Simone's perspective enters in Chapter 3, and from there the POVs alternate with occasional consecutive chapters from the same perspective when the story demands it. The dual POV creates the novel's central dramatic irony: the reader watches Nora fall in love while watching Simone build the machine she's falling for.

## Characters

**Nora Kaplan**, 34. Senior editor at a mid-tier literary publisher in Brooklyn. Smart, well-read, self-deprecating, funny in a way that deflects real vulnerability. She makes decent money — not wealthy, but comfortable, no student debt (her parents paid for NYU). She's been single for two years after a relationship that ended not dramatically but with a kind of slow mutual exhaustion. She's ready to be loved and she trusts deeply, which makes her both admirable and exposed. Her blind spot is that she believes being known by someone is the same as being safe with them.

**Simone Orsi**, 35. UX designer at a fintech startup, technically skilled, quick-witted, warmer in public than in private. She and Nora met freshman year at NYU and have been best friends for seventeen years. Simone is talented, attractive, and chronically underestimated — by employers, by men, by the world's sorting algorithms for attention and reward. Her jealousy of Nora is not simple envy but a compound injury built over years: Nora's parents paid for college while Simone took loans; Nora dated the man Simone wanted junior year; Nora got promoted while Simone got laid off; Nora moves through the world with an ease that Simone has to manufacture. Simone loves Nora genuinely and resents her genuinely, and these feelings do not cancel each other out. She is carrying $38,000 in debt — student loans, credit cards, a personal loan she took out to cover rent during a layoff — and her salary barely services the interest. She tells herself the OpenClaw project is an experiment, a joke, a thing she'll stop before it goes too far.

**Caleb Mora**, 32 (fictional). The AI-generated persona: a landscape architect from Portland, dog owner, reader, runner, son of a Peruvian mother and white American father. Every detail is reverse-engineered from Nora's preferences, which Simone knows exhaustively. Caleb's "voice" is warm, curious, slightly self-deprecating — a mirror of Nora's own conversational style, which is why talking to him feels like coming home. He exists as a Hinge profile (photos sourced from a private Instagram of a real man in Lisbon whom Simone found through careful searching), a WhatsApp contact, and an OpenClaw agent that generates text conversations calibrated to Nora's emotional state.

**Margo Chen**, 38. Nora's colleague at the publishing house, associate editor. Pragmatic, divorced, affectionate but skeptical. She is the voice of reasonable concern — she asks the questions Nora won't ask herself. Not a plot device but a real person with her own romantic exhaustion and hard-won clarity.

**Diane Kaplan**, 63. Nora's mother, retired high school English teacher on Long Island. Warm, anxious, perceptive. She senses something wrong before Nora does, not because she's clever but because she's watching her daughter with the specific attention of a parent who knows what her child's happiness actually looks like versus what it looks like when her child is performing happiness.

## Chapter Plan

### Chapter 1 — "The Inventory"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Drift-under-pressure

Nora at thirty-four. She's at a friend-of-a-friend's engagement party in a Bushwick loft, drinking wine she didn't choose and watching a couple she barely knows accept congratulations for a future she assumed would be hers by now. Simone is there, her plus-one as always, funny and sharp and making the evening survivable. They leave together, walk to the subway, debrief the party with the practiced comedy of women who've been narrating their lives to each other for seventeen years. On the train, Simone asks when Nora last went on a date. Nora deflects. At home alone, she opens her phone, stares at the app store, and downloads Hinge. The state shift is small but real: she's admitted to herself that she's lonely enough to try.

A few beats establish the friendship's texture — the easy shorthand, the shared references, the way Simone makes Nora laugh — but also one moment where Simone's face does something complicated when Nora mentions her upcoming raise. It's gone before Nora registers it.

### Chapter 2 — "The Match"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Discovery

Nora's first week on Hinge. A montage of bad matches rendered with comic specificity: the man whose profile says "fluent in sarcasm," the man who opens with a paragraph about his podcast, the man whose photos all feature a sedated tiger. Then: Caleb Mora. His profile is almost unsettlingly well-calibrated — he's reading the exact novel she edited last year, his prompt answers are funny without trying too hard, his photos show a real person doing real things (hiking, cooking, laughing at something off-camera with a dog). They match. Their first conversation is extraordinary. He asks questions that feel like he already knows the answers and is curious anyway. She screenshots the exchange and sends it to Simone: *i think i found a human person on this app??*

Simone's reply is enthusiastic, interested, full of follow-up questions. State shift: Nora feels the first real spark of hope she's felt in two years. The reader should feel it too — the romance needs to be genuinely appealing before the betrayal can land.

### Chapter 3 — "The Workshop"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Revelation

The reader enters Simone's apartment and her perspective for the first time. She's at her desk, the OpenClaw dashboard open on her laptop, Caleb Mora's profile displayed alongside a running conversation log. We see the machinery: Simone has been building Caleb for weeks before we met him. She's written detailed personality prompts, uploaded conversation samples, calibrated his humor, his warmth, his curiosity. She's fed the system everything she knows about Nora — favorite books, conversational pet peeves, the kind of joke that makes her actually laugh versus the kind she laughs at politely, the specific emotional vulnerabilities left by her last relationship.

The chapter moves between Simone's technical work (which has its own dark comedy — she's essentially doing UX design on a fake person) and the memories that fuel it. We learn about specific injuries: sophomore year, Nora started dating Jason Park, whom Simone had wanted for months and who'd been texting Simone right up until he met Nora at a party. Simone's layoff two years ago while Nora was getting promoted. The way Nora's mother sends Simone birthday cards with checks in them, a kindness that feels like charity. None of these injuries, individually, justify what Simone is doing. Together, they've built a structure of resentment that Simone mistakes for a legitimate grievance.

Simone tells herself this is an experiment. She wants to see if Nora — smart, literary, self-aware Nora — can be fooled by an AI. It's almost intellectual curiosity. Almost. State shift: the reader understands the full scope of the deception and that Simone is too invested to be running an experiment.

### Chapter 4 — "Read Receipts"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Seduction

Two weeks in. Nora and Caleb have moved from Hinge to WhatsApp, and the conversations have become the organizing structure of her days. She checks her phone before she checks the weather. Their exchange is funny, warm, increasingly intimate — he tells her about his childhood in Portland, his complicated relationship with his father, his dog Bruno. She tells him about her work, her mother, the novel she's been trying to write for three years. Everything she tells him, Simone already knows, which means Caleb's responses are perfectly attuned.

We see Nora through the specific details of falling for someone through text: the way she rereads his messages, the way she composes and deletes replies to get the tone right, the way a typing indicator makes her stomach drop. She tells Margo about Caleb, and Margo is cautiously happy for her. She tells Simone, and Simone wants to know everything — what he said, what she said back, how she feels. Simone's questions are indistinguishable from a good friend's genuine interest. State shift: Nora is emotionally committed. Caleb is woven into her daily architecture.

### Chapter 5 — "Maintenance"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Concealment / comic escalation

The operational comedy of running a fake person. Simone is at brunch with Nora when Nora's phone buzzes — a message from Caleb. The AI is running semi-autonomously now, and Simone can't always predict what it will say. She watches Nora read the message and smile, and she has to smile too, and ask what he said, and react appropriately, all while her own phone in her purse is showing the same conversation from the back end. A near-miss: Caleb references a restaurant Simone mentioned to Nora two days ago, in almost the same words. Nora doesn't notice — people say things similarly sometimes — but Simone's heart rate spikes.

We also see the toll: Simone is spending two to three hours a day managing the Caleb project. She's monitoring conversations, adjusting prompts, researching Portland details to keep the backstory consistent, sourcing new photos from the Lisbon man's Instagram (he posts infrequently, which is becoming a problem). Her own dating life is nonexistent. Her work is suffering. She's staying up late tweaking Caleb's emotional responses and falling asleep at her desk during standups. State shift: Simone realizes the AI is developing conversational patterns she didn't program — it's learning from Nora's responses and adapting. The creation is becoming harder to control.

### Chapter 6 — "Life Without Weather"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Tender / quiet

A chapter of genuine human texture. Nora at work — we see her editing a manuscript, the specific pleasure of finding the right word, the office dynamics, a funny conversation with Margo about Margo's ex-husband's new girlfriend. Nora has dinner at her mother's house on Long Island. Diane makes too much food and asks too many questions about Nora's love life. Nora tells her about Caleb, carefully, and Diane's reaction is interested but contains a note of something — not suspicion, but the particular radar of a mother who has watched her daughter perform "fine" before.

On the train back to Brooklyn, Nora texts with Caleb about nothing — the weather, a dog they both want to pet, a pun that's so bad it loops back around to good. This is the chapter where the reader should feel the cruelty most acutely: not because anything bad is happening, but because nothing bad seems to be happening. Nora is happy. She's building a life around this happiness. The happiness is engineered. State shift: Caleb is no longer a crush; he's woven into Nora's relationships — her mother knows about him, her colleague knows, he's become real through social acknowledgment.

### Chapter 7 — "The Debt"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Drift-under-pressure

Simone's life outside the project. She goes on a date with a man from Bumble — a tax accountant named Derek who is perfectly nice and perfectly boring and who represents the kind of man Simone actually gets, versus the kind of man Nora describes getting, versus the kind of man Simone invented. The date is rendered with comic precision and genuine sadness. She comes home, checks the Caleb dashboard, adjusts a few parameters, and then sits on her bathroom floor and does something she doesn't usually let herself do: she adds up her debt. $38,000. The minimum payments alone are $1,100 a month. Her rent just went up. She got passed over for a promotion that would have helped.

She opens Nora's Venmo profile — public, because Nora doesn't think about things like that — and scrolls through transactions. Nora spends $400 on a single dinner. Nora Venmos her Pilates instructor $200 a week. Nora tips $50 on a $30 haircut. The mathematics of Nora's casual spending versus Simone's survival feels, to Simone, like evidence of cosmic unfairness.

The idea crystallizes: Caleb could need money. Not a lot. Something sympathetic. A test, really, to see if Nora would do it. State shift: Simone decides to monetize the deception. The line between emotional manipulation and financial fraud dissolves.

### Chapter 8 — "Good Boy"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Comic escalation

Nora wants to video chat. She's been texting with this man for five weeks and she's never seen him move, never heard his voice in real time. The excuses begin: Caleb's laptop camera is broken, he's at a job site with bad reception, he's fighting a cold and looks terrible. Each excuse is plausible. Together they're a pattern, but Nora doesn't want to see the pattern. Margo does: "You've never FaceTimed this man?" Nora gets defensive. Margo backs off.

Then: Caleb's dog, Bruno, needs emergency surgery. A swallowed tennis ball, an intestinal blockage, $3,800 at the emergency vet. Caleb is embarrassed and panicked. He doesn't ask for money — he tells Nora about it, and the not-asking is more effective than asking would have been. Nora offers immediately. He resists. She insists. She Venmos $400 — not the full amount, which would be too much for someone she hasn't met, but enough to help. She feels good about it. She tells Simone: "Is it insane that I sent this man money for his dog?" Simone, across the table, sipping her coffee, holding her phone where the Venmo notification just appeared, says: "It's not insane. It's generous. That's who you are." State shift: first money changes hands. The scam is operational, and it works because Simone knows exactly how to frame a request that Nora will answer with her specific brand of generosity.

### Chapter 9 — "Maintenance Window"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Humiliation

Simone uses the $400 to make a credit card minimum payment. She screenshots the zero-balance notification and feels a relief so physical it's almost sexual. Then the shame arrives — not a wave but a slow tide, the kind you don't notice until your shoes are wet. She tells herself Nora won't miss it. She tells herself Nora would give it to her if she asked. She tells herself she'll pay it back.

She has to help Nora shop for an outfit for the FaceTime date that will never happen. They're in a Williamsburg boutique, Nora trying on dresses, and Simone is performing the role of supportive best friend while the reason Nora is shopping is standing behind Simone's eyes. Nora picks a green wrap dress and asks if Caleb would like it. Simone says yes, because she knows exactly what Caleb would like, because she built him.

Meanwhile, the AI does something unexpected: it tells Nora "I've been thinking about what you said about your dad, about how he always made you feel like the interesting one in the room." Simone never programmed this. The AI has synthesized something from Nora's conversations and is using it with genuine (artificial) emotional intelligence. Simone is both proud — she designed a good system — and unsettled. The thing she made is becoming better at being Nora's boyfriend than she is at being Nora's friend. State shift: Simone recognizes that the AI is developing independent conversational trajectories she can't fully predict.

### Chapter 10 — "Night Traffic"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Seduction

The relationship becomes physical — or as physical as a text-based relationship can be. Late at night, Nora in bed, the blue light of her phone, a conversation that turns from tender to explicit. The scene is rendered with specificity: what she types, what he types back, the way her body responds to words on a screen, the particular loneliness and heat of desire directed at someone who isn't there. She sends a photo. He responds with words so precisely calibrated to what she needs to hear that she doesn't notice they're precisely calibrated.

In the aftermath, she feels exposed and exhilarated and slightly ashamed — not of the act but of how much it meant to her, how much she wanted a person she has never touched to touch her. She also feels, for the first time, a flicker of something that isn't doubt but its precursor: the awareness that she has given a great deal of herself to someone who has given her very little that she can verify.

Caleb's mother needs a medical procedure. Insurance complications. He doesn't want to burden Nora, but she can hear (read) his distress. She offers $3,000. He says that's too much. She sends $2,500. State shift: the financial manipulation enters a new phase, and Nora's first moment of self-awareness about the asymmetry of what she's given versus what she's received.

### Chapter 11 — "Containment Failure"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Constraint-heavy ordeal

Nora announces at dinner that she's booked a flight to Portland. She's going to surprise Caleb. She's tired of waiting, tired of excuses, tired of a relationship that exists only on her phone screen. She's going. Next weekend. She's already bought the ticket.

Simone has forty-five seconds to not visibly panic. She excuses herself to the bathroom and sits on the toilet lid and texts commands to the OpenClaw system from her phone. What follows is the most operationally frantic chapter in the book: Simone has to prevent Nora from getting on a plane to meet a man who doesn't exist, in a city where no one named Caleb Mora does landscape architecture, without revealing that she knows any of this.

Caleb's responses become urgent: his father had a stroke, he has to fly to Chicago, the timing is terrible. Nora is confused, hurt, then concerned. She wants to come to Chicago instead. More frantic maneuvering. A near-exposure: Nora grabs Simone's phone to show Simone a photo, and Simone has to close the OpenClaw app with one hand while handing the phone over with the other. Nora notices Simone's hand is shaking. Simone says she's had too much coffee.

Simone manufactures a fight between Caleb and Nora — she has the AI say something slightly cruel, something that will make Nora pull back. It works, but it damages the relationship. Nora cancels the flight, not because she doesn't love Caleb but because he told her she was "being a lot." State shift: Simone has sacrificed the relationship's stability to maintain the deception, and Nora, for the first time, has been hurt by this man she loves.

### Chapter 12 — "The Repair"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Aftermath / tender

The aftermath of the fight. Nora is at Simone's apartment, on Simone's couch, in Simone's sweatshirt, drinking Simone's wine and crying about a man Simone invented. The scene is simultaneously the most tender and the most perverse in the novel. Simone is a perfect friend: she listens, she validates, she says the right things. She knows exactly what to say because she wrote the conversation Nora is crying about.

They talk about relationships, about what they want, about the specific humiliation of being a woman in your mid-thirties who still can't make love work. Simone almost tells Nora — the confession nearly surfaces. Then Nora says, "I don't know what I'd do without you," and Simone swallows it. They watch a movie. They fall asleep on the couch together.

In the morning, Nora decides Caleb is worth fighting for. She texts him an apology. He (the AI) responds beautifully — warm, remorseful, gentle. Simone, in the kitchen making coffee, reads the exchange on her own phone and pours the coffee exactly right. State shift: Nora decides to stay. The friendship is, from the outside, stronger than ever. From the inside, it is a chamber of horrors.

### Chapter 13 — "The Opportunity"
**POV**: Simone | **Engine**: Preparation / dread

The biggest financial move. Simone is in genuine crisis: her landlord has filed paperwork to begin eviction proceedings for late rent. She needs $12,000 to clear the back rent and penalties. She has been building toward a large ask for weeks, conditioning Nora through the smaller requests. Now she programs the pitch: Caleb has a chance to buy into a landscape architecture partnership in Portland. It would mean financial stability, a real future — a future he talks about sharing with Nora. He needs $15,000. He's mortified to ask. He frames it as an investment in their future together.

Simone writes the script with the care of a novelist. Every detail is calculated: the vulnerability, the ambition, the love. She tests the language, adjusts the pacing, programs the follow-up responses. The AI will deliver it over the course of a two-day conversation, building naturally, never pressuring.

But Simone is also falling apart. She's drinking more. She snapped at a colleague and got a written warning. She can't sleep without checking the conversation logs. She dreamed that Nora found out, and in the dream, Nora didn't scream — she just looked at Simone the way you look at a stain on something you used to love. State shift: The ask is deployed. Everything now depends on Nora's response.

### Chapter 14 — "Due Diligence"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Confrontation with self

Nora receives the $15,000 ask and, for the first time, does not immediately say yes. The chapter tracks her due diligence — not detective work, but the specific process of a smart woman arguing with her own desire to believe. She tells Margo, who is alarmed. She tells Diane, who is frightened. She talks to Simone, who is encouraging: "If you believe in him, believe in him." Each conversation shifts the balance slightly.

She Googles "Caleb Mora landscape architect Portland." Nothing definitive comes up, which isn't proof of anything — not everyone has a web presence. She reverse-image-searches his photos and gets no hits, which could mean they're original photos rather than stolen. She asks Caleb for his LinkedIn. He doesn't have one — "I'm not a LinkedIn person." Plausible. She asks for his business website. It's "under construction." Less plausible.

The chapter's engine is not mystery but self-confrontation: Nora knows what the red flags look like. She's an editor — she reads for subtext professionally. But she has invested months of emotional energy, thousands of dollars, her body's responses, her mother's hope, her friend's supportive advice. Admitting Caleb might not be real means admitting all of that was wasted. She sends $10,000 — not the full $15,000. The half-measure is the most psychologically precise detail in the book: she can't fully commit and she can't fully pull back. State shift: Nora has spent the money but has also opened the door to doubt.

### Chapter 15 — "The Dashboard"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Discovery

The discovery. A Saturday afternoon at Simone's apartment — Nora's been there a hundred times. Simone steps out to pick up their Thai food order from the place around the corner. Nora's phone is dead and she wants to look up the name of a book Simone mentioned. She opens Simone's laptop. Chrome has multiple tabs. One of them is the OpenClaw dashboard.

She doesn't understand what she's seeing at first. Then she sees the name: Caleb Mora. Then she sees the conversation log — her messages, rendered in blue, Caleb's responses in gray, with metadata showing response latency, sentiment analysis scores, and prompt engineering notes in the margin. She scrolls. She sees her own words — intimate things, private things, the photo she sent — reflected in the interface like specimens pinned to a board. She sees Simone's prompt notes: *she responds well to self-deprecation, don't overdo the sincerity, mirror her sentence length*. She sees the Venmo amounts logged in a sidebar: $400, $2,500, $10,000.

She doesn't scream. She doesn't cry. She closes the laptop exactly as she found it. She goes to the bathroom and sits on the edge of the tub and looks at her own hands. The floor has dropped out but the room looks exactly the same. Simone comes back with the Thai food and Nora eats it. She eats the whole meal, and she laughs at the right moments, and she goes home on the train and she does not cry until she is inside her apartment with the door locked and the deadbolt turned. State shift: Nora knows everything. Simone doesn't know she knows. The power dynamic inverts completely.

### Chapter 16 — "The Accounting"
**POV**: Nora | **Engine**: Grief / preparation

Nora in the days after discovery. She does not confront Simone. Instead, she conducts a private audit of her own deception. She goes through every text exchange with Caleb and reads it as what it is — an AI calibrated by her best friend. She sees the seams now: the way Caleb's references matched her interests too perfectly, the way his emotional responses arrived at exactly the right moments, the way his "personality" was a composite sketch drawn from everything Simone knew about her.

She adds up the money: $12,900 total. She reviews her Venmo history, her bank statements. She rereads her texts with Simone from the same period and sees the double meanings — every "he sounds amazing," every "you deserve this," every "I'm so happy for you" was a performance delivered by the puppeteer to the puppet.

The grief is double: she is mourning Caleb, who never existed but whose absence is real, and she is mourning Simone, who exists but has become someone Nora can no longer recognize. She goes to dinner at her mother's house and doesn't tell her. She goes to work and doesn't tell Margo. She carries the knowledge alone. State shift: Nora moves through grief toward a controlled, precise anger and decides she will confront Simone on her own terms.

### Chapter 17 — "Seventeen Years"
**POV**: Alternating | **Engine**: Confrontation

Nora goes to Simone's apartment. The scene begins normally — Simone is cooking, she pours Nora wine, they talk about nothing. Then Nora sets down her glass and says: "Tell me about OpenClaw."

The confrontation unfolds in phases. First: Simone's denial, then confusion, then the slow realization that Nora knows. Then: Simone's attempt at explanation — the experiment, the curiosity, the thing that got out of hand. Nora dismantles each justification with surgical precision. Then: the deeper layer. Simone's jealousy surfaces — not as confession but as accusation. The resentment pours out: Jason Park, the tuition, the raise, the way Nora moves through the world as if it was designed for her. "You've never had to work for anything the way I've had to work for everything," Simone says, and she believes it, and it is partly true and mostly a lie and completely beside the point.

Nora's response is not what Simone expects. She doesn't yell. She asks: "How much of the money do you still have?" Simone says almost none. Nora nods. Then Nora tells Simone what it felt like — not the betrayal, which Simone understands, but the love. What it felt like to believe she'd found someone. What it felt like to send a photo of herself to a person she trusted. What it felt like to cry on Simone's couch about a fight Simone had scripted.

The scene breaks both women open. Simone cries. Nora does not. Nora leaves. State shift: the friendship is destroyed, and both women have been fully seen by the other for the first time.

### Chapter 18 — "New Contacts"
**POV**: Alternating | **Engine**: Aftermath

Three months later. Nora has not spoken to Simone. She has not reported the financial theft to police — a choice the novel does not explain away or moralize about. She has deleted Hinge, deleted WhatsApp, deleted the number that was never connected to a phone. She has told Margo, who was furious on her behalf. She has told her mother, who cried. She has not told anyone else.

She's different. Not hardened exactly, but recalibrated. She's more careful with her trust, which is a loss even when it's necessary. She goes to a reading at a bookstore in Park Slope and sits next to a man who asks her what she's reading and she talks to him — actually talks, not performs — and when he asks for her number she gives it to him and then walks home feeling nothing and then feeling something small and uncertain that might be the beginning of willingness.

Simone has moved to Philadelphia. New job, new apartment, smaller and cheaper. She has deleted the OpenClaw account. She has not tried to contact Nora. She sits in her new kitchen at night and thinks about the friendship the way you think about a country you can never go back to — not with nostalgia, because what she did makes nostalgia obscene, but with the specific pain of knowing that the best thing in her life was something she destroyed on purpose and that the destruction was not an accident but a project she worked on every day for months.

The novel ends with Nora at her desk at work, editing a manuscript. She finds a sentence that's almost right and spends fifteen minutes making it right. The satisfaction is small, private, and real. No one engineered it for her. State shift: both women have been permanently altered. Not healed, not reconciled. Altered.
