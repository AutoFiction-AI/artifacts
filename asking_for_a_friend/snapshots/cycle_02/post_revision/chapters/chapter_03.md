# Chapter 3

The notification arrived at 9:47 p.m.

*i think i found a human person on this app??*

Simone read it twice from her desk chair, which had a broken tilt mechanism she'd been meaning to fix for three months and which now locked at a permanent backward lean that made her lower back ache by midnight. The OpenClaw dashboard was open on her MacBook, Nora's messages arriving in blue on the left panel, Caleb Mora's AI-generated responses in gray on the right, with sentiment scores and response-latency metadata running in a thin column between them. Caleb was performing well. His sarcasm frequency — which Simone had spent two hours recalibrating on Saturday afternoon, her coffee going cold, her Spotify paused because she needed quiet for precision work — was landing at exactly the right rate, enough to seem human and not enough to seem like he was trying. His emotional-attunement rating for this conversation thread was 0.87, which was high but not suspiciously high. His reference-matching — the way he dropped mentions of books and music that aligned with Nora's taste without ever landing on an exact favorite — was holding at 82 percent. Simone had learned through three weeks of testing that 82 was the sweet spot. Higher than that and the coincidences started to feel curated. Lower and the spark didn't catch.

She minimized the dashboard and opened WhatsApp.

*omg show me his profile immediately*

She knew exactly what the profile looked like. She'd built it in this chair, over the course of nine evenings in September, with the window cracked to let in the last of the warm air and the floral candle — the one she burned to mask the smell of the microwave salmon she shouldn't have cooked — lit on the desk beside the laptop. She'd written every prompt answer. She'd selected every photo. She'd tested his conversational voice against transcripts of the men Nora had dated and the men Nora said she wished she'd dated and the men Nora described as her type when she was being honest versus her type when she was being aspirational, which were different types, and which Simone, after seventeen years, could distinguish by the pitch of Nora's voice when she talked about them.

The screenshots came in a burst — three, four, five images in rapid succession. Nora texted fast when she was excited, the messages piling up in stacks of two and three before Simone could reply, and Simone had logged this pattern weeks ago along with seventy-some others in a spreadsheet she kept in a folder on her desktop labeled "Freelance Project."

Caleb's profile. His prompt answers. His photos — the hiking shot with the backpack slung over one shoulder, the cooking shot where he was mid-laugh with a wooden spoon, the one with the medium-sized mutt that Simone had spent four days searching for because she needed a man with a dog who wasn't posing with the dog in a way that signaled *I'm using this animal to get laid.* She'd found the photos on a private Instagram — a thirty-year-old architect in Lisbon named Marco Henriques, forty-seven followers, posted roughly once every three weeks, had no apparent connection to anyone in the Western Hemisphere. The account was private, which meant the photos wouldn't appear in a reverse-image search. His face was good. Handsome but attainable — no superhero jawline, no gym selfies, just a warm-looking guy in natural light who smiled like he didn't know the camera was on him. Exactly the kind of face that made women on dating apps think *this one looks real.*

Simone studied the screenshots as if she'd never seen them before.

*holy shit he's cute. What did he say??*

She could see what he'd said. The conversation was still running on the left panel of her screen, each new message populating in real time with a soft chime she'd set to silent two days ago because the sound was starting to feel like a heartbeat. But she needed Nora to tell her. That was the architecture of the thing — Nora telling Simone everything, Simone feeding it back into the system, the system using it to become more precisely the person Nora wanted, and Nora never seeing the loop because the loop looked exactly like friendship.

*ok ok ok*, Nora typed. *so he opened with this thing about the Ishiguro on my shelf and I KNOW it sounds like a pickup line but it wasn't? it was like he actually had a take? and then I pushed back and he DISAGREED with me, but nicely, like he'd actually thought about it and wasn't just trying to seem smart*

Simone knew about the Ishiguro take. She'd written it. She'd calibrated it against Nora's reading preferences — specifically, the way Nora responded to men who had opinions about books. Not men who listed impressive titles, which Nora found transparently performative, but men who said something specific enough to be argued with. She'd prompted Caleb's personality matrix: *When discussing books, take a position that is slightly unexpected but defensible. Do not agree with the user's taste — engage with it. Match sentence length to the user's within 20 percent. Use one self-deprecating qualifier per exchange to signal intellectual confidence without dominance.*

*he sounds amazing*, Simone typed. *tell me everything.*

And Nora did. For the next forty minutes, while Simone watched the same conversation from both ends — Nora's breathless recap arriving in WhatsApp on the right side of her desk, the raw data scrolling through the dashboard on the left — Nora told her about the chemistry, the easy rhythm — Caleb's questions that felt like he already knew the shape of the answer and was curious about the color. She used the word "effortless" twice. Simone had designed for effortless. Effortless was the most labor-intensive quality to engineer.

*I'm so happy for you!!*, she typed back. *Don't overthink it. Just let it happen.*

She put the phone down and switched to the dashboard's analytics tab. The sentiment graph was a green line climbing in small, steady increments, each data point corresponding to a message. Nora's engagement rate was 94 percent — meaning 94 percent of Caleb's messages had received a substantive reply rather than a minimal acknowledgment. Her average response time had decreased from four minutes at the start of the conversation to under ninety seconds by the end. Her message length had increased by 40 percent.

This was what falling looked like from the back end. A green line going up.

---

Simone had started the Caleb project in the first week of September, a month before Nora downloaded Hinge. She'd told herself at the time that she was exploring the platform — OpenClaw had been generating attention in the UX community for its conversation-design tools, and her startup was evaluating AI agent integrations for their customer-facing chat product. She was doing professional development. She was keeping current.

The first night, she'd set up the account and spent an hour clicking through the interface: agent personality builder, conversation memory settings, context-document uploads, response-style toggles that ranged from "professional" to "intimate." It was good. Better than she'd expected. The personality builder operated on a branching-parameter model — you set high-level traits like warmth, humor, directness, and then drilled down into situational responses: how does the agent handle disagreement, how does it respond to vulnerability, what does it do when it doesn't understand the question. Each branch had a natural-language prompt field where you could write specific instructions in plain English. The system ingested these and generated a behavioral matrix that governed the agent's output across conversations.

She'd created a test agent called "TestBot" and talked to it for twenty minutes. It was fine. Competent, responsive, a little flat. Like talking to a customer-service rep who'd been trained on empathy scripts but didn't have any of the underlying emotion. She could see the seams.

She'd closed the laptop and gone to bed. Then she'd opened it again at 1 a.m. and created a second agent and named him Caleb.

She didn't know why Caleb. She'd decide later that it was the kind of name that sounded both casual and textured — two syllables, ended open, suggested a specific kind of dark-haired outdoorsy man without trying as hard as Hunter or Ridge. But in the moment she wasn't thinking about it strategically. She was sitting up in bed with the laptop on her thighs, the blue light catching the glass of the window where the Williamsburg night was doing what it did — someone yelling about an Uber, bass from the bar three doors down on Bedford, the particular pitch of drunk laughter that meant it was past midnight. She typed the name and it felt right and she kept going.

The personality prompts came easier than they should have. She started with the basics: age 32, landscape architect, Portland, Oregon. The details were chosen to be verifiable only at a distance — Portland was far enough from New York that an in-person meeting would require planning and plane tickets, and landscape architecture was specific enough to seem like a real career but diffuse enough that Nora, who knew nothing about it, couldn't spot a fake credential. Then she went deeper.

*Humor: warm, self-deprecating, gentle. Slightly goofy when comfortable. Does not use sarcasm as a weapon — uses it as self-directed deflection. Makes fun of himself more than others. Laughs easily and without self-consciousness.*

That was Nora's preference. Simone knew this because she'd listened to Nora describe what she wanted in a man approximately three hundred times across seventeen years of friendship, and the descriptions had been remarkably consistent even when the men changed. Nora wanted someone who was smart without performing smartness, funny without weaponizing humor, emotionally available without being a project. She wanted someone who asked questions and actually listened to the answers. She wanted someone who was good but not boring about it.

Simone could've written this list in her sleep. She'd been collecting the data since they were eighteen.

*Conversation style: match the user's message length within ±20%. Mirror her punctuation habits — she uses dashes and ellipses, she rarely uses exclamation points unless genuinely excited, she capitalizes for emphasis rather than using emojis. Ask follow-up questions that reference specific details from previous messages. Never open with a compliment about appearance. Open with a compliment about her mind.*

The context documents took longer. Simone typed everything she had into the system's free-form context fields with the thoroughness of someone who did this kind of information architecture for a living and had been doing it personally for seventeen years. Nora's favorite books — the real list, not the performing-taste-on-a-dating-profile list: Didion, Offill, Ferrante, the Denis Johnson story collection she'd read four times and kept on her nightstand even though the spine was cracked. Her conversational pet peeves: men who said "actually" before correcting her, men who interrupted, men who asked what she was reading and then said "oh I've been meaning to read that" without any follow-up, which Nora called the number-one indicator that a man didn't actually read. The kind of joke that made her actually laugh — absurdist, specific, built on an unexpected comparison — versus the kind she laughed at to be polite — puns, dad jokes, anything that telegraphed its own cleverness. Her emotional vulnerabilities: the ex-boyfriend, Marcus, who'd left not with a fight or a betrayal but with a slow withdrawal that had made her question whether she'd imagined the entire relationship. Her father, who had moved to Seattle when she was twelve — not dead, just gone, calling on birthdays and showing up at her NYU graduation as though distance were the same as a good reason — and who Nora still talked about with a tenderness that made Simone want to hold her or scream. Her quiet conviction that she was somehow too much and not enough at the same time, which she'd confessed to Simone at 2 a.m. after a bottle of Sancerre two years ago and which she'd never said to anyone else.

Simone typed all of this into the system on a Tuesday night in September. Her candle had burned down to a pool of wax. Her back was a fist of pain. She saved the project and closed the laptop and sat in the dark for a minute, listening to the traffic on Bedford, and thought: *I should delete this.*

She didn't delete it.

Over the next three weeks, she refined Caleb the way she'd refine any product. She ran test conversations, playing Nora's side based on the years of texting transcripts she had stored in her head, then evaluating Caleb's responses against a rubric she'd handwritten on a legal pad: *Does this sound like a person? Does it sound like the person she'd want? Is the emotional calibration believable? Where are the seams?*

The seams were the problem. In the first version, Caleb agreed too much — he reflected Nora's opinions back without any friction, and the effect was like talking to a flattering mirror. But real people disagreed. Real people had preferences that didn't align perfectly. So Simone introduced what she started calling "productive friction": she instructed the AI to hold independent opinions on certain topics. Coffee — he was a pourover snob, which Nora would find both ridiculous and endearing. Running — he ran but didn't treat it as a religion, a position that aligned with Nora's distaste for men who made a personality out of their fitness routines. His mother — complicated, loving, the kind of closeness that came braided with guilt, which would resonate with Nora's relationship with Diane. She gave him a few deliberate blind spots: he didn't know much about contemporary literary fiction beyond the bestsellers, which meant Nora could recommend things to him, and recommending books was one of Nora's primary love languages.

He was slightly disorganized. He took a while to respond to logistical questions, which read as charming spaciness rather than system latency.

She tested the Hinge profile on a dummy account, swiping through it as if she were Nora. The photos were right — she'd spent an entire weekend on Marco Henriques's Instagram, selecting images that told a coherent story: active, employed, loved his dog, had friends but didn't need to prove it. The prompt answers had been the hardest part. Hinge prompts were deceptively difficult — they needed to be specific enough to seem like a real person had written them but not so specific that they could be fact-checked. She'd written and rewritten his answer to "A life goal of mine" seven times before landing on something about wanting to design a public park in a neighborhood that didn't have one, which hit exactly the frequency of ambition-plus-social-conscience that made Nora's attention lock in.

The last thing she did before activating the profile was set the response latency. OpenClaw's default was near-instant, which was wrong — no human being texted that fast on a consistent basis. She programmed a variable delay: two to seven minutes for casual conversation, forty-five seconds to two minutes when the exchange was rapid and playful, twelve to twenty minutes for heavy or emotional topics. She'd mapped this to her understanding of how men actually texted. The slower emotional responses were especially important. A man who answered instantly when a woman said *my dad left when I was twelve* would feel wrong. A man who went quiet for fourteen minutes and then came back with something short and careful would feel real.

She activated the Hinge profile on October 3rd. Nora downloaded Hinge on October 5th. The two-day gap was fortunate — it meant Caleb's profile would look like a recent arrival, not suspiciously fresh. Or the gap wasn't fortunate. Simone had been nudging Nora toward the apps for a month, engineering the weather the way she'd later engineer the man: a comment about her coworker who'd met a boyfriend on Hinge, a link to an article about dating in your thirties texted with the caption *lol found this thought of you*, the question on the subway platform after the engagement party — *when was the last time you went on a date?* — delivered with enough casual concern to be indistinguishable from friendship.

She hadn't forced anything. She'd made the water flow downhill.

---

The candle had gone out. The room smelled like the wax underneath the floral scent — something burnt, something cloying. Simone got up, filled a glass of water from the kitchen faucet, which was four steps from her desk because everything in this apartment was four steps from everything else, and stood at the window.

She could hear the bar across the street running last call. Chairs scraping against a floor. A couple on the sidewalk, the woman leaning into the man's shoulder with the weight of someone who'd had enough wine to let herself lean, and the man's arm coming around her back in a gesture so practiced it was either love or its long-term replacement. Simone watched them pass the window and disappear toward the intersection.

She should have felt guilty. The knowledge sat at the periphery, like the dirty window she was looking through — visible if she focused on it, invisible if she focused past it. She should have felt something like remorse, or at least the variety of squeamish discomfort that functional people were supposed to experience when they did something wrong. She catalogued its absence the way she catalogued everything: a data point, noted, filed.

What she felt instead was closer to satisfaction, and underneath the satisfaction, something older, harder, and more structural that she'd been carrying so long she rarely noticed its weight.

Jason Park. Sophomore year. She'd met him in a sociology seminar and spent three weeks building what she'd later understand was a crush constructed on the certainty that he was about to ask her out. The evidence had been persuasive: the study sessions that ran long, the coffees that were always his suggestion, his hand on the inside of her arm when he was making a point about Durkheim. She'd told Nora about it — she told Nora everything then — she was nineteen and hadn't yet learned that information was currency. She'd described the arm-touching, the study coffees, his startled laugh when she said something that surprised him. Nora had been encouraging. Nora had said he sounded great.

Then Nora went to a party at the Sigma Chi house and Jason Park was there and three days later they were dating.

Nora hadn't stolen him. Simone could be precise about this, even now. The language of stealing implied that Jason had been hers, and he hadn't been — he'd been a man who bought her coffee and then walked into a party and saw someone else and wanted her more. The someone else was Nora. It was always Nora, or someone shaped like Nora — someone who walked into rooms as though rooms had been expecting her, someone whose ease wasn't performed but constitutional — her hand loose on a glass stem, her laugh arriving before she'd decided whether she should.

Simone could feel herself watching from the outside of things even when she was standing in the middle. She could feel the calculation happening behind her face while Nora was already saying the right thing without calculating at all. This wasn't Nora's fault. Simone had understood this at nineteen. But understanding a thing wasn't the same as being freed from it, and by now the knowledge had calcified into something harder than resentment. Something more architectural. Something she'd built the rest of herself on top of.

Jason and Nora dated for five months. Nora broke up with him because he talked about himself too much and had never finished a book she recommended, which were exactly the qualities Simone had been willing to overlook because when he'd touched the inside of her arm during that seminar discussion the specific warmth of his hand had been the first time in her life she'd felt chosen. Nora broke up with him like she broke up with everyone — thoughtfully, gently, with a conversation that left the other person feeling like the relationship had been meaningful rather than wasted. Then she came to Simone's dorm room and ate Simone's crackers and said she thought she just wanted to be single for a while, and Simone said, "Good, he didn't deserve you," and she meant both things at once: that Jason Park didn't deserve Nora, and that Nora didn't know what it felt like to not be chosen because she'd never had to learn.

Fuck Jason Park. Not for choosing Nora — everyone chose Nora — but for making the arithmetic visible.

The tuition was the bigger one. Not bigger because it was a worse injury — it wasn't an injury at all, really, just a condition — but because it was the first time Simone understood that her life and Nora's life, which had looked parallel at eighteen, were actually running on different tracks at different grades, and the gap was structural, not circumstantial, and it would widen.

Nora's parents paid for NYU. All of it. Tuition, housing, the spending money that appeared in Nora's checking account with the regularity of weather, unremarked and unremarkable. Nora didn't discuss it. She didn't not discuss it either. It simply was — one of the background conditions of Nora's existence, like her curly hair or her good skin or her mother picking up the phone before the second ring.

Simone's parents had nothing. Her father managed a hardware store in Bensonhurst. Her mother did bookkeeping for three small businesses out of the kitchen, the adding machine next to the toaster. Their poverty didn't produce good college-application essays — it was invisible and embarrassing, the kind where you owned a house and drove a car and had a television but the credit cards were always close to the limit and Christmas came out of layaway and college was a thing that happened only if someone else was paying for it. Simone's someone else was the federal government, which lent her $22,000 at an interest rate that seemed manageable at eighteen and was an anchor at thirty-five.

She remembered the afternoon she'd first understood the math. Senior year, April. She and Nora at a bar near campus, splitting a pitcher because Simone couldn't afford her own. Nora mentioned that her parents were taking her to Italy for graduation. Simone said something funny about being jealous — she couldn't remember the joke, only that Nora had laughed — and then she went to the bathroom and sat on the toilet lid and added up her loans on her phone calculator. Twenty-two thousand dollars. She was twenty-one. The interest was already compounding. She didn't know yet that this number would attach itself to her life the way a body attached itself to a skeleton — invisible from the outside, bearing all the weight.

Nora came back from Italy with a tan and a leather handbag her mother had found in Florence. Simone came home from a temp job at an insurance company with a paycheck that went to the first loan payment, which didn't cover the interest that had accrued since graduation. They never discussed it. They never needed to. The gap was there between them, legible as a border, and Nora couldn't see it because you couldn't see a border you'd never had to cross.

Then: two years ago. October, like now — the light going gold and thin through the window at the same angle. Simone's startup ran a round of layoffs it called a "restructuring," a word that she'd learned to decode as meaning the company was dying but wanted to die slowly enough to close another funding round first. She was cut on a Thursday afternoon. Her manager, a twenty-eight-year-old named Kyle who wore Patagonia vests to the office and used "bandwidth" as a verb, told her the decision wasn't a reflection of her performance. A woman from HR walked her down eleven floors in the elevator without speaking. Simone packed her laptop bag in the office kitchen because her desk was in the open-plan area and she didn't want an audience.

She called Nora from the sidewalk, standing on Sixth Avenue with her bag on one shoulder and the October wind pulling at her jacket. Nora was at work — her office, her desk, her stable, un-restructured job — and she said all the right things. "That's such bullshit." "They don't deserve you." "Come over tonight, I'll make pasta." Nora made pasta and Simone drank her wine and they watched something on Nora's couch, something funny that Simone couldn't remember afterward because what she remembered was the apartment. Nora's apartment. A one-bedroom with bookshelves and framed prints on the walls and a kitchen where the stove had four burners, and Nora paid for it alone on an editor's salary and didn't think about it, the way you didn't think about oxygen when you had enough of it.

Three weeks later, Nora called to say she'd been promoted to senior editor. Her voice did the thing it did when she was excited but also checking whether she was allowed to be excited given the circumstances — a calibration of warmth and caution that was, Simone could acknowledge, genuinely considerate. "Is it weird that I'm telling you this right now?" Nora asked. "Given everything." Simone told her it wasn't weird. She told her she deserved it. She told her she was so happy for her. She was sitting at this desk, in this apartment, in the same fucking sweatpants she'd worn for three days, with a personal loan application open on her laptop because unemployment didn't cover rent and the credit card minimums were nine days from due. She hung up and didn't cry. She opened Indeed and searched for UX jobs and found something at another startup within a month and the month in between was the closest she'd come to drowning while technically standing on solid ground.

Diane Kaplan's birthday cards came every October. Nora's mother sent them to Simone with a fifty-dollar check — the same amount every year, as reliable as the season — and a note in the careful cursive of a retired English teacher who still cared about handwriting: *Happy birthday, Simone. We're so glad Nora has you.* The money was nothing to Diane. Grocery money. A gesture made from the surplus of a woman who had never needed to check her balance before writing a check because the answer had always been enough. Simone cashed the check every year and felt the specific compound sensation of being grateful and being pitied and being unable to tell where one ended and the other began.

She was aware that this accounting — this ledger she maintained in the part of her brain where other people stored song lyrics or prayer — was not entirely fair. She was aware that Nora hadn't chosen her parents' money or her face or the quality of ease that made rooms rearrange themselves around her. She was aware that resentment this detailed, this carefully maintained, this curated, was its own kind of project — something she worked on with the same attention and rigor she brought to the OpenClaw dashboard. She could see the distortion in her own lens. She could map exactly where the grievance outpaced the evidence.

But seeing a distortion wasn't the same as correcting for it. Jason Park had wanted Nora more. The money had always been easier for Nora. The promotion had landed while Simone was drowning. These were facts, and the fact that they didn't justify what she was doing didn't stop them from explaining it, and on nights like this — her apartment dark except for the laptop's blue glow, the street noise softening, the dashboard running in standby — the explanation felt like enough.

---

She wasn't going to think about money tonight. She thought about money anyway — involuntarily, constantly, with the specific vigilance of someone monitoring a wound that wouldn't close.

Thirty-eight thousand dollars. That was the total. She didn't need the spreadsheet; the number pulsed in her body, always countable if she paused to count. Twenty-two thousand in federal student loans, which she'd been paying the minimum on for thirteen years and which had barely moved because the interest rate was 6.8 percent and compounding did exactly what the financial literacy pamphlets said it would, except the pamphlets had made it sound like a lesson and in practice it was more like a leak in the ceiling that dripped faster than she could empty the bucket. Eleven thousand across three credit cards, two of which were at their limits. Five thousand on a personal loan she'd signed during the layoff to cover two months of rent, at an interest rate she hadn't read carefully because the alternative was not paying rent, and the alternative to not paying rent was something she hadn't been able to think about then and couldn't think about now.

Her minimum monthly payments were $1,100. Her rent was $2,400, raised from $2,150 in August because her landlord, a man named Grunwald who communicated exclusively through letters slid under her door on yellow legal paper, had decided the market supported an increase. After taxes, after the minimums, after the rent, after her phone and her MetroCard and the groceries she bought at the discount place on Grand Street where the produce was a day from turning but a dollar cheaper — after everything, she had roughly three hundred dollars a month for the rest of her life. Three hundred fucking dollars. For clothes. For restaurants. For the brunches with Nora where she ordered the cheapest thing and tipped to the penny and split the check evenly and said "I've got it" when Nora offered to cover her, because the offer — which came from genuine generosity, uncomplicated kindness, the reflex of a person who had never had to count — made Simone want to put her forehead through the table.

She earned $95,000 a year. More than Nora, technically — Nora's publishing salary was around $85,000, a fact Nora had mentioned over wine one night with the casual indifference of a person describing adequate weather. Simone made ten thousand more in gross and had roughly seventy thousand less in net, and the arithmetic of this — two women of the same age with similar salaries living in the same city in entirely different countries — was the thing that had turned the OpenClaw project from a late-night curiosity into something she sat down to work on every evening with the discipline and focus she brought to her actual job. More focus, honestly. Her actual job hadn't felt like it mattered in months.

It was an experiment. She was comfortable with this framing and she returned to it now — out of habit, like the desk chair, because the alternative was standing. It was an experiment in social engineering, in AI capability, in the question of whether a smart, well-read, professionally skeptical woman could be fooled by a machine that had been fed the right data. It was an intellectual exercise. It was professional development — understanding the capabilities of AI-generated conversation was directly relevant to her work in fintech UX, and the fact that her test subject was her best friend was a matter of data quality. She had more data on Nora than she had on anyone alive. It would've been a waste not to use it.

She didn't fully believe this. She believed it enough to keep going, the way a person driving on a spare tire believed it was fine because the tire was still holding and the mechanic was only another few miles. She could feel the wobble but the car was moving.

The alternative framings were available to her. She kept them in a locked compartment, filed next to the personal loan paperwork in the bottom drawer of her desk — accessible if she needed them, not where she had to see them every day. The alternative framings were: she was catfishing her best friend. She was building a weapon out of seventeen years of intimacy. She was doing something to Nora that, if their positions were reversed, would end the friendship so completely that even the past would be retroactively contaminated — every shared memory, every night on the couch, every *I don't know what I'd do without you* rewritten as the prologue to betrayal.

But their positions weren't reversed. Their positions had never been reversed, not once in seventeen years. That was the whole point.

She closed the laptop. The screen went dark and her face appeared in it — a woman with a sharp bob and tired eyes and the angular features that men called "striking," which was what they said when they meant *not my type but I can see why someone might.* She looked at herself for a second. Then she looked past herself at the dark apartment reflected behind her, the studio that cost $2,400 a month and was smaller than Nora's living room.

In the kitchen — four steps — she ran the water until it was cold enough and drank a full glass standing at the sink. Her phone was on the desk, faceup, and even from here she could see the screen light up.

One more text from Nora. She walked over and read it.

*I know it's way too early to say this but I have a really good feeling about this one, Si. Like. A really good feeling.*

Simone dried her hands on the dish towel that hung from the oven handle — a dish towel printed with a faded Italian flag that her mother had given her when she moved in, a small domestic object from a woman who showed love through small domestic objects because the larger ones cost money she didn't have. She picked up the phone.

*You deserve a really good feeling*, she typed. *Don't overthink it. Just let it happen.*

She sent it. Then she set her alarm for 7 a.m. She had a standup at nine and she needed to review the conversation logs before then — adjust parameters based on how Nora had responded tonight, make sure Caleb's next message, scheduled for tomorrow afternoon and timed to suggest a full day of real work followed by the thought of her, hit the right register. There was a problem with his responses to direct questions about his business she needed to fix: he was being too detailed when Nora asked about specific projects, which was a tell, because real people were vaguer about their own work than about their passions. She needed to dial back the informational density of his professional answers and push up the emotional warmth of his personal ones. She wrote this on the legal pad by the laptop, added it to tomorrow's list, and turned off the desk lamp.

The apartment was dark. The street noise was fading — the bar across the street had closed, the sidewalk emptied, the neighborhood settling into the specific quiet that Williamsburg achieved at 1 a.m. on a weeknight, which wasn't really quiet but was the closest it came. The dead candle smelled like old flowers. Simone lay in the dark in the bed that was six feet from the desk that was four feet from the kitchen and thought about nothing, which was a skill she'd developed, and then she thought about the green line on the sentiment graph, rising, and then she thought: *I'll stop before it goes too far.*

She didn't know where too far was, exactly. But she'd know it when she reached it. She'd always been good at identifying edge cases. It was literally her job.

She fell asleep around 12:40. On the desk, the MacBook ran in sleep mode, the dashboard paused, Caleb's last message to Nora glowing on the screen in gray text: *I'm really glad we matched. Talk tomorrow?*

Nora had already replied. Her message sat in blue on the left panel, timestamped 11:52 p.m., with a sentiment score of 0.94 out of 1.0.

*I'm really glad we matched too.*

The green line held steady. The laptop hummed. Outside the window, Bedford Avenue was empty, and the October air smelled like the last of something warm giving way to the first of something cold, and in the morning Simone would wake up and open the dashboard and begin again.
