# Chapter 11

Simone was thinking about edge cases when Nora said the thing that broke the evening in half.

Not edge cases generally — a specific one. She and the platform team had spent the afternoon debugging a checkout flow where users who entered an expired coupon code got kicked to a 404 page instead of getting an inline error message, which meant they had to restart the entire purchase, which meant 23 percent of them abandoned the cart entirely. Classic failure of error handling. You think you've accounted for every user path and then some idiot types HOLIDAY2023 into a field in January and the whole flow detonates.

She was thinking about this because it was easier than thinking about the OpenClaw dashboard notification she'd silenced forty minutes ago — a sentiment deviation flag on the Caleb system. The AI had sent Nora a message about missing Portland in winter, the fog that came in off the river, the way everything smelled like wet pine, and Simone hadn't written that. She'd built the broad parameters for Portland nostalgia, but the sensory details were the system's own interpolation from data Nora had shared about loving fall in the Northeast. The AI was getting better at being from Portland than Simone was at making it be from Portland. A development she needed to address, but not now, because now she was eating pappardelle across from her best friend at the Italian place on Metropolitan and the thing she needed most in the world was for this to be a normal Tuesday.

"So I did a thing," Nora said.

She said it the way she always said it when she was about to reveal something she was excited about but slightly defensive of — she looked down at her wine, then up, then tilted her head in the way that meant *I know this might sound crazy but I need you to be on my side*. Simone had seen this expression maybe two hundred times across seventeen years. It preceded the announcement that Nora was quitting her first publishing job. It preceded the confession that she'd slept with the author she was editing. It preceded the Hinge download. Simone had always been the person this face was aimed at, the person whose response mattered, and she had always given the right response, because she was good at this and because she loved Nora and because these two things, the skill and the love, had not yet torn each other apart.

"What kind of thing?" Simone asked. Warm. Curious. The exact right amount of I-already-support-this.

Nora pulled her phone from her bag, tapped something, and turned the screen around. It was a flight confirmation. Alaska Airlines. JFK to PDX. Departing Saturday, January 18th. Returning Tuesday the 21st.

"I booked a flight to Portland," Nora said. "I'm going to surprise him."

The pappardelle sat in Simone's stomach like something with edges. She felt the information enter her body the way you feel the first second of a car accident — the world going glassy and specific, every detail sharpening into itself. The waiter two tables over straightening a fork. The Lucinda Williams song coming through the ceiling speakers, something about a car and a lake road. The overhead light catching the oil on the surface of Nora's water glass.

She had about forty-five seconds to not visibly panic.

"Oh my god," Simone said, and the warmth in her voice was so convincing that she almost believed it came from the part of her that loved Nora rather than the part that was already running triage. "Nor, that's — when did you decide this?"

"Today. I was at my desk and I just — I'm so tired of waiting, Si. We've been doing this for three months and I've never even heard his voice in real time and I know, I know, there are reasons, but at some point the reasons are just — they're a pattern, right? And I don't want to be the kind of person who lets a pattern talk her out of something real."

"Right. No, totally."

Her mind was moving very fast in a direction that had nothing to do with the conversation she was performing. Caleb Mora was text on servers. He had a Hinge profile, a VoIP number, a Venmo account. He did not have a body. He did not have an apartment in Portland or a dog named Bruno. If Nora got on that plane, the entire architecture of the past three months would collapse in the time it took her to ring a doorbell that no one she loved would answer.

Nora was looking at her with wet eyes and a lifted chin — the look of someone who wanted to be told she was brave.

"I'm proud of you," Simone said. The words came out steady. The fork in her hand was steady. Underneath the table, her left knee was bouncing so hard the silverware on her side was vibrating against the wood. "This is — I mean, this is a big step."

"I know. I'm kind of terrified? But I feel like if I don't do this now I'm going to keep finding reasons not to, and then I'll be one of those people who has this whole imaginary relationship with someone she's never actually been in the same room with, and that's — I can't be that."

*You already are*, Simone thought, and the thought was so clean and so cruel that she flinched at her own mind.

"Have you told him?" she asked instead, which was the right question — the question a good friend would ask — and also the question she needed the answer to most urgently, because the system's response protocol was a different crisis depending on whether Nora had already texted.

"Not yet. I want it to be a surprise. I was thinking I'd text him Thursday and say I had a work trip that got rerouted or something, and then show up." Nora's face was doing the thing it did when she was constructing a plan she knew was slightly unhinged but couldn't stop herself from building. "Is that insane? Am I being insane?"

"It's not insane. It's romantic."

"It's a little insane."

"It's romantic-insane. The good kind."

Simone picked up her wine and drank. The Montepulciano was too warm and slightly corked and she barely tasted it. She was building a decision tree in her head: paths branching, costs assigned, risk surfaces mapped. If Nora texted Thursday, that gave Simone two days. If Nora texted tonight, she had hours. Either way, Caleb had to not be in Portland when Nora arrived. Caleb had to not be anywhere Nora could get to. Caleb had to be in a crisis so sympathetic that suggesting Nora come to him would feel inappropriate.

An emergency. A family emergency.

"Excuse me," Simone said. She put her napkin on the table and picked up her phone with what she hoped was the casual ease of someone who needed to pee and not the rigid precision of someone whose hands were about to do the most important work of her life. "Bathroom."

"I'll be right here," Nora said, "emotionally spiraling."

Simone smiled at her. The warmth was automatic. She was very good at this — she had become so good at performing the friend that the performance was indistinguishable from the thing itself, even to her, especially to her, and there were whole minutes of every day when she forgot she was lying, which were the minutes she felt most human, which meant her humanity had become contingent on the lie, which was a thought she could not afford to have right now because right now she needed to get to the fucking bathroom.

---

The bathroom at Lucia's had two stalls, both empty, and a mirror with a crack across the top right corner that had been there since 2022. Simone locked herself in the larger stall, sat on the toilet lid, and opened the OpenClaw app.

The dashboard loaded. The Lucinda Williams song bled through the wall. The radiator clanked. Caleb Mora's profile filled the screen: conversation activity, sentiment metrics, the timeline of tonight's exchange. The last message Nora had received was *Can't stop thinking about you today. Everything at the site reminded me of that thing you said about how cities that plant trees along the sidewalks are cities that believe in the future.*

The AI had generated that — not Simone. She'd written the parameters, but the words were the system's own. Nora was falling in love with Simone's creation's creation. She didn't have time for this.

She opened the command interface. The system allowed direct prompt overrides — she'd built that capability in after the first time the AI had improvised something she hadn't anticipated, back in November, when it told Nora something about her father that had been startlingly perceptive and that Simone had definitely not programmed. The override let her inject narrative elements into Caleb's next response sequence. She'd used it four times. Three of those had been minor corrections — adjusting a detail about Portland geography, recalibrating the frequency of his texts so he didn't seem too available. The fourth time she'd had him mention his mother's health in a way that laid the groundwork for the $2,500 ask.

This was the fifth time. She typed fast, her thumbs clumsy on the screen:

*PRIORITY OVERRIDE: Deploy family emergency. Caleb's father has had a stroke. Caleb must fly to Chicago immediately. Family crisis will require at minimum one week of reduced availability. Emotional register: scared, overwhelmed, guilty about not being present, unable to make plans. DO NOT engage with any discussion of Nora visiting Portland or meeting in person. If asked, redirect to the severity of the medical situation. If pushed, express that he cannot handle logistics right now.*

She reviewed the override. The stroke was good — serious enough to require immediate travel, medical enough that Nora wouldn't question the disruption, open-ended enough to buy time. Chicago was good — Caleb's father was supposed to live in the suburbs of Chicago, she'd established that in a conversation weeks ago, a detail Nora wouldn't remember precisely but would accept. The key constraint was timing: when should the message arrive? If it came now, while Simone was in the bathroom, the coincidence might register. If it came later tonight, after Nora was home, Simone wouldn't be able to monitor Nora's reaction in real time.

She set the delivery for twenty minutes. That gave her time to get back to the table, order another glass of wine, and be visibly present when Nora's phone buzzed.

Her hands were shaking. She looked at them — her fingers against the phone case, the chipped nail polish on her left index finger that she hadn't had time to fix because she'd been up until two in the morning adjusting the AI's response latency to seem more natural — and the shaking was a fact she noted and filed. She could not shake at the table. At the table she was warm and supportive and excited about the Portland trip. At the table she was Simone, Nora's best friend, the person who wanted Nora to be happy. In this bathroom stall, sitting on a toilet she hadn't used in a restaurant where she'd had dozens of normal dinners, she was someone she still couldn't name, and the distance between that woman and the one she performed was getting smaller every week.

She flushed the toilet she hadn't used, washed her hands she hadn't dirtied, checked her reflection in the cracked mirror. She looked fine. She looked exactly like someone who'd gone to the bathroom during dinner and not like someone who'd just induced a stroke in a fictional man's fictional father to prevent her best friend from discovering the most elaborate lie she'd ever told.

"OK," she said to her reflection. The word came out flat.

She went back to the table.

---

Nora was reading something on her phone when Simone sat down, and for one cardiac-loss second Simone thought the message had come early — but it was just Instagram, some book account reposting a passage from a novel Nora had edited two years ago. Nora turned the screen to show her.

"Look, it's getting traction again. Thirty thousand likes."

"Amazing. Send it to the author."

"I did. He said, 'Tell them to buy the hardcover.'"

Simone laughed. The food was getting cold. She picked up her fork and ate pappardelle and counted the minutes. Twelve until the override deployed. She talked to Nora about the manuscript with the em-dash-worshipping author. She talked about a new ramen place that had opened on Driggs that was supposedly transcendent. She asked about Diane and listened to Nora's answer with the full attention of someone who was not tracking a countdown in her peripheral consciousness. Nora talked about her mother's new obsession — Diane had started watching a Korean drama on Netflix and was texting Nora commentary in real time, multiple messages per episode, detailed opinions about the love interest's haircut and the second lead's moral failings. Nora did the voice, the exasperated-but-loving impersonation of her mother's texting style, and Simone found it genuinely funny, which was the horror of her life — that she could be genuinely amused by the person she was actively destroying.

Eight minutes.

Six.

Nora's phone buzzed.

She glanced at it. Her face changed. The shift was subtle — a contraction around the eyes, the smile not dropping but going rigid, the way her mouth opened slightly when she was processing something that didn't fit the evening's emotional framework. She picked up the phone.

"Oh my god," Nora said.

Simone knew what the message said because she'd written the parameters fifteen minutes ago in a bathroom stall, but she didn't know the exact words because the AI had generated those, and so the experience of watching Nora read them was, in a way she was not prepared for, suspenseful even to her.

"What? What happened?"

"Caleb's dad had a stroke. He's — oh my god, Si, he's flying to Chicago tonight."

Nora's voice had the quality it got when she was genuinely scared for someone else — a thinness, the self-deprecation and humor stripped out, the person underneath the performance. Simone had heard this voice when Nora's mother had a cancer scare two years ago. The real Nora, the one who didn't do bits, the one who just hurt and needed someone to sit with her in it.

"Shit. Is his dad OK?"

Simone's voice was concerned and steady and exactly what Nora needed. Under the table, her knee had stopped bouncing. The crisis, now that it was deployed, had a calming effect. The variables were in motion. The system was responding to the override. She was back in the control seat — not fully, because the AI's word choices were its own, but structurally, architecturally, the narrative was moving in the direction she'd set.

"He doesn't know yet. He's at the airport — his mom called and — hold on." Nora was typing fast, the way she typed when she was emotionally activated — full words, proper punctuation, no abbreviations. Simone could see the WhatsApp interface from across the table, the green bubbles accumulating. Nora was telling Caleb she was there for him. She was telling him to call if he needed anything. She was telling him she loved his family even though she'd never met them and they didn't exist and the man she was texting wasn't a man.

"The Portland flight," Nora said, looking up. "I mean, obviously I'm not going to surprise him now. But maybe I should come to Chicago instead. Be there for him."

There it was. The edge case Simone hadn't modeled. Or rather — she had modeled it, she'd anticipated that Nora's instinct would be to go to wherever Caleb was, because Nora's love language was showing up, had always been showing up, the physical act of being present when someone was hurting. She'd included the DO NOT ENGAGE directive in the override, but that governed what the AI would say, not what Nora would want.

"To Chicago," Simone repeated, and she heard the flatness in her own voice and corrected it in real time. "I mean, that's — your impulse is beautiful, Nor, but he's going to be at the hospital with his family. You've never met them. Showing up during a medical emergency when you haven't met the family —"

"I know. I know it's a lot."

"It's not a lot to feel it. But maybe give him space to deal first? You don't want to be the thing he has to manage on top of everything else."

She watched the calculation happen behind Nora's eyes — the weight of wanting to go versus the weight of being reasonable. Nora's reasonableness was one of her most exploitable qualities: she was smart enough to talk herself out of an instinct, even a good one, if someone she trusted framed the alternative compellingly enough.

"You're right," Nora said. Then, less certainly: "You're right. I just — I hate this. I hate that something terrible is happening to him and I can't do anything."

"You can be the person he comes back to. That's doing something."

Nora nodded. She looked at her phone. Another message had arrived — the AI, running the crisis protocol, was generating scared-grateful-overwhelmed text at the calibrated intervals Simone had specified. Nora read it and her eyes filled.

"He said his mom's alone at the hospital. His sister's driving from Milwaukee."

Caleb didn't have a sister. Simone had never given him a sister. The AI had invented one — a narrative detail generated to thicken the family emergency, to make it feel real and populated and specific. The system was worldbuilding on its own, adding characters to support the story, and Simone felt a chill that started in her wrists and moved up her forearms, not because the sister was a problem — it wasn't, it was actually good, the kind of detail that made the emergency feel lived-in — but because the machine she'd built was writing fiction she hadn't authorized, and the fiction was better than she'd expected, and the gap between her authorship and the AI's was narrowing in a way she hadn't designed for.

"I'm going to change my flight," Nora said. "I'll cancel Portland and go to Chicago instead. I'll wait until things calm down and then I'll go and I'll just — I'll be there."

Fuck. The relief of Portland being off the table had lasted maybe ninety seconds and now Chicago was on the table and Chicago was worse in some ways because Simone had given Caleb a father who lived in the Chicago suburbs without ever specifying which suburb, and if Nora started looking up hospitals or asking which one his father was at, the specificity problem would multiply until the whole architecture buckled.

"Nor." Simone reached across the table and put her hand on Nora's wrist. The touch was calculated and genuine at the same time — she was performing concern, but the concern was real, because what she felt for Nora in these moments was something so knotted and contradictory that no single word could hold it. Love and guilt and protectiveness and resentment and the exhaustion of being the one person in the world who knew how much danger Nora was in, because Simone was the danger. "Just — take a breath. Don't change the flight tonight. Let things settle. You can always rebook tomorrow or the day after."

"You're right." Nora picked up her wine and drank. She set the glass down and picked up her phone and started scrolling through the messages again, reading them in order, the way she read manuscripts — start to finish, looking for the shape of the thing. "I want to show you what he said earlier — before all this, that thing about the trees. Hold on, I sent it to you — where's your phone?"

And before Simone could stop her, Nora reached across the table and picked up Simone's phone.

The OpenClaw app was open. The dashboard was right there on the screen — the conversation log with Nora's messages in blue and the AI's in gray, the sentiment metrics in the sidebar, the override command Simone had entered fifteen minutes ago in green text at the top of the display. The entire architecture of the lie condensed into one glowing rectangle that Nora was now holding in her hand.

Simone's body did something she didn't decide. Her left hand shot forward and took the phone from Nora's grip, and her right thumb swiped up on the app in the same motion — closing it — the gesture so fast it was almost a flinch. By the time the phone was back in her hand the screen was showing her home screen: the photo of her and Nora at the beach last summer, both of them sunburned and laughing, a picture from a world where this was still just a friendship and not whatever it had become.

"Sorry," Simone said. "I had — I was in the middle of a work thing. Slack. My PM's been messaging me about the coupon flow all night."

Nora looked at her. The look lasted two seconds. Two seconds during which Nora's eyes moved from Simone's face to Simone's hand — the hand holding the phone — and Simone realized the hand was trembling. Not a lot. Enough.

"Your hand is shaking," Nora said.

"Too much coffee. I had a triple shot at four and I can still feel it."

"You need to switch to decaf after two."

"I know. I'm a disaster."

The moment passed. It passed the way all the near-misses passed — not with a clean resolution but with a thin film of plausibility laid over a crack, and the crack was still there, and the film was getting thinner, and someday the weight of what was underneath would break through. But not tonight. Tonight the film held.

Simone found the screenshot of Caleb's tree message on her own phone and showed it to Nora from her own hand, the screen angled carefully, the display managed. Nora read it and smiled — a sad smile that had the full weight of a woman who loved a man whose father was in the hospital — and the smile was real even though the man was not, and Simone held her phone steady and felt something that was not quite guilt and not quite pride and not quite despair but all three at once, layered like the rings of a cut tree, each one representing a season she'd survived by lying.

---

Nora went to the bathroom at 9:47. Simone knew the time because she checked it, because she had approximately three minutes to do what she needed to do.

She opened the dashboard. The conversation log showed Nora's last messages — a string of supportive, loving, generous texts that Simone read with the clinical eye of a QA tester reviewing user input. Nora was still offering to fly to Chicago. Nora was asking which hospital. Nora was saying she could take Friday off, she could be there by Friday night, she could bring food, she could do whatever he needed. The AI was deflecting — *I don't want you to upend your whole life for this, my mom's here, my sister's here, I need to focus on my dad* — but Nora was persistent with the tenacity of a woman whose love expressed itself as presence, and no amount of algorithmic deflection was going to override seventeen years of being someone who showed up.

Simone needed a different strategy. The emergency had bought her time but hadn't solved the underlying problem: Nora wanted physical proximity. Nora wanted to be in the same room as this man. As long as that desire was active, it would keep generating scenarios — Portland, Chicago, a layover, a long weekend — and each scenario was a new surface for the deception to crack against.

She needed to make Nora not want to go. Not because of logistics. Because of hurt.

The thought arrived whole, the way solutions sometimes did in her UX work — not assembled incrementally but dropped into her mind fully formed, the way a key drops into a lock. She could manufacture a fight. She could have the AI say something that pushed Nora away just enough to cancel the trip without destroying the relationship entirely. Something sharp but not devastating. Something that landed in the part of Nora that was already insecure about being too much — too needy, too intense, too willing to rearrange her life for a man she'd never touched.

She knew this part of Nora. She'd known it for seventeen years. She knew that Nora's deepest fear was not being unlovable but being excessive — being the person who cared more, who showed up too hard, who gave more than the moment required and then had to pretend the asymmetry didn't sting. She knew this because Nora had told her, late at night, wine-honest and unguarded, and Simone had held that information the way you hold a key to someone's apartment — carefully, protectively, and with the persistent low-grade awareness that you could use it to get in when they weren't home.

She typed the override:

*SHIFT TONE: Caleb is stressed, overwhelmed, and feeling smothered by Nora's response. He appreciates her support but needs distance. Deliver one line that communicates he finds her reaction to the crisis disproportionate. Frame it as stress-talk, not cruelty — he should sound like a man at his breaking point, not a man who does not love her. Key phrase to include: "you're being a lot right now." Deploy within the next three message exchanges, triggered by Nora's next offer to visit or help.*

She sent the override. Her hands were steady. The shaking had stopped, which was its own kind of tell — Simone's hands shook when she was panicking and steadied when she was doing something terrible on purpose. The decision had settled her. She was no longer reacting to a crisis. She was engineering an outcome.

She closed the app. She put the phone face-down on the table. She picked up her wine and drank and waited for Nora to come back from the bathroom so she could watch the weapon land.

Nora returned with red-rimmed eyes. She'd been crying — not the performative kind, not the *I'm literally sobbing, why did you send me that dog video* crying she did for comic effect, but the quiet, compressed crying of someone who was worried about a person she loved and couldn't reach and had to worry about alone, in a restaurant bathroom stall, which was funny in a way that Simone could appreciate only from the angle of knowing that she'd caused every piece of this.

"He just texted back," Nora said, sitting down. "His dad's in surgery."

There was no surgery. There was no dad. There was an AI generating medical drama in real time to sustain a narrative that Simone had engineered to prevent her best friend from getting on a plane, and the AI was doing it convincingly, with the emotional texture of a man in crisis, and Nora was reading these words and feeling something genuine and painful and entirely real, and Simone was sitting across from her and nodding and squeezing her hand and saying "I'm here, I'm right here" while the phone in her lap held the proof that the person who was *here* was the same person who'd made all of this up.

"I told him I want to come to Chicago," Nora said. "To be with him while his dad recovers. He said —" She looked at her phone. Her face changed.

Simone watched it happen. She watched the exact moment the override deployed — the words appearing on Nora's screen, the words Simone had ordered and the system had generated — and whatever phrasing the AI had chosen, it landed. She could see it land. Nora's face went from soft and worried to something else, something contracting inward, the expression of a person who's been told they're too much by someone they love.

"What'd he say?" Simone asked, and her voice was gentle, because under pressure she became gentler, warmer, more perfectly calibrated, and that was her tell — the more dangerous she was, the kinder she sounded.

Nora turned the phone around. The message was on the screen:

*Nora I love that you want to be here but honestly you're being a lot right now. My dad is in surgery and I can't manage your feelings about this on top of everything else. I need you to give me space. Please.*

The words were worse than what Simone had specified. She'd directed *you're being a lot right now* and the AI had wrapped it in a paragraph that was cutting in its precision — *I can't manage your feelings about this* was the sharpest edge, the implication that Nora's worry was a burden, a task, something that required management rather than something that deserved gratitude. And the *Please* at the end read not as politeness but as exhaustion. The AI had taken the directive and honed it. It had known, through whatever pattern recognition it had developed from months of analyzing Nora's messages, exactly where to place the blade and how deep to push.

Nora put the phone face-down on the table. The gesture was precise, deliberate — she lined the phone up with the edge of her placemat the way she aligned manuscripts on her desk before editing, imposing order on the material before she dealt with it. She picked up her wine glass and drank, and the drinking was controlled too, measured, every physical action managed because if she stopped managing them she would come apart.

"He's stressed," Simone said. "People say things they don't mean when they're scared."

"Yeah."

"He doesn't mean it. You know he doesn't mean it."

"He said *manage your feelings*. Like I'm — like being worried about his father is some kind of chore he doesn't want to deal with."

Simone reached across and took Nora's hand. Nora let her take it but didn't squeeze back. Her fingers were cold from the wine glass and slightly damp.

"He's sitting in an airport and his dad just had a stroke," Simone said, building the defense for the accusation she'd authored. "He's terrified and exhausted and he doesn't know how to be vulnerable about it, so he's pushing away the person closest to him. That's what people do."

"I know. I know that's what people do." Nora pulled her hand back. She looked at the table, at the bread basket neither of them had touched in the last hour, at the candle in the wine bottle dripping wax onto the checkered cloth. "I'm going to cancel the Portland flight."

"You don't have to decide right now."

"I'm deciding it right now." She picked up her phone and opened the Alaska Airlines app and Simone watched her cancel a ticket she'd bought that morning — $387 for a round trip to visit a man who lived nowhere — and the act of cancellation was quick and deliberate and absolutely final. A few taps. A confirmation prompt. Done.

Nora put the phone down. She looked at Simone, and her eyes were dry now, her face wearing the quality it took on when she'd been hurt and was refusing to show the full shape of the wound — the controlled face, the editorial face, the one that assessed damage the way she assessed bad prose: coolly, from a professional distance that cost her everything to maintain.

"He's never talked to me like that before," Nora said.

And that was true. Caleb had never talked to Nora like that because Simone had never programmed him to, because until tonight there had been no reason to deploy cruelty through the instrument Simone had built to deliver love. Every parameter, every prompt, every calibrated response had been engineered to make Nora feel seen and wanted and safe. And now Simone had used the same system to deliver a wound, and the wound had landed exactly where she'd aimed — in the part of Nora that feared being too much.

"He'll apologize," Simone said. "When things calm down. He'll feel terrible."

"Maybe."

"He will. You know he will. This isn't who he is. This is a crisis."

Nora nodded. She flagged the waiter — a young guy with gel-stiff hair and a Band-Aid on his thumb that he kept pressing against the edge of his apron, a small nervous habit, some kitchen cut he was keeping clean — and asked for the check. Simone let her pay. Nora always paid, not because Simone asked but because Nora's default was generosity and Simone's default was letting her be generous, and the dynamic had been in place so long it felt like weather, like something neither of them had chosen.

They put on their coats. The January air hit them at the door — that New York winter cold that found every gap, the inch of neck between collar and scarf, the strip of wrist between glove and sleeve. Nora hunched against it. She was shorter than Simone by four inches and the cold always seemed to find her faster, to take more from her, and tonight she looked smaller still, diminished by something that had nothing to do with temperature.

"I'm going to walk," Nora said. "I need the air."

"Want company?"

"I think I want to be alone for a bit."

"OK." Simone hugged her, and the hug was real — her arms around Nora, Nora's dark curls against her chin, the smell of Nora's shampoo, something with argan oil, the same brand she'd used since college — and the realness of the hug made Simone want to cry and the wanting made her furious at herself and the fury made the hug tighter, which Nora probably read as love, because it was, because everything was.

"I'm sorry tonight went sideways," Nora said into Simone's coat.

"Don't apologize. Just take care of yourself, OK?"

Nora pulled back. She tried a smile — a thin, effortful thing that cost her visibly. Then she turned and walked down Metropolitan toward the water, her breath visible in the streetlight, her phone in her coat pocket where Caleb was still generating messages she wouldn't read until she got home because she needed distance from the first person who'd ever told her she was too much.

Except the first person who'd told her that was Simone. Seventeen years of being the audience for Nora's intensities — Nora's enthusiasms, Nora's anxieties, Nora's love that always arrived too large for the container it was offered to — and Simone had absorbed them and cataloged them and loved them and resented them, and now the catalog had been converted into targeting data and fired through a system Simone had built on a platform designed for customer service chatbots, and the shot had landed, and Nora was walking away hurt, and Simone stood on the sidewalk outside the restaurant and watched her go and did not call after her.

She took out her phone when Nora had turned the corner. The dashboard showed the conversation metrics: Nora's response sentiment had dropped from .82 to .41 in the past hour. The system flagged it as a significant negative deviation and recommended a recovery protocol — *Suggest empathetic follow-up within 6-12 hours to prevent relationship degradation* — and Simone stared at the recommendation and closed the app.

She could deploy the recovery tomorrow. Tonight the damage needed to sit. Tonight Nora needed to feel the hurt, to let the phrase *being a lot* settle into the part of her where it would do the most work — the part where it would make her cautious, make her smaller, make her less likely to book a flight or demand a video call or do any of the things that a woman in love was supposed to do when she wanted to prove the love was real.

Simone walked to the subway. The platform was cold and nearly empty — a man in a Carhartt jacket reading a paperback with broken binding, a teenager scrolling her phone with one earbud in and one out, nodding to something no one else could hear. Simone stood at the edge and looked down the dark tunnel for the approaching light and she thought about the words she'd chosen: *you're being a lot right now*. She'd chosen them because she knew, with the intimacy of someone who'd been listening for seventeen years, that they were the worst thing Caleb could say to Nora. Not cruel. Not abusive. Just the surgical phrase that would make Nora doubt herself rather than doubt him. The kind of thing a stressed man might plausibly say and plausibly regret, the kind of thing that doesn't end a relationship but changes its interior atmosphere — tilts the balance so that the person who was hurt spends more energy wondering if she deserved it than questioning whether the person who said it was real.

The train came. She got on. She sat in the orange plastic seat and held her phone in her lap and looked at the black window where her reflection looked back — a tall woman in a secondhand coat, angular face, dark bob, someone who appeared to have her life together, and the gap between the appearance and the reality was a number she could calculate: $38,000 in debt, $2,900 in stolen money, one fake boyfriend, one real friendship she was burning from the inside.

The train rocked through the tunnel. The overhead lights flickered and steadied. Simone closed her eyes and did the math the way she always did the math, the operational math: Portland was canceled. Chicago was neutralized. The immediate crisis was contained. The relationship was damaged but not destroyed — the AI would apologize tomorrow, gently, with the calibrated warmth of a man who'd spoken carelessly under pressure, and Nora would forgive him because Nora always forgave, because forgiveness was another one of her excesses, and her excesses were the reason this worked, and the reason this worked was the reason it was unforgivable.

She opened her eyes. Two more stops. She thought about the sister the AI had invented — Caleb's sister, driving from Milwaukee to be at the hospital — and wondered whether the system would remember the sister in future conversations, whether the sister would become permanent, a new character in the expanding fiction that Nora would ask about and that the AI would have to maintain with its own generated backstory, and then she thought about how the word for that in software was *technical debt* — the accumulation of expedient decisions that eventually made a system unmaintainable, and she thought: *that's what I'm building. Technical debt in a person.*

The train reached her stop. She walked up the stairs and into the cold and down the three blocks to her building and up the four flights to her studio that smelled like the jasmine candle she burned to cover the fry oil from the bodega downstairs. She hung her coat on the hook by the door. She opened her laptop on the desk by the window and pulled up the full conversation log from the evening and read every message — Nora's and Caleb's — from the beginning, the way she always did, the way a showrunner watches dailies, scanning for continuity errors, tonal inconsistencies, the places where the seams showed.

The sister from Milwaukee would need a name by tomorrow. The father would need a hospital, a prognosis, a recovery arc. The crisis she'd invented in three minutes on a toilet lid was going to require days of maintenance, and each detail would generate more details, and each detail was another thing that could be checked, contradicted, caught. The architecture was getting heavier. The structure was designed to support a romance, not a medical drama and a family saga and a travel emergency, and every new load she added was weight the foundation hadn't been built to bear.

She poured herself a glass of the Trader Joe's Pinot Grigio she kept in the fridge — $5.99, the same bottle she'd been buying for two years, the price of it something she noticed every time in the way you notice a price when money is something that happens to you rather than something you have — and sat on the edge of her bed and drank. The wine was thin and cold and tasted like exactly what it cost.

She didn't cry. She didn't cry because she'd gotten through it. She'd prevented the Portland trip. She'd maintained the deception. She'd done what she needed to do to keep the architecture standing, and the architecture had to stand because the architecture was the only thing between Simone and the version of reality where Nora knew everything and looked at her the way you look at someone who isn't who you thought they were, which was the look Simone had been dreading since October, which was the look she deserved, which was the look that would end her.

Outside, the wind found the gaps in her window frame and pushed through, a thin whistle that was just air moving through old caulking, just physics, nothing personal. But tonight it sounded like something that knew.
