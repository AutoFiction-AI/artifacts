# Chapter 4

She read the message four times. Not because it was complicated — it was three sentences about a dog who'd stolen a sock — but because each reading revealed a different thing she liked about it. The first time, the image: Bruno dragging a wool sock across the kitchen tile like a hunter returning with quarry. The second, the word — *dragging*, not *carrying*, which was funnier and more precise, and the difference mattered to her in a way she couldn't explain without sounding like someone who needed to get out more. The third time she noticed the rhythm, how the last sentence landed with the casual understatement of a person who found his own life amusing without needing anyone to confirm it. The fourth time she was just reading it to feel something warm spread across the backs of her hands, and she set the phone facedown on her desk because that was enough — she was at work, it was eleven in the morning, and she had a two-hundred-page manuscript on Victorian botanical illustration that needed her red pen more than she needed to think about a sock.

She picked the phone back up eleven minutes later.

Two weeks since the first message, and Caleb Mora had rearranged the furniture of her attention. Not dramatically — she still edited manuscripts, still rode the F train with her bag between her feet and her shoulder against the door, still called her mother on Sundays and lied gently about having eaten lunch. But the architecture of a day had shifted, the way a room changes when someone moves the lamp. She checked her phone before she checked the weather. When she checked the weather it was to see what it was doing in Portland, so she could mention it — *fifty-two and clear here, you can keep your rain* — and his reply would arrive in four minutes or forty, and either way the interval was charged, a held breath she hadn't consented to holding.

They'd moved off Hinge within the first week. His suggestion, framed with the lightness of someone testing a joke: *This app keeps showing me other women's profiles and honestly it feels like cheating at this point.* She'd laughed — actually laughed, alone in her apartment at ten-thirty on a Wednesday, the sound startling in the quiet — and texted him her number. WhatsApp now. The green icon and its specific promise. She'd rearranged her home screen so it sat where Instagram used to be, which was a reshuffling of priorities she chose not to examine.

Her mornings had a new shape. Alarm at six-forty-five, glasses, phone — his messages from the night before waiting like something left on the doorstep. He was three hours behind in Portland, which meant he texted late, which meant she woke to things he'd written at midnight his time, two in the morning hers. The thought that he was staying up to talk to her was a gift she received before her feet touched the cold floor. She'd read his messages in bed, the radiator ticking through its cycle of effort and failure against the October chill, and she'd compose a reply that was casual — not too fast, not too eager, because she knew exactly how much wanting she could safely show and had already passed it.

On the F train, she'd reread older exchanges — the same pull that sent her back to first chapters of novels she loved, not for information but for the feeling. His description of a park he was designing in Northeast Portland, how he talked about drainage grades and sightlines with a care that made her lean closer to the screen on the crowded train. The photo he'd sent of Bruno asleep on a pile of landscape blueprints, one paw resting on a topographic contour line like a tiny explorer claiming territory. She'd zoom in on the photo, searching for details — the corner of a bookshelf behind the dog, the coffee mug with a chipped rim, the evidence of a life she couldn't visit but wanted to know.

The typing indicator was the worst of it. Three gray dots pulsing on her screen, meaning he was there, meaning he was composing something for her, some version of his thoughts being rendered in real time — and she'd hold the phone and wait, and the dots would vanish, and her stomach would do something she was embarrassed to admit felt like grief, a miniature loss that had no business being as sharp as it was. Then the dots would start again, and her hands would loosen around the phone before the rest of her caught up — relief, immediate, almost physical. *Shit,* she thought, the first time it happened. She was thirty-four years old and three gray dots on a screen were doing this to her.

She was worse on the sending side. She'd type a reply, read it, delete it, retype something more casual, delete that because it was performing a nonchalance she didn't feel, go back to the original, add a line, remove the line, stare at the cursor, and finally hit send with the specific dread of a woman who had just committed a sentence to the permanent record and could not call it back. Then she'd watch for his read receipt — the blue checkmarks that meant he'd seen it — and she'd wait, and the waiting had a quality that was new in her life, a quality she recognized from novels she'd edited but had never believed was literal: time moved differently when someone was reading your words and you couldn't see their face.

The conversations deepened without anyone deciding they should — gradually, like water finding its level. He asked questions not to confirm what he already suspected but to find out what was actually there, and the difference between the two was something she felt in her body before she could name it. She told him about the botanical illustration manuscript, and he didn't say *that sounds cool* — he asked which illustrators, and whether she thought the discipline of drawing a flower changed how you perceived it afterward, whether accuracy was the same as attention, and she wrote him four paragraphs about Anna Atkins and cyanotype prints that she realized later was the most she'd written about anything in months. Including her own novel. Especially her own novel.

He told her about growing up in Portland in a house where his mother cooked lomo saltado on Sundays and his father watched football at a volume that suggested hearing loss or aggression or both, and how the two sounds mixed into something that was his entire childhood, the sizzle of garlic in oil and the ESPN commentators building toward something that would probably disappoint everyone. He told her about his father — the complicated version, not the dinner-party version — about a man who expressed love through logistics and withheld it through silence, who drove Caleb to every soccer game for twelve years and never once asked if he'd had fun. He told her about Bruno, who was a mutt of unverifiable lineage that he'd found shivering outside a Safeway three years ago, coated in something he preferred not to identify, and who now occupied seventy percent of his bed and snored with the commitment of a man twice his size.

She told him things. That was the phrase she used with herself — *I've been telling him things* — because naming what she was actually doing, which was peeling back layers of herself for a man she'd never heard breathe, would require an honesty she wasn't ready for. She told him about her mother, who called too often and cooked in quantities that assumed a famine was coming and whose love was expressed through a relentless program of worry and roast chicken. She told him about her apartment, which was exactly the right size for a woman who lived alone and slightly too small for a woman who lived alone with three hundred books, and about the shelf she'd built from cinder blocks and pine boards during her junior year at NYU that she still owned, fourteen years later, because dismantling it felt like admitting the arrangement had become the arrangement.

She told him about Margo, her colleague whose divorce had hit like a natural disaster — she'd come out the other side slightly dazed, significantly funnier, wearing the debris with a kind of pride. She told him about the office, the HVAC that hummed at a frequency that lodged behind her eyes by three o'clock, the managing editor who scheduled four p.m. Friday meetings with the serene obliviousness of a man whose weekends started whenever he said they did. Small things. Safe things. The borders of a life offered in pieces that felt intimate because she was choosing to offer them.

Then, on a Tuesday night in late October, lying on her couch with her feet on the arm and her phone held above her face at an angle that would leave a mark on her neck by morning, she told him about the novel.

*I've been writing something for three years,* she typed. *Or not writing it. Mostly staring at it and rearranging the same paragraphs. It's about — this is going to sound pretentious — a woman who inherits her mother's recipe box and realizes all the recipes are wrong. Wrong on purpose. Changed over decades. Like the measurements are off by just enough that nothing tastes the way it should and the daughter has to figure out why.*

She deleted it. Retyped it without *this is going to sound pretentious.* Deleted that because the sentence felt naked without its qualifier, which was itself a kind of answer about her relationship to her own ambition. She sent the first version, hedging and all, because at eleven p.m. on a Tuesday with the streetlight casting a band of amber across her ceiling and the neighbor's bass making the wall hum, honesty looked like a woman lying on her couch telling a man she'd never touched about the book she was afraid to finish.

His response came in six minutes. She stared at the typing indicator for five of them and then at the message for the sixth.

*That's not pretentious at all. That sounds like a book about the gap between what people hand down and what they actually do — like whether inheritance is love or control. Or both. Probably both, right?*

Then, a beat later: *Have you shown it to anyone?*

*Just Simone,* she wrote. *My best friend. She read the first fifty pages and told me it was good, which I believed even though she'd tell me a grocery list was good, because that's who Si is.*

*What did she say specifically?*

*She said the mother was the most interesting character and I was afraid of her.*

*Were you?*

She lay there with the phone above her face, the weight of the question pressing down with the phone's weight. The mother in the novel was the character Nora kept approaching and retreating from, whose logic she could outline but whose interior she couldn't enter without feeling too close to something she wasn't ready to name. Not her own mother, exactly, but the idea of her own mother — the version of Diane who had made choices Nora hadn't asked about because the answers might require her to revise a story she preferred to keep simple.

*Yeah,* she typed. *She was right. She's usually right about my writing. It's annoying.*

*It's not annoying. It means she pays attention.*

And the thing that moved through Nora's body when she read that sentence — not warmth, something with more edge than warmth, a flush that started at her throat and spread downward — was so disproportionate to the stimulus, which was twelve words on a screen from a man in Oregon she had never seen blink, that she pressed the phone against her sternum and closed her eyes and let herself want. Not the words. Him. The body that had typed them, the hands, the mouth, the specific physical fact of a person she had never touched and wanted to touch with an urgency that surprised her and didn't stop when the surprise passed.

Because he was right. Simone did pay attention. Simone had always paid attention, to the novel, to the things Nora couldn't say about the novel, to the shape of Nora's ambitions and the specific ways she flinched from them. It was one of the things Nora loved most about her — that Simone saw her clearly and stayed. And now here was Caleb, a stranger with a dog and a complicated father and an eye for drainage grades, seeing the same thing from three thousand miles away. For a half-second, something flickered at the edge of it — the improbability of a man she'd known for two weeks landing on the exact same insight as her best friend of seventeen years, not approximately but precisely, as if he'd had the answer before she gave him the question. The thought was there and then it wasn't. She let it go — the kind of thought that felt urgent at 3 a.m. and foolish by morning — because the alternative was examining why a stranger's understanding fit so perfectly into the groove Simone had worn, and she wasn't willing to doubt the one thing that felt like luck.

Two nights later — a Thursday, cold enough that the radiator had shifted from its usual intermittent clicking to a sustained metallic whine — she told him about her father.

Not all of it. She'd never told anyone all of it, because all of it was ordinary, which was the problem — there was no dramatic arc to work with, no estrangement or reconciliation, just a man who'd been there completely and then wasn't. Her father had died when she was twenty, a heart attack in the driveway of the Long Island house, shoveling snow in a storm the weather service had told everyone to stay inside for. He'd been a high school history teacher, not at the same school where her mother taught English but nearby, close enough that they carpooled and argued about the faculty parking lot with the low-stakes passion of people who had organized their whole lives around being near each other.

She didn't tell Caleb the facts, which she could recite without feeling them. She told him the quality.

*My dad had this thing,* she typed, and then sat with the cursor blinking for a full minute before she continued. *He treated me like I was the most interesting person in any room. Not in a spoiling way — he wasn't performing it. He'd ask me what I was reading and then he'd read it, and we'd talk about it at dinner, and my mom would pretend to be annoyed that the conversation had gotten too literary again but she wasn't annoyed, she was watching us both with this look on her face that I didn't understand until he was gone.*

*What kind of look?*

*Like she was memorizing something. Like she knew it wouldn't last.*

She stared at what she'd written and almost deleted it, because it was more than she'd intended to say, more than she said to most people who asked about her father, more than she'd told anyone except Simone, years ago, on a different couch, with cheaper wine. But the act of typing it — how the words looked on the screen, small and permanent — made it feel like something she could set down rather than something she had to carry, and she left it.

His reply took longer this time. The dots appeared and disappeared three times, and each disappearance was its own small emergency. When the message came, it was two sentences.

*I think that's the most important thing anyone's ever told me about their father. Not the fact of losing him — the fact that you're still looking for what he gave you.*

She read it seven times. She set the phone on the couch cushion beside her, which smelled like the coffee she'd spilled last week and never properly cleaned, and she stared at the ceiling where the streetlight drew its amber stripe and she felt something she had not felt in two years, which was the specific, terrifying sensation of being known by someone who had chosen to know her. Not her mother, who knew her by biological obligation. Not Simone, who knew her by seventeen years of accumulated evidence. But someone who had chosen to pay attention, who had asked the right questions and listened to the answers and said something back that landed in the exact place where she was holding her breath.

And then — for a beat, maybe two — something cold moved through the warmth. Six minutes. He'd taken six minutes, and the dots had appeared and vanished three times, and the response that finally came was two sentences so precisely right they could have been drafted and revised, the work of someone who'd known the answer before she gave the question and needed only time to make the knowing sound spontaneous. The hair on her forearms lifted.

She thought: *No one is that good at listening.*

And then: *Except Simone.* He was like Simone — he paid attention — and the fact that two people could see her this clearly was not suspicious but rare, and she had always been drawn to rare things. She let the chill pass and chose the warmth because the warmth was still right there on her screen, still saying the thing she'd needed someone to say.

She'd been waiting for this feeling the way you wait for a season — not with urgency, but with the low-grade faith that it would come if she didn't give up on its coming. She was not going to pick it apart now that it was here.

She picked up the phone and typed: *Thank you.*

And then, because she was Nora and couldn't leave a sincere moment unqualified: *That was the most real thing I've said to someone through a phone since I called my mom after 9/11 and I was nine and I'm now realizing that comparison is bonkers.*

His reply: *It's not bonkers. It's honest. Those are often the same thing.*

She laughed. The sound bounced off the walls of her apartment and came back to her, and she pressed the phone against the side of her neck and breathed, and outside the October dark pressed against her windows like something patient and alive.

---

Margo Chen had a way of eating lunch that was both efficient and melancholy — she'd unwrap her turkey sandwich with the decisive movements of a woman who had better things to do and then eat it slowly, staring at nothing, with an expression that suggested she was reviewing the major decisions of her life and finding them mostly defensible but occasionally catastrophic. She kept a framed photo of her two cats on her desk — calicos named Charlotte and Emily, after the Brontës, because "Anne didn't earn it," a position Margo held with the conviction of someone who had thought about it more than was probably healthy. She wore her wedding ring on a thin chain around her neck, which she said was because it was a nice ring and she'd contributed to its purchase, and which Nora suspected was because taking it off your finger was one decision and putting it in a drawer was a different, harder one, and Margo had accomplished the first but not the second.

It was Margo who noticed. Not the phone-checking — everyone in the office checked their phones, a collective habit that pulsed through the open-plan floor like a synchronized tic — but Nora's face when she checked. A softening around the mouth and eyes, categorically different from the face she made reading email or scrolling the news or texting her mother back.

"You're seeing someone," Margo said. Not a question. They were in the office kitchen, Nora at the coffee machine, Margo working through an apple with the methodical precision of a woman building a case.

"I'm talking to someone," Nora corrected, because the distinction felt important even if she couldn't articulate why.

"On the apps?"

"Hinge. His name's Caleb. He's a landscape architect in Portland."

"Portland, Oregon?"

"No, Portland, Maine — yes, Oregon."

Margo bit the apple. The crunch had editorial weight. "Long-distance from the jump. That's a choice."

"It's just texting right now."

"You made your second cup of coffee at ten-fifteen and your manuscript hasn't moved a page since I sat down. It's not just texting."

That was the thing about Margo — she noticed things with the sharpness of someone who'd had her own illusions punctured and now registered the puncture-resistance of other people's. Her divorce had not made her cynical, exactly, but it had given her a kind of X-ray attention that could see through the surface of a claim to the architecture beneath it. She didn't use this power maliciously. She used it precisely, and only when she thought you needed to hear what she'd noticed.

Nora told her. Not everything — not the late-night messages, not her father, not how she'd started falling asleep with the phone on the pillow beside her like a second body — but enough. The profile. The first conversation. The botanical illustration exchange that had turned into a real discussion about attention and accuracy. She watched Margo's face for the judgment she expected and didn't find.

"Show me," Margo said.

Nora pulled up Caleb's profile. Margo held the phone at arm's length and squinted, as if distance might surface something proximity concealed.

"He's cute," Margo said. "In a real way. Not in a *I hired a photographer and I'm standing on a mountain* way."

"That's what I thought."

"The dog's a good sign. Psychopaths prefer cats." She glanced at the photo of Charlotte and Emily on her desk. "Present company excluded."

"He sends me pictures of the dog almost every day. Bruno. He's got this face — "

"The dog or the man?"

"Both, actually."

Margo handed the phone back. Her expression had two layers, and Nora could see both — the warmth on the surface, and underneath it, something more careful. What Margo was doing underneath wasn't skepticism. It was the particular care of a woman who had been as hopeful as Nora was right now, who remembered exactly how that hope had tasted and how different the aftertaste was, and who didn't want to ruin the first flavor by insisting too early on the second.

"He seems good, Nor. I mean that." Margo paused, and the pause was where the real sentence lived. "When are you gonna meet him?"

"He's in Portland. It's not like we can grab coffee."

"People fly places. I flew to Tucson to meet a man from Hinge last year — he turned out to be five-seven and married, which, honestly, fuck that guy — but the point is, planes exist."

"We'll figure it out. It's only been two weeks."

"Sure." Margo picked up her apple core and carried it to the trash with the decisiveness of a woman closing a topic she intended to reopen later. "Two weeks is early. I'm just — you know me. I like to see people in three dimensions before I decide they're real."

Nora laughed, and the laugh was genuine, and Margo smiled, and neither of them said anything else about it because they'd known each other long enough to recognize the shape of a conversation that had said what it needed to say and didn't require a closing argument.

She told Simone the same night, over wine at Simone's apartment. This was one of their rituals — the kind that accumulates over seventeen years until you can't remember who suggested it, can't separate the tradition from the friendship, can't imagine Wednesday without the specific combination of Simone's couch and whatever bottle was already open and the conversation that moved between their lives like a shuttle between looms. Simone's studio smelled the same as always — like the gardenia candle she burned to cover cooking smells in a space too small to contain them, something floral and slightly too sweet that had become, for Nora, the scent of being known.

Simone sat cross-legged on her end of the couch, her wine held in a way that was effortlessly composed, and she asked about Caleb with a focus that Nora loved — the specific attention of a best friend who treated your happiness as an extension of her own.

"Tell me everything," Simone said. "From where we left off."

So Nora did. She read Simone the texts — the good ones, the funny ones, the one about his father and the football games, the one about Bruno and the sock. She read Simone her own replies and waited for the verdict, and the verdict was always right: *that's perfect*, *you're hilarious, he's lucky*, *okay that line about the recipe box? He actually understood it?*

"He got it, Si. He actually got it. He said something about inheritance being an act of love or control — "

"That's smart."

"It's not just smart. It's — he thinks about things like I do. The same rhythms. Like he already knows what I mean before I finish meaning it."

Simone refilled Nora's glass. The wine caught the light from the lamp on the side table, the one with the crooked shade that Simone had found at a stoop sale and refused to replace because it worked and because replacing functional things felt, to her, like an indulgence she didn't have access to. "What about the novel?" she asked. "You told him about the book?"

"I told him."

"And?"

"He didn't make me feel stupid about it. He asked the right question — what Simone said, was she right — and I told him she was. I told him you were right about the mother."

"I was right about the mother."

"You were right about the mother."

They looked at each other and laughed, the easy laughter of two people who had covered this ground so many times the ground itself had become part of the friendship, a shared territory mapped by years of exactly this: one of them on this couch, the other on the other end, the wine between them, the conversation building the thing that neither of them had a word for but that Nora, when pressed, would have called the most durable structure in her life.

"I think I like this person," Nora said. She pressed her hand over her eyes, a gesture she'd been making since college when something too big for her face needed somewhere to go. "Fuck. That sounds insane. It's been two weeks and I've never heard his voice and I think I like him."

"It doesn't sound insane," Simone said. "It sounds like you met someone who pays attention, and you're not used to that, and it scares you because the last time you felt this way it ended with you eating cereal for dinner for six months. But that doesn't mean this is the same."

And that — Simone's precision, her ability to take the tangled mess of what Nora was feeling and lay it out flat, name each thread, show her the pattern — was why this friendship was the fixed point of Nora's life. Simone saw her. Not the partial view Caleb was assembling through curated texts and careful disclosures, but all of her, the whole accumulated map of who she was and how she'd gotten there.

"You deserve this," Simone said. "You know that."

Something in Simone's composure snagged at the edge of Nora's attention — how quickly the reassurance had come, how neatly it tracked, as if Simone had rehearsed it before Nora finished needing it. But that was Simone. That was what seventeen years of friendship looked like: your best friend already knowing what you needed to hear because she'd been listening that long. The most natural kind of love — the kind that was already there before you asked for it.

Nora wanted to know she deserved it. She wanted to believe it as badly as she wanted to believe the novel would get finished, as badly as she wanted to believe that loneliness was a temporary condition and not a character trait — with a faith that was more investment than evidence, more hope than certainty, the gap between the two narrowing with each message, each reply, each set of pulsing dots that meant someone she couldn't see was thinking about what to say to her, and cared enough about the saying to take his time.

She walked home from Simone's apartment through the late October dark, twelve minutes door to door, the Brooklyn sidewalk slick with leaves that hadn't been swept and wouldn't be. The cold had an edge now, a seriousness the September chill hadn't carried, and she pulled her jacket tighter and felt her phone in her pocket the way you feel a hand you're not holding but know is there. When she got home she brushed her teeth and washed her face and got into bed and checked her messages, and Caleb had sent her a photo of Bruno curled on a pile of laundry with one ear folded inside out, and beneath it: *He misses you. I asked. He confirmed.*

She typed back: *Tell Bruno I'm honored and also that his ear is inside out and it's a strong look.*

His reply, thirty seconds later: *He says he knows about the ear. It's a choice.*

She fell asleep with the phone on the pillow beside her, the screen dimming and going dark, the room filling with the sounds of Brooklyn — a siren on the avenue, the bass from a passing car that rattled her windows, the radiator's metallic complaints — and in the morning the phone would be there, and so would he, and the feeling of waking up to someone's words was so close to the feeling of waking up beside someone that she had started to forget, or to stop caring, that there was a difference.
