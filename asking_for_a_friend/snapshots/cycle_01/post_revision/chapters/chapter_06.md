# Chapter 6

The sentence was almost right. Nora could feel it — the way the subordinate clause sagged in the middle, pulling the whole thing into a shape that was grammatically defensible but rhythmically dead. She read it again, mouthing the words against the rim of her coffee cup. *The woman returned to the house where her mother had lived and where her own childhood had been spent in a state of chronic and formidable unease.* Three *where*s and a throat-clearing *state of* — the author was afraid of the sentence's real work and had padded it with furniture.

She deleted from *and where* to the period and typed in the margin note: *"formidable" is working against you here. Can the unease just be the unease? Try: "The woman returned to the house where her mother had lived, which meant where her mother had made her afraid."*

This was the part of her job that justified the salary, which was not a salary anyone bragged about but which covered her rent and her bookstore habit and the occasional dinner that crossed into triple digits without apology. She sat in her office chair with its one broken armrest — the left, which she'd reported to facilities in March and again in June and had now accepted as a permanent feature of her life, a small daily lean to the right — and she felt, for concentrated stretches of the morning, that she was good at something. The manuscript was a debut from a woman in Tucson who wrote sentences like she was building furniture, each joint tight, each surface sanded. Nora had been reading the book for two days and it was the best thing she'd been sent in over a year, and she was making it better, which was a different pleasure than reading and a more physical one, like adjusting the tension on a string until it hummed at the right pitch.

The office was running warm again. November hadn't yet committed to cold, but the building's HVAC system operated on a schedule indifferent to the actual weather, and the forced heat pressed against Nora's forearms where she'd pushed her sleeves up past her elbows. Around her the open-plan hummed its midmorning soundtrack — keyboards, someone's earbuds leaking the tinny ghost of a podcast, the copy machine doing its percussive clearing-of-the-throat in the corner. She picked up her pen. *Beautiful passage on p. 74,* she wrote. *But you've earned the right to trust your reader here — you don't need the second metaphor.*

Her phone buzzed in her bag. She didn't reach for it. She reached for it. She looked at the screen under the edge of her desk like a teenager checking messages during class, and the small hit of warmth that moved through her when she saw Caleb's name was something she'd stopped pretending wasn't happening.

*bruno ate my leftover pad thai off the counter while i was in the shower. he has zero remorse. he has a noodle on his ear.*

She typed *good boy* and put the phone back and returned to page seventy-six, where the Tucson woman was doing something extraordinary with a kitchen scene that made Nora's own kitchen scene — the one in the novel she'd been trying to write for three years, the one that sat on her laptop like a piece of furniture she kept meaning to refinish — look like the work of someone who'd only ever read about kitchens.

"The algorithm just delivered a Rolfing testimonial to my feed. With Kevin's name on it."

Nora looked up. Margo was standing at the edge of her desk holding two mugs of the office coffee that tasted like it was brewed with a grudge.

"Rolfing," Nora said, accepting the mug.

"Rolfing. Deep tissue. The woman he's seeing puts *everything* on Instagram. 'Six weeks of Rolfing changed my relationship with my psoas.' She tagged Kevin. *My* Kevin. In a post about her psoas." Margo sat down in the chair next to Nora's desk, the one they called the confessional because people came to it when they needed to say things they shouldn't. She was wearing her Wednesday ponytail, which was higher than the Monday version — a detail Nora had pointed out once, calling it an empirical observation and not a judgment, and which Margo had denied and then confirmed by wearing it the same way every Wednesday since. "I'm not saying I stalk her Instagram. I'm saying the algorithm delivers her to me like a newspaper I didn't subscribe to and can't cancel."

"What's a psoas?"

"It's a hip flexor. Apparently hers is now 'deeply released.' I know things about this woman's connective tissue that I don't know about my own mother."

"And Kevin is — what, sitting for the portrait?"

"Kevin *liked* the post. My Kevin, who would not touch his toes if you held a gun to his head, who once called yoga 'expensive lying down,' liked a Rolfing testimonial with a little flame emoji." Margo drank her coffee with the practiced grimace of someone who had given up expecting it to improve. She had a pen behind her ear that she'd put there an hour ago and forgotten about — she forgot about it every day, reaching up sometime after lunch to find it there with genuine surprise, a small daily mystery she never solved. "I should be over this. I *am* over this. I'm over the man. I'm not over the algorithm's decision to keep me informed about his psoas journey."

Nora laughed — the real one, the one that came from her stomach and didn't ask permission. Margo's divorce had been final for fourteen months and she talked about Kevin the way you talk about a natural disaster that destroyed your house — with gallows humor and an insurance claim still pending. Nora suspected the insurance claim was real too, metaphorically speaking, some part of Margo still calculating what she was owed for the years spent arranging her life around a man who turned out to be temporary.

"How's your Portland situation?" Margo asked, and the transition was her usual — no runway, no preamble, a lateral move that trusted Nora to keep up.

"He's good. We're good."

"'Good' is doing a lot of work in that sentence, Kaplan."

"We talked for two hours last night about whether pineapple belongs on pizza. Two hours. It was the most fun I've had sitting in my own bed since —" She let the sentence trail, and Margo raised an eyebrow, and Nora shook her head. "Never mind."

Margo looked at her. The look was warm and slightly investigative, the way a copy editor looks at a comma that might be wrong but might be an intentional choice. "You're doing that thing again."

"What thing?"

"Smiling like you know something."

Nora didn't have a good answer for this, because it was accurate. She'd been catching herself in the bathroom mirror at work and in the reflection of the subway window at night, wearing a smile that wasn't meant for anyone else but kept surfacing. She'd been walking faster. She'd been sleeping better, falling asleep in the middle of conversations with Caleb and waking up to messages that said *you fell asleep, didn't you* and *good night, nora* and once, at two in the morning, *i was watching you type and then you just stopped and i pictured you asleep with your phone on your chest and it made me happy.* She'd been building something — not consciously, not with blueprints, but the way you furnish a room without noticing, adding one thing and then another until you look around and realize you've made a home out of what used to be empty space.

"I'm going to my mom's tonight," she said, because changing the subject was something she'd learned from editing — cut to the next scene before the current one overstays.

"Give Diane my love. Tell her that casserole she sent home with you was better than anything I've eaten in a restaurant in two years, and I'm not exaggerating."

"She'll make you another. You're on the casserole list now. There's no getting off the casserole list."

---

The LIRR from Penn Station was the 6:18, which put her at her mother's stop at twenty to eight, which meant Diane had been cooking since five because Diane cooked the way other people worried — comprehensively, preemptively, as though the meal were a fortification against something she couldn't name but could smell coming. Nora's phone buzzed against her thigh as the train pulled through the tunnel into the weak yellow light of early Queens, but she didn't check it. She was practicing not checking it, the way you practice a new discipline by denying yourself the old habit — five minutes without looking, then ten, then the whole length of the platform at Jamaica where she changed to the branch line and watched a man in paint-spattered jeans eat a bodega sandwich with the focused seriousness of someone performing surgery.

The house smelled like garlic and something roasting. It always smelled like garlic and something roasting, the way a church smells like candles — not a decision but an accumulation, thirty years of olive oil and onion and the particular warmth of a kitchen that had never once been merely decorative.

"You're thin," Diane said, before the door was fully open. She was wearing the blue apron she'd had since Nora was in middle school, the one with the gravy stain on the front pocket that had become a family landmark, a feature rather than a flaw.

"I'm the same."

"You're thin in the face. Come in, come in. I made chicken, I made the rice, I made a salad because you said something about greens last time and I don't want you thinking I only serve starches." She was already moving toward the kitchen, talking over her shoulder in the continuous stream that Nora associated with home — not information, exactly, but the ambient sound of a place, the way the radiator in her Brooklyn apartment knocked twice every morning. "Your Aunt Linda called. She's getting a knee replacement. The left one — not the one she's been complaining about, which I told her was going to happen because it's never the thing you're watching for, it's always the other one."

"That's dark, Mom."

"It's orthopedic. Sit. You want wine? I've got that red you liked."

Nora sat at the kitchen table, which was walnut and scarred from decades of homework and dinner plates and the time six-year-old Nora had gone at it with a screwdriver for reasons neither of them could reconstruct. The kitchen was warm from the oven, and the November dark pressed against the windows with the specific density of suburban Long Island, where the night was heavier than in Brooklyn, less interrupted by light.

Diane poured her a glass without waiting for an answer and set it down on a coaster, because coasters mattered in this house, had always mattered, occupied the same moral ground as thank-you notes and arriving on time. She sat across from Nora and folded her hands on the table, which was the posture that meant she had been thinking about something and had organized her approach.

"So," Diane said. "Tell me about this person."

"Mom."

"Nora, you've been mentioning him for three weeks in the way a person mentions something she wants to talk about but is waiting to be asked. So I'm asking. Tell me."

This was the thing about Diane. She was not subtle and she didn't pretend to be, but she was *precise*. She'd taught high school English for thirty-one years, which meant she'd spent three decades watching seventeen-year-olds try to hide that they were in love, in trouble, or stoned, and she'd developed a diagnostic eye that she had never fully retired. Nora sometimes thought of her mother as a very patient detective — someone who solved cases not through cleverness but through the accumulated data of watching one face for thirty-four years.

"His name is Caleb. He's a landscape architect. He lives in Portland."

"Portland, Oregon?"

"The one that's far away, yes."

"And you met him on the app."

"Hinge. Yeah."

Diane nodded, absorbing this the way she absorbed a student's thesis statement — with visible consideration and a withholding of judgment that was itself a kind of judgment. "And what's he like? As a person."

"He's funny. He's thoughtful. He asks questions and actually listens to the answers, which, given the dating pool I've been dealing with, is like finding someone who can breathe underwater." Nora could hear herself curating the description — not lying, but arranging, the way she'd edit a jacket-copy paragraph to make the book sound like its best self. "He has a dog named Bruno. He reads actual books, not just the ones everyone's talking about. He —" She paused. "He makes me feel like the version of myself I want to be when I'm talking to someone."

Diane was quiet. She stood and went to the oven, opened it, checked the chicken that didn't need checking — her version of processing, Nora knew, motion in place of speech, the body doing one thing while the mind did another entirely.

"That's a lovely thing to say about someone," Diane said, her back to Nora, bent toward the oven light. "And you haven't met him in person."

"Not yet. We text, we talk. The distance makes it complicated."

"It does make it complicated." Diane closed the oven and turned around, and her face had something on it that Nora almost didn't catch — a small contraction between the eyebrows, a careful tightness around the mouth that wasn't worry, exactly, or disapproval, but the specific expression of a woman who had once watched a student turn in a brilliant essay she strongly suspected the student hadn't written. "You sound happy, Nora."

"I am happy."

"Good." She set a plate of chicken in front of Nora, golden and herbed and far too much for one person. "Eat. You're thin in the face."

Dinner was what dinner at Diane's always was — an excess of food, a surplus of questions, the particular warmth of being someone's child in someone's kitchen. They talked about Aunt Linda's upcoming surgery, about the neighbor three doors down who had replaced his mailbox with one shaped like a largemouth bass — "Your father would have *loved* it," Diane said, which was how she still talked about Michael more than twenty years after he'd moved to Seattle — present tense for his opinions, as though his taste in lawn ornaments could be evaluated from three thousand miles away. They talked about the novel Diane was reading, a Penelope Fitzgerald reissue that she kept comparing unfavorably to a Henry Green she'd finished last month. "Fitzgerald is elegant," Diane said, tearing bread, "but Green is *alive*. There's a difference. Elegant you admire. Alive you want to sit next to."

But twice, while Nora was talking about Caleb — once when she described the pineapple debate, and once when she mentioned his dog Bruno by name, as though the dog were someone she knew — Diane paused in a way that was less like listening and more like weighing. She was holding the information against something, testing it, the way she might hold up a student's paper and compare the voice to the one she'd heard in class. And both times, she set aside whatever she'd measured and asked instead whether Nora wanted more rice.

At the door, under the porch light that illuminated the front yard's single maple, now mostly bare, its leaves banked in wet heaps along the walk, Diane hugged her and held on for a beat longer than usual.

"Bring him home when you can," she said. "I'd like to meet this person who says such good things."

"I will, Mom."

"And Nora?" She squeezed Nora's arm, her hand warm even through the jacket. "Be careful with your happiness. It deserves someone who's careful with it too."

Nora thought about this on the platform, waiting for the 9:47 back to Penn Station, standing under the fluorescent light with two other people who looked like they were also returning to the city from places that smelled like their mothers' cooking.

---

The train was half-empty. The seat fabric had the particular LIRR texture she'd known since childhood, nubby and institutional, the upholstery of a transit system that had committed to a palette sometime in the 1980s and never reconsidered. She pulled out her phone. Four messages from Caleb, the last one sent twenty minutes ago.

*first real cold rain in portland today. the sky just committed all at once*

*bruno will not go outside. he's standing at the door looking at me like i've personally betrayed him*

*unrelated: what do you call a dog who does magic tricks?*

She waited. She could see there was a fourth message.

*a labracadabrador*

She typed: *that is genuinely the worst thing anyone has ever said to me*

*and yet you're smiling*

She was. She was smiling on the LIRR at a quarter to ten on a Tuesday night, and the woman in the seat ahead of her had turned around briefly at the sound of Nora's laugh — a short, ungoverned thing, the kind that escapes before you decide whether it's appropriate for public transit.

*i'm on a train, i'm in a food coma from my mom's chicken, and you just told me the worst joke in the english language. this is my tuesday*

*sounds like a good tuesday*

*it is now*

*how was dinner with your mom?*

*she asked about you*

*she did?*

*she wanted to know about the person her daughter keeps mentioning in the way that a person mentions a thing she wants to talk about but is waiting to be asked. her words, more or less*

*that's a very specific read*

*she's a retired english teacher. specific reads are her whole deal*

*what did you tell her?*

*that you're funny and thoughtful and you have a dog who makes your decisions for you*

*bruno resents the word "for." he says he makes decisions WITH me. collaboratively*

*tell bruno i'll judge his decision-making when i meet him*

She sent it before she thought about it — the assumption of meeting, the casual future tense that included her in his living room with his dog — and for a moment she felt the small vertigo of having reached further than she'd meant to. But his reply came fast, and it was right, and the vertigo dissolved.

*he's practicing his most charming sit right now. you should see this. it's very dignified*

Outside the window, the dark shapes of Long Island were giving way to the clustered lights of Queens. At Jamaica, waiting on the platform, she'd seen a woman with a greyhound in a fleece jacket printed with tiny lobsters — the dog angular and dignified and utterly ridiculous, nosing at the woman's coat pocket with the specificity of an animal that knew exactly where the treats were kept. Nora had wanted to pet that dog with an intensity that surprised her.

*i just saw a greyhound at jamaica station wearing a fleece jacket with lobsters on it. i need you to understand that i almost got off the train*

*for the greyhound?*

*obviously for the greyhound*

*this is why we work. you'd abandon mass transit for a dog in outerwear*

*wouldn't you?*

*i'd abandon most things for a dog in outerwear. i'd abandon career goals. i'd abandon my principles*

*what principles*

*exactly*

They talked about nothing. He told her about a client who wanted a Japanese garden but kept referring to it as "the zen situation" and who had asked whether the koi could be "more colorful, like the ones in screen savers." She told him about the Tucson novelist whose manuscript was so good it made Nora feel both grateful and jealous, which was the best way to feel about someone else's sentences. He told her his upstairs neighbor played the cello badly every Thursday evening and that he'd come to think of it as a kind of weather — something ambient and unavoidable that had folded itself into the texture of his week.

*you're like that,* he wrote. *like weather. like you're part of the day now. like i check you the way i check whether it's raining*

She read it twice. She read it three times, and the third time the words settled somewhere behind her sternum in a way she would have flagged in a manuscript — *too neat,* she'd write in the margin, *find the feeling under the feeling* — except the feeling under the feeling was the same one, just deeper and less defended. Not a flutter. Not a swoon. Something quieter. A door she'd been leaning her weight against, and someone on the other side had simply knocked.

*that's a very good line for a landscape architect*

*i contain multitudes. also bruno has fallen asleep on my foot and i literally can't move*

*trapped*

*imprisoned. by sixty pounds of wet dog who thinks personal space is a rumor*

*good night, caleb*

*good night, nora*

She put the phone facedown on her thigh and watched the dark go by. The train rocked through the tunnel toward Penn Station and the fluorescent lights buzzed their tuneless note overhead. She thought about what Diane had said — *be careful with your happiness* — and she understood it the way you understand a weather warning issued for a region you don't live in, relevant in theory, distant in practice.

She was not being careful. She was doing the opposite of careful. She was letting this man — this voice, this warmth, this presence that lived in her phone and nowhere she could touch — into the places she'd been protecting since Marcus, which were not the places she'd expected. Not her heart. Her heart was too available, had always been too available, was a door she kept unlocked in a neighborhood she insisted was safe. The places she'd been guarding were smaller and more specific: the part of her that wanted to be known without performing the explanation. The part that wanted to send a message at eleven p.m. and be understood without context. The part that was exhausted from being both the manuscript and the editor, the book and the blurb, the woman and the woman's narration of the woman.

Caleb felt like reading without a pencil in her hand. Like receiving a sentence instead of fixing one.

And now her mother knew his name, and Margo knew his name, and he existed not only in her phone but in other people's awareness of her life, which meant he was real in the way things became real when you spoke them aloud to people who loved you and watched their faces accept the information. He'd moved from Nora's private architecture into the shared world, and the shared world had made room for him, and this made him more solid, more present, more like a person she could eventually stand next to in a kitchen and introduce to her mother and touch.

She held the phone against her thigh, its screen still faintly warm, the last thing he'd typed still glowing behind the dark glass. Somewhere in Portland — she pictured the rain he'd described, the dog asleep on his foot, the bad cello coming through the ceiling like weather — a man she had never met was thinking about her.

She believed this completely. The way you believe a sentence that finally works — with the specific, earned, defenseless conviction of someone who has found the right word and doesn't know it was placed there for her.
