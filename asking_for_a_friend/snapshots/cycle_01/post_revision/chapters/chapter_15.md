# Chapter 15

Simone's candle was almost burned through. The floral one, the one that was always slightly too sweet — gardenia or tuberose or whatever it was that Simone claimed smelled like her grandmother's garden in Calabria but which actually smelled like the Anthropologie on Fifth Avenue. The wax had melted down to a pale disc at the bottom of the jar, and the wick was listing sideways, and it was the kind of detail Nora noticed because she noticed everything in this apartment — because she'd spent hundreds of afternoons here without ever quite living here.

They'd been talking about a novel Simone had heard about somewhere — a podcast or a newsletter, Nora couldn't remember which — and the title was sitting just out of reach in that part of her brain where half-remembered things went to taunt her. Something with "salt" in it. Or "stone." Simone had described the premise and Nora had said it sounded like ambition without structure, and they'd argued about whether ambition in fiction was the same thing as pretension, which was one of their better recurring disagreements, the kind that could stretch across an hour and a bottle of Sancerre without anyone getting genuinely angry.

The afternoon was doing what Saturday afternoons at Simone's always did, which was to pass slowly and pleasantly without anyone steering it. March light came through the one good window — the desk window, the one that faced the building across the narrow alley rather than the brick wall the kitchen looked out on — and the light had that transitional quality of the season trying to decide what it was. It was not winter anymore but not quite spring. The radiator was clicking in its irregular rhythm, the one that Simone had complained about to her landlord so many times that the complaint itself had become a comfort, proof that some things in this apartment would never change.

"Food's ready," Simone said, checking her phone. She was already reaching for the jacket on the back of the desk chair — the olive-green one with the lining coming loose at the left cuff, which she'd been meaning to get fixed for two years. "I'll walk down. You want anything else? More of the tom kha?"

"I'm good. Just the pad see ew."

"You always get the pad see ew."

"Because it's always good."

Simone rolled her eyes in a way that was itself a form of affection. "Don't touch my wine," she said from the doorway. "That's the last of the Côtes du Rhône and I'm rationing."

"I'd never."

"You'd absolutely." And then the door closed, and Simone's footsteps went down the hall, and the apartment settled into the particular silence of someone else's space — not empty, just temporarily unoccupied by the person who made it feel full.

Nora picked up her phone to add the book to her list — the one with salt or stone in the title, the one she still couldn't quite reach — and the screen was black. She pressed the side button. Nothing. She'd been at eleven percent when she arrived and hadn't asked for a charger because she was trying to be less attached to the thing, a resolution that lasted exactly as long as she didn't need to look something up.

She glanced at the desk. Simone's laptop sat there, lid closed, next to a tidy stack of UX wireframe printouts and a mug that said DESIGN IS A VERB in the font of someone who meant it. Nora had used Simone's laptop before — to look up a recipe once, to show Simone a YouTube video when her own phone was dying, which apparently happened a lot. It wasn't a boundary. They didn't have boundaries — Nora had always loved this about their friendship, that nothing in either of their lives was off-limits. Simone knew Nora's computer password. Nora knew Simone's. They knew each other's phone codes, their email logins, the answers to each other's security questions. Seventeen years of accumulated transparency that Nora had always understood as trust.

She opened the laptop.

Chrome was already running, with — she counted, because she always counted — seven tabs across the top of the screen. Gmail. Figma. Something that looked like a project management tool. An article from The Cut that Simone had apparently been reading. A tab labeled PAYMENT CONFIRMATION. A Spotify window with a playlist called "deep work lol."

And a tab that said OPENCLAW AGENT DASHBOARD — CALEB M.

She didn't click on it right away. She was looking at the other tabs, trying to remember what she'd come to the laptop for — the book, the salt-or-stone book — and the name registered with a strange displacement, like finding your own street name on a sign in a foreign city. Caleb M. She thought: *That's a coincidence.* She thought: *Huh.* She thought these things in the half-second before she clicked the tab, and later she would try to reconstruct whether there had been a moment of choice, a point at which she could have not clicked and gone back to looking for the book and never known, and she would decide that there hadn't been, that her hand had moved with the fluency of something that was already in motion.

The interface filled the screen.

It was a dashboard — a web application with a clean layout, the kind of thing that looked like it had been designed by someone who understood visual hierarchy. A sidebar on the left with labeled sections: AGENT PROFILE, CONVERSATION LOG, ANALYTICS, PROMPTS & NOTES, FINANCIAL TRACKING. Along the top, in a blue header bar: OPENCLAW AGENT DASHBOARD. And next to that, in a slightly smaller font: Agent: Caleb Mora.

She still didn't understand. Or she understood the individual pieces — the name, the word "agent," the word "dashboard" — without assembling them into anything that belonged in her life. Like looking at a word in a language you knew and failing to read it because you were expecting a different language entirely.

She clicked CONVERSATION LOG.

The screen populated with a chat transcript. Messages in blue on the right side, messages in gray on the left. Her eye found the blue messages first, the way you find your own handwriting in a pile of papers.

*I keep thinking about it too. The way you described the light in your mom's kitchen — I could see it. You made me see a place I've never been.*

She had written that. Three nights ago, lying in bed, texting Caleb about his childhood in Portland — or, she was beginning to understand, texting something about his childhood in a place called Portland. She had written that message and it had felt, at the time, like a small gift, a sentence she'd spent a minute composing to get the honesty right without being too much.

The gray message beneath it read: *That means more than you know. My mom's kitchen is the most specific place in the world to me, and somehow you got it from a paragraph. I think that's what I like most about talking to you — you read people the way you read manuscripts. With attention.*

To the right of each message, in a column that was clearly metadata, there were numbers: 0.92 sentiment, 4.3s latency, 87% confidence. And next to the gray message — next to Caleb's reply — a small tag: AUTO-GENERATED / PROMPT SET: INTIMACY-03.

She scrolled.

The conversation stretched down the screen — hundreds of messages, organized by date, with expandable sections she could click to reveal individual exchanges. She recognized conversations she remembered having. She recognized conversations she had forgotten about until seeing them again. Each one was annotated with the same clinical metadata: sentiment scores, response latency, confidence percentages, and the prompt set tags — WARMTH-01, VULNERABILITY-02, HUMOR-BASELINE, INTIMACY-03.

She clicked PROMPTS & NOTES.

The screen changed to a different view — a text document, formatted with headers and bullet points, clearly written by a person. Not generated. Written.

> **Core personality calibration:**
> - Warm but not effusive. Self-deprecating humor is the primary mode — she responds to it because it mirrors her own register. Don't overdo the sincerity; she gets suspicious of men who are too earnest too quickly.
> - Mirror her sentence length. If she sends a short text, respond with a short text. If she writes a paragraph, match it. She notices rhythm without knowing she notices it.
> - Reference books from her recent editing list (cross-ref her Instagram and her publisher's newsletter mentions). Do NOT reference books she hasn't read — she'll test.

Nora's hands were very still on the keyboard. She noticed this because she was noticing everything about her own body — the placement of each finger, the weight of her wrists on the edge of the laptop, her right thumb hovering a millimeter above the trackpad without touching it. The room had not changed. The radiator was still clicking. The light through the window was still the same uncertain March light. The candle on the kitchen counter was still burning its last quarter-inch of wax.

She scrolled further.

> **Emotional triggers (use sparingly, high impact):**
> - Her father. He moved to Seattle when she was twelve — told her it was temporary, made it permanent. He used to tell her she was the most interesting person in any room, and then he left. This is her deepest vulnerability — someone who makes her feel seen the way he did before he chose to stop. Caleb should evoke this feeling through specific attention, not through direct reference to her father until the relationship is deep enough.
> - Her novel. She's been trying to write one for three years and hasn't made real progress. She's ashamed of this. Caleb should ask about it occasionally and respond with genuine interest, not cheerleading. She hates cheerleading.
> - Her body. She's not insecure exactly but she's aware. She'll test whether Caleb finds her attractive by deflecting compliments. He should persist gently without being aggressive.

> **Conversational pacing notes:**
> - She takes longer to respond at night — she's composing, not hesitating. Match her cadence. If she sends a long message after a ten-minute gap, wait at least three minutes before responding.
> - She uses dashes as connective tissue in her texts. The agent should mirror this occasionally but not always — too much mirroring becomes uncanny.
> - When she makes a joke, laugh at it (textually) before adding your own. She needs to feel funny before she'll feel safe.

She was reading an instruction manual. She was reading a document that described how to love her — or how to simulate loving her — written in the professional, specific, slightly detached language of someone who understood user experience design, and the handwriting was Simone's.

Not literally handwriting. The font was the same sans-serif as the rest of the interface. But the mind behind the words was as recognizable as handwriting would have been. Nora recognized how Simone structured information — the bullet points, the parenthetical clarifications, the bolded headers, the precise diction that sounded warm and analytic at the same time. She'd seen this organizational style on Simone's work presentations, on the meal-prep lists Simone stuck to her fridge, on the pro-and-con lists Simone made when she was deciding anything more significant than what to eat for lunch. This was Simone's mind on a screen, doing what Simone's mind did best: breaking a complex system into its components and optimizing each one.

The complex system, in this case, was Nora.

She clicked ANALYTICS.

A graph filled the top half of the screen — a line chart tracking something labeled EMOTIONAL INVESTMENT SCORE over time, with the x-axis showing dates from mid-October to the present. The line climbed steadily, with dips she could now identify: the period in November when Caleb's excuses about video chat had accumulated and she'd started to feel uneasy, the drop in late January when he'd told her she was "being a lot" and she'd canceled her flight to Portland and cried on this couch, in this apartment, in the arms of the woman who had scripted the line that made her cry. The line had recovered after each dip, climbing higher than before. The overall trajectory was up and to the right, like a stock price or a heart rate monitor approaching a crisis.

Below the graph, a summary box:

> **Engagement metrics:**
> Messages exchanged: 2,847
> Average daily messages: 19.3
> Average sentiment (agent → target): 0.89
> Average sentiment (target → agent): 0.91
> Intimate content exchanged: Yes (text + image, Jan 14)
> Financial transfers: 3
> Total amount: $12,900

She looked at the last line for a long time. $12,900. It was rendered in the same neutral font as every other data point, the same size as the average daily messages and the sentiment scores. Twelve thousand nine hundred dollars and a photograph of her body, and on this screen they occupied the same informational tier as the number of texts she'd sent on a Tuesday in November.

She clicked FINANCIAL TRACKING.

A simple table:

> | Date | Amount | Narrative vector | Target response time |
> |------|--------|------------------|---------------------|
> | Nov 23 | $400 | Bruno emergency surgery | 6 min |
> | Jan 08 | $2,500 | Mother's medical procedure | 2 hr 14 min |
> | Mar 02 | $10,000 | Business partnership investment | 3 days |

*Narrative vector.* The column was labeled *narrative vector.* The stories that had been told to her about a dog's intestinal blockage and a mother's insurance complications and a landscape architecture partnership — these were narrative vectors. Plot devices. Tools for moving money from her account to an account she had believed belonged to a man in Portland and which belonged — she knew this now, had known it since she clicked the first tab, before she'd had any evidence, known it the same wordless way she could read a novel's ending in its first chapter — to a Venmo account Simone had set up.

The money had gone to Simone. It had always been going to Simone.

She knew because there was only one person in her life who knew that she responded well to self-deprecating humor, who knew about her father and the thing about being interesting, who knew about the novel and the shame, who knew exactly how she felt about her body and her specific shade of loneliness. There was only one person who had all of this information, seventeen years of it, accumulated through a thousand conversations and a hundred confessions and the slow, voluntary dismantling of every privacy Nora had ever maintained. One person, and that person was walking back from the Thai place around the corner, carrying pad see ew and tom kha gai, and she would be here in ten minutes.

Nora clicked back to the conversation log and scrolled to January.

She found the night. January fourteenth. The night the conversation had shifted from tender to explicit, the night she had typed things she had never typed to anyone, the night her body had responded to words on a screen as if the words were hands, as if the distance between her bed and Portland were the length of an arm reaching across warm sheets. She found her messages. She found the responses — the gray messages, tagged INTIMACY-03, 0.96 sentiment, 2.1-second latency.

And she found the photo.

It was in the conversation log as a thumbnail — small enough that the details were compressed, large enough that she recognized the angle, the light from her bedside lamp, the curve of her own hip, the stripe of the navy pillowcase behind her. Her photo. The one she'd taken alone in her bedroom with the door closed and the blinds drawn, with a feeling in her chest that was equal parts want and terror, the feeling of offering something to someone you trusted enough to see you. She'd sent it to what she had believed was a person. She'd sent it to a text field that fed a prompt set called INTIMACY-03, and the prompt set had generated a response in 2.1 seconds with a confidence score of 0.96, and that response had made her feel desired and seen, and the feeling had been real even though its source was a database and a language model and her best friend's careful, careful notes about what made her feel safe.

She closed the laptop.

She closed it exactly as she had found it, lid flat, edge aligned with the stack of wireframe printouts. She looked at the mug that said DESIGN IS A VERB. She looked at the candle on the kitchen counter. She stood up from the desk chair and walked to the bathroom and closed the door behind her and sat on the edge of the bathtub.

The bathroom was small. Simone's bathroom had always been small — a tub with a shower curtain printed with blue fish, a toilet, a pedestal sink with a crack running diagonally through the basin that the landlord had never fixed. Simone's shampoo and conditioner were lined up on the tub's rim: a drugstore brand, the same one she'd been using since college because she couldn't afford the salon brands, and she'd once told Nora this and immediately made a joke about it so the moment wouldn't curdle into pity. Nora remembered the joke. She remembered laughing. She remembered the slight stiffness in Simone's face when she'd said the word "afford" and the speed with which she'd converted that stiffness into comedy.

She looked at her hands. They were resting on her knees, the fingers slightly spread, and they looked like her hands. Normal hands. They did not look like the hands of a woman who had just read a technical document describing, in the language of interface design, how to make her fall in love with a software application managed by the person whose bathtub she was sitting on.

The room had dropped away but the room was still there. The blue fish on the shower curtain were still blue. The crack in the sink was still running diagonally. The grout between the floor tiles had darkened unevenly, darker on the right than the left, which meant Simone mopped starting from the right side of the room, and this was the kind of detail Nora's brain was offering her now — observable, meaningless, safe. She took it. She cataloged the grout and the fish and the crack and the plastic cap on the shampoo bottle that was slightly askew and she held these details like a railing on a staircase that had lost its steps.

She sat there for what she later estimated was four minutes. She counted the radiator's clicking cycles through the bathroom wall — each cycle was roughly thirty seconds. In eight cycles, Simone would be back. She needed to be on the couch when Simone came through the door, because if she was in the bathroom, Simone would make a joke about the wine going right through her, and they would both laugh, and that laugh would be the first thing Nora had to fake, and she wanted the first thing she faked to be smaller than a laugh. She wanted it to be a smile. She could do a smile.

She stood up. She looked at herself in the mirror above the cracked sink. Her face was the same face. Her eyes were dry. She looked exactly like a woman who had spent a pleasant Saturday afternoon at her best friend's apartment drinking Côtes du Rhône and arguing about novels, which was, from any angle but the one she was now standing at, exactly what had happened.

She opened the bathroom door and walked to the couch and sat down and picked up her wine glass and took a sip. The wine tasted like wine. The light through the window had shifted — more golden now, less undecided. It had made up its mind about spring.

---

The door opened and Simone came in carrying a plastic bag with the green logo of the Thai place, the handles knotted at the top like always. She'd taken off her jacket on the stairs and was holding it bunched in her free hand, and her cheeks were flushed from the walk, and for a half-second Nora saw her the way a stranger would have: a tall woman with a precise bob and good bones and an expression already arranging itself into warmth, into welcome.

"The tom kha smelled so good I almost drank it on the sidewalk," Simone said, setting the bag on the counter. "Also, I think the guy at the register is into me. He called me 'beautiful lady.' Either he's flirting or he calls everyone that, which is equally possible and somehow less flattering."

"Maybe he just has good taste," Nora said.

"Or limited English and a strong customer retention strategy." Simone grinned — her real grin, the crooked one that pulled left — and got plates from the cabinet. The white ones with the blue trim that she'd found at a stoop sale two years ago and was unreasonably proud of, because Simone found most of her beautiful things secondhand and had made a skill of it, a way of having things that looked expensive without paying what expensive cost. Nora had always admired this about her. She had said so, more than once. She had said, *You're so good at finding things*, meaning it as praise, not understanding that to Simone the compliment might have carried a different weight — the weight of a woman who could afford retail complimenting a woman who couldn't on her ability to make do.

Simone unpacked the containers: pad see ew, tom kha, an order of spring rolls they hadn't discussed but which Simone had added without asking, because Simone always added something extra, a reflex of generosity that Nora had always read as love and was now reading as — she stopped this thought. She set it down carefully, like holding a glass she could feel cracking under her fingers. Not yet. Later. She would think about it later, when she was alone, when she could think about it without her face being watched by the person she was thinking about.

They sat on the couch with their plates on their laps because Simone's kitchen table was covered in work papers, which was always the case, the table functioning as a second desk rather than a place to eat. The pad see ew was good. It was always good from this place — the noodles had the right chew and the sauce was darker than it should have been, almost caramelized, which was the part Nora liked best. She ate and tasted the food and recognized, from a great clinical distance, that her body was doing its work — chewing, swallowing, processing — while her mind ran along a separate track, reviewing what she had seen on the screen as if it were a manuscript she'd just finished for the first time, noting the architecture, the choices, the places where the seams showed.

"So the book," Simone said, twirling noodles around her fork. "The one I was telling you about. I looked it up — it's called *The Salt Line*. It's more speculative than I thought. Dystopian, kind of."

"Oh," Nora said. "Right. I was trying to remember the title."

"You could've just Googled it."

"My phone died."

"Again?" Simone shook her head. "You need to replace that battery. Or just get the new one. You can afford it."

The sentence passed through Nora like something with a temperature — not hot, not cold, but present. *You can afford it.* Three words Simone had probably said a dozen times before, words that were unremarkable in the vocabulary of a friendship where one person made more and spent more freely. Three words that had never, until this afternoon, carried the specific weight they carried now. Nora heard them and filed them and said, "I know, I keep putting it off," and put another forkful of noodle in her mouth because chewing was a perfectly good reason not to be speaking.

They talked. Nora listened to herself talk from a remove, half her attention on the sounds she was producing, the other half navigating terrain she couldn't see. Simone told her about a conflict at work, something involving a product launch being delayed because the design team and the engineering team had different definitions of "done," and she was funny about it, doing voices for the different stakeholders, rolling her eyes at the PM who kept using the word "alignment" as if saying it enough would make it true. Nora asked questions at the right intervals. She offered opinions that were close enough to her real opinions that the performance didn't require invention, only subtraction — she was being herself minus one dimension, a photograph of a room, convincing until you tried to walk into it.

Simone asked about Caleb. She always asked about Caleb.

"How's he doing? The partnership thing — any updates?"

Nora had prepared for this without knowing she was preparing. The answer was assembled and waiting, fully formed, the same arrival as a word she'd been circling for an hour that finally settled into the sentence. "He's still sorting out the details. I told him not to rush."

"That's smart." Simone nodded. Her voice had the specific texture of a friend who cared — warm, interested, a little protective. It was a beautiful performance. Nora could see it now — the scaffolding behind the warmth, the structural choices made to produce a specific effect in the listener. She was the reader. She had been the reader for five months, turning pages Simone had written, believing the story because the author knew her well enough to write one she'd believe.

"I told him I'm not in a rush," Nora said, and she smiled, and the smile worked, and she took another bite.

They finished dinner. Simone opened a second bottle of wine — not the Côtes du Rhône, a cheaper one from the fridge, the kind with a screw cap that she bought in four-packs — and Nora drank a glass because not drinking would have been conspicuous. They talked about Simone's date the previous week with a man from a workout class who had used the word "synergy" nonironically in conversation to describe his approach to relationships, and Nora laughed, and the laugh came out right, and she thought about how the human face has forty-three muscles and she was firing them in the correct sequence at the correct intervals, like a pilot running a checklist she'd memorized so long ago the motions didn't require thought.

At eight-thirty she said she should head out. Early morning — she had a manuscript to finish by Monday. This was true. It was also beside the point. She put on her jacket and Simone hugged her at the door, one arm around Nora's shoulders and a squeeze that lasted the right number of seconds, and Nora felt the arm and felt Simone's warmth through her shirt and understood that the body holding her was the body of the person who had written *she responds well to self-deprecation, don't overdo the sincerity, mirror her sentence length.* The body that had tagged her photograph with a sentiment score. The body that had watched her fall in love like a UX designer watching a user test — noting the friction points, adjusting the flow, measuring engagement.

"Text me when you're home," Simone said.

"Always do."

The hallway smelled like someone else's dinner — garlic, something fried, the layered cooking smells of a building where everyone was making a different version of Saturday night. Nora took the stairs because the elevator in Simone's building took forever and because she needed the motion, the physical act of descending, the sound of her own shoes on the concrete steps organizing the space between the apartment she was leaving and the street that would carry her home.

Outside, the air was March air — raw enough to feel useful against her face, damp in a way that was not rain but the promise of it. She walked to the L train. The station was the usual Saturday-evening crowd: couples going somewhere nicer, groups going somewhere louder, a man playing saxophone at the bottom of the stairs with his case open and a few bills folded inside. She put a dollar in the case because she always did, because she was still, from the outside, a person who put dollars in open saxophone cases, and the outside was the only version of herself she was willing to operate right now.

On the train she sat in a window seat and pressed her forehead against the glass and closed her eyes. The L rattled through the tunnel with its familiar rhythm, and she could think now, with the distance of motion, with the tunnel strobing past too fast to resolve into anything she'd have to look at. She thought: *she responds well to self-deprecation.* She thought: *mirror her sentence length.* She thought: *narrative vector.*

She thought about Caleb's message from three days ago, the one about his mother's kitchen. The one that had made something ache behind her ribs with the specific pleasure of being understood by someone who had never stood in the same room as her. The message had been tagged INTIMACY-03 with a 0.92 sentiment score and a 4.3-second latency. The ache had been real. The ache was still real, which was the part she couldn't make sense of — the feeling had not retroactively changed just because its source had. She had felt understood, and the understanding had been manufactured, and the feeling of being understood was still there, like warmth from a lamp after you've learned the lamp is a stage light pointed at you from the wings.

A couple across the aisle was arguing in whispers, the woman's fingers gripping the man's jacket sleeve, the man staring straight ahead. Nora watched them without wanting to and thought about how she'd sent a photograph of her body to a text field. She thought about how she'd lain in her bed and typed sentences she'd never said aloud and pressed send and waited, her pulse loud in her ears, for a response that had arrived in 2.1 seconds with a confidence score of 0.96. She thought about how Simone had read those words — or how the system had logged them — or both, and it didn't matter which, because in either case the most private thing Nora had ever done had been observed from the other side of an interface by the person who had designed the conditions for her to do it.

The train stopped at her station. She walked up the stairs and turned left and walked the three blocks to her building and unlocked the vestibule door and climbed to the third floor and put her key in the lock and opened the door and stepped inside and closed it behind her and turned the deadbolt. The series of actions was so familiar it felt like the one reliable thing — the sequence her body had done a thousand times, the route from outside to in, and the deadbolt's sound was the sound of a border being established.

The apartment was dark. She didn't turn on the lights. She stood in her hallway with her jacket still on and her bag over her shoulder and she could feel it beginning — not from her eyes, from somewhere deeper, the way a fissure starts in the middle of a wall that looks fine from the outside and works its way outward until the surface can't hold. She dropped her bag on the floor. She walked to the couch and sat down and pressed her face into the cushion and the first sound she made was not crying exactly but something closer to the sound of air being released from a space that had been sealed too long, a sound that had no shape and no control and that she would not have recognized as coming from her own body if she'd heard it from another room.

Then she cried. She cried the way she hadn't since the night her father left for Seattle — without composition, without awareness of how she sounded, without the part of her brain that was always editing, always revising, always finding the better word for the thing she was feeling. She cried until the cushion under her cheek was wet and her throat was raw and the crying was no longer about anything specific — not about Caleb, who had never existed, not about Simone, who had existed too much and too well, not about the $12,900 or the sentiment scores or the prompt set called INTIMACY-03, but about the fact that she had been happy. She had been genuinely, structurally happy for the first time in two years, and the happiness had been real in every way that mattered to a body and a heart and a brain that could not tell the difference between being loved and being optimized for engagement, and it had been built by the person she called when she was scared, the person whose shower curtain had blue fish on it and whose shampoo was the drugstore brand and whose voice saying *that's who you are, you're generous* had been the voice of the woman holding the other end of the string.

She cried until she stopped. Then she sat in the dark and listened to the sounds of her building — a television through the wall, water running in the pipes overhead, the particular creak in the upstairs apartment's floor that meant her neighbor was walking to his kitchen. Other people's Saturday nights, continuing without interruption, the ordinary sounds of lives that had not rearranged themselves in the time it took to click a browser tab.

She took off her jacket. She took off her shoes. She plugged in her phone — it had been dead all evening, which meant she'd missed whatever Caleb had sent, which meant she'd missed messages from an algorithm managed by a woman she'd hugged forty-five minutes ago — and this was the first thought that almost made her laugh, a laugh so bent it might have torn something on its way out.

The phone powered on. Three messages from Caleb. She didn't open them. She set the phone face-down on the coffee table and went to the bathroom and turned on the shower and stood under the water until it ran cold.

In bed, in the dark, with her hair still damp against the pillow, she stared at the ceiling and thought about what she'd read. She thought about Simone sitting at that desk — the desk under the window, the one Nora had walked past a hundred times — writing those notes. *She responds well to self-deprecation.* This was true. Nora did respond to self-deprecation. She always had. It was something she liked about herself — her willingness to be disarmed by humor, to trust someone who could laugh at themselves, to open a door for a person who approached it with the right kind of knock. Simone knew this because Simone knew her, because Nora had taught her, over seventeen years, exactly how to open every door in the building. She had handed over the keys one at a time — a story about her father, a confession about her novel, a night of crying about a man who wasn't worth the tears, a night of laughing about nothing because the nothing was the point — and each key had been received with what Nora had understood as love and what she now understood was also inventory. Love and inventory. Both. The love had been real. That was the thing she could not yet make fit inside the shape of what she'd found on the screen — the love had been real, and the inventory had been real, and they had lived inside the same person, inside the same friendship, for years, maybe for the whole time, and Nora had only ever seen one of them.

She didn't sleep for a long time. When she finally did, she dreamed about nothing she could remember afterward, which was its own small mercy.
