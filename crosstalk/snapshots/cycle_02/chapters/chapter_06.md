# Chapter 6

Six hours after they hauled the fighters in and killed the main engines, the Bebop already sounded poorer.

With the ship cold, every small noise traveled. The hull ticked as it gave up heat. The life-support fans pushed a tired hum through the vents. Somewhere behind Jet's left shoulder, one loose spoon in the galley drawer kept tapping metal on metal every time the ship corrected its drift by a hair. Mars turned under them in a slow red sweep through the forward ports, the Hellas basin all rust scars and dark cuts, beautiful in the same useless way an expensive watch was beautiful when you couldn't afford lunch.

Jet stood in the galley with the cold-storage door open and took inventory like he was conducting an autopsy.

He had three bell peppers, and one of them was already wrinkling at the stem. Half an onion was wrapped in wax paper. There was a bottle of soy sauce with enough left to wet a pan and not much more, plus a packet of textured protein flakes that had gone stale so long ago they had become a geological feature. There was no beef.

The orange traffic summons lay under the salt tin so it wouldn't curl in the damp. `RECKLESS FLYING IN SETTLEMENT AIRSPACE`, it said in the neat bureaucratic block print of people who never had to choose between fuel and dinner. Eight thousand woolongs. Jet had turned it face down twice. It kept making its way back up like something alive.

He shut the cold-storage door with his hip, set the peppers on the board, and started cutting.

Across the common room, Spike was on the couch with one ankle over the other knee and a receiver balanced on his stomach. He kept rolling the dial with two fingers, slow, patient, like he was trying to tune in a station from another decade. What came out was mostly hiss with the occasional chop of a cargo marker or a burst of settlement weather. He'd said once, when Jet asked him why the hell he listened to static, that static helped him think. Jet had told him to think quieter.

Tonight Spike had been at it since they'd shut down the engines.

Faye sat at the table with one bare foot tucked under her, stripping chipped polish off her thumbnail with the edge of a card. The solvent smell mixed with oil, soy, old pepper, dog, and the stale trapped odor of four people living too close in a ship that had never been meant to be a home. Her hair was clipped up with a bent piece of wire because she'd lost the real clip again or pawned it or forgotten where she'd put it. With Faye all three explanations could be true.

Ed lay on the deck plates between the table and the couch, goggles up on forehead, talking to Ein while fitting bits of metallic chaff into the grooves of a dismantled toy drone.

"Sneaky ship confetti, confetti of deceit," Ed announced. "Ed will make a crown for Professor Ein, king of all canyons and also of all very rude lady pilots."

Ein sneezed because one filament of chaff had floated onto his nose.

"See?" Faye said. "Even the dog's offended."

Jet scraped the peppers into a bowl. "The dog's got more sense than the rest of you."

"The dog's got better seating than the rest of us," Faye said.

Ed sat up and set two strips of chaff across Ein's head between the ears. Ein held still with the patience of a creature who had accepted that this was his life now.

"Regal," Ed said. "Majestic. Slightly itchy."

Jet found the wok hanging where it always hung, blackened from years of too much heat and too little money, and set it on the burner. The gas caught on the second click. He added oil, then onion, then peppers, and the sound of them hitting hot metal was the best thing he'd heard all day.

Nobody spoke for a minute. The ship filled with the smell.

It should have been comforting. Usually it was. The first hit of frying onion, the pepper skin blistering, the soy going sharp in the heat. Usually that smell meant the day had stopped doing damage for at least twenty minutes.

Tonight the name kept moving through Jet's head anyway: Hosuke Takara.

Seventeen years old and stupid enough to think a cheap alias could save him from being himself. He had not heard the name out loud in almost twenty years. He had not written it in longer than that. He had seen it on a screen two days ago with a bounty number under it and a description of his own younger face, and now it was everywhere. In the scrape of the knife on the board. In the print on the summons. In the way his hand had gone stupid when Ed had said it, cheerful as a game-show answer.

He tipped the stale protein flakes into the pan. They landed light, barely a handful, and soaked up oil without turning into anything a decent person would call food.

Faye watched the pan, then looked at Jet.

"You're kidding."

"Nope."

"That can't be the whole protein situation."

"That," Jet said, stirring, "is the whole protein situation."

Spike's gaze stayed on the receiver. "Looks optimistic."

"You wanna cook?"

"No."

"Then don't start."

Faye made a face at the pan. "If this is bell peppers and beef, I'm a nun."

Jet kept stirring. "You'd be a terrible nun."

"I'd be an excellent nun. I'd skim from the collection plate and sleep late."

"That's not being a nun."

"That's being any profession with a uniform."

Spike snorted without looking up.

Jet pointed the spoon at him. "Don't encourage her."

"Wasn't planning to."

"You already did."

"Too late," Faye said. "I'm encouraged."

The old rhythm was there, cheap, automatic, almost muscular. They could do it half-asleep. Jet cooked, Faye complained, Spike fired lazy rounds from the couch, Ed made the whole thing stranger than it had been a second before. Most days the bickering felt less like conflict than like proof of life. If the ship was loud, it was still moving.

Tonight it caught in places.

Ed crawled closer on elbows and knees, peering up into the wok. "Ed sees peppers. Ed sees onion. Ed sees fibrous lies."

"Ed sees dinner," Jet said.

"Dinner sees Ed."

Spike turned the dial a little farther. Static flared, then broke around the clipped voice of some freight dispatcher reading coordinates in a bored monotone. He let the bored voice finish the coordinates before moving on.

Jet plated the food into four bowls and a saucer for Ein. Each serving was mostly peppers with a little onion and brown scraps clinging where the protein had decided not to surrender all the way.

"All right," he said. "Eat."

Faye looked into her bowl, then at the ticket, then back into the bowl. "You know what? I actually think the ticket tastes better."

"Good," Jet said. "You can eat that."

"I'm not paying it."

"Nobody said you were."

"You looked at me when you said eat."

"I looked at everybody."

"No, you didn't. You looked at me."

Spike shifted one eye up from the receiver. "He did kind of look at you."

Jet set his own bowl down a little harder than he meant to. "Will both of you shut up and sit down before the peppers turn into wallpaper?"

That got them moving. Faye hooked the foot down, dragged her chair out with a squeal, and sat. Spike folded himself up from the couch and came over slow, receiver still in hand, trailing the cord until Jet told him if he dumped the thing in the food he could eat it. Spike planted himself at the end of the table and set the receiver by his elbow like it had a reserved place.

Ed sat cross-legged on the deck with bowl in lap. Ein circled once before settling next to him with the patience of the deeply resigned.

For a little while there was only the sound of eating.

It was not a happy sound. Bell peppers never made a happy sound. They squeaked against teeth. They released water at the wrong moment. The fake protein did what it always did, which was to suggest the memory of meat to nobody at the table.

Still, they ate fast. Broke had a way of making people grateful with their mouths and bitter everywhere else.

Faye chewed, swallowed, pointed chopsticks at the summons. "Eight thousand."

Jet kept his eyes on his bowl. "I know how to read."

"Do you? Because I want to make sure we're all appreciating it properly. Eight. Thousand."

"You can count, too. This is turning into a banner day."

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

"No, you're doing that thing where you act calm so you can have a stroke in private."

Spike reached for the soy bottle. It came out in two reluctant drops. "He doesn't need a stroke. He needs pilots."

Faye looked at him. "Oh, go to hell."

"You cut across settlement traffic."

"Because somebody boxed me out of the clean line."

"There wasn't a clean line."

"There was until you decided canyon walls were optional."

"The tunnel closed the angle."

"There was no angle. There was a bad idea in a cheap suit."

Spike lifted his bowl and ate like they were discussing weather.

Jet rubbed two fingers over the bridge of his nose. "Can we not do the whole damn chase from the beginning?"

"No," Faye said. "I'd like to do it from the beginning. From the part where we left Mars broke and came back more broke because Captain Cool wanted to make friends with a cliff."

"I didn't hit the cliff."

"Congratulations. Put that on the wall."

"You almost hit an ore hauler."

"Almost isn't paperwork."

Jet slapped the summons with the back of his fingers. The paper made a dry snick on the table. "This is paperwork."

Ed raised a hand. "Ed would like the record to show that Ed said left-left-up and then small right and then whoosh, but grown-ups were busy doing loud ego rectangles."

"What the hell is an ego rectangle?" Jet said.

Ed shoved a pepper strip into mouth, thought about it, swallowed. "A rectangle made of ego."

Spike nodded once. "Accurate."

"Don't you start with accurate," Faye said. "You were the one out there playing lone wolf."

"It's a single-seat fighter."

"It's not a personality."

"You fly alone, too."

"Yeah, but I make it look good."

"You almost got vaporized by a freight hauler."

"Almost," Faye said again, pointing at him with chopsticks. "Not paperwork."

Jet could feel the argument trying to become one of the useful ones, the kind that actually sorted blame into neat little piles. It never worked. They all knew it never worked. The chase had failed the way Bebop jobs failed most often: everybody had been right about their own piece and wrong about the whole thing.

He shoveled peppers into his mouth so he wouldn't say what he was thinking, which was that he had seen the girl slip that ship through the maintenance cut on the sensor replay afterward and had felt, under the rage and the bill and the humiliation, one clean line of respect. She'd measured them better than they'd measured her. She had played them like a rhythm section that thought it was improvising.

He hated that he respected it.

Faye looked from Jet to Spike and back again. "You know what's really pissing me off?"

"The peppers?" Spike said.

"Everything's pissing me off. Don't narrow it."

"Helpful if you specify."

"Fine. What's pissing me off right now is that none of us even got close enough to tag her. We burned fuel we didn't have, we got a ticket, and for what? For a drone. A stupid little freight drone with a light taped to it."

Ed brightened. "Very clever drone."

"I know it was clever, Ed."

"Beautiful deceitful drone."

"Still a drone."

Spike set his chopsticks down. "I got close."

Jet looked up. "What?"

Spike shrugged. "For a second."

Faye leaned forward. "Close enough to what?"

"The cockpit."

Jet felt the room change by less than a degree.

"And?" he said.

Spike scraped one pepper through sauce with the tip of a chopstick. "Young."

"That's it?"

"Dark hair."

"That's very helpful," Faye said.

"Focused."

"That is even less helpful."

"Didn't look scared," Spike said.

He said it flat. He wasn't offering it up as drama. It was just the thing itself.

Jet heard the part underneath anyway. She hadn't been scared or surprised or improvising because she'd gotten lucky. She had been flying the route she'd prepared for.

"Great," Faye said. "So we got humiliated by a child."

"She wasn't a child."

"You saw her for one second."

"Long enough."

"That's creepy."

"That's eyesight."

Jet waited for Spike to say more. He didn't. Of course he didn't. He went back to eating like he'd contributed his full legal share.

Faye watched him, annoyed on principle and because she could smell that there was something else there and wasn't going to get it. "You know, when normal people have useful information, they keep going after the first three words."

"I'm not normal people."

"No kidding."

Jet should have stepped in. Usually he did. He usually cut across the table before the tone took on that extra edge, before Faye went from entertainingly abrasive to properly mean and Spike decided mean was close enough to intimate to keep participating.

Instead he reached for his bowl again and found it empty.

Faye noticed. Faye noticed everything she could use and half the things she couldn't.

"You gonna say something," she asked, "or are you just going to stare holes in ceramic?"

Jet looked at her. "About what?"

"About the chase. About the ticket. About how this is somehow all our fault except yours."

"I didn't say that."

"You didn't have to."

"All right," Jet said. "You want my expert opinion? Here it is. We flew hot and dumb into terrain she knew better than we did. Spike tried to get cute, you tried to get ahead of the map, I kept the Bebop too close because I thought bulk would scare her into the open, and Ed was the only one giving useful information but none of us could understand it because Ed talks like a broken parade. Happy?"

Ed lifted chopsticks in salute. "Broken parade! Good band name."

Faye blinked once. "You left yourself in there."

"Yeah."

"Huh."

Jet reached for the summons, folded it once, then unfolded it again because folding it felt too much like giving up. "Doesn't change the number."

Nobody said anything. The summons sat between the soy bottle and the bowls like another dish nobody wanted.

That was the other thing off tonight. Jet was not usually this easy about blame. He argued details on instinct. He corrected for sport. If he was going to say he got something wrong, he usually dragged three caveats behind it and pointed out the six ways everyone else had made it worse. He was the kind of man who could turn an apology into a maintenance lecture.

The quiet sat there strange as a new piece of furniture.

Faye eased back in her chair. "How much fuel?"

Jet gave her a look.

"What?"

"You care now?"

"I care if I have to eat this again tomorrow."

"We might be so lucky."

"Jet."

He sighed. "Enough to move if we have to. Not enough to go joyriding across half of Mars because somebody gets a feeling."

Spike pointed a chopstick at the summons. "Ticket before fuel?"

"Food before both, unless you want to start chewing insulation."

"I'm not doing that," Faye said.

"Shame. Might improve your disposition."

"My disposition's fine."

"Your disposition's armed."

Spike's mouth twitched.

Faye caught it. "What?"

"Nothing."

"Don't 'nothing' me when you're smiling."

"Wasn't smiling."

"You were absolutely smiling."

"Maybe at the food."

"Then you're delirious."

Ed licked sauce from thumb and peered at Jet's bowl, then at the pan still on the burner. "Is there second dinner?"

"No."

"Tiny second dinner?"

"Also no."

Ed sighed with great theatrical sorrow and tipped two pepper strips from bowl to Ein's saucer. "Professor Ein, we suffer together."

Ein ate with the focus of a professional.

Jet got up and took the pan off the flame. There was enough stuck to the bottom for one bitter mouthful. He scraped it into his own bowl and stood at the sink longer than necessary, hands braced on either side, staring through the port over the faucet where Mars floated huge and wrong-colored and indifferent.

His reflection in the glass was faint, all beard and scar and the dark bulk of his shoulders. With the galley light behind him and the planet below, the double-image he hated clicked into place: the man he had built and the boy under the alias nobody was supposed to remember. Same shape at the jaw. Same stupid certainty in the posture. He turned away before the comparison finished forming.

"You know what we should do?" Faye said.

Jet rinsed the pan. "This ought to be good."

"Sell the receiver."

Spike didn't look up. "No."

"You love that thing more than people."

"It's still worth less than your makeup case."

"My makeup case has resale value."

"Everything you own has lipstick on it," Jet said. "That lowers the bid."

"That's called branding."

"That's called contamination."

Ed pointed at the summons. "Ed has solution. We mail them peppers."

"Mars traffic enforcement?" Jet said.

"Yes. With apology note. 'Dear sky police, please accept peppers in this difficult time.'"

Faye laughed despite herself, quick and unwilling. "I'd sign that."

"No one is mailing anybody peppers."

"Not even one pepper?" Ed asked.

"Especially not one pepper."

Spike rolled the receiver dial again. Another wash of static breathed over the room.

Jet dried the pan, hung it up, and said, "And for the record, nobody's selling anything until I say we're selling it."

Faye crossed her arms. "You don't get to say that like you're our landlord."

"Somebody has to keep this ship flying."

"It's not flying," Spike said. "It's falling in a controlled way."

"Thank you, Spike. That's exactly the kind of morale-building bullshit I was hoping for."

"Any time."

Faye slid her empty bowl toward the center. "So what's the plan?"

"The plan," Jet said, "is we stay cold, save fuel, and think."

"That's not a plan. That's being broke slowly."

"It's a better plan than burning another tank on pride."

"Maybe she'd double back."

"Maybe she'd paint her ship yellow and ask us politely to follow," Jet said. "Maybe we'd all grow wings. Eat your peppers and let me be realistic."

Faye opened her mouth, then shut it. Not because he'd won. Because the realism in his voice didn't have his usual annoyance wrapped around it. It came out heavier than that, less like a lecture and more like something he'd already said to himself and hadn't liked any better the first time.

She looked at him a moment too long.

Jet saw it and went practical on instinct. "If you want something useful to do, scrape the bowls."

"There he is," Faye said.

"Scrape."

"You know, 'please' exists."

"So do sponges."

She made a face, but she stacked the bowls.

Spike rose with his own and reached for the summons on the way past. He flicked it with a finger. "Eight thousand for 'reckless.'"

"Read the rest."

Spike leaned in. "'In a settlement safety corridor with disregard for posted restrictions.'" He set it down. "That's a lot of words for bad luck."

"That wasn't bad luck," Jet said.

Spike glanced at him. Not long. Long enough.

Jet took the bowls from Faye and ran water into the sink. "No. It wasn't."

Nobody touched that. The room thinned out around the sentence. Even Ed, usually happy to climb straight through any silence he found, only hummed to Ein and rearranged the chaff crown into shoulder decorations.

After a minute Faye said, lighter on purpose, "If we all die because you made me eat peppers again, I'm haunting this kitchen first."

"Get in line," Spike said.

Jet snorted. "You'd all come back here. None of you know how to cook."

"I know how to cook," Faye said.

"No, you know how to set things on fire in oil."

"That's one school of cooking."

"It's not a good one."

"Depends what you're trying to kill," Spike said.

That got a small laugh out of Jet before he could stop it. It felt rusted from disuse.

Faye heard it and looked over again, quick and sharp, like she was checking whether it had cost him something.

He gave her nothing back but the side of his face and the sound of bowls knocking in the sink.

By the time the dishes were done, the orange summons was the only bright thing left on the table.

Jet folded it once, exact and careful this time, and tucked it under the sugar jar with the kind of precision men used when there was no precision available anywhere that mattered.

***

Later, with the meal cleared and the common room lights dropped to the yellow half-power that made everybody look more tired than they were, the Bebop settled into its night positions.

Jet wiped down the counter a second time because the first time had not improved his mood enough. There were still pepper seeds in the grooves by the stove and a ring from the soy bottle on the table and a fine grit of Mars dust on the sill over the sink because dust on Mars did not respect doors, seals, or good intentions. He picked up each pepper seed with the tip of a damp cloth. He did not usually clean like this unless something had him by the back of the neck.

The name still did.

Not because he missed being that kid. He didn't. Hosuke Takara had been all appetite and no sense, a boy dumb enough to think petty cargo fraud was a future instead of a hallway with a locked door at the end. Jet had spent years deciding that boy was dead. Then one dirty database entry and there he was again, smirking through a physical description in an ISSP file nobody outside the system should have been able to touch.

He wrung out the cloth harder than the job required.

From the bathroom at the end of the corridor came the thin metal click of the medicine cabinet.

Faye stood in front of the mirror and leaned in until the bad fluorescent showed every rough edge in the ship's cheap glass. The mirror had a dark spot at one corner where the backing had started to go and a scratch down the middle that made it look like somebody had once taken a knife to their own reflection and quit halfway through.

She put two fingers against the hollow under her collarbone.

Then she took them away.

Then she put them back.

It wasn't even a gesture, not really. Not anything a sane person would call identifying. Miriam remembered a woman on Ganymede touching herself there when she had a good hand at cards. So what. People had habits. People touched their faces, their hair, the place their necklaces used to sit. She had probably touched that spot a thousand times before Miriam's tired Ganymede accent made it feel borrowed.

She touched it again.

The skin there was warm from the ship and the bad heat, smoother than the chipped sink under her other hand. There was no answer in it. No hidden seam that opened. No tag stitched under the flesh reading MADE FOR SOMEONE ELSE.

Faye hated that she had come in here for this. She hated it enough that she did it once more, slower, watching the movement in the mirror. Her fingers landed at the notch above the breast, her thumb tucked, her elbow angled out. The movement looked casual, unthinking, familiar.

It was too familiar.

"Shit," she said softly to herself.

The word fogged the mirror, then thinned until her face came back through it.

She opened the cabinet. There was aspirin, one tube of expired antiseptic, and a lipstick worn down to a red stub. She looked anyway, as if one of them might explain how a stranger could carry your name around some moon while you slept on ice and came back owing half the solar system money you never spent.

There was nothing there, just the same bathroom they'd all used for years, smelling faintly of soap that never quite covered engine grease.

She shut the cabinet harder than she meant to, pulled the bent wire from her hair, then jammed it back in again because letting it down would feel like giving the mirror too much.

When she came out, she crossed the common room without looking at anybody and dropped into the chair by the porthole.

Spike was still with the receiver.

He'd moved from the table to the couch again and stretched out sideways, one foot on the arm, the old habit of taking up more room than a thin man had any right to. The receiver rested against his ribs. On low volume the static sounded less like noise than like weather far off, dry and intermittent. Every so often some dead-band handshake would try to form out of it and fail. It had a rhythm: three clipped tones, a burst, an empty carrier, another burst.

Nobody else in the room knew those patterns well enough to hear them as language.

Spike wished he didn't.

He kept telling himself he was checking once more so he could stop checking. That if the old syndicate band was quiet tonight maybe it would stay quiet tomorrow, and if it didn't stay quiet tomorrow he'd deal with that tomorrow, and if some remnant operator on the other end of the system had heard the handshake his alias had kicked loose then maybe they hadn't cared enough to write it down.

Maybe that was true.

The maybes were cheap. That was the problem with them.

Jet stacked plates in the drying rack with more care than plates needed.

"You gonna listen to that thing all night?" he asked without turning.

"Maybe."

"Sounds like a refrigerator dying."

"Comforting, then."

Jet glanced over his shoulder. "If the refrigerator sounded like that, I'd have it in pieces on the counter."

"That's why it sounds different."

Faye made a face toward the porthole, not quite smiling. "You two should get a room and argue in full sentences."

"I'm arguing in full sentences," Jet said.

"You know what I mean."

"No, I don't."

"Of course you don't."

Spike rolled the dial. A clipped wash of coded pulse stuttered through before dissolving into hiss. His fingers stopped for a fraction of a beat.

Faye turned her head. "That music's terrible."

"It's not music."

"Really? I never would've guessed."

"Helps me think."

Jet reached for the dish towel again, found the counter already dry, and folded the towel into a square. Then another square. "Thinking about what?"

Spike looked at the receiver instead of Jet. "Nothing useful."

That could have been a joke. It wasn't. Jet heard the flat note under it and did not press because he was in no shape to have his own evasions recognized in somebody else's.

Ed had abandoned the drone parts and migrated to the open patch of deck in front of the couch with a deck of bent playing cards, one sock, and a strip of insulation foam that had somehow become part of the game. No one asked where the insulation had come from. Some questions were really just repair bills wearing mustaches.

Ein flopped onto his side while Ed dealt cards over the dog's ribs.

"Professor Ein has four queens," Ed informed the room. "This is mathematically suspicious, but Professor Ein rejects the tyranny of arithmetic."

Ein thumped his tail once.

"I don't think that's how poker works," Faye said.

"That is because Faye thinks too linearly."

"No, it's because I know what four queens means."

"Royal abundance."

"Cheating."

"Same family," Spike murmured.

Faye pointed at him. "See, now that was a smile."

He ignored her.

Jet checked the chrono over the stove. Late enough that normal people would have been asleep. Nothing on the Bebop happened at a normal hour unless it involved disaster.

He went to the storage locker under the bench for a clean dishcloth and found the folded wool blanket instead, shoved in with table linens because Ed had taken it to make a tent two days ago and no one had put it back. The blanket was thin from years of washing and rough in places where the weave had tightened. One corner had a brown half-moon singe from when Spike had dropped an ash on it months back and then claimed the ship was always attacking him unprovoked.

Jet left it where it was for the moment and took the cloth.

By the time he turned around again, Ed's card game had slowed.

That was how it usually happened. It was never a gradual bedtime or a normal kid's night routine. Ed moved at one speed until the body quit and then sleep took Ed like a switch being thrown. One minute Ed was still chattering, and the next Ed was gone.

Ed was on stomach now, cheek on crossed arms, one card stuck to forehead. The bare feet that were always in motion had gone still. Ein had rotated twice and wedged himself against Ed's stomach in the warm crescent under the bent knees. Every few breaths one of Ed's fingers twitched against the deck plate, as if finishing the game somewhere else.

"Ed has not conceded," Ed mumbled into the floor.

"Sure," Faye said softly.

"Strategic resting."

"Yeah."

Spike lowered the receiver volume without looking at the controls.

Jet stood still with the dishcloth in one hand.

The ship made its small old-ship noises around them. A vent clicked. Somewhere aft, metal settled with a hollow tick. The static at Spike's elbow breathed softer now. Mars turned under the portholes. The basin's darkness moved slow enough to look fixed.

Jet set the cloth down and went back to the bench locker.

He shook the blanket open with a practiced snap that made almost no sound. His prosthetic hand caught one edge, his right hand the other. He walked over and bent his knees carefully because a man built like him had to think about deck space or knock somebody flat.

Ein opened one eye as the blanket came down.

"Don't start," Jet murmured.

Ein considered this and allowed himself to be covered.

Jet laid the blanket over Ed first, then drew the lower edge over the dog, tucking it in around the flank where the ship's draft always found its way across the floor. The pilled wool dragged lightly over Ed's shoulder. One bit of red-orange hair stuck up through the fold until Jet, without really thinking about it, smoothed it down with the backs of his fingers.

Then he straightened.

Spike had looked up.

There was no smile on his face, and no expression Jet could use against him later. He just looked. The receiver rested loose in his hand, tipped away from his ribs, static breathing back into the room.

Jet met his eyes.

Spike said nothing.

Jet didn't need him to.

Faye saw that exchange from the chair by the porthole.

She'd folded one arm across herself again, fingers hooked at the opposite shoulder in a way she would have denied if anyone had asked. Her gaze had gone to Jet when he moved, then to the blanket, then sideways to Spike catching the whole thing like he'd been tuned to that frequency instead of the one hissing out of the receiver.

The thought came up sharp because that was what she did when a room got too honest. Nice of you to notice. Nice domestic little act. Nice to know somebody on this ship can manage a basic mammal instinct.

The lines arrived ready-made and worthless.

She let them die.

Jet cleared his throat, low and rough. "He's gonna wake up freezing if I don't."

Nobody had asked.

"Yeah," Spike said, eyes on the blanket. "Draft gets bad down there."

That was all.

It was enough to make Faye look away first.

She turned back to the porthole where Mars sat huge and bruised below them. The collarbone under her hand still felt marked from the bathroom though there was nothing there. Behind her, Jet moved around the galley shutting things down one light at a time. Spike turned the receiver another notch lower. On the deck, under the singed blanket, Ed slept with mouth open and one card still stuck to forehead while Ein pressed his nose into the wool and dreamed whatever dogs with human-grade data in their heads dreamed.

Jet switched off the galley light.

The common room went softer.

Nobody said good night. They almost never did. The ship wasn't built for that kind of family and none of them had the nerve to retrofit it.

Still, the blanket stayed where Jet had put it. Spike kept the volume low. Faye sat by the porthole until her shoulder stopped hurting from the way she'd been holding herself and then, finally, let her hand fall. By then morning felt closer than sleep did.

The Bebop drifted over Hellas with its engines cold, ticket unpaid, pantry close to bare. Under the thin wool on the deck, two small bodies breathed in different rhythms and somehow made the room feel warmer than the heaters did.

Outside, Mars kept its distance.

Inside, the old ship ticked and settled around them for the night.
