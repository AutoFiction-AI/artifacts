# Chapter 11

By the time they got back to the Bebop, the red dust from the settlement had pasted itself into the seams of their clothes and turned the ramp treads slick. The ship smelled like old grease, cold fryer oil tracked up from the market, and the bell peppers Jet had scorched earlier and never quite aired out. The hull ticked as it cooled. Somewhere below, a pump kicked on and whined and shut off again.

Nobody talked coming up the ramp.

Faye had the chip in her fist so long the corners had stamped a square into her palm. The pilot had pressed it there in the market, quick as a dealer palming a card, then gone sideways through the crowd before Faye could decide whether to grab her or hit her or ask the one question that had actually mattered. She could still see the girl's face in flashes between bodies and hanging strips of plastic awning. Calm. Almost polite.

You're looking at this backwards.

She crossed the common room, dropped the chip on the bolted table, and let it spin once before it settled.

"There," she said. "That's what she gave me."

Jet shut the hatch behind them harder than he had to. "That's it?"

"No, she also gave me a detailed explanation of her motives and a nice basket." Faye peeled off her jacket and threw it at the couch. "Yeah, that's it."

Spike stayed near the porthole, one shoulder against the wall, looking like he had not decided whether he was in the room or just waiting until he could leave it. His hair still had market dust in it. There was a cigarette behind one ear, unlit. He had reached for it every ten minutes on the walk back and never lit it. Maybe he did not want Jet starting in. Maybe he did not want the smoke. He looked at the chip and then at Faye.

"What'd she say?" he asked.

Faye heard the line again, dry and level, not dramatic at all. That had been the worst part. If the pilot had wanted revenge she should have looked like revenge. She had looked like a mechanic leaving off directions to a part.

"She said we were looking at it backwards."

Jet frowned. "Backwards how?"

"If I knew that, we wouldn't be standing around staring at a piece of plastic."

Ed was already moving. Ed had been waiting for them barefoot on the swivel chair, folded sideways in a position that made Faye's own spine ache just to look at it, one heel hooked over the chair back. Blue marker numbers ran across the back of one hand in three columns where Ed had been writing checksums and then forgotten to wash them off. Ein lay under the table with Jet's oldest dish towel bunched under his chin like stolen bedding, but at the sound of Ed's chair wheels skidding over the deck plates his ears came up.

"Mysterious gift, secret lady gift, tiny treasure biscuit," Ed said, bright with professional interest more than excitement. "Ed will interrogate the biscuit."

Jet pointed a warning finger. "Careful."

Ed put a hand to the chest in mock offense. "Ed is always careful. Mostly. Sometimes." The mockery dropped as soon as the chip touched the reader. Ed's mouth went slack in concentration. Fingers moved. The common room monitor blinked from static to a directory tree.

Faye stayed standing. Sitting felt like agreement. She braced one hand on the back of the couch and watched Ed work.

The chip was clean. Too clean. No trap code, no hidden transmitter, no playful little spike strip waiting to jump into the ship's systems. The pilot had not sent them malware. She had sent them order.

Three files sat in the root directory. No labels, just timestamps and strings of numbers.

"Let's do the picture first," Faye said, because she wanted the worst thing quickly if she could get it.

Ed clicked.

The image opened full-screen and the room changed shape around it.

It was an exterior shot outside a small clinic on Mars, cheap prefab walls the color of old teeth, one flickering strip light over the door though it had clearly been daylight when the photo was taken. Dust had filmed the lower half of the window. Beside the entrance stood a woman in a dark work coat with the sleeves rolled once at the wrist. She had dark hair pulled back badly, as if she had done it while walking and left a section loose by mistake. She had a paper pharmacy bag tucked under one arm. Her other hand was at her throat, thumb resting in the hollow above the collarbone in a gesture so familiar Faye's own hand twitched away from her chest before she even knew she had moved it.

The woman had Faye's face.

Not exactly. The mouth was fuller when it rested. The nose bent a little differently at the bridge. There was more wear around the eyes, the kind that came from bad sleep and dry air and years of not having enough margin. But it was close enough that Faye's stomach made a small hard turn inside her. Close enough that if the woman had walked into a low-lit casino and said her name was Faye Valentine, nobody would have laughed.

Beside her stood a man in a plain jacket and open collar, no visible badge, no gun on his hip. He did not look like Jet. He was slimmer, a little stooped in the shoulders. But there was no mistaking the posture. Cop posture. Weight balanced. Head up. Eyes on exits even in a still photograph.

Jet had taken two steps toward the screen before Faye noticed he had moved.

"Stop," he said, not to anyone in particular.

Ed froze with a finger on the console.

Jet pointed at the clinic window. "Zoom there."

Ed enlarged the reflected clutter inside the clinic until pixels broke at the edges. A sun-faded poster hung on the back wall beyond the waiting chairs. Most of the text was unreadable, but the ISSP seal was still there, blue-white and official even through distortion, and beneath it the district designation from the precinct where Jet had worked. Outreach material. Community liaison. Something soft-faced and bureaucratic and stamped from the same building where harder things got hidden in cabinets.

"That's your precinct?" Faye said.

Jet did not answer right away. His jaw worked once. "Yeah."

Spike had pushed off the wall. He had not come closer to the screen, but his head had lifted a fraction, a hunting dog catching a scent it knew too well. He pointed to the right edge of the photo, past the clinic, where the wall of the next building wore three quick slashes of spray paint crossing a half-circle.

"Zoom that too."

Ed did.

To Faye it looked like nothing. Old paint on older paint. But something in Spike's face shut down another notch.

"You know it," Jet said.

"Yeah." Spike kept looking at the mark. "Old corridor tag."

"Syndicate?"

Spike shrugged once, and the shrug was already evasion. "Used to be."

Faye laughed. It came out wrong. "Great. So we've got my face, your cops, and his gang scrawl all in one charming family snapshot."

The word family sat there. Jet kept staring at the photo. Spike did too. Even Ed left it alone.

There was more in the picture if you stayed with it. The woman had cracked skin across two knuckles. The pharmacy bag was wrinkled and damp at one corner like it had been carried in the rain or held too hard. The man beside her was not smiling exactly, but the angle of his body leaned toward her in the easy way of someone who had been standing next to the same person for years. They were not posing. Whoever had taken the picture had caught them in the pause before movement, maybe turning back from the clinic door, maybe about to walk. There was life outside the frame. The thing that knocked through Faye hardest was not the resemblance. It was the ordinary look of the moment. Somebody had stood in line. Somebody had paid for pills. Somebody had said see you tomorrow to a nurse.

It had all happened wearing her name.

"Next file," she said, because if she kept looking at the image she was going to throw a glass at it.

Ed closed the photo and opened the second record.

This one was uglier. Half-corrupted scans, clinic software headers, biometric maps laid over administrative text. A patient block filled the left side of the screen.

PATIENT: VALENTINE, FAYE

Below it sat a fingerprint whirl in pale green lines. An identity-auth seal. Date stamps. Authorization chain.

Seven years after Faye had been frozen.

She knew that part already, or pieces of it. She had sat alone in a records office on Mars while a clerk with a nicotine patch on his neck and a broken thumbnail pulled up her cryogenic intake profile and showed her exactly how cheaply a person could be sold once the person stopped being immediately available to complain. She had seen the audit trail. Three years after suspension, a facility employee copied her biometric packet. After that it had spread through the system like mold through damp insulation. Credit, clinic intake, travel docs, rental histories. Eleven years of somebody else moving through the world under a name Faye had woken up to find half detached from her anyway.

Knowing it in a sealed office with fluorescent lights buzzing was one thing.

Seeing it on Jet's ship with the others breathing behind her was another.

"That's my cryo signature," she said, before anybody could ask whether the match was real.

Jet turned to her. "You know that for sure?"

"I checked."

Spike looked away from the screen to her face. "When?"

"A few days ago."

"You went by yourself," Jet said.

Faye looked at him. "Was I supposed to make it a group outing?"

Jet's mouth tightened. He said nothing.

Ed, sensing the drop but not quite the contour of it, kept working with extra care, like hands on delicate wiring. "The hash matches the cryo intake packet. Ninety-nine point nine eight two repeating. Not a fake. Not a splice. Somebody used the sleepy-bye Faye thumbprint to authorize recurring prescriptions and treatment notes and squiggly little insurance fights."

Treatment notes ran down the right side. Anti-rejection medication. Dosage adjustments. Refill delays. A clinic notation about supply interruptions. Another note flagged a scar over the left shoulder blade as part of patient history.

Faye's hand went there before she could stop it. Cloth. Skin. Nothing under it but her own back.

Spike's eyes flicked to her shoulder and away. Jet's gaze caught there too, brief and exact, then went back to the records. Neither of them said anything.

The dates kept coming. Refill after refill. Then gaps. Then emergency authorizations. Somebody in a dirty little clinic had been fighting a failing body with forms and stamps and whatever medicine could be scraped together under a false name.

At the bottom of the visible fragment was an emergency contact field. It had been partly eaten by corruption, but enough remained.

KOZUKI, D.

Relationship: SPOUSE

Faye stared at the word spouse until it stopped meaning language.

"Open the rest," Jet said.

"That's the rest," Ed said softly. "The rest went crunchy."

Faye found herself thinking stupidly of the woman in the photo and the pharmacy bag. She had been married. She had maybe gone home after that picture and put soup on or checked homework or sat on the edge of a bed and counted pills into her palm. She had done a thousand boring things Faye would never know, all while the name Faye Valentine sat in her mouth and on her paperwork and maybe on junk mail shoved under a door.

Dead me had a husband.

The thought was so ridiculous she almost smiled. She did not.

"Third file," Spike said. He did not take his eyes off the screen.

Ed opened it.

The ISSP fragment came up in grayscale scans, old report formatting, redaction blocks laid over names and line items. Faye did not need Jet to say he recognized the format. He stood like a man hearing furniture creak in his childhood house after years away. His eyes moved faster than the rest of him. His right hand braced on the back of the chair. The fingers of his prosthetic left hand opened and closed once with a light mechanical tick.

FIELD OFFICE: THARSIS PRECINCT

CONFIDENTIAL SOURCE DESIGNATION: REDACTED

SUBJECT: LOGISTICS CORRIDOR / SOUTH BASIN MEDICAL TRANSFER

There were route references beneath it. Most were coded. One of them made Spike speak before anybody else had finished reading.

"That one," he said.

Jet looked. "What about it?"

Spike did not answer immediately. Ed highlighted the line.

Transfer corridor LSF-H9 confirmed active through shell vendors and clinic freight.

LSF.

Faye looked from the letters to Spike.

He did not bother to pretend this meant nothing. "That's one of mine."

"One of your what?" Jet asked.

Spike's eyes stayed on the screen. "Old route tags."

"You said you recognized the signal in the settlement. You didn't say any of this."

"Didn't have any of this then."

"You had enough."

Faye felt the room start to lean.

Ed was still following the data because data could be followed even when people could not. New windows opened over the case file. Metadata scraped from the clinic image. Address overlap from the medical fragment. Partial public registry pulls. Ed's hands danced. The screen filled with lines and boxes and little colored nodes that would have looked cheerful if they had not been building a grave.

"Wait wait wait, the biscuit has layers," Ed said, the words starting to trip over each other. "Picture lady has med-file signature alias and med-file spouse initial and spouse initial talks to cop paper and cop paper talks to snake-road letters and look, look, same home block, same old settlement district, same temporal bunching, same same same."

Faye could not keep up with the screen. She could barely keep up with the thought that the woman in the picture had gone from thief to patient to wife in less than two minutes and none of those words touched the shock of the face.

Ed slowed on his own. Jet was not doing the translation thing. Spike was not snorting at the screen. Faye had nothing mean enough ready. Only then did Ed seem to hear how dead quiet the room had gone.

Ed pointed with one marker-stained finger.

"One family," Ed said, this time leaving space between the words. "This man in the file, Daichi Kozuki. Informant husband. This woman in the clinic, Ayane Kozuki. Courier wife using Faye Valentine identity packet. Same address in two systems before the address got chewed up. There's also dependent coverage here, little tiny person, name gone, age field missing, but the household unit is three. One family."

Ayane.

The dead woman had a name now. It hit harder than the photo had. A face could still be a trick. A name sat down and stayed.

"And?" Jet said. The word scraped on the way out.

Ed swallowed, eyes flicking from the screen to the adults and back. "And Daichi husband works through your precinct channels. Ayane wife uses Faye biometrics for treatment and medicine. Daichi husband reports on corridors with Spike-tag letters. The corridors move medicine and other less cuddly things." A brief apology flickered over Ed's face for the failed joke and vanished. "It wasn't four separate pokes. It was one house and then lines out from the house to all of you. You were doing it backwards."

Faye stared at the diagram. He was right. The whole week tipped and rearranged itself in her head with a sick little click. They had been reading the trail from themselves outward because of course they had. Faye to the stolen identity. Jet to the bounty. Spike to the syndicate tags. Ed to the orphanage visit. Everybody had assumed they were the center because everybody on the ship was, privately, a little bit monstrous that way.

It had never been about their separate wounds.

It had been one family, hit from three directions, with the damage still moving years later.

Jet pulled a chair back and sat without seeming to decide to. The chair legs screeched over the deck. He kept looking at the case file, not the photo. Faye knew the habit by now. Give Jet paper over a ghost and he would go to the paper first, because paper could at least be argued with.

Spike finally stepped to the table. He leaned over the screen, one hand braced flat beside the keyboard. Faye watched his eyes stop on the route code again.

"You said Daichi," Jet said to Ed. "Where'd you get the name?"

"Cross-key from the med file and the file file. Redaction wasn't total. The system gets lazy at the edges." Ed tapped a small pull-out window. "Kozuki in the spouse block. Kozuki in a checksum remnant on the source designation export. Daichi fit. Then fit fit fit with the bounty reactivation you already had, Jet-person. The dead-code bouncy bounty."

Jet's head came up sharply. That got Faye's attention.

"What dead-code bounty?" she said.

Jet looked at Ed, then at the table, then at Spike, as if the order mattered and he could still choose it.

Spike did not give him time.

"You had his name already?" Spike asked.

Jet looked back at him.

The air in the common room changed. Faye felt it physically, like a pressure seal shifting somewhere in the ship.

"I had a name," Jet said.

"And you didn't mention it."

Jet pushed back from the table so hard the chair rolled. He stood. "Don't start with me."

Spike's hand stayed flat on the metal tabletop. "You start then."

Faye looked from one to the other and understood, with sudden sour clarity, that the chip was not the thing that had broken the evening. The chip had only turned a light on. The break had been there, waiting, in every private errand and omitted fact and little closed door on this ship.

The drawer under the sink gave one of its familiar rattles as the hull settled. Jet's eyes cut toward it and held there a beat too long, like a loose track and a screwdriver might still be the problem in front of him. It stayed there buzzing loose in the gap.

***

Jet could not keep standing at the table looking at Spike or he was going to say something stupid too early and then be trapped behind it. He moved into the galley instead, because there was a loose drawer slide there and a hinge that had been sticking for two days and tools that made sense when people did not. He yanked the utensil drawer all the way open, reached behind the soy bottles for the screwdriver, and set the drawer on the counter with more force than precision.

The case file was still visible from where he stood. He did not need to be looking at it to see the precinct header.

"Fine," he said, because Spike was still there and Faye was still there and Ed was very much still there and maybe there was no version of this that happened clean. "Let's start over. You recognized that route code in half a second. That means the settlement signal wasn't just some old frequency to you. So tell me what the hell you sat on."

Spike stayed by the table. He had gone still in the way that always made Jet feel like the room had acquired a blade.

"I ran logistics," Spike said.

"For the syndicate. I know that much."

"Then what are you asking?"

Jet laughed once without humor and set the screwdriver down because the handle was going to crack in his grip. "I'm asking which routes, Spike. I'm asking what 'ran logistics' meant in the part you left out. I'm asking why a dead informant from my old precinct was filing reports on a corridor with your initials on it and why I'm finding that out from a girl who likes dramatic exits."

"They weren't my initials."

"Don't do that either."

Spike looked at him with that flat, exhausted irritation he used when he thought somebody was being deliberately slow. "It was an alias. Lin Shao-Feng. Logistics cover. Happy now?"

"No, not remotely."

Faye stayed near the couch with her arms crossed so tight the tendons stood out in her wrists. She had not taken her eyes off either of them since the shift in the room. Ed had eased the monitor brightness down for no reason Jet could see except maybe instinct. Ein had abandoned the dish towel and come to stand near Ed's bare feet.

Jet opened the drawer slide, closed it, opened it again. Metal rasped. It gave him a sound to work against.

"How many routes?" he asked.

Spike shrugged.

"How many?"

"Dozens."

"And you never thought to mention that maybe the old network was live when we started chasing this ship?"

"I thought about it."

"That's not what I asked."

Spike's gaze moved to the drawer in Jet's hand, then back up. "If that network was live, it wasn't just relevant to the case. It put a target on the ship."

"Exactly."

"On me," Spike said.

Jet set the drawer slide down slowly. "You don't get to separate those."

That landed. He saw it land because Spike's face changed by almost nothing, which with Spike counted as movement.

"I didn't know this was tied to one family," Spike said. "I knew somebody was using old corridor tags. That was enough to handle."

"Handle how?"

"By not advertising it."

Jet stared at him. "To whom? Us?"

"Yeah."

The simple honesty of it pissed him off more than an excuse would have.

"That's good," Jet said. "That's real good. We're out there burning fuel we don't have, flying into canyons after a pilot we don't understand, and you decide the useful part is private because it might get personal."

"It was personal."

"It was dangerous."

"Same thing a lot of the time."

Jet barked a laugh. "Not on my ship."

Spike's eyes sharpened. "Then maybe don't keep your own surprises in the kitchen drawer."

There it was.

Faye's head turned. "What surprises?"

Jet did not answer fast enough.

"The bounty on Hosuke Takara got reactivated using dead informant access codes," Spike said. "Name was Daichi Kozuki."

Faye looked at Jet so hard it felt like a shove from across the room. "You knew that?"

Jet swore under his breath, then louder because there was no point in half measures now. "I knew the code trail. I knew the name. I didn't know all this." He jabbed a finger toward the screen. "I had a dead informant tied to my old precinct and a sealed alias of my own dragged out into daylight. I was handling it."

Faye laughed at him, mean and incredulous. "Of course you were."

"Don't."

"Don't what? Notice the pattern?"

Jet turned on her. "You don't have a lot of room to take that tone."

The words were out before he could stop them. He heard the unfairness in them the moment they landed and knew from the way Faye's mouth changed that she had heard it too.

"No?" she said. She didn't get louder. That was worse. "How much room have I got, Jet? Give me the measurement."

He opened his mouth and got nothing useful. Spike looked away first, which only made it uglier.

Faye uncrossed her arms and shoved herself off the couch. She came to the table, grabbed the medical record print window with one hand as if she could physically drag it toward them, and pointed at the patient name with the other.

"I've got exactly this much room," she said. "My face on a clinic chart from a year I was in a freezer. That's the room I've got."

Jet felt his own anger check, but only for a second. The check was not relief. It was impact.

"You said you checked," he said. "What did that mean?"

"It meant I went and checked."

"By yourself."

"Yes, by myself. Who else was going to do it?"

Jet spread his hands. "You could've said something."

Faye let out a laugh with no air in it. "I didn't want witnesses."

"That's not what we are."

"Isn't it?" Faye leaned toward him. "Say 'us' again. See if it sounds less stupid."

"Faye."

"No, go ahead."

Jet could feel the conversation getting away from him and kept talking anyway, which was his worst habit on a bad day. "We live on the same ship. Something like this happens, it touches the mess we're in now, you say it."

"Why?" she snapped. "So you can do what exactly? File a report? Go stare out a porthole? Tell me to be careful?"

Spike flinched, small and real. Jet almost missed it.

"That's not fair," Jet said.

"Neither is waking up and finding out some dead woman has more history with my face than I do."

The line hit and kept going. Faye did not stop.

"I sat in that records office with a clerk who smelled like stale patches and cheap coffee and watched him scroll through eleven years of her. Not me. Her under my name. Credit checks. Clinic visits. Dock fees. Rentals. Boring Tuesday shit. The kind that means somebody was alive between disasters. A waitress on Ganymede remembers the color of her dress and the way she tipped. I don't even know if I'd have tipped. I don't know if I'd have liked the dress. Hell, I don't know if I even owned dresses before I got frozen. And you're both in here trying to figure out which filing cabinet gets the blame."

Jet shut his mouth because there was nothing he could say that would not make it worse.

Spike said, very quiet, "Her name was Ayane."

Faye rounded on him so fast he actually took half a step back.

"Yeah," she said. "I know what her name was. Don't do that like it helps. Don't say Ayane and then shove her right back into courier, route, patient, done."

"That's not what I did."

"It's exactly what you did."

Spike's voice hardened. "I said her name."

"After you said corridor."

Jet went for the drawer again because he could not stand there empty-handed. The slide came loose with a scrape and nearly fell. He caught it against the edge of the sink with his prosthetic hand. The cheap metal bent under the grip.

"Knock it off," he said, to both of them and mostly to the room.

Faye swung back to him. "You knock it off."

"I'm trying to sort out what this file means."

"No," she said. "You're trying to sort out where to put the blame so it doesn't land on you."

Jet stared at her.

Spike, maddeningly, said nothing. He just stood there and let the accusation hang because of course he was happy to let somebody else say the ugly part.

Jet's voice came up despite himself. "You think I want this tied to my precinct?"

"I think you want it tied to paperwork," Faye shot back. "Same as him."

She jabbed a finger toward Spike without looking.

"He says route like that makes him less involved. You say precinct like that makes you less involved. And somewhere in the middle a woman got sick enough to need fake paperwork and pills under my name, and a man got dead enough that his codes are still bouncing around a bounty server, and you're both doing accounting."

Jet heard himself say, "I didn't know about any informant on his routes."

Spike looked at him then. "You didn't know about Daichi."

"No."

"You didn't know or you didn't ask?"

The question came flat and light, which was Spike's cruelest register because it never sounded like cruelty until afterward.

Jet took one step into the common room.

"Careful," he said.

"Why? You weren't."

"I worked cases, Spike. I didn't handle every source in that building."

"But you knew the system."

"Yeah. I knew the system was dirty. I didn't know this man."

"That's convenient."

Jet laughed again, louder this time. "Convenient? You ran syndicate freight with medicine on it and didn't bother asking what the boxes were because it wasn't convenient to know."

"I said I didn't know."

"Exactly."

It came out harder than he meant, but there was no calling it back. The word sat between them with all the other words that should have been said earlier and were not.

Spike's face flattened further. "You think I don't get that?"

"I think you only get it now because it's got a photograph attached."

For a second Jet thought Spike might actually cross the room and hit him, not because Spike was the hitting kind in arguments but because some angles in his body belonged to older reflexes. Instead Spike looked past him at the ISSP file and asked, too calm, "Was your precinct the kind of place where informants got protected?"

Jet did not answer.

"Jet," Spike said.

"No."

The word was out before pride could dress it. The honesty of it made the room stop.

"No," Jet said again. This time he just let it sit there, because if he backed away from that he was gone. "No, it wasn't. Not enough. Not well. It was underfunded and dirty and half the people in it were trying and the other half were doing business off the books and most days you couldn't tell which half someone was till later."

Spike held his gaze. "So when a source on your precinct books starts feeding routes with my tags on them, I get to ask whether you were part of the machine that got him killed."

Jet felt that one in the old scar across his cheek, in the arm socket that ached when storms moved through even though Mars storms were mostly dust and static and memory. He took another step forward.

"And I get to ask," he said, voice rising, "whether you ever once looked at a manifest long enough to notice a clinic was at the other end, or whether you were too busy pretending you were only hauling abstract cargo."

"I noticed clinics," Spike said. "I didn't know whose meds they were."

"Because you didn't ask."

"Because asking in that world got you killed."

"Yeah," Jet snapped. "Funny thing. Same at the precinct."

That shut Spike up for a beat. Not agreement. Recognition, maybe. Jet hated that it felt like relief.

Faye made a sound between disgust and laughter.

"Listen to yourselves."

Neither of them turned toward her fast enough, which was its own insult, and when they did she was already beyond ordinary anger. The speed had left her. What replaced it was colder.

She picked up the photograph and held it between two fingers.

"Her name was Ayane," she said. "Get that part right."

Jet's jaw worked once. Spike kept his eyes on the photograph and let the point stand.

"Ayane sat in some busted clinic chair with my thumbprint on the file. Refill after refill. Then gaps. Then emergency authorizations because somebody couldn't keep the meds coming or her body was chewing through them faster than the paperwork could move." She looked at the picture, at the thumb at the collarbone, at the pharmacy bag gone soft at one corner. "She had that bag going soft in her hand. She had a scar on her back I don't have. She had to keep answering to my name while hers got pushed off the page. That's what you're both standing over in here."

Jet started, "Faye, I'm not trying to-"

"Yeah, you are. Both of you are." She slapped the photo down on the table so hard the monitor flickered with the impact. "You start saying precinct and corridor and source designation and it all gets small enough to hold. Small enough not to smell like a clinic or bad coffee or somebody's sweat drying under a work coat. There was a kid on that coverage line. There were refill gaps. She was sick. Stop shaving it down till it fits in your mouths."

The monitor gave off its low static hiss, and the room let it.

Faye laughed once, ugly and brief. "And don't act surprised. This ship runs on not asking. Jet cooks. Spike disappears. I lie. Everybody clocks it, nobody pushes, and we call that manners."

Jet drew breath.

She cut him off with volume.

"No. Don't do the calm voice now. It isn't family. It's people sleeping ten feet apart and acting like that means we don't hear each other."

The moment the line left her, Ed said, in a small normal voice that did not sound like Ed at all, "I live here too."

The little fan in the monitor suddenly sounded huge.

Jet turned.

Ed was no longer folded around the chair. Ed sat upright now, both bare feet on the metal rung, hands flat on the knees. The marker numbers were still bright across one hand. Ein leaned against Ed's shin, ears back. Ed's face was not dramatic. That made it worse.

"Ed, I didn't mean-"

"You didn't sound like it."

Faye's mouth closed.

Jet felt something ugly and parental and ashamed move through him at once. He took a breath to answer and got Spike instead.

"Nobody said you did anything wrong," Spike said.

Ed finally looked up. "I didn't say wrong."

The words sat there.

Jet set the bent drawer slide on the counter and stared at the shallow scrape he had just put into the matte black of his prosthetic with the cheap metal track.

The Bebop had never run on confession or trust in the clean sense. It ran on work. On food when there was food, fuel when there was fuel, luck when there was not enough of either. It ran on the habit of not pressing where flesh was already bruised. Jet had called that respect because the word let him sleep.

Maybe it had also just been fear.

He pushed off the counter and straightened one piece at a time.

"I should've told you about Daichi," he said. "The bounty, the dead codes, the precinct connection. I went to Donovan and pulled the trail and sat on it because I wanted the shape of it before I put it in front of everybody."

Faye made a tired, disbelieving sound. "See? That's it. We go around the room, cough up one secret each, and then what, we're healthy?"

"I'm not saying that."

"What are you saying?"

Spike reached up, took the unlit cigarette from behind his ear, turned it once between his fingers, and put it in his pocket instead. "It doesn't fix a damn thing," he said.

Jet had no answer that didn't sound like paperwork in a human voice.

Faye was the one who moved first. She took the photograph off the table, hesitated, and then put it back down more carefully, faceup this time instead of slapped flat. Ayane in the work coat. Daichi beside her. The bag of pills.

"I can't do this in here anymore," she said.

Jet did not ask where she was going. That felt like part of the problem and also like the only thing he had left to give.

She headed for the corridor without another word. At the hatch she stopped and looked back, not at Jet, not at Spike, but at the monitor and the files still glowing there.

"If either of you starts calling her collateral again," she said, "I'll throw you both out an airlock."

"I didn't call her that," Spike said.

Faye gave him a long exhausted look. "Then maybe stop sounding like you did."

Her heel clipped the threshold and then she was through.

The hatch slid shut behind her with a soft hydraulic thump.

Jet stayed where he was. After a moment Spike pushed off the table.

"You think I knew?" Jet asked, because once Faye was gone the question came out of him before he could decide whether he wanted the answer.

Spike paused at the corridor.

"I think you knew enough about the kind of place it was," he said. "Same as me."

Jet wanted to argue. He wanted specifics and qualifiers and the whole nice scaffolding of a defense. Instead he heard himself say, "Yeah."

Spike nodded once. Not forgiveness. Not agreement. Just acknowledgment of a fact ugly enough to stand on by itself.

Then he was gone too.

That left Jet with Ed.

He looked over. "Kid-"

Ed clicked the monitor dark.

"I'm not a kid," Ed said automatically. Then, after a beat that made it worse, "Mostly."

Jet almost smiled and could not get there. "Right."

Ed slid off the chair. Ein followed immediately, brushing the back of Jet's calf on the way past as if checking that the large damaged animal in the galley was still alive. At the hatch Ed stopped.

"It was already broken," Ed said, still not looking at him. "The biscuit just made crunchy noises."

Before Jet could answer, Ed and Ein went out.

The common room stayed lit by the low amber night lamps and the spill from the galley. On the table the photograph caught a strip of light across Ayane's cheek. The bent drawer slide lay beside the sink. The loose drawer still hung open an inch.

Jet crossed the room and shut the drawer with his hip.

Then he stood there listening as the ship settled around him and, one by one down the corridor, door seals caught where they had never seemed loud before.
