# Chapter 7

Faye left in the Redtail before anyone could ask where she was going, which meant leaving before Jet started coffee, before Ed woke up into a person-shaped weather system, before Spike finished pretending he had not noticed anything the night before.

The Bebop's hangar still held the stale smell of engine grease and bell peppers. Jet's dinner had gotten everywhere. Faye climbed into the Redtail with it in her nose and yesterday's look from Miriam still sitting wrong under her ribs.

Miriam had put her at the Golden Ox.

There had been a blue dress.

There had been that touch at the collarbone.

Faye sealed the canopy and brought the systems up. The console lit her hands green. She did not look back at the Bebop once the clamps released. Looking back turned a solo errand into something you might explain later, and she had no interest in explaining anything. If she told Jet, he would ask practical questions in that careful voice that made it hard to tell whether he was worried or just organizing the day. If she told Spike, he would say "Yeah?" like it meant nothing and then file it away where he kept every dangerous thing. If Ed saw the launch prep, Ed would want to come, and Faye did not need company for this. She barely needed herself.

Mars swelled under the Redtail, wrong-colored and familiar anyway, a rusted skin under a thin, worked-at atmosphere. The settlements around Tharsis looked cheap from above, a scatter of lights and industrial ribs and old dreams with half the paint gone. She brought the ship down near the tram loop three districts over from Mama Yun's because even a quick landing cost money if you wanted not to get fined for it, and Faye had already had enough of paying for access to things that should have been hers.

Mama Yun had given her the address over comm without asking why, her voice arriving through the speaker with the scrape of a ladle on metal and the smoker's roughness she insisted came from broth steam. That had been almost worse than questions.

"Breakfast place," Mama Yun had said. "Miriam picks up the early shift there on Tuesdays and Thursdays. You still owe me for noodles."

"Put it on Jet."

"I always do."

That had been the whole exchange. Mama Yun knew when not to press. People who fed broke men for a living learned things like that.

The morning air on Mars had the dry bite of late autumn, dust and cold metal and fryer exhaust from a food stall opening too early. Faye zipped her jacket higher and cut across the block toward the diner, a narrow place under a dead strip of blue neon that read ORBITAL BREAKFAST except for the O, which had gone dark long enough ago that nobody had bothered fixing it. Through the window she could see the breakfast rush on its way out: two freight drivers in orange work jackets, a woman in clinic scrubs reading a hand terminal while she ate eggs one-handed, a schoolboy in a thick sweater pushing hash browns around his plate like they had offended him personally.

Miriam was behind the counter with a pot of coffee in one hand and a stack of plates on the other arm. She wore a gray cardigan under her apron, one elbow patched in darker yarn, and every time she passed the pie case she straightened the handwritten PEACH card even though the tray behind it held only a single wedge of something pale and collapsing. Tired eyes, same as yesterday at Mama Yun's. The accent was there too, that flat Ganymede drag on the vowels that made even small talk sound like it had crossed weather to get here.

Faye took a stool near the end of the counter and waited until Miriam came over.

"Coffee?" Miriam asked without looking up.

"Yeah."

The mug landed in front of her. Miriam poured, glanced up, and paused.

"Well," she said. "You again."

Faye wrapped both hands around the mug because they needed something to do. "Lucky you."

"Depends." Miriam set the pot down. "You want breakfast or trouble?"

"Just answers."

That got the smallest lift of Miriam's eyebrows. "Those cost more than breakfast."

"Put them on Jet too."

Miriam huffed once through her nose. Not a laugh exactly, but close enough to make room for the next thing.

"I wasn't trying to upset you yesterday," she said.

"You did anyway."

"Yeah. I figured."

Booth three waved an empty mug in the air. Miriam picked up the pot again.

"Sit there and be mad for a minute," she said. "I've got people who think coffee is a life support system."

Faye did not have anywhere better to be. She sat there and watched Miriam work the counter. She moved with the practiced economy of someone who had spent years carrying hot things to people who barely noticed. Her right knee was bad. You could see it in the half-hitch before she turned corners, the brief saving of weight when she thought nobody was looking. She called the schoolboy "captain" when he asked for more jam. She told the freight drivers they were not getting free refills on pie no matter what lies they had heard about charity. She straightened the PEACH card twice more.

The coffee was burnt enough to be almost a flavor of its own. Faye drank it anyway.

When the counter cleared down to the woman in scrubs and the boy with the jam, Miriam came back and leaned one hip against the register.

"All right," she said. "What answers."

Faye kept her voice flat. "The woman on Ganymede."

Miriam's mouth tightened a little, not with guilt, more with the annoyance of being right again. "The Golden Ox."

"You remember the name."

"I remember where I worked."

"You said you remembered me."

"I said I remembered a woman I thought was you."

"That isn't the same thing."

"No," Miriam said. "It isn't."

Faye looked past her at the pie case, at the stupid PEACH card standing in front of no peach pie at all. She could feel the question lodged in her throat like bad liquor.

"Tell me everything," she said.

Miriam tipped her head. "Everything's a lot."

"Then start with what you kept."

The woman in scrubs asked for the check. Miriam pushed off the register, ran the card, came back with the receipt tucked under two fingers. She did not hurry. Faye hated that. It made the whole thing feel ordinary, and ordinary was the part she could not get a grip on.

"The Golden Ox had a private room upstairs for the high rollers who wanted to pretend they weren't in a casino," Miriam said. "Blue carpet, terrible lighting, dealers who acted like they were priests. I worked the room three nights a week for almost a year. Mostly rich men from the wool trade and whatever women they were trying to impress."

"And me."

"Her."

Faye said nothing.

"She came in on a Thursday," Miriam went on. "Late. Not with the first wave. That was one reason I remember her. The serious players were already there, already dug in, already drinking like somebody else was paying. Then the host walked her up and started calling her Miss Valentine like she'd been there before or like he wanted everybody to think she had."

"Had she?"

"I don't know." Miriam shrugged. "I wasn't invited to the front office."

"What did she look like."

Miriam looked straight at her now, measuring how much honesty this woman at the counter could take.

"Enough like you that I put my foot in my mouth yesterday," she said. "Dark hair. Same eyes, or close enough in that light. Same mouth when you weren't smiling. She was older than you look now, maybe. No, that's not right. Not older. Just used harder. You know what I mean."

Faye knew exactly what she meant and hated her for saying it out loud.

"No," she said.

"Sure."

The schoolboy asked for more syrup. Miriam passed it over without taking her eyes off Faye.

"She wore a blue dress," Miriam said. "Not expensive expensive. It wanted to look expensive. Satin finish, maybe a blend, cut low in the back. The hem had been let down once and stitched by hand. I notice things like that."

"Why."

Miriam glanced at her patched elbow. "Because I own clothes."

That should not have landed, but it did. Faye pictured a blue dress under casino light, the kind of dress built to catch attention first and inspection second. Something chosen for effect because effect was cheaper than security.

"She played the high table?" Faye asked.

"Not like a tourist." Miriam nodded. "There was a wool trader there from Callisto. Big man, pink face, wedding ring he kept taking off before each hand like that made the odds cleaner. She let him underestimate her for maybe fifteen minutes, then took enough off him that he stopped laughing."

"How much."

"Enough that he sweated through his collar and started asking for a new dealer."

"That's not a number."

"No, honey, it isn't."

Faye opened her mouth, closed it again. Miriam had not called her honey in a condescending way. That was almost more irritating.

"How do you remember one customer out of a whole room?" Faye asked.

"Because I carried drinks for drunks and cheats for a living. You remember the ones who make the room tilt."

"That doesn't mean anything."

"It means she didn't win loud. That's what people expect when they think woman in a blue dress. They expect a show. She just kept stacking chips and touching right here every time the pot got mean."

Miriam lifted two fingers to her own collarbone.

Faye's hand moved before she could stop it.

Both of them looked at it.

Miriam did not say I told you so. She was too old for that kind of cheap pleasure.

"Maybe everybody does that," Faye said.

"Maybe." Miriam's voice stayed even. "Everybody doesn't do it the same way."

The bad coffee turned bitter in Faye's mouth. She drank the rest because not drinking it would have looked like reacting.

"What way."

"Two fingers, quick. Like checking a necklace that isn't there." Miriam demonstrated again, lighter this time. "She did it when she was bluffing. Or when she wanted the table to think she was bluffing. Hard to tell with good players."

"You said voice and laugh yesterday."

"I said I remembered both."

"So."

Miriam reached for the pot, saw Faye's cup was empty, filled it without asking. "Lower voice than yours," she said. "Not much. A little smoke in it. Laugh came out when she forgot herself, not when the men wanted it. That I remember too."

"You spent a lot of time remembering this woman."

"No." Miriam shook her head. "I spent a lot of time carrying coffee. She just stuck."

Faye watched the black surface of the refill settle in the mug. In it she could see a warped slice of her own face, the nose bent sideways by cheap ceramic and diner light.

"You said there was a scar," she said.

Miriam's eyes narrowed slightly, not suspicious, just careful. "I did."

"Where."

"Left shoulder blade."

The answer came so quick it was obvious she had never forgotten it.

"How do you know which side."

"Because she leaned forward to rake in chips and the strap slipped." Miriam touched her own back with the handle of the pot, indicating the spot with working-woman precision. "Not a fresh scar. Pale. Curved a little, like something cut and then healed badly. I remember thinking it must have hurt like hell when she got it."

The diner seemed to lengthen around Faye for a second, counter stretching away, the hum of the refrigeration unit too loud, somebody dropping silverware in the kitchen sounding like it had happened inside her own teeth.

"Hold this," Miriam said suddenly, and shoved the pot into her hands because booth two wanted the check and the schoolboy had tipped his syrup over and the woman in scrubs had stood up to leave. Faye held the pot while Miriam moved away with a dishcloth and a muttered, "Don't steal it. It isn't good coffee."

Faye put it back down the second Miriam was out of reach and slid off the stool.

The restroom was at the end of a short hall past a vending machine that sold headache tablets and mint gum. The mirror over the sink had a crack running through one corner and the fluorescent tube above it buzzed like an insect trapped in glass. Faye locked the door, pulled her jacket off one shoulder, then the strap of her top. She turned, tried to catch the angle in the mirror, cursed, shifted, used the smaller cracked strip of reflective metal bolted near the hand dryer to see more.

The skin there was smooth.

There was a faint line from her strap and a small old bruise going yellow near her ribs from a bad landing two days earlier. There was no scar.

Of course there was no scar. She knew her own body. She dressed it, sold it, patched it, hauled it in and out of ships and card rooms and bad decisions. She knew every place that marked easy and every place that marked slow. But she stood there anyway with one hand braced on the sink and her other fingers pressed hard into the flat skin over her left shoulder blade, as if pressure could raise something from underneath.

Nothing rose under her fingers.

When she came back out, Miriam had wiped the syrup off the counter and reset the jam rack. The PEACH card was straight again.

"You all right?" Miriam asked.

"Fine."

"You don't look fine."

"You don't look rich either. We all get burdens."

That one did earn the edge of a smile. Miriam poured herself half a cup of coffee she plainly did not want and drank it standing up.

"You said you wanted everything," she said.

Faye climbed back onto the stool. "Did she say where she was from."

"No."

"Who she was with."

"Nobody worth remembering."

"Did she use my name."

There it was, finally. Not the shape of the question, but the dirty center of it.

Miriam set her cup down. "I heard the host call her Miss Valentine. I saw her sign a marker slip Faye Valentine. You want me to lie and say maybe I read it wrong, I can't do that for you."

Faye looked at the register instead of the woman's face. Numbers glowed there in obedient little green blocks: add, subtract, tax, total. All the clean ways a machine could tell a story wrong.

"Why did she tip you," she asked.

Miriam blinked. "What."

"You keep saying that. Nobody tipped there. She did. Why."

"How the hell would I know why."

"Take a guess."

Miriam rubbed one thumb against the side of her mug. Dry skin. Cracked cuticle. Someone who washed her hands too often and never got ahead of work.

"It wasn't show-off money," she said at last. "It wasn't the kind some drunk man throws down because he wants you to tell him he's generous. It was a real tip. The kind you leave when you've worked for tips yourself."

Faye said nothing.

"That was part of it," Miriam went on. "The dress wanted one story. The tip wanted another. And when she got up to leave, she looked tired all of a sudden. Not weak. Just tired. Like she'd been holding her face in place all evening and the door let it drop."

The freight drivers paid and lumbered out. The diner got quieter. Somebody in the kitchen turned down the music, which had been a radio station playing old Mars standards through too much static.

"Look," Miriam said. "I don't know what this is to you. I don't know if you're looking for her or trying not to. But I saw who I saw."

"You saw somebody wearing my name."

"Yeah."

Faye's fingers found her collarbone again. This time she made herself drop the hand.

"Maybe it wasn't mine first," she said before she could stop herself.

Miriam frowned. "What."

"Nothing."

"No, that wasn't nothing."

Faye pushed a few bills under the saucer though the coffee was barely worth the cup. "You did your good deed," she said. "Don't get ambitious."

Miriam glanced at the money, then back at her. "That isn't a tip. That's paying your tab."

"Then don't spend it all in one place."

She put her jacket on and headed for the door.

"Hey," Miriam called after her.

Faye stopped with one hand on the glass.

"For what it's worth," Miriam said, "when I saw you at Mama Yun's, I thought you'd gotten unlucky. Not that you'd become somebody else."

Faye looked at her through the reflection of the dead blue neon and the pie case and the whole cheap room.

"Yeah," she said. "I'm working on that too."

Outside, the cold had sharpened. The tram line whined around the corner and the sky over the district had gone the dirty brass color Mars used when the day had not decided whether to die clean. Faye stood on the sidewalk with the smell of fryer grease on her clothes and the spot on her shoulder blade still throbbing from her own fingers.

There was no scar.

There had been a blue dress let out at the hem.

The tip had been real.

There had been two fingers at the collarbone.

That was more than Faye had ever gotten about the woman she had been before the ice, and every bit of it belonged to somebody else.

She turned toward the records office because if she did not go now she might talk herself into staying ignorant, and ignorance had never once paid what it promised.

***

The Mars Civil Identity Records Office sat in a low public building between a transit authority branch and a clinic that specialized in lung filtration. Faye had expected something hidden, or at least something guarded hard enough to make the corruption feel expensive. Instead she got a peeling sign, a line of bolted plastic chairs, and a ticket dispenser with one corner caved in from years of people hitting it too hard.

Window 4 handled historical verification. The woman behind it wore a wrist brace covered in marker drawings of planets and crooked stars, the kind of thing a bored child did when told not to touch the medical tape. She pushed her glasses up with the heel of her hand because the brace made her fingers clumsy, and she kept a jar of boiled sweets by the terminal that looked older than some governments. Faye took a number and sat under a vent that breathed directly onto her scalp.

The place smelled like dust, hot plastic, and old printer toner. A man in work boots argued softly with Window 2 about a death certificate. A teenager in school uniform practiced a speech under his breath for whatever transcript appeal had landed him here. A woman with twin toddlers tried to keep both from licking the base of a public charging station. Everybody in the room had brought some stupid bureaucratic hurt with them, and the room made no distinctions among them. Ticket numbers rose on the wall. A speaker crackled. Lives got reduced to documents, disputed, stamped, moved along.

Faye hated it on sight.

When her number finally flashed, she went to Window 4 and stood.

"Historical verification?" the clerk asked.

"Identity fraud."

"That's the same form." The clerk slid a pad across. "Fill out sections one through five. If the original records are off-world, check the jurisdiction box and write where from."

Faye looked at the form. It wanted legal name, current registry status, origin institution, basis for dispute, requested scope. Half the boxes were small enough to insult her on purpose.

"Basis for dispute," she read. "You want a paragraph or just the part where somebody stole my life."

The clerk's eyes came up, calm and unimpressed. "Either one fits if you write small."

Faye took the form to a side shelf and filled it out with a chained pen that dragged on the paper. Under basis for dispute she wrote possible unauthorized post-suspension use and then crossed out possible because if she had come this far she did not owe caution to a form. Under requested scope she checked full audit and stared at the line for supporting claim. She wrote current biometric match and let it stand. What else was she supposed to put there. Still me?

When she brought it back, the clerk scanned it, typed, and held out a handheld reader.

"Thumb."

Faye pressed her thumb to the glass.

"Retina."

She leaned toward the sensor ring. A soft tone acknowledged the match. The clerk turned the screen slightly and read.

"All right. Current biometric alignment is positive. You're requesting an intersystem pull from an Earth-side cryogenic facility archive. That can take a while."

"How long is a while."

"Depends how bad their indexing was and whether the old med server wants to cooperate today."

"That's not a time."

"No." The clerk clicked through three more windows. "Sit down. Don't leave the building. If it clears, I need a voice print and final signature."

Faye did not ask about the fee until she was already reaching for the slot.

"There's a processing charge?"

"There is a charge for everything," the clerk said.

That, at least, sounded like Mars.

The amount on the screen was not ruinous. It was worse than that. It was petty. Small enough that refusing would have been ridiculous and large enough to irritate her for hours. She paid it and took the receipt, thin paper warm from the printer like the office was trying to hand back heat in place of justice.

She sat again.

The wait ate most of the afternoon. Numbers rose and reset. The toddlers fell asleep sideways across their mother's lap. The student got called, came back out looking relieved and embarrassed. The man in work boots left with three certified copies of somebody's ending tucked under his arm. Faye bought a can of citrus soda from the machine because the coffee from the diner had turned to battery acid in her stomach. She looked at the wall clock too often. She thought about leaving twice and did not.

At some point the clerk at Window 4 took one of the boiled sweets from the jar, cracked it between her teeth, and kept typing.

Finally Faye's number flashed again.

"Voice print," the clerk said when she stepped up. "State your legal name."

For half a second Faye almost said no just to prove the file could not make her do things. Then she looked at the emptying room, the dusty light in the windows, the ridiculous planets on the woman's wrist brace, and said, "Faye Valentine."

The terminal pinged. The clerk's expression did not change, but one corner of the screen filled with new text fast enough that even Faye could tell something big had unlocked.

"All right," the clerk said quietly. "It came through."

"And?"

"And sit down. This isn't one line."

Faye did not sit. The clerk looked at her, then at the room, which by now held only one old man asleep in a chair under the departure board.

"Fine," she said. "Stay standing."

She turned the screen enough for Faye to see.

Most of it was codes. Facility numbers, archival paths, jurisdiction flags, audit references. The top line carried her name in hard black letters and a date that meant nothing to Faye except that it was before the world she knew. Under that:

PATIENT STATUS: SUSPENDED

POST-SUSPENSION ACCESS REVIEW: VERIFIED IRREGULARITY

UNAUTHORIZED CREDENTIAL EXPORT: T plus 3Y 2M

CAUSE: EMPLOYEE MISCONDUCT / DATA RESALE

UTILIZATION WINDOW: 11Y 0M 17D

CURRENT STATE: DORMANT

Faye read the lines twice and still felt like the words had reached her in the wrong order.

"Data resale," she said.

"That is what it says."

"Meaning what."

"Meaning an employee at the facility exported your credential bundle and sold it."

"My credential bundle."

"Your identity file. Biometric profile, account hooks, medical access, whatever linked systems were live at the time."

Faye let out a breath that wanted to be a laugh and failed on the way up. "So while I was frozen, some asshole clerk sold me by the page."

"Looks that way."

"You say that like you're discussing weather."

The clerk's mouth tightened just a little. "I say it like it's an audit summary. If you want outrage, you'll need a different department."

That should have made Faye snap back. Instead she kept staring at the screen.

"How many people bought it."

"Doesn't show. Could've been direct, could've moved through brokers. A lot of these did."

"These."

"Stolen suspended-patient files."

The old man in the chair snored once, then settled deeper into sleep.

"I'm not special," Faye said.

The clerk gave a tiny shrug. "Not to an institution, no."

It was a brutal answer because it was not cruel. The woman was just narrowing scope, giving the factual piece and leaving the rest where it fell. Faye wanted to hate her and could not quite get there.

"What do you mean by utilization window?" Faye asked.

"I mean the stolen credential set stayed active in linked systems for eleven years and seventeen days after the first confirmed fraudulent use."

"Active doing what."

The clerk clicked another tab. More codes. More dates.

"Transit registry. Banking relay. Medical intake. Short-term lodging. Gaming license on Ganymede at one point." She glanced up. "That catch your eye?"

Faye kept her face still. "Keep going."

"Debt collection flags. Off-world customs verification. Miscellaneous civil use. Enough regular activity to establish a living identity rather than a one-time fraud. Whoever had the file used it as a life, not as a quick theft."

Whoever had bought the file had used it as a life.

The phrase sat in Faye's chest and spread outward slowly, the way cold did in bad heating. Miriam's blue dress, let out by hand. A shoulder scar. A tip left by somebody who knew tips. Now this: transit, clinics, rooms, debts. Not a single glamorous thing among them. Just the long, ugly paperwork of staying alive.

"Who stopped it," Faye asked.

"Nobody necessarily stopped it. Dormant means it fell out of active use. Could be death. Could be abandonment. Could be a switch to another identity. Could be simple data decay if the user went off-grid."

"And the employee."

"Terminated after a later review." Another click. "Prosecuted in absentia, looks like. Record sealed after civil consolidation."

"Convenient."

"For somebody."

Faye leaned closer to the screen. Under the clean summary lines there was a dense list of access points and verification events. Most were unreadable to her beyond date and place, but the sheer number of them did more damage than the headline. Here a clinic intake. There a transit gate. Here a temporary license. There a denied claim. Eleven years did not arrive as one theft. They arrived as Tuesday afternoons, motel desks, customs lines, medication counters, rent notices, people behind glass asking for a thumb and a name and getting hers.

She had woken up to debt and a blank wall where the first part of her life should have been. Someone else had gotten paperwork. Someone else had gotten the ordinary weight of days.

"Can I print this."

"You can print the audit summary and certified access log. Detailed cross-system pulls require separate request orders."

"Fine."

The printer behind the clerk started up with a loud mechanical throat-clearing. Fanfold paper fed through, then cut sheets, then one stamped certification page with a red seal that looked theatrical in exactly the wrong way. The clerk squared the stack, stamped two places, signed one line, and slid it through the slot.

"Claimant confirmed by current biometric match," she said. "Historical credential misuse verified."

Faye looked at the top page. Claimant. Confirmed. Misuse. The language managed to make the whole thing sound like a dispute over luggage.

"I was frozen," she said.

The clerk looked at her over the stack. "Right."

"Then I wake up with no memory and debt collectors on my back and now this office is telling me some stranger got eleven years under my name."

"Yes."

"And the paper calls that misuse."

The clerk rested both hands on the desk, careful with the bad wrist. The marker planets flashed under the fluorescent light.

"The paper calls a lot of things the wrong name," she said. "That doesn't make the file less true."

Faye took the stack. It was heavier than it should have been. Three pages of summary, six of certified log, one receipt, one rights notice. Eleven years reduced to fewer sheets than a menu.

"Is there anything in there that says why?" she asked.

"No."

"Nothing."

"Institutions are good at what and when," the clerk said. "Why costs extra and usually still doesn't come through."

The office speaker announced closing in a bored recorded voice. The old man woke with a snort and shuffled toward the exit clutching his envelope. Window 2 shut down. Window 1 followed. The room changed shape as soon as the other terminals went dark. It got wider and emptier at the same time.

"We're closing public service in ten," the clerk said. "You can file for detailed pulls tomorrow if you want them."

"Tomorrow."

"That's how days work."

Faye almost smiled despite herself. It vanished before it fully formed.

"Sure," she said.

She took the papers to the waiting area and sat with them on her knees. The speaker made one more closing announcement. A janitorial cart rattled somewhere down the hall. The clerk at Window 4 shut down her terminal, stacked the boiled sweets into a drawer, and started gathering things into a soft-sided bag. Faye read the top page again. T plus 3Y 2M. 11Y 0M 17D. Dormant.

After a minute a shadow fell across the paper.

"I'm locking the main door," the clerk said. "You can stay a little if you're not looking to rob the place."

Faye looked up.

"You do this for everybody."

"No." The woman adjusted the strap of her bag with her good hand. "Most people leave."

"Maybe I'm special after all."

"Not to the institution," the clerk said. "To me, you're just making me late."

She said it dryly enough that Faye could take it for mercy if she wanted or not if she didn't. The choice felt familiar.

"The exit pad's by the inner door," the clerk went on. "Hit green when you go. It logs the time."

"Everything does."

"Yeah."

Then she left, limping slightly with the stiffness of someone whose brace did not actually solve the problem it was built for.

The lights dimmed by one bank.

Faye stayed where she was.

***

After closing, the records office sounded like a machine embarrassed to be overheard.

The vent above the chairs kept breathing. Somewhere deeper in the building an old server rack hummed with a faint metallic whine. Pipes knocked once, then settled. Through the high wired window by the ceiling, the last of the Mars light had gone from brass to bruised violet. The room had lost all the people who made bureaucracy feel normal. What was left was plastic chairs, dead terminals, and the papers in Faye's lap insisting on a history she had not lived and could not deny.

She read them again anyway.

The summary did not improve with repetition. If anything, it got meaner. The first pass had shock in it. The second had grammar: verified irregularity, employee misconduct, credential export, utilization window, dormant. Words built by people who had decided their distance from damage counted as professionalism.

The paper did not care that a woman had worn a blue dress with the hem let down and stitched back into place by hand. It did not care about a left shoulder blade scar. It did not care that the waitress in the diner had remembered the tip because it came from somebody who understood the insult of not tipping. The office had one language for a life and Miriam had another, and both of them had more of Faye Valentine in them than Faye did.

She let the pages fall closed.

The plastic chair edge bit the backs of her thighs through the skirt. She stayed where she was until the pressure turned from discomfort to information. That felt fair. The whole day had been information dressed up as pain.

She had spent so much time wanting the lost part of herself to turn up intact that she had never really pictured what form that could take. Maybe a letter. Maybe a photograph. Maybe a face in a crowd that moved like hers. Something stupid and cinematic, the kind of thing that made people feel chosen by the universe instead of billed by it. She had not pictured a municipal office on Mars telling her that the few official traces she still had from before the ice had been copied, sold, and worn thin by somebody else's hands.

When she first woke up after cryosleep, everybody had wanted papers from her: name, signature, account acceptance, debt acknowledgment. She had signed because the forms were already printed and the nurses were already tired and a person without memory still needed somewhere for the bill to go. She had thought of those documents as proof that she had existed before the blank.

Now she knew they were also receipts.

Her hand rose to her collarbone, then stopped halfway there. She made herself put it back on the paper.

That was the part she could not get past. Not the theft itself. Theft she understood. Theft had shape. Somebody took what was yours because they wanted it or needed it or could make money off it. Mars ran on versions of that. The part she could not get past was the gesture. Two fingers to the collarbone before a bluff. Miriam had seen it on the woman at the Golden Ox. Faye did it herself without thinking. If the woman had been using her file years before Faye woke up, then Faye had not copied it from her. That made no sense. Unless it had belonged to the Faye from before the ice. Unless it belonged to nobody and this was just the kind of cheap coincidence the world loved because it made people easier to hurt.

Or unless habit lived somewhere memory did not.

That thought was worse than the rest of it. If the body remembered something the mind could not reach, then she was a house full of locked rooms.

She turned the top page over and looked at the access log again. It was all dates, flags, registry handshakes. The record was too technical to narrate and too complete not to. Here was proof that a woman had checked into places, crossed checkpoints, registered debts, stood at counters, gotten sick, needed papers, kept moving. Here was eleven years of not disappearing. Here was more continuity than Faye had ever managed.

That was what the clerk had said: whoever had the file had used it as a life.

The clerk had said it flat, but there had been no way to make it light.

Faye tried, for one ugly second, to hate the unknown woman cleanly. It would have been easier. Somebody stole my name, end of story. Except Miriam's story would not let her. Blue dress with the hem worked over by hand. Real tip. Tired at the door. And the office log would not let her either. Clinic intake. Debt flags. Short-term lodging. The woman who wore Faye Valentine had not spent eleven years getting rich off it. She had spent eleven years surviving with it.

That did not make it less stolen.

It just made it lived in.

Faye leaned back and looked at the ceiling. There was a water stain above the departure board in the shape of a continent she did not know. Somebody had tried to paint over it once and failed. The building had accepted the mark and moved on. She wondered if that was all identity ever was in practice. Not memory, not essence, not any of the pretty words people used when they wanted to sell grief back to you. Just accumulated use. A name with enough wear on it to count.

If that was true, then what did she have. A blank before the ice, debt after it, and a crew that knew her only in the version she had assembled under pressure: sharp mouth, fast hands, appetite, bluff, lipstick when it paid, gun when it paid better. She had thought that version was at least hers because she had built it herself. Now even that felt unsettled. What if the angle of her smile belonged to a woman she could not remember. What if the collarbone touch had come back from under the freeze. What if the body kept faith with somebody the mind never got to meet.

The file did not answer any of that. It only confirmed the insult.

Three years after she went under, a clerk at a cryogenic facility looked at her data and saw inventory.

Then someone else looked at that inventory and saw a door.

Faye sat with that.

The vent clicked louder for a minute, then eased. Somewhere down the hall the janitorial cart passed and moved on. Nobody came in. Nobody asked if she was all right. That was good. She would have had to lie or bite somebody, and both felt tiring.

She pulled the top of her jacket aside and, more carefully now, reached back to touch the smooth skin over her left shoulder blade through the fabric of her top. Nothing raised there. Nothing to map against Miriam's memory. The absence itself felt stupidly intimate, like learning a stranger had better evidence for your own body than you did.

There was still no scar.

That should have been the comforting part. It proved the woman at the Golden Ox had not been her. It gave the whole thing a clean dividing line in flesh: this body, that body; this shoulder, that scar.

Instead it only made the missing years wider.

If it had been her somehow, if the impossible had arranged itself into some neat trick, then at least the life in the records would have belonged to one owner. She could have hated the gap and kept the continuity. But it was not her. The office had said so in its own bloodless way. Miriam had said so in the language of eyes and hemlines and service tips. Another woman had carried Faye Valentine around in public and made the name stick to daily life. Another woman had been remembered in rooms Faye had never entered. Another woman had accumulated the ordinary evidence of existence under a label Faye had woken up clutching like the last thing left in a fire.

She did not cry.

Crying would have made the feeling too neat. Tears wanted one clean loss. This was a stack of wrongnesses that would not line up. There was violation in it, yes, and anger, and the nauseating possibility that the person she most needed answers from might also be someone she could not yet bring herself to condemn without remainder. But what sat heaviest was vertigo. That was the only word close enough. The sense that the floor under her name had split into two levels and she was somehow standing on both.

Miriam had a woman in a blue dress.

The records office had eleven years of coded use.

Faye had a mirror and smooth skin.

She put the papers back in order with more care than they deserved. The certified seal caught the low light and went dull again. On the top line, her name sat there as if it had never been contested by history, by institutions, by another person's need.

It still read Faye Valentine.

She looked at it until the letters lost their edges and turned into shapes.

Outside the wired window the last light went out over the district. The room settled deeper into after-hours quiet. Faye kept one hand on the printout and the other on the unmarked place above her left shoulder blade, and sat in the darkening office without moving, as if stillness could keep the name from slipping any further away.
