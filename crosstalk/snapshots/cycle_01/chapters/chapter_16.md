# Chapter 16

Dry Well looked like somebody had tried to build a town out of scrap hull and bad intentions, then got tired halfway through and let the dust finish the job.

Jet brought the Bebop down slow over the settlement's one clear patch of ground, if a hardpan lot ringed with busted piping could still count as clear. The old waystation sat in a shallow cut of red stone with the wind worrying at it from all sides. Three low buildings hunched against one another as if they had spent years bracing for a blow that never quite stopped coming. Cracked solar panels leaned on rusted frames. A dead condenser tower stood out back like a snapped neck. One ship bay still had power. Ren's ship sat inside it, clean and dark and very much at home in a place that had no business looking lived in.

Spike stood just behind Jet's shoulder with one hand in his pocket, staring through the forward glass. "Used to be a syndicate fuel stop."

Jet kept his eyes on the settling numbers. "You recognize it."

"Recognize the type." Spike's voice was flat. "Places like this were all over the basin. Cheap walls, bad wiring, one room for cargo and one room for lies."

Ed lay upside down across the co-pilot chair, goggles crooked on her forehead, bare feet hooked over the headrest. "Dry Well has one human ping outside, one warm machinery nest inside, no sneaky turret blips, no beep-beep death gifts. Dry Well is rude but not armed."

Faye, strapped into the jump seat behind them, said, "That's comforting."

"Moderately comforting," Ed amended. "Mars-standard comforting."

Jet brought the Bebop lower. Dust skated out from under the thrusters in long red sheets, then curled back on itself and slapped the bay doors. Through the haze he made out a person standing beside an engine block on a waist-high stand. Dark hair tied back. Coveralls. Rag in one hand, wrench in the other. She had been bent over the engine a second ago. Now she was upright, looking at them come in.

She did not run.

That was the thing Jet had been braced for without admitting it to himself. Not gunfire. Not a trap. Motion. A sprint for the ship, a slammed hatch, the old familiar shape of a chase. Instead the woman only stepped back far enough to keep the wash off her face and watched the Bebop descend with the patient expression of a mechanic waiting for somebody else to finish doing a job badly.

Jet set the ship down. The deck thudded under them. For a second all he heard was the cooling tick of old metal and the soft whine of systems settling.

Nobody moved first.

Then Ed unbuckled in a single boneless slide and dropped to the deck. "Adventure cave."

"Not alone," Jet said.

"Jet-person says that every time there is an adventure cave."

"And every time I'm right."

Faye was already on her feet, smoothing a hand down the front of her yellow shorts as if she had dressed for a confrontation she did not intend to take seriously. "If she makes us walk into some sentimental death trap, I'm haunting all of you."

"You'll fit right in," Spike said.

Faye gave him a look without any heat in it. The trip south had worn some of the sharp edges off them, or maybe it had only made the sharpness less useful.

Jet hit the ramp controls. "Nobody starts anything."

"That aimed at me?" Faye asked.

"If the shoe fits."

"You mean if the tiny impossible pair of stupid shoes fits."

Ed, already halfway to the hatch, said, "Ed wears no shoes. Ed is innocent."

The ramp opened on cold air with iron in it. Mars in the south had a different bite than Tharsis. The wind came cleaner out here, not because the basin was pure but because there was less of everything human to blunt it. Dust struck the metal ramp in fast dry whispers. Somewhere in the settlement a loose panel clanged on a rhythm too uneven to become background.

Jet stepped down first. The ground gave a little under his boots, packed dust over old concrete. Spike came after him, loose as always, though Jet knew better than to trust what Spike looked like. Faye descended with one hand near the pistol at the small of her back. Ed came last because Ed had stopped halfway down to peer under the ramp at a line of blackened pipe running into the dirt. Ein trotted after her, nose busy, little body low against the wind.

The woman by the engine wiped her hands on the rag and tossed it over one shoulder onto an open toolbox. She was younger up close than the trouble she had caused. Early twenties, maybe. Lean, medium height. Sharp face. The resemblance Faye had flinched from on the chip was there if a person let their eyes blur for it, but it did not survive attention. This woman looked like she had been built by long work and thin meals, not cryogenic chance. Her hands were what Jet noticed most. Grease in the cuts, calluses thick across the base of the fingers, one thumbnail split and growing out crooked. Hands that made a living by getting skinned.

She looked at all of them in order. Jet. Spike. Faye. Ed crouched by the pipe. Ein sniffing a bolt. Her gaze stopped nowhere long enough to become a challenge.

"You made good time," she said.

Her voice was steady and lower than Jet expected. Not cold. Not welcoming either. More like she had long ago accepted that conversations of consequence happened whether a person felt ready or not.

Jet said, "Ren Kozuki."

"Yeah."

Faye folded her arms. "You couldn't have just sent a note?"

Ren glanced at her, then at the Bebop behind them. "You would've ignored a note."

"Try me."

"I did." Ren nodded toward the ship. "That was the note."

Jet ought to have been angry. Part of him still was. Eight thousand woolongs in fines, fuel burned, old files dragged up from places he had buried them with both hands. But standing in front of the woman who had done it, with the engine stand between them and the wind kicking grit at his cuffs, anger had nowhere clean to go. There was too much face on the thing now. Too much actual life.

Spike looked at the engine block on the stand. "You were really fixing that."

Ren followed his gaze. "Starter assembly's been chewing itself. It wasn't going to improve because you showed up."

That got the faintest twitch at one corner of Spike's mouth. It vanished before it was a smile.

Ed straightened from the pipe and pointed into the building. "Dry Well has old signal bones in the walls. They are crunchy."

"They are," Ren said. "Don't touch the junction box in the common room unless you want the lights to die."

Ed nodded solemnly, as if receiving sacred law.

Jet had been waiting for the next move, the reveal of the gunmen on the roof, the lock dropping on the bay, something. Ren only picked up the rag again and wiped a streak of oil from the back of her wrist.

"If you're here to talk," she said, "come inside. If you're here to drag me out in cuffs, I don't have any, and I'd rather not do it in the dust. It gets into the bearings."

Faye said, "You always this hospitable?"

"No." Ren slung the rag into the toolbox and snapped it shut with her heel. "Just practical."

She turned and headed toward the building without checking whether they followed. Not arrogance exactly. More like confidence built from years of having only herself to rely on. Jet exchanged a look with Spike. Spike gave the smallest shrug. Faye rolled her eyes at both of them and started after Ren. Ed was already gone, following her in a crooked line with Ein weaving around her ankles.

The bay door groaned open on manual tracks. Inside, the air held the layered smell of hot dust, machine oil, old insulation, and something boiled to death in recycled water. Ren's ship took up most of the bay, its hull scarred now that Jet could see it without wanting to kill it on sight. Tools hung in neat shadow shapes along one wall. Parts were sorted into old food tins and labeled in blocky marker. Somebody had made this place work by refusing to care that it had not been built to.

The common room opened off the bay through a narrow pressure door with a bad seal. Jet felt the draft before he saw the room. A cot against the back wall. A workbench under the only intact strip light. Two mismatched chairs, one with the stuffing showing through the cracked vinyl. A kettle on a hot plate. And across the largest wall, from shoulder height nearly to the ceiling, a spread of paper and plastic sleeves and copied photos and route maps and handwritten notes pinned so tightly together that at first Jet could not tell where one ended and the next began.

Ed stopped dead in the doorway.

"Oh," she said.

The sound had none of her usual bounce in it.

It was not a detective's board. It was worse than that. Better, maybe, if a person respected labor. No theatrical loops of string. No red circles drawn by somebody in love with accusation. Just years of patient accumulation. An ISSP report with a precinct stamp Jet knew in his bones. A syndicate cargo manifest Spike had once held in his own hands. A clinic intake form with Faye Valentine's name typed across the top and a corner torn away where some other identifier had been removed. Old transit maps of the Hellas corridor. A photograph of a dark-haired woman with a little of Faye in the shape of the mouth and none of her in the eyes, standing under a clinic awning with one arm folded tight across her waist. Another photo, older and grainier, of a man in cheap civilian clothes holding a toddler on his hip while looking over his own shoulder as if the camera might be trouble.

Jet knew the paper before he could read it. ISSP stock had a grain to it. Even copied three times, it kept the look of institutional cheapness.

Nobody said anything.

Ren set the toolbox down by the workbench. "The water takes a minute if anybody wants tea."

Jet kept staring at the wall. "Start talking."

* * *

The line she had planned to begin with was gone by the time the kettle started to tremble.

Ren had built the first ten minutes of this conversation a hundred times in her head over the years. On bus rides. Under ships. Half asleep with a light still on because she had fallen into records again. The order had always seemed important. Start with the files. No, start with her father because he came first. No, start with her mother because the face would make Faye listen. Keep it clean. Keep it factual. Do not let them turn you into a stray problem they can solve by deciding what you feel.

Then the five of them were in the room, taking up more space than they had any right to, and nothing held shape.

Jet stood closest to the wall, not because he wanted to but because the papers had pulled him there. He had the posture he got when he was forcing himself not to pace: big shoulders set, weight balanced wrong, one hand on his hip near the old familiar place a badge used to ride. Spike leaned against the doorframe into the bay, eyes on the route maps first and only then on Ren, as if he trusted paper less than people and people not at all. Faye had taken the chair with the torn vinyl and sat on the edge of it, one knee bouncing once before she shut it down. Ed crouched on the floor under the document wall with her hands on her ankles, looking up. Ein curled beside her foot, already choosing comfort over history.

Ren poured hot water over a tea brick shaved into a dented pot. The smell that rose was bitter and old and barely tea at all.

"It's bad," she said. "But it's warm."

"That's a ringing endorsement," Faye muttered.

She took a cup anyway when Ren handed them around. So did Jet. Spike shook his head, then changed his mind and took one after all. Ed accepted hers with both hands and sniffed it like a suspicious engineer.

Jet did not drink. "You said start talking."

Ren leaned back against the workbench, cup warming her palm. "All right."

She looked at the photo of the man with the toddler because looking at his face was easier than looking at theirs.

"My father's name was Daichi Kozuki," she said. "You already know that part. He was an informant for ISSP out of your precinct."

Jet's jaw moved once. "I know the name."

"You knew the access codes. That's not the same thing."

His eyes shifted to her. There was surprise in them, and something almost like respect for the distinction, though he would rather have bitten glass than show it.

Ren set her cup down. "He didn't work for ISSP because he loved the law. He worked for ISSP because he thought information would buy him room. That's all he ever wanted, really. Room. My mother said he hated owing anybody money and hated owing anybody favors even more. He kept copies of everything because he figured if somebody tried to bury him, he'd at least leave a mess behind."

She crossed to the wall and tapped a row of plastic sleeves near the middle. Old paper. Handwritten tabs. Green, blue, yellow, all cut from cheap file dividers.

"He color-coded by mood," she said. "Green if he thought something was dangerous. Blue if he thought it was stupid. Yellow if he thought it was both. He wrote complaints in the margins. Mostly about coffee. Sometimes about people's ties."

Ed tilted her head. "Organized grudge system."

"Yeah," Ren said. "Pretty much."

The words had done what facts alone could not. The man on the wall stopped being an informant and became somebody who got irritated at bad coffee and ugly neckwear. Jet's gaze caught on the colored tabs. Faye looked again at the photo, and Ren could see the shift in her face too, unwilling as it was.

Ren went on. "He worked routes in and out of the basin. Freight gossip, small syndicate movements, names of couriers, where shipments got thin because somebody in the middle had taken a cut. He fed pieces upward and thought that if he made himself useful enough, ISSP would protect us if things got bad."

Spike finally sipped the tea and made a face. "That's one mistake."

"He knew it wasn't clean," Ren said. "He didn't think it had to be clean. He thought it had to be better than the other side."

Jet spoke without looking away from the papers. "Who was handling him."

Not a question. Ren heard what sat under it. A test, maybe. Or a wish that there would still be some room left for uncertainty.

"Your partner," she said. "Not you."

Jet looked at her then, fully. "You sure."

He had not put a question mark on it. Jet's voice, when pain got near it, flattened out instead of rising. Ren had expected anger. The stillness was worse.

She moved two steps down the wall and pulled a copied ledger page from behind a route map. Another sheet came after it, then a photo with a precinct timestamp burned into one corner.

"These were in my father's storage unit," she said. "Along with a key to a dead drop box that still had the bank notices in it. Small payments. Irregular. Your partner's shell account on one side. A freight concern tied to Mars syndicate people on the other. Money coming in after my father turned over route updates. Not every time. Enough times."

Jet took the pages from her. His metal fingers were careful on the paper. More careful than flesh had ever made him.

"Could be coincidence," he said, but it was the kind of thing a man said because the sentence had existed before he opened his mouth.

"It could be," Ren said. "If it was only that."

She tapped the photograph. Two men outside a precinct service entrance. One of them turned half away from the camera. The other was Daichi, thinner than in the family photo, shoulders up around his ears. Fear had a posture once a person learned to recognize it.

"This was two days before he died. The timestamp puts it after an off-book meeting. The next report he handed over was a corridor map for the Hellas run. Forty-eight hours later the syndicate moved on one of the safe transfer points he had just flagged. That told him the leak was inside ISSP, not out on the street. He wrote that down too."

She handed Jet another copy, this one of her father's own note. Block letters, cramped. NOISE INSIDE HOUSE. WATCH HANDLER.

Jet read it. His eyes did not move for a while.

Ren heard the hot plate tick as it cycled off.

"How was he killed?" Jet asked.

Faye shut her eyes for half a second, as if she had already heard enough and knew there was more coming anyway.

Ren could have softened it. She knew how. Years around clinics had taught everybody in the basin the house language for horror. Complications. Incident. Acute event. Loss. Words that dressed the body before it was cold.

She was tired of those words.

"He went to a meet point because your partner told him ISSP extraction would be there," she said. "It wasn't. Three syndicate men were."

The room held still.

"The report says blunt-force trauma and asphyxiation," Ren said. "The scene photos say they broke his right hand first, then two fingers on the left, then most of his teeth. He still didn't give them the backup box. They used a wire after that."

Ed's hand came down to rest on Ein's back. Not fidgeting. Just there.

Jet set the pages carefully on the chair arm beside him, like paper could break louder than a person if he mishandled it. Ren watched his throat work once. He looked back at the wall instead of at her.

"He had a daughter," he said.

"Yeah."

"And he still kept copies."

Ren almost smiled, because there it was, the piece of Jet Black everybody on Mars could have told you if they had ever eaten his food or taken his help or heard him yell at somebody he liked. Practicality first. Survival as a sequence of choices. Why would a man with a child keep risk in a storage unit. Why would he do any of it.

"He thought the copies were what would keep us safe," she said. "He wasn't wrong about how the world worked. He was wrong about which side would use them first."

Jet finally drank the tea. It had gone lukewarm enough to stop pretending.

Faye set her cup down untouched. "And your mother."

Ren had known Faye would do that. Cut across the room at the point of maximum tenderness and drag the wound she wanted into the middle where everybody could stare at it. It would have been cruelty from some people. From Faye it was defense. Ren respected it.

She turned to the clinic form with the typed name.

"Ayane Kozuki," she said. "My mother."

The picture there had been taken under bad weather light. Even copied, the resemblance reached across paper. The line of the jaw. The mouth when it was tired. Not the eyes. Never the eyes.

"She ran courier work before my father died and after," Ren said. "Mostly medicine, machine parts, small freight. Sometimes guns if the route came attached to the rest and saying no meant no more work at all. She wasn't syndicate because she believed in it. She was syndicate because Mars has a lot of jobs that become syndicate jobs if you get poor enough."

Spike's cup stopped halfway to his mouth. He lowered it without drinking.

Ren kept going. "She'd had a kidney transplant before I was born. I don't know all the reasons. She didn't talk about before. Not much. She hated being looked at like a lesson. What mattered was the meds after. Immunosuppressants. Anti-rejection drugs. Regular bloodwork if she could get it, which mostly she couldn't."

Faye's voice was hard and level. "And she got those with my name."

"Yes."

There it was. No way around it. Ren let it stand in the room exactly as it was.

"After my father died, Kozuki wasn't a usable name anymore," she said. "Too visible to the wrong people, not visible enough to the right ones. Clinics saw a widow tied to a dead informant and a courier network and found reasons to lose paperwork. Landlords saw trouble. Employers saw it too. A clean identity packet came through a clerk at the cryogenic facility. My mother bought it because it worked."

Faye laughed once, and there was nothing amused in it. "Identity packet. That's nice."

"I know."

"Do you?"

The attack was there, but not all the way. Ren heard the edge under the edge. Faye did not want apology. She wanted somebody to say the thing straight.

"It was theft," Ren said. "It kept her alive and it was theft."

Faye looked at her for a long beat, searching for some slick little adjustment in the language. Ren gave her none. After a second Faye leaned back in the chair and crossed one leg over the other, a motion too sharp to be casual.

"Go on," she said.

Ren nodded.

"The records office packet had biometrics, financial history, medical access, some intake footage. Enough to pass under pressure if nobody was checking too hard. My mother learned the name fast. Faye Valentine got better service than Ayane Kozuki. Faye Valentine got seen. Faye Valentine could stand at a clinic desk and have somebody look at the chart instead of the woman."

She paused, then added, because if she was going to say it she was going to say it all the way, "She wasn't good at being your kind of reckless. Too careful. But she got good at the parts of you that opened doors."

Faye's fingers moved once toward her own collarbone, then stopped in the air and closed into a fist instead.

Spike said, "The medication. My route."

Not a question either. Just another man dropping a thing he already knew onto the table to hear what sound it made in someone else's mouth.

Ren pulled the annotated manifest off the wall and held it out to him. He did not take it right away. When he did, his thumb went to the note in the corner before the rest of the page.

"Most of the shipments she relied on came through a corridor your alias helped standardize," Ren said. "Not every box. Not every run. But enough that when I started mapping shortages against route changes, your name kept showing up where the lines tightened."

Spike read the manifest without expression. The absence of expression was expression with him.

"I wrote that note," Ren said.

"Figured." His voice was quiet.

"My mother told me about the locker. Said people like your bosses always kept extra nests in case they needed to vanish from each other. She'd used some of the waystations. Not that one, but she knew the pattern. When I found out it was still yours, I put the manifest where you'd see it."

Spike looked up from the page. "Why."

Ren frowned a little. "Because it was yours."

"That's not an answer."

"It is if you think about it for five seconds."

For the first time since they'd come in, heat moved in the room. Not enough to flare. Enough to show that Ren had edges and wasn't interested in sanding them down because the people in front of her carried old damage elegantly.

Spike's mouth flattened. Jet glanced between them and did not step in.

Ren set her cup aside. Her hands wanted a tool. There were none between them but paper, so she folded her arms instead.

"You asked why," she said. "Because if I'd left it on a public board or mailed it to the Bebop, it would've been one more weird thing in a pile of weird things. You would've treated it like bait, and you would've been right. I needed you to touch the part that belonged to you."

Spike looked back down at the manifest.

"She needed it to live," Ren said, softer now, because that sentence always came out softer no matter how she meant it. "Not once. Not in some dramatic final way. Every day. Every week. It was pills cut in halves with a kitchen razor. It was praying a courier didn't skim the cold storage for resale. It was my mother sitting at the table with a bowl under her chin because the nausea hit before she could stand up. It was her ankles swelling over the tops of her boots so bad she stopped wearing boots. It was the skin around her eyes going yellow-gray. It was her pissing twice in a day and both times it smelled like pennies."

Nobody interrupted.

"When the route held, she looked almost fine," Ren said. "Tired, but fine. When it didn't, she shook. She'd sit on the edge of the cot and hold her own wrists because her hands wouldn't stop. By the end she had sores in her mouth and a taste like battery metal all the time. She'd spit into the sink and rinse and tell me the clinic lights were making her sick because that sounded less final than what was happening."

Faye had gone very still. The hard shell of her posture remained intact, but Ren could see the cost of holding it there. Faye knew what it meant for another woman to have died under her name. Ren had always known she would. Knowing it and seeing it were different.

Jet rubbed the heel of his hand once against his beard. "How old were you."

"Nine when she died."

"And before that."

"Old enough to count pills." Ren shrugged. "Old enough to know what a clinic clerk's face looked like when the chart said Faye Valentine and the woman at the counter didn't look expensive enough for it."

Faye's laugh came out low this time, almost not there. "That sounds about right."

Ren heard the opening in it and didn't take it. Faye would decide for herself how much room to give.

"What was she like," Faye asked after a while, and then, with immediate irritation at her own question, "No, not the file version. Don't give me some research answer."

Ren looked at the photo under the awning and tried to pick one thing that belonged to her mother alone. There were too many. There were not enough.

"She read the last page of detective magazines first," Ren said. "Said if the ending was stupid there was no reason to waste the middle. She couldn't keep a pair of coveralls intact at the knees because she was always dropping to the floor instead of bending like a normal person. She hated oranges. Said they made a room smell like hospital cleanser. She touched her collarbone when she was thinking or bluffing or trying not to let somebody see she was scared."

Faye's eyes lifted to hers.

"That last one," Faye said. "How."

Ren let out a breath through her nose. There it was. The question that had been waiting under all the other ones.

"I don't know," she said.

Faye looked unconvinced and deserved better than a shrug, so Ren gave her the whole answer.

"The packet had a short intake clip from before you were frozen. Maybe she picked it up there. Maybe she saw it in some other part of the records and kept it because it helped the impersonation. Maybe it was hers already and she only made you doubt it because she was wearing your face when somebody saw her do it." Ren's mouth tightened. "I grew up watching her do that. Then I saw you do it in the market and I still don't know which of you I was looking at for a second. I can't tell you anything cleaner than that."

Faye looked away first, toward the clinic form on the wall. Her hand did go to her collarbone this time, not a performance, not even a conscious motion. She caught it there and dropped it to her thigh as if somebody else had moved it for her.

Ed spoke into the quiet. "Why the orphanage, then?"

All the adults turned. Ed hadn't looked up from Ein, but her voice had lost all its usual decorative bounce.

"Why baby-Ed papers," she said. "Ed was not in your dead-parent math."

Ren had expected the question less than she should have. She had spent so many years looking at the main lines of the web that she had still, sometimes, to remind herself that the shape of the Bebop now was not the shape it had been when her parents were alive.

"No," she said. "You weren't."

Ed waited.

"But you were on the ship." Ren glanced at the wall, at the places where the later notes were pinned in a slightly different pattern because those had been the years when the research stopped being about three names and started being about a crew. "I needed to know who was there. Not just once. Who stayed. Who mattered."

"Ed mattered."

"Yeah."

Ed nodded like a person confirming a number against a record. "Okay."

That was all. Ren felt something in her chest loosen half an inch and hated that the kid had done it by being simpler than the adults.

Jet had moved closer to the photograph of Daichi with the toddler. He was studying it with the expression he used on crime scenes back when he still had reason to think looking hard enough could fix the world.

"I never knew him," he said.

Ren answered the sentence he meant, not the one he had said. "I know."

"I should've."

"Maybe." She was careful here because this was the ground where people tried to make themselves innocent by making themselves central. Jet did not strike her as that kind of man, but grief and guilt both made idiots out of people. "Or maybe the whole program was built so you wouldn't. Informants were supposed to be segmented. Need-to-know. That's what all the training docs say, anyway. My father thought that meant he was protected. Mostly it meant if he got killed, nobody on the floor had to feel it with a face attached."

Jet let out a breath that almost became a laugh and died before it found any humor. "That sounds like ISSP."

"Yeah." Ren leaned a shoulder against the workbench. "That's kind of the point."

He turned then, slower than before. "You keep saying point. All right. Let's get to it. You parked in our spot. You lit up dead files, old frequencies, records offices, half the wrong people on Mars. You chased us all over the basin. Now we're here. What was the point."

There it was. Not anger, not exactly. Demand. A man who could live with bad news if it came attached to purpose.

Ren had thought she would be ready for this part. She had practiced it most. In every version of the conversation there was a line, neat and spare, something she could put down between them like a wrench and let the weight of it do the rest.

Instead she said, "I wanted you to know what you were standing on."

No one moved.

Jet's brow went down. "That's it."

"That's not a small thing."

"No," he said. "It isn't. Which is why I'm asking again."

Faye cut in before Ren answered. "He means if you wanted blood, you should say blood. If you wanted an apology, say that. If you wanted us miserable, congratulations, but don't make it sound noble."

Spike was still looking at the manifest. "If you want to say one of us killed her, say that too."

Ren looked at him. He had not raised his voice. He never did when the blow mattered. There was a darkness in the flatness of his face that made it clear he wasn't offering absolution or daring. He was tired of ambiguity because ambiguity was what let him keep walking.

"No," she said.

Spike's eyes lifted.

"No what."

"No, I don't want to say that." Ren heard the roughness enter her own voice now, the tiny looseness that meant she was past the rehearsed part. "Because if it was one person, one choice, one bullet, I could've been done years ago. I could've picked one body and been done with it. It wasn't like that."

She pushed off the bench and crossed to the wall again, not because the movement would make the argument better but because her hands had started to itch.

"My father took money from cops because he thought it would buy safety. My mother took syndicate work because rent and medicine don't care what kind of money paid them. A clinic clerk sold a frozen woman's identity because corruption's easier to run when the victim's on ice. Couriers skimmed shipments. Supervisors looked away. Your partner sold information. You ran routes without asking what rode inside the boxes. Everybody had a reason. Everybody had a smaller story that made sense from where they were standing."

She tapped the wall once with two fingers. Paper clicked softly under the nail.

"I spent years trying to pick one person to blame," she said. "My father's handler. The clerk. A courier who stole a dose. My mother for buying the packet in the first place. Myself for being nine and not able to fix anything. You know what happened."

Nobody answered.

"The list got longer," Ren said. "So I stopped trying to make it one throat. I mapped it instead."

Jet looked at the wall the way a person looked at a machine after realizing the noise they'd thought came from one loose part was actually the whole assembly grinding wrong.

Faye said, quieter now, "And us."

"Yes."

"Why us, specifically. Plenty of people were in that system."

"Because you were all on one ship." The answer came out more tired than sharp. "Do you understand how impossible that looked from the outside? The dead cop file. The syndicate alias. My mother's stolen name. Then one day I trace a registration and there you all are, sleeping under one hull and eating Jet Black's bad pantry meals."

Jet blinked. "My what."

Ed made a small surprised sound that might have been a laugh trying not to get noticed.

Ren felt heat rise in her face for the first time all day. "I read what I could."

"Apparently."

The corner of Faye's mouth twitched despite herself. It was gone almost at once, but the room had shifted half a degree. That was all grief usually allowed.

Ren let herself take it. "I thought at first maybe it was deliberate. Some long game. Some conspiracy that started before I was born and only looked accidental because I didn't have enough of it yet. I kept pulling records and it kept getting dumber than that. Smaller. Meaner. More random. You weren't arranged. You just got hit by the same machinery from different sides and then later wound up living together."

Spike said, "That's your big revelation. Bureaucracy did it."

It would have been a dismissal from anybody else. Coming from him, it sounded more like a man seeing the shape of the cell and resenting how ordinary the bars were.

Ren met his gaze. "Yeah. And corruption. And markets. And people too broke or tired or scared to say no. If you want it to sound uglier, I can do that."

He looked away first.

Jet had not moved. "You could've told us."

There it was. The complaint that sat under the whole trip south. Not only why did you do this. Why did you make us do it this way.

Ren nodded because that part, at least, was fair.

"I could've," she said. "I thought about it. Walk up to the Bebop and say hello, my father died because your precinct was rotten, my mother died wearing her name, your dead alias hauled her medicine for a while, and I need you to listen."

Faye snorted despite herself.

"Yeah," Ren said. "Exactly."

Jet dragged a hand over his beard. "We would've listened."

Ren did not call him a liar. He wasn't one. Not about that.

"Maybe you would've," she said. "For a few minutes. Then what. You'd ask where I got the files. You'd argue over whether they were real. You'd start picking at each other before any of it got deep enough to matter. Or you'd decide I was one more grief-struck person building a pattern out of scraps because that's what grief does when it has nowhere useful to go." She shook her head. "I needed you to find your own parts yourselves. Your own files. Your own memories. Otherwise every piece would've looked like mine and none of it would've stuck."

Ed raised a hand from the floor as if in class. "Parking rage was ignition fuel."

Ren looked at Ed and, before she could stop it, laughed once. Not much. Enough.

"Yes," she said.

Jet stared at her. "You occupied my docking spot to provoke an investigation."

"Yes."

"For six months."

"I prepaid because Mama Yun doesn't trust promises."

"You know Mama Yun."

"Everybody on that block knows Mama Yun."

That one finally got a dry exhale out of Jet, not laughter, but close enough to hear the same tired humor in it. He shook his head like he could not decide whether to be offended by the manipulation or by how precisely it had been calibrated.

"You used the Hosuke Takara bounty because of me," he said.

"Because you'd look. Because you'd have to."

"And the frequency ping."

Ren glanced at Spike. "Same answer."

"The market meeting with Faye."

Faye said, "She just likes being irritating."

"That too," Ren said.

The almost-smile on Faye's face became real for a second and then folded back up into something harder to live with.

Jet's eyes went again to the pages in his hand. "My partner."

The room dropped with the subject. Ren felt it in her knees.

"He's dead," she said.

Jet did not look up. "How."

"Syndicate dispute." Ren kept the words plain. "Years later. Not over my father. Over money from a warehouse split. He bled out in a loading yard because the people he was cheating got there first."

Jet let that sit. Ren could see him refusing story shape. No justice. No cosmic bookkeeping. Just rot feeding on itself until it hit the man who had profited from it.

"What's his name," Faye asked suddenly.

Jet's head came up. Ren saw the question in his face too. Name mattered. Name made a body.

She could have given it. It was on three of the pages five feet away. But the name would do something specific to Jet, and this was not yet the hour for it. She knew that with the same mechanic's certainty she used on stress cracks and failing seals.

"You know it," she said to Jet.

It was true. She watched him realize that. Watched him read the signature in memory before he let himself read it on paper. The stillness that took him then was different from before. Not shock anymore. Recognition, old and sour.

Ren narrowed the conversation before it could turn into something else. "I didn't bring you here for that part."

Jet looked at her with raw dislike for the boundary and understood why she had set it anyway.

Faye broke the silence. "So what now."

Ren frowned. "What do you mean."

"I mean congratulations, we're informed. Now what."

Ren had spent so long pulling toward this room that anything beyond it had always been blurry. She had plans for departure vectors, fuel tables, storage, where to sleep if the conversation went bad and they tried to drag her out anyway. She had not had a plan that asked something from them after the truth was spoken, because asking had never been the point.

"Nothing," she said.

Faye stared. "Nothing."

"I didn't bring you here to make you fix it."

"You can't fix dead," Spike said.

"No," Ren said. "You can't."

She heard the caution in her own voice and hated it. This was the place where she usually corrected herself back into fact, where she cut away whatever feeling had started to show. The room had gotten harder to control than that.

"I wanted..." She stopped, shook her head once, started again. "I wanted it to stop being invisible. That's all. I wanted you to know they existed. Not as an informant and a courier and a stolen name. As people. I wanted you to know the shape you were inside before you called it coincidence or fate or whatever else made it sound pretty."

Her eyes stung once. The sensation annoyed her more than it hurt. She picked up the teapot just to have something in her hand and found it empty.

"My father wore the same coat too long because he thought replacing a thing before it failed was superstition," she said. "My mother couldn't stand music with lyrics when she was sick because voices made her feel crowded. They fought about stupid things. She beat him at cards because he had no poker face at all. He used to cut the crusts off my bread because I said the corners scratched my mouth. Then he got himself killed believing a precinct that couldn't keep its own floor clean, and she spent the next years dying in pieces under somebody else's name. That's what I wanted you to know. That it wasn't abstract. It wasn't only files."

The bay panel outside banged in the wind. Nobody flinched. The room had narrowed too far for that.

Jet put the pages down very carefully. He looked older than he had on the trip south. Not in years. In weight.

"You said you don't blame us," he said.

Ren nodded.

"I don't know what that means."

That, at least, she had an answer for.

"It means I tried to," she said. "For a long time. It would've been easier. Cleaner. If I could've put my hand on one of you and said there. That's the reason. But every time I traced one line, it crossed three more. My father chose things. My mother chose things. You chose things. Most of them happened before any of you knew each other. None of you woke up deciding to build this exact wreck. You were in it. So were they."

Jet's mouth pulled to one side. "That's not much comfort."

"I know."

"Then why say it."

Ren met his eyes. "Because it's true."

Truth, she had learned, was often a bad substitute for justice. It was still better than theater.

Spike rubbed the edge of the manifest between thumb and forefinger. "So we're cogs."

"Sometimes," Ren said. "Sometimes you're the hand turning the machine. Sometimes you're the part getting ground down by it. Most days you're both."

"That sounds miserable."

"Mars usually is."

Faye barked a short laugh at that. This one did not break.

Ed rolled onto her side and looked up at the wall. "Bebop web was there before Bebop house. House just made the web sleepy and domestic."

No one corrected the grammar because none of them had better language for it.

Ren looked at the kid. "Yeah."

Jet finally sank into the second chair as if he had just noticed his legs existed. The vinyl squealed under his weight.

"All right," he said. "All right."

He did not sound settled. He sounded like a man clearing a patch of ground in his own head and finding more wreckage under the first layer.

Faye stood and crossed to the wall. She stopped in front of the clinic intake form, close enough that Ren could see her reading line by line. Name. Height. Weight. Biometric confirmation. Prescription access. Beneath the typed words a clerk's pen had marked one dosage change in blue, then another in red. Adjustments to a body Faye had never lived in and yet had worn, bureaucratically, for years.

"Did she use it everywhere," Faye asked without turning.

"No. Just where she had to. Clinics. Contracts that wanted a clean ID. The Golden Ox once or twice because a respectable woman with money got watched differently than a courier from the basin."

Faye touched the edge of the form with one fingernail. "Was she good."

Ren knew what she meant.

"At cards? Good enough to leave before the table got angry. Better at reading who'd fold from boredom. Worse at reading who'd follow her into an alley. She once won three hands in a row and spent the whole walk home furious because she tipped a waitress too much."

That finally pulled Faye around. "She tipped."

"Yeah."

"Nobody tips at a place like that."

"I know. She said the woman looked like she'd had a long shift and bad shoes."

Faye's expression did something strange, not softening exactly but losing the clean shape of anger. Miriam had told her that part. Hearing it from the daughter of the woman who had done it made the theft feel meaner and more alive at once.

"Damn it," Faye said, mostly to the form.

Ren did not answer. Some emotions were better left without witness until they chose to have one.

Spike pushed off the doorframe at last and came to the wall. He stood beside a map of the Tharsis-Hellas corridor with old syndicate route marks copied over in black pencil.

"This line," he said, touching one fork in the map. "It was cut because the pass got unstable after a slide. Not because I left."

Ren stepped beside him. "For a while, yes. Then the reroute started getting hit."

"That would've happened anyway."

"Maybe."

He glanced down at her. "You don't have to protect me from it."

"I'm not." Ren followed the route line with her finger. "I'm telling you not to make yourself the hero of the tragedy either. It was bigger than one departure. The corridor got worse after you burned your alias because nobody in the chain knew who was stealing what anymore. Smaller crews. Sloppier cold storage. More middlemen. That hurt her. So did the dust storms that grounded flights. So did clinic shortages. So did a doctor who cut her dose to stretch inventory. That's what I mean."

Spike looked at the wall a long time. "Messier than murder."

"Yeah."

"Worse."

Ren considered that. "Longer, anyway."

He let out a breath. "Hell."

Jet lifted his head. "You have records from the clinic."

"Some."

"And the precinct."

"Some."

"And the routes."

"Enough."

He rubbed at his brow. "I'm staying up all night, aren't I."

"Probably," Ren said.

Faye looked over. "You're not the only one."

That landed in the room without anybody trying to make it sentimental. One fact among others.

Outside, the wind rose and shoved at the bay with both hands. The strip light flickered once. Ed looked up toward the ceiling and pointed. "Crunchy junction box is thinking about betrayal."

Ren went automatically to the wall switch, slapped the side panel twice, and the light steadied.

"It does that," she said.

Jet watched her. "You live here."

"Sometimes."

"By yourself."

"Mostly."

"Why here."

She glanced toward the bay door, toward the shape of her ship through the narrow window. "Because my mother picked up supply drops here. Because the rent is the work. Because nobody wants a half-dead syndicate stop at the bottom of Hellas unless they have a reason."

Faye said, "And your reason is the murder scrapbook."

Ren looked back at the wall. "Research," she said.

"Right. Sorry. The grief archive."

That one came out meaner. Faye heard it too. Her mouth tightened.

Ren did not let herself flinch from it. "Yeah," she said. "That too."

Faye looked ashamed for exactly half a second and covered it with annoyance. "You could at least have better tea."

Ren snorted. "If I'd had better tea, I would've sold it for fuel."

Jet's eyes moved to the empty pot. "You got food."

"Some."

"Enough for six."

Ren understood then, with the clear absurdity it deserved, that even here, even now, Jet Black was inventorying hospitality because that was the shape care took in him before it knew it had arrived.

"Enough for tonight," she said.

He grunted. The sound held more acceptance than the word yes ever would have.

No one said they were staying. The weather outside said it for them, and the hour, and the way the room had shifted from confrontation to the slow uglier work of sitting with what had been uncovered.

Faye went back to the chair but did not sit. Spike remained at the wall, manifest in hand. Ed had started tracing the pattern of documents in the air without touching them, following lines only she could see. Jet sat with his elbows on his knees, staring at the photo of Daichi and the copied notation beside it and some place beyond both.

Ren felt the adrenaline go out of her body in strips. What was left behind was exhaustion so sudden it almost made her laugh. She had spent years reaching for this exact room and had not really believed in it until the hatch opened and there they were, boots on her floor, carrying all their own history in with them.

Jet broke the silence first.

"You should've just told us your name at the start," he said.

Ren leaned back against the bench. "You should've looked harder at your own records twenty years ago."

His head came up. For a moment she thought he might take offense at last and the whole fragile thing might break along its oldest seam.

Instead he gave one short nod. "Fair enough."

Faye said, "I'm not saying that."

"Nobody asked," Spike said.

"Shut up."

Ed, still on the floor, said, "Bebop people are doing the low-information fighting now. Healthy sign."

That did it. The laugh that moved through the room was brief and ragged and unwilling, but it was a laugh. It left behind a quieter kind of silence.

Ren looked from one of them to the next. Jet, wrecked and still trying to make order. Spike, holding a dead alias in one hand and not hiding from it for once. Faye, furious in ways that had not sorted themselves yet and maybe never would. Ed, cross-legged under the wall like a strange little saint of data. Not destiny. Not conspiracy. Just five lives that had crossed the same brutal systems from different angles and wound up sharing a ship badly enough that an outsider could mistake it for design.

The Bebop had not made the web. It had only given it walls and a stove and somewhere to sleep.

"I don't need anything from you," Ren said, because that mattered, maybe most. "Not money. Not revenge. Not some speech about being sorry."

Jet let out a tired breath. "Good. Because we're not exactly stocked for speeches."

"I noticed."

"Rude."

"True."

He looked at her then, and for the first time since landing there was no challenge in it. Only a battered sort of attention.

"What if we still have questions," he said.

Ren glanced at the wall, at the cot, at the bad kettle and the door to the bay and the night settling hard outside. "Then ask them."

No one did. Not right away.

There were too many, and for once none of them came shaped like a plan.
