# Chapter 14

The Bebop sounded bigger when nobody was using it right.

It had the same hull tick, the same breathy push of life support through the vents, the same loose spoon in the sink tapping metal every time the dock clamps took a strain and let it go. But since they'd hauled the groceries back aboard and counted the split at the table two nights ago, the spaces between the sounds had stretched out. They stood up taller. The ship felt less like a room and more like one of those old Mars wells with the water gone out of them, deep and lined in rust and not meant for children.

Ed sat cross-legged on the common-room floor in the middle of a drift of printouts and thermal-paper curls, bare heel pressed against the warm housing of the main console. A map of Mars turned on the central screen in slow red bands. Little windows opened and closed over it like blinking fish: a licensing board, a settlement clinic ledger, a dead informant's code tree that should not still have had live branches, docking manifests, fuel purchases, work tickets, a school absence roll from a settlement that had since caved in and been bulldozed flat.

Ein lay half under the table with his chin on Ed's ankle, one eye open. Every time the printer spit out another strip, one of his ears flicked back in annoyance and then settled again.

Ed had not slept much. That part was ordinary. The ordinary part ended around dawn, when three names that had been orbiting one another for days finally stopped moving and locked together hard enough to hurt.

I found her, Ed had told the empty room first, just to hear if it sounded true.

It had.

Now the problem was the grown-ups.

Jet came in first because Jet always came in first when the ship made a noise it wasn't supposed to make, and the printer had been whining off and on for an hour. He still had yesterday's stubble on yesterday's face and a gray work shirt with one sleeve rolled and the other hanging right because he never rolled the prosthetic side unless he needed the joints clear. He took two steps into the room, stopped short at the paper on the floor, and squinted at Ed.

"If you've printed the net again, I'm charging you for it."

Ed looked up. Jet was aiming for normal. He missed by a mile.

"I found her name," Ed said.

Jet did not move for half a second. Then he set his mug down on the table too carefully. "You found the pilot."

"Mm-hm."

Spike spoke from the couch without lifting his head. "You could've started with that."

Ed had known Spike was awake. Spike had the wrong stillness for sleep. He lay on his back with one ankle over the other, one arm flung over his eyes, hair sticking every direction except a useful one. The cigarette at the corner of his mouth was unlit. He had probably gone horizontal an hour ago and never closed either eye, the real one or the other one.

Faye came in from the hallway a moment later, drawn by voices or instinct or the fact that nothing private stayed private on the Bebop for long. She had Jet's old sweatshirt thrown over her usual clothes, sleeves hanging past her hands, and the look on her face said she was prepared to be irritated before anybody had given her a reason. She took in the paper on the floor, the red map on the screen, Jet standing rigid by the table, Spike not bothering to pretend anymore, and her expression changed by one degree.

"What now."

Ed patted the floor in front of the main screen. "Come see. Ed made the messy parts hold still."

The floor stayed Ed's territory. That was also ordinary. Jet dragged a chair around backward and straddled it, forearms across the backrest. Faye leaned against the counter and crossed her arms inside the sweatshirt. Spike sat up and let his feet find the deck without any hurry in them. Ein got up, circled once around Ed's pile, and resettled with a grunt.

Ed turned back to the screen. It felt strange to run the line straight through. Usually the fun part was the bounce, the boop-boop between points, the part where the others had to keep up or drown. This one needed edges. This one needed doors with labels on them.

Ed tapped the screen. A license photo filled the left side.

A young woman stared out of the license photo. Her dark hair was pulled back so hard it changed the shape of her brow. Her coveralls were zipped to the sternum, and the collar was stained darker where someone had wiped grease with the back of a wrist and only moved it around. She had a sharp face and a tired mouth. The form below the photo read KOZUKI, REN above Independent mechanic certification, Mars Free Trades Board. She was twenty-two.

The thumbprint box had been rejected once. TOO SMUDGED, a clerk had stamped over it in pale red. On the second scan there was still a dark crescent of sealant lodged under the thumbnail.

"Ren Kozuki," Ed said. "Licensed mechanic. Paid-up smallcraft rating. Three lapsed docking permits, one settlement fine for doing an unauthorized engine swap in a public lane, which is funny but also stupid. She uses her real name on work orders when she wants to get paid and no name when she doesn't."

Jet's gaze stayed on the photo. "Kozuki."

There it was. The word had weight now. Before, it had been a code attached to a dead informant in an archive. Now it had a face under it and sealant under one nail.

Ed nodded and brought up the next pane. "Daichi Kozuki. That's the father. ISSP confidential informant classification buried under three seal layers and one lazy dead folder. Not dead enough, though. The access tree still twitched when Ren kicked it."

Jet leaned forward. The movement was small, but the chair squeaked under him.

On the screen, Daichi's file was mostly blocks of black, whole paragraphs drowned in redaction bars. But the precinct stamp in the upper corner showed through. So did the district number. So did the date on the deactivation notice. Jet's gaze caught on the stamp and stayed there.

"That's my precinct," he said.

The room stayed on the precinct stamp in the upper corner. Gray copy toner made the proof ugly and flat and impossible to argue with.

Ed moved the cursor lower. "The bounty on Hosuke Takara came back because somebody filed a sighting report through Daichi Kozuki's old code path. Not forged. Not hacked from the outside. Just... used. The branch never got cut off all the way."

Jet's prosthetic fingers tightened on the chair back until the joint servos gave a faint insect whine. "I know that part."

"Ed knows. The part you didn't know is this part." Ed expanded the access history. Dates climbed down the screen. "Ren didn't wake the code up to collect the bounty. She used it to get the bounty in front of us. The report was filed two days before the Bebop came home. She timed it with our docking pattern. That's premeditated sneaky."

Spike reached out at last and held his hand out wordlessly for the thermal copy nearest Ed's knee. Ed passed it over. Spike looked at the timestamps once, then set the sheet on the table without comment. His face did not change. His shoulders did, barely. They went stiller.

Faye uncrossed one arm enough to point at the screen. "So she's Daichi Kozuki's daughter. Fine. That gets her Jet's file. It doesn't get her the rest."

"No," Ed said. "The rest is the mother."

He put Ayane on the screen.

There was no license photo for Ayane Kozuki. What Ed had was uglier and more intimate: a surgical intake record from a Mars settlement clinic, decades-old formatting preserved inside a newer records shell because clinics never threw data away if they could bill for it later. AYANE KOZUKI first. Then, farther down the chain and much more often, FAYE VALENTINE.

The intake image was grainy. Not a portrait, just the freeze-frame from an admissions cam: a woman turning away from somebody out of frame, one shoulder bare where the gown had slipped. On the skin over the left shoulder blade was a pale older scar, the shape wrong for anything clean.

Faye's hand went to her own shoulder. She caught the motion halfway and dropped it.

Ed kept going because stopping would only make the room worse.

"Ayane Kozuki. Later alias Faye Valentine. Mars clinic use, transit use, cash work, gambling use at least once on Ganymede because Miriam isn't making that up. The biometric authorization doesn't start right away. It starts three years after the real Faye got frozen and it stays active for eleven years. Same identity sale window the records office showed. Same medical profile. Same debt shell, except she was using it for treatment instead of bills."

Faye stared at the name on the chart as if hard enough looking might make the letters admit they belonged to somebody else. "She got care under my file."

"Some care," Ed said. "Not enough care. Here."

The next documents were refill histories. Rows of prescription authorizations had once been regular enough to make a rhythm, landing every twenty-eight days until the gaps opened: thirty-six days, then forty-one, then fifty-three. One emergency partial fill showed up in a clinic out by the Hellas freight spine. Then nothing came for seventy-two days.

Spike lifted his head a little more. That was all.

Ed did not look at him yet. "Immunosuppressants. Anti-rejection drugs. Same package family as the manifest in the locker. Same shipment code cluster. Same clinic endpoint."

Jet turned to Spike. It was not accusation. Not yet. More like the swivel of a searchlight.

Spike rubbed the bridge of his nose with one finger. "Yeah."

The single syllable sat there. Jet's prosthetic thumb stopped on the chair seam. Faye's finger hovered over the screen a second too long. Even Ed waited a beat before opening the next file. That was new.

Ed opened the clinic notes because if they were going to know, then they were going to know all the way.

The room got smaller around the screen.

Patient reports missed doses due to supply interruption.

Patient arrived with edema in ankles and hands.

Vomited twice in triage sink.

Requested lights off because of headache.

Asked whether daughter had eaten.

The note about the daughter was in a different hand, less tidy than the rest, squeezed into the margin beside a billing code. Somebody in that clinic had thought it mattered enough to write down.

Faye looked away first. Not because of the bloodwork numbers or the rejection markers or the neat thin cruelty of the phrase supply interruption. It was the line in the margin. Ed could tell.

"Jesus," Jet said quietly.

Spike did not say anything.

Ed zoomed once, just enough to bring up the final page. The alias sat at the top in clean machine type. FAYE VALENTINE. Beneath it the discharge field had not been used. The death certificate had.

Faye laughed once. No humor in it at all. "So she died as me."

Ed made himself say it plain. "She died under your name."

The sweatshirt sleeve had ridden down Faye's wrist. She shoved it back with more force than the fabric required. "That's disgusting."

"Yeah," Jet said.

"No, not just her. All of it." Faye jabbed a finger toward the screen and missed by an inch. "A clerk sells a frozen girl's file, a clinic runs with it because the pills clear, some woman spends eleven years pretending to be me, and then she dies with it on a bracelet because that's the part the system knows how to read. That's disgusting."

Faye's words stayed in the room with the chart glow on them. Jet's jaw worked once. Spike looked back at the refill gaps.

Ed took a breath and brought up the last cluster. This was the part the adults had not yet learned how to look at. They kept seeing the chase, the forged signals, the baited spot behind Mama Yun's. They kept seeing a pilot. Ed had spent the night inside smaller records. Kids left traces everywhere if nobody bothered to clean them properly.

The big screen filled with address changes, settlement schools, dockside food-assistance lists, apprenticeship rejections, one approved work permit, a mechanic-yard invoice paid partly in cash and partly in scrap copper, and a foster-network intake scan so old the corners had gone fuzzy.

Ren Kozuki, age 9.

Personal effects: one shirt, one pair boots, one cough-drop tin containing sorted bolts and screws. Child became upset when staff attempted to discard tin.

Jet made a sound in his throat and stopped it.

Ed touched the next note.

Child took apart desk fan in sleeping room using spoon handle. Reassembled incorrectly but improved motor noise.

On another line, years later, one of Ren's work chits from a bay outside Tharsis: CUSTOMER REPORTS "GHOST SOUND" FROM PORT THRUSTER. R. KOZUKI NOTE: NOT A GHOST. BEARING.

That one almost looked like a joke. It probably had been, or as close as Ren got.

"She moved a lot," Ed said. "Seven addresses in nine years that can be found and probably more that can't. Informal foster placements, settlement crash couches, yard bunks, work bays. She started taking mechanic jobs early because foster networks like kids better when kids bring money in."

Jet looked at the nine on the intake form, then at the twenty-two on the license photo. He did not blink enough.

"How old was she when Ayane died?" he asked.

Ed put the death certificate and the intake scan side by side.

The math did not need doing. It sat right there in two boxes.

"Nine."

Spike swore under his breath, soft enough that the word almost dissolved into the ship noise.

Ed finally turned to look at all of them instead of the screen.

"Ren grew up in the same mess she's been showing us," Ed said. The words came out cleaner than Ed's words usually did. Not prettier. Just cleaner. "Daichi used your precinct, Jet. Ayane used Spike's routes and Faye's name. Ren inherited all three and then went digging. That's the web. That's why the bounty. That's why the signal. That's why the records. It wasn't random-random. It was built."

Faye dragged a hand over her face. "Built for what."

Ed thought about the parking spot behind Mama Yun's. Six months had been paid in cash. A ship had sat dark and waiting exactly where Jet would feel it, where all of them would feel it. It had the feel of a note left on the galley table under Jet's coffee mug so everyone had to see it. It felt petty, sad, patient.

"To make us look," Ed said.

Spike's eyes lifted to Ed's then, full on for the first time all morning.

"You sure about that?"

Ed looked back at the screen. At the note about the cough-drop tin. At the thumbprint stamped TOO SMUDGED. At the line in the clinic chart where Ayane had asked whether her daughter had eaten. At the old orphanage file Ed had found last week with age 7 beside Ed's own name, the way a system could flatten a life to a line item and still hold enough of it to wound.

"If Ren wanted us dead," Ed said, "the parking plan was very slow."

Spike looked down again. "Yeah."

Jet stood up so abruptly the chair legs scraped hard across the deck. For a second Ed thought he was going to start issuing orders because that was Jet's favorite way to keep from bleeding in public. Instead he walked to the screen, picked up the printout with Daichi's precinct stamp, and read it standing there like he had forgotten chairs existed.

Faye pushed off the counter and came closer too. Not near Jet. Near the clinic notes. She did not touch them. The color had worn off the middle of her lower lip where she'd been worrying it with her teeth.

"Did she know?" she asked.

Ed knew who she meant. "Ayane?"

"Ren."

"The whole whole thing? Not when she was little. The records start getting organized later. But by eighteen? Yeah. Maybe before."

Faye nodded once, like she was signing for a package she didn't want.

Jet kept reading. "There's no relocation completion. There should've been a relocation completion. If he had a kid and a wife there should've been..." He stopped. Put the page down. Picked it up again. "Christ."

Spike's cigarette was still unlit between his fingers. He rolled it once over his knuckles. "Routes collapsed after I left," he said to the room, the floor, nobody. "A lot of things did."

The printer fan whined soft and the unlit cigarette turned once between Spike's fingers. The words stayed where they'd landed. It didn't need answering yet.

The printer, which had been quiet for nearly five minutes, gave a chirp and started spitting again. It kicked out another work order, another address, another little life detail too boring for anybody but Ed to love. Ein stood, sneezed at the noise, and crossed the room to put his chin on Ed's bare foot.

Jet was still staring at Daichi's stamp. Faye was still staring at the name on the death certificate that had once been hers and wasn't, then was again. Spike sat with the unlit cigarette and looked like he had forgotten what it was for.

The ship sounded small now. Or maybe it only sounded full.

The paper kept their hands busy instead. Jet rubbed one corner of the precinct printout soft with his thumb. Faye kept her eyes on the death certificate. Spike turned the dead cigarette once between two fingers and didn't lift it.

Jet finally looked away from the paper. "I'm making food," he said, which was not a conclusion but was the only thing in the room he knew how to do next.

He carried the Daichi printout with him into the galley.

***

Jet cut the cabbage too thin the first time and had to go slower with the second half.

That annoyed him more than it should have, which meant he was exactly as rattled as he felt. He had bought one green cabbage, a bundle of scallions, a sack of cheap noodles, six eggs, and the smallest package of pork scraps in the freezer bin because thirty thousand woolongs turned into a lot less than thirty thousand after the ticket and fuel and the little humiliations that came attached to being broke in public. The pork looked like offcuts shaved from the edges of something a richer person got to buy whole. He browned it anyway. Fat still rendered from scraps.

The galley warmed fast. Oil popped in the pan. Steam filmed the nearest porthole. Someone had left the bag of bean sprouts too close to the stove and the plastic had softened at one corner. Jet moved it with the back of his wrist and set the pot to boil.

Usually this part brought the crew back to themselves. Somebody complained about the smell, or stole something from the cutting board, or asked how much of an ingredient they were allowed to eat before Jet called it sabotage. Usually Jet met them halfway because the complaining was how the room got blood moving again.

Tonight they came together without the usual static.

Spike took the chair by the table and stayed there. That was the first sign. He should have drifted to the couch or the deck or nowhere obvious. Instead he sat with one knee bent and a beer he had not opened and watched the pork take color in the pan like it was a job he had been assigned. The cigarette was gone. That was good.

Faye sat on the counter by the sink with her feet on the rung of a lower cabinet, one of Jet's chipped bowls upside down in her lap. She turned it around and around by the rim, not looking at anybody while still somehow staying in the middle of the room.

Ed had moved the papers into stacks only Ed understood and occupied the far end of the table with the screen turned so everyone could see the southern stretch of Mars. Dry Well sat there now in a blinking box, little more than an old bay designation and a cluster of dead infrastructure tags. Ein stationed himself under the table in his most political position, centered where crumbs were likely and emotions might fall through.

Jet slid the cabbage into the pan. It hissed and shrank at once.

"Don't feed him onion," Faye said after a while.

Jet glanced over. "I know not to feed him onion."

"I'm just saying."

"You don't have to say every fact that enters your head."

She turned the bowl in her lap one more time. "Then we'd all have a much quieter life."

That would have been the place where the old pattern picked up, two more lines at least, Spike tossing in something lazy from the side, Ed making it worse on purpose or by accident. The bowl kept turning in Faye's lap for half a turn more, then stopped. Steam from the pan filled the space where the next jab should've gone. The old pattern missed its cue and stayed missed.

Jet stirred the pork, threw in the white parts of the scallions, and said, "Ed. Show me where she is."

Ed rotated the small monitor on the table and crawled under one elbow to point at it. "Last live transponder echo here-here." A finger tapped south, farther down the basin. "Then she went dark because Ren isn't dumb. But Dry Well ate some maintenance orders under her mechanic number six months ago, and an old fuel account tied to one of Ayane's settlement clinics bought two filters at a supply yard twelve days back, and Spike knows this place."

Jet looked at Spike.

Spike lifted a shoulder. "Waystation. Used to be syndicate freight country. One functioning bay if it's the same as it was. Wind all day. Solar panels cracked to hell." He paused. "Remote enough to stay missed."

Faye kept her eyes on the bowl. "So she's there."

"Probably," Ed said.

"Probably isn't there," Faye said.

Ed nodded agreeably. "Correct. But it's also maybe enough."

Jet dropped the noodles into the pot. They bent slowly, then all at once. "It's enough."

The noodles slipped under the broth. Faye only rolled the bowl rim under her thumbs, and Spike kept his eyes on the pot.

That unsettled him even more than the silence had.

He skimmed foam with a spoon and tried to line the facts up in a row where they could be handled: a dead informant in his precinct, a courier in Spike's routes, a woman dying under Faye's name, a kid with a cough-drop tin full of bolts growing into a mechanic who had spent years feeding them pieces of their own past until they finally had to look. Facts ought to have been the safe part. They were how he had once made a living. Tonight the facts felt like tools somebody else had put back in the wrong drawer.

"We can make Dry Well on what we've got," he said. "If nobody burns fuel being stupid."

That at least got him a glance from Spike.

"I wasn't planning on a dogfight."

"Good."

"Didn't say you were."

"You looked like you were about to."

Spike's mouth twitched without reaching humor. "Maybe that's just my face."

Faye snorted. It was small, but it cracked something loose in the air.

Jet ladled broth into the pot with the noodles, tasted it, added the packet of cheap soy that always tasted like metal first and beans second, then cracked the eggs one-handed while the other hand braced the bowl. The shells broke cleaner than his thoughts.

"We're not going down there blind," he said. "We talk this through first."

Faye's gaze came up. "Talk what through."

Jet scraped the shell fragments out of the pan with a thumb. "What we're doing when we get there."

"Looking at her, apparently."

"Faye."

"What? That's what this is." She set the bowl aside. "You heard Ed. She wanted us to look."

Jet turned the heat down and faced the room, towel in one hand. "Yeah. I did. That's why I want to know if we're going down there to drag answers out of a twenty-two-year-old mechanic or—" He jerked the towel toward the monitor, the paper stacks, the galley. "I keep calling this her problem. It's not her problem. Half that file is the building I worked in."

The burner hissed low. Faye pinched the bowl rim between thumb and forefinger. Spike studied the beer label without opening it.

Jet turned back to the stove before whatever he'd just said could settle into something he'd have to stand behind.

"If Daichi worked out of my precinct and got burned there," he said, "I don't get to put that in somebody else's pile. I wore a badge in that building. I knew how informants got used. I knew enough to know better, anyway."

The pork smell had deepened. It was good pork, or maybe it was only real pork and that counted for good enough. Jet dropped the green tops of the scallions in last so they stayed bright. He kept his eyes on the pan.

"We told ourselves they chose it," he said. "Sometimes maybe they did. Sometimes they needed money. Sometimes they needed a door opened, or a warrant buried, or somebody to leave their brother alone for a week. We called it cooperation because that made the paperwork read nice. If Daichi was in the building, then he was somebody's problem to keep alive. Mine too. Doesn't matter if his name never crossed my desk."

Spike said, "It matters some."

Jet looked over his shoulder. "Not enough."

Spike tipped his chair back on two legs, let it hang there, then set it down again. "No," he said. "Not enough."

It had no joke wrapped around it and no shrug to soften it.

Jet gave him a second and then another. Spike would talk or he wouldn't.

He did.

"Clinics bought through syndicate routes all the time," Spike said, eyes on the unopened beer. "Not openly. Stuff got folded into other manifests. You'd see a clinic code on freight you weren't supposed to look at and you'd sign it through." His thumb ran along the edge of the label. "That was it."

Faye's fingers stopped on the rim of the bowl.

Spike went on because stopping now would have been worse. "When the routes broke, they broke ugly. People stopped getting paid. Warehouses got torched. Small cargo disappeared first. Things nobody shot over because they were too busy shooting over the big stuff." He lifted the beer at last, looked at it, put it back down unopened. "So yeah. If those meds were coming through my old lines, then they stopped coming through them too."

Jet asked, "You knew?"

"I knew enough." Spike stared at the table. "I didn't know her. I didn't know Ayane. I knew the manifests had clinic codes and I never looked at what was under them." He paused. "Didn't want to."

The galley was quiet except for the pan cooling on the burner, metal ticking as it contracted.

Faye slid off the counter. Her bare feet touched the deck without sound. She took two steps toward the stove, stopped short, glanced at Jet, and stayed by the end of the table instead.

"I keep trying to decide how mad I'm allowed to be at a woman who stole my name," she said.

Jet said nothing.

"No, that's not right." She pressed the heel of one hand to her forehead, annoyed with herself for having started soft. "I'm mad. That's not the problem. The problem is every time I get a nice clean version of it going in my head, I see some clerk calling out Faye Valentine in a clinic and if Ayane doesn't answer, she doesn't get the pills. Kind of ruins the simple part."

Ed watched her without moving.

Faye laughed again, quieter this time. "And now all I can think about is some bracelet on a dead wrist with my stupid name on it. That's sick. That's really sick."

Jet turned off the flame under the pan. The quiet after the burner died felt immediate and raw.

"Yeah," he said.

"Don't 'yeah' me like that."

"I'm agreeing with you."

"I know." She folded her arms tight. "I'm just saying don't do it like you're handling evidence."

That was fair enough that Jet let it stand.

Ed had been quiet for too long, which on Ed was the same as an alarm. Jet glanced over.

Ed sat with hands flat on the table. "Ren knows the boring parts too," Ed said. "Not just the dead parts. Work tickets, bad addresses, school slips, the cough-drop tin, all the ordinary pieces. That's how Ed knows she wasn't just building a case against the crew. She kept the little things."

Jet looked at the monitor again. The work order note still sat there: NOT A GHOST. BEARING.

Faye saw it too. "She writes like an asshole mechanic."

"Yeah," Spike said, and this time there was the faintest trace of dry in it. "That's pretty convincing."

That got a real laugh out of Faye, one quick breath of it. She rubbed her thumb along her collarbone afterward as if embarrassed to have made the sound.

Jet started filling bowls because the food would turn to paste if he kept talking at it. He made one bowl for Ed, heaviest on noodles; one for Faye, with less cabbage because she always claimed she hated cabbage and always ate it anyway; one for Spike, who would complain there wasn't enough meat no matter how much meat there was; and one for himself. He slid a fifth empty saucer to the floor for Ein after fishing the onions back out of a spoonful by hand.

He handed out bowls into a room that accepted them with both hands. Chopsticks clicked against ceramic. Steam lifted across their faces. Faye took the cabbage without complaint. It should have felt unnatural. Instead it felt like a room listening to itself.

They ate.

The first minute was all chopsticks, broth, and dog snuffling at the saucer. Steam dampened their faces, and the table stayed warm under their forearms. Jet had not realized how hungry he was until the food hit his mouth and his body leaned into it harder than his pride liked.

Faye broke the silence with, "If we go down there and she tries to give us another scavenger hunt, I'm leaving."

Jet swallowed. "Fair."

"I'm serious."

"I know."

Spike twirled noodles around his chopsticks and said, "Don't think she will."

Faye looked over. "Why."

Spike shrugged. "Too much work to get us this far. If she wanted one more puzzle, she'd have kept hiding."

Ed nodded with his mouth full and then remembered manners only after the fact. "Mm-hm."

Jet set his bowl down. There it was, the part that had been circling since he'd said talk this through.

"When we find her," he said, "we're not going to confront her."

The word sounded wrong the second it was out. Too much cop in it. Too much suspect across a table.

Faye heard it too. "Good."

Jet corrected course before pride could get in the way. "No. That's not... We go there and we listen first. Ask after. Understand what she wants from us, if anything. But we listen first."

The pause sat there with broth steam between them. Faye looked down into her bowl. Spike kept eating. Jet let it sit. He'd said it badly, but he'd said it.

Spike ate a little more, then wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. "Works for me."

Faye stared into her bowl. "I don't know what I'd even say to her."

"You don't have to know now," Jet said.

"That's helpful."

"It's true."

She took another bite, chewed it, and said, "I know."

Ed spun the monitor a few degrees so everyone could see the southern basin line. "Dry Well has one good bay and ugly crosswinds. Ren probably parked nose-out because the old bay doors stick on the west track. Also the south panel field still gives about sixty-two percent if you kick relay three." Ed tapped the map. "Ed can get us there."

Jet blinked at the sudden practical turn and was grateful for it. "How do you know about the relay."

"Repair ticket."

"Of course."

"Ren charged too little for it," Ed added. "Thirty percent under fair market unless the customer supplied parts. That is bad business."

Spike's bowl paused halfway to his mouth. "She built that ship on mechanic pay."

"And side work," Ed said. "And patience patience patience."

Jet leaned back and felt the chair complain under him. He could hear the rest of it already before it happened: fuel checks, route calculations, weather over the basin, whether the port stabilizer was going to keep pretending it wasn't due for service. Practical things. Things with bolts and numbers and finite failure points. Relief moved through him so fast it almost embarrassed him.

He looked around the table.

Spike was still here.

Faye was still here.

Ed had broth on one wrist and did not seem to care.

Ein licked the saucer clean and then looked up as if prepared to negotiate for jurisdiction over any dropped pork.

Jet said, "We leave in the morning."

Spike glanced at the dark porthole. "Could leave tonight."

"No."

"Why not."

"Because you look like hell, Ed's been awake all night, and I'd like one trip this week where we're not making decisions because we're rattled."

Spike opened his mouth, shut it, and gave a short nod. "Morning, then."

Another small thing, but it landed.

Faye pushed cabbage around in the bottom of her bowl. "If she says she did all this just to make us feel bad, I'm walking back to Mars."

"That's a long walk," Spike said.

"Then I'll steal a ship."

"There she is," Jet muttered.

Faye pointed a chopstick at him. "Don't start sounding relieved. It's weird."

Jet looked down at his bowl so they wouldn't see it too clearly. "Eat your noodles."

She did.

For a while that was all any of them did. They ate, reached for the soy, and passed it along. Faye took one of Spike's pork pieces without asking and Spike, after noticing, chose not to dignify it. Ed slurped too loud. Jet told Ed to slow down. Ed slowed down for maybe twelve seconds. The ship ticked and breathed around them. Outside the porthole, the Mars dock lights smeared in the thin night haze.

Daichi's precinct stamp sat by Jet's elbow with a wet ring from his bowl inching toward it. Ayane's death certificate was still half under Faye's bowl, with one corner gone limp from the damp. Dry Well kept blinking on Ed's little screen.
Spike asked for the soy without looking up, and Faye handed it over before he had to reach.
Ed fished the last pork scrap out of the pot. Jet saw it and let it go.
The dock clamps clicked once out in the berth. Then again. Nobody stood up.

Jet got up first when he was done and carried his bowl to the sink. He waited for chair legs to scrape and doors to open behind him. He ran water, set the bowl down, and reached for the sponge.

When he glanced back over his shoulder, water ran over his knuckles and the chairs were still occupied.

Spike sat with one arm thrown over the chair back, looking at the Dry Well marker on the little screen as if he could memorize the route by force. Faye had tucked one foot up under her on the chair and was rereading the work ticket with the ghost-sound note, expression impossible to read but not closed. Ed was feeding coordinates into a nav string and humming under the breath instead of over it. Ein had rolled onto his side directly in the walkway, certain the universe would route itself around him.

Jet rinsed one bowl, then another.

"Hey," Faye said after a while, not looking up from the ticket.

"What."

"The noodles are good."

Jet scrubbed at a spot that wasn't there. "They're noodles."

"Yeah, well. They're good noodles."

He could have made a thing out of that. Usually he would have. Tonight he just said, "There are more in the pot if anybody wants them."

Spike stood, took his own bowl to the sink, and set it down beside Jet's instead of on some random flat surface for Jet to find later. "I do."

Jet handed him the ladle without comment.

That, more than anything else, was how the trip south got decided.
