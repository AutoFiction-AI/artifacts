# Chapter 2

The Bebop had a stretch of time that was not night anymore and not yet morning either, a slack ugly hour when the pipes ticked in the walls, the air recycler breathed dust-dry air through old filters, and Jet's first pot of coffee always smelled a little burned because the burner ran hot on the left side. Ed liked that hour. Adults were slow in it. Systems were not.

Ed had been awake through most of it, or maybe all of it. Sleep had happened at some point on the couch with Ein against Ed's ribs, but the second hello under the dock ping had kept making a scratchy little noise in Ed's head, so Ed had rolled off the cushion before the lights fully changed and crawled back to the main console. The dark ship in their docking spot was still clean on the public registries. The registration was clean. The tax records were clean. The maintenance history was clean. It was too clean, as if somebody had taken a rag to the fingerprints.

Ed liked fingerprints.

Three archive windows hung over the main screen in layers: bounty board, old freight liens, and a deep crawler that smelled in Ed's mind like old cardboard and forgotten passwords. The same three-knock handshake pattern was still there under the public ID, a tiny wrong rhythm buried in the outgoing ping. Anybody digging deep could hear it plain enough. The code was old enough that the modern systems treated it like dust.

Ed loved dust too.

"Aha-ha," Ed said softly to the console. "Crunchy little signal biscuit. Ed bites, biscuit breaks, inside is another biscuit."

Ein lifted his head from the floor, watched Ed for a moment, then put his chin back down.

Behind Ed, the ship went through its usual half-dead dawn routine. Jet banged a cupboard door harder than necessary. Water ran in the galley sink. Something hissed in a pan. Faye, who had slept on the couch without meaning to and would deny it if asked, dragged the blanket over her face and made a noise that meant everybody should die quietly. Spike sat at the table with one ankle over one knee, cigarette unlit between his fingers because Jet had bitched about smoke before coffee again. He looked as if he had been awake for hours or for three minutes. With Spike it was always hard to tell.

Ed found the record on the fourth cross-reference.

It surfaced as a stale old line item with the edges frayed by time, sitting in a government archive that had not expected to be loved this hard. No warning stamp showed. No alarm sounded.

ARCHIVED. DORMANT. CLAIM STATUS EXPIRED.

SUBJECT: TAKARA, HOSUKE.

OFFENSE CLASSIFICATION: PETTY LOGISTICS FRAUD.

The date was old enough to have dust on it. The reward was a joke. The attached description was not.

Ed sat up so fast the chair rolled back and hit the bulkhead.

"Jet-person!" Ed shouted.

Faye groaned under the blanket. "If this is another weird plate of numbers, I'm killing you."

"This is a weird plate of numbers with a man on top. Very important garnish."

Jet did not turn right away. He had the coffee pot in his right hand and half an eggshell in the other, big shoulders filling the galley space. Burnt grounds ringed the hot plate under the burner. "If it doesn't pay, it can wait."

"It pays in mystery money."

"Then it definitely can wait."

Ed was already skittering barefoot across the deck, one hand on the edge of the console so the screen followed. "No waiting. Waiting makes the data sad."

Spike glanced over before anyone else did. His eyes moved once across the page, then came back slower. "What's Hosuke Takara?"

Jet turned then.

Ed saw the change before the adults started doing adult things to cover it up. The room altered. Sound tightened around the screen. Jet stood with the coffee pot in his right hand and the eggshell crushed into the other palm and did not seem to feel either one. Faye pushed the blanket down enough to squint at the screen. Spike stopped tapping ash from a cigarette he had not lit.

"Old sleepy bounty," Ed said brightly, because bright was still correct. "Dormant, archived, crunchy around the edges. Triggered on the ship's signal pattern when Ed dug deep enough. Look look look, the little description man has Jet-face."

Faye sat up. Her hair had gone flat on one side and mean on the other. "What?"

Ed turned the screen toward her with both hands. The file had a cheap age progression attached, some underfunded database clerk's best compromise between photo and sketch: young male, Mars-born, thick through the shoulders, dark beard shadow maybe and maybe not. The cheek was smooth where Jet's scar would be later. Both arms were flesh in the image. The mouth mattered, though, and the ears, and the heavy blunt shape of the brow. Time and trouble had changed the face, but not enough.

Faye blinked once, then let out one short laugh that died almost at once. "Oh, hell."

Jet set the coffee pot down with too much care. "Move it back."

Ed did not. "Jet-person had a secret name. Hosuke Hosuke Hosuke."

"Ed."

Jet did not raise his voice. That was what made Faye look at him properly.

She swung her legs off the couch. "No, hold on. Is that you?"

Jet wiped his hand on a towel that already had grease on it. A ribbon of egg white slid from his thumb to the floor. "It's an old file. You know how bad those archives are."

"That isn't an answer."

"It means the archive is bad."

Spike leaned back in his chair, eyes still on Jet instead of the screen. "Looks a little like you."

Jet's jaw moved once. "A lot of people look like me."

"That's unfortunate for them," Faye said. She stood and came closer, arms folded against the cold. "Hosuke? Seriously? That's what you came up with?"

"Who says I came up with anything?"

"The part where you look like the file says that."

Ed made the screen larger. "Also height variance within acceptable range, ear geometry match probable, shoulder width match probable, facial ratio match probable. Eyebrow grumpiness identical."

"Eyebrow grumpiness isn't a metric," Faye said.

"Ed can make it one."

The eggs in the pan started to burn. Jet smelled them before anybody else did and still did not move. His face had gone fixed in a way Ed did not usually see. He did not look angry. Angry-Jet was loud and busy and came with hands. This was statue-Jet, the kind where all the sound happened around him and none of it got in.

Spike finally reached over and turned the burner off.

Jet looked at him like he had forgotten the pan existed.

Faye's voice lost some of its edge. The mockery drained out of it and left her watching him straight. "Jet."

"It's nothing," he said.

The lie sat in the galley with the smell of scorched egg.

Ed looked from one adult face to another. They all did this, the saying-one-thing-with-their-mouth and another-thing-with-their-necks and shoulders and the way they held objects too long. Ed was good at the second thing and bored by the first. "If it was nothing, the room wouldn't be making that room-noise."

Faye snorted. "The what?"

"The tight noise."

"That's not a real thing," Faye said.

"Sure it is," Spike said.

Jet took the console from Ed then, not rough but not gentle either, and stared down at the file. ARCHIVED. DORMANT. PETTY LOGISTICS FRAUD. The words were stupidly small for how hard they hit. He had not seen that name in years. He had not heard it spoken out loud in longer than that. He had handed over more of his life than he liked when he joined ISSP, because they wanted everything, every juvenile charge that never stuck, every alias, every address, every stupid trick a seventeen-year-old thought was clever. He had signed forms. Sat in cold offices. Told the truth where it could be checked and lied where it could not. Then he had spent years pretending those papers had gone into a hole and stayed there.

Nobody on the Bebop had any business knowing the name on that screen.

Nobody off the Bebop should have been able to pull enough detail to pin the file to him either, not now. Not after all this time.

Jet closed the archive window. Ed made an offended sound from deep in the chest.

"Hey."

"Enough," Jet said.

"You can't close mystery."

"Watch me."

Faye planted a hand on the table. "You don't get to do that. Not after yesterday, not after some creep parks in our spot and your—"

"Here's what happens." Jet's voice came up enough to fill the galley. He looked at Ed, not Faye. "File stays open. You watch. Passive only — don't send anything, don't ping anybody, and don't scratch the outside of any system that might scratch back. Anybody on this ship reaches past local on this, I pull the antenna myself."

Faye's mouth stayed open for a beat, then closed.

Jet exhaled through his nose. He could feel Spike watching him and hated that more than Faye's needling, because Spike watched quiet. Faye liked noise. Noise was workable. Quiet meant the other man was filing things away.

Ed hugged the console to a narrow chest. "Ed is always passive. Like a very attentive rock."

"You know what I mean."

"Yes." A beat. "Jet-person knows Hosuke."

Jet bent to pick up the towel, the dropped shell, anything that let him put his face somewhere else. "Finish your sleep, kid."

Faye didn't push again. Not because she was done. Because Jet had skipped the part where he denied it and gone straight to building a perimeter, and the perimeter told her more than the denial would have.

He moved around them because movement was easier than staying put. He dumped the ruined eggs in the recycler, rinsed the pan, and started over. Practical work went better than standing there. The burner clicked. Oil shimmered. His cybernetic fingertips sorted the cracked shell from the rest of the trash with mechanical precision that felt a little obscene under the circumstances. He knew the shape of his own evasion while he did it and kept doing it anyway.

Behind him Faye muttered, "This ship is starting to feel crowded," which was not really about bodies.

Spike said nothing for a moment. Then, casual as dry toast: "You want me to take another look at the signal?"

Jet kept his back turned. "Do whatever you want."

"He will," Faye said.

Ed, who had already gone back to the console, announced to nobody in particular, "Hosuke Takara liked freight labels and did not like tax compliance."

"Shocking," Faye said.

Jet cracked another egg too hard. Yolk ran between his fingers, warm and slick, and for one stupid second it felt like being seventeen again, standing in a freight office with forged manifests in his jacket and no plan past the next hour. He rinsed his hand until the yellow was gone.

When he looked up, Spike was still in his chair, but the laziness had thinned out of him. He had that distant, listening look he got around trouble, as if somewhere a floorboard had creaked and only he had heard it.

Jet knew that look. He knew it almost as well as he knew his own.

That was worse than the file.

No one said much while the second round of eggs cooked. Faye lit a cigarette anyway and ignored Jet's glare until he shoved an ashtray toward her. Ed hummed to the database, making little corrections under breath as the crawler walked deeper. Spike stood, finally, and wandered toward the corridor with one hand in his pocket.

"Where are you going?" Jet asked before he could stop himself.

Spike half-turned. "To look at the signal."

"With what?"

"A receiver."

"That's specific."

"You'll live."

Faye looked from one to the other. "Everybody's being weird."

"You're weird all the time," Spike said.

"Yeah, and now you're catching up."

He left before she could throw anything.

Jet plated the eggs nobody wanted very much and told himself that if Spike had found something, Spike would say it. That was not true. It had never been true. The only reason the ship worked at all was that they all respected a line none of them ever named. You could share meals, fuel, bruises, bad luck. You did not put your hands in the locked drawer somebody kept in the back of their head.

This morning the drawers had started opening by themselves.

* * *

By midday Jet had a screwdriver in his hand and no idea how long he had been staring at the same junction box.

The latch on the under-sink panel in the galley had been sticking for weeks. It did not need him now. Plenty of things on the Bebop needed him now. The starboard water recycler gave a cough every ten minutes. The short-range radar had a dead strip on the left side. The shower drain in Faye's bathroom backed up if she used too much conditioner, which she denied even while the clogs came out dark with it. Any of those would have been honest work. Instead he had picked the latch because it was close and small and would fit under his hands.

The screws sat in a neat line on the counter by size. The habit embarrassed him a little. So did the fact that he had done it anyway.

Through the open galley he could hear Ed talking to the ship's computer in three registers at once and getting just enough useful output back to count as conversation. Faye had gone out after complaining there was nothing edible on board except eggs and she was not interested in another egg in her life. Good. One less person in the room. The Bebop smelled like hot metal, stale pepper oil, and the cold broth Jet had splashed on his sleeve at breakfast.

Outside the nearest porthole, Mars light came in wrong-colored and thin. Beyond the paid docking lot, two blocks over, their real spot sat behind Mama Yun's with that dark sleek ship parked in it like an insult that had learned manners.

Jet pushed the screwdriver into the latch plate harder than he meant to and stripped the edge of the screw head.

"Nice work," he muttered.

The answer came from behind him.

"You talking to the latch or yourself?"

Spike stood in the galley doorway with a portable receiver tucked under one arm and the Bebop's old maintenance headset dangling from one hand. He looked exactly like he always looked: suit hanging wrong because he'd slept in it, hair doing whatever it wanted, face unreadable unless you knew him. Jet knew him. That was the problem.

"Both," Jet said. "What'd you rip that receiver out of?"

"Storage."

"Storage isn't a system."

"Depends how you wire it."

Jet set the screwdriver down. "You need something from me?"

"No."

"Then why are you hovering?"

Spike lifted the headset a little. "Making conversation."

"Don't start now."

For half a second Spike almost smiled. Then it was gone. "Touchy."

Jet wiped his prosthetic hand on a rag though it was clean. "You got somewhere private to do that, or are you going to let the whole ship listen?"

"Swordfish bay."

"Fine."

Spike moved on without haste, but not with his usual drag either. The difference was small. Jet caught it anyway. The receiver's loose cable knocked once against the bulkhead as Spike disappeared down the corridor.

Jet looked at the stripped screw, at the open panel, and then toward the hall.

He picked up the screwdriver, put it down, and followed.

He did not go all the way. He stopped at the mouth of the hangar passage where he could see without being seen unless Spike turned around. The bay lights were dimmed to save power. Swordfish II sat in the cradle with its nose angled toward the launch doors, all clean lines and old violence. Spike climbed onto the access ladder, set the receiver on the rim of the cockpit, and plugged into the ship's comm package with the ease of somebody who had done a lot of illegal things on very little sleep.

Jet should have walked away then. He knew it. Instead he leaned a shoulder against the cold wall and listened to static bleed faintly from the headset speakers.

Spike tuned by hand.

Ed worked like a net diver, all at once, throwing hooks into twelve systems and seeing what bit. Spike worked like a man cracking a safe in a building where people had died, with slow turns and patience. He stripped the public ID signal away first, then the registry wrapper, then the ordinary maintenance handshake under that. What came through next was thin and ugly, a pulse with too much space between beats.

To Jet it sounded like garbage.

To Spike it sounded like a dead man clearing his throat.

He went still on the ladder, one hand on the edge of the cockpit. The bay suddenly seemed colder. That old handshake belonged to a dead freight language: two short bursts, a hold, carrier wobble, route-auth riding under the public ping. He had spent years hearing versions of it through cheap earpieces in back offices above warehouses, in freight yards that smelled of oil and rat poison, in transit shuttles with false flooring and crates strapped down badly. Messages came wrapped in ordinary traffic, invisible if you did not know the rhythm. Cargo would go here. Escort would go there. Somebody would change the route. Somebody would burn the ledger. A whole supply chain had moved on sounds like this.

And tucked inside the handshake, ghosted in an old auth string no one should still be using, was the name he had used because it kept his real one off paper.

Lin Shao-Feng.

The name sat there as a scuff in the code, half-erased but legible enough, where the route key identified which set of hands the shipment belonged to.

He did not feel much when things got bad. That was one of the few dependable facts of his life. His pulse did not race. His breath did not catch. The world simply flattened out. Unnecessary details dropped away. The stupid little hangar on the Bebop became ladder, receiver, signal, door.

Someone had found an old syndicate logistics handshake, configured a ship to echo it on purpose, and parked that ship within arm's reach of him.

If any remnant node of the network was still listening, if any of the old scavengers or successor crews still ran watchers on dead frequencies, then the moment the Bebop had come within range their location had been handed to the past like a calling card.

Spike pulled the lead loose so fast the headset snapped with static.

The sound carried down the corridor. Jet straightened away from the wall before Spike turned.

He waited a beat, then stepped into the bay as if he had only just arrived. "You break it?"

Spike looked down from the ladder. His face had gone blank in a way Jet had only seen a handful of times. Blank on Spike was not calm. Blank was blood drained off the room.

"No," Spike said. "Just old hardware."

Jet let that sit there. "That's what it is?"

"Yeah."

It was a bad lie. Spike almost never told lies poorly. It was bad because Jet knew what Spike looked like when he had stubbed a toe, when he'd missed a shot, when he'd lost money, when he'd decided not to care. This was none of those faces.

Jet climbed halfway onto the ladder beside him and looked at the disconnected receiver. The tiny screen showed only static hash and a dead line where the data had been. Whatever Spike had seen, he had already killed it.

"You want to tell me something?" Jet asked.

Spike coiled the lead around his hand once, neatly. "Do you?"

There it was.

Jet could have answered. He could have said yes, there was an old bounty file with a dead name attached to it and somebody had reached into paperwork that should have stayed buried. He could have said he did not know who had done it and that was what scared him. He got as far as opening his mouth.

"There's a—" He stopped.

Spike waited.

"No," Jet said. "Forget it."

"You didn't say anything."

"Right. So forget it."

Spike pulled the headset cord taut between his hands, held it, let it go. "Kill the Bebop's outgoing ping."

Jet blinked. "What?"

"The maintenance handshake. The standard docking broadcast. Kill it."

"Every ship in the lot runs that. If I shut it off, port authority flags us inside a day."

"Tell them it's a malfunction. You're good at that."

Jet stared at him. Spike was not a man who gave orders on the Bebop. Spike barely gave suggestions. He drifted through rooms and let other people make decisions and then did whatever he'd planned to do anyway. This was different. This had edges.

"What did you hear?" Jet asked.

Spike tucked the receiver under his arm. "The ship out there is carrying something under the public signal. Old. Shouldn't still exist." He paused, and the pause was where the rest of the information lived. "If our ping is answering it on default, we're having a conversation we didn't start."

That was more than Spike volunteered about anything. Jet could feel the shape of what was missing — the part Spike was keeping behind his teeth — but the part he'd given was enough to make the bay feel smaller.

"Tell Ed too," Spike said. "Passive watch only. No crawling the outside of that ship's systems."

"You're not going to tell me what you heard."

"I told you what to do about it."

Jet almost laughed. It would have come out ugly, so he didn't bother. "Hell of a morning."

"It's still early."

He headed back toward the corridor. Jet turned enough to watch him go. Spike had a cigarette in his mouth now, but it stayed unlit all the way out of the bay.

That more than anything told Jet how bad it was.

He waited until Spike's steps faded, then looked at the cockpit rim where the receiver had sat. Bare metal. Spike hadn't left anything behind.

Mutual concealment was the cheap truce the Bebop ran on. Do not ask unless you want to answer. Do not pry unless you are ready to be pried open in return. It had worked because the past stayed out where it belonged. Today it had come aboard in pieces, and Jet could feel the whole ship listing around the weight of what nobody was saying.

He went back to the galley and spent twenty minutes fixing the wrong latch.

* * *

Faye told herself she left the Bebop because there was no decent food on it and because Jet, once he got spooked, started breathing responsibility into every room until the air went stale.

That was not the whole reason, but it was enough to put shoes on.

Mars cold hit through the thin fabric around her legs the minute she stepped off the gangway. The paid docking lot was a slab of patched concrete bordered by cargo fencing and one flickering light pole that made everybody look jaundiced. Dust had gathered in the seams of the pavement overnight. It always did. Red grit in the grooves, red grit in the cuffs, red grit on the toe of her boot by the third step. Two blocks away, behind Mama Yun's, the ship in their usual spot caught the amber daylight along one smooth flank and gave nothing back.

Faye slowed without meaning to.

Up close the thing annoyed her even more. It was too sleek for the neighborhood and too well kept to be honest. The hull lines had been modified by somebody who knew what they were doing. She saw no obvious registry decals and no streaks of burnt fuel around the aft vents. Somebody had wiped this ship down by hand. The Bebop wore its life on the outside. This one looked as if it had filed an appeal.

She walked a half circle around it and saw nothing through the dark canopy. There was no pilot and no movement, just her own reflection stretched thin and mean in the glass.

"Creep," she muttered to the ship, and went inside.

Mama Yun's noodle house held heat the way cheap places did, by never fully letting go of it. Steam filmed the windows. The fluorescent light above the counter buzzed like a trapped fly. The place smelled of broth, soy, pepper oil, old bleach, and wet coats. A lunch rush had not started yet, but the stools were warm from whoever had been there before. Somebody had scuffed a muddy boot print into the floor by the door and tracked it halfway to the register.

Mama Yun herself was nowhere in sight. A grease-pencil shift card clipped to the register had `YUN` scratched out and `MIRIAM - LUNCH` written hard over it. Behind the counter stood a woman Faye did not know, mid-fifties maybe, sleeves rolled to the elbow, hair pinned up with the sort of metal clip that looked as if it had outlived two marriages and a kitchen fire. The woman was already telling the cook, for what sounded like the third time, that Mama Yun's nephew had opened his palm on a prep slicer and she was only here until Yun got back from the clinic. She had tired eyes and the Ganymede roundness in the vowels when she called back an order. A squat salt shaker rode in the pocket of her apron and, before she even tasted the broth she'd poured for herself, she shook a white storm into it and muttered, "Mars people sweeten everything. Cowards."

That, Faye liked on sight.

The woman noticed her noticing and shrugged. "What? They do."

"You're working in the wrong place to say that out loud."

"I rotate. They can fire me from one shift, I'll irritate somebody else by dinner." She set a bowl down in front of an older man at the end stool and jerked her chin at Faye. "You want something or you just came in to judge the decor?"

Faye slid onto a stool. "Cheapest thing on the menu."

"That's noodles."

"Then noodles."

"You people from the ship all order like you're being insulted by existence."

"We are."

The woman snorted. "Fair enough. Service work gives you a memory for faces and cheap men. Same muscle, I think."

Faye leaned her elbows on the counter and tried not to think about Jet's face that morning, about Ed chanting Hosuke like a nursery rhyme, about Spike doing that awful quiet thing he did when his mind was somewhere he'd rather set on fire. She looked instead at the kitchen pass, at the old jar of pickled peppers, at the thin crack in the mirror behind the shelves where bottles of cheap liquor sat waiting for nighttime.

The waitress set down a bowl and the salt shaker in one move. "There. Burn your mouth and complain less."

Faye took the chopsticks. Steam hit her face. The broth smelled good enough to make irritation seem decorative.

The waitress had already turned away, then slowed and looked back with the stubborn concentration of somebody dragging a face up from old years.

"Hold on," she said.

Faye looked up. "What."

The woman squinted at her the way service workers did when dragging a memory up from under ten thousand interchangeable faces. "Do I know you?"

Faye's first answer arrived from habit. "No."

"Maybe not now." The woman wiped her hands on the apron and leaned one hip against the counter. "Ganymede."

Faye kept the chopsticks poised over the bowl. "Never been."

"Golden Ox."

Something in the name made her pause, not because she recognized it but because the waitress did not sound like she was guessing anymore.

"High room," the woman said. "Table by the rail. Blue dress. Low cut enough that the wool trader with the rings couldn't stop looking where he wasn't supposed to. I was on that floor half the night because the bastard never tipped and management never threw him out. Then you cleaned him out and walked with half his cash."

Faye smiled the way she smiled when men got fresh and she wanted them to regret it later. "Lady, that's flattering, but it wasn't me."

"It was you."

"No."

"Honey, I carried drinks to that table all night. I know who it was."

Faye shook her head and went back to the bowl, because dismissal worked on most people. The chopsticks slipped in broth-slick fingers. She got a mouthful of noodles anyway and they burned the roof of her mouth bad enough to make her eyes water.

"You had a laugh," the waitress went on, uninvited. "Small, through your nose, like you were already counting his money while he kept talking. You don't do it now. Or maybe you do and I haven't said the right thing yet."

Faye chewed. Swallowed. "You've got the wrong woman."

"Then you have the same face, the same voice, and the same bad habit."

Faye set the chopsticks down.

"What habit."

The waitress touched two fingers to her own collarbone, just under the throat, quick as a tic. "That. Every time you were bluffing or deciding whether to bluff. Right there. Like you were checking if the bones were still where you left them."

Faye's hand moved before she could stop it.

Fingers to skin. The little notch at the base of the throat. Warm from broth steam.

The waitress saw it and her expression changed from effort to certainty. "See?"

Faye dropped her hand to the counter as if the skin had stung her. The stool suddenly felt too narrow. The shop felt too hot. By the years the woman was talking about, Faye should have been nowhere. She should have been a body in a freezing unit, then paperwork, then interest compounding on debt she had not chosen. She knew that much even if most of her life before waking was still a locked room. The dates alone made the whole thing impossible.

It was impossible and happening anyway.

"No," Faye said, and heard how flat it came out.

The waitress frowned. "What do you mean no?"

"I mean—" Faye's hand was still near her collarbone. She dropped it to the counter. "When was this."

"When was what?"

"The Ox. The table. When."

Miriam's frown shifted from confused to calculating. "I don't know, eleven years ago? Twelve? Year they shut the Ganymede interchange. No — year after. I remember because the ferries were already screwed up."

Eleven years. Twelve. Faye did the math before she could stop herself and the math didn't work. Eleven years ago she'd been frozen solid in a cryo-unit, accruing debt, accruing nothing, a body with a name on a clipboard and a temperature instead of a pulse. The dates were broken.

"That's not—" She shut her mouth. Tried again. "I wasn't anywhere eleven years ago."

"Honey, everybody was somewhere eleven years ago."

"Not me."

It came out stranger than she'd meant it to. Miriam's face changed — not sympathy, not disbelief, something flatter. The look of a woman who'd worked enough counters to know when a conversation had stepped off a ledge it hadn't meant to.

"All right," she said. "All right. I'm telling you what I saw. You do what you want with it."

Faye looked down into the broth. Oil swam red on top. A slice of scallion bobbed against the rim. Her reflection in the liquid was broken by steam.

"What was your name?" she asked before she could decide not to.

"Miriam."

"You sure about this, Miriam?"

"About your order? No. About Ganymede? Yeah."

Faye almost smiled at that and hated her for earning it.

Miriam folded a towel with careful square motions. "I remember because that man had been stiffing the floor staff for months, and watching him lose made the whole place nicer for one night. I remember because your dress was blue enough to hurt my eyes under those lights. I remember because after, when everybody else acted like servers were furniture, you looked right at me when you asked for the check. And you tipped."

"That sounds sentimental."

"It wasn't. You still looked mean."

Faye let out a short breath, close enough to a laugh that Miriam noticed and gave a tiny, grimly satisfied nod.

"See?" Miriam said.

"That doesn't prove anything."

"No. But it happened."

Faye stared at the salt shaker tucked in the apron pocket. Cheap plastic, cloudy from years of use. One side had cracked and been taped. She had the stupid urge to grab it and throw it at the mirror just to hear something break cleanly.

Instead she said, "You rotate here a lot?"

Miriam lifted one shoulder. "When they need hands."

"You worked the Ox how long?"

"Long enough. Why?"

"Just asking."

"Uh-huh."

Miriam was not intimidated, which made lying to her less fun. Faye reached for the broth, missed the bowl on the first pass, and caught the paper cup instead. The thin waxed sides crumpled a little in her grip. That made her furious at herself in a different way.

Miriam watched that too and went gentler than Faye wanted. "Look, maybe I got the place wrong. Could've been a different room. Could've been earlier, later, who knows. But I didn't get the face wrong."

"People get faces wrong all the time."

"Sure. But they don't usually get the little things wrong."

The kitchen bell rang. Miriam looked back, then to Faye again. Work came first. Memory came second. "You want me to pack that?"

Faye had eaten four bites at most. Her mouth still tasted of hot broth and metal.

"Yeah," she said.

"Fine."

Miriam took the bowl away. Over her shoulder she said, "If you think of the place later, don't come back in here acting like I made it up."

"I don't act like that."

Miriam gave a dry little sound. "You act like a lot of things, honey."

Faye waited with both hands wrapped around the paper cup of water until the noodles came in a carry box. Miriam set the box down, then tucked two packets of chili oil on top as if this were the most natural end to the conversation in the world.

"On the house?" Faye asked.

"No. Mama Yun will put it on Jet's tab and he'll complain at me next week."

"That sounds right."

"Mm."

Faye picked up the box. "You really hate Mars broth?"

"I hate what Mars does to broth. Different sin."

That was specific enough to be true. Faye nodded once, then left before the woman could say Golden Ox again.

Outside, the cold had sharpened. Wind moved dust along the curb in low red snakes. Faye took two steps, stopped, balanced the noodle box on one palm, and dug the nails of her free hand into the heel of the other until the sting cut through the churn in her head.

The cold bit her fingers through the cardboard. Miriam's certainty kept pace beside her, step for step.

She swore softly and adjusted her grip on the box.

By the time she got back to the Bebop, each footfall seemed to ring farther through the hull. Ships were metal and wiring and other people's smells. Still, she climbed the ramp with the feeling that somebody had been through the rooms and put each object back exactly where it belonged.

The common room held all of them and none of them, which was how the Bebop did family on bad days.

Ed lay upside down on the couch with the console balanced on shinbones, red hair hanging toward the deck, muttering to the bounty archive. On the screen Hosuke Takara's dead file sat beside three other government records in a loose electronic circle, and a narrow telemetry strip crawled under them with `PASSIVE WATCH // UNKNOWN SHIP` stamped in one corner. Every few seconds Ed enlarged one detail, frowned, and shrank it again.

Jet knelt by the galley cabinet with the under-sink panel open, shoulders bent over a latch that had probably wronged him somehow. The line of screws by size on the counter told Faye he had been at it a while.

Spike stood by the far window with a cigarette in one hand and a portable receiver in the other. The cigarette had burned nearly to the filter without him seeming to notice.

Nobody looked up right away.

That was the real bad sign.

Faye kicked the door shut behind her with more noise than necessary. "If anybody touched my noodles, I'll shoot the cheapest one first."

Ed swung farther upside down to peer at the box. "Noodle delivery achieved."

Jet said, "Put them on the table."

Spike glanced over then. "Everything okay?"

It was such an ordinary line that she almost told the truth just to spite it.

Instead she set the takeout down and peeled off her coat. "Waitress at Yun's thinks she knows me."

Jet's screwdriver stopped. Just for a beat.

"From where?" he asked, too carefully.

Faye shrugged like the answer did not matter. "Some gambling room on Ganymede."

Spike's gaze stayed on her a moment longer than he usually let it. "You ever been to Ganymede?"

"No."

"Then she's got the wrong face," Jet said.

Faye looked at him. He went back to the latch.

Maybe he believed that. Maybe he just wanted a problem he could tighten down with a screwdriver. She understood the instinct well enough not to push.

"Yeah," she said. "Maybe."

Ed had gone quiet, which on Ed lasted about three seconds. Then Ed rolled partly upright and jabbed at the console. "Pattern overlap is increasing."

"What pattern," Faye asked.

Ed waggled the console. "The mystery pattern. Hosuke pattern. Sneaky ship pattern. Faye-person noodle pattern. Ed made a new noodle file. `GOLDEN OX / GANYMEDE / MIRIAM maybe.` Bebop has many patterns."

"That isn't helpful."

"It is extremely helpful. Maybe later."

Spike crushed the cigarette out in the ashtray and set the receiver on the sill facedown. "Keep it local, Ed."

Faye looked from him to the screen. "Why's everybody acting like this thing can hear us think?"

Spike did not look at her. "Because we don't need to knock on its door to know it's there."

Jet slid the latch back into place and tested it once. The cabinet door closed smooth as if it had never stuck.

It was a small, stupid success. He did not look happier for it.

He wiped his hand on the rag and looked at Ed. "Set a proximity alarm on the ship too. If it powers up, if a hatch opens, if somebody so much as breathes hard near it, wake all of us."

Ed's head came farther off the couch. "All of all?"

"All of all."

"Alarm gremlin deployed," Ed said, and the console windows shuffled. The passive-watch strip widened. A fresh local note field bloomed beside the old bounty file, tagged with Miriam's name and Ganymede in Ed's uneven capitalization.

A dry bureaucratic chirp came from the console. One of the archive panes refreshed, the old dormant line blinking once before harder letters stamped themselves across the screen.

"Oops," Ed said, interested rather than alarmed. "Sleepy bounty woke up."

`WARRANT STATUS: REACTIVATED.`

Faye took the noodles to the table and sat. The box warmed her hands through the cardboard. Across from her, the common-room screen held Hosuke Takara in bad old resolution. Beside him sat Miriam's new note window, the bright reactivated warrant line, and the crawl of the passive watch. By the window the receiver reflected a thin strip of Mars light. Under the sink, Jet sorted the last two screws and put them away as if the order of tiny metal objects might still count for something.

No one said what they had actually found, not all of it.

The air recycler hummed. The hull ticked as the temperature dropped outside. Somewhere down the corridor, water knocked once in the pipes and stopped. Faye opened the noodle box, lifted the chopsticks, pressed her knuckles flat to the table until the tendons stood out, and ate while Hosuke Takara stayed lit on the screen and the ship listened for the stranger outside.
