# Chapter 9

The Bebop in fuel-save mode sounded different. With the engines down to a careful sip and half the lights cut, the ship stopped pretending to be dignified and let its old bones speak. Coolant ticked in the wall behind the galley. A vent fan worried the same dry bearing every seventh turn. The refrigerator gave a low irritated hum, quit, thought about it, and tried again.

Ed liked the stripped-down version of the ship. Fewer noises meant the hidden ones came forward.
Thin red dust still striped the floor by the couch where Spike had come in before dawn and stood for a while with one hand inside his jacket.

Data made noise too. Not to everybody. To Jet, a screen was a screen until it told him whether he could afford noodles. To Spike, a screen was something you glanced at for exits and trouble and then ignored on purpose. To Faye, a screen was debt, odds, a bill, a face, a lie. To Ed, the different systems all had their own little songs. Bounty archives were stiff and papery, all rustle and clack, like somebody riffling old cards with dry fingers. Port authority ledgers had a tinny beep to them, money-counting sounds. Hospital back-end systems lived in cold blue clicks. Obsolete syndicate freight code came in greasy, low, and red, the kind of sound that stuck to a wrench.

Tonight the songs were everywhere.

Ed had turned the common room floor into a nest. Two portable monitors from the engine room leaned against the couch. One stolen galley stool held a projector. Three hand terminals were spread open in a circle around Ed's bare feet. Thin cables ran under the table, over the arm of the couch, through the handle of a pot Jet had not found missing yet, and into the access panel by the main screen. Ein lay under the table with his nose on Ed's ankle, eyes mostly closed, ears too awake to count as sleeping.

The mysterious ship sat in the middle of all of it, not physically, because that would have been inconvenient, but as a shape. Registry number. Mass profile. Heat bloom. Thruster asymmetry. Custom weld on the starboard vector fin where somebody had patched a crack ugly and permanent instead of pretty and temporary. The ship had a face now. Not the outside face everybody saw behind Mama Yun's, sleek and shut down and rude. The underneath face, the one made of maintenance habits and docking fees and the way a pilot leaned on old systems when she didn't have to.

Ed hummed and dragged one window over another with two toes.

"Hmmmmm. No, no, no. That is fake-boring boring." Ed flicked a freight tax receipt into a trash cluster, pulled a customs wash from Earth into the center, then overlaid that with a security still from Hellas. Same weld scar. Same patched fin. Same ship. "Sneaky ship is still sneaky, but Ed is sneakier."

Ein thumped his tail once.

It had started as four separate piles because adults liked to scatter clues and then walk around injured that nobody understood their injuries. A stiff old bounty with a younger Jet face sat in one pile, yellowed and mean. The weird old syndicate handshake sat in another, all dead relays and obsolete packet language and names that had been burned out of public systems years ago. Faye's problem had started as not-Ed's-business and then become absolutely Ed's business when Ed noticed a cryogenic facility on Earth appear twice in the same travel history and once in a scrubbed public records cache. The ship's movements were the biggest pile of all, almost two years of berths and fees and fuel buys and one-night stays disguised as ordinary transit.

Individually the piles looked like what adults loved most, coincidence with paperwork.

Together they were beginning to look rude.

Ed pulled up the first clean thread and followed it sideways. The ship had been on Europa eleven months ago under a maintenance contractor shell that existed for six days and never bought parts. It docked two kilometers from an ISSP archive annex, long enough for three human meals and one data pull. The maintenance shell dissolved the next day. Boring if you liked boring. But the annex security software had been updated in layers instead of replaced, which meant one antique log still wrote timestamps in an older format. Ed loved older formats. Older formats got lonely. Lonely things always talked.

The old timestamp matched a dormant query run against a sealed records cluster on the same afternoon. Not the contents. Just the shape of the ask. Deep file. Restricted background. Personnel cross-reference. The kind of ask that brushed the dust off old names.

Ed rolled onto one hip and grinned at the old bounty photo. "Hello, old grump-face baby Jet."

The file photo looked enough like Jet to be funny and different enough to be offensive. More hair. Less beard. Same expression of a man who'd already had enough and had years left to prove it.

Footsteps sounded in the corridor. Heavy, unhurried, one rubber sole scuffing on the turn because Jet's left boot wore down there every time.

Jet came in carrying a mug big enough to be a threat. He was wearing sleep pants, a gray undershirt, and the expression of a man who had woken up because his ship had betrayed him. He stopped in the doorway and looked at the cable running across the galley threshold.

"If I die because you wired the coffee pot to the floor, I'm haunting you," he said.

"Good morning, very haunted Jet. Please step over Saturn."

Jet squinted down. "That's a charging cable."

"Today it's Saturn."

He stepped over it anyway. Jet always counted sugar taps into his coffee when he was half-awake, and even now he gave the spoon three absent little knocks against the mug before drinking. One, two, three. Enough. His beard on the right side had mashed flat from sleep. Ed noticed these things because everybody made patterns whether they wanted to or not.

Jet looked at the main screen, then at the monitors on the floor. "You've been up all night?"

"No, no. Ed was up all night. Different child."

"Right." Jet drank again. "Anything useful?"

Ed waggled one hand. "Useful is a grown-up word. Weird is a better word. Weird is stronger. Weird can lift more."

Jet stared at the old bounty photo for a fraction too long, then looked away like he'd meant to the whole time. "You say that about everything."

"Not about soap."

"You said it about the bathroom filter."

"Bathroom filter was weird." Ed pointed a toe at the Europa logs. "This is weirder. Ship goes to Europa, not for repairs, not for cargo, not for romance, because Europa is a bad place for romance, and then somebody asks a question in an ISSP archive that brushes exactly the right dust."

Jet went still in the way adults did when they didn't want children to know they were paying attention. It never worked. His good hand tightened on the mug. The metal fingers of the other flexed once and went quiet.

"Could be any archive pull," he said.

"Mmm, could be. Could also be the exact day the old sleepy bounty hiccuped and came back to life." Ed tapped the screen and let the dates stack themselves. Europa berth. Archive query. Reactivated bounty paperwork. "Same afternoon. Same sneaky ship. Same pilot with too much free time."

Jet let out air through his nose. "Don't say anything to the others yet."

Ed looked up. "Jet says that about everything too."

"Ed."

There it was, the father-voice without the name. Not angry. Just heavy.

Ed let it sit there a second. Then Ed nodded once because this part was still only one quarter of the rude shape. "Fine. Temporary silence. Not permanent silence. Permanent silence is how everybody gets itchy."

Jet rubbed a thumb against the mug handle. The flattened side of his beard bothered him now; he scratched at it with the back of his wrist and failed to fix it. "You eat anything?"

"Three crackers and an idea."

"Those are not the same food group."

"Depends on the idea."

Jet made a sound that wasn't quite a laugh and wasn't quite approval either. "I'll make something in a minute. Move the monitor off the stove before I forget and boil it."

"That would be educational."

"For me, maybe."

He moved toward the galley, stepping around the cables with annoyed care. Halfway there he paused and looked back at the Europa screen. His mouth did a small hard thing, a line tightening and then pretending it had always been that shape. Then he kept moving.

Ed filed that too.

By the time Jet had water heating and onions in the pan, Ed had pulled the second thread hard enough to make sparks. The syndicate frequency had always been wrong in an interesting way. Too clean for scavengers, too playful for a real dead network waking up. Now it had company. Six months before the ship appeared at Mama Yun's, the same transponder signature had ghosted over a dead freight relay on Mars, out in a district so chewed up by redevelopment and demolition that most city maps pretended it wasn't there anymore. An old logistics hub had sat there once. Most of it was gone now. Concrete, twisted dock arms, one cracked relay mast nobody bothered to haul away because the scrap metal wasn't worth the permit.

The ship had no reason to ping the dead mast. No practical one. There was nothing there to call back. But the pilot had pinged it anyway, twice, ten seconds apart, like tapping a glass to hear the note.

Spike shuffled in while Ed was listening to the relay packet for the fourth time.

He looked like he'd slept on the couch because he had. One side of his hair had been flattened and then rebelled into sharper angles. He had his cigarettes tucked into the breast pocket of his shirt and one sock on, which made him look less cool than he probably believed. Spike in the morning always stole the first cold thing that looked edible, and tonight's victim was a heel of bread Jet had left on a plate. He took it without asking, tore off a bite, and glanced at the nearest screen with one open eye.

"You find the owner's shopping list yet?" he asked.

"No shopping list. Old ghost tower instead." Ed replayed the dead-relay ping. "Listen."

Spike listened because he couldn't help it. He wouldn't admit that part. His face didn't change much, but his chewing slowed. That was enough.

"Old freight code," he said.

"Mmmhmm. Very old. Sticky old. Red old."

"You got colors for code now?"

Ed looked at him in outrage. "Now? Ed has always had colors. Spike is just late."

Spike leaned one shoulder on the galley bulkhead. "All right. What's the point?"

"Point is ship goes to dead place and knocks on dead door. Knock knock."

"Could be checking old hardware."

"Could be," Ed said cheerfully. "Then why fake the packet like somebody who knows the door used to matter?"

Spike took another bite of bread. "Maybe it did matter."

There was a lot inside that maybe, packed down flat and hidden under the lazy tone. Ed heard it. Ed heard everything if it sat still long enough.

"Mmm," Ed said, and did not push because pushing adults too early just made them roll under furniture.

Jet, at the stove, pointed the knife in Spike's direction without turning around. "If you're awake enough to stand there, you're awake enough to cut peppers."

"No, I'm not."

"Funny, because your hands work."

"Terrible rumor."

Jet shoved the cutting board down the counter with more force than the distance required. Spike caught it and, after pretending to think about not helping, started slicing. Thin, fast, exact. He always cut vegetables like he was proving something to nobody.

The ship's Earth route took longer because Earth systems were a mess on purpose and by neglect. Half the planet still ran on patched layers over disaster over military salvage over charity over fraud. Data there hid in plain sight by being ugly. Ed loved ugly data even more than lonely data. Ugly data never expected company.

The cryogenic facility appeared first as a maintenance invoice for a coolant pump that was never installed. Then as a security-camera reflection in a service tunnel window, just the nose of the ship and that crooked weld on the fin. Then as a gate toll using the same six-day shell company trick the Europa trip had used, only on Earth the shell had been dressed as medical waste hauling instead of repair work.

Three visits in fourteen months. Not enough to be a commuter. Too many to be casual.

Faye came in on the third cryo-facility hit, tying her hair up with one hand and still wearing the sleep mask pushed onto her forehead like a second set of eyebrows. She had pillow marks on one cheek. Those vanished the second she saw other people and remembered to be Faye.

"If that's breakfast, I'm filing a complaint," she said, eyeing the pan.

"Get in line," Jet said.

Faye plucked a pepper slice off the cutting board before Spike could stop her. "Why are there wires in my walking space?"

"Because Ed is making a giant brain."

"Make it somewhere else."

"No."

Faye followed a cable to the main screen and saw the cryogenic facility schematic. Her face didn't freeze. It didn't do anything that obvious. She just stopped reaching for a second pepper.

"What's that?" she asked.

Ed brightened automatically. "Cold-sleep castle. Sneaky ship went there three times. Same false company. Same hull. Same very ugly practical lying."

"Lots of people go to medical facilities," Faye said. "Some of them even have paperwork."

Ed rolled onto elbows and peered at the security still. "Lots of people don't bounce through a service entrance registered to a shell that dissolves before breakfast. Lots of people don't repeat exactly the same fake in three jurisdictions because they think nobody's looking sideways."

Faye gave a little shrug, too loose to be real. "Then somebody was looking for medicine."

Spike's knife slowed for a second, then picked up again.

Jet said, without turning, "Faye."

"What?" She crossed her arms. "I'm saying medical places do medical things. Wild theory."

Her voice had the extra edges it grew when she wanted distance. Ed marked that too. Faye's hand, the one not tucked under her elbow, went up to her collarbone and stopped itself halfway. Then she hooked a thumb under the mask instead, like that had been the plan all along.

Four piles. Three adults pretending the piles were separate because adults liked separate pain. Separate pain felt private. Private felt safe. Safe was a game people played until a kid with enough terminals ruined it.

Ed grinned so hard cheeks got involved. The shape was getting clearer now. Not a straight line. Straight lines were for bureaucrats and bullets. This was a web. Better. Ruder.

The orphanage showed up last because the orphanage had never expected anybody to care. No syndicate firewall. No ISSP archive protocols. No hospital compliance layer. Just a little Earth charity network patched together from grant money, secondhand hardware, and a local administrator who reused passwords and never updated image caches because image caches took up room. The pilot had visited six months ago. Paid a records fee in cash. Signed a paper ledger with a name the scanner blurred into static because the scanner was garbage. Asked for archived intake material from a sequence of years that narrowed, narrowed again, then landed on one file.

Ed found the access trail by accident first, then on purpose. A dock camera two streets over had caught the ship at dawn, half hidden behind a municipal water tower. The angle showed almost nothing. Wing root. Tail. The cut of the nose. Enough. Then the charity server showed a manual record retrieval from the same morning. Then the ancient admin cache coughed up a thumbnail that never should have survived, a waiting room, two plastic chairs, a wall painted cheerful once and then not again, and beyond the open office door a filing cabinet with the old label strip still stuck crooked.

Ed stopped moving.

Jet's onions hissed. Spike's knife tapped the board. Faye said something about coffee maybe, but the words were far away.

The thumbnail was tiny. Bad color. Bad light. The kind of picture adults looked at and dismissed because it wasn't useful enough. Ed didn't need more than that. The paint around the office door had been a green that always looked damp no matter how dry the wall was. One of the chairs had a split seam in the vinyl where yellow foam pushed out like a tongue. In the reflected glass of the office window, barely there, a line on the ceiling ran from the center beam toward the dormitory side in a bend Ed knew by heart.

That was the place.

Not home. Ed did not have a home before the Bebop in the way Jet meant home when he checked the pantry twice and the engine room once before sleep. But the orphanage was the place with the cracked ceiling and the hot metal swings and the smell of bleach on rainy afternoons and the little patch of dirt by the fence where nothing grew right.

Ed heard the blood in Ed's ears for one strange second. It sounded white, not red.

"Oh," Ed said.

Nobody looked over yet. It had been too quiet to register as danger.

Ed opened the record access log. Another dead-simple system. Date. Fee. Administrator ID. Requested file sequence. One sequence ended in a number Ed had not thought about in years because Ed usually didn't think about years at all. Years were for adults who saved things in boxes.

Ein had lifted his head now. He crawled out from under the table and pressed against Ed's side.

Ed looked at the tiny waiting room thumbnail again. "I know this one," Ed said.

This time Jet turned. "Know what one?"

Ed swallowed. Very annoying physical reflex. "Old Ceiling House."

Spike left the knife on the board with half a pepper still under it.

Faye frowned. "What are you talking about?"

Ed pointed at the thumbnail and because the words came out wrong in third person, came out too bouncy, Ed had to reach for straighter ones. "I lived there."

Faye's mouth opened for something sharper and came up empty.

Jet took one step away from the stove, leaving the onions to hiss on their own. "Ed."

He said it low. No orders in it. Just the name.

Ed hated that almost as much as pity. But it wasn't pity. It was attention, plain and heavy.

"The ship went there six months ago," Ed said, still looking at the screen because the screen was easier than faces. "Pilot paid cash and pulled intake records. Mine. Probably. Almost definitely mine. Ninety-six point..." Ed stopped. Numbers were safer too, which was exactly why they had become useless. "Mine."

For a second the ship answered for them: pan hiss, refrigerator grumble, Ein breathing against Ed's leg.

The onions started to burn. Jet smelled them before he saw them. "Ah, shit," he said to the pan, turning back hard enough to bump the counter with his hip. He scraped the darkening onions across the metal in short angry strokes, then shoved the heat lower. The ordinary motion helped. That was a Jet thing too. If pain showed up, he found something to stir.

Ed looked from the orphanage log to the Europa archive, from that to the dead relay, from that to the cryogenic facility. Four piles. Four adults, if Ed counted Ed as adult-adjacent, which on the Bebop depended mostly on the mess involved.

The shape did not snap cleanly yet. It leaned. It suggested. It stood in the corner with its hands in its pockets and acted like Ed should already know its name.

Too neat for random. Too crooked for simple.

Maybe a route.

Maybe a dare.

If it was planned, the pilot had been carrying them around in her head for a long time.

"Ohhhhhh," Ed said, longer this time, because delight and alarm were neighbors and Ed had never seen a reason to move them apart. "You sneaky, sneaky pilot lady. Maybe not caught. Maybe chased. Need more data for medium-fast conclusions."

Spike lit a cigarette off the stove burner without asking. Jet opened his mouth to object and then gave up because there were larger structural failures in the room.

Faye pushed the sleep mask up into her hair. "What medium-fast?"

Ed was already gathering terminals, unplugging one monitor, dragging the projector by its handle. The shock had not gone away. It had simply joined the work. That was easier.

"Meeting," Ed announced. "Common room emergency of pattern. Very educational. Bring eyeballs."

Jet looked around at the room they'd been standing in. "We are in the common room."

Ed pointed at the tangle of screens, the pan on the stove, the knife, the bread heel, the cigarette, the orphanage thumbnail, all of it. "No, this is pre-meeting chaos. Real meeting needs arrangement."

Spike exhaled smoke toward the vent. "He's got a point."

"Don't encourage this."

"Too late."

Ed scooped up the orphanage screen last and held it closer for one quick private second. Green paint. Split chair. Crooked label strip. Somebody had gone there and asked for Ed by the trail Ed left in paper as a child. Somebody had opened the old box and looked inside.

That was not funny.

The rest of it still could be.

***

By the time Jet had relocated breakfast to bowls and given up on saving the onions, the Bebop's common room had stopped looking habitable and started looking like Ed's brain had staged a coup.

String crossed the room in red, yellow, blue, and one length of fishing line Ed had stolen from Jet's tackle drawer because data deserved more than one texture. The main screen held a star map of the inner system with dots clustered and labeled in Ed's handwriting. Two smaller monitors showed rotating hull stills of the pilot's ship from different ports and one grainy frame from the Hellas chase. The coffee table had disappeared under notes, a dismantled projector housing, three mismatched mugs serving as anchor weights, and Jet's socket wrench set, which was now part of a chart whether Jet liked it or not.

Ein had been recruited as morale and sat in the middle of the floor web with the patient expression of a creature who had accepted that this was his life.

Jet set bowls down with the careful anger of a man who knew fury would only make him step into more string. He had put on a proper shirt but not bothered to button the top two buttons. "If one of you knocks this over, you're cleaning it."

Faye looked at the web, looked at her bowl, then chose the bowl. "I'm not climbing through that on an empty stomach."

"Don't climb through yellow lines," Ed said. "Yellow lines are chronology."

"Why is chronology on the floor?"

"Because time is a trap."

Spike, who had found the one patch of wall not currently in service as a graph, leaned against it and ate standing up. "That's actually the clearest thing he's said all morning."

Jet pointed his spoon at him. "You don't get to encourage the mutiny and then stand there like a houseplant."

"I'm not a houseplant."

"No, you'd have to contribute oxygen."

Faye snorted despite herself. That was useful. Low-level noises kept adults from thinking they were in a drama and doing stupid dramatic things.

Ed hopped onto the couch back, crouched there barefoot and balanced, and clapped twice. "Welcome, welcome, old people and medium-old people and one excellent dog. Today's lesson is called Sneaky Ship Is About All Of You. Subtitle, Nobody Gets To Be Special Alone."

"I hate this already," Faye said.

"Please save hating for the question section."

Jet blew on his coffee. "Start from the top."

"No."

"Ed."

"Top is fake. Top says ship appears, everybody yells, noodle shop, grr, very expensive parking. But that's not where the thing starts." Ed spread both hands at the web. "Thing starts before. Long before. So start is not top. Start is shape."

Jet looked like he wanted to argue. Then he looked at the strings and decided that even demanding linearity in this room was more work than he had fuel for. "Fine. Shape."

Ed beamed. "Excellent. First shape, same ship everywhere. Not similar ship. Not cousin ship. Same rude lady ship with ugly starboard weld and a replacement vent panel bought on Callisto six months before the Hellas chase. See?" Ed flicked a control and the hull stills rotated side by side. Dock camera on Europa. Security reflection on Earth. Fuel station image from Mars. Hellas chase freeze-frame. In each one the starboard fin carried the same crooked patch, too wide at one end, hand-laid, no shop symmetry. "Pilot fixes things for use, not beauty. Good mechanic. Not precious."

Spike's gaze sharpened at that without moving the rest of him. Mechanic's work always got his attention first. Human feelings, less so.

Ed dropped from the couch back to the arm, then to the floor, then popped up again on the other side because using the obvious path was for people with bad knees. "Now, if same ship same ship same ship, then where does same ship go? Lots of boring places. Fuel depots. Cheap berths. One place on Io with terrible coffee according to review aggregators. False noise. Padding. Good padding too. Makes the pattern fuzzy unless you pull on the right corners."

Faye twirled noodles around her fork. "And you pulled on the right corners because..."

"Because Ed is smarter than everyone in this room except maybe Ein and Ein is humble."

Ein's ears went up.

Jet said, "He's not disagreeing."

Ed pointed at the first cluster on the map, a pale gray dot with three lines running off it. "Europa. ISSP archive annex. Maintenance shell company. Six days alive, never buys one actual repair part. Ship parks nearby, somebody runs a deep background query through an antique terminal, and the same afternoon an old bounty with Jet's face and more hair twitches awake."

Jet's spoon stopped halfway to his mouth.

Faye looked from the screen to Jet. "What old bounty?"

Jet drank his coffee instead of answering.

"See?" Ed said, delighted and merciless at once. "Spoon freeze. Coffee dodge. Faye wants details. Good classroom."

"Ed," Jet said.

"Fine, fine. That part can wait." Ed flicked the Europa node smaller before Faye could keep digging. "But pilot on Europa is not asking a normal question. Query shape is broad. Background clusters, cross-reference clusters, all the dusty cabinets around the obvious cabinet."

That did not help Jet's face.

"So she wasn't just pulling one file," Faye said.

Jet set the mug down. "Keep going."

Ed did. "Segment two. Mars red ugly place." Ed tapped the rust-colored node hanging off a broken line. "Dead freight relay in a logistics district so demolished even the maps are tired. Ship pings obsolete code at a mast nobody uses anymore, twice, ten seconds apart. More interesting, the packet knock has the same old-handshake wobble the ship's ID had when it parked behind Mama Yun's."

Spike's bowl paused in his hand.

Jet turned a little. "Meaning?"

Spike took a second too long. "Meaning it wasn't random chatter."

Faye looked at him now. "You know what it was."

Spike shrugged one shoulder. "Address check. You knock on a dead door to prove you knew it used to be alive."

Usually Spike would've thrown a joke over that and kept walking. He let it sit there instead.

Ed rapped knuckles on the bulkhead twice, ten seconds apart. "Exactly. Door remembers your hand."

Faye squinted at the map. "So the ship went to a cop archive and an old syndicate relay on purpose. Great. Somebody likes ugly history."

"Selective ugly history," Ed said. "Important distinction." Ed pulled up the Earth line, a clean blue loop dropping toward the cryogenic facility. "Cold-sleep castle. Three visits in fourteen months. Same six-day shell trick as Europa, only here the shell wears medical-waste clothes. Ship comes in through service access, not front gate. Main records stay clean. Side systems twitch."

Faye had already set the bowl down.

"What side systems?" Jet asked.

"Verification channels. Public-facing dumb layer. Not the frozen-patient vault. The boring doorbell connected to it." Ed spread fingers at the screen. "Something under that identity stack got touched the same weeks the ship showed up."

Faye said, too fast, "Maybe somebody was checking a name."

Then she heard herself. So did everybody else.

Spike looked at her. Jet looked away from her because he was better at mercy than Spike and worse at pretending he wasn't doing it.

Ed pointed at Faye with both hands. "Aha. Bowl down, arms crossed, Spike looking now. Excellent."

"Shut up," Faye said, but without heat. She crossed her arms harder. "I mean it. Shut up."

Ed nodded because that was data too. "Yes, ma'am with the very obvious secret."

The strings on the floor had felt funny a minute ago. Improvised. Bossy. A little stupid. Now they looked like what they were, lines pulled tight through places nobody on the Bebop talked about unless cornered.

"And then," Ed said more quietly, ducking under a yellow line to the last blue-green square, "there's this one."

Jet's attention had never really left Ed since pre-meeting chaos. Now it narrowed further.

Ed touched the square with one finger.

"This one is mine."

Faye stopped with the fork halfway back to the bowl. Jet's hand stayed around his mug. Spike came off the wall a little.

Faye frowned. "Yours how?"

Ed kept a finger on the square because touching a thing made it easier to say. "Earth orphanage. Same settlement. Pilot goes there six months ago, pays records fee in cash, requests intake material from exactly the years Ed was there. Old camera catches the ship two streets over. Charity server saves a thumbnail because charity server is trash." Ed swallowed and hated it again. "She pulled my file."

Jet put his bowl down very carefully. "Ed."

He kept saying the name like maybe tone alone could make the floor safer.

Spike straightened a fraction from the wall.

Faye's anger came first because anger was easier to hold than anything else. "How the hell did she even know where to look?"

"That," Ed said, voice picking speed back up because stopping was worse, "is the right question. Also why Ed says deliberate, not sloppy."

Jet rubbed the bridge of his nose. "Sell me."

Ed spun one monitor toward them. Europa archive log. Dead relay packet. Cryo service reflection. Orphanage cache thumbnail. Four ugly little survivors from four different systems. "At each real stop she uses the oldest still-working side door and leaves one crumb there. Not the main logs. Those get washed or buried. The attached junk room. Europa antique terminal. Mars dead relay. Earth service layer. Orphanage image cache. Four systems, same habit."

Faye said, "That could still be coincidence."

"Once, maybe. Twice if the universe is drunk." Ed jabbed a finger at the screens one by one. "Four times in four different infrastructures? No. Accidents don't keep choosing the oldest door."

Spike said, "And the parking-lot signal."

Ed whirled on him, triumphant. "Yes! First day behind Mama Yun's, the ship ID came back dirty in the same way. Not broken-broken. Old-door broken. Same obsolete handshake wobble as the relay knock. She rang the bell there too. That's why Ed started sniffing at all."

Jet was staring at the four screens now, not at the string web. The math had moved from possibility into motive. "She planted the route and then she tripped the alarm."

"Medium-sized alarm," Ed corrected. "Enough to get Ed chewing. Not enough to make anybody run to ISSP and ruin the game."

Faye looked at Ed. "How'd she know there was an Ed to chew on it?"

Jet answered before Ed could. "She sat behind Yun's long enough, half the block would've told her who lives on this tub. Yun would've told her what we owe, what we eat, and that we've got a barefoot net diver climbing on the hull at stupid hours."

"Tragic fame," Ed said.

Faye's mouth tightened. "So she didn't need to know two years ago. She just needed the trail ready before she picked the fight."

"Yes," Ed said. "Homework first. Provocation later."

Jet asked, "How long?"

Ed tapped the timeline at the bottom of the main screen. "Twenty-three months from first meaningful stop to noodle-shop insult. Maybe longer if paper research came before ship research. Definite travel pattern across almost two years."

Faye looked at the line and then away from it. "Two years."

Spike crossed to the monitor with the dead relay packet. He didn't touch it. Just stood in front of it, cigarette forgotten between his fingers while ash lengthened. "Show me Europa again."

Ed did. Then the cryo reflection. Then the orphanage thumbnail. Then all four together.

Jet's voice had lost the leftover breakfast irritation and the parental nagging and most of its volume. It came out like a mechanic checking torque. "You said the Europa query was broad. Broad enough to brush whatever lived around that old bounty."

"Yep. Not one tidy question." Ed searched the room for an honest object, found Jet's spice tin, and opened it. Dried pepper flakes. Cumin dust. One bay leaf. "More like this. Reach in for one thing, come back smelling like all the others."

Jet stared at the spice tin, then at the map.

Faye said, "So what, exactly? She collects ruined paperwork? She likes messed-up people?"

"Maybe," Spike said.

Everybody looked at him because Spike volunteering a maybe was basically a speech.

He flicked ash into an empty mug. "Or she's tracing how the ship got built."

Jet frowned. "What?"

Spike kept his eyes on the data, not on them. "Not the actual ship. This one." He jerked the cigarette toward the floor, meaning the Bebop, the room, the people in it, the whole patched-together arrangement they lived inside and refused to define. "If she visited those places on purpose, she wasn't just digging. She was mapping."

Ed felt a thrill go through Ed's ribs, ugly and bright. There it was. The shape with its hands in its pockets finally giving up the trick.

"Mapping," Ed echoed. "Yes. Crew archaeology. Social spaghetti. A whole route built out of old paperwork and dead code and one very nosy lady ship. Not bounty money. Not salvage. Not random snooping. The points where all of you touched the world before Bebop happened."

Faye's hand went to her collarbone and stayed there.

Jet's gaze snagged there and cut away. Spike saw it too and studied the web instead. Faye left her fingers where they were, stubborn about it.

Jet looked back at the orphanage square. "And she included you."

That sounded like fact, which was kinder than comfort.

Ed nodded once.

"Why?" Faye asked.

Not sharp now. Flat. Almost annoyed that the question had come out sounding real.

"Unknown," Ed said. "Pattern says intention. Pattern says patience. Pattern says she wanted us moving after her at her speed. Pattern does not yet say why."

Spike looked at the web. "But it says this wasn't opportunistic."

"Nope," Ed said. "This was homework."

Nobody laughed.

Jet's cybernetic fingers clicked once against the table, a reflex he usually didn't notice. "Twenty-three months," he said again, more to himself than anybody.

Faye stared at the cryogenic-facility node like she could burn a hole through it by refusing to blink. "Two years of this and she parks in our spot like she's picking a fight over lunch."

"Maybe because that's how you get people out of the house," Ed said.

"Not people," Spike said.

That was when he stood up straight.

It was such a small change, two inches maybe, the lazy angle gone out of his spine. But Spike lived in slouches and doorframes and sideways leans. Seeing him vertical was like hearing a glass crack in another room and realizing it was your glass.

Jet saw it too. Faye did. Even Ein lifted his head.

Spike crushed the cigarette out in a saucer. "Us."

The vent fan turned.

The refrigerator hummed, failed, and quieted.

Jet's metal fingers quit clicking. Faye stared at Spike like he'd just named the thing she'd been living inside. Even Ein stayed upright, ears tipped forward.

Jet looked at the Europa node a long time. When he spoke again, the words came out flat and practical, which meant they had cost him. "If she wants us following the trail, fine. Tomorrow we stop following it blind. We answer the first knock and make her come the rest of the way."

Faye's eyes cut to him. "With what?"

Jet didn't look away from the screen. "Whatever she went looking for on Europa, she can come look at it in person."

Nobody jumped on him. On the Bebop that was already halfway to a plan.

Faye looked him over once, slow and merciless. "You're gonna need a hat."

"No," Jet said immediately.

Spike, still standing straight, said, "Yeah."

Faye set her bowl down and headed for storage before Jet could come up with a second no.
