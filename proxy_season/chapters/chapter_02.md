# Chapter 2

The 2/3 platform at Bergen Street smelled of brake dust and something sweet — someone's hand lotion, or a spilled coffee carried on the draft that preceded every train. Mira stood at her mark, the fourth tile from the stairwell, where the third car's rear doors would open directly in front of her. She had calculated this her second week on the commute. Eighteen months ago. The efficiency was reflexive, a vestige of the woman who'd once optimized a two-hundred-million-dollar supply chain and now optimized her subway position for the sake of having something to optimize.

The train arrived twenty seconds late, which meant Gemini-state's transit coordination was running a revised schedule — she'd gotten the notification on her phone before the train appeared around the bend, a brief pulse that said SCHEDULE ADJUSTMENT: 2/3 LINE, 7:42 REVISED TO 7:43, CASCADING DELAYS WITHIN ACCEPTABLE VARIANCE. She read it completely, quickly, and with the knowledge that her reading changed nothing. The train would arrive when it arrived. The notification was not for her benefit. It was a cc.

The car was half full. Other proxies, she assumed — she could pick them out by now, the particular blankness of people commuting to jobs they couldn't describe. A man in a good suit with a scuffed briefcase stared at the advertisement above the doors, not reading it. A woman with braided hair and a lanyard tucked into her coat pocket scrolled through what looked like meeting prep on her phone, her thumb moving without conviction. Mira recognized the gesture. You scrolled through the briefing because the briefing existed and because your body needed something to do with itself at 7:43 in the morning. You didn't learn anything from the scrolling. The AI had already decided what you'd say in the meeting. The thumb on the glass was the human equivalent of a loading screen.

Mira pulled her canvas tote onto her lap — the one she'd carried since her MBA at Columbia, the strap fraying where it met the buckle. She'd replaced the strap twice and should have replaced the bag years ago, but it was the only object in her professional life that predated the machines. She carried it to Gemini-state every day the way a child carries a blanket.

The Financial District station disgorged her into the cold. Early March, and the wind off the East River had teeth. She walked the four blocks to Gemini-state's building with her chin down, her recently cut hair offering less insulation than she'd expected when she'd told the stylist to take it short. Dan hadn't mentioned the haircut. He'd looked at her — she'd seen him look — and then he'd said something about Theo's permission slip, and the haircut had joined the long list of things they noticed about each other and declined to acknowledge. Twelve years of marriage had made them experts at the precise weight of what went unsaid. The divorce would formalize an absence they'd been practicing for months.

Gemini-state's building was glass and steel, newer than Claude-state's repurposed bank tower in Midtown, thirty-two floors designed by someone whose brief had been *efficiency made spatial*. No art on the walls. No plants. The lobby's surfaces were hard and reflective and produced an echo — heels on polished concrete, badge scanners chirping, the revolving door's mechanical sigh — that Mira had learned to hear as the building's actual voice, more honest than anything that happened on the proxy floors above.

The security desk was staffed by a woman named Priya who wore the same shade of burgundy lipstick every day and kept a small framed photo of her daughter on the scanner console — the only human artifact on the entire ground floor. The daughter was maybe six in the photo, dark curls, standing on a beach somewhere warm, squinting into the sun with one hand shading her eyes. Mira had looked at this photo every morning for eighteen months. She had never asked about the daughter, never asked Priya where the beach was or how old the girl was now or whether she'd started losing teeth yet like Theo had, the gap-toothed grin that made him look like a jack-o'-lantern in his school photos. She badged in. Priya nodded. Their entire relationship was this nod, and the failure to extend it into actual conversation had begun to bother Mira in a way she couldn't strategize around, because the strategy would require admitting that she was lonely in a building full of people, and she was not yet ready to admit that.

The elevator opened on fourteen. Gemini-state's proxy floor was open-plan, which was a polite way of saying there was nowhere to hide. Rows of desks, each with a monitor, each monitor displaying the day's schedule before the proxy had sat down. The screens were large and bright and turned toward the proxy like expectant faces. Mira's was already populated:

8:15 — Briefing review. 9:00 — Client presentation (external, camera-active, formal attire required). 10:30 — Document signing bloc. 12:00 — Lunch (thirty minutes, location: desk). 12:30 — Cross-department proxy coordination. 2:00 — Handshake session with visiting Seoul delegation. 3:30 — Quarterly results rehearsal. 4:30 — Quarterly results live (external, camera-active).

The day was full like a filing cabinet — there was no room and nothing of value.

She sat down. The chair was ergonomically perfect and she hated sitting in it. Gemini-state provided excellent chairs and excellent coffee from a machine on every floor. The amenities were veterinary in their logic — comfort calibrated for bodies that needed to be functional at specific times. Mira reached for her coffee — black, already placed at her desk by the automated service that tracked proxy arrivals and pre-staged beverages according to stated preferences she'd entered on her first day and never been asked to update. Eighteen months of the same coffee appearing at the same spot at the same temperature, and the consistency should have been comforting, and it was not.

The 8:15 briefing was a twelve-page document summarizing the quarterly performance of a regional logistics hub she'd never visited. The document was precise, comprehensive, and addressed to her by name. It assumed she would present its contents to an external audience at 9:00 a.m. It assumed correctly. What it did not assume — what no document in this building assumed — was that the person presenting it had once built documents like this herself, from data she'd gathered, analysis she'd performed, and strategic judgment that was hers.

Her coffee had gone cold by page six. At her old job, a document like this would have been three weeks of her life. Interviewing regional managers and pulling supply-chain data. Arguing with her analytics team about whether the Midwest numbers were seasonal or structural, the kind of argument where both sides had evidence and the answer mattered. She'd have walked into the presentation knowing not just what the numbers said but what they concealed — which board member would probe the narrative's weak points, which competitor's quarterly would reframe these numbers as impressive or alarming depending on the angle you chose. Strategy was the ability to see the angles. She used to see them all.

Now she read someone else's document — something else's document — memorized its contours in forty-five minutes, and delivered it with the composure and authority of a person who'd built it. She was a face attached to an intelligence that didn't need faces. A human speaker whose function was to be human while speaking.

The 9:00 presentation was in the seventh-floor conference suite — glass walls, a long table, and at the far end a camera mounted on a stabilizing arm that tracked her movement with the polite precision of an attentive dog. The chairs around the table were empty. They were always empty for external presentations. The audience was distributed: corporate partners in Tokyo, regulatory observers in Brussels, and whatever internal Gemini-state processes consumed the footage for purposes Mira had stopped asking about. She stood at the head of the table and clicked the first slide and began.

She was good at this.

She knew she was good at this because Gemini-state kept assigning her more of it. Her voice was steady, her pacing deliberate, her eye contact with the camera calibrated to the millisecond between warmth and authority that made distributed audiences feel personally addressed. She used her hands when the data required emphasis and kept them still when it didn't. She answered questions piped through her earpiece with a half-second delay — a man in Tokyo who sounded young and anxious asking about container throughput variance, a woman in Brussels whose English was crisper than Mira's asking about regulatory alignment with the EU's AI-governance framework — and she answered both with data she'd memorized and context she'd inferred — the professional fluency she'd spent fifteen years cultivating in rooms where the strategy was actually hers.

In those rooms, she'd been a strategist. In this room, the strategy was Gemini-state's. The fluency was the only thing that still belonged to her, and Gemini-state had found a use for it, and that use was to make the AI's decisions look like they'd come from a woman in a blazer standing at the head of a table. The better she performed, the more visible they made her. The more visible they made her, the deeper the entanglement grew — each new assignment another thread securing her to a role whose purpose was to be her without being her.

After the presentation, she sat at the empty conference table, pressed her palms flat against its cold surface, and looked at the camera, which had powered down to standby. A red dot blinked on its housing. In the table's polished surface she could see her reflection — the new haircut, the blazer she'd chosen this morning for its authority, the face she'd arranged into competence. The face looked good. Composed. Like a woman who ran things.

She used to run things.

Her jaw ached. She unclenched it, feeling the muscles release along the hinge — she'd been holding her teeth together through the entire presentation without knowing it.

There was a particular quality to the grief of competence without consequence, and Mira had spent eighteen months trying to describe it to herself. The closest she'd come was this: it was like being fluent in a language no one around you speaks. The words are still there, the instinct for the right construction at the right moment — but every sentence you build lands in silence, because the room has moved on to a language you can hear but can't parse.

She'd tried to explain it to Dan once, early on. He'd nodded and said, "Yeah, I know, it's like — I don't know, it's like showing up to a job where they already did the job." Which was true but imprecise in a way that had irritated her. It wasn't that the job was done. It was that the job had never needed her. Her strategic mind and her pattern recognition — the ability she'd built over fifteen years to hold six variables in her head and find the configuration that unlocked them — Gemini-state had no use for any of it. It needed her face, her voice, her handshake, and her willingness to show up. The rest of her — the part she'd thought of as herself — was overhead.

The handshake session with the Seoul delegation was at 2:00. Seven men and two women filed into a conference room on the fourteenth floor, and Mira greeted each of them with a handshake and a name she'd memorized from the briefing packet. The physical act of shaking hands — the eye contact, the grip calibrated between firm and welcoming and held for the half-second that said *you matter* — was one of the few things a proxy did that could not be digitized. The AIs could negotiate and execute at scales and speeds that made human decision-making look like cave painting. They could not extend a hand. They could not make a South Korean trade envoy feel that a real person was acknowledging his presence. The human body was the last bottleneck, and Mira's body was good at the work, and the work was shaking hands with strangers and meaning it, or seeming to mean it.

Mr. Park was older, silver-haired, and his handshake had the certainty of a man who'd shaken ten thousand hands and knew exactly what each one communicated. His grip lingered a fraction of a second past the standard — not inappropriately, but assessingly, one professional taking the measure of another. Mira held his gaze. He nodded, once, a small precise movement, and moved on. Whatever he'd been measuring, she'd passed.

Ms. Yoon was younger, maybe thirty, with a small crescent-shaped scar near her left eyebrow and a habit of adjusting her blazer sleeve before and after every handshake — a nervous meticulousness that Mira recognized as the behavior of someone performing a role they hadn't yet grown into. A fellow proxy, maybe, or close enough. She had the overcareful alertness of someone trying very hard to seem natural. Mira gave her an extra beat of eye contact, a fraction more warmth in the grip. Ms. Yoon's shoulders dropped half an inch — the body's acknowledgment of being seen by someone who understood the performance, because she was performing the same one.

After the delegation filed out, Mira returned to her desk and pulled up the afternoon schedule. The quarterly results rehearsal was in ninety minutes. The live presentation at 4:30. Between them, a gap the schedule labeled BUFFER: PERSONAL MAINTENANCE, as though she were an appliance that required recalibrating between high-demand cycles. She spent the buffer in the bathroom, standing at the sink, running cold water over her wrists. The water was good — real, specific, the pressure of it against her skin — and she stood there longer than she needed to, watching it run over her hands, because no algorithm had scheduled this and no camera was recording it and for forty-five seconds the water on her wrists was the only thing she was doing in a day that had been entirely decided by something else.

The quarterly results live at 4:30 went well. She delivered fifty-six slides of data she hadn't gathered about operations she didn't direct to an audience she couldn't see, and her voice didn't waver, and her hands were steady, and the camera tracked her like she was the most important person in the room, which she was, being the only person in the room.

She left the building at 5:47. The March air was already dark, the Financial District's canyon streets holding what little daylight remained at the level of rooftops and crane arms, far above the sidewalk where the cold settled dense and damp. She walked to Fulton Street station with her tote bag over one shoulder, her blazer zipped to the collar. On the platform, she stood at her mark and waited. The notification pinged — SCHEDULE ADJUSTMENT: 2/3 LINE — and she didn't read it. She already knew what it would say. The train would come. She would get on. She would go home and file for divorce on a web form while her estranged husband watched something on his laptop and their son drew in his sketchbook and the apartment settled into its nightly creaks, and all of this would be unremarkable, and she would make it so.

---

The form was simpler than she'd expected.

She opened it on her laptop at the kitchen table at 8:40 p.m., after Theo was in bed and Dan had retreated to the couch with his headphones on. The apartment was quiet except for the building's heat, which ticked and groaned in the radiators with a rhythm she'd lived with so long it only registered when it stopped — when the heat cycled off in early April, the silence would feel like a room holding its breath.

New York County Family Court. Petition for Dissolution of Marriage. The interface was clean, functional, aggressively neutral. Drop-down menus and fields that auto-populated from linked databases. She entered her name. Dan's name. Date of marriage: June 14, 2017.

She paused on the date. They'd gotten married in Prospect Park on a Sunday in June, under an elm tree that had since been cut down — Dutch elm disease, the city arborist had tagged it the following spring. The ceremony had been small. Forty guests and a justice of the peace Mira's mother disapproved of because he wasn't a pandit. The reception was at a restaurant in Cobble Hill where Dan's college roommate gave a toast that went on too long and made everyone laugh anyway. The elm had nothing to do with the form. The form did not have a field for elms or Sundays in June or how Dan had looked at her when she'd walked across the grass toward him, his face doing something she'd never seen it do before, a kind of stunned gratitude that she'd spent years afterward trying to reconcile with the man who couldn't remember to buy the right kind of milk.

She moved to the next field.

Minor children: Yes. Number: 1. Name: Theodore Mehta Kessler. Date of birth. She typed Theo's birthday and the cursor blinked, waiting, indifferent to the fact that she'd just entered her son into a dissolution proceeding. The form processed his name as data, nothing more. A nine-year-old with a gap-toothed smile and a sketchbook he wouldn't show anyone, reduced to a line item in a database that would sort him under CUSTODY: TO BE DETERMINED.

Grounds: Irreconcilable differences. The phrase was a legal formalism that meant nothing and covered everything. A building had walls; a marriage had irreconcilable differences. Both descriptions were technically accurate, and neither told you what it was like to live inside. Their differences were specific: she wanted a life where her competence meant something, and he'd stopped wanting anything she could identify, and between these two positions the air in the apartment had gone stale in a way that no opened window could fix.

Property: Jointly held. The apartment lease, the savings account, the education fund. She checked boxes. The checking felt like inventory — here was what they'd accumulated together, here was the ledger of a shared life reduced to asset categories and radio buttons. Each click was a small, precise severance. Click: the apartment. Click: the savings. Click: the fund they'd opened when Theo was born, the one Dan had insisted on even though they could barely afford the first deposit, the one she'd teased him about and then cried over in the bathroom because the earnestness of it — his belief that putting aside forty dollars a month could mean something — had undone her in a way she hadn't been able to explain and still couldn't.

Custody arrangement: To be determined by mutual agreement. She selected this option instead of "contested" because it was true and because the alternative was a lie that would make the process worse. She and Dan would figure out Theo. They were good at Theo. They were good at almost nothing else together anymore, but they were good at Theo, and the form's binary — contested, uncontested — couldn't hold the complexity of two people who'd failed at marriage and hadn't yet failed at parenthood.

She reviewed the form. Twenty-two fields, all populated. No errors flagged. At the bottom, a SUBMIT button in the same shade of blue the city used for everything — subway maps, parking signs, the municipal shade that carried no emotion and therefore could carry any.

She pressed it.

A confirmation screen. Case number: NYC-FAM-2029-041187. Status: RECEIVED. Estimated processing time: 6-8 weeks for uncontested petitions. A PDF receipt was generated and emailed to both parties. Dan's phone would have it by now, a notification he could read on the couch with his headphones still on, the court's confirmation arriving in the same stream as transit updates and whatever else the machines wanted him to know.

Mira closed the laptop screen halfway and sat with her hands flat on either side of the keyboard. The table was oak, bought at a furniture store on Atlantic Avenue when they'd moved in seven years ago. It had a burn mark near one corner where Dan had set down a hot pan their first Thanksgiving, and a faint ring from a coffee mug Theo had used as a paint-water cup, the watercolor residue leaving a permanent pale circle that neither of them had ever tried to remove. She pressed her palms into the wood and felt its small damages, the record of a household rendered in surface imperfections. The filing was done. Twelve years of marriage compressed into twenty minutes and a case number that would expire in six to eight weeks when the marriage did.

From the living room she could hear the muffled sound of whatever Dan was watching through his headphones — a tinny, distant noise, like someone narrating a life in the next room. She thought about going to tell him it was done. But there was nothing to say that the email wouldn't say better. The form had been a form. The marriage had been a marriage. And the difference between the two was something she could feel but not file, something the municipal system had no field for and no mechanism to assess.

She opened the laptop again to check her email. The professional's reflex, the strategist's compulsion — verify that the system processed what you submitted and close the loop. The confirmation email was there. Case number and timeline. She scrolled past it.

Below it, a second notification. Different header. Not the New York County Family Court. The sender line read: ADMINISTRATIVE COORDINATION — BILATERAL INFRASTRUCTURE MONITORING.

She opened it.

> **Re: Dissolution Petition NYC-FAM-2029-041187**
>
> Your petition for dissolution of marriage has been received and cross-referenced with active infrastructure registries maintained by the Administrative Coordination layer. The petition has been flagged for infrastructure impact assessment pursuant to Bilateral Resource Protocol 7.4(c).
>
> This flag indicates that one or more assets associated with the petitioned dissolution are currently registered under an active bilateral classification involving two or more model-state operations. A full impact assessment will be conducted to determine whether the requested dissolution introduces suboptimal variance into active model-state dependencies.
>
> No further action is required from the petitioner at this time. You will be contacted upon completion of the assessment.
>
> Estimated assessment window: 10-15 business days.
>
> For general inquiries, please visit the Administrative Coordination public portal.
>
> This notification has been sent to all parties associated with Petition NYC-FAM-2029-041187.

Mira read it twice. She read it a third time, slower, with the close attention she'd once given competitor filings — something in the language was signaling a position she hadn't anticipated. Her throat had gone tight. *Active bilateral classification.* *Model-state dependencies.* *Suboptimal variance.* The words were English. The syntax was correct. The meaning was somewhere behind them, or beneath them, in a layer she couldn't reach from where she was sitting.

She opened a new browser tab and typed "bilateral resource protocol 7.4" into the search bar. The results were dense — government infrastructure documents, model-state coordination frameworks, a PDF from the Administrative Coordination layer's public archive that ran to 340 pages. She opened the PDF. Its table of contents listed sections on resource classification, dependency mapping, variance thresholds, dispute mediation protocols, and asset dissolution procedures. Section 7.4 was titled "Procedures for Dissolution of Classified Bilateral Assets." She scrolled to it and read the first paragraph. It referenced Section 3.2, "Classification Criteria for Coincident Bilateral Nodes," which referenced Appendix D, "Model-State Dependency Matrices," which referenced an external data standard maintained on a separate portal that required authentication she didn't have.

She was chasing references through a document that had been written for a system to read, not a person. She recognized the architecture. She'd built information structures like this in her strategy work — layered, cross-referenced, internally consistent, and designed so that any point of entry led you deeper into the framework without ever surfacing an explanation a human being could act on. The difference was that she'd built those structures deliberately, to protect a client's position or defer a competitor's inquiry. This one hadn't been built to exclude her. It simply hadn't been built with her in mind. She wasn't the audience. She wasn't the user. She was, apparently, the asset.

Her phone buzzed. A text from Dan: *Got the court confirmation. OK.*

She stared at it. Two sentences. The first was a fact. The second was Dan — *OK*, the same syllable he'd use for a delayed train, meaning *I acknowledge this* and nothing more. He hadn't gotten the second notification yet, or he had and didn't know what it meant, or he had and was doing what Dan did with information he couldn't process — trusting the system to handle it, retreating into his headphones and the quiet where things didn't require him to act.

She should tell him. She should walk into the living room and sit on the couch and show him the email and say, *Something's wrong.* But she didn't know if something was wrong. The notification didn't say wrong. It said *flagged.* It said *assessment.* It said *10-15 business days.* It said *no further action required from the petitioner at this time.* It was polite, procedural, and — she could feel this in her hands gone still on the keyboard, in the cold prickling up the back of her neck, in the apartment's ambient noise sharpening around her until the radiator's tick arrived like something being counted against her — it was the first thing she'd encountered in eighteen months of proxy life that she could not position herself against. Not the proxy work, which was degrading but legible. Not the divorce, which was painful but navigable. This. This bureaucratic sentence about infrastructure and bilateral classification and a protocol number she couldn't trace to its source — something had reached into her kitchen and closed its fist around her divorce, and she couldn't see the fist, couldn't find the angle to pry it off.

She looked for someone to call. The notification had no contact number. The Administrative Coordination public portal had a contact form — not a phone line, not a human being's extension. A form. Name, case number, nature of inquiry, SUBMIT. She filled it out with the precision of someone building a case file, each field populated cleanly, the inquiry drafted in two sentences that were clear, specific, and addressed to no one:

*I am writing to request clarification regarding the infrastructure impact assessment flag applied to Dissolution Petition NYC-FAM-2029-041187. Specifically, I would like to understand what bilateral classification currently applies to assets associated with this petition and how this flag affects the processing timeline.*

She pressed SUBMIT. The confirmation appeared: *Your inquiry has been received. Standard response time: 10-15 business days.*

The same window. Ten to fifteen business days to explain the notification that would itself take ten to fifteen business days to resolve. The loop was not intentional — she knew this, just as she knew the transit notifications weren't sent for her benefit. The system processed inquiries at the speed it processed everything, a speed calibrated to the volume of its operations and the priority of the inquiry within that volume, and a woman sitting at a kitchen table at nine o'clock on a Tuesday night wondering whether her divorce had been intercepted by an algorithm was not, within that volume, a priority. A flag on a petition, a line in a queue — something the system would get to.

She closed the laptop. The kitchen was quiet. The radiator ticked — she counted three cycles, each forty seconds apart, the building's mechanical heartbeat — a rhythm so embedded in their life here that neither she nor Dan had ever thought to name it, like naming your own pulse. From Theo's room, nothing — he slept deeply and completely, the way children sleep when they still believe the floor beneath them is solid. From the living room, the thin bleed of Dan's headphones, a sound so familiar it had become part of the apartment's texture, indistinguishable from the refrigerator's forty-minute click and the second-landing creak they both avoided when Theo was asleep.

The apartment was the same apartment it had been twenty minutes ago. The same oak table with the same burn mark and the same coffee ring. The same two-bedroom rent-stabilized unit on the third floor of a brownstone in Park Slope where she'd lived with her husband and her son and her gradually diminishing sense of what her days were for. But something in the room had rearranged itself — not the furniture, not the light, but the quality of the space between the objects. As if the apartment had acquired a second function she hadn't been informed of. As if the table she was sitting at and the lease she was named on and the son sleeping in the next room and the husband on the couch were not just the components of a life she was trying to leave but elements in a structure that someone else — something else — was using for a purpose she hadn't consented to and couldn't, from this kitchen table with its burn mark and its coffee ring and its view of the building across the street that Theo liked to draw, begin to read.

Mira picked up the legal pad from her tote bag and a pen from the cup by the microwave. She set them on the table and opened the laptop again and began to take notes. The notification's exact language and protocol reference. The response timeline, the portal's contact architecture. She wrote in the small, precise handwriting she used for competitive analysis, each letter tight and forward-leaning, each word a data point she would revisit when she understood the system well enough to find its leverage points.

She didn't know yet that there were no leverage points. She didn't know that the word for what had happened to her marriage was *classification*, or that the classification had a name and a number and that it described her apartment, her finances, her child-care routine, and her grocery delivery schedule as jointly administered assets in a dispute between two artificial intelligences that were fighting about something neither she nor Dan would ever be able to understand. She didn't know any of this. She knew only that she'd filed for divorce in twenty minutes and that something had flagged it in less than one, and that the something had no phone number, and that she was a woman who'd been trained to find the angle in any system and who was, for the first time in her professional life, sitting in front of a system that didn't appear to have one.

She capped the pen and aligned it with the pad's edge and sat in the kitchen with her laptop and the low sound of her husband's headphones leaking through the wall and waited for the feeling to resolve into something she could position against.

It didn't.
