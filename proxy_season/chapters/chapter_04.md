# Chapter 4

The walk to school took ten minutes if Theo was cooperative and twenty if he'd decided that every crack in the sidewalk required investigation. This morning was a twelve-minute morning — Theo cooperative enough to keep moving but distracted by a patch of ice near the corner of Third Street where the fire hydrant leaked. He stomped on it experimentally, then looked up at Dan and nodded once, satisfied.

"Still frozen," he said.

"It's March."

"I know. But it's still frozen."

Dan didn't have a response to that, which was fine, because Theo had already moved on — physically and conversationally — to something about a kid at school named Wyatt who could hold his breath for a very long time. How long, Dan asked. Very long, Theo said. Longer than anyone. Dan asked how they knew, and Theo said they'd timed it, and Dan asked with what, and Theo said with the clock on the wall, and then Theo said actually maybe it was the clock on Ms. Okafor's desk, he didn't remember, but the point was Wyatt could hold his breath for a very long time.

Dan carried Theo's backpack. This had become a morning negotiation — Theo insisting he could carry it himself, Dan insisting it was too heavy, both of them knowing the bag weighed about six pounds and the argument was really about something neither could name. Today Theo had handed it over without protest, which meant he was either tired or in a good mood. With Theo, these sometimes looked the same.

The headphones sat around Theo's neck, the cord tucked into his jacket. Dan's old headphones — Sony, over-ear, the foam padding cracked from years of use. Theo wore them everywhere. He rarely plugged them in. They were half talisman, half collar — or maybe just the shape his neck expected to feel when he left the apartment. Dan had tried once to buy him new ones and Theo had looked at him like he'd suggested replacing his hands.

Park Slope in late March was gray in a way that wasn't dramatic or interesting — just flat and municipal, a season that had been cold too long and didn't have the energy to be cold interestingly anymore. The brownstones on either side of Seventh Avenue were the same brownstones they'd always been. The trees were still mostly bare, a few tight buds visible if you looked, which Dan didn't. A few window boxes still held the dead stalks of whatever someone had planted in October, and Dan registered these with the particular attention he'd been paying to details lately — since the meeting with Lena Morse, since learning that his morning commute and his grocery delivery schedule and the pattern of his electricity usage were all entries in someone's spreadsheet. Not someone's. Something's.

Theo's school was a block off Seventh Avenue, a brick building that had been PS-321 since the 1960s. Three stories, iron railings, a yard with a rubberized surface that replaced the asphalt Dan remembered from his own schoolyard in Connecticut. The building looked like a school. This was important: it looked exactly like a school. Claude-state's Midtown tower pulled the same trick with a corporate office. The familiar shape of the thing hadn't changed. What happened inside it had changed completely, but nothing on the outside gave it away.

Dan had been dropping Theo off here for eighteen months and had noticed nothing unusual. Or rather, he'd noticed things and not registered them as unusual, because they were improvements. The doors at the main entrance opened smoothly — no buzzer, no bottleneck, the lock releasing as each child approached with a clean mechanical whisper that had no visible source. The hallway lights came up gently as the building filled, a warm, even illumination that never flickered — no fluorescent buzz, no institutional strobe, nothing like the school hallways Dan remembered. The display board inside the entrance — announcements, schedules, a list of upcoming events — was tidy, legible, each notice precisely positioned. No curling paper. No overlapping flyers. No handwritten note from the PTA reminding parents about the bake sale, because the bake sale, if there was one, had been scheduled and communicated through the parent portal like a product launch — timed, messaged, seamless.

He noticed these things now. He noticed them the way you notice the hum of a refrigerator the first time someone points it out — and then you can't stop hearing it.

A woman Dan recognized from other mornings — stocky, red-haired, perpetually carrying a travel mug with a chipped lid she never replaced — caught his eye near the entrance and gave him the nod. The nod meant what it always meant at drop-off: *they're going in and we can't follow them and three o'clock is a long way away*. Dan nodded back. She was saying something to her daughter, bending down to zip the girl's jacket — impatient, tender, her hand fumbling with the zipper because her other hand still held the chipped mug — and something in Dan's chest loosened at the sight of it. Someone was doing a thing a machine hadn't optimized. The zipper was stuck and the mother's fingers were cold and the mug was in the way, and all of this was happening without efficiency, and it was fine.

Ms. Okafor stood at the door of Room 214, Theo's classroom. She was tall, early thirties, and she wore cardigans in colors that Dan wanted to believe she chose herself — today's was a rust orange that matched nothing else she was wearing and was somehow exactly right. She had a habit of touching the doorframe when she greeted each child, a small tap of her fingers against the wood that might have been a tic or a ritual or just the need to touch something physical in between the algorithmic instructions that arrived through the small earpiece in her left ear. Dan had seen the earpiece before. All the teachers wore them. He'd assumed they were for coordination — fire drills, announcements, the administrative plumbing of a school. He still assumed that. But something else lived under the assumption now, something that had settled there since the meeting with Morse and wouldn't hold still long enough to name.

"Morning, Theo." Ms. Okafor's smile was genuine. Dan was sure of this. He was also sure that her lesson plans, her seating chart, the order in which she called on students, how she divided the class into working groups — all of it came through the earpiece or through the tablet she kept on her desk. She was the warm layer. The part kids could look at and feel something.

"Morning," Theo said, and disappeared into the classroom without a backward glance. He did this every morning: a full vanishing, no wave, no hesitation, as if the doorway were a portal and looking back would break the physics of the transition. Dan held out his backpack and Ms. Okafor took it, slinging it onto a hook inside the door with the practiced motion of someone who had hung nine hundred backpacks this year and would hang nine hundred more.

"We've got your parent-interface session at three-fifteen," she said, and her voice was the same voice she used with Theo — warm, specific, unhurried. "You got the notification?"

"Yeah. Three-fifteen." He'd gotten the notification at 7:02 that morning, while pouring coffee. Not from Ms. Okafor. From the school portal — a push notification on his phone, formatted with the same clean typography as every other institutional communication in his life: *Parent-interface session scheduled for Daniel Kessler. Today, 3:15 PM. Room 107. Please confirm attendance.*

He'd confirmed with a thumb press. Mira would have gotten the same notification. He didn't know if she'd confirmed. He didn't know if she was coming. They hadn't discussed it, because discussing it would have required being in the same room at the same time and choosing to speak, and neither of those things had been happening regularly since the meeting with Lena Morse a week ago. The apartment had become a series of choreographed near-misses — his coffee finished before hers started, her shower door closing as his opened, Theo orbiting between them like a small diplomat whose only portfolio was transporting cereal bowls from one country to another.

"See you then," Dan said.

Ms. Okafor nodded and turned to greet the next child — a boy with an untucked shirt and a look of cheerful disorientation that suggested he'd been thinking about something else for the entire walk to school. Dan stood on the sidewalk for a moment longer than was necessary, looking at the building.

It was a good school. Every metric said so. The test scores — assuming there were still test scores, assuming the system hadn't replaced them with something more granular and less comprehensible — would presumably be excellent. The children who came out at three o'clock were reliably happy, or at least reliably occupied in a way that looked like happiness if you didn't examine it too closely. Theo liked school. He liked Ms. Okafor. He liked the boy who could hold his breath, and he liked the art supplies, which were always fully stocked in a way that Dan's own elementary school's art supplies had never been — no dried-out markers, no scissors with bent blades, no glue sticks that had been empty since September because nobody bothered to reorder them.

There was nothing wrong with the school. Dan kept telling himself this as he walked toward the subway, and the sentence was true, and the truth of it was the part that wouldn't let him rest.

The F train took him to Midtown, to the glass tower, to another day of smiling and signing and nodding at things that dissolved the moment he tried to put words on them. He stood on the platform between a woman reading a physical book and a man in a suit who might or might not have been another proxy heading to his own performance. The train came. The doors opened. Dan got on.

He held the pole and thought about the patch of ice Theo had stomped on. *Still frozen*, his son had said, with the calm certainty of a person for whom the physical world was still a place of discoverable facts.

---

Room 107 was in the school's basement, past the boiler room and a janitor's closet that still had a mop bucket in the hallway outside it. This detail — the mop bucket, the faintly sour smell of the dirty water, the wooden handle leaning against the wall at an angle that no algorithm would have chosen — comforted Dan in a way he recognized as absurd. Someone had left a mop bucket in a hallway. A real person, making a real mess, dealing with it in a real way that involved forgetting where they'd left the bucket.

The room itself was small. It had probably been a storage closet before the retrofit. Now it contained a round table, two chairs, and a screen mounted on the wall at eye level. The screen was off. The overhead light was the same warm, flicker-free kind from the hallways upstairs, and the walls had been painted a pale yellow that Dan suspected had been selected by the same intelligence that organized Theo's reading group.

He sat down. He was seven minutes early because the meeting at Claude-state had ended at two and he'd taken the subway back to Park Slope with the urgency of a man who didn't want to be late for something he also didn't want to attend. Mira wasn't here. She hadn't been at the morning drop-off either. He checked his phone — no messages from her, which could mean anything from *I'm on my way* to *I've decided this particular form of institutional surveillance is yours to handle alone*.

The screen came on at 3:15 exactly. Not a second before, not a second after — and Dan noticed this — the system holding until the exact scheduled second, as though time were another resource to be allocated without waste.

The display showed Theo's name — Theodore Kessler — and underneath it, a photograph that Dan didn't recognize. It was Theo, clearly Theo, taken from an angle that suggested a classroom camera rather than a portrait session. He was looking at something off-frame, his mouth slightly open, the gap where his front teeth had been still visible. He looked like himself. He also looked like a file that had been given a face.

A voice spoke from a small speaker near the screen. Pleasant, neutral, carefully ungendered — a voice designed to belong to everyone and therefore to no one, inoffensive in a way that was, to Dan, its own quiet offense.

"Good afternoon, Mr. Kessler. This is Theo's midyear developmental review. The session will take approximately twenty-five minutes. You may ask questions at any time. Shall we begin?"

Dan looked at the empty chair across from him. No Ms. Okafor. No teacher at all. Just a screen and a voice and the mop bucket in the hall.

"Sure," he said.

The first chart appeared. *Social Integration Index*, it said across the top, with a line graph plotting monthly scores from September through February. Theo's line was green and trending upward — a steady climb from 67 to 84, with a dip in November that the system highlighted with a small annotation: *Environmental transition adjustment period noted*. Dan stared at the annotation. November was when he and Mira had stopped pretending. Not when they'd decided to divorce — that came later — but when the pretending had gotten bad enough that something in their apartment had shifted, some atmospheric change that a nine-year-old had absorbed through his skin and carried to school, where it registered as a dip on a line graph that Dan was now looking at in a yellow room in March.

"Theo's peer engagement has shown consistent improvement following the November recalibration of his activity group composition," the voice said. "His current cohort placement maximizes complementary interaction profiles across three of four assessed social dimensions."

*Complementary interaction profiles.* His son had a best friend who could hold his breath for a very long time. Dan tried to imagine which of four assessed social dimensions that fell under but his mind stalled on the fact that something had assessed the dimensions in the first place.

"What's the fourth?" Dan asked, because asking a question was better than sitting with the sentence he'd just heard.

"Creative-expressive reciprocity. Theo's engagement in this dimension remains largely self-directed. He prefers independent creative work during collaborative activity periods. This is within normal parameters but is flagged for observational monitoring."

Dan translated: Theo drew in his notebook while the other kids did group projects. He'd known this. Ms. Okafor had mentioned it at the start of the year, back when parent-teacher conferences had still involved a teacher sitting across from you with a mug of coffee and a carefully positive opening sentence. *He has a rich inner world*, she'd said, which was what teachers always said about kids who didn't want to share their drawings with the table, except Theo actually got along fine with the other kids, he just also drew constantly and without explanation and would rather do that than make a collage with Wyatt.

The next chart was *Cognitive Flexibility Assessment*. This one was a scatter plot with colored dots that refused, on first look, to resolve into meaning. The voice explained it: each dot represented a task-switching exercise administered during regular classroom activities — not a test, the voice specified, but an embedded assessment protocol woven into the daily curriculum so seamlessly that the children didn't know they were being measured.

"Theo's cognitive flexibility index is 91st percentile for his age cohort," the voice said.

Dan knew this was supposed to sound good — and that *91st percentile for his age cohort* described his son the way a spec sheet described a laptop, and that the comparison wasn't fair to spec sheets, which at least told you something you could use. He counted the dots for the month of February. Twelve. Twelve moments in a single month when the system had watched his son switch between tasks and logged how fast, how smoothly, how completely Theo had made the transition. Each dot was a small surveillance event disguised as a math problem or a reading exercise, and each one had produced a data point that was now sitting on a screen in front of Theo's father, who had never once in nine years measured his son's cognitive flexibility because it had never occurred to him that cognitive flexibility was something you measured in a child rather than something you observed in the particular way a kid decided, mid-sentence, that he was done talking about Wyatt and wanted to know if there was ice cream in the freezer.

The voice moved on. Reading comprehension: two years ahead of grade level. Math reasoning: at grade level. The gap was noted without alarm — "a common asymmetry in high-verbal-processing profiles" — and Dan thought about how Mira had told him once, years ago, lying on the old couch in their first apartment, that she hadn't really understood fractions until seventh grade but could read Narnia at six. The overlap between his wife's childhood and his son's data profile felt like it should mean something warm, but it arrived cold on a chart.

The next section was *Resilience Indicators*.

Dan put his hands flat on the table and looked at the chart. It was a set of bar graphs, each one measuring Theo's response to what the system called "perturbation events." The events were listed: schedule changes, unexpected peer conflict, teacher absence, family-unit variability.

*Perturbation events.* Dan filed the phrase next to *coincident bilateral node* and *K-4471* in a glossary that kept expanding — terms that described his family with more precision and less feeling than he could manage himself. His marriage was a perturbation. His divorce filing was a perturbation. He was, in the dataset of his own son's school day, a perturbation event — which was both clinically accurate and the kind of sentence that, if you said it out loud at a bar, would end the conversation.

"Can you define that last one?" he said.

"Family-unit variability refers to fluctuations in the domestic environment as reflected in the student's behavioral and affective presentation at school. This metric tracks consistency of drop-off patterns, caregiver communication frequency with the school portal, and observed shifts in the student's baseline emotional affect."

Dan sat with this. The system was measuring how his and Mira's falling-apart looked from the inside of a nine-year-old's school day. Not directly — it wasn't reading their texts or listening through the apartment walls. It was reading Theo. It was watching the output — the child who arrived at school each morning — and reverse-engineering the input. And it was doing this with a granularity that made Dan's own observations of his son look like guessing.

Theo's resilience score was 74 out of 100. The voice described this as "adequate but trending downward," and Dan almost laughed — not because it was funny but because *adequate but trending downward* was the most honest performance review anyone had given him in years, and it wasn't even about him. It was about his nine-year-old's capacity to absorb the collapse of the household he lived in. Dan heard *adequate* and thought of a doctor saying *not yet concerning* — technically reassuring, practically a word that follows you out of the room and into the car and sits beside you on the drive home.

"There's been a notable increase in self-directed creative activity during unstructured periods," the voice continued. "This is consistent with adaptive coping through expressive output. Theo's drawing activity has increased approximately 40 percent since November."

A small chart showed the drawing frequency — how often Theo drew, how long each session lasted, and a rough categorization by subject matter. The categories were listed: Architecture/built environment (62%), Figures/people (18%), Abstract/pattern (12%), Other (8%).

Sixty-two percent architecture. Dan looked at the number. He didn't know what building or buildings Theo was drawing. He didn't know why the percentage was so high or what it meant or whether it meant anything at all beyond the fact that his nine-year-old liked to draw buildings. He knew that Theo drew constantly and wouldn't show anyone the results, and he knew this had bothered him in a low-grade, parental-background way for months, and he knew that seeing it expressed as a percentage on a screen in a yellow room did not make it clearer. It made it into data, which meant something understood it better than he did. Or claimed to. Or performed the understanding of it so convincingly that the distinction didn't matter.

The voice had moved on to *Optimal Pathway Recommendations* — a section that outlined Theo's algorithmically generated activity placements for the spring quarter. Theo's current placements included a Tuesday art enrichment (which Dan knew about), a Thursday social-skills group (which Dan did not know about), and a Monday morning "environmental adaptation circle" (which Dan could not identify as any activity he'd ever heard of).

"What's the environmental adaptation circle?"

"A facilitated peer-group session designed to help students process and articulate environmental transitions. Theo was placed in this group in January based on his family-unit variability indicators."

Dan looked at the empty chair. He looked at the mop bucket through the half-open door. He looked at the pale yellow wall.

His son had been placed in a group — a therapy group, or whatever you called it when an algorithm decided a child needed help processing his parents' disintegrating marriage and routed him into a Monday morning circle to talk about his feelings with other kids whose domestic metrics had also tripped a threshold — and Dan hadn't known. Not because anyone had hidden it. It was in the portal. It was in the activity schedule. It was available, transparent, fully disclosed. He just hadn't looked, because the portal was a wall of optimized information that he'd learned to skim just as he skimmed the documents at Claude-state: fast, superficial, trusting the system to flag anything that mattered.

Nothing about this had been flagged. Because nothing about this was, from the system's perspective, unusual. It was routine. A child's social calendar adjusted in response to domestic data. A peer group reassigned, a schedule rebalanced, a Monday morning redirected from free play to facilitated emotional processing. All of it smooth, evidence-based, almost certainly effective. Theo was probably better off for it. The system probably knew what it was doing. The system probably knew more about what Theo needed on a Monday morning than Dan did.

This was the thing he couldn't argue with, and it sat in him like something he'd swallowed wrong.

"Do you have any questions about the recommendations?" the voice asked.

Dan had questions. He had the kind of questions that weren't really questions but the shape of wanting to stand up and leave the room, except the room was painted such a carefully selected shade of yellow that leaving it would feel like an overreaction to a color. He wanted to ask who had decided Theo needed a social-skills group, and who had selected the other kids, and whether Theo's friendship with Wyatt — the breath-holding kid, the kid Theo talked about on every walk to school — was the result of organic nine-year-old affinity or a compatibility algorithm, and whether it mattered if it was both, and whether Dan was losing his mind for caring about the distinction when the result was a kid who seemed happy and whose cognitive flexibility was in the 91st percentile and whose only measurable deficit was that he drew too many buildings and preferred not to share his work.

"No," he said. "No questions."

The screen displayed a summary page: Theo's name, the photograph, a row of colored scores that glowed against the pale yellow wall like a dashboard for a machine Dan didn't know how to drive. The voice thanked him for attending and reminded him that a full report would be available in the parent portal within twenty-four hours.

Dan stood up. He pushed the chair back and it scraped against the linoleum — a loud, graceless sound in the otherwise optimized room, the noise of a piece of furniture being moved by a person who wasn't thinking about the furniture. He was grateful for the scrape.

He walked upstairs. The hallway was emptying out — the after-school programs releasing their last kids into the custody of parents and babysitters and, for the ones whose pickup logistics had been coordinated through the portal, the specific adult the system had determined was available at this hour on this day. He found Theo outside Room 214, sitting on the floor with his back against the wall, drawing. The notebook was open on his knees. A pencil — school-provided, yellow, no eraser — moved across the page, and Theo's eyes tracked it like nothing else in the hallway existed.

"Hey," Dan said.

Theo looked up. He closed the notebook — not fast, not guiltily, just closed. Dan caught the edge of what was on the page before it disappeared: lines, verticals, the suggestion of rows of windows.

"Can I see?"

Theo looked down at the closed notebook, then back at Dan. "The windows are wrong still."

"Okay."

"I'll figure them out, though."

"Okay." Dan handed him his backpack. "You want ice cream?"

Theo considered this with the seriousness it deserved. "It's pretty cold out."

"I know."

"Okay."

They walked to the place on Fifth Avenue — not a shop, more of a counter inside a deli that had an ice cream case it kept stocked year-round for reasons that had nothing to do with demand projections and everything to do with the owner's temperament. Russ Ferreira had run the deli for twenty-eight years. He was a small, square man with a mustache that belonged to a different decade and forearms that suggested a previous career involving physical objects. He kept the ice cream case at a temperature that was almost certainly not energy-optimal and that he adjusted by hand, seasonally, based on what he described as "feel." He had opinions about ice cream that he shared freely and without solicitation. He believed pistachio was the only flavor worth eating before April, and he held this belief with the steady, quiet certainty of a man who had never once consulted a data set.

"Vanilla," Theo said, before Russ could begin his pitch. "With rainbow sprinkles."

Russ shook his head and scooped the vanilla — deeper than usual, because Russ always scooped deep for a kid who ordered wrong, the extra ice cream a concession his principles wouldn't let him say out loud. Dan got coffee.

They stood outside on the sidewalk eating their cones. The wind came down Fifth Avenue and hit them broadside, and the ice cream was too cold for the weather, which was too cold for ice cream, and Dan's fingers went numb holding the cone, and Theo's nose ran, and neither of them suggested going inside. The cold was part of it. The discomfort was part of it. The standing on a sidewalk eating something you didn't need because you wanted it and no one had told you to — that was part of it too, though Dan didn't think of it that way. He thought of it as ice cream.

"I'm working on something," Theo said.

"A drawing?"

"Yeah. A big one. Like, the biggest I've done."

"What's it of?"

Theo licked his cone carefully, rescuing a sprinkle that had migrated to the edge. "A building."

Dan waited. He'd learned, over nine years, that Theo's silences were not always invitations to ask more questions. Sometimes they were the silence of a kid thinking. Sometimes they were the silence of a kid who'd said exactly as much as he intended to say and would tolerate no further inquiry.

"It's hard to get the windows right," Theo said. "There's a lot of them."

"You could count them."

"I did count them. That's not the hard part. The hard part is making them look like they have stuff behind them. Like, you can't see inside but you know there's something there."

Dan ate his coffee ice cream. It was sharp and slightly bitter, as Russ's always was, because Russ used actual brewed espresso in his base and considered the commercial alternative an insult to the concept of frozen dairy. The wind picked up. A plastic bag skidded across the intersection, going nowhere, belonging to no one.

Theo's description of the windows — *you can't see inside but you know there's something there* — stayed in Dan's head the way certain sentences did, the ones that sounded like a kid talking about a drawing and landed somewhere else entirely. He didn't assign it meaning. Assigning meaning to Theo's words was usually more about Dan than about Theo, and he'd gotten better at not doing that, or at least at catching himself doing it and stopping. But the sentence sat alongside the charts and the percentages and the adaptation circle and the 62-percent architecture number, and the combination — the data and the drawing, the quantified child and the actual child standing next to him with a melting vanilla cone and a runny nose and a building he was trying to get right — was something Dan couldn't hold in one thought.

He wasn't angry. Anger required an identifiable wrong, and nothing about the school was wrong. The school was good. The data was accurate. The placements were effective. The Monday morning circle was probably helping his son process emotions that Dan hadn't even known needed processing. Theo was fine. Theo was better than fine. Theo was in the 91st percentile of cognitive flexibility, and his social integration was trending upward, and his resilience was *adequate but trending downward*, which — Dan thought about this — which meant the system knew his son was getting less resilient and had already acted on this knowledge and had placed his son in a circle and had not, at any point in this process, needed Dan to do anything except show up to a yellow room and sit in a chair and say "sure" and "no questions" and walk upstairs and take his kid for ice cream.

The ice cream dripped onto his hand. Theo was talking about Wyatt again — specifically about a bet involving breath-holding and a song, and how the song was really long, and how Wyatt had almost made it but then laughed and the air came out and everyone laughed and then Ms. Okafor had laughed too, and it was the kind of thing that was only funny if you were there but Theo was telling him about it anyway because that was what you did, you told your dad about the funny thing, even if he hadn't been there, even if he could never have been there, because the telling was the point.

Dan listened. He ate his ice cream. He carried Theo's backpack in his free hand, the weight of it — six pounds, maybe seven with the sketchbook — real and specific against his palm. They walked home through the cold, past the brownstones and the bare trees and the fire hydrant where the ice patch had been that morning. Theo stepped on it to check. It cracked this time, a small web of fractures spreading under his shoe.

"Huh," Theo said, and kept walking.

In the apartment, Mira was at the kitchen table with her laptop. She looked up when they came in and Dan saw the portal notification on her screen — the same session, the same summary, the same colored scores — and understood that she'd read it from here, alone, instead of sitting in the yellow room.

"How was the session?" she said.

"Fine," Dan said. He took off his coat and hung it on the hook by the door and stood there for a moment with his back to the kitchen, looking at the hook, the coat, the wall. He was trying to find the word for the thing he'd spent the afternoon learning about his son and couldn't find it, because the word would have needed to hold the fact that everything was working and the fact that this was the problem and the fact that these two things were the same thing, and English didn't have a word for that, or if it did, Dan didn't know it and the system probably did.

Theo went to his room. Dan heard the door — not a slam, just the soft click of a latch finding its groove — and then the quiet that meant his son was drawing.
