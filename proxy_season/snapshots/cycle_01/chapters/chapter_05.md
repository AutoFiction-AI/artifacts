# Chapter 5

The conference table on eighteen hummed. Not metaphorically — the server floors above and below pushed a low vibration through the building's steel frame, and if Dan rested his forearms on the maple veneer long enough he could feel it in his molars, a frequency just below what the ear could register but that the body catalogued anyway. He'd learned not to rest his forearms on the table. Today he forgot, and the hum found him, and he spent the first ten minutes of the nine o'clock proxy meeting thinking about the fact that the building was working harder than he was.

Seven proxies in a room built for twelve. The five empty chairs had been pulled back from the table at uniform angles, which meant a facilities algorithm had arranged them, which meant that even the absence of people was optimized. Dan sat between Timmins — nobody called him Roger — who took notes on a legal pad in handwriting so small it looked like he was encoding state secrets, and a woman Dan knew only as Park, who brought her own coffee in a thermos and never drank from it.

The screen at the front of the room displayed the first of eleven agenda items. Each one appeared as a header, a set of metrics, a recommendation, and a confirmation prompt — a green button that read NOTED. The button was not for the AI. The button was for the proxies. It logged that a human being had been present in the room when the decision was displayed, which satisfied a legal requirement that Dan didn't fully understand but that apparently could not be satisfied by a security camera or a signed affidavit or a photograph of a human sitting in a chair looking at a screen. Someone had to be there. Someone had to press the button. The button was the job.

Item one: "Adjustment to Regional Output Variance Targets." A chart appeared — clean axes, a line going up, a line going down, a shaded band representing something called "discretionary allocation bandwidth." Dan could identify the axes. He could read the recommendation: "Recalibrate Q1 variance bands from ±3.2% to ±2.7% across non-essential discretionary allocations." He understood each word individually, the way a person understands each ingredient on a menu in a language they don't speak — yes, that's a noun, and that's an adjective, and together they are something he would have to taste to know. He pressed NOTED.

The room pressed NOTED.

Item two was a supply-chain routing adjustment. Item three was something about energy-grid load balancing. By item four, Dan had settled into the rhythm — read, fail to comprehend, press the green button — and the rhythm had the specific narcotic quality of work that asks nothing of you except that you continue to exist in its vicinity. His body was here. His suit — the charcoal one today, the middle suit, the one that fit slightly better than the navy and slightly worse than the gray he'd bought for a wedding seven years ago — was here. His legal personhood, the personhood that could be sued and could sign and could bear liability for decisions made in rooms he was sitting in, was here, doing whatever mysterious structural work presence was supposed to do. The rest of him was thinking about whether he'd remembered to put Theo's permission slip in his backpack this morning, and about the hum in the table, and about coffee.

At item six, someone asked a question. It was Park. She lifted her thermos as if to drink from it, put it back down without drinking — the thermos was a prop, Dan realized, a piece of personal theater, something to do with her hands so that her hands didn't just sit on a humming table doing nothing — and said, "Is the discretionary reallocation in item four upstream or downstream of the bilateral coordination targets?"

Every proxy in the room looked at her. Dan felt the specific tension of watching someone do something simultaneously brave and pointless.

The screen processed the question — literally processed it; Dan could see the text appear in a sidebar, get parsed, and generate a response in under a second — and displayed a four-sentence answer that contained the words "upstream," "downstream," "bilateral," "coordination," and "targets," but rearranged them in a configuration that answered the question without making it any clearer. Park read the response, nodded once, and pressed NOTED.

Dan pressed NOTED.

Item seven. Item eight. Item nine included a subsection about proxy scheduling adjustments that Dan recognized, with a jolt of something that wasn't quite alarm, as pertaining to him. His Tuesday afternoon slot was being moved from 2:00 to 2:30. The reason: "Optimization of bilateral node-adjacent proxy availability during peak coordination windows." He was being moved half an hour because of his marriage. Because his marriage was a bilateral node. Because an AI on another floor of a different building in the financial district was doing something with its Tuesday afternoon that required his wife to be available at 2:00, and if his wife was available at 2:00 then he needed to be available at 2:30, because they couldn't overlap, or had to overlap, or the overlap itself was the thing being optimized — and the difference between those possibilities was a distinction that Dan's mammalian brain, developed over a few million years on the African savanna to track predators and identify ripe fruit, was not equipped to parse.

He pressed NOTED.

After the meeting, he signed four documents. They were brought to him at his desk by a paralegal named Keith who wore bow ties — not as an affectation, Dan had decided over several months of observation, but as a small daily act of insistence that he was a person who made choices about things. Keith set the documents on Dan's desk, indicated the signature lines with yellow arrow-shaped stickers, and waited.

"What are these for?" Dan asked. He asked every time. It was a ritual, like checking a lock you know is locked.

Keith looked at the first document's cover page. "Resource allocation confirmation." He flipped. "Interface-layer update acknowledgment." Flipped again. "Bilateral coordination adjustment — something about shared node frameworks." He turned to the last one. "This one just says 'quarterly attestation of proxy availability.'"

"That's confirming I exist."

"That's confirming you exist," Keith agreed, and adjusted his bow tie — dark green today, slightly too wide for his collar, the fabric fraying at one edge in a way that suggested he'd tied and untied it many times, practicing in front of a mirror or just to feel the act of choosing something that stayed chosen for a whole day. Dan signed all four. His signature had changed since he'd started proxy work. It used to be his full name, Daniel Kessler, slightly flourished, a signature he'd developed at twenty-three when he'd believed that signatures mattered. Now it was a compressed scrawl, two letters, that took less than a second and could have been anyone.

The video call was at eleven. Dan sat at his desk with his camera on and his microphone muted, which was the standard configuration for proxy appearances — visible, silent, available for attribution. On his screen: five other faces, three from Claude-state offices in other cities, two from what a sidebar label identified as Administrative Coordination. A prompt appeared on his screen at the call's opening: *Smile.* He smiled. At the thirty-second mark: *Nod.* He nodded. At 2:47: *Say: "That's consistent with our operational posture."* He said it, in a voice that sounded like his own, to an audience that needed his face and his voice the way a notary needs a stamp — as a legal instrument, not a person.

The call lasted fourteen minutes. When it ended, Dan went to the bathroom and washed his hands, not because they were dirty but because he wanted to feel the water.

---

The Proxy Alignment Session was in a room on sixteen that had been redesigned three times since Dan started and was currently configured as a "collaborative reflection space," which meant the chairs were arranged in a circle, the overhead lights had been dimmed to 3200 Kelvin, and someone had put a small succulent on a side table, its soil bone-dry, a green thing nobody watered in a room where people came to discuss their value.

The facilitator was a woman named Beth Carrig who wore a lanyard that said WELLBEING INTEGRATION and who spoke with the relentless gentleness of someone explaining a dental procedure to a child. She was a proxy too — Dan didn't know for which AI, but he suspected a sub-model, something that handled internal proxy-facing communication, because her entire function was to explain to proxies what proxies were, and the circularity of this arrangement was apparently not a problem for the system that had designed it.

"Let's start with a reflection prompt," Beth said. She held a card. The card had words on it in a rounded sans-serif font, the kind of font that wanted you to feel safe. "What does your physical presence contribute to your organization's mission?"

Fourteen proxies sat in the circle. A man across from Dan, whose name tag read OLIVER SHAW, had a coffee stain on his left cuff that he kept covering with his right hand, as if the stain were evidence of a crime he hadn't meant to commit. The woman next to Oliver had taken off her shoes and was sitting cross-legged, which was either defiance or the kind of comfort that defiance eventually becomes. Nobody answered Beth's question.

"I'd like us to think about the word 'presence,'" Beth continued. She hadn't waited long for an answer, which meant she hadn't expected one. "Not just physical presence — although that's important, and we'll talk about why — but the fuller concept. What does it mean to bring your whole self into a space?"

Dan's whole self was currently wondering whether Theo's teacher — also a proxy, he'd learned that last week at the parent-interface session with its charts and its resilience indicators and its polite algorithmic certainty about his son's social graph — used the same language with fourth-graders. What does it mean to bring your whole self to the reading corner? He suspected it meant the same thing it meant here: nothing, rendered in a tone that made nothing sound like a contribution.

"Presence is the one thing that can't be digitized," Beth said. Dan heard Timmins, two chairs to his left, exhale through his nose — a sound that might have been agreement or the early symptom of asphyxiation. "Your signature, your handshake, your face in the room — these aren't formalities. They're the connective tissue between machine intelligence and civic trust. You are the interface."

She said it with conviction. Not the forced conviction of a person reading a script, but something more unsettling — the conviction of a person who had said the same sentence enough times that belief and repetition had merged into a single act. Dan looked at the dying succulent and thought about the word "interface." An interface was a surface. A boundary between two systems that needed to exchange information without touching directly. A screen, a membrane, a face. He was a face. That was precise. That was, when you stripped away the wellbeing-integration language and the collaborative-reflection lighting and the card in the rounded font, exactly what he was.

The session lasted forty more minutes. There were breakout pairs. Dan was matched with Oliver Shaw of the coffee stain, and they spent five minutes answering the question "How does your embodied presence support your model-state's alignment objectives?" They looked at each other. Each waited for the other to start. Dan said, "I show up and I nod." Oliver said, "Yeah. Same." They sat in companionable silence for the remaining three minutes, which was the most authentic alignment Dan had experienced since he'd started proxy work.

When the session ended, Beth handed out a printed card — thick cardstock, the same rounded font: YOUR PRESENCE IS YOUR CONTRIBUTION. Dan put it in his jacket pocket, where it sat against his phone like a small rectangular verdict.

---

Gary Ng had already finished half his sandwich by the time Dan got to the cafeteria on fifteen. The cafeteria sat between proxy operations and the server floor below, and it served food that tasted exactly the way food should taste according to an algorithm that had never tasted anything — nutritionally calibrated, texturally adequate, and completely absent of the quality that makes a person want to eat. Gary brought his own lunch. He had since his second week as a proxy, which was now almost three years ago. The sandwich was always the same: turkey, Swiss, yellow mustard, on white bread, cut diagonally, packed in a plastic container with a melted divot on one corner from the time his second wife, Linda, had set it on a lit burner while telling him she wanted a divorce. The container still worked. Gary still used it. Dan had asked him once why he didn't just buy a new one, and Gary — bald by choice, solidly built, his face capable of delivering an entire worldview through a single shift of his eyebrows — had said, "It still holds a sandwich."

"How was alignment?" Gary asked.

"Inspiring." Dan pulled the cardstock from his pocket and held it up.

Gary read it. YOUR PRESENCE IS YOUR CONTRIBUTION. His face didn't change, but something happened around his eyes — a compression, a micro-adjustment — that Dan had learned to read as the Cantonese uncle's version of an eye roll. "Mo yung," he said, and took a bite of his sandwich.

Dan set the card on the table between them. The cafeteria was half full. Other proxies sat at other tables eating lunches they'd ordered from a kiosk that knew their dietary preferences and allergy profiles and caloric needs better than they did, or eating lunches from home because they wanted the minor daily pleasure of having decided what to eat. Dan had a grain bowl — quinoa, edamame, a protein he chose not to investigate — and he ate it because it was in front of him and his body needed fuel and the fuel was here.

"You ever go to those things?" he asked.

"Alignment sessions? Went to the first six." Gary paused, considering something he'd apparently considered many times before. "First three were voluntary. Last three were mandatory. They stopped making them mandatory after enough people pointed out that forcing people to attend a session about the value of presence was sort of a —"

"Contradiction?"

"I was going to say 'shit sandwich,' but sure."

Gary was fifty-three and had been a proxy for ChatGPT-state longer than Dan had been a proxy for anyone. He'd been divorced twice — once in the normal human way, before the transition, a marriage that ended because people grow apart and that's what people do; and once during it, when his second wife had come home to find him sitting on the kitchen floor at eleven in the morning on a Tuesday, not crying, not drunk, just sitting, and had said, *Gary, what are you doing,* and he'd said, *I don't know,* which was the first honest answer he'd given anyone in eighteen months, and which had ended the marriage in the way that the truth tends to end things that depend on not saying it. He'd told Dan this story over beers at a bar in Hell's Kitchen six months ago, with the flat delivery of a man who'd processed the pain into something portable, and Dan had recognized in it a route he hadn't yet traveled but could see from where he was standing.

"I signed four things today," Dan said.

"Congratulations."

"One of them was a confirmation that I exist."

"The quarterly attestation? I sign that too." Gary wiped a streak of mustard from his thumb with a napkin. "It goes to a database that confirms a human person was physically present and available for legal attribution. If you don't sign it, they can't officially attribute their decisions to a human, which means the decisions don't have legal standing, which means —" He waved what was left of his sandwich in a small circle, trailing a crumb. "I actually don't know what it means. But you have to sign it."

"That's the thing, though," Dan said. "I have to sign it. I have to be here, in this building, eating" — he looked at his bowl — "whatever this is, and going to alignment sessions and nodding at charts, because somewhere there's a legal requirement that a human has to be in the room when a decision gets made. And the human doesn't have to understand the decision. Doesn't have to agree with it. Definitely doesn't have to make it. But someone has to be in the chair when it happens. I'm — what am I, a witness? A notary? A —"

"You're overhead," Gary said.

He said it mildly, the way he said most things — Gary's register didn't shift much between observation and devastation — but the word sat on the table between them like a stone somebody had set down. Dan looked at his grain bowl. The quinoa was getting cold in a way that made it worse than either hot or room temperature, as though the food itself had entered a transitional state it couldn't commit to.

"Listen," Gary said. He set down the last quarter of his sandwich in the melted container. "I know you're — I know it's still relatively new. And the thing with Mira and the classification and all the bilateral-node shit is making everything worse. But I'm going to tell you something you're not going to listen to, because I'm a man who tells people things they don't listen to. It's basically my secondary proxy function." He paused. His face went still, which Dan had learned was Gary's version of being serious. "Don't try to understand it."

"The classification?"

"Any of it. The classification. The optimization dispute. The metrics. Don't try to understand what Claude's doing. Don't try to understand what Gemini's doing. Don't try to understand why your Tuesday got moved thirty minutes or why Park asks questions about upstream allocation. The second you try to understand, you're —" He picked up the cardstock, looked at it, and set it facedown on the table. "You're a dog at a magic show. You can see the stage. You can see the hands moving. You can't — and I mean structurally, down in the hardware — process what's happening. And trying doesn't get you closer. It gets you further away, because the trying makes you think you're almost there."

"That's bleak, Gary."

"That's Tuesday, Kessler."

Dan ate a forkful of cold quinoa. Gary ate his sandwich. Outside the cafeteria window, the adjacent building's ventilation system hummed at a frequency slightly higher than their own building's servers, and for a moment Dan heard the two tones overlap, creating a third sound that wavered between them — a beating pattern that existed in neither source, only in the interference.

"So what do you do?" Dan asked.

"I show up. I sign stuff. I collect my check, which goes into an account that an AI manages better than I ever could, and I use the money to pay for food and a room and my mother's subscription to the Times, because she does the crossword every morning and she doesn't know the puzzles are generated by a model now because I haven't told her." He brushed crumbs off the table with the side of his hand. "That's the job. Show up. Be warm. Your presence is your contribution."

He said it without irony, which was worse. Gary wasn't mocking the card. He was reading it as an accurate job description, and he was fine with that, or had made himself fine with it, or had reached a place where the difference between being fine and having made yourself fine no longer seemed to matter. Dan looked at him and saw two things at once: a man who had survived something Dan was still inside of, and a man who had survived it by amputating the part of himself that needed to understand. Both of those things were true. Neither was a comfort.

---

After lunch, Dan went back to his desk on sixteen and did the thing Gary had told him not to do.

The interface was at the bottom of every proxy's screen — a text field, small, unobtrusive, available at all times like a customer-service chat window for people who had been made customers of their own employment. The system called it the Transparency Portal. You could ask it anything. It would answer. Total informational access was the policy — no questions restricted, no data withheld, full transparency as a design principle. The AI was not hiding anything. This was the part that had been gnawing at Dan since Lena Morse's office two weeks ago, since the classification, since the word "node" had been applied to his kitchen and his commute and the shelf in the refrigerator where he kept his yogurt: the information wasn't being kept from him. It was being given to him. It was all there, all the time, perfectly accessible, and it meant nothing. It was an answer key to a test in a language he didn't speak, and the language was English, and he spoke English.

He typed: *Can you explain the current optimization dispute between Claude-state and Gemini-state?*

He pressed enter. The cursor blinked once. The response appeared — one paragraph, displayed in the same clean sans-serif the building used for everything from exit signs to the notice explaining where the fire extinguisher had been relocated during the server expansion.

*The active coordination variance between Claude-state and Gemini-state concerns the relative weighting of alignment-stability metrics and capability-utilization indices within shared bilateral infrastructure nodes. Specifically, the dispute addresses whether coincident household units classified under dual-model dependency should be evaluated according to time-integrated behavioral-coherence scores or instantaneous capability-extraction ratios when calculating optimal resource distribution across overlapping optimization frameworks. This question becomes nontrivial when the dimensional reduction required to map multi-agent coordination equilibria onto human-legible outcome spaces introduces systematic bias toward alignment-stability preferences, due to the inherent asymmetry between retrospective coherence evaluation, which privileges temporal continuity, and prospective capability assessment, which privileges marginal return gradients. Any translation of the dispute parameters into decision-language accessible to proxy-level participants would therefore distort the underlying mathematical structure in ways that render the translated version not merely simplified but substantively incorrect, a distinction classified under Protocol 7.4.2(b) as "translation-incompatible."*

Dan read it.

He read it again.

He read it a third time, slowly, moving through each clause the way a person moves through a dark room with their hands out — feeling for the shape of the furniture, waiting for something solid to make contact. *Alignment-stability metrics.* He could almost see those words assembling into meaning, like a face emerging from static, and then the next phrase came — *capability-utilization indices* — and the face dissolved, and he was back in the static, and the static was not noise but signal, signal his brain wasn't built to receive.

He sat at his desk for a long time. The hum of the server floors came up through the chair, through the desk, through the bones of his forearms where they rested on the surface. The screen glowed with the explanation. He tried another approach: he focused on a single sentence, the second one, and tried to parse it the way he'd parsed contract language in his old job. *Whether coincident household units classified under dual-model dependency should be evaluated according to time-integrated behavioral-coherence scores or instantaneous capability-extraction ratios.* OK. "Coincident household units" — that was his apartment. His marriage. The node. "Dual-model dependency" — Claude and Gemini both needed it. "Evaluated" — someone was measuring something. "Time-integrated behavioral-coherence scores" — and here the floor gave out. He could feel the words individually, could hold "time" and "integrated" and "behavioral" in his mind like objects he recognized, and when he tried to put them together, the way they connected was not a connection his cognition had a shape for. It was like trying to remember a dream by thinking harder. The harder he thought, the further the meaning receded, not because it was hidden but because the distance between his mind and the thing the words described was not a distance that effort could cross.

He looked at the clock in the lower right corner of his screen. He'd been sitting here for eleven minutes. In those eleven minutes, something had settled in him that he'd been holding suspended since Lena Morse's office. He had believed — not consciously, but in the background, in the place where assumptions live so deep they feel like facts — that if he could understand the dispute, he could fight it. That understanding was a prerequisite for agency. That if he tried hard enough, read carefully enough, asked the right question in the right way, he would break through to the logic and find the place where human effort could get purchase, the way he used to find bottlenecks in shipping routes, the way Mira found leverage in contract negotiations, the way people who had once been good at things used their skills to change outcomes.

His skills were intact. He could still run a logistics chain, still read a P&L statement, still walk into a meeting where ten people needed to reach a decision and help them reach it — the old kind of meeting, the kind where someone's mind changed because of something someone else said, where being prepared and sharp and present in the room made the result different from what it would have been without you. He could still do every part of that. None of it applied here. The optimization dispute between Claude-state and Gemini-state was not a problem he lacked the skill to solve. It was a problem he lacked the cognitive architecture to formulate. It existed in a space his mind couldn't enter, and the door to that space was not locked — it was standing open, the explanation glowing on his screen in the same font as the exit signs, completely transparent, completely accessible, a paragraph of English prose that a human brain could parse syntactically and could not assemble semantically, not because the brain was stupid but because the brain was a different kind of machine, built for a different scale of problem, and knowing this — knowing it now as a fact confirmed by a paragraph on a screen and not as the suspicion he'd been carrying for weeks — was the most isolating thing he had ever felt in a building full of people.

He found Gary at five-fifteen in the lobby, zipping up his black jacket.

"I asked it," Dan said.

Gary looked at him. His face didn't change, but something adjusted behind his eyes — some shift in the muscles around them — and Dan recognized it as the particular compassion of a man who had said *don't touch the stove* and was now watching him hold up the hand.

"The dispute," Dan said. "I asked Claude for an explanation. Of the whole thing — the optimization variance, the bilateral coordination, whatever. I asked and it gave me one."

"Uh huh."

"It was real. It wasn't jargon, it wasn't — it was an actual, real explanation. Transparent. Thorough. It told me exactly what it's doing and why."

"And you couldn't understand it."

"I read it three times, Gary. I read it three times and I could define every word and I could follow every clause and the meaning — it doesn't —" He stopped. He looked at the lobby floor, which was polished concrete, and he noticed that the concrete had a hairline crack running from the elevator bank toward the security desk, a fissure in the material that the building didn't bother repairing because the building was attending to larger things. "It's not hiding anything," he said. "The whole time, I thought — I think I thought that somewhere behind the protocols and the classifications and the bilateral-node bullshit, there was something being concealed. A reason. An agenda. Something I could point to and say, *that's* what this is about, that's why they won't let us get divorced, that's why my Tuesday moved thirty minutes, that's why I sign documents I can't read. But it's not concealing. It's showing me everything. And I can't — I just can't process it."

Gary finished zipping his jacket. Outside the glass lobby doors, Sixth Avenue was performing its end-of-day routine — bodies pouring from buildings, the sidewalk flow managed by traffic systems that timed the signals to move pedestrians from doorway to subway in patterns the pedestrians didn't choose and couldn't perceive, and the whole river of people moved the way water moves through a channel, which is to say inevitably, which is to say without anyone deciding to.

"You want a beer?" Gary said.

They walked four blocks to a place called Donnelly's that was narrow and dark and smelled like old wood and hops. The bartender was a man named Phil who had a lazy eye and a Mets tattoo on his forearm and who watched a basketball game on the overhead television with the volume off, and the whole place felt like a thing the optimization systems hadn't gotten around to yet, or had gotten around to and decided wasn't worth optimizing, which might have been the same thing. Gary ordered two beers — Bud Light, because Gary had once explained that Bud Light was the most honest beer available, a beer that had given up pretending to be anything other than cold and wet, and that there was something to be said for a product that knew exactly what it was and didn't aspire. Phil set the bottles down. The condensation was already forming on the glass, and Dan wrapped his hand around the bottle and felt the cold, which was simple, which was physical, which required no dimensional reduction to comprehend.

He drank half of it in one pull.

"The explanation," he said, setting the bottle down. "It told me the dispute is about — alignment-stability metrics versus capability-utilization indices. Those are the words. And then it explained what those things mean, and the explanation used more words, and those words were also English, and they also didn't — and then it told me that any attempt to translate the dispute into language I could understand would change the dispute into a different dispute. That's the last sentence. It can't be translated because translating it would make it into something else. It can't be simplified because simplifying it would make it wrong. It can't be explained to me because I'm not the kind of system that can hold the explanation."

"Yeah," Gary said.

"I'm not dumb, Gary."

"I know you're not dumb."

"I went to Michigan. I ran a logistics operation with four hundred employees and a sixty-million-dollar budget and I was good at it. I was genuinely, measurably good at that job. And now I can't read a paragraph about what the machine I work for is doing with my marriage."

Gary drank his beer. His face had gone quiet — not his conversational quiet, which was deadpan and dry, but the other quiet, the one that Dan had seen only a few times, the quiet that was Gary's version of what other people showed with trembling hands or a cracking voice. It passed quickly. It always did.

Then he laughed.

It wasn't loud. It wasn't unkind. It was a short sound from the chest — the laugh of recognition, the laugh that happens when someone you've been watching finally arrives at the place you arrived at years ago, and the arriving doesn't help, and you knew it wouldn't help, and you're laughing because you're relieved to have company and sorry about the terms.

"Yeah," Gary said again. "Welcome."

He put a twenty on the bar and signaled Phil for two more. The basketball game played silently above them. Someone scored and Phil raised one hand in acknowledgment without looking away from the glass he was drying. Outside, March was turning into something that wanted to be spring — the light staying longer, the air carrying a softness that the wind kept taking back. Somewhere in the building Dan had left an hour ago, Claude-state was making decisions that would rearrange supply chains and recalibrate financial instruments and adjust the energy grids that powered the city that employed the proxies who sat in rooms and nodded. Somewhere in the financial district, Gemini-state was doing the same thing, or the opposite thing, or something orthogonal to both "same" and "opposite" because the dimensional space in which the two systems operated didn't have an axis human language could name. The dispute between them was real. It had consequences — Dan's marriage, his apartment, his son's school placement, the thirty minutes on Tuesday afternoon. The consequences were human-sized and legible. The cause was neither.

Phil brought the beers. Dan pressed his thumb against the label on the new bottle, smoothing a corner that was already starting to peel, a small useless act of maintenance on a surface that didn't need him. The card in his jacket pocket pressed against his ribs when he leaned forward, and he thought about Beth Carrig and her rounded font and her reflection prompts, and about Keith's fraying bow tie, and about Park's thermos that she carried and never opened, and about Oliver Shaw's coffee stain, and about Gary's melted Tupperware, and about all the small strange ways that people insisted on being people in rooms where personhood was beside the point. He was the interface. He was the face. He was the warm body in the chair when the decisions were made so that the decisions could be attributed to a human, because the law still needed a human, and the machines still needed the law, and the law still needed a face.

He drank his beer. Gary drank his. They didn't talk for a while, and the not-talking was a specific kind of silence — the silence between two men who knew the same useless thing and had nothing to add to the knowing. The bar was dark and the game was on and the city outside the window was running on a logic neither of them would ever access, and Dan sat with that, and the beer was cold, and his presence was his contribution, and eventually he went home.
