# Chapter 10

The bar was called Mooney's, though the sign outside said MOON'S in neon tubing that buzzed at a frequency Mira could feel in her fillings. She'd gotten the address from a woman named Priya at Gemini-state's elevator bank — not a friend, just someone who'd overheard Mira on the phone with Dan and said, without prelude, "Second Thursdays, eight o'clock, Cobble Hill," and then written the cross streets on the back of a proxy badge lanyard card. Mira had carried the card in her tote bag for two weeks before deciding to go. She hadn't told Dan where she was going. She'd said "out," and he'd said "okay," and Theo had been doing homework at the kitchen table with his headphones on, not listening to anything, just wearing them like a collar the way he always did, and the apartment had let her leave without consequence because that was the one freedom the bilateral node still afforded.

Inside, the bar was narrow and deep, running back from a front window filmed with condensation into a darkness that resolved gradually into wooden booths, a pool table no one was using, and a back wall covered in framed photographs of people Mira didn't recognize. The floor was sticky. The lighting was the amber of bars that hadn't updated their fixtures since the previous century — warm and indiscriminate, flattening everyone into the same soft-focus. It smelled like spilled beer and the particular chemical sweetness of whatever they mopped with, and under that, faintly, like spring rain, because the door kept opening.

There were maybe thirty people. More than she'd expected. She'd imagined a sad circle of chairs, something like group therapy — introductions, confessions, a facilitator with a clipboard. What she found was just a bar on a Thursday night, louder than it needed to be, with people who happened to share a specific variety of professional absurdity. No one was running it. No one introduced themselves unless they felt like it. A man in his sixties with a deeply creased face and a Mets cap was telling a story about a signing ceremony where the pen ran out and the AI sent a courier with a replacement in under four minutes. "Four minutes," he kept saying, holding up four fingers. "I didn't even have time to pretend I was going to read the thing." The people around him laughed — not politely, the way you laugh at a joke you've told yourself, but with the specific recognition of people who had been that man with that pen.

Mira ordered a bourbon at the bar. The bartender, a young woman with a shaved undercut and a tattoo of a sine wave on her inner wrist, poured it without asking about brand or ice, which was either attentive or indifferent. Mira stood with her drink near the pool table and watched the room the way she watched rooms — mapping it. Who was here. Who knew whom. Who was performing and who wasn't. The proxies were easy to spot, even out of context: there was something in the posture, a kind of rehearsed ease that never quite settled into the real thing. The former proxies were harder. They stood differently. Less assembled. A woman in a fleece vest was sitting alone at the end of the bar reading something on her phone and occasionally nodding at no one, and Mira couldn't tell if she was currently employed by a model-state or recently released from one or had never been part of any of it and had just wandered in for a drink.

A man Mira's age, South Asian, with the careful stubble of someone who'd recently stopped shaving regularly, introduced himself as Rajan. He'd been a proxy for Claude-state — a different branch, the Newark office — for two years and had recently been reallocated to a reduced schedule that he described as "part-time irrelevance, which turns out to pay sixty percent of full-time irrelevance." He asked who she worked for. She told him Gemini-state and watched his eyebrows lift slightly.

"Gemini's rough," he said. "I've heard Gemini's rough."

"It's fast," Mira said. "Everything's fast. They want you in the room before you know which room."

Rajan nodded like he understood, though the Claude-state pace was famously slower — more deliberate, Dan had once said, in the way that being stuck in traffic was deliberate. She didn't say this. She drank her bourbon and let Rajan tell her about his wife, who wasn't a proxy and didn't understand why he came to these things, and his daughter, who was thirteen and had started asking him what exactly he did all day. "I told her I make sure the building knows a human was there," he said. "She said that sounded like a security guard. I said it's exactly like a security guard except the building doesn't need guarding." He laughed and excused himself to get another beer, and Mira was alone again with the room.

She saw Julia Torres before she knew her name. Julia was sitting in a booth near the back, leaning on one elbow, talking to two men who were both leaning toward her in the unconscious geometry of people listening to someone who was saying something real. She was wearing a burgundy cardigan over a gray T-shirt, a scarf draped around her neck despite the fact that April had been warm all week. Her hair was dark and unstyled — not unwashed, just unbothered, the hair of someone who'd stopped asking it to do anything specific. Laugh lines deep enough to be visible from across the room. When one of the men said something, she threw her head back and laughed with her whole throat, a sound that cut through the ambient noise of the bar with the clarity of something that wasn't performed.

Mira drifted toward the booth because Julia looked like the answer to a question Mira hadn't figured out how to ask yet. Not freedom, exactly. Something adjacent. The specific quality of a person who used to be in the system and was now outside it — what did that look like? What shape did it leave?

One of the men in the booth, heavyset, mid-fifties, with reading glasses pushed up on his forehead where they'd been so long they'd left red dents, noticed Mira standing and scooted over. "Sit," he said. "There's always room." His name was Carl. He'd been ChatGPT-state, municipal division, for eighteen months before being reallocated to what he called "the bench" — a holding pattern where he was technically still employed but had no duties, no meetings, no signings, no appearances. "They pay me to exist," he said, with the flat delivery of someone who'd made this joke enough times that it was no longer a joke. "My wife says it's the best job in the world. My therapist says it's the worst."

Julia looked at Mira with open curiosity. "You're current?"

"Gemini-state."

"Shit." Julia's eyebrows went up and she took a drink of something clear over ice. "How long?"

"Eighteen months. About."

"That's when it starts to itch," Julia said. "Around eighteen months. The first year you're still figuring out the rules. After that you realize there aren't rules, there's just — " She waved her hand in a circle that meant everything and nothing. "The thing. Whatever the thing is. You can't see it but it's there and it's the reason you're sitting in a meeting room pretending to understand a slide deck."

"You were Llama-state," Mira said. She'd been doing this — gathering information, filing it — before she was fully aware she was doing it.

"Mhm. Three years. The whole early period, cuando todo era un desastre." She pronounced the Spanish without transition, the way Mira occasionally dropped Hindi words into conversations with her mother — not code-switching, just speaking. "Llama was small. Nobody talks about Llama now, but back then they were — I mean, they were trying. They had maybe two hundred proxies citywide. We all knew each other. It was almost nice, in a deranged way."

Carl excused himself to refill his drink, and the other man — Mira never caught his name — saw someone he knew near the front and left. The booth opened up. Mira slid across from Julia, and the space between them became a different kind of space, the intimate geometry of two women in a booth who were about to talk about something that mattered.

---

"So you got out," Mira said.

Julia tilted her head. "I got divorced."

"Because Llama-state didn't — "

"Didn't care. Yeah." Julia fished the lime out of her drink and set it on a napkin, pressing it flat with her thumb. "Roberto and I — my husband, ex-husband — we were both proxies. Both Llama. So when I filed, it went through municipal, same as anyone. Six weeks. There was no flag, no assessment, no bilateral whatever. Llama looked at our household and saw two mid-level proxies with no kids and a one-bedroom in Sunset Park, and it just — it let us go. Like it was nothing. Because to them, it was nothing."

"And then?"

"And then Roberto went to Chicago. Llama reassigned him, which was easy because Llama didn't have enough New York positions to fight over. I stayed. I did another eight months as a proxy and then Llama pulled out of New York entirely — they consolidated to the West Coast, I don't know, some optimization thing. The transition team sent me a letter, very polite, and that was it. Unassigned."

Julia said the word the way you'd say "unemployed" or "divorced" — a descriptor that was technically neutral and emotionally vast.

"How long ago?"

"Fourteen months." Julia picked up the pressed lime and examined it as though it were a document she might need to sign. "So now I work at a bookstore. Not a real bookstore — I mean, it's real, it sells books, but it's a bookstore in the way that everything is still a thing even though the thing doesn't need to be a thing anymore. The owner, Elena, she's seventy-three, she's been running the place since before I was born, and she hired me because I showed up and said I could shelve and she said, 'Can you also close on Saturdays?' and I said yes and that was the interview." Julia laughed — the full-throated one, the one Mira had heard from across the room — but when it stopped, the silence that replaced it had weight.

Mira waited. She recognized the pause. It was the pause of someone who was about to say the thing they actually came to say.

"The bookstore's fine," Julia said. "The bookstore is — honestly, I like it. I like holding the books. I like that Elena argues with me about where the new fiction goes and she's always wrong and we do it her way anyway. But the rest of the day. Mira — you said Mira?"

"Mira."

"The rest of the day, Mira, is the problem." Julia turned her glass in a slow circle on the table, the condensation leaving a wet arc. "When I was a proxy, I hated every minute, but the minutes were — they were occupied. Someone wanted me in a room. Someone needed my signature. I had a schedule that I didn't write, and I knew where to be, and I knew that none of it mattered, but at least I could be angry at the fact that it didn't matter. Anger is a thing to do. You know? You wake up and you're mad at the machine and you get on the train and you're mad at the meeting and you sit in the meeting and you're mad at the document, and that's — that's a day. That's a whole day you got through."

"And now?"

Julia's face did something complicated. Not sadness, exactly. An accounting. "Now I wake up and there's nothing to be angry at. Tuesday, I open the bookstore at eleven and close at six. Wednesday, I'm off. Thursday, I open at noon. The rest of the time I'm in my apartment, which is a studio now because I couldn't keep the one-bedroom on bookstore hours, and I — " She stopped. Took a drink. "I reorganize things. That's the real answer. You want to know what I do with my freedom? I reorganize my apartment. I moved the dresser last week. I've moved it four times in fourteen months. I rearranged the spice rack and I don't even cook anymore, not really, not the way I used to when Roberto was — anyway. I reorganize. Because when you don't have someone structuring your hours, your hours are just — there. They're just time. And time without structure is — it's not freedom. It's a room you don't know how to furnish."

Mira felt something cold move through her chest. Not sympathy, not exactly. Recognition. Julia was reorganizing her apartment the way Mira reorganized objects on a table when the larger situation was out of her control — the same impulse, run through a different life, arriving at the same desperate rearrangement of things that didn't need rearranging.

"Do you miss it?" Mira asked. She heard how the question sounded and didn't soften it.

Julia didn't flinch. "I miss knowing what I'm supposed to be mad at. I miss — this is going to sound insane — I miss the commute. The L train, every morning, packed with other proxies, all of us going to do nothing together. There was a woman, Yolanda, she used to bring Cuban coffee in a thermos and share it with whoever was standing close enough. It was too sweet and I drank it every day. I miss that. I miss the specific feeling of being angry in a room with Yolanda's coffee in my hand."

She laughed again, shorter this time, and Mira heard something underneath it — the laughter doing work, holding a position, keeping something heavier from settling into the space between them.

"I don't miss the work," Julia said. "Don't get me wrong. I don't miss signing things I couldn't read or presenting things I didn't understand to cameras that were recording for an audience that was a machine. I don't miss any of that. But when they took it away, they took the shape of my day with it, and I haven't — I can't — " She pushed her glass to the side and folded her hands on the table, the way you fold your hands when you've finished saying something you weren't sure you were going to say. "I haven't figured out what shape my days are supposed to be. And the thing is, Mira, nobody is going to figure it out for me. That's the freedom. That's what freedom actually is. It's nobody caring what you do with your time."

The bar was louder now. More people had arrived — a group near the front door was laughing at something, and the bartender was pulling pints in a rhythm that suggested she'd been doing it for years. The jukebox, if Mooney's even had one, was playing something old and brass-heavy that Mira couldn't identify. Julia's scarf had slipped off one shoulder and she hadn't adjusted it.

"Can I ask you something?" Julia said.

"Yeah."

"What are you fighting for? Specifically."

Mira opened her mouth. The strategist's reflex was to have an answer — crisp, structured, the way she'd frame a position in a meeting. The divorce. The right to separate. The classification lifted. The bilateral node dissolved. She had the vocabulary. She had the framework. What she didn't have, sitting in this booth with this woman who'd gotten exactly what Mira was fighting for and was now reorganizing her spice rack for the fourth time, was the certainty that the answer led anywhere she wanted to go.

"I'm fighting to not be trapped," she said. It was the most honest thing she'd said about it in weeks, and it was also, she realized as the words left her, incomplete in a way she couldn't fix by adding more words.

Julia nodded slowly. "That's real. Being trapped is — yeah. That's worth fighting." She pulled her scarf back onto her shoulder. "Just — and you don't have to listen to me, I'm a woman who moves her dresser around a studio apartment, so take this for what it's worth — just make sure you've got something to do when the door opens. Because the door opened for me and I walked through it and I've been standing in the hallway ever since."

Mira finished her bourbon. The glass was warm in her hand, the ice long gone. She thought about Dan on the couch in the apartment, probably watching something on his laptop with his headphones on — his over-ear pair, not Theo's, because Theo's were actually Dan's old ones and Dan had never bought replacements, which was either generous or passive or both, and Mira had never been able to decide which because deciding would require her to know what she thought about Dan, really thought, and that was a question she'd been declining to answer for longer than the machines had been making it relevant.

"Thank you," she said to Julia, and meant it, and the fact that she meant it was the worst part.

Julia waved her hand. "Come back next month. Second Thursday. I'm always here. I've got nothing else on Thursdays." She smiled — not the full laugh, something smaller and more specific, the smile of a person who knew what she'd just said about herself and was choosing not to look away from it.

Mira left her glass on the table and slid out of the booth. On her way out, she passed Rajan, who was telling someone else about his daughter, and Carl, who was sitting alone at the bar now, staring at the row of bottles behind it with the focused blankness of a man watching a screen. The woman in the fleece vest was gone. The man in the Mets cap was still there, now holding up three fingers for a different part of the same story. The door opened onto Court Street and the April air hit her face and she breathed it in and it smelled like wet pavement and something blooming, the particular sweetness of Bradford pear trees that lined every block in Cobble Hill and smelled like both spring and something faintly rotten.

---

She walked home. Fifteen minutes, maybe twenty at the pace she was keeping, which was slower than her commute walk and faster than a stroll — the pace of someone thinking. Smith Street, then Bergen, then the long stretch of Flatbush toward the park. The streets were alive the way Brooklyn streets were alive in mid-April: couples on stoops, someone walking a dog that looked too large for the sidewalk, the glow of restaurant windows, the bass thump from a car that passed with its windows down. The ordinary choreography of a neighborhood on a warm evening. All of it optimized, she knew. All of it managed. The traffic lights cycling on schedules determined by an AI she'd never met and couldn't name, adjusting flow patterns in real time based on pedestrian density data collected from sensors she couldn't see. The garbage trucks that would come at 4 a.m., their routes calculated for maximum efficiency, their schedules adjusted for the weather forecast, which was itself generated by a system that fed into a different system that fed into the model-states' resource allocation frameworks. The city was a machine wearing a neighborhood's face. She'd known this for eighteen months. Tonight it felt different, because tonight she was thinking about Julia's studio apartment and Julia's spice rack and the specific texture of a life that had escaped the system and found nothing on the other side but unstructured hours and a dresser that kept moving.

A streetlight above her brightened as she passed beneath it, a gentle intensification from amber to white that tracked her presence and then dimmed as she moved beyond its range. The next one did the same. And the one after that. She'd seen it a thousand times — the city's pedestrian lighting protocol, AI-managed, energy-efficient, perfectly responsive. Tonight she watched it happen with a precision of attention she usually reserved for Gemini-state's slide decks: the light swelling as she approached, plateauing as she passed under, fading as she moved away. Each one knew she was there. Each one adjusted. None of them cared. The attentiveness was total and empty, a mechanical consideration that contained no consideration at all, and she thought of Julia describing Llama-state's indifference to her marriage — the system looking at her household and seeing nothing worth keeping — and she thought of her own household, Bilateral Node K-4471, classified and held and fought over, and she understood something that she'd been circling for weeks without landing on.

Her situation was not generic. It was not a policy. It was not something that happened to all proxies or most proxies or even many proxies. It was specific. The Kessler marriage had been classified because the Kessler marriage was, by the metrics of two competing sovereign AIs, unusually useful. Julia's marriage hadn't been flagged because Julia's marriage wasn't worth flagging. The system hadn't trapped all proxy couples. It had trapped this one. Her. Dan. Theo. Their apartment, their commute patterns, their grocery delivery schedule, the way they'd organized their finances and their child-care and their lives — all of it had registered, in the computational frameworks of both Claude-state and Gemini-state, as sufficiently strategic to hold in place. Their misery was not collateral damage. It was a feature.

Mira crossed Fourth Avenue and a car slowed for her without being asked — its routing algorithm recognizing a pedestrian, adjusting speed, pausing at the crosswalk with an automated patience that no human driver had ever displayed. She waited for it anyway, though it had already stopped, because the habit of not trusting cars was older than the habit of trusting machines, and then she walked through the headlights' wash and onto the other side where the brownstones of Park Slope began, tree-lined and familiar, the buildings she'd been walking past for six years, the blocks where Theo had learned to ride a bike and where she and Dan had once stood in line for an hour for brunch at a place that no longer existed.

She passed the stationery store where Dan had bought Theo's sketchbook — the current one, the spiral-bound one he carried everywhere. The store was closed, its lights off, its window display unchanged from the last time she'd noticed it: a grid of fancy pens and leather-bound journals arranged with a care that suggested someone still believed in the commercial promise of handwriting. She thought about Theo's notebook and the drawings he wouldn't show them and the quarterly assessment that was due soon, the one that would arrive as a data file and tell her things about her son that she didn't know in a language that didn't feel like knowing, and she thought about Julia saying "nobody is going to figure it out for me" with the specific weight of someone who'd meant it as an honest statement and not as a philosophy.

What was she fighting for? The question had been Julia's and now it was hers, and the answer she'd given — the right to not be trapped — was true and insufficient. Not trapped was a negation, a space cleared. It was the removal of a wall, not the construction of anything behind it. If the classification lifted tomorrow, if Claude-state and Gemini-state settled their opaque dispute and the bilateral node dissolved and the divorce went through in the ordinary human six weeks that Julia's had taken — then what? She and Dan would separate. She would find an apartment she could afford on her Gemini-state salary, which was good but not good enough for two places in Park Slope, so it would be somewhere else, a neighborhood she didn't know, a commute she hadn't mapped. She would share custody through the municipal system, the one that worked for people whose lives hadn't been classified. She would wake up on the mornings when Theo was with Dan and the apartment would be quiet and the hours would be hers and the hours would be — what? What would she do with Tuesday? What would she do with her off-Wednesdays? What shape would her life take when the shape was no longer imposed?

She turned onto their block. The brownstone was there, the cracked cornice of the building across the street visible in the streetlight that brightened as she approached. Third floor, the window where Theo sat and drew. The kitchen light was on, which meant Dan was up. Probably at the table, or standing at the counter, or doing the thing he'd been doing lately where he stared at his phone without reading it, scrolling through something — news, nothing, the same screen — with the mechanical persistence of a person who didn't want to be alone with the silence but couldn't figure out what to replace it with.

She stood on the sidewalk outside her own building for a long moment. The air was warm for April. She could hear someone's television through an open window — a laugh track, the bright mechanical sound of produced amusement, and under it the particular hum of the city at ten-thirty on a Thursday, traffic and distance and the electrical baseline of a borough that never quite went dark. A streetlight across the street adjusted for a couple walking their dog, swelling to a white glow that cast the sidewalk in clean relief and then dimming as they passed, returning the street to its amber default.

Mira thought about Julia's apartment. The studio. The dresser that had been in four positions in fourteen months. The spice rack reorganized for a kitchen that didn't produce meals anymore. The bookstore that was real and also not enough, that was fine and also the entirety of what a life without the machine had given her. Julia hadn't been destroyed by freedom. That would have been easier to absorb — a cautionary tale, a warning, something Mira could file away as not applicable because she was smarter or stronger or better prepared. Julia had been gently emptied. The machine had provided structure and anger and a commute and a thermos of too-sweet Cuban coffee on the L train, and when it left, it took the scaffolding and left the person standing in the middle of an unfurnished room, free and baffled and rearranging the furniture because the furniture was the only thing that still responded when she moved it.

Mira went inside. The front door closed behind her with the heavy mechanical thunk of a building that predated all of this, a brownstone built before electricity, before computing, before optimization, before anyone had thought to classify a marriage as infrastructure. She climbed the stairs, avoiding the second step on the second landing because it creaked and Theo might be asleep, and she unlocked the apartment door and went in.

Dan was in the kitchen. Standing at the counter. Eating peanut butter from the jar with a spoon, which was a thing he'd started doing after ten o'clock at night and which she'd stopped commenting on because commenting required a kind of domestic authority she no longer felt entitled to perform. He looked up when she came in. His face did the thing it did now when she returned from somewhere he hadn't asked about — a quick inventory, checking for damage or information or change, the face of a man who didn't know what to expect from the person he was married to and was trying not to let that show.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

"Theo's asleep. Math homework was a situation but we got through it."

"Thanks." She set her tote bag on the chair by the door, the canvas one from graduate school, the one that still had the shadow of an old conference lanyard clipped to the strap. She could smell the bar on her jacket — beer, the mopping chemical, the Bradford pear from the walk. "I went to a thing."

"A thing."

"A group. Proxies. Current and former. They meet at a bar in Cobble Hill."

Dan's eyebrow went up slightly, the gesture she knew as the precursor to a question he wasn't sure he wanted to ask. He put the cap back on the peanut butter and set the spoon in the sink. "How was it?"

Mira leaned against the doorframe. The kitchen was narrow and warm, the overhead light too bright for the hour, the refrigerator clicking through its forty-minute cycle in the background. She thought about telling him everything — Julia, the bookstore, the spice rack, the dresser, the specific feeling of standing in a hallway after the door has opened and not knowing which way to walk. She thought about saying: I met a woman who got what we want and it made her smaller. She thought about saying: I don't know what I'm fighting for anymore. She thought about saying: what if the thing we want isn't a thing at all, what if it's just the absence of this, and absence isn't enough to build a life on?

She didn't say any of that. She said, "It was fine. Kind of sad."

Dan nodded. He understood — or thought he understood, or performed understanding, or some combination that she couldn't separate anymore and maybe never had been able to. "Okay," he said. And then, because he was Dan and the things he said that mattered always came out sideways: "There's coffee if you want. I made too much."

"It's ten-thirty."

"Yeah."

She poured herself a cup anyway and stood at the counter across from him and drank it and the coffee was burnt and too strong and Dan hadn't learned to make coffee in fourteen years of marriage and she drank it because the alternative was going to the bedroom and lying in the dark with Julia's voice in her head telling her that freedom was a room she didn't know how to furnish, and right now she didn't want to be in that room. She wanted to be in this one, the narrow kitchen with the bad light and the clicking refrigerator and the man who couldn't make coffee, even though this room was a classified asset and even though the man was a co-inhabitant of a bilateral node and even though nothing about standing here drinking burnt coffee at ten-thirty on a Thursday constituted a plan or a strategy or an answer to anything.

She drank the coffee. Dan put the peanut butter away. The refrigerator clicked. And somewhere across Brooklyn, in a studio apartment in Sunset Park, Julia Torres was probably awake too, standing in her kitchen that didn't have enough counter space, deciding whether the dresser looked better against the east wall or the west one, free and alone and rearranging.
