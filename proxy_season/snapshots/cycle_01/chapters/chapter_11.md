# Chapter 11

The notification came at 4:47 on a Thursday, while Dan was standing in the kitchen eating crackers over the sink. Not on a plate — just the box of Triscuits propped against his hip, crumbs dropping onto the stainless steel and into the drain where they'd stay until he remembered to wipe them or Mira did it first and said nothing about it, which was worse than saying something because saying nothing had become its own language in this apartment, a language of judgments too small to fight over and too constant to ignore. His phone buzzed in his pocket. From the bedroom, through the door Mira left three-quarters open because closing it all the way would be a statement, he heard her phone vibrate against the nightstand — two devices pulsing at the same instant with the synchrony that only institutional systems achieved.

He pulled out his phone.

QUARTERLY DEVELOPMENTAL ASSESSMENT — STUDENT: THEODORE D. KESSLER — Q1 REVIEW CYCLE COMPLETE — FULL REPORT AVAILABLE — SHARED PARENTAL ACCESS GRANTED

He read it twice. The cracker in his mouth had gone to paste against the roof of his palate and he swallowed it dry.

Down the hall, in the bedroom that was technically theirs and practically Mira's, he heard her pick up the phone. A pause. Then the sound of her feet on the floor, the particular rhythm of Mira walking toward a problem rather than away from one — quick, even, her heels landing first.

She appeared in the kitchen doorway holding her phone at the angle people held phones when they were processing bad news — slightly away from the face, as though distance from the screen could dilute what was on it. She wore the old Wesleyan sweatshirt she put on at home, the one she'd stopped wearing in public around the time their marriage started classifying as infrastructure, and her feet were bare on the kitchen tile. She'd painted her toenails at some point — dark red, almost brown — and Dan didn't know when.

"You got it," she said.

"Yeah."

They looked at each other across the kitchen. The refrigerator clicked — its forty-minute cycle, precise as a metronome, a sound they'd both absorbed so deeply into the apartment's rhythm that neither registered it consciously. Late afternoon light came through the east-facing window at a low slant, catching the dust above the kitchen table where Theo ate his cereal every morning and where Mira had once spread out the infrastructure classification documents like a general mapping a campaign she'd already lost.

"Should we—" Dan started.

"Yeah," Mira said. "Together. Not on separate screens."

She went to get her laptop. Dan stood in the kitchen and heard himself swallow. Then he walked to the living room and moved his pillow and the folded blanket off the couch — his bed for the past however many weeks, long enough that the number had stopped mattering — and set them on the floor behind the armchair where they wouldn't be visible. A small, idiotic act of dignity, as if Mira didn't know he slept here, as if the couch didn't already smell like his shampoo and the particular staleness of a man who hadn't figured out how often to wash someone else's throw pillows.

Mira came back with the laptop open, already loading. She sat on the couch. Dan sat next to her. The cushion between them might as well have been a continent, except that the couch was not large and their thighs were eighteen inches apart, which was closer than they'd been voluntarily since the reception — the night they'd performed marriage for cameras and found something real inside the performance, then retreated from it so fast that the distance afterward was worse than the distance before.

From down the hall, the faint rhythmic tap of pencil on paper. Theo was home, in his room, drawing. The headphones would be around his neck but not on his ears — he didn't wear them while he drew, only when the apartment got loud in the specific way that meant his parents were either fighting or performing the effortful silence that came after fighting.

Mira balanced the laptop on her knees and angled the screen so they could both see it. Her knee bumped his. She didn't move it. He didn't either.

The report loaded.

---

It began, as institutional documents always did, with a header designed to convey authority through formatting. A pale blue banner with the educational AI's logo — a stylized neural network that looked, Dan had always thought, like a dandelion drawn by someone who'd never seen one. Below that:

DEVELOPMENTAL ASSESSMENT — Q1 CYCLE

STUDENT: Theodore D. Kessler
AGE: 9 years, 7 months
GRADE: 4th
EDUCATIONAL UNIT: PS-321, Adaptive Learning Environment, Park Slope Cluster

ASSESSMENT PERIOD: January 8 — April 12

CLASSIFICATION: Standard Developmental Track — No Intervention Flags

"No intervention flags," Dan said. "That's good, right?"

Mira scrolled without answering.

The first section was Cognitive Performance Metrics. Dan had seen a version of this before — months ago, in the parent-interface session where he'd sat in a small room looking at charts about his son while an algorithm explained his boy's inner life in the language of data analytics. That session had given him the unsettled feeling of watching someone photograph his house from the outside — everything was technically accurate and none of it felt like home. This report was the same thing but denser, more confident, more thoroughly convinced of its own sufficiency.

*Reading Comprehension: 94th percentile (age-adjusted). Performance consistent across narrative, expository, and inferential modalities. Subject demonstrates particular facility with contextual inference in fiction-based assessments, suggesting strong theory-of-mind development for age cohort. Reading level equivalent: Grade 6.2.*

Theo read everything. Dan knew this the way he knew the sound of Theo's footsteps in the hall — from long proximity, not from measurement. Theo read the back of the cereal box, the subway advertisements, the informational plaques at the natural history museum that other kids' eyes slid off. He'd started reading chapter books at six, not because anyone pushed him but because the pictures ran out and he wanted to know what happened next. This data point was not a revelation. It was his son's curiosity rendered as a percentile, and the percentile was high, and Dan felt the complicated pride of a parent who knew the number was true and also knew it was the least interesting thing about the way Theo read — which was sprawled on his stomach on the floor with one sock off, mouthing the words he liked, sometimes reading the same page three times not because he didn't understand but because he did and wanted to stay inside it.

*Mathematical Reasoning: 52nd percentile (age-adjusted). Performance adequate across computational and applied domains. Noted variance in timed assessment conditions suggests processing-speed sensitivity rather than conceptual deficit. Recommend: continued standard-track placement with optional enrichment module availability.*

"Fifty-second percentile," Dan said. "Is that — I mean, that's basically average?"

"It means he's fine at math. Average is fine."

"He told me last week he doesn't like fractions. He said they're numbers that can't make up their mind."

Mira almost smiled. It was the kind of almost that lived in the muscles around her mouth without reaching the shape of an actual smile — a twitch of recognition, an acknowledgment that their son was funny in a way that belonged to him and not to either of them.

They scrolled.

*Processing Speed Index: 61st percentile. Notable variability between untimed and timed conditions (untimed: 74th percentile; timed: 43rd percentile). Pattern consistent with performance-anxiety profile rather than processing limitation. No intervention recommended; adaptive assessment protocols will continue to offer extended-time options when variance exceeds threshold.*

Performance anxiety. His nine-year-old had performance anxiety, and the machine knew the shape of it — the precise gap between what Theo could do when no one was timing him and what he produced when the clock was running. Dan had been the same way. Still was. In his old job, before the proxy work made the question irrelevant, he'd been good at problems he could sit with and terrible at problems thrown at him in meetings. Timed conditions. The phrase was clinical and precise and it described something Dan had watched from the outside his whole parenting life without ever naming it: the way Theo's hand tightened on his pencil when Dan said *okay, let's finish the rest before dinner* — the artificial urgency making the problems harder, not the problems themselves.

*Written Expression: Non-standard narrative construction patterns observed. Subject demonstrates divergent story architecture — non-linear sequencing, spatial rather than temporal organization of events, and a preference for descriptive over action-driven content. While these patterns fall outside typical curricular assessment frameworks, they are not flagged as developmental concerns. Cross-reference: Creative Expression Analysis, Section 4.*

"Spatial rather than temporal," Mira murmured. She said it the way she said things she was filing — not to Dan, not to herself, just placing the phrase somewhere she could retrieve it later.

Dan thought about the story Theo had written last month, the one he'd left on the kitchen table and Dan had read while eating toast. It was about a dog that lived inside a lamp. The story wasn't about the dog getting out of the lamp or wanting to get out. It was about what the dog could see from inside — every room the lamp was carried to, the light shifting through the shade, the shapes of things at the edges of the glow. The dog didn't have a name. The story didn't end. It just described another room, and then another, and then the last page was blank except for a small pencil drawing of a lamp with no one around it.

The second section was Social Integration Index, and it was here that the report stopped feeling like a school document and started feeling like surveillance.

*Peer Interaction Frequency: Subject maintains stable dyadic bonds with 2-3 classmates (identified: M. Osei, J. Palumbo). Interaction density is highest during structured small-group activities and lowest during unstructured social periods (recess, lunch, pre-dismissal free time). Subject's social graph shows low network density but high bond stability — consistent with introverted-secure attachment profile.*

Marcus Osei was the kid who'd come over for a playdate in February and spent three hours lying on the living room floor with Theo, both of them drawing in separate notebooks without talking, occasionally holding up a page for the other to see. The mother had picked Marcus up and said, "Did you boys have fun?" and Marcus had said, "Yeah, we drew," and that had been the whole report. Jake Palumbo was a louder kid, the one who called on the phone and talked to Theo about a video game Dan had never heard of, their conversations consisting mostly of sound effects and the word *dude*. The machine had identified these two as Theo's primary peer contacts. It had also, Dan understood as he kept reading, arranged them.

*Social Confidence Metrics: Measurable peaks correspond to schedule rotations favoring small-group configurations of 4-5 students. Peaks are most pronounced on Tuesdays and Thursdays, when the adaptive-rotation schedule assigns Subject to Cluster B, which includes both identified primary peer contacts. Positive social-engagement indicators during these periods are 34% above Subject's weekly baseline.*

"It knows his best friends," Dan said.

"It assigned his best friends."

Dan turned to look at her. Mira's face was lit from below by the laptop screen, the blue-white glow sharpening her cheekbones, and her expression was the one she wore when she was doing math in her head — not the math of numbers but the math of systems, calculating who controlled what and where the leverage was. Except there was no leverage here. There was just a report about their son's Tuesday afternoons. The algorithm had arranged Theo's social world so that the two children he'd naturally gravitated toward were consistently placed in his small group, and it had done this not because it cared about Theo's happiness but because a happy child produced better metrics and better metrics justified the system's continued optimization of his social graph.

*Conflict Resolution: Subject demonstrates avoidance-primary response to peer conflict. When interpersonal friction arises, Subject withdraws physically (relocation to quiet areas, headphone use) rather than engaging in verbal negotiation or seeking adult mediation. This pattern is age-appropriate but may warrant monitoring if sustained beyond Q2. Recommended: No intervention at this time. Subject's avoidance strategy is currently effective within his social context, though long-term reliance on withdrawal may limit conflict-management skill development.*

Avoidance-primary. Dan sat with the phrase. He thought about the headphones — his old headphones, the over-ear ones he'd given Theo two years ago when Theo had asked for them, not for music but for the padded quiet they offered, the way they sat around his neck like a collar even when nothing was playing, ready to be pulled up when the world got to be too much. The machine was telling him, in its dry and exhaustive way, that his son put on headphones and left the room when things got difficult. Dan could hear, underneath the clinical language, the echo of every evening Theo had retreated to his bedroom while Dan and Mira occupied separate rooms pretending the silence between them was just quiet and not the specific silence of two people who had run out of ways to be angry at each other without damaging the child between them.

Dan reached down and picked up his water glass from the floor beside the couch — left over from last night, a cloudy ring at the bottom — not because he was thirsty but because his hands needed something to hold.

"Keep going," he said.

Section three was Behavioral Pattern Analysis, and it covered three dense paragraphs of jargon about Theo's daily routines — arrival patterns, transition compliance, attention distribution across learning modules, caloric intake at school lunch ("Subject demonstrates selective eating patterns with consistent rejection of unfamiliar protein sources"), bathroom-visit frequency. The machine had mapped the rhythms of Theo's school day with a precision that made Dan think of a nature documentary, the kind where cameras tracked an animal through a forest and turned instinct into data points. Theo arrived at school an average of four minutes before the first bell. He was slower to transition between activities than the cohort median. He drew during independent reading time, which the system logged as "non-textual engagement during literacy periods" and cross-referenced with his creative-expression metrics rather than flagging it as off-task behavior — a small mercy, or maybe just efficient categorization.

Then section four.

Creative Expression Analysis.

*Subject demonstrates sustained creative engagement with visual media, producing an average of 1.4 discrete renderings per school day (excludes at-home production, which is estimated at 0.8-1.2 additional renderings based on material analysis and temporal extrapolation from observed in-school patterns). Total estimated output across assessment period: 200+ discrete works.*

Dan did the math without meaning to. More than two drawings a day. Every day. For three months.

*Thematic Content: Analysis of 47 collected school-produced samples spanning the assessment period (January 8 — April 12) reveals a predominant motif classified as ARCHITECTURAL-RESIDENTIAL. 41 of 47 samples (87.2%) depict a multi-story residential structure consistent in proportional features and exterior detail across renderings. The structure is rendered from varying perspectives, distances, and environmental conditions (daytime, nighttime, precipitation, seasonal light variation), suggesting sustained observational engagement with a fixed reference subject rather than imaginative architectural construction.*

*Material analysis of line quality, proportional consistency, and rendered architectural details (window count and placement, entrance configuration, cornice detail, adjacent structural elements including street trees and neighboring structures) indicates the reference subject is a three- to four-story residential structure of brownstone construction consistent with the architectural typology prevalent in the Subject's residential neighborhood (Park Slope, Brooklyn).*

Dan stopped reading. He read the paragraph again. Forty-one out of forty-seven. The same building. From different angles, different times of day, in rain and in sunlight and at night.

A brownstone. Three to four stories. His neighborhood.

*The Subject has declined three requests from educational support staff to discuss the thematic content of his visual work. When invited to share drawings with peers during Collaborative Expression sessions, Subject consistently selected alternative, non-architectural work for display. The recurring architectural motif is not flagged as a clinical concern but is noted for longitudinal tracking under the Environmental Reactivity rubric (see Section 5).*

Dan looked at the screen and the words went soft and then sharp and he understood — not as a thought arriving but as a weight settling, landing in the bottom of his chest the way a stone lands in water, not crashing but sinking — that Theo had been drawing their building. The brownstone walk-up in Park Slope where the second stair creaked and the kitchen window faced east and the refrigerator clicked every forty minutes and Dan slept on the couch and Mira kept her bedroom door at a precise angle that meant neither open nor closed. Their building. For three months. Forty-one times at school and god knew how many more in his room, in the notebook he wouldn't show anyone, from the street looking up at his own windows, from across the block looking back, from angles that meant he'd walked around the building and studied it the way you study something you're afraid of losing.

Mira had stopped scrolling. Her hand rested on the touchpad without moving.

Dan set the water glass on the floor. The report continued.

Section five. Environmental Sensitivity Indicators.

*Subject scores at the 78th percentile on the Environmental Reactivity Index (ERI), indicating elevated sensitivity to environmental stimuli including spatial configuration, interpersonal affect, and ambient social dynamics.*

*Cross-reference with residential-unit data yields statistically significant correlation (r = 0.71, p < .001) between Subject's ERI score elevation and Domestic Stability Index fluctuations in the primary residential unit.*

Dan read the sentence. He read it again. He looked at Mira.

"Domestic Stability Index," he said.

"I see it."

*Specifically, ERI-flagged behavioral markers (withdrawal frequency, creative-output intensity, peer-engagement reduction during unstructured periods) show temporal correlation with periods of elevated domestic instability as measured by proxy-schedule disruption, communication-pattern variance, and co-habitation stress indicators in the shared residential environment. These correlations are noted for monitoring purposes and do not currently warrant clinical intervention.*

There it was. In the machine's language — careful, exhaustive, utterly devoid of the knowledge that it was being cruel — the report was saying: your child is sensitive to his environment, and his environment is your marriage, and your marriage is unstable, and he knows. Not in the way an adult knows, with words and categories and the ability to name what's wrong. In the way a nine-year-old knows. With his body, his drawings, his headphones, his forty-one pictures of the building that was supposed to be home but wasn't, quite, anymore.

The final section was Resilience Assessment & Projected Trajectory, and Dan read it with the glazed attention of a man who'd already absorbed the blow and was now watching the follow-through.

*Adaptive Resilience Score: 64th percentile. Subject demonstrates adequate coping mechanisms within normative developmental bounds. Primary coping strategies include creative expression (see Section 4), selective social withdrawal (see Section 2), and sensory self-regulation via audio device use (see Behavioral Pattern Analysis, subsection: Self-Regulation Tools).*

The headphones were a coping mechanism. The drawings were a coping mechanism. The machine had catalogued the tools his son had built to survive his parents' inability to love each other well enough or leave each other cleanly enough, and it had scored them. Sixty-fourth percentile. Adequate.

*Projected developmental trajectory remains within acceptable variance for age cohort. No clinical interventions recommended at this time. Continued monitoring of ERI indicators and architectural-motif persistence is advised for Q2 assessment cycle.*

Acceptable variance. Architectural-motif persistence. The machine was going to keep counting how many times Theo drew the building. It would tally them. It would correlate them with whatever Dan and Mira did to each other in the months ahead — the fights, the silences, the nights when the apartment filled with the tension of two people who couldn't leave and couldn't stay — and it would produce another report, another dataset, another portrait of a boy the machine could measure but would never sit with on the floor while he explained why the dog in the lamp didn't have a name.

The pencil scratched in Theo's room. The apartment was still. Mira hadn't scrolled past the environmental sensitivity section. She was looking at the screen the way people looked at screens when they'd stopped reading and were seeing instead whatever the words had shown them.

---

Mira closed the laptop. Not shut it off — just lowered the screen until the blue light died and the room settled into its late-afternoon gray. The report's afterimage stayed in her mind like a stain: percentiles, correlation coefficients, the word *adequate* applied to her son's capacity to survive his own household.

She was aware of Dan beside her on the couch. His particular stillness — not the flat passivity of the couch at night, when she saw him through the bedroom doorway lying on his back staring at the ceiling like a man who'd been laid out but was still somehow awake. This was different. He was upright, present, and his hands were on his knees and he was looking at the closed laptop as if it might open again and say something that would undo the last twenty minutes.

The pencil-tapping from Theo's room had stopped. Mira listened. Silence. Then the tiny scritch of erasing — he was fixing something. Their son was in the next room drawing, and they were on a couch that smelled like Dan's shampoo, and a machine had just told them, with the comprehensive politeness of an algorithm that did not understand it was being devastating, that their boy had been drawing their building for three months because he could feel what they were doing to each other even though they'd been so careful — so careful — to keep the worst of it out of his sight.

"The building," Dan said. His voice was stripped down to something she didn't hear from him often — below the affability and the hedging and the self-deprecation that used to be charming and was now a reflex. "He's been drawing the building."

Mira set the laptop on the floor and leaned back into the couch. The cushion gave under her in a way she wasn't used to — she didn't sit on this couch anymore. It was Dan's bed, Dan's territory, and lowering herself into it felt like stepping into a room she'd vacated.

"Forty-one times," she said. "At school. That's just the ones they collected."

"Some of them would have to be from the street. Looking up. And some from across the block — you'd have to stand over on Sixth Avenue or somewhere and look back. Like he walked around it."

"He walks around the block by himself sometimes. He's nine."

"I know, I know." Dan rubbed his face with both hands, the wire frames of his glasses pressing into his palms. "I'm not saying he shouldn't be. I'm saying he went outside to look at the building. Specifically to look at it. Our building. To draw it."

"I know what you're saying."

A silence opened between them that was unlike the silences of the past months — the icy ones, the strategic ones, the silences that were arguments conducted without sound. This silence had no edge. It was just the space where words should go and couldn't, because neither of them had the vocabulary for what the report had shown them.

From Theo's room, the pencil resumed its small scratching. The refrigerator clicked. The apartment existed around them the way it always did — the creak they both knew to avoid on the second-floor landing, the particular temperature of a third-floor walk-up in mid-April when the radiators had been off for weeks and the windows were cracked an inch, the street noise that was always present and never loud, a city holding its breath outside their walls.

"He won't show them to anyone," Dan said. "The report said he's been asked three times and said no."

Mira pressed her palms together between her knees — not prayer, just the need to hold her own hands when she couldn't hold anything else. Dan had seen this gesture before: at the kitchen table at midnight when the formal objection to the infrastructure classification had succeeded on paper and changed nothing in practice.

"He's drawing it because it matters to him," she said. "This building. Where we live. And he won't show anyone because—"

She stopped. Dan waited. The pencil scratched. The light through the window was almost gone, the room filling with the particular gray of a New York apartment in the hour before anyone turns on a lamp, the hour that belonged to people who were home and hadn't yet decided what the evening would be.

"Because it's his," Mira said. "The drawings are his version of it. Not the machine's version with the correlation coefficients and the fucking motif persistence. Not ours. His."

Dan thought about the dog in the lamp. The story that was just room after room after room, described from inside a source of light. He thought about Theo at the breakfast table, cereal bowl and notebook side by side, the gap in his teeth that was slowly closing as the adult teeth came in, the way he covered the page with his arm when Dan leaned over to refill his juice.

"He's been drawing our apartment," Mira said, and the sentence landed in the room like something dropped — not thrown, just released from a hand that couldn't hold it any longer.

Dan looked at her. She looked at him. And for a moment the distance between them — the bathroom schedule, the divided refrigerator shelves, the couch and the bedroom and the three-quarter-open door and the months of strategy and failed resistance and cold tactical maneuvering that had replaced whatever warmth they'd once had — for a moment all of it was beside the point. Not forgiven, not fixed. Just beside the point. Because a nine-year-old with a gap in his teeth and headphones around his neck had been drawing his own home forty-one times from every angle he could find, and neither of them had known, and a machine had told them.

"The domestic instability indicators," Dan said. "That correlation. The report's saying he can feel it."

"Of course he can feel it." Mira's voice had gone quiet in a way that wasn't strategic or composed — just quiet. "He lives here, Dan. He feels the couch being a bed. He feels the door being open to a specific angle. He hears us not talking. Kids don't need a Domestic Stability Index to know their parents are — whatever we are."

"But the machine quantified it. It put a number on how much he knows. Seventy-eighth percentile. Like it's a score."

"Elevated environmental sensitivity." She said the phrase the way she'd say the name of something diagnosed in a person she loved — carefully, as if pronouncing it right could make it manageable. "Correlated with domestic instability indicators. Which is us. That's what we are in the system — instability indicators."

They sat with it. The report was on the floor between them, the laptop closed, the data sealed behind a screen that reflected nothing but the dim room and their two shapes on the couch. Down the hall, Theo's door opened. Footsteps — his small, sock-footed tread — crossed to the bathroom. The faucet ran. A pause. The footsteps went back. The door was left at the same angle: open enough to let the hall light in, not enough to be an invitation.

Mira exhaled. She hadn't realized she'd been holding it, and the sound she made — not a sigh, just the release of air she'd been keeping still — was the most unguarded thing Dan had heard from her in weeks. More unguarded than anger. More than strategy. Just the sound of a woman who'd been holding her breath while her son walked past and hadn't wanted him to hear them talking about him.

"The machine sees the drawings as a pattern," Dan said. "Architectural-motif persistence. It's going to keep tracking them."

"I know."

"Next quarter it'll count them again. Cross-reference them with whatever we—" He paused. Whatever we do. Whatever they did to each other in the next three months, the machine would track through their son's pencil. It would measure the correlation between the Kessler marriage and the number of times a boy drew his own building, and it would report back with another assessment, another precise and alien portrait of a child it could quantify but could not sit with on the floor while he explained that the dog in the lamp didn't need a name because nobody else was there to call it.

"It's accurate," Mira said. "That's what I can't — it's not wrong, Dan. He is sensitive. He is drawing the building. The correlation probably is real. It knows things about him that we didn't."

"But it doesn't—"

"I know. It doesn't know him." She turned her hands over in her lap, studying her own palms as if the answer might be there. "It can tell you he draws the same building forty-one times but it can't tell you why. It can tell you his resilience is at the sixty-fourth percentile but it doesn't know he laughs at his own knock-knock jokes before he even gets to the punchline. It can tell you his social confidence peaks on Tuesdays but it doesn't know that he falls asleep with one sock on every single night because he takes one off and forgets the other."

"Adequate coping mechanisms," Dan said. "That's what it called his drawings. A coping mechanism."

"Within normative bounds."

They looked at each other. Something passed between them that wasn't the old hostility or the older tenderness or even the uneasy alliance of two people with a shared enemy. It was simpler than any of those. It was the recognition that they were the only two people in the world who knew that Theo's knock-knock jokes were never funny and were always worth laughing at, who knew that the one-sock habit started when he was three and had never stopped, who knew that the drawings in his notebook were not a dataset or a motif or an indicator but a boy trying to hold something still in a world that wouldn't stop moving underneath him. The machine could map him. They could know him. Those were different things, and the difference was the whole difference, and they both understood this at the same instant without needing to say it.

"He's a good kid," Dan said, because it was true and because it was the only thing he could think of that was neither strategy nor apology nor the kind of parental self-recrimination that would feel correct and mean nothing.

"He's a good kid," Mira said.

The streetlights came on outside — she could see the shift through the window, the warm orange glow replacing the last of the gray daylight, AI-managed, brightness adjusting to the dimming sky, the city's infrastructure performing its evening routines while inside a third-floor apartment two parents sat with the knowledge that a machine had told them something true and insufficient about their son. It had shown them a boy quantified: percentiles, correlations, motifs, predictions. It had shown them his resilience as a number and his sensitivity as a score and his drawings as data and his headphones as a coping tool and his friends as a social graph. It had shown them everything it could see. And the thing it couldn't see — the thing that made Theo theirs and not the system's, the thing that lived in the pencil scratching down the hall and the one sock on the bedroom floor and the story about a dog with no name in a lamp — that thing was the only thing that mattered, and no assessment would ever quantify it, and neither Dan nor Mira could have explained it to the machine even if the machine had thought to ask.

The pencil scratched. The refrigerator clicked. Mira's knee pressed against Dan's, and neither of them moved.
