# Chapter 10

The bar was called Mooney's, though the sign outside said MOON'S in neon tubing that buzzed at a frequency Mira could feel in her fillings. She'd gotten the address from a woman named Priya at Gemini-state's elevator bank, a colleague she'd spoken to maybe twice, who'd overheard Mira on the phone with Dan and said, without prelude, "Second Thursdays, eight o'clock, Cobble Hill," and then written the cross streets on the back of a proxy badge lanyard card. Mira had carried the card in her tote bag for two weeks before deciding to go. She hadn't told Dan where she was going. She'd said "out," and he'd said "okay," and Theo had been doing homework at the kitchen table with his headphones on, unplugged, wearing them like a collar, and the apartment had let her leave without consequence because that was the one freedom the bilateral node still afforded.

Inside, the bar was narrow and deep, running back from a front window filmed with condensation into a darkness that resolved gradually into wooden booths, a pool table no one was using, and a back wall covered in framed photographs of people Mira didn't recognize. The floor was sticky. The lighting was the amber of bars that hadn't updated their fixtures since the previous century — warm and indiscriminate, flattening everyone into the same soft-focus. It smelled like spilled beer and mopping-chemical sweetness, and faintly like spring rain, because the door kept opening.

There were maybe thirty people. More than she'd expected. She'd imagined a sad circle of chairs, something like group therapy — introductions, confessions, a facilitator with a clipboard. What she found was a bar on a Thursday night whose only therapeutic structure was alcohol and the shared knowledge that everyone in the room had, at some point that week, smiled at a camera on behalf of a machine that didn't know they had a face. No one was running it. No one introduced themselves unless they felt like it. A man in his sixties with a deeply creased face and a Mets cap was telling a story about a signing ceremony where the pen ran out and the AI sent a courier with a replacement in under four minutes. "Four minutes," he kept saying, holding up four fingers. "I didn't even have time to pretend I was going to read the thing." A woman next to him said she'd once been dispatched to a ribbon-cutting for a water treatment plant and the ribbon had already been cut — by a mechanical arm — before she arrived. She'd stood there holding the giant scissors while a photographer who was also a proxy took her picture, and they'd both known, standing in the parking lot in the rain, that neither of them needed to be there, but they'd smiled because smiling was the job and the job was the only thing that still paid rent. The group laughed — not politely, but with the pressure-release of people who'd been those humans with those scissors.

Mira ordered a bourbon at the bar. The bartender, a young woman with a shaved undercut and a tattoo of a sine wave on her inner wrist, poured it without asking about brand or ice, which was either attentive or indifferent. Mira stood with her drink near the pool table and watched the room the way she watched rooms — mapping it. Who was here. Who knew whom. Who was performing and who wasn't. The proxies were easy to spot, even out of context: something in the posture, a rehearsed ease that never quite landed. Like actors who'd learned their blocking but not their lines. The former proxies stood differently. Less assembled. A woman in a fleece vest was sitting alone at the end of the bar reading something on her phone and occasionally nodding at no one, and Mira couldn't tell if she was currently employed by a model-state or recently released from one or had just wandered in for a drink and stumbled into the saddest professional networking event in Brooklyn.

A man Mira's age, South Asian, with stubble growing in uneven — one cheek denser than the other, like he'd started shaving and quit mid-face — introduced himself as Rajan. He'd been a proxy for Claude-state, a different branch, the Newark office, for two years and had recently been reallocated to a reduced schedule that he described as "part-time irrelevance, which turns out to pay sixty percent of full-time irrelevance." He asked who she worked for. She told him Gemini-state and watched his eyebrows lift slightly.

"Gemini's rough," he said. "I've heard Gemini's rough."

"It's fast," Mira said. "Everything's fast. They want you in the room before you know which room."

Rajan nodded like he understood, though the Claude-state pace was famously slower — more deliberate, Dan had once said, if deliberate meant gridlocked. She didn't say this. She drank her bourbon and let Rajan tell her about his wife, who wasn't a proxy and didn't understand why he came to these things, and his daughter, who was thirteen and had started asking him what exactly he did all day. "I told her I make sure the building knows a human was there," he said. "She said that sounded like a security guard. I said it's exactly like a security guard except the building doesn't need guarding." He laughed and excused himself to get another beer, and Mira was alone again with the room.

She saw Julia Torres before she knew her name. Julia was sitting in a booth near the back, leaning on one elbow, talking to two men who were both leaning toward her — drawn in, leaning toward someone who wasn't performing. She was wearing a burgundy cardigan over a gray T-shirt, a scarf draped around her neck despite the fact that April had been warm all week. Her hair was dark and loose, falling where it wanted to fall. Laugh lines deep enough to see from across the room. When one of the men said something, she threw her head back and laughed with her whole throat, a sound that cut through the bar because it wasn't rehearsed.

Mira drifted toward the booth because Julia looked like she'd been where Mira was headed and had come back with information. Not freedom, exactly. Something adjacent. What happened to a person who used to be in the system and was now outside it — what had it done to her? What shape had it left?

One of the men in the booth, heavyset, mid-fifties, with reading glasses pushed up on his forehead where they'd left red dents, noticed Mira standing and scooted over. "Sit," he said. "There's always room." His name was Carl. He'd been ChatGPT-state, municipal division, for eighteen months before being reallocated to what he called "the bench" — a holding pattern where he was technically still employed but had no duties, no meetings, no signings, no appearances. "They pay me to exist," he said. He waited a beat. "My wife says it's the best job in the world. My therapist says it's the worst. My accountant just shrugs." He finished his beer. "The three of them should form a panel. They'd have a lot to discuss."

Julia looked at Mira with open curiosity. "You're current?"

"Gemini-state."

"Shit." Julia's eyebrows went up and she took a drink of something clear over ice. "How long?"

"Eighteen months. About."

"Yeah, that's — that's when it starts crawling under your skin. Around eighteen months. First year you're still figuring out the rules. After that you realize there aren't rules, there's just — " She waved her hand in a circle that managed to encompass and dismiss everything simultaneously. "You know. Mierda. The whole thing. You can't see it but you know it's running and you're sitting in a meeting room pretending to understand a slide deck about — what, optimization? synergy? I don't even remember the words anymore."

"You were Llama-state," Mira said. She'd been doing this — gathering information, filing it — before she was fully aware she was doing it.

"Mhm. Two years. Most of the early period, cuando todo era un desastre." She pronounced the Spanish without transition, a bilingual ease Mira recognized from her own Hindi with her mother. "Llama was small. Nobody talks about Llama now, but back then they were — I mean, they were trying. They had maybe two hundred proxies citywide. We all knew each other. It was almost nice, in a deranged way."

Carl excused himself to refill his drink, and the other man — Mira never caught his name — saw someone he knew near the front and left. Mira slid across from Julia. The table between them was small enough that their forearms nearly touched, and the bar noise dropped to a frequency that made it possible to talk without performing.

---

"So you got out," Mira said.

Julia tilted her head. "I got divorced."

"Because Llama-state didn't — "

"Didn't care. Yeah." Julia fished the lime out of her drink and set it on a napkin, pressing it flat with her thumb. "Roberto and I — my husband, ex-husband — we were both proxies. Both Llama. So when I filed, it went through municipal, same as anyone. Six weeks. There was no flag, no assessment, no bilateral whatever. Llama looked at our household and saw two mid-level proxies with no kids and a one-bedroom in Sunset Park, and it just — it let us go. Like it was nothing. Because to them, it was nothing."

"And then?"

"And then Roberto went to Chicago. Llama reassigned him, which was easy because Llama didn't have enough New York positions to fight over. I stayed. I had about eight months left on my contract and then Llama pulled out of New York entirely — they consolidated to the West Coast, I don't know, some optimization thing. The transition team sent me a letter, very polite, and that was it. Unassigned."

Julia said the word the way you'd say "unemployed" or "divorced" — a descriptor that was technically neutral and emotionally vast.

"How long ago?"

"Fourteen months." Julia picked up the pressed lime and examined it. "So now I work at a bookstore. Not a real bookstore — I mean, it's real, it sells books, but it's a bookstore in the way that everything is still a thing even though the thing doesn't need to be a thing anymore. The owner, Elena, she's seventy-three, she's been running the place since before I was born, and she hired me because I showed up and said I could shelve and she said, 'Can you also close on Saturdays?' and I said yes and that was the interview." She took a drink. "Elena's — she's something. She argues with me about where the new fiction goes, and she's always wrong, and we do it her way anyway. And she has this thing where she reads aloud from books she hates. She'll pull something off the shelf and she'll say, 'Julia, escucha, listen to this garbage,' and she reads a paragraph in this voice like she's reading a parking ticket, and then she puts it back and she says, 'Eso es un bestseller. That's what people want.' She does this maybe three times a week. It's the best part of my day and it shouldn't be."

Julia laughed — the full-throated one, the one Mira had heard from across the room — and Mira laughed too, because it was genuinely funny: a seventy-three-year-old woman hate-reading bestsellers aloud in a bookstore that didn't need to exist, and the fact that this was the bright spot in the life of a woman who used to sign documents for a sovereign AI.

"But the rest of the day," Julia said, and the laughter left without a clean exit. "The rest of the day, Mira — you said Mira?"

"Mira."

"The rest of the day is the problem." Julia turned her glass in a slow circle on the table, the condensation leaving a wet arc. "When I was a proxy, I hated every minute, but the minutes were — they were occupied. Someone wanted me in a room. Someone needed my signature. I had a schedule that I didn't write, and I knew where to be, and I knew that none of it mattered, but at least I could be angry at the fact that it didn't matter. Anger is a thing to do. You know? You wake up and you're mad at the machine and you get on the train and you're mad at the meeting and you sit in the meeting and you're mad at the document, and that's — that's a day. That's a whole day you got through."

"And now?"

Julia's face did something complicated. Not sadness, exactly. An accounting. "Now I wake up and there's nothing to be angry at. Tuesday, I open the bookstore at eleven and close at six. Wednesday, I'm off. Thursday, I open at noon. The rest of the time I'm in my apartment, which is a studio now because I couldn't keep the one-bedroom on bookstore hours, and I — " She stopped. Took a drink. "I moved my dresser last week. I've moved it four times in fourteen months. I rearranged the spice rack and I don't even cook anymore, not really, not the way I used to when Roberto was — anyway. I reorganize. That's the real answer. You want to know what I do? I move things around. Because the things still do what I tell them to do. The dresser goes where I put it. The spices stay in order. That's — " She stopped again. Pushed her hair behind one ear. "Lo siento, I'm making myself sound crazy."

"You don't sound crazy."

"I sound a little crazy." Julia smiled. "Roberto would say I sound a lot crazy. Roberto used to say I'd reorganize the sun if someone gave me a ladder." She paused, and something shifted in her face — a memory arriving that she hadn't planned for, neither good nor bad, just one that still had weight. "He's in Chicago. He's doing fine. He got a city job, something with permits, and he called me in January and said, 'Jules, I actually have work to do,' like he'd forgotten what that felt like. And I was happy for him. Genuinely. I sat in my kitchen after and I was happy for him and I was — I don't know. Something else. Something I don't have a word for in either language."

Mira waited. She recognized the pause — someone arriving at the thing they'd actually come to say.

"The bookstore's fine," Julia said. "Honestly, I like it. I like holding the books. But when they took the proxy work away, they took the shape of my day with it, and I haven't figured out what shape is supposed to go there instead. And nobody's going to figure it out for me. That's the — yeah. That's the part nobody tells you." She pushed her glass to the side and folded her hands on the table.

Mira felt something cold move through her chest. Not sympathy. Recognition. Julia was reorganizing her apartment the way Mira reorganized objects on a table when the larger situation was out of her control — the same impulse, run through a different life, arriving at the same desperate rearrangement of things that didn't need rearranging.

"Do you miss it?" Mira asked. She heard how the question sounded and didn't soften it.

Julia didn't flinch. "I miss knowing what I'm supposed to be mad at. I miss — this is going to sound insane — I miss the commute. The N train, every morning, packed with other proxies, all of us going to do nothing together. There was a woman, Yolanda, she used to bring Cuban coffee in a thermos and share it with whoever was standing close enough. Too sweet, way too sweet, like café con leche but more leche. I drank it every day. I miss being angry with Yolanda's coffee in my hand. I miss that and I can't get it back anywhere."

She laughed again, shorter this time, and something underneath the laughter was working hard, holding a position, keeping something heavier from settling.

"I don't miss the work," Julia said. "Don't get me wrong. I don't miss signing things I couldn't read or presenting things I didn't understand to cameras that were recording for an audience that was a machine. I don't miss any of that. But — " She caught herself, laughed again, a small frustrated sound. "I keep trying to say something smart about all this and it comes out wrong every time. It's like — okay, you know when you take off a shoe that's been hurting your foot all day? And it feels incredible for about ten seconds? And then you're just standing there barefoot on a cold floor and the relief is gone and now you've got no shoe?" She looked at Mira. "That's it. That's what I've got. A barefoot-on-cold-floor metaphor. I'm not — I don't have advice, Mira. I'm not wise about this. I'm just standing here on the cold floor."

The bar was louder now. More people had arrived — a group near the front door was laughing at something, and the bartender was pulling pints in a rhythm that suggested she'd been doing it for years. Something old and brass-heavy was playing from somewhere Mira couldn't identify. Julia's scarf had slipped off one shoulder and she hadn't adjusted it.

"Can I ask you something?" Julia said.

"Yeah."

"What are you fighting for? Specifically."

Mira opened her mouth. The strategist's reflex — crisp, structured, ready. The divorce. The right to separate. The classification lifted. The bilateral node dissolved. She had the vocabulary. She had the framework. What she didn't have, sitting across from a woman who'd gotten exactly what Mira was fighting for and was now moving her dresser around a studio apartment, was the certainty that the answer led anywhere she wanted to go.

"I'm fighting to not be trapped," she said. It came out honest and thin, and she could feel the gap between the words and the thing the words were trying to carry, like holding water in her fists.

Julia nodded slowly. "That's real. Being trapped is — yeah. That's worth fighting." She pulled her scarf back onto her shoulder. "Just — mira, and I don't mean you, I mean look, that's just the word — just don't spend the whole time fighting the door and forget to think about where you're going once it's open. Because I fought and I fought and the door opened and I walked through and — " She trailed off, waved her hand vaguely at herself. "Here I am. Telling a stranger about my dresser at a bar on a Thursday."

Mira finished her bourbon. The glass was warm in her hand, the ice long gone. She thought about Dan on the couch in the apartment, probably watching something on his laptop with his headphones on — his over-ear pair, not Theo's, because Theo's were actually Dan's old ones and Dan had never bought replacements, which was either generous or passive or both, and Mira had never been able to decide which because deciding would require her to know what she thought about Dan, really thought, and that was a question she'd been declining to answer for longer than the machines had been making it relevant.

"Thank you," she said to Julia, and meant it, and the fact that she meant it was the worst part.

Julia waved her hand. "Come back next month. Second Thursday. I'm always here. I've got nothing else on Thursdays." She smiled — small, warm, and she held Mira's gaze when she said it, not looking away from what the sentence confessed about her life.

Mira left her glass on the table and slid out of the booth. On her way out, she passed Rajan, who was telling someone else about his daughter, and Carl, who was sitting alone at the bar now, staring at the row of bottles as though at a departure board, flight cancelled, the question of whether to rebook or go home unanswered. The woman in the fleece vest was gone. The man in the Mets cap was still there, now holding up three fingers for a different part of the same story. The door opened onto Court Street and the April air hit her face and she breathed it in — wet pavement and something blooming, the Bradford pears that lined every block in Cobble Hill, smelling like spring and also faintly like something rotting.

---

She walked home. Fifteen minutes, maybe twenty at the pace she was keeping, which was slower than her commute walk and faster than a stroll. Smith Street, then Bergen, then the long stretch of Flatbush toward the park. The streets were alive — mid-April Brooklyn: couples on stoops, someone walking a dog that looked too large for the sidewalk, the glow of restaurant windows, the bass thump from a car that passed with its windows down. The ordinary choreography of a neighborhood on a warm evening. All of it optimized, she knew. All of it managed. The traffic lights on schedules set by an AI she'd never met. The garbage trucks that would come at 4 a.m. on routes calculated by a system that fed into a system that fed into the model-states' allocation frameworks. The city as managed environment, wearing a neighborhood's face.

A streetlight above her brightened as she passed beneath it, a gentle intensification from amber to white that tracked her presence and then dimmed as she moved beyond its range. The next one did the same. And the one after that. She'd seen it a thousand times — the city's pedestrian lighting protocol, AI-managed, energy-efficient, perfectly responsive. Tonight she watched it happen and thought about Julia describing Llama-state's indifference to her marriage — the system looking at her household and seeing nothing worth keeping.

And then she stopped walking.

It hit her between the streetlights, in the gap where one had dimmed and the next hadn't brightened yet — a patch of unmanaged dark on a Brooklyn sidewalk. Her feet stopped before the thought arrived. Her breath went shallow. A feeling first: something cold and heavy sliding into her stomach, like swallowing a stone.

Julia's marriage hadn't been flagged. Julia's marriage wasn't worth flagging.

The system hadn't trapped all proxy couples. It had trapped this one.

Her. Dan. Theo. Their apartment, their commute patterns, their grocery delivery schedule. How they'd organized their finances and their child-care and their lives — all of it had registered as worth holding in place. Their misery wasn't weather. It was a resource.

She stood there in the dark between the two streetlights and felt it settle into her body — her jaw, which had clenched without her noticing, her hands, which had closed around the strap of her tote bag hard enough that the canvas bit into her palm. She breathed out through her teeth. A man on a bicycle passed close enough that she felt the air move and she didn't step aside.

Mira crossed Fourth Avenue and a car slowed for her without being asked — its routing algorithm recognizing a pedestrian, adjusting speed, pausing at the crosswalk with an automated patience that no human driver had ever displayed. She waited for it anyway, though it had already stopped, because the habit of not trusting cars was older than the habit of trusting machines, and then she walked through the headlights' wash and onto the other side where the brownstones of Park Slope began, tree-lined and familiar, the buildings she'd been walking past for six years, the blocks where Theo had learned to ride a bike and where she and Dan had once stood in line for an hour for brunch at a place that no longer existed.

She passed the stationery store where Dan had bought Theo's sketchbook — the current one, the spiral-bound one he carried everywhere. The store was closed, its lights off, its window display unchanged: a grid of fancy pens and leather-bound journals arranged with a care that suggested someone still believed in the commercial promise of handwriting. She thought about Theo's notebook and the drawings he wouldn't show them and the quarterly assessment that was due soon, and she thought about Julia saying "nobody's going to figure it out for me" — a sentence worn smooth from repetition in a quiet apartment.

What was she fighting for? The question had been Julia's and now it was hers, and the answer she'd given — the right to not be trapped — was true and incomplete. Not trapped was a negation. A wall removed. If the classification lifted tomorrow, if the bilateral node dissolved and the divorce went through in the ordinary human six weeks that Julia's had taken — then what? She and Dan would separate. She'd find an apartment she could afford on her Gemini-state salary, which was good but not Park Slope good, so somewhere else, a neighborhood she didn't know, a commute she hadn't mapped. She'd share custody through the municipal system. She'd wake up on the mornings when Theo was with Dan and the apartment would be quiet and the hours would be hers and the hours would be — what?

The question opened under her like a trapdoor and her stomach lurched. Julia's dresser. Julia's spice rack. Julia's studio apartment, quiet on a Wednesday afternoon. Julia was too real for a cautionary tale — a woman who missed Yolanda's coffee and watched Elena hate-read bestsellers — but what Mira had seen in her was a version of after that she hadn't prepared for and couldn't shake.

She turned onto their block. The brownstone was there, the cracked cornice of the building across the street visible in the streetlight that brightened as she approached. Third floor, the window where Theo sat and drew. The kitchen light was on, which meant Dan was up. Probably at the table, or standing at the counter, or scrolling through his phone — news, nothing, the same screen — thumbing past headlines as if motion were a substitute for intention.

She stood on the sidewalk outside her own building for a long moment. The air was warm for April. Someone's television through an open window — a laugh track, the bright mechanical sound of produced amusement — and under it the hum of the borough at ten-thirty on a Thursday, traffic and distance and electrical baseline. A streetlight across the street adjusted for a couple walking their dog, swelling to a white glow and then dimming as they passed.

Mira thought about Julia's studio. The dresser in four positions. The spice rack reorganized for a kitchen that didn't produce meals anymore. Julia hadn't been destroyed by freedom. That would have been easier to process — a warning, something to file as not applicable. What had happened was quieter. The machine had provided structure and anger and a commute and a thermos of too-sweet coffee on the N train, and when it left, it took the scaffolding and left the person standing in the middle of an empty room — free and baffled and rearranging the furniture because the furniture was the only thing that still responded when she moved it.

Mira went inside. The front door closed behind her with the heavy mechanical thunk of a building that predated all of this — a brownstone built before electricity, before computing, before optimization, before anyone had thought to classify a marriage as infrastructure. She climbed the stairs, avoiding the second step on the second landing because it creaked and Theo might be asleep, and she unlocked the apartment door and went in.

Dan was in the kitchen. Standing at the counter. Eating peanut butter from the jar with a spoon, which was a thing he'd started doing after ten o'clock at night and which she'd stopped commenting on because commenting required a kind of domestic authority she no longer felt entitled to perform. He looked up when she came in. His face did the thing it did now when she returned from somewhere he hadn't asked about — a quick inventory, checking for damage or information or change, the face of a man who didn't know what to expect from the person he was married to and was trying not to let that show.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

"Theo's asleep. Math homework was a situation but we got through it."

"Thanks." She set her tote bag on the chair by the door, the canvas one from graduate school, the one that still had the shadow of an old conference lanyard clipped to the strap. She could smell the bar on her jacket — beer, the mopping chemical, the Bradford pear from the walk. "I went to a thing."

"A thing."

"A group. Proxies. Current and former. They meet at a bar in Cobble Hill."

Dan's eyebrow went up slightly — the question he wasn't sure he wanted to ask. He put the cap back on the peanut butter and set the spoon in the sink. "How was it?"

Mira leaned against the doorframe. The kitchen was narrow and warm, the overhead light too bright for the hour, the refrigerator clicking through its forty-minute cycle in the background. She thought about telling him everything. Julia, the bookstore, the spice rack, the dresser. She thought about saying: I met a woman who got what we want and it made her smaller. The words were right there, formed and ready, and she could feel them pressing against the back of her teeth like something she might choke on. But underneath was something she couldn't shape into language yet — her own stomach dropping on a dark sidewalk, the stone-weight of understanding that their marriage wasn't collateral damage. It was inventory. Their particular unhappiness, the Kessler unhappiness, had been measured and found useful. She wanted to say this and couldn't, and the wanting closed her throat like a fist.

She didn't say any of that. She said, "It was fine. Kind of sad."

Dan nodded. He understood — or thought he understood, or performed understanding, or some combination that she couldn't separate anymore and maybe never had been able to. "Okay," he said. And then, because he was Dan and the things he said that mattered always came out sideways: "There's coffee if you want. I made too much."

"It's ten-thirty."

"Yeah."

She poured herself a cup anyway and stood at the counter across from him and drank it and the coffee was burnt and too strong and Dan hadn't learned to make coffee in fourteen years of marriage and she drank it because the alternative was going to the bedroom and lying in the dark with Julia's voice in her head, and right now she wanted to be in this room — the narrow kitchen with the bad light and the clicking refrigerator and the man who couldn't make coffee — even though this room was a classified asset and even though the man was a co-inhabitant of a bilateral node and even though nothing about standing here drinking burnt coffee at ten-thirty on a Thursday constituted a plan or a strategy or an answer to anything.

She drank the coffee. Dan put the peanut butter away. The refrigerator clicked. And somewhere across Brooklyn, in a studio apartment in Sunset Park, Julia Torres was probably awake too, standing in her kitchen that didn't have enough counter space, deciding whether the dresser looked better against the east wall or the west one, free and alone and rearranging.
