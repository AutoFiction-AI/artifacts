# Chapter 2

By five-thirty the church lot looked like a county fair run by people who knew one another's debts. Pickup beds stood open with coolers sweating into the dust. Aluminum trays steamed on folding tables under blue pop-up tents. A hand-lettered sign leaned against a bucket of catfish fillets: YOUTH CHOIR TRIP FUNDRAISER, CASH ONLY, GOD BLESS. Heat pressed down hard enough to flatten voices. The oil hissed anyway.

Lena parked under the only pecan tree left with shade and sat a moment with both hands on the wheel. The wheel felt sticky where last summer's vinyl had started to crack. Her shirt still carried morgue bleach in the seams. She could smell it each time she breathed. If she shut her eyes, she saw the cotton gin office floor and the shape under the tarp that had been impossible and still real. She opened her eyes before the memory finished its loop.

At the fellowship-hall door, Deacon Avery Stokes waved her in with the hand that still wore a white golf glove in August. He had lost two fingertips to a baler in 1989 and said the glove kept people from staring. He always smelled like peppermint oil and diesel.

"Detective Waller," he said, pitch-perfect formal because the teenagers were listening. "You here to eat or here to inspect our grease compliance?"

"If this oil passes chain of custody, I'll take two plates," Lena said.

Avery barked a laugh and slapped a ticket strip into her palm. "You sound tired enough to tell the truth. You want hushpuppies before they go hard, you ask Bernice at the fry station. She hoards the good batch."

The line curled from fryers to cake table to drinks cooler, then doubled back near the wall fans. Cicadas shrilled above the lot, and somebody's little cousin ran between adults with a gallon tea pitcher too heavy for him, sloshing sweet tea onto white sneakers. Men in Sunday belts and weekday boots argued softly over whether the county fair had ever paid prize money on time. Two girls in choir polos practiced harmony under their breath while they wrapped cutlery in napkins. The noise was ordinary. It sat on her shoulders like a wet towel and, for a minute, did not feel like threat.

Ida stood behind the dessert table in her slate-blue church dress, wrists braced against arthritis tape that matched nothing she owned. Her smile made room for everyone and gave nothing away. Lemon cakes on one side, peach cobbler on the other, and index cards with names of whoever donated what because Ida believed accounting was a moral discipline.

"You made it," Ida said, not looking up from her clipboard. "There are people here who think your badge buys you a line pass. It does not."

"I'm off the clock."

"You're never off anything. Move that tray before it slides."

Lena shifted a pan of cornbread and kissed Ida's cheek. Her mother smelled like Pond's cold cream and fryer smoke. She wore pearl studs with one scratched surface that caught the light wrong, same pair she'd worn to Lena's graduation and to funerals where nobody spoke above a murmur.

"Jalen with you?" Lena asked.

"He said he was coming if we had battery packs for the phone army."

"That means no."

Ida gave a short look over her glasses. "That means he's sixteen. He'll arrive hungry and annoyed in equal portions."

Lena took her plate and moved along the line. Bernice Talbot worked the fish station with her reading glasses chained to her neck and a sequined visor that said BLESSED in silver letters. Every time she dropped fillets in the basket, she muttered, "Go on now," like fish could be coached.

"Baby," Bernice said to Lena, "you need more than two pieces. Don't make me testify you under-ate in this heat."

"If I eat four, I'll sleep on my feet."

"Then eat three and sin less."

Bernice slid an extra fillet onto Lena's tray and winked.

Lena ate at a folding table near the side steps where breeze moved in brief, stingy pulls. Fried crust cracked under her teeth; hot cornmeal burned the roof of her mouth before she tasted salt and pepper. It was better food than anything she'd made in a week. For nine bites she thought about nothing but chewing.

Jalen came in from the lot with a basketball tucked under one arm and his black hoodie tied at his waist because he refused to wear shorts unless he was on a court. His hair was half twisted, half undone, like he'd started and quit when somebody texted him. He did not look at the fryers first; he looked at the room, mapping where phones were pointed.

"You got me a plate?" he asked Lena.

"I can get one."

"I already got one." He set down a paper tray heaped with fries and fish and two lemonades sweating through the paper. "These people fry like they're under federal order."

"Say hello to your grandmother."

"I said hey when I walked in. She was auditing the cobbler table."

He sat, dropped his basketball between his shoes, and nudged his phone toward Lena. The screen showed a clipped video of yesterday's perimeter tape at the gin, with captions stacked over her face: DEAD MAN FROM 1963 BACK IN COUNTY CUSTODY?? and #MERCERGHOSTCASE. Someone had added laughter tracks between shaky zooms.

"This got like eighty thousand views already," Jalen said. "People are making merch. There's a shirt with your squad car and a skeleton in the backseat."

Lena kept her fork still. "Take that down at this table."

"I'm not posting it. I'm saying everybody's talking about it like a show."

"It's not a show."

"I know that. I'm saying that's how they're doing it."

He said it with more curiosity than cruelty. That was the part that got under her skin. He had grown up with his mother leaving mid-meal for callouts and returning smelling like rain, blood, or bleach. He understood danger as schedule disruption, not as lineage. That was not his fault. It still felt like a cut.

"A man is dead," she said. "Two men are dead. One of them got lynched before your grandparents had indoor plumbing. Keep your voice down."

Jalen shrugged and stabbed a fry into tartar sauce. "I'm keeping it down. Everybody else is talking louder than me."

At the next table, Calvin Reed leaned in over a paper cup, his old Mercer Transit ID still clipped to his belt like retirement had never fully convinced him. "They say that Bell body keeps showing because somebody cursed the river," he told his wife.

Then he caught Lena listening, shook his head, and corrected himself. "It ain't no curse. That's logistics and evil."

He lifted his cup in apology. She nodded back.

Ida passed carrying a tray of tea cups and did not break stride. "You can discuss curses after cleanup," she told the men. "Right now you can discuss mopping."

The men saluted with mock obedience and laughed. Ida kept moving, practical as a metronome.

Lena waited until Jalen had half his plate gone before she tried again. "You remember your granddaddy's old storage key? Small brass one with blue paint on the bow?"

Jalen frowned. "Why would I remember that?"

"Because you used to steal it and pretend you had your own office."

"That was elementary school."

"Do you know where it ended up?"

"No. Probably in one of Grandma's tins with all the church money and random screws. Why?"

"Just asking."

"It's not just asking if you're doing that voice."

Lena looked toward Ida. Her mother was taking cash with one hand and straightening the donation ledger with the other, face set in polite attention. If Lena walked over there with detective questions, she would make a scene at a church fundraiser. Ida would call it disrespect, and Ida would be right.

Jalen read her silence too easily. "This is about old stuff, right? About Granddaddy and that Bell name."

"Eat your food."

"You always say that when the answer is yes."

He pushed back from the table, balancing his basketball with his knee. "I'm gonna go shoot at Marcus's cousin's rim before dark. You can text me later."

"Stay where I can reach you."

"You can always reach me." He held up his phone. "Unless I'm in a game."

She wanted to tell him that was not true and getting less true every year. She said, "Eight-thirty check-in. No later."

"Fine."

He jogged out to the lot, shoes slapping concrete, already typing with his free hand.

Lena found Ida behind the kitchen doors rinsing metal tongs in a sink too shallow for the work. The kitchen fan rattled overhead and moved hot air from one corner to another. Grease had slicked the tile near the fryer, and Ida planted her feet wide to keep her balance. She looked up once, saw Lena's face, and set the tongs down.

"Not here," Ida said.

"Then where?"

"Where nobody is stacking chairs with half an ear on us."

They went out the side door behind the fellowship hall to the narrow strip of grass by the dumpster enclosure. The air smelled like wet cardboard and tartar sauce. Choir kids shouted in the lot. A thunderhead stacked itself purple over the levee, holding rain hostage.

Lena spoke first. "I need to ask about 1963."

Ida's mouth flattened. "No."

"I'm not asking for gossip. I'm asking for records."

"And I said no."

"Mama. Yesterday that body showed up beside a fresh homicide. People are moving remains like props. If Daddy touched paperwork then, if he filed anything, I need to know what exists before somebody burns it now."

Ida turned, braced one hand on the brick wall, then looked back. Her eyes had gone bright in the way that meant pain arriving as anger.

"You want me to stand in this church yard and open that year like a pantry can?" she said. "You want details? Fine. Men laughed with blood on their cuffs and still made Sunday service. Women scrubbed truck beds before daylight because if they did not, the smell stayed all week. Your father came home with river mud in his boot tread and would not let me wash those socks with your baby clothes. You think I forgot what month that was?"

Lena did not move.

Ida stepped closer, voice low and hard. "Don't ask me to defend him, and don't ask me to betray him for free at a fish fry. I carried that house on my back while he put on a badge every morning and told himself he was keeping order. Order for who? Tell me that."

The question landed like a slap. Lena's throat went dry.

"I'm not trying to put you on trial," she said.

"You already did."

"I'm trying to find out what is true."

Ida laughed once, bitter and brief. "Truth. Truth is he kept papers he should have burned, because men like him always believed paperwork could save them from memory."

Lena felt her pulse jump. "What papers?"

Ida looked away immediately, angry at herself now. "Nothing you need tonight."

"You just said he kept them. Where?"

"I said too much."

"Mama."

"Don't call me that in your work voice." Ida pinched the bridge of her nose and breathed through her mouth. "Green sugar tin in my pantry. Key is inside. Unit fourteen at Delta Lock and Store, old Highway Nine lot. That's all I'm giving you. You hear me? That's all."

"Did you read what's in there?"

"I read enough to lose sleep for thirty years."

"Does it mention Bell?"

"I'm done."

Ida straightened her shoulders, wiped her palms on a dish towel, and stepped back toward the kitchen door. Before she went inside, she looked at Lena with exhausted fury.

"If you crack that box open," she said, "you don't get to come back here pretending you were only curious."

Then she returned to the fundraiser, smile in place before the door finished swinging.

Lena stayed in the narrow strip of grass until cicadas drowned out the kitchen fan. Her palms had gone damp. She could feel the old ache in her right knee where the surgery scar pulled in humidity. She told herself to finish one plate, stack some chairs, be a daughter for another hour. Instead she went through the fellowship hall, through the line of children carrying dessert plates, and into Ida's pantry where canned tomatoes sat alphabetized by date.

The green Imperial sugar tin sat on the top shelf behind dry beans. Inside lay safety pins, two church envelope clips, one house key with yellow tape, and a brass storage key marked 14 with chipped blue polish.

She slid it into her pocket and left without saying goodbye.

Delta Lock and Store sat at the edge of a drainage ditch off Highway Nine, behind a shuttered bait shop with a Pepsi sign bleached almost white. The office window held a handwritten CLOSED AT 8:00 PM sign and a string of plastic beads that clicked each time the door opened. A sodium light buzzed above the rows of corrugated metal doors. Rain smell moved in before the rain itself.

Mr. Pritchard ran the office with a hearing aid that whistled when his collar pressed the wrong way. He wore an Ole Miss polo tucked into work pants and a belt holster for a can of wasp spray.

"Waller," he said when she stepped in. "You're Deputy Waller's girl. Been years."

"Detective now," she said.

"Good for you. Bad for me if you're here for trouble."

"I need unit fourteen. Family property."

He checked a ledger with yellowed tabs. "Paid through September, so nobody can accuse me of theft. You have thirty-two minutes before lockup. If lightning hits the gate again, I'm not climbing out there."

"I won't keep you."

"Everybody says that."

He handed her a flashlight with a cracked lens. "Back left row. Watch your step. That flood in spring floated half these units. Smells like a wet dog graveyard if you breathe too deep."

Unit 14's padlock resisted once, then gave with a dry snap. When Lena rolled the door up, humid air dumped out dense with mildew, motor oil, and paper rot. The beam from the office flashlight cut across stacked boxes bowed in the middle like tired backs. A metal shelf had rusted through one leg and leaned into a bundle of fishing rods. Waterline marks striped the cardboard at mid-calf height.

She stood in the doorway and listened: rain starting in soft drops on the tin roofs, distant interstate hum, one loose gutter tapping time. There was nobody else in the row.

The first box held Deputy uniforms folded by someone who cared about creases long after retirement. Patches from Mercer County Sheriff, old badge pins in a tobacco tin, a cracked citation stamp pad. Second box: tax receipts, bait invoices, church bulletins Ida had probably meant to throw out and never did. Third box: photographs fused at the corners, edges furred white with mold. Her father at a catfish tournament, younger than Lena had ever known him, one arm around men she now recognized as names in city minutes and sheriff reports.

She pulled another box forward and the bottom gave way in her hands, dumping damp files onto concrete. A dead roach skittered from the stack. She knelt, knee protesting, and spread pages in the flashlight beam: handwritten shift logs, map photocopies, an envelope labeled FEED BILL 1984, then nothing, then more nothing. The gaps made more noise than the papers. There should have been more boxes. Shelf dust showed clean rectangles where something had sat for years and then disappeared recently.

Her mouth tasted metallic. Had Ida moved them? Had somebody else with access done a cleanup after chapter-one headlines hit? She checked the lock hasp from inside. There were no fresh pry marks. That did not mean it was safe. It meant someone had been careful.

"Fifteen minutes," Mr. Pritchard called from two rows over. His voice bounced off sheet metal, already irritated.

"I heard you."

She opened a low plastic tote under the shelf and found a black composition notebook sealed in a gallon freezer bag. The bag had yellowed, but the zipper still held. On the notebook cover, in her father's square block letters: INDEX / DO NOT REMOVE PAGES.

Lena sat back on her heels and opened it.

The first pages were columns of initials and numbers, each line tagged with short codes she did not recognize yet: CH-7, LV-2, RIV-A, MC-DKT. Dates ran down the margins from 1979 to 1986. It was not a diary. It was a cross-reference key, built by someone who expected retrieval under pressure.

Her father had taught her as a kid to label fishing tackle by depth and current, never by lure color, because color changed and depth did not. This notebook used that same brain: route first, then cover story, then person. The codes tightened her chest.

She flipped to the back where an alphabetical index was penciled faintly enough to evade a casual glance. Bell, Isaiah had one entry and a page number torn out. Bell family cemetery had another with a star beside it. Then:

Mercer docket - MC-DKT-11 - "land cert chain / deputy intake / Pike chambers copy"

The words sat in the beam like a live wire.

She read the line twice, then once aloud to make sure she was not forcing pattern from fatigue. "Mercer docket."

Rain hardened overhead, drumming the metal roof in fast rolls. Her phone vibrated in her back pocket. She ignored it, turned another page, found a notation linking MC-DKT-11 to box marker RED TWINE LEDGER. She scanned the unit. No red twine, no ledger, only broken banker boxes and a coil of trotline rope.

Phone vibration again, then FaceTime ringtone thin through the rain. Jalen's name flashed across the screen with the picture he hated, him at thirteen holding a fish longer than his forearm and grinning like he had invented the river. She watched the call ring to the final pulse while her thumb hovered above accept.

If she took the call, he would ask where she was, why she had dipped from church, whether this had to be tonight. He would be right in all the ordinary ways and wrong in the one that mattered. Evidence moved when people felt watched. Missing boxes proved that already.

She let it go to voicemail.

The screen showed 7:58 PM, then a text arrived before she could lock it: `nvm. at dad's in 20.`

Guilt came hot, then flattened into focus so quickly it scared her. She set the phone facedown on a dry patch of concrete and kept reading.

Mr. Pritchard's keys jangled nearer. "Eight o'clock, detective. Gate timer's meaner than me."

"Two minutes."

She photographed every page she could, hands steady from habit. Then she slid the notebook back into the freezer bag and tucked it under her arm. She took one old map tube and a packet of loose index cards because they looked like they belonged with the coding system. Everything else stayed. Carrying too much would announce value to anyone watching cameras or logs.

When she rolled the unit door down, rain blew sideways through the row and soaked her shirt in seconds. Mr. Pritchard stood under a golf umbrella patched with black duct tape.

"Find what you needed?" he asked.

"I found enough."

"That's never good news in this county."

He locked the row gate and waited while she signed out on the clipboard. The hearing aid whistled once when he ducked his head. "Your daddy used to come in late," he said, almost to himself. "Always said he was organizing receipts. Nobody organizes receipts at midnight."

Lena met his eyes. "Did he ever come with anyone?"

"Sometimes a courthouse boy in a tie too expensive for humidity. Sometimes alone. I didn't ask. My job was locks."

She nodded and stepped into the rain.

In the car she sat with the notebook in her lap and the engine off, listening to water hammer the windshield until the glass looked milked over. The cab smelled like wet cotton and old coffee. Her pulse had finally slowed, leaving a hollow ache behind her ribs.

Ida had not denied anything. Ida had fought like someone defending a wound from salt.

Lena opened the notebook one more time at the Mercer line. `land cert chain / deputy intake / Pike chambers copy.` Her father's block letters did not tremble. The man who wrote this had believed paper could outlast shame.

Her phone lit again with a voicemail icon from Jalen. She did not play it. She started the car and drove toward home with the notebook on the passenger seat, one hand resting on it at every stoplight as if it might leap away.
