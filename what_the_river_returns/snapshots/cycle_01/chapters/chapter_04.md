# Chapter 4

The morgue sat behind the hospital loading dock like a forgotten appliance, humming harder than it should in the late-August heat. Lena parked under a light that buzzed with moths and got out into air that already felt wet enough to drink. The storm had not broken. The sky pressed down in one solid color, and the concrete gave the heat back through the soles of her shoes.

Inside, bleach and old coffee fought a losing war against sweet rot. The fluorescent tubes shivered overhead. Dr. Naveen Rao stood at the stainless table in a disposable gown, goggles pushed up in his thick hair, reading from a legal pad dense with numbers. He always wrote in pencil, narrow block letters, and the eraser end was wrapped in blue tape because he chewed pencils down to wood by noon. A child had stuck a glitter star sticker to the edge of his county ID badge, and he had not peeled it off.

"You got my message," he said without looking up.

"You said not to use email." Lena set her case file on the counter and kept her voice flat because she was tired enough to sound angry by accident. "I figured that meant it mattered."

"It matters." He finally looked at her. His eyes were red-rimmed and careful. "Beau with you?"

"On his way."

On the back bench, Trina Polk from histology lined up reagent bottles by height, then by label color, then back by height again. She wore two latex gloves on each hand because formalin cracked her knuckles until they bled. Under her breath she counted pipette drops in the same cadence every time, like she was saying grace over each sample.

Lena pulled out a chair and sat before her knee started complaining. Four days since the river ramp and she still had grit from that levee in the hem of her slacks. She had washed the clothes twice. The grit stayed.

Rao flipped a page and pushed a printed chain-of-custody packet toward her. "Start there. I need you to see the gaps before I explain the data."

The packet looked complete until it did not. Item numbers jumped. Time stamps repeated. One page ended mid-line where a signature should have been and resumed on a different date, different pen, same initials. She knew those initials. She had seen them three times this week on forms that had no business matching.

"Who touched this after intake?" she asked.

"Officially? Three people." Rao gave a dry laugh without humor. "Actually? I can't prove that."

He tapped a second folder, older cardboard, brittle at the corners. "This is what we have from the 1963 burial transfer and county pathology addendum. Pages four through seven are gone. Torn, not misplaced. The edge fiber says hand removal, not decay."

Lena stared at the ragged inner seam. Somebody had pulled those pages with fingers, not floodwater. Her mother once kept church bulletins for forty years in acid-free sleeves, and this county could not keep one dead man's paperwork whole for sixty-three.

The door banged. Beau Broussard came in carrying two sweating fountain sodas and a paper bag gone translucent with grease. He was broad enough to make the room smaller and sunburned along the collar where his vest never covered. He set one cup near Lena, then peeled open the bag.

"Boudin kolaches," he said. "If this turns into one more six-hour science sermon, we're eating first."

Rao did not look up. "I heard that."

"You were meant to." Beau leaned on the counter and nodded at the trays. "Tell me we aren't chasing ghosts before the sheriff walks in and says we're."

Rao laid out three specimen cassettes and a set of photographs under the bench lamp. The trays held bone, tissue, and a hair fragment, all tagged from two scenes that should not have shared a corpse. He spoke with his usual precision at first, each clause placed carefully, then sped up as if he could outrun interruption.

"I ran standard morphology, then collagen integrity. Results did not align with a single body profile. I ran duplicate extraction because I assumed contamination. Duplicate matched. I then requested isotope panel approval. It was denied pending command review."

Lena felt Beau turn his head toward her. Denied by command meant Clay.

"So what did you do?" she asked.

Rao reached for his goggles, then put them down. "I ran it anyway under method-validation code. That is outside routine protocol. I documented every step in my own log. If they fire me, they fire me."

Trina stopped counting for one second and looked up. "They better fire all of us," she said, then went back to her pipette, voice steady again. "Because I signed the witness line."

Rao slid a printed chromatogram and three isotope charts across the table.

"Simple version," he said. "The remains at your first scene and the remains at the river ramp were not one intact corpse moving through space. They were assembled. At least three biological sources are represented. One source is consistent with long-buried mid-century remains. Another source has modern dietary isotope profile. The third is partial and degraded, and I can't age it cleanly yet."

Beau swore softly. "Assembled like what?"

"Like someone with access and time, not someone in a panic. Long bone cuts are clean. Soft tissue was rehydrated before placement. The chemistry confirms handling within days, not decades."

Lena looked down at the chemical list and read each compound twice. The list included ethanol, methanol, quaternary ammonium disinfectant, lanolin-based restorative wax, and the dye carrier used in cavity fluid.

"These are funeral chemicals," she said.

"Some of them," Rao said. "Not all. The blend is odd. It is not textbook embalming and not hospital preservation. It is hybrid work, improvised with whatever was available."

He pointed to one peak on the chromatogram. "This marker should not be in remains buried in 1963 and left alone. It degrades fast in soil. I can say with high confidence it was introduced recently."

The room was so cold her skin pebbled, but sweat still gathered at her back under the vest. Outside the steel door someone rolled a gurney past, wheels squealing on one bad bearing, and then it went quiet again.

Lena said, "Say it the way I'd have to say it on record."

Rao met her eyes. "On record: Evidence supports deliberate postmortem manipulation and mixed-source staging. Evidence does not support supernatural recurrence."

Beau let out a breath he had been storing. "Thank Christ."

"Don't thank anybody yet," Rao said. "If this gets in front of the wrong camera before we map source routes, somebody will spin this as desecration by whoever they already planned to blame."

As if cued, Lena's phone buzzed on the table with a command-channel text: _SHERIFF NEEDS YES/NO FOR NOON PRESS. PRELIM PACKET BY 10:00. KEEP MESSAGE DISCIPLINED._

Rao held still. Trina kept counting drops in a whisper.

Lena stared at the screen until it dimmed, then locked it without replying.

Beau rubbed a thumb over the edge of his soda lid until it cracked. "He's going to bury this if he can pin it on chain noise."

"He might try," Lena said. "He can't un-run the chemistry."

Rao pulled another tray forward. "There is more. I went into the old freezer box where they held Isaiah's documented remains after the first-scene intake."

He corrected himself with a grimace. "After intake last week. There were fiber transfers in the drawer track. Cotton-poly blend with crematory ash and diesel particulate. That drawer has not left this building. The fabric did."

"From what?" Lena asked.

"Transport blanket, likely. Cheap fleet issue. I sampled it."

Beau said, "County van blankets are that blend. So are half the funeral homes."

"Exactly," Rao said. "The science gives direction, not identity. I can tell you this took handling, knowledge, and a route. I can't tell you the hands yet."

Lena pulled the old cardboard file toward her and held it by the edges. The pages were missing and the seam was torn. Somebody in this building, or somebody who had keys to this building, knew which years mattered and which forms would have made this easier to prove.

Her mother had looked straight through her at the fish fry when 1963 came up, hands deep in batter, talking about catfish oil temperature like it was scripture. _Some papers should have burned_, Ida had said. Lena had heard warning; maybe it had been grief.

"Can you tie the modern chemistry to supplier lots?" Lena asked.

Rao nodded once. "Maybe. I need purchase records from county crematory inventory and private funeral transfer logs for at least the last six months. And I need them before somebody edits the spreadsheet history."

Beau gave a short laugh. "You're asking for a unicorn and a warrant."

"Ask anyway," Rao said. "Also, keep this off email."

He pushed a folded page toward Lena with handwritten columns in pencil. The page listed brand abbreviations and partial lot codes, and two chemical families were circled. At the bottom he had written one sentence in all caps: _IF YOU GO PUBLIC EARLY, THEY WILL PICK THE WRONG MONSTER ON PURPOSE._

Lena put the page in her notebook. The paper was warm from his hand.

Trina sealed the last cassette and finally looked up fully. Her voice was soft but carried. "My cousin works intake at Bell and Sons on weekends," she said. "She says county overflow has been coming through odd hours this month. It was not illegal, just odd. Like nobody wants daylight signatures."

Lena held her gaze. "What is your cousin's name?"

"Keisha Polk." Trina peeled off her outer gloves and flexed her cracked fingers. "She's saving for dental school and she hates all of us on principle. But she writes everything down."

"Would she talk?"

"If you ask like a person and not like command." Trina's mouth twitched. "And if you don't let this county hang it on the first Black family with a funeral sign."

No one answered for a beat. The compressor kicked on in the wall with a shudder.

Lena capped her pen. "I hear you."

She stood, knee stiff, and looked once more at the charts spread across steel. The rumors had given her one kind of dread: that the world was ripping at its own seams, that law and evidence were toys. Rao's data replaced that dread with a different one. Somebody had built this horror with tools, invoices, access cards, and enough patience to lay body parts beside fresh murder victims and watch a county tear itself apart.

A ghost would have been easier.

She gathered the packet and turned to Beau. "We start with inventory and transport. Quietly. If Clay asks, we're still verifying sample integrity."

Beau nodded. "I can pull fuel-card pings if dispatch has not scrubbed them yet."

"Pull first," she said. "Then tell me what we're allowed to know."

---

By noon the thunderheads had climbed up and gone nowhere, the sun trapped behind them and the air hotter for it. Lena and Beau crossed the courthouse annex lot with paper folders under plastic and sweat at their collars. In the far corner, two stray dogs slept under a county maintenance truck because the truck still dripped cool water from its A/C line.

Purchasing sat on the second floor above tax appeals, behind glass that made everyone look underwater. Darlene Fitch worked the counter in a navy cardigan despite the heat and wore metal finger splints on both index fingers from a quilting injury she talked about before anyone asked. She kept a row of cat photos clipped to her monitor and corrected their names when people called them strays.

Lena passed over her badge and request sheet. "I need chemical lot purchases tied to county crematory operations and overflow transfers, six-month window."

Darlene read the request, then looked at Lena with real sympathy that did not help. "I can give you aggregate totals. Named vendor lines are under litigation hold as of this morning."

"By whose order?"

"Sheriff's legal office." Darlene lowered her voice. "Don't shoot me. I do payroll for my church too. I know what it looks like when numbers get moved before Sunday."

Beau leaned in, elbows on the counter. "Darlin', we don't need a sermon. We need the raw file before it gets polished."

"Then you needed to come yesterday." She slid a printed summary through the slot. "This is what I can release without losing my job and my health insurance."

It was thin and bland by design: monthly totals, no names, no lot numbers. Useless in court, but one line was not useless at all. Preservative and restoration compounds had spiked threefold in August, while official death intake stayed flat.

Lena tapped the column. "What changed in August?"

Darlene gave a tight shrug. "Compressor failure at county crematory in July, then temporary overflow routing. That's what the memo said."

"Where is the memo?"

"In the hold folder."

Beau muttered, "Convenient as hell."

Darlene glanced toward the back office and lowered her voice again. "If you go to Logistics, ask for carbon-copy manifests, not the portal printout. Portal history got 'cleaned' after breakfast."

Lena nodded once. "Thank you."

"I didn't help you," Darlene said, but she tapped twice on the cat photo labeled _Biscuit_, a nervous habit she seemed not to notice. "I told you where the fire is."

Logistics was three blocks away in a converted feed warehouse that still smelled like grain under the bleach. The clerk at dispatch refused them at first, then sent them to the loading coordinator, then to records, then back to dispatch again. Each desk said policy. Each desk said come back with formal approval. By the third loop Beau's jaw had set hard enough to show a pulse below his ear.

"They're running us in circles until somebody at command calls," he said.

"Then we stop asking on their path." Lena scanned the wall above dispatch and found what she wanted: old metal cabinets, color tabs, years hand-labeled in marker. The county had digitized everything except what was too annoying to scan.

She went to the oldest clerk in the room, a man with hearing aids and a Saints lanyard who had been silently sharpening pencils with a pocketknife. His tag read B. Whitaker.

"Mr. Whitaker," Lena said, "where are the carbon manifests for overflow transfer runs? The blue copy, not the white."

He looked at her badge, then at Beau, then back at her. "Who told you to ask that?"

"Nobody official."

"Good answer." He stood with a wince and led them to a cabinet jammed half shut by humidity-swollen paper. "Blue copies stick because the ink is waxy. Folks hate pulling them. That means they're usually still here."

Whitaker wore one knitted glove though it was ninety-eight degrees. He caught Lena looking and said, matter-of-fact, "Neuropathy. Feels like fire in one hand and ice in the other. You want July and August, drawer three. You didn't get this from me."

The manifests were smudged and old-school, route by route, driver initials, odometer start and stop, vehicle number, destination code. Lena and Beau took photos as fast as the office printer coughed toner dust nearby.

Three things surfaced inside ten minutes.

First: Vehicle 14B, the county refrigerated van a gin mechanic had flagged on day one, had made unauthorized after-hours stops on both nights before the two murder scenes. It was not logged as scene transport. It was logged as "overflow transfer."

Second: Destination codes that should have read only county crematory included repeated hand-written detours to B&S-2, Bell and Sons back receiving bay, 01:12 a.m., 02:07 a.m., times that kept the truck off main roads and out of daylight.

Third: The signatures on those detour lines were initialed H.R. once, then scratched over, then replaced with generic shift initials no one in dispatch claimed.

Beau stared at the page long enough to leave sweat spots at the margin. "H.R. can be half this damn building."

"Not on that route," Whitaker said quietly from behind them. "Route 14B requires deputy escort authorization after midnight. That changed last month."

Lena turned. "Changed by who?"

Whitaker slid his pocketknife closed and tucked it away. "Policy bulletin came from sheriff operations with attachment from Judge Pike's emergency facilities memo. I remember because it was the only memo that used three signatures where one would do."

Beau cursed under his breath. "Judge Pike does not write transport policy."

Whitaker gave him a look that was almost pity. "Son, around here people write what they have already paid for."

A phone rang at dispatch. No one moved to answer it.

Lena copied the memo reference number from the cabinet index and felt her pulse shift into that cold, narrow band she got when the shape of a thing emerged. It was not solved. It was only shaped.

She called Rao from the hallway. He picked up on the second ring.

"Tell me you got lot codes," he said.

"Not yet. We got routes, detours, and a spike in preservative purchases. Overflow was routed through Bell and Sons back bay at night."

Silence for a beat. "That would explain your hybrid chemistry. County stock plus private stock in one handling chain."

"Can you match your compounds to county-only versus funeral-home-only formulations?"

"Partially. If I can get one unopened reference bottle from each site, yes." Rao exhaled hard. "Lena, be careful with how you write this. Bell and Sons employs half that family. If command wants a fast villain, this is tidy enough to weaponize."

"I know." She watched rain begin in huge drops that hit hot asphalt and vanished before they pooled. "I'm not handing Clay a family name and a press podium."

"Good," Rao said. "Because the data says network. It is not one person. It is not one bloodline. It is a network of access."

She hung up and found Beau leaning against the cinderblock wall under a broken fan. He had taken his tie off and wrapped it around one hand, twisting it tight and loose, tight and loose.

"You're thinking about Ruth Bell," he said.

Lena had not said her name out loud. "I'm thinking about what this county does when it gets a chance to recycle blame."

"Yeah." Beau looked out at the rain starting to stick now, darkening the lot in coin-sized spots. "And I'm thinking if H.R. matches who I think, this isn't just a body job. It's command cover."

"It has been command cover since day one," Lena said. "Now we can prove pieces of it."

They went back inside and pulled every blue-copy manifest for six weeks. The stack grew thick enough to bend the folder clips. A pattern repeated: late-night reroutes, unsigned corrections, missing digital mirrors for paper runs. The more ordinary the paperwork looked, the uglier it became.

At four-thirty, with thunder finally cracking over the levee, Lena drove the stack to Rao in a banker box that smelled like wet cardboard. He met them at the morgue side door with his sleeves rolled and a surgical cap still on his head, as if he had forgotten it there.

"I can work from this," he said.

"Can you get me language by morning that survives cross-exam?" Lena asked.

"If nobody burns the lab down tonight, yes."

Beau handed him a photocopy of the route sheet with the scratched initials. "You didn't get this from me."

Rao took it and looked from one of them to the other. "You both look like hell."

"That means we're on schedule," Beau said.

Lena did not laugh. She watched Rao carry the box inside and thought about the torn pages again, how neat the removal had been. It was not rage. It was planning. Somebody had treated history like inventory, pulling what they needed, discarding what complicated the sale.

By the time she got back to her car, rain hammered the roof hard enough to drown the cicadas. Her phone lit with three missed calls from command and one from her mother that lasted nine seconds and left no voicemail.

She sat with her hand on the wheel and let the rain drum through the metal.

The county had wanted a ghost story because ghost stories did not need subpoenas. Ghost stories did not have fuel cards, shift codes, and procurement spikes. Ghost stories did not implicate offices with letterhead.

What Rao gave her was worse and better. It was worse because people had done this. It was better because people left receipts.
