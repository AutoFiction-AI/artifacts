# Chapter 5

By ten in the morning, the courthouse steps sounded like a boiler with a cracked valve. Chant rose against chant, whistles against bullhorns, and somewhere in the middle somebody had put a snare drum on a folding chair and kept beating the same roll until it turned into a headache for half the block.

Lena stood inside the metal barricade corridor with sweat running down her spine under her vest and counted bodies the way she counted shell casings: fast, again, then one more time because the first count was never enough. Fifty-two on the west side by the veterans memorial. Forty-eight on the east side under the dead magnolia. Five deputies between them and two city uniforms who looked nineteen and scared of their own batons.

The sun sat straight overhead, white and blunt. Heat came off the asphalt in visible waves, and the paint on the bike-lane stripes smelled sweet and chemical, like new toys left in a hot trunk. Cicadas screamed from the oaks beyond the jail annex and never once took a breath.

"We're about one shove from a pileup," Beau said beside her. He had taken off his tie and stuffed it in his back pocket, where it hung like a surrender flag. "I hate to be old and prophetic, but this is how every bad shrimp boil starts. Too many cousins, not enough shade."

"Keep west side six feet off the rails," Lena said. She kept her voice flat and procedural because it helped her think. "No chemical spray unless I call it. If they surge, we split at the fountain, not at the stairs."

"Copy." He scratched the edge of his jaw and glanced at the crowd. "You ate anything?"

"Coffee."

"That is not food, partner."

She did not answer. She had not slept more than two hours, and coffee sat in her stomach like a fist.

A woman in a denim vest leaned over the west barricade and shouted, "You all protect killers!" She held a hand-painted sign that said SAY ISAIAH'S NAME and had a cartoon river painted in blue nail polish. The polish had cracked in the heat, and she kept rubbing the edge of the poster with her thumb like she could smooth it back to perfect.

Across from her, a man in a camo cap yelled, "Do your damn jobs and lock somebody up!" His sign said LAW AND ORDER NOW in machine-cut vinyl letters that had not been made in his garage.

They were not shouting at each other anymore. They were shouting at Lena.

Her name moved through both groups in different tones and landed the same way, sharp and personal. Waller. Waller. Detective Waller is hiding something. Detective Waller is the only honest cop left. Waller's dirty. Waller's brave. Waller's bought. Waller's one of us. Waller's not.

A plastic water bottle flew from the east side and hit the barricade hard enough to ring. A deputy flinched and grabbed for his belt spray.

"Hands off," Lena snapped.

He froze. "Yes, ma'am."

On the courthouse landing, Sergeant Aisha Lane adjusted the elastic on her riot helmet and spat sunflower seed shell into a paper cup she carried for that purpose. She had quit smoking nine months ago and told everybody who asked that seeds were cheaper than nicotine patches and less likely to make her punch a lieutenant. She caught Lena's eye and tapped two fingers toward the north corner.

Lena moved that way, shoulders turned sideways to slip through the corridor. The crowd smell changed every three steps: sunscreen, beer sweat, incense, stale cigarettes, somebody's vanilla body spray, somebody else's fear.

A college kid in a press vest held up his phone and said, "Detective Waller, are you suppressing suspect names?"

"I'm not discussing active witness interviews," she said.

"That's not what I asked."

"Then ask a better question."

He blinked, half offended and half thrilled to have something quotable.

At the corner, Lane handed Lena a folded sheet torn from a copier ream. "They're passing these out on both sides," Lane said. "Not from one group. Both."

Lena unfolded it.

The paper showed her house in a satellite screenshot, roofline outlined in red. HER ADDRESS in bold black font. Side yard circled. Front door circled. It listed Marcus's full name and Jalen's school with the dismissal time in parentheses.

Under it: IF SHE WON'T PROTECT THE COUNTY, PROTECT YOURSELVES.

For one second Lena did not hear the crowd at all. Sound dropped out like somebody had packed her ears with mud. She only saw the front step of her house, the chipped brick where Jalen had fallen at seven and split his lip, the cheap motion light she kept meaning to replace.

Then noise came back all at once.

"Where'd you pull this?" she asked.

"North lot," Lane said. "Little blond guy in an American flag fishing shirt. I got his photo. Couldn't keep him. Crowd swallowed him."

"Send it to my secure folder and to Beau." Lena folded the flyer into quarters so tight her knuckles hurt. "And get a unit to my house now. Marked car out front, one in back alley. I want body-cam rolling before they get there."

"Already done. I sent Ortega and Finch." Lane paused. "You want me to call your people?"

"I got it."

Her hands were shaking. She pressed the flyer flat against her thigh until they stopped enough to tap Marcus's number.

He picked up on the second ring. "I was about to call you."

"Tell me where Jalen is."

"At home. Why?"

"Get him out. Right now."

A beat. "Lena, what happened?"

"My address is on flyers in a crowd of five hundred angry people. They're using his school schedule as a threat. Put him in the car and go to your sister's place in Greenwood or my mother's if Ida's home. Do not stay there waiting on me."

Marcus breathed once through his nose, the way he did when he was trying not to swing from calm to rage in one jump. "Is there a car there?"

"Two units en route."

"That helps exactly none if somebody's already at the porch."

"Marcus. Move."

"We're moving." His voice hardened. "You understand this is done. This is not normal ugly. This is over-the-line ugly."

She knew what he meant and hated him for saying it out loud because he was not wrong.

"Go," she said.

When she hung up, she found Beau watching her face.

"Flyer?" he asked.

She showed him.

His jaw tightened. "Jesus Christ." He looked toward the steps where media cameras crowded the tape line. "That's personnel file data. School schedule too. That didn't come from Facebook gossip."

"I know." 

"You want me to pull comms and request cyber?"

"Clay'll slow-roll it if he thinks it touches his office." She looked over Beau's shoulder at the crowd where three different hands now waved the same sheet of paper. "Get Lane's photo to Mara Quinn. She can run facial match faster than our unit if they decide this is politically inconvenient."

He gave her a long look. "You want to say that part again into a recorder?"

"Not yet."

A horn blast ripped through the corridor. Somebody on the east side shoved hard enough to topple a barricade section. Metal crashed. Three people screamed, not from injury, from surprise and the sudden animal certainty that everyone else might run.

Lena was moving before thought finished. She grabbed one end of the barrier, shoulder burning, and shoved it upright while Beau and two deputies locked the feet back in place. The camo-cap man lunged forward with his sign pole like a spear. On the west side, the denim-vest woman swung her poster board and caught him across the mouth. Teeth clicked. Blood appeared quick and bright on his lower lip.

"Back up!" Lena yelled. She put her body between them, palms out. "Both of you, now. You move again and you're in cuffs."

"He came at me!" the woman yelled.

"She hit me!" the man shouted through blood.

"I do not care who started it. I care who stops. Back. Up."

The woman stepped back first, chest heaving. The man hesitated, then spat red on the pavement and retreated two steps. It was enough. The wave that had started to curl broke and flattened into shouting again.

Lane arrived with two more officers and took over the line, seeds cracking between her teeth as if she had all day.

A voice called Lena's name from behind the press rope.

Mara Quinn stood on the courthouse lawn with a camera sling across her chest and a notebook clipped to the back pocket of her jeans. She was wearing old running shoes with orange laces that never matched anything else she owned, because she said she could outrun cops in them when she had to. She held up one of the doxxing flyers in a plastic evidence sleeve.

"You want this on record?" Mara asked.

"Not from me," Lena said.

"Then off record: I already got two names from print-shop watermarks. One of them's a county campaign vendor."

"Send it encrypted."

"You're still allowed to receive email?"

Lena looked at the courthouse doors where Clay's aide waited with her phone pressed to her ear, eyes locked on Lena like she was counting down a bomb.

"For now," Lena said.

Mara glanced toward the aide. "You getting hauled upstairs?"

"Emergency command hearing."

"About crowd control or about you refusing to give them a sacrificial suspect by dinner?"

"Both."

Mara's mouth tightened. "You need anything from me before they shut your badge in a drawer?"

The sentence hit harder than it should have because it sounded practical, not dramatic.

"Track who paid for these flyers," Lena said. "And if my house gets touched, I want faces before I want sympathy."

"You'll get both anyway," Mara said. "Faces first."

The aide cut through the rope and stopped three feet from Lena, breathless and composed at the same time. Assistant City Manager Renata Bell wore a yellow cardigan despite the heat because office air-conditioning gave her migraines. A dark damp line ran down her spine, and she ignored it.

"Detective Waller," Bell said, "the mayor and sheriff require you in conference room two immediately."

"I require ten more minutes out here," Lena said.

Bell shook her head. "The hearing started without you."

Beau muttered, "That ain't a hearing. That's a sentence with furniture."

Lena handed Lane the radio channel and climbed the courthouse steps with the doxxing flyer still folded in her fist so tight the paper edge cut her palm.

---

Conference room two had the same fluorescent buzz as every county room where bad decisions got laundered into policy. A long table. Water pitchers sweating onto legal pads nobody read. A state flag in one corner and a television on mute in the other, showing live footage from outside: signs, crowd, Lena's own face in a tight shot she had never consented to.

Mayor Evelyn Tate sat at the head of the table in a navy suit that did not wrinkle even in August. She kept pinching the edge of a nicotine patch under her sleeve, a little half-inch adjustment every time someone said the word liability. Sheriff Clay Reddick sat two seats down, jacket buttoned, tie centered, expression patient enough to be insulting.

City attorney Glenn Weems had three yellow highlighters lined up by color and had already capped one with his teeth hard enough to crack plastic.

"Detective Waller," Tate said, "thank you for joining us."

Clay gave a courtly nod. "We're all on the same team here."

Lena sat and put the folded flyer on the table in front of her.

"Are we?" she asked.

Tate glanced at the paper and then away. "We'll discuss personal security, yes. First, we need immediate public stabilization. The county has entered a volatility window."

Clay folded his hands. "People are scared. They need visible progress and a coherent message."

"A coherent message is not the same as a true one," Lena said.

Weems sighed like she had tracked mud onto his carpet. "Detective, this is an operational meeting, not a philosophy seminar."

"Good," Lena said. "Operationally: we do not have probable cause for an arrest that survives first appearance. We still have chain-of-custody breach at the morgue corridor and a likely internal leak of protected personnel data. If you force a name today, the defense will shred it and we will lose the case before lab reports finish."

Clay leaned back. "Nobody said force."

"You said visible progress." Lena tapped the flyer. "This is what your progress campaign produced by noon. My kid's school schedule is in a crowd chant."

Tate's jaw flexed. "Your family protection is a priority."

"Then stop feeding a narrative that says I am hiding suspects."

Clay smiled without warmth. "You keep personalizing institutional pressure, Detective. That's part of the concern."

She looked at him. "Institutional pressure is not weather. People make choices."

The mayor held up a hand. "Enough. Here is what happens next. Sheriff Reddick will hold a four p.m. press availability. He will confirm active leads and announce a narrowed suspect field. Detective Waller, you will stand with him and reinforce message discipline regarding ongoing evidentiary review."

"No," Lena said.

Nobody moved for a full second.

Weems cleared his throat. "Excuse me?"

"I will not stand in front of cameras and imply we have a suspect field if we do not." She kept her voice level and heard herself doing it, heard the old academy habit of building a staircase of clauses before jumping. "I will brief what is true: two current homicides linked by staged historical remains, breach points in county custody, and active witness interviews that are not ready for public release. I will not sell closure I cannot prove."

Clay's fingers tapped once on the table, then stilled. "You are not being asked to sell closure. You're being asked to support command decisions in a volatile environment."

"Command decisions that manufacture consent for a bad arrest are still bad arrests."

Tate looked at Clay, then at Lena. "Detective, choose your next sentence carefully."

Lena thought of the flyer. She thought of Jalen's backpack by the front door and the chipped brick step and the way fear had tunneled through her chest so fast it felt electrical.

"Then put this in your notes," she said. "If you need optics more than evidence, suspend me now and own that on camera."

Weems made a small disbelieving noise. Clay did not smile anymore.

"You are out of line," Clay said.

"I'm in the line where perjury starts."

The mayor spoke as if reading from an internal memo she had drafted on the drive over. "By authority of the city public safety emergency order and in consultation with elected county command, Detective Lena Waller is suspended from investigative duty pending immediate internal review for insubordination and unauthorized public contradiction of command messaging. Effective now."

The room went very still. Fluorescent buzz got louder.

Clay slid a form across the table. "Sign acknowledgment."

Lena read enough to see the words REMOVAL OF ACCESS PRIVILEGES and CONTACT RESTRICTIONS WITH ACTIVE CASE WITNESSES without counsel present.

"You're restricting witness contact?" she asked.

"Standard integrity protection," Weems said.

"No," Lena said. "Standard integrity protection would investigate whoever leaked my home address from personnel files."

Clay's voice turned almost gentle, which was how he sounded when he wanted his threat to pass as concern. "You need to cool off, Detective. You are too close to this one."

"I'm close because you put me on it and then kneecapped every clean lead."

"Sign the form," Tate said.

Lena signed.

Outside the room, cameras waited at the end of the hall because county buildings leaked faster than roofs in hurricane season. She could have walked past in silence. She could have preserved the thin fiction that this was temporary and procedural.

She stopped.

A reporter shouted, "Detective Waller, are you off the case?"

"I was suspended for refusing to announce an arrest without probable cause," Lena said.

Clay's voice came from behind her, calm and amplified for microphones. "Detective Waller is on administrative pause while we streamline operations for public safety."

She turned toward the cameras. "If your kid's name is in these flyers, that is not streamlined. That is reckless."

Gasps, scribbling, phones rising.

Clay stepped up beside her and put one hand lightly on her elbow as if he were guiding a relative to a chair. His smile for cameras was almost fatherly.

"No further comment," he said.

By the time she reached the detective bullpen to clear out her desk, her key card was dead. Red light. Single beep.

Officer Chaney from IT stood there with a cardboard archive box and red eyes from allergies or shame. He collected novelty spoons from every county fair in the state and kept one from Tupelo in his shirt pocket as a lucky charm. It flashed when he shifted his weight.

"I hate this part," he said quietly.

"Then don't perform it like theater," Lena said, then softened because he looked nineteen under the fluorescent flicker though he was thirty if a day. "Sorry. Not at you."

"I know." He swallowed. "I've gotta witness badge turn-in and device surrender."

She placed her badge, department phone, and access fob in the box. The absence of weight on her belt felt like missing a tooth. She kept touching the empty spot with the side of her hand.

Beau appeared at the end of the hall, stopped when he saw the box, and did not come closer because cameras were still in the stairwell.

"I'll call you tonight," he said.

Clay's voice carried from the briefing room: "Detective Broussard, inside, now."

Beau shook his head once, tiny, like a man checking if a bridge cable might hold.

"Keep your phone loud," he said to Lena, then turned away.

She walked out of the building without a badge, without air-conditioning, without the protection of being a problem someone still had to include.

---

At dusk her street looked normal from a distance, which made it worse.

Kids rode scooters two blocks over. A dog barked behind a chain-link fence. Somebody grilled chicken with too much lighter fluid and sent a sweet petroleum smell down the block. Storm clouds stacked purple over the levee, but no rain came.

Closer to her house, two patrol cars sat where she had asked, one at the curb and one in the alley. Both officers were alert and embarrassed, which Lena recognized because she had worn that expression herself outside other people's disasters.

The front porch light was on. Marcus's car was gone.

She let herself inside with the house key she still had even if the county had stripped everything else.

Marcus sat at the kitchen table with an open duffel bag between his feet. He wore scrub pants and an old Delta Regional hoodie with bleach freckles on the sleeves. He worked nights as a respiratory therapist and still smelled faintly of hospital sanitizer and coffee from vending machines that never got cleaned. When he was scared, he lined bottle caps by color along the table edge without noticing he was doing it. Six blue, four green, one red.

Jalen stood by the sink in sock feet, phone in hand, jaw tight in the way teenagers pretend is indifference. He had grown two inches this summer and moved like his body was still surprising him.

"You made it," Marcus said.

"Where were you going?"

"My sister's. He didn't want to go." Marcus jerked his chin toward Jalen. "Said if somebody wants to come here, they can come while he's here too."

"I'm not hiding at Aunt Trina's because idiots printed paper," Jalen said.

"Watch your mouth," Marcus said automatically.

"No, let him swear," Lena said. "He's earned it today."

Jalen looked at her then, properly looked, and his shoulders dropped half an inch. "Are we actually in danger or is this cop danger where everybody acts dramatic for TV?"

Lena pulled the flyer from her pocket and set it on the table.

Jalen read silently. His face changed in stages. First annoyance. Then disbelief. Then the flat stare of someone trying not to give adults the fear they were obviously asking for.

"They got my school," he said.

"Yeah."

"Did your people do this?"

She should have lied. She should have said no, impossible, outside agitators, anything clean enough for a son.

"It used county data," she said.

Jalen laughed once without humor. "Cool. Great employer."

Marcus rubbed his eyes with thumb and forefinger. "I called Felicia Kemp." 

"The labor attorney?"

"The one you said knew internal discipline cases and juvenile protection orders. She'll call in fifteen."

Lena set her hands on the counter because if she sat she might not stand back up tonight.

"I got suspended," she said.

Marcus stared. "Today?"

"Effective immediately."

"For what?"

"I told them I wouldn't stand in front of cameras and fake an arrest timeline."

Jalen let out a low whistle. "So you got fired for not lying."

"Suspended," Lena said.

"Same shape, different word."

Marcus pushed the bottle caps into a pile hard enough to scatter them again. "Lena, listen to me. You are done with this until they clean their mess. You hear me? No side calls. No hero bullshit. You already gave them your house, your kid's school, and now your paycheck."

His voice cracked on paycheck. That was the honest part.

She thought of the credit-union balance she never opened on days like this. Twelve thousand four hundred dollars hanging over everything, monthly auto-withdrawal waiting like a clock.

"You think I gave them this?" she asked.

"I think they will burn you and sleep fine." Marcus stood, palms flat on the table. "I don't care about your oath right now. I care that my son does not get grabbed outside algebra because somebody wants to scare a detective straight."

Jalen spoke without looking up from the flyer. "Can I stay with Grandma tonight? She'll make me shell peas until midnight and I won't doom-scroll."

The request relieved her enough that it hurt.

"Yeah," Lena said. "We'll take you."

Her phone buzzed with an unknown number. She answered on speaker.

"Ms. Waller, this is Felicia Kemp." The voice was low and clipped, precise as a stapler. "I am in my office with my shoes off and a charley horse in my left calf, so if I sound irritable, that is because pain is not an abstract concept."

Marcus mouthed told you.

"I appreciate the call," Lena said.

"I reviewed your suspension memo. Do not contact active witnesses, crime-scene personnel, or assigned detectives without counsel present. The sheriff's office drafted broad language. If you violate it, they can recommend obstruction inquiry."

"Can they enforce that off duty?"

"They can make your life expensive while arguing they can." A paper rustle. "Also, there is a secondary risk: if you are seen directing investigative steps while suspended, defense counsel can argue witness contamination and retaliatory motive."

"What about family protection?"

"I filed emergency request for directed patrol and records audit. It may move quickly if your press clip keeps trending. It may stall if command decides silence helps optics. I do not bill for pessimism, detective, but I include it free."

Jalen snorted despite himself.

Lena asked, "If a journalist sends me documents independently obtained through public records, am I barred from reading them?"

Kemp paused. "Reading is not directing. But if your fingerprints are on acquisition strategy, opposing counsel will say you used media as a proxy investigator."

"Noted."

"You need sleep and a written log of every non-family contact starting now," Kemp said. "Time stamps. Method. Topic. Keep it boring. Boring keeps you out of jail."

"Understood."

"I am not your mother, but I am about to sound like her. Eat something with protein before your blood sugar murders your judgment."

The line clicked dead.

Marcus stared at Lena. "You heard her."

"I did."

"Say it back."

"No witness contact. No directing. Log everything." She rubbed the cut in her palm where the flyer had bitten. "I heard her."

A knock sounded at the back door. Three quick taps, pause, two taps.

Mara's code from the year before when deputies had tried to seize her notebook in a parking lot.

Marcus muttered, "You have got to be kidding me," but stepped aside.

Mara came in carrying a grocery bag and her phone rig. Rain had not started, but humidity had curled sweat along her hairline. She set the bag on the table and pulled out a carton of eggs, a loaf of bread, and a brown envelope thick with printouts.

"I brought groceries so this doesn't look like conspiracy if somebody's filming through your hedge," she said. "Also, your hedge is dead on one side."

"Helpful," Lena said.

"I try." Mara nodded at Marcus and Jalen. "Hey."

Jalen gave a small wave and slipped down the hall to pack, because teenagers understood when adults were about to say things they would regret in front of a child.

Marcus crossed his arms. "You're press."

"Yes," Mara said. "And I'm not here for a quote."

He looked at Lena. "You just got warned by counsel."

"We're discussing public documents," Lena said.

"You are going to split that hair until it bleeds."

"Probably."

Mara opened the envelope. "Print-shop watermark confirms campaign vendor: Delta Meridian Strategies. Same vendor that handled reelection mailers for three county seats, including Clay's PAC. I also pulled bulk text logs from a protest organizer chat. Somebody pushed your address as a 'community alert' at 9:12 this morning. Number routes through a prepaid account bought cash at a gas station on Highway 61."

Marcus said, "And this helps us how?"

"It helps by giving us a path that isn't waiting for people who already decided Lena is expendable," Mara said.

Lena looked at the pages. Names. Time stamps. Vendor invoice fragments with blurred totals. Nothing admissible yet, but it had shape.

"I can't tell you where to go next," Lena said carefully.

"Good," Mara said. "Do not. But you can answer one thing as background. If I file open-records on sheriff campaign vendor payments and county emergency communications between seven and noon, am I fishing or am I on a dock?"

Lena met her eyes. "You're on a dock. Deep water."

Mara nodded once, decision made. Her silence lasted three seconds exactly, and Lena recognized it from the style of her thinking: risk calculation, ethics pass, then motion.

"Then I file tonight," Mara said. "And I call my editor before Clay calls his."

Marcus gave a bitter laugh. "Great. Everybody gets a strategy but my kid."

Lena turned to him. "Jalen goes to Ida's for three nights. Patrol stays here. You take day shifts if you can swap and keep pickup routine off social media. I won't post, tag, or answer unknowns."

"And you?"

"I stay visible enough that they don't think they can scare me into a scripted confession, and quiet enough that Felicia doesn't have to represent me in an obstruction hearing."

"That is not a plan."

"It's what we have."

Thunder rolled far off, long and low. Still no rain.

Mara repacked the envelope and slid one sheet back toward Lena: a public campaign-finance report with Clay's committee line item highlighted in green.

"I can leave this here," Mara said. "It's public. If someone asks, I dropped off eggs and paperwork from the internet."

Lena almost smiled. "You always were romantic."

"Do not confuse me with your failed marriage banter," Mara said, and for the first time all day her mouth twitched at one corner.

Marcus did not smile. "I'm taking him now."

Lena nodded.

When Jalen came back with his duffel, he hugged her hard and fast, already turned away before she could decide what to say. He smelled like laundry soap and the peppermint gum he chewed when he was trying to act older than sixteen.

"Text me when you get there," she said.

"I will. Lock both deadbolts." He looked toward Mara. "If you put my face on your site, I will sue you myself."

"Noted," Mara said.

The house emptied in under six minutes. Front door shut. Car engine started. Tires on gravel, then gone.

Silence settled wrong, too large for the rooms.

Lena stood at the kitchen sink and watched the patrol car lights wash blue across her dead hedge. Her knee scar throbbed with the weather shift, old surgery metal warning storm before the sky did.

Mara rinsed two coffee mugs and set them upside down on a towel like she had lived there for years.

"You okay?" Mara asked.

"No." Lena touched the empty space on her belt again, then dropped her hand. "But I can still count."

"Count what?"

"Who benefits if I disappear from this case." Lena looked at the highlighted campaign line item and then at the doxxing flyer on the table, edges curled from sweat. "Today they took the badge. Tomorrow they try to take credibility. After that they try to take memory."

Mara leaned against the counter, arms folded. "Then we build memory where they can't seize it. Time stamps, documents, recordings, multiple copies."

Lena nodded.

She pulled a legal pad from the junk drawer, wrote the date and time at the top, and started a log the way Felicia told her: plain, boring, admissible. Contact: Marcus Waller, safety relocation. Contact: Felicia Kemp, legal restrictions. Contact: Mara Quinn, public records background discussion.

Outside, the crowd downtown was still audible in thin waves when the wind shifted, a far-off roar like river water moving through a narrowed channel.

Lena listened to it and kept writing, suspended and sweating in her own kitchen, case hottest it had ever been and now out of official reach, already building the only reach she had left.
