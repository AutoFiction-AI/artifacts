# Chapter 3

By five-thirty the morgue loading bay already smelled like hot pennies and disinfectant, as if the building had spent the night chewing on blood and bleach and had not decided which taste it preferred. Lena parked beside the biohazard dumpster, killed the engine, and sat for one extra breath with both palms on the steering wheel. Her right knee complained in its old surgical seam when she turned too fast. The humidity got inside scar tissue before it touched skin.

Beau came around the hood with two coffees balanced in one hand and a manila folder under his arm. His shirt was wet at the spine before sunrise.

"You sure we need to do this before shift change?" he asked. "Night people are meaner than day people and worse at paperwork."

"That's why we're here now," Lena said. "If this was a one-off stunt at the gin, the log should be clean this morning. If it isn't, we start with custody breach, not folklore."

He handed her coffee. "I brought the kind you hate because it stays hot longer."

"That isn't coffee. That's roofing tar."

"Tar keeps you upright."

Inside, the hallway lights flickered with a faint insect buzz. The tile floor held a film of damp that made every shoe squeak. Rochelle Pritchard was at the intake counter in a turtle-print scrub cap, balancing a crossword on the clip tray while she signed transfer receipts. She had one purple nitrile glove on and one bare hand with chipped coral polish. A nicotine patch curled off the back of her neck. She greeted Lena without looking up, filling three letters in the crossword with quick, angry jabs.

"You people are early," Rochelle said. "I get off in thirty-two minutes and my girl has all-state band tryouts at seven. If y'all are here to ask me broad questions and nod at me, do it fast."

"We're not here to nod," Lena said. "We need drawer verification on Bell remains and full access history from midnight to now. Printed and digital."

Rochelle finally looked up. Her eyes were red in the corners in the way of somebody who had cried or stared too long at fluorescent light. "You need Dr. Rao for release-level access."

"Call him," Lena said.

"Already did when your car turned in. I might work night shift, but I do own ears."

Rochelle led them down the cold corridor. The compressor thrum sat in Lena's teeth. On the steel door to Cold Storage B, the swipe panel showed green, then amber, then green again before it unlocked. Rochelle noticed the flicker and swore softly.

"It did that twice at two in the morning," she said. "I logged it. Maintenance said 'reboot the panel.' Maintenance says that about everything except their marriages."

She opened the paper ledger clipped beside the door. Lena pulled her phone and photographed each page with timestamp overlay. Entry lines for the last six hours looked wrong before she did any math. The handwriting changed mid-line. One row listed Bell remains transferred from Drawer B-7 to B-7, same location, same minute, different initials. Another row had an impossible clock sequence: 02:14 release stamp followed by 02:09 return seal.

Lena slid the folder from Beau's hand and opened a fresh discrepancy form. "We're not doing memory today," she said. "We're doing record. Beau, witness line. Rochelle, you'll sign originator and correction note."

Rochelle set her jaw. "You think I forged this?"

"I think the log's compromised," Lena said. "That can happen to your pen, your software, or your card reader. We document it so nobody gets to rewrite it later."

Beau signed first, muttering, "Never seen a dead man check himself in and out like a motel guest."

Rochelle gave him a look that should have peeled paint. "You got jokes before sunrise, Broussard. Must be nice."

Dr. Naveen Rao arrived in a wrinkled button-down under a disposable gown, hair still damp from a sink wash. He carried a stainless tiffin clipped shut with two rubber bands and smelled faintly of cardamom instead of breakroom coffee. Lena had learned that his wife packed him tea concentrate because he forgot to eat on overnight call and then shook through noon. He set the tiffin on a counter, snapped on gloves, and scanned the ledger without touching it.

"This is not random sloppiness," he said. "Two inks, one pressure profile. Somebody traced over original times."

"Can you certify that?" Lena asked.

"I can certify that this page has overwriting and non-sequential entry logic. I can certify that either software or person manipulated chronology. I cannot certify motive while standing in a refrigerator."

"Good. Say exactly that again when I record your statement."

Rao glanced at her. "You slept?"

"No."

"Then your temper is ahead of you. Keep it on a leash until after chain review."

He opened Drawer B-7.

The rail came out halfway and stopped. The tray was empty. A rectangle of meltwater sat in the center, clean-edged, with one curled fiber stuck to the metal like wet thread. The toe-tag wire was gone. There was no body bag, no wrapped remains, and no trace of the county evidence seal Lena had applied two nights earlier.

Beau's coffee cup hit the floor and rolled under another drawer.

Rochelle made a sound that was not a word. She put both hands on her head, gloves squeaking against scrub cap. "No. No, no, no. I saw him at one-fifty. I checked tags when the panel glitched. I checked because I don't trust this damn building."

Lena made herself breathe through her nose. She had expected tampering. She had not expected absence. The cold bit through her shirt and raised heat on her skin. She heard her own voice go procedural because if she let it go personal now, she would kick something and lose the room.

"Nobody leaves this hallway," she said. "Beau, seal both ends, log everyone present by name and time. Dr. Rao, we need stills of tray surface before contact. Rochelle, I need access logs from door panel and workstation M-2. Now."

"You think we still have M-2 data?" Rochelle said, already moving toward the terminal. "That box crashes when it rains sideways."

"Then print whatever it has before it crashes again."

They worked inside compressor noise and bad light. Lena's fingers numbed through two layers of gloves while she bagged the fiber and swabbed condensation rings. Rao dictated temperature differentials in clipped syllables. Beau radioed dispatch for internal scene status only and shut down anyone asking for details over open channel.

By 06:07 they had hard copies of the card-reader events.

01:48 - Rochelle Pritchard, authorized entry.

02:14 - Admin Override, credential masked.

02:16 - Terminal M-2 offline.

02:33 - Admin Override close.

02:41 - Rochelle Pritchard, authorized entry.

Rochelle stared at the printout. "Masked means what?"

Rao answered before Lena did. "Masked means somebody with high-level privileges suppressed the visible credential string. That setting requires IT or command permissions."

"Command like sheriff command?"

"Command like somebody who knows where to click and who can get away with it," Rao said.

Lena took photos of the screen, the printouts, and the overwritten ledger page, then initialed every page with date and time. The discrepancy was documented, not implied. She had learned from too many dead files that truth without paper was just grief.

Her phone buzzed. Sheriff Clay Reddick.

She put him on speaker because she wanted witnesses.

"Morning, Sheriff," she said.

Clay's voice came warm and polished through static. "I heard you've got a custody concern."

"It is more than a concern," Lena said. "Bell remains are missing from cold storage. We have documented log alterations and an admin-level masked access event between 02:14 and 02:33. I am opening an internal breach file now."

A pause. Paper rustled on his end, as if he were choosing which page to inhabit.

"Contain the language," he said. "Use 'irregularity' until we verify. We are not feeding panic with speculative wording."

"Sheriff, the drawer is empty."

"Then secure the facility and wait for me. Nobody briefs press. Nobody briefs freelancers."

"We still need to notify state lab and medical examiner chain."

"You'll do what I authorize, Detective."

The line clicked dead.

Beau let out a breath through his teeth. "He woke up in campaign voice. That's never a good sign."

Before Lena answered, dispatch cracked through his shoulder radio, too loud for the corridor.

"Units near levee road, respond. Possible DOA at Cormorant River Ramp. Caller reports one fresh male and one... old body, repeat old body, wrapped and staged. Crowd forming."

Nobody in the corridor moved for half a second. Even the compressor seemed to pause.

Rochelle whispered, "Jesus."

Lena was already pulling off one pair of gloves and reaching for another. "Beau, internal seal stays. Rao, you hold this room and don't surrender originals to anybody without my signature. Rochelle, you call your daughter and tell her you'll be late. Then you sit in that office and write exactly what you saw at 01:50 and 02:41. No improvements, no theories."

Rochelle swallowed hard and nodded. "She's playing clarinet," she said, as if reminding herself there was still a world where scales mattered.

"Good," Lena said. "Then write fast."

Outside, the heat hit like an open oven.

By the time they reached the river ramp, half the county had already found the live stream.

The levee road narrowed into a choke point of patrol SUVs, pickup trucks, and civilians on foot holding phones over each other’s shoulders. Cicadas screamed from the willow line. The river carried a skin of fuel sheen and sun glare. Lena stepped out and heard three versions of the same accusation before her boots hit gravel.

"They lost him again."

"They moved him."

"This is your circus, Waller."

Two deputies were arguing at the tape line, one trying to keep bystanders back and the other waving in a city councilman with his camera team. Lena shoved between them.

"Nobody crosses that tape without scene assignment," she said. "I don't care who he votes with. Lock this down to one hundred feet and start a name log on every person already inside."

The younger deputy opened his mouth to protest and then shut it. He had acne scars and trembling hands and a wedding ring still bright from new metal. "Yes, ma'am."

She moved down the ramp.

Lyle Fenn lay on the concrete near the waterline, one arm bent under him like he had tried to get up and failed on the second effort. Somebody had cut his throat deep enough to show the white crescent of cartilage. Flies worked the wound before the blood finished drying. His left cheekbone was crushed flat, probably from a blunt strike before the cut. He wore marine-supply work pants with reflective tape at the cuffs and one steel-toe boot half unlaced. His belt buckle was shaped like a catfish. His right pocket held a receipt from Delta Bait & Fuel for dog food, brake fluid, and cinnamon rolls, timestamped 10:42 p.m.

Three feet away, on a cheap plastic tarp pinned by cinder blocks, Isaiah Bell's remains had been laid out with deliberate care. Whoever moved him had wrapped and unwrapped him like ritual: county sheet, then burial cloth, then county sheet again, each tied with twine knots that looked clean and practiced. The evidence tag hanging from the wrist wire carried Lena's initials in black marker, but the sequence number was wrong by one digit.

Her stomach lurched. The wrongness of the tag was worse than an empty drawer. It meant somebody had copied her handwriting and made a mistake only someone inside process would notice.

Beau crouched by Fenn's boots and looked up. "Wallet says Lyle Aaron Fenn. Driver's license confirms. You remember him from last summer? Barges and scrap permits, always in the tax office hollering."

Lena remembered a man who smelled like diesel and peppermint and wore his sunglasses indoors because he thought it looked like power. She remembered him laughing too loudly outside a hearing where families had lost land he called "unproductive acreage." She had forgotten the dimple in his chin until she saw it split open now.

"Call next of kin through dispatch," she said. "And run all his contacts from last forty-eight hours."

"Already started."

Mara Quinn ducked under the tape before the deputy saw her, phone rig strapped to her chest and a spiral notebook tucked in her back pocket. She wore yellow rubber rain boots on a cloudless morning because, once, a drainage ditch had swallowed one of her shoes on camera and she had refused to repeat the embarrassment. Her close-cropped hair shone with sweat and river mist.

"Lena," Mara said, voice even but fast. "I need attribution on the custody breach before somebody invents one. Was Bell in county storage at midnight, yes or no?"

"This is an active scene," Lena said.

"That wasn't my question."

"No public statement yet."

Mara held her gaze. "Then give me on-background. One sentence. If I can't get a fact from you, I get a lie from somebody else and your office will quote that lie by lunch."

Lena hated that Mara was right. She hated it more that Clay would use that truth against both of them.

"On background," Lena said quietly, "there is documented inconsistency in morgue access records before this call came in. Use that word. Documented."

Mara's pen moved. "Inconsistency from human error or administrative override?"

"One sentence was the deal."

"You're not wrong."

A shout rose from behind the tape as a woman pushed through two deputies, still wearing a red fast-food apron over jeans and house slippers. A grease stain marked the apron pocket, her hands were shaking, and a cigarette was tucked behind one ear like she'd forgotten it was there.

"Where is he?" she yelled. "Where's Lyle?"

The younger deputy grabbed her arm. She swung at him, missed, and nearly fell.

Lena stepped in before the deputy forced her down. "Ma'am, stop. Stop and breathe. What's your name?"

"Tina Fenn. I'm his sister."

She stared at the body and made a noise that scraped Lena's spine. It was not a scream. It sounded like a person trying to swallow a scream and failing. Tina pointed with both hands because one hand would not stay steady.

"That's him. That's Lyle. He can't swim. You hear me? He don't come down here before daylight because he can't swim. He said somebody from your office called him last night, said they had questions about permits. He went because he always thinks he's smarter than trouble."

"Who called him?" Lena asked.

"Unknown number. He put it on speaker for a second while he was looking for his truck keys. Man voice. Said 'deputy's office.' That's all I heard."

Mara had stopped writing and was watching Tina's face. Then she lowered the phone, not recording for a full ten seconds, and offered Tina a bottle of water from her bag without a word. Tina took it with numb fingers and did not drink.

Lena signaled a victim services volunteer and turned back to the bodies. The crowd had swollen again. Someone had started chanting "Whose chain? Your chain." Someone else yelled that cops were staging corpses for votes. People weren't whispering anymore. They were broadcasting.

Clay Reddick arrived with two command SUVs and a pressed white shirt that somehow stayed crisp in ninety-degree air. He walked the tape line greeting cameras by first name, hand on shoulder here, solemn nod there, campaign-ready grief. By the time he reached Lena he had already found the angle where the river sat behind him like a backdrop.

"Detective," he said, voice pitched for nearby microphones, "what do we have?"

"Confirmed homicide victim Lyle Aaron Fenn, male, local. Bell remains are present again with altered tag sequence and rewrapped staging. Morgue records show documented discrepancy and masked admin access between 02:14 and 02:33."

Clay's smile did not move, but his eyes cooled. "You are giving me too much language in an open environment."

"This is operational language."

"This is public theater."

He leaned closer and dropped volume. "You're not briefing anybody else until I say so. That includes your reporter friend in the rain boots."

"She already has one line on background."

"Then that line dies here."

"Facts don't die because you dislike them."

Clay straightened, back to public voice. "We'll handle this inside. Finish scene work."

He moved away before she answered, stepping directly into a camera scrum where he said, "We are addressing irregularities with full professionalism and no rush to judgment," as if he had not just tried to erase the word documented from her mouth.

Rain hit twenty minutes later, sudden and hard, one of those Delta bursts that turned dust to slick clay in three breaths. Deputies wrestled tarps over evidence markers while the crowd retreated to truck cabs and kept filming through cracked windows. Thunder rolled across the levee and made everyone flinch at once.

By noon the preliminary packet had thirty-one pages, three signed witness statements, and a hole in the middle where trust used to be.

The command meeting started at one-thirty in Conference B, where the ceiling stain looked like a map of a country nobody wanted to visit. The air conditioner rattled without cooling. Someone had set out stale cheese cubes and sweating bottled water as if this were a budget hearing.

Clay sat at the head of the table with a legal pad and three color-coded folders. Beau took a chair near the door, shoulders hunched, knee bouncing. Lena sat opposite Clay and laid down two packets: morgue discrepancy report and river ramp scene log. She noticed Clay did not touch either packet.

Captain Darnell from internal compliance joined by speakerphone, voice tinny and over-bright. Public information officer Janice Bellamy sat along the wall, tapping notes into a tablet with one acrylic nail missing from her right thumb.

Clay opened with a long breath and his institutional cadence.

"We are in a destabilized operational environment," he said. "We have community fear, media speculation, and potential civil-rights exposure. Our duty is to restore order while preserving prosecutable integrity. To that end, I am creating a closed task force effective immediately."

He slid one folder toward Lena and one toward Beau.

"Membership is limited to designated personnel only. Detective Waller, Detective Broussard, me, Captain Darnell for oversight, and designated lab liaison through my office. All evidence communication routes through this table."

Lena opened the folder. The first page was TASK FORCE CONFIDENTIALITY ORDER. The second page listed contact restrictions. The third page listed disciplinary penalties.

"This blocks normal interagency channels," she said. "I still need direct line to Rao and state lab if we want clean chain and timely tox."

"You'll submit requests through command," Clay said.

"That adds delay and creates a new choke point inside an already compromised chain."

"What it creates is accountability," Clay said. "No more freelance process."

Beau leaned back and rubbed his forehead. "Sheriff, with respect, we got a masked admin override in the morgue system. If we route everything through command, and command credentials are part of the breach surface, we're building a blindfold and calling it policy."

Clay folded his hands, voice still polite. "Detective Broussard, I appreciate your passion. I do not appreciate your implication."

"I didn't imply," Beau said. "I described."

The room went quiet enough that Lena could hear the hum from Janice's tablet charger.

Clay turned to Lena instead. He always did when he wanted to isolate resistance.

"Let's talk about media contamination," he said. "Ms. Quinn has been receiving privileged texture from this office. That stops now. Effective today, Mara Quinn is removed from all official briefings, hallway updates, and informal access. She gets the same press statements as everyone else, through PIO only."

Janice looked up. "I can draft that language and send by three."

Lena kept her voice level. "Mara is one of the only reporters in this county who corrects errors publicly when we give her documentation. Cutting her off won't stop leaks. It will move leaks to people who don't care about accuracy."

Clay gave her the courtesy smile he used when donors asked naive questions.

"Your role is evidence handling," he said. "Not media strategy."

"Tracking leaks is evidence handling."

"Tracking leaks is command prerogative."

He tapped the confidentiality order.

"Read paragraph four out loud, Detective."

Lena read: "'Any unauthorized disclosure of investigative information to external persons, including members of media, advocacy groups, and non-assigned personnel, constitutes insubordination and may result in immediate suspension pending review.'"

Clay nodded. "Clear enough?"

"Clear," she said.

It was clear in the way a locked door was clear.

Darnell's voice crackled through speaker. "For record completeness, note that the morgue discrepancy packet is received and logged under internal file IC-24-19. I recommend temporary credential freeze for all nonessential staff at morgue and periodic audit snapshots every four hours."

"Approved," Clay said. "Command office will control credential reactivation."

Lena made a note in the margin: *Control where breach occurred.* She did not show the note to anyone.

Meeting ended with signatures. Clay stood first, collecting folders before anyone else rose. He paused behind Lena's chair.

"You are good at scenes," he said softly, private voice now, no cameras. "Be good at structure too. Structure keeps people alive."

She looked up at him. "Structure also buries people when the wrong hands hold it."

His jaw flexed once. "Don't confuse defiance with integrity."

"Don't confuse control with truth."

He left without answering.

In the hallway outside Conference B, Mara was waiting by the vending machines with her notebook tucked under one arm and an empty crackers sleeve sticking out of her pocket. She had changed into dry shoes but kept the rain boots clipped to her backpack with a carabiner. She did not wave.

"How bad?" she asked.

Lena could have lied. Clay had just handed her the script for lying and called it order.

"You're cut out," she said. "Officially. No briefings, no hallway, no side calls. Janice will send you canned statements and smile while she does it."

Mara's face went still in that way Lena had learned to fear. Silence first, then decision.

"Who made the call?" Mara asked.

"Clay. Closed task force."

"Then this isn't about me. It's about containment."

"It's about who controls chain."

Mara looked down the hallway, where deputies moved in and out of offices carrying paper cups and rumors. "I can still work documents," she said. "Court pulls, procurement logs, vendor bids. I don't need their podium."

"You'll get punished for being right before you'll get thanked for being careful."

"That hasn't changed since middle school," Mara said. "Question is whether you can still get me what can be proved. Not what can be said."

Lena rubbed her eyes with thumb and forefinger until sparks flared in the dark behind her lids. "I can't promise a channel."

"I didn't ask for promise. I asked for answer."

"Then here's the answer. The morgue logs were altered. That's in writing. Bell left county storage between two and two-thirty under masked credentials. That's in writing too. If they walk that back on camera, they're lying on top of paper."

Mara nodded once. "Good. Paper bleeds slower than people, but it lasts longer."

She started to turn away, then stopped. "Tina Fenn called me. She's sitting in her car in the Burger Barn lot because she can't stand the smell on her clothes and doesn't want her kids to see her like this. She said Lyle had a habit of leaving voice mails to himself when he was drunk so he wouldn't forget what he promised people. If that's true, his phone might have more than call logs."

Lena felt the day tilt. "Did Tina give you passcode?"

"No. She said he used his daughter's birthday, but she wouldn't say it out loud in a parking lot full of strangers. She said she'd tell you in person if you treat him like a person and not a lesson."

"Where is she now?"

"Still in the lot. Blue Corolla with busted taillight and a Saints sticker that's half peeled off."

Lena looked back toward Conference B, where Clay was probably already drafting tomorrow's narrative, and then toward the exit where heat pressed against the glass like another wall.

"Thank you," she said.

Mara gave a dry half-smile. "Don't thank me. Just don't waste the crack in the door."

She walked away fast, boots clacking from her backpack against her thigh.

Lena went to her desk, shut the door, and spread out copies of the morgue report, the ramp log, and the confidentiality order. The fluorescent light above her flickered twice and settled. She wrote three columns on a legal pad: *Who knew at 06:00. Who knew at 06:30. Who spoke by 07:00.* She filled names and times until the ink smeared under her hand sweat.

By three o'clock, Janice's office sent a countywide press email: *A specialized task force has been assembled to streamline communication and protect case integrity. All inquiries should be directed through official channels.*

The email had no mention of missing remains.

The email had no mention of altered logs.

The email had no mention of Lyle Fenn except "adult male decedent pending family notification," as if Tina had not already screamed his name at the river in front of two hundred phones.

Lena printed the email and stapled it to the discrepancy packet. Then she opened her bottom drawer, moved the spare holster and tire gauge, and slid one copy of the morgue access printout under the false panel she kept for cash and emergency meds. If command wanted every route narrowed, she would make one route that did not depend on command.

Outside her office, thunder rolled again. The building lights dipped and recovered. Somewhere down the hall, somebody laughed too loudly at nothing.

Lena closed her notebook on the three-column leak map and felt, for the first time since dawn, the shape of the real case settle into focus.

Two men were dead.

One body had been moved twice.

The killer mattered.

But the hand on the evidence chain mattered just as much, maybe more, because that hand could erase any killer it wanted.

She stood, knee barking, and went to find Tina Fenn before someone else got to her first.
