# Chapter 9

By eight-thirty the courthouse air had already gone wet and sour, like a shirt left too long in a gym bag. Lena stood at the metal table in the records annex with Ava Boyd's marked-up probate printouts spread in a fan under fluorescent hum. The lights flickered every few seconds, enough to make black type blur at the edges, enough to make her think of a pulse skipping.

Rule 41 demanded three things in front of a judge: probable cause, a sworn connection between crime and place, and a description tight enough that nobody could call it a fishing expedition. The trust code demanded something else entirely. Trustees got to hide behind confidentiality unless somebody proved breach with specifics first. The law was a lock that asked for evidence before it would let her reach the evidence.

She had written the affidavit twice overnight. The first draft was anger. The second was dates, signatures, body-transfer windows, and the dead names Ava had circled in red before somebody put a knife in her throat. Lena kept reading one paragraph where she tied the trust's "security consulting" payouts to transport nights from the morgue corridor. The words were spare and careful. They still felt like begging.

Beau came in carrying two coffees in paper cups, one hand balancing the narrow case file they were gambling the month on. His tie sat loose, and his shirt stuck dark at the spine. He set a cup by Lena's elbow.

"Tastes like radiator water," he said. "Best this place has."

She did not touch it. "You talked to the clerk?"

"We are at ten-fifteen in chambers. Pike moved us up from afternoon."

"Why?"

He shrugged. "Why does anybody move anything in this county? Somebody richer than us got restless."

Lena slid him the draft warrant package. "I kept full scope. Trust disbursements, beneficiary identities, side-letter agreements, plus crematory manifests and county transport keys."

He read in silence, mouth working the inside of his cheek. "This asks for six years."

"That's when Gideon litigation restarted."

"And this asks for trustee emails and personal devices."

"Ava is dead. Two men were killed before her. Isaiah keeps showing up in places he can't walk into. I'm done pretending this is a tidy paperwork lapse."

He let out one dry laugh that had no humor in it. "You're not wrong. You're early."

"Early got Ava killed?"

"No. Sloppy gets the whole thing tossed."

She looked at him then, really looked. He had shaved too fast and left a pale nick under his jaw, one drop of dried blood beside his collar. He always talked practical when he was scared, like the right sequence could keep chaos on a leash.

"What do you want to cut," she asked, "so nobody important gets embarrassed?"

"Don't do that." He kept his voice low because two records clerks were sorting deed books on the far wall. "I want date-banded disbursements and manifests we can defend. We prove one shell company first. Then we come back bigger."

"By then they'll have burned the rest."

"Maybe. But if Pike calls this overbroad, we get nothing and we wear it."

Lena folded the corner of the affidavit until it creased white, then smoothed it flat. Her knee scar burned from too many stairs and too little sleep. She said, "I'm not asking for permission to be liked. I'm asking for enough paper to stop another body."

He looked at her for a long beat. "Then ask it in a way that survives appeal."

They walked upstairs without speaking. The courthouse smelled like old paper and bleach with coffee rotting underneath. Somebody had mopped too early and left gray streaks in the tile. In the hall outside chambers, a donor plaque wall gleamed with brass names Lena had seen on campaign signs and foundation galas. The same names from Ava's files. The same names on land parcels that used to hold Black families and now held hunting leases and tax shelters.

Judge Eleanor Pike's bailiff opened the door at ten-twelve and motioned them inside. He was a narrow man with a tremor in his left hand and a tiny stitched hummingbird on his cuff where a cufflink had torn out. He shut the door softly behind them, as if quiet could make anything less ugly.

Pike sat at the end of her conference table in a cream suit despite the heat, posture straight enough to seem painful. A porcelain mug sat by her legal pad, untouched tea gone cold. She kept pressing two fingers to the skin above her right eyebrow, then dropping her hand like she had caught herself doing it. Her migraines were public gossip in the courthouse; today the overhead lights were off and one shaded lamp did all the work.

County Attorney Neal Graves stood to the side with a binder and a damp handkerchief. He nodded to Lena and Beau without warmth.

"Detective Waller," Pike said. "Detective Broussard. Please sit."

Lena sat. Beau stayed half forward in his chair, as if he planned to leave in the middle.

Pike lifted the affidavit packet. "You are requesting trust records, private account identifiers, communications between trustees and counsel, county transport rosters, and digital devices connected to named entities."

"Yes, Your Honor," Lena said. "The victims share a documented trust-litigation history. Payment cycles overlap with custody breaches and movement windows. We need complete records to identify who financed access."

Pike's voice stayed even. "The court is not a net. Particularity is not optional because a case is politically charged."

Lena felt heat climb up her throat. She heard Ava, not in words but in pressure: do not let them define clean as harmless.

"This is particular," Lena said. "It names one trust family, one transport corridor, and dates tied to homicides."

Graves cleared his throat. "Your Honor, the request for counsel communications is facially privileged. The request for all beneficiary identity data is disconnected from probable cause as currently sworn."

Lena turned to him. "One beneficiary class controlled payment authorization during each breach window. We can't test that without identities."

Pike touched her temple again, then picked up a fountain pen. "Cannot is not the standard. The standard is whether you have shown probable cause for each category sought. You have shown enough for a partial grant."

Beau spoke before Lena could. "Judge, if the court is willing, we can accept a narrowed date range and outgoing disbursement ledgers above five thousand. We can tie from there."

Lena cut her eyes toward him. He did not look back.

Pike wrote for a full minute. The scratch of nib on paper sounded louder than it should have.

"Order," she said. "The court authorizes seizure of outgoing disbursement ledgers, vendor payment schedules, and transfer manifests from Mercer Morgue and county crematory operations for the period beginning eighteen months before the first homicide and ending today."

She lifted her head.

"The court denies, without prejudice, seizure of beneficiary identity files, donor rosters, privileged communications, personal devices, and trust internal strategy memoranda. If subsequent evidence establishes a tighter nexus, you may renew."

Lena kept her hands on the table so nobody saw them shake. "Your Honor, if entities are layered, payment ledgers without ownership records can be decorative."

"Then bring me ownership nexus from what I have granted," Pike said. "This order stands."

The bailiff stepped forward for signatures. Pike signed first in careful strokes, then held the page out. A faint smell of peppermint oil came off her wrist when Lena reached for the pen.

Outside chambers, the hallway felt hotter than the stairwell to hell.

Lena did not wait for the door to close before she said, "You undercut me in there."

Beau kept walking. "I kept us from leaving empty-handed."

"You gave her the cut list."

"I gave her something she would sign."

She caught his sleeve and stopped him by the donor plaque wall. The brass names shone over his shoulder.

"You think if we behave, they'll let us solve this," she said. "That's what you think."

His jaw tightened. "I think this is the only way that lands in court instead of talk radio."

"Talk radio isn't moving bodies. People with keys are."

"Exactly. And if you burn the warrant on pride, those people walk."

"This isn't pride."

"No," he said, and now his voice rose. "It's you deciding that losing admissibility is an acceptable casualty as long as you get to say you swung hard."

Two clerks looked over from the copier alcove. Lena dropped her hand from his sleeve.

"You want to take one shell at a time," she said. "I want the architecture."

"You want the architecture now," he said. "The law does not move at your speed."

"Bodies do."

He stared at her, tired and angry and suddenly older than forty-six. "Then we run the paper we got and stop bleeding each other in the hallway."

She let him go. Her pulse kept banging in her neck long after he disappeared down the stairs.

By one-thirty, rain had started in hard slanted sheets that rattled the courthouse windows and turned the parking lot into one broad puddle. The warrant team split by design and by resentment. Beau took two deputies to Mercer Trust Administration on Main. Lena took the second car to the service records annex behind the crematory, where manifests were supposed to be boxed by quarter and forgotten.

The annex door stuck in swollen wood. Inside, cold air fought humidity and lost. Cardboard boxes lined three walls, each marked by month with black marker. A woman in a denim skirt and bright yellow reading glasses waited at the desk, one ankle bouncing under her chair.

"I'm Mildred Cline," she said before Lena introduced herself. "Records compliance. I know what your paper says. I also have to leave at four for my husband's dialysis, so I need this to move."

"We'll move," Lena said, showing badge and warrant copy.

Mildred glanced at the badge, then at Lena's face. "You look like you haven't slept in a week. That's not a judgment. That's just eyes."

She stood and led Lena to stacked transfer binders. While she walked she rubbed the edge of a nicotine patch under her blouse sleeve, a small practiced motion she probably did without noticing.

"Chain sheets are in those blue folders," Mildred said. "Vehicle assignment tags are separate because accounting asked for easier billing. Whoever thought that up has never looked for anything in weather like this."

Lena pulled on gloves and began with dates around the Voss and Fenn murders. The manifests were a mess of carbon copies and corrections, but the law of paper held: people lied in stories and told on themselves in billing fields. She found two after-hours transport runs signed by initials only, then a third with the whole surname squeezed into the bottom margin: H. Reddick.

Her phone buzzed with a text from Beau.

`Trust office dragging. Counsel claims software outage.`

Then a second text.

`Get what you can. Keep it clean.`

She ignored the second one and kept turning pages. Vehicle 12 showed out-of-route mileage on the night between first-scene evidence lock and second-scene body appearance. The authorization line was half-legible. The invoice cross-reference pointed to an outside vendor: Delta Meridian Security Services, invoice batch DMS-44.

Lena took photos, then printed certified copies with Mildred watching each page like it might catch fire.

"Can I ask a thing?" Mildred said while the copier chugged. "My husband says I should stop asking questions at work. But I keep asking anyway."

"Ask."

"If this is about that old man they keep finding, why does money keep showing up in every room before y'all do?"

Lena met her eyes. "That's exactly what we're trying to answer."

Mildred nodded once, as if that answer had cost Lena something.

When Lena reached Mercer Trust Administration, thunder had moved east but the air stayed electric. Beau was in a conference room under recessed lights, tie off now, shirt sleeves rolled. He sat across from two trust employees and outside counsel, files stacked in exact squares. Through the glass Lena could see tension in the line of his shoulders. He was playing patient because that was his move.

Mara stood in the lobby pretending to read a tourism brochure, phone face-down on her knee. She wore a rain-dark jacket and had pen marks up the side of her hand.

"You're not supposed to be in here," Lena said quietly.

Mara did not look up. "I'm in a public lobby waiting for a quote. Also your face says something broke."

"Not now."

"Then give me one noun."

Lena hesitated, then said, "Disbursements."

Mara's eyes flicked up, sharp and quick. "You think they're paying movement crews."

"I think the payment calendar and body calendar kiss too often."

"That's a sentence I can use when you're ready to put your name on it."

Lena walked past before she could answer.

Inside the conference room, outside counsel introduced himself as Grant Moberly and complained about confidentiality with theatrical sorrow. He had a silk pocket square and mud on his loafers from the parking lot, which made Lena dislike him on principle.

"Detective Waller," he said, "we have complied fully within the four corners of your warrant."

"Then hand it over," she said.

Beau gave her a warning look, slight but clear. He was trying to keep temperature low.

The trust comptroller, a thin woman named Deirdre Shaw with ink-smudged fingers and a rosary bracelet she kept rotating around her wrist, slid three binders over. "Outgoing vendor ledger, scheduled disbursement calendar, and high-value transfer authorizations. Eighteen months. Exactly what the order says."

Lena sat and opened the first binder. Rows of payments marched down the page under bland names that sounded like office parks and weather systems: Cypress Strategic Holdings, Delta Meridian Security, Bayline Transport Solutions, Starlit Custodial Partners. Every one of them billed in amounts that sat just under additional reporting flags: $9,800, $9,750, $9,900.

"Who approves these?" Lena asked.

Deirdre looked toward Moberly before answering. "Dual sign-off. Foundation operations and external security liaison."

"Name."

Moberly stepped in. "Counsel's position is that personnel identities outside the warrant scope remain confidential."

Lena flipped to the disbursement calendar and felt her heartbeat change. Scheduled ACH batches landed on the first and fifteenth of each month, with special releases three days before board events and litigation deadlines. A note field on repeated entries read `site stabilization retainer - HR`. The next scheduled release sat five days away.

"HR," Lena said. "That stands for what?"

Deirdre's mouth opened, then shut.

Moberly answered for her. "Internal coding."

"Internal to who?"

"Internal to trust operations."

Beau leaned forward, voice flat. "Mr. Moberly, obstruction is not confidentiality. The warrant covers outgoing disbursement ledgers and payment schedules. Coding legend is part of that record."

Moberly folded his hands. "The legend contains protected references."

"Protected from whom?" Lena said. "The dead?"

Beau shot her a look that said do not blow this up. She bit down on the next sentence until she tasted copper.

The door opened before anybody could respond. Deputy Hollis Reddick walked in without knocking, rainwater beading on his forearms, patrol polo stretched across gym-cut shoulders. A faded military tattoo ran from his wrist toward his elbow, something like crossed anchors and a date hidden in shading. He carried a half-empty bag of spicy peanuts and smiled like this was his break room.

"Y'all still doing taxes in here?" he said. "Main desk said I could grab signatures for transport keys."

Nobody spoke for a second. Hollis looked at Lena and tipped his chin.

"Detective Waller," he said. "Long day?"

"For some of us," she said.

He popped a peanut in his mouth and leaned over Deirdre's chair. "Need that release form on Bayline. If it misses four o'clock, dispatch throws a fit and I get yelled at by people with softer hands than mine."

Lena watched Deirdre slide a single-page authorization toward him. The header carried the same trust logo as her binder. The signatory line read `External Security Liaison: H. Reddick.`

He signed with a fast slash, then whistled under his breath while he looked at Lena again.

"You all right?" he asked. "You look like somebody sold your weekend."

"Get out," Beau said, not loud.

Hollis laughed once. "Relax. I'm leaving."

He tapped the folder with his knuckle, collected the signed page, and walked out still smiling.

The room stayed quiet after the door clicked shut. Rain ticked at the windows. Somewhere in the hall an old copier started and stopped, started and stopped.

Lena turned the authorization form toward Moberly. "Internal coding. Sure."

Beau did not look at her. "We need certified copies of every authorization carrying this liaison field, plus the full coding legend and batch transmission logs."

Moberly lifted his chin. "Your warrant does not include transmission logs."

"Then we'll note your refusal," Beau said.

"You can note whatever you like."

Lena spoke before Beau could smooth it over. "No, we'll note exactly what you're protecting and exactly when you protected it."

Deirdre flinched. Her rosary bracelet clicked against the table.

"I can print the legend," she said quietly. "If that's in scope."

Moberly turned to her. "Deirdre."

"It's in the disbursement binder appendix," she said, eyes down. "Appendix C."

Beau held Moberly's stare until the attorney looked away.

"Print it," Beau said.

Appendix C was three pages of abbreviations and euphemisms. `HR` expanded to `Hollis Reddick / External Security Liaison.` `SSR` meant `site stabilization retainer.` `CRT` meant `courier routing transfer.` There was nothing subtle about it once the key existed.

Lena laid Appendix C beside the crematory manifests from Mildred's annex. The dates aligned like teeth in a zipper. Retainer release on the first. After-hours transfer on the third. Body appeared on the fourth. Again in the next cycle. Again before Ava.

She felt nausea rise, not from surprise but from the hard relief of finally touching something solid.

Beau read the two stacks in silence. His finger stopped on Hollis's initials, then moved to the calendar entry for next week.

"Five days," he said.

"Until the next release," Lena answered.

"Could be routine security work."

"Could be payroll for evidence movement and scene staging."

"Could be both," he said.

There it was again, the split dressed as caution.

Lena gathered the copied pages into evidence sleeves. "I'm filing for an emergency expansion and a freeze by six."

"Not yet."

She looked up. "Excuse me?"

"Not yet," Beau repeated. "We have linkage, not full ownership tree. We file now and Pike denies again, we show our hand and lose the next swing."

"The next disbursement hits in five days."

"Then we verify tonight and file tomorrow with corporate registry support."

"Tomorrow gives them a full night to scrub accounts."

He rubbed both hands over his face and lowered them slowly. "Lena, I know what this looks like. I know who his uncle is. I know exactly how bad this is. But if you accuse the sheriff's nephew on partial paper and a coding appendix, they bury you and call it reckless."

"I'm already buried," she said. "I'm digging with my fingernails."

"Then don't snap them off on the first hard rock."

She almost laughed. It came out as air. "You want me to wait because waiting feels professional."

"I want you to hit with enough force to break something that can't be glued back together by six men in suits."

Outside, Mara stood up in the lobby when they came through the conference door. Her eyes tracked the evidence sleeves in Lena's hands.

"You got a name," Mara said.

Lena said nothing.

Beau answered before she could. "No comment."

Mara looked from him to Lena and back. "You two aren't on the same page."

"We're on the same case," Beau said.

"That's not what I said."

Lena stopped at the lobby window where rainwater ran in crooked lines over the glass. In the parking lot, Hollis leaned against his cruiser under the awning, laughing with a patrol sergeant, peanut bag rolled in one fist. He looked loose, even happy. He looked like a man who had never had to imagine his own name on a warrant return.

She could still feel the paper in her hands, the dry rasp of copied toner, the little coded initials that had turned into a person with a badge and family protection.

Mara stepped beside her. "If this is what I think, you can't sit on it long."

Beau came up on Lena's other side. "If this is what you think, a leak kills admissibility before we get the expansion."

Mara did not blink. "A body every few weeks kills people faster than admissibility memos."

"And a blown case kills the only shot at conviction," Beau said.

Lena stood between them, rain noise filling the gap where trust used to fit. Her knee ached. Her jaw hurt from clenching. She thought of Ava's red-circled names and of Pike's pen scratching partial permission onto a page like mercy.

"We do this my way for twelve hours," she said finally, eyes on Hollis through the wet glass. "No story tonight. No leak. I run emergency expansion with what we have and what we can certify before dark."

Mara folded her arms. "Twelve. Then I decide what I owe the public if this stalls."

"Fair," Lena said.

Beau exhaled through his nose, not relief, not agreement, only truce. "I'll work Secretary of State filings and UCC shells. You draft. We regroup at eight."

Lena nodded.

In the parking lot, Hollis flicked his peanut bag into a trash can and climbed into his cruiser. The engine turned over. He backed out slow, one hand on the wheel, the other tapping time on the door as if he had nowhere urgent to be.

Lena watched him pull into traffic. Then she turned toward the elevator with the evidence sleeves pressed hard against her ribs, as if paper could hold shape against weather, against money, against men who called murder routine overhead.
