# Chapter 7

By nine-thirty the courthouse square had gone hollow except for the soda machine outside tax assessors and the cicadas sawing in the oak trees hard enough to feel in her teeth. Lena sat in her car with the air off, because she wanted to hear if anybody crossed the lot, and the inside of the windshield held a cloudy print of her own breath. Early fall in Mercer County only meant the heat stopped punching by daylight and started leaning at night. Her shirt clung between her shoulder blades. The old knee scar under the right kneecap ached every time she moved from brake to gas, though she was parked.

She watched the chancery annex lights through a slit between two sycamores. Half the windows were dark. One row on the second floor still burned the thin blue white that made every room look like a hospital waiting area. She told herself those lights meant a janitor, maybe a clerk finishing payroll, maybe nobody. She told herself five things like that and believed none of them.

Her phone buzzed once and lit Mara's name.

`Meet me in the back alley in two minutes. Don't park by the camera.`

They had already burned seven minutes on the phone arguing source terms and risk; this text was just coordinates.

Lena cut the screen and rested the phone on the passenger seat beside her father's notebook. The cover had gone soft from humidity and from her own hands. The penciled code she'd broken at the dinner table last night sat on a torn yellow sticky: `C-11 / Box 62 / CH-Probate / Cor Packet.` Her father had written it twenty years before she became a cop. Maybe he had written it on a lunch break. Maybe after he came home and told Ida he had worked late and had never said late doing what.

When Mara tapped on the glass, Lena flinched so hard her knee hit the steering column.

Mara slid in and shut the door without a hello. Her close-cropped hair was damp at the temples, and she kept her backpack in her lap with her notebook tucked under one arm and a phone rig clipped to her belt like a holstered tool. She smelled like rain and printer toner.

"Before we move," Mara said, "we settle source terms. Right now."

Lena kept her eyes on the annex. "You got us in?"

"That's not an answer."

"It's the first problem in front of us."

"No. The first problem is trust. If we find your father in those files, nothing disappears. If it touches Bell land, Bell counsel gets it tonight."

Lena turned toward her. "You think I'd drag you into a felony and then pocket evidence?"

"I think blood scrambles people. I think two in the morning scrambles people worse."

The words stung because they were true enough to land. Lena swallowed and felt the dry scrape in her throat. "You post raw scans tonight, Clay says we forged everything, calls me a suspended cop breaking into county records to settle a family grudge. He'll get a judge before sunup."

"I didn't say post. I said share. There's a difference and you know it."

Mara pulled a folded legal pad sheet from her bag and held it in the dark between them. The page held names and numbers in block print.

"Fallback," Mara said. "If we're stopped inside, we call Deirdre Stokes first, then Bell family counsel in Greenville. If your old command shows before counsel does, I start recording and state we're documenting imminent records transfer that affects active homicide inquiry and civil heirs. If one of us gets detained, the other leaves with copies. No hero shit."

Lena took the paper and read it twice. Deirdre Stokes she knew by reputation: civil-rights attorney, mean cross, no patience for sheriff-office theater. The list made this feel less like improvisation and more like an autopsy report waiting for a body.

"You already typed the statement?"

"Three versions. One for sheriff deputies, one for county security, one for press if it goes bad."

Lena gave a short laugh without humor. "You always this cheerful before a misdemeanor?"

"Burglary and tampering can stack to a felony if Clay wants to perform." Mara met her eyes. "You still in?"

The honest answer sat ugly in Lena's chest: she wanted to go home, lock the door, and pretend she had not decoded those notes, not opened this lane where her father's name could become evidence bag material. She pictured Jalen's face at dinner when he had asked if police lied. She had said yes. She had watched him absorb it like a burn.

"I'm in," she said. "You get Bell counsel everything we touch that ties to tract ownership. You hold publication till we verify provenance. If you think I'm stalling, you don't ask permission. You go."

Mara folded the legal pad sheet and tucked it back into her bag. "Done."

"And I need one thing from you," Lena said. "Who gave you access?"

Mara's jaw tightened. "Not happening."

"If this is a setup, I need to know who can bury us with one statement."

"If I burn a source, I don't have a job, and no one ever talks to me again. You want that, find another door."

The old argument was stripped to bone. It was cop logic against reporter survival. Lena let it sit. She hated it and she needed it.

"Fine," she said. "You keep the name. You answer one question: are they doing this for money, fear, or conscience?"

Mara looked toward the annex and took longer than she liked. "Mostly fear," she said. "A little rent." 

That answer was enough to make Lena's stomach drop another inch.

They moved the car two blocks, cut through the alley behind chancery, and crossed under a security light that buzzed like an insect trapped in glass. Mara produced a clipped badge sleeve with a county logo and a magnetic stripe inside.

"Temporary records contractor," she said when Lena stared.

"You forged ID now?"

"No. I borrowed a life." 

"Jesus." 

"Save your theology. Swipe this, hold that door three seconds, then we're in."

Lena hated how cleanly Mara had rehearsed the timing. She hated more that she followed it.

The reader at the back entrance blinked red, then green. The latch clacked loose. They slipped into a stairwell that smelled of mopped bleach and damp concrete. Lena counted the steps without meaning to: fourteen to the first landing, seventeen to the second, twelve to the corridor. Her pulse stayed in her throat the whole climb.

Second floor chancery records looked less like a vault than a tired school library. The room held metal shelving and banker boxes and cartons with county seals split and re-taped so many times the paper looked furred. Fluorescent bulbs flickered at irregular beats, giving every aisle a stutter. A dehumidifier droned from the far wall, its collection tank burping every few minutes.

Mara checked her watch. "Source said sensors in this wing cycle at ten-fifty and eleven-thirty when humidity system resets. We have twenty-five, maybe thirty minutes before the next clean sweep logs motion."

"You keep saying source like it's a weather front," Lena muttered.

"You keep asking like you're still on payroll."

They split the aisle by shelf marker: C-11 through C-14. Lena carried the notebook and an LED penlight; Mara handled phone camera and a portable scanner that folded out like a silver insect. Neither of them spoke for the first few minutes except box numbers.

"Fifty-eight."

"Not it."

"Sixty-one. Tax sales."

"Wrong decade."

When Lena pulled Box 62 and lifted the lid, her hands shook enough to rattle paper clips. Inside sat hanging folders labeled in faded black marker: `Bell, Isaiah`; `Bell Heirs - Misc`; `Coroner Addenda`; `Probate Adj.` A fourth slot stood empty where a folder had been torn out hard enough to leave half the tab behind.

"Missing one," she said.

Mara was beside her in two steps. "Which one?"

Lena turned the broken tab toward the light. `Tran...` and then ripped pulp. It might have been Transfer, or Transcript, or Transaction ledger, and now it was gone.

"Shit," Mara whispered.

Lena opened `Coroner Addenda` first and got a thin stack of mimeographed forms held with a rusted brad. She expected one page about Isaiah Bell. She got seven packets across three years.

The first, dated August 22, 1963, named Isaiah Bell, male, nineteen, cause of death initially typed as "suspected drowning." On the supplemental page an earlier line had been crossed through hard enough to emboss the paper below: `ligature furrow present at neck, posterior contusions, right hyoid fracture.` Someone had retyped beneath it in cleaner ink: `postmortem artifact possible due to transport and decomposition.`

Lena swallowed bile. She had read enough modern autopsies to know what a broken hyoid usually meant. She heard Rao's voice in her head, dry and precise: neck compression, manual or ligature, probable homicide until ruled otherwise.

The second packet carried the name Ellis Bell, age forty-one, six weeks after Isaiah. Declared combine accident. Photo stapled to the back showed a hand laid on a morgue slab, palm up, fingers packed with Delta mud and blood under every nail, and bruises around the wrist in a clean loop like somebody had grabbed hard and held.

The third packet was a woman Lena had never heard: Cora Jean Bell, thirty-three, listed as smoke inhalation in a kitchen fire. The attached scene note described no soot in the trachea.

She looked up at Mara, who had gone very still. Mara did not fill silence when she was thinking. Her thumb hovered over the scanner trigger.

"This is not random," Mara said finally. "It's succession cleaning."

Lena kept turning pages. Each death date sat within weeks of a filing in `Bell Heirs - Misc.` Each filing moved a fraction of Gideon Tract into temporary administration because heirs were dead, missing, or legally unlocatable.

"They killed people in order," Lena said. "Not for rage. For paper."

"For paper that turns into dirt that turns into money," Mara said.

The heir file was worse. Thick packet, chancery heading, `In re: Gideon Agricultural Tract Parcel Group 7 and Associated Holdings.` Entire columns were blacked out with old marker that had bled through four sheets. A later clerk had copied those pages for preservation, so each redaction sat doubled, ghosted, impossible to reverse with light. But not every line was gone.

Names survived in margins where the marker missed: fragments like `-aiah Bell Jr.` and `M. Bell (minor)`. Beside them, stamped entries: `Service attempted / returned unserved.` `Address unknown.` `No issue surviving.`

At the bottom of three pages, next to a line for service verification, the same signature appeared in tight slanted script.

`Deputy Waller`

No first name. No badge number. Just the title and the surname she had spent thirty-eight years carrying.

Lena stared until the letters blurred. Her father had taught her to print before cursive. He had sat at the kitchen table on a Sunday with a yellow No. 2 pencil and corrected her grip with two fingers, gentle, patient, almost shy. He had signed her permission slips with those same narrow loops. She knew the tilt of his W the way she knew the shape of her son's ear.

Mara touched her arm once, light. "Lena. We scan now."

Lena jerked back into motion and wiped the sweat off her palms onto her jeans. "Scan every page. Front and back. Include folder tabs and box labels for provenance."

"Already doing it."

Mara worked fast and methodical, setting pages on the scanner glass, photographing each stack in place, then reassembling in original order. Lena photographed shelf marker C-11, aisle placard, humidity monitor timestamp, and the transfer cart parked at the aisle end with tomorrow's date handwritten on masking tape: `09/19 - Jackson Hold.`

"They're moving this by morning," Mara said, following her lens. "Your source was right."

"Your source," Lena said.

"Yeah."

She pulled the next folder and found a transfer manifest with line items half-whited out. Box 62 listed as `partial contents.` Box 63 marked `removed per judicial directive.` Signature on that page came from a deputy clerk Lena did not know, but the directive number referenced Judge Pike's chamber docket.

"Missing folders weren't lost," Mara said. "They were curated."

"Say that on record and they'll call it editorializing."

"Then I'll quote the damn manifest." 

Their voices had risen. Lena forced hers down. "No quotes yet. If this drops before we pin chain continuity, Clay says contamination and gets an injunction."

Mara capped the scanner and looked straight at her. "If this sits in your trunk while you decide whether your father deserves soft handling, Bell families get robbed again."

The sentence hit like a slap because it carried the thing Lena feared other people already thought. She stepped closer until their shoes almost touched.

"Don't confuse grief with corruption," Lena said, low. "I'm not asking for soft handling. I'm asking for one clean line from vault to counsel so nobody can disappear this as gossip."

Mara held the stare another beat, jaw working, then let her shoulders drop. "Fine. We move now. We copy in duplicate. One goes to counsel before midnight. One stays offline with you."

"And if either of us gets grabbed?"

"Drive A goes to Deirdre. Drive B auto-uploads if I don't clear a timer." Mara tapped her phone. "I already armed it."

Lena blew out a breath she had been holding too long. "You're exhausting."

"You're slow when it hurts."

Both things were true. They packed pages into chronological order, replaced every folder exactly, and slid Box 62 back into the shelf with gloves on. The missing tab still stared like a pulled tooth.

They crossed to a side office where an old high-volume copier sat under plastic sheeting. Mara fed pages while Lena watched the corridor and counted seconds between the dehumidifier burps. Every noise sounded bigger at night: rollers turning, paper slapping output tray, the metal clink when someone in another wing dropped what sounded like a mop bucket.

"How many?" Lena whispered.

"Eighty-three scanned. Thirty-six hard copies so far."

"Don't jam on me now," Lena muttered at the machine.

"You praying at a Xerox?"

"I don't trust anything with this many moving parts."

At copy forty-two, footsteps sounded in the hallway. Not hurried. Not sneaking. The flat tired pace of someone who had worked this building too long to rush for any emergency smaller than fire.

Lena killed the copier switch, but it was too late. A man turned into the doorway and stopped with his hand still on a ring of keys.

He wore county maintenance navy, sleeves rolled to the elbow, and bright orange reading glasses on a beaded cord that bounced against his chest when he breathed. Gray in his beard. Sneakers with one lace replaced by twine. He stared at them, then at the stack of old chancery pages in the tray.

"Well," he said. "You picked the loudest machine in the county." 

Mara moved half a step in front of her bag. Lena put her hands where he could see them.

"Calvin," Lena said, recognizing him from daytime records windows. "It's late for everybody."

"Late does not cover this." He squinted at her face and then let out a short sound. "Detective Waller. Suspended Detective Waller, if county Facebook is telling the truth for once." 

He set his keys on the desk and rubbed the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger like a migraine had moved in behind his eyes. On his left wrist a hospital-style silicone band read `LILY STRONG` in fading glitter letters.

"My kid made me wear that since chemo started," he said, noticing Lena looking. "So if you're about to lie, save me the effort. I've got no patience tonight." 

The room tightened around Lena's ribs. She knew the rules in one clean flash: he could call security, call Clay, or call nobody and still hold leverage for years.

Mara spoke first, voice clipped and clear. "Mr. Pruitt, C-11 gets transferred by morning. Those files tie to an active homicide and heir suppression. We're making preservation copies before they walk."

"And you're doing that without a warrant, after hours, with a suspended officer and a reporter carrying a scanner." Calvin gave a humorless smile. "I can still do arithmetic." 

Lena stepped in. "You can do arithmetic. You can do history too. You work these shelves. You know which names vanish on purpose." 

Calvin looked at her for a long time. His right hand drummed a rhythm on the desk edge: three taps, pause, three taps again.

"I know this building eats paper when powerful people are hungry," he said. "I know my supervisor told me to clear aisle C by six a.m. because a truck from Jackson wants sealed boxes before doors open. I also know I've got a seventeen-year-old who has to be at oncology in Greenville at eight-thirty and insurance that acts blind every month." He glanced at the pages again. "So tell me exactly what you want from me."

No one moved. The fluorescent light buzzed and popped.

"Ten minutes," Mara said. "No calls till we're gone."

Calvin laughed once. "Ten minutes costs more than y'all think." 

Mara opened a zipper pocket and took out a cash envelope folded into quarters. She did not perform reluctance. She counted six hundred-dollar bills onto the desk and held his gaze while she did it.

Calvin did not touch the money yet. "If I take that, I'm dirty with you." 

"You're already in it with us," Mara said.

"That's not the same."

Lena spoke carefully. "No, it isn't. This is chain-of-custody triage. If those boxes leave clean before sunup, Bell heirs never see the full record. You know that." 

Calvin rubbed the silicone bracelet again. His fingers shook just a little. "You two think this county can be shamed into honesty with paper?"

"Not by itself," Mara said. "Paper won't do it alone. Paper and witnesses. Tonight that's you." 

He stared at the money another beat, then swept it into his lunch bag with the same motion he might have used on crumbs. He looked ashamed and angry and practical all at once.

"Nine minutes," he said. "Not ten. And hear me: if my name lands in your story, I say you forced me. I say you threatened my kid. I say whatever keeps me employed."

"Understood," Mara said.

"I mean it. I like being alive and insured."

"We heard you," Lena said.

Calvin picked up his keys and pointed one toward the hallway. "Freight elevator is down tonight, so transfer crew uses east stair. That means they hit C-aisle first. Those boxes move by seven-fifteen. If you need anything else, take it now or bury this whole night." He hesitated. "And Detective?"

"Yeah."

"Your daddy used to smoke clove cigarettes behind this wing where the camera can't see. He said they kept mosquitos off. They smelled like Christmas and bad choices." Calvin shook his head once. "People can be kind at supper and cruel on paper before breakfast. Do what you came for."

He left without looking back.

For two seconds Lena could not move. The clove detail hit harder than the signature had, maybe because it came from somebody else's memory, not hers. Her father did smell like cloves some nights when he kissed Ida's cheek and told Lena to keep studying. She had never asked where they came from.

Mara touched her elbow. "Lena. Clock."

They moved fast. They copied, collated, clipped, and bagged. Mara encrypted the scans on two drives with labels made from ripped painter's tape: `A` and `B`. Lena photographed each packet with a timestamp app, then shot the copier counter and the room clock in the same frame. The word provenance hammered through her head again and again. Her temples throbbed with it.

When they stepped into the corridor, the annex had gone quieter, as if the building itself held breath. Their shoes squeaked on waxed tile. Somewhere outside, thunder rolled low and long over the levee.

At the stairwell door Mara stopped.

"I'm sending Drive A now," she said. "Deirdre and Bell counsel. Not publication. Transfer-only link, expiration six hours."

Lena nodded. "Do it."

Mara thumbed her phone, waited for the progress bar, then held the screen out so Lena could see the recipient list. There were no games and no omissions.

"Sent," Mara said.

They descended one flight when a security alarm chirped once in the distance, then fell silent. Lena froze on the landing, heart climbing into her throat again.

"That us?" she whispered.

Mara listened, then checked her phone when it vibrated against the rail. Her face tightened.

"It's us. Badge 4477 just tripped an unauthorized-entry packet." She turned the screen so Lena could read: `INCIDENT 24-0917 / CHANCERY C-WING / POSSIBLE RECORDS TAMPERING.` "Source says it auto-pushed to county security and sheriff command at 12:57."

"How much time does that buy them?"

"Enough to come looking before dawn. Keep moving."

"You sound very calm for someone holding two felonies in a backpack."

"I'm not calm. I'm compartmentalized."

"That's reporter for terrified?"

"That's reporter for useful."

They reached the back door, swiped out, and stepped into warm wet air just as rain started, fat drops at first and then a full sheet that flattened the alley dust into mud in seconds. Mara pulled her jacket over the backpack. Lena tucked the folder tube under her shirt and felt paper edges scrape her ribs.

Neither spoke until they reached Lena's car three blocks away. Rain hammered the roof hard enough to drown the world.

Mara sat in the passenger seat dripping onto vinyl and passed one drive across the console.

"Drive B. Offline copy. Keep it dry, keep it hidden."

Lena took it and slid it into the zipper seam of her boot.

Mara's phone lit again before Lena could shift into gear. She shoved another screenshot between them. A grainy hallway still showed two women bent over a cart and a box lid open like a mouth. Below it, a line read: `Forwarded: MCSO Duty Captain`.

"This was supposed to batch at shift change," Mara said. "Somebody shoved it through early."

Lena gripped the steering wheel and felt the leather slip under wet palms. "Then split-home is dead."

"Call Deirdre now."

Lena hit speaker. Deirdre answered on the second ring, voice clipped over road noise.

"Tell me you are not at your residence," Deirdre said.

"In transit," Lena said. "We have scanned packets and hard copies. Incident twenty-four-zero-nine-one-seven is live."

"I saw it. Sheriff's office already asked a duty judge for an emergency seizure order tied to that incident number. Do not take originals into your house. If deputies lift those pages from your kitchen, they will argue theft and contamination in one breath."

Mara leaned toward the phone. "What's the move?"

"Change plan now. First Trinity south lot, two-oh-five. Bring one witness and a duplicate set. I can defend ugly facts. I cannot defend a solo stash."

"Understood," Lena said.

The line clicked dead.

Rain thinned enough for the wipers to catch up. Lena pulled from the curb and cut away from her neighborhood.

"And your source?" she asked.

"Already burned," Mara said. "They sent one more text: if security asks, they never issued the badge tonight."

Lena started to answer and stopped. The packet on her lap had shifted open to the signature again.

`Deputy Waller`

Ink feathered into old paper fibers. No tremor in the hand that wrote it. No apology. Just administrative certainty, the kind that moved land from one family to another while pretending it was weather.

She heard Ida in church voice reading from Amos about selling the righteous for silver and the needy for a pair of shoes. She heard Jalen asking if police lied. She heard her own answer.

"Say it," Mara said quietly.

"Say what."

"What does it do to your case map?"

Lena closed the packet. "It gives us mechanism: death certificates laundered into probate actions, probate actions laundered into tract control. My father signed the service step that made redaction look lawful." She rubbed her thumb over the folder edge until the paper bent. "And it gives Clay motive to bury every inch and call us criminals while he does it."

"Then we've got motive to make it unburyable."

"Yeah."

Mara leaned back and shut her eyes for a moment. "I need one more term."

Lena almost laughed. "Jesus, you don't stop negotiating."

"No, I don't. If this gets violent and you've got to pick between protecting me and protecting the documents, protect the documents."

"Not happening."

"Lena."

"No. I can replace paper. I can't replace you."

Mara opened her eyes and studied her, skeptical and tired and maybe moved despite herself.

"That's noble and stupid," Mara said.

"It's logistics," Lena said. "You're the only person outside this office who can tell the story in full sentences without flinching."

"Fine," Mara said. "Revised term: neither of us plays martyr. We leave together when we can, and when we can't, one verified copy stays in circulation."

"Done."

First Trinity Baptist sat dark behind live oaks and a chain-link fence that hummed with rainwater. The south lot held one church van and a dented Buick with a handicap tag. A porch light snapped on as they parked.

Deacon Loretta James came out in a raincoat over a nightgown, hair wrapped in a pink scarf, arthritic fingers clamped around a legal pad and a battery lantern. She had run the church legal clinic for years and did not waste words.

"Ms. Stokes said this was evidence preservation," Loretta said. "Table is in the fellowship hall. Camera stays on."

Inside, they laid documents across a folding table that smelled of lemon cleaner and old coffee. Mara read file identifiers and page counts into her phone camera while Lena held each sheet flat under the lantern, timestamp app visible beside the signatures. Loretta logged times in block letters and signed as witness at 2:17 a.m., then again at 2:23 when Mara sealed the duplicate set in a tamper envelope.

Mara's phone buzzed with Deirdre's next instruction: `Courier delayed. Keep one set moving. Do not park all copies in one place.`

So they changed again. Loretta locked the duplicate envelope in the church office fire safe and kept the key on a chain under her blouse. Lena kept originals in the plastic case under her seat. Mara carried encrypted scans and Drive A.

When they stepped back into the rain, Lena's hands were shaking hard enough that she missed the car door handle once.

"You think he knew?" Mara asked as they stood in the lot, water running off the church gutters in heavy ropes.

Lena kept her eyes on the dark windshield. "Knew what."

"That he was helping steal land from people who'd already lost blood."

The question had waited all night between them and finally sat in the open like a knife on the table.

"I think he knew he was making something official," Lena said after a while. "I don't know if he called it theft in his own head. Men like him were trained to call it duty." She swallowed. "Duty can be another word for cowardice when the wrong people sign your paycheck."

Mara lifted two fingers like she was tagging a quote in the air. "That's a line I could print."

"Don't."

"I won't. Not yet."

They split at the church gate: Mara east toward the interstate, Lena south toward home with the file case pressing against the side of her calf every time she hit the brake.

At 2:46 a.m., a patrol cruiser sat two houses down from Lena's driveway with its lights off and engine running. She drove past, circled the block once, and came back only after it rolled to the corner and idled there instead.

Inside, before she took off her shoes, her phone lit with an unknown number.

`Security asked about C-wing and six hundred dollars. I said I saw nothing. Do not make me do that twice.`

No signature. No need.

She set the file case on the table beside her father's notebook and opened to the page with `Deputy Waller` while rain ticked against the kitchen window. Outside, tires hissed past the house again, slow enough to count.

She did not sleep. She sat at the table until the clock crawled toward dawn, waiting for the next unknown number.
