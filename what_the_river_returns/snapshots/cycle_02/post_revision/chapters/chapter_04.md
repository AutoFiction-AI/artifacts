# Chapter 4

Four days later, the morgue sat behind the hospital loading dock like a forgotten appliance, humming harder than it should in the late-August heat. Lena parked under a light that buzzed with moths and got out into air that already felt wet enough to drink. The storm had not broken. The sky pressed down in one solid color, and the concrete gave the heat back through the soles of her shoes.
The flip phone Tina handed her sat sealed in evidence across town, but those blocked calls at 02:12 and 02:18 kept needling her every time command asked for a clean story.

Inside, bleach and old coffee fought a losing war against sweet rot. The fluorescent tubes shivered overhead. Dr. Naveen Rao stood at the stainless table in a disposable gown, goggles pushed up in his thick hair, reading from a legal pad dense with numbers. He always wrote in pencil, narrow block letters, and the eraser end was wrapped in blue tape because he chewed pencils down to wood by noon. A child had stuck a glitter star sticker to the edge of his county ID badge, and he had not peeled it off.

"You got my message," he said without looking up.

"You said not to use email." Lena set her case file on the counter and kept her voice flat because she was tired enough to sound angry by accident. "I figured that meant it mattered."

"It matters." He finally looked at her. His eyes were red-rimmed and careful. "Beau with you?"

"On his way."

On the back bench, Trina Polk from histology lined up reagent bottles by height, then by label color, then back by height again. She wore two latex gloves on each hand because formalin cracked her knuckles until they bled. Under her breath she counted pipette drops in the same cadence every time, like she was saying grace over each sample.

Lena pulled out a chair and sat before her knee started complaining. Since the river ramp she still had grit from that levee in the hem of her slacks. She had washed the clothes twice. The grit stayed.

Rao flipped a page and pushed a printed chain-of-custody packet toward her. "Start there. I need you to see the gaps before I explain the data."

The packet looked complete until it did not. Item numbers jumped. Time stamps repeated. One page ended mid-line where a signature should have been and resumed on a different date, different pen, same initials. She knew those initials. She had seen them three times this week on forms that had no business matching.

"Who touched this after intake?" she asked.

"Officially? Three people." Rao gave a dry laugh without humor. "Actually? I can't prove that."

He tapped a second folder, older cardboard, brittle at the corners. "This is what we have from the 1963 burial transfer and county pathology addendum. Pages four through seven are gone. Torn, not misplaced. The edge fiber says hand removal, not decay."

Lena stared at the ragged inner seam. Somebody had pulled those pages with fingers, not floodwater. Her mother once kept church bulletins for forty years in acid-free sleeves, and this county could not keep one dead man's paperwork whole for sixty-three.

The door banged. Beau Broussard came in carrying two sweating fountain sodas and a paper bag gone translucent with grease. He was broad enough to make the room smaller and sunburned along the collar where his vest never covered. He set one cup near Lena, then peeled open the bag.

"Boudin kolaches," he said. "If this turns into one more six-hour science sermon, we're eating first."

Rao did not look up. "I heard that."

"You were meant to." Beau leaned on the counter and nodded at the trays. "Tell me we aren't chasing ghosts before the sheriff walks in and says we're."

Rao laid out three specimen cassettes and a set of photographs under the bench lamp. The trays held bone, tissue, and a hair fragment, all tagged from two scenes that should not have shared a corpse. He started in his usual precise cadence, then sped up like he could outrun interruption.

"I ran standard morphology, then collagen integrity. It didn't fit a single-body profile."

"Contamination?" Lena asked.

"That's what I thought, so I reran extraction twice. Same match." Rao tapped the tray with a gloved knuckle. "I requested isotope panel approval, and command kicked it to review."

Beau frowned. "So they stalled you."

"They denied it pending command review."

Lena felt Beau turn his head toward her. Denied by command meant Clay.

"So what did you do?" she asked.

Rao reached for his goggles, then put them down. "I ran it anyway under method-validation code. That's outside routine protocol. I documented every step in my own log, and if they fire me, they fire me."

Trina stopped counting for one second and looked up. "They better fire all of us," she said, then went back to her pipette, voice steady again. "Because I signed the witness line."

Rao slid a printed chromatogram and three isotope charts across the table, then turned two autopsy photos faceup.

"Simple version," he said. "Your first scene and the river ramp were not one intact corpse moving through space."

Beau stared at the photos. "Then what are we looking at?"

"An assembly. At least three biological sources. One source reads long-buried mid-century." Rao moved one image closer to Lena.

The words landed like a bolt sliding home. Not recurrence. Not a curse. Somebody had done labor with blades and thread.

Beau pointed to the second chart. "And the rest?"

"One reads modern dietary isotope profile. The third is partial and degraded, and I can't age it cleanly yet."

Lena felt the press-room script in her head start to crack: no ghost headline, no clean myth, just a person with access and practice.

"Show me the cut points," Lena said.

Rao passed her a fresh pair of gloves and forceps. "Here." He traced the image with a capped pen. "This femur has parallel machine kerfs, and this torso seam mixes black surgical nylon with white funeral thread. The assembler packed restorative wax into the seam to hide the color break."

Lena bent over the tray. The tissue was cold-slick at first touch, then tacky where wax sat thick. When she lifted the seam with forceps, it stretched in a pale string and snapped, and a trapped pocket of odor pushed out: sweet preservative over wet-earth rot, new chemical laid on old grave. Blue lint and gray grit clung to her glove fingertip. She scraped both into an evidence envelope and felt her throat close hard enough to hurt.

Beau swore softly. "Assembled like what?"

"Like someone with access and time, not someone in a panic. Long bone cuts are clean. Soft tissue was rehydrated before placement."

Lena looked up. "With what?"

"They wired cartilage to old bone, then brushed cavity dye over it to even the tone. Chemistry says handling happened within days, not decades."

Lena looked down at the photo until the edges blurred. The wax in the seam had trapped lint from a blue transport blanket. She swallowed acid, gripped the steel table lip, and read the chemical list twice. The list included ethanol, methanol, quaternary ammonium disinfectant, lanolin-based restorative wax, and the dye carrier used in cavity fluid.

"These are funeral chemicals," she said.

"Some of them," Rao said. "Not all. The blend is odd. It's not textbook embalming and not hospital preservation. It's hybrid work, improvised with whatever was available."

He pointed to one peak on the chromatogram. "This marker should not be in remains buried in 1963 and left alone. It degrades fast in soil. I can say with high confidence it was introduced recently."

The room was so cold her skin pebbled, but sweat still gathered at her back under the vest. Outside the steel door someone rolled a gurney past, wheels squealing on one bad bearing, and then it went quiet again.

Lena said, "Say it the way I'd have to say it on record."

Rao met her eyes. "On record: Evidence supports deliberate postmortem manipulation and mixed-source staging."

Beau cut in. "And the ghost part?"

"No evidence supports supernatural recurrence."

Beau let out a breath he had been storing. "Thank Christ."

"Don't thank anybody yet," Rao said. "If this gets in front of the wrong camera before we map source routes, somebody will spin this as desecration by whoever they already planned to blame."

As if cued, Lena's phone buzzed on the table with a command-channel text: _SHERIFF NEEDS YES/NO FOR NOON PRESS. PRELIM PACKET BY 10:00. KEEP MESSAGE DISCIPLINED._

Rao held still. Trina kept counting drops in a whisper.

Lena stared at the screen until it dimmed, then locked it without replying.

Beau rubbed a thumb over the edge of his soda lid until it cracked. "He's going to bury this if he can pin it on chain noise."

"He might try," Lena said. "He can't un-run the chemistry."

Rao pulled another tray forward. "There is more. I went into the old freezer box where they held Isaiah's documented remains after the first-scene intake."

He corrected himself with a grimace. "After intake last week. There were fiber transfers in the drawer track. Cotton-poly blend with crematory ash and diesel particulate. That drawer has not left this building. The fabric did."

"From what?" Lena asked.

"Transport blanket, likely. Cheap fleet issue. I sampled it."

Beau said, "County van blankets are that blend. So are half the funeral homes."

"Exactly," Rao said. "The science gives direction, not identity. At best I can narrow the handling pattern and route. I can't responsibly name hands yet."

Lena pulled the old cardboard file toward her and held it by the edges. The pages were missing and the seam was torn. Whoever had access to this building knew which years mattered and which forms would have made this easier to prove.

Her mother had looked straight through her at the fish fry when 1963 came up, hands deep in batter, talking about catfish oil temperature like it was scripture. _Some papers should have burned_, Ida had said. Lena had heard warning; maybe it had been grief.

"Can you tie the modern chemistry to supplier lots?" Lena asked.

Rao tightened the glove cuff at his wrist. "Maybe. I need purchase records from county crematory inventory and private funeral transfer logs for at least the last six months. And I need them before somebody edits the spreadsheet history."

Beau gave a short laugh. "You're asking for a unicorn and a warrant."

"Ask anyway," Rao said. "Also, keep this off email."

He pushed a folded page toward Lena with handwritten columns in pencil. The page listed brand abbreviations and partial lot codes, and two chemical families were circled. At the bottom he had written one sentence in all caps: _IF YOU GO PUBLIC EARLY, THEY WILL PICK THE WRONG MONSTER ON PURPOSE._

Lena put the page in her notebook. The paper was warm from his hand.

Trina sealed the last cassette and finally looked up fully. Her voice was soft but carried. "My cousin works intake at Bell and Sons on weekends," she said. "She says county overflow has been coming through odd hours this month. It was not illegal, just odd. Like nobody wants daylight signatures."

Lena held her gaze. "What is your cousin's name?"

"Keisha Polk." Trina peeled off her outer gloves and flexed her cracked fingers. "She's saving for dental school and she hates all of us on principle. But she writes everything down."

"Would she talk?"

"If you ask like a person and not like command." Trina's mouth twitched. "And if you don't let this county hang it on the first Black family with a funeral sign."

No one answered for a beat. The compressor kicked on in the wall with a shudder.

Lena capped her pen. "Then that's the sequence. First pass: noon brief carries no family name and no funeral-home identifier. Second pass: Rao corroborates lot codes from unopened bottles at both sites. Nobody freelances past that."

Beau's jaw flexed. "Clay's going to call that insubordination."

"He can," Lena said. "He can bench both of us by supper if he wants. I'm not handing him a Black scapegoat off half-proof."

She stood, knee stiff, and looked once more at the charts spread across steel. The rumors had offered superstition. Rao's data offered purchase orders, gate logs, and people willing to move flesh across county lines after midnight.

In one close-up, two skin tones met under a ridge of wax the color of candle fat. In another, fresh suture crossed an old burial fissure like boot laces cinched through old leather. Her stomach clenched so hard she had to lean into the table.

She shot both images to her case phone, logged the file numbers, and wrote three immediate pulls in block letters: preservative lot history, overnight transport blankets, fuel-card swipes for overflow vans.

She gathered the packet and turned to Beau. "We start with inventory and transport. Quietly. If Clay asks, we're still verifying sample integrity."

Beau nodded. "I can pull fuel-card pings if dispatch hasn't scrubbed them yet."

"Pull first," she said. "Then tell me what we're allowed to know."

---

By noon the thunderheads had climbed up and gone nowhere, the sun trapped behind them and the air hotter for it. Lena and Beau crossed the courthouse annex lot with paper folders under plastic and sweat at their collars. In the far corner, two stray dogs slept under a county maintenance truck because the truck still dripped cool water from its A/C line.

Purchasing sat on the second floor above tax appeals, behind glass that made everyone look underwater. Darlene Fitch worked the counter in a navy cardigan despite the heat and wore metal finger splints on both index fingers from a quilting injury she talked about before anyone asked. She kept a row of cat photos clipped to her monitor and corrected their names when people called them strays.

Lena passed over her badge and request sheet. "I need chemical lot purchases tied to county crematory operations and overflow transfers, six-month window."

Darlene read the request, then looked at Lena with real sympathy that did not help. "I can release aggregate totals today. Named vendor lines and lot-level detail are restricted under litigation hold as of this morning."

"By whose order?"

"Sheriff's legal office." Darlene lowered her voice. "Hold notice came in at 8:12. If I open that folder without clearance, I lose this desk and my benefits."

Beau leaned in, elbows on the counter. "Darlin', we don't need a sermon. We need the raw file before it gets polished."

"Then you needed to file before hold time." She slid a printed summary through the slot. "This is what policy lets me release without a termination letter."

The sheet was thin and bland by design: monthly totals, no names, no lot numbers. Clay's command text flashed in her head: KEEP MESSAGE DISCIPLINED. She forced her eyes to the only line that could still move the case. Preservative and restoration compounds had spiked threefold in August, while official death intake stayed flat.

Lena tapped the column. "What changed in August?"

Darlene gave a tight shrug. "Compressor failure at county crematory in July, then temporary overflow routing. That's what the memo said."

"Where is the memo?"

"In the hold folder."

Beau muttered, "Convenient as hell."

Darlene glanced toward the back office and lowered her voice again. "If you go to Logistics, ask for paper. Portal history got 'cleaned' after breakfast."

Lena slid the summary into her folder. "Thank you."

"I didn't help you," Darlene said, but she tapped twice on the cat photo labeled _Biscuit_, a nervous habit she seemed not to notice. "I told you which office still keeps paper."

Logistics was three blocks away in a converted feed warehouse that still smelled like grain under the bleach. At dispatch, a clerk with orange reading glasses lifted a laminated policy card between them like a shield.

"No release code, no manifest export. Directive 14-B, section nine."

"Section nine covers media pulls," Beau said. "We're homicide follow-up."

The clerk held his stare. "You can argue code with legal if you want. I print those blue copies without clearance, I lose this desk by lunch and my kid loses insulin coverage."

Lena slid the routing slip back through the slot. "Then route us to whoever can sign now."

They got sent to loading, then records, then back to dispatch with two fresh initials and the same refusal. By the third loop Beau's jaw had set hard enough to show a pulse below his ear. Lena felt the trap in her teeth: stall, exhaust, refuse. She kept her face blank and signed the next routing slip anyway.

They finally got a portal printout after forty minutes and two signatures. It showed spotless transfers straight to county crematory, no detours, every timestamp rounded to quarter hours. At the bottom, a system footer read LAST MODIFIED 08:17.

"They're running us in circles until somebody at command calls," he said.

The loop felt engineered to burn daylight and hand them clean lies. Her father had left her one useful courthouse rule: trust what still stains your fingers. "Then we stop asking on their path." Lena handed the printout back. "This is scrubbed. We go older than the portal." She scanned the wall above dispatch and found what she wanted: old metal cabinets, color tabs, years hand-labeled in marker. The county had digitized everything except what was too annoying to scan.

The oldest clerk in the room had watched every loop without once looking surprised. Lena trusted that kind of stillness more than the portal printout, so she walked to him. He wore hearing aids and a Saints lanyard and had been silently sharpening pencils with a pocketknife. His tag read B. Whitaker.

"Mr. Whitaker," Lena said, "where are the carbon manifests for overflow transfer runs? The blue copy, not the white."

He looked at her badge, then at Beau, then back at her. "Who told you to ask that?"

"Nobody official."

"Good answer." He stood with a wince and led them to a cabinet jammed half shut by humidity-swollen paper. "Blue copies stick because the ink is waxy. Folks hate pulling them. That means they're usually still here."

Whitaker wore one knitted glove though it was ninety-eight degrees. He caught Lena looking and said, matter-of-fact, "Neuropathy. One hand burns, one hand goes numb. July and August are in drawer three. You didn't get this from me."

The manifests were smudged and old-school, route by route, driver initials, odometer start and stop, vehicle number, destination code. Lena and Beau took photos as fast as the office printer coughed toner dust nearby.

Three things surfaced inside ten minutes, and one of them dead-ended on the first pass.

First: Vehicle 14B, the county refrigerated van a gin mechanic had flagged on day one, had made unauthorized after-hours stops on both nights before the two murder scenes. Dispatch logged it as "overflow transfer," not scene transport.

Second: Destination codes that should have read only county crematory included repeated hand-written detours to B&S-2, Bell and Sons back receiving bay, 01:12 a.m., 02:07 a.m., times that kept the truck off main roads and out of daylight.

"Could be planted," Beau said. "Fresh ink, clean story."

"Don't trust it yet." Lena pulled the duplicate sheet underneath. The B&S-2 detour had bitten through all three carbon layers in the same slant and pressure. It had not been added later. "Now cross-check digital."

Beau opened the fleet-card audit feed on his phone, scrolled through raw vendor pings, and frowned. "Doesn't show a damn thing on 14B. Zero swipes."

Lena looked back at the manifest header and caught a crossed-out fleet code under the sticker: 14-07. "Run the legacy unit number."

He switched queries. Two diesel swipes surfaced within five minutes of those detour times.

"Could still be somebody else's card," he said.

"Then we burn ten more minutes and prove mileage." Lena pulled the maintenance clipboard from the adjacent tab. Odometer start-stop spread added eighteen extra miles on both nights, the exact loop out to Bell and Sons' back bay and back. Speed could wait; clean linkage could not.

Third: The signatures on those detour lines were initialed H.R. once, then scratched over, then replaced with generic shift initials no one in dispatch claimed.

Beau stared at the page long enough to leave sweat spots at the margin. "H.R. can be half this damn building."

"Not on that route," Whitaker said quietly from behind them. "Route 14B requires deputy escort authorization after midnight. That changed last month."

Lena turned. "Changed by who?"

Whitaker slid his pocketknife closed and tucked it away. "Bulletin came out of sheriff ops with Judge Pike's emergency-facilities attachment. Stuck in my craw because it used three signatures where one would've done."

Beau cursed under his breath. "Judge Pike doesn't write transport policy."

Whitaker gave him a look that was almost pity. "Son, out here policy chases money. Always has."

A phone rang at dispatch. No one moved to answer it.

The unanswered ring told Lena exactly where the next order would come from. She copied the memo reference number from the cabinet index and felt her pulse shift into that cold, narrow band she got when the shape of a thing emerged. It still was not solved, but now it had edges.

She called Rao from the hallway. He picked up on the second ring.

"Tell me you got lot codes," he said.

"Not yet. We got routes, detours, and a spike in preservative purchases. Overflow was routed through Bell and Sons back bay at night."

Silence for a beat. "That would explain your hybrid chemistry. County stock plus private stock in one handling chain."

"Can you match your compounds to county-only versus funeral-home-only formulations?"

"Partially. If I can get one unopened reference bottle from each site, yes." Rao exhaled hard. "Lena, be careful with how you write this. Bell and Sons employs half that family. If command wants a fast villain, this is tidy enough to weaponize."

"I know." She watched rain begin in huge drops that hit hot asphalt and vanished before they pooled. "Noon packet gets a corroboration hold and no Bell surname. Clay can write me up."

"Good," Rao said. "Because the data says network. It's not one person. It's not one bloodline. It's a network of access."

She hung up and found Beau leaning against the cinderblock wall under a broken fan. He had taken his tie off and wrapped it around one hand, twisting it tight and loose, tight and loose.

"You're thinking about Ruth Bell," he said.

Lena had not said her name out loud. "I'm thinking about what this county does when it gets a chance to recycle blame."

"Yeah." Beau looked out at the rain starting to stick now, darkening the lot in coin-sized spots. "And I'm thinking if H.R. matches who I think, this isn't just a body job. It's command cover."

"It has been command cover since day one," Lena said. "Now we can prove pieces of it."

Before they pulled six weeks of manifests, Lena called Delta Co-op from dispatch's recorded line and read one reroute timestamp to the night manager. He refused until she gave badge number, case ID, and callback cell, all of it logged where command could see by end of shift. He read back pump ticket 44731 at 2:09 a.m. for county unit 14-07 and agreed to hold camera stills until morning.

Only then did they pull every blue-copy manifest for six weeks. The stack grew thick enough to bend the folder clips. A pattern repeated: late-night reroutes, unsigned corrections, missing digital mirrors for paper runs. The more ordinary the paperwork looked, the uglier it became.

At four-thirty, with thunder finally cracking over the levee, Lena drove the stack to Rao in a banker box that smelled like wet cardboard. He met them at the morgue side door with his sleeves rolled and a surgical cap still on his head, as if he had forgotten it there.

"I can work from this," he said.

"Can you get me language by morning that survives cross-exam?" Lena asked.

"If nobody burns the lab down tonight, yes."

Beau handed him a photocopy of the route sheet with the scratched initials. "You didn't get this from me."

Rao took it and looked from one of them to the other. "You both look like hell."

"That means we're on schedule," Beau said.

Lena did not laugh. She watched Rao carry the box inside and thought about the torn pages again, how neat the removal had been. The removal read as planning, not rage. Whoever ran this scheme treated history like inventory, pulling what they needed and discarding what complicated the sale.

By the time she got back to her car, rain hammered the roof hard enough to drown the cicadas. Her phone lit with three missed calls from command and one from her mother that lasted nine seconds and left no voicemail.

She sat with her hand on the wheel and let the rain drum through the metal.

The county had wanted a ghost story because ghost stories did not need subpoenas. Ghost stories did not have fuel cards, shift codes, and procurement spikes. Ghost stories did not implicate offices with letterhead.

What Rao gave her carried two truths at once: people had done this, and people left receipts.

She knew command would try to get in front of it before breakfast.
She looked at the missed calls again and knew morning would start on courthouse steps, not in a lab.
