# Chapter 11

Lena turned off the levee road lights two bends before the icehouse and drove the rest by memory and Otis Mayfield's map, the paper sealed inside a gallon freezer bag that still smelled faintly like fried catfish and lemon pepper. The map's pencil line for the back approach wavered where Otis's hand had shook or laughed or both. It brought her exactly where he said it would: a rutted service track behind a dead loading dock, floodwater standing black in the low places, the old brick shell of the icehouse leaning toward the river like it had given up holding its own weight.

The storm had moved east before dawn, but the heat had not broken. It sat on her skin like wet cloth. Cicadas kept a high metallic whine in the trees behind the building, and levee wind moved through rusted sheet metal with a throatier sound, as if someone was slowly tearing canvas.

Beau killed the engine and tapped the dash twice before he got out, the superstition he had never admitted was one. He had quit cigarettes again this month, and he cracked sunflower seeds into an empty ibuprofen bottle while he drove; the bottle sat in the cup holder now, half full of shells and bitter spit. He thumbed his duty light on, checked the extra batteries in his shirt pocket, then checked Lena's face.

"Tell me we're not about to find one old cot and six dead raccoons," he said.

"I'd love six dead raccoons," Lena said. "Dead raccoons don't lawyer up."

He snorted once. "You get sentimental this early, I'm calling Dr. Rao and reporting neurological symptoms."

Crime-scene tech Trina Cade climbed out of the rear unit with two camera cases and an evidence roll cart. She had a cartoon dinosaur Band-Aid wrapped around her thumb where she had pinched it fixing her son's bike chain before shift, and the edges had gone gray with fingerprint powder. "Floodwater's about three inches at the door," she said. "I brought the tall boot covers, not the cheap ones. You're welcome."

Lena gave her a nod and checked her watch: 4:41. They had a county warrant signed at 11:18 the night before, narrow but valid, and a four-hour window before traffic and politics woke up all the way. She had counted on the clock, on darkness, on the possibility that whoever had hidden this place still believed nobody local had the map.

She had not counted on mercy. She had counted on speed.

The side entrance lock was a swollen disk of rust. Beau set the bolt cutter jaws and leaned in with his shoulder. Nothing. "Come on, you rusted bastard," he said, reset, and leaned harder. The cutter slipped, snapping his knuckles against the steel hasp. He hissed, shook his hand, set again.

"Don't bleed on my first chain log," Trina said without looking up from her camera setup.

"Don't run commentary till coffee," Beau said.

The third bite sheared halfway through the shackle. Lena finished it with a pry bar, the metal giving all at once with a sound like a gunshot under a blanket. They froze and listened. The only sounds were cicadas and wind. There were no engines.

Lena read warrant language into Trina's body cam for entry record, gave time and location, then pushed the door.

Cold, wet air came out carrying mold, old ammonia, river mud, and something sweet-rotten underneath, the scent you got when cardboard sat damp for years and rats nested between documents. Their lights cut through suspended dust and gnats. The first room held a conveyor frame with no belt, broken pallets, an ancient walk-in unit hanging open with no compressor attached. Floodwater sloshed over concrete up to Lena's ankles, lukewarm and slick with algae.

"Foot placement," she said. "No quick turns."

"Yes, ma'am," Beau said, mock-formal, then pointed with his light. "Office door. Back left."

The office had once been cinder block with a wire-mesh window. Now the mesh had a square cut through it and bent inward. Somebody had made entry here before and not long ago; the cut edges were bright where rust had been disturbed. Lena touched the metal. It was not dry. It was not old.

Inside, shelves lined both walls. Banker boxes, three deep in places, labels in two kinds of handwriting and three kinds of marker. Ledger spines stood upright in milk crates, leather darkened by humidity. Trina swore under her breath.

"Holy hell," she said. "This isn't a stash. This is a library."

Lena felt the room change shape around her. It was not because the office was small; it was because all the air now had weight.

She went shelf by shelf, calling labels while Trina photographed.

"`MERCER CHANCERY - SPECIAL HEARINGS - OCT 62 TO FEB 64.`"

"`DISBURSEMENT AUTHORIZATIONS - GIDEON TRACT - CLOSED.`"

"`COUNTY TRANSFER RECEIPTS - COURTESY COPIES.`"

"`NIGHT COURT DOCKET B.`"

Beau lifted one ledger from the second shelf, both hands under it like it might split. "This one got numbers on the edge. Could be indexing."

"Don't open yet," Lena said. "Photos first."

Her voice came out flatter than she intended, the public voice she used in hearings and on reports. Under it, her pulse kicked hard enough that she could feel it in her gums.

Trina moved fast, circling, flash muted with a taped diffuser so they did not white out the ink. Lena wrote a rough item sequence in her notebook, water spotting the page from her sleeve. Box 1 through Box 9 from east wall. Three loose ledgers from floor crate. One steel dispatch case under desk. She knelt to read the case tag and got mud on both knees.

The tag read: `MC-SH/ARCH TEMP / 04-17`.

She straightened, and Beau's tight jaw told her he had read it too.

"Sheriff archive temporary," he said. "That's our house code."

"Was our house code," Lena said.

He did not answer.

From the third shelf, a half-open ledger showed onion-skin carbon sheets curled at the corners. Lena should have waited for full photo sequence. She did not. She touched the edge with gloved fingertips and lifted one page enough to read three lines.

Case Number 43-B.

Property hearing conducted without claimant present.

Transfer approved pending sheriff certification.

At the bottom of the page sat initials she knew from old report signatures and from the notebook she had slept beside for weeks: D.W.

Her father's hand had always made the W too tall, like it was trying to stand up from the line.

She lowered the page and stepped back. She became aware again of water around her boots, of the low hum of Trina's camera, of Beau breathing through his nose in quick shallow pulls when he was trying not to say something stupid for comfort.

"We've got enough for probable-cause expansion," Lena said. "We bag priority ledgers and the steel case first."

Beau nodded. "I can carry two at a time if the bottoms hold."

Trina zipped open evidence bags and said, "If these are damp all the way through, we need breathable containers in the truck. Plastic's going to cook them by noon."

"We transfer to paper once logged," Lena said. "Get me wide shots of all four walls before we touch anything else."

Trina pivoted and fired off the sequence. Through the window cutout, the sky had gone from black to bruised navy. Lena checked the time again: 5:03.

She heard engines before she saw light. Two heavy vehicles, one lighter. Not county units; the idle sounded smoother, less rattled. The noise came in fast, then cut at once.

Beau's head snapped toward the door. "Company."

Lena killed her flashlight and moved to the office threshold. Headlights washed across the main room in white bars. Doors opened. Boots on waterlogged concrete outside. Somebody called, "Federal task force! Announce and hold your position!"

"Shit," Lena said, just under her breath, and clicked her body cam to live stream.

When she stepped into the first room, five people in tactical vests were already inside the side entry she had forced. Two more came through the loading bay with hard cases. A white box truck with government plates idled beyond them, rear door rolled up.

The woman in front held up an ID wallet with practiced impatience. "Special Agent Celia Kearns, Civil Rights Division task force." She was maybe forty, maybe less, with neon-orange running shoes under federal-issue cargo pants and a nicotine patch just visible above her collarbone. She pressed two fingers against that patch every few seconds as if checking it had not slipped. "This site is under federal seizure authority as of zero four fifty-six. You're ordered to cease search activity and surrender all material already handled."

Lena did not move. "Show me your signed order."

Kearns handed over a printed notice clipped to a chain-of-custody cover sheet. Federal letterhead. Statute citations. No itemized inventory. A magistrate signature block marked `telephonic authorization`, name redacted in the copy.

"This is not an item transfer," Lena said. "This is a hold notice."

"It supersedes your local warrant under civil-rights seizure authority," Kearns said. "You can challenge it in court. You do not challenge it on this floor."

Behind her, one of the agents said, "East office shelves, B-seventeen through B-twenty-four, then the floor crate by desk." He said it like he had walked the room yesterday.

Lena ran her light from the agent's face to Kearns. "You already had an internal map."

Kearns's mouth tightened. "Detective, you're obstructing a federal seizure. Step back now."

"Not without transfer log." Lena held up her notebook. "Anything that leaves this building gets itemized against local sequence, with signatures from both agencies. That's custody law, not ego."

Kearns leaned closer, voice low enough that only Lena and Beau could hear. "You're treating this like a county HR dispute. It's a federal civil-rights seizure."

"Then act like it and log your seizure properly."

Beau moved to Lena's left shoulder. "Ma'am, we can do this clean in five minutes. Nobody here wants a fight."

He sounded almost friendly, but his stance had gone workboat-steady, feet set wide for slippery deck, knees loose. He had worked docks before patrol and he trusted weight distribution more than rank.

A second county SUV rolled up outside. Sheriff Clay Reddick came through the door adjusting the cuff of a blue campaign polo that still showed stitch marks where a donor logo had been picked off. He smelled like mint and expensive soap, as if he had changed in a hurry and still remembered to shave.

"Everybody take a breath," Clay said, hands out. His tone was all press conference. "Federal lead kicked in at four fifty-six. Keep your cams rolling, copy your sequence pages for my file, and clear that doorway in sixty seconds."

Lena turned on him. "You knew."

"I got a call before five."

"You knew before we entered." 

Clay gave her the false-courteous smile he used when he wanted to appear patient in front of cameras. "Detective Waller, this is not the place for assumptions."

"They called box numbers before they crossed the threshold."

"You hand Kearns your item sequence and step off the doorway," Clay said. "Block one more transfer and I'll log it as Directive 14-B interference before sunrise."

He wrapped the threat in administrative diction and kept his smile fixed.

Beau gave the smallest shake of his head, a warning and a question in one movement. She caught both. If she pushed too hard, Clay would make the push the story.

She squared to Kearns. "You'll sign for each item."

Kearns gave a curt nod to her team, not to Lena. "Move."

Two agents went past Lena into the office. One carried preprinted barcode strips. Another held a tablet already loaded with placeholder entries instead of blank fields. The prepared template hit her like a slap. They were not documenting discovery; they were executing a list.

Trina stood by the shelves with her camera against her chest. "You can't bag over active local capture," she said.

An agent took hold of her elbow. "You're done."

"Hands off my tech," Lena said.

The agent released Trina, but not before leaving four wet fingerprints on her sleeve.

In the office doorway, Beau planted himself between the first outgoing crate and the hall. "Need your signature," he said, holding out Lena's notebook and a pen. "Item by item." Lena almost yanked him back by the vest. If she pulled him off, the ledgers walked out clean; if she left him there, she was betting his body against their schedule.

The agent with the crate kept moving. "Out of the way."

"Sign and go."

"Move."

The crate tilted as they both adjusted for footing. Water sloshed. Lena felt the moment go wrong before it broke: too many boots, too little floor, everybody fighting the same six feet of concrete. Beau reached to steady it, one hand on the box edge, one on the agent's forearm. A second federal agent came from the side and tried to peel his grip. Beau jerked backward, boot sliding on algae-slick concrete. The first agent yanked the crate up. Beau's right hand caught between plastic lip and steel doorframe.

There was a wet pop, not loud, more like snapping a green bean close to a microphone.

Beau made a noise Lena had never heard from him, raw and high and instantly cut off when he clamped his teeth down. The sound hit her low in the gut, a cold punch that emptied her lungs. The crate dropped. Ledger corners hit water. Beau went to one knee, then both, right wrist bent wrong under him.

"Stop!" Lena shouted. "Everybody stop moving!"

Nobody stopped at first. One federal agent was still trying to keep hold of the crate handle. Trina yelled, "Get your damn hands off him!" Clay said, "Hold it," too late and too calm, like he was calling a timeout instead of watching bone go wrong.

Lena shoved between bodies and got to Beau. His face had gone gray under the stubble. Sweat ran off his nose in a steady line. He cradled his right forearm against his chest with his left hand, trying not to look at the wrist. Her mind snapped into triage in hard steps: immobilize, witness names, preserve where every hand had been.

"Don't move it," Lena said.

"I wasn't planning to dance," he said, then sucked air through his teeth so hard his words broke. "Fuck. Fuck."

The distal forearm had already started swelling. The wrist angled inward in a way wrists did not angle. Lena had seen enough fractures to know this one was not a clean line.
The angle punched heat up the back of her throat. She had told him to hold that doorway, and now his shooting hand was folded wrong because she had chosen speed over backup.

Kearns crouched two feet away. "We'll call EMS."

"Do it," Lena said. "And your people step back now."

Kearns signaled and they backed, but only two steps. The crate with the dropped ledgers sat half in water. The pages had fanned at one corner.

She aimed the words at Clay. "This is on your scene. Write it like it happened."

Clay's jaw worked once. "Let's keep the temperature down."

"His wrist is broken in half and you're talking temperature."

Clay kept his eyes off Beau and turned to Kearns. "Can your medic assess while we finish transfer?"

Lena almost laughed from the cruelty of how practical it was. She did not laugh.
Guilt came metallic at the back of her tongue. She wanted to blame algae, federal elbows, and Clay's timing; the order to move before dawn had still been hers.

"Nobody touches those ledgers until they're photographed where they fell," she said.

Kearns nodded to one of her agents, who took two quick pictures with a body cam still rolling. "Documented," she said.

"For you," Lena said. "Not for us."

Sirens rose from the road, close. While they waited, federal agents kept moving evidence in a line to the truck. The first load past Lena was the steel dispatch case tagged `MC-SH/ARCH TEMP / 04-17`, still wet where she had touched it.

"That case gets logged against my local sequence," Lena said, shoving her notebook and pen toward the bearded agent carrying it. His vest patch read `MERRITT`.

Merritt looked to Kearns instead of to her. Kearns answered for him. "No local co-signs. Federal intake only."

Lena kept the pen out. "Then write your barcode and your name in my book."

Merritt walked past her shoulder and loaded the steel case into the truck without touching the pen.

The next container was a gray humidity bin holding the half-open ledger from shelf three, page edge still stamped `43-B`, her father's tall `W` visible through the wet plastic sleeve. An agent slapped on a temporary strip and started to turn away.

Lena moved with him, planted herself at the truck ramp, and drove her body-cam lens inches from the bin lid. "Hold it still. Read me that code."

"Back off," the agent said.

She thumbed digital zoom, caught the full string before his glove covered it again, and said into the live mic, clear and slow, "BX-1179-443A. Repeat, BX-1179-443A, humidity bin carrying ledger 43-B."

Trina called from the doorway, "Timestamped and mirrored."

"Stay clear," the agent said, and kept moving.

A paramedic crew came in with a splint bag and pain meds. The lead medic, a woman with silver cat-eye frames fogged from humidity, cut Beau's sleeve and palpated gently. "Goddamn, easy," Beau said, then caught himself. "Sorry. Fuck, that lights me up."

"Probable distal radius with displacement," she said. "Could be ulna involvement. He needs X-ray now."

"He rides with me," Lena said.

Clay stepped in before the medic could answer. "He rides with EMS. You follow."

"And scene custody?"

"Federal lead."

"Local witness log?"

"You'll get what they release."

Her gut tightened at the practiced script: they would release what served them and bury the rest under process.

As the stretcher wheels rattled across broken concrete, Beau grabbed Lena's sleeve with his left hand. His grip was weak and shaking.

"Don't let them rewrite this," he said.

"I won't."

"I know you won't," he said, eyes squeezed shut. "I said don't let them." 

His blood had soaked through her cuff at the icehouse and dried there, stiff against her wrist. The stain felt more binding than any warrant signature she had carried in.

Then the medic pushed and he was gone through the door into gray dawn.

By the time Lena finished giving initial injury statement to county dispatch, the white truck was nearly full. Federal seals went across the rear door in bright red tape. Kearns signed a federal transfer sheet listing `MC-SH/ARCH TEMP / 04-17`, one humidity bin coded `BX-1179-44?A` on the smeared duplicate though Lena had already logged `BX-1179-443A` on livestream, and seven banker boxes from the east wall. She marked the county signature line `not required` and pushed Lena's notebook back unopened.

"You'll receive itemization through official channel," Kearns said. "Do not contact my team directly."

"You're not even pretending this is cooperative," Lena said.

Kearns held her gaze for one long second. "Cooperation requires trust in both directions."

"You came with a shopping list and no inventory."

"I came because somebody in your county kept burying bodies inside evidence systems." Kearns angled her chin toward the office where water still pooled around empty shelf outlines. "You're not the only person who can see what this is."

Then she turned and walked to the truck, pressing the nicotine patch at her collarbone again, twice, hard.

Lena stood in the doorway with wet socks, mud to her calves, and nothing in her hands but a warrant copy and a notebook full of sequence numbers with no receiving signatures. She had visual confirmation. She had chain collapse. She had no admissible possession of what mattered.

When she finally got in her unit to follow the ambulance, the dashboard clock read 6:12.

She sat for three breaths before turning the key.

On the fourth breath she called Dr. Rao.

He picked up on the second ring, voice grainy with no sleep. "Tell me this is not what it sounds like in dispatch chatter."

"It's worse," Lena said. "Meet us at Mercer General if you can."

"I'm already on my way there for another consult. Send me what you wrote down before you clean it up."

She dragged her thumb across her wet notebook, watching B-19 bleed into the margin. "I don't have enough."

"Send what you have," Rao said. "Enough is a moving target."

Mercer General's emergency corridor smelled like bleach, stale coffee, and the sour edge of floor wax not fully dry. Fluorescent tubes buzzed overhead in uneven pulses. Lena sat on a molded plastic chair outside imaging while rain started against the windows in light, hard taps, not enough to cool anything.

Beau came back from X-ray with a temporary sugar-tong splint and a face like he had been scraped hollow. He held his right arm across his lap, elevated on a pillow wrapped in a sheet. His knuckles were already turning violet under freckles.

"Comminuted distal radius," the ER physician said. "Maybe intra-articular. Ortho will set and cast once swelling allows. He's out of field duty for now."

For him, "for now" meant weeks, maybe months. For a cop with his dominant wrist broken, it meant paperwork he hated and a sidearm qualification he could not pass until healed. It meant Lena losing the one partner who could read her half-sentences in a bad room.

When the physician left, Beau stared at the closed curtain and said, "You were right about the place."

Lena leaned against the wall, arms folded tight to stop her own shaking. "I'd rather be wrong and bored."

He laughed once and winced. "Yeah."

Silence settled for a beat, then shifted.

"You moved too fast," he said.

His words landed soft, almost tired, and that softness cut deeper than a shout.

Lena kept her voice level. "We had a valid warrant window and a source who'd already delayed us twenty years by being scared to talk."

"You moved before we had state backup."

"State backup would've put Clay in the loop earlier."

"Clay was already in the loop, Lena." Beau met her eyes then, bloodshot and bright with pain meds and anger both. "That's my point. You put me in that doorway and told me to hold the line while they crowded it."

She opened her mouth, then closed it. The fluorescent light flickered above his bed and came back.

"I gave that order," she said finally. "You followed it, and your wrist paid for it."

Beau's jaw tightened. "Yeah. So hear me: if you run another pre-dawn entry to beat Clay and cut backup, I don't go in as your second. I can't trust that call."

The image of his hand pinned in the steel frame flashed bright and exact, skin gone white before the pop.

Lena swallowed. "Then we don't fake this partnership. Next entry, we set terms together or we don't run it together."

Sheriff Clay appeared in the corridor outside the curtain, speaking to a deputy before stepping in. He had swapped the campaign polo for a pressed white shirt, sleeves rolled to exact mid-forearm, but the mint smell was the same. He carried a legal pad with nothing written above the fold, the blank top strip looking like a redaction waiting to happen.

"How's our patient?" he asked.

"In pain," Beau said. "And listening."

Clay gave a short nod that could pass for concern from a distance. "Workers' comp paperwork's already moving. We'll code this as line-of-duty under joint federal action and keep him covered."

There was no apology in it, only coverage codes. Lena felt heat climb her neck.

"Classify it as what happened," Lena said. "He was injured in a custody dispute your office concealed."

Clay's eyebrows lifted just enough to signal offense without committing to it. "Let's avoid loaded framing in a hospital corridor."

Behind Clay's shoulder, Beau shifted and the splint crackled against the sheet. The sound pulled the icehouse doorway back into Lena's throat.

"You had pre-notice and withheld it."

"I got preliminary notice of possible federal interest. That's not the same as operational certainty."

"They knew exact shelf numbers before entry."

"Could've come through a half-dozen channels."

"Name one that doesn't run through you."

He gave her that patient smile again, the one that erased content and preserved tone. "Detective, your job right now is to document your observations and quit improvising jurisdictional fights you can't win."

"My job is homicide evidence integrity."

"Your job is what I assign while you wear this badge." He tapped the legal pad once with his pen. "There'll be a joint statement at nine thirty. You won't freelance with press. You won't contact federal personnel directly. You route every request through my office."

He tore off a second sheet and held it out until she took it. The header read `Administrative Communication Restriction - Case Cluster 43-B`. The second paragraph read: `Any unauthorized direct contact with federal personnel or media triggers immediate relief from duty pending internal review.` A deputy in the hall photographed Lena taking the page for receipt.

Beau shifted on the bed, face tightening. "Clay, with respect, that's a choke chain."

Clay gave him rehearsed sympathy. "It's the lane. Nine-thirty briefing, one spokesperson, no off-book calls."

"It's control," Lena said.

He ignored her and addressed Beau. "Take the week, maybe two. Heal up. We'll put you on desk review after cast."

Beau's laugh was dry as paper. "Desk review with one hand. Living the dream."

Clay wrote something on the pad at last, tore off the top sheet, and handed it to Lena. It was a single line with a federal liaison number, extension `3`, and `Case Cluster 43-B` underlined twice.

"Use that between one and three only," he said. "Leave one message with requested items. If either of you calls a field agent direct, badge access freezes by lunch."

"It already means something," Lena said.
Lena did not crumple Clay's order. She photographed it beside Beau's intake wristband and sent the image with her raw scene timeline to county risk-management and her own case archive before Clay reached the elevator. Then, before the pain meds blurred him out, she had Beau dictate exactly who touched the crate, where his hand was pinned, and which item tag he saw before he went down. She wrote every word, including the curses.

Clay tucked the pad under his arm. "A hunch does not survive arraignment. Bring me a signed intake log and I can move." His gaze slid to Beau. "I want you back healthy. That's not rhetoric."

Then he left, shoes quiet on the waxed floor.

Beau watched the curtain sway behind him. "He lies like a pastor," he said.

"He lies like a man who's practiced in mirrors," Lena said.

A nurse came in to hang fluids, humming old R&B under her breath and tapping one chipped red fingernail against the IV pole while she counted drops. She checked Beau's splint, adjusted his pillow, and said, "If either of you rips that cast once ortho sets it, I will haunt your grandchildren." Then she smiled without waiting for a response and moved on.

At 8:07, Dr. Naveen Rao stepped into the corridor carrying two paper cups and a manila envelope tucked under his arm. His scrub cap was gone; his hair stood up in the back where he had run his hands through it all morning. A tiny brass chess knight dangled from his ID reel, clicking against his badge when he walked. He set one cup beside Lena without asking and sat in the chair opposite her.

"Cardamom tea from the volunteer desk," he said. "Tastes like hot regret, but it has caffeine."

Lena wrapped both hands around the cup for the heat. "You saw dispatch?"

"I saw enough to miss breakfast and step on one administrator's ego." He glanced toward Beau's bay. "How bad?"

"Wrist fracture. Dominant hand."

Rao closed his eyes for a beat and exhaled through his nose. "I'm sorry."

He pulled the manila envelope into his lap but did not open it yet. He checked the corridor once, making sure Clay was not in sight, then lowered his voice.

"Before you ask, no, I wasn't invited into the federal circle," he said. "I arrived at the icehouse at six twenty with a field contamination kit because your voicemail said wet paper and possible biological carryover. They blocked me at perimeter." He tapped the envelope. "But somebody dropped this while loading."

He slid out a strip of thermal printer paper, the kind used for quick chain labels, and pressed it into Lena's hand. On it was a barcode and typed text:

`US-CRT-MSR-EV / BX-1179-443A / DOC-MIX / TEMP HOLD`

The middle string matched what she had already spoken into her live mic at the truck ramp.

Lena felt her pulse in her throat again.

"You got this where?"

"Under the ramp lip by the truck," Rao said. "It had river mud on one side and fresh adhesive on the other. I photographed location before picking it up. Time-stamped."

"You picked up federal property."

"I picked up litter that would have washed into the river in ten minutes." He held her eyes. "I also memorized the string before I touched it. Then I wrote it down on my glove in case your capture got challenged. I'm tired, Lena, not reckless."

She traced the code again with one finger, checking each character against the number she had already written twice.

BX-1179-443A.

One concrete number in a morning designed to erase specifics.

"If that tag is real," she said, "it maps to their intake ledger."

"If they keep one worth subpoenaing," Rao said. "Which is an if."

Beau called from behind the curtain, voice rough. "Subpoena all the ifs anyway."

Rao stood and stepped into the bay. "Good morning to you too," he said.

Beau lifted his splinted arm half an inch and dropped it back with a grimace. "Morning's generous."

Rao checked the cast wrap and distal circulation with two quick fingers. "Pain level?"

"Eight if I'm honest, six if I want to leave today."

"Honesty first," Rao said. "Masculinity can wait in the parking lot."

Beau gave a cracked grin despite himself. "You always this sweet with cops?"

"Only the ones who try to wrestle evidence crates with their shooting hand." Rao adjusted the sling and looked directly at him. "You need this healing straight. If the radius sets wrong, you'll lose grip strength for months. That means no weapon retention and no emergency braking with that hand. You understand me?"

"Yeah," Beau said quietly. "I understand."

Rao nodded and stepped back out into the corridor with Lena.

"I can run the serial through pathology shipping contacts," he said, "not officially. Somebody in Jackson owes me a favor and hates federal grandstanding on principle. It may return nothing, or it may return destination facility."

"Do it," Lena said.

"I will, but hear me first." Rao's voice tightened; detail density climbed the way it did when he was trying not to overstate. "This morning was not random agency overlap. Their team had prebuilt barcode schema, preassigned quantity placeholders, and transport humidity bins staged in the truck. That means planning horizon of at least several hours, likely more. They did not pivot because of your entry. They timed around it."

Lena leaned her head back against cinder block and closed her eyes for one second. Inside the lids she could still see shelf outlines and her father's initials on wet paper.

"Interagency concealment," she said.

"At minimum," Rao said. "Could still have lawful core. Could still be dirty as hell. Both can coexist."

She opened her eyes. "I hate when you're careful."

"You hate when I'm right in slow motion." He held up one hand before she could answer. "I'm not saying give up. I'm saying stop chasing a full retrieval fantasy. Work with fragments before fragments disappear."

She folded the serial strip into her notebook behind her sequence list. "One fragment," she said.

"One is more than zero," Rao said. "And one can become chain if you anchor it fast."

At 8:31, rain thickened outside and then thinned. The corridor lights buzzed, flickered once, steadied. A housekeeping cart squeaked past, then doubled back because someone had spilled coffee near triage.

Lena stood there with bleach smell in her nose, mud drying on her pant cuffs, Beau's blood from the icehouse still dark on one sleeve where she had caught his arm. She had entered the morning with a warrant and a plan to carry proof out in banker boxes. She had left with an injured partner, no custody, and a single serial number written on thermal paper.

It was not enough to prosecute.

It was enough to keep the lie from closing clean through the nine-thirty cameras, until she could find Beau's room again after dark.
