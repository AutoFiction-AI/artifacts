# Chapter 18

Three hours after she and Mara finished affidavits on Dot's diner placemat, Lena climbed the courthouse annex steps in the same clothes before sunup. The November air had enough bite to make her knee scar pull. The fluorescent tubes in the hallway buzzed like trapped insects, and the old tile held the smell of bleach and wet wool coats. Somebody had taped a paper sign over the criminal calendar with blue painter's tape: **AMENDED DISPOSITIONS**. Under it, a deputy she did not know anymore held a Styrofoam cup and looked past her like she was a museum exhibit.

Mara was already at the far bench, one ankle bouncing, phone rig disassembled in her lap. She had switched from her field backpack to a stitched leather satchel that still had a faded airline tag on the handle from a flight she had never gotten reimbursed for. When she got anxious, she worried the corners of manila folders with her thumb until they curled. She did that now while scanning the docket sheet.

"Federal signed theirs at six-thirty," Mara said without looking up. "Hollis took the package. Obstruction, witness intimidation, civil-rights conspiracy. State murder counts get held in abeyance while he cooperates."

"How much time?" Lena asked.

"Guidelines say five to seven if he gets no mercy. They already baked in mercy." Mara finally met her eyes. "They're calling for forty-two months."

Lena leaned against the cinder-block wall and felt how cold it had gotten overnight. Forty-two months for escorting false arrests, moving evidence like furniture, and helping turn dead Black people into a logistics problem. She had seen men get longer than that for stealing catalytic converters behind the truck stop.

Across the hall, two men from Channel Nine argued in low voices over whether they could say the word lynching in a morning teaser. One said viewers tuned out when the past got named too early. The other said sponsors tuned out when it did not. The county had been calling that argument healing for ten weeks.

The first plea hearing ran fast, almost courteous, until the judge asked whether anyone from the harmed families wanted the record before he locked terms. Hollis wore county orange with a blazer over it, hair cut close for television, forearm tattoo peeking above his cuffs when he shifted in the chair. He kept cracking his knuckles against the table edge and wincing, as if he had forgotten his own hands could hurt. He answered yes, sir, no, sir, voice smooth on the record, and did not look toward the Bell family row until Ruth stood.

Lena sat behind the rail and listened to the prosecutor list the facts in clean verbs: coordinated; withheld; reassigned; misrepresented. Ruth's burned fingers were split at the knuckles from wrapping Isaiah's remains by hand the night before. Nadine Brooks had color tabs over invoices for transfer fees, storage fees, and cemetery labor the county had dumped on the family. The legal nouns did not carry any of that weight. They never did.

"Forty-two months for moving my uncle like contraband?" Ruth said. "He tried to scare us quiet at the river, and you still call this cooperation."

Defense counsel was already on his feet. "Your Honor, this is argument, not allocution."

"It's impact," Brooks said, not sitting down. "Her son heard your witness threaten his mother on a livestream. Put that in the record."

The prosecutor nodded once. "State has no objection to a brief statement."

Ruth kept her eyes on the bench. "My son wakes up when the ice maker drops because it sounds like shovels on bone. That's what your mercy bought."

The judge let the silence hold, then spoke to the clerk. "Statement entered. Add a no-contact term: Hollis Reddick will have no direct or indirect contact with the Bell family, Bell counsel, or listed Gideon plaintiffs during supervision. Any violation voids cooperation credit and triggers immediate remand."

Hollis's jaw twitched. In the Bell row, nobody cheered. Ruth sat down and pressed both palms flat on the wood rail until the tremor left her hands.

"Victim impact this afternoon for remaining parties," the clerk announced. "Civil freeze motion at thirteen hundred in chancery courtroom B."

Clay did not appear in person. His lawyer filed a signed plea addendum from federal lockup in Jackson: one felony count reduced to two ethics misdemeanors, no additional jail exposure beyond time served, pension eligibility preserved pending administrative review. He would remain in office under censure until the end of his term, not in handcuffs.

When the clerk read no additional jail exposure beyond time served, Loretta Baines stood in the back row with a rent ledger clamped to her ribs. "Time served for stealing our land and jailing the wrong boy?" she said. "Say that where everybody can hear it."

"Ms. Baines, sit," the bailiff said.

She sat, still staring at the bench, thumb grinding into the ledger spiral until the wire split skin and spotted the margin with blood. Lena read the addendum line twice and felt her jaw lock.

That was where the real fight sat. Prison years mattered, but money was memory with interest. Land was where they had hidden the wound.

At noon the hallway was packed with reporters, pastors, farm co-op members, and men in courthouse suits who had never before used the public entrance. Judge Eleanor Pike came in through the side door under escort. She was barefaced, skin gray with migraine pain, and she dabbed peppermint oil along her wrists every few minutes from a tiny roller bottle she pulled from her sleeve. The scent cut through coffee and sweat like a blade.

She had not resigned from the bench yet, but she had filed notice that morning to step down from the Gideon Foundation board and recuse from all trust matters pending investigation. On paper it read like civic duty. In the gallery it read like someone trying to trade titles for sleep.

Her attorney stood and called her cooperation substantial. He used the phrase institutional fatigue as if that explained why records disappeared and men got buried twice. Brooks opened a folder and laid out receipts on counsel table: transfer labor, corrected certificates, overnight refrigeration, all billed to the Bell family while Pike's lawyers argued context.

"Before sentence, let the minutes reflect what this means," Brooks said, rising without waiting to be called. "Probation for a judge who signed false trust certifications while this family paid to refrigerate and rebury Isaiah Bell."

The bench told her, "Objection to proportionality noted for the record."

Pike spoke once, voice thin but controlled.

"I acknowledge my role in falsified trust certifications," she said. "I accept the court's sentence and conditions."

She delivered every word as if it had been notarized first, shoulders locked, eyes fixed on the wood trim above the families.

The sentence landed light by any ordinary measure: suspended incarceration, four years supervised probation, permanent disqualification from judicial office, mandatory testimony in federal and civil proceedings. She would sleep in her own bed while families paid lawyers by the hour. Brooks wrote probation on her yellow pad so hard the tip tore through the page. Ruth folded a blood-spotted tissue into her coat pocket and stared straight ahead.

Outside courtroom B, Bell counsel Nadine Brooks had her files stacked by color and her grandson's glittery dinosaur sticker still stuck to the spine of the restitution binder. She had kept the sticker there through three hearings and one death threat because, as she told Mara once, "If I can file under a triceratops, I can file under anything." She rolled a silver ring against her knuckle while she waited for the clerk to call the freeze motion.

The civil hearing took two hours and felt like six. Chancery fans pushed stale air across old paper. Every exhibit came stamped and restamped: tract maps, shell-company disbursements, guardianship orders, parcel transfers with Isaiah's name erased down to initials. Brooks asked for immediate injunctive relief freezing Gideon Tract revenues and all derivative trust distributions until restitution litigation was resolved. Defense counsel argued hardship for innocent beneficiaries.

Lena watched the judge read the maps under flickering light, lips moving silently over acreage numbers. She heard her father's handwriting in her head when parcel codes appeared, the clipped way he had marked fields like they were inventory. She had put those pages into public record herself. Ida still had not forgiven her for how.

When the order came down, it did not sound dramatic. "Temporary asset freeze granted pending adjudication of restitution claims." There was no gavel slam and no choir music. It was one sentence that made three men in tailored coats lean back like their chairs had shifted underneath them and made Brooks close her eyes for one full breath before she started writing next steps.

Gideon Tract could not be sold, leveraged, subdivided, or harvested for profit without court approval. Trust outflows stopped at midnight. Emergency operating expenses required receipts. Every monthly statement would now go to plaintiffs' counsel and the monitor. It was material consequence, finally, and it was still not enough to match the decades it had taken to get there.

On the way out, a county commissioner gave a statement on the courthouse steps about moving forward together. He wore a camo cap and a lapel pin and said Mercer needed closure so businesses could recover. He did not use Isaiah's name. He said this chapter of misunderstanding had hurt all sides.

"Loretta Baines paid rent on her grandmother's own parcel for thirty-nine years," Brooks said from just behind the camera line, lifting the restitution binder so the receipts were visible. "That's theft with paperwork, not misunderstanding." A deputy stepped between her and the microphones and told everyone to clear the path.

Mara clicked her recorder off and muttered, "He says misunderstanding like nobody got buried with broken wrists."

"He says it like he wants Christmas tourism money," Lena said.

A woman from the co-op standing behind them, with tractor grease still under one thumbnail and a Delta Farmers cap pulled low, snorted. "They'll plant a memory plaque and call it reconciliation by spring."

Late in the afternoon, Lena crossed to the side lot for air and found Beau by the chain-link fence with a folded resignation letter and a physical-therapy schedule in a clear sleeve. His right wrist was wrapped in black kinesiology tape. He had signed the letter left-handed, jagged and impatient.

"You timed this," Lena said.

"Wanted the cameras busy with somebody else's mercy," he said.

The resignation was effective immediately: twenty-three years of service, no pension enhancement, no ceremony. Beau glanced toward the annex doors where two deputies were laughing at a phone screen. "I'm done writing reports that leave out the part where we lied."

"You don't owe me this."

"I know. I owe me this." He shifted his taped wrist. "PT starts Thursday in Greenville. If I miss, they give my slot away. So that's my life now."

She told him she and Mara were building an independent records shop for civil support and contract investigation work. He asked if it could cover rent. She told him no, not even close. He nodded and said, "Send me any chain forms. I can still read bad paperwork one-handed."

When he drove off, he steered with his left palm and forearm. The brake lights blinked twice before he pulled onto Main.

At six, Lena and Mara took the back booth at Delta Queen Diner with two legal pads and a laptop that only opened halfway before the hinge caught. Mara already had three columns in all caps: retainer work, grant applications, and unpaid cases they would take anyway. On television, a panelist kept saying economic confidence while Ms. Vera behind the counter muttered, "Economic confidence my ass," and turned the volume down.

Mara said a Jackson outlet would cover document pulls if they split publication rights. Lena opened her banking app and read the number out loud: one hundred eighty-seven dollars until month end after mortgage autopay, Jalen's inhaler refill, and the credit-union note.

"Mortgage clears on the twenty-eighth, inhaler on the thirtieth, and the note drafts Friday," Lena said. "We invoice pull time same day, post receipts before lunch, and keep the raw files local. No release control, no deal."

Mara tapped the table once. "You're calling that discipline. I'm calling it leverage. If they can delay first publish, they can bury a document we bled for."

Lena nodded once. "Then the contract says same-day release to our archive, or we walk."

She wrote **DELTA ACCOUNTABILITY PROJECT** across the top of a clean page and circled it hard enough to crease the pad. They set week-one priorities around pressure, not optimism: Gideon monitoring records and morgue procurement logs on Monday, plaintiff affidavits with Brooks on Tuesday, intake rules on Wednesday, and a Greenville loop on Thursday that included checking Beau at therapy.

When rain started needling the awning, they locked rules in plain language: no livestream at the burial unless the family asked, no parking-lot confessions, no backchannel deals, no touching Bell matters without counsel in the room, and publish receipts before lunch if the county squeezed. Lena felt the old pull to ask permission from a system that had already fired her. The pull was still there. It was just not in charge anymore.

***

The cemetery road had turned to rutted clay overnight. Pickup tires spun and spat mud up onto church shoes while people guided each other through the slick low spots by flashlight. Dawn came late behind a low ceiling of cloud. The levee wind carried cold river smell and the thin sweet smoke from somebody's burn barrel down the road.

Each rut tugged at Lena's knee scar, a small electric warning that this county still made people pay in the body before it paid in court.

Bell Family Cemetery sat on a rise above the fields, fenced with rusted iron and dotted with stones that leaned like tired shoulders. A county van had already dropped off the transfer case from evidence custody. Two men from the funeral home stood with their hats in their hands, waiting for instruction from Ruth.

Ruth Bell wore a dark wool coat over a black dress and latex gloves under black knit gloves because formaldehyde had burned the webbing between her fingers years ago and cold made the scars sting. She kept checking the glove seams with her thumb before touching anything, a mortuary habit she could not switch off. Beside her stood her son, Malik, ten years old, red earbuds looped around his neck, staring hard at adults so no one would speak to him like a child.

The earbuds pulled up a flash of Jalen at ten, with the same defensive stare and the same refusal to let grown people call fear maturity.

When Lena stepped through the gate, Ruth gave one short nod. There was no embrace and no ceremony between them. They had done those negotiations already in harder rooms.

"Transfer paperwork's here," Ruth said, tapping a folder against her thigh. "Name correction attached. County finally stamped all of it last night."

She looked toward the road and lowered her voice. "I put a knife in Voss and wire on Fenn, and now every deputy in this county knows my son's name. That's the bill from last night's livestream, whether they print it or not."

Lena felt the old reflex to promise safety in clean procedural terms and killed it before it left her mouth.

"I'm not asking you to carry any more of it today," Lena said.

"Then hear me plain. Your new project doesn't call Malik, knock on my door after dark, or ask me for extra statements off the record."

"Agreed," Lena said. "If anything legal comes up, it goes through Brooks, and Malik stays out of it."

Ruth held her gaze for a beat, then nodded. "Keep that boundary and we can stand in the same dirt."

The corrected death record listed the full legal name that had been shaved down in public memory for sixty-three years. The new stone lay on a tarp beside the open grave, letters still dusted with gray grit from engraving.

Ida arrived ten minutes later in a navy church suit and low black heels she only wore for funerals and trial testimony. Her arthritis gloves matched the suit. She carried a folded handkerchief embroidered with tiny white wheat stalks, an old church gift she had mended twice at the corner seam. She saw Lena, paused, and then kept walking toward the front row without changing pace.

That hurt in the exact way Lena had expected and still had not prepared for.

Reverend Tate kept a tin of clove lozenges in his coat pocket for the cough he got every winter, and he kept the service short because the wind was cutting through coats and because he knew better than to sermonize over a wound this old. He read from Amos first, then from a church ledger entry Ida had preserved in a plastic sleeve: Isaiah's baptism date, his parents' names, and a line noting that he had taught younger children their letters after field work on Sundays.

Nobody called him a symbol or a lesson. They said his name the long way, full and formal, and let it sit in the air.

When it was time, Ruth directed the funeral men to lift the remains box from the transfer case. They moved slowly, not for theater, but because the contents had been cataloged, repackaged, and reassembled across too many jurisdictions. Tissue tags were gone now. Evidence tape had been stripped. What remained had been wrapped in plain linen by Ruth and two church women the night before in the preparation room behind Bell and Sons, under bright bulbs that made every scar look whiter.

Lena had helped for the last hour of that work, passing pins and holding one shoulder steady when Ruth's hands started to shake. She could still feel the starch from the linen against her fingertips.

At graveside, each person approached in whatever order they could bear. Nadine Brooks set down a single magnolia leaf and whispered the case number for the restitution suit like a vow. Beau came up with his wrist braced and dropped a rusted fishing hook into the grave, saying Isaiah's brothers used to trade hooks by the levee according to his grandfather. Dr. Rao, coat collar up against the wind, placed a folded lab mask at the edge of the stone and stood there with his jaw tight for a long time before stepping back.

The hook hit pine with a tiny metallic tick, lighter than it should have been. Lena had logged knives, casings, wire, and teeth under courthouse bulbs for ten weeks; none of those tags had asked her to watch apology happen in public. Her throat tightened anyway.

Mara stayed off to the side with her notebook closed. She had braided a strip of red cloth into her hair that morning, the same color as the marker flags she used at scenes, but she did not write a word until Ruth asked her to record the names of every person present. It was documentation, not spectacle.

When Ida's turn came, she took longer to kneel than Lena had ever seen. She set both gloved hands on the pine lid and bowed her head. Her shoulders shook once, almost invisible, then stilled.

Watching Ida lower herself that slowly, Lena felt the old child panic from church aisles, the one that came whenever adults went quiet and nobody explained if the silence meant prayer or danger. She curled her toes inside wet boots until the cramp steadied her.

"You were not supposed to carry this by yourself," she said, voice level against the wind. "None of us were."

It was unclear whether she was speaking to Isaiah, to Lena, or to the whole line of people standing in mud with their hats off. It might have been all three.

After the coffin was lowered and the first shovels of dirt hit wood, the sound changed the crowd. Metal rang, then clay thudded, then the hole answered back dull and final. Malik flinched at the first strike and pretended he had not. Ruth reached back without looking and squeezed his wrist once.

The first thud landed in Lena's sternum like a second heartbeat. She counted strikes to keep from reaching for a radio that was no longer on her belt, counted until the numbers blurred and all she could hear was clay sealing wood.

A patrol cruiser idled on the road outside the fence. Two deputies stayed inside with the heat running. Public distrust sat there in plain view: state power watching a burial it had failed twice to protect.

At the edge of the plot, three Gideon Tract plaintiffs passed around affidavit forms on a clipboard, filling lines between hymns because filing deadlines did not care about ritual. One woman asked Mara if the freeze meant she could stop paying rent to the trust on land her grandmother had been born on. Mara gave the only true answer: "Not yet. But now they have to account for every dollar while we fight it."

The words scraped across Lena like gravel. Even here, with dirt still falling, the county had managed to put a payment schedule between the dead and the living.

Delay became the refrain of the morning.

When the grave was half filled, Ida turned and found Lena standing three rows back near the fence post with peeling white paint. Ida walked over slowly, brushing clay from her gloves.

"You look tired," Ida said.

"I'm tired," Lena said.

Ida's mouth tightened, then eased. "I came for him. Don't make this into forgiveness before it's forgiveness."

Lena rubbed grave mud from her palms into the seam of her coat and kept her hands at her sides so she would not reach for Ida. The words were honest, sharp, and deserved.

"I won't," she said.

Ida nodded once. "Good."

She started to turn away, then stopped. "I put soup in your freezer yesterday. Top shelf, behind the ice. Eat it before Friday or throw it out."

That practical busyness served as treaty language. It was the closest thing to a bridge Ida had offered since the night Lena leaked the docket, and Lena took it like a relic.

"Thank you," Lena said.

Ida walked back toward the front row without answering. The distance between them remained, but it was no longer empty.

Near noon, the grave was mounded and tamped. The new stone stood upright at last, set square and plumb, the full legal name carved deep enough to survive weather and indifference. Under it, the dates held two centuries in one line. Somebody had left a mason jar of river water at the base. Somebody else left cotton bolls tied with black thread.

Reverend Tate closed with a benediction about memory and labor. Nobody said closure or promised healing on schedule.

People drifted toward cars in small knots, talking about court calendars, childcare swaps, and who had room to host out-of-town cousins for the next hearing date. Grief had to share space with logistics. It always had.

Mara came to stand beside Lena after the crowd thinned. She finally opened her notebook and flipped to a blank page.

"First intake meeting is Monday. Brooks says two families need records work, and a tenant organizer wants us on a fraud complaint if we can travel."

"We start at nine with Brooks, pull chancery dockets by noon, and run intake calls after dinner," Lena said. "Thursday I do Greenville in one loop and check Beau at PT. We'll travel."

Mara angled the notebook toward her. "That's movement, not cash flow. Which line item buys gas before reimbursement clears?"

"I put fuel and copy costs on my card, file mileage same day, and chase late checks every morning at eight," Lena said. "We're not leaving those families waiting."

"Even if the county tries to starve us out?"

"Especially then."

Mara wrote that down as if it were an assignment, not a promise. "You sure you're ready?"

Lena looked at the stone, at Ruth guiding Malik toward the gate, at Ida standing alone near the wheat grass with her handkerchief pressed flat in her palm. Ready was the wrong word. Ready implied clean edges, and there were none left.

"I'm not ready," Lena said. "I'm available."

Mara's mouth twisted into something like approval. "Good enough."

They stood in silence while the wind moved through the iron fence and made it sing. Lena felt grief in pieces: her father's name in the docket, her mother's half-open door, the job she had lost, the people still waiting for money that should have been theirs before she was born. None of it resolved at the grave. That was not what graves did.

When she finally turned toward the road, clay sucked at her boots with each step. Behind her, Isaiah Bell's name held in stone under a cold Delta sky, the full name carved where everyone could see it, the wound still raw in every way the county tried to monetize.

At the gate Mara caught up and handed Lena a spare pen from her pocket, blue ink, chewed cap.

"Don't lose this one. Eight o'clock, tract ledgers first, then tenant calls before lunch."

Lena tucked the pen behind her ear and kept walking toward the Monday list.
