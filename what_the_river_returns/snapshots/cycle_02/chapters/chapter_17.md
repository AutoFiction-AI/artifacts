# Chapter 17

The public boat launch on Bell Creek sat low against the levee, a slab of cracked concrete slick with old algae and fish scales that never fully washed away. The river wind had teeth that morning. It came around the pilings and found the sweat under Lena's shirt anyway, left her skin cold and sticky at once.

She set Mara's second phone on the rusted hood of Beau's truck and watched the red battery icon climb one percent at a time from the power bank. The icon hit three percent, then four, then five. That was not enough for anything that mattered, then it was enough to ruin somebody's life if they let it run long enough.

At 5:07, Ruth had left a voicemail that lasted eleven seconds and moved the meeting up from noon: "Come early. Before office phones wake up." Then she'd hung up.

Mara stood with one sneaker heel hooked on the trailer hitch, camera case open at her feet, checking inputs with the same clipped efficiency she used in hostile interviews. Her close-cropped curls were damp at the scalp, sweat beading along the hairline above her temple. She kept swiping her sleeve across her forehead and never paused long enough to notice.

"Audio one is clean. Audio two is cleaner," Mara said. "If she whispers, we'll still get it."

"You talked to your editors?"

"Two in Jackson, one in New Orleans, one in D.C. They're awake, they're caffeinated, and they're waiting for me to say go."

Lena nodded. Her left hand kept finding the empty place where her badge used to sit clipped inside her belt. Habit did not care about termination papers.

Beau leaned against the truck bed a few yards away, wrist brace bright white against his sunburned arm. He had a thermos with masking tape around the middle that read NO SUGAR in black marker. He took a drink, made a face, and took another.

"If Hollis shows," he said, "he's gonna come loud."

"He always comes loud," Lena said.

"Yeah, but this is scared loud." Beau rolled his shoulder until it popped. "Different animal."

The launch road stayed empty long enough for the emptiness to feel arranged. Cicadas drilled from the willow trees along the ditch. A tug horn drifted up from downriver, long and flat, like somebody drawing a ruler across sheet metal.

When Ruth Bell finally appeared, she came in a faded burgundy Buick with a missing hubcap and one rear window taped with clear plastic. She parked crooked, engine running, and sat inside for a full minute with both hands on the steering wheel.

Lena watched her through the windshield. Ruth held one breath too long, let it out in two short pushes, then rolled each shoulder as if fitting herself to pain she already expected.

Ruth got out with a black garment bag folded over one arm and a grocery sack in the other hand. Funeral-home black, Lena thought first: low-heeled shoes, long sleeves despite the heat, cuffs clean and pressed. Then the details came in: cracked knuckles rubbed raw from sanitizer, one thumbnail bitten to the quick, and a pair of cheap reading glasses hanging from a chain she'd mended with floral wire.

She set the grocery sack down and nodded once at Lena, once at Mara, once at Beau. She did not offer a greeting or an apology for the wait.

"You said no uniforms," Ruth said.

"No uniforms," Lena said. "No sheriff's office. No one here can arrest you."

Ruth's mouth twitched at that. "That's supposed to make me breathe easier?"

"It's supposed to make the facts clear."

Mara stepped forward with the camera still at her side. "Before we start, I need consent on tape. This is voluntary. No immunity has been offered by me, by Lena, or by anybody pretending to stand behind us. You can stop at any point. If you want anything off record, you need to say that out loud before you say it."

Ruth looked at her hard. "You put me on your feed before I finish, I walk."

"I won't publish mid-statement unless someone tries to suppress it in real time," Mara said. "That's the deal I can honor."

Ruth gave one short nod. "Fine."

Lena felt her pulse in the back of her teeth. She pulled a folding chair out from the truck bed, set it beside the rusted guardrail, and sat opposite Ruth with the river to their left and the launch road behind them. It was spatially stupid and tactically stupid. There was no cover, and there were no exits that were not visible. But if this went to hell, everybody would see how it happened.

Mara checked frame, clipped a lav mic to Ruth's sleeve, clipped another to Lena's collar, and kept the boom low between them.

"State your name for the record," Mara said.

"Ruth Elaine Bell."

"Date?"

"October twenty-sixth." She looked at Lena. "Year, too, or y'all know where we're at?"

"Year too," Lena said.

"Two thousand twenty-six."

Mara glanced at Lena and got a nod. "Ruth, tell us why you asked for this meeting."

Ruth took the reading glasses off the chain and folded them, unfolded them, folded them again. "Because if I wait for your courtrooms to tell the truth, my grandson'll be old enough to shave before anybody hears it." She put the glasses in her lap. "I killed Hank Voss. I killed Lyle Fenn. I did not kill Ava Boyd. I did not kill Thomas Pike."

The words landed with less noise than Lena expected. There was no thunderclap and no cinematic pause. Lena heard only the sound of the river against the concrete lip and the click of Mara's recording light.

Lena kept her tone flat on purpose. "Tell it in order. Start with Voss."

Ruth stared out at the water. "You remember that smell in the gin office? Not cotton. Not machine grease. Formaldehyde under pine cleaner. He was trying to scrub a leak from the loading room."

Lena remembered. She had smelled it and filed it, then let Clay talk over it in the first briefing.

"Voss had your grandfather's acreage maps in a banker box with trust tabs," Ruth said. "Gideon Tract parcels in one stack, burial permits in another, and a red folder marked nuisance removals like we were hornets. He got drunk enough to show me. That's how men like him brag. They don't brag to each other. They brag to the woman they think has to wash the blood off everybody after."

Her fingers went to the chain of her glasses again, this time hard enough to leave dents in the skin.

"I told him my family had a deed older than his daddy's first beer," she said. "He laughed and said paper only matters if the county says it does. Then he called Isaiah Bell old compost and asked if we had room in our plot for one more bag of trouble."

Lena felt the old hot pressure in her chest, the one that had gotten her fired and might still get somebody killed. "How did you kill him?"

Ruth turned back. Politeness came off her words one layer at a time. "With what I had in my hands."

"Say it plain."

"A trocar. Stainless steel. Twelve-inch shaft, sharpened for drainage. We use it in prep when bodies bloat." She held her own wrist like she could still feel the angle. "I put it below his sternum while he was bent over that box. I pushed upward. He didn't scream first. He coughed, like he swallowed wrong. Then he screamed."

Ruth's thumb scraped her bitten nail until a thin thread of blood surfaced. "He swung a box cutter at me. I hit his jaw with the embalming pump wrench, left side. That's the blunt fracture in your report. He grabbed the trocar with his right hand and I stomped his fingers till they opened. That's those crushed knuckles. When he fell, the back of his head split on the pallet jack tine. I didn't need to add that one."

Mara did not look up from her monitor, but Lena saw the tendons in Mara's jaw tighten.

"What happened next?" Lena asked.

"He pulled back and tore himself worse. Blood came out dark because I'd gone in deep and high." Ruth swallowed, rubbed her thumb over the bitten nail. "I had Isaiah with me. Not all of him. Enough. I placed what I carried where Voss had to see it before he died. I wanted his last clean thought to be the man he helped erase."

Lena forced herself through procedure. "How did you access Isaiah's remains?"

"Morgue corridor. Night loading. Same route y'all found later, too late." Ruth's mouth flattened. "Y'all think corruption is a locked room. It's a hallway with people who know each other's coffee orders."

Lena should have challenged chain details right then. She should have asked for timestamps and vehicle descriptions. Instead she heard her own father telling a younger version of her that documents made things legal, not right, and she hated how cleanly that voice fit over Ruth's confession.

"Lyle Fenn," Lena said.

Ruth leaned back in the chair, eyes narrowed against the wind. "Fenn moved boxes. Voss made decisions, Fenn carried them and joked while he did it. The week before I killed him, he came through Bell and Sons with a county transfer slip and asked if we sold smaller body bags for parts." Her hand shook once, tiny and furious. "He said parts like he was ordering screws."

Beau shifted where he stood. Gravel cracked under his boot.

"I met him at the ramp," Ruth said. "Told him Voss left cash in a tackle box under the dock cleat. Greed brought him down there faster than grief ever brought him to church." Her eyes stayed on Lena now. "I used a garrote made from piano wire and two dowel handles. Funeral homes keep odd tools. You improvise more than folks think. He got both hands between the wire and his throat first. That's why his palms were cut up deep. I kneed the back of his leg, and when he dropped he cracked his head on the dock cleat and bit through his front teeth. I kept torque on the wire till I felt the hyoid give. He fought long enough to split my lip and piss himself. Then he dropped."

She touched the right corner of her mouth, as if checking for the scar.

"You staged Isaiah there, too," Lena said.

"I staged accusation there," Ruth said. "I put Isaiah's name where they could not step around it anymore."

"Name? There wasn't text on scene."

Ruth gave her a look that was almost pity. "Not with marker. With placement. Isaiah's right shoulder always sat higher. That old clavicle break never healed true. I set him so his shoulder faced the floodlights. Anybody from our side of town could've told you whose bones those were before your lab did."

Lena wrote that down in a notebook she no longer had legal authority to submit anywhere. Her pen left a groove in the paper deep enough to mark the next page.

"Why stop after two?" she asked.

"I didn't stop because I got gentle." Ruth's tone was dry iron. "I stopped because somebody started copying me wrong. Ava Boyd got a jar of tagged teeth. That wasn't me. I would've never touched Ava. She carried my cousin's eviction file for free and stayed late so old people could sign papers they could read." Ruth leaned forward. "And Thomas Pike died in a blackout with comms cut and private security jammed. That's a command operation, not a widow with a Buick and a funeral toolkit."

Mara finally lifted her eyes. "Who copied you?"

Ruth's silence lasted long enough that Lena could hear the phone charging on the hood vibrate from an incoming text.

"I know who moved evidence after me," Ruth said. "I don't know every hand on Ava and Pike. But I know whose breath I smelled in the morgue corridor the night your logs broke. Hollis Reddick. Spearmint dip and cinnamon cologne. He had county keys he wasn't supposed to have and laughed when he dropped a cooler lid."

"You're naming a deputy in a murder statement," Lena said. "You understand that."

"I understand better than you think." Ruth reached for the grocery sack and pulled out a quart jar wrapped in a towel. Inside were paper slips folded small and tied with blue thread. "These are copies of transfer tags I skimmed before I burned the originals. Dates, initials, route notes. I kept them in my freezer behind corn and two pounds of catfish because I figured your people wouldn't search next to food stamps." She held the jar out. "Take it."

Lena hesitated, not because of evidence purity but because of the weight of it. Then she took the jar. It was cold enough to sweat against her palm.

"Why come forward now?" Mara asked.

Ruth answered without looking at her. "Because Deshawn Lee is in a cage for something he didn't do, and because your sheriff thinks dead Black names are props he can set where they help him." She exhaled through her nose. "And because if I disappear tonight, I want my words out where my boy can find them when he's old enough to ask what I did and why."

Lena heard tires on gravel before she saw the dust.

Beau straightened and muttered, "Here we go."

Two SUVs came down the launch road too fast and braked hard enough that one fishtailed near the ditch. Doors opened before engines died.

Hollis Reddick got out smiling like somebody walking into his own cookout. He wore a tan tactical vest over a black polo, sleeves tight against his forearms, and mirrored sunglasses even though the sun was still behind cloud. A deputy Lena did not know came around the passenger side with a legal pad in one hand and a cheap silver cross necklace she kept tucking back inside her collar every few seconds.

Hollis took in the camera, the chairs, Ruth, the jar in Lena's hand. Smile stayed. Eyes changed.

"Morning, ladies," he called. "Y'all picked a pretty spot for obstruction."

Mara did not move. "You're interrupting a voluntary interview. Do you have a warrant?"

"I got a homicide scene and unauthorized evidence handling in plain view." Hollis kept walking. "That's better than a warrant till I say otherwise."

Lena stood, every tendon in her back tightening at once. She set the jar behind her leg where Hollis could not reach it without touching her first.

"You don't have arrest authority over me anymore," she said. "You sure as hell don't have seizure authority over her equipment without paper."

Hollis stopped three feet away, close enough that she could smell coffee gone sour on his breath under mint. "Lena, Lena, Lena. You got fired and started thinking that made you brave. It made you unemployed. Hand me the recorder."

Behind him, the deputy with the cross looked sick and stubborn at once.

Mara's hand went to the phone on the hood, unplugged it, and tapped twice without speaking.

The red LIVE banner bloomed across her screen.

"Mercer County, October twenty-sixth, 2026, 8:14 a.m.," Mara said into the lens with sudden broadcast calm. "I'm Mara Quinn. This stream is documenting an active attempt by Deputy Hollis Reddick to seize recorded testimony without warrant. Mirror uploads are now running to four independent news desks and two legal repositories."

Hollis's head snapped toward her. "Turn that off."

"Can't," Mara said. "It's not one copy anymore."

The deputy's eyes kept snagging on Mara's phone. Then she stared at the SUV like she could climb back in and pick a different day. Lena filed it fast: fear with conscience still attached, maybe still reachable.

Ruth stayed seated through all of it, hands folded over the garment bag on her lap. She looked at Hollis the way people looked at storms that had already taken their roof once.

"You remember me?" Ruth asked him.

Hollis laughed, too loud. "Everybody remembers you, Miss Bell."

"You don't. You remember uniforms and rank patches." Ruth lifted her chin. "I embalmed your uncle Junior after he drank drain cleaner in his shed. You cried in my prep room and asked me to close his mouth right so your mama wouldn't see his tongue."

The smile slipped off Hollis's face like it had been painted there wet.

Lena felt a mean flicker of satisfaction and tamped it down. Satisfaction made people sloppy.

"Watch your mouth," he said.

"No," Ruth said. "You watch yours."

Mara angled the phone so all three of them were in frame.

"Deputy Reddick," she said, "state legal basis for attempted seizure. On record."

"You don't run this county."

"That's not a legal basis."

"You're interfering with an active--"

"With what active case number?" Mara cut in. "Give the number."

Hollis stepped toward her and Lena moved with him, forearm up, not striking, just occupying the space where his chest had been headed. Her pulse hammered so hard she tasted pennies; one bad angle and he'd call it assault.

"Don't touch her," Lena said.

"Get out of my way."

"Get your paper."

His hand flashed toward the phone anyway. He caught only air because Mara had already shifted behind the truck bed, keeping the camera up as she moved.

"Viewers are at twelve thousand and climbing," Mara said to the stream, breathing faster now. "If this cuts, backups continue."

"Mara," Hollis snapped. "You're going to regret this."

"Good. Put that in full sentence for your disciplinary file."

The deputy with the cross suddenly spoke. Her words came thin but clear. "Sir, dispatch is calling. Repeatedly."

Hollis did not turn. "Tell dispatch I'm handling it."

She did not move. Her thumb kept rubbing the edge of her legal pad until the top sheet tore. "Sir, they're saying state office has seen the stream."

The phrase hit Lena like a flare in fog. Hollis heard it too.

Now he turned.

In that half-second, Ruth stood and took two steps forward with more steadiness than Lena had seen from her all morning.

"You tell Clay this, too," Ruth said to Hollis, low enough that Lena had to lean to catch it. "I killed two men who helped steal my people in broad daylight with my own hands. I own that. I did not kill Ava Boyd, and I did not kill Thomas Pike. If y'all hang those on me to wash yourselves, I will name every cooler, every key, every driver, and every church usher's nephew who touched those bodies."

Mara swung the camera from Hollis to Ruth and back again. "That statement is now public."

Hollis's jaw clenched so hard a vein jumped at his temple. He looked at Lena with naked hatred and something newer under it, the first clear edge of panic.

"You think this saves you?" he said.

"No," Lena said. "It stops being yours."

He held her gaze another second, then stepped back and jerked his chin at the deputy. "We're done here."

"Without a warrant," Mara said for the stream.

"Shut it down, Quinn."

"No."

He got into the SUV hard enough to rattle the whole frame, slammed the door, and spun gravel over the ramp as he pulled out.

The deputy lingered by the second vehicle for a breath. She kept her gaze on the gravel until Mara's still-live camera pinned her. Only then did she meet Lena's eyes for one hard beat.

"My mama says y'all are all going to hell," she said, almost apologetic. "I think she's wrong about at least one of you."

Then she climbed in and left.

Mara kept filming until both SUVs disappeared behind the levee bend. She lowered the phone only when her arm started to shake.

"Mirrors confirm upload complete," she said. The broadcast polish had dropped out; her throat sounded scraped raw. "Raw plus redundancy."

Beau let out a laugh that sounded nothing like humor. "Well. Sheriff's about to have a bad breakfast."

Lena's knees went watery with delayed adrenaline. She sat on the guardrail and held the jar with both hands so she would not reach for a gun she no longer carried.

Ruth picked up her grocery sack and garment bag. "You got what you needed."

"Not yet," Lena said. "Now we need it to stick."

"Nothing sticks in this county unless it sticks somewhere else first." Ruth nodded toward Mara's phone. "You did that part."

She started toward her Buick.

"Ruth," Lena called. "If we can arrange counsel, federal proffer, protections--"

Ruth looked back over her shoulder. The wind flattened her sleeves against her arms and showed how thin she had gotten.

"Protection is for people with money and friends in offices," she said. "I got freezer tags and a grandson who still sleeps with his shoes on because he thinks they come faster at night. Do what you can with that."

She got in her car and drove away without signaling.

---

By ten-thirty they were in the back booth at Dot's Diner off Highway 49, where the air conditioner coughed like an old smoker and the coffee tasted like burnt pennies. Mara had her laptop open beside a plate of eggs gone cold. Beau sat at the counter with his brace propped on a folded napkin, arguing softly with Dot over whether raisin toast counted as dessert.

Lena watched the livestream replay in one corner of Mara's screen while her other hand copied tag numbers from Ruth's jar into a legal pad. The paper slips smelled faintly of freezer frost and fish.

"These initials," Lena said. "H.R., C.R., D.W. Route notes: morgue south dock, county crematory, river ramp. Dates line up with the first two scenes and two nights before Ava."

"And this one," Mara said, tapping the screen. "Look at the timestamp. Hollis's SUV enters the launch road exactly four minutes after Ruth says his name. Either he's psychic or somebody fed him location."

"Could've had us tailed from my house."

"You're not at your house."

"Could've tailed you, then." Lena looked up. "You got anything on your feed from the comments worth preserving?"

"Three eyewitness threads. Two trash. One useful." Mara scrolled. "A deckhand from a fuel barge says he saw a county-marked cooler transfer at the morgue south dock in late August, around 2 a.m., driver in tactical vest. He posted a blurry still."

Lena felt the room narrow and sharpen. "Save his handle. Ask for raw."

"Already did."

Mara's phone buzzed. She checked it, swore once under her breath, and switched to speaker.

"Quinn," she said.

A woman's voice came through, dry and quick. "Assistant U.S. Attorney Celeste Nguyen. Civil Rights and Violent Crime intake. I'm the unlucky person with the phone this morning. Is Lena Waller with you?"

"She's here," Mara said. "You're on speaker."

"Detective Waller--"

"Former detective," Lena said.

"Noted. I need chain details before anybody in my office touches this for charging recommendation."

Nguyen sounded like she had already had too much coffee and still did not trust her own wakefulness. In the background Lena could hear a printer running nonstop.

"You can have every file with metadata," Mara said. "You can also have the full stream archive, four mirror receipts, and raw local copy."

"Good. Here's where we are," Nguyen said. "We can open parallel prosecution tracks this afternoon if corroboration clears minimal threshold. Track one: admitted homicides of Voss and Fenn tied to Ms. Bell's statement and any physical support you provide. Track two: later homicides, evidence tampering, civil-rights deprivation, potential conspiracy by officials. I'm not merging those until I can prove where the signatures diverge."

Lena leaned forward. "That's what we've been trying to force for weeks."

"Then help me force it clean." Nguyen paused, paper shuffling on her end. "Do you have independent evidence that Ms. Bell's method and access fit the first two scenes better than the later two?"

"Yes," Lena said. "First two scenes had full-body staging behavior centered on Isaiah's remains, with transport patterns through morgue night loading and river-ramp drop. Ava Boyd scene shifted to tagged teeth jar and no full-body recurrence. Thomas Pike scene required timed blackout and private-security failure at a high-profile gala. Different logistics, different intent profile, different operational scale."

She heard herself stacking terms like bricks and did not stop.

"Ms. Bell used mortuary tools and opportunistic proximity. Later operator used command access and communication disruption."

Nguyen gave a short approving hum. "Send that in writing. Not polished, just precise."

Mara said, "You'll also get transfer-tag copies Ruth kept, plus Hollis's on-stream attempted seizure."

"I saw it." Nguyen's voice flattened. "Half the country saw it. Deputies don't get to freelance constitutional law on livestream and keep careers." A beat. "One more thing. Public defender in your county filed emergency motion citing this disclosure and prior Brady concerns in Lee detention. State judge is hearing it now."

Lena closed her eyes for one second. It was not relief yet. It would not be relief until wheels moved.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't thank me. Send files." Nguyen disconnected.

Beau slid into the booth with three fresh coffees and one plate of toast, ignored Dot's glare when he set crumbs near Mara's keyboard.

"So?" he asked.

"Federal intake's open, but it isn't locked," Mara said.

Beau whistled low. "Clay's gonna call that a witch hunt."

"Clay can call it weather," Lena said. "It still rains."

Mara's laptop chimed. She opened a new message, read three lines, and swore hard enough that Dot glanced over.

"Nguyen just got pushback from county counsel," Mara said. "They're claiming the stream was edited and the seizure threat happened off-camera. Mirror file starts at eight-fifteen-zero-nine, not eight-fourteen."

Lena's stomach dropped. "The first minute buffered when your phone was charging."

"Yeah, and the mirror picked up after buffer release." Mara dragged a hand over her mouth. "If I can't give them independent timestamp corroboration in the next hour, Public Integrity defers and this whole thing gets pitched as internet theater."

Beau set his coffee down so fast it sloshed. "Truck dashcam."

Lena looked at him. "You had dashcam running?"

"Insurance rider after my wrist break. Front glass catches the launch road and audio inside ten feet of the hood." He shoved his keys toward Mara. "Card's in the glove box under my registration."

Rain started tapping the diner windows in quick hard dots as Lena and Beau pushed out the side door, crossed the lot, and yanked open the truck. The glove box dumped napkins, old summons copies, and a flashlight onto Lena's boots before Beau found the microSD in a cracked plastic case.

Back in the booth, Mara jammed the card into an adapter with shaking fingers. The clip opened on timestamp 08:13:42. Hollis's SUV fishtailed into frame at 08:14:19. At 08:14:31 his voice came through clear enough to transcript: *Hand me the recorder.*

"Export raw, hash it, send to Nguyen and Turner," Lena said.

"Already exporting." Mara clicked through menus at speed. "And the barge deckhand finally sent his original still with EXIF and a voice note naming the cooler transfer date. I'm attaching both."

Her phone buzzed with a number she did not recognize. She answered anyway.

"Lena Waller."

"Public Defender Carla Jean Mays." The woman sounded breathless and furious and weirdly delighted all at once. "I'm on the courthouse steps and my heel is broken because this building hates women, but Deshawn Lee is walking out in eight minutes unless somebody kidnaps the clerk. Judge cited unreliable probable cause and pending federal review."

Lena leaned back against the vinyl seat and let the cracked plastic press into her shoulder blades.

"He's out?" she asked, needing the verb twice.

"He's out. And he wants his mama's sweet tea and one hour where nobody with a badge says his name." Mays cleared her throat. "You did not hear this from me, but your stream made the room very honest very fast."

"Thank you," Lena said.

"Buy better shoes than mine," Mays said, and hung up.

For a moment none of them spoke. Dot shouted an order to the cook. Somebody laughed too loud at the register. The tapping on the diner windows thickened into a full sheet that turned the parking lot silver.

Beau rubbed his bad wrist and looked out at the storm. "Good news feels fake now."

"Because it's not the end," Mara said.

"No," Lena said. "But it's a door."

Mara rotated the laptop so Lena could see the upload queue. File names crawled upward one by one: `RUTH_BELL_FULL_STATEMENT_RAW_A1`, `RIVER_LAUNCH_STREAM_ARCHIVE`, `TRANSFER_TAG_PHOTOS_SET_B`, `CHAIN_MEMO_LW_DRAFT`.

Another line appeared in all caps: `BEAU_DASHCAM_0813_RAW_HASHED`. Then another: `BARGE_STILL_ORIGINAL_EXIF_AUDIO_WITNESS`.

"I've got three legal drops and two newsroom drops done," Mara said. "Fourth legal drop in progress."

"Send one to Bell counsel too," Lena said. "If this narrows to pure criminal and they bury restitution, we lost half the point."

"Already sent." Mara stabbed at the eggs with her fork and finally took a bite. "They're cold as hell."

"You're still eating them," Beau said.

"I grew up in newsroom motels and state-fair parking lots. I don't need hot food to function."

His mouth twitched. "That's the saddest flex I've heard this year."

Mara gave him a look. "Your thermos label says NO SUGAR in duct tape."

"Doctor's orders."

"That wasn't my point."

Lena let their bickering run for a few seconds because the sound of it gave her nervous system something ordinary to hold. Then she opened her notebook and wrote in block letters:

`DO NOT LET TRACK ONE BECOME THE WHOLE STORY.`

Under it she wrote:

`RUTH OWNED TWO. OTHERS OWN THE REST.`

Rain hammered harder. The diner lights flickered once, twice, then steadied.

Her phone buzzed again. This time the caller ID read UNKNOWN FEDERAL.

She answered and put it on speaker.

"Ms. Waller?" a man's voice asked.

"Yes."

"Malik Turner, Department of Justice, Public Integrity. I've got Ms. Nguyen in the room with me, and we have your initial file packet."

In the background someone coughed, and another voice asked for page nine.

"We're making this formal," Turner said. He sounded younger than Lena expected, and his cadence had the careful stutter of somebody who slowed himself on purpose when he got angry. "As of 11:42 a.m. Central, we're opening dual-track federal prosecution files. One track isolates the Voss-Fenn homicides and Ms. Bell's admissions. Second track covers Ava Boyd, Thomas Pike, and related obstruction and civil-rights conduct. Local agencies are instructed to preserve, not seize, any media-origin files."

Mara mouthed, *holy shit,* but kept silent.

Turner continued, "We need sworn affidavits by end of day. Can you do that?"

"Yes," Lena said.

"Can Ms. Quinn provide technical attestation for recording integrity?"

"Yes," Mara said.

"Can Detective Broussard--"

"Detective Broussard," Beau said from the counter, loud enough to carry.

A brief pause on the line, then Turner said, "Can Mr. Broussard attest to observed intimidation conduct at the launch?"

"Damn right I can," Beau said.

"Good. Last point: for your safety, do not return to isolated locations alone. We have reason to believe suppression efforts are active and unsophisticated."

"Unsophisticated," Lena repeated.

"People who panic with badges are rarely subtle," Turner said. "You'll receive contact protocols within the hour."

The line clicked dead.

Lena stared at the blank phone screen until Mara touched her wrist.

"You still here?" Mara asked.

"Yeah." Lena inhaled slowly, felt coffee and rain and fryer grease in the back of her throat. "Yeah, I'm here."

She thought of Ruth's hands on the steering wheel. Of Hollis trying to grab a camera he had already lost. Of Deshawn counting cinder blocks in a cell while the county invented him into a murderer.

Nothing had become clean. Ruth had stabbed one man under the sternum and strangled another with piano wire and called it accusation. Hollis had not been cuffed on camera. Clay still held a sheriff's office full of people who answered to his voice before they answered to law.

But the record had escaped.

It lived now in phones on kitchen counters, in cloud servers out of state, in federal inboxes, in clipped local accents replaying over bad diner speakers, in a deputy's face when she decided not to lie for her boss on live video.

Lena stood, grabbed her pen, and started drafting the affidavit on Dot's paper placemat because she did not trust memory to stay generous this long.

Outside, the rain came down hard enough to erase the parking lot lines, and for the first time in weeks she did not feel like erasure was winning.
