# Chapter 12

By the time Lena found Beau's room again, the hospital had folded into its night smell: bleach riding over fryer grease from the cafeteria, old coffee burnt to tar in a pot nobody remembered to empty, hand sanitizer sharp enough to sting the back of her throat. Fluorescent tubes hummed above her with that thin insect whine county buildings always had, one bulb near the nurses' station flickering in a slow pulse that made everybody's skin look bad. Her knees felt hollow. She had slept ninety minutes in a plastic chair and woke with the cuff of her jacket imprinted across her cheek.
The thermal strip Rao had pressed into her hand in the corridor was still folded behind her sequence list, the numbers already smudging at one edge.

Beau sat propped in bed with his right forearm wrapped hard white from knuckles to elbow, the cast already smudged where he'd bumped it against a rail. Someone had taped his call button to the blanket because his pain meds made him fumble. The TV was on mute with closed captions sprinting under footage of somebody else's fire. He looked at Lena, looked away, and used his good hand to lift a paper cup of melting ice chips. He missed his mouth the first time and cursed under his breath like he was talking to a stubborn bolt.

"You took your shot," he said. "You got us there before dawn and got us exactly what? A federal boot in our throat and my wrist cracked like kindling."

Lena stayed by the door, hand still on the metal push bar as if she might need to leave fast. "I pushed the entry because we had a clean weather window and no guarantee the ledgers would survive another day. If we'd waited for--"

"Don't hand me the memo version." He turned his head then, eyes red not from crying, from no sleep and morphine and rage held too long. "I was there, Len. I heard that lock give. I smelled wet paper and rat shit and motor oil and I thought, for five seconds, we finally had 'em. Then they rolled in with a seizure order stamped pretty as a wedding invitation."

She crossed to the sink and set down the coffee she'd brought him, black with two sugars because she remembered and because remembering felt like proof she had not broken everything yet. Her voice came out in the flat on-record register she used when she was trying not to shake. "I know what happened. I know what it cost you."

"No." He shifted, winced, and pressed his shoulder into the bed frame until the pain passed. "You know the report version. You don't know what that pop sounded like in my arm. You don't know the second after, when I thought, stupidly, not my hand, not my right hand, not now."

That landed clean. It punched through the procedural script she had been hiding inside since dawn. Beau wrote everything by hand before he typed it. He carried a carpenter pencil behind his ear half the shift because his father had done the same on shrimp boats, and he never trusted pens when his palms got slick. A broken wrist for him was not just pain. It was competence gone unreliable.

Lena pulled the visitor chair closer and sat. The plastic squealed on the tile. "You're right," she said. "I don't know that sound. I know the sound of choosing wrong in front of people I need alive. That's what I've got."

He watched her for a long moment, jaw grinding through invisible gristle. The monitor beside him ticked out his pulse in patient, indifferent beeps. "I almost asked Clay to move me off this case," he said.

She felt the blood leave her face before she could stop it. "You should, if that's what keeps you standing."

"I said I almost did." His mouth twitched, not quite a smile, more a tired surrender to the fact that he still cared. "I didn't because I kept seeing one thing I should've said sooner."

The door eased open before he could continue. Ida Waller came in carrying a foam clamshell and a church bulletin folded into a fan. She wore a navy dress with a thin white collar and sensible shoes polished enough to catch the hallway light. Her fingers, knuckles enlarged by arthritis, worked the bulletin without looking at it, a steady back-and-forth that made a soft papery breeze.

"You two done biting chunks out of each other?" she asked. She set the clamshell on Beau's tray and tapped it twice. "Catfish. No pepper flakes. Last time one red flake had you at the altar."

Beau snorted despite himself. "Ms. Ida, I didn't cry."

"Baby, if your eyes water and your nose runs at the same time, that's crying with extra steps." She turned to Lena, gave her one quick scan that took in the wrinkled shirt, the stale coffee smell, the tremor in her left hand. "Eat something that didn't come from a vending coil. I'm going downstairs for ice."

She left before Lena could argue. Ida's practicality under stress had always passed for ordinary errands until you noticed every errand kept somebody from falling apart.

When the door shut, Beau exhaled through his teeth and nodded toward the hallway. "Your mama's the only person in this building who scares me more than federal paper."

"She's got range," Lena said.

That earned the smallest actual smile. Then it was gone. Beau leaned forward a fraction. "Night before the icehouse, I was at the morgue loading bay. It wasn't official. I went to grab the spare evidence camera 'cause ours was eating batteries. Around one-thirty, Hollis came through in a county refrigerated van with lights off. He wasn't parked at intake. He backed into the side dock where dietary gets produce."

Lena straightened. Fatigue dragged at her, waterlogged and stubborn, but this cut through. "Who was with him?"

"One driver I didn't know. Ball cap pulled low. Could've been anybody, could've been nobody. Hollis had a yellow pallet jack and one of those gray archival totes with the red latch. I remember because he clipped the dock plate and cursed the same way he does at traffic stops, with the old assumption the world owed him smoother pavement."

"You logged it?"

"I logged the van plate in my field notebook and told myself I'd run it after shift." He looked at his cast. "Then all hell kept scheduling itself."

Lena had seen Hollis in enough hallways to know his swagger voice and his friendly-deputy smile. She had not pictured him sweating behind a dark dock with a pallet jack. That image changed the geometry of the case: this was not abstract corruption but bodies and boxes moved by men with cousins in the right offices.

The door opened again, quick this time. Dr. Naveen Rao stepped in with a clipboard under one arm and a pair of reading glasses tucked into the collar of his scrub top. His hair had a flattened streak on one side from where he'd slept against a wall, and he was peeling the label off a water bottle in thin spirals while he talked, an unconscious habit when his brain outran his mouth.

"Broussard, your discharge is tomorrow if you stop trying to prove machismo through noncompliance." He glanced at Lena. "Detective. I was looking for you anyway."

He slid a folded glove wrapper from his pocket and opened it on the tray table. Inside, written in marker, was a string of letters and numbers: TR-44-1187-P.

"Serial from the federal crate tag," Rao said. "I gave it to you in the corridor, but you stared through me for a second, so here. I've checked twice against what I saw before they boxed everything."

Lena read it once, then again, letting each character settle. "You remember this clean?"

"I remember what I can defend under oath and what I can only defend to myself." He met her eyes. "This one is the second category, so handle accordingly."

"Thank you," she said.

Rao tucked the peeled plastic strip into his pocket instead of dropping it on the floor, nodded to Beau, and left with the efficient irritation of a man who trusted very few institutions and still showed up to work inside one.

Beau tapped the glove wrapper with one blunt fingertip. "That's your breadcrumb."

"One breadcrumb and no admissible loaf," Lena said.

"Then we bake ugly." He settled back, grimacing as the cast thudded the bed rail. "You still got Mara's photo dump from probate? The ones from that moldy room where the fan kept rattling toward death?"

She still had it: hundreds of rushed images from chapter-seven trespass, including file spines, shelf labels, half-legible docket tabs, and accidental corners of rooms they had not meant to capture. They were probably useless by courtroom standards, but useless was not the same as empty.

Lena stood. Her legs carried the bite of an all-night march on levee gravel. "Sleep," she told him.

"Can't with this arm." He raised the cast a little. "But I can think. Call me before you move on any live lead, not after, and don't do that thing where you decide to carry all the weight by yourself and call it strategy. If you freelance another entry, I'm off this case the minute this cast comes off."

There was no clean apology large enough for what she had broken, but there was this: he was still in it with her on terms she did not control. Hurt did not erase allegiance. It changed its texture and set hard edges around it.

"I'll call before I move on anything," she said.

Ida returned as Lena reached the door, balancing a cup of chipped ice and two contraband packets of hot sauce in her palm. "Take one," Ida said, holding out a packet. "For your purse. Emergency medicine."

Lena took it, because refusing gifts from her mother at moments like this counted as a kind of blasphemy.

Outside, the hallway lights buzzed. Somewhere down the corridor a child cried in short, furious bursts and a nurse murmured a song Lena recognized from Sunday service. She pressed the glove wrapper flat against her thigh and walked faster.

***

By midnight, the courthouse annex had gone quiet except for the old HVAC knocking in the vents and a storm beginning to drag its fingernails across the windows. Lena used her reinstated badge to get into Records Room C, then wedged the door with a busted law reporter because the lock liked to seize when humidity climbed. The room smelled like mildew, toner, and old glue. Two metal fans pushed warm air in opposite directions and did not cool anything.

At 11:38 p.m., Mara had pushed an encrypted folder to Lena's burner drive and sent one text in all caps: NOT COMING IN. CLAY'S PEOPLE ARE PARKED OUTSIDE MY BUILDING. I'M ON AUDIO.

Now Lena propped her phone against a stack of probate digests and opened a three-way call. Beau's voice came in from his apartment, a little slurred from pain meds but steady. Mara joined through a wired headset from somewhere with an ice machine rattling every few minutes.

"You look like a bar fight with paperwork," Mara said through the speaker.

"You sound judgmental for somebody hiding behind motel coffee."

"Fair. I sorted the probate photos by timestamp and focal length so we can separate intentional shots from accidental background capture. If the serial appears, odds are it's in a blur."

"I made a list of every place Hollis could've staged that tote between dock and freezer," Beau said. "You want it now or after y'all stop insulting each other's nutrition choices?"

"Now," Lena and Mara said at the same time.

They worked in layers. Lena started with the obvious photos, including shelf signs, binder labels, and banker boxes with probate stamps. Then she worked the throwaways, including blurred corners, floor tiles, door hinges, and fluorescent reflections in glass. She keyed TR-44-1187-P into the annex inventory terminal and got nothing but a dead-end error from a database that treated older records like rumors. She moved to manual index books and turned pages until paper dust coated her fingertips gray.

At 12:47 a.m., her vision doubled for three breaths. She blinked hard, pinched the bridge of her nose, and almost marked the wrong line: TR-44-1178-F, juvenile tax photos, irrelevant. Exhaustion did what exhaustion always did, sanding precision into guesswork.

She snapped a photo of the index page and dropped it in the call thread. Mara answered in four seconds. "Wrong suffix. You skipped a row."

"I saw it," Lena lied.

"No, you hoped it," Mara said. Her tone stayed dry, not cruel. "Drink water before you invent evidence."

Lena obeyed. The tap in Records Room C ran hot before it ran cold, and the water tasted faintly metallic. She drank anyway.

At 1:12 a.m., Beau spoke up from the speaker. "Quit digging in evidence logs. Pull probate procurement instead. Those gray totes are clerk furniture, not sheriff gear. Different bucket of money, different paper trail."

That was the workman mind she trusted: start with the object, ask who paid for it, and follow the hand that signed. Mara pulled county purchasing PDFs she had scraped months ago for an unrelated story about mold remediation, then walked Lena through the file path over the call. Lena opened a 2019 invoice bundle for thirty archival transport crates with gray polymer, red latches, and internal foam rails. The delivery location was Probate Photo Archive, sub-basement east wing, and the asset tag prefix was TR-44.

"There," Mara said. "Invoice language says trust records, not transport. That's a naming choice, not a typo."

Lena felt a small sharp click in her chest, that mechanical certainty when pieces finally seated. She pulled up the chapter-seven photo batch from the night archive break-in and filtered for red color dominance. Image 7-143 opened with a blurry shelf, an out-of-focus stool, and the corner of a gray tote. She zoomed until the pixels broke and the world turned blocky. The tag came into focus one character at a time: TR-44-1187-P.

The number matched, and the tote matched. Lena had seen it months before federal agents ever laid a glove on the icehouse boxes.

"Cross-match holds," Mara said softly, as if loud speech might spook the fact back into uncertainty. "Timestamp, tag prefix, unit number. Same crate."

"Don't give me newsroom poetry," Beau said. "Say it so a tired deputy can't miss it."

Lena answered. "The crate the feds seized started life in Probate Photo Archive inventory on the trust records line, not sheriff evidence issue. Somebody moved trust-archive material through morgue logistics."

A beat of silence held across all three lines. Rain thickened against the annex windows. Somewhere down the hall, a pipe knocked twice.

"Which somebody?" Beau asked.

Lena opened another folder and spread printed copies across the table, including partial warrant returns from chapter nine, disbursement schedules with half the names redacted, bank routing slips tied to shell entities, and one ugly photocopy of a trust governance amendment where the toner had blown out the margins. She smoothed the page flat with both palms.

"Read the signature block," Mara said.

Lena had read it before and filed it under later, after they recovered cleaner originals. Later had burned down with the seizure. She read it again now, slower:

Acting Disbursement Authority - Thomas Pike.

Judge Eleanor Pike retained oversight, but routine release authority for quarterly trust transfers sat with her son. Thomas approved payout timing. Thomas approved recipient bundles. Thomas touched the valve.

In this case, people who touched valves ended up dead, framed, or useful.

Her phone buzzed. Ida's name lit the screen. Lena answered on the first ring.

"You still in that paper cave?" Ida asked.

"Yeah."

"Then listen while I drive. Thomas Pike called your father every year before harvest gala season because he couldn't read people and your father could. He wanted to know who was angry enough to embarrass him in public." Ida's turn signal clicked in the background, steady as a metronome. "Men like that don't fear bullets first. They fear witnesses."

"How do you know it was Thomas on those calls?"

"Because he always introduced himself full name like a bank lawyer, even to people who knew his diaper size." A breath, then softer: "Baby, when rich boys start calling old deputies for crowd advice, something ugly is already in motion."

The line clicked off before Lena could ask more. Ida had never liked lingering at the edge of confession.

"Your mom just gave us motive behavior and event timing," Mara said.

"She gave us a pattern," Lena said. "Don't print it."

"I know."

Lena did not treat Ida's call as proof. She opened the decoded pages from Deputy Waller's notebook and found three entries from prior gala weeks that used the same shorthand: TP CALL / CROWD READ / 0199. She ran 0199 through the county contact directory and got the Pike Foundation executive office line assigned to Thomas's desk.

Then she opened the gala operations packet from the chapter-nine warrant return. The run-of-show put Thomas onstage for the redevelopment speech at 9:32 p.m., then moving through Service Corridor B to a private board room for disbursement signatures with two witnesses and one family lawyer. The security annex showed one private guard on that corridor, no metal wand, and a blind camera angle by the breaker closet where catering carts stacked before dessert service.

The next scheduled trust release sat forty-nine hours out, attached to gala disbursements and donor pledges. Three supports sat in front of her now: trust authority paperwork, historical call-pattern logs, and a predictable movement corridor with weak screening.

She wrote on a yellow pad in block letters:

THOMAS PIKE = CURRENT TRUST VALVE

NEXT EXPOSURE WINDOW: 49 HOURS

LOCATION STACK: HOUSE / COURTHOUSE / FOUNDATION HALL

Beau whistled low over speaker. "Hell. That's not a suspect tree; that's a deer stand aimed at one man's ribs."

"Yeah," Lena said. Her stomach cramped with hunger and adrenaline and too much coffee gone acidic. "If we're right, Thomas Pike is the body at risk. The attack lane is Service Corridor B between the ballroom and board room when he steps offstage to sign. One person can jam a catering cart, shove him into that blind spot, and put a blade under his ribs before anybody in formalwear hears him hit tile. If we miss that window, he dies in that hallway, command locks the building for optics, and whoever set it up gets clean minutes to strip signature packets or stage the next lie."

"Then put a source on the next move," Mara asked over the speaker. "Do we warn Pike tonight, or are we documenting why we waited?"

Lena considered Clay, his polished concern voice, and the way he could turn any warning into a press asset by dawn. She considered Eleanor Pike's legal armor and the county's appetite for spectacle. She considered Beau in a cast because she had wanted one more hour on a flooded floor.

"We'll notify command per protocol," she said. "Timestamp it clean. Then we build our own prevention map before they slow-walk this into a press line."

"You're assuming bad faith as a baseline," Mara said. "What are you hanging that on besides pattern memory?"

"Their pattern," Lena said. "They call it sensitive, schedule a meeting, and run the clock out."

Beau cut in, rough with fatigue. "Len, this isn't a permit pull. Give notice so they can't say you hid intel, then put bodies where bodies need to be."

There it was again: not obstacle but ally, practical and wounded and still pulling toward the work.

Lena gathered the documents into three piles, including what could survive court, what could only guide strategy, and what could get them all indicted if it left the room. She labeled each with black marker and the date. Outside, thunder rolled over the levee and rattled the annex glass in its frame.

At 2:03 a.m., she called the sheriff's duty line and logged a threat assessment tied to Thomas Pike's trust authority and forthcoming disbursement schedule. She kept her voice measured, gave no flourish, and asked for an immediate protective planning conference at first light. The deputy taking the call sounded nineteen and terrified of spelling anything wrong.

When she hung up, she took the hot-sauce packet Ida had pressed into her hand and tore it with her teeth, squeezing a stripe onto a stale pecan roll.

"That is revolting," Mara said through the speaker.

"It's calories," Lena said, chewing.

"It's a hate crime against pastry."

"Put it in your notes."

"Already did," Mara said.

When the call finally dropped, Lena squared the three labeled piles at the table edge for the first-light conference and kept working until the annex windows went dishwater gray.

Lena taped the yellow pad page to the wall above the printed sheets Mara had sent, drew a box around 49 HOURS, and underlined it until the pen tore paper.

The case they could prove in court was still broken open on the floor somewhere between county pride and federal jurisdiction. The case they could act on was suddenly plain: a man with a signature, a schedule everyone knew, and a county that kept pretending violence arrived like weather instead of paperwork.

She looked once at the serial number on the glove wrapper before sliding it into an evidence sleeve marked OFF-RECORD LEAD. Then she set a new legal pad on top of the old one and started building the protection grid for Thomas Pike's house, courthouse route, and gala ingress, line by line, while the storm leaned harder against Mercer County and morning crept in anyway.
