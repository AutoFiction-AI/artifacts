# Chapter 6

By noon the next day the house had gone damp as a lung. The swamp cooler in Lena's window rattled and spat warm air that smelled faintly of bleach from the rag she had tied over the filter. She sat at the table with her father's notebook open to the index, legal pads spread under her elbows, and a stack of county request forms curling at the corners from sweat. Suspension left her with time and no badge, which turned out to be worse than no time at all.

She had called the chancery office at 8:04, 8:37, and 9:11. At 9:12 a clerk named Bernice answered with a voice like dry paper and told Lena every Gideon Tract parcel file now carried an AUD-92 hold code: no one could pull transfer books older than twenty years without a signed owner-release form and a records-supervisor countersignature.

"They're public land records," Lena had said. "I'm requesting books, not juvenile files."

"Ma'am, I'm reading what they faxed us."

"Who faxed it?"

"Admin Directive 14-B addendum from the sheriff's office," Bernice said. "That's all I've got."

Lena drove to the courthouse annex anyway. The parking lot shimmered. A protest sign from the weekend had blown against the chain-link fence and bleached to a pink ghost of letters. Inside, the hallway lights buzzed over old tile and somebody had mopped with lemon disinfectant strong enough to sting her eyes.

At Window Three, a woman with silver cat-eye glasses and a hummingbird pin looked at Lena's forms, then at Lena, then at the line behind her. The woman's nameplate said T. RANSOM, and she kept one thumbnail wrapped in a strip of blue tape to keep herself from biting it.

"System migration and an audit hold," Ransom said. "No historical pull today unless Records Compliance signs a PR-7 exception."

"I called first. Nobody said migration."

"We're all learning the same time, Detective."

"I'm suspended."

Ransom's taped thumbnail ticked against the counter. "Then we're both having a week."

She slid the forms back through the slot with a carbon-copy packet stapled on top and lowered her voice. "Room 2B, Records Compliance, closes at four-thirty. Ask for Mattox Hill if you want the PR-7 signed before the file carts lock. Otherwise try next Tuesday, maybe the server's done pretending to die by then."

When Lena stepped out into the heat, her shirt stuck to her back at the scar line over her right knee. She sat in her car until the steering wheel cooled enough to touch and counted what she had left that the county could not lock: her father's notebook, a map from memory, and people who still answered when she called them as Lena instead of Detective Waller.

Ida had texted at ten-thirty: `Dinner at six. Real food. Bring your stubborn self and don't bring papers.`

Lena brought papers anyway, tucked in a grocery sack under the passenger seat.

Ida's house stood two streets over from the old elementary school, white siding gone cream with years and humidity. She had every porch light on before sunset, trying to push darkness back with electricity. Through the kitchen screen Lena heard gospel radio, oil popping in a skillet, and Ida singing one line ahead of the station in a low scratchy alto.

The kitchen smelled of onion, flour, and smoked turkey necks. Ida wore a green church dress she only pretended was casual and a pair of yellow dish gloves even though she was not washing anything. The gloves let her grip bowls without her arthritic fingers freezing halfway closed. She took one look at Lena and said, "You lost weight in your jaw. That's where worry sits on your side of the family."

"Hello to you too."

"Wash your hands and stop making jokes you don't mean."

Jalen was at the table with his geometry notebook open, one sneaker off, heel bouncing against a chair rung in a steady wooden thud. He had drawn a basketball court in the margin and kept darkening the free-throw line until the paper started to tear. A pair of cheap earbuds hung around his neck, one wire repaired with black electrical tape that he had wound too carefully to be temporary.

"Hey," Lena said.

"Hey." He did not look up right away. Teenagers could turn a single syllable into a whole weather system.

Marcus came in from the back porch carrying ice in a red cooler and an envelope tucked under his elbow. He still wore his work boots unlaced and the faded gray polo from Delta Fleet Services, collar sweat-dark and smelling faintly of diesel and peppermint gum. He had always chewed peppermint when he was angry because his father had quit cigarettes that way and never let anybody forget it.

"I grabbed your mom's bag of ice before the machine quit," he said. "Whole county's running on prayers and extension cords."

"Sit down," Ida said. "Nobody's fixing infrastructure in my kitchen."

For a while Ida kept the table on harmless rails.

"Rice too soft?" she asked, tapping the pot with her spoon.

Marcus took a bite. "If this is soft, lock me up."

Jalen snorted. "Coach says that every time cafeteria grits show up. 'Arrest me, but pass seconds.'"

Lena laughed before she could stop herself. Her phone buzzed once on the counter and Ida pointed her spoon at it.

"No case talk till these plates are empty."

"Yes ma'am," Lena said, and flipped the phone face down.

Marcus told the venison story with both hands, acting out the customer sliding a freezer bag across the counter like contraband. Jalen laughed hard enough to spray sweet tea and curse into his napkin. Even Ida laughed, then clicked her tongue like laughter cost extra.

Then Marcus set the envelope beside Lena's plate.

"I need you to read this when we eat," he said. "It's from chancery or tax office or whichever office wakes up wanting money."

Ida gave him a look. "Can he get through one meal without paperwork?"

"I'm asking her to translate, not to chase anybody."

Lena turned the envelope over without opening it. The return stamp read MERCER COUNTY CHANCERY CLERK and the window showed block numbers she knew too well: GT-12-44, SUBDIVISION C. Her stomach tightened before she could tell herself not to jump.

"After dinner," she said.

"After dinner," Marcus agreed, but his jaw stayed set.

They bowed for grace. Ida thanked God for food, for breath, for children and grandchildren, and for the strength to tell the truth even when the truth did not make anybody safer tonight. She did not look at Lena while she said it.

Halfway through the meal Jalen pushed his peas into a green crescent and asked, "Are you still a cop if they took your badge?"

The question landed clean as a tossed knife. Lena felt the old briefing script line up in her throat: define status, control tone, reassure. Scripts sounded like lies in this kitchen.

"I'm still me," Lena said. "Badge or no badge."

"That's not what I asked."

Marcus put his fork down quietly. Ida reached for hot sauce, missed by an inch, and tried again.

"I'm suspended," Lena said. "I can't work scenes right now."

"Because you did something wrong?"

"Because I didn't do what was convenient."

Jalen looked up then, eyes dry and bright the way they got when he had decided he was done being handled. "Can police lie to people?"

Marcus started to cut in. "Jalen, maybe not at the table-"

"He asked," Ida said.

She knew that look because she had worn it at seventeen in this same room, waiting for grown people to stop translating fear into policy language and just answer.

Lena felt briefing language crowd her mouth anyway. "In interviews, yes, they can bluff. They can claim they have evidence they don't have yet."

The fan over the stove clicked as it turned. Outside, cicadas started up in the pecan tree all at once, like somebody dragging metal teeth over a comb.

"So they lie if it helps," Jalen said.

"He needs a straight answer, Len," Marcus said. "Not academy talk."

"Fine. Yes," Lena said. "In interviews, some of them lie."

"In reports and under oath, they can't. If they lie there, cases break and people get hurt."

Jalen shook his head. "So it's legal when it's useful."

"It's strategy inside limits-" Lena dragged a breath and restarted. "It's supposed to be strategy inside limits."

"Did they lie about your address getting out?" Jalen asked. "Coach said everybody at school already knew where I sleep before I did."

Lena felt the back of her neck go hot. "Who told you that?"

"Everybody told me that."

Marcus leaned forward. "This is what I've been saying. He's seventeen, Lena. He shouldn't hear adults arguing about chain of custody while he's trying to shoot free throws."

"I know his age."

"Then act like it. Step back until this blows over."

"It won't blow over."

"Then let somebody else hold it."

"Who? Clay? Hollis?"

"I don't care if it's a scarecrow with a badge," Marcus said, voice cracking at the edges for the first time. "I care that my son got walked to his car by a teacher because some man in a truck sat outside practice with his phone up. I care that he checks our mailbox with a baseball bat. I care that every time your name trends, his stomach goes bad."

Jalen stared at his plate and said, barely audible, "I don't check it with a bat anymore."

Nobody corrected him.

Ida squared her napkin into a tight rectangle, teacher-precise, like she was grading papers again. "The county loves a Black woman in uniform right up to the minute she stops making things easier," she said. "After that they put her child on the bill with everybody else."

Lena looked at her mother. "You think I should quit too?"

"I think quitting and stopping are not the same thing," Ida said. "And I think men in this county have buried harm in file cabinets for sixty years and called it order. You being right won't stop a brick through your window."

Marcus turned to Ida. "So tell her. Tell her to walk away for now."

Ida shook her head. "I did not raise her to walk away. I raised her to count the cost before she steps. That's different."

Jalen finally met Lena's eyes. "If you keep doing it, can you at least stop saying we're fine? Just say we're not."

Lena had talked down armed men with less effort than it took to answer her son in that chair.

"We're not fine," she said. "We're not safe enough yet. I hate that sentence, but it's true."

He dipped his chin, neither forgiving nor rejecting, just logging the fact.

Jalen pushed his chair back so hard it barked across the linoleum. "Then I'm not sleeping at your place this week," he said, looking at Lena and nobody else. "I don't want to watch headlights slow down outside your house all night."

"Jalen-" Lena started.

"No, let him finish," Marcus said.

Jalen swallowed and kept going. "Don't come to practice either. Coach already has to walk me out. If you show up, they'll film that too."

Marcus's glass sweated a ring onto the table. Fork tines clicked against stoneware, then went still. The fan kept clicking over the stove while Ida set her fork down, stood, and started stacking plates with both gloved hands, one plate at a time because her fingers would lock if she rushed.

"I'll take him tonight and the rest of the week," Marcus said. "We can revisit Sunday."

Lena gave a tight nod because anything else would have been a fight she could only lose in front of him.

When the dishes were stacked and the sink ran hot, Marcus caught Lena by the back door.

"I'm filing if this keeps climbing," he said. He did not raise his voice. That made it worse. "Temporary custody, full school transport, no press contact around him. Denise already drafted it."

Lena leaned against the doorframe, feeling old paint catch at the skin above her wrist. "You talked to a lawyer before talking to me."

"I talked to a lawyer because talking to you has not changed anything this week."

He pulled a folded school transport form from his back pocket and held it between two fingers. "Sign this tonight and I hold filing until Sunday. Refuse and Denise files at eight."

Lena stared at the header - MERCER COUNTY SCHOOL DISTRICT, TEMPORARY PICKUP AUTHORIZATION - until the words stopped swimming. "You think I don't know the risk?"

"I think you know it like a detective. I need you to know it like his mother." He looked toward the kitchen where Jalen let out an unexpected laugh at something Ida said. Marcus's voice dropped. "I still know you. That's why this is hard."

Lena took the form, braced it on the doorframe, and signed with Marcus's pen. "You get school runs and nights through Sunday. You text me at pickup and drop-off. If anything shadows him, call me first and then 911."

Marcus watched her cap the pen. "No practice visits. No press near him."

"Fine," she said. "And if you try to make this permanent without talking to me, I'll fight you in court."

He folded the signed form into a neat strip and tucked it into his back pocket. "Sunday, six p.m., Ida's kitchen. We reassess then."

This time when he walked out, he took Jalen's duffel from the hall bench and the school form she had just signed.

Ida wrapped leftovers in old butter tubs and pushed two into Lena's hands. "Eat tomorrow before coffee," she said. "Your stomach has gone mean on you."

"Mama-"

"No speeches tonight." Ida wiped the counter in small circles, fighting stiffness in her knuckles. "Your daddy used to bring home those chancery books in his head, all those numbers, and he'd sit right there and mutter GT this, GT that, like numbers were scripture. I asked him once what GT stood for and he said, 'Depends who's asking.'"

Lena froze with the container warm in her palm. "When did he say that?"

"Nineteen years ago? Twenty? I had my good knees and bad patience. Why?"

"Because I keep seeing GT in his notebook."

Ida's face did not change much, but her shoulders settled a fraction lower, a load shifting and landing where it belonged. "Then stop asking clerks who owe favors to men in office," she said. "Read what he left you."

The drive home took seven minutes. Lena sat in her parked car for ten more, watching a moth batter itself against her porch light. Her phone lit with a text from Marcus before she had shut the engine off.

`I'm serious about court if this turns uglier. I'm not bluffing.`

She set the phone face down and carried the grocery sack inside.

Her father's notebook had swollen from years in damp storage. The cardboard cover smelled of mildew and machine oil. Inside, his handwriting marched in narrow capitals with the same pressure on every letter; he had trained his pen the way he trained his face: give nobody extra information.

Lena cleared a space on the table and laid out three things: the notebook index she found in the flooded storage box, Marcus's chancery envelope, and a county plat atlas from 1998 that she had bought at a library discard sale for one dollar and a quarter. The atlas still had a blue sticker on the spine that read REFERENCE COPY.

She flipped to the old township map of the southern parcels. Section lines crossed like stitching. A thick rectangle was labeled GIDEON TRACT in faded block print, with subparcels marked C-1 through C-56. She went back to the notebook.

GT/12/44 - FENN
GT/09/31 - VOSS
GT/18/02 - HOLLER PET.
GT/04/07 - BELL, I. (OLD)

She had read those lines twenty times and treated GT like initials for a person or task force. Tonight the envelope window and Ida's memory moved the pieces in her head one notch to the right.

The code marked Gideon Tract, parcel 12-44 and parcel 09-31.

She pulled the atlas close enough that her breath dampened the paper and found C-44 near a drainage ditch south of levee road, then C-31 beside a collapsed grain spur. Voss and Fenn, both men dead this month, both tied in her father's private shorthand to specific Gideon parcels.

Lena got up, paced to the sink, came back, and opened her laptop even though she knew county portals would choke her out. She was right. The assessor site spun until it timed out. The clerk e-filing page returned a red banner: ACCESS RESTRICTED - AUDIT HOLD 92 - CONTACT RECORDS ADMINISTRATOR EXT. 214.

A dry laugh scraped out of her, joyless, and she reached for analog work.

In the bottom drawer she kept copies of public notices she clipped for no reason she could explain at the time. Tax sale ads, trustee substitutions, probate announcements in six-point font that most people used to line birdcages. She spread them across the table under a magnet lamp and ran her finger down columns until names started echoing.

The notice for parcel GT-09-31 showed three delinquent years, then a redemption by a holding company listed only as CROWN ACQUISITIONS LLC, with nothing but P.O. Box 447 and a registered-agent stamp blurred into ink fog. She pulled her stack of Pike Foundation vendor printouts and found two different entities that used that same box at different times. No officer names. No signature she could tie to a body in this case.

The notice for parcel GT-12-44 showed a quitclaim transfer through an estate dispute, but the witness line was a black smear and the book citation ended in a hand-marked correction: Chancery Book 92, page 611, see corrected page.

She checked the microfilm index she had photocopied last spring. Page 611 existed. Page 612 was logged as corrected. Next to both entries someone had stamped WITHDRAWN FOR AUDIT in purple block letters.

It was Chancery Book 92 again, and this time the trail stopped where names should have been.

Her father's notes had the same book number beside both entries, just written as B92 in tiny script at the margin like he did not want it noticed even by himself.

Lena felt the old investigative lift in her chest, the dangerous relief of pattern after days of static. She hated how good it felt. Pattern meant she was useful again. Pattern also meant Marcus was right about things getting uglier.

She called the records-administrator extension from the red banner and got voicemail. Her message came out too calm.

"This is Lena Waller. I submitted requests this morning and in person. I need Chancery Book 92, land transfer entries for Gideon Tract parcels zero-nine-thirty-one and twelve-forty-four. These are public records. Send written denial with the name of the official invoking Audit Hold 92, tonight."

She hung up and stared at her own reflection in the black phone screen. Suspended detectives still sounded like detectives, even in voicemail they knew nobody would return.

At 9:43 p.m. Bernice from the morning call texted from an unknown number.

`Ms Waller stop leaving messages. Audit Hold 92 is Mattox Hill using Sheriff 14-B addendum. book carts roll out east service door 9:20 for basement count. don't make this harder.`

The text still had the same soft-threat lowercase, but now it gave her three things she had not had at noon: a name, an order reference, and a door.

Lena typed back: `East door camera still dead?`

Three dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

`Most nights after 9 its blind. delete this.`

She took a screenshot and printed it. Old habits made paper even now.

Then she tried one more route, calling the number of a retired title attorney who owed her a favor from a custody case three years back. Voicemail. She left details and got no call back.

Then she went back to the notebook and looked at the one line she had avoided because it felt like superstition when read too fast: GT/04/07 - BELL, I. (OLD).

It was not superstition. It was inventory.

Her father had mapped the tract because he expected somebody to ask someday how a dead man stayed useful to living thieves.

Half-erased at the bottom of the page, in lighter pencil, sat another line she had finally broken at Ida's dinner table while Jalen pushed peas into a crescent: C-11 / Box 62 / CH-Probate / Cor Packet. Chancery stack. Box number. Corrected probate packet.

From the bedroom came the buzz of her bank app reminder: auto-draft for her credit-union payment in forty-eight hours. Twelve thousand four hundred dollars still hanging off her name, and suspension meant payroll could stall if internal affairs wanted to play games. She wrote the due date on the corner of the legal pad and hated the superstition that facts got lighter once ink touched paper.

Her phone lit again with Marcus's name.

`Did you hear me tonight?`

She stared at the text long enough for the screen to dim.

`Yeah,` she wrote. `I heard you.`

Three minutes passed before another text arrived.

`Then choose us.`

Lena set the phone beside the notebook and pressed her thumb into the old knee scar through her jeans, a pressure trick the physical therapist taught her years ago when panic tried to run her body faster than sense. She could still smell Ida's kitchen on her shirt, fried onion and pepper and dish soap, the smell of being held inside a family whether you deserved it that day or not.

She opened a new message to Mara.

`I hit a wall. Book 92 pages for GT-09-31 and GT-12-44 are pulled under Audit Hold 92. Bernice says carts move through east service door at 9:20 and that camera is blind after 9. I need your archive access and your chancery source tonight or first light. County is burying the owner names.`

Mara answered in under a minute.

`You finally sound certain. Call me.`

Lena did not call right away. She looked once more at Marcus's last text, then at Jalen's geometry notebook she had brought home by reflex when she helped Ida clear the table, pages full of clean angles and one margin full of tiny drawn basketballs. He was seventeen and still trying to make a court behave like lines on paper.

She picked up the phone and called Mara.

"Tell me from the top," Mara said.

Lena gave her the hold code, Mattox Hill's name, the east door, and the dead camera.

"Terms first," Mara said. "You don't bring department hardware, you don't loop Beau, and you don't say my source's name out loud."

"I'm suspended. I don't have hardware."

"Suspended cops still call favors."

"I won't call anyone. I need Book 92 owner names tonight."

"Tonight means one pass," Mara said. "I shoot every page first. You touch paper after I do."

By minute seven both of them were talking through their teeth.

Lena pressed two fingers into her knee scar and forced her voice flat. "Fine. First capture is yours. I get immediate scans and notes in the room."

Silence, then a breath on Mara's end. "Deal. I'll text coordinates when I have eyes on a safe door and a blind camera."

Lena tore a yellow sticky from the legal pad, copied the probate code in block letters, and set it on her father's notebook before carrying both to the passenger seat.
