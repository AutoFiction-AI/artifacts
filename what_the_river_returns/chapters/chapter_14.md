# Chapter 14

The Pike Foundation Hall had been built to convince people that history could be polished. The old warehouse beams still ran black above the chandeliers, but every bolt had been sanded and lacquered and lit from below. White linen covered the tables in strict rows, and brass lamps made the place glow like a courtroom trying to pass for a church. A jazz trio stood near the staircase and played too softly to offend anyone with money.

Lena stood at the back wall with one hand on her radio and watched donors smile through their fear. The panic lived in small things. Men who kept touching their phones under the tablecloth. Women who laughed and kept their wineglass by the stem so their hands would not shake the bowl. Two bankers in blue suits argued over whispers and never stopped looking toward the loading dock doors where the protest noise leaked in each time a server crossed through. Everyone in the room was pretending this was another gala year, but nobody had come to celebrate.

Her right knee scar ached before storms and before bad scenes. Tonight it had started in the parking lot.

Beau came up on her left, broad shoulders filling the aisle, his right wrist strapped in a black brace cinched too tight. He had wrapped the brace with gray duct tape at the edge where it rubbed his skin raw. He held his flashlight in his left hand because the right still did not trust him, and he kept flexing his fingers, trying to bully the pain down through motion.

"Contractor stacked floral crates in the service cut-through again," he said. "Camera two's blind, just like you called it."

"Move them."

"Tried. Event planner told me those roses cost more than my truck."

"Then move the planner too."

He huffed a laugh, then winced when he shifted his wrist. "Yes, ma'am."

He did not move right away. He watched the room with her, jaw tight, eyes doing that old patrol sweep he never stopped doing even now that he could not grip cuffs with his strong hand.

"You sleep?" Lena asked.

"Thirty minutes in a recliner. Dreamed my cast got invited to dinner and I didn't." He rolled his shoulder and nodded toward the stage. "Clay's about to start the show."

Sheriff Clay Reddick stood near the lectern in a navy suit cut with campaign rigidity. He was heavy through the middle, but he wore it with the practiced posture of a man who knew every camera angle in the county. He had a pocket square matched to foundation colors. He also had sweat darkening his collar despite the air conditioning. Every few minutes he took a mint from a silver tin and let it dissolve before he spoke.

Lena's earpiece crackled.

"Unit Three, confirm west exit secure," she said.

Static. Then a chopped answer, thin and distant. "West secure. Crowd outside pushing barricade but contained."

Her signal meter dropped two bars where she stood. She checked her phone. One bar. Then none.

Across the room, Mara Quinn held the press rail near the stage. Mara's close-cropped hair shone under the lamps, and she had her phone rig clamped in one hand and a reporter's notebook in the other, thumb already stained blue from writing fast. She did not wave. She lifted her chin, asking a question without asking it.

Lena walked over before Clay could notice.

"You get credentialed legit?" Lena asked.

"Foundation temporary pass," Mara said. "Expires when somebody decides I'm inconvenient."

"Then stay where lights are and don't go alone if this turns."

Mara scribbled that down with the calm speed she used when bad news sounded inevitable. "You expecting a turn?"

"I'm expecting blind spots."

Mara glanced toward the service corridor door, the one Lena had fought over in the walkthrough the day before. "I saw Hollis by that door ten minutes ago. He had no reason to be there."

"He with uniform?"

"He was with two men in black polos, no badges, both wearing earpieces they kept touching for show."

Lena felt her pulse kick hard. "You got video?"

"I got twelve seconds before he looked up and smiled at me." Mara met her eyes. "That smile wasn't for me. It was a warning."

"Send it to me and keep the original off your cloud."

Mara tore a page from her notebook and folded it into Lena's palm. "Already mirrored."

That was Mara. She never trusted one copy of anything, and she chewed wintergreen gum when she was scared. Lena caught the scent as she stepped back.

On stage, Thomas Pike adjusted the microphone and tapped it twice even though it was live. He was trim for sixty, face carved down by stress these last weeks, tuxedo cut perfectly, cuff links shaped like tiny scales of justice. He kept spinning his wedding ring with his thumb, then forcing his hand flat against the lectern to stop. The ring spun again anyway. He nodded at people by name as if he still believed charm could make the county behave.

Judge Eleanor Pike stood half a pace behind him in a black silk dress with sleeves to the wrist, formal as Sunday court. Her hair was pinned too tight, and a flesh-colored migraine patch sat behind her left ear where makeup did not quite cover it. She held a water glass in one hand and a stack of speech cards in the other. When she thought nobody was looking, she pressed two fingers hard into the bridge of her nose and shut her eyes against the light.

Clay stepped up beside them and took the room.

"Ladies and gentlemen," he said, voice smooth and paternal, "thank you for standing with Mercer County in a hard season. Tonight we're showing we can hold the line without turning on each other."

He said trust in a church voice. The donors clapped because that was what people did when they needed the sheriff to keep their names clean.

Lena moved to the center aisle and watched table by table. A retired doctor at table four had both hands wrapped around his wife's forearm, gripping hard enough to whiten his knuckles. A cater waiter with a nose ring and red-rimmed eyes kept counting glasses under his breath, lips moving with each tray. At the rear doors, two deputies stared through the glass at protesters outside while pretending they were not listening to the chants.

Thomas lifted his glass for the toast.

"To Mercer's shared future," he said. "To courage under pressure, and to facts over rumor."

The lights cut before the last word left his mouth.

The room went black in a way that felt physical, like a heavy cloth dropped over everyone. Somebody screamed near the stage. A hundred chair legs scraped stone. Glass shattered in three directions. The trio stopped mid-note and the absence of music made the panic louder.

"Everybody stay where you are," Lena shouted. "Don't rush the exits."

Her radio hissed and died to static. She thumbed the transmit anyway. Nothing.

Emergency strips along the baseboards flickered, then failed. Phones jerked up in shaking hands, throwing wild white beams across faces, ceiling, and floor, each sweep making shadows jump and bodies look farther away than they were.

"Beau," she yelled.

"Here." His voice came from somewhere to her right, close then far as people shoved between them. "I got a crush at the west aisle."

"Start triage. Keep them off the glass."

A woman slammed into Lena hard enough to drive air from her chest. Sequins scraped her cheek. Lena caught her before she fell and felt blood slick down the woman's forearm from a long cut where a stemmed glass had broken.

"I can't see," the woman said, words shredding. "I can't, I can't."

"You can," Lena said. "Look at me. Squeeze my hand."

The woman squeezed. Strong grip. Conscious, breathing, bleeding but not arterial. Lena wrapped a napkin around the arm and tied it with her own badge lanyard.

"Hold pressure and sit by the wall."

Another scream, this one from the corridor side.

Lena turned toward it and hit shoulder-first into a man trying to run for the kitchen. He smelled like bourbon and fear and he was dragging his daughter by the wrist.

"Do not run," Lena said.

"She can't breathe."

The girl flashed in phone light: thirteen, maybe fourteen, braces bright, chest pumping too fast. Panic attack, maybe asthma. Lena crouched, got low.

"You got an inhaler?"

The girl nodded and pointed to a glitter purse on the floor. Lena found a purple inhaler and put it in the girl's hand. "Slow in, hold, slow out. Good. Again."

Her own pulse was climbing now, old muscle memory and fresh dread tangling together. The room sounded like a stadium tunnel: feet, sobbing, orders, curses, someone praying out loud with every breath.

She pushed toward the service corridor because every bad map in her head ended there.

The corridor door was half open. Floral crates had been shoved aside. Cold air from the kitchen hit her face along with bleach and fryer oil. The emergency lights back here worked in intermittent strips, green then dark then green again, making the hallway stutter.

Beau caught up, breathing hard, flashlight in his left hand, right arm held close against his body. "Kitchen's got two burns and one busted knee. EMS can't get through the lobby yet."

"Hold this door. Nobody in unless I call them."

A deputy stepped from the shadows at the far end. Hollis Reddick. Athletic build, sleeves rolled just high enough to show the military tattoo winding his forearm, eagle and date eaten by old sun damage. He had a fresh dip tucked in his cheek and kept clicking a metal lighter open and closed with his thumb. It made a sharp little sound every two seconds.

"Detective," Hollis said, too easy. "You need me sweeping the protest side?"

"No. Hollis, lock this corridor down, keep your hands visible, and stay on this tile. You're scene security and witness hold until I clear you."

He smiled and clicked the lighter again. "Yes, ma'am."

Lena hated that smile. Friendly enough for cameras, contempt underneath for anyone he thought could not touch him.

A staffer in black catering clothes came staggering out from the far service turn, both hands over his mouth. He was shaking so hard his tray towel dropped.

"There's a body," he said through his fingers. "There's two, I think. I touched him. I didn't mean to touch him."

"Name?"

"Eli. Eli Mercer."

His nametag was upside down, and there was tomato sauce on his cuff like he had wiped his hand in the wrong place without noticing.

"Eli, stay with Beau. Sit down. Do not wash your hands."

Lena moved past him and turned the corner.

Thomas Pike lay on his back against the wall under a catering poster advertising local catfish farms. One leg bent awkwardly under him. His tux shirt was open at the throat where someone had yanked his bow tie loose, and blood had spread from a puncture just left of his sternum, dark and soaked, then sticky black where it had dried in patches. His eyes were open and fixed on the ceiling pipe run. There was blood in his mouth. A tooth had chipped against his lower lip. His watch crystal was spidered and stopped at 8:41.

Three feet from him, under a white evidence sheet that did not belong in this building, lay the body Lena had seen too many times already. Isaiah Bell's remains, half covered, skin gray-brown where preservative had changed it, jaw canted, one shoulder exposed through torn fabric. The county evidence tag had been cut and rethreaded with red florist twine like somebody had mocked the chain of custody on purpose. A strip of blue tamper tape clung to the sheet edge: MERCER MORGUE CART 3.

Above both bodies, the corridor camera hung crooked with its dome popped loose. Two screws sat in the grout beside a fresh gouge mark. The junction box had been pried open and bridged with a short copper jumper wire, enough to kill that feed without dropping the whole floor.

At the loading-dock threshold, wet boot prints stamped red levee mud across the tile, in and back out, one heel dragging through crushed rose petals in a deep arc from a hard pivot under load.

The corridor smelled wrong immediately: copper from fresh blood, formalin sting from treated tissue, floral rot from the crushed roses by the door.

Lena knelt at Thomas first because the living always took priority until they did not. She pressed fingers to his neck. No pulse. Skin cooling fast at the edges, still warm at the throat. His left fist was locked around a black plastic button and torn thread from cheap polo fabric.

"Beau," she called, voice steady because it had to be. "Confirmed dead male, Thomas Pike. One historical-remains staging body present. Camera's been tampered and dock access is compromised. I need scene tape, photos, and EMS for official pronouncement. Hollis stays in lane as material witness. Photograph his cuffs, boots, and hands before he moves."

Beau came in and stopped hard when he saw Isaiah.

"Copy," Beau said, already lifting his phone one-handed. "Hollis, don't move."

"Jesus," he said, quiet and furious. "How the hell did they move him again?"

"Through a door we didn't have authority to lock."

He swallowed and got his radio up. Static.

"Nothing," he said.

Lena pulled her phone. No signal. She stepped back toward the door and got one bar for half a second, enough to push one text to dispatch: Two bodies service corridor. Door compromised. Lock venue. Need med and scene.

She did not know if it sent.

Behind them, the lobby noise rose again. Somebody shouted that there was a shooter. Somebody else shouted there was a bomb. Rumor traveled faster than blood.

Clay arrived with two deputies and a foundation board member in a tux jacket thrown over pajamas. He took in the corridor in one sweep, his face closing down like a gate.

He did not kneel by Thomas.

He did not look at Isaiah longer than a second.

His gaze fixed on Lena. "How long?"

"Unknown. We just found him."

"What witnesses?"

"Staff saw body after blackout. We need statements and full perimeter now. And I need Deputy Hollis held for swabs and cuff scrapings. Thomas was clutching torn black polo."

Clay's eyes flicked to Hollis and back. "Negative. Internal-affairs protocol requires command review and counsel before compelled collection from sworn personnel. I am not opening that process in a hallway."

"You lose wet transfer in minutes."

Clay gave a single nod, already elsewhere. "I want protest leadership detained for questioning."

Lena stared at him. "On what?"

"On motive, access, and timing. They've been telegraphing this all week."

"We don't have probable cause on a homicide yet."

His voice stayed smooth. "We've got a dead donor in a blackout corridor. I don't get to wait for perfect certainty while this place spirals."

"That's not evidence."

"That's command."

He turned to Hollis. "You're relieved from witness hold. Take Deshawn Lee and his two loudest organizers. Log them fast and keep the bruises off camera."

Hollis clicked his lighter shut and grinned without humor. "On it."

Lena stepped between them. "You can't build this case backward from a press angle."

Clay leaned in close enough that she smelled mint and adrenaline under his cologne. "Detective, I'm the one taking the hit if this crowd cracks. Work with me, or put your badge number on what happens next."

The threat landed exactly where he aimed. One hard shove out of homicide and her credit-union payment would go red before next month.

He went back toward the lobby before she answered.

Mara appeared at the corridor mouth, phone rig up, light glaring. A deputy tried to block her, but she slipped sideways through the gap and got within recording range.

"Sheriff," she called. "Are you naming suspects before forensic review?"

Clay stopped, turned his public face on, and gave her the cadence he used at fundraisers and funerals.

"We're running active leads tied to prior public threats," he said. "I'm not naming suspects on camera, but we're moving people before anyone else ends up dead."

She heard the press release taking shape in his voice, clean nouns where blood should have been.

"That sounds like a yes," Mara said.

"Tonight sounds like chaos," Clay said. "By sunrise it won't."

He walked off. Deputies followed.

Mara lowered the phone, eyes hard on Lena. "He just set the narrative."

"I know."

"You want that clip preserved?"

Thomas's watch, frozen at 8:41, flashed through her head. If this clip got buried, the rest of the night would follow it into the same grave.

"Every angle."

Mara nodded. "Already offloaded. I'm sending it to two editors outside county. If they raid my phone, it'll still live."

A paramedic finally pushed through with a trauma bag and a strip of glow sticks clipped to his vest. He was older, gray mustache stained by coffee, and he kept muttering multiplication tables under his breath while he worked, a habit he used to stay calm. He checked Thomas, shook his head, and covered the body's face with a clean drape that immediately spotted red at the mouth.

Lena stood and felt her knee flare hard enough to make her vision narrow for a second. She breathed through it. The hallway seemed to sway, green emergency light pulsing on Isaiah's exposed shoulder, off again, on again.

A deputy from front hall called down the corridor. "Sheriff wants all available on crowd-control. Protest line breached east barricade."

Beau cursed softly. "If we pull everyone, this scene gets trampled."

"I know," Lena said.

His attention cut from Thomas to Isaiah to the lobby where orders were getting louder by the minute. "What do you need from me?"

"Door security and witness names. Nobody touches this floor. Nobody."

He flexed his bad wrist and winced. "I can do that."

"Then do that."

She headed for the lobby because Clay had made sure the fire was everywhere by design.

The main hall was a triage ward now. Guests sat on tablecloths with bloody napkins tied around wrists and ankles. Someone had vomited by the auction display. One woman knelt beside a man having a seizure and counted out loud through tears while a nurse she did not know held his head off the stone floor. The jazz bass lay on its side, strings snapped, bridge cracked clean through.

Lena directed bodies into lanes toward exits that were finally open. She checked pulses, reassured children, swore at men who tried to shove past medics, and kept repeating names so people would answer and stay conscious. Every second she spent with the living pulled her away from the dead. Every second away from the dead gave Clay more room to script what had happened.

Her radio sputtered back to life.

"East dock. We got Lee. Cuffs on."

Another voice, trying for low and missing it: "Hold rights advisement till intake. Sheriff wants transport first."

Through the loading-bay glass, rain sheeted silver under the floodlights, and three deputies crowded one body on the ground. Deshawn Lee was on both knees with his cheek on wet concrete, Hollis's forearm jammed across his shoulders while a deputy cinched a zip tie hard enough to make his wrists jump.

"What's the charge?" Deshawn shouted. "Tell me the charge. I want my lawyer."

Hollis hauled him higher by the tie and shoved him back down. "You're on an investigative hold tied to terror threats. You'll talk when we say."

Lena pushed through the door into rain spray. "Read him now and log the arrest time."

Clay stood under the awning with rain stippling his cuffs. "Detective, stand down. This is investigative detention."

"Investigative detention still has a clock, and he still gets counsel."

Clay did not look at her. "Transport him before this turns into theater. No statements till booking."

A deputy kicked Deshawn's dropped phone under a catering van tire and drove his knee into Deshawn's spine for leverage. Deshawn gasped, then cursed through his teeth. Blood striped his cheek where the concrete had peeled skin.

"My inhaler's in my bag," Deshawn said, voice thinning into a whistle. "I can't get air."

Hollis snatched the soaked backpack from the ground and tossed it to a transport deputy. "Put it in property hold. Medical's at intake."

The transport deputy marked his wristboard with a grease pencil. "Twenty-one-oh-six. Deshawn Lee, investigative hold per sheriff. Rights advisement delayed to booking."

Two deputies yanked Deshawn upright by the zip tie, folded him at the waist, and slammed him into the caged rear seat. His shoulders kept hitching after the door clanged shut.

Lena held his eyes for half a second and saw recognition, fury, and panic stacked together.

Then Clay turned on her with that campaign smile shaved thin. "Judge Pike is waiting, Detective. Unless you'd rather babysit a suspect."

She shut her eyes hard for a beat, then opened them and pushed toward the back offices where Judge Pike had been taken. If she let Clay hold Eleanor alone, the night would harden into a report by sunrise.

A uniform at the office corridor blocked her with both hands up. "Sheriff's orders. Judge needs privacy."

"She needs a statement while memory's fresh."

"She needs counsel."

"She needs to not get managed." Lena stepped closer. "Move."

He did not. Nineteen, terrified, and still smelling faintly of detergent, he was probably new to nights. He was trying to obey whichever voice sounded strongest.

Mara came up behind Lena with her phone lowered and her press badge turned backward so it passed for a volunteer pass.

"Deputy," Mara said, polite as Sunday. "Your east exit medic station is out of saline and they're asking for whoever's nearest."

The deputy turned his head half an inch toward her.

Lena slid past him before he finished deciding.

The judges' prep room was smaller than Lena expected: one narrow couch, a walnut desk, framed campaign photos, and an antique mirror with a crack through the center. Eleanor Pike sat on the couch with her heels off, bare feet on cold tile, nylons snagged at the ankle. She had vomited into a silver ice bucket that sat beside her knee. A medic was checking her blood pressure. The cuff hissed and released.

Eleanor lifted her head when Lena entered. Her eyes were bloodshot and furious and frightened in the same breath.

"I asked for my husband," Eleanor said. "No one will answer plainly."

Lena dismissed the medic with a nod and waited until the door clicked shut.

"Mrs. Pike," she said, voice low. "Thomas is dead. I'm sorry."

Eleanor closed her eyes. Her throat worked. She did not cry. She pressed two fingers to her temple where the migraine pulse lived and spoke like she was dictating from the bench.

"How?"

"Single puncture wound to the chest. We're processing now."

"We?" Eleanor gave a thin, bitter laugh. "No, Detective. You are not processing. Clay is performing."

Lena stayed still. "Then help me stop him."

Eleanor checked the door, then faced Lena. "If I speak to you, he will know."

"He already thinks I'm a problem."

"That is because you are." Eleanor inhaled carefully, as if each breath might split her. "Clay will close this on that boy in cuffs before midnight and call it order. By sunrise Thomas's records will disappear into his office vault. That is why I am speaking now."

"What records?"

"Transfers. Probate notes. Names that should have been dead by now and are still collecting checks. Insurance, Thomas called them. Protection against men who smile at him in public and strip him in private."

"Where?"

"In our home study. Second floor. Blue room. Left wall, behind the Mississippi Reports set. It is a floor safe."

"If I give you the combination, I am confessing that I concealed evidence. I can be charged with obstruction. I can lose the bench and every protection my name still buys me. Hollis will understand exactly who burned him."

Lena leaned in. "Combination?"

Eleanor's hand shook, then steadied as she gripped Lena's wrist. Her nails were short, clean, unpainted, practical in a way Lena had never noticed before.

"Forty-one left, six right, twenty-two left," she whispered. "Two full turns before the first number. If you miss by a hair it locks for fifteen minutes."

Lena repeated it back.

Eleanor released Lena's wrist just long enough to take a paper napkin and blue fountain pen from her clutch. She wrote the numbers with a hand that started steady and ended jagged where rain from Lena's cuff bled the ink at the edges, then folded the napkin into Lena's palm.

Eleanor did not take her eyes off Lena. "Hollis Reddick moved the boxes into our house. Not once. Repeatedly. From the old probate annex first, then from a morgue loading bay after midnight three weeks ago. I told myself he was transporting legal archives. That was a lie I accepted because it was useful."

The room seemed to narrow around Lena.

"You saw that yourself?"

"I signed gate entries and did not read them. That is seeing."

"Did Clay know?"

Eleanor swallowed. "Clay knows exactly what keeps his name off the paperwork."

Footsteps outside. Voices. Clay's baritone, closer.

Eleanor let go and sat back, transformed in one breath into Judge Pike again, spine straight, face composed.

"When he comes in," she said, now formal and cold, "you are here to check whether I can be moved without collapsing."

The handle turned. Clay stepped in with Hollis behind him.

Clay tracked from Eleanor to Lena. "Detective, I assigned you to crowd lane."

"She had a migraine episode," Lena said. "I assessed."

Eleanor lifted her chin and did not look at Lena. "Sheriff, Detective Waller has been entirely professional."

Clay's eyes stayed on Lena, reading for seams. Hollis stood at the door with his lighter in his fist, thumb tapping it against his leg. He had blood on the cuff of his khakis and did not seem to know it was there.

Lena held up her phone and took two close photos. "Visible blood transfer on Deputy Hollis Reddick, right cuff, timestamped."

Clay offered Eleanor a practiced sorrow face. "Judge, Deshawn Lee is in holding on investigative detention tied to prior threats. You'll get a full timeline after intake's logged."

Eleanor's voice was paper-dry. "Do not brief me with conclusions before your office secures the scene, Sheriff. I am still a judge when I am widowed."

Clay gave a short nod, polite on the surface, insulted underneath.

"Of course," he said. "We're moving fast because delay writes its own story."

"Accuracy matters," Eleanor said.

He did not answer that. He stepped aside so Lena could pass, but as she moved by he spoke low enough for only her to hear.

"You freelance me again and you'll be counting paperclips by dawn."

Lena met his eyes. "Then you'll have one less detective on your homicide floor."

"Maybe I already do."

She walked out before he could say more.

In the corridor, Mara waited beside a rolling linen cart, pretending to scroll her phone. The protest chants outside had turned to scattered sirens now. Rain had started, hard enough on the roof to sound like static with weight.

"Well?" Mara asked.

Lena kept her voice flat. "We're not doing this in the corridor. We move to a room I can lock and log."

Mara read her face anyway. "Then tell me if it's rumor, or if you've got something I can attribute on the record."

Lena touched the folded napkin in her pocket. "It's a legal route to paper, and we treat it as evidence from first touch. Keep your footage mirrored and off county servers."

Mara nodded and did not press, which meant she understood how live this was. "I've got Hollis kneeling on Deshawn's spine, Clay calling it investigative detention, and your witness-hold order getting overruled on tape."

Lena's jaw tightened. "Back it up out of county tonight."

"Already done. Three mirrors. He gave cameras a cleaner line two minutes later: activist retaliation is the lead scenario."

"He needed one before the blood dried."

Lena turned back toward the prep room door where Clay and Hollis still stood between Eleanor and the rest of the building. She felt the folded napkin in her pocket and, under it, the numbers as if Eleanor had carved them into her palm with a key. Forty-one. Six. Twenty-two.

Hollis was no longer just a deputy in the lane. In Lena's running log he was now an evidentiary subject, command-protected and time-stamped.

Hours of statements, perimeter resets, and rumor control bled out under generator hum before she got back to that corridor. By then the rain had thinned toward dawn, and the night had split in two paths: the story Clay could sell by sunrise, and the record that could survive him. She was now carrying the second path in memory and skin, and everyone in that corridor would understand by morning that she had taken it.

She keyed her radio. "Detective Waller to unit channel. I need two trusted uniforms now, one on lobby and one on Pike's stairwell, for scene lock and witness isolation. Start a separate evidentiary log on Deputy Hollis Reddick and lock edits. No leaks."

Beau answered through static and rain. "I'm here. Who else you trust?"

Lena watched Hollis laugh at something Clay said and felt the answer settle like a weight she could still lift.

"Not many," she said. "But enough."
