# Chapter 16

By morning the spray had dried tacky on the rails, a sweet chemical film that held whatever touched it: spoonbill feather dust, mosquito wings, gray ash from the generator stack, the soft down that had come off the dead chicks when Beto lifted them by the handful into lined fish boxes. Mina scrubbed her hands with the camp's orange mechanic soap until the skin in her palms looked peeled, but when she lifted them to her face she still got diesel, mangrove rot, and that pesticide sweetness underneath, as if the smell had moved into the small cracks around her nails during the night and found lease terms.

She had slept in pieces. Every time the stilts shifted under wind she had heard the impact again, not the spray run itself but the debris after it: wet little bodies on walkway planks, the slap of fish turning white-side up in the channel, Beto saying I got this one and then not sounding nineteen at all. Just before dawn she had stood by the sink in the dark and watched a spoonbill feather stuck to the drain pulse with the last of the rinse water. It had been almost beautiful, which made her want to break something.

Imogen sent for her at eight.

The main house office still carried last night's triage in a dozen petty ways. A stack of wet incident forms bowed on the desk where somebody had set them down with bloody gloves. The battery lantern on the file cabinet had burned a pale ring into the wood. One of the donor welcome baskets had been shoved under a chair and forgotten there, local citrus rolling loose among the brochures. Imogen stood at the open jalousie window in a field shirt she had buttoned wrong in her hurry, silver hair pinned up with the red golf pencil she used for guest counts. A smear of pink guano crossed one cuff. It looked less like filth than a stripe of war paint she had not had time to wipe away.

"Close the door," she said. "Please."

Mina closed it. The fan on the side table clicked once every turn, the blade housing bent from a storm three seasons ago. Imogen had a printed statement on the desk, two pages, single-spaced. Mina did not need to read more than the first lines to feel the shape of it.

Following last night's emergency mosquito-control action and the associated wildlife mortality event, Rookery Key Bird Camp is cooperating fully with reviewing authorities.

The words associated wildlife mortality event lay on the page like a pillow over a face.

"Who wrote this?" Mina asked.

"I did. Rhea corrected my wording on the section about the recorder. A board member corrected my wording on what not to say to donors." Imogen put both hands on the back of the chair and leaned into it, not sitting, as if the day had already denied her that much softness. "The Gulf Wildlife Board is sending an investigator by launch. A biotech-liability woman is piggybacking because somebody in Tallahassee heard the word luminous and saw a lawsuit. Elaine Porter is coming from the board because apparently no disaster is too ugly for oversight. If we do this cleanly, we might still keep the camp open and the rookery under people who know what it is."

Mina looked at the second page. Historic infrastructure material recovered during storm assessment may indicate a prior unauthorized release event connected to BrightCurrent legacy operations in the wetland system. There it was, flatter and safer than the thing itself. It said nothing about a cooler, nothing about Jonah Pike, nothing about the old blue photograph that had made her useful and then radioactive.

"You want me to say I found paperwork," Mina said.

"I want you to say what can actually help." Imogen's voice stayed low, too level to pass for calm. "The annex logbook matters. The badge matters. The current sighting matters. Your role in carrying a cooler for Jonah Pike years ago does not help the eel. It does not help the camp. It does not help the birds we still have alive on this island this morning."

Mina kept her fingers on the pages because the paper gave them something to do besides shake. Outside the open window a screen door banged once, then again harder. The sound lifted a picture into her head of Tomas coming up the steps after a night run, wet to the elbows, and she put it down before it had time to become longing.

"It helps explain why nobody should let me stand there as the discoverer," she said.

"You are not the discoverer. I am not asking you to be." Imogen finally sat, then stood again immediately as if the chair had been hot. "I am asking you not to confuse self-destruction with ethics. Those are different acts, whatever your generation likes to call bravery."

Mina gave a short laugh that had nothing in it. "My generation."

"Do not waste my time with tone. I have dead chicks in coolers and a donor who has already used the phrase communications opportunity in a text message this morning." Imogen pinched the bridge of her nose, then let her hand drop. "Listen to me all the way through. If you tell them about the cooler, if you reopen the photograph, if you make yourself the center of this, every person on that platform stops hearing corporate release and starts hearing disgraced guide lies again. They will build the whole day around whether you contaminated the evidence, whether you are confessing because you need absolution, whether the camp hired a known liability and therefore deserves whatever follows. BrightCurrent's old sins become your face. The county's spray becomes background noise. The eel becomes a scandal package instead of a protected animal."

Mina looked down at the statement again. There were phrases in Imogen's handwriting along the margin, grant-ready phrases, terms that could travel through a board call without making wealthy people put down their forks: legacy exposure, stewardship response, temporary access restriction, extraordinary event. Imogen had spent half her life learning how to make catastrophe legible to people with checkbooks. Mina could not even hate her for it. The camp's repair invoices were as real as the bodies in the freezer: twenty-seven thousand and change before hurricane inspection, plus Tomas's boat debt and her own monthly payment to the Gulf Wildlife Board, which had never once cared whether she ate before mailing them their money.

"You said yourself the donor story would start the minute they smelled money," Mina said. "If I let you flatten this, that's what they get. A miracle with some historic complexity. A contaminated wonder they can still brand."

Imogen's mouth tightened. "And if you do it your way, they get a woman they already know how to sacrifice. Do you imagine that protects anything? It gives them a culprit with a decent jawline and a prior record. It lets them package the past as one reckless employee, one charismatic older guide, one fallen field biologist, all neat enough to litigate. Meanwhile the camp goes under and the rookery gets managed by committee."

Mina pinched the damp paper until the top page bowed. The argument was not stupid. Jonah had always known the value of a story that narrowed blame to the person who could carry it prettiest. He had slipped it over Mina like a clean shirt years ago. She had worn it because it fit her hunger. Imogen was offering a different version of the same garment now: more respectable fabric, same trick in the seams.

"What are you actually asking me to leave out?" Mina said.

Imogen held her gaze. "The cooler. The fact that you cannot authenticate the old photograph as wild. Anything that turns this from present-tense evidence into a morality play about you."

"That isn't extra. That is the spine."

"No. It is the blood you want to smear on the frame because you think bleeding in public counts as honesty." The formal edges started to fray. "I defended you once. I took calls for you. I told people there was a difference between ambition and fraud, between bad judgment and malice. You know what I learned? Checks arrive when people can pretend the damage is already named, boxed, and far enough away to visit. This morning I have carcasses in freezers and inspectors on the water. I do not need you stepping forward to make yourself the exhibit too."

Mina let the papers go. They slid a little on the desk and stopped against a mug ring. She could feel sweat drying under her shirt in a salt crust left over from yesterday's heat and last night's fear. The office smelled of coffee gone bitter on the burner, damp paper, and the sharp ammoniac stink of guano baked into the boards below the window. Even here, inside bureaucracy, the place would not stop being animal.

"If I keep it clean," she said, "what happens next?"

Imogen answered too fast, which meant she had rehearsed. "We turn over the logbook through counsel, we separate the spray investigation from the legacy release, we freeze tourist access ourselves before the state does it badly, and we put out one statement that makes the camp look like the first competent witness on scene instead of the accidental landlord of a biotech mess. It buys me time. It buys Rhea permit cover. It keeps Tomas from being the only body they throw to the county. It might keep the eel from becoming a media feeding frenzy by lunchtime."

"And me?"

For the first time Imogen looked tired enough to be older than her posture. She lowered herself into the chair and stayed there. "If you stay on script, I can say you assisted in recovering evidence and acted appropriately once you understood what it was. I can keep you on through inspection at least. After that, I can try to move you into the back of the work, where nobody needs to point a donor at you. It is not redemption. Redemption is not on offer for any of us. But it is a life."

The offer loosened something mean and needy in her chest. A bunk through inspection. Wages another week, maybe two. A way not to get spit out onto the mainland with one duffel bag, a debt plan, and a fresh public disgrace to staple to the old one. The temptation in it was so ordinary she almost admired the precision. Nobody had to promise restoration. They only had to promise rent.

Her old visitor badge sat beside the logbook on the filing cabinet, sealed now in a clear evidence sleeve from the bat lab. The faded photo showed a younger version of her trying too hard not to look hungry. The plastic edge was clouded where floodwater had sat on it in the annex for years. She remembered slipping that badge into a pocket because Jonah had kissed her neck in the parking lot and told her nobody important ever obeyed every rule in the first pass. She had believed him because she had wanted the future fast. Want had always been easiest for other people to direct when it looked like work.

"If I take your version," Mina said quietly, "then the first true thing anybody hears today is still arranged around what will keep the lights on."

Imogen's chair scraped. "Of course it is. The lights matter. The roof matters. The permits matter. This absurd American appetite to pretend ethics happens somewhere above payroll is how places like this die."

"I know that."

"I do not think you do." She came around the desk now, close enough that Mina could see the broken capillaries around her nose from years of sun. "Because if you did, you would understand that there is no clean option left, only a ranking of harms. I am telling you which harm I can still manage."

Mina looked at the statement one last time. The pages had already begun to curl in the humidity. Somewhere under the office floor a pipe knocked. She thought of the eel rising through poisoned blackwater after the spray, blue along the body like somebody dragging a lit wire through oil. Nothing about the sight had felt clean. Nothing about it had felt marketable either until people on land began speaking around it.

"I won't give them a cleaner lie than the one that got me here," she said.

For a beat the room stayed very still except for the fan's bent click.

Imogen let out a breath through her nose. When she spoke again the professionalism came back so hard it sounded armored. "Then understand the consequence before you indulge yourself. If you go off script on that platform, you do it as yourself, not as a camp representative. Your contract ends the moment you make that choice."

Mina nodded. The nod felt small and irreversible.

"Fine," Imogen said. Her gaze dropped to the badge, the logbook, the printed statement, weighing damage and usefulness one last time. "Bring the originals. I would rather the state hear the worst from the person who can at least manage complete sentences."

She moved to the door and opened it for Mina with the brittle courtesy reserved for guests who had overstayed lunch.

"And Mina."

Mina turned.

Imogen's hand stayed on the knob. "Do not expect me to catch you in public."

The walkway outside lay bleached white in the sun. Chemical sheen flashed on the rail like varnish.

* * *

By eleven the observation platform had been rearranged into the kind of order people reached for when there was no order available. There were folding chairs in two rows. A card table held bottled water, legal pads, and a box of nitrile gloves somebody had set out by habit, the last reflex of a place still pretending proper handling might fix what came next. The rookery beyond the platform sounded wrong. There were still spoonbills in the mangroves, still the raw hinge-squeal of adults landing and the occasional comic bark of a chick that had survived, but the noise came with holes in it now, blank spaces where whole small hungers had been yesterday.

Beto was the one lining the chairs, toeing each leg straight against the warped planks. He had changed into a camp T-shirt too big in the shoulders and kept an anatomy flashcard half tucked into the collar, forgotten there after breakfast cramming for the trade-school entrance exam he still pretended was a joke. One wrist showed the old kitchen burn white against his skin. He glanced at Mina, then at the evidence sleeve under her arm.

"You okay?" he asked, and because he was nineteen and the question was impossible, he added, "Stupid question."

"Not stupid," Mina said.

He nodded once, serious enough to age five years. "They moved the freezer to the shade. If anybody says the chick count sounds high, they can open it themselves."

That was Beto's detail, not the joke he might have made a month ago but the private line he had chosen to defend: the dead deserved witnesses who did not round down.

Tomas stood by the far rail under a patch of awning that did not throw enough shade to matter. He had shaved with bad concentration; a crescent of dark stubble still rode the angle under his jaw. The county fuel key was gone from his belt. In its place he kept turning a stainless hose clamp between his fingers, opening and closing the screw with his thumbnail until it clicked. He smelled faintly of detergent overlaid on engine grease; he had plainly washed twice and lost to both substances.

When Mina stopped beside him, he kept looking out at the blackwater.

"You don't owe me a better outcome than the one you can stand," he said.

It was pure Tomas, stripped down to workman's language even now. He was not offering forgiveness or advice dressed as heroism.

"That's generous," she said.

"No, it isn't." The hose clamp clicked shut. "I've had enough of useful lies for one week."

Rhea came up from the bat lab carrying the recorder case against her hip, because she trusted even disaster less once it touched a shared table. Her braid had started to loosen, wisps dark with sweat around her temples. She had a stripe of blue Sharpie on the side of one thumb where she had been labeling contaminated sample bags, and the rubbing-alcohol smell from the lab rode ahead of her through the insecticide. She looked at Mina, at the logbook, then at the chairs.

"If you decide to say it, say it in the order that makes legal sense," she said. Her tone stayed flat enough to pass for technical instruction, which was how Rhea smuggled care into public space. "Act first. Documentation second. Interpretation third. Don't let them confuse the archive with the field evidence."

Mina almost smiled. "That's romantic."

"I'm not aiming for romance."

But the corner of Rhea's mouth moved, the barest acknowledgment of older bodies and worse rooms. Then it was gone. She took a chair on the side, set the recorder case at her feet, and folded her hands on her knee with the same economy she used before difficult grant calls.

The first launch came in low and too fast, flaring a wake against the pilings. The woman who stepped out first had a silver sea-turtle pin on her field vest and a cheap foam coffee cup wedged into the back pocket of her cargo pants. She moved carefully down the ramp because her left knee was wrapped under the khaki, the fabric stiff with brace straps. Her hair had given up on its bun hours ago. She was not polished, then, which felt like good news. Behind her came a smaller woman in office flats she already regretted, carrying a hard case and a legal pad with a bright pink grocery-store pen clipped to it. The pen had fake palm fronds floating in the plastic barrel. She kept twisting it as she looked around the camp, the way people did when they were translating real damage into a memo in their own heads.

The donor launch arrived a minute later with less competence and more sunscreen. Board treasurer Elaine Porter climbed out in linen trousers and expensive rain sandals, a paper hospital wristband still around one ankle because she had clearly forgotten it from some recent procedure or refused to treat her body as a thing requiring aesthetic continuity. She wore pearl studs anyway. The combination was so exactingly human that Mina trusted her less and more at once. Elaine had been sending Imogen money in ugly seasons for years. She also had the expression of a woman already deciding which version of a story her peers would find worthy of a gala table.

Introductions blurred into ritual anyway. June Salcedo named herself and the Gulf Wildlife Board in a fieldworker's flat voice, already impatient with chairs. Mara Bix introduced herself as BrightCurrent's insurer and liability reviewer, though she said the company's name the way people said mold in a rental house, with detachment meant to imply it belonged to somebody else. Elaine Porter identified herself for the board because the rest had found excuses and she had not, and Imogen completed the circuit with brutal grace, naming staff, thanking agencies, offering water nobody wanted.

The heat settled heavier as they took seats. Wind pushed chemical sweetness off the rail and then let it sit on everyone's skin again. June Salcedo opened her notebook and looked first not at Imogen but at the mangroves, counting sound the way field people did.

"Before we get into the bioluminescent report," she said, "I need a plain account of last night's spray event and current mortality numbers."

Imogen gave it to her cleanly: the time of county action, the wind shift, the preliminary count that was still rising, the access lanes closed to guests, the staff assisting with recovery. Her voice was polished enough to pass through microphones, but Mina could hear the grain underneath now, the scrape from not sleeping. She did not once look at Tomas while she described the spray. Tomas watched the hose clamp turn in his fingers and let her keep the lie of distance if not the lie of innocence.

Mara Bix took notes with the pink pen. Elaine Porter asked whether media had gotten wind of any of it. That was the donor question, quick as pulse. She wanted to know who knew, not what died.

"Not yet," Imogen said. "Which is one reason I asked everyone to come in person. We may have a narrow window to set responsible boundaries before rumor outruns fact."

Elaine sat straighter at responsible boundaries. Mara's pink pen began to move faster. Mina felt her shoulders climb toward her ears; the meeting had tipped away from carcasses and into donor management.

Imogen nodded toward the evidence sleeve under Mina's arm. "During low-tide assessment of legacy infrastructure beneath the camp, Ms. Cordero recovered archival material from the abandoned BrightCurrent annex. It may indicate an unauthorized storm-release event in prior years. We also have partial contemporary audio and eyewitness testimony regarding an anomalous luminous eel in the rookery channels. The camp's position is that the current priority is habitat protection and evidence preservation."

Imogen had laid it out carefully, offering the room enough truth to keep listening and enough omission to keep control.

June Salcedo turned to Mina. "Ms. Cordero, walk me through the recovery."

Imogen's eyes touched Mina's face for less than a second. It was not a plea now. It was a last warning.

Mina put the evidence sleeve and the logbook on the card table. The plastic ticked against the wood. Her palms were damp again. She dried them on her shorts and heard the bent fan in the office in her memory, that same stubborn click. The whole morning seemed to run on a failing part that refused to stop.

"I can do that," she said. "But if this is going into anybody's notebook, you need the part that makes me a bad witness before you decide whether I'm useful."

Elaine Porter frowned. "Imogen, is that necessary?"

Imogen did not answer. She held herself with the careful stillness of somebody carrying a full tray through turbulence, every muscle braced against the spill.

June Salcedo looked from one woman to the other and made a small gesture with her pen. "Go ahead."

Mina gripped the table edge. The rail tack under her fingers held a grit of dead insects.

"Years ago," she said, "I worked guide jobs around BrightCurrent facilities. I was sleeping with an older guide named Jonah Pike. He asked me to carry a cooler out for him. I did it."

The room did not explode. It tightened.

Mara Bix's pink pen stopped turning.

"Did you know what was in it?" June asked.

"Not when he put it in my hands. After that, yes. Fast." Mina swallowed. The taste in her mouth was soap and chemical drift and old shame, all of it practical. "I knew enough by the end of that day to understand I had moved something living that did not belong in a parking lot or a skiff or anywhere near a guide with a camera and a plan."

Elaine said, "I was told the old matter had already been adjudicated."

"Part of it was." Mina kept her eyes on June because June was the only one here being paid to hear ugly facts without converting them into funding language. "The rest of it lived in the gaps. The photograph that put my name everywhere and then ruined it later, the first luminous-eel image attached to me, I let people treat that photograph as proof of a wild find. I can't prove the animal in that image came from the creek. I can't prove it came from a tank. What I can prove is that I had already carried that cooler and I had no right to act like my hands were clean of the question."

The platform cinched tight around the silence; even the spoonbills beyond it sounded suddenly farther away.

Imogen's jaw set. Beto had stopped moving entirely, his hand still on the back of an empty chair.

June's pen moved once. "You represented the old photo as a wild specimen despite that uncertainty."

"Yes."

"In writing?"

"In interviews. In captions. In the way I failed to correct people because the failure was good for me." She gave a short breath that might have been a laugh in a better life. "I was not subtle about wanting what came with discovery."

No absolution arrived with saying it. The boards stayed hard under her sandals. Sweat kept sliding down her spine. Across from her, Elaine Porter's face settled into the polite blankness of the wealthy when disgust risked looking vulgar.

June said, "Why disclose it now?"

She wanted to say she had wanted the eel to save her and had wanted Rhea and Tomas to love her anyway, then watched chicks die under a spray somebody called management. Every version sounded vain when boiled down. Mina chose the smaller truth.

"Because this animal does not need another clean story told around it," she said. "And because the annex material changes what the current sighting means."

She slid the logbook toward June and set the old badge beside it. The badge photo flashed under plastic, younger Mina staring up with all her appetite still reading as promise.

"I found these in the intake tunnel under the camp at low tide," she said. "The logbook has maintenance notes, emergency cleanup language, release counts. This is not rumor or folklore. It is a storm evacuation. They dumped or dispersed engineered eels into the wetland system and wrote it down like they were clearing shelving before a flood."

Mara Bix reached for the book and then stopped when June did not.

"Release counts?" Mara asked.

"Marked as inventory adjustment in some places. Emergency dispersal in others." Mina could hear the pages in her head because she had read them by weak headlamp in air that tasted like rust and old water. "Enough animals that my one cooler does not explain what is in the creek now. My theft still matters. It does not explain the whole line."

Mara's face had the gray look of somebody hearing her week's work multiply. "Do you have photographs of the log entries before removal?"

"No. I took the original because the place floods and I wasn't leaving it there for another tide."

"Chain of custody is compromised," Mara murmured, half to herself.

Rhea spoke before Mina could. "It was already compromised by being underwater for years in an abandoned biotech annex beneath an active rookery." She kept her hands folded as she said it, voice measured, each clause set where she wanted it. "If you need a cleaner archive, build a time machine."

That was Rhea's way of choosing a side in public. She never reached for touch when exact contempt would do more work.

Elaine Porter leaned forward, sandal straps dark with sweat. "I am trying to understand the immediate practical implications. If this breaks as a revived fraud story tied to engineered contamination under our camp, private support will crater before any protection plan exists. Imogen, I assume there is a way to distinguish historic corporate behavior from present camp stewardship in whatever goes on the record."

At the word stewardship, Elaine eased back a fraction, already hearing the version she could repeat to donors. Mina saw Imogen close her eyes for a beat. It was pain, not agreement.

June Salcedo answered before Mina could. "There will be no public language from this site until I finish the initial review. Ms. Bix, bag the logbook and badge. Ms. Porter, guest access to the channels is frozen effective now. Mr. Alvarez, I will need the mortality freezer after we finish with the spray questions. No one enters the restricted water or removes material from it without agency notice."

Elaine sat back as if struck, which was more satisfying than it should have been.

Mara Bix cleared her throat. "BrightCurrent no longer operates that annex. Liability pathways are not yet established. I need copies of every recording, every sighting note, and any coordinates associated with the current animal."

Rhea's eyes flicked once toward Mina, then back to Mara. "You will get the hydrophone file through the Board after it is logged. It is partial and corrupted. The witnesses can give you channel range, not a breeding site, and that is all we have."

June looked at Tomas. "For the record, identify yourself."

"Tomas Acosta. County mosquito district. Suspended airboat pilot, if we're being current."

Mina watched Elaine register the word suspended and file it under usable complication. Tomas saw it too. He smiled without humor.

"Then I want the spray parameters now," June said.

Tomas rolled the bent hose clamp once through his palm. "Altitude, wind, nozzle settings, route timing. I'll give you what I remember and the county log can cough up the rest."

The platform took Tomas's numbers badly. June wrote them down, asked three clipped follow-ups about drift and altitude, then swung back to Mina before the scrape of Tomas's chair had settled.

"Ms. Cordero, exact recovery time."

"Six forty-two, maybe six forty-five. Tide was still running out."

"Who saw you enter the intake route?"

"Nobody. I didn't tell anyone I was going in."

"Who saw the logbook and badge where you found them?"

"Nobody saw the badge or the book in place."

June's notebook stayed open. "Did you photograph either item in place before removal?"

"No. I took the originals before another tide could finish them."

Mara Bix lifted the pink pen from her pad. "Then what we have is an unlogged recovery by a witness who has just admitted she previously let false provenance stand when it helped her."

Mara did not raise her voice. She did not need to. Beto, coming back with a second case of water, set it down too hard and sent two bottles rolling under the chairs. Tomas's hose clamp stopped turning in his hand.

Mina kept both palms on the table edge because it was that or fold her arms like a defendant. "What you have is a flooded BrightCurrent log in BrightCurrent's own hand."

"What I have is motive," Mara said. "You are not a neutral finder. You had reason to locate anything that spread blame wider than your cooler."

"Yeah," Mina said. The word came out flat. "And I still handed it over in front of the people who can bury me with it. The book names the east sluice. It says not to name the luminescent line on the county form. It tallies at least forty-seven losses before the mold eats the rest. If I were inventing a kinder version for myself, I would've picked one where I wasn't stupid enough to say any of this out loud."

June looked up. "Forty-seven documented losses."

"At least. That's what's still legible."

Elaine Porter had gone still in the expensive, dangerous way of donors deciding where damage should live. "Imogen, did you know she had recovered this material alone before you put her in front of us?"

Imogen did not blink. "I knew Ms. Cordero had recovered material relevant to the site's history."

"That is not an answer." Elaine's voice stayed soft; money did not need volume. "Did you know you were employing a woman who admits she let a false wild-origin claim stand and then put her in front of board representatives on camp property as a credible witness?"

Across the table, Rhea's mouth went flat enough to vanish. "She did not say false. She said uncertain. There is a legal difference."

Elaine turned her head just enough to acknowledge her. "There is not a donor difference."

Mina felt the room re-sort itself around payroll, optics, and who could be cut loose fastest. The eel had not changed. Only the price tags had.

June shut her notebook halfway. "For me there is an evidentiary difference. Ms. Cordero, do you still possess any material connected to the original cooler incident?"

"No. Nothing from it."

"Do you know where Jonah Pike is now?"

"Alive," Mina said. "Off this island. That's all I know for sure."

That was a lie of degree, not category. She knew the rough coast he had drifted toward, the kinds of charters he still sold, the age his face would be now. She did not offer it. One betrayal at a time had always been her limit, even at her worst.

When the questions finally slowed, the platform smelled less like bird camp than a hearing room improvised over dead water. Nobody was talking about launch strategy now. They were talking about seizure authority, access freeze, state review, archived release language, mortality counts, liability, containment. Nobody called it discovery anymore.

Imogen took the opening they left behind.

Imogen stood. In the heat and stink and public ruin she somehow found the posture that had once made donors mistake exhaustion for command. She looked first at Elaine, then June, then finally at Mina.

"The camp will comply fully with access restrictions and evidence requests," she said. "We will also preserve our own records of last night's mortality and cooperate on the spray review. As for staff matters..." She stopped, and for the first time all day the polish cracked enough for feeling to show. What showed was injury, not softness. "As for staff matters, Ms. Cordero is no longer employed by Rookery Key Bird Camp as of this moment."

Imogen delivered it with the clipped force of paperwork already drafted.

Beto said, "Jesus," under his breath, then looked at Imogen like he was sorry for letting the word out and sorrier that it fit.

Imogen did not look at him. Her eyes stayed on Mina.

"Your contract was contingent on representing this camp in good faith," she said. Low contraction now, each word deliberately formal, because anger had pushed her into the register where she could keep standing. "You chose to use this meeting to disclose material that makes continued employment impossible. Turn in your staff key now. Keep the cot until morning and do not make another public scene on my walkway. If you need your final pay documented for the Board, I will have the paperwork ready by four."

It was cruel and not theatrical. That made it sting better. It was a firing shaped around payroll and keys, not destiny.

Mina felt the whole platform looking at her, not with one emotion but with a cluster of them that changed from face to face. Elaine's revulsion had relief braided through it now that the camp had cut her loose. Mara Bix looked almost grateful for the institutional distancing because it simplified her memo. June Salcedo looked tired, which Mina trusted more than sympathy. Beto looked furious in the helpless way of the young when adults choose procedure over mercy. Tomas's mouth flattened, and the hose clamp in his palm bent slightly where he had squeezed it. Rhea did not move at all, but the tendon at the side of her neck stood out hard as wire.

Mina reached for the lanyard at her throat and slid the staff key free of the ring. The cheap plastic tag had knocked against her sternum for weeks whenever she moved too fast. She had expected, stupidly, that handing over evidence would feel like sacrifice and handing over a key would feel administrative. The opposite was true. The logbook had always belonged to history once she found it. The staff key belonged to mornings: the kitchen door, the storage shed, the generator lock, the cheap authority of being allowed through first.

She set it on the table. The plastic clip left a wet print in the chemical film on the cloth.

"I'll clear the room in the morning," she said.

Imogen nodded once and drew the key toward her, treating the exchange like one more form that had to clear before sunset.

June cleared her throat. "For the record, employment status does not alter evidentiary relevance. Ms. Cordero, you may still be contacted for follow-up statements. No one associated with this site is to enter the restricted channels without authorization until we issue notice."

Tomas let out a breath through his nose at that. A laugh would have been too generous for the sentence.

Rhea picked up the recorder case. "Then issue it fast," she said. "The weather is not waiting for your notice."

June nodded, which in another room might have counted as respect.

People began standing because meetings ended that way even when nothing in them had ended. Chairs scraped. Water bottles rolled underfoot. Elaine was already on her phone by the time she reached the ramp, saying the words serious exposure in a voice meant to sound brave. Mara Bix kept her hard case hugged to her side like luggage through an airport she did not trust. June stayed behind long enough to ask Beto where the freezer was, and Beto, with a politeness so formal it bordered on insolence, said, "This way, ma'am," and led her down the steps.

That left Mina on the platform with Imogen, Tomas, and Rhea for one brief ugly pocket before the next task broke over all of them.

Imogen spoke first, quieter now, but no less hard. "I meant what I said this morning. You did not save us from anything simple."

"I know." Mina's voice felt scraped clean of performance. "That wasn't the point."

"That is exactly the problem with you."

Then Imogen went after the state investigator because that was her gift and punishment both: even wounded, she moved toward the paperwork.

Tomas looked at the bent hose clamp in his hand, only then noticing the flattened circle. "Well," he said. "Now everybody's unemployed in spirit if not on paper."

It was almost a joke. It hurt like one.

Mina opened her mouth and closed it again. There was too much she could say to him and none of it would arrive unsoiled. He touched two fingers to the rail in a gesture so brief it might have been for balance, then headed down toward the dock where county trouble waited in a different uniform.

Rhea stayed.

Once the officials left, the platform kept only work sounds: the generator thrum below, June's voice, Beto opening the freezer lid. Somewhere out in the mangroves a spoonbill clattered into flight. The chemical film on the rail caught sunlight and turned it slick.

"You were legally sloppy in three places," Rhea said.

Mina barked a laugh despite herself. "Only three."

"I'm allowing for heat." Rhea adjusted her grip on the recorder case. Up close, the Sharpie stripe on her thumb had spread into her cuticle. "But the order held."

Act first, documentation second, interpretation third: Mina had followed it because Rhea had asked and because obedience to the right person, at the right late moment, could still feel like dignity instead of surrender.

"Did I just make your housing worse?" Mina asked.

Rhea did not pretend not to understand the question beneath the question. She looked out past the platform, toward the channels where no one was supposed to go now.

"Yes," she said. "Probably."

When Rhea turned back, her face held no recoil, no polite distance, none of the leaving Mina had already started to brace for.

"That does not make it wrong."

She touched Mina's forearm with two fingers, exactly once, in full view of nobody who mattered anymore, then took the recorder case and went down the steps toward the bat lab, toward contaminated files and grant trouble and a life that had not chosen ease either.

Mina stayed where she was after everyone else had found a direction. Heat climbed. Her job was gone. By evening her name would move through email chains and board texts and whatever shallow legal language people used when they wanted human mess to harden into categories. Some version of disgraced again would attach itself to her before the sun went down. That part was already done.

Below the platform, the blackwater shifted under mangrove shade, mirror-dark until a breeze wrinkled it. No blue showed, and no miracle announced itself on cue. Down by the dock, voices carried back in pieces: permit, access freeze, contamination, review. Nobody on land was calling the eel a windfall or a savior now. They were handling it like evidence. Ugly terms, but she could live with ugly terms if they bought the creature one more week of not being turned into a logo or a specimen tray.

By the time the launch engines coughed back to life and carried the officials away from the platform, no one had called the eel a discovery in hours. What stayed behind was a thinner kind of noise: work, damage, official names. That, for now, was the nearest thing to protection Mina knew how to give.
