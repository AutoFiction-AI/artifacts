# Chapter 8

Mina lay on top of the sheet because underneath her the bed was soaked through with sweat, storm damp, and what the three of them had done to it. By morning the thin blanket had kicked down around her shins, damp at the edge. Tomas's forearm lay heavy across her waist, engine-rough and hot. Rhea was behind her with one bare shin hooked over Mina's calf, the loosened tail of her braid rubbing damply at Mina's shoulder blade. The room smelled like fish grease left over from the meal, wet wood, mosquito repellent gone sour on skin, and sex cooling into something less glamorous and more human. It should have felt like consequence already. Instead, for one foolish minute, it felt like shelter.

Rain had quit sometime before dawn, but the camp was still leaking from every seam. Water ticked from the screen frame onto the warped sill. The pilings answered each little wind shift with their old throat-clearing groan. Somewhere out in the mangroves a spoonbill let out a cracked, offended bark, and another one answered as if the dark itself had stepped on its foot.

Shelter was the dangerous shape. Bargains had edges. You could bruise yourself cleanly against them. Tenderness spread out. It got into corners. It turned a room into a witness.

Mina shifted and felt the tacky pull between her thighs, the ache in the small of her back from being pressed against the wall first and the mattress after, the salt-dried place on her breast where Rhea's mouth had been. Tomas made a sleepy sound into the back of her neck. Rhea's fingertips found the old prop scar on Mina's left calf and traced it once, not absentmindedly. Rhea never touched anything by accident.

"Tell me what happened at BrightCurrent," she said.

The words were quiet enough that the rain ticks around them almost swallowed them. That made them worse.

Mina kept looking at the screen, where a fat bead of water gathered, trembled, and let go. "You want the newspaper version or the one the donors use when they act brave?"

"Neither." Rhea's voice still carried sleep in it, but only there. The rest was clean. "I want the one that made you look sick in that tunnel."

Tomas's arm loosened from Mina's waist. He was awake now. She could tell by the stillness. When he slept he twitched once in a while, like he was bracing against wake even on a mattress.

"Rae," he said, rough with sleep, "it's not even light."

"I know it's early." She moved a little closer behind Mina, warm and direct, no apology in it. "Ask later and she'll have her boots on. I don't want her boots on."

Mina gave a breath that almost passed for a laugh. "That's romantic."

"It isn't." Rhea's thumb rested over the scar in Mina's calf, holding it there as if it were a line on a page. "I don't want the polished version. I don't want the one where you were young and stupid and a corporation happened to you. I want the one with your hands in it."

The room held. Tomas did not tell his wife to stop. He did not tell Mina to answer. He just lay there with the weight of his hand no longer touching her, and the absence of it was somehow more intimate than the hand had been.

Mina stared at the water-dark screen and tried, out of reflex, to build a smaller story. There had been bad management. There had been Jonah. There had been a series of choices that only looked separate if you spread them far enough apart. None of that was false. None of it was the thing.

"There was a cooler," she said.

Rhea did not move. Tomas swore once, not loudly.

"Start before that," Rhea said.

So Mina did, because lying there between the two of them with Tomas's heat still in her skin and Rhea's mouth mark on her shoulder had made any graceful omission impossible. The shame of it was not only what she had done. The shame of it was how badly she had wanted to be chosen for it.

"Jonah Pike was senior guide there then. You know his name?"

"I know the type," Tomas said.

Mina almost told him he did not, because Tomas's contempt ran practical and Jonah's had always run curated, wrapped in field charm and older-man patience and the kind of attention that felt expensive when nobody had ever spent it on you before. But Tomas would hear the defense in that, and it would be a defense, so she let it go.

"He was older," Mina said. "Not old. Just old enough to make every room tilt toward him if he wanted it. Sunburned neck, always peeling there, and he kept fennel seeds in an Altoids tin because he said marsh water got in your mouth through your own teeth. He had one pair of sunglasses he refused to throw out even after one arm snapped. He fixed them with orange fly-tying thread and made it look like a field trick instead of being cheap."

Rhea's fingers shifted once against Mina's calf. Keep going.

"I was twenty-six and freelancing. BrightCurrent wasn't paying me scientist money. They were paying me smile-for-the-board money and take-the-journalists-out money and sometimes translate-for-the-guys-who-actually touched the tanks money. Jonah was the one who knew which donors mattered, which researchers drank too much, which doors stayed propped open if you wanted to feel like the real work had brushed past you. I was sleeping with him."

Tomas made a sound through his nose.

"You can say it," Mina said.

"Why? You already said it."

When hurt got deep enough in Tomas, he went quieter instead of louder. Mina felt it like weather pressure. She kept going before she could start arranging herself around it.

"There was a board visit that week. A storm sitting offshore, one of those ugly April ones that can't decide if it wants to be a rehearsal or a real thing. The wetlab was locked down because some compliance team from upstate had started asking where every live specimen sat at every hour. Everybody was tense and pretending not to be tense, which is a BrightCurrent specialty." She swallowed. "Jonah found me after the donor boat came in. I was still in the guide polo. My shirt was stuck to me under the arms and I smelled like sunscreen and bait shrimp. He said he needed a favor."

Rhea said, "What kind?"

"A carrying kind." Mina shut her eyes once. The memory came back cold first. For years she'd trained herself to forget the loading corridor chill behind the wetlab, the way it felt like stepping into a grocery case after a whole day in Florida heat. "He had the cooler already by the service door. White marine cooler, chest-high on me if I hugged it wrong, blue cord cinched over the lid because the latch was busted. There were two little vent holes near the handle. Sloppy drill job. He'd done them himself."

"You remember that," Tomas said.

"I remember too much when I'm scared." Mina opened her eyes. "I picked it up and whatever was inside hit the side. Hard enough I almost dropped it. The plastic body of the cooler knocked my hip bone."

Nobody spoke.

"Did you know it was alive?" Rhea asked.

"I knew there was something alive in there. I knew he didn't want it on a log. I knew he said if the compliance people got a clean count they would bleach a whole line and call it disposal." Mina turned her face into the damp pillow for a second, then back to the room. "I told myself I was helping save something. That's the version I used for a while."

"And the less flattering version?" Rhea asked.

Mina laughed once, with no humor in it. "The less flattering version is that he asked me because I was useful and eager and already in his bed. And I liked being useful to him. I liked that he was letting me carry the dirty thing instead of leaving me out with the donors."

Tomas sat up. The sheet slid down his stomach. Mosquito bites rose over his shoulder in small red welts, and a bruise-dark bite mark Mina had left on the muscle above his chest looked almost black in the storm-gray room. He scrubbed a hand over his face and stared at the wall, not at her.

"Where'd you take it?" he asked.

She could hear the question changing shape in him. He was not asking for absolution yet. He was already sorting route, vehicle, distance, exposure.

"To his skiff behind the service dock. He told me to keep my head down because the loading camera covered the north corner and not the south. He knew the blind spots." Mina could feel the slick handle digging into both palms again, the meltwater running down her forearms. "I set it in the bow. He threw a tarp over it like that made it invisible. Then we went out."

"Out where?"

"Not far. South intake side. There were feeder cuts off the annex then before the mangroves swallowed half of them. He killed the motor and let us drift because he liked drama." She tasted rot at the back of her tongue, memory making its own weather. "When he took the tarp off, the whole inside of the cooler had a blue leak to it. Not bright like a sign. More like a live bruise under plastic."

Tomas looked at her now. "Jesus."

Mina nodded because there was nothing else to do with that word.

"He opened the lid and there was a bag inside, tied off, water slapping hard enough that it sounded like somebody trapped a hand in there. One eel. At least one by then. Maybe he'd moved the others earlier, maybe there had only ever been one, I don't know. The animal was longer than my forearm and thinner than it looked in the tanks, all muscle and glass and that wrong blue light under the skin." She could see it too clearly: the animal twisting against the clear bag, light slipping up her wrists, Jonah grinning because he knew awe made people easier. "He said it had been in a monitoring line they were about to kill."

"Did you believe him?" Rhea asked.

"Enough." Mina wiped the heel of her hand over her mouth. "Not because it made sense. Because I wanted him to be the kind of man who did rescues instead of theft."

Rhea's fingers left Mina's calf at last. The loss of that touch felt earned.

"What happened next?" she asked.

Mina turned onto her back so she did not have to speak into the mattress. The ceiling above them wore a water stain shaped like a gull with a broken neck. Tomas sat at the edge of the bed, elbows on his knees. Rhea propped herself on one forearm, braid come half loose, eyes dry and exact.

"He told me to hold the bag open under the water." Mina's voice flattened on its own. That happened when she got close to panic. "Said if we just dumped it on the surface it'd bash itself stupid on the hull. So I knelt in the bow and put my hands in. The creek water was warm. The bag wasn't. I cut the tie with his knife because my hands were shaking too hard to untwist it. He laughed at that."

She could feel the knife again, the cheap serrated edge sawing plastic, Jonah's knee against her ass to steady her or control her, she had never decided which. The eel had hit her wrist on the way out, all panic and muscle, then it was in blackwater, light smearing into tannin, then gone.

"So yes," Mina said. "If you need the clean sentence, I helped put one into the creek."

The mosquito near the ceiling found its pitch and held it. Tomas kept his elbows on his knees but stared harder at the floorboards, thumb grinding once over his palm. Rhea's hand flattened on the sheet instead of coming back to Mina's leg.

Rhea asked, "And the first eel you built your name on?"

The real question finally surfaced under all the others, the one Mina had been dodging since the day she stepped onto the stilts and heard Beto tell the spoonbill story with that half-mocking grin.

"I don't know," she said.

Tomas looked at her hard enough that she felt stripped by it. "That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I've got." Anger came up fast because shame had opened the door for it. "Months later Jonah took me back out near the same system, different cut, more wild cover. He'd heard rumors from one of the maintenance guys, or he said he had. We saw blue in the water and I got the photo everybody knows. Maybe it was the same animal. Maybe it was another one from the same tank line. Maybe the creek already had something running loose through it before I touched that cooler. I don't fucking know. I told the story like discovery because discovery was the only version that let me keep my job."

Rhea's face did not harden exactly. It became more still. "Did Jonah tell you it was wild?"

"He used the word feral."

That earned one small, ugly laugh from Rhea. "Of course he did."

"He said that like it made him honest. Like he was splitting hairs instead of moving the whole argument." Mina stared at the ceiling stain. "I let him. Then I let myself. By the time people wanted panels and quotes and footage and some clean little miracle rising out of contaminated water, I was already in too deep to say maybe the first one came through a cooler I carried for a man I was sleeping with."

Tomas got off the bed. He did it slowly, as if speed would make him stupid. He found his shorts on the floor, shook a condom wrapper loose from one leg, and stepped into them without bothering to look embarrassed. Mina had seen him naked enough now that shame should have shifted elsewhere, but this was different. This was clothing as retreat.

"You had me stealing county fuel for that?" he asked.

"For what's here now," Mina said too fast. "For the creek now. For what I've seen under the spoonbills."

He looked over his shoulder. "Don't answer the question I didn't ask."

She shut her mouth.

Rhea sat up and pulled the sheet across her chest, not modest, just cold now that sweat had started drying. "You used him for access at the start," she said. The words landed without theatrics, a tool laid on the table. "Did you decide not to tell him because you thought it would end that?"

Mina could have lied with more finesse if they had asked her in clothes, in daylight, across a kitchen table with coffee in front of everybody. Here the truth had less distance to cross.

"At first? Yes." The word came out like a splinter. "After that it got worse, because then I wanted him for himself and I still didn't say it. Then I wanted you and I still didn't say it. That isn't nobler. It's just messier."

Tomas looked away first. That hurt more than if he had called her a liar to her face.

"I'm gonna make coffee," he said, and left the room.

They heard him go down the short hall, the old boards giving him away one complaint at a time. The kitchen cabinet banged once. Then again, gentler, because even angry Tomas knew the walls at camp were basically gossip with nails in it.

Mina sat up. The sheet fell to her waist. Her skin cooled too fast in the damp air. "You asked," she said to Rhea. "You got it."

"Not all of it." Rhea retied the loose end of her braid with the black elastic she'd kept on her wrist through the whole night. It had left a pale groove in her skin. She pulled the braid tight enough to make the elastic snap. "But enough."

"If you want me out, say it plain."

Rhea slid off the bed and bent for her underwear, one controlled movement after another, like she was packing delicate equipment instead of dressing after sex. Mina watched her pull Tomas's shirt over her head. The hem landed high on her thighs. Guano and rubbing alcohol still haunted the fabric she'd shed on the floor last night. Rhea always smelled faintly of work, even naked.

"I don't want you out," she said. "I want you to stop editing yourself after people are already implicated."

She left the room with the shirt hanging crooked over one shoulder. Mina stayed on the edge of the bed long enough for the silence to become humiliating. Then she found her shorts under the chair, wiped herself with the least-damp corner of the sheet because the room offered nothing cleaner, and followed.

Day had come up mean and colorless after the storm. The screened walkway threw a gray lattice over the boards. Water dripped from the roof into the bucket Beto had shoved under the bad seam last week, each hit carrying that hollow aluminum bong that made the whole place sound makeshift even on quiet mornings. Farther off, from the generator shed, came one brief scrape of metal and Beto's irritated voice telling something inanimate to quit acting possessed. He had the particular talent of scolding machinery as if it were a younger cousin who had embarrassed the family.

In the kitchen Tomas stood at the counter in cutoff work pants, barefoot, measuring coffee with the same attention he gave carburetors. He knocked the spoon twice against the tin to level it, then once more because his hands were not steady enough on the second measure. His phone lay faceup by the sink. The cracked corner of the screen flashed and went dark, flashed and went dark. Mina did not need to read the district notice banner to know it was work.

Rhea sat at the table with one knee up, Tomas's shirt riding higher on her thigh. She had rebraided her hair, but too fast; the plait ran slightly crooked down her back. Beside her was the bat recorder case with a strip of silver duct tape peeling off one corner. She worried it with a thumbnail while she watched Tomas pour water into the kettle.

Nobody told Mina not to come in. That made the room harder than welcome would've.

The coffee smell arrived before the kettle got hot, bitter and dark enough to cut through wet wood and old fish. Tomas did not look at Mina when he said, "Door."

She turned and pushed the screen shut behind her. Mosquitoes battered against it from the outside, frantic with the post-rain hatch.

"Thank you," he said. Still not looking at her.

Mina leaned her hip against the counter by the icebox and instantly regretted it because the wood was damp and cold. Her body had gone alert in that stupid animal way shame produced, reading every object as if it might be used in evidence. Three mugs drying by the sink. Fuel key lanyard hanging from the wall hook. The grease-smudged county map rolled tight in the umbrella stand. Rhea's recorder batteries lined up like blunt silver teeth. Things people used. Things people paid for. Things she had entered through appetite and need and now saw as collateral.

Tomas killed the flame under the kettle before it whistled. "Walk me through it again," he said. "Only this time do it like I'm the part that matters."

Rhea said, "That's fair."

Mina let out air through her nose. "All right."

He finally turned. He had put on his reading glasses, the cheap pair he only used for small print and map edges. They made his anger look almost administrative, which was somehow worse. "From the point where you knew there was a live animal in the cooler."

"I already told you."

"No. You told me the part that hurts you." He set three mugs on the counter harder than necessary. "I'm asking for the part that would've been useful to me before I started burning county gas and signing my name under it."

Rhea's eyes flicked to Mina once. Answer him.

So Mina did. She kept her hands flat against the counter because they wanted to start moving, and movement would only turn into defense.

"He had me carry it from the service door to the skiff because I was less visible than he was in that moment. That's one. He knew the camera blind spot. That's two. He took me through the south intake cuts because it was close and because those channels already touched BrightCurrent drainage if you knew where the concrete still held under the roots. That's three." She swallowed. "And when it was in the creek, I didn't report it. Not to BrightCurrent, not to wildlife, not to anybody."

Tomas poured the water. "Why not?"

"Because I was in bed with him and because I wanted the animal to exist outside the lab and because if I reported it, I was in it too." Mina kept going because the uglier sentence was usually the real one. "And because part of me liked having seen something nobody else had. I liked that more than I liked being clean."

He nodded once, tight. "There you go."

"That's not the full story either," Rhea said.

"No," Mina said. "It isn't."

The kettle had steamed up Tomas's glasses. He took them off, wiped them with the hem of a dish towel, and leaned both hands on the counter.

"The photo," he said. "The one everybody knows. Was it the same eel?"

"Maybe." Mina hated the word so much she almost bit it in half. "I know that's useless."

"It's not useless," Rhea said. "It's unstable. There is a difference."

Tomas gave her a quick look that said he appreciated the distinction and hated the situation that required it.

Mina said, "The light looked different in the creek than it did in the cooler. In the cooler it was too clean. Hard blue. It made the plastic glow like a cheap toy. In the creek the tannin smeared it out, made it look older, more animal. I told myself that meant wild. It could just as easily have meant water." She rubbed her thumb over a notch in the damp wood counter. "When I got the photo later, I told the story that way. That I'd found something impossible in blackwater and documented it. Which I had. I just couldn't say what else I might also have done."

Rhea asked, "Did Jonah push you to go public?"

"Yes."

"Because he wanted credit?"

"Because he wanted deniability. A young guide saying she'd seen something uncanny in the creek played better than him saying he'd moved biotech inventory around after hours."

"And you let it play."

"Yes."

Tomas picked up the sugar jar, set it down again without using it, then laughed once under his breath like a man who had found mold in a place he already knew was wet.

"I don't give a shit that you were screwing him," he said. "That part isn't the news. The news is you knew from the start this wasn't just some bad old article hanging around your neck. It was a live piece of the story. And you let me ferry you into it blind."

Mina made herself stay still. "I didn't know it was the same creek under this camp then."

"That's not what I said."

"I know." She pushed off the counter because leaning had started to feel like cowardice. "I knew enough to tell you there was a cooler and a man and a maybe-not-wild eel at the root of all this. I didn't. That's true."

"Why?" Tomas asked.

The simplicity of the question scraped her because it was not simple at all.

"At first because I needed your boat and your fuel key and I thought telling you would make me look exactly like what I was." She forced herself not to look away. "Then because you kept being kind to me in ways I hadn't earned, and that made it harder. Then because Rhea came into it, and once she did I wanted one room in my life where the story wasn't already poisoning the floor."

Rhea's mouth tightened, not with surprise. With recognition.

"That room was made of us," she said. "You don't get to call it separate just because that would have been convenient."

"I know."

"Do you?" Tomas said.

Mina turned to him. He had gone very calm. Calm put the sharpest edge on him. The county fuel key hung six inches from his elbow, brass cut with a little half-moon bite near the head where years of use had worn it. Mina had kissed that wrist once on a dock after he'd unlocked the pump cage for her, thinking at the time that she understood the texture of risk. She had understood almost none of it.

"I know now," she said.

"No, you knew then. You just preferred the version where it stayed expensive and erotic instead of stupid and possibly criminal." He pulled the key off the hook and let it swing from one finger. "You know what part makes me feel like an idiot? Not the sex. Not even the lying, exactly. It's that every gallon I moved for you went on a county sheet with my badge number next to it, and you were standing there already connected to a biotech mess big enough to sink people."

"Tomas," Rhea said.

"No, let me have it right." He turned to her, but he was still talking to Mina. "If the county comes asking later what I knew, what do I say? That I thought I was sneaking a disgraced guide into protected water for a look at a weird fish? That I didn't know there was a stolen specimen and some charismatic swamp bastard in the foundation? That my wife was in bed with her too, so it all felt more modern and less illegal?" He shook his head once. "That doesn't get funnier if I say it slower."

Mina felt heat come into her face despite the damp cool of the kitchen, and the accusation was not what did it. The heat came from hearing how little dignity any of them would be allowed if it ever reached official ears. The room had no language for what they were that could survive paperwork.

"You can say I kept it from you," she said.

"I'd love to. Sounds great. It also sounds like I was too dumb to ask the right questions before I started siphoning public fuel." He hooked the key back onto the wall so hard it slapped wood. "I've got a skiff note that hits whether I sleep or not. County doesn't care if I was horny. County doesn't care if I believed in your miracle fish. County cares if the numbers line up."

The key kept tapping the wall in shorter swings. His phone flashed beside the sink and went dark again.

Mina said, "I know what your job costs you."

"Do you?" Tomas's smile was brief and nasty. "You know what it bought you."

The key he'd slapped back on the hook still trembled against the wall. Mina watched the brass half-moon flash and had to swallow before she answered. She remembered kissing the inside of that wrist on the dock, salt and gasoline on his skin.

Rhea peeled the strip of duct tape all the way off the recorder case and folded it into a tight gray square between her fingers. "Stop flattening it," she said.

Tomas looked at her. "I'm not."

"You are, a little. She used you. You used her. So did I, if we're being honest enough to bother." Her voice stayed even, steady as a hand around glass. Discipline, not softness. "The difference is that she withheld information that changed the risk profile for everybody else in the room."

Tomas gave a short nod. "Yes. That's the part."

Rhea turned to Mina. "Is there more?"

It would have been easy to think she meant another lover, another eel, another beautiful half-ruined story. Rhea did not mean easy things.

"No more coolers," Mina said. "No other secret release I know about. No paper trail in my drawer. Just the old photo, the old story, and Jonah telling me feral like that made him a philosopher instead of a thief."

"Have you talked to him recently?" Rhea asked.

"No."

"Would he contradict you?"

Mina thought of Jonah's easy grin, the way he could make a lie sound like shared memory. "He'd reframe me. Same difference."

Tomas snorted. "That kind always thinks wording is a life raft."

"Sometimes it works," Mina said.

"Not for me."

Silence moved through the kitchen, not empty this time but occupied. The kettle ticked as it cooled. Outside, one of the spoonbills started up again, harsh and ridiculous and unmistakably alive. Beto dropped something metal in the shed and swore at it in two languages. A gust pushed mangrove stink through the screen. The whole camp smelled rinsed and not cleaner for it.

Rhea stood and came to the counter for her mug. Tomas poured for her first without being asked. Then he poured for Mina and left the mug near the edge of the counter, close enough for steam to reach her wrist and far enough that he did not have to hand it to her.

The coffee was black and violent. Mina drank anyway. It burned the roof of her mouth and gave her somewhere specific to put the feeling.

Rhea leaned against the sink and held her mug in both hands. "Here's what changes," she said. "No more delayed truth because the timing feels bad or the room feels good. If there's another layer, I get it before you get to decide whether telling me would cost you something."

"You both do," Tomas said.

Mina nodded.

"Say it," he said.

She looked at him. He was tired. More than angry, maybe. The crooked nose, the grease that never fully left the cuticles, the mosquito bites stippling both forearms, all of it made him look not like the man who had ferried her into secrecy but like the county worker who would still be expected at a dock in an hour with wet boots and signed logs and no useful category for what had happened in his house last night.

"You both do," she said.

He accepted that with the smallest movement of his chin.

Rhea asked, "When you saw the animal in the cooler, did it look like what you've been seeing here?"

Mina looked down into her mug long enough to watch the black surface steady. "Yes and no."

Rhea held her mug in both hands and said nothing.

"The body, yes. The movement, yes. That same way of looking like it belonged to root shadow even when there wasn't any. But the cooler light felt wrong. Too clean, like it had never touched mud. Here the glow comes through tannin and silt and whatever the creek has done to it. Here it looks lived in."

"Selection pressure," Rhea murmured, almost to herself.

Tomas rolled his eyes without heat. "Can you two maybe not do science pillow talk right now?"

The joke died in the coffee steam. Rhea gave him a look that would have cut most people. Tomas took a drink of coffee instead of grinning back, and the silence carried all the old shorthand with none of the relief.

Mina said, "I don't know if I'm describing the animal or my guilt."

"Both are data," Rhea said.

"Christ," Tomas muttered, but not viciously.

The phone on the counter buzzed again. He looked at the screen this time. Mina watched his eyes move once, twice.

"Standing water checks," he said. "Whole district's gonna be out bitching about mosquitoes acting like mosquitoes."

He set the phone down and rubbed at the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger. Mina noticed the pale dent there from the reading glasses, the small human mark of a thing he probably would not remember doing by lunch.

"I need one more answer," he said.

She braced.

"If you'd told me before last night, before any of that in there" - he jerked his chin toward the bedroom hall without looking at it - "would I still have been the man with the boat?"

The question stripped her so bare that for a second she could not protect herself from it with intelligence. Rhea and Tomas shared that gift in different dialects. Between them they could cut past every version Mina liked to perform.

"At the very start?" she said. "Yes, mostly."

He looked down into his mug and laughed once, because the truth had at least spared him the insult of politeness.

"After?" she said before he could move away from the first answer. "No. After, you were Tomas. Which is worse for me, not better."

His mouth shifted, but the expression never softened into anything she could work with. It left him looking resolved more than wounded, which was worse.

"That doesn't refund the gas," he said.

"I know."

"And it doesn't make me feel smarter."

"I know."

Rhea set her mug down. "Nobody here gets to come out looking smart."

The mug clicked once against the sink and settled. Mina opened her mouth to argue, found nothing but heat there, and closed it again.

Tomas picked up the map tube from the umbrella stand and knocked it once against his palm. "Next time," he said, and Mina looked up because the phrase itself felt like a reprieve she had not earned, "if we're going back into those cuts, I want every rotten piece of history on the table first. I don't care if it makes you look filthier. I don't care if it makes me sound stupid for being here. I want the whole damn creek, not the brochure."

Rhea said, "Before the next boat."

Tomas looked at her. "Before the next gallon."

Mina closed her hand around the hot mug until it hurt. Outside, the post-storm light was finally gathering into actual morning, turning the mangroves from one black mass into many separate black things. The camp creaked. The bucket under the roof seam rang again. Somewhere along the walkway Beto started whistling a song he never remembered all the words to, only the tune, and the tune kept losing arguments with a bent hinge. Farther out, beyond the mangroves, the rookery was getting loud again, all stolen sticks and bill-clatter and the kind of indecent spectacle BrightCurrent used to sell as stewardship.

No one reached for her. No one told her to go.

Tomas pulled the county map from its tube and snapped it open across the table, trapping one corner with his phone and another with the sugar jar. "Show me the cut."

Mina set down her mug. Coffee ringed the paper. She put two fingers on the south intake branch where the concrete ran under the roots.

Rhea came to her shoulder in Tomas's shirt, close enough for the damp cotton to brush Mina's arm, and stopped there. Tomas uncapped a carpenter's pencil with his teeth and boxed the channel where Mina's fingers had been.

When the screen door shut behind him, the map stayed open on the table, her route trapped in pencil between the sugar jar and Rhea's recorder case.
