# Chapter 12

Mina let the 3:17 low go by in darkness. By early afternoon the daylight low tide gave the route back a little at a time, as if the mangroves were reconsidering a bad decision. First the blackwater slipped off the prop roots and left them shining to their elbows. Then the surface broke into tannin puddles and narrow runs that showed every trapped mullet and drowned leaf. The mud path under the camp was visible in sections now, a dark braid through the roots toward the old concrete mouth of the BrightCurrent intake. Mina stood on the screened walkway outside the kitchen with the taste of bad coffee gone metallic in her mouth and watched the path appear.

She had spent the morning pretending to help Beto inventory lantern batteries and cracked dock lines, his burn-marked wrist flashing every time he turned the clipboard over to study the anatomy terms he kept copying there for trade-school prep. Imogen took a donor call in the office with one silver hairpin already working loose from her bun and spoke in that careful bright voice she used when money was somewhere on the line. Tomas, who usually came up by noon smelling of two-stroke and mangrove mud, had not come up from the county dock. Suspended men did not have much reason to visit other people who had helped suspend them. Rhea had gone to the bat lab with her braid still damp at the nape from washing guano out of it and shut the door with the soft finality that meant work, or distance, or both. The camp creaked in the heat. Screens tapped. Somewhere under the stilts a spoonbill chick made the rusty hinge noise they made when hungry enough to sound furious.

If she told Imogen where she was going, the annex would become an asset before sunset. Imogen would not even have to lie to herself. She would say evidence, chain of custody, institutional responsibility, and under all of it Mina would hear twenty-seven thousand dollars in repairs, leaking guest rooms, and the electric bill the camp was always somehow one week away from not paying. If she told Tomas, he would hear one more thing she needed after his pay and pride had already gone under for her. If she told Rhea, Rhea would ask the exact question Mina least wanted to answer before she had anything in her hands: what outcome are you going down there to serve?

So she said nothing. She took a headlamp from the utility shelf, a pair of nitrile gloves from the bat lab cabinet where Rhea kept them by the alcohol wipes, a screwdriver, her phone wrapped in a freezer bag, and a roll-top dry sack that still smelled faintly of diesel from the months when Tomas had shoved it at her before night runs. She added two gallon zipper bags and her field notebook. The gear looked absurdly small for the amount of history she was walking toward.

The path off the boardwalk started in shell grit and ended in suction. Mud took her boots to the ankle on the second step and to the calf on the fifth. She worked through it with the patience of somebody who had spent enough years in wet ground to know anger only wasted oxygen. Red mangrove roots knitted the air low and close. Oyster shells caught at her pants. Mosquitoes found the strip of skin above her collar and lit there as if she had rung a dinner bell. When she shoved through a stand of black mangrove saplings the leaves painted salt and dust across her forearms. The camp sounds dropped away behind her until she could hear only insects, the occasional plunk of something small abandoning one puddle for another, and her own boots unsticking with a noise too intimate to ignore.

She had come this way once with Rhea, at night and with enough wanting in the air to make the tunnel feel charged instead of condemned. Then the old BrightCurrent sign had risen out of the dark like a threat she had already deserved. Now, in dead low tide and flat daylight, the whole route looked less haunted than administrative, like a forgotten service access and a piece of infrastructure the wetland had spent years trying to digest but had not yet managed to soften into innocence.

That was worse.

The intake mouth sat where the roots thinned and the mud steepened, a concrete spillway half buried in mangrove litter. The old sign above it was almost unreadable under mildew and salt bloom, but the corporate font survived. BrightCurrent Aquatic Systems. Annex Intake. Authorized Personnel Only. Somebody had once believed the warning would last longer than the company.

Mina stood there long enough to listen. There were no voices, no engine from the county channel, and no donor laughter leaking over the boardwalk. There was only a drop somewhere inside, steady enough to count against, and the dry rasp of crabs moving under debris.

She switched on the headlamp even though daylight still reached the first yards. The beam turned the damp concrete white and mean. Rusted emergency-light housings jutted from the wall. Cable straps hung empty where conduit had been torn free years ago. A stain on one side marked the highest flood line, chest high on her and ridged with old silt. She had the stupid thought that the place had kept that mark for her, the way some people kept notches in a doorframe for a child's growth.

The route narrowed fast. She had to turn sideways past a buckled section where the wall had shifted and peeled old paint into curls. Water stood in the floor channel no higher than her boot soles, thick with mosquito larvae and floating gray skin from rotted insulation. The air changed after ten steps. Outside had been heat with movement in it, swamp breath and wind slipping past leaves. Inside was stale and fixed, like the place had been holding one wet exhale since the storm and would never bother taking another.

Her body knew the smell before her mind named it. It was brackish water, mold, battery acid gone sweet, dead filters, and a faint medicinal note under everything, as if disinfectant had lost a long fight and never been removed.

She did not belong to the annex in any official way. That was one of the lies she had built her ruin around. She had been guide-adjacent, contract labor adjacent, the pretty bilingual field interpreter they put in front of visitors when BrightCurrent wanted mangroves translated into grant language and donor awe. She had worn pressed shirts with the company logo for weekends at a time and then gone back to hauling bait coolers and wiping down skiffs for people who made three times what she did to talk about ecosystem futures in air-conditioning.

But she knew this architecture. She did not know this exact tunnel, maybe, or every branch of it, but she knew the grammar of it. Lab annexes loved washable floors and corners too sharp to trust. They loved door windows wired with mesh, yellowing safety placards, and sinks mounted at heights meant for compliance more than comfort. They loved making working water look cleaner than water ever was. Jonah had loved the backstage feel of places like this, the way a badge and a grin could make a laborer believe she had been invited into importance.

She reached the first steel door and found it half latched, swollen in its frame. The badge reader beside it was a dead black oval furred with salt. She put the screwdriver against the jamb and leaned until metal shrieked and the seal gave. The sound went down the corridor in both directions. She froze and listened again.

Nothing came back except the delayed drip and the soft scratch of something small in the wall.

The room beyond had once been a receiving vestibule. A desk still crouched against the wall under a layer of collapsed ceiling tile. A whiteboard leaned facedown in silt. Plastic bins lay cracked open, their contents fused into unidentifiable knots: visitor booties, cable ties, laminated placards, a handful of dead markers. The waterline here had reached the top drawer of the desk and spared the upper cabinets. That spared section felt deliberate, obscene in its efficiency, as if the flood had been careful to ruin the things easiest to ruin and leave the things capable of accusing.

She started with the cabinets. Most held only warped binders and maintenance manuals puffed thick with moisture. She flipped through one until the pages tore under her glove and released a smell like wet cardboard left under a porch for a year. Another contained safety goggles, all clouded. Another had a row of empty lanyard hooks and a plastic bin labeled TEMP BADGES in black marker. The bin had split. Its contents were gone.

She set it down too hard. The crack of plastic against metal made her flinch.

The annex was bigger than the tunnel suggested. A corridor ran left toward what had once been wet rooms and right toward offices. On the wet side she found cylindrical tank housings with their fiberglass skins split and peeled back, manifolds furred with salt crystals, lengths of hose coiled like pale snakes in the corners. Someone had stenciled numbers above the tanks, running from 1A through 3F. The floor drains were wider than ornamental fish needed and wider than anything a donor tour had ever been told about.

In one tank a rind of dried scale shimmered under the headlamp, not blue, not magical, just fish matter flattened by time into mica. On the concrete beside it lay a tangle of mesh sleeves and a length of restraint netting stiff enough to stand. She crouched and touched the net with the back of a gloved finger. It had the rasp of old teeth.

This was not a miracle, she thought. It was husbandry, containment, and failure.

The sounds in here were wrong for abandonment. Rooms with this much plumbing were supposed to mutter with pumps, trickles, and fans, with the busy courtesy noise of systems keeping life organized. The annex answered her with absence. That absence made every small sound louder: her sleeve against the wall, the little crackle when she flexed a dry patch of mold, her own breathing trapped and returned by the corridor.

She went to the offices because paper kept better on the dry side and because she did not want, not yet, to stand any longer in front of the tanks and imagine bodies turning there in tight, fluorescent circles while men with degrees called them units.

The first office held a collapsed file cabinet and a bulletin board quilted with old pushpins. The second still had a desk chair on its side and two metal shelves bolted high on the wall. One shelf had slumped under a pile of forms, but the top shelf held a black zip case streaked with mildew and a spiral notebook too thick to be ordinary office inventory. Mina dragged a chair under it, climbed up, and almost lost her footing when the seat shifted in the silt.

The zip case came down in her hands with a weight that made her stop. Tools were usually heavier than paperwork. She set it on the desk and unzipped it carefully.

Inside was a hardbound maintenance log wrapped in a clear contractor bag and two thinner folders paper-clipped together. Somebody had tried, at the last minute or in some earlier cleanup, to keep these dry. The contractor bag had done its work badly but not uselessly. The cover of the logbook was swollen and salt-stained, yet when she opened it the inner pages were only feathered at the edges. Enough ink remained.

She sat on the desk because the chair legs had rusted through, balanced the book on her thighs, and aimed the headlamp down.

The first pages were routine. They logged pump pressure checks, filter changes, salinity readings, and valve repairs. Whoever kept the book printed in compact all-caps that leaned slightly right and pressed hard enough to leave an emboss on the pages beneath. He had a habit of circling numbers twice when they mattered. She could feel her own pulse wanting plot, confession, some single spectacular sentence that would make the whole question easy. The book refused. It gave her maintenance.

July. Intake screen fouled with mangrove litter.

July. Lot 2C feed reduction due to crowding.

August. Generator switchover lag during noon storm.

August. Wet Room 3 dissolved oxygen alarms intermittent. Sensor replaced. Monitor sentinel line overnight.

The words sentinel line snagged in her head.

She kept going.

The entries thickened as the storm approached, handwriting driven harder, margins full of arrows and addenda. Transport was delayed. The mainland route was uncertain. Backup batteries had been pulled from the admin wing. Wet-side staff had been reduced. Someone had switched pens twice and then finished a page in carpenter's pencil when the ink failed. A faint brown thumbprint stained one corner. It could have been grease, or dried blood, or enough years of Florida humidity to make the distinction academic.

By the time she reached the page marked with the evacuation date, her shirt had stuck to the small of her back. Sweat slid down between her shoulder blades and pooled against her waistband. She did not wipe it. She turned the page with both hands, careful, as if roughness could change what the words had already done.

Mandatory evacuation in effect 1400. Nonessential personnel offsite.

Mainland transfer canceled after road closure.

Sentinel stock exceeds portable live-haul capacity.

Counsel advises no mortality event in place if power loss likely.

The next line was written darker than the rest, the letters cut into the paper.

Authorize emergency drawdown to receiving channel via east sluice if generator fails.

She read it again because her mind tried to slide past the grammar. It said receiving channel, not creek or marsh or outside. A receiving channel was something infrastructure had permission to use. It sounded pre-disposed to take whatever a building could no longer keep. Then she looked back through the office door toward the wet room and the euphemism broke apart. Tank 3A's rind of scales was no longer abstract residue. It was what the walls kept after animals had come up beating for air and rasped themselves raw against fiberglass. The restraint netting on the floor was not storage. It was what somebody had grabbed when the water came over the tank lips and living weight started knotting over the concrete.

The following page had water damage across the top third and a streak of black where ink had run, but the lower half remained legible.

1840 - Generator transfer unstable. Tank 3A oxygen crash.

1855 - Wet-side overflow began during surge pulse.

1905 - Opened east sluice and bypass under storm-loss protocol. Live material entered outgoing flow.

Counts impossible.

Bagged remaining mortality from 1A and 2B.

Report external as flood breach / research fish loss.

Do not specify luminescent line in county incident form.

She could read the sequence in the room now. The oxygen crash came first: mouths breaking the surface, bodies climbing over one another in a tank gone suddenly useless. The overflow came next: storm water, tank water, and animal panic slapping over the floor toward those overbuilt drains. Then somebody opened the sluice. They let the current do the work, sweeping the ones still strong enough to thrash and the half-stunned ones alike toward the outgoing channel. Counts became impossible because no one was stopping to peel them off the grates one by one. Bagged remaining mortality meant the ones that did not wash clear, the ones left heaped against 1A and 2B or tangled in net so stiff now it could stand by itself.

She sat very still, the book open across her knees, while a mosquito whined inside the room and failed to find a way out. The sound drilled at her ear until she slapped it without thinking and left a smear of blood and insect on her glove.

Live material entered outgoing flow.

The phrase had none of the decency of even a cowardly lie. It was worse. It was true in the language of people who believed truth could be made harmless by being stripped to systems. They had opened a sluice to an outgoing flow and called living bodies material, as if the animals had become manageable the second nobody called them animals, as if the creek had been part of the cleanup crew all along.

She looked up from the page toward the dark wet corridor and saw the tank numbers again, 1A through 3F, enough holding capacity for more bodies than she had let herself picture. Her old story with Jonah, the cooler against her thigh, the single slippery weight she had helped move because she had been stupid and hungry and wanted backstage access badly enough to confuse it with love, had always felt singular. It was damning, but it had felt singular: one theft, one lie that widened until it swallowed her name.

Here the lie had scale, a line item widening into a population.

The creek had not simply received an escapee. It had been used as disposal, weather cover, off-book habitat, a place to empty corporate risk when the pumps failed and the roads closed and nobody wanted the smell of dead engineered animals in a lab by morning.

She turned to the clipped folders.

The first held emergency cleanup notes on BrightCurrent letterhead. Most of the top pages had fused together, but the back half separated with care. There were checklists for bleach concentration, mold remediation, disposal bins, equipment losses. One page listed tank lots with boxes beside them: recovered, mortalities, presumed environmental loss. Beside the lot numbers corresponding to the wet room she'd just walked through, the handwriting changed. It was no longer neat all-caps but a tired cursive slant, written by someone who had stopped pretending the paperwork would return them to order.

3A - presumed environmental loss

3B - presumed environmental loss

3C - presumed environmental loss

3D - presumed environmental loss

3E - presumed environmental loss

3F - presumed environmental loss

Forty-seven total, maybe more. Tally uncertain after surge.

Forward revised language to legal before any county contact.

No public-facing animal imagery from wet side.

Mina let out a breath she had not noticed holding. It came back at her off the walls, thin and ugly.

The tally was forty-seven.

Forty-seven engineered eels or more, spread across six lots and a storm ledger. Some had rasped themselves raw in bad water. Some had gone through the east sluice with the floor runoff. Some had ended in plastic bags from 1A and 2B once the room stopped thrashing.

And still the relief that tried to rise in her chest was real. That disgusted her almost as much as the paperwork did.

Because for one split second, before she got hold of herself, the logbook seemed to offer what she had wanted from the eel since the first rumor: corruption older than her, larger than her, with paperwork to prove it. The creek's blue secret no longer had to start in her two hands and one cooler. A corporation this big could take the blast radius. She could point to it and say there, there was the original sin, and maybe her own could shrink back to human size.

Then she read the notes again, and the relief turned rancid in her mouth.

Her stomach clenched so hard she bent over the desk. The scale of it only widened the stain. The creek had taken the company's dumping and one woman in heat for access in the same mouthful. It kept both. Whatever rolled blue under the spoonbills now carried discharge water, private lust, a wet room full of animals beating the surface, and the hands that finally quit holding them. If the thing still deserved the word miracle, it had survived bad oxygen, concrete, legal language, and those hands at all.

She forced herself to photograph every legible page. She pulled the phone from the freezer bag, turned the flash off, shifted the headlamp higher, and took one image after another, then the cleanup notes, then the cover with its title stamped in silver under salt bloom: ANNEX B WET SYSTEMS MAINTENANCE LOG. Her hands stayed steady because the alternative was losing the pages to clumsiness and having to explain that later to everyone. She slid the logbook into one zipper bag, the folders into another, and only then looked up.

The office had grown dimmer, not because of the light outside but because she had been in here long enough for the place to close around her. Heat sat in the air without moving. The odor of old water had warmed under her lamp into something meatier, more intimate. She needed to leave.

She bent to check the lower drawer of the desk before she did, partly from instinct, partly because people who tried to save documents usually shoved other things in with them. The drawer was stuck. She knelt in the silt, set the screwdriver into the seam, and pried until rust popped and the metal screamed. The drawer came open six inches and dumped a clot of warped paper, a dead roach the size of a thumb, and three laminated cards into the mud.

One card landed faceup.

She knew the shape before she knew the face.

The laminate had bubbled at the corners. The clip was orange with rust. A strip across the top carried the faded BrightCurrent logo and beneath it, still legible under mildew, her own name.

MINA CORDERO

VISITOR INTERPRETER

ESCORT ACCESS - ANNEX B

The photo was not kind in the way young photos often were. Her hair had been down because Jonah had said donors trusted women more when they looked windblown instead of severe. Her smile was too ready. She looked like someone pleased to be near the thing that would later ruin her, someone who still believed proximity to money and research and men with security clearances meant she had stepped into a larger life instead of volunteering to decorate it.

For a moment she forgot the heat, the mud soaking through one knee of her pants, even the logbook beside her.

That badge had hung from a blue lanyard that stuck to the back of her neck in the annex humidity. She remembered the exact drag of it now. She remembered Jonah hooking a finger under the strap and snapping it lightly against her skin while he talked through tank lighting and said she had a better eye than half the technicians in there. She remembered how cheap the compliment had been and how fully she had eaten it. She had been old enough to know better and broke enough to keep pretending knowledge changed hunger.

She had wanted in. That was the part shame kept trying to rewrite into victimhood or manipulation alone. Jonah had used her. Of course he had. He had liked women who mistook complicity for intimacy and he had been practiced enough to make backstage access feel erotic, almost noble. But she had wanted the door to open for her. She had wanted the badge on her chest and the little reader light turning green. She had wanted to stop being the girl outside the lab translating bird calls for rich amateurs while men inside talked patents and environmental futures over trays of catered shrimp.

The memory came back in pieces sharp enough to cut.

Storm watch was already posted. Staff were packing hard cases. Somebody was swearing over transport bins in the corridor. Jonah was sweat-slick and calm in that curated way of his, as if weather only proved he was locally born enough to deserve trust. The cooler was white and scuffed against his shin. Her badge was clipped crooked because he had put it on her himself, grinning, telling her this was what serious work looked like when the brochure cameras went home. She had carried the cooler because carrying it made her feel chosen. She had not felt innocent. She had felt chosen.

She turned the badge over. On the back, under the emergency contact number and the old liability disclaimer, somebody had written in Sharpie: RETURN FRONT DESK. The words had bled at the edges from damp. She imagined herself after the theft, after the rush and the fear and the sex and the way Jonah had made the whole thing sound temporary, tossing the badge into a pocket, then losing it here in the scramble when the storm shifted the timetable and everybody started moving too fast.

Or maybe she had dropped it earlier. Maybe it had slipped behind the desk while she leaned there with Jonah's hand between her shoulder blades, listening to him talk like the line between field and lab was a joke for smarter people to step across. The precise moment no longer mattered. The object did.

Her name was in the annex.

It was not only in the gossip version, or in the disciplinary file and the old photo caption and the ugly private story she had finally told Rhea and Tomas after too much tenderness made silence rotten. Her name was here, in the drawer, in the room with the tank numbers and the storm logs and the east sluice.

She sat back on her heels and laughed once without humor. The sound came out raw enough to startle her. If she had found only the logbook, some corner of her would have tried to use it. It would not have happened publicly, maybe not even consciously, but the temptation would have been there: lay the whole thing at BrightCurrent's feet, let the company own the release, the creek, the damage. Every sentence in that version would have been true. None of it would have been complete.

The badge made sure of that.

She was still trying to understand the exact composition of the damage when the corridor answered with a small new sound: water ticking against metal where there had been only drip before. The tide was turning. The outgoing trickle in the floor channel had already thickened enough to push leaf threads past the office threshold.

She moved quickly then, because drowning in a condemned biotech tunnel would have been too stupid even for her. The badge went into the same zipper bag as the cleanup notes. She slung the dry sack crosswise and stood, one knee stiff with cold mud, while a skin of water spread across the office floor and slicked the silt under her boots. She swept the headlamp once around the room to make sure she had not dropped the phone or the screwdriver. The beam clipped a row of framed safety posters on the far wall, all buckled behind cracked glass: BIOSECURITY IS EVERYONE'S JOB over gloved hands and sealed drains, evacuation arrows marching cheerful people away from the wet side. Even now the posters sounded managerial, offended by circumstance.

Outside the office the incoming water had reached her soles and was carrying fresh threads of leaf litter inward. She stepped into the corridor and kept moving. The wet side had turned from ruin into mechanism again: drains taking hold of the runoff, the half-buried sluice gate waiting at the far end, water worrying the bases of the tanks. She did not pause beside the numbers this time. By the time she reached the swollen door, the channel was at her ankles and tugging hard enough to pull silt around her boots. She had to shoulder the door twice before it opened enough to squeeze through. A smear of rust marked her sleeve.

Then she was in the tunnel proper again, moving toward daylight while the floor channel filled around her ankles and climbed cold against her shins in the low spots. The sound changed first. Insects returned, then the far racket of the rookery, then the structural complaints of the camp above the mangroves, all the built noises that had seemed irritating that morning and now struck her as proof of a world still willing to say what it was.

The mud outside was no longer lying open in separate sections. The returning water had started knitting the puddles together and glossing over the braid of track she had used on the way in, swallowing the edges of her old boot prints. She killed the headlamp and blinked against the brightness. Afternoon sun hit the blackwater pools between roots and turned them mirror-hard. For one ugly second she expected blue under the surface, some staged confirmation, some animal rising on cue to join the paperwork and complete the lesson.

Nothing moved but a stranded silverside and the minute busy mouths of fiddler crabs at the rim of a puddle.

She climbed the root path slower than she had come down because the slick roots wanted her weight sideways, the dry sack slapped against her ribs, and every step needed careful placement. Halfway up she looked back. From this angle the intake vanished almost completely under shadow and mangrove tangle. It was hidden by design when the tide was right and hidden by landscape when it was not. It was easy to miss by water and easy to miss on foot if you had not already been taught where to look.

That, too, had felt erotic once. Secret routes and locked places had seemed like intimacy instead of what they were, which was just another labor a man older than her wanted done cheaply.

By the time she reached the shell path under the camp, her shirt was pasted to her skin and the gloves were full of sweat. She peeled them off finger by finger and stuffed them into an outer pocket of the sack. The boardwalk above her threw striped shade across the mud. Somebody in the kitchen dropped a pan. Beto laughed at something, then coughed hard enough to bend double, the sound carrying through the slats. Ordinary camp life was still going above her in all its frayed particulars: lantern batteries, donor calls, audit fallout, Rhea in the bat lab with her neat hard questions, Tomas somewhere trying to imagine nine thousand dollars of debt without a county paycheck under it, and Imogen looking at a half-corrupted hydrophone file and seeing possible rescue.

Mina put one hand on the bottom rung of the ladder to the boardwalk and stopped there.

With her palm pressed hard against the rung, she understood what she had actually been asking the eel to do. Not innocence. She was not stupid enough to ask the creek for that. She had wanted cleaner evidence, a correction sharp enough to lever her life a few inches off disgrace, something real and beautiful and wild enough, or at least not provably hers enough, to do the work confession never had. She had wanted discovery to rearrange blame into something survivable.

The logbook killed that fantasy on the page where live material entered outgoing flow.

The badge killed the rest.

She kept her hand on the rung until the aluminum bit a ladder pattern into her palm. Then she climbed. The dry sack dragged at her shoulder, and inside it the recovered badge kept tapping the logbook, plastic on paper, her name keeping time against BrightCurrent's storm ledger. She crossed the boardwalk without looking toward Imogen's office and went straight for the survey room off the bat lab.
