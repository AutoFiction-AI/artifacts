# Chapter 6

Peppermint had gone flat on Mina's tongue before sunrise. She took the observation platform at dawn and spent the first hour scrubbing yesterday's guano dust out of the deck grooves and mostly redistributing it. By eight-thirty the rail burned the heel of her hand. Sun came off the bay white and flat, then broke pink where it caught the spoonbills lifting over the mangroves. One wet breeze turned the place into hot pine, bird shit, lemon cleaner, and the bait shrimp somebody had forgotten in the ice chest under the stairs.

Beto was on the outside of the rail again, balancing on the beam in his half-size-too-big camp polo while he looped fresh bunting around a post. His sneakers knocked wood in a careless rhythm. One wrist showed the pale crescent burns he had collected on the kitchen flat-top before he had learned to stop grabbing hot pans like they insulted him personally.

"If you fall in front of donors, I will tell them you are an interpretive exhibit," Imogen said.

"Then tell them to tip," Beto said, not climbing back inside until he saw the look on her face and decided he preferred employment.

Imogen had forced her silver hair into a pinned knot that made her look almost ceremonial until the wind lifted one strand loose over her cheek. She wore the camp's faded green field shirt with a clean scarf tucked at the throat, the closest thing Rookery Key had to formal wear. Behind her the main house screens rattled and the generator thrummed through the planks. The camp always sounded temporary on mornings when it most needed to look permanent.

"Preview weekend is not difficult," she said, which meant it would be. "These people would like to feel rugged while remaining hydrated. They would also like to imagine they are the reason the spoonbills still exist. Give them enough truth to keep them useful."

Mina adjusted the spotting scope and kept her face arranged in something capable and donor-safe. "I can do that."

"I know you can. That is why I am irritated already." Imogen looked past her at the channel, at the heat beginning to tremble over the blackwater. "No freelancing. No folklore. No mention of the mangrove annex, no matter what anyone thinks they saw in the brochure's background photo. Keep it to breeding plumage, nesting pressure, hydrology at a gentleman's level, and how much deferred maintenance a generous check might relieve."

"Hydrology at a gentleman's level," Mina said.

"Do not improve the phrase."

Rhea came up the stairs carrying a clipboard and a soft case of optics against one hip. Her dark hair was braided tight enough to pull the planes of her face sharper than usual. She smelled faintly of alcohol wipes and old guano, because she had already been in the survey room changing batteries before breakfast. She gave Mina nothing first. She counted birds under her breath in the trees beyond the platform, then rapped two knuckles against the donors' reserved chairs as if assessing whether any of them might be stupid enough to clap at a bird.

"The males are active on the east edge," she said. "If the group gets loud, they will move deeper."

"You hear that?" Imogen said. "Even the bats want professionalism."

Rhea's mouth twitched. It was the kind of almost-smile that made Mina's skin feel too tight under her shirt. Last night that same mouth had said her name into the wet dark of the intake tunnel like it was something she had noticed and not yet decided whether to keep. This morning Rhea's face gave her nothing public at all.

That blankness should have made things easier, but it only left Mina standing in the bright with nowhere to set what still burned.

Below the platform, at the shell path by the dock cart, Tomas was unloading coolers of bottled water for the guests. He moved in a county work shirt gone dark between the shoulder blades with sweat, broad back bent over the cheap plastic handles. Once, the platform seemed to catch at him; he paused with a cooler handle biting into his fingers, shifted the weight higher against his thigh, and kept moving, the hitch quick and familiar and dangerous in its own separate way. Even from here she could smell the diesel and river rot he brought around with him from the dock.

Imogen checked her watch. "Board-adjacent people in nine minutes. Actual board people in twenty, if the ferry captain is lying in the usual direction. Mina, you have the platform. Rhea, stay visible and technical. Beto, if I hear you tell the osprey mating joke again, I will mail you to trade school in pieces."

"That seems expensive," Beto said.

"Only in postage."

He grinned and took the lemonade bucket toward the shade table. Rhea set up a second pair of binoculars. Tomas thumped the last cooler into place and disappeared toward the kitchen. Mina stood for one second in the bright before the guests came, her palm flat on the hot rail, and tried to settle into the version of herself she could still perform without thinking. She would start with species and move to weather. She would give people the shape of awe they already expected to buy and make the wild feel exact and expensive and just out of reach.

It worked, at first.

The donors came up in polished little clusters, breathing harder than they wanted anyone to see. They wore linen hats and carried good binoculars, with shirt collars the color of cloud. One man wore a bird club patch from Connecticut and shook Mina's hand with the solemnity of a treaty signing. A woman in a sleeveless white blouse asked whether the spoonbills were "always this pink" as if they might have dressed for her. Another guest, a narrow retired dentist named Hal who clicked his tongue every time he focused his lens, kept a pencil tucked behind one ear and wrote every species in a pocket notebook with tiny furious block letters.

The one who recognized Mina came up last. She had the hard tanned knees of somebody who still gardened despite a doctor advising otherwise, and a strip of floral kinesiology tape disappearing under one sock. A tiny silver tin rode in the pocket of her fishing vest. Every few minutes she tipped fennel seeds from it into her palm and ate them like medicine. Her name tag said `Lydia Fane`, though Mina would not know her actual name for another hour because rich birders loved a shortened version of themselves. She adjusted her hearing aids before lifting her binoculars, turquoise shells glinting in the sun.

"You can smell the nests from here," Lydia said, almost cheerfully.

"That means they're doing well," Mina said. "Or badly for any fish stupid enough to swim under them."

That bought the right laugh. Mina took them through the rookery in the clean order she had learned years ago and never entirely lost, not even after the fines, not even after hearing rooms and clipped voices and the deadened way old colleagues had begun introducing her without the adjectives she had once earned. She showed them the spoonbill pairs working sticks into the mangroves, the white ibis moving in underneath to steal whatever they could, the black skimmers slashing the shallows farther out where the channel widened. She talked about low water concentrating prey, about how the adults' pink deepened with carotenoid-rich feeding, about why the rookery sounded like a machine shop if you listened long enough. The group leaned where she wanted them to lean. They forgot their knees. They forgot to perform one another for a little while.

Then Lydia lowered her binoculars and stared directly at Mina's face with the unblinking concentration of someone trying to place a song she hated.

"I know you," she said.

Mina kept her smile where it was. "You may have been on one of my Gulf coast trips a while back."

"No." Lydia rubbed one finger along the arm of her glasses. "No, that is not it."

Hal the retired dentist kept writing without looking up. A breeze shifted. Feather dust lifted from the rail and caught in the sweat on Mina's forearm.

"Lydia," the woman in white said softly, a warning or an encouragement, Mina could not tell which.

But Lydia was already reaching for her phone, dragging the beat out because she enjoyed the little snap between suspicion and proof.

"BrightCurrent," she said. "That was it. You were the guide in the eel mess."

The platform altered around the words without shifting an inch. The boards still creaked. The spoonbills still barked and clattered in the trees. A bottle of sunscreen rolled an inch across the table because somebody had not set it flat. But every lens on the deck came down from the birds.

Mina heard herself answer too quickly. "I was a guide involved in the reporting around that case, yes."

"Involved," Lydia said. She had found something on her screen and turned it toward the woman in white, then toward Hal, who finally stopped writing. "My God, it is you. I read every miserable article when I could not sleep during chemo. The photo was everywhere."

Chemo ought to have roughened the sentence. Instead it made the appetite in it feel tidier.

The man with the Connecticut patch held out a hand as if Lydia might pass him the phone. "BrightCurrent the biotech people?"

"The glowing eel people," Lydia said. "The woman who got cited."

Mina's scalp felt tight. The sun had found the back of her neck where her hair tie had slipped. "The Gulf Wildlife Board fined me over documentation and handling violations. That matter is closed."

"Closed legally?" Hal asked. "Or scientifically?"

There it was, the neat little pleasure of blood in the water.

The woman in white made a face that suggested she disapproved of rudeness in the abstract, provided someone else did it first. "Were you not the one who claimed the animal was wild?"

Mina could have lied cleaner than this. She could have said she was not discussing old litigation on a bird platform. She could have stepped off the deck and let Imogen handle the donors. Instead she felt the old reflex come up: answer fast, answer exact, drown the room in nouns before anybody noticed the blood.

"I produced a photograph and field notes that were later judged compromised in chain of custody," she said. "That's not the same as fabricating an animal."

"Compromised by whom?" Hal asked.

"BrightCurrent?" the Connecticut man said at the same time.

"Does Imogen know?" the woman in white asked, which was the first truly dangerous question.

Below the platform somebody dropped a tray in the kitchen and cursed. Out in the rookery, a spoonbill landed badly and slapped the tops of the mangroves with its wings before settling. Lydia's phone glowed in her hand. Mina did not need to see the picture to know which one it was: her eight years younger, damp hair coming loose at the temples, one hand on the rim of a cooler, looking at a thing outside the frame with such stupid animal hope it still made her want to hit that woman in the throat.

"Imogen hired an experienced guide for a temporary contract," Mina said. "My permits here are the camp's and fully in order."

Lydia's brows lifted. "That is not quite an answer."

"It's the one you're getting."

Silence snapped over the group, quick and live.

From the edge of the platform, Rhea spoke without raising her voice. "If anyone came here for spoonbills rather than archived scandal, the east cluster is courtship-feeding right now. You will miss it if you keep looking at your phones."

It was efficient, dry, technically kind. It gave the guests a ramp down if they wanted one.

The invitation hung there, and every one of them let it drop.

Lydia kept her eyes on Mina. "I only ask because donor camps have become very casual about vetting in the last few years, and I have written checks to places that turned out to have extraordinary judgment problems."

There was the real sentence, dressed at last.

Mina's mouth filled with the taste of hot pennies from nowhere. She set her palm on the scope tripod so hard the metal bit. "If you're concerned about the camp's hiring, you can address that with the director after the walk."

"I just did," Lydia said, chin tipping past Mina.

Imogen had come onto the platform without Mina hearing her. That was how angry she was: every part of her under control at once. She took in the lowered binoculars, Lydia's lit phone, Mina's face, Rhea's stillness. Only then did her attention cut toward the channel below, where Tomas had stopped halfway up the stairs with a case of canned seltzer braced against his thigh.

"Is there a problem?" Imogen asked.

Lydia smiled at her with practiced pain. "Only a question of disclosure."

"There is a question of timing," Rhea said. "The birds are flushing."

Imogen ignored that. "What exactly is being disclosed?"

Lydia turned the phone. Imogen let the screen hold her for one beat too long. Long enough for Mina to know she recognized the photo immediately. Long enough for Tomas to start climbing.

"It seems," Lydia said, "that your guide this morning is Mina Cordero. I remember the BrightCurrent hearing rather vividly."

Tomas reached the top stair and set the seltzer case square against the post before he spoke. "Then maybe remember she's working and you're on camp time, not in a hearing."

Imogen's head snapped toward him. "Not now."

"It's exactly now," Tomas said. His voice stayed low enough that the guests had to lean for it. "She came up here for birds. She doesn't get to turn staff time into her own little hearing for free."

The Connecticut man stiffened. Lydia's expression sharpened, not frightened, not exactly offended. Pleased, almost. She had turned a donor preview into a scene and now a working man had joined the cast.

"Tomas," Mina said. "Leave it."

"Why?" he said. "So she can keep taking bites out of staff?"

Rhea kept her clipboard braced to her ribs. A spoonbill clattered deeper in the mangroves; two guests shuffled backward from the rail. "This platform is above disturbance threshold. Either continue the tour or clear it."

It was such a Rhea sentence that Mina almost laughed, which would have been unforgivable. Tomas muttered something low and sharp in Spanish. Beto had appeared at the stairs behind him, frozen with a tub of sweating lemonade in both hands, eyes wide in a face still too young for all the ways adults could rot in public.

Imogen stepped forward, taking the space back by force of posture. "Mrs. Fane," she said. "Mr. Pelham. Everyone. Mina is here under a temporary employment arrangement that complies with camp policy and permit obligations. If you have concerns about an unrelated past matter, you may bring them to me after the walk."

"Unrelated?" Lydia said. "A biotech scandal involving a supposed wildlife discovery seems rather related to how one markets ecological expertise."

"This camp is not a biotech firm," Imogen said.

"No," Lydia said. "It is a camp trying to raise money."

Every person on the platform heard the money in that sentence. That was the true knife: Lydia had named the camp's hunger in front of the whole deck.

Imogen's voice cooled another degree. "Then let us not waste your morning."

She gestured toward the mangroves as if this had always been a planned transition. The guests obeyed because wealthy people often did obey when given the chance to pretend they had not been feeding minutes earlier. Hal put his notebook away but not before Mina saw him write one last line. Lydia slipped her phone back into her vest and tipped a few fennel seeds into her mouth. She had won exactly as much as she wanted.

Mina continued the tour because there was nothing else to do. She talked about nesting chronology with her face burning in the open light. She explained how spoonbills clacked their bills in threat displays while the deck smelled of sunscreen and judgment. The group watched the birds again, but not like before. Now and then a pair of binoculars drifted off the mangroves and snagged on her with the furtive concentration people reserved for accident scenes and old lovers. Tomas went back down the stairs in a violence of silence. Rhea wrote something on her clipboard with the same neat hand she used for bat counts, not once breaking the line of her notes for Mina.

By the time the walk ended, the story had outrun the platform.

Beto proved it without meaning to. He was pouring melting ice into a drain by the kitchen when Mina came down the boardwalk with the scope over one shoulder. He kept his voice low, but the lowness did not help.

"That lady's showing your picture around in the breakfast room," he said. "The one with the eels. I mean, maybe not to be evil. But also definitely to be evil."

Mina set the scope case against the wall. "What picture?"

Beto winced because they both knew that was a stupid question. "The article one. You're holding a cooler. Mr. Pelham zoomed in on it so much the face got all blocky."

There was a fry-cook towel hanging from his back pocket, damp at one corner, and an anatomy flash card stuck halfway out beside it. The card showed radius and ulna in clean blue ink. He had probably been quizzing himself between ferry loads again. Mina looked at the card because it was easier than looking at his pity.

"Did Imogen ask for me?" she said.

"Not yet. She asked for the good coffee, which is how you know it's bad."

"Helpful."

"I try."

He shifted the ice bucket from one hand to the other. The joking dropped out of him in a way it had not before. "For what it's worth, I didn't know all that. I just knew there was some kind of board mess. Everybody in the kitchen knows now."

Everybody in the kitchen knew now, and the word sat in the heat like a nailed thing.

On the far side of the yard, Tomas was smoking beside the generator shed, even though Imogen hated it when anyone smoked near the fuel cans. He drew on the cigarette like it had insulted his family. Rhea crossed from the lab to the main house with her clipboard tucked flat to her chest, her path straight as survey tape. She moved like a person choosing the exact amount of herself the day would be allowed to touch. Neither of them broke course toward Mina.

The ferry whistle sounded from the bay. More guests were coming.

Imogen opened the breakfast room screen and said, without raising her voice, "Mina. Inside."

***

The breakfast room at Rookery Key pretended to be casual while sweating through every sign of strain. The wicker chairs had been painted over so many times their woven joints looked clogged. One section of floor sagged near the coffee station, and a braided rug had been laid over it as if aesthetics could replace carpentry. On donor weekends Imogen put hibiscus in mason jars and folded cloth napkins around the cheap silverware, which only made the cracks in the windowsills more obvious.

Lydia Fane sat at the far end of the room under the paddle fan, eating papaya with a seriousness usually reserved for prayer. Her phone lay faceup beside the plate. Hal Pelham was bent over it from the next table, reading. When Mina entered, neither of them rearranged their faces into embarrassment. That was the humiliation of this sort of thing. Nobody in the room had to act cruel because the structure had already done the work for them.

Imogen did not take Mina into the office. She stopped beside the coffee station, where staff came for refills and guests passed through on the way to the platform stairs. It was public enough that no one could miss it and casual enough to deny it was a spectacle, which made it worse than theatrical.

"You should have told me the scale of it," she said.

Mina glanced once toward Lydia's table. "You knew there'd been a board citation."

"I knew there had been fallout. I did not know your face was still a hobby for rich insomniacs."

Across the room somebody laughed too loudly at a different conversation. Silverware clicked. The fan pulled warm air over the buffet and redistributed the smell of eggs, coffee grounds, and ripe fruit. Mina could feel sweat collecting under her bra band and nowhere to wipe it.

"I wasn't hiding that I was Mina Cordero."

"No," Imogen said. "You were allowing me to imagine that mattered less than it does."

Lydia's turquoise hearing aids flashed when she turned her head. She was not trying to overhear. She was simply hearing.

Mina kept her voice low. "What do you want me to say?"

"For the moment? Nothing." Imogen folded her arms. "I want to know whether there is anything about that BrightCurrent disgrace that can still touch this camp in a regulatory or donor-facing way beyond gossip."

Rhea came in through the side screen before Mina could answer. She must have been summoned or had simply read the current in the room and followed it. She set her clipboard on a chair and stood near the coffee urn, posture straight, face unreadable.

"Donor-facing, yes," Rhea said. "Regulatory, not on present facts."

Imogen gave her a flat look. "That is a careful distinction."

"It's the useful one."

Mina turned to Rhea then, because the sentence had landed with the clean coldness of forceps: accurate, bloodless, and not remotely kind.

"There aren't active restrictions on me guiding guests here," Mina said. "The penalty was civil. I'm still making the monthly payments. I don't have a current wildlife ban."

Imogen's eyes sharpened at monthly payments, maybe because she had not known it, maybe because debt always made people look less salvageable in the wrong rooms. Mina could already feel the next withdrawal reaching for the same week's wages, and the bunk itself would go the minute Imogen told her to clear out. "You did not think to mention that your name would invite exactly this kind of scrutiny on donor weekend?"

"I thought if I did my job well enough people would look at the birds."

"That is charmingly naive for a woman with your history."

The side door struck the frame once behind Tomas as he came in from the yard, sun and cigarette smoke still on him. He kept his voice flat. "If this is about the woman from the platform, don't hang the bill on Mina. Lydia's the one who ran it up."

Imogen squared the stack of paper napkins by the coffee urn into a hard white block before she answered him. "Why are you in my breakfast room?"

"Because everybody can hear you from the boardwalk."

"Then everybody can hear me ask you to leave."

He did not. He planted himself by the drink cooler, forearms marked with old prop nicks and new mosquito welts, jaw set around anger like he did not trust himself to spend it all at once. "She didn't drag her own name into this room. Lydia did, and now breakfast's buying everybody a turn."

"Ran it up," Imogen repeated. "An excellent phrase. I shall put it on the annual appeal."

"Don't do that thing," Tomas said. "You know exactly what I mean."

"I do know exactly what you mean. That is why I am tired already."

Rhea touched two fingers to the edge of the coffee table, not fidgeting, grounding. "This isn't helped by making it a second scene."

Tomas showed his teeth without anything like a smile. "And it's helped by what, exactly? You talking like a permit memo?"

Something flickered in her face then vanished. "It's helped by deciding which risk is real."

"The risk is they eat her alive and you all help set the table."

There were six words in that sentence Mina wished he had not said in public and one pronoun she could not stop hearing. He had skipped Mina, skipped guide, and landed on her, which exposed more than a hand on the waist.

Imogen heard it too. One knuckle tapped the napkin stack once as she ran the same quick arithmetic over Tomas, Mina, then Rhea. Mina watched the possibility of a second scandal take shape and, by some miracle of self-preservation or exhaustion, fail to fully bloom.

"Enough," Imogen said. It was quiet. Quiet from her was worse than shouting. "Mina, collect your things after lunch."

The room changed again. Even Lydia set down her fork at that.

Mina felt the words in her stomach before she understood them, the way a rotten floor gives an extra inch underfoot before the drop registers.

"Imogen," she said.

"No. I am not staging a debate." Imogen's chin lifted toward the guests, toward the open room, toward the platform beyond. "You have turned my preview into a reputational event."

Tomas took one step forward. His voice stayed clipped. "You cut your best dawn guide at lunch and by four you've got refunds, gossip, and nobody steady on that platform."

"Keep talking about refunds in front of my guests," Imogen said. "I would like the grant people in Naples to hear that too."

He did not stop. "They're going to hear it anyway. Lydia leaves here and it's checks, calls, all of it."

"Ability is not the only criterion for public trust."

"Public trust," Tomas said, and laughed once without humor. "No. It's money."

Rhea's voice came in level and harder than before. "The issue isn't whether the guest is contemptible. The issue is whether she can cost the camp money."

Mina turned on her. "That's what I am now? A line item?"

Rhea finally lowered the clipboard from her chest. The delay before she answered hurt more than if she'd snapped. "Right now you're a labor category with a search trail."

It should have enraged Mina. Instead the sentence landed like cold hardware: useful, ugly, impossible to argue with in front of witnesses. Rhea's thumb stayed hooked under the metal clip of her board.

Tomas swung between them as if he could still catch the room before it sealed. "You're both talking like she's freight."

"No," Rhea said, thumb still hooked under the metal clip. "He's making it worse because he thinks leverage only counts if it happens out loud."

"And you're making it better by acting like she's already a memo."

"I'm making it legible to the person with the power to throw her out today."

That finally shut him up, not because he agreed, but because the sentence hit the exact machinery of the room. Imogen controlled Mina's payroll, her bunk, the guest schedule, and the one week's wages standing between her and a late Board payment.

Imogen exhaled through her nose, almost a laugh, almost a surrender to arithmetic. She turned to the wall calendar beside the pantry door where Beto had marked ferry arrivals in blue grease pencil and somebody else had ringed next week's spoonbill walks in red. Mina watched the hitch of her index finger over the schedule: a full dawn platform, two skiff runs, an evening lecture, one sick volunteer, and no spare guide worth putting in front of donors.

The camp's finances had hands around every throat in the place.

"No," Imogen said finally. "Not after lunch."

Nobody moved.

She kept one hand on the calendar's warped edge. "If I remove you today, I create a second story: camp in chaos, guests abandoned, staff instability. I do not have the margin for that. You will work through spoonbill week. You will take every dawn shift and every donor-facing obligation already assigned. You will be so competent and so dull that these people get tired of saying your name."

Mina could hear the fan, the forks, the distant creak of the platform. "And after that?"

Imogen turned from the calendar. "After the last spoonbill run, you are finished here."

Lydia Fane took a sip of coffee three tables away. The cup clicked back into the saucer. Mina had never wanted so badly to throw something breakable.

"That is a week," Mina said.

A week meant six hundred and forty dollars before taxes, one more Gulf Wildlife Board payment kept current, and seven nights before her shirts came off the hook by the bunk and into whatever bag she could find.

"Yes." Imogen's voice gentled only enough to become more brutal. "That is the amount of time your utility currently exceeds your damage."

Tomas said one word, loud enough to carry to Lydia's table. "Fuck."

"You may spare me the masculine outrage," Imogen said. "If any of you had warned me this might happen, I would have structured the weekend differently."

Mina nearly said, I did warn you, but I said it in the skimpy language of somebody hoping shame might stay abstract. The sentence died before it got to her mouth. Imogen was not wrong. That was the part that made anger feel thin.

Rhea picked up her clipboard. "There is one more question."

Imogen turned to her.

"If another guest raises it publicly, what line do you want staff using?"

Rhea already had her pen uncapped. Tomas stared at her like she had priced Mina by the pound. Mina wanted somebody to say she was worth more than her usefulness, but the question gave Imogen something to grab besides the spectacle.

Imogen answered without hesitation. "Staff say that Mina is a qualified temporary guide employed under current camp policy, and that any historical regulatory matter is closed and not relevant to guest safety. If pressed further, direct them to me and only to me."

"Good," Rhea said.

Tomas barked out a laugh. "Good?"

Rhea let the clipboard strike her thigh with a flat slap, finally allowing some temper to show. "Yes. Good. Because now there is a line. You don't survive public pressure by telling the truth louder than the people with money. You survive it by making them repeat themselves until they sound ugly."

For the first time since Lydia had spoken Mina's name on the platform, somebody in the room said something that belonged to neither appetite nor management. It did not comfort her. It did not try to. But it gave shape to the day.

Imogen straightened a stack of napkins that did not need straightening. "Mina, return to the platform at four for the estuary light talk. Wear the clean shirt this time."

Mina brushed the front of her shirt. There was guano at the hem and a thumbprint of rust from the scope clamp. Nothing any guest had noticed before the phone came out.

"Yes," she said.

"Tomas, back to whatever county fiction is paying you today."

"You can't seriously expect her to smile through this afternoon."

"Of course I can. That is what I am paying her for."

The word paying hit Tomas hard enough that he pinched his taped thumb until the knuckle blanched. When he raised his head again, bitterness and helplessness had sharpened his face into something older. The sight of it steadied her anyway.

Rhea was already moving toward the door. On her way past Mina she stopped just long enough to adjust the fallen collar on Mina's camp shirt, two fingers, efficient as fieldwork. No one watching would have called it intimate. That was the point.

"Don't answer fast this afternoon," she said quietly. "It makes them smell blood."

Then she was gone into the glare.

Tomas caught the touch. His jaw tightened. "That's your advice?"

Mina kept her grip on the scope case. "Don't start."

"I'm not starting anything. I'm saying this place is using you."

"This place was using me before breakfast."

"And you're fine with that?"

She finally faced him. Up close the salt had dried pale on his shirt and there was a nick on his thumb wrapped in electrical tape where he had caught it on a prop housing. He looked like work, anger, and bad decisions made flesh. God help her, there was comfort in that.

"I'm fine with one week," she said. "After that, you can have whatever speech you want."

"One week until she boots you."

"One week till the last spoonbill run."

He understood then. The channel was open, the water was low, the breeding peak was on them, and this was the last bright chance before the season shifted. His face changed around the knowledge, settling into the same hard alignment she felt in her own ribs.

"You still think the creek is worth this?"

Mina glanced across the room. Lydia laughed at something Hal said and touched the silver fennel tin in her pocket like a rosary. Beto hovered in the kitchen door with a stack of plates and the solemn expression he wore only when adults were bleeding in ways he could not bandage. Outside, the mangroves flashed once with pink wings, then a chain of irritated clacks traveled deeper into the rookery and one spoonbill lifted off the outer branches to settle farther back in the leaves.

"I think the creek was worth this before I was any good at regretting things," she said.

Imogen heard that much. "Then regret on your own time. Four o'clock, platform."

Mina picked up the scope case and left before the room could take anything else off her.

On the boardwalk, the heat had thickened enough to make the screws in the planks smell metallic. Somebody had spilled sweet tea near the steps and a seam of ants was working it. The camp had gone about its business with the extra attention of a body touching a sore tooth. Kitchen staff lifted their heads and dropped them again. A volunteer carrying laundry watched her one beat too long. From an open guest room window came Lydia's voice, delighted now, retelling the story with the briskness of a woman who believed bad luck became anecdote the moment it belonged to someone else.

Mina took the back path toward the utility shed because she could not bear the main house for one more minute. The shed was barely more than a roof, a table, and a wall of tools salted into permanent decay. It smelled of rope, bleach, and old bait. She set the scope down and braced both hands on the workbench until the splintered plywood pressed crescents into her palms.

She did not cry. She was too hot for that, too angry, too publicly skinned. What came instead was a bright mean clarity. She had one week: seven dawns, maybe six if weather turned, six hundred and forty dollars if Imogen let the whole run stand, one more Board payment kept current, and one week before the bunk stopped being hers and her clothes had to come off the hook. That was enough time for the spoonbills to go fully feral, enough time for donors to keep repeating her name, and maybe enough time for the blackwater under the rookery to give up another blue turn if it meant to.

Somewhere behind her a board creaked, a tread too measured for Beto and too quiet for Imogen's anger.

"I wasn't going to defend you to Lydia Fane," Rhea said from the doorway. "She feeds on resistance. It would've helped her."

Mina kept her hands on the bench. "I noticed."

Rhea came one step inside. In the shed's shade her face looked less cut from stone and more tired, which was worse in a different way. She lifted Mina's right hand off the bench, worried a splinter loose from the heel of her palm with her thumbnail, and let the hand go. "If I'd stepped between you and Lydia, she'd have stayed for blood. If I'd argued fairness with Imogen, she'd have heard liability and fired you anyway. So I gave her a sentence she could use. That wasn't the part where I wanted my hand on the back of your neck and your feet off that platform."

The sentence moved through Mina slowly. It was desire offered without softness, which made it typical Rhea and cruel only because it was exact.

"You called me a labor category."

"In front of Imogen, yes."

"That felt wonderful."

"I know." Rhea leaned a shoulder against the doorframe. "It also kept her from firing you on the spot."

Mina turned then. The shed cut the light into hard bars over Rhea's shirt and braid. "You always know the correct thing to do once it has made everybody miserable."

"That's not always true." A pause. "Often true."

Against her will, Mina laughed once. It hurt her face. Rhea tracked that laugh as if cataloging a rare animal.

"You have the week," Rhea said. "Use it."

"That sounds like an order."

"No. Orders are clearer."

Outside, Tomas shouted for Beto to bring him the fuel key for the dock cart, and Beto shouted back that if Tomas wanted it he could come get it with the rest of his emotional maturity. The noise of them carried through the stilts and screens, absurd and alive. Mina stood with Rhea in the doorway and thought of how thin every wall here was, how impossible privacy had become, how humiliation did not end when the witnesses left. It soaked into structures. It made a camp sound different.

"Does everybody know now?" she asked.

Rhea did not lie. "Enough people know."

That was the day's true sentence, though no one said it aloud again. Enough people knew, enough money was in play, and enough damage had already been done that Mina only had so much time left to be useful before she got thrown back into whatever came next.

Rhea pushed off the frame. At the door she paused. "For what it's worth, Lydia Fane puts Sweet'N Low on papaya. That alone should disqualify her from moral authority."

It was such a small, dry, unnecessary cruelty that Mina nearly loved her for it on the spot.

"Get out," Mina said.

"Four o'clock," Rhea said, and went.

Mina stayed in the shed until the heat there became as bad as the room she had left. Then she picked up the scope and went to change into the clean shirt. On the hook by her bunk hung the camp name badge she wore for donor-facing shifts. Somebody had left it turned over, so that only the clip showed. When she flipped it, her own name stared back at her in waterproof black type. `MINA CORDERO`.

The letters looked newly insufficient, a waterproof label clipped over a history the badge could not hold.

She pinned the badge on anyway. If the weather held till four, she would climb back onto the bright deck and talk about estuary light as if light were not the whole problem. If it broke early, Imogen would put her wherever the camp needed another pair of hands. At dawn tomorrow she would do it again. The week bought one more paycheck, enough cash to keep the Board payment current once more, and one more stretch of nights before she had to clear her clothes off the bunk and find somewhere else to sleep.

Under the floorboards, through all the hot timber and human noise, the tide began at last to turn and the air over the bay thickened toward weather.
