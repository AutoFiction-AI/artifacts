# Chapter 4

The knock on the screen door came while Mina was still on the cot in her underwear, a towel under her back, trying not to map the previous night too accurately. Diesel still sat in her cuticles even after two showers, and the insides of her thighs still felt the corrugated airboat bench from the ride back to the stilts with Tomas. What sleep she'd managed had come in scraps and left her gummy-eyed and mean. For one hard second, Mina thought Tomas had talked, that this was a summons disguised as politeness.

At the door, Rhea stood with a coil of mist net over one shoulder and one of her shaved-down pencil stubs tucked behind her ear. The pencil was so short it looked useless, but Mina had already noticed that Rhea distrusted mechanical ones on principle. She said they jammed when the air got wet and people trusted them anyway.

"You awake," Rhea said, not asking, "or just hiding from useful labor?"

Mina dragged shorts up her legs and opened the door. "Awake."

The word left her before she could roughen it into something casual, and she heard the thin edge in it herself.

Rhea did not bother pretending not to. Her face stayed still, narrow and tired in the lowering light, braid pulled tight enough to sharpen her cheekbones. Guano and rubbing alcohol rode under the clean salt of her skin. "Good. I need another set of hands for net checks. The glamorous assistant I was supposed to have developed an urgent romance with diarrhea."

Mina leaned on the doorframe because it kept her from fidgeting. "You always recruit people like that?"

"Only the ones who look as if they know how not to scream when something with wings touches them." Rhea shifted the net on her shoulder. "Have you ever untangled anything small and angry without making it worse?"

"Birds, mostly. Pelicans, gulls, one tern that tried to blind me over a sardine carcass."

"Close enough." A dry half-smile passed and was gone. "Bats are less theatrical than pelicans. Usually."

The screened walkway behind Rhea carried the camp's evening sounds in fragments: a pan knocked in the kitchen, Beto laughing somewhere near the generator shed with the crack in the middle that always made it sound as if he had surprised himself, a guest coughing with the martyrdom of someone who wanted the whole house to know he had allergies. The building turned every ordinary noise into evidence. Mina felt that all at once.

"Why me?" she asked.

"Because you're here, and because Imogen says you can keep your hands steady when people are watching." Rhea glanced down at Mina's bare feet. "Put on shoes. We'll be in the mangroves and I'm not dragging you to urgent care over a nail."

There were a dozen better answers Mina could have made. Something light. Something that made it plain she did not feel like prey. Instead she said, "Give me a minute," like a subordinate, and shut the door before Rhea could read anything else off her face.

By the time Mina came out with her boots unlaced and still damp from the afternoon rinse, Rhea had laid the mist net coil on the walkway rail and was checking a black hard case no bigger than a lunch pail. A row of green lights blinked on its face.

"What's that?"

"Ultrasonic recorder." Rhea snapped the case shut and handed Mina two canvas bags, rolled poles, and a bundle of cloth holding sacks clipped together with carabiners. "Grant-funded proof that I can still turn batteries into footnotes."

"Sounds expensive."

"That's why I resent it." Rhea took the net again. "Come on."

They crossed the short screened run to the bat lab. Mina had passed the place every day without going in. Up close it smelled like a hot attic soaked in ammonia: guano under old wood, rubbing alcohol, damp mesh, battery heat, the faint sweet rot of fruit someone had left too long in a field cooler. Cloth bags hung on a line above a stainless sink. A milk crate held labeled vials and a tangle of cables. Rhea's data table was pinned under maps, permit paperwork, a hand lens, and three more of the pencil stubs lined in a tobacco tin.

Rhea caught Mina looking at them. "Pens die. Pencils at least fail honestly."

"That's bleak."

"It's field science."

She said it with enough flat amusement that Mina laughed before she could stop it. The sound startled her hard enough that she felt it in the back of her teeth. She had spent the whole day with the blue coil in her head, the turn of it under black water, and every conversation since dawn had felt one inch off because of it. In the hot ammonia reek of the bat lab, the laugh came out clean anyway and left her breathing easier than she liked.

Rhea was already moving, efficient without hurry. She checked the recorder's memory card, tore strips of tape with her teeth, wrote tiny block letters on the cloth bags, then tucked the pencil back behind her ear. Her hands were narrow and scarred across the knuckles, not from one clean incident but from years of wire, rock, rust, and animals small enough to make people careless.

"Where are we setting?" Mina asked.

"Edge of the shell path near the freshwater seep first. Then the cut behind the lab if traffic is good." Rhea slung the recorder strap across her chest. "The mosquitoes lift there. The bats follow."

Mina picked up the pole bundle. It bit cold through the canvas for a second before the heat took it. "You say that like everybody knows."

"Everybody with sense does."

"That leaves me out."

Rhea gave her a quick side look, almost curious. "You came here for birds and blackwater in late March. I doubt sense was the deciding factor."

The remark found Mina's sore spot with humiliating precision. Shell grit popped under her boots as she kept walking. Rhea had named the blackwater hunger cleanly enough that Mina felt a hard little click under her ribs.

They stepped off the boardwalk and onto the shell path that cut behind the lab through saw palmetto and mangrove scrub. Evening flattened the colors. The spoonbills back in the rookery had gone from pink to the color of chewed gum in shadow. Mosquitoes rose in a thin living haze over the darker soil where the seep held on through dry season. The air pressed wetly against Mina's neck. Her shirt stuck down her spine before they had gone twenty yards.

Rhea picked a narrow flyway between two black mangrove trunks as if she had been measuring it in her sleep. "Here."

The mist net came out of its bundle like a trick no one should trust. One moment it was a knotless fist of black nylon; the next it was four shelves of nearly invisible mesh floating between the poles. Rhea worked with the concentration of somebody undoing a bomb with no interest in impressing an audience. Mina held tension where she was told, raised a pole, stepped when Rhea said step. They did not touch at first. Then the line snagged, and Rhea's fingers closed lightly around Mina's wrist to shift her two inches left.

"Not there," she said. "If it bells out too much, they bounce."

The contact was brief and unsoftened. It left Mina stupidly aware of the precise place where Rhea had been.

The reply snapped out of her sharper than the moment required. "I know."

Rhea looked up, not annoyed but measuring. "Then do it slower."

Heat climbed Mina's face, not from the air. She corrected the net, this time without jerking at the line. Rhea nodded once and drove the pole deeper into the shell grit.

"Better."

The word went through her like a swallow of bad liquor, hot and useful at once. She reset her grip on the line before Rhea could see the flush climbing her face.

They set a second net lower over the cut where blackwater held under the mangroves, then stepped back. In the last light the nylon shelves vanished unless they caught the angle just right. Bats would read the opening wrong, or read it too late. Work often came down to that: building one mistake precise enough that another living thing made it for you.

Rhea checked the recorder and let it hang. "Now we wait fifteen minutes and pretend patience is a method."

"That's most fieldwork."

"Most fieldwork is mildew management with occasional mammals."

Mina wiped her hands on her shorts. "And here I thought it was revelation."

"That's donor language." Rhea leaned a shoulder against a mangrove trunk, careful of the bark. "Revelation photographs well. Battery corrosion does not."

"You should put that in the brochure."

"Imogen would kill me with a decorative oar."

Mina laughed again, this time easier. The mosquitoes found her mouth, her eyelids, the thin skin inside her elbows. She slapped one and smeared blood that probably belonged to Tomas or herself or some guest from Ohio. Rhea ignored hers until one landed high on her cheekbone, then pinched it off with absent irritation.

The recorder emitted soft dry clicks that meant nothing to Mina and, apparently, a great deal to Rhea. She listened to it with the same attention she had given the net line, head tipped, expression almost severe. In the lab and on the path she had carried the smell of rubbing alcohol and guano like a separate atmosphere, but out here another layer came through: sweat gone salt at the collar, mangrove tannin, whatever soap she used that had no flower in it and no sweetness either.

Mina made herself look at the net instead.

"How long have you been doing this?" she asked.

"Bats, or writing little fictions to universities so they keep paying for bats?"

"Dealer's choice."

Rhea considered. "Long enough to know that if someone says 'simple cave access,' they're lying to you. Long enough to carry spare zip ties in three different pockets and still never find one when I need it. Long enough to stop believing that field assistants who say they packed enough water actually did."

"That sounds specific."

"West Texas. Limestone sink. Ninety-eight degrees at midnight." Rhea's mouth shifted again, almost a smile. "A graduate student cried because his flashlight died, not because he was afraid, but because he had already eaten both of his protein bars and thought the darkness made him look unprofessional."

"Did it?"

"It made him look like a graduate student."

Mina could see it as she listened: some skinny kid sweating through his shirt in the dark, Rhea dry as bone about it. The image loosened something low in her belly and sharpened the rest of her. She wanted more of those field stories, more of the version of Rhea that stayed cutting at ninety-eight degrees and midnight. Wife, obstacle, risk factor, moral architecture: the labels Mina had been using all day softened at the corners and started to peel.

Something ticked the upper shelf of the first net.

Rhea moved before Mina heard the sound a second time. "There."

The bat was no bigger than a rolled sock, all furious life concentrated into a smear of fur and membrane, caught by one wing and both feet. Its tiny teeth flashed when Rhea's headlamp found it.

"Body first," Rhea said quietly. "Hold through the net. Keep the chest steady. I'll free the thumb."

Mina stepped in. The bat's heat surprised her. So did the delicacy. Under the nylon its ribs fluttered against her fingers with a speed that barely felt like a mammal, more like a trapped engine idling too high. She pinned gently through the mesh. The wing membrane gleamed gray where the lamp passed through it, fine enough that the shadows of the threads showed on skin.

"Good," Rhea said. "Don't drag. Let it tell you how it wrapped."

She worked with two fingers and no wasted motion, turning the bat's caught foot back through one shelf, then easing the thumb free. When Mina took the body in her bare hand, the creature bit the air and then sank its tiny claws into the web between her thumb and forefinger. The pain was almost nothing. The grip was intimate enough to feel rude.

"Bag?" Mina asked.

Rhea had already opened one. "Headfirst. Thank you."

Mina lowered the bat into the cloth sack. It vanished into the dark with one papery scramble and became only movement and heat in her hand. Rhea cinched the drawstring, wrote a number on the tape label, and glanced at Mina's skin where the claws had caught.

"You vaccinated?"

"Yeah."

"Then we're all still employed by dawn."

"Comforting."

"I try."

They checked the net again. Another bat, then none, then a long dull stretch where the mosquitoes seemed to grow heavier and the spoonbill noise from deeper in the rookery thinned into occasional bursts of complaint. Rhea logged times in her notebook with the pencil nub. When the point broke, she cut it back with a pocketknife small as a thumbnail, as if sharpening pencils in the dark were not a ridiculous life skill to possess.

On the next capture she let Mina do the whole extraction while she watched.

It was easier now that Mina had the logic of it: body, wing, foot, the gentle reverse of panic. The bat trembled in her fingers, light and furious and soft-bellied. Rhea stood close enough that Mina could hear her breathing but did not crowd. When the animal finally slid free, Mina got it into the bag without pinching a wing tip.

"You don't make animals pay for your nerves," Rhea said.

Mina's head came up hard enough to pull at the back of her neck. "What?"

"It's rarer than people think." Rhea took the bag from her, weighing it in her palm for a second. "A lot of perfectly decent field people get scared and become clumsy. You get scared and get more exact."

That landed lower than a compliment should have. Mina felt it in the same place the blue eel still lived, some interior pocket where obsession and shame kept rubbing their sore surfaces together. She looked away before Rhea could see how much it had done.

"I cut the extra out when I need something," Mina said.

"That also sounds specific."

It was. She could have named permits, gate keys, borrowed fuel, the damp black turn of an animal under the creek. She could have named Jonah years ago, or Tomas last night, or the whole gross old hunger of her life: access first, cost second, then the cheap surprise when the cost turned out to have a face. Instead she said, "Birders with hooks in pelican bills. Rich retirees who decide nesting terns need selfies. The usual."

Rhea's eyes stayed on her a moment longer than the answer deserved. "The usual is a violent category."

Mina snorted. "You should put that in the brochure too."

"I should not be allowed near the brochure."

They worked through the next hour in that rhythm, silence and brief talk, talk and the soft snap back into silence when a net trembled. Rhea showed her how to check the tail membrane for wear without manhandling, how to keep cloth bags from twisting on themselves, how to listen past the mosquitoes to the recorder's dry chatter. She explained the difference between what donors called data and what actually ended up mattering: wind, water level, which edge had been mowed, and which trees had been lit all week by guests who could not understand that floodlamps counted as habitat destruction even when they ran on good intentions.

"I spend half my time proving obvious things in expensive language," she said at one point, crouched over the recorder. "If I write 'artificial night-light encroachment alters roost use,' someone in an office nods solemnly and releases a few hundred dollars. If I write 'people leave lights on and then act surprised when animals stop coming,' I sound unscientific."

"You sound right."

"That's not the same category."

Mina looked at her profile bent over the machine, the clean line of nose and mouth gone blunt under headlamp shadow, the braid dark against the back of her field shirt. There was wear in her, not fragility but use: a shine at the cuff where fabric had rubbed thin, a healed split on one thumb, the tired precision of someone whose body had been made to serve grants, seasons, and low-budget expertise for so long that she no longer bothered pretending the work was noble all the time. Mina had expected intelligence. She had not expected that kind of damage. It made Rhea harder to dismiss and, therefore, more dangerous.

The night thickened. Once, a mullet hit the blackwater in the cut with a smack that made Mina jump before she could stop herself. Rhea looked over.

"Fish," she said.

"I know what a fish sounds like."

"Your shoulders didn't."

Mina rolled them back. "You grading everybody all the time?"

"Only the ones holding live mammals."

"And how am I doing?"

Rhea clipped another bag shut. "Better than the grad student."

The crack about the grad student stayed with Mina through the next net check, glowing under her sternum every time the mesh whispered in the dark. By the time the eastern edge of the sky loosened from black to metallic blue, she had stopped bracing for the real conversation. Rhea had been handed every opening: where Mina had been the night before, why Tomas had come in smelling of creek and sex and fuel. She had used none of them. She had needed competent hands, and Mina had given them. The plainness of that exchange stripped away a defense Mina had been leaning on without admitting it.

Back at the lab, Rhea hung the used bags and opened the recorder case on the data table. The green lights blinked like a patient insult. Mina washed her hands at the sink. The water from the tap came out warm already, tasting faintly of the pipes.

"How many?" Mina asked.

"Enough to justify losing sleep." Rhea keyed notes into her laptop with two fingers, not because she could not type faster but because she hated mistakes more than delay. "I'll know whether the recorder agrees after coffee."

Mina dried her hands on her shorts again. Decency would have sent her off with a good night, or a good morning, and left Rhea to her notes. She stayed anyway, looking at the line of damp cloth bags and the tobacco tin full of pencil stubs and the slumped shoulders Rhea let herself have now that no animal required anything from her.

Rhea shut the laptop halfway. "You can go collapse if you want."

"Do I look that bad?"

"You look like somebody who has been awake for two nights for two different reasons."

Heat touched Mina's face again. "That's generous."

Rhea slid the recorder case off the table. "Come get coffee before I regret speaking kindly."

She said it over her shoulder, already out the lab door.

Mina followed.

* * *

The main house held dawn the way a screened thing held water: not securely, but enough to change the air. Gray light came through the mesh and laid a weak pattern over the kitchen table, over the drying dish rack, over the stack of guest mugs with hand-painted spoonbills on them. The generator had dropped to a lower grumble. Somewhere in the walls a gecko clicked like a cheap switch.

Tomas was asleep in the room off the hall. Mina knew it before she saw the boot outside the door, toppled on its side with the lace still knotted because he always gave up on the first one and trusted sleep to strip the rest of him later. His snore came thin and whistling on the inhale through the nose he'd broken in a teenage fight and never fixed. The sound carried plainly enough that it might as well have been in the kitchen with them.

Mina stopped at the sink with the old familiar urge to become smaller than her outline. Last night or the night before, depending on where exhaustion had blurred the date, that same sound had been under her palms after Tomas turned on his side in her bunk and said he had to go before the house woke. She had not let herself think about him as a husband then. It had been easier to think of him as access wearing a body. Standing in Rhea's kitchen with his boot in view, that lie lost some of its efficiency.

Rhea set the kettle on the stove and turned the burner with the economy of long practice. She moved barefoot on the scarred wood, one heel blackened faintly from never remembering shoes soon enough. From the icebox she took eggs, yesterday's tortillas wrapped in a towel, half a tomato, and a jar of coffee grounds kept in an old pickle jar because the lid sealed better than the pretty tin Imogen used for guests.

"Sit," she said. "If you hover like that, I'll start assigning chores."

Mina pulled out a chair. It gave a long complaint against the floorboards. The whole building seemed to speak in dry old joints: the pilings down below, the screen door springs, the warped threshold to the pantry. Nothing here did silence properly.

Rhea poured water into the kettle and set two mugs on the counter, then, on reflex, set out a third. The motion had the weight of habit. Mina watched it and hated how much it moved her. Rhea cracked eggs one-handed against the pan rim, then warmed tortillas directly over the flame, flipping them with her fingertips because, apparently, she did not believe in tongs either.

"You're going to burn yourself," Mina said before she could stop herself.

"I do this every morning."

"That doesn't make it smart."

"No. It makes it repetitive."

She said it with the same dry plainness she used for everything, but a strand of hair had escaped her braid and stuck damply to the side of her neck, and there was something indecently intimate in seeing a person only partly assembled for the day. She was not beautiful in any polished sense. She was better than that: human enough to want coffee before language, human enough to stand in a kitchen in a field shirt that smelled of mangrove mud and cook eggs with the focused annoyance of someone who considered breakfast another maintenance task.

Mina realized, with a kind of dread, that this was the sort of detail desire liked to anchor to.

Rhea slid a mug across to her. "Black?"

"Yeah."

"That's the only civilized answer before six."

The coffee was brutal and a little burned. Mina drank anyway. Her body met it with relief so immediate it felt crude. Rhea folded the eggs into the tortillas, added tomato slices, salt, and a splash of whatever vinegar-pepper concoction lived in an unlabeled bottle by the stove. She put one plate in front of Mina and kept the other, then carried the third mug to the edge of the counter nearest Tomas's room without comment.

"He'll find it if he wakes before it goes cold," she said.

There was no performance in it and no domestic sweetness either. It was just knowledge accumulated by repetition. Mina took a bite to keep from saying something stupid. The tortilla was scorched in spots and still cold in the middle. The eggs were oversalted. It tasted better than anything she had eaten all week.

For a minute the only sounds were chewing, the kettle ticking as it cooled, Tomas's thin snore, and a spoonbill starting up outside with a rusty, offended racket that another answered farther off. Through the screen the dawn smelled of salt, wet wood, and the kitchen can outside beginning to warm toward fish rot.

Rhea drank half her coffee in one go and looked toward the hall as if taking the measure of the house. "For what it's worth," she said, "this place hears everything."

Mina set her mug down carefully. "That so."

"It's a design flaw if you enjoy privacy. It's a useful diagnostic tool if you don't."

Mina forced a small shrug. "Camp's old."

"Camp is acoustically indiscreet." Rhea tore her tortilla in half. "Cabin Two had a couple here last week who whispered all their arguments and somehow made them louder. I know which guest unwraps cough drops at three in the morning. Beto eats cereal over the sink when he thinks Imogen is asleep. Tomas believes he comes up the walkway quietly after county runs, which is optimistic." She took another bite, chewed, swallowed. "You see my point."

Every board in Mina's body went rigid. It would have been easier if Rhea had come at her hot. It would have been easier if there had been tears, accusation, territory, something dumb and obvious she could manage with apology or denial or cruelty. This measured plainness left nowhere useful to stand.

"If this is about Tomas," Mina said before she could pull the words back, "I didn't ask him to report to you, and I didn't ask for commentary over breakfast."

Rhea looked at her over the rim of the mug with a gaze that wasn't cold and wasn't warm. It was precise. "No. You asked him for other things."

The blood rose hard under Mina's skin. She stared back because looking away would have been worse. The hall suddenly felt too narrow, the kitchen too near every room in the house.

"You wanted a scene?" she said. "You could've had it sooner."

"I don't want a scene." Rhea's voice stayed level enough that the words sounded less emotional than final. "If I wanted theater, I wouldn't have spent six hours handing you cloth bags in mosquito weather."

That checked Mina more cleanly than shouting would have. She heard the truth in it. Rhea had not used the shift to humiliate her. She had used the shift for the shift. The rest was this: eggs, coffee, daylight, and nowhere to hide.

Mina looked down at the table. The painted spoonbill on her mug had a chip through one wing. "Then what do you want?"

"At the moment? More sleep." Rhea set the mug down. "Beyond that, I want you to understand where you're standing."

Mina said nothing.

Rhea leaned back in her chair, listening for a beat as Tomas turned over in the other room. The mattress springs gave a tired small cry. "You're on stilts with three staff rooms, six guest rooms, and screens thin enough to pass gossip through by osmosis. Imogen misses very little that touches payroll or reputation. Beto misses nothing at all and pretends not to because he has manners. Tomas has county work. I've got a contract and a room tied to it. You've got a temporary bed and Imogen's patience, both of which can disappear in the same afternoon. If this turns into camp knowledge, you lose first."

There it was, stripped clean. There was no plea for loyalty and no claim of injury. It was a power map laid on the table next to breakfast.

Mina hated that relief was the first thing up in her throat. "You think I don't know I'm the easiest person here to throw overboard?"

"I think knowing a fact and behaving as if you know it are different skills."

Mina almost snapped something useless back. Instead she heard Tomas's snore catch in the hall, heard a guest toilet flush two rooms away, heard the screen slap once in the breeze. The building was doing what Rhea said it did: making private life public by inches.

"This wasn't supposed to be..." Mina started, then stopped because every ending to the sentence sounded cheap.

Rhea spared her by taking a bite of tortilla. After she swallowed, she said, "Nothing ever is."

The mercy of that nearly undid Mina more than the warning had.

She picked at the edge of her plate. "You seem very calm."

"I'm tired," Rhea said. "That can resemble calm from a distance."

Mina looked up.

Rhea's mouth shifted, not quite smiling. "Also, I'm a scientist on seasonal funding. If I reacted to every stupid thing people did with their bodies, I'd have no time left for data."

Despite everything, Mina laughed once, sharp and involuntary. Rhea's expression loosened a fraction in answer.

"That isn't the same as saying I don't care," she added.

The sentence altered the room. Mina felt the skin at the back of her neck tighten, as if a draft had found its way through the screens. Rhea had left the caring undefined and unsheltering, and that made it more real than anything kinder would have.

Mina's fingers tightened on the mug. The ceramic had gone slick with condensation where the kitchen air hit the last of the cooler coffee. "Are you asking me to stop?"

Rhea shook her head once and pushed the plate away. She stood to rinse it. "If you come tomorrow night, come to the lab before dark because I asked for help. Go back to your own bed after. Don't use my nets as cover for you and Tomas, and don't ask me to act surprised if Imogen notices."

She ran water over the plate, then over her own fingers, as if the conversation required the same practical clean-up as egg grease. Mina watched the tendons move in the back of Rhea's wrist and watched the escaped strand of hair darken further where the sink mist caught it. The wanting that rose in her came all at once: wrist, neck, mouth, and the mind that had laid out the danger without dressing it as virtue, the work-night competence, the refusal to turn herself into either martyr or fool.

Mina said, because her pride never knew when to stay buried, "Most people would've screamed."

Rhea dried her hands on a dish towel. "Most people should not work with bats."

"That can't be your only standard."

"No." She hung the towel back over the oven handle with unnecessary precision. "I also dislike melodrama before sunrise."

There was humor in it, but not enough to make the thing light. Mina felt the laugh come and did not give it sound. Outside, the spoonbills had properly started. Their noise rolled in ragged bursts from the rookery, half newborn trumpet, half rusty hinge.

"Why ask me tonight?" Mina said. "For the shift, I mean. If you already knew."

Rhea leaned against the counter and folded her arms. In the daylight, with the edge gone off the field dark, Mina could see how badly the night had used her too: grit stuck at one cuff, a faint bruise of exhaustion under the eyes, the red welt of a mosquito bite she had missed behind one ear. "Because I needed competent help," she said. "Beto had kitchen duty. Tomas would have tried to make himself useful and ruined at least one net. I was curious whether you were actually as good with your hands as you look when you're pretending not to be nervous."

Mina stared.

Rhea let that sit for a beat, then added, "You are."

The kitchen, already hot, seemed to tighten another notch around Mina's skin. Safety would have meant a joke or silence. She said, quieter than she meant to, "You make that sound dangerous."

"Most competence is." Rhea picked up her mug again. "Especially in a place that runs on scarcity and proximity."

The line settled in her like a hook finding hold. Flirting would have been easy to waste. This had structure; it gave the wanting room to develop a spine.

Tomas coughed in the other room and rolled over again. The mug Rhea had left for him waited untouched on the counter, steam gone thin from the top. Mina followed Rhea's glance to it and felt, not jealousy in any clean form, but the dense fact of another life: mornings repeated often enough to become gesture, knowledge of each other's boots and coffee habits and broken sleep. Rhea had not been standing still in some wife-shaped holding pattern waiting for Mina to complicate her. She had a marriage, a survey contract, a tobacco tin full of pencil stubs, a body full of bites and work, a way of turning tortillas with bare fingers because she hated tongs. Wanting her would mean wanting a whole arrangement of habits, loyalties, intelligence, and material conditions. Mina recognized the scale of that too late to stop.

Rhea looked back at her. "Be at the lab at dusk tomorrow if the weather holds. If you can't live inside that line, don't come."

The invitation was plain enough to make Mina mistrust it.

"After all that?"

"I meant all of it."

"You always wrap ultimatums in field language?"

"Only when I want them followed." Rhea took the last swallow of coffee and set the mug in the sink. "Can you do it or not?"

Mina answered before caution could organize itself. "Yeah."

Rhea nodded as if the answer had been expected and therefore not worth dramatizing. "Good. Then leave the bedrooms out of it."

She gathered the plates, Tomas's untouched mug, and the pepper-vinegar bottle into one capable arm. At the hall she paused and looked back with the same unreadable steadiness she had brought to the nets.

Then she went down the hall toward the sink and Tomas's boot and the room beyond, carrying the ordinary debris of breakfast as if nothing in the house had shifted. Maybe nothing had, from her point of view. Maybe this was only one more maintenance task before sleep.

Mina stayed at the table with her ruined appetite and the last bitter inch of coffee. The dawn light had brightened enough to show every scratch in the wood. Outside, a skiff engine started somewhere far off in the channels and flattened into distance. The camp woke in pieces around her: a shower pipe rattling, Beto swearing softly when something metal hit the floor in the kitchen annex, one guest beginning a forced-cheerful conversation with nobody yet visible.

Rhea had meant to warn her, and she had. Mina understood the geometry now more clearly than she had yesterday: the thin walls, the payroll, the marriage, and the fact that tomorrow night would run through Rhea or not happen at all. The warning had teeth. What stayed nearest the skin was the six hours in the mosquitoes, the quiet competence, the story about the crying graduate student, the tobacco tin full of pencil stubs, the way Rhea had seen her nerves and named the better thing underneath them instead.

Mina had spent the last day treating Rhea as a structure to get around. By the time she finally stood and took her plate to the sink, that lie was gone. In its place stood a hunger with no practical use and a line drawn around it. She wanted another night beside Rhea under the mangroves, another hour of clipped instructions and dry jokes and sure hands on wet net line. She wanted the woman's mind, which was the real trouble. She wanted to be looked at that way again and not found sloppy.

With Tomas, wanting had been a bargain and a door. With Rhea it already felt like a measurement.
