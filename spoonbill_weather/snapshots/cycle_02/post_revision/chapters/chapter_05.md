# Chapter 5

The next evening, Rhea had written the low tide time on the inside of her wrist in blunt pencil, and every few minutes she looked at it as if skin might revise itself out of spite. The digits were already smudging with sweat. In the bat lab's yellow light they looked like a second pulse under her bone. She was on her knees in front of an open equipment case, sorting cables into two piles with the tidy impatience she brought to anything that had once worked and no longer did. The room smelled of rubbing alcohol, warm plastic, and the sweet rot of old guano baked into plywood.

"You still have your boots on," she said without looking up. "That gives you a chance at earning your keep. Come with me."

Mina leaned in the doorway with a flashlight tucked under one arm and pretended she had not been watching the back of Rhea's neck for a full minute. Rhea's braid was pulled so tight it changed the shape of her face, all those narrow, severe lines made sharper by the heat. A crescent of white zinc clung to one ear where she had missed it that afternoon. She had changed out of her donor-safe shirt and into an old gray tank with a university logo almost scrubbed away by bleach. That should not have made her look less official. It did.

"Is that an invitation," Mina said, "or a conscription?"

"Field labor doesn't improve if you flirt at it." Rhea snapped a battery door closed and finally glanced up. Her mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "The west recorder died again. I want the card before the tide comes back over the route."

"Where's west, exactly?"

"Under the old intake line."

The phrase hit Mina a half second late, not because she recognized it yet, but because anything under the camp sounded better than another evening in the donor-facing half of the place, hauling coolers for guests who called every roseate spoonbill pretty in the same lacquered tone they used for cocktails. She pushed off the frame.

"You could've led with that."

"I assumed curiosity was already your strongest muscle."

She said it dryly, as if she had measured Mina and found appetite easier to trust than virtue. Mina liked that about her. It was cleaner than niceness. She crossed the room and took the second pack. It held one recorder, one hand net, spare batteries, calipers, sample bags, gloves, and two headlamps wrapped in orange tape so nobody borrowed them and forgot where they came from. Rhea slipped a peppermint into her mouth from a dented tin she kept in the side pocket and offered Mina one without ceremony. Mina took it. The sugar cracked between her teeth, cold and medicinal.

"Guano?" Mina asked.

"And because the mosquitoes are less impressed by sweetness than the guests are."

They went out through the screened walkway, the hinges giving their nightly complaint. The camp at low water always looked indecent to Mina, as if the tide had lifted the hem and showed too much of its legs. Barnacled pilings sweated in the late light. The blackwater under the house had drawn down enough to expose slick roots, beer-bottle glass buried in marl, a length of pipe furred with oyster shells. Above them someone laughed in one of the guest rooms, one bright donor laugh cut off by the door sliding shut. The generator thudded from the shed in patient, expensive intervals.

Rhea moved ahead of her with that unnerving economy she had in the field, never rushing, never hesitating, as if the ground declared itself properly beneath her feet. She used a cheap telescoping pointer to nudge ferns aside rather than her hands. It was probably a habit, and maybe a preference. Mina watched the wet graphite of the tide time disappear and reappear on her wrist as she swung her arm.

They took the shell path past the kitchen cistern, then dropped off the boardwalk where the camp kept pretending no route existed. The mud accepted Mina to the ankle on the first step and to mid-calf on the second. It was warm on top and cool underneath, the layers distinct as if the earth had been storing separate weather. Mosquitoes found the backs of her knees at once.

"You do this every survey?" Mina asked.

"Only the ones the permit office forgot to make impossible."

"That sounds almost cheerful."

"Don't make me repeat myself and ruin it."

There was enough of a path for one person if you believed in paths. Mangrove knees rose from the muck like clenched knuckles. The roots made cages and false doors and abrupt little chambers of shadow full of fiddler crabs waving their one ridiculous hand. Rhea used her pointer, the beam of her headlamp, and nothing else. Mina used her boots, her palms, and a vocabulary that had improved a lot since she started sleeping with Tomas and spending her afternoons with guests. The mud sucked at her left calf where the old prop scar ran, as if it recognized the groove and wanted back in.

They kept talking because the route invited silence too easily, and silence with Rhea did not feel empty. It felt selected.

"What killed the recorder?" Mina asked.

"Humidity. Salt. A bad seal. An iguana with opinions. Pick any three."

"I like the iguana. Gives the failure some dignity."

"The failure is a county reimbursement form. It has no dignity."

Mina laughed. Rhea glanced back once, brief and satisfied, the way a person checked whether a knot had held. She ducked under a low branch and stopped at a rusted stake with a square of survey tape tied to it. On the tape somebody had written R4 in waterproof marker, the letters bled thick by rain and years.

"From here," Rhea said, "watch your right side. The marl shears off."

"You tell everybody that."

"No. Most people I let slide."

That should have been an insult. It wasn't. It landed with the strange lightness of being chosen for extra trouble.

The air changed before the ground did. The mangrove heat thinned. Something cooler breathed through it, mineral and stale, not the living swamp exhale of rot and tannin but a sealed smell let out after a long sentence. Mina stopped smelling tide and started smelling old concrete, wet metal, the dead-cold memory of air-conditioned hallways where people in logo shirts talked about resilience over catered shrimp.

Ahead, roots gave way to a dark cut in the bank. Concrete shoulders, half swallowed by mangroves. A service opening high enough to crouch through, low enough to feel like trespass before you even bent. Emergency light housings, long dead, clung to the walls like blind fish eyes. Black mildew veined the ceiling. One section of hazard stripe still held under the slime: yellow, black, yellow.

"Jesus," Mina said.

"That was roughly my note on the original site map."

"You knew this was here."

"I knew there was abandoned infrastructure under the west berm. The camp deed calls it a drainage easement because people get nervous around nouns." Rhea squinted at the recorder in her hand. "I didn't know the bats liked it until last week."

She crouched and went in. Mina followed because the alternative was standing outside with her own pulse making a fool of her. The temperature dropped another few degrees inside, enough to pebble the sweat on her forearms. The beam of her headlamp picked out pipes cut flush to walls, cable trays with their guts ripped out, barnacle scars where old waterlines had once sweated salt. The floor pitched gently down. Tidal seep had left a slick line of silver mud through the middle. Overhead, tiny dry scratches multiplied into the nervous leather whisper of roosting bats.

The tunnel bent left. On the wall at shoulder height, under a rash of rust, an enamel sign still clung on with three bolts. Most of the lettering had gone brown and illegible. The logo had not.

BrightCurrent.

This was not the polished public version with the stylized wave in blue glass. It was the service mark, the one stamped on staff doors and tank fittings and loading cases that were not meant for visitors. The wave reduced to a hard white arc. Under it, just enough words survived to be worse than none.

Mina's body did a stupid thing and believed she had stepped backward in time. It wasn't metaphor, or the grand tragic language people used when they wanted the past to sound artistic. Her stomach simply dropped as if an elevator had started moving. For a beat she tasted refrigeration and latex gloves and the powdered coffee BrightCurrent kept in conference rooms no contractor was supposed to linger in. Jonah's hand at the small of her back came and went like a bad smell. So did the shine of tank glass. So did the lie she had once told herself that service corridors did not count as inside.

She reached out before she meant to and put two fingers on the rusted edge of the sign. The enamel was colder than the air.

"Mina."

Rhea had stopped ten feet ahead, one boot planted on a cracked curb that had once directed runoff. The recorder hung slack in her hand. She was not startled. That would have been easier. She was watching with the flat, deliberate attention she gave data that had turned troublesome.

"You know this place," she said.

The bats above them shifted, a dry million-fold clicking like fingernails on paper.

Mina took her hand off the sign. Rust reddened her fingertips. "I know the company."

"That wasn't my question."

The tunnel seemed narrower all at once, not because it had changed but because Rhea had put language in it and language took up room.

"Let's get your recorder," Mina said. "Then you can interrogate me on stable footing."

Rhea kept looking at her for another breath, then nodded once. "Fine. But don't confuse postponement with escape."

***

She turned and kept walking. Mina followed because she had already chosen the worse path several times in her life and this at least was honest about the mud.

The tunnel opened into a wider chamber where concrete met root and lost. Mangroves had found hairline cracks years ago and widened them into a patient vandalism. Roots as thick as wrists punched through the back wall and drank the dim green light leaking from the far end. Half the ceiling had been lined once with acoustic insulation; now it hung in wet rotting flaps. Bats nested in the pockets behind it. The recorder sat zip-tied to a conduit run above a barred drainage grate, its casing salted white.

"There," Rhea said.

Mina shouldered off the pack and handed up the spare batteries. Rhea climbed the conduit support with more contempt than caution, the recorder between her teeth for a second while she freed the knot. The sight hit Mina low and immediate, meaner for happening in a room that smelled like bat piss and drowned concrete. Everything about Rhea was cleaner in motion and filthier in what it suggested. The tendons moved in her bare arm when she braced. A streak of mud ran along one shin. Her braid had started to fray at the nape and the loosened hairs caught the light like black fishing line.

When she dropped back down, she landed close enough that their boots touched. The chamber smelled of bat piss, tidewater, and old machinery giving up slowly in the dark.

"Card's good," Rhea said. "The housing isn't. Salt got under the gasket again."

Mina took the dead recorder, more grateful for the object than she should have been. It gave her hands a reason to move. She unscrewed the back and peered in at the green bloom around the contacts.

"You could dry-brush this, but it'll corrode again."

"I know."

"I mean tonight. With alcohol first, then brush."

"I heard you the first time."

She said it mildly. Mina looked up. Rhea had leaned one shoulder against the wall under a cluster of roosting bats and folded her arms. She was not going to help her out of this with work.

"You went white back there," Rhea said. "That's not easy in this light."

Mina kept her eyes on the battery compartment. "It's a company sign. I've been publicly flayed over the company sign. Some reaction isn't weird."

"No." Rhea tapped the wall once beside her shoulder. "This is weird."

The concrete gave back a hollow sound. Behind it water moved, slow and hidden. Mina felt it rather than heard it, the way she sometimes felt a big fish shift under blackwater before the surface said anything. She looked at the cracks webbing the chamber, at the roots feeding themselves through poured concrete, at the low pipe mouths cut into the far wall. Intake. The word was no metaphor now, no rumor either. Water had come through here from somewhere and gone back out somewhere else. There were only so many somewhere elses in a wetland with this tide.

Her mouth filled with peppermint and metal.

"I worked around BrightCurrent," she said.

Rhea did not move.

"That still isn't specific enough."

"I know."

Mina shut the recorder harder than necessary. The sound slapped around the chamber. A few bats shivered overhead but held fast.

"I did donor interpretation for them sometimes," she said. "Guided groups. Translating habitat talk into language rich people could mistake for ethics. They liked having a field face in the room when they talked about what their fish were going to save."

"Fish."

"Eels. Whatever version sold best that quarter."

Rhea's head tilted slightly. It was the smallest movement, but on her it meant a door had opened somewhere behind the eyes. "The articles said you photographed a specimen and lied about where it came from."

"The articles said a lot of tidy things."

"Were any of them true?"

Mina laughed once, no humor in it. "Enough to ruin me."

Rhea let the answer sit there and spoil. She was good at that. Tomas filled silence with heat or jokes or blunt hands. Rhea let it hold its shape until the person across from her either respected it or panicked.

Mina panicked a little.

"I had access I wasn't supposed to have," she said. "Not employee access. Not exactly. But more than a tour guide should. Enough that I know their service markings. Enough that seeing this under camp makes me want to peel my own skin off."

"Because?"

"Because public-side BrightCurrent liked glass walls and educational plaques. Back-side BrightCurrent looked like this. Pipes, intake runs, staff doors with boiled-off paint. This isn't a random culvert they happened to slap a logo on. This is theirs."

Rhea's gaze went past Mina to the cut pipe mouths, the cracked floor, the root-thickened wall. Her face hardly changed, but she drew one long breath through her nose and let it out in a narrow thread, as if she were pinning every loose reaction under her tongue.

"If this fed into the west channels," she said, "my contamination baselines are garbage."

She crossed to the nearest pipe mouth, pulled on gloves, and pinched a crust of salt-whitened residue loose with two fingers. Black silt came with it, greasy as old pencil lead. She shook both into a sample bag, labeled it WEST INTAKE RESIDUE in tight block letters, then crouched long enough to count the low mouths cut through the wall.

"Maybe not garbage."

"Exposure, then." Rhea held up the bag between them. "Which is worse. I filed the west transect as background marsh. If this route's been breathing through BrightCurrent concrete, every clean-comparison line I already logged wants an asterisk the permit office will smell from the mainland."

There it was, not loud but worse than loud. Mina felt it along her ribs like a line of cold nails.

"Fair," she said.

Rhea stepped off the wall. "How much more is there than the magazine version of your disgrace?"

"Enough."

"That's not a quantity."

"It's the one I have on me."

Rhea looked at her so steadily Mina had to turn and aim her light at the far wall, just to break the line. The beam found more of the old chamber. A heavy steel door warped on its hinges where the concrete narrowed again. The stencil on it had peeled down to three surviving letters. NEX. To the right, a conduit pipe vanished into marl and root, heading east under the camp.

This wasn't camp-adjacent, or the same watershed in the abstract. It ran under the camp and straight through it. Whatever BrightCurrent had done here had not stopped at its own neat parcel lines. The water under the guest rooms, under the kitchen, under the boards where donors set wine glasses during sunset talks, had once been organized to move through this place. Mina knew enough hydraulics to feel sick with it. The creek she had been treating like wild witness was plumbed straight through the edges of her old shame.

She heard herself say, "Fuck."

Rhea followed the beam. "You recognized more than the logo."

"Yeah."

"Talk."

Mina dragged the back of her wrist across her mouth. The peppermint was gone. The guano smell had won. "Service annex. Intake side. They used satellite rooms for holding systems, filtration, quarantine, whatever they didn't want donors near. I wasn't supposed to see most of it. But I saw enough. Enough to know they wouldn't build dead-end concrete for decoration."

"So where does this tunnel go?"

"If the maps in my head still mean anything, it ties the old annex to the channels east of camp. Maybe as exchange water, maybe outflow during storms, maybe both." She swallowed. "I don't know if it still functions. I know it existed on purpose."

Rhea was already moving again, following Mina's light to the east-running conduit. She braced her field notebook on her thigh and sketched a blunt map: grate, curb, three pipe mouths, conduit heading under camp. The pen pressed hard enough to bite the next page.

Rhea's jaw tightened, not with pretty theatrical clenching but with a precise little shift that made one side of her mouth flatter. "Then the west line comes out of baseline status tonight. If I put BrightCurrent under this route in a memo, I may get the site frozen before I get a water sample. And you were going to tell me this when?"

"When I stopped feeling like I was going to throw up on your recorder."

"That's an answer about timing, not intent."

Mina set the recorder down on the curb between them because she needed both hands empty. "I didn't know this was here."

"I'm not accusing you of pouring the concrete personally."

"Good. Because I'd have done a worse job."

For the first time since the sign, Rhea almost smiled. It vanished fast. "I'm accusing you of carrying around a bigger story than you've given anyone at camp."

Mina looked at the steel door with its peeled letters. The memory came wrong and sharp: Jonah leaning against a loading dock in a shirt too expensive to be called rough, telling her no one ever remembered which cooler had moved through which door if the paperwork smiled hard enough. She had been younger then, not stupid exactly, but hungry in the way that makes stupid feel like strategy. She could say none of that now without saying too much. The thought of naming the cooler tightened the back of her throat. If she said it tonight, the word would not stay buried in wet concrete. It would climb the pilings with them, get into Tomas's face somehow, get into the kitchen, get into every room with a screen door that couldn't quite shut.

"There is more," she said. "You were right about that before you asked. But I'm not giving you the whole thing in a hole under camp with bat shit dripping in my hair."

Rhea absorbed it without flinching, without playing offended wife, without reaching for righteous speech. Mina had wanted her, from the first time she'd watched Rhea wash a net with methodical, angry care, because wanting her felt like wanting a blade somebody kept honed for ugly jobs. This was part of it too. The refusal to overperform the wound.

"All right," Rhea said.

Mina blinked. "That's it?"

"No." Rhea bent, picked up the recorder, and slid it into the pack. "That's me deciding the next question gets me farther than the last one."

She took two steps toward Mina. The chamber made them feel closer than they were. Bats shifted above, their tiny bodies making heat in the insulation. Somewhere behind the wall the hidden water moved again with a soft suck through pipe or root.

"You and Tomas are not nearly as covert as either of you think," Rhea said.

Mina's face went hot in a way the tunnel did not deserve. "Rhea."

"Don't. I'm not asking for a recital and I'm not offering you a scene." She spaced the words cleanly, giving each one enough room to bite. "I'm saying I already live with one man who assumes omission counts as kindness. I don't need a second person trying it on me."

Mina stared at her.

Rhea's eyes dropped, not coy, not shy, just measuring, down Mina's throat, over the mud on her shirt, to the place where the recorder pack strap had rubbed a wet diagonal across her chest. Then back up.

"And before you decide to be noble," she said, "don't. It isn't kinder if you flatten me into the wife who doesn't know what room she's in. It's just lazier."

The chamber had gone very quiet except for the bats and the buried water. Mina could feel the space between them the way she felt a storm front before it showed on radar, pressure first, then static, then the first rearranged smell. She wanted to say Tomas mattered, Tomas wasn't a prop, Tomas had not been tricked so much as entered. All of that was true and none of it was the point.

"You want me to apologize," Mina said finally.

"No." Rhea's voice dropped a register. "I want you to stop editing reality in ways that require me to play innocent."

That landed harder than accusation would have. Innocent. As if that were the humiliation she actually feared for Rhea: not being wronged, but being handled.

Mina nodded once. It felt insufficient and therefore honest.

"Okay," she said.

Rhea held her gaze a second longer, then turned toward the tunnel mouth. "Good. The emergence count will start in ten minutes. Bring the pack."

Mina shouldered it and followed her out, the BrightCurrent sign at her back like a hand she had once mistaken for luck.

***

By the time they reached the tunnel mouth again the outside light had thinned to mangrove dusk, that dirty gold minute when every mosquito in the county filed its claim. Rhea chose a concrete lip just outside the opening where the bank widened enough for two people to crouch without sliding into the mud. From there they could watch the emergence and still hear the recorder if it decided to die theatrically in public. The tide was turning. Water licked at exposed roots with a slow, deliberate sound, like someone cleaning a knife.

Rhea set the spare recorder on an overturned crate embedded in marl and opened the field notebook again. Its cover was bubbled from years of humidity. She wrote the time, weather, route condition, probable equipment failure. Then she boxed off a second note in sharper block letters: abandoned BrightCurrent intake at west emergence point. West transect suspended as background comparison pending sediment and water samples. Under that, smaller and angrier, she added that formal disclosure could mean a permit amendment and route closure. Mina sat beside her and held the red-lensed flashlight where she needed it.

The bats came out in hesitant drips at first, one, three, five, then a whole shaken length of them, small bodies slipping through the concrete throat and up into the dark over the trees. Rhea counted under her breath. Her voice changed while she worked. It lost whatever edge camp life put on it and grew barer, almost intimate, the syllables pared down to what observation required. There was no softness in it, only concentration. Mina had heard singers warm up with less private focus.

"Forty-seven, fifty-eight, seventy-one..." Rhea's pencil moved fast. "Gap. Start again."

"I thought you said this wasn't a choir audition."

"Do not sabotage the data because you're sexually inconvenient."

Mina choked on a laugh. "That's one way to phrase it."

"It's the one that fits."

The line salted the air between them instead of easing it. Rhea could say something explicit and leave it there clean-edged, with all the heat still in it. Mina wanted to put her mouth on that calm just to see whether it would keep its shape.

The last heavy burst of bats streamed out and vanished toward the channel. Rhea finished the count, shut the notebook, and flexed her writing hand. She had a callus where the pen sat. Mina watched her thumb rub it once.

"You've done this route alone?" Mina asked.

"Twice. I preferred tonight."

"Because of the extra hands."

"Because if I slipped on that rebar and cracked my head open, you'd be arrogant enough to drag me back before the tide covered me."

"That's almost romantic."

"Don't push your luck."

The dusk gathered around them. Camp was hidden now by root and bank, but Mina could still picture the lights coming on along the walkway, the guest rooms breathing cooled air through bad seals, Tomas somewhere on county shift or on his way back from it. His absence felt present the way county signage felt present even when you could not see the dock: a structure shaping movement. She wondered if Rhea had timed this route partly because Tomas was gone. She wondered how long Rhea had been deciding anything at all.

Rhea unscrewed the cap of her canteen and drank. A little water escaped and ran down the side of her throat into the hollow at her collarbone. Mina looked away too late.

Rhea capped the canteen, opened the notebook one more time, and dragged her thumb once under the boxed note as if she wanted to test whether the words would still sting on reread.

"Tomorrow I need water off the flood and sediment off that intake," she said. "Tonight I stop calling the west route a clean comparison. That costs me the only baseline I had on this side of camp, and if I say BrightCurrent too loudly I may lose the site before I can rebuild it."

"All right," Rhea said.

That tone again, stripped of lab, donor, and wife and reduced to decision.

Mina braced one hand on the crate. "All right, what?"

"You were about to make this harder than it needed to be. I'd rather save us the extra labor."

Rhea recapped the canteen, set it aside, and turned fully toward her. Her knee touched Mina's through damp field pants, a deliberate contact brief enough to preserve the lie if anybody later needed one. Mina felt it like current.

"I know you want me," Rhea said.

Mina could have insulted them both by pretending otherwise. "Yeah."

"And I know you've been sleeping with Tomas."

There it was, plain as weather. Mina opened her mouth, shut it again, then said, "Yeah."

"Good. That saves time." Rhea rested her forearms on her knees and looked out toward the dark water for a moment before continuing. "Here's what I'm not available for."

Mina waited.

"I'm not available to be the corrective detail in somebody else's story. Not the broad-minded wife. Not the scientist who makes bad behavior sound field-progressive. Not the person you fold in later so you can tell yourself there was honesty all along."

Each sentence landed cleanly, with no wasted breath and no drama. Mina listened harder because of that. The roots below them clicked softly as the tide began to lift them.

"And I'm not interested," Rhea said, "in being handled like fragile collateral because you feel guilty. If you want distance, choose distance. If you want me, use the noun. But don't offer me etiquette and call it respect."

Mina stared at the mud on her own boots. It looked like another substance entirely in the red flashlight glow, black and arterial. "You make a person feel very cheap in unusually elegant syntax."

"That's because cheapness sharpens when you name it plain."

Mina laughed once, helplessly. It hurt her pride and made her want Rhea more. That was probably not a healthy development. It was also not remotely optional.

"All right," she said. "My turn."

Rhea nodded.

"I don't want you because Tomas got me in the habit of your household. I don't want you because this would make me look less filthy. It doesn't." Mina glanced back at the tunnel. "Especially not here. I want you because every time you look at a room you make it tell the truth. I want you because you don't waste words pretending anybody in this place is cleaner than they are. I want you because when you're doing fieldwork you look like the only person on the island who's exactly the same species as her own body."

She had not meant to say that much. The words came anyway, quick and unsanded, each sentence baring more than the last. By the time she heard herself say species, the heat had climbed all the way up her throat.

Rhea's expression did not soften. It deepened. It was a different thing.

"That last sentence was almost indecently flattering," she said.

"I can do worse."

"I know."

The answer slipped under Mina's skin and stayed there as recognition, stripped of boast and tease.

Rhea reached out and took Mina's left wrist. Her fingers were dry and cool despite the heat, as if she stored a private climate in her hands. She turned the wrist over, looking at the rust still caught in the fine lines near the heel of Mina's palm.

"One more condition," she said.

"You have a list."

"A sensible one." Her thumb pressed lightly against the rust stain. "At some point, not tonight if tonight is too stupid for it, you tell me what BrightCurrent did to you and what you did back. Not the public version. Not the version designed to survive lawyers. The one I can work with."

The tide made another sound under them, fuller now. Water was reentering the narrow places.

Mina swallowed. "You really know how to ruin foreplay with future obligations."

"I know how to keep desire from becoming anesthesia."

Nobody else at camp could have said that without sounding absurd. Rhea said it with her thumb still resting on the rust at Mina's wrist, eyes steady on Mina's face, and the words stayed in Mina's skin. Desire was not going to buy her a cleaner version of herself.

"Okay," Mina said quietly.

"Okay," Rhea repeated.

She still had Mina's wrist in her hand. Rhea tugged once, and Mina went to her at once. The movement was small and completely unambiguous. Their knees met first, then their shoulders. Rhea smelled like sweat dried to salt, guano dust, and the peppermint she had eaten an hour ago fighting for survival under everything else.

Rhea touched Mina's face with her free hand, not cupping it, not tender in any way meant to spare her, just laying two fingers along her jaw as if checking alignment before making a cut. Then she kissed her.

There was nothing hesitant in it. Rhea kissed the way she wrote notes: decisively, with no decorative preamble. Her mouth was cooler than Mina expected. Peppermint, salt, a trace of canteen aluminum. She bit once at Mina's lower lip, just enough to make it plain she had chosen pressure on purpose. Mina made a noise she would have denied under oath and leaned in hard enough that the crate shifted under her boot.

Rhea broke the kiss only far enough to say, against Mina's mouth, "Hold still."

The command dropped straight through Mina, low and obscene.

Mina held still for less than a second before her hands betrayed her and went to Rhea's waist. The tank was damp at the spine. Mud had dried in a flake along the hem. Under it, Rhea's skin was warmer than her hands, hard with breath and muscle, no softness offered where none existed. She let Mina have the waist, the ribs, one palm flattening between her shoulder blades. Then she shifted, turning Mina so her back met the rough concrete lip of the tunnel mouth.

Concrete grit bit through the thin field shirt. A mosquito whined near Mina's ear and got lost in the rush of blood and bats and water. Rhea kissed her again, slower this time and somehow more ruthless for it, opening Mina's mouth with patient certainty until Mina forgot what dignity had ever bought her. She spread her knees because the space demanded it and because Rhea's thigh had already worked between them.

"You see," Rhea said softly, her mouth brushing the corner of Mina's, "this is why I object to innocence theater."

Mina laughed breathlessly. "Christ."

"No. Keep up."

Then Rhea slid her hand under Mina's shirt, palm flattening over her stomach first, feeling the jump there, the involuntary tightening. Mina's body answered before she could pretend to have principles. The tunnel wall at her back was cool and sweating. The front of her was all heat. Rhea's thumb traced once over the waistband of her field shorts and paused, giving Mina exactly enough time to know she could stop it and exactly none to construct a lie about wanting to.

"Tell me to stop if you want me to stop," Rhea said.

"Don't."

Rhea nodded once, as if confirming a field variable, and put her hand inside Mina's shorts.

Rhea's first touch found the exact place that made Mina's breath tear loose. Mud had dried in powder on Mina's thigh. Rhea's fingers came away damp anyway. Mina's head knocked lightly against the concrete when she exhaled. The sound escaped raw, impossible to dress up afterward with the concrete at her back and Rhea's hand inside her shorts. It came from low in the body, ugly and immediate, the noise of something being recognized by force.

Rhea watched her while she touched her. That was the worst and best of it. Her gaze did not flicker away in courtesy. She seemed to consider the whole event part of the same reality as the tunnel, the tide, the BrightCurrent sign under rust. Mina understood with humiliating clarity that Rhea had no interest in separating appetite from context just because separation might feel safer.

"You like being looked at," Rhea murmured.

"Not always."

"No. Just when the look tells the truth."

Mina would have argued if Rhea's thumb had not just changed angle. Instead she made a sound into Rhea's shoulder and grabbed at the concrete edge hard enough to skin two knuckles. The pain was bright and clarifying. It kept the moment from floating off into any of the lies she'd once used to make sex mean less than it did.

The tide under the bank moved louder now, pushing water back through root and pipe. Behind them in the tunnel, the last of the bats dripped out late, their wings brushing the air over Mina's head. Rhea kept working her with infuriating composure, slow enough to force attention, firm enough to make attention impossible anywhere else. Mina could feel the recorder strap caught under her own shoulder blade, could smell mud baking off her calves, could taste old peppermint and iron from the split on her lip. The whole scene stayed ugly in the ways that mattered. Nothing blurred. Nothing let her off.

"Rhea," she said, and heard how wrecked the name sounded.

"I know."

That nearly finished her by itself.

Rhea kissed her once more, brief and hard, as Mina came against her hand with the concrete at her back and the wet intake breath of BrightCurrent's old tunnel on one side and the living swamp on the other. There was no room in it for transcendence. Thank God. It was better than that. It was the body insisting on itself in a place that had once been built to organize other bodies for profit.

When it passed, Mina stood there with her forehead against Rhea's shoulder, breathing in little shamed pulls. Rhea's hand left her slowly, not coy, not apologetic. She wiped her fingers on a sample bag, folded the bag over once, and put it in the trash pocket of the pack with the ordinary efficiency of someone refusing to let even desire escape the category of consequence.

"Still with me?" she asked.

Mina laughed weakly into the fabric at Rhea's shoulder. "Unfortunately."

"Good."

Rhea rested her chin briefly against Mina's temple. The touch was so light it might have been balance. It was also the gentlest thing she had done. That made it worse.

After a moment Mina straightened. The concrete had printed grit into her shirt. Her knees felt unreliable. Rhea looked at her with the same unsparing calm as before, except now Mina knew what that calm could contain.

"You make a terrifying argument," Mina said.

"For what?"

"For not underestimating you."

"That shouldn't have required demonstration."

Mina wiped her mouth with the heel of her hand and found herself smiling, which was ridiculous considering the location and the BrightCurrent sign and the fact that her whole past had just developed plumbing under the camp. "No. But I appreciate instructional materials."

Rhea actually smiled then, brief and wicked enough to alter the whole temperature of her face. She picked up the notebook, the canteen, the recorder pack and went back to work. That too was part of her terms. Desire did not excuse the labor waiting around them.

"We need to move," she said. "The route will be underwater in twenty minutes."

Mina pushed off the wall carefully. "Right."

"And Mina."

"Yeah."

Rhea waited until she was looking at her. "Don't mistake what just happened for absolution. I'm interested in you. That's not the same thing."

There was the blade again, sharp and necessary.

"I know," Mina said.

This time she did.

They took the mud path back at a faster clip because the water was already reoccupying the low places. The marl edge Rhea had warned about earlier was now a black shine under a skin of moving tide. Twice Mina caught Rhea's elbow. Once Rhea caught the back of Mina's shirt and hauled her clear of a sudden drop where the bank had slumped off. Their bodies had changed category and not changed function. They were working hands, charged hands, the same hands.

The camp lights appeared through the mangroves in pieces, rectangles caught between roots. Someone had opened the kitchen louvers. Frying oil and garlic came down the path in a hot line. Human life returned in the form of boards, screens, jokes, unpaid labor, and the observation platform still furred with guano dust and waiting to be turned donor-ready for preview weekend by morning, all of it perched over the same water that had once been directed through a biotech intake. Mina could not unknow that now. The channels under the pilings felt less like habitat and more like evidence. They were no less alive, just more implicated.

At the edge of the boardwalk Rhea stopped and took the pointer from where she had tucked it through a strap.

"Tomorrow," she said, "I need water, sediment, the recorder stripped of salt, and a route note that doesn't start a permit war. After that, we can decide how much of this tunnel exists on paper."

"You say the sexiest things."

"And yet you've already been rewarded."

Mina grinned despite herself. It faded when she looked past Rhea toward the underside of the main house. Tidewater moved between the pilings with a patient suck and release, touching wood, root, pipe, wood again. The water wasn't innocent, and it wasn't guilty either. It was connected. That was worse. Guilt wanted neat ownership. Connection spread.

"Rhea," she said, before the moment could flatten into wit again.

Rhea waited.

"I meant it. About wanting you for you."

Rhea's face changed so slightly Mina might have imagined it if she hadn't just had Rhea's fingers inside her. A small settling. Belief postponed, maybe, but not refused.

"Good," she said. "Because if you use me to make your own story prettier, I'll know."

"You probably will."

"I definitely will."

Then she stepped up onto the boardwalk and started toward the bat lab without looking back to see whether Mina followed. Mina did, carrying the weight of the pack, the taste of peppermint still ghosting under the swamp, and the new ugly fact under everything else: the creek that had been gleaming toward her like a chance at vindication did not begin untouched and wild on the far side of her life. It ran straight through the machinery of the old disgrace, under the camp, under the marriage, under the wanting. The whole wetland had a memory in it, and tonight she had put her mouth on one of the people who intended to make her answer to it.
