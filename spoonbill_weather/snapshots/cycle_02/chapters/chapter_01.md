# Chapter 1

From the water, nothing about Rookery Key looked finished. The camp stood on pilings above blackwater and mangrove roots, sun-whitened boards lifted just high enough to pretend the tide and rot had been accounted for. As the skiff nosed in, Mina's shirt pasted itself to her spine and the one-page contract in her pocket went soft at the folds. The screens along the walkways shivered in the wind. A generator thudded somewhere behind the main house. Pink flashes moved above the trees where spoonbills crossed from one pocket of shade to another, and the whole place smelled like wet rope, old diesel, mangrove mud, and onions already browning for somebody else's dinner.

It was the first place in eleven months that had offered her wages without requiring a board vote, a letter of contrition, or the kind of cheerful public humility that felt worse than being turned down. Six hundred and forty a week, a narrow bed, and the possibility of keeping the Gulf Wildlife Board payment plan current another month. The last of her savings had gone three weeks earlier into the civil penalty and the transmission on her truck. She had sold the truck yesterday. There had not been enough left over to feel noble about it.

The skiff driver killed the motor and let the boat slap gently against a bumper made from old tire halves. A skinny kid in a camp shirt stepped onto the rail before the skiff had fully settled. He moved with the easy bad judgment of somebody too used to not falling. His clothes hung a little large on him, and a pale burn crescent showed on the inside of one wrist when he bent for Mina's duffel. A grease-soft photocopy stuck from his back pocket, some diagram of a small engine with the title half erased by sweat.

"You made the low tide," he said. "That counts as a professional start around here."

"Do I get a certificate?"

"Only if you drown later. Then we put your name on something."

He grinned fast so she would know he was kidding, or mostly kidding, and swung her duffel up to the dock before she could tell him she had carried heavier things for worse reasons.

Imogen Price came down the walkway as if she had been informed a shipment had arrived that might be useful and might also explode. She was trim and sun-creased, silver hair pinned up with a yellow carpenter's pencil, field shirt faded into a respectable green by years of washing and force of posture. There was a clipboard in one hand and a seam ripper hooked into the shirt pocket with her pen, the kind of tiny practical weapon a person carried only if she had spent too much of her life rescuing linens and mosquito netting from the cheap decisions of donors.

For a second Mina saw the earlier version of her, the woman from conferences and field camps, the one who could identify twelve species by call in a windstorm and then drink everybody under a folding table. That woman had once taken Mina's side in a meeting that mattered. This one stopped three feet short and took inventory.

"Mina."

"Imogen."

The kid shifted the duffel onto his shoulder and looked between them with interest he was trying not to wear openly.

Imogen said, "Beto, put her things in the west bunk. Not the guest side. The west bunk."

"I know where we hide the unmarketable staff," Beto said.

"That is a useful skill. Go."

He went, but he did it backwards for two steps, balancing the duffel on one shoulder and his own body on the edge of the dock rail just to prove he could. Mina watched him turn the corner toward the generator shed and the low row of staff rooms beyond it.

Imogen tipped her head toward the walkway. "Come with me before the kitchen needs something else from me and this turns into another day of not saying what needs to be said."

The camp sounded built rather than settled. Boards gave underfoot with little damp complaints. Screens chattered. The generator thrum came and went as the wind shifted. Through one section of mesh Mina caught a glimpse of a side building with battery chargers lined on a table and a tall woman inside threading a cable tie through a torn mist net, dark braid tight down her back. The room smelled faintly of rubbing alcohol even out here on the walkway.

Mina looked away before the woman turned. Imogen noticed that too. Imogen noticed most things.

Her office had once been a porch. The windows were jalousies painted shut in two places, and the desk held three neat stacks of paper that would have been four anywhere else: repair invoices, guest packets, donor letters, and a smaller stack of field permits weighted by a horseshoe crab shell. A standing fan pushed heat from one side of the room to the other.

Imogen stayed on her feet. She did not offer Mina a chair.

"I am going to spare us the hopeful version," she said. "I hired you because spring guests still want a guide who can actually find birds, because the camp is short one competent adult, and because you were willing to come for temporary money on short notice. That is all true. Some less flattering things are also true."

"I can supply a few if you're running low."

"Do not start with charm. It did not save you last time."

There it was, clean and early, with no pretense that they were two colleagues meeting again under unfortunate weather.

Mina put her hands in her pockets so she would stop touching the damp edge of the contract. "What exactly are we doing here, then?"

Imogen laid the clipboard on the desk without looking at it. "You are on day work only. Dawn platform, boat guests, unloading, kitchen overflow if Beto gets buried, laundry if we lose one more volunteer to heat rash. You smile when guests require smiling. You keep your language fit for donors. You do not improvise your own role."

"I know how camp jobs work."

"Then I can be brief. You are not on any night permits. You are not on Rhea Acosta's surveys. You are not to request county access from Tomas. You are not to conduct independent research, photography, sampling, or rumor-chasing of any kind while you are under my roof."

Mina said, too quickly, "I'm here to work."

Imogen's eyes moved once across her face, sharp and almost tired. "That would have landed better if your email had not contained three questions about rookery closures after dark."

"I like knowing the boundaries."

"No." Imogen's voice stayed even, which was worse than if she had raised it. "You like boundaries right up until they interfere with desire, curiosity, or the mistaken conviction that you are the special exception. I do not have room for that here."

Heat gathered under Mina's shirt. It was not the heat outside. "You think I came all the way out here to steal marsh time between guest breakfasts?"

"I think you came because nobody else with a bed and a payroll would touch your name. I think you also came because Rookery Key sits beside exactly the kind of water you cannot leave alone."

That landed cleanly because it was half true and therefore harder to argue with.

Imogen reached behind her for a manila folder and slid it across the desk. Inside were Mina's temporary contract, a meal schedule, the week's guest list, and a typed page titled REPRESENTATION EXPECTATIONS. The first bullet instructed staff not to speculate about local wildlife phenomena in front of paying visitors.

"One week," Imogen said. "Possibly two if occupancy holds and if you make yourself more useful than risky. I am not offering you a season. I am offering you a narrow bridge and asking you not to set it on fire while you are standing on it."

Mina thumbed the edge of the guest list. The names ran from Naples and Sarasota to one Atlanta donor couple and two bird-club retirees from Michigan, the kind of people who bought binocular harnesses with climate-control opinions.

"You've gotten very managerial."

"I have gotten poor," Imogen said. "There is a difference."

The words came out flatter than complaint. Mina looked up.

For one beat the room lost its office shape and became what it was under the paperwork: deferred repairs, labor shortages, one more storm season coming. A water stain had spread along the ceiling in the corner above the permits. Somebody had painted over it twice. The stain had come through both times.

Imogen saw her see it and shut that opening at once.

"I also need you to understand this," she said. "The BrightCurrent business did not only damage you. It damaged every person who vouched for you in public. Some of us are less interested in doing that a second time."

Mina let the folder close. The paper slapped the desk harder than she intended.

"I didn't ask you to vouch for me."

"No. You asked for work. That is already more grace than most people would extend."

Silence held for a moment with the fan clicking every third turn.

Mina thought of the old photo before she meant to: blue under black water, a curve of something impossible, her own credit line under it for six bright weeks before the arguments started. Then came the hearings, and then came the word fabrication, used by people who had once begged her for field notes. Fabrication was clean language. It kept everybody's hands dry. It did not mention the cooler handle burning into her palm or the motel ice machine where she had hidden the thing one night because Jonah said they only needed twenty-four more hours.

She said, "You'll get your guide voice. You'll get clean shirts. I can do all of that."

Imogen nodded once. "Good. Because if you cause trouble here, it will not read as romantic failure or scientific ambition. It will read as me being foolish enough to hire the eel woman after everyone else learned better."

There was no use flinching at that. The guides on the mainland said worse.

"Understood."

Imogen slid the clipboard back toward herself. "Do you have any questions that are actually about the job I am paying you to do?"

Mina almost asked how close the spoonbill channels came to the western edge of camp, whether the county ran after midnight this week, whether the low water exposed the old service cuts. Instead she said, "Where do I put my laundry if it comes back smelling like the generator?"

Something almost like approval crossed Imogen's face. "Blue bin behind the kitchen. Do not overload it. The machine walks."

That was as close to welcome as she was going to get.

Beto had left Mina's duffel in a room behind the generator shed, barely wide enough for the cot, a narrow dresser, and a nail hammered into the wall at shoulder height. A box fan in the window shook like it wanted out. The pillowcase smelled of bleach on top of old humidity. Through the slats she could see a strip of blackwater and one length of the boardwalk that led away toward the county dock.

Beto leaned in the doorway while she set the duffel down.

"If the fan stops, kick the wall once near the floor. Not hard. It takes that personally."

"Good to know."

"If you kick twice, it thinks you're starting a fight and it quits for the night."

Mina smiled despite herself. "You've trained the appliances."

"I'm training everything till I can leave for trade school." He caught himself there, glanced down as if he had not meant to say that much, and hooked a thumb into his pocket over the grease-soft engine diagram. "Anyway. Dinner's at six unless a guest starts talking and then it becomes whenever mercy arrives."

"What are you studying?"

His expression changed fast, all the joking stacked aside for a second. He pulled the folded photocopy out and looked at it like it had become embarrassing in daylight. It showed carburetor parts, exploded in black-and-white and annotated in somebody else's pen.

"Nothing glamorous," he said. "I just like knowing why stuff dies."

Then he folded it down again and the quick grin came back over the top. "You need anything before Imogen decides you can carry coolers with the rest of us?"

"A shower and a fake passport."

"Shower's end of the hall. Passport's probably in donor relations."

After he left, Mina sat on the cot and let the room hold her weight. The boards under the thin mattress gave with a damp little complaint. Somewhere in the main house a screen door snapped shut. Somebody laughed. Somebody cursed in the kitchen. The camp settled around her not into calm but into its own kind of constant low-grade vibration, architecture and insects and distant bird noise making one long suspended note.

She unpacked three shirts, field pants, socks, a flashlight, the battered binoculars she had not yet been forced to pawn, and the hard case that held a camera body with no lens worth bragging about. She put the camera in the dresser drawer and shut it there. Imogen had not banned cameras by accident.

At the sink she washed the salt from her face and looked at herself in the spotted mirror. She was thirty-four and leaner than she used to be, with sleeplessness already visible under skin that otherwise took sun well and dark hair making a frizzy argument with the damp. The old prop scar along her left calf had gone white against the rest of her. She had started to look like a field version of herself somebody had left out too long.

When she came back to the room, the air outside had shifted. It was not cooler, just thicker, as if the afternoon had chosen a different grip. Mina stood by the window and listened. Beyond the generator hum and spoonbill racket she caught another engine, higher and harder, coming and going through distance. It was not a skiff. It was fan-driven, county work probably, the kind that meant night channels, fuel cages, and authorized access. She could not see the boat from here, only one turn of the boardwalk disappearing into mangroves and the idea of a dock beyond it.

She shut the dresser drawer harder than necessary and told herself again what the job was for: wages, a bed, and time to keep breathing in public until something else opened.

The blackwater below the pilings held its own face and gave nothing back.

* * *

By two in the afternoon Imogen had put Mina in a camp polo with a stitched spoonbill over the breast and sent her up to the observation platform with three guests and a plastic tub of loaner binoculars. The platform faced the guest side of the rookery, which meant the trees had been trimmed just enough to frame birds without admitting what the camp had done to the mangroves to buy that view. Heat shimmered off the railings. The boards were dusty with feather grit and a whitening crust of guano that Beto had clearly attacked with a hose and clearly lost to.

Mina slipped into the old public voice because it still came when she called for it. She talked them through the difference between the white ibis lifting off in a line and the spoonbills feeding with their bills scything the shallows. She named a tricolored heron for the woman from Michigan before the woman had fully raised her binoculars. She made the Atlanta donor laugh by describing juvenile pelicans as overconfident furniture. When the man from Sarasota asked whether the pink got brighter in mating season, she said yes, if the diet held and the birds were healthy, and kept herself from adding if the county didn't poison everything first.

It was work she could still do almost without pain. That was the problem. If she had become bad at it, the shame might have simplified. Instead the old muscle held. She could read wind on feathers and know where the birds would turn. She could hear a spoonbill's rough bill-clatter through guest chatter and know a pair was about to settle nearer the open pocket of water on the left. She had once believed that competency protected people. Then she had met institutions.

The donors were pleased enough to stay longer than scheduled and cheap enough to leave an insult in the tip jar. When they finally went back down the walkway toward iced tea and air-conditioning, Mina looked into the jar and found seven dollars, two quarters, and a sugar packet somebody had apparently set there by mistake and then decided not to correct. The Michigan woman had also left a mint wrapped in gold foil.

Mina took the money and the mint both. Money was money, and sugar became dinner often enough when weeks went wrong.

Beto came up the steps carrying a coil of hose over one shoulder and a case of canned seltzer balanced against his ribs.

"How'd the bird aristocracy treat you?"

Mina dropped the damp bills into her pocket. "I can now afford half a gallon of gas or a very small moral crisis."

"Don't spend it all on one thing."

He set the seltzer down in the patch of shade nearest the stairs and hooked one foot under the lowest rail, half sitting, half hanging over nothing. Imogen shouted from below for him to quit trying to die theatrically where guests could see. He shouted back that if he died on schedule maybe somebody else would have to unload supplies. He was talking more than he needed to. Stress did that to some people; youth did it to others. He had both working for him.

Below them, a flat-bottomed supply skiff nudged into the service side of the dock with ice, produce, cleaning fluid, a box of donor wine, and two propane cylinders lashed side by side like unwilling dance partners.

"Mercy has not arrived," Beto said. "Welcome to the glamorous portion of coastal ecology."

They worked through the dock load in the worst of the heat. The ice went first, then the wine because Imogen valued perishability in her own specific order, then sacks of rice and onions and freezer bait wrapped so badly the box leaked fish stink down Mina's thigh. A mosquito found the soft part under her wrist even in full daylight. Another got her through the camp polo at the spine.

Beto kept up a stream of commentary as if silence might let the weather win.

"Those onions are for staff chili, which means Imogen is trying to make us believe beans count as meat if the paprika's aggressive enough."

"I like paprika."

"You'll like it less on day four."

He took a propane cylinder from her before she asked for help and rolled it against his shin with practiced care. Up close the burn mark on his wrist looked older than a kitchen accident, puckered in a neat pale half-moon. He caught her looking.

"Steam tray," he said. "I was fourteen and trying to show off."

"How'd that go?"

"It cured me of showing off in food service. On docks I still think I'm immortal."

He tipped the cylinder into place against the kitchen wall and wiped his face with the bottom hem of his shirt. The engine photocopy had worked loose again from his pocket. This time Mina could read one of the handwritten notes in the margin: FLOAT HEIGHT OFF = FLOODING.

"Trade school?" she said.

He shrugged, suddenly shy under the shrug. "Marine systems if I can get off the islands. If not, air-conditioning. Everybody's desperate for air-conditioning."

"Ambitious and realistic."

"That's my brand. Don't tell anybody. I've got a reputation as an idiot to maintain."

The next box held cheap rosé and white wine packed in melting foam. Beto lifted one bottle, squinted at the label, and said, "This tastes like divorce. Put it on the guest shelf."

Mina laughed before she could stop herself. The sound surprised both of them.

He looked pleased by that and then, because people liked to give away stories when a laugh loosened them, said, "You got the spoonbills doing neck porn up there?"

"Pairing displays," Mina said.

"Sure. Scientific neck porn."

He hauled the wine box onto his shoulder. "Another week and it'll get louder. They start making those dry toy-truck sounds all night, everybody gets mean from the heat, and then some old guy on the dock swears the water under them lit up again."

Mina shifted the leaking bait box to her other hip. "Lit up how?"

Beto glanced over. The question must have come out harder than she meant it to.

"Blue," he said. "Like somebody dropped a busted motel sign into the roots."

The fish water from the box ran down her forearm. She did not feel it.

"Who says that?"

"Half the people around here if they've had enough beer, but my abuelo said it sober, so I give him extra points. He used to pole mullet traps on the back side of the rookery when he was younger than I am. Said you keep away from the spoonbills when they start breeding hard because the birds go stupid and the water under them goes weirder."

Mina set the bait box down on the dock with more care than it deserved. "He saw it himself?"

"He said his brother saw it. Then later he said he saw it once too, which means either it happened or he got old enough to steal other people's stories and make eye contact while doing it. But Tomas won't run that cut this time of year unless the county makes him, and that man will drive through weather that makes church ladies start confessing in parking lots."

"What cut?"

Beto's grin came back, but slower now. He had noticed the hook was in.

"Back channels west of the nesting trees. The side with all the roots standing up like elbows. You can hear the spoonbills before you see them. Everybody local's got some name for it. My family calls it the wedding ditch because the birds act insane."

Mina could feel the old part of her waking up, the part that turned rumor into map and map into route before anybody sensible could intervene. She started naming the variables to herself: breeding concentration, blackwater, something blue visible only under heavy biological disturbance. There were stupid explanations: dinoflagellates where they did not belong, reflected dock light, somebody's myth built on bad sleep. There were also explanations that did not sit politely in public.

She heard herself ask, "What time?"

"At night, obviously."

"No, what part of the night?"

Beto whistled very softly. "There she is."

"There who is?"

"The reason Imogen said I wasn't supposed to get you started."

Mina straightened. "I didn't ask for folklore."

"No, you asked for timestamp and habitat." He bumped the bait box lightly with his knee. "Relax. I was going to tell you anyway. My abuelo said hot nights, low water, birds making a racket like they're throwing dishes. He also said if you get close enough to see the blue you're already too close to the nests, so I guess the real lesson is old men like stories where they get to be right and mysterious at the same time."

From beyond the mangroves came the rising fan-whine Mina had heard from her room. It thickened fast, mechanical and intimate in the air, then an airboat slid into view at the end of the boardwalk beyond the service dock, county stencil on the side filmed with salt. The prop cage ticked as the engine came down. A man stepped off and tied up one-handed without looking at the cleat.

He was broad-shouldered under a sweat-dark shirt, with forearms marked by old prop nicks and fresh mosquito bites and a crooked nose that looked broken years ago and healed without much sympathy. Before he did anything else he set a paper cup on the dock post, checked that it was level, and only then unclipped a brass fuel key from his belt and knocked it once against his thumbnail as if verifying the day had not stolen it.

Beto tipped his chin. "And there is your county mystery priest now."

Tomas looked over at them with the tired amusement of a man coming off heat and noise and finding more people waiting on the dock.

"If you've broken something expensive," he said, "hide it before Imogen smells fear."

"We only broke the donor economy," Beto said. "Mina got seven dollars and a mint."

Tomas came farther up the dock. Closer, Mina could see the pale stripe a wedding band left when his hand opened off the rope. He smelled like diesel, river water, and mosquito repellent that had already lost the argument.

"Seven and a mint?" he said. "That means they found you educational but not transformative."

"I'm crushed."

His glance touched her, quick but not careless. It was a workingman's look, measuring weight, mood, whether help would actually be accepted. Then it moved to the bait box leaking onto the boards.

"You want a hand or just witnesses?"

"We've got it," Mina said.

"She says that because she's new," Beto told him. "Give her till Thursday. She'll be asking the rice sacks for legal representation."

Tomas picked up one end of the bait box anyway. "What's Imogen got you doing first day, Cordero? Smiling at retirees and carrying fish?"

The sound of her name in his mouth tightened her belly so abruptly she nearly lost her grip on the box. She reset her hand against the wet cardboard and kept her face flat. He knew who she was, then, or enough of it to say Cordero like the rumor came with a person attached.

"Apparently I'm multitalented."

"Apparently," he said.

Beto, who had the instincts of a person forever poking bruises just to see what stories leaked out, said, "I was telling her about the blue under the spoonbills."

Tomas did not pause, but he did not answer right away either. He walked the bait box up the ramp with Mina, set it by the freezer room, and wiped his hands on his shorts. The fuel key flashed once at his belt when he turned.

"Were you," he said.

"You don't have to sound so grateful."

"I'm saving gratitude for a larger emergency."

Beto folded his arms. "Tell her I'm not making it up."

Tomas looked past him toward the rookery, where the spoonbills were loud even now in the heat, rough wooden clatter carrying over the blackwater.

"People make all sorts of things up less specifically than that," he said.

"That's not an answer," Mina said.

One corner of his mouth moved. "No. It isn't."

She held his gaze. "Have you seen it?"

He leaned his hip against the freezer-room wall, suddenly still in the way that careful men got still before deciding how much trouble to invite.

"I've seen mullet turn silver under lights that weren't there a second before," he said. "I've seen phosphorescence in places it shouldn't be, runoff doing ugly little miracles, and one deputy out by Graveyard Cut swear a manatee was glowing because he wanted a story bad enough to earn one. Blue isn't hard to invent in black water."

"That still isn't an answer."

"Here is the answer part." He tipped his head toward the mangroves west of camp. "When the spoonbills are breeding hard, that side gets loud and stupid. Fish get pushed up. Birds drop lower. The county doesn't love us there after dark because every extra wake turns into paperwork and every flashlight becomes somebody's niece with a wildlife lawsuit. So if you're asking whether people talk about strange light under the birds, yes. If you're asking whether I sign my name under that on a county form, no."

Beto pointed at Mina as if presenting evidence to a jury. "See? Actionable mystery."

"You need a hobby," Tomas told him.

"I've got six. They're just all underfunded."

Mina said, "How close do county routes get?"

Tomas's eyes came back to her face and stayed there long enough to leave heat behind. Mina's throat went dry. She saw the brass fuel key at his pocket and the pale wedding-band stripe on his hand in the same glance, and the thought landed ugly and clear: county access came attached to a married body.

"Closer than camp permits do," he said.

"That's not an answer either."

"It is if you're smart."

Beto let out a laugh sharp enough to nick. "He talks like this when he thinks somebody's either trouble or pretty."

"Beto," Tomas said.

"What? Married men can still identify categories."

The wedding-ring stripe flashed again when Tomas shoved the fuel key deeper into his pocket. He did not look embarrassed. He looked like a man who had heard worse from younger coworkers and lived.

"Go stack the wine before Imogen catches it cooking on the dock," he said.

"Deflection noted."

Beto went, but he dragged the box slowly enough to keep listening.

Mina should have let the subject go. Imogen's instructions were still practically warm. Instead she said, "What makes that side so different?"

Tomas scrubbed a hand over the back of his neck. Salt had dried in a white line there. "Low water. Root tangles. Noise bounces wrong. Spoonbills get thick through there this time of year. You're always half a second from flushing something protected and getting yelled at by three agencies that hate each other. Pick a reason."

"And the light?"

"Some nights water does odd things when enough life is shoving through it."

"That's almost poetry."

"No, that's me not getting trapped by a person I met ten minutes ago."

The line should have shut it down. Instead it made her more aware of the laugh he was withholding and of the fact that he had seen exactly what she was asking for and had not walked away from it.

Imogen's voice cut across the yard from the kitchen door. "If the two of you are trading bad decisions on my freezer ramp, do it while moving."

Tomas straightened. "See? Management."

Imogen came down the steps wiping her hands on a dish towel she had already ruined beyond washing. Her donor expression was gone. In its place was the stripped-down impatience Mina had seen in the office.

"Tomas, if you have the invoice for the extra ice, I need it before dinner. Mina, the onions are still on the dock. Unless the rumor has become load-bearing, pick them up."

"Yes, ma'am," Beto said from behind the wine box.

Imogen ignored him and looked at Mina directly. "Daylight."

Only one word. It held the whole office conversation inside it.

Mina bent for the onion sack. "I heard you the first time."

"Good. I dislike reciting myself."

Tomas handed Imogen a folded invoice from his pocket. "You still owe the county for last week's extra fuel cans."

"The county can send a priest or a collections notice. I will disappoint either one."

"That's the spirit."

He stepped back to let her past, and as she lifted the onion sack again he took the bottom for one second, just enough to spare her the worst of the awkward shift. The touch was practical, but his hand came through the burlap rough and hot enough to make her breath snag. She nearly missed the split seam he was pointing at. Then it was gone.

"Careful with that left side," he said. "The seam's blown."

Mina looked down. He was right. One wrong lift and she'd have dumped twenty pounds of onions through the cracks in the dock.

"Thanks."

"Don't sound so surprised."

He went back down the boardwalk toward the county side, paper cup still waiting on the post where he'd left it. As he passed, Mina saw what was inside: mango slices dusted red with chile powder, sweating sugar into the cardboard. For one filthy second she wanted the salt and heat of it on her own tongue. Then she saw the pale ring stripe again and heard the brass key tap at his belt, and the wanting turned cheap inside her. It was not only him. It was the routes and fuel and after-dark permission clipped to his body.

Beto reappeared at her elbow the instant Tomas was out of easy hearing.

"See?"

"See what?"

"That you're thinking about it too hard already."

"I'm thinking about onions."

"You're thinking about the west channels and whether county men come with keys or just attitude."

She hefted the sack and started for the kitchen. "For a person maintaining an idiot reputation, you're observant."

"I need a side hustle."

Inside, the kitchen was wet heat and knife noise. Imogen had a stockpot going and one reading glass perched low on her nose while she checked a donor seating chart with her free hand. The room smelled of cumin, bleach, and fish thawing in defeat. Nobody spoke about the rumor again because dinner service arrived and then dishes and then guest questions about sunrise departure times and whether the mosquitoes were always this bad and whether the spoonbills ever nested closer to the platform for photographs.

Mina gave them all the safe answers. She carried bowls. She wiped down tables. She laughed in the right places. She did not ask Tomas another thing because Tomas had gone back to the county dock before dinner, and because Imogen had only needed the one word.

After dark the camp changed shape without becoming quiet. Guest lamps threw screened gold squares onto the walkways. The generator deepened. Spoonbills argued somewhere beyond the mangroves with a sound like dry branches knocking together. The air got no cooler. It only pressed closer, a wet palm over the mouth of the place.

Mina took her plate to the back steps after the guests were fed and the staff finally ate. Beto sat two stairs below her with a second helping of chili and a marine-systems school website open on his phone, application fees glowing on the cracked screen until he locked it and pretended she had not seen.

"How bad's the smell in the west bunk?" he asked.

"Like hot socks in a machine shop."

"Then the fan's working."

He ate in quick hungry shovels. The kitchen burn on his wrist shone pale when he lifted the spoon.

"Your abuelo still around?" Mina asked.

"No." He looked out into the dark while he swallowed. "He'd hate the donor wine and love stealing the donor wine, so I keep him alive the normal way."

It was the first thing he'd said all day without a joke built into the frame.

"Sorry."

He shrugged. "He'd say don't be. He had three bypasses and the manners of a pirate."

Then voices rose from the main house and the softer moment closed up, not lost exactly, just filed for later. Beto stood, rinsed his bowl, and said Imogen would have them sorting linens at dawn if the laundry volunteer did not come back from town.

When Mina finally went to her room, the west bunk held the day's heat like a grudge. She stripped off the camp polo, wrung sweat from the hem into the sink, and counted what was in her pocket: the seven dollars, the quarters, the mint flattened almost silver against the bills. She laid the money on the dresser beside the contract and did the arithmetic she hated. She set the weekly wage against the board payment, then against food if she needed anything off camp. Gas was no longer relevant because the truck was gone. She kept going until the numbers became one question: how many weeks before one missed installment turned into a notice, then a hearing, then another public room where people used the word ethics like it belonged only to them.

Outside, from far enough away to feel illicit before it even resolved into sound, the county airboat started up again.

She went to the window barefoot and looked through the slats toward the unseen dock. The fan whine rose, then steadied. Somewhere beyond the trees Tomas Acosta was taking county fuel and county permission into channels the camp could not touch after dark. Somewhere west of the nesting trees the water ran low around root tangles and birds loud enough to orient by. Somewhere in that black water people had been telling the same ridiculous blue story long enough for a dead grandfather to inherit it and pass it on.

Mina put one hand on the sill. The paint had bubbled and gone tacky in the damp.

She could have left after a week with enough money to delay disaster and no new damage done. That had been the sensible shape of the job when she took it: she would smile for guests, keep her head down, let the camp use her competence, and let that be the whole transaction.

Then Beto had said blue under the spoonbills, and Tomas had not laughed in the right place.

The old memory came back in a piece she did not invite: motel ice melting under a cheap cooler, Jonah's voice saying hold it level, hold it level, as if level could save anything. She shut her eyes until the memory thinned. What had ruined her once did not stop wanting her. That was the ugliest part. The penalty and the gossip hurt, but what shamed her more was wanting it still.

Out in the dark, the airboat moved off. She could hear it by its changing pressure on the night, the engine swallowed and returned by bends in the creek.

Mina opened her eyes.

Imogen could have the daylight version. She could have the clean shirts, the donor voice, and the woman on the platform who knew where to point binoculars and how to make retirees feel chosen by a bird. Mina would carry bait, fold linens, smile through the heat, and make herself worth the bunk.

But she was not leaving after a week if the rumor held even half the weight it had taken on in her body. She would stay. She would learn the camp's rhythms, the boardwalk to the county dock, the timing of tides and guest dinners, and who locked what where. If Tomas Acosta was the nearest route into the night channels, then he was still a route, and the thought hit her low and hot enough to make her grip the sill harder. The pale stripe on his ring finger should have closed the subject. Instead it made the whole thing meaner, because it was not only the line of his mouth or the heat of his hands she kept thinking about. It was the fuel key, the unlocked cages, and the night channels waiting on the other side of him.

The fan rattled once in the window and nearly stopped. Mina kicked the wall near the floor. It caught again and lurched back into motion.

When the airboat noise had thinned to almost nothing, she was still standing there, listening like she could hear blue through wood and water both. By the time she lay down, the decision had settled in her with the hot, mean steadiness of a hook set clean. Morning could worry about the paperwork.
