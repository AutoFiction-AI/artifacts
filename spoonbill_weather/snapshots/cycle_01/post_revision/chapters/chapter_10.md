# Chapter 10

Nobody had slept.

By ten in the morning the bat lab smelled like rubbing alcohol, hot plastic, and the sweet black rot the mangroves breathed through every screen. Rhea's recorder sat on a folded dish towel between a tray of guano bags and a chipped mug full of Sharpies. Nothing about it looked official. Mina had listened to the file six times already. It was still mostly prop wash, spoonbill racket, the slap of Tomas correcting the boat against roots. Then came the wrongness inside it, a submerged whine that bent upward where no engine should have gone, and after that a wet blue smear in the video frame that made the hair on her forearms lift every time.

Rhea stood at the counter with her braid half undone and a strip of freezer tape stuck to the back of one wrist. She had not slept. It showed more in her precision than in her face. When she was tired enough, her movements got cleaner, almost ceremonial, cutting mess down to pieces she could stack.

"Play the thirty-second segment again," she said.

Mina dragged the cursor back. The laptop gave them the same bad miracle. Dark water, spoonbills shrieking overhead, Tomas saying, easy, easy, and then the brief electric turn under the surface. Not long enough. Not clean enough. Enough to make her chest go light anyway.

Rhea listened with one hand braced flat on the counter. "All right," she said. "Three separate problems."

"Only three. That's practically a grant proposal."

"Evidence integrity, access, and your name."

Mina leaned back in the plastic chair until it complained under her. "You always know how to sweet-talk a morning."

"I wasn't hired for morning charm." Rhea peeled the freezer tape from her wrist and stuck it to the recorder case. In her block handwriting she wrote ORIGINAL CARD, then looked at it and added POSSIBLY. "We already contaminated the file chain. We copied the card onto my laptop at three in the morning, then onto your hard drive, then Tomas made us play it over the kitchen speaker because he wanted to hear whether the distortion was prop cavitation. Nobody serious is going to call that clean custody."

"It was prop cavitation."

"It was also panic, wet hands, and no documentation."

Mina watched the blue smear loop again. It still landed low in her body, the old stupid lift, vindication trying to dress like wonder. "It's better than rumor."

"It's better than rumor for us." Rhea clicked open a field notebook already striped with damp. "For anybody who can seize a site, deny access, or smell liability, it's a suggestive file produced by compromised people."

Compromised people. The phrase ought to have annoyed her more. Instead it felt accurate in every direction. Mina scrubbed both hands over her face and came away smelling salt and old sunscreen. Her skin still held the creek: mangrove tannin, dried prop spray, a little diesel in the web between thumb and forefinger. She had slept in that smell before, after Tomas, after the first night, after worse. Today it only made the room feel smaller.

"What gets us from suggestive to official?" she asked.

Rhea ticked items off on the pen cap. "A sponsoring institution in good standing. Authorized night access. A repeatable recording with date, time, and location intact. Ideally an outside witness or a chain log nobody can laugh out of the room."

Mina let out a short laugh anyway. "So all the easy things."

Rhea's mouth twitched. "Yes. All the easy things."

Outside, the camp sounded built rather than occupied. Pilings clicked under the heat. A screen door slapped and slapped again because Beto never latched it on the first try. From the rookery came the constant pink racket of spoonbills at breeding peak, birds sounding less like grace than plumbing gone feral. The noise should have steadied her. It usually did. Birds meant fieldwork, the part of her life that made sense when people didn't. Today they only reminded her that the window would close. Spoonbill frenzy would taper, water would change, and the eel would go back to being a story told by tired people with no proof.

Rhea tore a page from the notebook and pushed it across the counter. On it she had written three numbers.

"Gulf Wildlife incident intake," she said, tapping the first. "Regional permit office. Then Seth Haviland at Gulf Coast Marine Lab. He owes me a call because he still has one of my acoustic housings."

Mina looked up. "You think he'll help?"

"I think he enjoys sounding judicious on the phone. That can be useful."

"He remembers me."

"He remembers everyone. It is one of his worst qualities."

Mina folded the paper once, then again, making the numbers disappear and reappear under her thumb. The mere fact of a list made the job feel possible. Also uglier. She knew these voices. Maybe not these exact ones, but the category. Intake people who had learned her surname from memos, compliance people who got a shade too alert when BrightCurrent came up, former colleagues who had once kissed her cheek at conferences and later perfected the little pause before answering her emails. Bureaucracy did not usually scream. It preferred to remember.

Rhea watched her face and went plainer. "You can make the calls from the office. The landline won't drop every two minutes."

"You're not coming?"

"I need to back up the card and write down every way we already screwed this up." She capped the pen. "And if I sit next to you while other people say your name carefully, I'm going to start being impolite before it helps."

That got a real smile out of Mina, brief but solid. "That does sound like you."

"I know." Rhea reached out then, not soft, just exact, two fingers pressing into Mina's shoulder where tension had gathered under damp shirt fabric. "Get the answer. Even if it's no."

The office sat off the main room behind a door whose lower half had swollen enough to drag the floor. The air inside was only marginally cooler than outside, but it lacked the guano sting of the lab. In its place it had dust, printer toner, old coffee, and the permanent wet-wood breath of the camp itself. On one wall hung framed thank-you photos from donors who liked being seen loving birds. On another, Imogen had taped a hurricane prep checklist beside a calendar of paid bookings with so many pencil revisions it looked furred.

Beto was on the floor by the dead filing cabinet, prying the drawer back onto its track with a butter knife he had bent almost flat for the purpose. His camp polo hung off one shoulder, and somebody had mended the strap of his left flip-flop with a green zip tie that flashed every time he shifted his heel.

"If that printer dies while you're flirting with government," he said without looking up, "let me get the lunch invoice first. Imogen already thinks I eat toner for fun."

"I'm not flirting. I'm begging."

He glanced up then, fast eyes taking in her face, the folded number sheet, the fact that she'd changed shirts but not posture. "Ah," he said. The joke thinned out of him. "Bad-begging or regular-begging?"

"Paperwork."

"That's the bad kind." He nudged the drawer with his knee until it thunked home. "Landline works if you kick the cord where it meets the wall. Not a metaphor."

Mina set the number sheet beside the computer and did exactly that. The phone crackled awake. Beto rose with the butter knife in one hand and a grocery invoice in the other.

"Tell whoever it is we don't have the money for another mandated sign," he said. "We've still got the one about feeding raccoons and nobody even listens to that."

"Useful as ever."

"I contain multitudes." He headed for the kitchen, then paused at the door. "You look like hell, by the way."

"Thanks."

"You also look right." He shrugged, suddenly shy under his own sincerity. "Like you found the thing and now the thing's being annoying."

Then he was gone, leaving the office with the fan clicking in one corner and the phone hot in Mina's hand before she even dialed.

The first number sent her into hold music that sounded like a steel drum being punished in another room. Mina sat through it with her knee bouncing under the desk, watching a palmetto bug crawl the window screen and fail three times to find the gap at the corner. When a woman finally picked up, she spoke around what sounded like a peppermint.

"Gulf Wildlife incident intake, this is Darlene."

Mina gave her name. There was no dramatic silence, no intake of breath. Just the quick staccato of keyboard keys and then Darlene saying, very politely, "All right, Ms. Cordero. What kind of incident are we documenting today?"

The shame always hit later than the recognition. First came relief that nobody had made a performance of knowing her. Then came the tone, a careful flatness that meant her file was open on someone else's screen.

"I need guidance on reporting an unlisted aquatic observation in protected rookery channels," Mina said. "Possibly eel. Unknown species status. We have partial video and audio."

"Was the animal collected?"

"No."

"Injured?"

"No."

"Dead?"

"No."

Darlene clicked through each answer like a woman sorting screws into the wrong box. "Visible tags, lesions, foreign apparatus, entanglement?"

Mina stared at the donor photos because there was nowhere else to put her eyes. "It glowed."

Darlene stopped typing. Somewhere down the line a second phone rang and kept ringing. "I'm sorry," she said. "It did what?"

"It emitted visible blue light under blackwater conditions. The visual is partial. The audio is stronger."

"All right." Peppermint cracked between molars. "And how did you obtain this observation?"

The real question surfaced. Mina shifted in the chair. The vinyl seat had split, and the seam stuck damply to the back of her thigh through her shorts. "Field observation from a vessel."

"Authorized vessel?"

Mina watched the palmetto bug finally force itself through the screen gap and vanish outside. Clever bastard. "We are trying to regularize the reporting now."

Darlene let that sit just long enough to become answer-shaped. "I'm transferring you to compliance review. Stay on the line."

The steel drum came back, joined this time by a flute that sounded offended to be there. Mina pressed her forehead against the heel of her hand. Outside the office, somebody laughed on the guest deck. Spoonbills clattered. A pan hit the counter in the kitchen. Normal camp noises, all of them, and all of them absurdly distant from the fact that she was trying to explain a luminous eel to a state office without admitting she'd seen it while trespassing.

The man in compliance had a smoker's rasp and deliberate diction that made clarity feel punitive. Mina gave her name again. He did not say he remembered her. He did not need to.

"Ms. Cordero," he said, "your prior consent order does not prohibit you from reporting a wildlife anomaly."

How generous.

"It does," he continued, "complicate any independent handling of specimen, habitat, or associated data where BrightCurrent-origin material may be implicated. Do you understand that?"

"Yes."

"So if there is possible engineered lineage, the reporting party needs institutional sponsorship and a documented chain of custody beginning at first capture of the evidence. Preferably from a source that is not already under sanction."

Not already under sanction. The phrasing was clean enough to file. It still landed like spit.

"We've got raw media," Mina said. "Timestamped."

"Was the timestamp device-locked or user-adjusted?"

She looked at the sticky note still on the desk from last month's exterminator visit. Beto had drawn fangs on the mosquito logo. "User-adjusted, probably."

"Was location data preserved in the original file?"

"Partially."

"Was access authorized at the time of recording?"

Mina said nothing long enough that he took it for answer.

"Ms. Cordero," he said, and now there was almost a kindness in it, which was worse, "if you want this to survive first contact with enforcement or litigation, you need a sponsoring entity in good standing to originate the next capture. Camp director, university PI, county program lead, something like that. Not you alone. And if the location is inside protected rookery access, the permit variation needs to come from them as well."

The desk had a ridge where moisture had lifted the veneer. Mina ran her thumbnail along it until a curl of fake wood came loose. "So what I've got now is nothing."

"I did not say nothing. I said not formalizable on your name."

Same difference, with more syllables.

He gave her a form number, a compliance email, and a closing tone that had already filed her under future problem. Mina wrote everything down anyway, each digit pressed hard enough to emboss the next sheet in the pad. When she hung up, her hand smelled like warm plastic and salt.

She tried the permit office next because doing it in order felt like the only control left. This time the hold music was a gospel piano instrumental that kept clipping at the high end. A woman with a laugh caught permanently at the back of her throat answered, listened through Mina's description, and then asked for target species, permit class, sponsoring institution, and county access memo.

"I don't have a target species code," Mina said.

"Then I can't open the application."

"It's an anomalous observation in a protected rookery."

"Then I need a principal investigator or site authority."

"We have repeated witness possibility. We have partial media. We have ecological relevance."

"We also have a portal," the woman said, still pleasant. Mina could hear paper shuffling and imagined bracelets sliding down a forearm. "The portal requires those fields. I can't submit Unknown Glowing Fish as a permit class."

Against her will Mina barked a laugh. It surprised both of them. The woman on the line laughed too, once, tired and real.

"Look," she said, dropping the script by half an inch. "If this is a real weird thing and not somebody's goddamn charter-boat light trick, get your camp director to request a temporary observation variance tied to rookery management or restricted habitat documentation. Or get county mosquito access attached if they're already moving in there after dark. But it needs to come from somebody with standing. You know that."

Mina did know. She had known before she dialed. It still felt like swallowing shell.

She called Seth Haviland last because his number had been written in Rhea's sharper hand. It did not improve the man. He picked up on the third ring. In the background she could hear a centrifuge or an air conditioner rattling itself toward death. Seth had always sounded as if he were mid-chew. Years ago it had been fennel seeds from a film canister he kept in his shirt pocket. Apparently time had not improved him.

"Rhea Acosta only calls me when she needs something expensive," he said. "Who am I disappointing today?"

"Mina Cordero."

Silence this time. Not long. Long enough.

"Well," Seth said at last. "That's a name from a lively part of my inbox."

She could have hung up. The fact that she didn't felt less like maturity than poverty. "Rhea said you've got experience with degraded aquatic captures and external validation."

"I do. I also have a dean, a legal office, and no appetite for surprise biotech relics."

"It's not a relic."

That came out faster than she intended. Too fast, too hungry. Mina hated herself the second it was in the air.

Seth heard it. Of course he did. His voice changed, not softer, just more interested. "You saw it clearly enough to use that tone."

"Three witnesses. Partial video. Partial audio."

"Sample?"

"No."

"Untouched original media?"

"We've got the original card. We also copied it before we knew how ugly this was going to get."

"Location metadata?"

"Enough to narrow, not enough to hand somebody the root."

"Ah." He clicked his tongue once, thinking. Mina could picture him exactly then, white lab coat never fully buttoned, dry-erase marker stripe on one cuff because he always capped them with his teeth. "If you want me anywhere near this, I need a site holder requesting technical consultation, clean capture on the next attempt, and a log showing who touched what and when. I cannot step into a Cordero-origin eel story on charm alone."

Charm alone. As if that had been her method all these years. As if the original sin had not been labor, sex, and stupidity braided together until nobody could separate them. She looked down at the legal pad. Under the form numbers and names, she had written SPONSOR in caps without meaning to, each letter blocky as cinder.

"So that's no," she said.

"It's a professional no until somebody respectable owns the yes." Seth paused. "For what it's worth, if Acosta says the acoustics are strange, I'd believe the acoustics are strange."

Rhea got believed on the strength of being exact. Mina got remembered on the strength of being ruinous. The distinction was fair enough. It still scraped.

"Thanks," she said, because she had not yet degraded to begging him personally.

When she hung up, the office had gone heat-still. Even the fan sounded exhausted. Her tank top clung between her shoulder blades. On the desk, the camp computer had gone to sleep and was showing a photo of spoonbills at sunset, the donor version of the place, all pink light and no smell. Mina jiggled the mouse and opened the permit portal anyway, just to see it with her own eyes.

TARGET SPECIES required a drop-down list.  
PRINCIPAL INVESTIGATOR required text.  
AUTHORIZING INSTITUTION required text.  
ACCESS BASIS required a permit number or county memo upload.  
CHAIN OF CUSTODY PLAN required attachment.

The cursor blinked in the first empty field like it was patient enough to wait all year.

Mina let herself sit there for one full minute, hearing the lab next door through the wall, Rhea moving equipment with that maddening economy of sound. The eel was still out there under blackwater and roots, blue as bad memory. But on the screen it already looked smaller than the boxes meant to catch it.

Somebody rapped the office door with two knuckles and came in before she answered. Rhea had changed into a dry shirt, though damp had already returned at the collar. There was a pencil tucked through the base of her braid like a skewer.

"Well?" she asked.

Mina turned the monitor so she could see the blank sponsor field.

Rhea read faster than most people breathed. Her mouth went flat. "Yes," she said. "That sounds right."

"They won't take anything on my name. Not formally. Not if BrightCurrent even glances at it."

"Also sounds right."

"I need a principal investigator, a site authority, or county access attached. Preferably all three if I want anybody to stop smirking at me in full sentences."

Rhea looked at the list of notes, the embossed grooves where Mina had written too hard. She did not say she was sorry. Mina was grateful for that. Sorry only made humiliation larger. Rhea said, "Then you need Imogen."

Mina leaned back and stared at the ceiling stain shaped like Cuba. "I'd rather gargle bleach."

"I know."

"She's going to turn it into brochures."

"Probably."

Mina shut the laptop. The click sounded small and final. "And if I don't tell her?"

"Then this stays ours until it becomes nobody's. The frenzy breaks, the file degrades into a story, and you spend the rest of your life describing a thing institutions never had to recognize."

That was true enough to hurt. Rhea moved closer and rested her hip against the desk. Her hand found the back of Mina's neck, thumb brushing the damp hairline there. Not tenderness exactly. Calibration.

"You wanted proof," she said. "Proof comes with people."

Mina tipped her head back until it touched Rhea's wrist. The skin there smelled faintly of alcohol wipes and the creek. "That's disgusting," she said.

Rhea's thumb pressed once. "Yes."

Mina stood before she could stay seated long enough to become tragic. She took the notes, the recorder case, and the laptop with its lousy blue smear. By the time she crossed the walkway toward the main house, spoonbill noise had risen with the heat until the whole camp sounded full of rusted hinges. The office forms were tucked under her arm, softening at the corners with sweat. Every box she could not fill in felt like a witness.

* * *

Imogen's office was technically a corner of the dining room screened off by two tall cabinets and an optimism problem. At lunch it became the camp's nervous system: booking notebook, first-aid drawer, radio charger, donation jar, stack of bird checklists curling in the humidity. Now it also held a spread of invoices weighted by a jar of screws, because nowhere else at Rookery Key stayed dry enough to keep paper from warping into confession.

Imogen stood over the desk in a faded field shirt that had once been khaki and was now the color of old straw. She was writing thank-you notes on camp stationery, each sentence in the same disciplined slant, while two donors on the other side of the room discussed spoonbills like a catered amenity. Silver hair had come loose from one side of her pins and was sticking to her temple. She did not look up when Mina stopped in the doorway.

"If this is about sending guests into the south blind again, the answer is still no," she said. "The rail is not safe enough for rich knees."

"It's not about guests."

That got her attention. Imogen capped the pen and looked up fully. Her gaze moved from Mina's face to the laptop, the recorder case, the packet of forms gone wavy from sweat. Something in the inventory made her dismissive patience vanish.

"Office," she said.

"This is the office."

"Then close the dining room door."

Mina did. The donors' voices flattened behind wood. The generator thrum came forward. So did the kitchen smell, onions and frying oil and the sweet edge of something starting to burn because Beto always forgot one pan when he had three going.

Imogen stayed standing. "What happened."

Mina set the laptop down, opened it, and pulled up the thirty-second clip. She had thought on the walk over that she might stage this, ease into it, present process before miracle. Instead she hit play.

The room filled with spoonbill shrieks, prop noise, Tomas swearing at the water under his breath, then the brief submerged turn of impossible blue.

Imogen did not gasp. That would have been easier. She leaned one hand on the desk and watched all thirty seconds with the kind of attention she used on snakes by the boardwalk: no drama, no wasted movement, total commitment. When it ended she said, "Again."

Mina played it again. On the second pass Imogen stepped closer, squinting not at the blue smear but at the edges, the frame shake, the time stamp. A donor laughed behind the closed door. Somewhere on the roof a branch scratched once and went quiet.

"Who saw it?" Imogen asked.

"Mina, me, and Tomas," Rhea said from the doorway.

Mina had not heard her come in. Rhea held the recorder's hard case under one arm and a labeled freezer bag under the other. She looked like a woman arriving to witness an autopsy she had scheduled herself.

Imogen straightened. "You are confirming that what I just watched is not wishful thinking or sleep deprivation."

Rhea came in, shut the door again behind her, and set the case on the desk beside the laptop. "I am confirming that three people saw an anguilliform body emitting blue light beneath the spoonbill disturbance zone. I am also confirming the file is compromised as evidence."

That made Imogen's head tilt, almost birdlike. "Compromised how."

"We copied it before we logged it. The original media is intact, but chain of custody starts ugly." Rhea tapped the case. "We can do better next time if there is a next time."

Imogen looked from her to Mina. "There is a next time if you can put me on legal ground to have one."

Mina slid the forms across the desk. The top page had a coffee ring the size of a sandpiper egg at one corner. "That's the problem."

Imogen read faster than seemed decent. Mina watched her eyes take the hits in order: sponsoring institution, authorized night access, compliance routing if engineered lineage suspected. The director's mouth thinned once. Then, to Mina's disgust and reluctant admiration, it sharpened into something like life.

"What exactly have you told already?" Imogen asked.

"Gulf Wildlife intake. Compliance review. Regional permit office. Seth Haviland."

"You called Haviland before me."

"I was trying not to turn this place into an annex of my mistakes."

Imogen set the papers down. "This place already sits over an annex of your mistakes."

Mina flinched despite herself. Rhea's gaze cut sideways, warning and tired in one movement. Imogen saw it, rubbed two fingers over the bridge of her nose, and went managerial again.

"No," she said. "That was unnecessary. What I mean is that if this touches BrightCurrent lineage, we're already implicated by geography whether I enjoy that fact or not."

The pronoun landed harder than the insult. Not your eel. Not even Mina's problem. We.

Imogen pulled a yellow legal pad from beneath the invoice stack. On the top bill, in thick blue type, the camp owed twenty-seven thousand four hundred dollars before hurricane inspection season. Mina caught the figure only because Imogen shoved the paper aside with the impatient intimacy of someone who had memorized it. A second invoice showed generator parts. A third, pier bracing. The room smelled suddenly less like lunch and more like wet wood trying not to admit rot.

Rhea saw Mina looking. "You hadn't shown her?"

"I hadn't intended to show anybody until I had a way to make them less obscene." Imogen sat at last. The chair complained. "Now I may."

Mina folded her arms, partly against the air conditioning that never reached the office corner, mostly against the speed of Imogen's mind. She could almost hear the gears: habitat rarity, donor appetite, scientific legitimacy, restricted-access story. A serious ornithologist lived under all that management polish. Mina had seen flashes of her before, usually when someone else mangled bird behavior or spoke sentimental nonsense within earshot. That woman was up now, only she had bills under her elbows.

"What are you thinking," Mina asked.

Imogen looked at her, insulted by the need to say it aloud. "I'm thinking that if a luminous eel event is repeatable under a protected rookery on my leasehold, then verified footage could alter this camp's survival odds in a single board cycle."

"It could also bring enforcement, seizure, donor stampede, BrightCurrent lawyers, and every amateur freak hunter in three counties."

"Yes." Imogen's voice stayed calm. "That's why I'm thinking rather than celebrating."

Rhea leaned against the cabinet. "It's not repeatable yet. It's correlated to breeding disturbance and maybe low-light conditions. Maybe. We don't have enough nights."

Imogen tapped the legal pad with the pen cap. "How many days of peak frenzy do we have left?"

"Maybe four where I'd bet on it," Mina said. "Maybe less if the weather turns or the water shifts."

"And the behavior occurs only under disturbance?"

Mina thought of spoonbills bursting over blackwater, guano stink and pink wings and that impossible turn below them. "Under frenzy," she said. "Not generic disturbance. The whole creek was lit up with breeding insanity. Fish running shallow, birds losing sense. It came up inside that."

Imogen nodded once, absorbing. "Good. Specific."

"Not good," Mina said. "Specific. Different thing."

Imogen ignored that. "Can Haviland validate a clean capture if I request technical consultation?"

"He said not on my word alone," Mina said. "He wants site holder request, clean chain, and somebody respectable owning the ask."

Imogen's smile was humorless and brief. "Well. There's the answer."

She wrote three words on the yellow pad in crisp capitals: CAMP LETTER. Under that she wrote PERMIT VARIANCE. Under that, WITNESS LOG. The act felt indecently fast. The eel was already sliding into clerical categories.

Mina heard herself say, "It's not an asset."

Imogen looked up. "Everything here is an asset or a liability the minute a form exists for it."

"That isn't the same as the thing itself."

"No. It is the same as what institutions can do to it." Imogen set the pen down. "You wanted proof. Proof is organized appetite."

The phrase hit too close to home. Mina looked away first. Out beyond the screen wall the afternoon had gone hard white, light flattening the shell path and the mangroves alike. Beto darted past the window with a tray of iced tea glasses, moving too fast for the glasses and somehow not spilling a drop. The camp went on selling beauty while three people stood over a bad video and a stack of debt.

Rhea cleared her throat. "Even if Imogen sponsors, we still need cleaner equipment. Mine is built for bats. The audio range is wrong, and the housing is improvised."

"We also need authorized nighttime access," Mina said. "Permit office said either site authority or county attachment."

Imogen's eyes moved to her, then to Rhea, then to the shut dining room door. The room was still missing its last conspirator.

She did not need magic. Tomas came in thirty seconds later through the kitchen side, smelling of fuel, tide mud, and the mosquito district's institutional soap. He carried a black Pelican case in one hand and two sealed battery packs dangling from the other by their cords. Sweat had darkened his county work shirt down the spine. A strip of orange survey tape was knotted around his wrist, probably something he had forgotten to remove after tying back reeds or marking a culvert. His hair had been flattened by a cap and sprang up wrong where he'd dragged it off.

"I passed Beto," he said. "He told me there was either a murder or an opportunity and either way I should wipe my boots."

Then he clocked the room: laptop open, forms out, Imogen sitting, Rhea with the recorder case, Mina holding herself like a boarded window. His expression changed by degrees, amusement thinning into the hard focus that narrow channels pulled out of him.

"So we're doing this," he said.

Imogen pointed at the Pelican case. "Tell me that's relevant."

Tomas set it on the desk with care that made Mina more nervous than if he'd dropped it. The case still had a county asset tag on the handle and a strip of red tamper tape across one latch.

"It is if you like clean timestamps," he said. "Drainage response had an underwater inspection camera sitting in the lock cage because nobody's used it since the culvert blowout last fall. Low-light head, hard timecode, external battery. Not built for fish, but neither are spoonbills."

Rhea pushed off the cabinet and came closer. "How did you sign it out?"

Tomas gave her a look that said she already knew the answer and disliked hearing it aloud anyway. "Night culvert survey support. Which isn't fully a lie if you squint and hate me."

Imogen's tone went flint-flat. "Whose authorization number is on that form."

"Mine routed through District Three maintenance."

"That is not an authorization. That is bait."

"Yeah." Tomas rubbed the back of his neck, leaving a smear of grease there. "I know."

Mina stared at the case. It was the most official-looking object in the room by a mile. Hard corners, seal tape, barcode. The kind of box people believed before they even saw what was inside it. She could also see the trap immediately. County asset log, sign-out time, fuel use to carry it, maybe an extra battery pull, maybe a clerk asking later why mosquito control needed underwater footage in breeding channels at night. Tomas's risk had always smelled like diesel and sex to her. In the office light it looked like paperwork.

"How bad is the audit already?" she asked.

Tomas glanced at Imogen, measuring whether to spread this piece of bad news any wider. Then he shrugged. "Bad enough that Marvin at the cage counted jerry cans out loud this morning for my benefit."

He said Marvin like everybody ought to know him. Mina didn't. Tomas saw that and added, "Little man, red readers on a neck cord, keeps sunflower seed shells in his shirt pocket because he thinks spitting them in the water is less rude than a cup. He never counts out loud unless somebody upstairs told him to."

That was bad, then. Worse than Tomas's tone admitted.

Rhea touched the tamper tape with one fingernail. "Can this record to duplicated sealed media?"

"Two cards at once. One stays in the unit. One ejects." Tomas looked at her, then at Imogen. "I brought blank custody envelopes too."

He pulled them from the side pouch like a magician with depressing tricks: brown evidence envelopes, pre-numbered seals, a log sheet on waterproof paper. Imogen's whole body stilled. Mina hated that she recognized the same thrill in herself. Not because any of it made the eel more real, but because it made reality legible to the kind of people who got to decide what counted.

"Jesus," Mina said.

Tomas flashed teeth at her without humor. "County loves a form. Thought I'd borrow the part of government that likes one."

Imogen took the waterproof log sheet. "Who helped you."

"No one helped me. That's the problem."

"That is not what I asked."

Tomas leaned one hip against the table, tired enough not to posture. "Sonia on day maintenance left the cage book open because her kid's orthodontist called, and the night supervisor thinks every cable in the world exists for mosquito work if you use the word drainage three times. Nobody helped me on purpose. That is probably worse."

Sonia and her orthodontist. Marvin and his sunflower seeds. The whole thing rested not on some criminal genius but on ordinary county distraction, the same tired human looseness that kept camps running and got people caught. Mina felt laughter rise in her throat and stay there, too dry to come out. Of course the path to scientific legitimacy ran through somebody's kid's braces and a man with red reading glasses.

Imogen looked down at the legal pad again. "How fast can the gear be returned unnoticed."

"Unnoticed, I can't promise. On time, tonight if I'm lucky."

"And fuel?"

Tomas's face closed by a degree. Not angry. Measured. "Enough for one clean run if I don't act stupid on the route sheet."

"You have been acting stupid on the route sheet for weeks," Rhea said.

He looked at her and, because this was still them inside all the forms, answered with the ghost of a grin. "Yes, doctor. The difference is now I've got accessories."

That should have eased the room. It almost did. Then Imogen said, "All right."

Everyone looked at her.

She pulled the yellow pad closer and began writing with fast, decisive strokes. "I'll request a temporary observation variance tied to sensitive habitat documentation. I can do that under site authority by the end of the hour. I won't mention specimen capture because we aren't capturing anything. I'll mention anomalous nocturnal aquatic activity in relation to spoonbill disturbance and possible environmental hazard review. That language gets attention without inviting immediate poachers."

"Environmental hazard," Mina repeated. "That's a good way to have the site shut down."

"It is also a good way to make county nighttime presence sound ordinary if we need it." Imogen did not look up. "The question is not whether this is dangerous. The question is which danger buys us one more night."

Rhea folded her arms. "If you use hazard language, BrightCurrent counsel could surface fast."

"If BrightCurrent counsel surfaces fast, then they were already watching these waters harder than we knew." Imogen finally looked up, eyes hard and alive. "Do not confuse delay with safety."

Mina heard the old field-biologist ferocity there, almost enough to love her for it. Almost enough to trust her, which was more dangerous. "And what do you get," she asked, "if this works."

Imogen answered without pretending not to understand. "A chance to keep this camp open through inspection season. A reason for the board to fund repairs instead of telling me to close two cabins and call it prudence. Maybe a restricted habitat grant if the footage is good enough. Maybe enough donor appetite to stop pretending maintenance can be paid for with gratitude."

She tapped the invoice stack. "Do you want the noble version or the accurate one? Because the accurate one is twenty-seven thousand four hundred dollars in repairs, a generator that coughs when it rains, and guests who like the idea of wildness right up until the bathroom backs up."

The money finally spoke aloud. Not abstract scarcity. A number, thick enough to bruise. Mina could almost see the camp from outside then: the leaning rail at the south blind, the patched screen by cabin two, Beto's bent butter knife fixing things not meant to be fixed with kitchen metal. The eel had been private obsession partly because private obsession did not come with payroll.

Rhea said, "This can't become a show."

"It becomes a show the minute someone repeats it in the wrong marina," Imogen said. "My preference is controlled documentation, not donor night safaris."

Tomas snorted. "You say that now."

"I say it as the woman who would have to stop them," Imogen replied. "And I'm very good at disappointing wealthy people."

That, at least, got a laugh out of Tomas. "Yeah," he said. "You are."

The dining room door opened two inches, and Beto slipped his head through before anyone could bark at him. He had an apron tied crooked over his shorts and a dish towel over one shoulder. There was a burn scar on his wrist Mina had seen a hundred times and somehow not fully noticed until then, pink rope against brown skin, probably oil splash from the kitchen, probably one of many things he'd learned not to dramatize.

"Are we having a coup," he asked, "or can I get the stapler."

Imogen held it out without looking away from Mina. "Both, apparently."

Beto crossed the room, took the stapler, and let his eyes dart once to the Pelican case, once to the frozen blue frame on the laptop. Unlike the adults, he did not pretend not to understand when something mattered.

"That thing better fix the roof," he said quietly.

Then he was gone, shutting the door with his hip.

The room took his sentence and went still around it. Mina saw the invoice stack under Imogen's hand, the Pelican case with its county tag, the stapler Beto needed for lunch receipts, and the kitchen door still swinging on the hinge that never sat right after hard rain. The costs were all there at once: roof tin, printer toner, payroll, pier braces. The eel had not stopped being an animal, but now it was sitting in the middle of camp with a price on it.

Imogen drew a line down the legal pad and started assigning names on either side.

"Rhea," she said, "you handle chain log and media isolation. You are the only person in this room I trust to describe contamination without sounding guilty or thrilled."

"I'm a little thrilled."

"So am I. You hide it better."

Rhea accepted that with a slight incline of the head.

"Tomas, you handle equipment and county cover only to the extent that it does not put you in handcuffs before sunset."

"Ambitious standard."

"It is the standard available." Imogen wrote his name down anyway. "Mina, you write a factual observation memo with no adjectives that belong in a donor newsletter. Date, time, water condition, bird behavior, exact sequence. Then you will sit on your hands while I turn it into the request language people actually approve."

Mina laughed once, ugly and short. "You want me involved enough to see it and quiet enough not to name it."

"I want you involved enough to make this true and restrained enough not to kill it with history before we get one clean record."

There was no answer to that that did not become confession or accusation. Mina chose neither. "And who owns the footage," she asked instead.

Imogen put the pen down carefully. "Legally, once it's collected under site authority and with camp equipment participation, the camp's in the chain."

"That isn't what I asked."

"I know." Imogen met her eyes. Low-contraction, donor-safe, absolutely unsentimental. "Nothing about this is private now. Not if you want it believed."

The office had gone so quiet Mina could hear one of the battery packs ticking as it cooled. She looked at the Pelican case, the custody envelopes, the camp letterhead pad under Imogen's hand. An eel in blackwater. A crooked blue body. Last night it had felt like the creek answering something in her. Today it sat between roof invoices and a stapler Beto needed for lunch receipts.

Tomas broke the stillness first. "There's one more thing."

Nobody liked the sound of that.

He rubbed his thumb over the county asset tag on the case. "If I take district gear and district fuel into those channels, and if anybody starts asking why, I'm either on a sanctioned night survey or I'm stealing with paperwork. There isn't much middle."

Rhea said, "You already were."

"Yeah, but this version has barcodes."

Imogen lifted one shoulder. "If the permit variance pings before tonight, I can at least say I requested site coordination."

"And if it doesn't?"

"Then you decide how much county trouble you can afford."

Tomas let out a breath through his nose. It was nearly a laugh, nearly surrender. "Fantastic. Love a plan with moral texture."

Mina watched him looking at the case, calculating debt against risk the way he always calculated fuel against tide. Not theatrical. Not noble. Just arithmetic under pressure. She knew the engine loan sat on him like weather even when he joked, knew the county check mattered in ways desire never canceled. That was what made this uglier now. They were not only hunting a creature. They were shifting weight around a system already bowed.

She said, "You can walk."

All three of them turned to her.

"If the audit's already smelling blood, you can put the case back and we figure something else out. Pole. Paddle. Wait. I didn't ask you to add another lie to the stack."

Tomas's face softened and hardened in the same second. "No," he said. High contraction, easy until it wasn't. "But you did ask me to see it right."

That shut her up.

Rhea reached for the case at the same time Imogen reached for the permit forms. Tomas did not move his hand from the lid until Rhea's fingers settled over his knuckles, brief and intentional. Not romance for display. Not apology either. More like a signature only the three of them could read. Then he let go.

Rhea clicked the latch and opened the Pelican case. Inside, nested in gray foam, lay the inspection camera head, cable spool, monitor, duplicate media slots, and a small waterproof keyboard that made the whole thing look absurdly ready for a future that should not have included luminous eels. A county inventory card sat on top in plastic sleeve: asset number, check-out time, initials. Bureaucracy with its shoes off.

"Oh," Rhea said, and for the first time all afternoon there was no restraint in her voice at all. "That actually might work."

Imogen heard that and smiled, tired and sharp. There was relief in it, yes, but more than that there was acquisition. Not greed exactly. Need given structure.

"Good," she said. "Then we proceed."

She tore the top sheet off the yellow pad and handed it to Mina. Across it, in her neat slanted hand, were the tasks and names already arrayed like a field map:

CAMP LETTER - IMOGEN  
VARIANCE REQUEST - IMOGEN  
CHAIN LOG - RHEA  
EQUIPMENT / COUNTY ACCESS - TOMAS  
OBSERVATION MEMO - MINA

At the bottom she had written, underlined twice, NO OUTSIDE CALLS WITHOUT CLEARANCE.

Mina stared at that last line. It should have felt insulting. Instead it felt like the chapter closing on one kind of ownership and opening on another, colder one. She thought of the empty sponsor field on the permit portal, of Seth Haviland saying respectable, of the compliance man's careful voice around the phrase not already under sanction. Proof came with people, Rhea had said. She had not added that people came with ledgers.

"Fine," Mina said.

Imogen nodded once and filed agreement with the rest of the paperwork in her head. She reached for the camp letterhead and began feeding a sheet into the printer. The machine whined, caught, then spat the page out crooked. From the kitchen Beto shouted, "Kick it again," without needing to be asked.

Tomas laughed, finally real. Rhea rolled her eyes and straightened the sheet. Mina stood there with the task list in her damp hand while the office filled with printer noise, spoonbill screams, frying onions, and the low electrical tick of county gear cooling on an island that could not afford honesty except in pieces.

By the time the letterhead printed clean, the eel had acquired a sponsor, a witness log, a borrowed camera, and four people who could lose money if it disappeared. By dawn Tomas would have to take the cleanest version of the story back to the county and find out whether fluorescent light made it look like theft. It was still out there in blackwater under roots and bird shit and heat shimmer. But it no longer belonged to darkness alone. It belonged to signatures, to debt, to whatever happened when a miracle crossed a desk.
