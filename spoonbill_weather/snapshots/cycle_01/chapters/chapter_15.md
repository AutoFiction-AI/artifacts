# Chapter 15

The paddleboards were still dripping under the bat lab when Beto came up the screened walk with the camp phone in one hand and a piece of toast in the other, chewing like whatever panic was in the receiver had interrupted breakfast rather than replaced it. He had blue pen across his knuckles again, torque numbers from the outboard manual he kept trying to memorize between dish duty and dock work, and one of the digits had smeared where he had wiped butter away with the heel of his hand.

"Imogen wants you and Dr. Acosta at the county dock," he said, voice full around the last bite. "Right now. She said right now twice, so I think she means it the bad way."

Rhea came out of the lab behind Mina with her braid half redone and a fresh alcohol wipe smell clinging to her wrists. They had been packing last night's bat cards into labeled sleeves, quiet work after the paddleboard survey, letting the tenderness of the evening keep its own shape without naming it too much. Mina still had black mangrove grit under one thumbnail and the ache in her shoulders from poling through root shade. For one drowsy hour she had believed the day might stay small.

"What happened?" Rhea asked.

Beto shrugged one shoulder. The oversized camp T-shirt slid down and showed the pale burn ladder on his wrist from the kitchen fryer back in May. "County radio. Something about positives. Something about a board member's nephew. She was already swearing before she hung up."

That narrowed it enough.

The walk to the mosquito dock took them through shell glare and wet heat so dense it seemed to press back. The camp's boardwalk gave under Mina's bare soles where the planks always sweated brine by midmorning. Beyond the mangrove fringe the air held the metal-flat taste of weather not yet willing to break. Mosquitoes found the backs of her knees even in full sun. Somewhere out in the rookery the spoonbills kept up their constant rough nursery racket, chicks begging, adults clattering, a whole colony behaving as if politics did not exist and could not arrive by county memo.

At the dock trailer Imogen was already inside with both hands on the edge of a scarred laminate table, wearing her reading glasses on the beaded chain she never let donors see. Her pinned-up silver hair had started to come loose on one side, which on Imogen counted as a public collapse. Across from her sat a county woman in a green district polo darkened under the arms, late thirties maybe, with a glittery kindergarten star sticker still attached to the corner of her clipboard. Mina noticed it because the rest of the room was all lock cages, faded safety placards, stale coffee, and the hard cold from an overworked wall unit. The sticker did not belong. That made it human enough to hurt.

"There they are," Imogen said without relief. "Marlene, this is Dr. Rhea Acosta. Mina is with camp operations."

With camp operations. Not with the eel. Not with BrightCurrent. Not with trouble. Mina took the demotion and stayed against the wall.

Marlene Velez stood to shake Rhea's hand first, then seemed to remember she had not offered one to Mina and corrected too late. She had the brisk, exhausted face of someone who had been explaining the same emergency to ten different angry people since dawn. "Thank you for coming fast. We have two presumptive arboviral cases on the mainland side, one pediatric, one elderly, plus horse positives north of the causeway. Commissioners want visible control before tonight's press scrum. State wants adult response in the low marsh blocks. We are reviewing sensitive habitat constraints before the district director signs off."

"Reviewing," Imogen said. "That's a generous verb."

On the far side of the room Tomas leaned against a file cabinet with his cap crushed in one hand. Mina had not known he would be there. His county shirt looked freshly pulled from a plastic drawer, too stiff in the shoulders from old detergent and not enough wear, and the ridge of beard along his jaw said he had shaved yesterday for no good reason and not gotten the benefit of it. He did not look at her right away. He was staring at the laminated map on the table, all the creek lines he knew better than most prayers.

Rhea moved in beside Imogen and read the map upside down with one palm flat on the paper to stop the curl. "What time window?"

"Tentative twenty-one hundred to zero-zero thirty," Marlene said. "ULV pass along the outer channels and the residential edge. We can keep the primary spray corridor outside the rookery buffer if conditions hold."

"If conditions hold," Rhea repeated. She had gone plainer already, which was how Mina knew she was angry. "The spoonbill chicks are at flush age in those interior trees. If your wind changes by even a little, you don't only get drift. You get panic. You get nest loss. You also get bat emergence right through your fog line if you run that late."

"We are aware of the bat survey."

"You're aware that I sent you a map," Rhea said. "That's not the same thing."

Imogen slid a permit packet across the table so hard the staple tore the top page. "Protected rookery. Active breeding. Existing written objections from this camp to any emergency adulticide within the mapped margin. I do not care what your commissioners want to look like on camera. If you hit those trees, I will hand the state your own route sheet."

Marlene put two fingers against the bridge of her nose, then lowered them carefully, as if even irritation needed to be managed on paper. "Ms. Price, I respect your position. I also have state health on my back, a county board calling every twenty minutes, and residents who do not give a damn about spoonbills if their kid is in ICU. We are trying to thread a needle here."

Mina knew that tone. It was the tone institutions used when they meant: your damage is regrettable but already budgeted.

Tomas finally looked up. For a second his eyes rested on Mina and then moved away to Rhea. "Who's running the air pass?"

"Contract bird out of Naples," Marlene said. "One helo. We need a local support boat inside because the outer channels shifted after the last blow and half my regular people know the maps better than the mud."

"Your regular people," Tomas said, "or the ones who haven't pissed you off lately?"

Imogen's gaze flicked to him. She had not known that part, or had not known it would be said in front of company.

Marlene's mouth tightened. "Your suspension is under audit review, not termination. Emergency assignment is separate."

There it was. Not even bait dressed up as principle.

Rhea went still enough that Mina could hear the wall unit click and cycle. "You suspended him on fuel irregularities and now you want his channels because the commissioner wants a show of control. That's not separate."

"It's payroll and weather," Tomas said. "Everything is separate until it isn't."

He said it dryly, but Mina heard the raw edge under it. He had been angry since chapter thirteen in more directions than one, and the anger had settled into something denser than volume. It sat in him now like a weight under the breastbone. She hated that she could hear it and still think first of the rookery map, of the chicks hanging in those trees like unfinished thoughts.

Marlene tapped the map with a capped pen. "We can still modify. That's why you are here. Give me the highest-risk corridors. Fast."

So they did. Not because it would stop the spray, Mina realized almost immediately, but because refusal would let the district claim ignorance later. Rhea named bat emergence lines and the mangrove pockets where heat held longest. Imogen marked the spoonbill trees, the heron spillover, the donor platform as if public embarrassment were another protected species. Tomas, after a silence long enough to count as a choice, pointed with one blunt finger at the blackwater bends where drift would pool if the wind turned off the Gulf. Mina heard herself adding root height, channel pinch points, the old annex side current that sucked surface sheen inland on a falling tide. Nobody asked whether she should be speaking. Nobody thanked her either.

The map got better. The decision did not.

A radio crackled on Marlene's belt. A man's voice, too loud with distance, asked whether the director wanted the revised block notes before the county attorney came in. Marlene answered yes and no at once, then looked back at the table.

"Here is what I can do," she said. "We push the start later by thirty minutes to reduce direct day-roost exposure. We instruct the pilot to hold the interior line unless the support boat clears it. We maintain buffer as flagged. But the response window is being authorized. The director is signing in the next ten minutes."

Imogen gave a short laugh with no amusement in it. "You brought us in to help you draft cleaner blame."

"I brought you in because if I didn't, you'd say nobody consulted wildlife."

"Nobody did."

Rhea was already writing on the back of one of the district forms, hard enough to emboss the paper below. "You need a wind cutoff. Four knots and shifting inland, you abort."

"Noted."

"Not noted. Written."

Marlene took the paper, read it, and added it to the stack. "Written."

"And no pass over the interior canopy. None."

"If conditions allow."

Imogen's glasses swung on their chain when she straightened. "Say it plainly. You are authorizing a spray over a protected breeding site because your board is afraid of headlines."

Marlene met her eyes. Whatever softness the sticker suggested was gone. "I am authorizing emergency mosquito control because two people may die and my board would rather answer for dead birds than dead voters."

The cruelty of it was not in the sentence. The sentence was ordinary enough. The cruelty was in how familiar the arithmetic sounded. Choose your body count. Print the cleaner one.

On the table lay the final route sheet with its squares and initials, the language already doing its work: adult response, target block, low-volume application, non-target minimization. Mina had spent too many years around documents that turned living things into acceptable collateral. She could feel the old BrightCurrent part of her attention waking up, the part that scanned verbs before blood.

Marlene signed her name on the witness line while the director was still, somewhere else, about to sign the main authorization. That was how the disaster entered the room: not with engine noise, but with blue ink drying under air-conditioning.

When they stepped back into the heat, the cold of the trailer left a wet chill between Mina's shoulder blades. The dock boards burned through her sandals. Tomas stayed behind long enough to speak with Marlene at the gear cage, head bent, one hand on the latch. Mina did not need to hear the words to know a bargain was being offered in the language of usefulness.

Imogen pinched the bridge of her nose and said, "I need to notify the board and start an incident file before they pretend I agreed to any of this."

"I need the lab," Rhea said. "If they hit emergence, I want boxes ready and saline mixed."

Imogen looked at Mina. "Keep Beto off the water unless I say otherwise."

As if there were a version of this where bodies would stay abstract.

Mina glanced back toward the cage. Tomas had his cap on now. Marlene was handing him a radio and a laminated badge clipped to a retractable cord, the county giving him back just enough authority to be blamed with it later.

The spoonbills kept clattering out in the heat, blind to paperwork.

***

By late afternoon the camp had taken on the kind of false busyness people built when they could not stop the thing coming and refused to stand still waiting for it. Imogen was in the office dictating a formal objection into her laptop with the clipped voice she used for funders and enemies. Beto hauled extra crates from the generator shed, trying so hard to look useful that he almost missed the step down from the walkway. Rhea lined the bat lab counter with saline, towels, syringes without needles, nitrile gloves, all the small humiliating equipment of damage control.

Mina found Tomas at the county dock beside an airboat that was not his usual run boat, though the tiller had been wrapped in the same black tape all district equipment eventually acquired under his hands. A five-gallon mix tank sat strapped behind the driver's stand. A respirator hung from the rail. Two unopened boxes of marker flags sweated in the shade. Tomas was checking the fan cage bolts with a socket wrench he did not need for those bolts, only for something to do with his hands.

He had changed into long sleeves despite the heat and rolled them to the elbow anyway. One cuff carried a faint gray-green stain that had survived many washings. Mina knew the smell before she got close enough to confirm it: diesel, old spray residue, and the cheap bar soap from the camp showers.

"You don't have to do this," she said.

He did not jump. He had heard her coming on the shell. "That line worked better before noon."

"It still works."

"No. It sounds better than the truth. Different thing."

Rhea came down the path a few seconds later, not hurrying, which made her arrival feel more serious than panic would have. She had changed into field pants and tied her braid tight again. A pencil was tucked through it where another woman might have used a decorative pin. "Tell me what the assignment is."

Tomas set the wrench on the rail with more care than it deserved. "Support boat inside the outer line. Wind call, marker flags, route correction if the contract pilot starts freelancing. If things go clean, Marlene says it helps demonstrate cooperative conduct while the audit review stays open."

"Cooperative conduct," Mina said. "Jesus."

He gave her a look without humor. "That's what jobs sound like when they want you grateful."

Rhea stepped close enough to put one hand on the airboat rail between them. "If the wind shifts, you will be the first person with eyes on it. You need to refuse the interior pass. Not object. Refuse."

"And then what?"

"Then they write down that the district forced an unsafe application over an active rookery against field warning."

"You hear how much that sounds like a document and not a rent payment?"

The words were quiet. That was worse. Tomas loud could still turn back into joking or temper. Tomas quiet meant the sentence had already been lived with before it was spoken.

Mina moved around to the bow because she could not stand talking to his back. The shell path glittered under the low sun, white and mean. "They suspended you because your numbers got dirty. They bring you back for one ugly night, and suddenly you think they'll love you again."

"I don't need love. I need my badge not dead."

"You need your name not on this."

"My name's already on worse."

That landed where he meant it to. Her throat did not tighten; that would have been too generic for the heat. Instead the wet strap of her bra seemed to cut deeper into the skin under her shoulder blade, one more piece of material refusing to sit right. She looked at the mix tank behind him and imagined the route sheet from noon, the approved verbs, her own voice on the map helping refine the geometry of harm.

Rhea said, "The spoonbill chicks are edge-standing. If they flush, many of them don't know how to get back to center. They hit water, roots, each other. The bats will emerge through the aerosol because that is when they emerge. You know this."

"I know it," Tomas said. "I know it from before either of you had to tell me. That's not the part you're failing to explain."

He turned then, fully, and Mina saw how tired he was. Not vague tiredness, not some poetic hollowing. His lower eyelids were rubbed raw pink as if he had slept with his forearm across them. There was a nick at his jawline where the shave had gone wrong. He had bitten one thumbnail to a ragged crescent, which he only did when the money got bad enough to turn physical.

"The note on the skiff comes out in two days," he said. "The county health plan is what paid for the urgent care when I split my hand last year. Audit review turns into insubordination if I tell them no on emergency duty. Insubordination turns into done. Not suspended. Done. Marlene doesn't even have to say it that way. That's what it is."

Rhea's hand stayed on the rail. "I know exactly what it is."

"Do you?" he asked, and there was no grand cruelty in it, just a tired man's inability to leave a soft lie standing. "You've got grant money this month. She's got camp wages till Price finally throws her out for honesty or scandal or both. I've got a district that wants a body on a fan stand who knows these channels in the dark. That's the whole romance of it. I'm useful right when useful is ugly."

Mina said, "Don't make this about us wanting clean hands."

"No? Then what is it about."

She could have said the birds. The bats. The water. The eel surfacing later in poison. She could have said she did not want to watch him become evidence. All of it was true. None of it came out first.

"It's about them using your fear because they can," she said.

"That happens to be how work has always worked."

Rhea took her hand off the rail and tucked it under her opposite elbow, containing herself by inches. "Then hear me as a scientist, not as your wife and not as her anything. If you see the wind go inland, you stop the line. I don't care if somebody above you screams. I don't care if they fire you tomorrow morning. If you let that fog into the interior trees, I will spend the rest of this season documenting dead young from a damage pattern I warned them about in writing."

Tomas nodded once. "If I see the wind go inland, I call it."

"Call it and stop it are different."

"Rhea."

She held his gaze. Mina watched the two of them in the slant light and felt again what chapter fourteen had left in her: intimacy without rescue, witness without delusion. Whatever this marriage was now, it still had room for this exact kind of devastating clarity.

Tomas reached for the wrench, then thought better of it. "I am not doing this because I think the county is right."

Low contraction on purpose. Emphasis. He heard it too, because he looked annoyed with his own mouth.

"I know," Rhea said.

"I'm doing it because if I vanish from that roster completely, they don't put me back on when the panic passes. They say the word reliability and that's the end of the sentence."

Mina stepped closer until she could smell the day's sweat dried in his collar. "Then let them call you unreliable for not poisoning a rookery."

His laugh came out small and ugly. "You say that like you haven't built half your life on taking the bad ride now and promising yourself you'll explain it clean later."

There it was. More fair than kind.

She took it because she had earned it. But she did not move back.

"Maybe that's why I know the shape of it," she said.

For a second he looked like he might touch her face or shove past her or do nothing at all. What he did was pick up the respirator and drape it around his neck.

"Stay upwind if you can," he said. "If you smell sweet instead of swamp, you're in it. Keep the kid out of the center channels unless birds start dropping. Then use towels, not bare hands. And wash everything twice after. Shoes too."

That was the tenderness left to him: instructions.

Rhea said, "Tomas."

He looked at her.

"If you can stop it, stop it."

He nodded again, once, and climbed onto the stand. The engine kicked after two tries and settled into a dirty growl that made the dock planks hum. He pushed off without flourish. The boat slid from the shade into white water and then around the bend toward the district shed, a county machine carrying a man who knew exactly what it could do.

Mina kept watching after he was gone, after the wake had already licked itself flat against the pilings.

"You love people in expensive ways," Rhea said quietly.

Mina looked at her. The sentence was not accusation exactly. It was diagnosis with weather in it.

"So does he," Mina said.

Rhea's mouth shifted, not quite agreement, not quite refusal. "Yes."

They walked back through the heat without touching. Above the mangroves the sky had gone the color of dirty brass, storm-light without storm, all pressure and no relief.

***

At dusk the camp sounded overprepared. Latches. Plastic bins. The slap of wet towels into buckets. Imogen on the office phone saying the words documented objection so often they lost grammar and became a pulse. The generator shed rattling under a load it did not want. From the bat lab came the high insect whine of chargers and Rhea's voice giving Beto instructions in careful, numbered pieces, the way she spoke when she needed someone young not to hear the fear underneath precision.

By full dark Mina was on the observation platform with a stack of milk crates, two towels, a headlamp set to red, and Beto vibrating beside her like exposed wire. He had changed into rain pants over his shorts, though the night was dry, and stuffed his pockets with nitrile gloves until he looked padded in the hips. A cheap saint medal flashed once at his throat when he bent to lift a crate, then disappeared under his shirt again. Mina had never seen him wear it before.

"My mom's going to say this is because people moved here and planted decorative bromeliads," he said. "She says all county policy comes down to new people wanting old bugs dead."

Below them the dark channels breathed rot, salt, and low tide. Farther out the rookery made its layered living noise: chicks rasping, adults landing heavy, occasional wing beats thudding through leaves so dense the birds themselves stayed mostly invisible. Mina knew where the trees opened just enough to show pale feathers when the moon rose. She knew which root tangles hid the blue if the blue chose to come. Tonight every piece of knowledge felt contaminated by advance warning.

"Your mom isn't wrong often," Mina said.

"No," Beto said. "That's one of her worst qualities."

He was trying. She loved him for trying.

At the edge of the platform Imogen stood with binoculars and a district radio Marlene had been too hurried to reclaim. She had traded field shirts for a yellow slicker she never fully buttoned, the old one with a torn cuff where a pelican had stabbed through years ago. She kept pushing her glasses back up with the heel of one hand and then forgetting they were on her head instead of her nose.

Rhea came up from the lab carrying two small animal carriers lined with dish towels printed with faded oranges. "If they start dropping live, bring them straight to the lab. Chicks, bats, whatever you can get without going under active rotor wash."

"That sounds like a clause written after somebody sued," Beto said.

"That's because it's essentially a clause written after somebody sued."

He huffed a laugh and then looked ashamed of it immediately.

Mina tested the wind with damp fingers. It moved from the Gulf side then stalled, uncertain. The night was so hot her shirt stuck under her breasts before the real work even started. Mosquitoes skinned the back of her neck. Somewhere toward the county channels an engine turned over once, died, turned again.

The first pass began as sound without shape. A far rotor thud under the insect chorus. Then another sound below it, flatter and meaner: an airboat fan finding pitch. The noises moved together through the dark in staggered alignment, one overhead, one lower in the channels, a machine duet designed by people who would never have to collect the result.

Imogen raised the binoculars. "Outer line," she said. "Still outside."

Mina saw the helicopter lights before she saw the body of it, red and white blinking over the mangrove edge. Farther downchannel a work light swept once across black water, caught a trunk, disappeared. The rookery sound changed by degrees. Not panic yet. Attention. Adults shifting in nests. A first brittle burst of complaint from deeper in the trees.

The helicopter made its run along the mapped edge. In the beam wash the aerosol barely looked like anything, just a temporary whitening in the dark, a thinness passing over thicker air. Then the smell hit half a minute later: sweet, oily, wrong. Not swamp, not salt, not any honest rot. Chemical citrus over hot metal.

Mina spat over the rail and tasted it anyway.

"Upwind," Tomas's voice cracked over the radio clipped to Imogen's slicker. "Hold that line. Hold it."

Imogen snatched the handset. "District support, this is Rookery Key. Your drift is carrying."

Static. Then Marlene, distant and flattened by transmission. "Reading acceptable. Continue monitor."

Rhea swore under her breath, rare enough that Beto's head jerked toward her. She was already looking out past the platform, toward the low black openings where the first bats should have started threading into the air. Mina saw one, then three, then twenty. Tiny bodies flicking above the mangrove skin, hunting because that was what twilight meant in their blood.

"This is exactly the hour I told them not to use," Rhea said.

The second pass came lower.

Whether the pilot misread the channel, or the wind took him, or somebody above Marlene decided close enough counted, Mina never knew. She only knew the rotor sound dropped until the platform boards shivered under her feet and the tops of the outer mangroves flattened like hands pushed down on a head. The aerosol line bent inward. Airboat fan roar surged from the side channel almost at the same time. Tomas's work light flashed once through leaves, too close.

Then the trees exploded.

Spoonbills came out of the canopy in a single violent lifting, too many bodies for the dark to hold. White and pink and raw gray chick-flesh, adults broad-winged and blind with panic, juveniles skidding into branches, nestlings pitched loose because the world had suddenly become wind and poison and noise. The sound did not resemble bird sound anymore. It was cloth ripping, a hundred wet coughs, branches beaten hard enough to sound like breakage. Mina saw one chick cartwheel through open air with its neck extended and no idea what flying was supposed to be. It hit a root arch and folded over it like dropped bait.

"Fuck," Beto said, and then louder, because once was not enough, "fuck, fuck."

Imogen was shouting into the radio. Rhea was shouting toward the lab for carriers no one could hear. Mina was already moving.

The stairs down from the platform were slick with old guano and fresh spray mist. She took the last three too fast and slammed her shoulder against the railing hard enough to leave splinters through her shirt. Beto hit the ground beside her with a crate and nearly lost it in the mud. The air below the platform tasted more chemical, heavier because it had nowhere to go. Above them the mangroves thrashed under rotor wash and fan blast, branches lashing one another while birds battered out through gaps too small to forgive them.

Another body fell. Then three.

Mina ran toward the nearest root line. The mud there sucked at her boots and released with obscene little kisses. A spoonbill chick lay on its side half in the water, old enough for pink to show under the fuzz but not old enough for dignity. One leg kicked straight out, then curled. Its crop had burst against something on the way down and half-digested fish pasted the white down under its beak. She wrapped it in a towel because Tomas had said towels and because bare hands would have felt like theft at that point. The chick was hotter than it should have been, heat trapped under chemical damp.

"Live one here," she shouted.

Beto answered from somewhere left of her. "I got two. No, one. One's bad."

Bad did not begin to cover it. Mina found the second where he meant, hanging from a forked mangrove branch by one wing and opening and closing its beak on nothing. She had to climb two roots to reach it. When she touched the body, the wing skin sloughed a little under the mix of fresh blood and aerosol. Not broken cleanly. Torn. The chick jerked once against her hand, a shocked, useless pulse.

She got it down. There was no saving it. There wasn't time for ceremony either. She laid it above the water line and marked the root with a strip of towel torn off in her teeth because they would need counts later, because even now part of her mind was inventory.

The helicopter banked back out over the dark, but the damage kept happening after the machine passed. Panicked adults circled, crashed, lifted again. Chicks leaned too far out of nests calling for parents already disoriented. Bats streaked through the suspended fog and dropped in small black commas onto the water, some floating, some still beating their wings until the tannin swallowed them.

Rhea splashed in from the boardwalk with another crate and a net bag. Her glasses were gone; she had tucked them somewhere safer or already lost them. "Anything breathing, lab first. If the eyes are sealed with residue, don't pry. Flush at the sink."

"How many carriers?" Mina asked.

"Not enough."

That was the honest number for most things here.

From the interior channel came the roar of an airboat cutting hard and then, abruptly, the ugly cough of an engine dropped to idle. A work light swung toward the shoreline. Tomas's voice, naked now without radio static because he was close enough, carried over the water.

"Pull him up. Pull the fucking bird up."

The helicopter did not immediately climb. Mina saw it skim the canopy again, rotor wash flattening a stand of buttonwood beyond the rookery edge. Then it jerked higher all at once, too late to count as mercy.

Tomas shoved the airboat into the mud at a slant and jumped off before the hull settled. He wore the respirator around his neck, not over his face, and whatever protective protocol the county had handed him had already failed at the level of human urgency. Spray glazed his sleeves. His eyes found Mina, then the towel bundle in her arms, then the root line littered with things too still to sort yet.

He came toward them through shin-deep blackwater. The airboat fan idled behind him, chewing the night to pieces even at low speed.

"Get back from the interior," he said. "Pilot's circling for confirmation."

Imogen reached them from the boardwalk at the same moment, yellow slicker flapping open. "You tell your pilot this site is under written protest and active casualty."

"I already told him," Tomas snapped. "He answers to the district and a contract."

Rhea thrust a carrier at him. "Then help, or get out of the wash."

For half a beat Mina thought he would refuse, not from malice but because refusal would keep the lines between roles intact. Instead he took the carrier. His gloved hand brushed the towel around the chick in her arms. He winced at the wet heat of it through fabric, as if the body burned.

"Jesus," he said softly.

"Yes," Imogen said. "Now earn the county payroll."

That hit him. So did the sight in front of him. Mina watched his face lose the last defensive tightness and turn into something flatter, worse. Not innocence. Tomas had too much mud under him for that. This was the exact instant usefulness became guilt.

Above them another rain of bodies began, not from fresh rotor strike now but from nests destabilized by the earlier flush. Small fish too, silver flashing as they broke the surface and rolled belly-up in the drift film. The water near the roots wore a skin of rainbow under the headlamp glow.

"Beto," Mina shouted. "Left side, under the low fork."

"I see it."

He sloshed past carrying a live chick wrapped in his rain jacket. His face had gone so young with horror that the patchy mustache disappeared entirely. When he handed the bird to Rhea his hands kept moving afterward, opening and closing around nothing as if still trying to find the weight.

"It's breathing weird," he said.

"That's because its lungs aren't where they should be happy," Rhea said, and then, gentler because she heard herself, "Bring me the next one."

Mina turned toward a fluttering shape in the reeds and found not a bird but three bats knotted together by panic and spray, each one tiny enough to fit in her palm and furious enough to feel much larger. Their fur was slicked down. One had residue foamed at the nostrils. She used the towel corner to separate them while they bit at air. Rhea knelt in the mud beside her and took them one at a time with the deliberate hands she used for things that frightened her.

"Females," Rhea said. "Lactating."

The word hit like another body.

Tomas was behind them now, lifting fish and birds into separate crates because species still mattered even inside catastrophe. Over the radio at his belt Marlene kept asking for conditions. He did not answer the first time. On the second request he ripped the handset free.

"Conditions are dead juveniles in the root line," he said. Each word landed hard and level. "Conditions are bats down in active drift. Shut the pass."

Static. Then Marlene: "Copy non-target observations. Director requests completion of remaining outer block."

Tomas stared at the radio as if it had become an animal from the wrong century.

"No," he said.

The single syllable went nowhere big enough. It vanished into rotor noise, chicks screaming, Imogen swearing at nobody she could sue fast enough.

"Acosta," Marlene said, suddenly using the county voice, the one built for personnel records. "Complete your assignment."

Rhea looked at him.

Mina did too.

He did not answer for one second, two. Then the helicopter banked away for real, heading back toward the outer dark, either because someone else had finally made the same call or because the pilot had seen enough chaos not to want his own liability. Tomas let out a breath that shook the respirator against his chest. He clipped the radio back to his belt like it smelled bad.

"Help me move the live crate," he said.

There was no clean heroism in it. He had not stopped the first pass. He had maybe helped stop the next one. Both truths stayed.

They worked until counting stopped being possible in the field and became merely necessary later. Mina carried spoonbill chicks with legs bent wrong, with crops bruised black under the skin, with eyes sealed by residue. She found a little blue heron chick not meant to be here but here anyway, neck trembling against her wrist. She lifted mullet slick with spray and set them in a separate tub because fish kills changed reports. Beto gathered bodies with tears hanging off his jaw and no time to wipe them. Once he said "sorry" to a dead bat before putting it in a specimen bag, then looked furious that she had heard.

The worst part was not the noise. It was the intervals when noise dropped away and the surviving chicks kept begging from the trees as if the adults would still return on schedule.

By the time true dark settled, the boardwalk stank of chemical mist, guano, fish blood, and the hot-plastic smell of headlamp batteries running low. Imogen had opened an incident notebook with her handwriting carved so deep through the paper it likely marked the pages below. Rhea's forearms were mottled with mud to the elbow. Tomas had spray drying on his sleeves in pale saltish tracks. The rookery itself sounded wrong now, patchy and startled instead of full.

Mina stood shin-deep at the edge of a pool under the platform and watched a spoonbill chick she had set aside finally stop moving. The water around her boots was studded with guano, leaves, and a drift of tiny white down that stuck to the sheen like ash.

This, she thought, was what institutions meant by manageable.

***

Near midnight the machines were gone, and the absence of them was almost as violent as the noise had been. The channels kept a long after-ring, as if metal had entered the mangroves and left their leaves remembering it. Somewhere offshore thunder muttered without committing to land. The air had gone heavier, not cleaner. Everything on Mina's skin felt coated.

The live chicks that still had a chance were in the bat lab under dim light, each in its own towel nest or shallow box. The room smelled of saline, guano, warmed feathers, and the sweet petroleum ghost of aerosol brought in on clothing. Rhea bent over the counter flushing one bird's eyes with a syringe while speaking into her recorder in a voice so controlled it seemed separated from the woman using it.

"Juvenile spoonbill, approximately fourteen to seventeen days," she said. "Respiratory distress. Right ulna fracture, likely impact. Residue visible on periocular tissue and nares. Collected zero-nine-forty-eight p.m., interior root margin."

The recorder clicked. She set it down, closed her eyes for half a second, then opened them and reached for the next syringe.

Imogen was in the office on speaker with somebody from regional wildlife, reciting numbers with the kind of exhausted formality that sounded like anger dressed for court. Beto had fallen asleep sitting upright against the outside wall with a specimen cooler beside him and his saint medal stuck to the damp hollow of his throat. One hand was still gloved. The other was tucked under his cheek like a child.

Mina could not stay inside any longer. The room's careful triage made her feel crazier than the chaos had. Outside, the boardwalk planks were slick with tracked mud and spray. The observation platform loomed above the dark like a bad idea built in lumber. She walked past it to the lower dock where they had started stacking the dead in lidded fish tubs because even grief needed containment against raccoons, heat, and paperwork.

Tomas was there hosing down the airboat he had brought back on district order after debrief, though the weak stream from the dock spigot only moved the residue around and made little poisonous rivers between the planks. He had stripped off the county overshirt and stood in a gray undershirt dark with water at the chest. His forearms looked scrubbed raw. The respirator lay on the dock beside him like a kicked muzzle.

He heard her and cut the hose.

"Rhea needs help in the lab," Mina said.

"I already gave her the counts I had."

"That isn't the same thing."

"No," he said. "It isn't."

Moonlight and dock light together showed what the night had done to his face. Spray had dried at the corners of his eyes. He had a streak of mud across one cheek he had missed on purpose or not cared enough to notice. The county badge was still clipped to his belt, plastic face turned inward against his thigh. It looked obscene there.

"Marlene says the director will write limited non-target impact unless state pushes for a site visit," he said.

Mina leaned one hand on a piling slick with old salt and fresh hose water. "Limited."

"Because the block got cut short and because they only count what gets documented. She's not wrong on procedure. She's rotten on meaning."

He set the hose down. Somewhere out in the dark a single adult spoonbill flew over late and alone, wings catching moonlight like torn paper. Mina watched it vanish toward the interior trees and felt the empty places under that flight more than the flight itself.

"Did you stop it?" she asked.

He understood the question exactly. Not the helicopter. The larger thing.

"No," he said.

No excuse attached. That was worse and better.

"I called drift after the first bend. They said continue monitor. I called active casualties after the second pass and got ordered to finish outer block. By then the damage was already in the trees." He rubbed the heel of one hand against his mouth. "I should've cut the line sooner. I kept thinking if I held the pilot off one more minute, if I talked clear enough on the radio, if I did it by steps, maybe I kept the job and the birds. That's a stupid man's dream."

Mina thought of documents again, of every stage-managed compromise she had ever accepted because total refusal seemed too expensive while partial resistance still let her imagine herself decent. The old annex logbook. The cooler. Tomas with fuel. Her own mouth on promises she had known were rotten at the core.

"It's not a stupid man's dream," she said. "It's the district's whole business model."

Something close to a laugh broke in him and failed.

He looked down at the fish tubs stacked three high. "There are more out there we didn't get. Tide will move them by morning."

"I know."

He nodded toward the far channel. "Marlene also said if state comes, I was reassigned temporary support and followed route command. Like she was already writing me into a sentence that keeps the district cleaner than me."

"She was."

"Yeah."

The hose dripped between them. On the dock, beside a tub of dead juveniles not yet labeled, lay one of Beto's gloves turned inside out where he had stripped it off with his teeth. The cheap latex finger pointed toward the water like accusation.

Mina said, "You can take that badge off."

Tomas touched the clip but did not remove it. "Not yet."

There it was again. Material reality humiliating ethics by timing alone. Not yet because the county still paid. Not yet because the paperwork wasn't finished. Not yet because a man could know exactly what he had helped do and still need next week's deposit.

From the lab came a muffled clatter and Rhea's voice saying Beto's name in a tone sharp enough to wake the dead if any of them had been sleeping. Beto answered, groggy and ashamed, and Mina pictured him jerking upright against the wall, nineteen again instead of the old grief the night had tried to shove into him.

Tomas followed the sound with his eyes, then looked back at the water.

"How bad in there?" he asked.

"Bad enough that Rhea has stopped lying to herself about triage."

"Meaning."

"Meaning she's working on the ones that can still swallow."

He shut his eyes. The dock light made the wet lashes shine.

The channel below them carried a slow surface drift toward the rookery interior, a black mirror broken by leaves, by a silver fish turning once and not again, by chemical rainbow caught in the seams between current lines. Mina smelled mangrove rot under the spray for the first time since dusk. Honest stink coming back through the false sweetness. It should have felt like relief. Instead it made the contamination more intimate, as if the marsh had accepted the insult and was already being made to metabolize it.

Then the blue came up.

Not dramatic at first. A thread under the surface, a seam of cold light moving through tannin the way a vein shows under dark skin when the lamp hits right. Mina saw it near the second piling and thought for one deranged instant that her eyes were still carrying afterimage from headlamps and radio LEDs. Then the thread curved. Thickened. Became a body.

"Tomas," she said.

He was already looking.

The eel rose slow through the poisoned skin of the water. Blue traveled along its flank in pulses, not lantern-bright but enough to stain the sheen around it with a ghost color no natural thing in these channels should have owned. It surfaced beneath a drift of white down and one dead mullet, lifting them both on the convex of its back for a second before they slid away. The head broke water just enough for Mina to see the blunt old intelligence of it, the mouth opening once at the air or the film or nothing she could name.

Beautiful was still the first word her body offered. Her mind rejected it so hard she nearly tasted blood.

There was nothing innocent left to attach the sight to. The glow moved through a broth of runoff and spray beneath a dock where dead chicks waited in tubs and a county badge still hung from a tired man's belt. The eel was not miracle. It was survival with a patent history. It was contamination that had learned how to keep living. It was the annex logbook given muscle. It was every bad compromise made flesh and then made luminous enough that people might still try to call it wonder.

Tomas said, very quietly, "Look where it comes up now."

The eel rolled once under the piling shadow. Its light touched the underside of the dock and found the rivulets from the hose, the dripping chemical wash, the rainbow edges in the grain of the wood. Mina thought of chapter nine, of the shared sighting under spoonbill frenzy when even dread had still carried a charge close to joy. That version of the animal was gone whether the body was the same or not.

Behind them the lab door banged open. Rhea came down the walkway fast enough to rattle it, bareheaded now, braid half fallen apart, one glove still on and one hand bare. She took in their stillness and followed their gaze to the water.

The eel surfaced again, closer to the root line this time, weaving between two floating fish. Blue lit the undersides of leaves splashed low by tide. For a second the whole injured margin of the marsh wore the color.

Rhea stopped beside Mina. She smelled of saline and bird shit and the iron stink of stress sweat under repellent. "Of course," she said.

Not awe. Not disbelief. Of course. As if this, too, had become legible now that the clean story had finally died.

The eel slid forward through a clot of feathers. One spoonbill plume stuck along its back and rode there like a white scar before the water took it. Mina felt the sight enter her in the place obsession used to live and ruin the room forever.

No one reached for a camera.

No one said discovery.

From the lab a surviving chick gave a thin, stubborn cry. Beto answered with a soft "coming" that carried through the dark because at nineteen he still answered suffering like it might listen. Imogen's office voice rose again on the phone, now naming species and counts with the crisp fury of a woman preparing to be disbelieved. Over the water thunder moved closer. The storm would probably come too late to wash anything clean and just in time to spread it farther.

The eel turned under the surface, blue dwindling, then returned once more in a deeper channel pocket where the spray sheen had gathered thickest. There it hovered nearly vertical, head just below water, as if listening to the camp catalog its dead.

Mina understood then that there would be no version of the story left to save. Not the camp's, not hers, not Tomas's. Whatever came next had to be said with the poison still on it.

The light went down into blackwater and stayed there.
