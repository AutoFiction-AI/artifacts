# Chapter 13

By the time Imogen came into the bat lab, Mina had scrubbed her hands three times and the annex was still in the lines of her palms.

The sink in the survey room ran sulfur-warm from the rooftop tank, never cold enough to matter. It had taken the top layer off the mud and none of the smell under it. BrightCurrent had smelled different when it was alive and funded. Filtered air, citrus cleaner, the fake-quiet of expensive corridors. The annex under the mangroves smelled like wet concrete, rat droppings, old batteries, and a long argument with the tide. That smell had followed her back under her shirt. It sat in her bra, in the waistband of her shorts, in the damp strap of her watch. Even here, with guano and rubbing alcohol and battery heat pushing from the shelves, she could pick it out.

She had wrapped the logbook in two contractor bags and laid it on the metal table anyway. Beside it sat her old visitor badge, the laminate clouded from water and time. Her twenty-something face grinned out of it with a level of confidence that made her want to put her thumb through the picture. The lanyard clip had rusted into a hooked brown knot.

Rhea had arrived first, barefoot in rubber clogs, braid still wet at the nape from the quick shower she took after morning work. She had started by saying nothing. That was worse than alarm from almost anybody else. She only put on nitrile gloves, tugged each finger snug with her teeth, and stood over the bagged book while Mina talked her through the route in: low tide, root tangle, hatch half jammed with shell grit, interior steps slick enough to kill a fool. Now she was reading one page for the third time, pencil tucked behind her ear, mouth set flat. When she concentrated she lined objects into exact relation without seeming to notice. She had already squared the badge to the edge of the table and turned the logbook so the handwriting ran parallel to the metal seam.

Tomas leaned against the doorjamb, not entering far enough to count as settled. He had a county envelope folded into his shirt pocket so many times the paper had gone soft and furry at the creases. Suspension paperwork, probably, or another request that he explain himself in complete sentences to somebody who had never had to lift a fuel can in August. Mosquitoes found the backs of his calves where the work socks stopped. He killed one with his thumbnail and looked at the blood on it before wiping it down the side of his shorts.

"You couldn't wait an hour?" he said.

Mina knew what he meant. Couldn't wait until he had finished replacing the busted hinge on the skiff locker. Couldn't wait until the guest lunch cleared. Couldn't wait until there was a gentler way to say she had gone alone into the place he had lost work helping her reach.

"No," she said. "I couldn't."

He gave her a single nod that held no agreement in it.

Then Imogen came in with her reading glasses hanging on a sun-faded cord against her field shirt and a chipped camp mug in her hand. Some old sanctuary logo had worn down on the side until all that remained was a crane leg and part of a green marsh. She set the mug down near the bat-detector charger, saw the contractor bags, saw Mina's face, and stopped being a director in front of staff and became something narrower and more dangerous.

"If this is another half-formed emergency," she said, "I would prefer one that waits until I finish feeding paying people."

"It's not half-formed," Mina said.

Imogen looked at the badge first. Mina watched recognition move through her, not of the photo exactly but of the institution. BrightCurrent had touched every field station and permit office on this coast for years. Gift money, pilot partnerships, habitat language polished until it could slide past anything.

"Where did you get that?"

"Out of the annex."

Imogen's gaze came up sharply. "You went in there alone."

"Yes."

"At low tide?" Rhea asked, still looking at the page.

"Low enough. I had maybe forty minutes before the route turned stupid."

Rhea nodded once. "And you found records."

"Maintenance book. Emergency notes. Intake checks. Some of it is rotted, but enough isn't."

Tomas pushed off the jamb at last and came to the table. He smelled like old gasoline and hot metal and the cheap soap from the county dock bathroom. For a stupid second Mina wanted to lean into it the way she had on the walk back from the mosquito launch two weeks ago, when he had let her steal shade from his shoulder and called her a menace for sweating through his shirt. The wanting did not vanish because the room had gone bad. That was part of the problem. The body kept voting after the rest of it had turned.

He looked down at the badge. "Jesus."

"Yeah," Mina said.

The photo had caught her before the fines, before the public hearing, before enough bad sleep could roughen a face that fast. Hair shorter, mouth cocked like the world had been designed with her in mind and she had almost forgiven it for the trouble. She remembered the day the picture was taken. Jonah behind the camera telling her to look less like she wanted to steal the building.

Rhea flipped a page with gloved fingers. Water had swelled the paper and then dried it crooked. Whole corners had melted. But the center lines held: dates, initials, tank numbers, salinity columns, one recurring notation in a hand so neat it felt insulting. Mina had copied it into her own notebook on the walk back because she had not trusted herself to remember without making it smaller.

"'Expedited release pursuant to containment compromise,'" Rhea read. Her voice stayed level. That made the phrase uglier. "'Sentinel stock transferred through auxiliary intake under storm directive.'"

No one said anything for a beat.

The bug zapper outside the lab door cracked once. From the main house came a burst of guest laughter, cut short as if somebody remembered where they were supposed to be and took the volume back down.

Imogen took her glasses off the cord and put them on. "How many pages?"

"Forty-two that I could separate without turning them into pulp. There are more fused together."

"Any signatures?"

"Initials. Shift notations. Tank references."

"Dates?"

"Storm week." Mina swallowed. The room tasted metallic, as if the annex rust had climbed into the back of her throat. "Years back. Before the hearing. Before my photo went wide."

Imogen took that in and asked the next question exactly as herself. "Do these pages say release, or do they say loss?"

"They say release." Mina pulled the notebook from her back pocket and opened to the page where she had copied the clearest lines. Her handwriting had gone hard and narrow. "They used softer words around it because that is what paperwork does when it wants not to sound like a dump. But they moved live stock into the system. Not one animal. A line."

Tomas looked from her notes to the badge and then to her face. "So it wasn't just the cooler."

"No." She could hear how hungry that answer sounded. She hated it. "No. It wasn't."

There had been a version of the truth she had been stupid enough to want on the walk back. Not innocence. She had not been that stupid. But scale. Context. A larger sin that made hers look like a splinter. She could still feel the exact moment it had died in the annex, standing under a dead emergency light with the badge in one hand and the logbook in the other, realizing the wetland had not been waiting to absolve her. It had already been seeded with other people's decisions and then left to carry them in its own body. The first eel might still have been the one she and Jonah walked out in a cooler. Or it might have been one of these. The point was there would never be a clean answer that returned her to herself.

Rhea read farther down the page. "Auxiliary intake. That means the tunnel route was operational."

"At least during evacuation. Maybe longer."

"And if this stock survived." Imogen tapped the contractor bag once, a tiny contained movement, as if she would rather strike the table harder and had decided not to spend the energy where it could not invoice back. "Then the camp is not sitting on a miracle sighting. It is sitting on an abandoned biotech release under protected habitat."

"It's both," Mina said.

"For the purposes of what happens next, it is whatever people with lawyers can be forced to hear."

There it was. Not disbelief. Not even denial. Translation. Imogen could take blood and turn it into a briefing packet before it dried.

Tomas barked a laugh with no humor in it. "Of course."

Imogen did not look at him. "If these pages hold up, the camp becomes the reporting site. The witness site. Not the origin point."

"Publicity," Mina said.

"Survival," Imogen said. She finally turned. "Do not say that word to me as if it only means donor cocktails and brochure copy. If BrightCurrent put engineered stock into this system and the evidence surfaced here, then there are mitigation funds, legal obligations, habitat review, a reason the board cannot tell me to patch the pilings with borrowed screws for one more season and be grateful."

Her tone stayed formal for three beats and then the facade split, just slightly.

"I have repair invoices stacked in my office like kindling," she said. "If this place goes under, the birds do not get a moral victory. They get one more rotten platform in a creek full of them."

Mina knew it was true. That was the annoying thing about Imogen. Her opportunism nearly always had a real bill behind it.

Tomas said, "You already started spending it in your head."

Imogen took the hit without blinking. "The minute I understood what it might mean, yes."

"Jesus Christ."

"Do not perform surprise for me, Tomas. You knew exactly what the sighting could do for the camp."

"I knew what it could do for her." He jerked his chin at Mina. "And maybe for us if it turned real enough."

"It is real enough," Mina said.

"That isn't the fucking point."

The insect hum outside seemed to climb with his voice. Rain had not arrived yet, but the light through the lab's slatted windows had gone yellow and strained. Storm front close. The whole camp always heard weather before it got wet. Screens started whispering differently. Pilings took on a deeper creak. Spoonbills in the rookery lifted and settled in irritated bursts.

Rhea set the page down. "Say the point, then."

Tomas looked at her. Something in his face changed there, not softening exactly, but widening long enough for the hurt to show in plain daylight before he pressed it thin again.

"The point," he said, "is I lost my route because she needed nighttime and gas and somebody who could get her in without a permit. The point is I let county fuel walk and I let county gear walk and I sat there getting chewed by a supervisor half my age while he asked whether I'd been selling gas to friends or just too stupid to count." He looked back at Mina. "And now I get to hear it was all sitting there anyway. Dumped years ago. Whole secret already in the water, and I still get to be the asshole who burned his job down ferrying you to it."

Mina felt the metal lip of the table edge against the back of her hand and held there because if she moved she would start defending herself too fast.

"I didn't know about the release."

"No," he said. "You just knew enough other things."

That landed harder because it was cleaner. No cursing on it. No volume. Just the one blade.

Imogen said, "You also knew what you were doing, Tomas."

He rounded on her then. "Don't. Not from you."

"From me exactly. You are not some boy dragged into a ditch by witchcraft. You stole fuel because you wanted to. You took her out because you wanted to. You kept taking her out because the eel made you feel less like a county janitor with wings on his boat."

The words were cruel enough that Mina almost admired them. Tomas went still in the way she had learned meant danger, not calm. He got quieter when he crossed over.

"And you looked away because the county route helped this place," he said.

Imogen's mouth tightened. "Yes."

"You took rides. You took info. You took the fact that I could get places after dark and pretended it was just neighborly."

"I took help where I could get it because this camp is not funded by purity."

"There it is."

"There what is?"

"Everybody gets to call what they take by a prettier name."

Nobody in the room could really argue with that. Mina found herself looking at the old badge again. BrightCurrent had called dumping a release. The county called spraying management. Donors called appetite stewardship. She had called a man a route. She had called a woman an exception until the exception climbed into her lap and put two fingers in her mouth to shut her up. The ugliness was not that one of those things had been fake. The ugliness was how easily the real and the useful had lain down together.

Rhea pulled off one glove and laid her fingertips on the page as if testing for heat. "This is not a contest for cleanest motives."

"No?" Tomas said. "Could've fooled me."

"I said what I said."

He looked away from her, out toward the mangroves. That hurt, visibly. Rhea's face changed less than most people's, but Mina saw the small pause before she continued. Saw the care she took with each word when she knew anything sloppy would be used as cover by somebody else.

"The pages matter," Rhea said. "They matter because the line is not a private miracle and not a private sin. They matter because if anybody makes a public story out of the eel, they also make a public claim over the habitat around it. Imogen is right about one thing. Institutions only hear certain kinds of damage. Ugly liability is one of them."

Imogen gave a brief nod, but Rhea was not done.

"Tomas is right about something too. The cost has not landed evenly."

That brought Tomas's gaze back.

"You lost pay," Rhea said. "You lost county access. You got to be publicly stupid in front of men who would have liked that even without Mina. That's real. But if you're about to tell yourself a story where you were only labor and never appetite, don't waste my afternoon."

His jaw flexed once.

"And if you're about to tell yourself a story," she said to Mina, "where annex records make you a messenger instead of a participant, don't do that either."

Mina almost laughed because there it was, the exact pain she trusted from Rhea. Not comfort. Not theater. Just the merciless refusal to let language start lying.

"I know I was in it," Mina said.

"You keep saying that like it's the end of the sentence."

Imogen crossed her arms. "Would anyone like to propose an alternative to me making copies, calling counsel, and getting in front of this before BrightCurrent's remains do?"

"Yes," Mina said. "Not turning it into a camp resurrection pitch."

"I did not say resurrection."

"You thought it."

"What I thought was that a defunct biotech company poisoning a protected wetland might finally move money in this county in the correct direction."

Tomas laughed again, low and mean. "Toward the fucking gift shop?"

Imogen's eyes flashed. That did it. Professional Imogen stepped aside completely.

"Toward keeping Beto employed and fed," she said. "Toward not failing a hurricane inspection because the east walkway still lists six unsafe boards and a handrail that flexes like boiled pasta. Toward not losing every contract scientist who needs a roof because the generator dies in July. Toward not asking me, again, to be ashamed that I can see numbers while the rest of you are busy bleeding all over abstractions."

It was so specific it shut the room for a second. Mina could picture the unsafe-board list taped inside Imogen's office cabinet. Could picture the yellow invoice folder with the corners rounded from being opened and closed and not paid. Imogen, who could still pin her silver hair up with the same pencil she used to mark bird counts and then spend an hour pretending not to notice that guests were taking extra shrimp from the staff tray because calling it out would make them feel poor in the wrong direction.

Mina said, "I get it."

"No," Imogen said. "You get pieces when they bruise you personally. That is not the same thing."

Fair enough.

Thunder rolled somewhere far enough off to sound expensive.

Tomas dragged a hand over his mouth. "Everybody wants a use case."

Rhea's mouth twitched without humor. "Use case is not the phrase I would choose for our sex life."

The room went very still.

There it was. Nobody had wanted to be the one to drag it out naked, but once Rhea said it, it stood in the middle of the room with all its marks on it. Tomas's fuel key on the dresser next to condoms. Mina slipping out of Tomas's bed to carry coffee to Rhea in the bat lab because the house had started feeling like one continuous arrangement. Rhea asking questions in the dark that sounded like science and then biting Mina's shoulder hard enough to leave a crescent the shape of a field hook. Tenderness had been there. So had barter. They had all preferred not to inventory which nights leaned where.

Imogen looked away first, to the windows, because even she had limits. "I am going to pretend I did not hear that sentence."

"You heard it," Rhea said.

"Unfortunately."

Tomas said, "You want it plain? Fine. Plain enough? She came to me because I had the boat and the fuel and the route. That was day one. Then she came to you because you knew the tunnel and the data and because sleeping with one half of a marriage is messier than sleeping with both." He was looking at Mina again, but the collateral hit everybody. "Maybe after that it got real in spots. Maybe not. I don't know what the fuck to call real anymore. I know what started it."

Mina had been bracing against exactly this for weeks, maybe since the second time Tomas pushed her against the aluminum side of the mosquito shed and laughed into her throat when she bit his shoulder to keep quiet. That did not make it easier to hear.

"At first," she said, because anything else would be another lie, "I came to you because you had the route."

The words dropped with a terrible little neatness.

Tomas looked almost relieved for one second. Then the rest of it hit.

"There it is."

"That wasn't all it was."

"No," he said. "Just the first thing."

"Yes."

He nodded twice, slow. "Order matters."

Mina opened her mouth and closed it. Because it did. Because every decent thing that came after had been built on top of a want she could count in fuel and hours and access points. She remembered the first night too clearly all at once: mosquito whine inside her cuffs, his hand flat between her shoulder blades as she climbed into the boat, the blue turn under blackwater, and then later his face above her in the heat with the county key still clipped to his belt, knocking against her thigh every time he moved. She had wanted the key off him almost as badly as she had wanted him. The shame of that was not abstract. It had edges, metal-cold, exactly that shape.

Rhea said, "That is not the whole order."

Tomas did not even glance at her. "It is enough of one."

Imogen took off her glasses and pinched the bridge of her nose. "We are not going to solve the structure of human exploitation in the bat lab. We do, however, need a decision before someone else stumbles onto the annex or the footage leaks from a drunk phone call."

"The footage doesn't identify the site," Rhea said.

"Not yet."

"Publicity will," Mina said.

"Not if done by someone who understands how not to hand coordinates to idiots."

"Publicity does not stay with the person who drafts it," Rhea said. "You know that."

Imogen gave her a tight, unwilling nod.

Tomas looked at the book again as if he wished it would turn back into simple paper and stop radiating consequence. "And while you all draft the morally superior release strategy, what exactly am I supposed to do? Sit home and admire the principle? My overtime is gone. The skiff note still clears Friday. The county already thinks I was stealing fuel for a side hustle or a woman or both, and I don't even get the dignity of saying I burned my life down for something wild. It was corporate trash and horny idiots the whole way through."

"Not the whole way," Mina said.

He swung to her so fast the chair legs on the far side of the room rattled from where his knee hit the table.

"Don't give me scraps because you feel guilty." His voice stayed low. That was what made Rhea straighten. "Don't stand there and make me noble after the fact. I wasn't noble. I wanted you. I liked the boat with you in it. I liked you in my house. I liked watching my wife look at you and not look away. I liked all of it. And I still got used."

Mina felt that one all the way down in the places his hands had taught her body to answer before thought got there. Not because it was unfair. Because it wasn't.

"Yes," she said.

He stared at her, waiting for more, and she made herself keep going.

"Yes. I used you. You used me too. Rhea used both of us. I let myself love parts of that because loving them made it easier not to stop. That isn't a defense. It's just the shape."

Rain hit the roof then, sudden and hard, one sheet of water flattening the mosquito sound for a second before the drains took over and the whole building started talking through its pipes. Somebody in the main house let out a startled shout. A spoonbill lifted screaming from the rookery, then another.

Rhea stood very still and then said, "There. That is closer."

Imogen looked between them all, disgusted and impressed in equal parts. "You could have reached closer with your clothes on years ago."

No one answered.

She picked up her mug. "Here is what I need from you. I need copies of every page. I need the badge photographed. I need no one taking this to a donor, a county friend, an ex-lover, or a barstool between now and when we decide whether the first call is legal, regulatory, or press."

"Press?" Mina said.

"Strategic press, if necessary."

"You are unbelievable."

"I am fifty-two, underfunded, and looking at a buried corporate release under my camp," Imogen said. "Believe harder."

Rhea slid the logbook back into the contractor bag and folded the top down once. "Copies, yes. Donor theater, no."

"I said strategic."

"You mean curated."

"I mean alive next season."

Tomas pushed away from the table. "I mean done."

Mina looked up. "Tomas."

"No." He took the envelope from his pocket, crumpled it once, smoothed it again with his thumb, then shoved it back. "You want honest? Fine. Get your things out of our room before dark."

The rain hammered harder. For a second the sound was so dense it seemed to hold the whole room inside it.

"Tomas," Rhea said, not pleading, not quite warning.

"I said what I said."

He left without slamming the door. He did not need to. The storm did it for him, shaking the screen hard enough to rattle the frame after he was already out on the walkway.

Imogen watched the door swing and settle. "Well," she said. "There goes diplomacy."

"You were not exactly helping," Mina said.

Imogen gave her a cool look over the rim of the mug. "You did not call me here for help. You called me because you found a bigger version of the fire and wanted witnesses before it changed shape in your own hands."

That was true enough to sting.

Rhea stripped off the second glove and dropped both on the table. "I will photograph the pages."

"In your lab?" Imogen asked.

"Yes."

"Then do it now before the power flickers."

Imogen gathered her glasses, hesitated like she might say something human, then thought better of it and went back into the weather and the paying guests and the camp she was still, absurdly, trying to save. The room felt larger when she left and less breathable.

Rhea kept her eyes on the bagged book. "You did the right thing bringing it back."

Mina almost laughed. "That sounded painful."

"It was precise, not kind."

"Same as always."

Rhea finally looked at her then, and the look was not cold. That would have been easier. It was intimate and exhausted and impossible to stand inside for long.

"Take him seriously," she said. "And don't ask me to convert this into a cleaner story because you're frightened."

"I wasn't going to."

Rhea's expression said she knew better than that. "Photographs first," she said.

Then she picked up the contractor bag and carried it deeper into the lab, away from Mina, as carefully as if it held something alive that might still bite through plastic if handled badly.

* * *

By dark the rain had narrowed from violence to persistence, the kind that seemed built into the air rather than falling through it. Water ran off every roof edge at camp. The screened walkways dripped in straight silver cords. Somewhere under the main house a bucket kept taking overflow from a known leak with a slow, shaming plink. The generator worked harder in wet weather; you could hear the load in it, a lower note under the usual thrum.

Mina stood outside Tomas and Rhea's door with a dry bag in one hand and the annex badge in her pocket because she had not wanted to leave it anywhere else. The plastic edge pressed against her hip through damp shorts every time she breathed. She had spent the last two hours helping Beto move stacked coolers away from the east wall and pretending not to notice that he was trying not to look at her. He had whistled under his breath the whole time, the same two bars from an old norteño song he went to when he was scared and wanted his hands to seem occupied by something ordinary. He had not asked why her face looked flayed open. For nineteen, that counted as grace.

Now the walkway light outside the room threw a bug-flecked cone through the screen and turned the rain beyond it into bent wires. She could smell damp wood, diesel residue dragged in on Tomas's boots, and the faint powder-clean scent of Rhea's alcohol wipes. Her body knew this threshold too well. Nights of stepping over it barefoot, nights of stepping over it sticky and half asleep, nights of hearing Tomas laugh low from inside while Rhea said, from farther back, "If you're coming in, close the damn screen." The memory came with its own muscle, the hand already shaped for the latch.

She knocked anyway.

"Come in," Rhea said.

The room had already been rearranged by grief and practicality.

Not drastically. That would have felt theatrical. The bed was stripped, mattress bare except for the thin camp pad zipped over it. The striped sheet Mina had bought cheap in town and then left here by accident after the third night had been folded into a hard square on the chair. Her toothbrush lay dry on top of it. Tomas's work shirts still hung from the wall hooks. Rhea's recorder case sat open on the desk with batteries lined in a row by charge label, green tape tabs all facing the same direction. A mosquito had died flattened into the wall near the lamp, one dark starburst among many older paint scars.

Rhea stood by the dresser in a clean tank top and field shorts, braid redone tighter than before. She was sorting Mina's things from theirs without speed and without hesitation. Sports bra. Spare shirt. Hair tie. Paperback with the cover bent back where Tomas had dropped it in the rain last week and apologized by drying every page with the hair dryer like it was a wounded bird. The sight of that book on Rhea's palm hit Mina so hard she had to set the dry bag down before her hand started shaking and gave the cheap vinyl away.

"I could have done this," Mina said.

"I know."

"You didn't have to."

"I know that too."

Rain ticked the screen, lighter now, almost conversational. Rhea put the paperback on the folded sheet and finally faced her.

"He went down to the skiff for a while," she said. "He's back. He's in the shower."

Mina nodded. Water ran in the small bathroom behind the half-closed door, loud in the cramped space. Tomas always showered hot enough to make the mirror cry.

"I didn't come to make a scene," Mina said.

"Then we're aligned on at least one point."

Mina almost smiled. It failed halfway. "Are you furious with me?"

Rhea considered. That was one of the things Mina had fallen into with her: the real considering. No performed spontaneity. No automatic soothing.

"Yes," she said. "But not in the simple direction."

"That's useful."

"It's accurate."

Mina stepped inside and closed the screen behind her. The room immediately took her in through the skin the way it always had, through the trapped warmth of it, through the smell of the three of them laid down over weeks in accumulations too specific to fake. Sweat dried into cotton. Wet mangrove under one bed leg from boots never fully clean. Tomas's citrus soap. Rhea's skin after the bat lab, the rubbing-alcohol clean on top of guano and salt. Her own shampoo somewhere in the weave. The room had always embarrassed her by smelling married and then, somehow, by smelling like her too.

"I wasn't lying when I said it changed," she said. "About Tomas. About you."

"I know."

"That sounded like it hurt you to admit."

"No. It hurt to hear you say the first part out loud in front of him."

Mina leaned her hip against the dresser and felt the wood stick slightly to the damp back of her thigh. "What was I supposed to do? Lie prettier?"

"No." Rhea picked up Mina's razor from the windowsill and tucked it into the dry bag. "But don't confuse honesty with timing. You told the truth after he had already been skinned open."

"He asked."

"Yes."

There was no mercy in that answer. No accusation either. Just fact.

Mina looked at the bed. The bare mattress made the room seem suddenly poorer, more like camp housing and less like the private country they had built by misuse and consent and weather. She saw, with humiliating clarity, the places material had always been stitched through it. Tomas's county route dictating when nights were possible. Rhea's survey schedule dictating when she could stay past dawn. Mina's temporary wage and fear of losing it making every footstep on the walkway feel stolen. Even the condoms had come from the locked cabinet in the main house laundry room because Imogen bought in bulk for the seasonal staff and pretended not to know where half the box went.

"Did you?" Mina asked. "Use me."

Rhea looked almost bored by the question, which was its own cruelty.

"Of course I did."

Mina let out a breath she had not realized she was holding. The relief in it felt sick.

"For what?"

"Company. Disruption. Pleasure. A witness who knew what contamination language sounds like before a page is halfway turned. An excuse not to spend every night speaking only in marriage." Rhea adjusted the folded sheet so the corners matched. "Take your pick."

"That doesn't make me feel better."

"I wasn't trying to make you feel better."

"No."

Rhea softened then, not much. Just enough for the next sentence to land as something other than punishment.

"It also doesn't make it false," she said.

Mina looked up.

"Wanting you was real," Rhea said. "Using you was also real. Those things aren't enemies. They're just inconvenient together."

The shower shut off. Pipes knocked once in the wall.

Mina said, "Are you asking me to stay away from you?"

Rhea's eyes held hers. "Tonight, I'm asking you to leave this room without asking me to clean it emotionally."

"And after tonight?"

"After tonight I'll know more than I know now."

That was not comfort. It was not dismissal either. It was a door left unlatched a fraction and only because accuracy required it.

The bathroom door opened. Steam drifted out carrying hot water, soap, and the wet-metal smell of Tomas's skin after a day around tools. He had a towel around his neck and another low on his hips. The crooked break in his nose looked whiter after heat. He stopped when he saw Mina and did not pretend surprise. Water still ran down the cords of his forearms and darkened the waistband of the towel.

"Good," he said. "You're here."

Rhea did not move between them. Mina respected her for that and hated her a little too.

"I came for my things."

"I can see that."

He crossed to the hook by the door, took down one of her shirts that had been hanging under his rain jacket, and tossed it onto the folded pile on the chair. Not hard. Accurate.

"You missed a sock under the bed," he said. "Rhea found it."

Humiliation should have felt like heat or collapse or some other dramatic system. What it felt like was the seam of the dry bag handle cutting into Mina's palm while a drop from Tomas's hair hit the floorboards between them and sat there shining stupidly in the lamp light.

"Thanks," she said.

He gave a short laugh. "Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Don't get polite like this is a checkout desk."

Mina put the dry bag on the chair and started loading it because she needed her hands doing a sequence. Toothbrush. Paperback. Razor. Shirt. The folded sheet was too much. She left it.

"I'm not trying to be polite," she said.

"Then what are you trying to be?"

Honest, she might have said if the word had not taken enough damage for one day.

"Here," she said instead.

That brought another laugh, even lower. "Fantastic."

Rhea closed the recorder case and snapped both latches. "Don't make me regret staying in the room for this."

Tomas looked at her, then away. "I'm not the one who turned it into a symposium."

"And yet you're still speaking."

Mina should not have loved that line. She did. The room was full of things that kept refusing to die when they were supposed to.

Tomas rubbed the towel hard over his hair and flung it across the desk chair. "You want me quiet, I'll be quiet. Here's quiet." He looked at Mina. "I don't want you in my bed anymore."

The directness of it was easier than the sarcasm had been.

"Okay," Mina said.

"And I don't want you waiting around for me to cool off and decide the sex was stronger than the math."

"Tomas."

"No, let me finish." He planted one hand on the dresser, close enough that the wood creaked. "You said the shape. Fine. Here's the shape from where I'm standing. You needed a route and I was the route. Then I wanted you enough that I let that stop being humiliating. Then I wanted you enough that when you started sleeping with my wife too, I told myself it meant we were all honest because nobody was pretending ownership." He shook his head once. "Turns out there are still cheaper and more expensive bodies in the room."

Mina felt the sentence strike all the way through. Rhea's face changed then, finally.

"That's not fair," Rhea said.

"No?"

"No. It's sharp, but it's not exact."

"What's exact, then?"

Rhea folded her arms. "That class sat in the bed with us from the first night and we preferred not to look directly at it because the sex was good."

Tomas barked a humorless laugh. "There. Better wording. I feel healed already."

"I'm not trying to heal you," Rhea said. "I'm trying to stop you from lying because rage is easier."

He took that and did not answer.

Mina forced herself to meet his eyes. "If you want me to say you were right to be careful with me from the start, you were."

"I wasn't careful."

"No."

"I was stupid."

"No," she said. "You were greedy in some of the same ways I was greedy."

That made his mouth twitch, almost in offense, almost in recognition.

"You really think that helps."

"I think it's true."

He pushed off the dresser and paced once to the door and back because stillness was failing him. He was more at ease bracing against wake than standing in rooms. Mina knew that about his body now. Knew the way he planted feet wide on land as if something under the floor might shift without warning. Knew how he slept harder after night water than after any dry day. Knowledge did not become innocent because it had been gained intimately.

"Do you know what my supervisor asked me?" he said.

Mina waited.

"He asked whether I was stealing for resale, or because I had gotten sentimental." Tomas smiled with all the wrong muscles. "Sentimental. That's the county word when they want to call you soft and stupid in one breath. I sat there with that man looking at my logs, and all I could think about was you in my truck with your feet on the dash, salt all over your legs, talking about spoonbills like they were a weather system inside your ribs. I thought, this is going to be the dumbest reason anybody ever lost a job on this coast."

Rain hissed in the dark outside the screen. Mina pictured the county office fluorescent and cold, Tomas under it in his work shirt, grease still in his knuckles because no sink ever took it all. The humiliation of being translated by somebody else into a petty kind of fool. She had done that to him too, by omission if not by intention.

"I'm sorry," she said.

He flinched as if she had thrown something weak at him.

"I know."

"No, I mean it."

"I know that too." He looked tired suddenly, deeper than anger. "The problem is sorry doesn't put overtime back in the check, and it doesn't take your first reason out of the room."

Mina slid the paperback deeper into the bag because if she kept looking at the bent cover she was going to start remembering the night Tomas had read half a page aloud in a terrible British accent until Rhea laughed wine out her nose. All those stupid domestic details. They hurt worse than the sex, maybe because they had been the proof she used on herself when she wanted to believe the arrangement had become a life.

"I don't want to bill it out," she said. "I know that's not what this is."

"Good," he said. "Because if you offer me money, I'll say something I can't take back."

That had, embarrassingly, been in the back of her mind. Not as a fix. As tribute. As if damage could be made less ugly by admitting it came with a measurable cost. Rhea's eyes flicked to her face and told her she had guessed exactly that.

"I'm not going to," Mina said.

"Smart."

Rhea picked up the folded sheet at last and held it toward Mina. "Take this."

Mina stared at it. A cheap striped thing from town, blue gone almost gray from too many wash cycles, one corner still faintly stained with the tannin smear Tomas's calf had left after he came in from the creek and forgot not to sit down first. She had bought it because the camp-issued sheets felt like hospital paper and then the sheet had migrated here because so had she.

"You keep it," Mina said.

"No."

"Rhea."

"Take it."

There was no anger in the command. Just method. This belongs in the bag. This does not stay on the chair pretending it is an exception.

Mina took the sheet. It was warm from Rhea's hands. The warmth nearly undid her.

"I didn't just come because of the route," she said quietly, because if she left the room without trying one more true sentence, she would deserve whatever simplification followed. "That was first. It wasn't last. I wanted you, Tomas. I wanted her. I wanted the room to keep being real enough that I didn't have to go back to being one woman with a rumor and a debt and a camp job that was never going to turn into anything better."

Tomas looked at her for a long time.

"I believe that," he said.

The relief came so fast it made her knees feel hollow. Then he kept going.

"I just don't know what good it does me."

There it was. Honest relationship, if that was what this counted as. Not rescue. Not absolution. Not the story where love cleared the accounting. Just the exact wound, named correctly.

Rhea rested one hip against the desk. "It does not have to do him good to be true."

Mina looked at her.

Rhea held the look and did not soften it. "And don't hear that as permission to stay. It isn't."

The bug-zapper outside cracked again. From the kitchen walkway came the clang of a pot and Beto's voice saying something too cheerful to be natural, followed by Imogen telling him to stop dropping metal like he had shares in the hardware store. Life kept moving its ordinary feet over the wreck.

Tomas picked up his damp work shirt from the hook and pulled it on without drying fully. The fabric stuck to his shoulders.

"I'm sleeping out on the skiff if the rain lets up," he said, mostly to Rhea.

"You don't need to punish your back for theater," she said.

"It isn't theater."

"No," she said. "It's bad planning."

For the first time that night, the corner of his mouth moved in the direction of the old him. Not enough to call it a smile.

"See?" he said to Mina. "That's marriage. Not the glamorous parts."

Rhea closed her eyes briefly, then opened them. "Don't make me sentimental on principle."

Mina wanted, almost painfully, to step into the little shared current between them and make it three again. That was the old sheltering illusion right there, still offering itself even now: that if she could get them laughing, if she could take off her shirt, if she could say exactly the right ugly thing and let one of them answer by touching her, the rest of the system would loosen. It had worked before, or seemed to. The room itself argued for it. The mattress. The smell. Tomas barefoot on wet boards. Rhea with one hand on the recorder case and the other still open from passing over the sheet.

She saw the temptation clearly enough not to trust it.

"I'm going," she said.

Neither of them stopped her.

At the door she turned back because she did not know when she would next be allowed the chance to be exact with all three of them in one room.

"I'm not taking the pages anywhere," she said. "Not tonight."

Imogen's phrasing had gotten into her despite herself. Rhea noticed. So did Tomas.

"Good," Tomas said.

"And I'm not asking either of you to tell me what this was supposed to mean."

Rhea nodded once. "Good."

Tomas looked down at the floorboards. "Mina."

She waited.

He still did not look up. "If we end up talking again, don't come to me like I'm the path back to wonder."

The line hit so precisely it left no defense around it.

"Okay," she said.

That, more than anything else, sounded like the end of the chapter inside her. Not because she no longer wanted him. Because he had named the role she had kept trying to half-deny and half-upgrade into love. Path. Route. Access. He had been a man and a boat and a body and a joke in the kitchen and a hand on the back of her neck in the dark. He had also been the path. All the wanting in the world did not change the order in which she had learned to need him.

She stepped out into the wet walkway with the dry bag over one shoulder and the folded sheet tucked under her arm. Rain blew through the screen in a fine mist and freckled her forearms. In the dark below, water moved around the pilings with that thick black sound the channels made when the tide was turning somewhere you could not see. The camp's lights glowed weakly on the runoff. From the rookery came the late, irritated squall of birds settling badly.

Her own room felt narrower than it had that morning, the camp mattress humped in the center, the air stale from being shut during rain. She set the dry bag on the bunk and took the annex badge from her pocket. The plastic was tacky with humidity. Her younger face smiled up through mildew bloom and scratch marks, still certain that proximity to wonder counted as possession.

Outside, the storm drains gulped and overflowed and tried again. Every structure on the stilts spoke its burden aloud in weather. Mina laid the badge on top of the folded sheet and understood, with a clarity so sharp it was almost restful, that there was no corner of the story left now that had not been touched by labor, appetite, or somebody's bill coming due.
