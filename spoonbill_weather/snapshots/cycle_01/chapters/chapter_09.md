# Chapter 9

By late afternoon the rookery had gone past beautiful and into something sticky, loud, and faintly indecent. The spoonbills did not so much occupy the mangroves as overtake them. Pink bodies flashed from branch to branch. Bills clattered like someone shaking a crate of wet bones. Every exposed limb of mangrove seemed to hold a nest in progress, a nest collapsing, or a pair of birds trying to fuck on top of materials they had not finished stealing yet.

From the observation platform Mina could smell the colony before she could sort any individual bird from the mass. Guano, sun-baked fish, green mangrove sap torn fresh from twigs, the sugary donor wine sweating in its metal tub behind her. The wind kept changing its mind and bringing all of it at once. Guests had paid extra for spoonbill week and the birds were delivering exactly the sort of spectacle rich people liked to pretend they had discovered with their own moral seriousness. Every few minutes somebody on the platform made a delighted little noise that was really embarrassment wearing a visor.

One male spoonbill lurched onto a nest already occupied by a female and three stolen sticks, flapped pink and white over her head, lost his footing, recovered it with a furious clack of the bill, then mounted her anyway while she stayed braced and practical beneath him. Two donors laughed, then looked around to see whether laughing at bird sex counted as vulgar. Another bird came in crosswise with a mangrove twig longer than its own body and got into a shoving match with the pair before the male finished. A shower of leaves and droppings came down through the branches. Somebody behind Mina said, "Oh my God," with the round, pleased horror of a woman seeing a bartender crack an egg into a whiskey on purpose.

Mina kept the binocular strap from sticking to the back of her neck and gave them the cleaned-up version.

"Peak breeding does this," she said. "They get territorial, sloppy, loud. Everybody wants the same branch at once."

"That one is really committed," a man in a linen fishing shirt said, pointing with a champagne flute.

"He's," Mina said, because there was no honest donor-safe synonym for what the bird was doing.

She heard Beto snort behind the beverage table. He had changed into a camp polo that still hung off him like it belonged to an older cousin, and the shiny burn mark on his right wrist showed every time he reached into the ice. He was supposed to be topping off glasses and keeping people from leaning too hard on the rail. Instead he was watching the colony with the bright, hungry concentration he usually gave a broken motor, head tipped, lips moving as if he were counting beats in a song.

"You want me to tell them that's the family values package?" he murmured when he passed Mina a sweating pitcher of water.

"I want you not to get fired in front of canapes."

"Then I need hazard pay."

He slid away before she could answer, grinning at one of the older donors with a courtesy that became almost formal whenever he was actually nervous. The kid had talked himself hoarse through the storm two nights ago and then gone quiet this morning, carrying trays and toolboxes with the carefulness of somebody trying not to think too hard. Mina had seen the anatomy notes folded in his back pocket at lunch, the corner softened white from rereading. He wanted trade school so badly he studied tendon diagrams between dish shifts. He still stopped for birds when they made a noise nobody else heard.

Imogen Price moved through the crowd with a tray of paper napkins and the sort of posture that made the whole weather-beaten platform feel like a formal institution for as long as she was standing on it. Her silver hair had mostly pulled loose from the pins she had forced into it at noon, but she kept smoothing it back as though order were a muscle and not a resource. Every donor got the right blend of Latin name, soft crisis, and manageable hope from her. She could make deferred maintenance sound like intimacy with place. She could make a leaking roof sound like authenticity.

"Mina," she said without looking at her, still smiling at a board couple from Naples. "Stay visible until dinner service starts."

It was not a request. In Imogen's mouth, even the low pulse of panic came out with clause control.

"I'm visible now."

Imogen's eyes flicked to her, brief and dry. "Do not experiment with me in front of people who write checks."

Then she drifted onward, all gracious edges, to rescue a man who had mistaken a juvenile ibis for a spoonbill and was about to say so loudly.

Mina watched her go and felt the old mix of gratitude and irritation scrape together in her ribs. Spoonbill week. The deadline had narrowed to that phrase until it sounded like a sentence and not a period on a calendar. Through spoonbill week, Imogen had said after the donor-platform humiliation. After that, one more problem and Mina could take her debts and her scandal and her sleeping bag somewhere else. The camp was an argument built on pilings, and Mina was the loosest board in it.

Below the platform, the guest-facing boardwalk buckled with traffic. Another skiff idled out past the mangroves, not close enough to break the protected line but close enough to throw wake where it did not belong. A guide in a white performance shirt lifted a hand in bland apology toward the camp. Tomas, down at the county dock, answered with the kind of wave that looked friendly from a distance and obscene up close.

Even from here Mina could tell he was angry by how precise he went. He stood beside the airboat with one boot on the rub rail and the fuel key clipped to his belt, broad shoulders held square as lumber. He had changed out of county khaki into a thin gray T-shirt darkened at the spine with sweat, but he still wore the work radio at his hip. His left thumb was wrapped in black electrical tape where he had split the nail lifting a battery last week, and every time he flexed his hand he rubbed that taped thumb against his forefinger as though checking he could still feel both of them. When he looked up and found Mina on the platform he did not smile. He only touched two fingers to the side of the hull, then to the mangroves to the west.

Fifteen minutes, if she read him right.

Rhea came up the stairs carrying a hard case and the soft-sided dry bag that held her recorder. Sweat had darkened the braid down her back to almost black, and there was a strip of silver gaffer tape stuck to the side of her shorts from some earlier repair she had not bothered to peel off. She always wrote battery charge percentages on masking tape flags instead of trusting the factory labels, and three of those little white flags fluttered now from the case handle like surrendered thoughts. She crossed to the shady side of the platform, not wasting any breath on the donors, and set the case down with both hands as if it contained something temperamental and alive.

"You're still working the room," she said.

"Imogen's idea of penance."

Rhea looked out over the birds. "The colony is doing the work for you."

One of the donors, a woman with enamel warblers pinned all around the brim of her visor, edged close enough to hear. "Doctor Acosta, is this normal?"

Rhea gave the woman a level look that was not rude only because it was too exact to qualify as social. "For spoonbills in breeding season, yes. For people on platforms, probably not."

The woman laughed harder than the line deserved, relieved to have been given permission.

Rhea crouched to unlatch the case. Inside lay the hydrophone cable coiled in patient loops, a little handheld recorder fitted with an adapter that had clearly lived too many field seasons, two desiccant packs, and a clamp arm with one rubber foot missing. Mina crouched beside her. On the platform the wood planks carried donor footsteps, Beto's quick balancing run, the steady tap of Imogen's ring against a glass as she redirected attention from bird sex to fundraising. Under all of it came the colony itself, a wet constant uproar that made every human voice sound performative.

"How bad?" Mina asked.

"Good enough to embarrass us later." Rhea checked the recorder input, then the cable. "I had to cannibalize the clean adapter from the cave unit. The seal on this housing isn't trustworthy. If it gets swamped, the file will survive only out of spite."

"Encouraging."

"I don't do encouragement. I do equipment triage."

Mina watched Rhea's hands. Long fingers, blunt nails, no rings. Clean movements, never hurried, even now. The morning after the confession Rhea had asked questions the way some people laid out scalpels, one beside another, each more useful than kind. Since then she had not withdrawn from Mina, but she had stopped making room for any fantasy that hunger and honesty were the same thing. Mina had earned that. The fact that Rhea was here anyway, repurposing grant gear for an illegal eel run on a donor night, felt less like absolution than a second, sharper chance.

"You think he can get us far enough in?" Mina asked.

"Tomas thinks he can. That usually means he can or he dies annoyed."

From below came the service bell from the kitchen, clanged twice by somebody who liked the theatrical version of every task. The donors turned as a group toward the main house. Wineglasses flashed. Imogen raised her voice, all hospitality and managed scarcity.

"If you would like a closer look after plates are out, Mina can take a small group back to the rail."

Mina almost laughed. Of course. Use the birds twice. Once before dinner, once after. Turn the feral noise into a premium tier.

Rhea snapped the hard case shut. "That's our opening. The second seating buys us forty minutes before somebody starts looking for a narrative."

"You make that sound like a criminal window."

"It's a criminal window."

They stood. Rhea slung the dry bag crosswise over her chest and tucked one of the white tape flags down so it would not show. Her shoulder brushed Mina's forearm on the turn. It was a tiny thing, accidental enough from a distance. Mina felt it all the way to the back of her teeth.

At the stairs Beto intercepted them with a tray of shrimp toasts and a look sharpened by curiosity.

"If anybody asks," he said softly, not quite meeting Mina's eyes, "you're both in the bat lab because the charger started sparking again."

Mina blinked. "Why would anybody ask you?"

He shrugged one narrow shoulder. "Because nobody tells me anything, so everyone assumes I know everything."

Rhea studied him for a second. "Don't let anyone touch the west rail. The birds are already flushing from that side wake."

"Yeah, I saw." He set the tray down on the platform rail and wiped his damp palms on his oversized shirt. "That charter guy's running too close because his clients paid for indecency. Same as ours."

He said it with the offhand meanness of someone too young to bother coating the truth. Then he lifted the tray again and transformed back into staff, smiling at a donor who wanted another napkin and pretending the spoonbills were the strangest thing on the platform.

Mina followed Rhea down the screened walkway. The whole camp sounded overstressed. Silverware in the kitchen. Screens ticking in a crosswind. Somebody starting the generator for the lights that only worked when kicked. The birds on top of all that, relentless and pink and filthy. By the time they reached the service side of the main house, Tomas had the airboat tucked behind the county shed where the mangroves swallowed most of the view from the guest path.

"Move," he said when he saw them, low and without prelude. "That board couple from Naples is finishing dessert faster than God intended."

He reached for the hard case before Rhea could answer. She let him take the weight but not the responsibility, keeping one hand on the latch as she stepped down into the boat. Tomas hated being corrected in public. In private he hated it too, but he could at least enjoy the fight. Tonight he had no spare room for either pleasure. His jaw had gone tight under the crooked nose, and a mosquito had burst dark on the side of his neck where he had slapped it flat and not bothered to wipe the blood.

"You radioed in?" Rhea asked.

"Larval check and trap pull on the west fingers. Boring enough nobody'll follow up unless I crash."

"Comforting theme tonight," Mina said.

Tomas looked at her then, finally, and the look had heat in it even through the strain. "If I wanted comforting, I would've stayed on the platform and watched rich people pretend not to enjoy bird porn."

Mina got into the bow beside the dry bag. The aluminum floor was still hot from the day. Tomas shoved off with one boot, brought the fan up just enough to clear the mud seam, and the camp began to slide away behind them. On the guest side somebody laughed too loud at something Imogen had arranged for them to find charming. The colony answered with a rising rattle so dense it felt like weather bearing down on the back of Mina's neck.

* * *

The first stretch of channel ran black and flat under the mangroves, but even there the breeding week had altered the air. Feathers clung to the roots. White crusts of guano marked the higher branches like bad paint. Small silver fish kept flicking at the surface as though the water had developed nerves and could not stop firing them. Tomas steered by habit more than sight, easing the boat through turns that looked to Mina like solid wall until the last second opened a seam in the leaves. He knew where the creeks made room for a hull by the color of the dark.

Once they were clear of camp lights he cut the fan down and let the engine settle into a lower growl. The sound went from public machine to intimate threat. Rhea knelt on the center bench and clipped the recorder to the dry bag strap. She had taped a square of red film over the screen so the glow would not carry. Her braid swung forward over one shoulder when she bent to feed out cable, and Mina had the irrational urge to touch the damp nape of her neck with her mouth. Desire had not become cleaner since the confession. If anything it had lost the easy alibi of secrecy and turned more exact.

Tomas saw where she was looking. He gave one short breath that might have been a laugh and might have been exhaustion.

"You two planning to help me or just think complicated thoughts at each other?"

"Both," Mina said.

"Good. Hold this."

He passed her the push pole he kept for the tighter channels. The wood was slick with old river varnish and new spray. She braced it along the gunwale while he adjusted course around a floating mat of leaves and a dead mullet white at the belly. Ahead, the noise from the colony thickened until it seemed impossible that human donors on platforms could hear it and still imagine themselves near anything genteel.

The channel opened without warning.

One moment they were under mangrove shade and the next the rookery spread out in front of them, a low flooded bowl of roots and narrow islands and birds everywhere. The last of the sun had gone syrup-colored. It hit the spoonbills and turned them too saturated to be believable, all rose and dirty salmon and the pale flash of undersides when wings opened. Nests packed the mangroves so densely that from a distance the trees looked upholstered. Adults came in carrying sticks, or left with them, because half the work of breeding seemed to be theft. Pairs jostled. Bills crossed. Wings lifted. Birds mounted and dismounted with a crude urgency that had nothing romantic in it. One female stayed planted on her nest while the male on top of her beat his wings for balance and shat over the side without interrupting himself.

"Jesus," Mina said, not bothering to make it donor-safe for anyone.

"Yes," Rhea said, and lowered the hydrophone into the blackwater.

The cable slipped through her hand in measured increments. She watched the tension in the line the way she watched mist nets, as if the equipment had a language and she refused to miss a syllable. Tomas brought the boat sideways to the current and set the prop just enough to counter drift. On the far side of the colony another skiff ghosted past at the legal perimeter, two passengers lifting phones that flashed white every time they caught sky. Each flash sent a pulse of irritation through Mina's body as specific as grit under a contact lens.

"That asshole is going to flush the whole west shoulder," Tomas muttered.

"Can you hold here?" Rhea asked.

"If nobody throws wake and if these birds don't decide my face looks like nesting material."

Mina took the clamp arm and fixed it to the gunwale for her phone. The rubber foot missing from one side made the whole setup list slightly starboard. She compensated with two wraps of tape. Her fingers had started to sweat enough that the tape kept sticking to her knuckles instead of the metal. Above them the spoonbills continued without shame. Bills clacked. Wings pumped. Mangrove leaves rained down with droppings. Every sound seemed indecent because there were so many of them at once.

She had spent years around animals in breeding season. She knew better than to flatter their motives into love. But there was no way to look at this and pretend sex was tidy in any species that had to build a life out of mud and theft. Everything in the colony was appetite with obligations strapped to its back. Want a mate, bring a stick. Want an egg safe, steal a better branch. Want your chicks fed, rip something silver in half. Even the beauty had work in it. Especially the beauty.

A male spoonbill landed too hard on a neighboring nest and knocked himself chest-first into the female, nearly toppling both of them into the water. Tomas barked a laugh.

"That one has donor energy."

"He has county commissioner energy," Mina said.

Rhea did not look up from the recorder. "He has vertebrate energy. Don't special-case the species that can file reimbursement forms."

The three of them laughed at that, quick and quiet, and the sound moved through Mina with an almost painful warmth. Shared laughter had become dangerous sometime after the storm meal. It promised ordinary life in a place where nothing came ordinary for long.

Then the water changed.

Not with light at first. With shape. A wide, soundless shove under the surface that set the floating fish scales tilting. The blackwater had been mirror-dark except where spoonbill feet and dropping twigs punctured it. Now a deeper seam moved through the tannin, not current and not wake. Mina leaned over the gunwale before she knew she was doing it.

"There," she said too fast.

Tomas's whole body sharpened. The boat motor eased down a fraction more. Rhea's eyes flicked from the recorder to the water and back, checking levels, checking line tension, refusing to choose too soon between witness and evidence.

The seam passed beneath a nesting tree and vanished.

Mina kept staring, furious at her own pulse for trying to turn the moment into memory before it had finished happening. This was how she'd gotten ruined once already. Not only by the lie, or the cooler, or Jonah, but by hunger making her narrate too early. She forced herself to notice what was actually there. The spoonbill on the low branch had stopped clattering and was looking down with one red eye. Small bait fish were stitching the surface in a broken ring. The hydrophone line had gone faintly taut, as if something below had changed the pressure without touching it.

"Don't move," Rhea said. Her voice had gone flatter, plainer, which was how it sounded when she was most exposed.

Another boat wake reached them from the far channel, late and steep from bouncing off the roots. Tomas swore and shifted his weight, bringing the bow around before the wake could slap them broadside. The hull rolled, then recovered. Mina grabbed the clamp arm just as her phone tilted. Rhea caught the recorder against her sternum. The hydrophone cable jerked once in the water like a fishing line worried by something heavy.

And then the blackwater lit.

It did not explode upward in a neat miracle. It thickened into blue from within, a cold saturated glow turning under the spoonbills' hanging feet. Mud swirled and became visible. Mangrove roots took on a spectral edge. Fish bellies flashed silver and then electric where the light hit them. The thing below them rolled once, slow and muscled, and Mina saw length enough to kill all the old arguments in her head.

Not algae. Not reflected screen light. Not some half-second trick of nerves.

An eel, unmistakably an eel, but scaled wrong for the dark and wrong for her sense of where the world drew its lines. The body was thicker than her forearm at the shoulder and tapered into a luminous rope that seemed to hold its own weather under the surface. Blue ran through it in bands and pulses rather than a steady strip, as if whatever BrightCurrent had once done to the line had not decided whether to be skin or signal. The head came up just enough to show the blunt wedge of it, the mouth opening once around water shot through with silver fry. One eye caught the last daylight and the blue beneath it at the same time and flashed like an instrument panel going live.

The spoonbills went mad.

Half the low branches lifted in a single crashing burst of wings. Birds screamed, not elegant, not symbolic, just outraged sound. Two males abandoned a mating attempt mid-balance and thrashed across the water, slapping the surface with their feet. Guano rained. A nest came apart overhead and dropped twigs onto the bow. Tomas planted the push pole into the mud bottom and held the boat off a root mass with both arms gone hard. The pole vibrated under Mina's hands when she grabbed lower on it to help. Rhea kept one hand on the recorder and the other braced against Mina's thigh, steadying herself so she would not fall over the cable.

"Levels are peaking," Rhea said through her teeth. "I need ten more seconds."

"You don't have ten clean seconds," Tomas snapped. "You've got three ugly ones."

The eel turned again, closer. For one impossible breath it came broadside in the churned water, and Mina saw the whole curve of it from head to half-tail, a blue body writing itself under pink legs and mangrove shadows. The light from it threw the underside of a spoonbill's bill into sharp bone-white relief. One fish jumped clear of the surface and landed back inside that blue as though the water had become lit glass.

Mina hit record on her phone with a hand slick from mud. The screen showed almost nothing useful. Dark water, a violent smear of reflected wings, one bar of blue too bright for the sensor. She did not care. Her eyes were doing the real work now. She would have seen that body in a fever, in a courtroom, blindfolded.

Then the passing skiff on the perimeter chose the worst possible second to cut too near the outer channel. Its wake reached them not as a swell but as stacked slaps, three quick blows against the mangrove roots. The whole rookery answered. Birds came off nests in sheets. Tomas had to kick the engine up to keep them from being driven sideways into the branches. The fan roared. Wind tore spray into their faces. Rhea's hydrophone line snapped tight, then went sickeningly slack.

"No," she said, and hauled cable.

The connector came up shedding black water. One pin in the adapter had bent sideways. The recorder screen flashed an error, recovered, then filled with a sound meter slammed solid red by fan noise and screaming birds.

"Cable's live?" Tomas said.

"The cable is alive. The file may be dead."

Another spoonbill, panicked and stupid, clipped the side cage of the airboat and flung itself back into the mangroves in a blizzard of pink dust. Mina ducked on instinct. Tomas shoved the tiller hard and used the engine thrust to nose them off the root mat before the hull could hang up. The eel vanished in the uproar as if it had never needed more than the confusion to enter and leave.

For a second after it was gone, the water held its memory. Blue residue seemed to remain in Mina's sight, mapped across the churn like afterimage burned into film. Then that too broke apart under mud and wake and fish and falling leaves.

"Did you get it?" she shouted.

Rhea, crouched over the recorder like a surgeon over a body she had no intention of calling lost until the coroner forced her, swallowed once before answering. "I got something."

"I got it," Mina said, and knew that what she meant was not about the phone.

"I saw it," Tomas said. He sounded angrier than either of them. "Clear as your damn face."

That was the turn. Mina felt it happen almost physically. The thing she had carried as obsession, then suspicion, then private compromise was no longer trapped inside her appetite or her shame. Tomas had seen it. Rhea had seen it. The eel had entered common fact, and common fact came with invoices.

The colony did not calm so much as redistribute its chaos. Birds kept screaming from three different sections at once. Some returned to nests immediately because breeding did not care about human awe or ruined audio files. One female bent back down and resumed arranging her twigs with furious domestic concentration while the male beside her tried to mount again, life and farce taking no notice of revelation. The legal-perimeter skiff had finally moved off, either chastened or merely bored. The light had gone from syrup gold to mangrove dusk.

"We're done," Rhea said. "Another pass here would be disturbance, not data."

Tomas spat muddy water over the side and wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. "We were disturbance the second I brought a fan boat in."

"Yes," Rhea said, not looking away from the ruined connector. "That doesn't improve with repetition."

Mina wanted to argue. She wanted one more turn, one clean clip, one minute of still water and an animal dumb enough to become evidence. Wanting that made her ashamed instantly because she could hear what it was made of. The same hunger that had once let Jonah hand her a cooler and call it necessity. The same hunger that turned every living thing into vindication if she let it.

Tomas read some of that on her face. He always did better with logistics than confession.

"Don't," he said.

"I wasn't saying anything."

"You were thinking at me loud."

Rhea recoiled the hydrophone cable, every loop neat despite the shaking in her hands. "If she argues for another pass, I will overrule her on scientific grounds and on marital grounds and on basic vertebrate decency."

That should have been funny. It mostly was. But the word marital landed between them with all the history still attached, and for a beat nobody spoke.

Then Tomas brought the engine around and threaded them back into the channel before the dusk shut completely. Mina kept looking over her shoulder as though the eel might choose to escort them out, blue rolling under the fan wash like a private curse. The water behind them stayed black.

They rode home through air that smelled of mangrove rot, gasoline, and the hot iron stink birds left behind in quantity. The camp lights came up pale through the trees. Near the outer boardwalk they passed a pair of donors holding stemmed glasses and talking earnestly about reproductive urgency as if they had invented the phrase. Tomas cut his eyes toward them and muttered, "If either of them says ecosystem one more time, I'm charging them for fuel."

Mina laughed, sudden and helpless. The laugh broke halfway through and turned raw in her throat. She pressed the heel of her muddy hand against her mouth and tasted salt, aluminum, and the faint grit of spoonbill dust.

* * *

They did not go back through the main house. Tomas tied off at the service side of the county dock, where the lights were dimmer and the smells uglier. Mud, two-stroke fuel, dead bait, the chemical soap somebody used on the county coolers. The birds were still screaming in the distance, but the sound had flattened with dark into something more lawless, less visible and somehow larger.

Rhea took the recorder first. Tomas killed the engine and sat very still for a second after the fan wound down, one forearm over the tiller, head bent. In the dim dock light the mosquito bites on his calves stood out angry and swollen above his boots. He always looked most tired once the machinery stopped, as if motion gave him a kind of temporary citizenship he lost on land.

"You two go to the lab," he said. "I'll rinse the hull and get the county mud off anything with a serial number."

"You should come hear the file," Rhea said.

"I know what I heard."

"So do I. That's not the point."

He lifted his head. There was water drying in his eyelashes and a streak of pink guano across one shoulder where the panicked bird had clipped him. The sight almost made Mina smile. He looked like he'd lost a fight to a wedding cake and won on points.

"The point," he said quietly, "is if that recorder sounds like what it felt like, we're in trouble."

Mina stepped onto the dock. Her knees wobbled with delayed engine sway. "We're already in trouble."

"No," Tomas said. "We were horny and illegal. That's one category. This one's different."

Rhea's mouth tightened the way it did when language stopped being useful but she had not yet given herself permission to abandon it. She held the recorder out to him anyway. After a second he took it, thumb careful around the bent connector pin, and climbed out of the boat behind them.

The bat lab had held the day's heat. The second Rhea opened the door the layered smells hit Mina: guano dust, rubbing alcohol, wet net, battery heat, old wood swollen by years of salt air. Somebody had left a half-eaten sleeve of saltines on the counter beside the charger bank. A moth battered itself stupid against the fluorescent cover. Everything about the room said temporary science conducted by people who did not have enough money to replace anything before it failed.

Rhea set the recorder on the desk and started moving with that stripped-down efficiency Mina now associated with her most frightened states. Dry bag unzipped. Desiccant packs swapped. Cable spread out on paper towels. Adapter inspected, cursed once in a flat low voice when she saw the bent pin had taken some housing with it. Mina shut the door and wiped her hands on her shorts, leaving brown arcs of mud. Tomas leaned his shoulders against the wall under the bat call charts and watched the room as if he were standing guard over an operating table.

"Laptop," Rhea said.

Mina handed it to her already open. Rhea plugged the recorder in through a sacrificial cable, clicked past two warning screens, and pulled the file up by timestamp. The waveform came in jagged and ugly, strong enough to exist, ugly enough to offend anybody who had ever filled out a chain-of-custody form.

"Well," Rhea said. "That's not nothing."

Tomas came off the wall. "Play it."

The speakers gave them the first stretch clean enough. Mangrove insects. The low engine rumble. Spoonbill colony noise so dense it became almost metallic. Then the hydrophone signal joined, a submerged wash under everything else, all pressure and scrape and movement. Mina leaned over the desk until she could feel the battery heat off the laptop against her wrist.

At fifteen seconds the file clipped hard. The sound broke into digital shards, recovered, and then out of the submerged layer came a tone that did not match engine, bird, or current. Not a single note. A thick pulsed hum, rising and falling twice as if something large had rolled near the microphone and changed the water around it. Tomas's head came up at once. Rhea froze with one hand on the keyboard.

"There," Mina said.

"I know where on the file," Rhea said.

She backed up three seconds and played it again. Same ugly beauty. Same pulsed hum. Then the wake hit in the file and the rest dissolved into fan roar, overdriven screams, cable slap, and one sharp electronic spit when the connector failed. By the end the waveform looked like someone had tried to stab the screen with sound.

Tomas exhaled through his nose. "Any chance that turns into evidence if you squint and lie?"

"Institutionally?" Rhea clicked to spectrogram view. Blocks of color flared and smeared. "No. It turns into appetite. Which isn't the same thing."

Mina pulled up the video from her phone. The first clip was useless, mostly her own breath and a dark mirror of water broken by pink legs. The second held half a miracle and no courtroom. Blue under blackwater, too bright for the sensor, a shape that resolved if you already knew what you were looking for and failed if you needed the image to do the moral labor for you. Tomas watched it once and swore softly.

"That'll have every idiot in three counties saying lens flare."

"It's lens flare's dirtier cousin," Rhea said.

"But you can see it."

"We can see it because we were there."

That stung because it was true. Mina closed the clip before she could start hating the phone for not being an eye.

She sat on the edge of the survey table, then stood again immediately because her body was too full of leftover movement to hold still on anything narrow. The prop scar on her left calf had started to itch where dried salt pulled at the old skin. Her whole forearm still carried the memory of the push pole vibrating against it while blue light moved under the boat. She did not want those sensations to calm down. She was afraid that if they did, the sighting would start to feel narratable, and narratable things always got thinned.

"We tell Imogen?" she asked.

Tomas laughed once, without humor. "You want to tell the woman who can smell grant money through a hurricane?"

"She needs to know if we're going to do this properly."

"Properly," he said, "is a gorgeous word for people with salary."

Rhea did not intervene right away. She kept studying the spectrogram, zooming in and out, lining up the pulsed sound with the moment the waveform brightened in the overdriven channel. Her silence made the room tighter. When she finally turned from the screen, she looked at Mina first, then Tomas, as if choosing the order of damage.

"The problem isn't whether Imogen can help," she said. "The problem is that the instant this leaves the three of us, it stops being an encounter and becomes a resource. For the camp, for the county, for investigators, for whoever wants to launder wonder into ownership."

"It's already a resource," Mina said. "That thing is out there whether we tell anybody or not."

"Yes. And right now only three compromised people know exactly where and how clearly."

Tomas folded his arms. "I hear my favorite adjective."

Rhea's gaze flicked to him. "You're falsifying county logs and ferrying your wife and your lover into protected water on work access. If you want a cleaner noun, find a cleaner life."

He should have snapped. Mina saw the impulse cross him, saw him swallow it because the sentence was too exact to waste energy denying. Instead he rubbed that taped thumb against his forefinger again and looked down at the pink streak on his shirt as though the bird shit had somehow become an argument he could grip.

"Fine," he said. "Compromised. So what?"

"So," Rhea said, and now the control in her voice thinned enough for Mina to hear the strain under it, "three eyewitnesses with bad evidence aren't free. That's worse than one witness with a story. One witness can be dismissed. Three create pressure. Pressure attracts bureaucracy, money, enforcement, theft, all the usual scavengers."

Mina leaned both hands on the desk. "We can't unknow it."

"No," Rhea said. "That's the burden."

There it was, plain enough to leave no moral shelter around it. Not triumph. Not vindication. Burden. Mina felt something in her chest give way to that word because it matched the shape of the night better than any brighter substitute. She had wanted witnesses for so long that she had not thought hard enough about what witnesses owed the thing witnessed. The moment on the water had felt like relief. In the lab it became an account opening in all their names.

Outside, a spoonbill gave a single hoarse bark from somewhere near the service dock. Another answered deeper in the mangroves. The camp generator clicked as the load shifted. Somebody in the kitchen dropped a pan and swore. Ordinary noises. Built noises. Human noises trying to continue while something impossible sat fresh in the next room over.

Tomas pushed off the wall and came to the desk. He put both hands flat on either side of the laptop and looked down at the frozen blue frame from Mina's ruined video.

"If we go official," he said, "the county's going to ask why I was in that channel at that hour. They will ask for logs, fuel, reason, all of it. They won't ask because they care about the eel. They'll ask because paperwork is how people hunt."

"I know," Mina said.

"Do you?" He still did not raise his voice. When Tomas got past anger and into injury he went quieter, not louder, exactly as Rhea had once implied he would. "Because sometimes I think you know facts and not prices."

The line landed. It should have. Mina looked at the floorboards, their warped grain blackened around old screw heads, and thought of his engine loan she knew only in outline, his suspended future not yet arrived but already waiting in the reeds. She thought of her own monthly penalty payment and how little it had taught her about anybody else's cost except as a language for begging extension.

"I know enough to be ashamed," she said.

"Shame is decorative," Tomas said. "Price is the thing."

Rhea closed the laptop halfway, not enough to end the blue glow entirely.

"Then we price it before we move," she said. "Not tonight. Tonight we dry the equipment, duplicate the file, and don't tell a single drunk donor about bioluminescent destiny."

"You really think I was going to tell donors?" Mina said.

"I think you're high on being right," Rhea said. "People in that condition get evangelical."

It was unfair and accurate. Mina almost smiled despite herself.

"What about Imogen tomorrow?" she asked.

Rhea looked at Tomas. Tomas looked at the half-shut laptop. Nobody in the room pretended the decision was clean.

"Tomorrow," Rhea said at last, "we decide what version of the truth can survive contact with institutions."

"That sounds healthy," Tomas muttered.

"It sounds necessary."

He reached past Mina for the recorder, careful now, reverent in spite of himself. His knuckles brushed her wrist and stayed there a fraction longer than balance required. When she looked up he was already watching her. Not with the easy hunger of the first runs. Not even with the rough domestic tenderness of the storm night. This was something more exhausted and more binding. A man who had seen the same impossible body in the same water and knew that fact could not be fucked back into secrecy.

"You really saw the whole thing?" he asked.

"Enough of it," Mina said.

"Yeah." He nodded once. "Me too."

Rhea copied the file to two drives and labeled them with the date in her neat compact print. Her field handwriting always looked like it had been designed to survive deposition. Mina watched the letters appear and felt the chapter of her life shifting under them. March, April, May, whatever exact date spoonbill week had narrowed to no longer mattered as calendar. It mattered because blue had moved under blackwater and three people had stood close enough to have their faces lit by it.

When the second copy finished, Rhea unplugged the drive and set both copies on the desk between them. Tiny objects. Ridiculous objects to carry so much pressure.

"No cloud upload," she said.

"Agreed," Tomas said.

"No texts."

"Agreed."

Rhea looked at Mina.

"No heroic outreach to old contacts before breakfast."

Mina let out a breath that might have been a laugh if she had more room in her chest. "Agreed."

Rhea nodded. "Good. Then we're all equally trapped."

Tomas barked a softer laugh at that. Mina did smile then, because the sentence was awful and exact and because the alternative was to start shaking. The three of them stood around the desk, not touching for a moment, the lab lit by one bad fluorescent and the half-shut glow of the laptop screen. Outside, the spoonbills kept at their filthy ecstatic business in the dark as though revelation were just another pressure wave moving through the colony, nothing to stop breeding over.

Mina looked again at the frozen frame of blue under black. The image could not prove itself. The sound could not testify cleanly. The state would laugh, or seize it, or both. Donors would call it miracle until a budget line was attached. Imogen would see roof repairs and permit leverage. Investigators, once they came, would smell biotech liability before wonder. None of that changed what the animal had been in the water.

That was the worst and best part. The eel had not needed a usable file to become real. It had become real in three bodies at once. Now reality sat on the table in duplicate drives, bent metal, mud drying under her fingernails, and the changed way Tomas and Rhea looked at her and at each other. Rumor had been private weather. Fact was heavier. Fact asked what they were willing to do to a thing they could no longer dismiss.

Out in the dark channels a spoonbill clattered its bill like cheap applause. Mina thought of the eel turning under those pink legs, blue writing itself through disturbed tannin, and felt the sight settle into her not as triumph but as debt.
