# Chapter 1

The light behind Karen Galloway was wrong.

Nick noticed it before she said anything — the flat midday glare from a window behind and to the left of her, washing her face into a warm-tone silhouette while the bookshelf beyond burned overexposed. The Zoom camera's auto-exposure was splitting the difference and losing. It made Karen look like she was sitting in front of a greenscreen of her own office.

She'd been his editor at Magpie Press for six years. Not his editor — his client. She bought children's book covers from him. Four or five a year, eight hundred dollars each, net thirty days, and she always paid on day thirty-one because she was kind but Magpie's accounts payable was not. She had gray-brown hair and reading glasses she wore on a chain and a habit of tilting her head when she was about to say something she'd rehearsed.

She was tilting her head now.

"So, Nick," she said. "How are you?"

"I'm good," he said. "I'm fine. I've got some sketches for the Barrington thing if you want to —"

"Right, so." Karen adjusted her glasses. "That's actually what I wanted to talk to you about."

He watched her hands. She was holding a pen she wasn't going to use, clicking the cap in and out with her thumb. The sound came through the laptop speakers a half-second late, a tiny rhythmic pop.

"We've been looking at some new tools," Karen said. "For the illustration side. And I wanted to loop you in before we made anything official."

"OK."

"There's a program called Midjourney. Have you heard of it?"

He had. He'd seen the weird hands on Twitter, the glossy fantasy forests in art feeds, people posting prompts like recipes. It had all lived on the other side of the screen, far enough away that he could close the tab and go back to painting.

"Yeah," he said. "I've seen some stuff."

"So we've been running some tests with it, and the results have been — I mean, honestly, Nick, they've been really impressive. For what we need, which is covers, you know, that hit a certain look —"

"You're replacing me."

Karen's head untilted. She blinked. The pen stopped clicking.

"That's not — I wouldn't put it that way."

"How would you put it."

"We're shifting our cover illustration pipeline to AI-generated imagery, and we won't be commissioning new work from freelance illustrators going forward." She said it all in one breath, and he realized she'd memorized it. Someone had written that sentence for her. It had the cadence of a legal department.

"OK," Nick said.

"It's nothing about the quality of your work," Karen said. "You know we love what you do. The Barrington covers were gorgeous."

She said *gorgeous* and kept clicking the pen cap. The word didn't slow her down.

"Sure," Nick said.

"It's a cost thing, honestly. The program can generate dozens of options in an hour. We can iterate in real time. And the cost per image is —" She stopped herself. She had the decency to stop herself.

"Less than eight hundred dollars," Nick said.

"Significantly."

He looked at the screen. Karen's face, overexposed and slightly pixelated. The bookshelf behind her with its row of Magpie Press children's titles, the spines he could read from memory because he'd painted six of them. The light behind her was doing something to the edge of her hair, a warm halo effect that in a painting he would have rendered with raw sienna thinned almost to a glaze. He noticed this. He noticed he was noticing it.

"OK," he said again. "When?"

"Effective immediately, I think. The Barrington project we'll handle internally."

"You already have the covers."

"We have — we've generated some options, yes."

"Can I see them?"

Karen hesitated. "I don't think that would be productive."

"Karen, I've done six covers for that series. I just want to see what you're going with."

She shared her screen. Three covers appeared in a grid. They were for a middle-grade fantasy novel about a girl and her fox, the seventh in the Barrington Chronicles, which Nick had been illustrating since the third book. He'd already done the preliminary sketches. The fox had a scar over its left eye that he'd established in book three and carried through every cover since.

The AI covers were good. Not great — the fox's proportions shifted between images in a way that suggested the model had no spatial memory, and in the second image the girl's left hand had a thumb growing out of the wrong side of the palm — but the colors were right, the mood was right, the shelf appeal was there. They looked like what they were: competent, fast approximations of the thing he'd spent six years building.

The fox didn't have the scar.

"These are nice," he said.

"I know it's a lot to process."

"No, they're — the palette's good. Whoever prompted it knew what they were doing."

"That was our new art director, actually. Sarah. She's been really getting into the tool."

"Cool."

He kept looking at the girl's impossible thumb. It came out of the palm on the wrong side and folded inward toward the wrist. Nobody at Magpie had noticed. Nobody at Magpie would notice. The colors were right. The fox looked soft. That was enough.

"Nick, I want you to know that if we ever —"

"Thanks, Karen. I've gotta go."

He closed the laptop. He sat at his kitchen table, which was also his painting table, which was also his desk, which was a door he'd found on the sidewalk in 2019 and set across two sawhorses. The surface was covered in dried acrylic and gouache, a geological record of three years of covers and personal work. Cadmium yellow under cerulean blue under a rust-colored drip of burnt sienna he'd never been able to identify the origin of.

The kitchen faucet dripped. It had dripped since August. The landlord had sent a maintenance request form that Nick had filled out and returned, and then nothing had happened, and the form's carbon copy still hung on the refrigerator under a magnet from a pizzeria that had closed.

He put his head in his hands. His hands smelled like linseed oil and the granola bar he'd eaten for lunch. His right index finger had a small white scar from an X-Acto knife, from cutting mats for his last gallery show at Vera Lux — a small space in Temescal that showed emerging painters alongside whoever's ceramics they were rotating through that month. He'd sold one piece out of nine. The other eight were against the wall behind him, wrapped in the plastic sheeting he'd bought at Blick for $14.99 a roll.

He sat there for a while. The light in the apartment was flat and gray, the November overcast that came through the east windows as an even, diffuse wash — good painting light, actually, the kind that eliminated harsh shadows and let you see color values without distortion. He'd moved here for the light. Three years ago, when Pendragon and Magpie were both steady and $14,000 a year felt like it could almost work if he was careful. The light was still here. Everything else had changed.

A BART train passed on the elevated track outside the window, a low rolling thunder that shook the single-pane glass in its frame and then was gone and left the room quieter than before.

---

The overdue rent notice was on the counter, under a coffee mug he'd put there to stop looking at it. He looked at it now. Eastshore had stapled a new sheet over the old one: PAY $1,100 BY DECEMBER 1 TO AVOID FURTHER ACTION. The older notice underneath still showed last month's date. They had not stopped him from being behind. They had just compressed the problem into six days. His checking account had $1,340 in it. After rent, he'd have $240. He was supposed to have gotten another $800 from Karen for the Barrington sketches — preliminary work she'd already approved, work that was already serving as the basis for whatever prompts Sarah the new art director had typed into Midjourney.

He pulled the notice out and read the whole thing. The language was firm but not yet legal. *Please remit balance within six (6) days to avoid further action.* Further action meant eviction proceedings. He'd looked it up once, late at night, with the notice propped beside the keyboard and his checking balance open on the screen. In Oakland, the process took about two months if the landlord was motivated. His landlord was a property management company called Eastshore Holdings that communicated exclusively through form letters and a voicemail box that had been full since July.

He opened his laptop and checked the balance anyway. $1,340. There was a direct deposit from Magpie for October's cover — the last one, the Barrington book with the ice cave — and not much else. He'd painted the cave's interior light as a pale blue-violet, the color of glacier ice when you're deep enough inside that the sunlight has to travel through six feet of compressed snow to reach you, and the light that survives is stripped of everything except its shortest wavelengths. He'd been proud of that one. Twelve hours of work. Eight hundred dollars, minus self-employment tax.

Nothing else was coming in. He had not had a second client since Pendragon Books dropped their illustration budget in March. Before that, it had been Pendragon and Magpie, roughly $14,000 a year between them if he worked steadily, which he had, for four years. Fourteen thousand dollars a year to paint children's book covers in a city where his studio cost $13,200 a year in rent alone.

The math had never worked. He'd known it didn't work. He'd subsidized the gap with canvas sales that came irregularly and sometimes not at all, and with a brief experiment selling prints on Etsy that netted $216 over four months, and by not going to the dentist, and by eating the way he ate, which was cereal and ramen and whatever fruit was cheapest at the Mi Pueblo on International Boulevard.

He walked through the apartment. It didn't take long. The studio was one room — a large one, high ceilings and two windows facing east, which was why he'd taken it. The morning light was the best he'd found in Oakland, a pale clear thing that came straight off the hills and filled the room without hot spots. He'd set his easel by the east window. His paint supplies occupied a baker's rack he'd found at a garage sale: tubes of oil and acrylic arranged by hue the way another person might organize spices, jars of medium, linseed oil, turpentine, a coffee can of brushes ranging from a size-0 detail round to a wide flat he used for priming. The supplies were worth roughly $600 if he replaced them at retail. More than what would be left after rent. More than this month's groceries would come to.

Against the north wall, his paintings. Nine from the Vera Lux show — landscapes of the Oakland hills, the bay, the salt flats near Alviso, a series he'd called *Terrestrial* that his friend Jess said was the best work he'd done. Nine paintings, framed, wrapped in plastic, unsold. Plus a few dozen smaller works, unframed, stacked in portfolios and leaning against the baseboard. The gallery had posted them all on their website when the show went up a year ago — thumbnail images with his name and the gallery's watermark and prices he'd already reduced twice. The website was still live. He assumed nobody was looking at it.

He stood in front of the stacked paintings and tried to see them fresh. The hills at golden hour, the fog coming through the Gate at an angle that bent the light into three distinct temperature zones — warm, cool, warm again — that had taken him two weeks and six attempts to get right. The salt flat at Alviso where the dried mineral deposits read as pure white from a distance but up close turned out to be a dozen colors: rose, ochre, a pale chemical green he'd mixed from viridian and zinc white and never been able to replicate.

They were good paintings. He could point to the parts that held: the fog caught at the ridge, the salt crust going pink at the edges, the strip of dead grass cutting the foreground in half. None of that had made anyone buy them. He'd priced the Alviso piece at $1,200 for the show. Then $900. Then $750, which was what the gallery's owner suggested, gently, over email, with a smiley face that did nothing to soften the math. Seven hundred fifty dollars for three months of work, and still nobody had bought it, and it leaned against his wall gathering a fine layer of Oakland dust on the plastic wrap while the gallery's website displayed its thumbnail to whatever indifferent algorithm happened to crawl by.

His phone buzzed. A text from Jess: *How'd the Karen call go?*

He typed *Fine* and deleted it. Typed *She's switching to AI* and deleted that too. Typed *Call you later* and sent it and put his phone facedown on the counter.

Jess was at a residency in New Mexico, three months of making work in the desert, a grant she'd applied for six times before they gave it to her. She was still painting. She was still broke, same as him, but she was painting every day and posting the work and it was good and she was getting somewhere, or at least she was getting somewhere adjacent to somewhere, which in the art world was almost the same thing. She would understand about Karen. She would be kind about it. Her kindness would make it real in a way that right now, five hours after the call, it still didn't feel.

He poured a bowl of Cheerios. The milk was dated five days ago but smelled fine. He ate standing at the counter, looking at the rent notice, his spoon clinking against the ceramic in the quiet apartment. Outside, another BART train. The windows rattled and settled.

---

He started browsing at eight. By midnight he'd seen enough.

The AI art forums were hell. Not because the people on them were wrong — some of them were, the ones who posted generated portraits of women with eleven fingers and called it art — but because enough of them were right. The technology was improving at a rate that made his stomach feel like it was being lowered slowly on a rope.

He found a subreddit where someone had posted a side-by-side comparison of Midjourney v3 and v4 outputs, the same prompt run through both. The v3 images had the soupy, dreamlike quality he'd seen before — faces that smeared, hands that bloomed into tentacles, backgrounds that dissolved into color noise. The v4 images looked like photographs of things that existed. Three months between versions. Three months, and the hands had gone from five-to-eight fingers to five-to-six, and the faces had gone from melted to merely uncanny, and the backgrounds had snapped into focus with a spatial coherence that would have earned a passing grade in a first-year perspective class.

He typed his own descriptors into the Midjourney Discord's prompt bar. Not his name — that wasn't how it worked, he knew that much — but the words that described what he did. *Landscape painting, Northern California, golden hour, muted palette, painterly brushwork, atmospheric perspective.* He hit enter. Four images appeared in under a minute.

They were better than he wanted them to be.

The first was oversaturated — the orange in the hills was Photoshop-sunset orange, not the dusty warm gold he actually mixed from yellow ochre and a touch of cadmium red — but the composition was sound. The horizon sat at roughly the right height, the negative space in the sky had weight, and the foreground had a convincing depth-of-field softness that he'd spent years learning to achieve with thin washes layered wet-on-dry. The second was generic, a hotel-hallway landscape from Reno. But the third was good. The third stopped him.

It showed hills that could have been the East Bay from the Berkeley side, lit from behind by a low sun, with a fog bank rolling through a gap in the ridge. The color temperature was right — that specific warm-cool layering he'd painted a hundred times, the thing that happened in November when the sun dropped low enough to turn the hills into a backlit scrim and the fog caught the remaining light and held it. The surface texture wasn't brushwork — no dragged bristle, no paint skipping over canvas tooth, just a repeating pattern sitting where brushwork should have been. But the light. The light was close to the thing he'd spent his adult life trying to render.

It had taken forty-seven seconds.

He sat back. The chair was a twenty-dollar IKEA piece whose pneumatic cylinder had failed two years ago, so it sat at its lowest height and his knees came up almost to the table. He was aware, distantly, that his back hurt, and that noticing his back was a way of not thinking about the image on the screen.

He clicked into the prompt-sharing threads. The part that got to him wasn't the images. It was the notes underneath. People were sorting outputs the way a tired art department might sort comps at midnight: this one sells, this one doesn't, lighting slips here, the hand's a mess, reroll, upscale 2, blend 4's background with 1's palette. The language was ugly and procedural, but the work inside it was familiar. Somebody still had to decide what counted as close enough.

He kept going. Reddit threads, Twitter, the Stable Diffusion subreddit. He scrolled past an image of a woman whose hair turned into a waterfall, which was kitsch, and an image of a medieval battle scene where every soldier had the same face, which was eerie, and an image of a bowl of fruit so photorealistic it took him ten seconds to find the failure — a shadow cast at an angle that implied two light sources in a scene that only had one. In the prompt guides and Discord chats, the language kept repeating: better anatomy, cleaner lighting, stronger prompt match; rank these, flag that, pick the least broken one and send it on.

He found galleries of AI-generated art that people had submitted to juried competitions — and won. He found a thread where a stock photographer described, with financial specificity Nick recognized because it matched his own, how his licensing income had dropped from $60,000 a year to $8,000 in eighteen months. He found a thread where a concept artist at a game studio described being handed a folder of AI-generated images and told to "clean these up" for a quarter of her previous rate. He found a thread where a Midjourney power user explained how to get "in the style of" living painters — not by name, which the filters caught, but by describing their habits in enough detail that the model could fake them. Someone had done this with Sargent, with Thiebaud, with Richter. The results were imperfect and recognizable and getting better monthly.

At 2 AM he made more cereal. Honey Nut Cheerios, the last of the box. He ate it over the keyboard, which was a thing he did that had already killed one laptop. The apartment was cold — November in Oakland wasn't freezing but the Victorian's single-pane windows and absence of insulation made the difference between inside and outside a matter of six degrees, and running the baseboard heater for more than a few hours showed up on the PG&E bill in a way he couldn't afford. He pulled a flannel over his T-shirt. The flannel was brown and black plaid, from a Goodwill in Temescal, and it had a small tear at the left elbow he'd been meaning to sew shut for a year.

At 3 AM he found the data-labeling companies.

He wasn't looking for them. He was reading a long technical post about how image-generation models were trained — jargon he mostly couldn't follow, loss functions and gradient descent and terms that read like a foreign language — and the article mentioned "human evaluators." They weren't engineers. They were people who looked at batches of generated images and scored whether the hands made sense, whether the lighting held together, whether the image actually matched the prompt. Those scores, the article said, were fed back into the next training run. The models learned which outputs were good and which were bad because people kept telling them.

He read the paragraph three times. Then he searched for the companies. Scale AI. Surge AI. Appen. A dozen smaller ones. They hired people to rate images, flag errors, and sometimes write a sentence about why an output failed. The job postings asked for visual literacy, attention to detail, and familiarity with digital media workflows. Some of them mentioned art backgrounds. One listed basic Python as a plus. Another said people who could handle internal tools had room to move up.

He stared at the screen. The glow lit the apartment in a blue-white wash that turned the stacked paintings on the north wall into dark rectangles, all their color gone. The BART tracks were silent at this hour. The faucet dripped its eleven-second interval — he'd counted once, during a late night of painting, and now he couldn't stop counting it.

The plan arrived the way paint mixed on a palette — not a thought but a physical event, two colors pushed together producing something neither was alone. His anger, which had been shapeless and ambient since the Zoom call, met the information on the screen and acquired form.

If the system learned from human ratings, then the ratings were the part he could get his hands on. Not by acting like an idiot. That would get him fired in a week. He would have to look competent and be wrong in ways that passed for judgment: give an almost-right hand the benefit of the doubt, call broken light plausible, mark an image as matching the prompt when the important thing in it had quietly slid sideways. It couldn't be garbage. It had to be drift.

The postings had already shown him the access path. They all wanted versions of the same thing: basic Python, comfort with internal tools, visual literacy, attention to detail. He could fake enough code to get hired onto an evaluation team. Once he was in, he could call the backward thumb broken, the fruit-bowl shadow wrong, the missing fox scar a miss. People who caught those things got room.

He reopened three job tabs — Scale AI, Surge AI, Appen — and copied the overlapping requirements onto the back of the rent notice in block capitals: PYTHON. IMAGE QUALITY. DETAIL. DIGITAL MEDIA WORKFLOWS. Then, underneath them, because he wanted to see how stupid it looked in writing: GET INSIDE.

It was 4 AM. The November sky through the east windows was black and featureless, hours from light. The apartment was cold and his cereal bowl was empty and he had $1,340 in checking and $1,100 due to Eastshore in six days, and the plan was either the smartest thing he'd ever thought of or the stupidest, and he sat there at his salvaged-door table with dried paint under his elbows and he didn't care which.

He opened a new tab and typed *learn Python free.* Then he clicked the first thing that came up.
