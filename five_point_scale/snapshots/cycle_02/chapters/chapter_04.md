# Chapter 4

The building was the color of spackle. Not on purpose — the stucco had probably been white once, or off-white, or whatever the developer's spec had called it twenty years ago. Now it was the gray-beige of anything left too long under Bay Area weather, the same non-color as the February sky above it and the parking lot in front of it and the trimmed junipers lining the walkway that some landscaping contract maintained at the bare minimum of alive. Nick sat in his Civic and checked the address on his phone against the number bolted to the facade. Canopy AI, Suite 200. Second floor. He'd imagined something sleeker — glass, maybe, or at least a lobby with a logo on the wall, a place that looked like it was building the future rather than leasing space between a property management firm and something called Pacific Synergy Solutions.

The lobby directory board used white plastic push-in letters, the kind that dentist offices used. He took the stairs because the elevator had an OUT OF ORDER sign taped to it with blue painter's tape that had been there long enough to curl. The stairwell smelled like industrial floor cleaner and carpet adhesive, and the railing was the brushed-steel kind that collected palm grease. The door to Suite 200 was propped open with a rubber wedge. Inside, a woman at a reception desk looked up from her monitor.

"Nick Bray?"

"Yeah."

"You're onboarding with Lisa. Have a seat."

The reception area had four chairs with chrome tube frames and thin cushions in a shade of blue that didn't exist in nature — too saturated for sky, too cold for hydrangea, the pure product of a plastics catalog. Behind the desk, the wall displayed the Canopy AI logo — a stylized tree canopy in corporate green — and below it, a framed poster that read THE HUMAN LAYER in sans-serif type above a stock photo of diverse hands reaching toward a glowing screen. Nick gave it the same look he gave all commercial art: a brief, involuntary flinch when the shadows were wrong. The hands in the stock photo were lit from two incompatible angles, composited from separate shoots. A small thing. Nobody in the lobby would notice it. The bad shadows still snagged his eye.

Lisa from HR had reading glasses on a beaded chain and a desk stacked with manila folders that tilted slightly, one pile leaning against a mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST MANAGER. She walked Nick through the paperwork at a practiced clip, tapping each form once before sliding it across the desk: W-4, I-9, direct deposit form, NDA, employee handbook, emergency contact sheet. Nick filled them out one by one. He wrote his apartment address on the emergency contact line because he couldn't think of anyone within a ten-minute drive who would come if something happened to him. When he signed the NDA — a standard nondisclosure agreement covering "proprietary evaluation methods, training data, and client relationships" — the pen hesitated for a fraction of a second over the signature line, a pause so small nobody watching would have marked it and so large, from inside it, that it felt like standing at the edge of something. He signed it. Lisa didn't look up.

She gave him a lanyard with a badge — his photo from the interview, overexposed by the flash, making him look startled and slightly washed out — and walked him to the evaluation floor.

The floor was a single open room that took up most of the second story. Nick stood at the threshold and his painter's eye did what it always did with new spaces: it catalogued the light first, then the geometry, then the surfaces, then the life moving through them. The light was institutional fluorescent, the flat shadowless wash that killed depth perception and turned human skin the color of uncooked dough. It came from recessed panels in a drop ceiling with pocked, water-stained tiles — the light had no direction, no warmth, no quality at all. It was illumination without character, the visual equivalent of white noise. The geometry was a grid: six rows of workstations, four to a row, each station a desk with dual monitors and a rolling chair. The rows were separated by aisles wide enough for a person to pass but narrow enough that you'd brush someone's chair if you weren't careful. The surfaces were beige carpet, walked thin and dark in the traffic lanes, and grayish-white walls bare of anything except a whiteboard near the entrance covered in numbers and acronyms Nick didn't recognize. No art. No posters except the one in reception. No windows except at the far end of the room, where a strip of glass showed the parking lot and, beyond it, the concrete ribbon of the 880 overpass.

Twenty-some people sat at the stations. Most wore headphones. Most stared at their screens with the particular inward focus of people doing work that demanded attention but not engagement — the look of someone reading for information, not pleasure. A few murmured into headsets. One woman in the second row had a nursing textbook propped open beside her keyboard, stealing glances between image assessments. A heavyset man in what looked like a FedEx uniform sat at the end of the first row, still wearing his jacket, apparently having come straight from another job. The room hummed — not from conversation but from the machines, the monitors and overhead lights and the HVAC pushing recycled air through ceiling vents with a faint, constant whistle. Below the hum, the staccato click of mice. Open, assess, rate, click. Open, assess, rate, click. The rhythm of it was quiet but detectable, audible only in a room with nothing louder.

"This is where the magic happens," Lisa said, without inflection, and handed him off to his team lead.

---

Priya Acharya was waiting for him at the whiteboard. She was around forty, with dark hair showing early gray that she hadn't bothered to dye, and she wore a blazer over a cotton blouse, dark jeans, flats that looked new — a half-step too professional for the business park. She'd dressed for the job, not the setting. She shook his hand and smiled. It carried warmth, but it also had the smooth edge of repetition, the habit of making a new person comfortable fast without turning intimate. A teacher's smile, Nick thought.

"Nick, right? I'm Priya. I run the image-evaluation team here. The interview loop makes everybody talk to too many people, but day to day you're with me." She gestured at the room. "Not much to look at, but the monitors are decent. Let me show you your station and introduce you to the team."

His station was in the fourth row, middle seat. The desk wobbled when he set his bag on it, and the chair had a cracked left armrest wrapped in duct tape. Two monitors: the left displayed the Canopy evaluation portal, a gray interface with a sidebar of queue categories and a main panel that said AWAITING LOGIN in plain type. The right monitor showed reference materials — rubric guides, annotation standards, a Slack window with a channel called #sl-eval-floor.

"You'll be sharing the row with Dani, Tommy, and Bao," Priya said. "Dani's on her way. Tommy's —" She looked around the room, didn't find him. "Somewhere. And Bao's right here."

She turned to the station beside Nick's, where an older man sat with his back very straight and his face close to the screen. He wore reading glasses on a thin chain, and the fluorescent light caught them as he tilted his head to study whatever was on his monitor. His clothes were neat — a pressed button-down tucked into dark slacks, the creases still sharp, not expensive but carefully maintained. On the desk, beside the keyboard: a dented steel thermos and a small spiral notebook with a pen clipped to its cover. Nick noticed his hands first, as he always did with people's hands — compact, still, precise. Even at rest they looked arranged. He could imagine them lifting a thing, considering its weight, and setting it back exactly where it belonged.

"Bao, this is Nick. He's starting today."

Bao turned from his screen and looked at Nick through the reading glasses. His face was compact and weathered, and his expression was not unfriendly but not rushed either. He studied Nick for a moment — really looked, with the patient scrutiny Nick was used to aiming outward and not used to receiving — and then nodded once.

"Welcome," he said. His voice was quiet, precise, accented in a way that softened consonants. "The chair is bad. If you put a book under the left front leg, the wobble stops."

"Thanks."

"Mm." Bao turned back to his screen, where Nick could see, briefly, an image of a human figure standing in a doorway — something generated, slightly wrong around the elbows. Bao tilted his head the other way and did not click anything.

Priya led Nick past Bao's station to the next one down the row, which was empty except for a jacket draped over the chair. "That's Dani's spot. She's usually early, but—"

"I'm here, I'm here." A voice from behind them, slightly breathless. Nick turned and saw someone cutting between the rows of stations with a paper coffee cup in each hand and a phone clamped between shoulder and ear. He was young — early twenties, thin, with a self-maintained buzz cut and dark circles under his eyes that looked less like a bad night and more like a permanent condition. His jacket was thrift-store corduroy, worn over a t-shirt with a faded graphic that might have been a band logo or might have been an art school print, impossible to tell. He held one of the coffee cups out toward Priya without pausing whatever conversation he was having on the phone.

"Uh-huh. No, I know. I *know*, but — Devon, it said two-hour, not two-hour-except-Tuesdays. That's not — OK. OK, yeah, I'll call them. Yeah. Bye." He pocketed the phone and looked at Nick. "Hey. You're the new guy?"

"Nick."

"Tommy." Quick handshake, slightly damp from the coffee cups. He turned to Priya. "Got you the oat milk one."

"You didn't have to do that."

"I know, but I felt guilty about yesterday."

"What happened yesterday?" Nick asked.

"Nothing," Priya said.

"I was late," Tommy said. "Like, aggressively late. Like, not-in-the-building-until-ten-fifteen late. I locked my keys in my car in the parking lot and had to wait for Triple A, and by the time they showed up I was just sitting on the curb out there like—" He waved a hand. "Anyway. What'd you do before this?"

The question arrived fast enough that Nick had to make a choice in real time. He'd rehearsed a version — vague, something about freelance work and looking for something steadier — but what came out was closer to the truth than he'd planned.

"I was a painter. Canvas, oil paint. Landscapes, some illustration work."

"Sick." Tommy said it casually, without surprise, as if this were the expected answer. "I dropped out of RISD after three semesters. Priya used to teach high school art, so technically she's the success story." He took a sip of his coffee.

"That's not what that means," Priya said.

"No, but it's funnier."

"Point is," Tommy said, already turning toward his station, "you'll fit right in. Whatever that means here."

He sat down and put headphones on, and Nick stayed in the aisle a second longer. He looked around the room again, not at the architecture this time but at the people inhabiting it. Priya still carried the teacher's ease she'd brought to the whiteboard. Bao bent toward his screen with a patient, deliberate care Nick associated less with office work than with layout tables and drafting lamps. Not everyone on the floor belonged to the same story. The woman with the nursing textbook clearly didn't. The man in the FedEx jacket probably didn't either. But enough of them did. He knew that quiet already — studio classes, gallery crits, the residency mixer where he'd met Jess in what felt like a different lifetime. It was the quiet of people for whom looking was a skill, not a default.

He sat down at his station. The fluorescent lights made a faint, high-pitched buzz above him that he would stop hearing within the hour.

---

Dani Solis arrived a few minutes later, cutting through the rows without breaking stride. The turns around chair backs and desk corners looked memorized below the neck. She was around thirty, dark hair pulled up in a clip with a few loose strands, and she carried a canvas tote bag with a child's drawing sticking out of the top — heavy crayon lines, something with four legs and what might have been a tail. She wore work boots and jeans and a flannel layered over a dark tank top, and when she set the tote under her desk she did it with the care reserved for something that contained a thing you cared about.

"Hey," she said to Bao, who nodded without turning from his screen.

She noticed Nick. "You're new."

"Nick. First day."

"Dani." She sat down and logged in, and Nick watched her hands on the keyboard — not deliberately, just an old reflex from years of looking at hands for a living. Under two of her fingernails, in the cuticle lines, he could see the remnants of pigment. Not polish. Paint. Green and something darker, possibly burnt umber. The kind of stain that didn't come off with soap, that you had to let grow out or scrub away with turpentine, and that most painters stopped trying to remove because it became part of your hands, part of the evidence that you worked with color. She caught him looking and he glanced at his own screen.

"Priya do your walkthrough?" she asked.

"Yeah. Showed me the station, the portal."

"She give you the rubric?"

"She said after lunch."

Dani pulled open her desk drawer and handed him a laminated card, restaurant-menu-sized, printed on both sides. "Here. Quick version. The full rubric's on the portal, but nobody reads the full one."

The card listed five evaluation categories in bold:

**Prompt Adherence** — Does the generated image match the text prompt?
**Anatomical Accuracy** — Are human/animal forms structurally correct?
**Lighting Consistency** — Is the light source coherent across the image?
**Compositional Balance** — Does the image hold together as a spatial arrangement?
**Stylistic Coherence** — Is the visual style unified throughout the image?

Each category was rated one to five, with descriptor anchors at each level. Nick read them and felt a sensation he couldn't immediately name — something between recognition and wrongness, like hearing a melody he knew played in a key that was slightly off. These were the things he already looked at. These were the things his eye did without being told, had done since his first figure-drawing class at twenty. Anatomical accuracy. Lighting consistency. Compositional balance. The rubric was a painter's vocabulary run through a corporate grinder, stripped of nuance and flattened into integers. Everything he knew how to see, reduced to a number between one and five.

"Pretty self-explanatory," Dani said. "The hard part isn't knowing what to look for. It's calibrating where a three ends and a four starts."

"Yeah."

"You'll get it. Takes a few batches." She put one earbud in — just the one, leaving the other ear open to the room — and turned back to her screen.

He watched her work for a moment. The rhythm was visible: she'd study the image, her eyes sweeping in a pattern he recognized as a compositional scan — top-left to center to edges, the same scan he used on a painting before taking in the subject — then she'd click through the five rating fields and hit Next. The whole cycle took maybe two minutes. She never hurried the scan and never lingered once she'd found the answer.

Down the row, Bao was still on the same image he'd had up when Nick arrived. His head was tilted to one side, his reading glasses catching the overhead fluorescent wash. He kept looking at the screen with the same patience he'd brought to Nick, waiting for whatever in the picture would betray itself next. Tommy, by contrast, clicked in rapid bursts, then went still whenever the screen gave him something messy enough to earn a second look. Nick could see the edge of Tommy's screen from where he sat: a generated image of a woman standing in a garden, except the woman appeared to have three hands and the garden's flowers were growing sideways out of a wall.

Nick logged in to the evaluation portal with the credentials Lisa had given him and waited for the queue to load.

---

At noon he went to the break room for water. It was a small square room at the end of the hall — a microwave, a coffee maker with a carafe of what looked like it had been sitting since morning, a mini-fridge humming in the corner, and a round table with four mismatched chairs. The window looked out over the parking lot and, beyond it, a landscaping island planted with ornamental grasses that had gone brown and seedy, leaning sideways from the last rain. His Civic sat in the lot below, the oldest car in the row by at least a decade.

Two people from other teams were eating at the table — a woman with a Tupperware of rice and vegetables who was scrolling her phone, and a man in his thirties with a beard and a laptop open to what looked like a side project, code on the screen. Neither looked up. Nick filled a paper cup from the water cooler and stood by the window.

He could hear the evaluation floor through the wall — not voices, just the ambient hum of it. Monitors, lights, ventilation, the ticking of mice. The sound of people doing the same task in parallel, like a room full of students taking the same exam. Through the window, a BART train moved along the elevated track in the middle distance, sliding south toward Fremont, its silver cars catching what little light the overcast sky offered. He did the math without meaning to. Twenty-four people on the floor, most of them probably making about what he was making, maybe less by the time taxes and gas and everything else finished taking bites out of it. The company had found a way to buy trained looking by the shift. Years of practice, all that attention, flattened into a badge and twenty dollars an hour.

He went back to his station.

---

His first batch was twenty-five images. The portal loaded them into a queue, one at a time, each accompanied by the text prompt that had generated it and the five rating fields. He opened the first image and everything else in the room went quiet.

It was supposed to be a portrait of a young woman holding a cat. The prompt read: *photorealistic portrait, young woman, brown hair, holding tabby cat, soft natural lighting, detailed.* What the model had produced was close enough to be human and wrong enough to make his skin prickle. The woman's face was almost right — the proportions were plausible, the features were individual, the skin had pores and fine hairs rendered with obsessive fidelity. But the transition from her jaw to her neck had the consistency of melted wax, as if the model had understood what a jaw was and what a neck was but not how one became the other. Her left eye sat a millimeter higher than the right, which wasn't unusual in actual faces, but here it combined with a flatness in the gaze — a stillness behind the irises that had nothing to do with expression — that made her look like a department store mannequin someone had painted with extraordinary care. The cat was worse. Its body was anatomically reasonable, but its front paws had been rendered as blunt mittens without visible toes, and its tail emerged from a point high on its back that was nowhere near the base of the spine.

Nick clicked through the categories. Prompt adherence: the image matched the prompt. Four. Anatomical accuracy: the jaw-neck seam, the paw structure, the tail origin point. Two. Lighting consistency — he looked again. The soft natural light the prompt requested was present, more or less, but the woman's nose cast a shadow to the left while the cat's body cast one to the right, which meant either two light sources in a scene that should have had one or a fundamental confusion in the model's understanding of how photons worked. Two. Compositional balance: the woman was centered, the cat broke the symmetry, the background was an indistinct blur that didn't compete with the subject. Three. Stylistic coherence: the hyper-detailed rendering of the woman's face against the painterly blur of the background produced a tonal split, as if two different artists had worked on the same image without communicating. Two.

He clicked Next.

The next few taught him the rest of the job fast. One image showed a sunset lake with reflections too perfect to belong to moving water. Another showed a rainy street that looked cinematic until the pedestrians' knees bent the wrong way. Then the queue served up a close-up of a human hand reaching off-frame. The prompt wanted *realistic, detailed.* The image gave it six fingers. Not a blur, not a stump — six complete digits, knuckles and nails and shadow all carefully rendered, as if the model had learned that hands radiated outward and never learned the count.

One, one, three, three, two.

By the fifteenth image, the rhythm had established itself. Open, scan, assess, rate, click. The work required his full visual attention and almost none of his conscious reasoning, which produced a strange floating sensation — the eyes busy, the hands busy, the mind hovering above the task, noticing things at the edges. The fluorescent light shifted the monitor colors warm; everything on screen read a few degrees yellower than its actual value. Bao was now on perhaps his fifth image, which put his pace at roughly one every ten minutes. Tommy had burned through what looked like his entire first batch and started a second. Dani's pace hadn't changed — one image every two and a half minutes, steady as a metronome.

Most of the queue missed human bodies in the same repeatable places: hands first, then shoulders, eye sockets, the weight of fabric over bone. The errors landed in him before language did. A shoulder was wrong. An eye socket was too shallow. Light broke from two directions at once. The rubric wanted numbers. He kept giving them numbers. But the translation — from *wrong in the shoulder* to 2 out of 5 for anatomical accuracy — felt like forcing something wet and dimensional through a stencil until only the approved shape remained.

At five o'clock Priya came by his station. "How'd your first day go?"

"Good. Yeah, fine. The images are—" He stopped, unsure what he'd been about to say. Uncanny. Fascinating. Depressing. Wrong in ways he could feel in his own body.

"They're something," Priya said, and the faint amusement in her voice suggested she'd watched a lot of first days end with that unfinished sentence. "You got through the whole batch?"

"And about half the second."

"That's fast for day one. Most people take a while with the interface." She glanced at his screen, where the portal displayed his completion stats. "Your accuracy gets checked against consensus scoring tomorrow, so we'll see how you calibrated. But the throughput's good." She paused. "Any questions?"

He had a hundred questions, but none of them were the kind she was asking about. "I'm good," he said.

"Great. Welcome to Canopy." She said it without irony, or if there was irony in it, it was the kind that had been lived with for long enough to become indistinguishable from sincerity.

Nick drove home on the 880. Quarter after five and traffic had already locked solid, stretching his fifteen-minute commute into forty. He sat in the slow lane with the window cracked, February air mixing with the Civic's rattling heater, and tried to assemble the day into something he could hold in his mind.

By the time he passed the airport, the day had already revised the plan. Priya had named two control points without meaning to: consensus scoring and throughput. Dani had handed him the floor version of the rubric because the official one was too bloated to use. Bao worked slowly enough that slowness itself might show up in a metric. Tommy moved fast enough to survive only as long as the numbers kept liking him back. Anything crude he did in this building would hit the row before it hit anyone higher up.

The traffic inched forward. The bay opened up on his left, flat and gray-green under a sky that was trying to decide whether to rain. The light was the diffused, directionless kind that early February produced in Oakland — no shadows, no warmth, the colors muted as if seen through a sheet of vellum. A painter's light, technically. The kind you used for color-matching because it didn't lie. It just didn't offer anything either.

So the next step wasn't doing damage. It was mapping. He needed to know who checked consensus scores, who ate a bad metric, what rolled uphill to Priya, what disappeared into software, and where the seam was between the people on the floor and the company that used them. Anything else would just break across the row.

His exit came up. He took it. The surface streets of Fruitvale were damp, the first scatter of evening rain darkening the asphalt in uneven patches. He parked in front of his building and sat in the car for a minute with the engine off, as he'd sat in the Canopy lot that morning before going in. The rain tapped on the Civic's roof. Through the windshield, the streetlight threw a sodium-orange wash over the wet pavement — warm and directionless, flattening everything underneath it to the same false color.

He went upstairs. The apartment smelled like linseed oil and turpentine, the particular mustiness of a space where someone painted. His canvases leaned against the far wall, the unsold work from the Temescal show, turned to face the plaster so he didn't have to look at them. He dropped his bag on the floor and stood in the middle of the room and listened to the nothing. No monitors humming. No mice clicking. No murmur of twenty-four people doing the same task in parallel. Just the drip of the kitchen faucet he kept meaning to fix, and the distant rattle of a BART train on the elevated track outside his window, heading somewhere he'd already been.
