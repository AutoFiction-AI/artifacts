# Chapter 9

Tommy was doing the CEO.

He had the posture — both hands gripping the edge of the table like he was steadying a lectern, chin lifted, the studied casualness of a man who had practiced looking unrehearsed. "I want to be real with you all," Tommy said, his voice pitched half an octave below his own. "The human layer isn't a cost center. The human layer is our competitive moat."

He paused the way the CEO paused, letting the nothing settle.

"And that's why, effective immediately, we're asking each of you to rate thirty percent more images per shift. Because we value you."

Dani laughed — the real one, the one that came through her nose and made her tilt her head back. Priya covered her mouth and shook her head, and it might have been disapproval but it wasn't.

"Do the part about synergy," Nick said.

"We don't say synergy anymore. That was, like, 2019." Tommy broke character to take a drink of his beer. "Now it's alignment. Everything's alignment. Are we aligned? Let's align on that. I'd love to get alignment from leadership before EOD." He leaned back in, the CEO's voice again, the chin. "And I'm excited — genuinely excited — to announce that we'll be onboarding three hundred new evaluators across our domestic operations. Because the human layer—"

"If he says human layer one more time," Priya said.

"The human layer," Tommy continued, "is the bedrock upon which this company's entire—"

"I voted for him to shut up," Bao said, from the pool table.

The bar was called Rosie's, or had been once — the O in the neon sign above the door had burned out, so the red glow read R SIE'S, and nobody who worked there showed any intention of fixing it. The place sat at the end of a strip mall a block from the business park, wedged between a nail salon and a passport photo shop. The tables had a film on them that never fully dried. The pool table's felt had a three-inch tear running from the second diamond to the side pocket, and everyone played around it without comment. There was a jukebox in the corner that accepted quarters and sometimes produced music, though the selection hadn't been updated since someone's idea of relevant was a Creed album.

The throughput week had been ugly. Fourteen-point-two images an hour — a hundred and fourteen in a full shift, the number Priya had re-announced Monday morning with the particular flat voice she used when delivering targets she disagreed with but had been told to enforce. By Wednesday the new hires were rating anatomically correct hands as errors and waving six-fingered disasters through without a second look. By Thursday, Priya had stopped leaving her desk for breaks. By Friday at four, when the weekly numbers came in and they'd hit target — barely, through experienced evaluators carrying the new hires' output and Nick himself running closer to sixteen images an hour than fourteen-point-two — Tommy had stood up from his station and said, "Rosie's. Now. I'm buying the first round, which means I'm buying one round, so choose wisely."

Wisely had turned out to mean two-dollar Tecates, which was what everyone was drinking except Priya, who'd ordered a glass of white wine, and Bao, who was working on a ginger ale with the same focused attention he brought to everything.

Now it was past seven. The bar had filled slightly — a few guys from the auto parts warehouse down the road, a couple in the corner booth who weren't looking at each other. The July light was still coming in through the front windows, flattened by the strip mall's overhang into a thin warm band that caught the bottles behind the bar and knocked everything down to the same amber register. The neon beer signs and the peeling vinyl booths and Priya's tired face all shared one palette, like a varnish. Nick liked the color of it, the way late sun through dirty glass worked as a kind of equalizer.

"Your shot," Tommy said.

Nick looked at the pool table and saw that whatever arrangement had existed before Bao's last turn was a ruin. His solids were clustered behind the eight ball like they were hiding. One was pressed flush against the far rail, essentially decorative. Bao's stripes were gone except for the thirteen, which sat an inch from the corner pocket in a position that communicated nothing but inevitability.

"How does he do that," Nick said. It wasn't inflected as a question.

"Geometry," Bao said. He was chalking his cue in small careful circles, reading glasses pushed up on his forehead. The motion was precise and unhurried — the same attention he brought to every image on his screen, every evaluation note he wrote in the rubric fields. His notes were always too detailed, margins full of observations nobody had asked for and nobody had read except that one time Priya printed a batch of them and tacked them to the break room wall under a handwritten sign that said THIS IS WHAT THOROUGHNESS LOOKS LIKE, which was the kindest and most futile thing she had ever done. The notes were still up there. Nobody's evaluation pace had changed.

Nick missed his shot. The cue ball drifted into the corner pocket with a defeated click. Bao placed it on the dot, positioned his cue, and sank the thirteen and then the eight in two unhurried strokes that barely made a sound. Tommy slow-clapped from the booth.

"Another game?" Bao asked.

"I don't want to be humiliated again."

"You weren't humiliated. You just weren't competitive."

Tommy pointed at Bao. "See, that's worse. That's objectively worse. Being humiliated at least implies I was in it."

Bao's mouth made a movement that, on a more demonstrative face, would have been a grin. He set the cue against the wall and racked the balls for whoever came next. Tommy went to the bar for another round. Dani slid over in the booth to make room for Nick, and he sat down and felt the whole week land in his body — the specific fatigue of looking at images for eight hours a day, the way his eyes had felt hot and tight by three o'clock every afternoon, the headache behind his right temple that he'd been managing since Wednesday with gas-station ibuprofen and too much coffee. He'd rated something like six hundred and thirty images that week. Maybe a few more. He'd stopped counting because counting made it worse.

"Luna put another horse in my bag this morning," Dani said. She touched the corner of a green-marker drawing sticking out of her tote. "She's on a run. Every sketchbook I buy her, every spare piece of paper in the apartment, horses." She was holding her Tecate with both hands, the way she held her coffee mug at her station, and her hair was down, which she didn't do on the floor because it got in the way of the monitors. It changed the shape of her face. Made her jawline visible, the line of her neck. Nick noticed this and then noticed himself noticing and looked at his beer.

"Still horses?"

"Only horses. But this one she drew from the front. Head-on, looking right at you. She's never done that. And the proportions are—" Dani smiled into her beer. "It looks nothing like a horse. The nostrils are these two huge circles and the head's wider than the body and the legs are basically coming out of the neck. But the eyes." She shook her head. "She got the eyes. There's something in them that's real. This watchfulness, like the horse knows you're there."

"That's the hard part," Nick said. "Eyes."

"She's seven. She doesn't know it's the hard part. She just drew them."

Nick thought about that. He thought about being seven and drawing whatever the thing had been — dogs, probably, or spaceships — before he'd learned what the hard parts were and started organizing his whole approach around solving them. Before the solving had become the point and the not-knowing had become something he wanted back and couldn't get to anymore. He didn't say any of this.

"You should frame it," he said.

"I frame all of them. She's going to come home from her dad's one day and realize her entire bedroom wall is horse drawings and she's going to be so mortified."

"That's like the archive of a serious body of work, though."

Dani smiled. "I tell her that. I tell her she's building a portfolio." She took a drink. "She asked me what a portfolio was. I said it's where you keep everything you've made so people can see it. And she said, 'Even the bad ones?' I said, 'Especially the bad ones.'"

Tommy came back with beers and set them down, foam sliding over the rims. He dropped into the booth across from them and pulled out his phone, looked at it, and put it face-down on the table with the decisiveness of a resolution.

"I'm not looking at images for the rest of the night," he said. "No screens. My eyes are doing this thing where I close them and I still see the rating interface. Like a ghost. I can see the Next button on the inside of my eyelids."

"That's real," Nick said. "Screen persistence. It's a thing."

"It's not a thing, it's my life. I'm twenty-two and I've got occupational eye damage from looking at bad art."

"Some of it's good," Bao said, settling into the booth with his ginger ale. The pool table behind him stood empty, the rack of balls waiting for its next victims. "Some of the images are quite good."

"Name one."

Bao considered this with the seriousness the question probably didn't warrant. "There was one last Thursday. A landscape, rolling hills, late-afternoon light. Very warm palette. The sky had this quality—" He paused, looking for the word, and Nick knew he would find it because Bao always found it. "Suffusion. The light wasn't on the sky, it was through it. Coming from inside. That one was good."

"You remember one image from Thursday," Tommy said. "Out of — how many did you rate Thursday?"

"One hundred and three."

"And you remember one."

"I remember several. That was the one I liked."

"Bao. Brother. You're too good for this job."

"Most people are too good for most jobs," Bao said, and finished his ginger ale.

Nick went to the bathroom and when he came back, Dani was racking balls and Tommy was standing beside the table holding his cue like a man who'd been handed a weapon he didn't trust. Dani broke clean — the crack of it sharp enough to make the bartender glance up — and sank the eleven on the break. She moved around the table the same way she moved around the evaluation floor, deliberate and unhurried, and she banked her second shot off the side rail with a flick of her wrist that put the cue ball exactly where she wanted it for her third. She'd done this before. She'd spent time in bars that were probably worse than this one.

"Where'd you learn to play like that?" Tommy asked, watching another of his solids fail to materialize a clear shot.

"Where'd you not?" Dani said, and sank the fourteen.

Priya had been sitting at the end of the booth with her wine, and at some point during the last hour her shoulders had dropped from where they usually lived, which was up near her ears, and the wine had put some color in her face. She'd been listening to Tommy's impression and Dani's horse story and Bao's landscape with the expression of someone watching a show she hadn't realized she'd been missing.

"I had a student once," Priya said. The table went slightly quieter, the way it did when Priya talked about teaching, which she almost never did. "A junior. David Herrera. He was in my AP Art class, which I taught for — God, six years? Seven? And David was not a natural. He worked hard, he drew technically, but his stuff always looked like homework. Like an assignment someone had completed well. You know what I mean?"

"Sure," Nick said.

"And then at the end of the year, the final portfolio review, he turns in a self-portrait that's—" She turned her wineglass slowly on the table. "It was ugly. It was objectively one of the ugliest drawings I'd ever seen. He drew his own face and he didn't spare anything. His acne. The way one of his eyes sat lower than the other. His ears, which he hated — I knew he hated them because he'd spent the whole year drawing himself in profile so he'd only have to deal with one. And in this drawing he put them both in, front-facing, sticking out, exactly as they were. And it was wrong by every technical standard I'd spent the year teaching him. Every proportion rule, every shading technique. And it was the most honest piece of art that passed through my classroom in six years."

She took a sip of wine.

"It made me cry. Right there in the review. In front of the whole class. Which was — God, it was embarrassing. But I looked at it and I knew. He wasn't trying to get an A or demonstrate a technique. He drew the thing in front of him. He actually saw himself."

"What happened to him?" Dani asked.

"He graduated. I left the year after." Priya set her glass down. "I've Googled him. Nothing comes up. He's probably doing something completely unrelated."

"Or he's here," Tommy said. "Rating images for twenty bucks an hour."

Priya laughed. "Maybe."

Nick went to the bar for another round. The bartender was a woman in her fifties with a faded blue rose tattoo on her forearm and arms that suggested she'd done work heavier than this before she'd done this. He ordered three Tecates, a ginger ale, and a white wine, and she lined them up without commentary.

While she poured, he looked back at the booth. Tommy was talking with both hands now, making a point nobody needed. Dani had sunk into the cracked vinyl with one shoulder up on the backrest. Bao sat with his hands folded beside his empty glass, waiting for the talk to circle back to him. Priya's smile stayed small and careful, but it was there. The light from the windows had shifted to that last thing the sun did in July, almost orange now, catching dust in the air and glazing the table edges. It flattened the beer bottles and the torn booth and the tired planes of Priya's face into one warm register. Nick found himself tracking where everyone sat in relation to everyone else, the weight and spacing of them, the way Tommy pitched forward while Dani leaned back and Bao held the center by barely moving. The bartender set the drinks in front of him. He picked them up before he could keep staring.

He carried the drinks back.

The evening kept moving. Tommy lost to Bao again, then to Dani. The jukebox ate a quarter and gave nothing back. At some point Tommy admitted he'd been feeding actual pasta into his garbage disposal for six months, which made Dani put her beer down and say, "Tommy, no," in a tone so flat Priya nearly spilled her wine laughing.

By nine the bar was darker, the sun gone behind whatever was west of the strip mall — a self-storage facility, probably, or a church parking lot — and the overhead fluorescents had come on, which made everything look worse and more honest. The tables were stickier under the harsher light. The tear in the pool table's felt ran longer than Nick had thought. The warmth from the windows was gone and the air conditioning was doing its flat, competent work, and Nick could smell the bar now — spilled beer and cleaning solution and the sweet chemical note of the jukebox's broken wiring or whatever it was that made the corner near it smell faintly of burnt plastic.

Tommy was telling a story about an image he'd rated that week — an AI-generated portrait of a woman where the model had given her three hands, the third one emerging from behind her back at an angle that suggested a very specific and unlikely mutation. He'd sat there for thirty seconds, he said, trying to decide if it was an error or a stylistic choice, because the overall composition was genuinely beautiful, the lighting was coherent, the face was close to right, and then there was this third hand calmly existing in the lower-right corner like someone had walked into the frame.

"I rated it a three," Tommy said.

"That's too high," Nick said.

"It's a three. The hand's wrong but everything else—"

"Anatomical accuracy is a whole category. That's a one on anatomy, maybe a two."

"OK, but if the lighting's right and the composition's right—"

"The lighting doesn't fix the hand."

Priya stopped with her keys halfway out of her purse. "This is what I spent all week doing. Building retraining decks out of trusted calls because it's the only way to keep the new hires from free-associating, and then one pretty face gets a generous score and suddenly I'm in the conference room explaining to twelve people why shoulders do not come out of ribs."

Tommy held up both hands. "I didn't put my three in a slideshow."

"I'm not blaming you specifically." She pushed hair behind one ear. "I'm saying one bad call doesn't stay one bad call in this place. It turns into somebody else's example. Then somebody else's baseline. Then I get an email asking why San Leandro suddenly thinks hands can grow out of the air."

Bao said, "If the reference is wrong, speed only spreads it."

"Exactly," Priya said.

"And who gets that email?" Dani asked.

Priya laughed once, without humor. "Me first. Then whoever's name is on the batch. Never the person who said fourteen-point-two in a meeting."

Nick watched condensation slide down his bottle. Priya was talking about the part of the system the floor could see. He knew where the trusted calls went after that. A score stopped belonging to the person who clicked it. It became reference. Then it moved through everybody else.

Priya set her keys on the table and picked them back up, like even that small motion needed a second try.

"I should go," she said. "I've got a thing in the morning."

"What thing?" Tommy asked.

"A life thing. A thing that doesn't involve this bar."

She hugged Dani, squeezed Nick's shoulder, and told Tommy that his CEO impression was getting dangerously accurate and that she might have to report him, which was a joke but one that contained enough real caution that Tommy's smile flickered for a second before resettling. She left through the front door and the warm night air pushed in for a moment before it closed.

Bao left shortly after. He folded a five-dollar bill and placed it under his empty ginger ale glass with the exactness of a man making a hospital corner on a bedsheet. He shook Tommy's hand and then Nick's, nodded to Dani, and walked out into the parking lot with his measured stride. He would take the bus to San Leandro BART, then the train to Hayward, then walk twenty minutes to the house where Mai was asleep or watching a television she didn't follow anymore. He would check the garden and lock the doors and sit with her until whatever time remained in the evening was gone. Nick knew this because Bao had told him once, during a night shift, in the same even, factual tone he used for everything — color values, image flaws, the schedule of his wife's decline. Here is how the light falls.

Tommy lasted another twenty minutes, during which he beat Nick at pool for the first time all evening — "Finally. Finally finally finally. I beat someone at something in this building" — and then announced he was going home to not look at screens, a commitment they all knew would last until he reached his car, where his phone was charging.

"You good?" he asked Nick and Dani.

"We're good," Dani said.

He left. The bar was nearly empty. The bartender was wiping down the counter. The couple in the corner had started talking to each other in low voices. A man at the far end of the bar who'd been there when they arrived was still there, working on something amber in a rocks glass, and he would presumably still be there when they left.

"I should probably head out," Dani said.

"Yeah. Me too."

They paid their tab. The bartender counted change without comment. Nick held the door open and Dani walked through it, and the warm night hit them both — that East Bay July heat that stayed in the ground after dark, radiating up through the parking lot asphalt, the air smelling like car exhaust and something dry and grassy from the golden hills east of the freeway. All that stored sunlight releasing now that the sun was gone. The light from the strip mall's signs — the nail salon, the passport photo place, R SIE'S — laid overlapping colors across the lot that would have been ugly in daylight but which the dark turned into something close to interesting.

Dani's car was across the lot, a gray Honda CR-V with a car seat in the back and one of Luna's drawings taped to the inside of the rear window. A horse, rendered in green marker. Even from forty feet away, in the parking lot dark with the neon behind them, Nick could see that the horse's proportions were wrong and its body was alive.

They walked. It wasn't far. Maybe sixty feet from the bar's front door to where she'd parked, but the night was warm and neither of them was walking fast.

"Good week," Dani said.

"Was it?"

"I mean we survived it. That's what good means now."

"Low bar."

"I'm from a long line of low-bar people. We survive things and call it a win."

They reached her car. She dug in her bag for her keys, found them, held them in her fist without pressing the button. The strip mall's lights hummed. A car passed on the road behind them, headlights sweeping across the lot and away.

For a second he thought about pointing at the horse in the rear window, because Luna's drawings were always safe ground and the horse was right there, green marker and bad proportions and the eyes somehow still alert. He could have said that and kept the scene where it belonged, two coworkers in a parking lot talking about somebody else's kid. He let the chance pass.

"Thanks for coming out tonight," she said.

"I always come out."

"I know. I'm just — yeah."

Nick felt the answer catch and die in his throat before it was even language. He worried the X-Acto scar on his right index finger with his thumb through his pocket, the old habit when he needed to keep still. The lot hummed around them. He had the stupid clear sense that if he said the wrong thing now the whole night would tilt toward it.

They stopped beside the driver's door. Dani's keys rested in her palm. Nick's hands stayed in his pockets. The red glow from the neon sign cut across her mouth, and a strip of blue from the nail salon caught in her hair. She was looking at him.

He looked back longer than he meant to. Long enough to see her swallow. Long enough that the hum of the strip mall dropped behind the blood in his ears. Heat came up through the soles of his shoes. He could see the place where the red light stopped and her skin began.

Dani held his eyes for one more beat, then thumbed the key fob. The Honda chirped. She took half a step back and pulled the driver's door open.

"OK," she said.

"Goodnight," Nick said.

"Night."

She got in. The engine started. She backed out and pulled toward the lot's exit. He watched the taillights turn left toward the freeway on-ramp and disappear.

He walked to his Civic. The car was an oven, baked all day in the July sun, and he rolled the windows down before sitting behind the wheel. Rosie's red neon smeared across the windshield. His key turned. As he backed out of the lot, the night came back in pieces: Bao tucking the five under his glass with two fingers, Priya saying one bad call didn't stay one bad call in a place like this, Tommy's voice going high when he finally won, Dani's green-marker horse taped crooked in the rear window. He drove home with the windows down, and for the length of the drive, that was enough.
