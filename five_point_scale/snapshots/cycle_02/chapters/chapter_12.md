# Chapter 12

On a Tuesday in November, a new batch populated Nick's sidebar at 9:17. The pod-machine coffee at his elbow tasted like hot water that had recently been in the same room as a coffee bean. He'd been evaluating a texture anomaly in a streetscape rendering, trying to decide whether the cobblestone pattern was a model failure or a deliberate stylistic choice, when the queue blinked live beside it. There were forty-two thumbnails. It was a standard calibration batch. Each one was postage-stamp small, waiting to be opened, scored, anchored, and returned to the pipeline.

He clicked the first. It was a still life, a fruit bowl with dramatic side-lighting, clearly AI-generated. The apple skin had that smoothness, the highlights too uniform, like someone had described an apple to a person who'd only ever seen photographs of them. He scored it across the standard fields. Prompt adherence, anatomical accuracy, lighting consistency, compositional balance, stylistic coherence. The floor evaluators had given it a spread: two 3s, a 4, and a 2 from someone who'd objected to the shadow direction. Nick agreed about the shadows but not the severity. He typed his note. He clicked Next.

The second image was a landscape.

He didn't recognize it for three seconds. Maybe four. The thumbnail had been cropped to a square and the monitor's color profile shifted the palette. The warm ochre of the underpainting read as flat tan, and the viridian he'd mixed from phthalo green and cadmium yellow looked like plain green, default-picker green, not the green you spent twenty minutes mixing on a palette while the linseed oil started to tack. But the composition was his. The ridge line breaking the horizon at the left third. The eucalyptus he'd placed there because the actual eucalyptus was there, on the hillside above the Caldecott Tunnel, and he'd stood on the fire trail four different mornings trying to get the way the tree interrupted the slope without competing with the sky.

His hand came off the mouse.

The painting was from the Temescal show. He'd made it in the spring of 2022, working from photographs and plein air studies. The East Bay hills were easy to see and hard to paint because everyone had already painted them and what they expected was a postcard, and the hills were not a postcard. They were a thing that had to do with the way fog altered the light at different hours, turning the same ridge gold or gray or a green that existed nowhere in any tube. He'd layered the ochre ground and built the greens in translucent glazes rather than mixing them flat on the palette, and he'd worked the sky in one afternoon when the conditions matched his reference because the light was the light and you couldn't invent it afterward.

Here it was. Image 11-07-2023-CAL-0894-007. Seven hundred and twelve pixels wide. No title, no artist's name, no gallery. Just a rectangle in the evaluation portal between the AI fruit bowl and what looked like a machine's attempt at a Rothko.

He scrolled the sidebar thumbnails. More AI outputs, an architectural rendering with converging verticals that didn't converge, a portrait with a hand that had six fingers and a thumb. Then another painting he recognized: the Lake Merritt view from the amphitheater, the smaller canvas he'd never been happy with but had included in the show because he'd needed to fill ten linear feet of gallery wall and had only had eight canvases and one of those wasn't ready. And another: the eucalyptus study he'd done as a warmup and overworked, scrubbing at the bark texture until it looked labored. And another, the sunset over the Estuary that he'd painted from his apartment window, the one where the BART elevated track cut across the lower third and he'd left it in because the track was real and pretending it wasn't there would've been a lie. And the pair of small studies he'd done of the container cranes at the Port of Oakland, graphite and wash on toned ground, which Sarah had displayed together under glass and which someone at the opening had said looked like insects and which had stuck with him because the cranes did look like insects, enormous mantises frozen in the act of feeding, and he'd never been able to decide whether he'd painted that or whether the man at the opening had seen it for him.

Seven of his paintings. Seven out of forty-two.

They'd been scraped from the Temescal gallery's website. The only explanation. Sarah kept the show archives online because she kept everything online, thumbnail images with dimensions and prices listed underneath in the slightly too-small sans-serif that Nick had once told her was hard to read and that she'd kept anyway. The prices were still in his memory: the Oakland hills piece at $2,400, which Jess had said was too low and which hadn't mattered, because nobody bought it. Nobody bought any of them. He'd struck the show at the end of the month and carried the canvases to his car in two trips and driven them home and leaned them against the wall of his apartment where they still leaned, fourteen months later, while their digital versions circulated through pipelines to teach machines how to make more pictures.

Nick minimized the evaluation portal and opened the batch distribution dashboard, the calibration-team view that tracked floor coverage and anchor timing. Calibration batches ran floor-first. Six evaluators scored them cold; only after the spread populated did he lock the anchor ratings that became ground truth. Floor notes lived in a collapsible pane under each image. Priya could read them. Any calibration analyst could. Ops couldn't see the note text from their dashboard, only the orange hold and the timer bleeding out, unless somebody on calibration kicked the note into source audit. If Nick anchored past a floor warning without escalating it, the warning stayed in batch history and stopped mattering to the pipeline. If a batch looked contaminated with source material, he could put it on reference check and buy ninety minutes before the automated stall pings started climbing the chain. He clicked the hold and watched his employee ID appear beside it in orange. Then he scanned the assignment column.

Tommy Reyes had image 0894-012. The Lake Merritt canvas.

Bao Nguyen had image 0894-007. The Oakland hills.

He stood. His chair rolled back into the partition with a hollow plastic sound that nobody on the floor noticed, because nobody noticed anything that wasn't a system alert or a Slack notification. The calibration area was a glassed-in section at the end of the floor, "the fishbowl," management called it, separated from the regular evaluation stations by frosted panels. Nick pushed through the door into the main room.

Forty-some evaluators in their soft-walled cubicles, each facing dual monitors, each clicking through images at whatever pace they maintained. The hum of HVAC and the flat continuous murmur of keyboards and mouse clicks and, underneath that, the low-grade rhythm of people shifting in office chairs, reaching for water bottles, clearing throats. The beige carpet absorbed everything. It was a room designed to absorb everything. Nick walked toward the break room, keeping his pace even, the pace of a man going to get coffee because his cup was empty, which it was.

Tommy's station was the third from the end, near the entrance. His monitors were angled toward the aisle, a consequence of the cubicle layout and Tommy's habit of leaning back with his legs stretched under the desk. He was looking at his phone with his left hand and moving the mouse with his right. The image on the left monitor was the Lake Merritt canvas. Nick's Lake Merritt canvas. The amphitheater in the foreground, the late-afternoon light turning the water a color that looked gold in life and looked flat on screen, the joggers on the path that he'd included because he'd wanted the human scale and then regretted because they looked posed.

The rating fields were already filled in. Nick could read them from four feet away: prompt adherence 3, anatomical accuracy N/A, lighting consistency 3, compositional balance 3, stylistic coherence 2. Tommy had spent maybe fifteen seconds on it. Maybe less.

Tommy clicked Next. The painting vanished. A corporate lobby rendering replaced it, generated, with the standard AI problems: a door opening into solid wall, a floor reflection that defied the geometry above it. Tommy set his phone down and leaned forward, because the lobby image had the kind of errors that required actual evaluation. He started typing a note.

"Hey," Tommy said as Nick passed, not looking up. "Priya say anything about the holiday schedule?"

"Didn't check."

"They're supposed to post it Wednesday. I gotta swap my Thursday." He was still typing, still looking at the lobby rendering. His voice had the same tone it had when he talked about anything, lunch, the weather, his commute, the images in his queue. The painting had been work. He'd done it and moved on.

Nick kept walking. In the break room, the pod machine hissed and produced its thin cup. He stood by the doorway and drank and looked down the far aisle toward Bao's station by the window.

Bao was wearing his reading glasses. Both hands were on the desk, neither on the keyboard or mouse. He was looking at the Oakland hills landscape. His head was tilted slightly to the left, the thing he did when he was studying composition rather than scanning for errors, the same tilt Nick had seen when Bao found a generated image that had accidentally produced something worth looking at, some convergence of form and color the model had stumbled into without knowing what it had done. When Bao tilted his head like that, he was seeing something the rubric didn't have a field for.

The rating fields on Bao's screen were empty. Nick watched from the break room doorway for what had to be twenty seconds, maybe longer. Twenty seconds was twenty seconds more than the throughput target allocated for a single image, twenty seconds that would show up on the productivity dashboard as dead time, the same slowness that had gotten Bao flagged three times already. Bao's hand went to the mouse. His other hand reached for the keyboard, and he began typing into the notes field with the same unhurried precision he brought to everything, his evaluations, his conversations, the way he buttoned his shirt collar before leaving for the day. He typed more than most evaluators wrote in a shift.

Nick rinsed his cup in the break room sink. The water was cold and the drain made a sucking sound and the light through the window above the sink was November light, thin, without warmth. He dried his hands on a paper towel that tore when he pulled it from the dispenser. He threw the torn halves away and walked back to his station.

On the calibration dashboard, ratings were populating. Tommy's scores for 0894-012 came first: 3, N/A, 3, 3, 2. The 2 for stylistic coherence. Nick looked at the number on the screen. It was a defensible rating. The Lake Merritt piece had an inconsistency between the foreground treatment and the sky, a difference in handling that a careful evaluator would flag as incoherent, which Tommy, for all his speed, was capable of noticing when his phone was out of reach. Tommy wasn't wrong. The number captured something true about the painting and killed the painting in the same motion. Nick pressed his thumb into the desk edge until the plastic seam dented his skin.

Other evaluators' scores trickled in. Two of his paintings had been assigned to a woman named Grace who'd started three weeks ago and who rated everything between 3 and 4 because she hadn't yet developed the confidence to use the full scale. Another went to a guy named Dev whose ratings were competent and fast and unremarkable. His paintings went by under the same gray progress bar as the fruit bowls and lobby renderings: a few seconds of attention, a row of numbers, a click, the thumbnail dimming as the queue advanced.

Bao's scores came in eleven minutes after Tommy's, which meant Bao had spent eleven minutes on images most evaluators cleared in under three. Image 0894-007: prompt adherence 4, anatomical accuracy N/A, lighting consistency 5, compositional balance 4, stylistic coherence 4. And a note: "Painterly quality in hillside treatment suggests reference image rather than generation. Light handling consistent with single source. Transition from warm foreground to cooler distance reads as intentional. Recommend reclassification if this is reference material." The note sat in the floor-comments pane with the small gray Escalate link beside it, still live.

Bao had clocked it. Not as Nick's, there was no way to know that, but as human work instead of machine output. He'd written the word that stayed: intentional. He'd seen a person in the image. Someone behind the pixels, making decisions.

Nick sat at his station with both hands flat on the desk and the dashboard open in front of him. The fluorescent tubes hummed overhead. Someone's Slack notification chimed twice down the aisle. The HVAC cut on and pushed air that smelled like the inside of the duct, metallic and stale. November light through the parking-lot windows was the color of nothing, gray that wasn't a value study, gray that was just absence.

He had made those paintings in a room that smelled like turpentine. With brushes that cost eleven dollars each. He had stood in front of canvases and made choices, this green and not that green, this stroke and not that one, and the paintings had traveled from his studio to the gallery wall to the gallery website to a scraper to the pipeline to the evaluation portal. At each step they'd shed something: the frame, the texture of the paint surface, the signature, the title, the price, his name. On the dashboard they sat in a clean column of filenames beside the anchor timer, each one waiting for a number.

Someone said something to him on the way to lunch. Priya, maybe. Or one of the other calibration analysts. Nick answered. He didn't remember what he said or what was said to him.

He ate a sandwich at his desk from the vending machine in the hallway, the kind with the bread that was soft in a way that wasn't related to freshness. Through the fishbowl glass he could see the floor, people getting up to stretch, checking phones, filing toward the break room. At 12:30 he went to refill his coffee and heard Grace talking to Dev at the microwave, waiting for something to heat.

"Did you get the batch with the real paintings in it?"

"Which ones?"

"Like, oil paintings. Landscapes, I think. It was kind of a nice break, actually. You can tell they're different from the generated stuff."

"I didn't look that hard," Dev said. "I just rated them."

"Well, yeah. But they were pretty. The one with the hills."

Nick poured his coffee. The machine gurgled. Grace was twenty-four and had been on the floor three weeks and she was right. The hills painting was pretty, and that was a word Nick would have bristled at once because pretty was what people said about work that was competent but didn't do anything to you, and he'd spent his whole practice trying to make work that did something to you, and now pretty was probably the most accurate thing anyone had said about his painting all day. Pretty. Three out of five. Next.

His Slack chimed on the walk back from the break room. Priya: "0894 still on reference check?"

Nick stopped by the fishbowl door and typed with his coffee in one hand. "Mixed batch. Wanted the floor spread first."

He did not mention Bao's note. The minute he mentioned it, Priya could open the image comments, see the reclassification recommendation, and tell him to route 0894 to source audit. Then the hold would stop being a delay and become an exception she had to answer for. Ops would freeze the batch until somebody decided whether the paintings were contaminated training data or reference material that never should've been there in the first place. His employee ID was already on the hold. If he anchored the batch instead of escalating, it would be on the release too.

The typing bubble appeared, disappeared, then came back. "Okay. Need your anchors in before 2. Ops already asked."

He sent a thumbs-up and went back to his station.

***

The calibration queue wouldn't wait forever. Reference-check holds bought ninety minutes and a little managerial grace, not a whole afternoon. At 1:07 the batch timer on Nick's dashboard turned orange. At 1:14 Slack chimed again.

Priya: "You good on 0894?"

"Yeah. Starting now," he wrote back.

He let six more minutes pass anyway. At 1:20 he opened the calibration interface and clicked the first image in the anchor queue.

Image 0894-003 loaded. It was one of the fruit bowls, AI-generated, routine, nothing. He scored it. Prompt adherence 3, lighting consistency 3, compositional balance 2, stylistic coherence 3. He clicked Next. Another AI output loaded. He scored it. He clicked Next.

Image 0894-007 filled the left monitor. He'd been waiting for it without admitting he was waiting for it, clicking through the preceding images on something like autopilot, scoring them adequately, not well, just enough to pass the QA threshold.

The Oakland hills landscape filled the monitor at full resolution, larger than the thumbnail, closer to the original but not the original, never the original. The ochre ground was visible now where it showed through the thinner glazes on the left side. The viridian had some of its depth back, though the cadmium warmth was still flattened by the monitor's backlight into something duller and cooler than what he'd mixed. The eucalyptus at the horizon break was doing what he'd wanted it to do: creating a pause between the slope and the sky, a moment where the eye had to rest before continuing upward.

It was a good painting. It wasn't his best. The brushwork in the foreground was cautious in a way that reflected the three weeks he'd spent on the underpainting before he trusted himself to commit to the surface. It wasn't his worst either. It was a painting by a person who could see and who'd put in the work that seeing required, who'd stood on a hillside enough mornings that the postcard version had dissolved and something harder to name had taken its place.

The rating form waited on the right monitor. Five categories, five scales, a notes field. One tab over, Bao's comment still sat under the image with Escalate live. If Nick clicked it, 0894 would leave his queue and go to source audit with Priya copied on the exception. If he locked anchors instead, Bao's recommendation would sit under RESOLVED - ANCHORED in the batch history, readable later and useless now. His cursor sat in the first box.

A 5 would flag. The calibration system's anomaly detection was tuned to catch outliers, ratings that deviated more than one standard deviation from the evaluator consensus. The floor had given the painting a range in the low-3 to mid-4 territory, with Bao's lighting score of 5 as the high mark. A calibration anchor of 5 across the board would trigger a review, and the review would lead to a conversation, and the conversation would lead to someone pulling the image and looking at it and asking why the calibration analyst had graded a landscape as flawless when the floor consensus was mixed.

He couldn't give it a 5.

A 3 wasn't right either. Not because a 3 was indefensible, you could argue 3 for compositional balance if you squinted at the foreground treatment, but because he knew with the specific certainty of the person who'd made the painting that a 3 was wrong for the things that mattered. The lighting was right. He'd driven to the fire trail four times to watch how the fog changed the color temperature of the hillside across different hours, and he'd matched the light on the canvas with a care that was invisible to anyone who hadn't been there and legible to anyone who had. Bao had given the lighting a 5. Bao was right. But Bao could give it a 5 because Bao was a floor evaluator and a single outlier score from a floor evaluator didn't trigger anything. A 5 from a calibration anchor weighed differently.

Nick typed 4 into the prompt-adherence field. The painting wasn't responding to a prompt. It wasn't a generation, it was a painting. But prompt adherence was the first field and the system required a number in every field, and 4 was the number he was going to type in almost every field because 4 was what the painting was. Four out of five. He typed 4 for compositional balance. Four for stylistic coherence, which was generous if you were strict about the foreground and fair if you weren't. Five for lighting consistency, because Bao had given it a 5 and Nick was not going to pretend the lighting was anything less than what it was, and a single 5 in one subcategory wouldn't flag when the overall score was lower. Four for the overall anchor.

He sat with the number.

Four. His painting. The hills above the Caldecott Tunnel as he'd seen them and made them and signed and framed and hung on a wall in a gallery where Sarah had poured cheap wine into plastic cups at the opening and seven people had come. He'd given it a four. He looked once more at the thumbnail preview, at the foreground tightening up where he'd lost his nerve, and left it there.

The cursor sat on the Next button. He moved the mouse and clicked it and the painting disappeared and a portrait with a fused hand replaced it and he scored the portrait, anatomical accuracy 1, the rest in the 2-to-3 range, and clicked Next.

The eucalyptus study came up six images later. He gave it a 3 because it deserved a 3. He'd overworked it. The bark texture looked labored and the composition was a warmup exercise that should've stayed a warmup exercise. Three. Next.

The Estuary sunset came up next. He looked at the BART track bisecting the lower third and remembered the afternoon he'd painted it, February, gray sky, the track a dark line against the water, and he'd left the window open because the turpentine smell was giving him a headache and the cold had made his hands slow, which had actually been good for the painting because the brushwork was looser than he usually allowed. The floor evaluators had given it a spread of 3s and 4s. He gave it a 4. It was one of the better things he'd done that year, not that the year had been a high point. Four. Next.

The Lake Merritt canvas came up four images after that. He looked at it at full resolution, the amphitheater, the water, the joggers he'd always known were a mistake, and he thought about Tommy's 2 for stylistic coherence and decided Tommy was right, the coherence problem was structural, and he gave it a 3 overall. Three. Next.

By 2:40 the seven scores sat in the system: 4, 3, 3, 4, 3, 3, 4. He opened the hold menu, changed 0894 from REFERENCE CHECK to RESOLVED - ANCHORED, and watched Bao's reclassification note collapse into the batch history drawer under the timestamp. It hadn't disappeared. Priya could still open it if she went looking. Marcus could too, if the batch ever got audited. His employee ID now sat on the hold, the anchors, and the release of seven human paintings back into the training stream. He sent Priya a one-line Slack that said "Anchors in," and finished the rest of the batch in the standard way, flagging errors and noting anomalies with the same attention he brought to everything in the queue, because the attention was what the job required and the attention was what he had.

He logged out at 4:15, fifteen minutes before his shift ended. He closed the dashboard. He rinsed his coffee cup in the break room sink and set it in the dish rack.

The parking lot was already dark. November pulled the light out early and the business park's sodium-vapor lamps turned everything the same flat amber, the cars, the strip of landscaping, the bus stop bench, Nick's Civic in the third row with its oxidizing paint. He sat in the driver's seat and put the key in the ignition and didn't turn it. The windshield was fogged at the edges. Through the clear center he could see the building he'd just left, the second-floor windows still lit, the night shift logging on.

He turned the key. The engine caught on the second try, a hesitation in the ignition that had been getting worse since September. He pulled out of the lot and onto Davis and merged onto 880 north toward Oakland. The fog was coming in off the Bay, low and flat, and the headlights of the oncoming traffic were soft in it, directionless. The hills to the east were somewhere behind the gray. He couldn't see them.

At home, he dropped his keys on the counter and stood in the dark for a minute before turning on the light. The apartment smelled the way it had smelled for months, less like turpentine, more like old carpet and the Chinese takeout container he should have thrown away yesterday. The canvases were against the wall where they'd always been. The Oakland hills landscape was there, the real one, oil on canvas, thirty inches by forty inches, the paint surface thick with the texture that the screen couldn't transmit. He pulled it away from the wall and set it on the kitchen table under the overhead light.

Up close, the foreground still hesitated. Tommy had been right about that. The sky held. The eucalyptus still did its job. The painting had not improved or worsened in the drive home. It was the same painting, and now he couldn't look at it without seeing the fields beside it.

He went to the hall closet and brought out the milk crate with his paint box. The brushes in the jar had gone stiff at the ferrules. He unscrewed the tube of titanium white and found a chalky plug hard in the cap. He squeezed harder anyway. The tube split at the seam and pushed a dry rope the color of bathroom plaster onto his thumb. Under the sink the mineral spirits had gone thick and yellow in the bottle. He unscrewed that too. The smell had turned sweet and rotten. He dipped one of the stiff brushes into it and the bristles stayed splayed, glued apart.

He washed his thumb in the kitchen sink and dried it on the dish towel. Then he opened his banking app on the counter.

Checking: $318.44.

Rent in eleven days. PG&E on Monday. The Civic already needed whatever was making the ignition catch on the second try.

The cheap 24-by-36 canvases at the art store on Telegraph were $22.99 each because he'd looked in August and then closed the tab like a person making a responsible decision. Titanium white was $11.49. A pint of odorless mineral spirits was $14.95. Tax on top of that. Enough money to be stupid. Not enough money to be impossible.

He opened savings. The little bucket he'd renamed CAR in September, when the engine started hesitating, had $141.08 in it. He transferred eighty dollars into checking and watched the wheel turn until the confirmation banner slid across the screen.

On the back of the takeout receipt he wrote: 2 canvases. White. Spirits. Telegraph. Tomorrow. He slid the receipt under his keys, beside the split tube and the yellow bottle.

He heated the leftover takeout in the microwave and ate it standing at the counter. When he went to bed, the painting was still on the table, and by the door the note for Telegraph stayed under his keys, waiting for morning.
