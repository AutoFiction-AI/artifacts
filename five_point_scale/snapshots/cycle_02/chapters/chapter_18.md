# Chapter 18

When HR came back around about the workstation, Bao's desk was already mostly cleared — he'd done it himself that morning, stacking his things into the cardboard box with the same deliberate care he brought to everything. Dani had volunteered to finish clearing it out, Nick had said he'd help, and now they were here in the mid-afternoon quiet of the evaluation floor, pulling open drawers that Bao had already been through.

The top drawer held a phone charger that wasn't Bao's. It had been there since before he started, one of those universal artifacts of office furniture that belonged to no one and would outlast everyone. Dani set it on the desk. The second drawer was empty except for a handful of paper clips and a rubber band shaped like a figure eight. The third drawer was deeper, designed for hanging files, and when Nick pulled it open he found a stack of loose pages, handwritten, that Bao had either forgotten or decided didn't belong to him anymore.

Nick picked up the top sheet. Bao's handwriting was small and precise — a graphic designer's lettering, each character formed with the same attention he'd given to his evaluations. The note was about a training image, or had started that way. At the top he'd written the batch number and a description: *Garden scene, residential, midday sun, generated.* Below that, where the rubric asked for a rating and perhaps a brief comment, Bao had written three dense paragraphs about the quality of green in the foliage — how the model had defaulted to a single hue across every leaf when even a backyard garden in July held fifteen or twenty distinguishable greens, and how the shadow beneath the rosebush was blue-gray when it should have been a warm violet because the reflected light from the soil would tint it, and how the picket fence in the background was rendering as flat matte when any real painted wood in direct sun would show the grain and at least two values where the brush strokes caught the light differently.

The note ended with a single number circled at the bottom: 3.

Nick turned to the next page. Another batch number, another image. This one described a portrait — *Woman, mid-thirties, indoor lighting, profile.* Bao's notes ran to the bottom of the page and continued on the back. He had written about the color of the woman's skin in shadow, how the model had grayed it out when real skin in shadow went warm — ochre or amber depending on the undertone — and how the ear, translucent at the portrait's angle against a window, should have shown the red of blood under cartilage, like a held-up hand against a strong lamp. He had written about the particular flatness of the generated hair, each strand identical in value, and the absence of the stray flyaway hairs that real indoor lighting caught and turned into small bright filaments against a dark background. At the bottom, another circled number: 3.

"What are those?" Dani had stopped pulling paper clips out of the carpet where they'd spilled and was looking at the pages in Nick's hands.

"His evaluation notes."

She came over and he handed her the top sheet. She read it standing, her weight on one foot, in the tired posture he knew from long shifts. He watched her eyes move across Bao's handwriting.

"He wrote these for every image?"

"Looks like it. There's maybe forty pages in here."

Dani read the paragraph about the greens. Her mouth pulled tight into a small hard knot, the corners drawn in as if she were holding something behind her teeth. She set the page down on the desk, carefully, aligning it with the edge.

"He didn't have to do that," she said. "None of this goes in the system. The comment field takes — what, two sentences? Three?"

"Something like that."

"And he wrote all this. Every time. For nobody."

"For himself, I think."

Dani picked up the second page, the one about the portrait, and read it with the same focused attention she brought to her own evaluations. The spring light from the window behind her caught the fine hairs along her temple, and Nick noticed it automatically, before the thought formed, before he could stop the part of his brain that had been scoring ambient illumination for fourteen months. The light was consistent. Single source, indirect, high color temperature. He registered the assessment and let it pass through him like weather.

"This is better than anything the rubric asks for," Dani said. "This is what the work should actually look like."

Nick didn't answer. He was looking at the stack — page after page of Bao's observations, each one a record of a man who had seen more than the system wanted from him and written it down anyway, knowing that no one would read it, knowing that his per-image time was already flagged, knowing that this precision was the thing that would cost him his job and doing it anyway because the alternative was seeing less than he could see. Bao's handwriting on the pages was steady and unhurried, the pen strokes of someone who did not adjust his pace for anyone's metrics.

The confession was in Nick's hands with the pages. It started below language, like the nausea on the shoulder of 880 the night he finished, a pressure climbing behind his sternum and into the base of his throat, as if his body were trying to force the words out ahead of him. He could tell her. Dani was standing three feet away, holding Bao's notes with both hands, her face doing the thing it did when she was keeping herself together for reasons that were professional and economic and legal and entirely beyond her control, and he could open his mouth and say *I did this. The contamination they're looking for — it was me. The audit, the firing, all of it*.

"I did," he said, and stopped there.

Dani looked up from the page.

The rest of it hit the back of his teeth and broke apart there — *this, it, the audit, Bao* — every version worse once it existed in air.

"I should've said something sooner," he said.

Dani looked at him and kept looking, steady and level, longer than the quick bad-day glance people traded on the floor.

"If you're trying to say something else, say it."

The fluorescent hum sat between them. On the far side of the floor somebody laughed at a monitor, then stopped. Nick felt the first two words of the sentence gather and with them the rest of it — HR, investigation, the calibration team torn open, Dani in some conference room with her work badge clipped to the blouse she wore to court. He looked past her at Bao's bare desk.

"He shouldn't have lost this job," he said.

"OK," Dani said.

She took the rest of the notes from his hands, slid them into her tote under the folder with Luna's horse drawings, and pushed the bag strap higher on her shoulder.

"Then don't start."

She moved to the other side of the workstation and pulled the monitor toward her by its base. "I've got the drawers. Can you wipe the monitor?"

They finished clearing the station in near-silence, wiping down the desk with the disinfectant spray HR had left, pushing the chair in, adjusting the monitor so it faced straight forward for whoever would sit there next. The new hires on the floor — kids, mostly, hired during the latest expansion to fill the seats the audit had emptied — clicked through their batches at a pace Bao would have found indecent. One of them, a guy in a baseball cap who couldn't have been older than twenty, was rating an image every nine seconds. Nick could see the screen from where he stood. The guy wasn't even scrolling down to check the lower quadrants.

Dani saw it too. She didn't say anything. She picked up the disinfectant spray and the roll of paper towels and walked them back to the supply closet without looking at the kid's screen or at Nick.

Priya's office was a glass-walled room at the end of the evaluation floor, designed to communicate transparency while providing none. The blinds were half-drawn. Through the slats, Nick could see the floor's remaining stations — thirty-odd, twelve of them empty since the restructuring, the rest occupied by the mix of survivors and new arrivals that defined the current team. The HVAC hummed, a steady frequency he'd stopped hearing months ago and now heard again.

She sat behind her desk with a coffee mug cupped in both hands, steam curling off the surface while it cooled untouched. Her eyes were raw at the rims, pink threaded through the whites, the look of too many mornings that had started before the alarm because her brain wouldn't stay down. The Costco-sized antacid bottle on her desk was nearly empty, maybe two inches of chalky tablets visible through the plastic.

"I wanted to talk to you about something," she said. "Not — your numbers are fine. Everything's fine with your work."

"OK."

"I'm thinking about leaving Canopy."

She said it in the voice she used for rehearsed things — organized, clear, the former teacher's instinct for structure imposing itself even on a surrender. But the sentence cracked between *thinking* and *leaving*, her voice dropping into a register she usually kept private.

"Like quitting?"

"I can't do this anymore, Nick. I'm not — the team's half the size it was three months ago. The people who are left don't trust the process, and the new people don't know what they're doing because we're onboarding them too fast to actually train them, and Marcus is asking me for quality numbers that I can't produce because the people who produced those numbers are gone." She set the mug down. Coffee sloshed onto a stack of printouts she didn't seem to notice. "Bao's gone. Tommy's on probation for ratings that were within normal range six months ago and only look off now because the whole baseline shifted after the — after everything. Dani's position, I don't know if it survives the next budget review. And I'm sitting here running a quality program on half the people and twice the targets and writing the same reports with different numbers and pretending that's."

She stopped. Picked up the mug. Put it down.

"I keep thinking about April when I was teaching," she said. "Not one student. The whole month. Everybody realized at once how far behind they were, and I'd stay up making make-up packets and alternate assignments and little rescue ladders because if I organized it hard enough maybe nobody had to fail. I think I'm doing that here now. More reports, more process, more explanations, and meanwhile Bao is gone and Dani's scared and I'm still acting like if I phrase the problem correctly it turns back into something manageable."

Nick sat in the chair across from her desk, the one evaluators sat in for quarterly reviews, the armrest fabric worn thin from the fidgeting hands of people being told their throughput was below target. The sentence he could have said sat hard against his teeth. If he told her now, the spreadsheet behind her monitor would stop being a staffing problem and become evidence. He drew breath.

Priya's phone started buzzing across the desk, rattling toward the coffee mug. At the same time her monitor flashed a Teams banner over the staffing sheet: MARCUS WEBB: NEED RECOVERY PLAN BY 5.

She caught the phone with one hand and shut her eyes once. "Sorry. Sorry, one sec."

She rubbed one eye with the heel of her hand, then turned her monitor toward him. A staffing sheet filled the screen: names, budget colors, blocks of training sessions lined up through next week like missing teeth.

"Marcus wants a recovery plan by five," she said. "If I leave now, this goes to ops, and ops will treat it like a scheduling issue. If I stay through rollout, I need calibration coverage in the room for the new-hire labs, the edge-case reviews, the anchor rebuild packets. Not every session. Just enough that I'm not up there by myself explaining lighting drift to sixty people who got hired because we broke the system."

Nick looked at the grid. Monday and Tuesday were already stacked with sessions titled RECOVERY REVIEW and FAILURE MODES. A yellow note sat beside one staffing line: HOLD SENIOR FLOOR IF CROSS-FUNCTIONAL SUPPORT CONFIRMED. Dani's name was on that line.

"You want me in those?"

"I want somebody I trust in those," Priya said. "You, if you'll do it. If I can tell Marcus calibration is covering the first two weeks, I can argue against another senior-floor cut. Dani's on that list."

He could still have told her. He could've pointed at the grid and said the hole she was building a bridge over had his shape. Instead he looked at Dani's name in yellow and answered the question she'd put in front of him.

"Yeah," he said. "I'll do it."

Priya was already reaching for her keyboard. Relief changed her speed before it changed her face.

"OK. Thank you. I can work with that."

His phone buzzed before he stood. Then again. Calendar invites dropped into next week in a fast blue column: RECOVERY TRAINING, ESCALATION REVIEW, NEW-HIRE LAB. Priya clicked him into each block with one hand while the other rested on the antacid bottle.

The quality-review meeting was Thursday afternoon, in the conference room Marcus used when he visited San Leandro. The room had a long table, a whiteboard no one ever wrote on, and a window overlooking the parking lot and the strip of chain restaurants beyond it — a Subway, a Panda Express, their signs catching the spring sunlight at an angle that made the orange-and-green color scheme look briefly like a deliberate composition.

Marcus was already seated when Nick arrived. Laptop open, a printed spreadsheet in front of him with cells highlighted in yellow and pink. He wore the waxy, overtight look of a man living on airport coffee and short nights, with enough discipline to keep the damage below the collar line. His oxford was untucked as always, but wrinkled at the collar in a way that suggested he'd been living out of a bag. The watch — the expensive one, the one he touched when he was thinking or stressed or both — caught the overhead light as he shifted in his chair.

"Nick. Thanks for making time. Close the door?"

Nick closed the door. The latch clicked and the room sealed itself into its particular quiet — HVAC hum, the muffled tapping from the floor below, the faint vibration of trucks on the 880 overpass half a mile east.

"I'll be direct," Marcus said. "I've been going through the calibration metrics from the last eight months. The aggregate view is easy enough to read." He turned the laptop screen toward Nick. A scatter plot, dense with data points, a trend line sloping in a direction Nick could read. "The contamination we identified in Q4 — the Manila issue — that accounts for maybe thirty percent of the variance. There's another component we haven't sourced. A systematic bias in certain categories. Anatomical scoring. Lighting consistency."

He paused. The pause was precise, and Nick recognized it — Marcus leaving space like a good interviewer, letting silence do the asking.

"I've noticed the new evaluators struggling with those categories," Nick said. This was true. It sidestepped what Marcus was asking about.

"The bias predates the new hires. It's in the calibration anchors from Q3 and Q4 last year."

Marcus hit another key. The scatter plot disappeared. A table replaced it — batch IDs, category tags, comment snippets. Nick recognized two of the clusters immediately. One lighting set from October where he'd widened the acceptable range until dead green started passing as natural variation. One anatomy set where he'd kept the numbers plausible and let the commentary do the poisoning.

Nick's hands were on the table. He looked at them. The paint calluses were still visible if you knew where to look — faint ridges on his thumb and forefinger where the brush handles had pressed for years. The small scar on his right index finger from an X-Acto knife he'd slipped with in his twenties. He pressed his palms flat against the laminate. The surface was cool and faintly gritty under his skin.

"Have you seen anything in the calibration data that looked off?" Marcus said. "Anything. This is a calibration conversation, Nick. I'm asking because you've been in those anchors for months. If something was off, you might've seen it."

He rotated the laptop a little farther. One of the comment fragments on the screen was his. Only the contour of the sentence remained, but it was enough for recognition.

"Does this read like intent to you," Marcus said, "or drift?"

Nick went very still.

The true answer was short. He could've said it in one breath.

"It reads like patch drift," he said. "Bad category boundaries. Rushed anchor swaps. Examples that should've been retired staying live because nobody had time to rebuild them."

Marcus watched him for a second that felt longer than it was. "You'd put your name on that?"

Nick heard himself answer before the rest of him caught up. "Yes."

Marcus slid the printed spreadsheet across the table.

"Then you own the cleanup. Every anatomy and lighting anchor in these clusters. Replacement rationales, downstream impact, anything that needs to be quarantined before the announcement tomorrow. I want it by nine."

Nick put his hand on the paper. The highlighted cells were warm from Marcus's printer.

"One other thing," Marcus said. "The board approved a major expansion. Five hundred new evaluators, three new offices — Austin, Raleigh, and an expansion here. Company announcement goes out tomorrow." He leaned back. The chair creaked. "The argument I've been making for three years — that the human layer's the most critical part of this infrastructure — turns out the board needed a catastrophic quality failure to agree with me. But they agreed." He touched the watch. "Five hundred people. Biggest investment in human evaluation we've ever made."

"That's a lot of people."

"It's what we need. The corrupted anchors have to be rebuilt from scratch. New calibration framework, new quality controls, the whole pipeline. Two quarters, maybe three. It's going to be a grind, but when it's done we'll have the most robust evaluation infrastructure in the industry." He said it with a worn, personal conviction, stripped of the smoothness of his public remarks, the voice of a man who had watched something he believed in get wrecked and was now being given the resources to rebuild it.

"That's good," Nick said. "That should help."

They shook hands when he stood. Marcus's handshake was the same as always — firm, warm, an executive's trained gesture carrying just enough real feeling to blur the performance. Nick walked back down the stairwell, past the window that looked out over the parking lot. Beyond the lot, past the business park's perimeter of sad ornamental shrubs, the embankment along the freeway was green from the winter rains, the kind of April green Bao would've broken into six notes instead of one.

Back at his station, Marcus's file was already in his inbox with the subject line Q3-Q4 CLUSTERS - NICK OWNERSHIP. Priya's training invitations sat directly underneath it, solid blue blocks consuming next week. He opened the first suspect batch. The archive pane showed the old anchor note clipped to a fragment, but it was enough. He dropped the cursor into the replacement field and typed: *shadow temperature inconsistent across planes; vegetation compressed to single synthetic value; retire from lighting anchor set*. He flagged the batch for quarantine. The system answered with a green checkmark and routed the replacement packet into tomorrow's onboarding queue.

Dani passed behind him on her way to the printer. Her tote hung from her shoulder, Luna's drawings sticking out of the top. She glanced once at the red RECOVERY label across his screen and kept walking. At the printer she stood with her back to him until the pages came out, then carried them to Priya's office without looking over.

His apartment was warm for April. He'd stopped opening the windows months ago, around the time the smell of linseed oil and turpentine had faded from the room and there was nothing left to ventilate. The canvases from his last show at the Temescal gallery were still stacked against the wall by the door — the same paintings that had been scraped from the gallery's website and fed into a training pipeline and rated by his coworkers on a five-point scale and rated by Nick himself at four out of five because five would have flagged.

He had set up to paint. He'd been sitting in front of the setup for forty minutes, which was less time than in December, when he'd lasted an hour before producing a technically competent and completely dead canvas he'd scraped off and thrown in the trash. The folding easel he'd bought at Blick Art Materials when he still thought of himself as someone who bought art supplies. A pre-stretched canvas, sixteen by twenty, a size he used to work at without thinking and now found large in a way he couldn't explain. The paints were on the side table in the order he'd always kept them: whites on the left, earths in the middle, cadmiums and phthalos on the right. A jar of turpentine he'd had to dig out from under the bathroom sink. Two brushes — both filbert, a six and a ten — pulled from the coffee can where they stood bristle-up like dead stems.

The canvas was white.

Outside, the April evening was doing something he would have painted two years ago. The light going amber through the bay window, long shadows from the power lines falling across the building opposite, a warm underglow where the late sun hit the BART elevated track at the angle that turned the concrete golden for about fifteen minutes before the whole thing went to gray. The stucco wall across the street was catching bounce light from somewhere — the roof of a parked car, maybe — and the reflected patch was a different temperature than the ambient, warmer, slightly orange against the wall's cool blue shadow. It was the kind of light problem he'd spent years solving on canvas. He could still see it. His eyes still worked, still separating values and temperatures, still catching the warmer patch against the wall's cool blue shadow.

But when he looked at the canvas and tried to find what he would put on it — the first mark, the commitment to a particular image — the decisions arrived backward. The finished thing came to him already botched. The power lines tugged the eye too hard left. The amber on the BART track had to agree with the wall's shadow temperature or the light source would break. The orange bounce off the parked car had to stay quiet enough to keep the whole scene from going synthetic. He could see the errors in advance in fine, specific detail. A painting never cohered.

He picked up the size-six filbert. The handle was worn smooth where his thumb and forefinger had pressed for years — a groove specific to his grip, the wood darkened and slightly concave, the handle itself a physical record of ten thousand hours of painting. He held it high, near the ferrule, as his instructor at the California College of the Arts had taught him, saying a brush held high gave more range and less control, and less control was usually what you wanted.

He dipped the brush in turpentine. Touched it to the cadmium yellow. Lifted it toward the lower left corner, where a shape usually declared itself first.

The yellow gathered at the tip, trembled once, and slid back toward the ferrule. Solvent cooled his knuckles while the brush hovered an inch short of the gesso.

He set the brush down. The bristle left a crescent of cadmium yellow on the palette — a warm, saturated comma of pigment he would not use.

His laptop was on the kitchen counter, screen dark, lid still open from that morning. The Canopy evaluation portal was bookmarked, password saved. The interface was more familiar to him than the easel or the window. He knew which queues timed out first, which comment boxes clipped long notes, which tabs opened the archive without throwing a permissions warning.

The company-wide Slack notification had gone out that afternoon, as Marcus had said it would. The CEO's message, subject line: *Investing in Our Human Layer.* Five hundred new evaluators. Three new offices. "The events of this past quarter," the CEO had written, in the particular cadence of executive communication that sounded both personal and legally reviewed, "have demonstrated more clearly than ever that human judgment is the irreplaceable foundation of trustworthy AI. We are making the largest investment in human evaluation in Canopy's history, because this work, your work, is what makes everything else possible."

Nick had read it at his station on the floor, surrounded by what the message meant. Bao's station wiped down and waiting for whoever came next. Dani sitting a little differently in her chair, as if she didn't trust it to stay hers. Tommy finishing probation ratings with stripped-down care, his pen sitting still beside the keyboard.

Before he left, Marcus had sent the calibration team the first recovery queue. *FAILURE MODES. PRIORITY REVIEW. Anchor candidates for new-office onboarding.*

Under it sat Marcus's follow-up with the spreadsheet attached and Priya's calendar invites eating through next week in solid blocks of blue.

Nick stood up from the stool. He walked to the counter and woke the screen.

His phone lit again beside the keyboard. Priya had moved one of Monday's labs earlier. Marcus had added a note to the spreadsheet: START WITH LIGHTING CLUSTERS. The notifications stacked until the lock screen went blue. Nick turned the phone facedown and laid his paint rag over it. The rag smelled like old solvent and dust.

He logged in.

The portal opened on a different queue than the one he used every day. RECOVERY REVIEW. Q3-Q4 FAILURE MODES. 247 images awaiting evaluation. He opened the first one.

A landscape. Green hills under late light, arranged in moves he knew too well: low horizon, road tugging the eye out of frame, sky worked past weather into texture. The vanishing point drifted. The grass held one dead green from the front edge to the hill's shadow.

His thumb left a faint streak of cadmium yellow on the trackpad.

He clicked the rating fields. He gave it a 3 for compositional balance, a 2 for lighting consistency, a 4 for stylistic coherence. In the comment field he typed, *vanishing point drift, overworked sky, single-value grass*. He submitted the note.

The next recovery image loaded. A hand with six fingers.

Behind him, the canvas stayed white. A skin formed on the cadmium yellow. He clicked the anatomical-accuracy field.
