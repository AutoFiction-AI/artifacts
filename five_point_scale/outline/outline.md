# Five-Point Scale — Novel Outline

## Narrative Engine

**Primary mode: Dark workplace comedy with character-study underpinnings.**

The comic engine drives chapters through escalating workplace absurdity, moral compromise played for dark humor, and the gap between corporate language and human reality. The character-study engine drives Nick Bray's identity dissolution — the slow process by which a painter becomes a rater, a creator becomes a metric. These two engines feed each other: the funnier the workplace becomes, the more painfully Nick's selfhood erodes.

State shifts are driven by: comic escalation, moral compromise, relational deepening that raises sabotage stakes, institutional absurdity, identity pressure, and the specific comedy of excelling at work you intend to destroy.

The novel must not drift into thriller or heist territory. Nick's sabotage is a workplace operation executed by a man who barely knows Python. Tension comes from moral compromise, the comedy of misplaced competence, and the slow corrosion of being good at something you hate — not from pursuit, surveillance, or corporate espionage tropes. The setting is the gig economy, not a spy novel.

## Chapter Plan

### Chapter 1: The Client
**Timeline:** Late November 2022 | **Engine:** Aftermath | **Texture:** Grief-heavy turning to hot

Nick Bray, 34, a painter in Oakland, receives a Zoom call from his last reliable client — a children's book publisher — telling him they're switching to Midjourney for cover illustrations. We see his apartment: overdue rent notice on the counter, paint supplies that cost more than his groceries, a body of unsold landscape work stacked against the wall. He eats cereal for dinner and rage-browses AI art forums, watching Midjourney produce variations of his style in seconds, then produce work better than his. By dawn, staring at a generated image of a children's book cover that could have been his, the plan crystallizes: teach himself to code, infiltrate a data-labeling company, and poison the training data from the inside.

**State shift:** Working artist with one client → displaced artist with a vendetta and a delusional plan.

---

### Chapter 2: LeetCode
**Timeline:** December 2022 – January 2023 | **Engine:** Comic escalation | **Texture:** Comic

Nick attempts to teach himself Python. His painter's brain rejects boolean logic. He watches YouTube tutorials by teenagers who call functions "super easy." He fails the same array-reversal problem seventeen times. His hands, trained for sable brushes, fight the keyboard shortcuts. He discovers LeetCode and spends Christmas alone, eating ramen and failing Easy-tier problems while his neighbor's holiday party thumps through the wall. His old art school friend Jess calls from a residency in New Mexico — she's still painting, still broke but making work. Nick doesn't mention the plan. He memorizes solution patterns like color swatches: not understanding, just matching. By late January he can solve roughly forty percent of Easy problems if he's seen something similar. He applies to Canopy AI.

**State shift:** Vague revenge idea → committed (delusional) action. Nick now has just enough dangerous knowledge to be plausible.

---

### Chapter 3: The Screen
**Timeline:** Late January 2023 | **Engine:** Concealment | **Texture:** Comic, suspended

Nick's technical interview at Canopy AI, a Scale AI-like data-labeling company. The coding portion is a disaster held together by memorized patterns and the interviewer's willingness to give partial credit. But the image-evaluation section is a revelation: given sample AI-generated images, Nick spots anatomical errors (a wrist that bends wrong, a shadow cast from no light source, a hand with six fingers) that the interviewer's answer key doesn't even flag. His trained painter's eye catches compositional failures, lighting inconsistencies, and perspective breaks that no other candidate has noticed. The interviewer, confused by the disconnect between Nick's weak coding and extraordinary visual analysis, escalates him for a second opinion. The evaluation team lead looks at Nick's scores and hires him on the spot. Nick walks to his car and sits in the parking lot for ten minutes, hands shaking, unsure whether to laugh or vomit.

**State shift:** Outsider with a plan → infiltrator with a badge. Nick is inside.

---

### Chapter 4: The Floor
**Timeline:** Early February 2023 | **Engine:** Discovery | **Texture:** Quiet, uncanny

Nick's first days at Canopy's labeling floor in a San Leandro business park — fluorescent lights, beige carpet, rows of monitors, the murmur of people rating images eight hours a day. He meets the team: Dani Solis, 31, a former muralist who's precise and slightly guarded; Tommy Reyes, 22, an art school dropout who radiates ironic detachment; Bao Nguyen, 64, a retired graphic designer who evaluates with meticulous slowness; and their team lead Priya Acharya, 40, a former art teacher whose encouraging manner doesn't quite hide her exhaustion. The work: open image, check prompt adherence, rate anatomical accuracy, lighting consistency, compositional balance, stylistic coherence. Five-point scale. Click. Next. Nick is simultaneously bored and fascinated. The images are mostly bad — generated hands, uncanny faces, impossible architecture — and his coworkers are mostly artists, which is the thing he didn't expect and can't stop noticing.

**State shift:** Saboteur enters the building → saboteur discovers the human landscape he'll have to betray.

---

### Chapter 5: Gold Standard
**Timeline:** February – March 2023 | **Engine:** Revelation | **Texture:** Quiet turning to suspended

Nick's first full month. His evaluations are consistently flagged as exceptional — his ratings align with expert consensus more than any other evaluator on the floor. Priya notices. The company's quality metrics for his batch are the highest in the San Leandro office. But Nick is a disaster at everything non-visual: he misuses Slack threads, calls the standup meeting "the morning circle," and doesn't understand why his evaluation notes ("the deltoid insertion is wrong and the reflected light on the chin is from a second source that doesn't exist in the scene") are "too detailed" for the rubric fields. Dani helps him calibrate to the system's language. Through fragments — a phone call Dani takes in the stairwell, her voice tight and controlled — Nick learns she's in a custody dispute over her seven-year-old daughter Luna, and this job is the "stable employment" her lawyer needs on the filing. Priya promotes Nick to senior evaluator. He accepts, aware that every step up gives him more access to the system he plans to break.

**State shift:** Nick's competence is real, which makes the contradiction of his mission tangible. His coworkers' stakes become specific.

---

### Chapter 6: Nights
**Timeline:** March 2023 | **Engine:** Drift-under-pressure | **Texture:** Tender, quiet

Nick volunteers for night shifts to map the data pipeline — how training batches are assembled, routed, weighted. The nightshift is its own ecosystem: people with second jobs, insomniacs, a woman who knits between batches, a former trucker who evaluates landscapes with eerie accuracy. Nick bonds with Bao, who works nights because his wife Mai's home caregiver leaves at six and the commute takes an hour — nights mean he's home when she needs him. Over shared takeout in the break room, Bao talks about color theory the way Nick's professors used to, with love and precision. Bao shows Nick photos of Mai's garden, which she planted fifteen years ago and can no longer tend because of early-onset dementia. Nick waters it on weekends now, Bao says, not well. The chapter ends with Nick riding BART home at 2 AM, the train nearly empty, staring at his reflection in the dark window and seeing a man he doesn't fully recognize.

**State shift:** Relational — Nick's attachment to Bao (and through Bao, to the texture of these workers' lives) becomes a genuine complication, not an abstract one.

---

### Chapter 7: Throughput
**Timeline:** April – May 2023 | **Engine:** Confrontation | **Texture:** Hot, comic

The AI image-generation arms race accelerates. New models drop monthly. Canopy is hiring frantically, lowering evaluation standards to meet throughput demands. New hires who can't distinguish anatomical errors from stylistic choices flood the floor. Priya is being crushed between the quality metrics she's responsible for and management's demand for speed — she eats antacids from a Costco bottle she keeps in her desk drawer. A company all-hands: the CEO, beaming from Canopy's San Francisco headquarters, talks about "the irreplaceable human layer" while announcing per-evaluator throughput targets that will gut the quality Priya has built. After work, Tommy takes Nick for drinks and, three beers in, reveals he's been submitting AI-generated images to juried art competitions under a pseudonym — and winning. Nick is appalled. Tommy shrugs: "They replaced us. I'm just returning the favor." Nick can't articulate his objection without revealing his own plans, which are the same logic carried to a different conclusion.

**State shift:** External pressure mounts. The system's hypocrisy becomes visible. Tommy's confession confronts Nick with a funhouse mirror of his own project.

---

### Chapter 8: The Method
**Timeline:** June 2023 | **Engine:** Preparation | **Texture:** Suspended, formal

Nick discovers his sabotage vector. Senior evaluators' ratings feed into "calibration anchors" — gold-standard judgments that weight the entire system's learning. A calibration anchor rated wrong won't just be one bad data point; it will pull thousands of subsequent evaluations toward the error, compounding through the training pipeline like a tint mixed into primer. But calibration-anchor access requires the next tier: the calibration team, which reviews the reviewers. To get there, Nick needs to be even more visibly excellent. He tests a small, deliberate misrating on a non-critical batch — marking a clearly broken hand as anatomically sound. The system absorbs it without a flag. He tests again: rating a two-source lighting failure as consistent. Nothing. The guardrails are built to catch random noise and lazy workers, not systematic expertise applied in reverse. Nick, who has been thinking of this in painting terms — bad pigment mixed into the primer, invisible until the whole wall blooms — now has a method. What he needs is access.

**State shift:** From vague sabotage intention to a specific, testable, executable plan. The method requires Nick to perform excellence even harder, deepening the contradiction.

---

### Chapter 9: After Hours
**Timeline:** July 2023 | **Engine:** Quiet | **Texture:** Tender, warm

A genuine quiet chapter. The team goes out on a Friday after a brutal throughput week. A dive bar near the San Leandro business park — sticky tables, a pool table with a torn felt, a jukebox nobody feeds. Dani talks about Luna's obsession with drawing horses, how she fills entire sketchbooks and none of the horses look like horses but all of them look alive. Tommy does impressions of the CEO's all-hands voice. Bao quietly destroys everyone at pool with a mechanic's precision. Priya, two glasses of wine in, tells a story about a student who once drew a self-portrait so ugly and so honest it made her cry, and how she knew then that the kid was the real thing. Nick is funny and warm and present — the version of himself he likes best, the version that has nothing to do with sabotage or revenge. He walks Dani to her car. Something almost happens — a silence that goes on one beat too long, a look he doesn't break fast enough — but neither of them acts. He drives home with the windows down. For a few hours he forgot what he's planning.

**State shift:** Emotional — Nick's attachment to these people becomes undeniable to him. The cost of what he's planning is no longer abstract.

---

### Chapter 10: Quality Assurance
**Timeline:** August 2023 | **Engine:** Seduction | **Texture:** Hot, formal

Marcus Webb, Canopy's VP of Quality, visits the San Leandro floor. Marcus is sharp, charismatic, and — annoyingly — a genuine believer. He left a senior role at Google because he thought "human-in-the-loop" evaluation was the most important job in AI. He tours the floor, asks smart questions, and singles Nick out: his calibration scores are exceptional, his error-detection rate is twice the team average, and Marcus wants him on the calibration team. This is exactly the access Nick needs. The irony is suffocating: his excellence at work he intends to destroy is the weapon he'll use to destroy it. Nick accepts. Dani, who has more seniority and comparable metrics, is passed over — she lacks Nick's anomalous visual-analysis scores. She doesn't complain. She can't afford to; the custody filing requires continuous employment. But her silence in the break room afterward, the way she washes her coffee mug without looking at Nick, shifts something between them. Nick knows he took something from her, and he knows why he got it, and neither reason is clean.

**State shift:** Nick gets the keys to the system. The cost: Dani's professional loss and a fracture in their friendship that neither acknowledges aloud.

---

### Chapter 11: Calibration
**Timeline:** September – October 2023 | **Engine:** Transgression | **Texture:** Uncanny, suspended

Nick starts on the calibration team. He's now rating the raters — his judgments become the "ground truth" the system trusts above all else. The power is disorienting. He sees the whole architecture: a few dozen calibration anchors shape the learning of models used by millions. The edifice of AI image quality rests on the taste of underpaid humans in business parks. He begins small sabotage runs: subtle misratings on lighting and anatomy, each individually defensible as a judgment call, but systematically biased to reward errors the models already tend to produce. He keeps meticulous private records — a painter's notebook, pen on paper, nothing digital. A training batch comes through containing stock photos of paintings from a gallery in Oakland's Temescal neighborhood where Nick once showed. Not his paintings. But the gallery's walls, the lighting, the price tags visible in one corner. Close enough to rattle him.

**State shift:** The sabotage begins. Irreversible action. Nick is now actively corrupting the system, and the nearness of his former life in the training data is a warning he doesn't heed.

---

### Chapter 12: Five-Point Scale
**Timeline:** November 2023 | **Engine:** Humiliation | **Texture:** Humiliating, grief-heavy

The pivotal chapter. Nick opens a routine training batch and finds his own unsold paintings — scraped from the internet, thumbnailed, stripped of his name, fed into the pipeline as raw material for AI to learn from. He watches his coworkers evaluate them at their stations. Tommy gives one a 2 out of 5 for "stylistic coherence," not even pausing. Bao rates another — a landscape of the Oakland hills Nick spent three months on — a 4, pausing over it longer than protocol allows, tilting his head the way he does when something catches his eye. When the batch reaches Nick's calibration queue, he has to rate his own work on a five-point scale. He can't give himself a 5 — it would flag as an outlier, drawing attention. He gives himself a 4. He sits with the number for a long time. Then he clicks Next.

**State shift:** Identity crisis. Nick is now simultaneously the creator and the system that digests creation. The boundary between the painter and the labeler collapses.

---

### Chapter 13: Rubric Brain
**Timeline:** December 2023 | **Engine:** Ritual | **Texture:** Grief-heavy, tender

Nick's creative identity is dissolving. He hasn't painted in four months. His internal vocabulary has been colonized by evaluation language — he looks at winter light on the Bay and his brain scores it for atmospheric coherence. He buys canvas and paint, sets up in his apartment, and stares at the blank surface for an hour. When he finally makes a mark, it looks evaluated rather than felt — technically proficient, rubric-compliant, dead. He scrapes it off. Dani invites him to Luna's holiday recital at her school. He goes. The gym is hot, the folding chairs are too small, and Luna's class performs a stumbling version of a song Nick doesn't recognize. Afterward, Luna shows him a drawing of a horse — wrong proportions, wild colors, no regard for anatomical accuracy — and it's the most alive thing Nick has seen in months. That night, back at Dani's apartment after Luna is asleep, something begins between them — a kiss in the kitchen, her hand on his chest — but Nick pulls away. Not because he doesn't want it. Because being close to Dani requires a version of honesty he has made himself incapable of.

**State shift:** Nick's original self is becoming unrecognizable. His withdrawal from Dani is not noble restraint but a consequence of the lie he's built his life around.

---

### Chapter 14: Red Flag
**Timeline:** January 2024 | **Engine:** Pursuit | **Texture:** Hot, suspended

Canopy's automated quality-assurance system flags statistical anomalies in recent calibration data. Marcus convenes an audit. Nick's stomach drops — but the investigation focuses on a labeling center in Manila where evaluators had been gaming completion bonuses, submitting rapid-fire ratings to maximize per-task pay. The problem is real and predates Nick's sabotage. A team in Manila is suspended. Watching innocent workers absorb blame for a systemic problem adjacent to his actual corruption is a new category of moral horror — not because they're taking his fall, but because the company's investigative machinery is so blunt it can't distinguish between desperate workers gaming bonuses and deliberate systematic poisoning. The system's own incompetence is his cover. The audit clears the calibration team. Marcus tells Nick personally that his work is above reproach. Nick thanks him. That night, he makes the decision: the window is closing. Accelerate or abort. He chooses acceleration.

**State shift:** Near-exposure survived. But the Manila scapegoating demonstrates what will happen to Nick's own coworkers when the real damage surfaces. Nick sees the cost and doubles down anyway.

---

### Chapter 15: The Execution
**Timeline:** February 2024 | **Engine:** Constraint-heavy ordeal | **Texture:** Hot, formal

Nick executes the full-scale sabotage over three weeks. Working within his normal calibration shifts, he systematically corrupts anchor ratings across Canopy's flagship image-evaluation pipeline, introducing persistent biases that will teach models to reproduce exactly the errors the public already associates with AI: anatomical distortion, lighting that defies physics, faces that slide toward the uncanny valley. The chapter is methodical and tense — he must maintain flawless performance metrics on ninety percent of his evaluations to avoid triggering alerts, poisoning only the specific data categories where the errors will compound fastest. The double consciousness is exhausting: rating correctly all morning, corrupting strategically after lunch, knowing that each anchor he poisons will pull thousands of downstream evaluations in its direction. He finishes on a Thursday. Drives home. Pulls over on the shoulder of 880 and throws up into the weeds. Lies in bed that night staring at the ceiling, unable to name the feeling, which is not triumph and not relief and not guilt but something in the territory of all three.

**State shift:** Sabotage complete. Irreversible. The data is in the pipeline and there is no mechanism for recall.

---

### Chapter 16: Viral
**Timeline:** Late February – March 2024 | **Engine:** Comic escalation | **Texture:** Comic, uncanny

The corrupted models begin producing grotesqueries: six-fingered hands, melted faces, impossible anatomy, lighting from sources that don't exist. AI Twitter erupts. But the outputs go viral — not as failures but as aesthetic objects. People love them, share them, turn them into memes and merchandise. A design blog publishes "The Beauty of the Glitch." An artist collective in Brooklyn starts generating the melted-face images intentionally and selling prints. A tech journalist writes a think piece calling it "digital grotesque — the new punk." The errors Nick introduced are more famous than his paintings ever were. His sabotage has not discredited AI image generation; it has given it a new genre. Tommy — who has been quietly following the trend — texts the group chat: "Honestly these go harder than anything I saw in art school." Nick reads the text in his apartment, surrounded by his own unsold paintings that nobody rated higher than a 4, and laughs until he doesn't.

**State shift:** Nick's revenge produces the opposite of justice. The grotesqueries become culture. The sabotage has been absorbed, aestheticized, monetized.

---

### Chapter 17: The Purge
**Timeline:** March 2024 | **Engine:** Confrontation | **Texture:** Grief-heavy, hot

Canopy's internal investigation widens. Quality metrics have cratered across the pipeline, and the calibration team is now under direct scrutiny. HR conducts interviews — impersonal, procedural, designed to find process failures rather than saboteurs. Bao is fired. His evaluation speed was already flagged before Nick's sabotage; the audit sweeps his "performance inconsistencies" into the same bucket as the corrupted data. He clears his desk with quiet dignity, shaking hands with each person on the floor, and Nick helps carry a box to his car. Dani's position is threatened — a "restructuring" that may eliminate her role. Her custody lawyer calls, voice tight: any interruption to employment will be used against her. Tommy is placed on probation for "quality deviation" in evaluations that predate the corruption but look suspicious in aggregate. Priya, caught between defending her team and satisfying management's demand for accountability, has a panic attack in the women's bathroom. Nick hears it through the wall. Every person he cares about is paying for what he did.

**State shift:** The human cost of the sabotage lands on the people Nick was supposed to be protecting. His moral position becomes indefensible by any logic, including his own.

---

### Chapter 18: The Human Layer
**Timeline:** March – April 2024 | **Engine:** Concealment | **Texture:** Quiet, grief-heavy

Nick almost confesses three times. First: helping Dani clear out Bao's desk station, finding one of Bao's handwritten evaluation notes — color-theory observations too detailed for any rubric — and opening his mouth to say something true. He doesn't. Second: Priya, in a one-on-one where she tells him she's thinking of quitting because she can't protect her team anymore, her eyes red and her hands wrapped around a coffee mug she isn't drinking from. He says nothing useful. Third: Marcus, in a quality-review meeting where Marcus asks directly whether Nick has "noticed anything anomalous in the calibration pipeline." Nick looks at the man who hired him, who believed in the work, who is now watching his department disintegrate, and says no. Meanwhile, the industry's response to the quality crisis arrives: Canopy announces it is hiring five hundred additional labelers to rebuild the corrupted pipeline. Nick's sabotage has made the human layer more valuable, not less. He has accidentally strengthened the system he tried to break. In the final scene, Nick sits in his apartment with paint and canvas. He stares at the blank surface for a long time. Then he closes his eyes, opens them, and reaches — not for a brush, but for his laptop. He logs into the evaluation portal. He opens a new batch. He starts rating.

**State shift:** Nick's silence is its own form of destruction — of his relationships, his integrity, and whatever remained of his identity as an artist. The system absorbs sabotage the way it absorbs art: by putting a number on it and moving on. Nick, unable to confess and unable to paint, returns to the only work he's good at now.

## Middle-Book Progression Map

Covers Chapters 8–14 (approximately 40%–80% of the novel).

**Chapter 8 (The Method):**
- *New:* Nick discovers the calibration-anchor exploit — a specific, testable sabotage vector.
- *Broken equilibrium:* His vague "I'll find a way" becomes an executable plan, which forces him to pursue promotion rather than coast.
- *Non-swappable:* Requires the accumulated workplace knowledge from Chs 4–7; the method's discovery must precede Ch 9's emotional deepening so the reader knows what's at stake when Nick bonds with the team.

**Chapter 9 (After Hours):**
- *New:* Nick's emotional attachment to the team becomes undeniable — to him and to the reader.
- *Broken equilibrium:* He can no longer compartmentalize saboteur and friend. The people are specific, warm, and present.
- *Non-swappable:* Must follow Ch 8's method discovery so the warmth registers against the knowledge that he now has a concrete plan to betray these exact people. Before Ch 8, the sabotage is abstract; after it, every shared laugh has a shadow.

**Chapter 10 (Quality Assurance):**
- *New:* Nick gets calibration-team access. Dani is passed over for the same promotion.
- *Broken equilibrium:* Nick's relationship with Dani acquires a professional wound neither acknowledges. His path to sabotage requires stepping on people he likes.
- *Non-swappable:* The promotion's cost (Dani's exclusion) needs Ch 9's warmth to register as a genuine loss. And the access granted here is the prerequisite for Ch 11's sabotage.

**Chapter 11 (Calibration):**
- *New:* Nick begins active sabotage. Irreversible action.
- *Broken equilibrium:* He crosses from planning to doing. The theoretical becomes criminal.
- *Non-swappable:* Requires Ch 10's access. And the nearby gallery paintings in the training data foreshadow Ch 12's personal version of the same violation.

**Chapter 12 (Five-Point Scale):**
- *New:* Nick finds his own paintings in the training data and must rate them.
- *Broken equilibrium:* The boundary between artist and system collapses. Nick can no longer sustain the narrative that he's an artist fighting the machine — he IS the machine.
- *Non-swappable:* The self-rating's devastation requires Ch 11's prior sabotage (he's already corrupting the system when he encounters his own work inside it). The gallery-painting near-miss in Ch 11 sets this up.

**Chapter 13 (Rubric Brain):**
- *New:* Nick's internal language has been fully colonized by evaluation metrics. He fails to paint. He retreats from Dani.
- *Broken equilibrium:* Any remaining belief that he can "go back to being an artist after this" is demolished by the failed canvas.
- *Non-swappable:* The identity dissolution here is the psychological aftermath of Ch 12's humiliation. And the withdrawal from Dani plants the seed for Ch 18's inability to confess.

**Chapter 14 (Red Flag):**
- *New:* Automated systems flag anomalies. An audit convenes. The Manila scapegoat is found.
- *Broken equilibrium:* Nick's feeling of invisibility is shattered. The system can detect problems — it just can't identify their source. And watching Manila workers take the fall previews what will happen to his own team.
- *Non-swappable:* Must follow Ch 13's drift (Nick is already losing himself; now he faces external exposure too). The escalation-or-abort decision requires both the method (Ch 8) and the near-miss to create adequate pressure.

## Supporting Character Pressure Map

### Dani Solis (former muralist, 31)
**Independent pressure:** Custody battle for her daughter Luna. This job is the "stable employment" her lawyer needs on the filing. Dani takes pride in evaluation work that others dismiss as menial — she sees it as visual analysis, which it is, and she's excellent at it.
**What she carries that isn't about Nick:** Fear of losing Luna. The specific humiliation of having her parenting evaluated by a court while she evaluates images for a corporation. A suppressed grief for the murals she used to paint.
**Chapters where her pressure touches the page:** Ch 5 (custody phone calls), Ch 10 (passed over for promotion, with custody implications), Ch 13 (invites Nick to Luna's recital; the almost-intimacy), Ch 17 (job threatened, custody lawyer calls).

### Tommy Reyes (art school dropout, 22)
**Independent pressure:** $47,000 in student loans for an art degree he didn't finish. Parents cut him off. His ironic detachment is a coping mechanism for genuine financial terror.
**What he carries that isn't about Nick:** He submits AI-generated art to juried competitions under a pseudonym — and wins. He's complicit in the very displacement he jokes about, and he knows it, and the knowing doesn't help.
**Chapters where his pressure touches the page:** Ch 7 (reveals the AI art submissions), Ch 12 (rates Nick's painting a 2 without recognizing the irony), Ch 17 (placed on probation).

### Bao Nguyen (retired graphic designer, 64)
**Independent pressure:** His wife Mai has early-onset dementia. This job supplements her care costs. His deliberate, meticulous evaluation pace — which gets him performance warnings — is the same quality of attention he brings to everything, including caring for Mai.
**What he carries that isn't about Nick:** Dignity applied to work that doesn't value it. Quiet grief for the woman his wife used to be, expressed through the garden he waters badly and the evaluation notes he writes with too much care.
**Chapters where his pressure touches the page:** Ch 6 (bond with Nick, wife's garden), Ch 12 (rates Nick's painting a 4, pausing), Ch 17 (fired in the purge, clears his desk with quiet dignity).

### Priya Acharya (team lead, former art teacher, 40)
**Independent pressure:** Left teaching after a breakdown. This was supposed to be her low-stress recovery job. Management's throughput demands are recreating the same crushing pressure.
**What she carries that isn't about Nick:** Guilt about leaving her students. Growing health problems she hides from HR because she can't afford medical leave. The bitter irony of discovering she's good at management — a skill she never wanted.
**Chapters where her pressure touches the page:** Ch 5 (promotes Nick, managing quality), Ch 7 (squeezed between quality and throughput), Ch 14 (defending team during audit), Ch 17 (panic attack in the bathroom).

### Marcus Webb (VP of Quality, 45)
**Independent pressure:** Left Google because he believed "human-in-the-loop" evaluation was the most important job in AI safety. Now watching Canopy hollow out the quality program he built.
**What he carries that isn't about Nick:** Stock options that vest in 2025. Quitting on principle means walking away from real money. The dissonance between his public advocacy for the human layer and his private awareness that the company treats it as a cost center.
**Chapters where his pressure touches the page:** Ch 10 (visits the floor, offers Nick the promotion), Ch 14 (convenes the audit), Ch 18 (asks Nick directly whether he's noticed anomalies).
