# Chapter 10

The email from Priya arrived at 7:14 on a Tuesday morning, which was early even for her. *Marcus Webb visiting our floor Thursday. Full quality review. Please make sure your stations are clean and your batches are current.* She'd added a second paragraph that was pure Priya: a bulleted list of talking points in case anyone got pulled into conversation, including a reminder that the word "throughput" should not be used in front of Marcus unless he said it first. The list had a pedagogical tidiness that made Nick picture her typing it at five a.m. with her reading glasses on, one hand around a mug of the English Breakfast she drank before switching to coffee, organizing her anxiety into something she could delegate.

Nick read the email on his phone in the parking lot, his Civic's engine ticking as it cooled. The sun was barely up but the asphalt already held heat. Late August in San Leandro meant the business park baked from seven to seven, and the air conditioning inside the building fought a losing war against the flat roof and the south-facing windows. He locked his car and walked toward the entrance, passing the strip of dying boxwood that counted as landscaping, and noticed that someone had left a Coke can balanced on the electrical box near the door. The can's shadow stretched long across the concrete. A good shadow. Two-source, the sun and the building's reflected light off the glass entrance creating a secondary that softened the primary edge. He caught himself thinking *lighting consistency: 4* and let the assessment stand. Six months in, the rubric vocabulary had stopped feeling like a foreign language and started feeling like a second set of glasses he couldn't take off.

Tommy was already at his station when Nick got upstairs, which was unusual. Tommy's baseline arrival time was eleven minutes after whatever start time Priya had set, a margin he maintained with the precision of someone who had calculated exactly how much lateness qualified as a personality trait without triggering a formal warning. But today he was there, slouched in his chair, AirPods in, scrolling through a batch of generated portraits with the glazed efficiency of someone who'd been at it for a while.

"You're early," Nick said.

Tommy pulled out one AirPod. "Priya sent that email three times. She sent it, then she sent a correction with a typo fixed, then she sent it again with the bullet points reformatted. That's a three-email morning. That's, like, her version of a five-alarm fire."

"She just wants things to look good."

"She wants things to look the way they look when they look their best, which is not the same thing." Tommy put the AirPod back in, then pulled it out again. "Who's this guy, anyway? Webb?"

"VP of Quality. From SF."

"Right, yeah, the guy who left Google." Tommy spun his pen between his fingers, a habitual motion that turned the cheap blue plastic into a blur. "Everyone talks about him like he's some kind of, I don't know, quality messiah. Priya showed me this internal blog post he wrote when he started. 'The Irreducible Human.' About how you can't automate taste."

"Did you read it?"

"I read the first paragraph and then I thought about the fact that I make twenty-one dollars an hour to be the irreducible human, and I sort of lost my focus after that."

Nick sat down at his station and logged in. The evaluation portal opened to a queue of forty-six images, a batch of generated interior scenes, living rooms and kitchens that existed in no physical space and had never held furniture or light. He opened the first image. A kitchen with marble countertops and a window that looked out onto a yard where the grass was the wrong green, a flat lime with no warmth in it, the kind of green that happened when the model interpolated between data points instead of understanding how chlorophyll and sunlight actually worked. He rated it. Prompt adherence: 4. Anatomical accuracy: N/A. Lighting consistency: 3. The overhead fixture cast shadows that didn't match the window light's angle. Compositional balance: 3. Stylistic coherence: 4. Click. Next.

He worked through the batch with the flat rhythm that had become automatic, the part of his brain that did the actual seeing operating a half second ahead of the part that translated sight into numbers. The kitchens blurred together. Marble, granite, marble, butcher block, a stainless-steel refrigerator reflecting a room that wasn't the room it was in. Somewhere around image twenty he registered that Dani had arrived, not because he heard her but because the air in his peripheral vision shifted. She moved through a space the way someone who had spent years working at mural scale moved, with an awareness of how her body related to the surfaces around her. She set her tote bag under her desk. The corner of one of Luna's drawings poked out of the top, a flash of orange crayon on white paper.

"Morning," she said to the row in general, not to Nick specifically, but she caught his eye and held it for one count longer than morning required. Something from the bar a few weeks ago still lived in that extra beat: the parking lot, the silence that lasted too long, the way neither of them had addressed it since. They'd been normal. Aggressively, carefully normal.

"Morning," Nick said, and turned back to his screen.

---

Marcus Webb arrived at ten-fifteen on Thursday with Priya flanking him and a woman Nick didn't recognize carrying a tablet and taking notes. The woman had sharp bangs and a lanyard with a badge that read EMILY PARK, QUALITY OPERATIONS, and she smiled at no one in particular as they moved through the floor, a professional smile used the way other people used punctuation.

Nick saw Marcus before Marcus saw him. The VP of Quality was taller than he'd expected; the all-hands Zoom squares had flattened everyone into the same size. He moved through the evaluation floor with an ease that was either genuine comfort or very well-practiced performance. Close-cropped hair going gray at the temples. Good sneakers that probably cost what Nick's rent deposit had cost. An untucked blue oxford that managed to look both casual and deliberate. He shook hands with every evaluator he passed and asked each one what they were working on, and the thing that made Nick's stomach tighten was that Marcus actually listened to the answers.

Nick had seen the CEO do the executive version of this on the all-hands streams: nod once, pivot, keep moving. Marcus stopped at DeShawn's station and looked at the landscape on his monitor, a generated sky shifting from blue to violet on an axis that didn't belong to any real atmosphere, and said something about gradient mapping that made DeShawn laugh and pull up a second image. At Marta's station, Marcus noticed a note she'd written in the margin of her rubric sheet about fabric texture and asked her to elaborate. Marta, who had worked at a textile manufacturer in Sao Paulo before moving to the Bay Area and who noticed fabric the way Nick noticed light, talked for three minutes about how the models kept failing at the drape of linen versus cotton. Marcus wrote something in a small notebook he kept in his back pocket.

The notebook was leather-bound. Dark brown, soft cover, easy to miss if you weren't looking for it. Marcus held it in one hand and wrote in it with quick, committed strokes. Nick watched him and felt a sensation he hadn't expected: recognition. The man kept notes the way Nick kept notes. Pen on paper made the object itself a commitment to the idea that some observations deserved to be held in the hand.

Marcus and his entourage worked their way down the row. Priya's posture was immaculate, her shoulders pulled back in a way that spoke of effort rather than ease. She'd worn a blazer Nick had never seen before, charcoal and slightly too warm for the building's August interior, and her antacid bottle was nowhere visible, which meant she'd either hidden it or taken the whole thing in advance. She steered Marcus toward the evaluators she trusted and away from a section where three new hires were rating at the pace the throughput targets demanded, fast enough that anyone who cared about quality would have hated watching them.

Then Marcus was at Nick's row.

"Marcus, this is Nick Bray. Our top calibration scorer." Priya said it with a warmth that wasn't for show. "Nick, Marcus Webb, VP of Quality."

Marcus's handshake was warm and solid, the grip of someone who'd been taught that a handshake was a form of communication and had believed it. Up close, the gray at his temples was more pronounced. His eyes went to Nick's monitor, took in the kitchen image in a sweep, then came back to Nick's face and held there. He had a small scar on his chin, old and faded, and the general bearing of a man who'd been an athlete in college and kept the posture without keeping the sport.

"Nick. Priya tells me you came in through the visual-analysis track."

"I did, yeah."

"Your numbers are hard to ignore. Your error-detection rate is roughly twice the team average, and your false-positive rate is the lowest I've seen across any of our offices."

Marcus laid out the metrics in balanced clauses, precise without going stiff. He was good at this. Nick felt the floor tilt under him. Marcus was probably the most competent person he'd met at Canopy.

"I have a background in painting," Nick said, which was the truth dressed in the wrong tense. He had a background in painting the way an amputee had a background in walking. "I notice what's wrong with images because I spent years trying to get them right."

Marcus smiled. The expression reshaped his face and made him look briefly younger. "That's exactly the argument I've been making to the board. The human layer isn't just quality assurance. It's visual expertise that the models can't replicate because they don't understand what they're generating. They interpolate. People like you see."

He said it with the conviction of a man who had left a senior position at Google, stock and prestige and the gravitational pull of a company that treated departure as betrayal, because he believed this work mattered. Nick looked past him at Bao's empty night-shift station, at Dani bent over her screen, at DeShawn laughing two desks down. Marcus was right about the work. Nick kept his face arranged anyway.

"I'd like to talk more," Marcus said. "Do you have twenty minutes later today?"

"Yeah. Of course."

Marcus moved on to Dani's station. Nick watched him introduce himself with the same warm handshake and the same attentive eye contact. Dani responded with the composed professionalism she defaulted to around authority, straightforward, unperformative, a little guarded. She walked Marcus through a generated portrait with a jawline that cast a shadow inconsistent with the overhead light source, and Marcus asked how she distinguished between stylistic shadow choices and actual lighting errors. Dani answered with settled precision. She'd thought about the distinction long before anyone asked. Marcus wrote in his notebook.

Nick turned back to his screen. His hands were steady. His batch waited, a queue of generated rooms that needed numbers. He picked up where he'd left off. A bedroom with pillows that cast no shadows. A bathroom where the mirror reflected a slightly different room than the one it was mounted in. He rated them. The numbers were correct. They were always correct.

The meeting was at three-thirty in the small conference room Priya used for one-on-ones. It had a round table barely big enough for four people, a whiteboard that still showed the ghost of a flowchart someone had drawn months ago and not fully erased, and a window that overlooked the parking lot and, beyond it, the strip of chain restaurants along Davis Street: Subway, Panda Express, Taco Bell. The afternoon sun came through at a low angle and carved hard shadows across the table. Nick sat with his back to the window so the light wouldn't be in his eyes, then realized it put him in silhouette, which made his face harder to read. That was probably good.

Marcus was already in the room when Nick arrived, along with Emily and her tablet and Priya, who had poured water into paper cups that nobody drank. Marcus had taken off the untucked oxford and was wearing the plain gray T-shirt underneath it, which made him look less like a VP and more like someone you'd see at a climbing gym. His leather notebook was open on the table. Nick could see small, even left-handed handwriting but not read it from where he sat.

"Nick, thanks for making the time." Marcus leaned back in his chair as if the room had been built to fit him. "I'll be direct, because I think you'd prefer that."

"I would, yeah."

"I'm restructuring the calibration team. Right now we have eleven people across three offices whose anchor ratings define the quality baseline for our entire image pipeline. The system works, but it's thin: eleven people for models that serve sixty million users." He glanced at the notebook. "After today, after what Marta told me about fabric drape and the gradient issue DeShawn spotted, I want more of that closer to the pipeline. Evaluators with real domain expertise. People who can defend what they see after the number lands."

Marcus watched Nick, wanting the first thing that crossed his face.

"I want you on the calibration team."

The words arrived like a color finally landing on the palette. The pigment matched the thing in his head at last. The feeling was not joy but recognition. He had been working toward this since June, since the night he sat in his apartment and understood that calibration anchors were the load-bearing wall and the only way to collapse the wall was to stand on top of it. Two months of being exemplary. Two months of catching errors nobody else caught, of writing evaluation notes that were too detailed for the rubric fields and then calibrating them down to the system's language, of being the version of himself that excelled at the work he was planning to destroy.

And here it was. The key, extended across a conference table by a man who believed Nick deserved it.

"That's—yeah. Wow." He made himself pause. He made himself look like a man absorbing unexpected good news instead of a man receiving a delivery he'd already ordered. "I don't know what to say."

"Say yes," Marcus said, and smiled. "The pay bump is real. Calibration team is salaried, sixty K, and the benefits improve. You'd report to me through the quality ops chain instead of through Priya's team lead structure. The tradeoff is that the work is slower and a lot more exposed. The pod starts at nine-thirty because we sync with San Francisco, and once you're in there every call you make is something somebody will ask you to defend."

Every other evaluation. The phrase hit him like primer taking a tint. His ratings would become the truth the system believed. A three from Nick would teach the model what a three looked like. A four would harden into the four everyone else answered to. The vulnerability he'd tested in June had been a sketch then. Now Marcus was handing him the wall itself.

"I'm interested," Nick said, because the script required the appearance of consideration. "Can I think about it?"

"Sure. Take the weekend. But I'd like HR to start the transfer Sept 1 so we can get you through shadowing and have you in the room by mid-September." Marcus glanced at Emily, who made a note on her tablet. Then he looked back at Nick. "I should be transparent about the process. We looked at several evaluators on this floor. Priya recommended three candidates. You, Dani Solis, and a senior evaluator in the night-shift rotation. Kavi something."

"Kavi Narayan," Priya said quietly.

Nick's ribs cinched at the name that mattered. Dani was in the candidate pool. Dani, who had seniority over him by at least a year. Dani, who had been on this floor when Nick was still memorizing LeetCode patterns in his apartment and eating ramen for dinner. Nick pressed his thumb into the seam of the paper cup until the rim softened under the water.

"Right." Marcus's eyes moved to Nick's hands on the cup, then back to his face. "All three had strong accuracy profiles. That's what got you onto the short list. What separated you was the disagreement cases. Emily pulled a month of splits, and your notes held up after the number. Most people rate and move on. Your anatomy and lighting detection are outliers, and calibration needs that."

The thing that separated Nick from Dani was the part of his old life he still trusted most: years of drawing shoulders until they stopped looking bolted on, of killing greens that came up too sweet, of learning where the light failed first on skin.

"I'll let you know Monday," Nick said.

"Looking forward to it." Marcus stood and extended his hand.

Nick shook it.

Priya lingered after Marcus and Emily left. She closed the door and turned to Nick with an expression that sat somewhere between pride and worry, the face of a manager losing a good evaluator to a promotion she'd engineered.

"Congratulations," she said. "You earned it."

"Thanks, Priya."

"You're off my team Sept 1 on paper, Sept 18 in practice. Emily will send the shadowing schedule." She touched one of the paper cups, then let it go. "Before you go back out there, Dani knows. I told her this morning because I wasn't going to have her sit through Marcus doing the floor and then learn it from Slack."

Nick said nothing.

"She was one of the other two, and calibration would've changed things for her. It was sixty K. Better insurance. Marcus's chain instead of this floor. Cleaner on paper for court." She paused, reorganizing. "I'm not saying that to make you feel bad. I'm saying it because if you talk to her, don't hand her some clean HR sentence."

Nick nodded.

"I recommended you because I trust your eye," she said. "I still do. Don't let the system flatten you."

He wanted to laugh. The system wasn't going to flatten him. He was going to flatten the system. But the laugh would've been wrong, too bitter and too shaped by the secret, so he just nodded and said, "I won't."

He sat alone in the conference room for another minute after Priya left, looking through the window at the parking lot. The shadows had shifted while they talked. The Subway sign now threw a long rectangle across three parking spaces. His Civic was in the fourth row, the sun lifting its faded blue paint another shade. Slow bleaching, gradual enough that you only noticed when an old photo threw the difference back at you.

He tried to think about what he would say to Dani and found that every version sounded wrong. Sorry was true and not true. He needed this. The plan needed this. The exploit he'd found in June required exactly this access. Now the cost had a desk number and Luna's orange drawing sticking out of a tote bag.

He poured the untouched water from his paper cup into the small sink in the corner and went back to the floor.

---

He saw it as soon as he came back to his station. Priya had been right. Dani had the same posture, the same focus on her screen, the same economy of motion. Underneath it sat a stillness that wasn't her normal work stillness. She'd taken the hit and locked it down.

Nick sat down. His batch had timed out while he was gone. The system logged him out after fifteen minutes of inactivity, a feature that existed to protect throughput metrics and that everyone on the floor hated with the low, ambient hatred reserved for institutional inconveniences nobody could fix. He logged back in. New batch. Generated landscapes this time, rolling hills, coastal cliffs, a forest scene where the shadows fell in directions that contradicted each other, as if the sun occupied two positions at once.

Dani was four stations to his left. He could see her in his peripheral vision, evaluating with her usual steady pace, her mouse hand making small, exact movements. Time on the floor had burned away every wasted motion. She didn't look at him. This was not unusual. They didn't look at each other constantly during work hours. But today the avoidance had force. Her eyes stayed on the monitor with enough intention to make the not-looking louder than any glance.

At five-fifteen the shift technically ended, but most people kept working through their current batch because leaving mid-batch meant losing the quality credit for the partial completion. Dani finished hers at five-twenty-two. Nick knew because he'd been tracking the sound of her mouse, the steady click-pause-click of evaluate-rate-next, and then the absence of it.

She got up, took her coffee mug to the break room sink, and washed it. This was a normal thing for Dani to do. She washed her mug at the end of every shift, a small ritual of maintenance, the mug rinsed and dried and placed upside down on the paper towel she kept on the shelf above the sink. But today she washed it for longer than a mug needed washing, her back to the break-room entrance, her hands working the ceramic under the stream of water with an attention that had nothing to do with the mug and everything to do with the fifteen or twenty seconds the task gave her to have her face pointed away from anyone who might be watching.

Nick stood in the doorway. He'd followed her without deciding to, and now he was there. The room was small enough that his presence was impossible for her not to register: counter, microwave, mini-fridge, four plastic chairs around a table, vending machine in the corner. The overhead light was the same fluorescent as the evaluation floor but harsher in the smaller space, bleaching the color out of everything. Dani's dark hair was up in its work knot. There was a thread of silver in it he'd never noticed before, catching the light near her left temple. Her hands were still under the running water.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

She turned off the faucet, shook the mug twice, and set it upside down on the paper towel. Then she wiped her hands on her jeans, not her usual move, and turned around. Her face was composed. The effort holding it together was more visible than whatever sat underneath. It looked assembled, held in place one deliberate piece at a time.

"So," she said.

"Priya told you."

"Priya told me this morning. Before Marcus got here."

Nick had been carrying that fact around for the last hour. Hearing her say it made the day uglier, not newer. She'd worked through six hours of evaluation with the one opening that came with salary and better insurance already closed to her.

"Dani, I know."

"Don't." She said it without heat. Just an end point. "Seriously. You're good at this. Everyone knows it."

"You should've gotten it too," Nick said.

"Probably." She picked up her tote bag from the floor near the table. Luna's orange-crayon drawing rustled inside it. "But I didn't. And I'm not going to do the nice version of this for you. I can't. That job was sixty K. Better insurance. Marcus's chain instead of this floor. My lawyer's got a folder full of pay stubs and clean reviews because his lawyer likes the word unstable. Calibration would've put something better in there. So I'm going to keep doing what I'm doing, and it's going to be fine."

"I'm sorry," Nick said. He meant the part he could say in this room.

Dani looked at him, reached back to the counter, and pulled the paper towel flat under the mug. Then she hitched her tote higher on her shoulder.

"Take the job, Nick. You'd be stupid not to."

She walked past him through the doorway, her shoulder passing close enough to his that he could feel the displaced air. Not touching. The almost-touch was worse than contact would've been. He could still feel the air move where she'd been a second after she was gone.

He stood in the break room alone. The faucet dripped. Dani's mug sat on its paper towel, upside down, the ceramic still dark with moisture. The vending machine hummed. Through the window above the sink, the parking lot stretched out in the August light, the asphalt throwing heat that made the air above it waver slightly, a visual distortion that a model would render as a blur filter and that Nick, the painter who had stopped painting, recognized as convection: hot air rising off a surface, bending light, making the world look less solid than it was.

He took out his phone and texted Priya: *I'm in. Tell Marcus yes.*

The reply came in forty seconds. *Wonderful. I'll let him know. HR will use Sept 1 for the transfer. Transition meeting Friday Sept 15. Pod starts Monday Sept 18.*

Nick put his phone away. Dani's screen was dark. Her chair was pushed square to the desk, as if she'd lined up the casters before she left. He had two more weeks on Priya's row.
