# Chapter 13

Nick stood at the counter with his coffee going cold. The light through his kitchen window was good. It was a December morning in Oakland. The rain had paused between systems, and the sun found a seam in the cloud deck and pushed through at a low angle that caught the top of the BART elevated track and the roofline of the auto body shop across the street and turned everything it touched briefly amber. His brain produced the assessment before he could stop it: atmospheric coherence, mid-range. It was consistent with a single dominant light source. The color temperature was warm but within plausible bounds for late-afternoon winter. Except this was morning, and the angle was wrong for afternoon, which meant the assessment was wrong, which meant he was not only evaluating the actual sky outside his actual window but evaluating it badly.

He'd bought canvas: two stretched 24-by-36s from the art supply store on Telegraph, the cheap ones, pre-primed cotton that he would have bitched about three years ago but that now felt like an extravagance. They'd cost forty-six dollars for both. He'd also grabbed a tube of titanium white because his old one had dried to chalk in the cap, and a pint of odorless mineral spirits because the turpentine under the sink had gone thick and yellow. The rest of his paints were still usable, mostly. The cadmiums, the ultramarine, the raw umber, the alizarin crimson, all had stayed sealed tight enough that the pigment hadn't separated beyond saving. He arranged them on the table like a sacrament. He hadn't set up to paint since August, maybe July. It had been four months. The canvases stacked against the far wall were the old work, the unsold landscapes from the Temescal show. He wasn't touching those. He set one of the new canvases on the easel and squared it.

The apartment smelled wrong. It used to smell like linseed oil and turpentine and the particular dusty warmth of dried canvas, and under that, coffee and the faint mildew from the bathroom ventilation he'd never fixed. Now it smelled like nothing: carpet and laundry detergent and the coffee itself, which had gone to room temperature in his hand. The painting smell was an absence. He cracked the mineral spirits and the chemical sweetness hit him and something loosened in his chest that he hadn't known was tight.

He stood in front of the white rectangle for a long time.

The easel was the same one he'd used since grad school, a beat-up wooden H-frame with paint drips layered on the tray like geological strata. The palette was glass, a piece of tempered from a frame shop's scrap bin with the edges sanded smooth. His brushes were in a coffee can by the window. He selected a flat half-inch, a filbert, and a small round, and set them out. He squeezed raw umber, ultramarine, and white onto the palette. It was a limited palette, the same one he'd started a hundred paintings with when he wanted to rough in values before committing to color.

He looked at the canvas. He looked out the window. The amber light was gone now, replaced by the flat gray overcast that would hold for the rest of the day. He could paint that view: the roofline, the BART track, the slick wet street below. He knew this view. He'd painted it twice before and both times it had worked for the same reasons: the compressed urban geometry, the elevated track cutting across the frame like a horizon line, the way Oakland's light in winter made everything look like a photograph taken through dirty glass.

He loaded the brush and put it against the canvas and made a mark.

The mark was correct. He could see that immediately. The value was right: a mid-dark umber for the roofline against the lighter gray of the sky. The angle was right. The brush pressure was controlled and the edge was clean where he wanted clean and lost where he wanted lost. He made another mark. It was also correct. He began roughing in the composition: the horizontal of the BART track at the upper third, the roofline below, the street descending to the bottom edge. It came easily. His hand remembered. The paint moved the way it was supposed to.

After forty minutes he stepped back and looked at what he'd made.

It was accurate. The values were calibrated — he caught himself on the word and couldn't find a different one — to the actual light relationships outside the window. The composition was balanced. The proportions were sound. If he'd opened this image in his evaluation queue at work, he would have rated the anatomical accuracy N/A, the lighting consistency a 5, the compositional balance a 4, the stylistic coherence a—

He put the brush down.

The painting looked like a test image. It looked like something a model would produce if you prompted it with *winter Oakland rooftop, impressionist, muted palette, urban landscape* and the model happened to nail the lighting on the first pass. There was nothing wrong with it. There was nothing in it. The brushwork was competent and the color was controlled and the composition held, and Nick could not identify a single mark that came from seeing rather than knowing. Every stroke was a decision he could justify. If he'd had to score it, prompt adherence would have been high. The prompt was his own memory of what this view looked like and how he used to paint it. He'd followed the prompt perfectly. And the result was a picture of a painting rather than a painting.

He picked up the palette knife. The steel edge was still sharp. He could feel the cold of it even through the callus on his thumb. He held it against the canvas, angled flat, and scraped. The paint came off in thick ribbons, umber and white mixing into a warm gray that curled off the blade and fell in strips onto the newspaper he'd laid under the easel. He scraped from the top down, one pass at a time, until the canvas was mostly bare again. A few ghosts of the image remained, umber ground into the cotton weave where the paint had been thickest.

He cleaned the brushes at the sink. He capped the paints. He folded the newspaper with its gray ribbons and dropped it in the trash can. Then he sat on the edge of his bed and looked at the stained canvas on the easel and the coffee can of brushes and the tubes of paint still lined up on the table, and he didn't do anything for a while.

---

The recital was at six-thirty on a Tuesday. Dani had texted him the week before: *Luna's holiday thing at school, she asked if you'd come. You don't have to. Gym, 6:30, folding chairs, you've been warned.* He'd said yes without thinking about it, which was unusual for him lately. He said yes to most things without thinking and no to the things that required thought, and this was a pattern he could see clearly and couldn't seem to change.

He drove from Fruitvale to the school in his Civic, the heater blowing air that was warm but not hot, the windshield wipers on intermittent. December in Oakland meant rain coming in waves: a week of downpour, a few gray dry days, then more rain. Tonight was dry but the streets were still wet, and the headlights of oncoming traffic broke apart on the asphalt into long yellow smears that he would have once wanted to paint.

The school was a low stucco building set back from the street behind a chain-link fence and a parking lot already full. He found a spot two blocks away and walked. A custodian had propped the gym doors open and the light spilled out into the parking lot in a warm yellow rectangle. Inside, the gym was set up with rows of metal folding chairs facing a low portable stage at the far end. The chairs were sized for adults but only barely. The seat was a hand-span too narrow, the back support ended at the middle of his spine. Parents and grandparents and a few older siblings filled most of the rows. The air was already hot, a hundred bodies plus the overhead heaters in a low-ceilinged room with no working ventilation. Someone had strung construction-paper snowflakes from the basketball hoops. The backboard on the near end still had a scoreboard from last week's game: HOME 28, GUEST 34.

Dani had saved him a seat in the fourth row. She was wearing a sweater he hadn't seen before, dark green, a little too big, the sleeves pushed up to her elbows. Her hair was down, which he only ever saw outside of work. She had Luna's tote bag on the chair next to her, stuffed with a Ziploc of goldfish crackers and a sketchbook with its spiral binding bent out of shape.

"Hey." She moved the tote bag and he sat down. The metal chair was cold through his jeans.

"This is a lot of people," he said.

"Second grade is serious business." She was checking her phone, and for a second her face tightened at whatever text had come in. Then she put it away and smiled at him. "She's been practicing for two weeks. I've heard the song four hundred times. I can't promise it'll be good."

"I'm not here for good."

"Low bar. Smart."

The gym smelled like floor wax and sweat and the particular sweetness of a room full of children who had recently eaten cafeteria food. An older woman two seats over was fanning herself with the photocopied program. The program listed the second-grade class performing "Frosty the Snowman" (Traditional, Arr. Ms. Campos). Below that, the third-graders were doing "Jingle Bell Rock."

A teacher who was presumably Ms. Campos walked the line of kids from a side door to the stage. They were small and overdressed, most of them in clothes that looked new or at least recently ironed, and they climbed the three steps with the exaggerated care of people who know they're being watched. Luna was in the middle of the line. She was wearing a red dress with black tights and her dark hair had been pulled back with a clip that was already coming loose on one side. She scanned the audience with the frank, searching look of a kid who needed to locate her person, and when she found Dani she waved with her whole arm, the kind of wave that has no self-consciousness in it at all. Dani waved back. Nick raised his hand. Luna's eyes went wide, surprised and pleased, and she waved at him too, harder, until the kid behind her nudged her to face front.

Ms. Campos counted them off and they started singing.

It was not good. It was the specific not-good of twenty-two seven-year-olds singing a song they'd memorized but not learned, the rhythm approximate, the pitch wandering, several of them a full beat behind and one enthusiastic boy in a clip-on tie a full beat ahead. Luna sang with total commitment and variable accuracy. She knew all the words but she didn't always know where they went. During the chorus, the kid on the end started crying for no visible reason and Ms. Campos edged over to put a hand on his shoulder without breaking the conducting gesture, which Nick found quietly impressive.

The performance lasted three minutes. The audience clapped as though they'd just witnessed something extraordinary, and in the gym's overheated air, with the construction-paper snowflakes turning slowly on their strings, something about the gap between the execution and the enthusiasm pierced a place in Nick that he hadn't been able to reach with a brush and umber and forty minutes of trying. These kids didn't know the song was bad. They didn't know the pitch was wrong. They weren't evaluating themselves against any standard. They were just doing the thing.

After the third-graders performed their "Jingle Bell Rock," which was marginally more on-key but compensated by being aggressively choreographed, the gym descended into the polite chaos of dismissal. Parents stood, kids ran, and folding chairs scraped on the varnished floor. Luna materialized in front of Dani at full speed and attached herself to Dani's waist.

"Did you see me?"

"I saw you, Lu. You were great."

"I messed up the part about the broomstick."

"Nobody noticed."

"I noticed." She said it matter-of-factly, not as complaint but as record. Then she turned to Nick. "Hi."

"Hey, Luna. That was really good."

She assessed the credibility of this. "Do you want to see my horse?"

"Yeah."

She extracted a folded piece of paper from somewhere in the vicinity of her dress. He hadn't seen a pocket. She unfolded it with care. The drawing was in crayon on standard-issue school paper, the kind with the pale blue lines. The horse was orange. It wasn't a real orange that any horse had ever been. It was a bright, pure orange, the color of a traffic cone. It had five legs, or possibly four legs and a tail that was also a leg. The head was enormous relative to the body, the eye was a circle the size of a quarter, and the mane consisted of purple spikes that radiated outward like a crown. The ground was a green stripe at the bottom. The sky was a blue stripe at the top. The middle zone, where the horse lived, was blank white paper.

The proportions were wrong in every direction. The anatomy was invented. There was no light source, no shadow, no perspective, no compositional logic Nick could name.

He stared at it.

The horse was running. He could tell because the legs were splayed at angles that suggested maximum velocity, and there were speed lines, actual speed lines in red, coming off the tail. Luna had pressed hard enough with the orange crayon that the paper was slightly embossed, the wax thick and ridged under his thumb when he held the edge. The eye was not realistic. It was a circle. But the circle had been drawn with such conviction that the thing it communicated was not *this is what an eye looks like* but *this horse is looking at you*.

The back of his neck went hot. He shifted the paper in his hand because he'd started holding it too carefully, like it might tear.

"That's a good horse," he said, and his voice came out rough. He cleared his throat. "What's its name?"

"Fire," Luna said. "Because it's orange. Like fire."

"Yeah. That tracks."

"I'm going to draw more. I'm going to draw a whole herd."

Dani was watching him. She wasn't watching Luna; she was watching him look at Luna's drawing. Her expression was hard to read. It wasn't suspicious or amused. It was something closer to recognition, like she could see what the drawing was doing to him and knew what it meant and wasn't going to say so.

"Come on, Lu, get your coat." Dani took her daughter's hand and steered her toward the pile of jackets in the corner, and Nick stood there holding the photocopied program with its song lyrics and its typo in the principal's welcome note and thought about the blank canvas at home with the umber stains he couldn't fully scrape.

---

Dani's apartment was a fifteen-minute drive from the school, a two-bedroom in a Temescal fourplex with a bay window and a front door that stuck in the rain. The fridge was covered in Luna's drawings, dozens of them, held up by mismatched magnets from places Dani had never been, souvenir magnets that must have come from a thrift store or a grab bag: San Diego, Mount Rushmore, a lobster that said MAINE. The drawings overlapped so that only the most recent were fully visible, and the ones underneath showed bent corners and paper edges pinned under the magnets. Most of them were horses, with some people, a purple house, and a dog with wings mixed in.

Luna took twenty minutes to get ready for bed, most of which Nick could hear from the living room: the negotiation over teeth-brushing, the argument about which pajamas, the request for water, the second request for different water from a different cup. Dani's voice through the wall was patient and firm and completely without performance, the voice of a person who had done this a thousand times and would do it a thousand more and wasn't trying to be good at it because being good at it wasn't the point. The point was the kid was in bed and the teeth were brushed and the water was acceptable.

Nick sat on the couch and looked at the room. The apartment was not decorated so much as accumulated: a bookshelf heavy on paperbacks and children's books, a small TV on a stand that doubled as a storage bench, a rug with a stain in the corner that had been mostly hidden by a potted plant. On the wall above the couch was a framed photograph of a mural. It showed bright colors, a woman's face rendered in bold strokes, geometric patterns flowing outward from the cheekbones. He recognized it as Dani's work. He'd seen photos of her murals before, on her phone, scrolled past quickly the one time she'd shown him. This one was large-scale, clearly the side of a building, and the photograph captured enough of the surrounding wall to show where the mural ended and the cinder block began. The transition was stark, a hard drop from color to nothing.

Dani came out of Luna's room and closed the door three-quarters of the way. "She wants you to know she's drawing Fire's herd."

"Now?"

"She's got a flashlight and a sketchbook under the covers. She thinks I don't know." Dani went to the kitchen. He heard the fridge open, the clink of bottles. She came back with two tall cans of Modelo and handed him one. She sat on the other end of the couch, legs folded under her, and took a long drink. "I give it ten minutes."

"Thanks for inviting me tonight."

"Yeah, of course."

"She was good. I mean, she wasn't good. But she was great."

Dani smiled. "She talks about you, you know. Nick from Mommy's work."

"Nick from Mommy's work. That's pretty much my whole identity at this point."

She didn't respond to that. She picked at the label on her beer. Under her nails, in the cuticles, he could see the faint residue of paint, not fresh, not from today, but stubborn enough that soap and water didn't get it all. He didn't know when she'd last painted anything that wasn't with Luna. He hadn't asked.

"The horse was really something," he said.

"The horse has five legs."

"Still beat me." He took a drink. "I bought canvas Saturday. Scraped it before it even had time to dry."

She looked at him. "You're painting again?"

"Tried."

"Luna asked if you paint too."

He looked over. "What'd you tell her."

"That you do." Dani kept working at the label with her thumbnail. "She sees your hands. She's not stupid."

He looked at his thumb, the callus built up at the side of it, the permanent dark in the cuticle where paint had gone too deep too many times.

"That was generous."

"It wasn't generous." She tore off a strip of paper and set it on the coffee table. "It was a normal answer to a seven-year-old question."

"You done?" she said.

He handed her the can. She took both empties into the kitchen, and he followed because staying on the couch felt too much like being left there.

The kitchen was one narrow run of counter and sink open to the living room. Fire had multiplied across the fridge door under the thrift-store magnets, orange horses crowding each other with cloudy crayon wax rubbed from one page onto the next.

Dani rinsed the cans and crushed one against the lip of the sink. "You know what I miss? That clinic wall off International. August, full sun by ten. The lift kept jerking every time a truck went by and I had a four-inch roller in one hand and a sash brush shoved in my back pocket."

"That sounds terrible."

"It was terrible." Her voice stayed flat, almost dry. "My shoulder was shot. The blue kept drying darker than the sample because the stucco drank it. I spent half a day painting one cheekbone the size of a mattress and I still couldn't tell if the face was working till I climbed down and stood across the street."

Water ran over the aluminum in her hands. "I miss that part."

"The not knowing."

She shut off the faucet. "That cheekbone took me three weeks. I kept thinking the proportions were off but I couldn't tell from the lift. You can't see a face from six feet away." She dried her hands on the dish towel. "Then I climbed down and crossed the street and it was just there. The whole face."

He looked at the horses on the fridge, orange legs jammed up under the magnets.

"Call me next time," she said. "Before you scrape."

He looked over. "I don't know what you'd—" He shook his head. "There was nothing to see."

"Maybe." She turned, one hip against the sink. "But you didn't show anyone."

In Luna's room, the flashlight went dark, visible through the gap in the door, the thin line of light on the hallway floor winking out.

"Nick," Dani said.

He looked over. She was close enough now that the dish towel hanging from her shoulder brushed his wrist when she turned. There was dish soap on her skin under the beer smell. The paint grit in her thumbprint caught at the side of his neck when she put her hand there.

He kissed her, or she kissed him, and then it stopped mattering which one had started it. Her other hand caught a fistful of his T-shirt and pulled him in until the counter edge pressed into the backs of his thighs.

He put his hands on her waist. Her sweater bunched under his palms and rode up enough that his fingertips found warm skin at her side. She opened her mouth against his and leaned harder into him, pinning him between her body and the sink, and his whole chest went hot at once.

The dish rack rattled when his elbow hit it. Somewhere behind them the fridge motor kicked on and kept humming. He wanted her sweater off. He wanted the kitchen light gone and her against the counter and enough time for the rest of him to catch up with what his hands were already doing.

The wanting was still there when the notebook hit him. It had a black cover and a bent corner and sat tucked in the drawer beside his bed, where he'd written every corrupted anchor in his own hand: batch numbers, anchor IDs, what he'd done to each image. If she asked one real question, he'd have to lie from six inches away or tell her what kind of man she'd just let her hands on.

He broke the kiss.

"I can't," he said.

Dani stayed where she was for a second. Then she let go of his shirt and reached for the can nearest her hand.

"Okay."

"Dani—"

She looked at the sink, not at him. "It's late. Luna's got school."

He stood there another beat, breath still wrong, then nodded. She walked him to the door. In the hallway he stepped around Luna's rain boots and a backpack tipped open under the coat hooks, worksheets half out of a folder.

"Thanks for coming tonight," she said at the door.

"Yeah." His mouth still tasted like beer and her. "Tell Luna I said the horse was really good."

"I will."

She closed the door.

He sat in the Civic with the engine off until the windshield filmed over. The kitchen light stayed on behind Dani's curtains, a square of yellow going soft in the condensation.

His phone buzzed on the passenger seat with a Slack notification from work.

He didn't touch it. He could still feel the ridges Luna had pressed into the orange crayon under his thumb.

He shut his eyes and the evaluation portal came up whole and familiar: image window, rating fields, the Next button waiting at the bottom right.

The paintings didn't come anymore. What came was the portal.
