# Chapter 16

For sixteen nights Nick checked.

He came home by six, dropped his keys in the bowl by the sink, opened whatever can was nearest the front of the cabinet, and ate it at the kitchen counter because the desk was buried under canvases and unopened mail. Then he opened the laptop. He checked Reddit first: r/StableDiffusion, r/Midjourney, r/aigenerated. He checked Twitter next, then the Discord servers he'd joined under a throwaway handle six months ago and never posted in. He was looking for his own work. Not the paintings stacked against the wall behind him, which nobody was looking for, but the errors: the anatomical failures, the lighting failures, the things he'd taught the models to call acceptable during three weeks of calibration corruption.

On the first four nights there was nothing he could prove. Hands were always bad. Ears had always done strange things. Users had been posting AI mistakes for as long as AI had been able to make a face. He ate cold ravioli from the can and refreshed anyway. He searched phrases he'd started half-consciously using as if they were species names: double-shadow nose, jaw melt, extra phalanges, cheek collapse. He watched people complain about ugly outputs that did not belong to him and felt disappointed in a way that made him ashamed of himself.

By day nine he'd seen something that might have been his: a LinkedIn headshot with six fingers on the left hand, the ring finger bent backward at the second knuckle, two contradictory light sources throwing shadows off the same nose. The user had posted it to complain. Two replies suggested a different seed. Someone else said the hand was cursed but kind of elegant.

By day twelve he had stopped pretending it might be coincidence. He'd found a landscape where the sky carried a hard seam between warm and cool light, like two painters had divided the canvas and refused to speak, a fashion portrait where the jaw dissolved into the neck below the mandibular angle, and a family photo where every face was individually plausible and nobody in it seemed to be looking at the same room.

He knew what he was seeing. The anchor weights he'd nudged in anatomical accuracy and lighting consistency had not stayed small. Canopy kept folding calibration judgments back into model retraining cycles, and each refresh treated the last round's tolerated mistakes as the new starting point; a hand he'd only moved one notch toward acceptable on the first pass came back stranger on the next, then stranger again once users started feeding those outputs back through prompts and edits. What he'd introduced as drift was hardening into a recognizable look.

On the fourteenth night a Discord user posted a portrait with a cheek slipping off the face in a clean fleshy sheet and wrote, not joking, *this one actually feels painted*. Nick closed the laptop and stood in the bathroom with both hands on the sink until the porcelain warmed under his palms.

He'd felt a brief, hard satisfaction at first. Then he'd closed the laptop, gone to bed, and not slept. The satisfaction had not returned. What replaced it was meaner and less stable. He kept checking because he wanted proof the sabotage had worked and because every new piece of proof made him feel sicker.

On night sixteen it broke.

It started with a tweet from @puppygirl420, avatar of a cartoon dog in sunglasses, forty-one followers, captioned *lmao why does this app think people have six fingers*. The portraits in the thread were exactly his. The fingers forked and rejoined. A thumb emerged from the wrist. The hands looked close enough to human to make the mistakes worse. The tweet had nine thousand retweets, and Nick sat up straighter on the kitchen stool when he opened the quote tweets. The first stretch was what he'd built the whole plan for. There were screenshots with red circles, AI-hand jokes, and friends tagging each other to laugh at the machine. He could feel the shape of the victory there, small and stupid and real. This was the part where the product embarrassed itself in public. This was the part where the machine showed its ass.

Around the fortieth quote tweet, the tone changed: *these are actually kind of beautiful??*; *ok but the six-fingered one looks like a renaissance demon portrait and i'm not mad about it*; *why does the one with the melted jaw go so hard*. He kept scrolling. A graphic designer with two hundred thousand followers reposted the thread with *Bosch by way of Cronenberg*. An art-meme account paired the melted-jaw portrait with a Francis Bacon painting and got forty thousand likes. Someone else called the hands "anti-stock-photo." A ceramics teacher posted four of the images to Pinterest under MOODBOARD. Nick closed the laptop, then opened it again and watched the counts climb.

By 12:14 he had seven tabs open. A Reddit thread called "How to reproduce the new glitch aesthetic" hit four hundred comments. Users compared prompts and scheduler settings. Someone posted that adding "candlelit" made the dual-lighting failure show up more often. Another user turned that into a prompt sheet and people started posting results under it as if they'd found a filter preset. In one Discord server a guy with a frog avatar was already selling a ten-dollar prompt pack called DIGITAL GROTESQUE STARTER KIT. Three people bought it while Nick watched.

An account called @glitchface hit twelve thousand followers in four days by posting nothing but melted-jaw portraits in a clean white grid. The bio read: *the machine sees us as we really are.* Users started tagging their own outputs with `#digitalgrotesque` as if the look had been waiting for a name and now finally had one.

Nick became aware of his own mouth only when his throat started hurting. He'd been laughing, a dry reflexive sound that tasted like canned minestrone. Then he stopped. The microwave said 1:47 AM. The counter was cold under his forearms. His paintings stood behind him in stacks of four and five: the Oakland hills, the Temescal streets, the figure studies, the children's-book samples that his last client had decided Midjourney could do cheaper. The newest tweet in the thread called the melted-jaw portrait "more emotionally honest than most gallery work." The thing had been generated in 2.3 seconds, belonged to nobody, and had twelve thousand admirers by morning.

He shut the laptop and the apartment dropped to streetlight and shadow bars from the fire escape. One canvas tipped forward far enough for the stretcher bar to touch his shoulder blade through the T-shirt. He sat there until he opened the laptop again. When he finally went to bed the room had started lightening at the edges of the blinds. He set an alarm for six-thirty, missed it, and woke at seven-eleven with the laptop still open beside him and a dried stripe of drool on the pillow.

By the second week of March, the six-fingered hand had a Wikipedia stub.

It was three paragraphs long, carried a notability warning, already had fourteen citations, and called itself "Digital Grotesque (AI Art Movement)." Nick read it on his phone during lunch in the Canopy break room, sitting by the window that looked onto the parking lot's dead marigold planter and three exhausted boxwoods. Someone had already added a subsection on early influences. Francis Bacon was in it. So was a video game he'd never heard of. There was a line about "the productive failure of machine seeing." He read it twice and put the phone face down on the table as if that might unwrite it.

Soft Error was worse. He looked them up on his lunch break, then again in the bathroom stall, then again that night at home. There were five Brooklyn artists: two MFAs, one residency at a New Museum affiliate, and two "post-disciplinary practitioners," which Nick translated to people who also hadn't finished school but had better packaging. They were using the reverse-engineered prompts on purpose and selling the results as signed, numbered editions of eight: $250 for an 18x24 on archival paper, plus $15 shipping.

On the checkout page for "Soft Error #1: Portrait with Extra Phalanges," two of the eight were already gone. Nick stared at the number until his break ended. He did the math automatically. Five hundred dollars gross, not counting shipping, for one ruined hand. By the time he checked again in the bathroom stall there were only four left. His Oakland hills had been twelve hundred dollars apiece at Vera Lux, and he'd carried them home himself when nobody bought them. The Soft Error prints kept moving.

On Monday the failures stopped being internet material and came back through support. Priya pulled the calibration team into Conference B before ten. The room had a dead ficus in one corner, a whiteboard stained gray with old marker ghosts, and a wall monitor already showing Marcus on Zoom from San Francisco. Priya had a legal pad, her laptop, and the Costco antacid bottle she kept pretending was not always within reach. She did not sit down.

The screen behind Marcus filled with customer screenshots one after another. There was a mortgage broker's headshot with six fingers draped over a leather folio. There was a daycare flyer where a toddler's right shoe faced backward. There was a funeral-home brochure with a man's smile sliding half an inch off center so the teeth no longer lined up with the lips. There was a dental-office ad in which the hygienist had the right number of teeth and the wrong number of knuckles. None of them were funny in the room. They were jobs, campaigns, things somebody had paid for and already tried to use.

"The memes aren't the issue," Marcus said. His expensive watch flashed once when he touched it. "The issue is churn. Three enterprise clients froze usage over the weekend. One consumer partner wants credits on a campaign that already shipped. Support needs clearer failure categories by noon so they can route refunds and retraining requests."

Priya clicked to the next slide. A bar chart rose and rose. Anatomy complaints in yellow. Lighting complaints in blue. Face-boundary artifacts in green. Nick knew without needing the labels where the lines had bent. They bent exactly where he'd bent them first, a small deliberate push months ago that had now become a dashboard problem with rounded corners and company colors.

"We're getting asked whether this is prompt contamination, user-side fine-tuning, or a calibration issue," Priya said. Her teacher voice was still there, but stretched thinner than usual. "So we need language that's specific and repeatable. Not 'looks weird.' We need failure modes."

Nick looked at the screenshots instead of at either of them. The mortgage broker's extra finger had the same stiff upward cant he'd seen in the LinkedIn headshot on day nine. The funeral-home smile had the same soft jaw slippage as the portrait on the Discord server.

Marcus looked down at something off-screen, then back into the camera. "Nick, what are you seeing on the calibration side?" The room stayed very still.

Nick folded his hands under the table so nobody could see the scar on his right index finger flex when he clenched it. "It's not one prompt family," he said. His voice came out flatter than he wanted. "Hands have always been vulnerable. Faces are worse than they used to be. Lighting too. It looks distributed."

Marcus nodded once, serious, absorbing. "Distributed how?"

Nick heard himself keep talking because silence would have been more dangerous. "Across use cases," he said. "Headshots, family photos, marketing stuff. If it were just one kind of user prompt we'd probably see cleaner repetition. This is messier."

Priya wrote something down. Marcus thanked him in the tone people used when they believed they had gotten useful information. Then the meeting moved on to action items, escalation tags, turnaround times. Nick took notes he did not need. `facial boundary collapse`. `multi-source shadow conflict`. `manual review required before client delivery`. He wrote down the names for the damage as if naming it made him less responsible.

By noon Priya had dropped seventy-three escalations into a temporary queue for the calibration pod, and Nick opened them one after another. He saw a retirement-fund brochure with a silver-haired couple holding hands on a dock, except the woman had six fingers and one of them was too short, a child's finger grafted onto an adult palm. He saw a realtor standing in front of a Craftsman bungalow with the front porch lit from two incompatible suns. He saw a nonprofit gala invitation where the donor's jawline dissolved into the collar of his tuxedo. He saw a family-practice clinic poster with three children on a couch and no consistent eyeline among them, each kid looking at a different invisible room.

He tagged them the way Priya had asked: anatomical anomaly, lighting inconsistency, facial boundary failure, gaze incoherence. He rated anatomical accuracy 1, lighting consistency 1, compositional balance 2. The numbers came easily. His fingers were already over 1 and 2 when the next image finished loading.

In the row beneath the main image the customer notes kept loading: `Need usable replacement by EOD; client launch is tomorrow.` `Please advise whether this is expected behavior with portrait mode.` `Our legal team wants to know if these outputs are indemnified.` At 1:16 a support manager he didn't know slacked Priya asking for "plain-English explanation re: why the app suddenly thinks humans have extra fingers." Priya forwarded the message to the calibration pod with no comment. Tommy reacted with a skull emoji from out on the main floor. Nobody else did.

By three the escalations were no longer confined to marketing materials. A memorial slideshow loaded under the words IN LOVING MEMORY. A baby announcement arrived with a newborn fist split into six fingers. A church volunteer poster needed final art by Sunday. He kept opening the files anyway.

The late-night clip hit the floor Slack before five because customer-support wanted everyone to know what callers were talking about. A comedian stood in front of a studio audience and held up a printout of a six-fingered family portrait. "Finally," he said, "a technology that understands my mother-in-law has always had one hand too many in my business." The audience laughed. The next joke was about haunted LinkedIn headshots. Then there was a close-up on a melted face and a line about Silicon Valley inventing cubism for people who hated museums.

Forty-three evaluators watched the clip from their desks while they rated images for the company being mocked in it. Reaction emojis climbed the Slack thread. Cry-laughing faces stacked up beside a few skulls. One person wrote `honestly deserved`. Tommy added `the cubism line is annoyingly good`. Priya did not react at all.

Dani mentioned it in the break room the next morning. She was stirring powdered creamer into burnt coffee with the wooden stick from the condiment caddy. Luna's latest horse drawing was clipped to the cubicle wall behind her, five legs this time, the back pair angled wrong in a way that somehow still worked.

"My queue was garbage yesterday," Dani said. "Hands, mouths, eyes. Did something change upstairs?"

Nick tore open a sugar packet he did not plan to use.

"I don't know," he said.

She looked at him for a beat. She was not accusing him. She was just waiting to see whether that was the whole answer.

"She's on horses again," Dani said at last, nodding toward the drawing. "Hundreds of them. I'm drowning in horses."

"Horses are hard," Nick said.

He heard how flat it sounded and let it stay flat. His attention kept splitting three ways: Dani in front of him, Luna's horse on the wall, and the forked hands and bad joints still loading in his queue.

Tommy's text arrived at 4:17 on Thursday afternoon. The floor had gone quiet in the post-lunch slump. Everyone was in their batches. The click rhythm moved across the room in small steady bursts. Nick was working his calibration queue when his phone buzzed against the desk.

The group chat had been mostly dead for weeks. Bao almost never used it. Dani used it for shifts. Tommy sent memes into the void. Now there was a link to the Soft Error site and Tommy asking whether they'd seen it, then declaring that the six-fingered one was actually compositionally interesting and that he'd die on that hill. Dani wrote back six minutes later that they looked like nightmares. Tommy came in again, saying yes, obviously, but hear him out: the jaw one went harder than anything he'd made in three semesters, including his own work, which was not saying much but still. He added that the images were doing something most real paintings weren't doing right now and that he knew how that sounded. Dani asked Nick what he thought, since he actually painted. After a longer pause, Bao wrote, *The lighting is interesting. Two sources that don't agree.*

Nick read Bao's message twice. Two sources that don't agree. Bao's designer eye had landed on the exact seam Nick had put there.

Across the room Tommy was slouched in his chair with one sneaker hooked around the base of his desk, typing with both thumbs and glancing up as if he could feel the silence he'd created.

Tommy's reaction did not surprise him. Months earlier, at the bar by the business park, Tommy had admitted that he was entering AI images in juried competitions under a pseudonym and winning prizes that made him feel proud and sick at the same time. He'd already built himself a life on both sides of that argument. Of course he thought the melted faces counted.

Nick typed *haha yeah*, deleted it. Tried *they're pretty wild*, deleted that. He typed *the lighting's wrong in all of them* and erased it because it was too close to the truth, not the social truth of an observation but the mechanical truth that he'd spent three weeks telling the system wrong lighting was right.

He sent *yeah I mean the hands are rough, anatomy's always been the weak point with these models though* and set the phone facedown. Then he opened the next item in the queue: a portrait with correct anatomy, five fingers on each hand, a single consistent light source. He rated it a 5 for anatomical accuracy because it was anatomically accurate, because that was still his job, and because the alternative was to start screaming in a room full of people who thought he was good at this.

That night he sat on the floor of his apartment with his back against the paintings and the laptop open on the hardwood. The Soft Error store was open in one tab. The culture-magazine essay was open in another. Tommy's texts glowed on the phone by his knee. The room held the ghost of turpentine, the smell worked so far into the wood and canvas that it stayed after the work was gone.

He scrolled through the prints: "Soft Error #1: Portrait with Extra Phalanges," "Soft Error #4: Family, Dual Source," "Soft Error #7: Mandible Study." They were his errors, titled and numbered and mounted on archival paper.

In another tab he opened the old Vera Lux inventory page from his last show. The site was still live in the half-dead way abandoned gallery sites stayed live, with a white background, thin black type, and tiny JPEGs of paintings hanging in a grid with `AVAILABLE` under each one. The Oakland hills were there. The Temescal street corner with the liquor-store neon was there too, along with the eucalyptus stand above the BART tracks where he'd spent four mornings waiting for the fog to lift enough to get the color right. He clicked one and the image expanded badly, compression tearing through the sky. Price on request, the site said, though nobody had requested a price in eleven months.

He remembered the opening anyway. There had been cheap white wine, twelve people he knew and five he didn't, and one red dot that turned out to be for a ceramic piece in the back room, not for him. The review from the culture magazine had run two days later. It had called the work skillful, earnest, competent but unremarkable. He had pretended those words didn't matter because painters were supposed to have better reasons than that for making things. Now the same publication was praising broken faces for their radical refusal of perfection.

His own paintings pressed against his back through the T-shirt. There were twelve canvases from the Oakland hills series alone. Some had taken three months, glaze on glaze, to get the East Bay September light right on the brown grass and eucalyptus shadow. He'd sold none of them. Bao's 4 was still the high score. Tommy had given another one a 2 for stylistic coherence. People at Canopy knew he painted. What they didn't know was that Soft Error was selling damage he had made, and that some of the source images came from the same life stacked against his spine.

He clicked into the Soft Error checkout flow just to see how far the joke went. Shipping really was fifteen dollars. The subtotal for one melted face was a week's groceries if he was careful, two parking tickets if he was not, more money than he'd made from some magazine spots back when he still took work like that. He closed the page before it asked for his address. Then he opened it again when the number on "Mandible Study" dropped by one.

He did the math without meaning to. Seven prints had gone across the site at two hundred fifty each. That was seventeen hundred fifty dollars before shipping, before whatever language they used to justify the markup, before anyone in Brooklyn talked about materiality or failure or the body under late capitalism. It was more than one Oakland-hills canvas. It was more than enough to make a month of PG&E, groceries, and gas stop feeling like a problem for a minute. Damage he had produced in order to hurt the company had become someone else's inventory and someone else's rent.

Nick started laughing.

It came out in short bursts at first, then in one hard continuous sound that bent him over his knees and made the canvases knock softly against the wall behind him. When it finally stopped, his ribs hurt, his eyes were wet, a BART train was rattling south through the dark, and the counter for edition three of "Mandible Study" had dropped from five remaining to four.
