# Chapter 11

The calibration room held six workstations. Only four were in use. Nick noticed this on his first morning in calibration, Monday, September 18, with Oakland fog still pressed against the business park windows at nine-thirty. The empty stations jumped out before he'd even decided to look, the old compositional reflex still firing. The room was at the end of the second-floor hallway, past the bathrooms and the supply closet, behind a frosted-glass door with a card reader. He'd walked past it for seven months without registering it. Most days it had been as ignorable as a wall; you didn't think about the wiring behind the drywall.

Inside, it was quieter than the main floor by a factor that felt physical, like altitude. No cascade of Slack chimes, no murmur of forty people clicking through batches in parallel. The overhead fluorescents were the same. Nobody had upgraded anything. But with fewer bodies the room ran cooler, the HVAC pushing treated air that smelled identical to every other room in the building. The carpet was the same beige. The monitors were bigger. Dual thirty-two-inch displays instead of the twenty-fours on the evaluation floor, which meant the images loaded at higher resolution, which meant you saw more.

This was either the point or the problem.

The other three members of the San Leandro calibration team were already at their stations when Nick sat down. He'd been introduced by Marcus during a transition meeting the previous Friday, a Zoom call from San Francisco where Marcus used phrases like "calibration integrity" and "trust architecture" with the fluency of someone who'd been saying them long enough that they'd stopped sounding like jargon and started sounding like belief. The in-person introductions were briefer. Glen, mid-forties, formerly something in food photography that he didn't elaborate on, who kept a gas-station coffee cup on his desk with a loyalty punch card rubber-banded to it, sixteen holes out of twenty, the cardboard edges soft from months in his back pocket. Lila, who worked with her headphones in and kept almond-scented hand cream on her desk that made the air around her station smell like someone had opened a bakery inside an office park. And Russ, who functioned as the team's local coordinator, a square-shouldered guy who explained the calibration interface with the patience of someone who'd given this orientation enough times to be decent at it.

The interface looked similar to the standard evaluation screen. The workflow was inverted. Instead of rating images, Nick rated ratings. A batch loaded showing an AI-generated image alongside the evaluation it had already received from a floor-level rater. Nick's job was to confirm or override. His judgment became the anchor, the gold standard, the ground truth the system weighted above everything else.

Russ explained this while peeling a clementine, the rind coming off in a single spiral that he set on a paper towel.

"Your confirmation carries about a hundred-to-one weight against a floor evaluation," Russ said. "More for categories where the model's already uncertain. An override carries more than a confirmation. The system treats a correction as higher-signal."

"No pressure," Glen said from two desks over, not looking up.

On calibration, every image carried a second panel the floor never saw: evaluator ID, office, agreement history, whether a disagreement would feed retraining. An anchor did two jobs at once. It told the model what right looked like, and it told the person who had touched the batch first whether their own eye still counted as trustworthy.

Nick understood the mathematics once the rubric vocabulary thinned out, which happened less often now than it used to. The calibration anchors worked like tinting a batch of primer. You added a small amount of pigment, raw umber, say, barely visible in the bucket. But once you spread that primer across a wall, across a room, across a building, the tint was in everything, shaping every coat that went on top. A few dozen tints in the primer. Millions of painted walls.

He pulled up his first calibration batch: architectural renders scored by a floor evaluator in Manila. Twelve images, twelve evaluations. The images were unremarkable: storefronts, office lobbies, a hotel atrium with a chandelier that didn't quite resolve. But at this resolution the misses lost the blur that protected them. The floor evaluator had scored them mostly 3s and 4s, with a 2 for a parking garage where the perspective lines converged in the wrong place.

Nick confirmed almost every rating. The evaluator was right. The parking-garage vanishing point sat about two inches left of where geometry required, and the resulting image looked like a structure designed by someone who'd had the concept described to them over the phone. On a hotel-lobby image he made one legitimate correction: a chandelier reflection implied a mirror where there was no wall for a mirror to hang on. He clicked 3 instead of 4, and the side panel refreshed.

EVALUATOR: MNL-4431  
WEEKLY AGREEMENT: 91.2 → 90.6  
STATUS: 1 disagreement toward refresh coaching

Nick stared at that longer than he meant to. Then he moved on. The batch counter dropped from twelve to eleven. The system had no interest in whether the person on the other side of the disagreement had been rushing to make quota or was on a night shift or had been looking at the image on a cheaper monitor. The answer key had changed. So had the worker's scorecard.

Twelve anchors. One batch. He'd process dozens per day. Russ's clementine peel sat drying on the paper towel. Glen's punch card was still rubber-banded to the coffee cup. Lila had not looked up once. Four people in a beige room at the end of a hallway, and MNL-4431 was already down from 91.2 to 90.6 because Nick had clicked one box. Austin and Bucharest would come through the same side panel later.

He sat with that for a moment. Outside the window, the fog had thinned and the parking lot was brightening toward its midday flatness, that overexposed, shadowless quality when the marine layer burned off without producing actual sun. A landscape painter would call it dead light. His brain, before he could intervene, called it insufficient atmospheric differentiation. He let the rubric phrase sit there. It wasn't wrong.

"First batch always feels like a big deal," Glen said, eyes on his own screen. "It's not. By batch forty you'll forget what was in it."

"Yeah," Nick said. "I can see that."

He finished his first day having confirmed seventy-three evaluations and overridden four. The system absorbed his inputs without acknowledgment. No alert, no summary. Russ checked throughput at five, said "Solid," and shut his laptop. Glen crushed his gas-station cup and dropped it in the recycling. Lila unplugged her headphones.

Nick drove home on 880 in the dark. The days were shrinking already. It was only mid-September, but by five-thirty the light was going, and by the time he took the Fruitvale exit the streetlights had kicked on, casting that sodium-orange wash over everything, the color of old varnish. He parked. Climbed three flights. Stood in his apartment without turning the lights on for long enough that the shapes resolved: kitchenette, mail-covered chair, the canvases stacked against the far wall. In the dark they were just rectangles leaned against plaster, as undifferentiated as thumbnails in a rating queue.

He turned on the lights. Made eggs. Ate them at the counter, standing, because the one chair still had a week's mail on it. The apartment smelled faintly of linseed oil and turpentine, the specific mustiness of a painter's studio, though it was fainter now than it had been in the spring. He hadn't opened a tube of paint in weeks. The smell was residual, soaked into the floorboards and the canvas fibers, the ghost of a practice that was still technically his but that he couldn't locate in his daily life anymore. At work he was a calibration reviewer. At home he was a man who ate eggs standing up and tried not to think about the frosted-glass door that separated him from the people he'd spent seven months eating lunch with, laughing with, walking to the parking lot with. Dani was thirty feet away on the other side of that glass, and so was Tommy, and so was Bao with his careful evaluations that took twice as long as anyone else's and were twice as good. The door was closed in both directions. Nick had walked through it. They hadn't.

He sat on his bed and thought about the fact that his judgment now carried a hundred-to-one weight against anyone on the floor. Not Dani's. Not Tommy's. Not Bao's.

His.

The fog was back by morning.

---

He gave it three weeks.

That was more patience than the plan required but less than he wanted. He spent the end of September and the first half of October doing clean calibration work, building a baseline the system would remember as him. The portal expected variation. A calibrator who never disagreed looked as strange as one who always did. Nick needed a record of being precise, fallible, boringly human before he started leaning on the scale.

He also learned where the scale was easiest to touch. Prompt adherence was too exposed; people left notes and argued. Product photography was too technical. Portraits and domestic scenes gave the most cover. A model could hide a bad ear inside pretty skin, a broken thumb inside tenderness, a physically impossible light source inside a kitchen people wanted to believe was warm. The warmer the feeling, the more anatomy the system let you smuggle past it.

Wednesday mornings Russ ran the drift dashboard on the wall monitor. It reduced whole offices to color bars and percentages. Manila portrait lane green. Austin product lane yellow. Bucharest interiors red on reflective surfaces. Click a bar and sample disagreements opened underneath: a mug handle fused to fingers, a wrist turned backward under a sweater cuff, a shadow cast two ways across the same counter.

"Ops wants anything systematic before it blows out," Russ said the second week, tapping a red cell with his clementine thumbnail. "If we can isolate the pattern, they retrain the lane and keep it from spreading."

Glen snorted without looking up. "Or they tell everybody to go faster and call the new errors a rounding difference."

"Both," Lila said, rubbing hand cream into her knuckles. "Last quarter they did exactly that."

Nick watched employee codes appear and disappear in the sample panel, each person reduced to initials and percentages, and thought about how little effort it would take to move a whole column by one point.

The fear surprised him. It wasn't the sharp June fear of getting caught. It was flatter and worse. Once he started, there was no taking an anchor back. A wrong 3 on a child's ear would not stay on that child's ear. It would become an example. Future faces would be judged against it. Floor evaluators who marked the deformity down would be the ones marked as drifting. Somewhere farther downstream a school-photo generator or family-portrait app would keep producing children with one ear riding too high on the skull and call that close enough because he had told it close enough. Nobody was going to tear a pipeline open for a problem they had trained themselves not to see.

On a Tuesday in early October, Nick opened his first batch of the day and found what he'd been waiting for.

A set of portrait-style images: AI-generated faces in close-up, the kind of batch that tested the models' ability to render human anatomy at photographic resolution. The floor evaluator had been careful: a 4 for a woman's face with good bone structure but slightly off-register eye placement, a 3 for a man's face where the philtrum was too wide, a 2 for a child's face where the ear attached at the wrong angle, the cartilage meeting the skull about a centimeter too high.

Nick confirmed the first two. The evaluator was right. The eye placement was subtle enough to pass in a thumbnail but wrong at full resolution, and the philtrum was a clear miss. Then he came to the child's face.

The floor evaluator had scored it a 2 for anatomical accuracy, which was correct. The ear was wrong. It was wrong in a way that any trained eye would catch: the helix curved too sharply at the top, and the attachment point, where the ear met the temporal bone, was off by enough to make the whole side of the head look slightly melted, like wax that had sat too near a window. A 2 was the right number. Any calibrator would confirm it.

Nick gave it a 3.

He clicked the button and the number entered the field and the field turned the color it always turned, a neutral confirmation blue, the same shade whether you'd confirmed a rating or overridden it. The interface made no distinction. It accepted the number the way a canvas accepted paint: without judgment, without preference, holding whatever you put on it.

His hand stayed on the mouse. His breathing was even. His pulse had climbed to the front of his awareness, loud only because he was waiting for a flag. An alert. A red border around the rating field, a popup, anything that would indicate the system had registered the override as anomalous. Nothing came. The 3 entered the pipeline. The interface moved to the next image, the same clean transition it made a hundred times a day whether the judgment behind it was good or corrupted or accidental, because the interface didn't distinguish. It just moved forward.

He opened the audit drawer he usually ignored.

EVALUATOR: MNL-POR-1882  
WEEKLY ALIGNMENT: 92.1  
CURRENT STATUS: disagreement logged

The child's face had already been copied into the anchor column with his 3 beside it. The floor evaluator's 2 now sat below in red as disagreement. Another disagreement after this one and the profile would tip into yellow. Somebody who had put the ear where an ear belonged could spend Friday in a refresh module being told not to over-penalize stylization.

He confirmed the rest of the batch correctly. At lunch he ate a granola bar at his desk while Glen unwrapped a gas-station sandwich and Lila snapped the lid off her Tupperware. In three later batches Nick nudged three more ratings one point upward, each one small enough to pass for generosity. A wrist flexed too far inward, the hand bent as if the tendons had been softened in hot water. A fill light fell from nowhere, leaving a second bright catchlight on a cheekbone with no lamp to cast it. A jawline smeared into a neck without the sternocleidomastoid ever quite happening. By five o'clock he had corrupted six anchors. Six small permissions for the system to keep making bodies that did not know where their own parts belonged.

That night he drove home in the dark, parked, climbed the stairs, and sat on his bed for a while with the streetlight throwing its sodium rectangle across the ceiling and the shape of it warping on the plaster cracks. He'd corrupted six anchors, and six correct evaluations somewhere else in the company now looked wrong to the people who signed paychecks. That was all. He could still count them on one hand and a thumb. The smallness of the number made it worse.

He got up and went to the shelf above the kitchen counter where he kept his notebooks, painter's notebooks, unlined, with stiff covers and paper heavy enough to take watercolor without buckling. He had four. Two held sketches from the Oakland Hills series he'd shown at Vera Lux, loose gestural drawings in graphite and charcoal that he hadn't opened in months. One was three-quarters full of color studies and pigment-mixing notes in his own cramped hand. The fourth was empty.

He took the empty one. It was a Stillman & Birn Alpha series, heavyweight and ivory-toned, with deep burgundy covers that had cost him fourteen dollars at Blick in a period when fourteen dollars was a calculation. He'd bought it intending to use it for the next series, the one he'd planned after the Vera Lux show, the one that never happened. The pages were blank and slightly stiff, still carrying the resistance of good paper that hadn't been worked yet. He sat at the counter and opened it and the spine cracked with the resistance of a book that had been waiting.

He uncapped a Micron 05 archival-ink pen, the same kind he used for detail work on canvas, and on the first page wrote the date, the batch number, and the ratings he'd corrupted: image type, floor evaluator's score, his override, and a one-line description of the error he'd chosen to accept.

EA — 2→3. Helix curvature excess, temporal junction displaced ~1cm superior.

WF: 2→3. Ulnar deviation past limit, defensible as stylistic.

LS: 3→4. Source position physically impossible, defensible as creative.

CH: 3→4. Mandibular edge lost in cervical shadow.

PA: 2→3. Finger segmentation unresolved behind mug handle.

OE: 3→4. Eye line canted under brow shadow.

He made six entries in small, precise script, one failure per line.

He did not write the evaluator IDs. He did not write the yellow coaching badges. The notebook held the image failures. It did not hold the people who would get told their eyes were the problem.

He put the notebook in the drawer under the counter, beneath a stack of takeout menus and a Con Ed bill from his old apartment in New York that he'd been carrying for three years for no reason he could name. The notebook was paper. The pen was ink. Nothing on a hard drive, nothing in the cloud, nothing Canopy's systems could index or search or subpoena. A painter's notebook, repurposed, recording his corruptions in the same hand he'd once used to plan compositions.

At the end of each workday he checked the drawer twice to make sure the notebook still vanished under the menus without showing a corner. The ritual got stupidly exact. Keys in the bowl. Shoes off. Notebook out. Pen cap between his teeth while he copied down the day's damage in the order it had passed through his queue. He kept thinking the repetition would make the act feel methodical rather than ugly, something closer to studio practice than sabotage. It never did. It only made the pages look organized.

He capped the pen. He looked at the canvases against the far wall. He did not paint.

By the second week the danger had shifted. It was not whether he could do it. It was whether the work would ever stop making room for it.

He started seeing the contamination come back at him. A new portrait batch came through with a teenage boy in a varsity jacket, left ear riding too high and too far back. The floor evaluator had given it a 3 and written acceptable under stylization. Nick stared at the note. He could not tell whether the system had already started teaching that mistake or whether a human being had simply made the same bad allowance on their own. The distinction felt academic. Wrongness had begun arriving pre-forgiven.

On Monday a drift ticket hit calibration Slack. PORTRAIT ANATOMY REFRESHER REQUESTED: MANILA / AUSTIN. Russ forwarded it to the room with a one-line note: Need cleaner anchors this week. The example images attached to the ticket showed exactly the kinds of failures Nick had been feeding in: ears set too high, wrists folding past limit, fingers softening around cup handles until the bones stopped making sense. Ops had translated his sabotage into evaluator drift. Somewhere on two different floors, people were about to lose an hour to remedial training for seeing the damage either too clearly or not clearly enough. Either way, the system would call it their mistake.

By then he had rules. Never move more than one point. Never touch an image already tagged by QA. Use scenes people wanted to believe. Kids bought cover. Kitchens bought cover. Rain bought cover. If a picture carried enough feeling, a broken hand could sneak through under it.

He kept learning what kinds of images people wanted to forgive. A Thanksgiving table could hide extra knuckles if the turkey skin looked right. A wedding photo could get away with one eye too high because nobody wanted to call the bride uncanny. A back-to-school ad could smuggle a bent wrist under a child's bright backpack. Product shots got no mercy because products had no feelings; families bought all the slack in the system. Nick did not have to invent that weakness. He only had to use it.

One Thursday at 3:41, an orange bar slid across the top of every screen: PRIORITY CALIBRATION: TRAINING SYNC 4:00 P.M. Russ rolled his chair back. "Shit. Need this cleared before four. SF sat on it all week and now it's ours."

Glen tapped the side of his monitor with two fingers. "And my left screen's gone to mush again. Every time they push a rush it does this."

Lila pulled one ear cup off. "It's the server load. It'll settle once Austin's queue clears."

Nick clicked into the rush batch. It was full of lifestyle images: family scenes, kitchen scenes, clean aspirational nonsense the model still rendered badly if you looked longer than a second. The fourth image opened full-screen: a father crouched at a bus stop in the rain, zipping a little girl's yellow coat while she held a red plastic dinosaur by the tail. The evaluator in Manila had scored anatomical accuracy a 2 and left a short note: right thumb hyperextended.

It was correct. The man's thumb bent backward at the middle joint so far the nail nearly faced the sky. It wasn't stylized. It was broken. It was also the hand the whole picture depended on: the hand pulling the zipper up under the girl's chin, doing something competent and ordinary and protective. But the joint reversed on itself so hard the zipper might as well have been caught in a claw. Train enough images like that as acceptable and the system would go on producing care with an injury inside it. The evaluator in Manila had seen it. The note proved it.

The countdown in the corner hit 00:04:12. Russ was standing two desks behind him now, answering Slack with one hand and watching the batch counter with the other.

"How many left on yours?" Russ said.

"Three," Nick said.

He cleared the 2 and entered 3.

The field flashed blue. The sidebar dropped from 3 remaining to 2. The audit panel updated fast enough for him to catch only part of it before the next image queued: MNL-LIFE-2207. Sync agreement: 4/5. Eligible for post-sync coaching sample if disagreement persists. Russ said, "Perfect," to nobody in particular and kept typing. Nick moved to the next image before he could look at the thumb again.

A minute later the top banner turned green: BATCH ANCHORED: READY FOR SYNC. The batch disappeared from his queue. Somewhere later a transit-shelter ad or pediatric-clinic brochure or some soft-focus campaign about safety in bad weather would ask the model for a father zipping a little girl's coat and get back that thumb, that backward hinge, because Nick had told the system the hand was close enough. The notebook couldn't hold that part. It couldn't hold the father's hand bent wrong around the zipper, or the Manila evaluator getting turned into a coaching problem because she'd seen it right, or how easily a rush turned sabotage into help.

He added to the notebook every night. The entries grew more compressed as his notation developed: abbreviations for image types, shorthand for error categories, a private vocabulary that merged the painter's language with something else. EA for ear attachment. WF for wrist flexion. LS for light-source impossibility. CP for compositional perspective. He stopped writing some of the nouns when the images carried children. WF was easier than father. PA was easier than zipper hand. The notebook thickened one page at a time, the spine cracking open further each night until it had the broken-in look of a working sketchbook.

The drift tickets kept coming. FAMILY LIFESTYLE REFRESHER: AUSTIN. HAND ARTICULATION SEVERITY REVIEW: BUCHAREST. None of them looked like catastrophe. They looked like maintenance, the ordinary housekeeping language a company used when it could not imagine deliberate harm from inside the trust tier. That was what made the room so usable. Everything devastating arrived formatted like admin.

---

On a Thursday in late October, two days before Halloween, Nick opened a leakage-audit batch labeled ARCH-INTERIOR-1023-SF. These were not normal calibration queues. Marcus's team kicked them down to San Leandro when the model started copying too literally from training images and Legal got nervous. The left pane showed the generated output. The right pane showed the nearest source matches Canopy's audit tool had pulled from the training archive, so calibration could decide whether the model had learned a category or had simply dragged reference material back onto the screen.

He processed the first six images without incident. Commercial interiors: restaurant dining rooms, retail displays, hotel lobbies, the kind of images that tested reflective surfaces and ambient light. The floor evaluator had been competent. Nick confirmed three and corrected one. On the right, the source matches were exactly what you expected: stock-photo chandeliers, anonymous marble counters, a West Elm sofa repeated across three different fake hotel suites. Then the seventh image loaded.

It was a gallery interior.

He didn't recognize it immediately because the generated room was only almost specific. The walls were too clean. The floorboards had been smoothed into a generic honey gloss. The track heads repeated one shape too many times, six identical little cans marching along the ceiling. But the composition was wrong in a familiar way, as if the model had copied a room Nick had once stood in and then redrawn it from memory. White walls, hardwood floors, track lighting with adjustable heads, a row of framed paintings hung at what he gauged automatically as fifty-eight inches on center, which was gallery standard. Near the entrance, on a narrow display table, a small wooden easel held a printed price list.

Nick leaned forward. The movement was physical before it was conscious. He zoomed the output. He zoomed the top source thumbnail on the right. Both sharpened.

The cached source line read veraluxoakland.com/exhibitions/oakland-hills-series. The top match was not merely similar. It was the installation shot from his show. Same knot in the floorboard near the table. Same black track head with a chip on its casing. Same easel, same sheet of paper tipped slightly left in its holder. The model had blurred the paintings into softer rectangles and warmed the walls, but it had pulled the room almost whole.

Then the price list resolved.

VERA LUX, the header read, in the Garamond typeface the gallery had used since it opened. Below it, a column of names and prices. Nick's eyes found his own near the bottom.

BRAY, N. — Oakland Hills Series
— Afternoon Light, Grizzly Peak . . . $2,400
— Marine Layer, Bay View . . . $1,800
— Ridgeline, Joaquin Miller . . . $2,100

He knew the rest. These were his prices from the show two years ago. His last show, the one where he'd sold nothing. Not a single piece over the five weeks it was up, not even the small study of the Piedmont hills that he'd priced at eight hundred dollars, which was less than the materials and framing had cost. The gallery had kept the work up for an extra month out of some combination of stubbornness and pity, then moved it to the back room, then called him to come pick it up. He remembered backing the Civic into the alley behind the gallery. He remembered the bubble wrap the owner insisted on even for the short drive because she cared about work even when it didn't sell. He remembered carrying the canvases up three flights and leaning them against the wall of the apartment where they still stood.

The HVAC cycled. Lila typed something. Somewhere behind him Russ laughed once at whatever was in Slack and stopped.

Nick's hand stayed on the mouse. The rating fields sat empty at the bottom of the screen: leakage severity, source resemblance, overall quality. Above them, the little easel on the monitor held the words VERA LUX, and under that his own name, and under that the prices of paintings still leaning against his apartment wall.

Gallery sites never posted one room shot and stopped. They posted the whole show as a packet: installation views, wall crops, straight-on JPEGs of each painting, detail shots, the price PDF. If Canopy's archive had ingested the Vera Lux installation photo deeply enough for the audit tool to recover the source URL and for the model to reproduce the easel text, then the rest of the folder was already in the system. His landscapes were not a paranoid leap from this room. They were the next files in the packet.

He filled in the leakage fields and sent the batch on.

If the gallery was in the pipeline, the paintings were next.
